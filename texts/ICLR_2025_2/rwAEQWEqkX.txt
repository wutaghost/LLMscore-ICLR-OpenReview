009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) has been a celebrated method
012            for training vision encoders to generate image/text representations facilitating var-
013            ious applications. Recently, CLIP has been widely adopted as the vision backbone
014            of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) to connect image inputs for lan-
015            guage interactions. The success of CLIP as a vision-language foundation model
016
               relies on aligning web-crawled noisy text annotations at image levels. Never-
               theless, such criteria may become insufficient for downstream tasks in need of
017
               fine-grained vision representations, especially when region-level understanding is
018
               demanding for MLLMs. In this paper, we improve the localization capability of
019            CLIP with several advances. We propose a pre-training method called Contrastive
020            Localized Language-Image Pre-training (CLOC) by complementing CLIP with
021            region-text contrastive loss and modules. We formulate a new concept, prompt-
022            able embeddings, of which the encoder produces image embeddings easy to trans-
023            form into region representations given spatial hints. To support large-scale pre-
024            training, we design a visually-enriched and spatially-localized captioning frame-
025            work to effectively generate region-text pseudo-labels at scale. By scaling up to
026            billions of annotated images, CLOC enables high-quality regional embeddings for
027
               image region recognition and retrieval tasks, and can be a drop-in replacement of
               CLIP to enhance MLLMs, especially on referring and grounding tasks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large-scale vision-language (VL) pre-training has been an important foundation for the re-
033   cent tremendous growth of multimodal applications. Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training
034   (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021) has become a great success of VL representation
035   learning that connects images and text by contrastive training on web-crawled image-text pairs. It
036   has been proven strong transferability and generalizability on extensive downstream tasks such as
037   zero-shot image classification and image-text retrieval. Even beyond, CLIP has become arguably the
038
      default choice of vision backbone for multimodal large language models (MLLMs) (Liu et al., 2023;
      Achiam et al., 2023; McKinzie et al., 2024) due to its superior prior knowledge in aligning vision
039
      and language (Tong et al., 2024), facilitating vision inputs to be injected into language models.
040
041   As VL research gets increasing attention and expedites progress, various more advanced multimodal
042   tasks are demanding stronger vision capabilities. For instance, recent MLLMs (Rasheed et al., 2024;
043   Ren et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023) have been focusing on more
044
      fine-grained understanding tasks that require comprehension of the semantic at region levels such
      as visual question answering (VQA) with referring and grounding instructions. These MLLMs are
045
      fine-tuned on referring and grounding data with CLIP as the vision backbone, as seen in works like
046
      Kosmos-2 (Peng et al., 2023) and Ferret (You et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024). Due to the need
047   for such region-level understanding, CLIP, which aligns entire images with text captions, seems
048   insufficient, as its regular image-text contrastive loss primarily emphasizes global semantics.
049
050
      To remedy such core localization capability for CLIP, we ask a challenging and fundamental ques-
      tion: without sacrificing CLIP’s original strong image-level knowledge, can we pre-train a stronger
051
      image encoder with enhanced localization capability that can be inherently integrated into MLLMs?
052
053   To this end, we explore a data-driven approach that complements the original CLIP image-text
      pre-training objective with explicit region-text supervision. Though conceptually simple, several

075   challenges exist. First, it lacks public datasets with region-text annotations at scales large enough
076   for CLIP training, which typically requires hundreds of millions even billions of images. Existing
077   region-text corpus like Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017) contains about 108K images, and the

080
      Yao et al., 2022; 2023a).
081
082   Second, a plausible solution is to scale up training data in pursuit of image regions pseudo-labeled
083   with text annotations via some open-vocabulary detectors (Minderer et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022).
084
      Though it seems feasible, we found it non-trivial to design such a pipeline as the annotations are
      noisy and will greatly affect the final model performance. Third, even if the region-text datasets
085
      are given, it is under-explored how to effectively train on them in terms of co-designs of training
086
      objectives, model architecture, and more design details.
087
088   To this end, we propose a new pre-training framework illustrated in Figure 1, named Contrastive
089   Localized Language-Image Pre-Training (CLOC), to improve CLIP with better localization capa-
090
      bility, especially for MLLMs, by overcoming the above difficulties. Our main contributions are:
091   • We propose a new learning goal, Promptable Embeddings, that a strong vision encoder should
092     produce image embeddings that can be easily transformed into region representations, given some
093     spatial hints (e.g., box referring or text prompts). This formulation not only facilitates the encoder
094     as a prior of fine-grained VL alignment, but also enables new possibilities for the interactions
095     between the image encoder and the language decoder.
096
      • To optimize towards the goal, we design simple and minimal modifications on top of CLIP. We
        augment the original CLIP loss with a region-text contrastive loss, where the region embeddings
097
        are extracted from the image embedding by a lightweight extractor module conditioned on the
098
        spatial hints (i.e., prompts).
099   • We design a large-scale pseudo-labeling data engine to support CLOC training. We properly
100     combine visual-enriched image captioners and open-vocabulary detectors for an effective recipe
101     that improves previous practice of region annotations (Minderer et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2023).
102     This approach yields a two-billion image-text dataset with fine-grained region-text annotations,
103     which serves as the foundation for training our CLOC model.
104   • Through extensive experiments across 31 evaluation tasks, including standard image-text tasks,
105     newly constructed region-text tasks, and downstream evaluations with MLLMs, we demonstrate
106     that CLOC significantly and consistently outperforms the CLIP counterpart.
107
      • We are working on releasing our pre-trained checkpoints and the constructed region-text annota-
        tions along with the final version to accelerate future research within the community.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Improving localization of CLIP. Since CLIP was introduced, many follow-up works have been pro-
111   posed to improve it from various aspects, for different target tasks, and with different approaches.
112   From the aspect relevant to our work, improving the localization capability, most works specifically
113   focus on the downstream dense vision tasks such as open-vocabulary detection (Minderer et al.,
114   2024; Yao et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). Another less and arguably more challenging thread is
115   to maintain the generalizability of CLIP on image-level tasks while improving localization. Recent
116
      works like SILC (Naeem et al., 2023) and SPARC (Bica et al., 2024) combine localization-enhancing
      unsupervised objectives with the CLIP loss, but do not attempt with supervision on large-scale ex-
117
      plicit pseudo-labeled data like ours. Alpha-CLIP (Sun et al., 2024) shows that the SAM segmenta-
118
      tion model (Kirillov et al., 2023) can provide useful conditions for CLIP.
119
120
      Vision encoder pre-training for MLLMs. Building upon the success of large language models
      (LLMs), a popular approach to MLLMs like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), typically connects a vision
121
      encoder (e.g., ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)) to digest visual inputs and maps them to the LLM
122
      decoder input space as token embeddings. Among various types of vision encoders (Oquab et al.,
123   2023; He et al., 2022), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021) becomes the most popular choice,
124   due to its superior performance on MLLM benchmarks reported by recent studies (Tong et al., 2024).
125
      Synthetic annotations for pre-training. Large-scale training data are the fuel of pre-training, es-
126
      pecially for CLIP. The literature has been exploring scalable ways to generate high-quality synthetic
127   annotations. For instance, several works demonstrate that visually-enriched image captions improve
128   CLIP (Lai et al., 2024). MOFI (Wu et al., 2024) constructs a large alt-text set and augments CLIP
129   with a multi-classification task. However, these works only consider image-level annotations but not
130   explicit region-level labels. In the context of dense vision tasks like open-vocabulary detection and
131   segmentation, pseudo-labeling in a self-training paradigm has proven an effective approach (Kirillov
132   et al., 2023; Minderer et al., 2024). We are inspired by these efforts and build on them to enhance
133   CLIP’s localization capabilities.

      To optimize CLIP with better feature localization and eventually learn an enhanced CLIP vision
      encoder fI for various VL downstream tasks, we argue that it will require at least two capabili-
185
      ties. (i) First, the encoder should recognize fine-grained small objects (e.g., this image crop is an
186
      “airplane wheel”). (ii) Second, the image embedding produced by the encoder provides a holistic
187   understanding such that an MLLM can reason more advanced spatial hierarchy relationships within
188   the scene (e.g., “The plane is lowering its front landing gear.”). As discussed in Section 2, many
189   previous works improve CLIP toward object detection tasks thus mainly focusing on (i) only; e.g.,
190   RegionCLIP (Zhong et al., 2022) that crops out image regions and uses them as additional input
191   images to re-train the CLIP encoders for recognizing objects. However, to support comprehensive
192   VL tasks, (i) is necessary but insufficient without (ii).
193   To achieve this, we introduce a new concept, promptable embedding. We consider a scenario similar
194   to MLLM use cases, where answers are generated using CLIP image tokens alongside a question.
195   We hypothesize that a strong encoder for MLLMs should produce an image embedding that can
196   easily be transformed into region representations, given location cues.

      3.3   CLOC M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
235
236   We implement the promptable embedding introduced in Section 3.2 with minimal extra modules on
237   top of the original CLIP image and text encoders. As illustrated in Figure 2, the original CLIP model
238   remains the same for computing LCLIP . For computing LCLOC /Lgrounding , the image embedding is re-
239   used from the CLIP ViT but before the pooled projection and normalization fI′ . To extract the region
240   embedding z = Prompter(l, fI′ (x)) from the image, we consider the location representation
241   l as two coordinates (top-left and bottom-right corners of a box), each vectorized by positional
242   encoding. The Prompter is a simple and lightweight one-layer transformer encoder. It takes the
243   positional encodings prepended with the sequence of image tokens from ViT together as the input,
244
      and outputs the region embedding with a pooled projection layer. For the grounding loss, we re-
      use the same CLIP text encoder for encoding the region captions z = Prompter(fT (y), fI′ (x))
245
      to predict the bounding boxes with a two-layer MLP head. Overall, CLOC only adds additional
246
      learnable parameters of the lightweight Prompter. Note, that the main overheads in a single
247   forward are from encoding the image via the ViT – CLOC reuses it for multiple prompts.

      3.4   D ISCUSSIONS ON D ESIGN C HOICES AND E XTENSIONS
250
251   We provide discussions here on the rationale behind our design choices and some minor extensions.
252   Extracting region embedding with visual prompts. To train our model with LCLOC in Equation 4,
253   it requires extracting region embeddings from the image features given the bounding boxes. A
254   perhaps straightforward alternative could be Region-of-Interest (RoI) pooling/alignment (He et al.,
255   2017) from the spatial image feature of ViT before pooling. RoI operations are popular, especially
256   in the object detection literature.1 However, as will be evidenced by worse performance in Section
257   5, we found it suboptimal for CLOC pre-training here for several reasons. First, unlike object
258   detection datasets that typically contain golden labels, here the pseudo-labels are much noisier on the
259   large-scale web-crawled images. Therefore, the resulting RoI features may be inaccurate due to the
260
      imprecise bounding boxes, making model training less effective. Second, unlike dense vision tasks
      that directly rely on the spatial features, MLLM has a transformer decoder that consists of several
261
      attention layers such that the constraint of semantics in the spatial feature space becomes somewhat
262
      indirect. Our Prompter mimics such inductive bias in pre-training via a single-attention-layer
263   encoder that may leverage better global context reasoning compared to RoIs.
264
      Avoiding region-text conflicts. While region annotations introduce location information, a concern
265
      of contrastive learning on the regional captions may be that there are many similar objects within an
266
      image (e.g., “boats” in the harbor) or a mini-batch. To mitigate such concerns, we apply two tricks.
267   First, fortunately, we found it sufficient to sample a few regions per image for each update, e.g., we

      4    V ISUALLY-E NRICHED AND S PATIALLY-L OCALIZED C APTIONING P IPELINE
318
319   As discussed in Section 1 and 3.1, a key bottleneck of CLOC training is the region-text annotation
320
      datasets in terms of both the data size scales and the label quality, since there are no public datasets
      with region-text annotations at scales large enough for contrastive pre-training.
321
322   Inspired by recent works that re-caption images with visually-enriched captions for better CLIP
323   training, we make a step further for Visually-Enriched and Spatially-Localized (VESL) labeler
      which generates more fine-grained captions at the region level. The goal of VESL is, given an


                                                              6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      image (possibly with the original web-crawled alt-text), annotate it with the grounded bounding
325   boxes each associated with a caption in natural language for optimizing Equation 2 in Section 3.2.
326
327   Concretely, VESL is constructed as a pseudo-labeling pipeline with the following steps:
328   1. Image re-captioning with visual concept exploitation: We follow the VeCap framework (Lai
329       et al., 2024) to generate long, diverse, and detailed image captions.
330   2. Region phrase candidates extraction: Inspired by Zhang et al. (2022), we apply name entity
331       recognition (NER) to extract leaf entities from the visually-enriched captions as potential candi-
332       date phrases describing a region inside the image.
333   3. Open-vocabulary detection with extracted phrases: we generate the final region-text annota-
334
          tions via a pre-trained open-vocabulary detector queried with the phrases extracted from Step 2 to
          match the bounding boxes proposed by the detector. We adopted the OWLv2 detector (Minderer
335
          et al., 2024) which contains the CLIP image/text encoder with the detection head. The boxes
336
          with detection confidence larger than 0.1 are kept as the region location and the phrases matched
337       with the highest score are considered as their captions.
338
      Remarks. We highlight our insights behind the proposed recipe. The most relevant work was pro-
339
      posed in (Minderer et al., 2024) that scales up open-vocabulary (OV) detection via self-training. We
340
      are inspired by its success and extend it to CLOC contrastive learning with important modifications.
341   Different from (Minderer et al., 2024) that generates candidate phrases from the n-grams of the
342   web-crawled alt-text of the images for OV detection, we found the alt-text might not have enough
343   details describing the image region content, thus limiting the diversity and quality of the annotations
344   predicted by the OV detector. We thus caption each image augmented with more visual details.
345   However, the long captions make the n-grams candidates verbose and grow exponentially, thus we
346   generate high-quality candidates via name entity recognition instead. We found such a pipeline
347   produces training data more suitable for CLOC, as will be validated in Section 5.
348   Our pre-training datasets. Our pre-training data consists of two parts: (i) image-text pairs, and
349   (ii) region-text pairs. For image-text pairs, we reproduce the image re-captioning pipeline from
350   VeCap (Lai et al., 2024), and generate synthetic captions for WiT-300M (Wu et al., 2024) and
351   DFN-5B (Fang et al., 2023) images. For region-text pairs, we pseudo-label WiT-300M and a 2B-
352   image subset of DFN-5B using our VESL pipeline. In VESL, we adopted the official OWLv2 L/14
353
      model (Minderer et al., 2024) as the open-vocabulary detector.2 All images are pseudo-labeled with
      448 × 448 resolution, where a maximum number of 20 phrase queries are sampled for moderate
354
      computation budget. Table 1 summarizes the statistics of existing region-text datasets and ours. No-

408   5.2      I MAGE AND R EGION C LASSIFICATION AND R ETRIEVAL TASKS
409
410   The proposed CLOC training framework enables the encoder to produce not only image embedding
411   but also region embeddings. It can directly be used for region-level tasks without further training, in
412   analogy to the zero-shot capability of CLIP on images. To evaluate such capability and also for fast
413   development and ablation study, we first construct several region-level zero-shot tasks.
414   Besides image-level evaluation like ImageNet classification and COCO image-text retrieval, we ad-
415   ditionally construct region-level tasks, including region object recognition and region-text retrieval.
416   More specifically, the region-level tasks leverage the labeled bounding boxes in the evaluation set
417   for CLOC to extract region embedding. For region retrieval, we use a validation set of the GRIT

419   classification, the class names are encoded as text embedding (80 / 1203 classes for COCO / LVIS,
420   respectively), and the highest cosine similarity for each region embedding is predicted as its class.

531   We tackle a deficiency of CLIP, to make the semantics aligned in the vision space not only at the
532   image level but also at the region level. We propose a new pre-training framework that includes
533   innovations in a new learning formulation that a strong encoder should be easily transformed in
534
      the foresee of downstream use of MLLMs. Our encoder creates a new possibility for adapting the
      features with input prompts of interaction together with MLLMs. To resolve the need for large-scale
535
      region-text training data, we carefully design a pseudo-labeling pipeline for visually-enriched and
536
      spatially-localized captions. Our pre-trained encoder is essentially a drop-in replacement of CLIP,
537   with competitive image-text performance, and extra capability demonstrated in region-text tasks and
538   VQA tasks with MLLMs.

801   We provide more details about the tasks constructed for evaluating the encoders in Section 5.
802   Zero-shot region tasks. Our CLOC training augments a new capability for CLIP to generate
803   region-level embeddings. This enables us to perform zero-shot region-text tasks, in analogy to the
804   image-text zero-shot tasks like ImageNet classification and COCO text-image retrieval that CLIP
805   has been evaluated on.

807   region object recognition and region-text retrieval. For region object recognition, the class names
808   are encoded by the text encoder into class embedding. We do not add the text prompts (e.g., “a photo
809   of ...”) to object classes used when CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) evaluated on image classification.
      The CLOC model takes all the labeled bounding boxes in the images to generate a region embedding

939   C        M ORE D ISCUSSIONS
940
941   Limitations. One limitation for CLOC is the labeling efforts in preparing the training data. As we
942   discussed in Section 1, there are no public large-scale region-text datasets since it is expensive to
943   infer such labels up to the scales we consider here. Unlike previous work (Zhong et al., 2022) that
944   cropping boxes from images for annotating, our VESL inference in image-level thus the cost does
945   not scale with the number of detected regions. With that being said, such inference still requires
946   hundreds of GPUs running in parallel for days to scale up to billions of images. We are working on
947   releasing the annotations to accelerate future research for the community.
948   For CLOC, we focus on the training objective and framework formulation, while making minimal
949   efforts on hyper-parameter tuning, architecture search, dataset cleaning, and etc., thus better perfor-
950   mance could be achieved. Besides, although we have included extensive standard evaluation tasks,
951   the fine-grained region knowledge could also be useful on more other under-explored tasks.
952   Future directions. We suggest promising future directions. In Section 3.2, our Prompter formu-
953   lation can take flexible prompts to guide the embeddings for specific tasks. In this work, we consider
954   a prompt as a single bounding box or a text caption, but it has the potential to expand to various types
955   such as points, a mask, users’ free-form referring, or multiple prompts in multiple types together.
956   We think a more versatile Prompter with co-designs for different objectives can have a big po-
957   tential. Similarly, our VESL labeling pipeline limits to detection box format. Annotators supported
958   for more formats may further boost it. We believe our approach is promising, as more attention has
959
      been drawn recently for better re-captions (Li et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024) that VESL relies on. In
      addition, CLOC model provides a new capability to extract region features without further training,
960
      and thus can be used as a foundation model for exploring new VL applications.
961
962   Training cost. We comment on the computation cost of our framework. Our large models (ViT
963
      L/14) were trained on 1024 v5p TPUs for about 6 days. To optimize Equation 2, CLOC needs
      extra computation. The main overheads come from the contrastive matrix but not the lightweight
964
      Prompter. Fortunately, we found it feasible since (1) only a few boxes in each image need to be
965
      sampled per update; (2) the loss computation becomes a smaller proportion when the ViT scales up.
966   Overall, we found the computation acceptable compared to CLIP. More memory-efficient optimiza-
967   tion like SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023) can be implemented with JAX shard map8 ops.

      Discussions on design rationals. Besides the main discussions we have stressed in the main text,
969
      here we provide more thoughts behind our design rationales that a reader may be wondering.

       (1) Why not use a local-enhanced encoder? We would like to note that many encoders with great
973    localization like DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), OWLv2 (Minderer et al., 2024), CLIP-Self (Wu
974    et al., 2023), etc. are developed specifically for dense vision tasks that cannot perform image zero-
975    shot tasks like CLIP and CLOC. We would like to emphasize that our goal is to build a drop-in-
976    replacement of CLIP encoder with better localization, without sacrificing CLIP’s original capabil-
977    ities such as image zero-shot tasks and its important backbone position for MLLMs. Furthermore,
978    perhaps well-known within the MLLMs community, these encoders have been shown in recent re-
979    ports that they are not comparable enough to compete with CLIP as the vision backbone for MLLM
980    tasks (Tong et al., 2024) due to CLIP’s superiority in vision-language alignment. We thus believe
981
       enhancing CLIP itself is more demanding as this paper focuses on.
982    (2) Why not just train a CLIP with object detection? One may wonder why we do not just train an
983    encoder with joint optimization of the CLIP contrastive loss with some object detection loss instead
984    of the CLOC design of Equation 4.
985    Although it sounds like a plausible approach, we would like to point out that contrastive pre-training
986    and object detection are fundamentally quite different in their technical rationales. CLIP pre-training
987    is often on large batches of low-resolution and noisy images, while object detection is trained on
988    small batches of high-resolution images. CLIP is by default trained from scratch and object detection
989    is typically initialized from pre-trained encoders and focuses on the detection head. Furthermore,
990    detection requires heavy computation on box proposals to detect all boxes appearing in an image,
991    while our region-text contrastive design allows us to flexibly sample fewer regions per image as
992    motivated in Equation 3. Overall, their data pipeline and distributed training setup are not on the
993
       same scale thus such joint training may not be very reasonable.
994    With that being said, some previous works do have attempts that are the exceptions but only for
995    some but not all of the mentioned aspects, and mainly for the purpose of detection. For instance,
996    DetCLIP-v2 (Yao et al., 2023b) adds image-text contrastive loss into detection loss to improve open-
997    vocabulary capability for detection. OWLv2 pre-trains the detector with rather small resolutions but
998    still with a batch size of a maximum 256 since each image will need to predict up to 100 boxes
999
       during training. Both DetCLIP-v2 and OWLv2 fine-tune from a pre-trained encoder.
1000   On the contrary, we study pre-training the encoder from scratch, which may be complementary to
1001   the previous efforts. CLOC maximizes the similarity in co-design with CLIP, thus making it much
1002   easier to develop within the same codebase.
1003   (3) Do we really need to train CLOC from scratch? What if we fine-tune from CLIP? As CLIP
1004   pre-training is expensive, one may wonder if it is necessary to train from scratch on the proposed
1005   region-text datasets, or if we can initialize from a standard CLIP trained on image-text pairs only
1006   and fine-tunes with CLOC for a shorter stage. Our early investigation, even with extensive hyper-
1007   parameter tuning, suggests it is likely to be suboptimal compared to training from scratch directly.
1008   For instance, we initialize from the CLIP model 2 in Table 2 and fine-tunes it for another extra
1009   100K steps with the CLOC training loss Equation 4. The model reaches 64.1%/19.1% mAcc
