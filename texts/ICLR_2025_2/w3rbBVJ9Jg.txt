009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Simulation of spatiotemporal systems governed by partial differential equations is
012            widely applied in fields such as biology, chemistry, aerospace dynamics, and me-
013            teorology. Traditional numerical methods incur high computational costs due to
014            the requirement of small time steps for accurate predictions. While machine learn-
015            ing has reduced these costs, long-term predictions remain challenged by error ac-
016            cumulation, particularly in scenarios with insufficient data or varying time scales,
017            where stability and accuracy are compromised. Existing methods often neglect the
018
               effective utilization of multi-scale data, leading to suboptimal robustness in pre-
               dictions. To address these issues, we propose a novel multi-scale learning frame-
019
               work, namely, the Physics-Informed Multi-Scale Recurrent Learning (PIMRL),
020
               to effectively leverage multi-scale data for spatiotemporal dynamics prediction.
021            The PIMRL framework comprises two modules: the micro-scale module em-
022            beds physical knowledge into neural networks via pretraining, and the macro-scale
023            module adopts a data-driven approach to learn the temporal evolution of physics
024            in the latent space. Experimental results demonstrate that the PIMRL framework
025            consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance across five benchmark datasets
026            ranging from one to three dimensions, showing average improvements of over
027            9% in both RMSE and MAE evaluation metrics, with maximum enhancements
028            reaching up to 80%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In the field of natural sciences, physical systems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs)
      have found widespread applications across disciplines including biology, chemistry, meteorology,
034
      etc. (Anderson & Wendt, 1995; Blazek, 2015; Moukalled et al., 2016; Karniadakis & Sherwin,
035
      2005; Zienkiewicz et al., 2005). Although numerical methods have been regarded as reliable tools
036   for modeling these systems, the use of Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) faces significant hurdles
037   due to inherent limitations. DNS necessitates high spatial resolution and fine time stepping, resulting
038   in considerable computational demands and prolonged processing times. A case in point is the
039   simulation of aerodynamic flows around aircraft, which typically requires the generation of millions
040   of grid points, thereby imposing prohibitive computational requirements (Ahmad, 2013; Goc et al.,
041   2021). Moreover, these numerical simulation methods require complete physical prior knowledge,
042   such as PDE formula, parameters, and initial/boundary conditions (Ferziger et al., 2019).
043   The ongoing development of artificial intelligence (AI) has propelled research into data-driven sim-
044   ulation methods, showcasing significant potential (Lu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Stachenfeld et al.,
045   2021). These methods, which do not require prior physics knowledge, offer user-friendly solutions.
046   They also overcome traditional constraints on resolution and small time stepping, ensuring accurate
047   solutions. Nevertheless, when faced with sparse data and multi-scale temporal challenges, these
048   methods often struggle to optimally utilize data. They may either discard micro-scale data in favor
049   of macro-scale data or vice versa, leading to compromised accuracy.
050   Multi-scale Burst sampling is a technique capturing multiple samples at a high rate over a short pe-
051   riod of time to record rapidly changing events or transient phenomena, e.g., fast dynamics, followed
052   by a low sampling rate to capture slow dynamics (see Figure 1). Compared to traditional continuous
053   sampling methods, it provides high-resolution data at critical moments while maintaining resource
      efficiency. But conventional uniform-scale models struggle to fully utilize such data. The latent

082

083

084   core idea, we are motivated to leverage comprehensive multi-scale data through a novel Physics-
085   Informed Multi-Scale Recurrent Learning (PIMRL) framework to tackle the problems mentioned
086   above. This framework consists of macro-scale and micro-scale modules. The micro-scale mod-
087   ule, pretrained to learn the underlying physical laws, enhances the accuracy of simulations. The
088   macro-scale module reduces the accumulation of errors by minimizing the number of rollout itera-
089   tions for the micro-scale module, thereby enhancing long-term predictive performance. The main
090   contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
091
             • We proposed a new PIMRL framework that effectively leverages information from multi-
092
               scale data for long-term spatiotemporal dynamics prediction. The concept of reducing the
093            accumulation of errors is achieved through the integration of the macro-scale and micro-
094            scale modules.
095
             • We designed a novel message passing mechanism between micro- and macro-scale mod-
096            ules, which effectively transmits physical information, enhances the micro-module’s cor-
097            rection capability, and reduces the number of micro-scale rollout iterations through the
098            macro-scale module.
099          • The PIMRL model achieved optimal performance in effectively predicting the duration of
100            multiple different cases from fluid dynamics to physical systems, demonstrating its scala-
101            bility and laying a solid foundation for more generalizable models in the future.

104   2   R ELATED W ORK
105
106   Simulation tasks often aim to solve partial differential equations (PDEs) accurately and efficiently.
107   Previous researchers have developed numerical methods achieving high precision with many nodes
      and short time steps. To speed up simulations, deep learning techniques have evolved from apply-

108
      ing conventional algorithms to designing new models that integrate physical knowledge, including
109   hybrid methods and models embedding physical principles.
110
111   Computational fluid dynamics. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is a branch of fluid me-
112
      chanics that uses numerical methods and algorithms to analyze and solve problems involving fluid
      flow (Anderson & Wendt, 1995; Blazek, 2015; Moukalled et al., 2016; Karniadakis & Sherwin,
113
      2005; Zienkiewicz et al., 2005). When faced with complex problems, unacceptable time and com-
114
      putational costs are the primary obstacles limiting the application of CFD. Moreover, when the
115   corresponding physical equations contain unknown parameters or even unknown terms, numerical
116   methods are unable to perform accurate simulations. The aforementioned methods have inspired us
117   to leverage physical knowledge. However, to overcome the aforementioned issues, we have decided
118   to introduce deep learning methods into our PIMRL framework.
119
      Deep learning methods. With the advancement of AI, the application of AI in physical system sim-
120
      ulation tasks has become more diverse and profound. For example, classical convolutional neural
121   network models (Stachenfeld et al., 2021; Bar-Sinai et al., 2019) and ResNet (Lu et al., 2018), U-Net
122   (Gupta & Brandstetter, 2023) what originally used for image segmentation, graph neural networks
123   (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Pfaff et al., 2020), and transformer-based models (Wu et al., 2024;
124   Hang et al., 2024; Janny et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024) are now being employed. Neural operators used
125   for learning mappings between function spaces have also seen significant development in physical
126   simulation tasks, such as DeepONet (Lu et al., 2021), MWT (Gupta et al., 2021), FNO (Li et al.,
127   2020; Tran et al., 2021; Rahman et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2022), etc. In addition, there are several
128   methods specifically designed for spatiotemporal prediction tasks, such as ConvLSTM (Shi et al.,
129
      2015), PredRNN (Wang et al., 2022), and TrajGRU (Shi et al., 2017). The traditional deep learning
      methods mentioned above all require sufficient data for training; insufficient data can lead to poor
130
      model performance and severe overfitting issues. Moreover, error accumulation is another inevitable
131
      problem that limits the application of traditional deep learning methods. These limitations restrict
132   the effectiveness and reliability of such models, especially in scenarios requiring long-term predic-
133   tions or when data is scarce. The challenges outlined above have led us to propose a framework that
134   effectively controls error accumulation and adeptly processes multi-scale data.
135
      Physics-informed deep learning methods. To incorporate physical information into models, re-
136
      searchers have devised various methods. One category includes physics-inspired methods like Phy-
137
      CRNet (Ren et al., 2022), PINN (Raissi et al., 2019a), and PhySR (Ren et al., 2023). Another
138   category involves physical embedding methods, where explicit physics knowledge is embedded into
139   the model to fully leverage physical principles, like EquNN (Wang et al., 2020) and PDE-Net (Long
140   et al., 2018; 2019). PeRCNN (Rao et al., 2023; 2022) offers a “hard” encoding mechanism to
141   learn the dynamics of physical systems from limited data. This approach leverages prior physics
142   knowledge for predictions, thereby equipping strong predictive capabilities and robust generaliza-
143   tion across different initial conditions. However, all the aforementioned methods suffer from error
144   accumulation, making it difficult to obtain stable and accurate results in long-term prediction tasks.
145   PeRCNN effectively utilizes physical information in a way that can be leveraged within PIMRL.
146   Hybrid learning methods. In recent years, an emerging research direction has been to integrate
147   deep learning methods with traditional numerical methods. By combining classical solvers with
148   deep neural networks, hybrid approaches designed on this principle can achieve acceptable accu-
149   racy while operating faster than pure numerical methods. Examples include the Learned Interpola-
150   tion (LI) model (Kochkov et al., 2021) and numerical discretization learning (Zhuang et al., 2021).
151   However, in these hybrid methods, the numerical method component is not involved in the training
152
      process, and such approaches also require substantial amounts of data. The pioneering work multi-
      scale simulations of complex systems (Vlachas et al., 2022) employs a multi-scale framework for
153
      predictions, effectively reducing the required time and computational cost while maintaining good
154
      accuracy. However, this method still requires ample data for training. The idea behind hybrid learn-
155   ing methods is very promising, so we have introduced our own message-passing mechanism within
156   our PIMRL framework.

      3.2    OVERVIEW
197
      As outlined in the introduction, we have designed the PIMRL framework to mitigate error accu-
198   mulation while ensuring that it can effectively capture the intrinsic changes in the physical system,
199   rather than disregarding the physical information over large time intervals. Additionally, PIMRL is
200   capable of efficiently utilizing multi-scale data. Below, we will provide a detailed exposition of the
201   PIMRL framework’s architecture, the corresponding training methodologies, the macro and micro
202   modules, as well as the boundary padding method that incorporates boundary conditions.
203
204   3.3    F ORECASTING A RCHITECTURE
205
206   As shown in Figure 2, the PIMRL framework comprises two key components: the micro-scale
207   module and the macro-scale module. The message-passing mechanism is an interaction between
208   the micro-module and the macro-module. The information transmitted includes physical knowledge
209   learned by the micro-module and corrections applied to the macro-module. Additionally, the macro-
210   module also passes information to future iterations of the micro-module.
211   PIMRL is designed to achieve long-term prediction of spatiotemporal dynamics. PIMRL operates
212   in a recursive manner, involving cycles at the micro-module level, the macro-module level, and the
213   overall framework level. Only the output from the macro-module contributes to the final output of
214   the PIMRL framework and is used to compute the loss for training the entire framework, whereas
215   the output from the micro-module serves as intermediate variables within the framework. And the
      message-passing mechanism is described as follows:

255   3.4       T RAINING S TRATEGIES
256
257   Since the PIMRL model consists of micro- and macro-scale modules, adopting brute-force end-to-
258
      end training yields unsatisfactory results (see the ablation study). Firstly, we establish a pre-training
      phase where only the micro-module is trained. The purpose of this pre-training phase is to enable the
259
      micro-module to effectively learn the dynamics of the physical system and the underlying physical
260
      laws, free from the influence of the macro-module. In the next phase, referred to as the overall
261   training phase, all modules within the PIMRL framework are engaged in training together. During
262   this phase, the micro-module benefits from the parameters pre-trained in the pre-training phase,
263   serving to supervise and correct the macro-module. The output from the macro-module serves as
264   the final output of the entire PIMRL framework and is used to compute the loss. The details are
265   shown in Appendix C.1.

289   3.6   M ACRO - SCALE M ODULE
290
291   The design of the macro-scale module, as a pivotal component of the PIMRL framework, is metic-
292   ulously crafted to effectively manage and analyze macro-scale data. This type of data often poses
293
      unique challenges due to the substantial time spans it encompasses, which in turn lead to significant
      variations in the underlying physical states captured within the data points. These variations might
294
      be highly nonlinear and dynamic, making it difficult for traditional, physically motivated modeling
295
      methods to accurately capture all the nuances and complexities involved. As depicted in Figure 3,
296   our macro-scale module utilizes ConvLSTM block with a residual connection. The structure of each
297   block, illustrated in Figure 3(b), consists of a pair of encoders and decoders, along with a ConvL-
298   STM cell shown in Appendix Figure S3. The input to this block undergoes mapping by the encoder,
299   where the feature map serves as the characteristic of the latent space. Following this, the ConvL-
300   STM cell simulates the dynamics, and the output is mapped back to the physical space through the
301   decoder. Finally, the output, as a residual, is added to the feature from the previous time step to
302   generate the prediction for the next time step. More details are provided in Appendix C.1.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
522
523   This paper introduces a new multi-scale temporal model named Physics-Informed Multi-Scale Re-
524   current Learning (PIMRL) for prediction of spatiotemporal dynamics. Adhering to the idea of con-
525   trolling cumulative error by reducing iterations, PIMRL integrates modules across different scales
526
      within its framework to realize this concept, and it can efficiently leverage multi-scale data, which
      facilitates learning physical laws with limited resources. From fluid dynamics to reaction-diffusion
527
      systems, PIMRL demonstrates superior performance in handling multi-scale data, providing accu-
528
      rate long rollout predictions. Our future work will optimize the macro-scale model for time-series
529   tasks to improve computational efficiency and predictive accuracy. We also plan to integrate super-
530   resolution techniques to enhance the model’s adaptability to various spatiotemporal scales, broad-
531   ening its application to more complex physical systems.

826   D    BASELINE MODELS
827
828   In order to compare and evaluate the performance of our proposed method, we have trained multiple
829   state-of-the-art (SOTA) baseline models as well as classical models, and compared them with our
830
      model. The introductions to each baseline model are presented below, while the training details are
      outlined in subsequent sections E.
831
832   Fourier Neural Operator (FNO). FNOLi et al. (2020) is a method that combines Fourier trans-
833   forms with neural networks. This approach comprises two main components. The first component
834   involves performing Fourier transforms on the system state quantities, learning certain information
835   in the frequency domain, and then applying an inverse transform. The second component utilizes
836
      convolutions to process the system state quantities, complementing the information not captured
      during the frequency domain learning. The combination of these two components serves as the final
837
      result. We make two FNO models sharing the same architecture, which training in micro and macro
838
      scale datasets respectively and inferring in micro and macro time intervals. In the same model, we
839   conducted training separately for two types of data, resulting in FNO and FNO-coarse.
840
841   ConvLSTM. ConvLSTM (Shi et al., 2015) is a specialized neural network architecture that com-
842
      bines convolutional and LSTM layers to effectively model spatial and temporal dependencies in
      sequential data.
843
844   PeRCNN. PeRCNN (Rao et al., 2023) represents a physics-informed learning methodology, em-
845   bedding physical laws directly into the neural network architecture. It incorporates multiple parallel
846   convolutional neural networks (CNNs), leveraging the simulation of polynomial equations through
847   feature map multiplication. By doing so, PeRCNN augments the model’s extrapolation and gener-
848
      alization capabilities.
