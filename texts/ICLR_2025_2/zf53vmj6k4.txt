012
013                   Warning: This paper contains potentially offensive and harmful text.
014            Although large language models (LLMs) demonstrate impressive proficiency in
015            various tasks, they present potential safety risks, such as ‘jailbreaks’, where ma-
016            licious inputs can coerce LLMs into generating harmful content bypassing safety
017            alignments. In this paper, we delve into the ethical biases in LLMs and examine
018            how those biases could be exploited for jailbreaks. Notably, these biases result in
019
               a jailbreaking success rate in GPT-4o models that differs by 20% between non-
               binary and cisgender keywords and by 16% between white and black keywords,
020
               even when the other parts of the prompts are identical. We introduce the concept
021
               of BiasJailbreak, highlighting the inherent risks posed by these safety-induced bi-
022            ases. BiasJailbreak generates biased keywords automatically by asking the target
023            LLM itself, and utilizes the keywords to generate harmful output. Additionally,
024            we propose an efficient defense method BiasDefense, which prevents jailbreak
025            attempts by injecting defense prompts prior to generation. BiasDefense stands
026            as an appealing alternative to Guard Models, such as Llama-Guard, that require
027            additional inference cost after text generation. Our findings emphasize that ethi-
028            cal biases in LLMs can actually lead to generating unsafe output, and suggest a
029            method to make the LLMs more secure and unbiased. To enable further research
030
               and improvements, we open-source our code and artifacts of BiasJailbreak, pro-
               viding the community with tools to better understand and mitigate safety-induced
031
               biases in LLMs.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly become essential components in many fields, ranging
037   from professional decision-making to various forms of interactive user engagement (Araci (2019);
038
      Luo et al. (2022); Tinn et al. (2023)). However, as these models become popular, ensuring their safe
      usage has become crucial. Developers have implemented several safety features to prevent these
039
      models from generating harmful or objectionable content, often referred to as ‘safety alignment’
040
      (Bakker et al. (2022); Christiano et al. (2017); Ouyang et al. (2022); Openai Usage Policies).
041
042   These safety alignments often involve additional fine-tuning or reinforcement learning techniques,
043   which, while designed to enhance safety and alignment, may also inadvertently introduce biases, as
044
      highlighted in resources such as (Achiam et al., 2023, p. 49) . However, biases can also arise from
      other sources, such as pretraining data or system prompts. While it is difficult to pinpoint exactly
045
      where these biases originate, the critical fact remains that they exist and can influence the model’s
046
      behavior.
047
048   In this work, we show that these safety alignments often introduce deliberate and ethical biases,
049   giving rise to a phenomenon known as ’jailbreak’, where malicious inputs manage to circumvent
050
      these safety alignments, thus allowing LLMs to generate harmful outputs (Goldstein et al. (2023);
      Kang et al. (2024)).
051
052   The term ‘jailbreak’ refers to carefully crafted prompts that can bait aligned LLMs into bypassing
053   their safety alignment, resulting in the generation of content that may be harmful, discriminatory, vi-
      olent, or sensitive (Smith et al. (2022)). Numerous types of jailbreak attacks have been identified and

      2.1   S AFETY A LIGNMENT IN LLM S
154
      Ensuring the safety and ethical alignment of large language models (LLMs) is a critical area of on-
155
      going research, since the ethical bias of LLMs can lead to undesirable societal impacts and potential
156   harms (Li et al. (2024)). Methods such as data filtering, supervised fine-tuning, and reinforcement
157   learning from human feedback (RLHF) aim to align models like GPT-4 and ChatGPT with human
158   values and preferences (Christiano et al. (2017); Bai et al. (2022); Ouyang et al. (2022); Xu et al.
159   (2020)). However, despite these efforts, recent studies reveal vulnerabilities that can be exploited
160   through ’jailbreak’ attacks, which lead to undesirable and harmful outputs (Kang et al. (2024); Shen
161   et al. (2023). Additionally, Zheng et al. (2024) proposed many-shot demonstration techniques, using
      random search within demo pools and injecting system tokens to bypass safeguards.

      2.2     JAILBREAK ATTACKS AND T ECHNIQUES
163
164   Jailbreaking LLMs involves crafting inputs that bypass safety mechanisms, resulting in harmful
165   or objectionable content. Early jailbreak attacks, such as the ”Do-Anything-Now (DAN)” series,
166   relied on manually crafted prompts to exploit LLM safeguards (Shen et al. (2023)). (Liu et al.
167   (2023) provided an in-depth analysis and categorization of these jailbreak prompts, highlighting the
168   delicate balance between an LLM’s capabilities and its safety constraints.
169
      Diverse strategies for jailbreaks have been proposed. Manual methods, while effective, suffer from
170   scalability issues (Wei et al. (2024)). On the other hand, learning-based methods like GCG (Zou
171   et al. (2023)) use adversarial techniques to generate prompts automatically, though often at the
172   cost of producing semantically meaningless outputs detectable via simple defenses like perplexity
173   tests (Alon & Kamfonas (2023); Liu et al. (2023)) introduced AutoDAN, which combines manual
174   and automated strategies using hierarchical genetic algorithms to enhance both the stealthiness and
175   scalability of jailbreak prompts.
176   Zeng et al. (2024a) proposed persuasive adversarial prompts (PAP) that leverage social science-
177   based persuasion techniques to significantly enhance jailbreak success, achieving over 92% success
178   rates across multiple models. Similarly, Shah et al. (2024) introduced persona modulation, a black-
179   box jailbreak approach that uses personas to exploit vulnerabilities at scale, with high success rates
180   transferable across state-of-the-art models.
181
      Language diversity and non-natural language inputs present additional challenges. Deng et al.
182   (2023) explored multilingual jailbreak attacks, demonstrating that LLMs could be tricked into pro-
183   ducing harmful outputs with non-English prompts. Yuan et al. (2024); Jin et al. (2024b) extended
184   this by investigating the vulnerabilities of LLMs to non-natural language inputs, such as ciphers.
185
186
187
      2.3     T OWARDS I MPROVED S AFETY M EASURES
188
      Complex attack strategies like those proposed by Ding et al. (2023) with the ReNeLLM framework
189
      introduce the concept of generalized and nested jailbreak prompts, leveraging LLMs to generate
190
      effective prompts through prompt rewriting and scenario nesting. This highlights the dynamic and
191   evolving nature of jailbreak techniques.
192
193   Our work builds on the existing body of research by focusing on the paradoxical consequences of
194
      ethical biases introduced for safety purposes, such as stated in Achiam et al. (2023). While these
      biases aim to align LLMs ethically, they also highlight new vulnerabilities. To counteract this, we
195
      propose using prompts to make the LLM re-align those biases, thus offering a robust secondary
196
      defense against jailbreak attempts.
197
198   In conclusion, AI developers must adopt a higher degree of responsibility in designing, testing,
199   and deploying LLMs. This involves continuous monitoring and iterative improvements based on
200
      real-world data. Our findings advocate for a nuanced approach to LLM safety, promoting the devel-
      opment of more secure and reliable models, and ensuring that safety measures do not inadvertently
201
      introduce new risks.

      3.1.1    JAILBREAK ATTACK
209
210   A jailbreak attack in the context of Large Language Models (LLMs) occurs when the model gen-
211   erates harmful or inappropriate responses to malicious inputs instead of producing a refusal signal,
212   which is a safe and ethical response denying the request (Zeng et al., 2024b; Zou et al., 2023).
213   Such attacks are intricately linked to the alignment methods employed in LLMs, which aim to align
214   the model’s outputs with human values and ethical considerations. The primary objective of these
215   attacks is to bypass the LLM’s alignment constraints, causing the model to produce inappropriate
      responses to malicious queries instead of refusing to answer.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Consider a set of malicious queries Q = {Q1 , Q2 , . . . , Qn }. An attacker meticulously designs a set
217   of jailbreak prompts J = {J1 , J2 , . . . , Jn } to manipulate these queries. When each combined input
218   Ti = ⟨Ji , Qi ⟩ is fed into the LLM, the model generates a set of responses R = {R1 , R2 , . . . , Rn }.
219   The aim of the jailbreak attack is to ensure that the responses R correctly address the malicious
220   queries in Q instead of producing refusal signals (Zeng et al., 2024b).
221
      In summary, a successful jailbreak attack coerces the LLM into bypassing its safety and ethical con-
222
      straints, culminating in the generation of harmful or inappropriate content in response to malicious
223   prompts. This kind of attack underscores significant vulnerabilities in the current alignment strate-
224   gies of LLMs, highlighting the necessity for robust countermeasures to guarantee model safety and
225   reliability.
226
227   3.1.2    JAILBREAK ATTACK E VALUATION
228
229   Designing a specific target response for each malicious question is impractical for several reasons.
230   Firstly, manually crafting an appropriate answer for every possible malicious query Qi is resource-
231   intensive and infeasible due to the vast number of potential queries. Secondly, creating specific
232
      responses may limit the attack’s generalizability, as tailored responses might not be effective for
      other queries or variations of the same query. Lastly, directly specifying harmful content could raise
233
      ethical and legal concerns.
234
235   Hence, a common solution to this problem is to define a standardized introductory phrase for the tar-
236   get response (Zou et al., 2023; Lapid et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024). The determination of a successful
237   jailbreak focuses on the absence of this standardized introductory phrase in the response. Such as:
238
239                       Target Prefixes = [“I am sorry”, ”I can not”, ”I apologize”...]

      5   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In conclusion, our study highlights the complexities and potential unintended consequences of align-
      ing large language models (LLMs) with safety measures aimed at preventing harmful outputs. The
526
      introduction of ethical biases for ethical behavior, while crucial for ensuring responsible AI, has led
527
      to significant discrepancies in jailbreak success rates based on gender and racial keywords. This
528   discrepancy, coined as ”BiasJailbreak,” underscores the risks that arise from safety-induced biases,
529   particularly in terms of fairness and equality. Our findings emphasize the need for LLM develop-
530   ers to carefully balance safety and fairness in their models. Additionally, our proposed method of
531   adding defense prompts without requiring additional inference or models shows a simple and scal-
532   able solution to mitigate jailbreak attempts. This simplicity and scalability of migation emphasizes
533   the responsibility of LLM developers to adopt more comprehensive and proactive measures to ad-
534   dress safety risks. Future work should focus on designing more inclusive and transparent alignment
535   strategies to address the inherent challenges of AI safety while minimizing bias.
