009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) show significant potential in economic and
012            strategic interactions, where communication via natural language is often preva-
013            lent. This raises key questions: Do LLMs behave rationally? Can they mimic
014            human behavior? Do they tend to reach an efficient and fair outcome? What is
015            the role of natural language in the strategic interaction? How do characteristics
016
               of the economic environment influence these dynamics? These questions become
               crucial concerning the economic and societal implications of integrating LLM-
017
               based agents into real-world data-driven systems, such as online retail platforms
018
               and recommender systems. While the ML community has been exploring the po-
019            tential of LLMs in such multi-agent setups, varying assumptions, design choices
020            and evaluation criteria across studies make it difficult to draw robust and mean-
021            ingful conclusions. To address this, we introduce a benchmark for standardizing
022            research on two-player, sequential, language-based games. Inspired by the eco-
023            nomic literature, we define three base families of games with consistent param-
024            eterization, degrees of freedom and economic measures to evaluate agents’ per-
025            formance (self-gain), as well as the game outcome (efficiency and fairness). We
026            develop an open-source framework for interaction simulation and analysis, and
027
               utilize it to collect a dataset of LLM vs. LLM interactions across numerous game
               configurations and an additional dataset of human vs. LLM interactions. Through
028
               extensive experimentation, we demonstrate how our framework and dataset can
029
               be used to: (i) compare the behavior of LLM-based agents to human players in
030            various economic contexts; (ii) evaluate agents in both individual and collective
031            performance measures; and (iii) quantify the effect of the economic characteris-
032            tics of the environments on the behavior of agents. We believe that our framework
033            can contribute to the growing intersection of LLMs, ML, and economics, and we
034            encourage researchers to explore it further and build on its foundation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Recent research has increasingly focused on the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in
      decision-making tasks, revealing their potential to operate as autonomous agents in various eco-
040
      nomic environments, that typically require involving complex strategic thinking (Horton 2023;
041
      Wang et al. 2023; Zhu et al. 2024; Li et al. 2024). Applications of LLM-based agents in such
042   environments include Chess-playing (Feng et al. 2024), task-oriented dialogue handling (Ulmer
043   et al. 2024), financial advisement (Lakkaraju et al. 2023) and beyond. The promising capabilities
044   of LLMs in strategic decision-making call for the study of these agents’ behavior through the lens
045   of game theory, e.g. whether and when LLM-based agents naturally converge to Nash-equilibrium
046   (Guo et al. 2024), and how well they learn to cooperate in repeated interactions (Akata et al. 2023).
047   The rise of LLM agents also calls for a clear benchmark for assessing the extent to which such agent
048   behaves rationally, which is addressed by a recent work of Raman et al. (2024).
049   An important property of many real-world economic environments is that communication between
050   agents typically occurs through natural language. While economic modeling usually abstracts away
051   these nuances for the sake of keeping the model simple and tractable, in practical scenarios the fact
052   that natural language is involved may significantly affect the interaction outcome. For instance, in
053   bargaining between two parties, the same offer can be interpreted very differently depending on
      how it is framed. Consider the following two offers for a business partnership: (a) ”We propose a

      40-60 split as we believe your expertise will drive the majority of the success.” (b) ”We propose a
055   40-60 split because we’ve already invested significant resources and need a lower share to maintain
056   balance.” Both propose the same numerical terms, but the framing in each case may lead to different
057   reactions based on how the rationale is presented. A similar rationale applies to a broader range of
058   economic scenarios. To illustrate this further, let us consider the following representative use cases:
059
060          • Bargaining. Alice and Bob co-own a startup valued at a million dollar, and must decide
061            how to divide the proceeds from its sale. To reach an agreement, they take turns proposing
062            how to split the shared value, with each delay in reaching a decision reducing the overall
063            value for both parties. The interaction often involves free text in the proposal exchange.
064          • Negotiation. Alice aims to sell a product to a potential buyer, Bob. They negotiate by
065            taking turns proposing prices, with Alice deciding whether to accept Bob’s offer and sell,
066            and Bob deciding whether to accept Alice’s price and buy, continuing this process until they
067            reach an agreement or the negotiation concludes. The negotiation often involves natural
068            language, such as descriptions of the product, which can influence the outcome.1
069          • Persuasion. Alice is a seller trying to sell a product to Bob at an exogenously set fixed
070            price. Alice knows the true quality of the product, while Bob only has a rough expectation
071            of the product’s quality. Alice sends a message to persuade Bob to buy the product, aiming
072            to convince him of its value, regardless of its true quality. Bob, however, benefits only
073            if the product is genuinely high quality. The interaction then repeats for multiple rounds,
074            where product qualities are realized independently at different rounds. In such a scenario,
075
               Alice balances between her maximizing one-shot gain and building positive reputation, and
               linguistic communication can play a significant role.
076
077
      These three types of interactions are inspired by influential models in the economics literature. They
078
      are also broad and flexible enough to capture a wide variety of real-world applications. The bargain-
079   ing model, inspired by the seminal work of Rubinstein (1982), forms the basis for understanding how
080   parties negotiate the division of shared resources, applicable in scenarios such as business mergers,
081   partnership dissolution, and legal settlements. The negotiation game reflects celebrated models of
082   bilateral trade (Myerson & Satterthwaite 1983; McAfee 1992) where buyers and sellers negotiate
083   over the prices of an indivisible good, which often arises in various applications, including real es-
084   tate transactions, corporate acquisitions, and e-commerce platforms. Lastly, the persuasion model
085   draws on classical models of information asymmetry and strategic communication (Akerlof 1978;
086   Crawford & Sobel 1982; Farrell & Rabin 1996), which play a pivotal role in advertising, marketing,
087
      political campaigning, and recommendation systems.
088   A key feature of all these games is that they are sequential, meaning players take turns acting rather
089   than acting simultaneously. This makes communication, and in particular language-based communi-
090   cation, a crucial element of the interaction. In sequential games, communication is typically direct,
091   with players able to attach messages to their actions (i.e., in the bargaining example, the textual
092   message is attached to the proposal). In contrast, in simultaneous games, communication tends to
093
      be indirect, as players act independently and typically learn to cooperate by observing and reacting
      to past actions rather than through direct message exchange. A prime example of a simultaneous,
094
      language-based economic interaction is the competition among web publishers in search engines.
095
      Publishers simultaneously create content (usually in the form of textual documents) for their web-
096   sites, and then gradually learn and adapt based on outcomes, such as their relative search engine
097   rankings and exposure. However, they usually lack the opportunity for real-time communication or
098   coordination during this process. These simultaneous, language-based interactions are well-studied
099   within the information retrieval community, and the induced publishers’ game is indeed modeled as
100   a simultaneous game.2 Our work is complementary, focusing on sequential settings where direct,
101   language-based communication is prevalent.

      The AI and NLP communities have recognized the potential of integrating natural language into
109   stylized economic models of bargaining, negotiation and persuasion. The also recognize the im-
110   portance of studying the capabilities and limitations of LLM-based agents within such frameworks.
111   A growing body of research is dedicated to evaluating and optimizing LLM-based agents in vari-
112   ous bargaining and negotiation scenarios (Abdelnabi et al. 2023; Deng et al. 2024; Xia et al. 2024;
113   Bianchi et al. 2024; Noh & Chang 2024). In the realm of persuasion games, recent studies have
114   introduced language-based frameworks that explore the design of optimal information transmission
115   policies (Raifer et al. 2022) and develop methods for generating data to predict human players’
116   behavior (Apel et al. 2022; Shapira et al. 2023; 2024).
117   Although each of these economic setups has been studied in the context of LLM-based agents, the
118   approaches have varied widely across different studies. Each paper often adopts distinct modeling
119   and implementation assumptions, poses unique research questions, and applies diverse evaluation
120   criteria, making it challenging to compare results and draw broader conclusions about the capabil-
121   ities and limitations of LLM-based agents in economic environments. To enhance the real-world
122   reliability of LLM-based agents, it is essential to establish a clear benchmark that provides a stan-
123
      dardized framework for modeling these economic interactions. This benchmark would ensure com-
      parability across studies and enable generalization of findings, facilitating a deeper understanding of
124
      how various factors influence interaction outcomes and leading to more robust and reliable conclu-
125
      sions about the performance of LLM-based agents in real-life economic situations.

      1.1    O UR CONTRIBUTION
128
129   We introduce GLEE, a unified framework for Games in Language-based Economic Environments,3
130   focusing on the case of two-player games.4 Our framework relies on a clear and comprehensive
131   parameterization of the space of all bargaining, negotiation and persuasion games (as described
132   above), and defines degree of freedom and evaluation metrics that are consistent across economic
133   contexts. The generality of the space of games spanned by our parameterization follows from the
134   richness of the degrees of freedom considered, which include the game horizon (number of rounds
135   within each game), information structure (whether agents know each others’ preferences or not),
136
      and communication form (whether agents communicate via free language of structured messages).
137   Through this framework, we have collected a dataset of LLM vs. LLM games, containing 7.15M de-
138   cisions made by LLM-based agents in over 954K games, using four different LLMs. Our framework
139   allows for running controlled experiments across various degrees of freedom, language models, and
140   game scenarios, making the dataset valuable for comparing and evaluating LLM performance in dif-
141   ferent economic setups. It also facilitates deeper insights into how characteristics of the economic
142
      environment influence agent behavior and ultimately shape the outcomes of interactions. Addition-
      ally, we design and develop an interactive interface we run experiments in which human players
143
      play against LLM-based agents in those considered games, for a variety of game parameters, and
144
      collect a human vs. LLM interaction dataset. We utilize this human-generated dataset to introduce
145   a benchmark for several human choice prediction tasks, in which ML algorithms can be used to
146   accurately predict the behavior of human players in a wide range of economic contexts.
147
148
      To recap, the contribution of this paper is fourfold. First, we introduce a clear parametrization
      of a large, general, and representative set of sequential, two-player, language-based games. This
149
      parametrization is inspired by both the relevant economic literature, as well as advances in the LLM
150
      agents literature. Importantly, our parameterization can be naturally extended beyond the two-player
151   case. Second, we release open-source code for GLEE, a unified framework that enables controlled
152   experiments across the representative families of games considered. Notably, our framework is
153   flexible and facilitates the collection of additional interactions with novel LLMs, game parameters
154   and evaluation metrics, which is crucial given the fast advancements in the field. Third, we utilize
155   GLEE to collect a dataset of LLM vs. LLM and human vs. LLM interactions in a wide range of eco-
156   nomic environments, building on the foundations of our comprehensive parametrization. Finally, we

167
168
      In this section, we formally define the three game families discussed in the introduction: bargaining,
169
      negotiation and persuasion.5
170
171   In our modeling, we focus on three main degrees of freedom that are critical for understanding
172   the dynamics of LLM-based agents in economic interactions: game horizon, information structure,
173   and communication form. The game horizon refers to the number of time periods during which
174
      the game is played and whether the length of the horizon is known or unknown to the agents.7
      This factor influences the strategies agents adopt, particularly in terms of long-term planning and
175
      anticipation of future moves. The information structure determines whether agents are aware of each
176
      other’s preferences, impacting their ability to predict and respond to the actions of others. Lastly, the
177   communication form specifies whether communication between agents occurs through free language
178   or structured, concise messages, which affects the richness and clarity of the exchanges.
179

      2.2    N EGOTIATION GAMES
220
221   In the second family of games, referred to as negotiation games, a seller (Alice) and a buyer (Bob)
222   are negotiating over the price of a product. At the outset, Alice owns a product she subjectively
223   values at VA . The subjective valuation of the potential buyer, Bob, is VB . To capture the notion
224   of valuation scale in negotiation games, we parameterize Vi = M · Fi for i ∈ {A, B}, where
225   Fi ∈ (0, 1) is a factor parameter M is a scale parameter.
226   As in the case of bargaining games, Alice and Bob alternate offers: at each odd stage, Alice posts a
227   price and Bob decides whether to buy the product or move on to the next stage. At each even stage,
228   Bob is the one to post a price and Alice decides whether to sell at this price or reject and move on
229   to the next stage. The game is played for T stages, which again can be either finite or ”infinite”
230   (i.e., large and unknown to both players). Unlike the bargaining game, here we assume no discount
231   factors on the utilities, hence whenever a price p is accepted, the utilities for Alice and Bob are
232   p − VA and VB − p respectively. If no trade is made, then the utilities are defined to be (0, 0).
233   In this class of games, we consider the following degrees of freedom: (i) whether or not the players
234   know their opponent’s product valuation (complete vs. incomplete information); (ii) whether or
235   not the players know when the game terminates (finite vs. infinite); (iii) whether or not players
236   communication involve natural language (structured vs. linguistic); (iv) the values of FA , FB ∈
237   (0, 1) and M ; (v) the value of T in the finite horizon case.
238   An outcome of a negotiation game is captured by pev , which is the price at which the product is
239   sold when there is a trade, and defined to be pev = ∅ whenever no trade is made. We consider the
240   following evaluation measures of the game outcome fairness and efficiency. When there is trade,

250   2.3    P ERSUASION GAMES
251
252   In a persuasion game, a seller (Alice) tried to persuade a buyer (Bob) to buy a product at a fixed
253   price π. Alice privately knows the true product quality, which can be either high or low. Bob only
254   knows that the prior probability of the product being of high quality is p. Alice gets a utility of 1 if
255
      Bob buys (regardless of the true product quality), and 0 otherwise.
256   Bob values a high-quality product at v > π and a low-quality product at u < π. Therefore, the utility
257   of Bob from buying a high-quality product is v − π > 0, and from buying a low-quality product is
258   u − π < 0. For simplicity, we normalize the price to π = 1 and the value of a low-quality product to
259   u = 0. In addition, we consider a currency scale parameter M that serves as a multiplicative term of
260   Bob’s utility function. Overall Bob gets a utility of M (v − 1) from buying a high-quality product,
261
      −M from buying a low-quality product, and 0 from not buying the product.
262   The timing of a single round is as follows. First, Alice observes the product quality (which is
263   realized to be high-quality w.p. p, independently of other rounds). Then, Alice sends a message
264   to Bob. Lastly, Bob decides whether to buy or not to buy the product, and utilities are realized
265   accordingly. The game then consists of T such rounds. Alice’s goal is to maximize her cumulative

388   4   E XPLORATORY DATA A NALYSIS
389
390   In this section, we first present a method to ensure an adequate comparison between models that
391   played different configurations. Then, we address two key research questions: Q1: How did the
392   language models perform in each game family in terms of self-gain, efficiency, and fairness? Q2:
393   How did the humans perform compared to the language models? Q3: How do the parameters
394   defining the games influence the various metrics?
395
396   Adequate Comparison Between Models For the four language models introduced in §3, we
397
      collected data covering the entire set of the configurations defined in Table 1 for every possible
      pair of language models. This enables seamless comparison of their performance across all defined
398
      metrics. Henceforth, this dataset will be referred to as the baseline set, and the models that generated
399
      it will be referred to as the baseline models.
400
401   When evaluating data generated by a new model or by humans, it is often not feasible (due to cost
402   considerations) to have them play all games against all existing language models.14 To evaluate
403
      model performance on a large variety of language games, we can collect data from a subset of
      configurations, calculate the metrics for those, and use the results (along with the metrics from the
404
      baseline set) to train a predictive model that estimates the metrics for configurations for which data
405
      was not collected. This method allows for an adequate comparison between models that played
406   different configuration sets.
407
408   For the calculation of the metrics, we train a simple regression model for each metric. The model
409
      receives as input the values defining the configuration as binary features (e.g., ”Is M = 100?”) and
      binary features describing the interaction between the language models and other parameter values
410
      (e.g., ”Did Alice played by Gemini-1.5-Flash and M = 100?” and ”Did Alice played by Gemini-
411
      1.5-Flash, Bob played by Llama-3.1-8B, and M = 100?”). To prevent the model from learning
412   relationships between parameter values directly from configuration settings, we used binary features
413   even for parameters that contain numerical values. We trained the models on the data from all games
414   and predicted the values for all configurations: both those for which data was collected and those for
415   which it was not. Therefore, to maintain consistency, all the metrics are calculated by averaging the
416   predicted values over all game configurations. This approach allows us to reasonably estimate the
417   metric values expected for configurations where no human data was collected. The exact formulas
418   defining each regression model are presented in Appendix D.
419   To assess the quality of the models, we examined the adjusted R-squared values of the regression
420   models. In 10 out of 12 trained models, we obtained adjusted R-squared values higher than 0.38.
421   Table 3 shows the adjusted R-squared values of the models. The table shows that, in general, persua-
422   sion games are the easiest to predict, and we hypothesize that this is because their outcomes consist
423   of a collection of multiple decisions. Negotiation games are the most difficult to predict, possibly
424   because, unlike the other games, players could exit the game at any given moment. It seems that fair-
425   ness is the most challenging metric to fit with a regression model, with negotiation games showing
426
      an especially low adjusted R-squared.

525   5               C ONCLUSION
526
527
      We present GLEE, a framework for evaluating the behavior of Large Language Models (LLMs) in
      language-based economic games. The goal of GLEE is to provide a comparative tool for assessing
528
      the performance of LLMs in various economic scenarios and enable their comparison to human
529
      players. We defined the game space within three main families of games: bargaining, negotiation,
530   and persuasion, and introduced metrics to measure player performance. We developed a frame-
531   work that allows for large-scale data collection from games between diverse LLMs and created an
532   interface that facilitates the collection of data from games involving human players. Through this
533   interface, we gathered data from 954K games between LLMs and from 3,405 games involving hu-
534   man players. The data is available for future research, which could advance the field of machine
535   learning in language-based economic games, such as for predicting human decisions using artificial
536   data and building more successful and human-like agents based on the metrics we define in GLEE.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This paper aims to provide a platform for experimenting with agents in language-based economic
543   environments. Naturally, this line of research may have various societal and ethical implications, as
544   we now discuss.
545
546
      First, studying the economic aspects of LLM-based agents has the potential to enhance the ability of
      agent designers to control and optimize the behavior of these agents. This capability can be utilized
547
      for a variety of purposes, ranging from encouraging self-interested behavior at the expense of other
548
      participants in the environment (e.g., for maximizing revenue in competitive settings) to promoting
549   efficient trade and fair behavior, or any combination of these sometimes non-aligned objectives. As
550   this research increases the power of LLM-based agents in economic environments, it is essential to
551   emphasize that with great power comes great responsibility. We call for the responsible and ethical
552   use of these emerging capabilities to ensure they are leveraged for socially beneficial purposes rather
553   than exploitative ones.
554
      Furthermore, our framework demonstrates the capability of collecting data from human players to
555   understand the differences and similarities between LLMs and humans in economic environments.
556   While this line of research has the potential for a strong scientific contribution, particularly in the
557   field of behavioral economics, it also raises several ethical considerations. The collection of human
558   data must be conducted with careful regulation and adherence to clear ethical guidelines, such as
559   those established in venues like ICLR. The authors declare that there are no violations of the ICLR
560   ethics code in our study. The entire process of data collection from human participants is elaborated
561   in §3.2.
562   In addition to the challenges associated with the collection of human data, enhancing our under-
563   standing of LLMs from the lens of human behavior carries inherent risks. For instance, this research
564   could enhance our ability to design LLM agents that are difficult to distinguish from real humans.
565   Such capabilities could be misused for malicious purposes, including deception or manipulation.
566   While the answer to whether these capabilities could be used for harmful causes is likely yes, we
567   believe that the benefits of pursuing this line of research outweigh the risks when balanced with
568   proper regulations. We advocate for pushing research forward while ensuring that any new tech-
569
      nologies are accompanied by safeguards to prevent harmful usage, particularly when human-like
      LLMs are involved.
570
571   Our proposed framework can make these research directions more accessible to researchers from the
572   ML community and beyond, thereby encouraging a broader understanding of LLM behavior in eco-
573   nomic contexts. However, as such accessibility increases, it is crucial to maintain ethical oversight
574   and foster an open dialogue on potential misuse. We encourage researchers to use our framework
575
      with full transparency and careful attention to potential misuse and negative consequences.

      A        G AME FAMILIES THROUGH THE LENS OF ECONOMIC LITERATURE
757
758
      In this section, we discuss the economic literature related to the three game families considered in
759   this paper, and review some known theoretical results.
760
761   Bargining As mentioned in §2, the standard bargaining model of Rubinstein (1982) consists of
762   two players, Alice and Bob, engaging in alternating offers for a finite horizon (T = ∞) with com-
763   monly known discount factors δA , δB . Our parameterization considers several additional degrees
764   of freedom, such as finite vs. infinite time horizon, complete vs. incomplete information, and free
765   language messages vs. structured and concise messages. In the standard model, Rubinstein (1982)
766   showed that in the unique subgame-perfect equilibrium an agreement is reached in the first stage
                                                                                                   1−δB 17
767   (i.e., utilities are not discounted), and the share of Alice is given by M p∗ , where p∗ = 1−δ  A δB
                                                                                                           .
768   The case of finite horizon can be solved using backward induction, and typically results in a different
769   outcome compared to the infinite case. As T grows, the equilibrium outcome approaches the one of
770   the infinite case. Extensions to incomplete information regarding the opponent’s discount factor are
771
      typically more challenging, and some of them are studied in the literature, e.g. Rubinstein (1985).
772
773
      Negotiation In a negotiation game, Alice and Bob negotiate over the price of an indivisible good.
      The negotiation game differs from the bargaining games in several key aspects:
774
775            1. Negotiation involves an indivisible product (e.g., Alice’s product), while bargaining focuses
776               on dividing a divisible resource, such as money.
777
               2. In negotiation, Alice and Bob may have different subjective valuations of the product,
778
                  whereas in bargaining, both parties value the divisible resource similarly.
779
780
               3. Negotiation has no discounting, so the utility remains constant over time. In bargaining,
                  delays reduce the total value, encouraging faster agreement.
781
782   If the seller is uncertain regarding the buyer’s valuation but has a prior belief distribution, a classical
783   result by Harris & Raviv (1981) and Riley & Zeckhauser (1983) states that it is always optimal to
784   sell the product at a fixed price. In contrast, if the seller does not have a prior belief over the buyer’s
785   valuation, and instead aims to minimize regret, then an optional pricing policy will be to randomly
786   choose a price from a carefully chosen distribution (Bergemann & Schlag, 2011).
787
788   Persuasion Our persuasion game follows the structure of a cheap talk game (Crawford & Sobel,
789   1982; Farrell & Rabin, 1996), where the sender (Alice) cannot commit to a signaling policy in
790   advance, unlike in Bayesian persuasion models (Kamenica & Gentzkow, 2011). Under the particular
791   payoff structure considered in our persuasion game, it is well-known that the cheap-talk game only
792
      admits a babbling equilibrium, i.e., an equilibrium in which all information is kept hidden (this is
      due to the strong misalignment of interests between the two players). In contrast, if the seller can
793
      commit to a signaling policy at the outset, as in Bayesian persuasion, then there exists a subgame-
794
      perfect equilibrium in which the seller commits to the following policy: When the product is of
795   high quality, the seller recommends buying the product with probability 1. When the product is of
796                                                                             p
      low quality, then the seller recommends with probability q = min{ 1−p         (v − 1), 1}. This policy
797   is also incentive-compatible, in the sense that the buyer always buys the product when the seller
798   recommends buying.18 While the long-living buyer case is well-studied in the economic literature,
799   such games often admit multiple equilibria, which makes the games difficult to analyze and predict
800   (Kim, 1996; Aumann & Hart, 2003). As for the case of myopic buyers, Best & Quigley (2024) draws
801   a connection between the repeated cheap talk game and the case of one-shot Bayesian persuasion,
802   leading to an elegant analytical solution of the repeated game. Intuitively, the repetitive nature of the
803   game induces a reputation effect, which plays a similar role to the commitment power in standard
804
      one-shot Bayesian persuasion.
805       17
           A subgame-perfect equilibrium is a strategy profile in which every player responds optimally in every
806
      hypothetical subgame of the game, including off-path scenarios that are not reached in practice. This solution
807   concept can be seen as capturing a higher level of rationality compared to the alternative of Nash equilibrium.
808     18
           In fact, the probability of lying q is determined such that the buyer is indifferent upon receiving a recom-
809   mendation, taking into account his belief updating, which relies on using Bayes’s law and knowing the seller’s
      committed policy.

937

938

1244   In this appendix, we describe the two attention tests that human players were required to complete.
