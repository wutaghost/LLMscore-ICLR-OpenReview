009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Model attribution is a popular tool to explain the rationales behind model pre-
012            dictions. However, recent work suggests that the attributions are vulnerable to
013            minute perturbations, which can be added to input samples to manipulate the at-
014            tributions while maintaining the prediction outputs. Although empirical studies
015            have shown positive performance via adversarial training, an effective certified
016
               defense method is eminently needed to understand the robustness of attributions.
               In this work, we propose to use uniform smoothing technique that augments the
017
               vanilla attributions by noises uniformly sampled from a certain space. It is proved
018
               that, for all perturbations within the attack region, the cosine similarity between
019            uniformly smoothed attribution of perturbed sample and the unperturbed sample
020            is guaranteed to be lower bounded. We also derive alternative formulations of the
021            certification that is equivalent to the original one and provides the maximum size
022            of perturbation or the minimum smoothing radius such that the attribution can not
023            be perturbed. We evaluate the proposed method on three datasets and show that
024            the proposed method can effectively protect the attributions from attacks, regard-
025            less of the architecture of networks, training schemes and the size of the datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The developments and wider uses of deep learning models in various security-sensitive applications,
030
      such as autonomous driving, medical diagnosis, and legal judgments, have raised discussions of the
031   trustworthiness of these models. The lack of explainability of deep learning models, especially the
032   recent popular large language models (Brown et al., 2020), has been one of the main concerns. Reg-
033   ulators have started to require the explainability of the AI models in some applications (Goodman
034   & Flaxman, 2017), and the explainability of AI models has been one of the main focuses of the
035   research community (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017). Model attributions, as one of the important tools
036   to explain the rationales behind the model predictions, have been used to understand the decision-
037   making process of the models. For example, medical practitioners use the explanations generated
038   by attribution methods to assist them in making important medical decisions (Antoniadi et al., 2021;
039
      Hrinivich et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022). Similarly, in autonomous vehicles, the explainability helps
      to deal with potential liability and responsibility gaps (Atakishiyev et al., 2021; Burton et al., 2020)
040
      and people naturally requires the confirmation of the safety critical decisions. However, since these
041
      users often lack expertise in machine learning and technical details of attributions, there is a risk
042   that the attributions have been manipulated without noticing. Attackers may specifically target attri-
043   butions to mislead investigators, propagate false narratives, or evade detection, potentially leading
044   to serious consequences. Consequently, practitioners could lose trust in these methods, resulting
045   in their refusal to use these methods. Thus, a trustworthy application requires not only the predic-
046   tions made by the AI models, but also its explainability produced from attributions. However, the
047   attributions have also been shown recently to be vulnerable to small perturbations (Ghorbani et al.,
048   2019). Similar to adversarial attacks, attribution attacks generate perturbations that can be added to
049   input samples. These perturbations distort the attributions while maintaining unchanged prediction
050
      outputs. This misleadingly gives a false sense of security to practitioners who blindly trust the at-
      tributions. An effective defense method is emergently needed to protect the attributions from the
051
      attacks.
052
053   Unlike adversarial defense, which has been extensively investigated to mitigate the harm of adver-
      sarial attacks that using both empirical (Madry et al., 2018; Athalye et al., 2018; Carlini & Wagner,

055   2019) defense methods, attribution defense is neglected. Almost all attribution protection works
056   focus on adversarially training the model by augmenting the training data with manipulated samples
057   to improve the robustness of attributions (Boopathy et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2019; Ivankay et al.,
058   2020; Sarkar et al., 2021; Wang & Kong, 2022). Levine et al. (2019) and a series of subsequent
059   works (Liu et al., 2022; Huai et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023) study the certifications of attributions

061   work by Wang & Kong (2023) attempts to derive a practical upper bound of cosine similarity for the
062   worst-case attribution deviation, while suffering from strict assumptions and heavy computations.

      Overall, there is a gap in scalable methods for providing generalized certification of attribution ro-
      bustness. In this work, we put our focus on the smoothed version of attributions and seek to provide

      budget.

101   2     R ELATED WORKS
102
103   2.1   ATTRIBUTION METHODS
104
105   Attribution methods study the importance of each input feature, and measure that how much every
106   feature contributes to the model prediction. One of the most popular attribution approaches is the
107   gradient-based method. Based on the property that gradient is the measurement of the rate of change,
      the gradient-based methods measure the feature importance by weighting the gradients in different


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ways. Examples of gradient-based methods include saliency (Simonyan et al., 2014), integrated
109   gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), full-gradient (Srinivas & Fleuret, 2019), and etc. Other
110   attribution methods include occlusion (Simonyan et al., 2014), which measures the importance of
111   each input feature by occluding the feature and measuring the change of the model prediction, layer-
112   wise relevance propagation (LRP) (Bach et al., 2015), which propagates the output relevance to the
113   input layer, and SHAP related methods (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020; Kwon
114   &
      PZou, 2022). An important property of many attribution methods is the axiom of completeness that
115      i gi (x) = fj (x), which indicates the relationship between attribution and the prediction score.
116   Note that the gradient-based attribution methods are upper-bounded since the gradients of the model
117
      output with respect to the input are upper-bounded.
118
119   2.2   ATTRIBUTION ATTACKS AND DEFENSES
120
121   Ghorbani et al. (2019) first pointed out that attributions can be fragile to iterative attribution attack,
122   and Dombrowski et al. (2019) extended the attack to be targeted that attributions can be changed
123
      purposely into any preset patterns. Similar to adversarial attacks, attribution attacks maximize the
      loss function that measures the difference between the original attributions and the target attributions.
124
      In addition, the attribution attacks are controlled not to alter the classification results. To defend
125
      against attribution attacks, adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018) approaches have been adopted.
126   Chen et al. (2019) and Boopathy et al. (2020) minimizes the ℓp -norm differences between perturbed
127   and original attributions, and Ivankay et al. (2020) considers Pearson’s correlation coefficient. It is
128   worth noting that these methods empirically improve attribution robustness. Meanwhile, a series
129   of certification methods Levine et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2022); Huai et al. (2022); Gu et al. (2023)
130   have been proposed to ensure that attribution changes do not exceed a certain threshold under any
131   perturbations within the allowable attack region. These methods measure attribution changes using
132   top-k intersection, while a continuous alternative, cosine similarity, remains unexplored. It has
133   been proved that using cosine similarity to measure attribution differences is consistent as top-k
134
      intersection and Kendall’s rank correlation (Wang & Kong, 2022), and it is more likely to extend to
      other domains.
135
136
137   2.3   R ANDOMIZED SMOOTHING
138
139   The smoothing technique has been popular in improving certified adversarial robustness (Liu
140
      et al., 2018; Lecuyer et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020). The smoothed clas-
      sifiers take a batch of inputs that are randomly sampled from the neighbourhood of original in-
141
      puts under certain distributions µ and make the decisions based on the most likely outputs, i.e.,
142
      arg maxy Pη∼µ [F (x + η) = y]. They provide the certification of the a radius such that no pertur-
143   bation within the radius can alter the classification label. Cohen et al. (2019) certifies the ℓ2 attack
144   based on the Neyman-Pearson lemma and the result is alternatively proved by Salman et al. (2019)
145   using explicit Lipschitz constants. Lecuyer et al. (2019) and Teng et al. (2020) consider Laplacian
146   smoothing for the ℓ1 attack. Yang et al. (2020) derived a similar result in ℓ∞ though the radius be-
147   comes small when the dimension of data gets large. Kumar & Goldstein (2021) applied randomized
148   smoothing to structured output, which the attributions belong to, but the specific bound for attribu-
149   tion is too loose to be meaningful. Thus, there are no existing works that provide defense and valid
150   certifications for attribution using randomized smoothing due to the difficulty of defining the attri-
151
      bution robustness and the computation of the attribution gradient. In this work, an effective method
      to formulate the smoothed attribution robustness as a simple optimization problem is proposed to
152
      provide certifications against attribution attacks.

207   4      C ERTIFYING THE COSINE SIMILARITY OF SMOOTHED ATTRIBUTIONS
208
209
      We now consider S as an ℓ2 -norm ball for the ease of analyzing the certification. Adapted from the
210   formulation in Eq. (1), the robustness of attribution is defined as the minimum possible attribution
211   similarity when a natural image is perturbed by attribution attacks. As mentioned in Section 2, this
212   work studies cosine similarity as the measurement of similarity, as it has been shown to be the most
213   suitable alternative to the non-differentiable Kendall’s rank correlation, the most common evaluation
214   index (Wang & Kong, 2022). Suppose that the ℓ2 attribution attack is performed upon input sample
215   x, the maximum allowable perturbation is ϵ, i.e., x̃ = x + δ, where ∥δ∥2 ≤ ϵ. For all ∥δ∥2 ≤ ϵ, we
      want to find out the minimum value of cos(h(x), h(x̃)), i.e., the lower bound of cosine similarity

      for attributions. Thus, the optimization problem we are interested in is formulated as follows:

221   sine similarity between the original smoothed attribution and the corresponding perturbed smoothed
222   attributions is guaranteed to be lower bounded by the optimum of (3). Alternatively, in a more prac-
223   tical perspective, given a threshold T for the cosine similarity, we want to know the maximum size
224   of perturbation, or the minimum smoothing radius, such that no perturbations inside would cause
225   the attribution difference to exceed the threshold.
226   However, although optimizing the cosine similarity for attribution can be an intuitive way to find
227   the lower bound, it is difficult in this problem to directly study the cosine function. Moreover, it is
228   also intractable to optimize the cosine similarity with respect to a vector δ. To address this issue,
229   we first reformulate the problem into an optimization over two scalars and then solve the alternative
230   problem to obtain the lower bound of cosine similarity. All the proofs and derivations of theorems,
231   lemmas and corollaries are provided in the Appendix A.

478   6     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we attempt to use the uniformly smoothed attribution to certify the attribution robust-
481   ness evaluated by cosine similarity. The smoothed attribution is constructed by taking the mean of
482   the attributions computed from input samples augmented by noises uniformly sampled from an ℓ2
483   ball. It is proved that the cosine similarity between the original and perturbed smoothed attribution
484   is lower-bounded based on a geometric formulation related to the volume of the hyperspherical cap.
485   Alternative formulations are provided to find the maximum allowable size of perturbations and the
      minimum radius of smoothing in order to maintain the attribution robustness. The method works


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      on bounded gradient-based attribution methods for all convolutional neural networks and is scalable
487   to large datasets. We empirically demonstrate that the method can be used to certify the attribution
488   robustness, using the well-bounded integrated gradients, and the state-of-the-art attributional robust
489   models on MNIST, CIFAR-10 and ImageNet.

      7   L IMITATIONS AND BROADER IMPACTS
493
      The method in paper can be generally applied to any convolutional neural networks and any bounded
494
      attribution methods. Although the existence of an upper bound has been shown for all gradient-
495   based methods, in some extreme cases when the upper bound for certain attribution is trivial, i.e., an
496   extremely large value, the proposed lower bound for attribution robustness also becomes trivial. In
497   future work, we will investigate the upper bound for other bounded attribution methods and provide
498   the corresponding lower bounds for attribution robustness. Besides, our current smoothing technique
499   is restricted to the uniform distribution, and we will explore other distributions for the smoothing
500   technique in future work.
501   Our work attempts to draw the attention of the community to the need for a guarantee of attribution
502   robustness. With the increasingly large number of applications of deep learning, the transparency
503   and trustworthiness of neural networks are crucial for users to understand the outcomes and to avoid
504   any abuse of the techniques. While the study of the security of networks could reveal their potential
505   risks that can be misused, we believe this work has more positive impacts to the community and can
506   encourage the development of more trustworthy deep learning applications.
