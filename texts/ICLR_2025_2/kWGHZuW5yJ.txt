008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            With the rapid advancement of Vision Language Models (VLMs), VLM-based
011            Image Quality Assessment (IQA) seeks to describe image quality linguistically
012            to align with human expression and capture the multifaceted nature of IQA tasks.
013            However, current methods are still far from practical usage. First, prior works focus
014            narrowly on specific sub-tasks or settings, which do not align with diverse real-
015            world applications. Second, their performance is sub-optimal due to limitations in
016
               dataset coverage, scale, and quality. To overcome these challenges, we introduce
               Enhanced Descriptive image Quality Assessment (EDQA). Our method includes
017
               a multi-functional IQA task paradigm that encompasses both assessment and
018
               comparison tasks, brief and detailed responses, full-reference and non-reference
019            scenarios. We introduce a ground-truth-informed dataset construction approach
020            to enhance data quality, and scale up the dataset to 495K under the brief-detail
021            joint framework. Consequently, we construct a comprehensive, large-scale, and
022            high-quality dataset, named EDQA-495K. We also retain image resolution during
023            training to better handle resolution-related quality issues, and estimate a confidence
024            score that is helpful to filter out low-quality responses. Experimental results
025            demonstrate that EDQA significantly outperforms traditional score-based methods,
026            prior VLM-based IQA models, and proprietary GPT-4V in distortion identification,
027
               instant rating, and reasoning tasks. Our advantages are further confirmed by real-
               world applications including assessing the web-downloaded images and ranking
028
               model-processed images. Datasets and codes will be released publicly.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Image Quality Assessment (IQA) aims to measure and compare the quality of images, expecting to
      align with human perception. With the emergence of Vision Language Models (VLMs) (Liu et al.,
034
      2023a; OpenAI, 2023; Ye et al., 2023b), VLM-based IQA begins to attract more research interest (Wu
035
      et al., 2024a;b;d;e; You et al., 2023). These methods leverage VLMs to describe image quality using
036   language, recognizing that language better mirrors human expression, and captures the multifaceted
037   nature of IQA tasks (You et al., 2023). However, existing VLM-based IQA methods still fall short
038   especially in aspects of functionality and performance.
039
      Functionality. There are various application scenarios of IQA, but existing VLM-based IQA models
040
      only support a few of them. For example, one scenario involves assessing a single image downloaded
041
      from the web, while another requires comparing multiple images handled by different algorithms.
042   Also, image restoration needs to assess an image against a reference, while image generation requests
043   non-reference assessments. Therefore, a superior IQA model should be multi-functional to cater
044   to such diverse scenarios. However, existing methods limit to a specific subset of these tasks, such
045   as single-image assessment (Wu et al., 2024b), multi-image comparison (Wu et al., 2024d), or
046   full-reference setting (You et al., 2023), etc. Hence, the limitations in functionality hinder the wide
047   applications of prior methods.
048   Performance. Many IQA methods perform well on some specific datasets but may generalize poorly
049   to other images with different contents or distortions. For instance, Co-Instruct (Wu et al., 2024d)
050   performs well on TID2013 dataset (Ponomarenko et al., 2015) (85.0%), but drops significantly to
051   50.7% when testing on BAPPS dataset (Zhang et al., 2018). A more comprehensive comparison
052   on our newly created benchmark is given in Fig. 1, where it shows that previous works (Wu et al.,
053   2024b;d) under-perform even within their defined tasks and settings. One potential cause for this is the
      limited scope of their training datasets. For example, the added distortion category in Q-Instruct (Wu

079   To address these challenges, we propose a multi-functional IQA model to handle various image quality
080   assessment tasks. We categorize these tasks into two types, as shown in Fig. 2. (a) Single-image
081
      assessment aims to evaluate the quality of a single image by identifying distortions (e.g., “blur” in
      Fig. 2a top). It can also analyze the distortions’ impacts on contents (e.g., blur “affecting the definition
082
      of mountains and trees” in Fig. 2a bottom). (b) Paired-image comparison focuses on comparing the
083
      quality of two distorted images based on the clarity, colorfulness, and sharpness of presented contents.
084   For example, in Fig. 2b, despite reduced contrast, “Image A maintains more scene integrity”, as
085   “Image B’s serious noise level is more detrimental”. We omit multi-image comparison since it is
086   an easy extension of a pairwise one (Gu et al., 2020). Each type includes basic brief sub-tasks for
087   fundamental assessments and detailed sub-tasks to enhance reasoning abilities. Moreover, the model
088   supports both full-reference and non-reference settings, making it adaptable to diverse scenarios.
089
      Under the multi-functional task paradigm, we construct a new large-scale dataset, EDQA-495K, for
090   comprehensive and accurate training and evaluation. First, for diverse distortion, we design and
091   implement 35 types of distortions, each with 5 levels. Second, to enhance the label quality, we inform
092   GPT-4V of the low-level ground truths (e.g., distortions) to leverage its strong high-level perception
093   and language abilities, while avoiding its sub-optimal IQA capabilities. Third, to increase the dataset
094   scale, we scale up the data amount to 495K under the brief-detail combined framework (You et al.,
095   2023). Moreover, our dataset is suitable for both full-reference and non-reference settings.
096   With EDQA-495K dataset, we then train a VLM model, named Enhanced Descriptive image Quality
097   Assessment (EDQA). During training, the original image resolution is retained, leading to a better
098   quality perception regarding resolution. Furthermore, we estimate the confidence of responses from
099   key tokens, providing vital auxiliary information, especially for filtering low-quality responses.
100
      The performance of EDQA is evaluated in Fig. 1 and Sec. 5. In brief tasks, our model surpasses
101
      general VLMs, IQA-specific VLMs, and score-based IQA methods by a large margin. For example,
102   we achieve 95.9% in non-reference comparison on TID2013 dataset, remarkably surpassing Co-
103   Instruct (85.0%) and GPT-4V (88.1%). In detailed tasks, our model also excels, e.g., recording
104   74.9% in non-reference comparison reasoning, compared to 48.1% for Co-Instruct and 60.3% for
105   GPT-4V. At last, experiments on real-world applications including assessing web-downloaded images
106   and comparing model-restored images further demonstrate our superiority. We hope that our multi-
107   functional model could serve as a stepping stone towards a unified VLM-based IQA model. Although
      not yet fully realized, our method showcases the potential of VLM-based IQA models.

      2      R ELATED W ORKS
124
125   Score-based IQA methods. Traditional IQA methods rely on scores to assess image quality and
126   can be divided into full-reference and non-reference methods. (a) Full-reference methods compute a
127   similarity score between a distorted image and a high-quality reference. Early works rely on human-
128   designed metrics such as image information (Sheikh and Bovik, 2006), structural similarity (Wang
129   et al., 2004), phase congruency with gradient magnitude (Zhang et al., 2011), etc. The rapid
130
      advancement of deep learning has also inspired learning-based IQA methods that measure image
      quality through data-driven training. Pioneered by PieAPP (Prashnani et al., 2018) and LPIPS (Zhang
131
      et al., 2018), data-driven approaches (Bosse et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2020; 2021;
132
      Ghildyal and Liu, 2022; Yin et al., 2022; Zhou and Wang, 2022) have spurred innovations in IQA,
133   exhibiting high consistency with human judgments. (b) Non-reference methods directly regress
134   a quality score without a reference image. Initially, human-designed natural image statistics are
135   adopted (Ma et al., 2017; Mittal et al., 2012; 2013; Moorthy and Bovik, 2010; 2011; Saad et al., 2012;
136   Tang et al., 2011). Subsequently, deep-learning-based methods (Kang et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2017;
137   Pan et al., 2018; Su et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020) replace hand-
138   crafted statistics by learning quality priors from extensive data. Recent works focus on enhancing
139   performance by introducing multi-scale features (Ke et al., 2021), CLIP pre-training (Wang et al.,
140   2023), multi-dimension attention (Yang et al., 2022), continual learning (Zhang et al., 2022), multitask
141
      learning (Zhang et al., 2023b), and so on. However, as discussed in You et al. (2023), score-based
      IQA methods limit themselves in complex analyses and multi-aspect weighing of IQA, since the
142
      information provided by a single score is far from sufficient.
143
144   Vision Language Models (VLMs) incorporate visual modality into large language models (Chiang
145   et al., 2023; Openai, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a), aiming to leverage their emergent ability to achieve
146   general visual ability. These VLMs (Alayrac et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; OpenAI,
147
      2023; Wei et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023a; Yin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; 2024; Zhu et al., 2024a)
      have demonstrated a general visual ability and can tackle a variety of multi-modality tasks, including
148
      image captioning (Agrawal et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2015; Young et al., 2014), visual question
149
      answering (Goyal et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2023b; Lu et al., 2022), document understanding (Masry
150   et al., 2022; Mathew et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2019), etc. Although proficient in these high-level
151   perception tasks, we demonstrate in Sec. 5 that general-purpose VLMs still struggle with IQA tasks.
152
153
      VLM-based IQA methods aim to achieve better alignment with human perception leveraging
      the power of VLMs (Wu et al., 2024e). Q-Bench (Wu et al., 2024a) establishes a comprehensive
154
      benchmark for evaluating general-purpose VLMs in low-level perception tasks. Zhu et al. (2024b)
155
      evaluates various VLMs on the widely-adopted two-alternative forced choice (2AFC) task. Q-
156   Instruct (Wu et al., 2024b) enhances the low-level perception ability of VLMs by introducing a
157   large-scale dataset. Q-Align (Wu et al., 2024c) employs discrete text-defined levels for more accurate
158   quality score regression. Co-Instruct (Wu et al., 2024d) concentrates on the quality comparison among
159   multiple images. DepictQA (You et al., 2023) performs quality description, quality comparison,
160   and comparison reasoning in the full-reference setting. Nonetheless, as highlighted in Sec. 1, these
161   methods focus only on specific aspects of IQA tasks, diverging from the original intents of VLMs’
      universality and practical usage requirements, and their performance remains sub-optimal.

      3      TASK PARADIGM AND DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
169
      3.1     TASK PARADIGM
170
171   As highlighted in the introduction, there are various application scenarios for IQA models. First,
172   the evaluation objective can be either single-image assessment or paired-image comparison. The
173   former is useful to rate a web-downloaded image, while the latter suits comparing images processed
174   by two different algorithms. Second, the reference setting may be full-reference or non-reference.
175   For example, image restoration requires assessments based on references, while image generation
176   needs non-reference evaluations. Third, the response could be either brief or detailed. Brief
177
      responses suit well-targeted tasks (e.g., comparison without reasons), while detailed responses
      enhance interpretability and human interaction. To cater to such diverse scenarios, a practical IQA
178
      method should be multi-functional. Therefore, we aim to establish such a multi-functional task
179
      paradigm for VLM-based IQA research. As shown in Fig. 2, we focus on two tasks, each containing
180   both brief and detailed sub-tasks, and supporting both full-reference and non-reference settings.
181
182
          • Task1: single-image assessment. (a) Brief sub-task: distortion identification. Given a distorted
            image, the model should identify the most obvious distortions. (b) Detailed sub-task: assessment
183
            reasoning. In addition to identifying distortions, the model should also describe how these
184
            distortions affect the perception of image contents and the overall image quality.
185
186       • Task2: paired-image comparison. (a) Brief sub-task: instant rating. Given two distorted images,
187
            the model should find the image with better quality. (b) Detailed sub-task: comparison reasoning.
            Building upon the comparison results, the model should first compare the content loss caused
188
            by distortions in the two images, then weigh different aspects to draw inferences, and finally
189
            justify its comparison results. Note that we omit the multi-image (>2) comparison since it can be
190         achieved easily as the extension of paired case (Gu et al., 2020).
191
192
      Compared with previous works, our design unifies various tasks, response types, and reference
      settings into a multi-functional paradigm. In contrast, Q-Instruct (Wu et al., 2024b) focuses on
193
      non-reference single-image assessment, Co-Instruct (Wu et al., 2024d) targets comparison among
194
      multiple images in the non-reference setting, and DepictQA (You et al., 2023) primarily addresses
195   the full-reference setting. Although one can achieve unified IQA by combining these task-specific
196   IQA models, it is impractical due to the significant increase in network parameters, considering that
197   current VLMs are already quite large.
198
199   3.2     D ISTORTION L IBRARY
200
201   Existing IQA datasets (e.g., BAPPS (Zhang et al., 2018), PieAPP (Prashnani et al., 2018)) usually
202   introduce distortions (e.g., noise, blur) into high-quality reference images to create distorted images
203   for evaluation. However, these datasets do not publicly release the distortion information of each
204   image, and their distortions only cover limited scenes. Therefore, we aim to develop a comprehensive
205   large-scale distortion library.
206   Distortion generation. Our distortion system comprises 12 super-categories in total, with each
207   super-category consisting of multiple sub-categories. For instance, the “blur” category encompasses
208   “Gaussian blur”, “motion blur”, “lens blur”, etc. In total, there are 35 sub-categories. For each sub-
209   category, there are 5 severity levels: “slight”, “moderate”, “obvious”, “serious”, and “catastrophic”.
210   A summary is illustrated in Tab. 1. Considering the need to assess high-quality images as well, we
211   retain the original image without any distortions in 5% proportion. See details in Appendix B.1.
212   Multi-distortion setups. In practical usage, multiple distortions may occur simultaneously on
213   the same image. While a simple way to simulate them is to add multiple distortions recursively,
214   real-world scenarios are more complex. First, one distortion may weaken another, such as “brighten”
215   weakens “darken”, “blur” weakens “over-sharpen”. Second, certain distortions exhibit similar visual
      results, such as “pixelate” looks similar to “blur”, making it challenging to identify both if they are

      applied simultaneously. We also observe that humans can identify at most two distortions when three
217   or more are applied, as illustrated in Fig. A3. Hence, we limit the distortion number to two and
218   manually review all possible combinations to exclude contradictory or similar combinations. See
219   details of multi-distortion setting in Tab. A1 and Appendix B.1.

      3.3   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
222
223   High-quality and large-scale datasets are crucial for training VLMs. Following Liu et al. (2023a);
224   Yin et al. (2023), training VLMs requires {images, question, response} triplets, where “images” are
225   the ones to be evaluated, “question” describes the task, and “response” is the ground truth answer. In
226   this section, we detail the construction of our dataset from the selection of images and the collection
227   of questions and responses.
228   Image collection. Typical IQA datasets involve two types of images: high-quality reference images
229   and distorted images to be evaluated. Generating distorted images is easy given our comprehensive
230   distortion library introduced in Sec. 3.2. Existing studies often collect a large number of distorted
231   images from a small number of references (Gu et al., 2020; Ponomarenko et al., 2015). However,
232   the semantic richness of images is also crucial for VLM training. Therefore, we primarily source
233   reference images from the KADIS-700K dataset (Lin et al., 2020), which offers 140K pristine
234   reference images from diverse natural and daily scenes. We also leverage other IQA datasets for their
235   convenience to generate responses (details are below).
236   Question collection. Humans often express similar questions using different sentences, necessitating
237   model robustness to various user questions. For each task, we initially prompt GPT-4 (Openai, 2023)
238   to generate 50 candidate questions. Subsequently, we manually eliminate ambiguous and repetitive
239   ones and correct inaccurate ones, creating a question set of 20 questions (see Appendix B.2). These
240   questions are randomly sampled during training and testing to form the data pair.
241   Response collection. We employ two response types as shown in Fig. 3. The first comprises brief
242   templated responses that are easy to produce, where we emphasize the quantity to bring robust
243   fundamental skills. The second consists of detailed responses, where we emphasize the quality to
244   enhance the model’s advanced reasoning abilities. Existing methods to collect detailed responses
245   mainly rely on human annotation (Wu et al., 2024b; You et al., 2023) and GPT-4V generation (Wu
246   et al., 2024d). However, human annotation can be biased and vary in quality particularly when
247   annotators are untrained or tired (You et al., 2023). Also, GPT-4V is not fully reliable since its IQA
248
      performance is still unsatisfactory as evidenced in Sec. 5.
249   We rethink the key aspects of our desired responses and GPT-4V’s corresponding abilities, introducing
250   GT-informed generation by prompting the Ground Truth (GT) details to enhance GPT-4V’s generation.
251   Specifically, a high-quality detailed response should contain image contents, key distortions, the
252   impacts of distortions on contents, and conclusions (e.g., comparison results). While GPT-4V excels
253   at identifying contents and analyzing impacts, it struggles with distortion identification and quality
254
      comparison, which will be shown in Sec. 5. To compensate for that, we directly provide it with
      explicit GT information. The response generation for each task is detailed subsequently.
255
256   Task1-brief: distortion identification. As shown in Fig. 3a, we first establish a response pool containing
257   20 templates with unspecified distortions. Next, we add distortions into the reference to create its
258   distorted counterpart and populate a sampled template with the specific distortions to complete the
259   response. For streamlined evaluation, we randomly select half of the questions and append the short
260
      answer prompt: “Answer the question using a single word or phrase.” Correspondingly, the response
      will be a single phrase, like “noise”, specifying the distortions.
261
262   Task1-detail: assessment reasoning. Given the reference image, we initially introduce distortions to
263   corrupt the reference. Then, GPT-4V is input with both two images and the distortion information,
264   and requested to assess the quality of the distorted image, as illustrated in Fig. 3b. We instruct
265   GPT-4V to respond from three dimensions: contents, distortions along with their impacts on contents,
266
      and overall quality. Here prior studies (Wu et al., 2024b;d) primarily focus on low-level properties,
      while we consider how these low-level distortions influence the display of high-level contents.
267
268   Task2-brief: instant rating. We begin by sampling a reference image and its two distorted versions
269   from existing IQA datasets, and then compare the Mean Opinion Score (MOS) to determine the
      better one, as shown in Fig. 3c. Similar to distortion identification, we assemble a response pool

320   4         M ODEL D ESIGN
321
322   We primarily follow LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2024) and mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye et al., 2023b) to construct
323   our model, which is composed of a vision encoder, vision abstractor, and LLM. The vision encoder
      is a frozen CLIP pre-trained ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021). The vision abstractor is a four-layer


                                                                                                              6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      transformer, reducing the number of vision tokens to 64 and mapping the vision tokens to textual
325   space. The base LLM is Vicuna-v1.5-7B (Chiang et al., 2023). Following You et al. (2023), we also
326   adopt the unique tag technique, i.e., using specific tags for various types of images, to ensure that the
327   language model can distinguish different input images. To increase robustness, an external high-level
328   perception dataset (detailed description dataset in Yin et al. (2023)) is incorporated during training as
329   a regularization, similar to Wu et al. (2024b); You et al. (2023). See model details in Appendix C. Our
330   experiments in Tab. A9 also show that model architecture has little influence on model performance.
331   Retaining resolution in training. Although previous VLM-based IQA models typically resize all
332   input images to a fixed resolution (Wu et al., 2024b;d), we find this might hurt their performance, as
333   resolution variation may affect visual quality. Instead, we retain the original image resolution during
334   training. Specifically, we interpolate (in bicubic mode) the position embedding in CLIP (Radford et al.,
335   2021) to accommodate varying image resolutions. Ablation studies detailed in Sec. 5.4 demonstrate
336   our model’s capability to assess quality variations attributable to resolution, even without explicitly
337   training on such tasks.
338   Confidence estimation. In many applications, it is important to know a confidence score that
339   indicates when the model is uncertain of its response. Here we use the confidence scores of some
340   key tokens as the confidence of the entire answer. Intuitively, the key tokens are distortion names
341   in distortion identification, and are either “Image A” or “Image B” in instant rating. For detailed
342   reasoning tasks, which feature diverse and non-structured responses, we utilize semantic change
343   testing (Duan et al., 2023) to identify the top 20 tokens with the highest importance scores as key
344   tokens. In semantic change testing, we employ all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (Reimers and Gurevych, 2019) as
345
      the similarity model, due to its high processing speed (14K sentences per second). The predicted
      likelihood of key tokens is averaged as the confidence score. Fig. 6 and Fig. A6 verify that confidence
346
      and model performance are highly correlated.

351   5.1   M ETRICS AND BASELINES
352
353   Accuracy, SRCC, and PLCC. The accuracy metric is utilized for distortion identification and instant
354   rating tasks. VLMs usually produce diverse textual outputs, and we transform them into brief results
355   for accuracy calculation. Specifically, we prompt our EDQA with “Answer the question using a
356   single word or phrase” to encourage direct output of brief responses. For baseline models, we include
357
      all potential answers in the prompt and instruct the model to identify the most accurate one. We
      emphasize that our key motivation is to generate descriptive language rather than quality scores.
358
      However, our approach can produce quality scores using pair-wise comparison if required. The
359
      quality scores are assessed using Spearman Rank Correlation Coefficient (SRCC) and Pearson Linear
360   Correlation Coefficient (PLCC). The results of quality score are given in Appendix D.1.
361
362   GPT-4 score, BLEU, and ROUGE-L. We employ the GPT-4 score to evaluate assessment reasoning
363
      and comparison reasoning tasks, following Liu et al. (2023a). Specifically, we provide GPT-4 with
      both the model-generated response and the corresponding ground truth response. GPT-4 assesses the
364
      helpfulness, relevance, accuracy, and level of detail in the model-generated response relative to the
365
      ground truth, assigning an overall score on a scale of 0 to 10, where a higher score indicates better
366   quality. This average score is subsequently normalized to a scale of 0 to 100%, reported as the GPT-4
367   score metric. We further evaluate the reasoning tasks with classical metrics including BLEU and
368   ROUGE-L score following See et al. (2017); Vaswani (2017).
369
      Baselines. We categorize our baseline methods into general-purpose VLMs and IQA-specific VLMs.
370
      For general VLMs, we include mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye et al., 2023b) (based on LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron
371
      et al., 2023b)), LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2024) (based on Vicuna-v1.5-7B (Chiang et al., 2023)), and
372   the proprietary GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023). IQA-specific VLMs are represented by Q-Instruct (Wu
373   et al., 2024b), Co-Instruct (Wu et al., 2024d), and CLIP-like LIQE Zhang et al. (2023b). Note that
374   Q-Instruct only supports single-image inputs, thus we only test it on non-reference single-image
375   assessment tasks. Additionally, we compare traditional score-based IQA methods including full-
376   reference ones (PSNR, SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), DISTS (Ding et al.,
377   2020)) and non-reference ones (NIQE (Mittal et al., 2013), ClipIQA (Wang et al., 2023), MUSIQ (Ke
      et al., 2021), MANIQA (Yang et al., 2022)) in instant rating task and score regression experiments.

      Co-Instruct is stably superior in the non-reference setting compared to the full-reference setting,

      2023) outperforms other general-purpose VLMs and exceeds prior specialized IQA VLMs. Third,
408   EDQA significantly surpasses all baseline methods, demonstrating our model’s efficacy. Finally, we
409   evaluate our model in an out-of-distribution (OOD) setting. Specifically, for a particular category
410   of distortion (e.g., noise), we use some sub-categories (e.g., Gaussian noise) during training, and

414   context, traditional score-based methods, even the simplest PSNR, outperform all general VLMs

421   large margin, showcasing its substantial advantage.

528   6       C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   We introduce EDQA, a VLM-based IQA model, empowered by a new multi-functional task paradigm,
531   dataset enrichment, and training technique, surpassing baseline methods in both benchmarks and two
532   real-world applications, showing the potential of descriptive quality assessment.
533
      Limitations. First, the fine-grained abilities requiring more high-level perception skills are still
534   unsatisfactory. For example, in Fig. 5c, though identifying noise and pixelation successfully, our
535   model fails to point out that they are respectively located in the left and right parts. One possible
536   solution is to take the segmentation model to add various distortions to different regions. Second, for
537   the convenience of evaluating, analyzing, and improving the model, we mainly focus on standardized
538   answers. To achieve more flexible responses, LLM rewriting and human annotation can be introduced
539   to increase linguistic diversity during dataset construction. Third, whether our assessment can be
      used as feedback to improve the quality of generation or restoration models is still under-explored.

      As stated in Sec. 3.2, to facilitate the dataset construction, we
757   design and implement a comprehensive distortion library. Our
758   distortion system contains 12 distortion super-categories in total,
759   with each category consisting of multiple sub-categories. For
760   instance, the “blur” category encompasses “Gaussian blur”, “motion
761   blur”, “lens blur”, etc. In total, there are 35 sub-categories. For
762   each sub-category, there are 5 severity levels: “slight”, “moderate”,
763   “obvious”, “serious”, and “catastrophic”. In this section, we
764   elaborate on our distortion implementations, including the principles,
765
      formulas, and severity setup. We also provide one example for each Figure A1: Reference for dis-
      implementation in Fig. A2, with the reference image in Fig. A1.        torted images in Fig. A2.
766
767   Blur.
768
769    • Gaussian blur. The distorted image is generated by convolving the reference image with a
770
         Gaussian blur kernel. We set the kernel size (sk ) to be a function of the standard deviation (σk )
         of the blur kernel: sk = round(4 × σk ) + 1.
771
772    • Motion blur. Linear motion blur is applied to the reference image using the linear filter, where
773      (r, σ) ∈ [(5, 3), (10, 5), (15, 7), (15, 9), (20, 12)].
774    • Glass blur.        Filter the image using a Gaussian filter, then randomly jitter each
775      pixel in the image by x pixels, and repeat this process n iterations.      [σ, x, n] ∈
776      [(0.7, 1, 1), (0.9, 2, 1), (1.2, 2, 2), (1.4, 3, 2), (1.6, 4, 2)].
777    • Lens blur. This distortion uses the circular average filter, where r ∈ [1, 2, 4, 6, 8].
778
       • Zoom blur. The image is gradually zoomed in and overlaid to calculate the average.
779
780    • Jitter blur. Each pixel is randomly displaced by a shift of randint(−p, p) pixels both in x and y
781      dimensions, with a total of 5 displacements, where p ∈ [1, 2, 3, 4, 5].
782
      Noise.
783
784    • Gaussian noise in RGB space. Additive Gaussian noise is applied to each of the RGB channels of
785      an image, where σ ∈ [0.05, 0.1, 0.15, 0.2, 0.25].
786
       • Gaussian noise in YCrCb space.                     Similar to the Gaussian noise in RGB
787
         space, this distortion is implemented in YCbCr space, where (σl , σr , σb )                 ∈
788      [(0.05, 1, 1), (0.06, 1.45, 1.45), (0.07, 1.9, 1.9), (0.08, 2.35, 2.35), (0.09, 2.8, 2.8)].
789
790
       • Speckle Noise. Speckle Noise is also known as Multiplicative Gaussian noise, where σ ∈
         [0.14, 0.21, 0.28, 0.35, 0.42].

792    • Spatially correlated noise. The reference image is first corrupted by an additive Gaussian noise,
793      which results in each pixel being corrupted by an independent and identically distributed noise
794      pattern. The resultant image is then filtered with an average filter of kernel size 3 × 3, correlating
795
         the intensity of each pixel with those of the neighboring pixels. More specifically, the distorted
         image is given by:

961   Model Architecture. EDQA primarily adopts the architecture from LLaVA-1.6 (Liu et al., 2024)
962   and mPLUG-Owl2 (Ye et al., 2023b), structured as follows. Specifically, the input images and
963
      the question texts are first tokenized, then fused, finally processed by the Large Language Model
      (LLM) for response generation. (1) Tokenizing input images and question texts. We use a frozen
964
      CLIP pre-trained ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) as the image encoder to convert the input images
965
      into visual tokens. The question texts are tokenized into textual tokens using the SentencePiece
966   tokenizer (Kudo and Richardson, 2018). To bridge the different embedding spaces of visual and
967   textual tokens, we implement a trainable image abstractor, which is a four-layer transformer network,
968   to map visual tokens into the textual space following Ye et al. (2023b). The abstractor can also
969   significantly reduce the number of vision tokens, relieving the computing pressure. (2) Token fusion.
970   We integrate the visual tokens into pre-defined positions within the textual tokens as token fusion. (3)
971   Response generation using LLM. The fused tokens are fed into LLM to generate the final response.
      Here we mainly conduct experiments with Vicuna-v1.5-7B. Despite their capabilities, pre-trained

992    LLMs typically do not perform well on IQA tasks without adjustments. Therefore, we employ
993    LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), a fine-tuning technique that efficiently modifies a small subset of parameters
994    within the LLM. Specifically, we apply LoRA to adjust the projection layers in all self-attention
995    modules, following Hu et al. (2021); Yin et al. (2023). This approach allows for targeted refinement
996    of the model’s performance on IQA tasks without the need for extensive retraining.
997    Model setup. Since the CLIP pre-trained ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) encodes each 14 × 14
998    patch to a visual token, the resolution of the input image should be integer multiples of 14. Therefore,
999    we first pad the size of input images to integer multiples of 14 with zero-padding. We encode the
1000   image patches into visual tokens using the CLIP pre-trained ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021), with
1001   each token having a channel of 1024. The vision abstractor can reduce the number of vision tokens
1002   to 64 and map the vision tokens to the hidden dimension of the LLM, which is 4096. Without the
1003   vision abstractor, the maximum resolution is limited to 672, constrained by computation resources
1004
       (RTX A6000 GPUs). However, with the vision abstractor, we can process images with much larger
       resolutions (up to 2500 × 2500). In our experiments, the maximum image resolution is 1092 × 1456,
1005
       thus the resolutions of all images are retained. The vision abstractor consists of four transformer
1006
       layers with 64 learnable query embeddings. In LoRA of LLM, the parameters of rank and scale
1007   factor are both set as 16. There are 32 attention layers in the LLM in total. In each attention layer, the
1008   projection weights of “query”, “key”, “value”, and “output” are adjusted using two delta parameters
1009   with the shape of 4096 × 16 and 16 × 4096, respectively.
1010
       Training and inference setup. In our experiments, we set the LoRA rank to 16. EDQA is trained for
1011
       1 epoch with batch size 64. Adam optimizer with (β1 , β2 ) = (0.9, 0.95), weight decay 0.001, and
1012
       learning rate 0.0002 is used for training. During inference, the temperature is set to 0, since lots of
1013   predicted information (e.g., distortion, comparison result) need to be certain.

1052
1053   Our key focus in this work is to generate descriptive language rather than quality scores. We focus
1054   more on linguistic descriptions because language is an effective interaction tool in an LLM-based
1055   intelligent agent. With the rapid development of LLMs and multi-modal techniques, in an LLM-
1056   based intelligent agent, language could be a useful tool for interacting and communicating across
1057   quality-related tasks such as image assessment, refinement, editing, and recommendation. Still, if it
1058   is required, our approach can produce quality scores.
1059   Quality score regression. The score regression results are evaluated on the PIPAL, KADID, TID2013,
1060   and CSIQ datasets. These datasets include high-quality reference images and their distorted versions
1061   under various distortions. We calculate the win rate of an image against others to determine its quality
1062   score. Specifically, for an image A, we randomly sample comparison candidates, such as B, C, D,
1063   etc., which share the same content as A but have different distortions. Image A is then compared
1064   pairwise with each of its comparison candidates (B, C, D, etc.). In the full-reference setting, the
1065
       reference image, Image A, and one compared candidate are input into our model for comparison.
       In the no-reference setting, only Image A and its one compared candidate are input for comparison.
1066
       Finally, the win rate of Image A against all its compared candidates is calculated as its quality score.
1067
       The comparison numbers per image for PIPAL, KADID, TID2013, and CSIQ datasets are 58, 62, 60,
1068   and 15, respectively. We show that the comparison number per image could be reduced significantly
1069   without large performance degradation in Tab. A7. The results of quality score regression are given
1070   in Tab. A4 and Tab. A5, proving that our method can generate accurate quality scores.
1071
       Assessing in-the-wild images with different contents. Existing real-world IQA datasets like
1072
       KonIQ Hosu et al. (2020) and SPAQ (Fang et al., 2020) contain real distorted images with various
1073
       contents. To regress quality scores from such a dataset, our model needs to compare images with
1074   different contents though it is trained only to compare images with similar contents, as shown in Task
1075   2 of Fig. 2. The results in Tab. A6 show that even with a task gap between training and test, our
1076   original EDQA still achieves comparable results with previous score-based IQA methods in score
1077   regression. Furthermore, we formulate real-world IQA datasets into instant rating tasks to re-train
1078   our EDQA, i.e., trained on KonIQ then evaluated on SPAQ, and vice versa. Our re-trained EDQA
1079   outperforms all baseline score-based methods trained on the same dataset. These results indicate that
       our method is capable of assessing in-the-wild images with different contents.
