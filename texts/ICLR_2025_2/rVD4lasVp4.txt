009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Bilevel optimization has recently gained popularity because of its applicability in
012            many machine learning applications. Hypergradient-based algorithms have been
013            widely used for solving bilevel optimization problems because of their strong theo-
014            retical and empirical performance in many applications. However, computing these
015            hypergradients requires the evaluation of Hessians (or Hessian-vector products) of
016            the lower-level objective, which presents a major computational bottleneck. To
017            address this challenge, in this paper, we propose LazyBLO (Lazy Hessian Evalu-
018
               ation in Bilevel Optimization), an algorithmic framework that allows infrequent
               Hessian computation during the execution of the algorithm for solving stochastic
019
               bilevel problems. This allows the algorithm to execute faster compared to the state-
020
               of-the-art (SOTA) algorithms that evaluate either a single or multiple Hessians in
021            each iteration. We theoretically establish the performance of vanilla SGD-based
022            LazyBLO and show that, despite the additional errors incurred by the infrequent
023            Hessian evaluations, LazyBLO surprisingly matches the computation complexity of
024            the existing SGD-based bilevel algorithms. Extensive experiments further demon-
025            strate that LazyBLO enjoys significant gains in numerical performance compared
026            to the SOTA approaches. To our knowledge, this is the first work to theoretically
027            establish that multiple Hessian computations are not necessary within each iteration
028            to guarantee the convergence of stochastic bilevel algorithms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Bilevel optimization refers to the class of problems with two levels of hierarchy, wherein the solution
      of the upper-level problem depends on the minimizer of the lower-level problem. Formally, a bilevel
034
      problem is stated as:
035
                                  n                                                   o
036                          minu ℓ(x) ≜ f (x, y∗ (x)) ≜ Eξ∼πf [f (x, y∗ (x); ξ)]
037                         x∈R
                                                      n                              o
038                          s.t. y∗ (x) = arg min g(x, y) ≜ Eζ∼πg [g(x, y; ζ)] ,                          (1)
039                                              y∈Rl

040
      where f (x, y) : Ru × Rl → R and g(x, y) : Ru × Rl → R are upper (UL) and lower-level (LL)
041
      objectives, respectively. Both the UL and LL objectives are assumed to be smooth while the LL
042   objective is strongly convex with respect to y. Moreover, ξ ∼ πf (resp. ζ ∼ πg ) represents a sample
043   of the UL (resp. LL) objective from distribution πf (resp. πg ).
044
045
      Stochastic bilevel problems in (1) have recently gained prominence as many popular machine
      learning problems can be modeled in this form. A few typical examples include hyperparameter
046
      optimization (Franceschi et al., 2018; Shaban et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2021), meta-learning (Franceschi
047
      et al., 2018; Rajeswaran et al., 2019; Ji et al., 2020), adversarial training (Li et al., 2019; Tian et al.,
048   2021; Zhang et al., 2022), reinforcement learning (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999; Hong et al., 2020), neural
049   architecture search (Liu et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2020; Lian et al., 2019), data hyper-cleaning (Franceschi
050   et al., 2018; Shaban et al., 2019), dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2011; Lecouat et al., 2020a;b),
051   and more recently, the pretraining-finetuning pipeline (Li et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) and data
052   reweighting (Pan et al., 2024) in large language models (LLMs). Consequently, a major research
053   effort has been focused on developing efficient algorithms for solving stochastic bilevel optimization
      problems.

      Among all existing methods for stochastic bilevel optimization (see Section 2 for detailed discussions),
055   a state-of-the-art (SOTA) approach is the approximate implicit differentiation (AID) method, which
056   relies on directly computing the approximate implicit gradient of the objective ℓ(·) using the implicit
057   function theorem (Ghadimi & Wang, 2018). Because of its ease of implementation, AID is usually
058   the algorithm of choice for many machine learning applications. A typical AID algorithm updates
059   the LL variable using standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) while the UL variable is updated
060   in each iteration using: x+ = x − αhf , where the descent direction hf (also often referred to as
061   hypergradient) is an approximation of the implicit gradient, i.e.,
                                                                                  −1
062       hf ≈ ∇ℓ(x) = ∇x f (x, y∗ (x)) − ∇2xy g(x, y∗ (x)) ∇2yy g(x, y∗ (x)) ∇y f (x, y∗ (x)). (2)

063
064   Although AID has been widely adopted for stochastic bilevel optimization in the literature, the
065   computation of the hypergradient hf in AID faces two major challenges:
066   ① The hypergradient in Eq. (2) requires multiple Hessian-vector product (HVP) evaluations for
067      approximating the Hessian inverse in each iteration. This creates a major computational bottleneck
068      for solving the problem in Eq. (1) since the explicit Hessian evaluations are computationally
069
         expensive. For example, the Hessian contains one million elements even for a moderately sized
         problem of dimension d = 1000. What is worse is that inverting such a Hessian typically
070
         has a computation complexity of O(d3 ), which is time-consuming even for a moderately sized
071
         problem. Some modern automatic differentiation tools (e.g., Pearlmutter trick (Pearlmutter, 1994)
072      and Jax (Bradbury et al., 2018)) have been proposed to accelerate the Hessian computation,
073      and HVP computation may not be a major computational bottleneck in some situations where
074      extremely computationally powerful GPUs are available. However, for many resource-constrained
075      and computation-constrained settings (e.g., using small or edge-based devices without GPUs),
076      HVP computation is still a computational bottleneck. For example, each HVP computation
077      could be at least two to six times more expensive than gradient computation using Jax when
078      performed on CPUs, which is still non-trivial, and the cost due to HVP remains not negligible
079      in such systems. Moreover, we note that one Hessian inverse estimation needs multiple HVP
080
         computations (Ghadimi & Wang, 2018; Hong et al., 2020). As a result, the total cost of the
         HVP computation depends on the Hessian-inverse estimation accuracy. This would make the
081
         computational cost even higher.
082
083
      ② The hypergradient in Eq. (2) depends on the optimal solution of the LL problem y∗ (x). However,
         solving the LL problem often requires an iterative method. Thus, solving the LL problem to
084
         optimality to obtain an exact value of y∗ (x) may be expensive or even infeasible in practice.
085
086
      We note that, although Challenge ② has been intensively studied in the literature and addressed to
      some extent (e.g., the hypergradient is approximated with y∗ (x) being replaced by y+ ≈ y∗ (x)),
087
      Challenge ①remains under-explored. So far, a foundational open problem in the theory of stochastic
088
      bilevel optimization naturally arises:
089
090    (Q): Can we design algorithms that require fewer Hessian evaluations compared to SOTA, and is it
091    feasible to guarantee any theoretical performance for such algorithms?
092
      In this paper, we answer the above question by developing a new algorithmic framework
093
      called LazyBLO (Lazy Hessian Evaluation in Bilevel Optimization), which allows infrequent
094
      Hessian (Hessian-vector product) evaluations in solving stochastic bilevel problems. Thus,
095   LazyBLO alleviates the computational bottleneck in stochastic bilevel optimization. Specifically, in
096   our LazyBLO approach, a stale version of Hessian is used for multiple iterations while new gradients
097   are computed at each step, thus leading to computational savings. The intuition behind LazyBLO is
098   that, for iterations that are not separated too far from each other, the parameter values usually do not
099   vary significantly. This implies that the Hessians evaluated at these points are highly correlated. Thus,
100   a stale Hessian can still be used to approximate a new one.
101   However, due to the additional errors accumulated because of the use of these stale Hessians,
102   approximate Hessian (HVP) evaluations, and the coupling hierarchical structure of the bilevel
103   problems, it is unclear whether LazyBLO will converge or not. Somewhat surprisingly, we prove that,
104   despite the previously mentioned accumulated errors, LazyBLO not only converges but also achieves
105   the same convergence rate as those of the SOTA non-lazy bilevel algorithms. To our knowledge, this
106   is the first work that uses infrequent Hessian computations for computational savings but still can
107   achieve convergence guarantee in solving stochastic bilevel problems.
      Our major contributions in this work are summarized as follows:

      • We develop a new algorithmic framework LazyBLO that allows the stochastic bilevel algorithms
109     to compute HVPs infrequently. Specifically, the proposed framework updates the HVPs only over
110     a subset of training iterations, while using stale Hessian information in the rest of the iterations.
111
      • We theoretically establish the performance of LazyBLO when the UL and LL updates are performed
112     using vanilla SGD-type updates. We show that the proposed lazy approach, which is supposed to
113     perform worse due to stale Hessian information, can actually match the convergence performance
114     of the SOTA bilevel algorithms. Specifically, we show that to achieve an ϵ-stationary point,
115     LazyBLO requires O(ϵ−2 ) partial gradient and HVP evaluations. Moreover, thanks to the less
116     frequent Hessian evaluations, the wall-clock time of LazyBLO is significantly reduced compared
117     to the SOTA approaches.
118   • We corroborate our theoretical findings via numerical experiments on data hyper-cleaning and
119     deep hyper-representation tasks with real-world datasets. Our numerical results verify that the
120     infrequent evaluations of HVP lead to considerable computational savings.

      error of the HVP. If N gets too large, the error of the HVP approximation would also increase,
294   thus inevitably degrading the performance of LazyBLO. With less frequent Hessian computations,
295   LazyBLO executes faster per iteration in terms of wall-clock time compared to standard bilevel
296   algorithms that require multiple Hessian/vector evaluations in each round of updates (Ghadimi &
297   Wang, 2018; Arbel & Mairal, 2022; Ji et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021), resulting in a significant
298   reduction in computational cost and savings in implementation time.
299
      Another insightful remark on the Jacobian-vector product in (5) is also in order. To date, most
300   of the existing bilevel algorithms compute only one single Jacobian-vector product (JVP) in each
301   iteration, whereas HVPs are computed multiple times in each iteration even in some single-loop
302   bilevel algorithms (e.g., SUSTAIN (Khanduri et al., 2021b), TTSA (Hong et al., 2020), BSA (Ghadimi
303   & Wang, 2018), and ALSET (Chen et al., 2021)). Due to this difference between JVP and HVP in
304   bilevel optimization algorithms, reducing the number of HVP computations is far more important
305   than reducing the computations of JVPs. Therefore, we only focus on reducing the HVP in this paper.
306   We further note that reducing the computation of JVPs can be done in a similar manner as the HVPs
307   established in our work.

309   5      T HEORETICAL P ERFORMANCE A NALYSIS
310
311   In this section, we conduct the theoretical convergence analysis for the LazyBLO framework for
312   solving the bilevel optimization problem in Eq. (1). Note that, although LazyBLO executes faster per
313   iteration, we have a noisier hypergradient due to the use of stale Hessian information. As a result,
314   it remains unclear whether LazyBLO can converge and, if yes, what theoretical convergence rate
315   (i.e., iteration complexity) it can achieve. Intuitively, due to the lazy Hessian information updates,
316
      one can expect that the theoretical convergence rate of LazyBLO cannot outperform its non-lazy
      counterpart. Surprisingly, in this paper, we show that LazyBLO achieves the same convergence rate as
317
      their non-lazy counterpart. This, together with the much lower per-iteration wall-clock time, implies
318
      that LazyBLO will enjoy a much faster speed in terms of wall-clock time. This will also be verified
319   by our experiments in Section 6.
320
321   The convergence analysis for LazyBLO is highly non-trivial due to the following technical challenges:
322   i) The use of lazy Hessian evaluation increases the error of stochastic gradient estimator hft,n for
323   the upper-level function; ii) Due to the hierarchical and coupled structure of bilevel optimization
      problems, the error resulting from the stochastic gradient estimator hft,n with stale Hessian information

      further propagates to and increases the approximation error of y∗ (x) and the approximation error of
325   z∗ (x, y). What is even worse is that z∗ (x, y) is also associated with y∗ (x). All the above complex
326   couplings of laziness-induced errors and the complications associated with these approximation errors
327   are unseen in bilevel optimization algorithm analysis, which significantly increases the difficulty of
328   analyzing the convergence of LazyBLO and necessitate new proof techniques.

      7    C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we proposed the LazyBLO algorithmic framework for solving bilevel optimization prob-
534   lems. Compared to existing works, LazyBLO reduces the Hessian-vector product (HVP) evaluations
535   by updating them periodically and less frequently. Although LazyBLO uses stale HVP evaluations
536   that introduce additional errors, our theoretical analysis demonstrated that LazyBLO not only sur-
537
      prisingly enjoys the same convergence rate guarantee, but also achieves a much faster wall-clock
      time performance. Specifically, to reach an ϵ-stationary point, LazyBLO requires O(ϵ−2 ) for both
538
      partial gradient evaluations and HVP evaluations, which matches the SOTA non-lazy methods. We
539
      conducted experiments on multi-hyperparameter optimization tasks to verify our theoretical findings.

      A     A DDITIONAL RELATED WORK
811
812   Bilevel Optimization: The history of bilevel optimization dates back to 1973 (Bracken & McGill,
813   1973). Some early attempts for solving bilevel problems include: value function (Liu et al., 2021;
814   Sinha et al., 2018; Zemkoho & Zhou, 2021), Karush–Kuhn–Tucker conditions based reformula-
815   tions (Allende & Still, 2013; Sinha et al., 2019; Zemkoho & Zhou, 2021), penalty function (White &
816   Anandalingam, 1993; Anandalingam & White, 1990; Wan et al., 2014), approximate descent (Falk
817   & Liu, 1995; Vicente et al., 1994), and trust region methods (Dempe & Bard, 2001; El-Sobky &
818   Abo-Elnaga, 2018). Among these approaches, approximate descent methods have gained promi-
819   nence recently because of their ease of implementation as well as strong theoretical and empirical
820
      performance in many machine learning applications. Two standard descent-based approaches to
      tackle problems of form (1) are iterative differentiation (ITD) (Domke, 2012; Maclaurin et al., 2015;
821
      Franceschi et al., 2017; 2018; Shaban et al., 2019; Grazzi et al., 2020; MacKay et al., 2019) and ap-
822
      proximate implicit differentiation (AID) (Domke, 2012; Pedregosa, 2016; Liao et al., 2018; Ghadimi
823   & Wang, 2018; Grazzi et al., 2020; Lorraine et al., 2020; Gould et al., 2016; Ji & Liang, 2021;
824   MacKay et al., 2019; Khanduri et al., 2021a; Hong et al., 2020). The basic idea of ITD is to obtain
825   an approximate hypergradient of the loss function ℓ(x) in Eq. (1) by differentiating the unrolled
826   iterates of the LL problem. Consequently, ITD-based approaches need to store all the LL iterates in
827   the memory (Shaban et al., 2019). On the other hand, AID relies on the implicit function theorem
828   to compute the implicit gradient of ℓ(x) without the need to maintain the sequence of LL iterates.
829   Instead of differentiating the iterates of the LL problem, AID computes the implicit gradient by
830   approximately solving a linear system of equations using HVPs. In this work, we focus on AID-based
831
      approaches for solving stochastic bilevel problems.
