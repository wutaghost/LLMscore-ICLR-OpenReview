011                                               A BSTRACT
012
               This paper introduces the Kernel Neural Operator (KNO), a novel operator
013
               learning technique that uses deep kernel-based integral operators in conjunction
014            with quadrature for function-space approximation of operators (maps from func-
015            tions to functions). KNOs use parameterized, closed-form, finitely-smooth, and
016            compactly-supported kernels with trainable sparsity parameters within the integral
017            operators to significantly reduce the number of parameters that must be learned
018            relative to existing neural operators. Moreover, the use of quadrature for numer-
019            ical integration endows the KNO with geometric flexibility that enables operator
020            learning on irregular geometries. Numerical results demonstrate that on existing
021            benchmarks the training and test accuracy of KNOs is higher than popular opera-
022
               tor learning techniques while using at least an order of magnitude fewer trainable
               parameters. KNOs thus represent a new paradigm of low-memory, geometrically-
023
               flexible, deep operator learning, while retaining the implementation simplicity and
024
               transparency of traditional kernel methods from both scientific computing and ma-
025            chine learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Operator learning is a rapidly evolving field that focuses on the approximation of mathematical op-
030   erators, often those arising from partial differential equations (PDEs). Modern approaches leverage
031   machine learning (ML) to approximate complex operator mappings between infinite-dimensional
032
      spaces. Recent approaches include the DeepONet family of neural operators Lu et al. (2021; 2022);
      Zhang et al. (2023); Jin et al. (2022), the family of Fourier neural operators (FNOs) Li et al. (2021);
033
      Kovachki et al. (2021); Li et al. (2023; 2024), graph neural operators (GNOs) Li, Zongyi and Ko-
034
      vachki, Nikola and Azizzadenesheli, Kamyar and Liu, Burigede and Bhattacharya, Kaushik and Stu-
035   art, Andrew and Anandkumar, Anima (2020); Li et al. (2020), and kernel/Gaussian-process-based
036   methods Batlle et al. (2024).
037
038
      In this paper we propose a new method, the kernel neural operator (KNO), that improves upon
      existing operator learning techniques (namely FNOs and GNOs) by leveraging kernel-based deep
039
      integral operators. While numerous works have shown that such methods can produce accurate
040
      approximations of non-linear operators, e.g. Li et al. (2021); Kovachki et al. (2021), this accuracy
041   comes at the cost of an extremely large model parameterization that induces onerous memory and
042   training requirements. These challenges arise because existing methods choose specific discretiza-
043   tions of the aforementioned integral operators without directly learning the kernels; for example, the
044   FNO uses a fast Fourier transform on an equispaced grid to learn the kernel in spectral space while
045   the GNO uses a graph parametrization to discretize the integral. This implicit kernel learning also
046   prevents some desirable properties from being directly encoded into the kernel and enforces other
047   properties that may not be necessary: e.g., FNOs implicitly restrict the class of learnable kernels to
048   radial and periodic ones.
049   In contrast to existing approaches, the KNO directly uses closed-form trainable kernels in con-
050   junction with quadrature to approximate the action of its integral operators. Our numerical results
051   show that the ability to utilize specific types of trainable kernels – namely sparse and compactly-
052   supported kernels – significantly improves the accuracy of the operators learned. Moreover, our
053   approach comes with many other immediate benefits: (1) the use of quadrature allows us to tackle
      operator learning on irregular domains with little to no difficulty; (2) the use of specific closed-form


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      trainable kernels allows us explicit control over the number of trainable parameters; (3) the use of
055   these explicit kernels allows us to directly operate on point-cloud inputs rather than being tied to a
056   regular grid; and (4) the use of closed-form trainable kernels improves the transparency of our neural
057   operator architecture. Additionally, like the FNO family of neural operators, the KNO is formulated
058   entirely in function space and therefore inherits the associated benefits: e.g., zero-shot super reso-
059   lution, superior generalization capabilities in both input and output spaces, discretization-invariance
060   in the input domain, and the ability to evaluate the architecture at arbitrary locations in the domain
061   of the learned operator.
062
063   In addition to the beneficial properties of the KNO outlined above, KNOs obtained state-of-the-
064   art accuracy on a variety of challenging operator learning benchmark problems involving PDEs,
065   including those on non-rectangular domains. Moreover, the KNO was able to accomplish this with
066   1-2 orders of magnitude fewer trainable parameters than reported in the literature for other neural
067   operators.

      1.1   C ONNECTIONS TO OTHER METHODS
070
071   Other operator learning techniques can handle irregular domains but possess restrictions. For exam-
072   ple, the DeepONet family of architectures Lu et al. (2022); Peyvan et al. (2024) can handle input and
073   output functions sampled on irregular domains, but require that all input functions must be sampled
074   at the same input domain locations. The FNO was generalized to tackle arbitrary domains as well,
075   first through the “dgFNO+” architecture Lu et al. (2022), then more recently through the geoFNO
076
      architecture Li et al. (2023; 2024). The latter accomplished this by simultaneously learning both the
      operator and a mapping from input locations to a regular grid, allowing for the use of the FFT. How-
077
      ever, such mappings may not always exist or be feasible to compute. In contrast, the KNO possesses
078
      none of these limitations, requiring only information transfer to a set of quadrature points through
079   straightforward function sampling, in a manner similar to Solodskikh et al. (2023) (though the latter
080   as presented was restricted to regular grids). In summary, the KNO leverages the rich literature on
081   compactly-supported kernels and the even richer literature on quadrature, resulting in a relatively
082   simple, parsimonious, and powerful architecture.
083
084
      More broadly, kernel methods have been in use for decades in machine learning Rasmussen &
085   Williams (2006); Cortes & Vapnik (1995); Boser et al. (1992); Broomhead & Lowe (1988); Sharma
086   & Shankar (2022). Kernels have also been designed to fit data McCourt et al. (2018); Fasshauer
087   & McCourt (2015) and sparsified using partition-of-unity approximation Han et al. (2023). Addi-
088   tionally, kernel methods based on regression have been applied recently to operator learning prob-
089   lems Batlle et al. (2024) using an extremely small number of trainable parameters, albeit with gener-
090   ally lower accuracy than the KNO. The KNO falls on the spectrum between these kernel/GP operator
091   learning methods and FNOs (which are also kernel-based), being more parameterized than the for-
092   mer and less than the latter. Kernels have also been heavily leveraged in scientific computing within
093
      (shallow) integral operators Gingold & Monaghan (1977); Peskin (2002); Kassen et al. (2022a;b);
      Hsiao & Wendland (2008); Cortez (2001); Shankar & Olson (2015) or as generators of finite differ-
094
      ence methods Wright & Fornberg (2006); Fornberg & Flyer (2015); Bayona et al. (2019); Fasshauer
095
      & McCourt (2015); Shankar et al. (2014); Shankar & Fogelson (2018), and more recently to accel-
096   erate the training of physics-informed neural networks Sharma & Shankar (2022). Our development
097   of the KNO was the result of aggregating insights from this very broad body of work on kernel
098   methods and applying them deep learning and, more specifically, deep operator learning.
099
100
      Limitations: Much like the FNO and other neural operators, our method is subject to a curse of
101
      dimensionality, in our case for two reasons: first, because the kernel interpolant in our pipeline re-
102   quires decreasing fill distance of the data sample locations in order to converge; and second, because
103   the number of quadrature points in most standard quadrature rules grows exponentially with dimen-
104   sion (the FNO faces the same problem). There are some well-known approaches to ameliorate these
105   issues Zech & Schwab (2020); L’Ecuyer (2018), but we opt for a general presentation and so do
106   not use those approaches here. Finally, our results for other methods were based on reported data
107   from Lu et al. (2022); Batlle et al. (2024), not our own implementations; reported parameter counts
      for those methods may hence not be optimal.

      2     K ERNEL N EURAL O PERATORS (KNO S )
109
110   Given Euclidean domains      Ωu , Ωy and du , dy ∈ N, neural operators learn mappings from a Banach
111   space U = Ωu ; Rdu of Rdu -valued functions to a Banach space Y = Y(Ωy ; Rdy ) of Rdy - valued
112   functions through supervised training on a finite number of input-output measurements. From a
113   statistical learning point of view, neural operators are  learned from measurements of input functions
114   drawn from a probability measure ν on U Ωu ; Rdu . In the following, we present the formulation
115
      of KNOs, which are a special class of neural operators that leverage properties of certain kernel
      functions for the benefit of efficiency and accuracy.
116

524   4   C ONCLUSION
525
      We presented the kernel neural operator (KNO), a novel, simple, and transparent architecture that
526
      leverages kernel-based deep integral operators discretized by numerical quadrature. The use of ex-
527
      plicit, closed-form, diagonal, matrix-valued kernels allowed the KNO to achieve superior accuracy
528   with far fewer trainable parameters than other neural operators (on both regular and irregular do-
529   mains). We found that compactly-supported kernels used throughout (save the final layer) were the
530   optimal choice to obtain a general purpose architecture well-suited to a wide variety of operator
531   learning problems. In our view, our results also indicate that it may be possible to achieve similar
532   parameter counts (and possibly relative errors) with other neural operators such as DeepONet and
533   the FNO, albeit with architecture tuning, careful training, and problem-specific initializations.
534   For future work, we will prove the universal approximation capabilities of the KNO and leverage
535   the closed form kernels to derive rigorous error estimates for the approximation of PDE solution op-
536   erators. We will also explore interpretable lifting and projection operators, problem-specific archi-
537   tectures (for instance, for linear operators), novel quadrature schemes, and other types of problem-
538   dependent kernels not discussed in this work. We anticipate that the KNO will be widely applicable
539   to a variety of machine learning tasks beyond approximating PDE solution operators. We plan to
      explore these in future work as well.

      A.2 C OMPLEXITY OF C OMPUTING I NTEGRALS VIA Q UADRATURE
774   We now discuss the complexity of evaluating the kernel integrals using quadrature, in contrast with
775   The FNO’s approach which leverages the FFT and admits O(n log n) per integral (following the
776   runtime comparisons reported in section 3.3). If n is the size of the input sample grid, the cost of
777
      quadrature is dependent on the number of quadrature points. Our approach, that is, to precompute
      a quadrature rule with Nq points and weights incites a cost of O(Nq ) per output location; thus,
778
      incites a total cost of O(nNq ). Now in practice, Nq < n and also with the KNO, n = Nq for
779
      ℓ ∈ [L − 1] so that the total cost is closer O(Nq ) for a given n, which makes this competitive
780   with the O(n log n) FNO cost. Regardless, both the FNO and the KNO likely suffer from the curse
781   of dimensionality on their respective grids and their costs are dominated by MLP operations. The
782   quadrature and polynomial approximation literature contains many approaches to tackle this (sparse
783   grids, composite quadrature rules, and so forth). We plan to tackle this in future work.
784
      However, it is important to keep sight of the fact that quadrature allows us to tackle problems with
785
      irregular domain geometries and point cloud data, unlike the standard FFT used in the FNO; the
786   alterative would be to use deformation maps to learn coordinate transforms to regular grids Li et al.
787   (2023; 2024), but such mappings do not always exist. Further, it may be possible to accelerate
788   our framework by mapping KNO layers to standard convolutional layers. This could be done by
789   imposing structure on the feature detector and/or filter to mimic the operation of the kernel in order
790   to further leverage existing ML toolchains (while losing geometric flexibility as in the FNO). Similar
791   work was done on the function approximation side in integrated neural networks (INNs) Solodskikh
792   et al. (2023). It may also be of interest to explore things in the other direction: engineering kernels
793   and/or loss functions that mimic the effects of certain convolutional layers (say, specific kinds of
794
      filters, stencils/filter sizes, and feature detectors).
795   A.3 VARYING THE MATRIX - VALUED KERNEL’ S STRUCTURE
796
      Our decision to parameterize the KNO’s integral operators with a diagonal matrix-valued kernel was
797
      made with the intention of developing a parameter-efficient neural operator that performed on-par
798   with or better than existing neural operator on the various benchmark datasets. Nonetheless, the
799   KNO allows us to adopt other formulations of this matrix-valued kernels, and it is unclear if this is
800   possible in FNOs. This is particularly relevant in the context of PDEs, where the solution operators
801   can be expressed in terms of Green’s functions that themselves have inherent structure Boullé &
802   Townsend (2023).

808   kernel may be application dependent; note that the tridiagonal one performed better on the Darcy
809   (cont) problem and the dense one performed best on the Burgers’ equation. A cautious reader
      might be skeptical as to why a diagonal-matrix valued kernel was effective, given it does not couple

901

      us to avoid the exponential ill-conditioning inherent to interpolation on boundary-anchored equis-
902
      paced grids. The Gaussian kernel, on the other hand, is infinitely-smooth and capable of exponential
903
      convergence on infinitely-smooth target functions. Its corresponding linear system hence suffers
904   from exponential ill-conditioning (much like polynomial Vandermonde matrices); this follows di-
905   rectly from the impossibility theorem Platte et al. (2011); Adcock et al. (2019).
906
907   We also ran another experiment (results not shown) to investigate the impact of limited smoothness
908   of the Wendland kernels on efficacy. Specifically, we replaced the Wendland kernels with C 4 (R3 )
909
      Matérn kernels, which are finitely-smooth but not compactly-supported. We observed worse errors
      in all our experiments using Matérn kernels over Wendland kernels (but still better results than using
910
      the Gaussian everywhere). It may be possible to understand this in terms of the Fourier transforms
911
      of these kernels. In general, in the context of interpolation, the rate of decay of the Fourier transform
912   of a kernel can affect its approximation power Fasshauer (2007). In this context, we believe it affects
913   trainability also. Wendland kernels, being compactly-supported, have Fourier transforms with heavy
914   frequency tails (by the Fourier uncertainty principle), thus carrying more information. In contrast,
915   Gaussians and even other less smooth Matérn kernels have more concentrated Fourier transforms
916   with fast decay (exponential in the frequency for Gaussian kernels, algebraic for the Matérn kernels),
917   which likely results in a loss of information during training. In future work, we plan to apply Fourier
      analysis tools to further understand and clarify this intuition.
