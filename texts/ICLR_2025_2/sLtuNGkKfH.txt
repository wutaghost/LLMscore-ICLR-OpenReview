009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Unsupervised novelty detection (UND), aimed at identifying novel samples, is es-
012            sential in fields like medical diagnosis, cybersecurity, and industrial quality con-
013            trol. Most existing UND methods assume that the training data and testing normal
014            data originate from the same domain and only consider the distribution variation
015            between training data and testing data. However, in real scenarios, it is common
016
               for normal testing and training data to originate from different domains, a chal-
               lenge known as domain shift. The discrepancies between training and testing data
017
               often lead to incorrect classification of normal data as novel by existing methods.
018
               A typical situation is that testing normal data and training data describe the same
019            subject, yet they differ in the background conditions. To address this problem,
020            we introduce a novel method that separates subject information from background
021            variation encapsulating the domain information to enhance detection performance
022            under domain shifts. The proposed method minimizes the mutual information
023            between the representations of the subject and background while modelling the
024            background variation using a deep Gaussian mixture model, where the novelty
025            detection is conducted on the subject representations solely and hence is not af-
026            fected by the variation of domains. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
027
               model generalizes effectively to unseen domains and significantly outperforms
               baseline methods, especially under substantial domain shifts between training and
028
               testing data.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Novelty detection (Markou & Singh, 2003; Pimentel et al., 2014; Sabokrou et al., 2018; Pang et al.,
034   2021) has received considerable attention for its essential applications in finance, healthcare, and
035   security. In these fields, models must accurately predict in-distribution data and detect out-of-
036   distribution (OOD) inputs, representing novel or unseen cases. Failing to detect such inputs can
037   have serious consequences. OOD detection, often used interchangeably with novelty detection, is
038   also closely related to outlier detection (Hodge & Austin, 2004), anomaly detection (Chandola et al.,
039
      2009), fault detection (Isermann, 1984), and one-class classification (Khan & Madden, 2014). For
      instance, unsupervised anomaly assumes most or even all training data represent normal behaviour
040
      or patterns and identifies the test data with any large deviations as anomalous. Therefore, it can be
041
      regarded as a special case of unsupervised novelty detection.
042
043   Numerous novelty detection methods (Rumelhart et al., 1986; Schölkopf et al., 1999; Breunig et al.,
044   2000; Liu et al., 2008b; Schölkopf et al., 2001; Ruff et al., 2018; Viroli & McLachlan, 2019; Hu
045
      et al., 2020; Cai & Fan, 2022) have been proposed, For classical methods, kernel density estimation
      (KDE)(Parzen, 1962) utilizes a kernel function to estimate the density of data and treats the density
046
      as novelty score. OC-SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001) tries to separate normal data from novel data
047
      by a hyperplane. Local outlier factor (LOF) (Breunig et al., 2000) regards data with lower density
048   than its surrounding data as novel data. Autoencoder (AE) (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006) uses
049   reconstruction error as a novelty metric. Isolation forest (IF) (Liu et al., 2008a) uses the length of
050   iTree to detect novel samples. As for recent state-of-the-art methods, ALAD (Zenati et al., 2018)
051   based on bi-directional GANs, uses reconstruction errors based on these adversarially learned fea-
052   tures to determine if a data sample is novel. (Ruff et al., 2018) released DeepSVDD, which utilizes
053   a neural network to enclose the representations of normal data in a hypersphere in the latent space
      with minimal volume. MO-GAAL (Liu et al., 2019) can directly generate informative potential

      outliers based on the mini-max game between a generator and a discriminator and n generate a
055   reference distribution for the whole dataset to provide sufficient information to assist the classifier
056   in describing a boundary that can separate novel samples from normal data effectively. DROCC
057   (Goyal et al., 2020) which is on the basis of low-dimensional manifold assumption on normal data,
058   generates negative samples to provide general and robust identification on novel samples. SUOD

      and the perturbed data into two different classes. Then, data classified as perturbed is considered to

      2008b), enables non-linear partition on subspaces of varying sizes, offering a more effective novelty
065
      (anomaly) isolation solution.

      A significant limitation of many existing unsupervised novelty detection (UND) methods is that,
089
      while they acknowledge the difference in distributions between training and test data, they often
090   assume that both training and normal test data originate from the same domain. However, in real-
091   world scenarios, it is common for normal test data and training data to be sourced from different
092   domains, a challenge referred to as domain shift. For example, training and normal test data may be
093   collected under varying environmental conditions, from different individuals, or at different times.
094   A typical instance of this issue arises when training and test data describe the same subject but differ
095   in background conditions, as illustrated in Figure 1. Such domain shifts can significantly affect
096   model performance by leading to misclassifications, particularly when the domain differences in the
097   test data are not accounted for during training (Wu et al., 2023).
098   To address the challenges of domain shift, methods such as domain generalization, empirical risk
099   minimization(ERM) (Vapnik, 1991) and invariant risk minimization (IRM)(Arjovsky et al., 2019)
100   have been developed. These approaches generally require task-specific labels (e.g., classification la-
101   bels) and domain labels to address domain differences in the training data. In some instances, hybrid
102   or auxiliary labels are necessary to further improve model performance. However, labelling domain-
103   specific information for each data point can be resource-intensive. In contrast, our proposed model
104
      simplifies this process by only requiring the number of domains from which the data is sourced,
      reducing the labelling burden.
105
106   To address the challenge of background (domain) shifts between training and normal test data in
107   unsupervised novelty detection, we propose a novel and effective method called Subject-Novelty
      Detection (SND). SND disentangles subject information from background features in the training

108
      data, allowing the model to focus on subject-specific features during novelty detection. This enables
109   SND to maintain strong performance even when the normal test data exhibits entirely different
110   background characteristics from the training data. Unlike other domain adaptation methods, which
111   often require both task and domain labels for each data point, SND only necessitates knowledge of
112   the number of domains, making it more efficient while preserving high accuracy in novelty detection.
113   Our main contributions are as follows:
114
115          • We introduce Subject-Novelty Detection (SND), which isolates subject information from
116            background variations, enabling robust detection even under significant domain shifts.
117
118          • SND eliminates the need for prior knowledge of the subject or background details. It only
119
               requires information on the number of domains in the training data, making it efficient and
               adaptable for detecting novelty in domain-shift scenarios.
120
121          • We extensively compare SND with existing methods and domain shift techniques, demon-
122            strating that SND achieves state-of-the-art results in various scenarios.

      2.1   U NSUPERVISED N OVELTY D ETECTION
128
129   Novelty detection (ND) is usually an unsupervised learning problem, where training data are un-
130   labeled and most or all of them are normal data. Novelty detection can be divided into two types.
131   The first type is to identify novel samples in a dataset by training a machine learning model, where
132   the novel samples or outliers are identified once the model training is finished. The methods of this
133   type are often based on density estimation or use some robust loss functions. The typical methods
134   include robust kernel density estimation, Gaussian mixture models, robust PCA (Xu et al., 2010;
135   Candès et al., 2011), low-rank representations (Liu et al., 2012), robust kernel PCA (Fan & Chow,
136   2019), etc. The second type is to train a model on a training dataset without any outliers or with a
137
      very small fraction of unlabeled outliers. This setting is the same as unsupervised anomaly detection
      and one-class classification. Typical methods include PCA, autoencoder (Rumelhart et al., 1986),
138
      LOF (Breunig et al., 2000), Isolation forest (Liu et al., 2008b), OC-SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001),
139
      SVDD (Tax & Duin, 2004), Deep SVDD (Ruff et al., 2018), DAGMM (Viroli & McLachlan, 2019),
140   AnoGAN (Schlegl et al., 2017), HRN (Hu et al., 2020), PLAD (Cai & Fan, 2022), DPAD (Fu et al.,
141   2024), etc. In this study, we focus on the second type.
142
143
144   2.2   D OMAIN A DAPTATION AND T RANSFER L EARNING
145
146   Domain adaptation and transfer learning strategies play a pivotal role in enhancing the performance
147
      of learning models when faced with new tasks or domains. Traditional machine learning models are
      often trained on specific datasets, but real-world scenarios frequently present data distributions that
148
      vary across tasks or domains. Domain adaptation techniques address discrepancies between data
149
      distributions in source and target domains. These include instance re-weighting, feature mapping,
150   and adversarial learning (Tzeng et al., 2017). Transfer learning leverages knowledge from related
151   tasks to mitigate the data and computational requirements of new tasks, finding success in computer
152   vision and natural language processing domains (Pan & Yang, 2009).
153
      Recent advances in domain adaptation and transfer learning include unsupervised domain adapta-
154
      tion techniques that align source and target domain features without requiring target domain labels
155
      (Tzeng et al., 2017). Multi-source domain adaptation improves model performance by integrating
156   data from multiple source domains (Zhao et al., 2018). Cross-modal transfer learning has made
157   strides in knowledge transfer between different modalities (Chen et al., 2019). Meta-learning tech-
158   niques, such as Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), excel in rapid adaptation to new tasks
159   (Finn et al., 2017). Self-supervised learning reduces the need for labelled data in transfer learning
160   scenarios (Chen et al., 2020). More recently, some researchers explored OOD detection combined
161   with domain adaptation (Oza et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Carvalho et al., 2024), focusing primar-
      ily on transitioning from one scene to another.

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
185
186   To be precise, suppose we have a training dataset consisting of N images, denoted as D =
187   {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN }, in which each xi ∈ RC×H×W has a background bi chosen from a set of K
188   different backgrounds B = {B1 , B2 , . . . , BK } and all or most of the N samples are normal. No-
189   tably, although the total number of backgrounds K is known, the specific background type of each
190   image is unknown. This setting is practical since data or images collected often come from different
191   backgrounds (or domains more generally) and labelling the backgrounds is costly. We consider a
                             ′   ′      ′                              ′              ′
192   test set D′ = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xM }, where the background bi of each xi is chosen from a larger set
193   B̃ = {B1 , B2 , . . . , BK , BK+1 , . . . , BK+K ′ }. Note that BK+1 , . . . , BK+K ′ are actually new back-
194   grounds different from B1 , B2 , . . . , BK and K ′ is unknown. Our goal is to learn a model from D
195   to determine whether a new sample from D′ is a novel sample in terms of the subject information
196   rather than the background information. This is a nontrivial task because the domain of normal data
197
      changed, or in other words, the distribution of normal data changed.
198   A simple example of the task is shown in Figure 1, where the training set contains images of digit
      ′ ′
199    0 with 4 different coloured backgrounds (white, yellow, blue, pink), and the testing set contains
200   images of digits (0 − 9) with 5 different coloured backgrounds (white, yellow, blue, pink, green). To
201   evaluate the performance of methods under extreme background shifts, the background of digit ′ 0′
202   (testing normal data) is set to a completely unseen green background, while backgrounds of digits
203
      (1 − 9) (testing novel data) are set to all 5 colours. Our aim is to identify digits (1 − 9) as novel
      samples which contain different subject information while treating digits 0 as normal samples which
204
      differ from training data only in background information.
205
206   Classical ND tasks only consider the distribution difference between the training data and testing
207   novel data. Our tasks consider not only the distribution difference mentioned before but also the
208   background (domain) shift between training data and testing normal data, which leads to distribu-
209
      tion difference between them. Thus unsupervised subject novelty detection is a more complicated
      novelty detection task. Classical ND methods have high false positive rates on this task because they
210
      will label the normal samples with new backgrounds as novel samples.

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we propose a novel approach to novelty detection (ND) named SND. This method
530   disentangles subject and background information across different scenes and detects novelties using
531   only subject features. By reducing the mutual information between subject and background, we
532   achieve effective separation, demonstrating that our model significantly outperforms existing meth-
533   ods in ND scenarios with domain shifts. Experimental results demonstrate the method’s exceptional
534   performance in novelty detection scenarios where the testing data distribution differs from the train-
535   ing data. The proposed SND offers new insights and methods for ND research, holding significant
536
      importance for real-world novelty detection tasks.
537   Future work could further optimize the SND method and explore its performance on more complex
538   datasets and practical applications to validate its broad applicability and robustness. We anticipate
539   that SND will play a greater role in high-stakes domains (such as finance, healthcare, and defence
      intelligence), helping achieve more reliable novelty detection.

783   C    A LGORITHM OVERVIEW
784
785   In Algorithm 1, we present the flowchart illustrating our method. This process is divided into two
786   main stages: the extraction of subject and background information, followed by novelty detection.
787   The algorithm begins by initializing the network parameters and performing feature extraction, de-
788   composition, and mutual information minimization to ensure statistical independence between sub-
789   ject and background features. To model the background components, a deep Gaussian Mixture
790   Model (GMM) is employed, and the overall loss function is computed for optimization. The testing
791   stage focuses on computing the novelty score for each test sample, which is used for identifying
792
      novel subjects.
793
794   D    M ODEL S TRUCTURE
795
796   In our proposed anomaly detection model, we integrate a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), a Con-
797   trastive Mutual Information Upper Bound (CLUB) module, and a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)
798   to address complex background and subject separation. The Encoder consists of four convolutional
799   layers with 64, 128, 256, and 512 filters, respectively. Each convolutional layer has a kernel size of
800
      4x4, a stride of 2, and padding of 1, followed by batch normalization and LeakyReLU activation.
      The output is then flattened and passed through fully connected layers to generate 128-dimensional
801
      latent vectors for both the background and the subject.
802
803   The Decoder reconstructs the input image using transposed convolutional layers with the same struc-
804   ture as the encoder, but in reverse. Specifically, the decoder has four layers with 512, 256, 128, and
805   64 filters, and similarly employs batch normalization and LeakyReLU activation. The final output
806
      layer uses a Sigmoid activation function to produce the reconstructed image.
807   To ensure effective disentanglement between the background and subject features, the CLUB mod-
808   ule estimates mutual information by learning mean and log variance through two fully connected
809   networks. Each network consists of linear layers with 128 inputs, followed by a 64-unit hidden
      layer, LeakyReLU activation, and dropout.

844   The Colored MNIST dataset was originally developed by IRM (Arjovsky et al., 2019) to encourage
845   classifiers to overfit on spurious features such as colour, rather than focusing on the intrinsic shape
846   features of the digits. For our specific task, we expanded on this concept by creating the Multi-
847   background MNIST and Multi-background Fashion-MNIST (Fashion-MNIST) datasets. These are
848
      based on the original MNIST and Fashion-MNIST datasets but introduce significant domain shifts
      to further challenge the models’ generalization capabilities.
849
850   The training set consists of images of digits (0-9) displayed on backgrounds with four different
851   colours: yellow, purple, red, and blue. The testing set consists of digits (0-9) placed on a green
852   background, which was not seen during training. Each digit is treated as the normal class in turn,
853   with the remaining digits considered anomalies for both training and testing.
854   In our study, we modified the original MNIST dataset, which features white digits on a black back-
855   ground. We randomly selected 4,000 images for the training set and 1,000 images for the test set. In
856   the modified dataset, we replaced the white digits with red, and the black backgrounds were sequen-
857   tially changed to various colours. In the first variation, the training set images have backgrounds
858   in white, purple, and blue, while the test set images feature a green background. In the second
859   variation, the training set backgrounds include yellow, white, purple, and blue, with the test set still

      featuring a green background.
