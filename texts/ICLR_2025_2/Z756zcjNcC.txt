                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            A common theme across multiple disciplines of science is to understand the un-
011
               derlying dependencies between variables from observational data. Such depen-
               dencies are often modeled as Bayesian Network (BNs), which by definition are
012
               Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). Recent advancements, such as NOTEARS and
013
               DAG-GNN, have focused on formulating continuous DAG constraints and learn-
014            ing DAGs via continuous optimization. However, these methods often have scal-
015            ability issues and face challenges when applied to real world data. In this pa-
016            per, we propose Denoising Diffusion Causal Discovery (DDCD), a new learning
017            framework that leverages Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) for
018            causal structural learning. Using the denoising objective, our method allows the
019            model to explore a wider range of noise in the data and effectively captures both
020            linear and nonlinear dependencies. It also has reduced complexity and is more
021            suitable for inference of larger networks. To accommodate potential feedback
022
               loops in biological networks, we propose a k-hop acyclicity constraint. Addi-
               tionally, we suggest using fixed-size bootstrap sampling to ensure similar train-
023
               ing performance across varying dataset sizes. Our experiments on synthetic data
024
               demonstrate that DDCD achieves consistent competitive performance compared
025            to existing methods while noticeably reducing computation time. We also show
026            that DDCD can generate trustworthy networks from real-world datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Learning a network structure that represents underlying causal dependencies between variables in
033
      observational data has long been a crucial goal. It plays an important role in multiple disciplines,
      including genetics, epidemiology, and economics (Slonim, 2002; Pearl, 2009; Uhler et al., 2013;
034
      Spirtes & Zhang, 2016; Pingault et al., 2018; Glymour et al., 2019). Such networks, where nodes
035
      represent feature variables and edges capture potential relationships, are often represented as Di-
036   rected Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), which allow no cycles.
037
038   There has been a great deal of prior work developing methods to address this problem. The PC
039
      algorithm (Spirtes & Glymour, 1991) is a constraint-based approach that iteratively testing condi-
      tional independence. GES (Chickering, 2002) is a score-based method that searches for the causal
040
      structure that maximizing a scoring function. LiNGAM (Shimizu et al., 2012), on the other hand,
041
      is a structural equation model (SEM)-based method. However, they scale poorly as the size of data
042   increases; in fact, the learning inference problem has been proven to be NP-hard (Chickering et al.,
043   2004). To address this challenge, Zheng et al. (2018) proposed a method called NOTEARS that
044   introduces a continuous acyclicity score, which can be solved by a regular numerical optimizer.
045
      Many others have since extended this formulation, focusing on scalability (Yu et al., 2019; Lee et al.,
046
      2019; Ng et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021), convexity (Bello et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2023; Ng et al.,
047
      2024), sparsity control (Wei et al., 2020; Ng et al., 2020), and nonlinearity (Yu et al., 2019; Ng et al.,
048   2019; Zheng et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2022; Ng et al., 2022; Kalainathan et al.,
049   2022). Its variations also have been applied to a wide range of settings, such as time series (Pamfil
050   et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Shang et al., 2021) and gene networks (Shu et al., 2021; Agamah
051   et al., 2022; Zhu & Slonim, 2024). There are also analyses and discussions of the application of
052   such models to datasets with unequal variances and different data types (Reisach et al., 2021; Kaiser
053   & Sipos, 2021; Ng et al., 2024). Methods that focus on topological ordering instead of a DAG
      structrual constraint have also been explored (Sanchez et al., 2022).

054
      In this paper, we propose a set of Denoising Diffusion Causal Discovery (DDCD) models that offer
055   significant improvements on scalability and the models’ ability to capture nonlinear transformations.
056   DDCD was inspired by the designs of Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho
057   et al., 2020) and Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022). In DDCD, we introduce
058   noise progressively into the data during a forward diffusion process and then reverse the process by
059   predicting the added noise under the constraint of the learned adjacency matrix. By replacing the
060   least squares loss in NOTEARS with a denoising objective, DDCD allows the model to explore a
061   broader range of noise, which enhances its ability to capture both linear and nonlinear relationships.
062   We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on both synthetic data and large scale
063
      real-world datasets. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
064
             • We prove the validity of the denoising objective, which paves the way to using diffusion
065            models for causal structural learning.
066
067
             • We push the boundary of structural learning on nonlinear data by showing that the nonlinear
               transformation function can be approximated together with the adjacency matrix.
068
069          • We introduce a k-hop acyclicity constraint, which approximates acyclicity within a k-hop
070            neighborhood. It is a relaxation of the acyclicity constraint in NOTEARS and has improved
071
               complexity. We also applied gradient clipping to avoid gradient explosion when the graph
               is large. We discuss when acyclicity is helpful and when it may not be.
072
073          • We propose a fixed-size bootstrap sampling technique so the learning process proceeds
074            similarly for data sets of different sizes.

079
080   Given a dataset X ∈ Rn×d , where n is the number of samples and d is the number of feature
081   variables, the objective is to learn a meaningful dependency graph G represented by the weighted
082   adjacency matrix W ∈ Rd×d . Such a graph is often defined as a Bayesian Network (BN), which by
083   definition is a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), where there are no cycles or self loops.

      2.3   C ONTINUOUS DAG CONSTRAINT
109
110   Traditional network inference approaches often rely on combinatorial optimization, which becomes
111   computationally infeasible for large graphs. To address this issue, Zheng et al. (2018) proposed a
112   method called NOTEARS that introduced a continuous score characterizing graph acyclicity:
113
114
                                           h(W ) = tr(eW ◦W ) − d,                                       (5)
115   where ◦ is the Hadamard product, eW is the matrix exponential of W , and tr() is the trace of a
116   matrix. Essentially, matrix W is a DAG if and only if h(W ) = 0. Since the function h(W ) has a
117   simple and smooth gradient function, it can be used in many gradient-based continuous optimization
118   algorithms. In NOTEARS, the DAG W is learned by optimizing a modification of Equation 2 (with
119   only the L1 loss) while keeping h(W ) near zero with an augmented Lagrangian method using a
120   L-BFGS optimizer. In terms of complexity, since the score function h(W ) requires the matrix
121   exponential, the runtime of NOTEARS is at least O(d3 ).

127   forward process aims to generate a series of noisy samples x0 , x1 , ..., xT over T steps, where xT
128   usually stands for pure noise. In each step, a small amount Gaussian noise is gradually introduced
129   following a diffusion schedule β as shown in Equation 6.

      3.3   S CALE HANDLING WITH SMOOTHED FEATURES AND ADJACENCY MATRIX
251   While continuous optimization causal discovery algorithms have yielded great success on synthetic
252   SEM datasets, recent studies (Reisach et al., 2021; Kaiser & Sipos, 2021) have shown that they often
253   don’t work that well on datasets with standardized features or on real-world datasets where the scales
254   are unknown. The root cause, as pointed out in Kaiser & Sipos (2021), is the use of SEM equations
255   in Equations 1 and 3. An underlying assumption of these two SEM equations is that the variables
256   in X must be on the same scale. If the scales of the features are altered differently due to data
257   standardization, the equation will no longer hold. The DDCD Linear and DDCD Nonlinear methods
258   we propose here have the same problem. To overcome this issue, we propose a smoothed version
259
      of DDCD. Instead of trying to learn the exact values in the adjacency matrix of the SEM, DDCD
      Smooth tries to learn a normalized adjacency, where the expected values of the edge weights are d1 .
260
      This normalized adjacency matrix is conceptually similar to the one used in graph convolution (Kipf
261
      & Welling, 2016). To do this, we first use an MLP with Tanh activation to normalize all features to
262   the range of -1 to 1 regardless. An illustration of the architecture of this model is provided in Figure
263   1c. In appendix A.2, we also have a short proof that extends Theorem 1. Basically we show that
264   in this case, instead of estimating the expected Z, we can directly estimate Z when the number of
265   nodes is large.

282   3.5   F IXED - SIZE B OOTSTRAP S AMPLING
283
284   In this work, we use the fixed-sized bootstrap sampling design from RegDiffusion (Zhu & Slonim,
285   2024). Basically, in each training iteration, we sample a fixed size batch of samples with replacement
286   and add different amounts of noise to the samples. By doing so, we remove the dependency on
287   the number of samples n from the algorithm’s runtime and gain more similar behavior on data of
288   different sizes.

290   3.6   O PTIMIZATION
291
292
      In this experiment, the models are optimized using the Adam optimizer since it has fewer restric-
      tions. However, as shown in the NOTEARS-Denoising example, the denoising diffusion objective
293
      could be applied to the NOTEARS model directly and optimized with L-BFGS-B without any issues.
294
      Another experiment design we tested involves replacing the dual-ascending augmented Lagrangian
295   method used in many methods, such as NOTEARS and DAG-GNN, with a simple linear multiplier
296   using training epoch steps. Our justification for this is that our training pattern is much smoother
297   with the fixed-size bootstrap sampling, so we can replace the automatic scaled Lagrangian multiplier
298   with a simple linear multiplier. Another motivation is that in the case when edge weights are mostly
299   smaller than 1 (for example, when features are normalized due to the use of neural networks), it no
300   longer makes sense to use the quadratic term used in the augmented Lagrangian as a heavy penalty.

420   f1 and the relationship between Y W and X̂ will be f2 . In addition to DDCD Nonlinear, DDCD
421   Linear also presents competitive benchmark performance despite being a linear model.

      4.4   I MPACT OF K - HOP ACYCLIC CONSTRAINT
424
425
      In the original NOTEARS constraint, the impact of large circles is reduced by a factorial denomina-
426   tor, as shown in Equation 19 in the supplement. With the k-hop acyclic constraint, we simply ignore
427   those large circles. This raises concerns about whether the inferred graphs are DAGs. To answer this
428   question, we analyzed the number of DAG violations on synthetic graphs with 100 nodes at various
429   degrees. Our results show that small k (as low as 3-hop) is, in fact, good enough to avoid DAG vio-
430   lations in most cases, except for ER graphs with high degrees. The detailed results of the experiment
431   are included in supplement section A.3. Overall, we recommend setting k a little bit higher (e.g. 5
      or 10) depending on assumptions about the underlying graphs and computing resources.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
518
519
      Our benchmarks on synthetic data demonstrate the superior performance of the DDCD models in
520
      both linear and nonlinear cases. The denoising nature allows the model to explore a broader range
521
      of noise and to yield better estimates, especially when the number of observed samples is limited.
522   The capacity for recovering an accurate approximation of nonlinear transformation functions further
523   assists the model in approximating the truth. Since it runs very quickly, it may even be used as a
524   low-cost nonlinearity test in appropriate scenarios.
525
      Compared to existing methods, the most similar model to DDCD is DAG-GNN, which also models
526
      the noise term of the SEM equation. To some degree, both DDCD and DAG-GNN train a decoder to
527
      reconstruct the original input from pure noise under the constraint of the weighted adjacency matrix.
528   This is similar to the comparison of diffusion models to VAEs with infinite latent spaces (Luo, 2022).
529   By modeling the noise prediction and expected noise under the constraint of the parameterized
530   adjacency matrix at the same time, we eliminate the requirement of doing matrix inversion, which
531   runs in O(d3 ), in DAG-GNN. At the same time, DDCD allows us to experiment with more flexible
532   neural network architectures, addressing multiple types of assumptions.
533
      Since both DDCD Linear and DDCD Nonlinear are based on the same assumptions that NOTEARS,
534   GOLEM, and DAG-GNN make, they also suffer from the same problems pointed out in Reisach
535   et al. (2021) and Kaiser & Sipos (2021). In our real-world experiments, the results from DDCD
536   Smooth are also much more explainable than the results from DDCD Linear and Nonlinear. This
537   once again marks the gap between real and current synthetic data in this field. However, there have
538   been several proposed solutions (Ng et al., 2024; Nazaret et al., 2023) to solve these problem with
539   unequal variance. Incorporating these into our linear and nonlinear models is an important topic for
      future research.

870            two directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). It counts the number of operations, including adding
871            an edge, removing an edge, and reversing an edge, required to transform one graph into an-
872            other. Here, the SHD is the sum of reversed positive predictions, false positive predictions
873            regardless of direction, and false negative predictions regardless of direction.
