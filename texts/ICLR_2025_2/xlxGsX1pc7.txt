009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011               The current evaluation of mathematical skills in LLMs is limited, as existing
012               benchmarks are relatively small, primarily focus on elementary and high-school
013               problems, or lack diversity in topics. Additionally, the inclusion of visual elements
014               in tasks remains largely under-explored.
015               To address these gaps, we introduce U-MATH, a novel benchmark of 1,100
016               unpublished open-ended university-level problems sourced from teaching materials.
017               It is balanced across six core subjects, with 20% of multimodal problems. Given
018               the open-ended nature of U-MATH problems, we employ an LLM to judge the
019               correctness of generated solutions. To this end, we release µ-MATH, an dataset to
020
                  evaluate the LLMs’ capabilities in judging solutions.
021               The evaluation of general domain, math-specific, and multimodal LLMs highlights
022
                  the challenges presented by U-MATH. Our findings reveal that LLMs achieve a
                  maximum accuracy of only 63% on text-based tasks, with even lower 45% on
023
                  visual problems. The solution assessment proves challenging for LLMs, with the
024
                  best LLM judge having an F1-score of 80% on µ-MATH
025
                  During review, we publish the U-MATH and µ-MATH datasets on OSF.1 .

050
051   Mathematical reasoning is a fundamental domain for assessing the true capabilities of Large Language
052   Models (LLMs) to reason (Ahn et al., 2024). While existing benchmarks like GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,

      2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) provide valuable insights, they primarily focus on school-
055   level mathematics. This leaves a significant gap in understanding how LLMs perform on more
056   advanced, university-level problems. Moreover, these benchmarks are becoming saturated, as GPT-4,
057   using advanced prompting techniques, has achieved over 92% success rate on GSM8K and 80% on
058   MATH (Achiam et al., 2023).
059
      Recent works, such as CHAMP (Mao et al., 2024) and MathOdyssey (Fang et al., 2024), aim to
060
      introduce more challenging problems but are limited in size (<400 samples) and lack comprehensive
061   topic coverage. The most challenging problems stem from school-level competitions or olympiads,
062   missing the crucial middle ground of university-level coursework that reflects academic demands.
063
064
      Furthermore, there is a growing interest in assessing multi-modal LLMs’ abilities to perform mathe-
      matical reasoning involving visual elements (Ahn et al., 2024). Large datasets like MathVista (Lu
065
      et al., 2023), We-Math (Qiao et al., 2024), or MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024) provide an extensive set
066
      of (mostly) visual tasks but may lack university-level problems and often rely on multiple-choice
067   validation, leading to easier problems and faster saturation of benchmarks.
068
069   In turn, evaluating complex free-form answers remains a significant challenge for the field (Hendrycks
070
      et al., 2021). Current methods often rely on LLM judges to assess problems, which introduces
      potential biases and inconsistencies (Zheng et al., 2023). Errors introduced by automatic evaluators
071
      are often overlooked in popular benchmarks. This oversight makes it impossible to account for judge
072
      biases, which detracts from the reliability of the evaluation results.
073
074   Recent studies also indicate that evaluation of mathematical solutions is a demanding task (Zeng
075   et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024) and that an LLM’s ability to judge mathematical solutions is correlated
076
      with its problem-solving performance (Stephan et al., 2024), further signifying the importance of
      evaluations designed to asses the evaluators themselves — also called meta-evaluations.
077
078   Popular datasets for the task of mathematical meta-evaluation are PRM800K (Lightman et al., 2023),
079   MR-GSM8K (Zeng et al., 2023) and MR-MATH (Xia et al., 2024). However, these are all based on
080   the GSM8K and MATH datasets, still leaving a gap in meta-evaluations for university-level problems.
081
082   Aiming to bridge these gaps and provide a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs’ mathematical
083
      capabilities, we introduce U-MATH (University Math) and a supplementary meta-evaluation dataset,
      which we refer to as µ -MATH (Meta U-MATH). Our main contributions are:
084
085
086
            1. U-MATH Benchmark (Section 3): We open-source a set of 1,100 of university-level
087            problems collected from actual coursework with final answers and solutions. About 20% of
088            problems require image understanding to be solved. The text-only part of the benchmark
089            is balanced across 6 key subjects: Precalculus, Algebra, Differential Calculus, Integral
090            Calculus, Multivariable Calculus, and Sequences&Series.
091
092         2. µ-MATH Meta-Evaluation Benchmark (Section 3.3): Additionally, we introduce a set of
093            1084 meta-evaluation tasks sourced from U-MATH problems and designed to rigorously
094            assess the quality of LLM judges. We manually select approximately 25% of the U-MATH
095            problem statements and golden answers, supplying each with four solutions produced by
096
               different top-performing LLMs, and label them based on whether the generated solutions
               are correct or not. The benchmark is designed to be challenging for LLM judges yet
097
               representative of the typical university-level math grading tasks.
098
099
            3. Comparison of Models (Section 4): We conduct a comparative analysis of various open-
100            source and proprietary LLMs on U-MATH. Our analysis highlights the high performance of
101            specialized models in text-only problems and the superiority of proprietary models in visual
102            tasks with the best U-MATH accuracy of 49%. Additionally, we examine several popular
103            LLMs on µ-MATH to assess their ability to judge free-form mathematical problems. Our
104            results show the best model achieving the macro F1-score of 80%.
105

113   multi-step problem-solving, thus improving overall reasoning abilities in LLMs (Chen et al., 2024).
114

137   Textual Mathematical Benchmarks. Early efforts to assess LLMs’ mathematical abilities have
138   emerged in datasets like MathQA (Amini et al., 2019) and the mathematics subset of MMLU
139   (Hendrycks et al., 2020). These early benchmarks emphasized the importance of operation-based
140   reasoning in solving mathematical word problems, typically in a multiple-choice format. Nowadays,
141
      even smaller models (e.g., 7B parameters) have achieved high scores on these tasks (Li et al., 2024b),
      suggesting that these benchmarks are becoming saturated. In response, more comprehensive datasets
142
      have emerged, such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), or MGSM
143
      (Shi et al., 2022) (multilingual version of 250 GSM8K samples). These popular benchmarks are
144   crucial for evaluating LLMs’ mathematical reasoning skills. However, they primarily focus on
145   school-level problems, which may not fully assess the depth of mathematical reasoning.
146
147
      Recent efforts attempt to address more advanced mathematical concepts. MathOdyssey (Fang et al.,
      2024) with competition problems, OCWCourses (Lewkowycz et al., 2022) from actual MIT courses,
148
      and ProofNet (Azerbayev et al., 2023) focusing on proofs aim to evaluate undergraduate-level or
149
      olympiad-level knowledge. However, these datasets are constrained by their small sizes (e.g., 387, 272,
150   and 371 samples), limiting their statistical robustness and topic coverage. For example, MathOdyssey
151   is limited to 101 samples in university-level topics (Calculus, Algebra, and Diff. Equations and
152   Statistics). Other specialized datasets like MiniF2F (Zheng et al., 2021) provide valuable parallel
153   corpora in formal languages, while CHAMP (Mao et al., 2024) offers helpful context and hints,
154   but both are similarly limited in scale with 244 and 270 samples. Additionally, both heavily rely
155   on already published resources: CHAMP sources material from a book, while MiniF2F re-uses
156   international olympiads and MATH dataset problems. An attempt to provide a more robust evaluation,
157   GHOSTS (Frieder et al., 2024) dataset, provides 728 problems (both from other datasets and new
158
      ones) but does not provide reference solutions and answers, focusing instead on human evaluation,
      making cheap automatic evaluation impossible.
159
160   The current datasets are either too small, leading to higher measurement errors, or focus mainly
161   on elementary and high school math, leaving a gap in evaluating LLMs’ proficiency in advanced
      university-level math topics.

      Visual Mathematical Benchmarks. As multimodal LLMs gain prominence, there is a growing
163   need for visual mathematical benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2024; Qiao et al., 2024). Early efforts in this
164   domain focus primarily on geometric problems, as seen in datasets like GeoQA (Chen et al., 2022b),
165   UniGeo (Chen et al., 2022a), and Geometry3K (Lu et al., 2021). These datasets have a narrow focus
166   that does not encompass the breadth of mathematical visual reasoning required at advanced levels.
167
      More recent benchmarks attempt to broaden the scope of visual mathematical evaluation. One of the
168
      first comprehensive attempts is the mathematical subset of MMMU (Yue et al., 2023), which offers
169   505 college-level multiple-choice questions, all with images. However, its multiple-choice format
170   limits the complexity of problems that can be posed. MathVista (Lu et al., 2023) collects 28 existing
171   datasets and introduces 3 new datasets with a total of 5k samples (1k testmini samples). However, as
172   shown by Qiao et al. (2024), it faces challenges with data quality due to its compilation from older
173   datasets.
174
      The latest benchmarks, such as MATH-V (Vision) (Wang et al., 2024a) and We-Math (Qiao et al.,
175   2024), extend this approach to collect 3k and 1.7k visual samples, respectively. However, both
176   datasets rely on multiple-choice questions in the test set, leading to faster saturation. MathVerse
177   (Zhang et al., 2024) further extends this approach, relying on visual elements and providing some
178   simple text problems with 1.2k brand-new samples. Among these, only the We-Math dataset includes
179   university-level mathematical problems.
180
      Our U-MATH dataset improves on existing benchmarks with 225 of 1,100 university-level problems
181   that require visual elements (graph, table, diagram) to be solved. This balanced ratio ensures models
182   are challenged to handle both traditional and visual problem-solving without over-relying on visuals,
183   mirroring real-world scenarios.
184
185   Large Language Models for Mathematics. The application of LLMs to mathematical problem-
186   solving shows promising results, particularly with models like GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 demonstrating
187   strong reasoning abilities for complex tasks such as those in the MATH dataset (Achiam et al., 2023).
188
      While open-source models initially lagged in performance on advanced mathematical tasks, the
      Llama-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) is approaching parity with proprietary models. The most popular
189
      benchmarks, MATH and GSM8K, are nearing saturation, with Llama 3.1 405B achieving scores
190
      of 73.8% and 96.8%, respectively. Similarly, a Qwen2.5-Math-72B model (Yang et al., 2024b;
191   Team, 2024) reach 85.9% on MATH while Qwen2-Math-72B (Yang et al., 2024a) reaches 96.7% on
192   GSM8k.
193
194
      To enhance LLMs’ mathematical capabilities, researchers develop various prompt-based methods (Liu
      et al., 2021). These include techniques for encouraging chain-of-thought generation (Wei et al., 2022),
195
      selecting final results from multiple sampled outputs (Wang et al., 2022), and using external tools such
196
      as calculators, WolframAlpha or Python interpreters (Gao et al., 2023) to reduce arithmetic errors.
197   Additionally, instruction tuning during pre-training has been identified as a key factor in improving
198   performance (Wang et al., 2017). While these approaches show promise, their effectiveness on
199   university-level problems still needs to be explored due to the lack of suitable large-scale benchmarks.
200
201   Mathematical solution verification. Evaluating mathematical solutions is uniquely challenging
202   due to the open-ended nature of answers and the inherent ambiguity in mathematical expressions.
203   Consequently, many benchmarks opt for multiple-choice formats due to their grading simplicity.
204
      However, this approach often simplifies tasks, providing hints that models can exploit (Li et al.,
      2024c; Pezeshkpour and Hruschka, 2023).
205
206   While free-form evaluation using LLM judges is widespread (Zheng et al., 2023), it is known to
207   introduce potential errors (Zheng et al., 2023), since evaluating mathematical solutions is a complex
208   task in its own right (Zeng et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). These evaluation errors are largely overlooked
209   and unaccounted for, limiting the reliability of inferences drawn from such evaluations.
210   Hence, it is important to be able to estimate the performance of automatic evaluators and to choose the
211   most adequate among them. Recent studies show that evaluation performance is correlated with but
212   does not equal problem-solving performance (Stephan et al., 2024). This underscores the importance
213   of benchmarks designed specifically to asses the evaluators — also called meta-evaluations.
214   There are existing benchmarks that are well-suited for meta-evaluations. PRM800K (Lightman et al.,
215   2023) contains 800K annotated steps from 75K solutions to 12K MATH dataset problems, designed

226   3     U-MATH
227
228   We present U-MATH (stands for University Math) — a benchmark designed to challenge LLMs with
229   problems requiring deep understanding and advanced reasoning. The problems span 6 core topics
230
      and range in difficulty and number of questions. A subset of 20% of problems includes images to
      test the models’ ability to interpret and reason with graphical information. Reference solutions and
231
      answers accompany all problems.
232
233   Accuracy is the primary performance metric for U-MATH, its text-only problems (U-MATHT )
234   and problems that include a visual component (U-MATHV ). The main performance measure for
235   µ-MATH is macro-F1.
236   We use an LLM as a judge (Zheng et al., 2023) to measure the accuracy of the free-form answers
237   against the golden solutions. A problem is considered solved only if all required questions are
238   answered and all requested items (e.g., all saddle points) are correctly identified.
239
240   3.1   DATASET C OLLECTION
241
242   To create a benchmark that authentically reflects university-level mathematics, we collaborate with
243   [ANONYMIZED], a platform providing learning content and software for top US universities spe-
244   cialized in mathematics. The problems are sourced from ongoing courses across various institutions
245   currently run on the [ANONYMIZED] platform. Problems and solutions are crafted by subject matter
246   experts and represent real-world academic standards. These samples are unpublished and have not
247   been exposed to any external sources. Thus, the dataset could not be leaked to current LLMs.
248   We employ a multi-stage filtering process to select challenging problems from tens of thousands of
249   available samples. First, we filter out problems with short solutions (< 100 characters) and problems
250   in multiple-choice format. As LLMs are not designed to perform arithmetic calculations and are
251   prone to errors (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Lewkowycz et al., 2022), we focus on testing mathematical
252   reasoning rather than calculations. We filter out problems marked as allowing calculator usage. As
253   for the visual problems selection, we chose to keep problems with a single image for convenience.
254   Next, we employ several small LLMs (LLaMA-3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2-7B (Yang et al.,
255   2024a), Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023), Mathstral-7B, NuminaMath-7B (Beeching et al., 2024)) to
256   solve the problems. We select 150 most challenging problems for each subject based on the average
257   problem solution rate. For this step, we use the same pipeline as described in Section4. This way, we
258   ensure that none of the individual models influence problem selection largely and that there is no
259   overfitting to a specific LLM. As the last step, we hold extra validation high risk problems (with low
260
      solve rate) using our in-house math experts and [ANONYMIZED] content team.
261   After collection, we enlist a team of paid experts from the [ANONYMIZED], who actively teach
262   various Calculus courses. The experts verify that each problem is suitable either for assessing the
263   subject knowledge expected of college or university students or for testing prerequisite knowledge.
264   The team thoroughly reviewed and affirmed that the selected problems meet these criteria. Overall,
265   only 4.3% of the problems are categorized as school-level rather than university-level, highlighting
266
      the robustness of the selection process.

294   3.3   M ETA -E VALUATION F RAMEWORK (µ-MATH)
295
296   Mathematical problem evaluation is not straightforward. Even simple expressions like x · 0.5 may
297
      have valid forms like x2 , x ÷ 2, x/2, or unsimplified variants like 9x/18. In practice, evaluating
      free-form solutions requires testing expression equivalence in much less trivial cases, especially with
298
      more advanced problems (refer to Section A.3 in Appendix for an example).
299
300   To systematically study the ability of LLMs to evaluate free-form mathematical solutions on advanced,
301   university-level problems, we introduce the µ -MATH (Meta U-MATH) benchmark. It consists of a
302   curated subset of U-MATH samples, supplied with LLM-generated solutions — both correct and not.
303
      The solutions are labeled using a combination of manual inspection and automated verification via
      [ANONYMIZED]-API, which allows to test formal equivalence of mathematical expressions.
304
305   We selected 271 U-MATH problems (around 25%) based on their assessment difficulty to create a
306   challenging meta-evaluation set. This subset does not aim to reflect the overall U-MATH distribution
307   but rather to provide a robust test for LLM judges. We focused on text-only problems, excluding
308   those needing images, due to the limited size of the labeled U-MATH subset. Four solutions have
309
      been generated for each of the selected problems — using Qwen2.5-72B, Llama3.1-8B, GPT-4o and
      Gemini-1.5-Pro models — 1084 samples in total.
310
311   A tested model is provided with a problem statement, a reference answer, and a solution to evaluate.
312   We treat this as a binary classification task, using the macro-averaged F1-score as the primary
313   metric to minimize the effect of class imbalance. Additionally, we report Positive Predictive Value
314   (PPV or Precision) and True Positive Rate (TPR or Recall) for the positive class as well as Negative
315
      Predictive Value (NPV) and True Negative Rate (TNR) for the negative class, offering a finer-grained
      performance evaluation. We also report all of the scores computed both across the entire set of
316
      samples and only across those with solutions produced by a specific model, separately for each of the
317
      author models.

344   All LLMs are tested using the same prompts and settings for fair comparison. The LLMs are restricted
345   to a single generation of 4096 tokens with the temperature set to 0. We employ chain-of-thought
346   (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) to encourage models to ‘think’ before providing an answer.
347   Images are included directly in the prompts for multimodal LLMs. To text-only LLMs the problem
348   description is provided as-is without visual elements.
349
      We report accuracy based on widely available GPT-4o-2024-08-06 as-a-judge for the final results,
350   despite this not being the best judge, yet conservative in false negative rate (as discussed in Section 4.3).
351   The judge is presented with the problem statement, golden answer, and generated solutions. The
352   temperature is set to 0. The judge is asked to extract the ‘student’s answer’, make derivations that
353   may be necessary, and compare solutions. After this ‘reasoning phase’, we ask the model to provide
354   a Yes/No response given previous reasoning, which we interpret as a desired binary metric.
355
      For meta-evaluation we additionally experiment with two prompting schemes — a standard Automatic
356   CoT prompt with a simple task description and an instruction to think step-by-step, and a manual
357   CoT prompt with explicit instructions on how to tackle the task. We find the best performance to be
358   achieved with the latter, so we use the manual prompt (referenced as CoT) for the main results. The
359   CoT output is then given to the extractor model (we always use Qwen2.5 72B for consistency) to
360   produce a single label — either ‘Yes’, ‘No’ or ‘Inconclusive’. We include ‘Inconclusive’ for cases
361   when a judge refuses to complete the evaluation or generation fails; such judgments are treated as
362   incorrect. Refer to Appendix C.2 for full prompts.

473   and worse performance on Qwen solutions), most pronounced with smaller models and Automatic

478   be more conservative — having relatively high TPR compared to their TNR, while Qwen family of
479   models exhibits the opposite pattern. Furthermote, proprietary models mainly lose in TPR when
480   going from a larger model to a smaller one, while Qwen models, once again on the contrary, lose
481   more in TNR. The behavior differences are further studied in Appendix I.
482   Overall, judges have imperfect performance, they exhibit varying behavior patterns, judgment
483   performance is different from problem-solving performance, and different prompting schemes induce
484   nontrivial changes in judges’ behaviors, biases and even their rankings. All of these findings
485   underscore the importance of performing meta-evaluations, since such things are impossible to
      quantify and comparisons impossible to make in the absence of benchmarks designed for judges.

502   5    C ONCLUSION
503
504
      We introduce U-MATH, a novel multimodal benchmark for evaluating the university-level mathe-
      matical reasoning of LLMs. U-MATH includes 1,100 unpublished free-form problems from real
505
      teaching materials, covering 6 core mathematical subjects, with 20% involving image-based reason-
506
      ing. Additionally, we provide µ-MATH, a meta-evaluation dataset, to assesses LLMs’ ability to
507   evaluate free-form mathematical solutions.
508
509   Our experiments highlight significant challenges for LLMs in advanced reasoning and visual problem-
510
      solving. The highest accuracy achieved was 63.4% on text-based tasks and 45.0% on visual problems
      (Gemini-1.5-pro-002). Solution assessment remains difficult, with Gemini hiy top µ-MATH F1-score
511
      of 80%, showing room for improvement and underscoring the limitations of widely used models like
512
      GPT-4o in evaluation tasks.
513
514   Limitations. While U-MATH offers diverse university-level problems, it does not cover the full
515   range of advanced topics and may introduce biases by favoring certain problem types. Also, selection
516   process may introduce biases, potentially favoring certain problem types or difficulty levels (e.g.,
517   more accessible topics like Precalculus and Algebra). The inclusion of 20% visual problems, yet
518   reflect real distribution, limits the evaluation of visual reasoning. Furthermore, reliance on LLMs for
519   valuation introduces potential, as models struggle with complex reasoning and instructions, evidenced
520
      by our findings with the µ-MATH. The µ-MATH dataset encompass of 25% of U-MATH problems
      narrows the evaluation scope, but provide 4 diverse model families as solution generators.
521
522   Future Work. Future research can focus on enhancing LLM performance by integrating existing
523   tool-augmented models and exploring their effectiveness on U-MATH and µ-MATH tasks. For
524   instance, incorporating external tools, such as formal solvers, could improve complex textual and
525   multimodal reasoning capabilities. Additionally, our findings indicate that widely used models like
526   GPT-4o are not a silver bullet for solution evaluation; thus, developing specialized (finetuned) models
527   or techniques for more accurate and unbiased assessment is a promising direction. Expanding µ-
528   MATH with formal verification methods could further enhance the evaluation processes. Additionally,
529   conducting deeper prompt sensitivity analyses would provide valuable insights for the field.
530
531   By open-sourcing U-MATH, µ-MATH, and the evaluation code, we aim to facilitate further research
532   in advancing the mathematical reasoning capabilities of LLMs and encourage the development of
533   models better equipped to tackle complex, real-world mathematical problems.
