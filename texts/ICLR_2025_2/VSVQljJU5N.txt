009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNN) are well-known for successful applications in rec-
012            ommender systems. Despite recent advances in GNN development, various au-
013            thors report that in certain cases GNN suffer from so-called oversmoothing prob-
014            lems. Sheaf Neural Networks (SNN) is one of the ways to address the issue of
015            oversmoothing. In the present work we propose a novel approach for training
016            SNN together with user and item embeddings. In that approach parameters of the
017            sheaf are inferred via minimization of the classical BPR loss and sheaf diffusion
018
               on graphs subjected to orthogonality and consistency constraints. Performance of
               the novel technique is evaluated on synthetic test cases and standard benchmarks
019
               for recommendations.
020
021
022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Graph Neural Networks (GNN) Gao et al. (2022) is a rapidly developing research area in Machine-
025   Learning. GNN has been already successfully applied to solve problems in different areas includ-
026   ing Recommender Systems He et al. (2020), drug discovery Duvenaud et al. (2015); Han et al.
027   (2021) search for new materials Park & Wolverton (2020), materials properties prediction Louis
028   et al. (2020) and Natural Language Processing Gui et al. (2019). One of the possible reasons for
029   such success is utilization of information about pairwise interactions due to processing of graph
030   connections data.
031   Despite numerous successful applications, there is a space for further improvements. One of the is-
032   sues that reduces GNN performance is so-called oversmoothing effect Rusch et al. (2023). There is a
033   variety of quantitative definitions of oversmoothing Chen et al. (2020a), but all of them are related to
034   the variability of vertices feature vectors over the graph. In the extreme case of high oversmoothing
035   feature vectors associated with each vertex of graph are almost constant. The latter means that such
036   features are not informative and can not be utilized to solve machine-learning problems.
037   The oversmoothing occurs because of a message-passing procedure that allows one to aggregate
038   information from neighbours. It has been reported Rusch et al. (2023) that smoothing of feature
039   vectors can increase the quality of machine-learning model trained on the graph, because it performs
040   denoising of vertices feature vectors. Therefore, addressing the issues of oversmoothing is not so
041   straightforward.
042   Different techniques emerged to tackle the oversmooting in GNN. One of the options of oversmooth-
043   ing reduction is regularization of GNN training. In Zhou et al. (2021) regularization technique that
044   promotes variability of graph vertices features was proposed. Various stochastic methods for graph
045   edges dropout can reduce the oversmoothing effect ?Hasanzadeh et al. (2020). In other works au-
046   thors consider modifications of message-passing procedure to produce informative features. Such
047   methods can be inspired by physical systems on graphs Rusch et al. (2022a); Eliasof et al. (2021);
048   Wang et al. (2023); Giovanni et al. (2022). Alternative methods for modification of message-passing
049   procedure include Gradient Gating Rusch et al. (2022b). It has been demonstrated that addition of
050
      residual connections to Graph Neural Networks can He et al. (2016); Li et al. (2019); Chen et al.
      (2020b).
051
052   In recent years a novel technique for oversmoothing tackling emerged. The novel approach is based
053   on Graph Sheaf Theory Curry (2014) and is referred to as Sheaf Neural Networks (SNN) . In SNN
      each graph vertice and edge is equipped with a linear space and linear maps between vector spaces

      of edges and vertices connected by those edges. Such construction provides more accurate descrip-
055   tion of data and relations between graph vertices Hansen & Gebhart (2020). The latter results in
056   improvement of recommender systems Barbero et al. (2022).
057
058
      SNNs can be considered as a modification of the message-passing algorithm in classical GNNs,
      where feature vectors from neighbours are passed directly. In SNN parameters feature vectors of
059
      neighbouring vertices are multiplied by matrices that are computed for each pair of vertices. For
060
      instance, if one wants to pass vector x from vertex u to verix v, then x should be mapped first to the
061   linear space of edge uv: y = Au x and mapped to the space of features at v: x∗ = A⊤       v Au x. The
062   procedure description has certain important benefits. First of all, it provides means for oversmooth-
063   ing reduction by aligning vectors from different vertices in modified message-passing algorithm,
064   Secondly, it provides a consistent way for comparison between vectors associated with different
065   vertices by mapping such vectors to the space of the edge that connects these vertices Purificato
066   et al. (2024). This is beneficial for classical recommender methods based on dot-product of user and
067   item embeddings.
068   In the current work we present a novel approach on learning SNN parameters. Basically, we intro-
069   duce orthogonality constraint on sheaf linear maps similar to Barbero et al. (2022) and the novel
070   consistency constraint explained in the Section 2. Sheaf linear maps are parameterized as functions
071   of feature vectors and are tuned to minimize the vertex feature vector diffusion. Finally, Bayesian
072   Personalized Ranking loss-function is added to train the recommender. Cases of known and learn-
073   able vertices feature vectors are considered. Our work adopts the approach similar to Bamberger
074   et al. (2024). However, we utilize a different regularisation approach to learn the sheaf structure.

125   In the present work we utilize several constraints on sheaf linear maps. The first one is the orthog-
126   onality constraint similar to Barbero et al. (2022). We assume that the dimension of the edge space
127
      is (significantly) lower than the dimension of the linear space associated with the vertex. Therefore,
      orthogonality constraint takes the following form:
128
129                                           A(u)A⊤ (u) − I = 0                                          (7)
130   Here I is the identity matrix. In other words, A(u) projects vector x(u) and performs some rotations
131   in the projected space to compute the edge feature vector.
132
      There is a geometric interpretation of the orthogonality constraint Barbero et al. (2022). Graph Sheaf
133
      can be considered as a discretisation of a vector bundle over the manifold. In the case of the tangent
134
      bundle over the Riemannian Manifold so-called parallel transport can be introduced. In other words,
135   it is possible to take a tangent vector at one point on the manifold and bring it along a curve to the
136   other point. In such a procedure the length of the vector does not change and the smooth curve
137   that connects two points on the manifold determines the linear map between tangent spaces at these
138   points that preserves the vector length. Therefore, such a linear map is a rotation, which is expressed
139   in the equation 7.

427   4     C ONCLUSION
428
429   The SNN is an attractive method for recommender systems from both theoretical and practical points
430   of view. In SNNs, feature vectors of users and items are mapped to a single vector space by sheaf
431   linear maps. The latter enables consistent comparison of user and item vectors, thereby simplifying
      the theoretical analysis of recommendations through SNNs.


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      The practical benefits of SNNs include feature denoising and reduction of the oversmoothing as il-
433   lustrated by numerical experiments with synthetic and real data. Moreover, SNN achieves similar
434   recommendation quality in comparison with classical GCN methods. Therefore, further develop-
435   ment of SNNs is a promising area of research.
436
      In summary, one of the main contributions of the present work is the novel approach for SNN
437
      training via loss-function minimization under constraints. Constraints introduced provide sheaf lin-
438
      ear maps regularization and simplify the inference procedure: SNN requires only feature vectors
439   to compute sheaf linear maps. In addition to that, we demonstrate that sequential application of
440   sheaf layers can provide relevant recommendations for users. One of the limitations of the work is
441   increased computational cost in comparison with GCN of similar architecture due to the need for
442   sheaf linear maps calculation. Despite that fact, presented results demonstrate that SNN has high
443   potential for applications in recommender systems.
