                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Representation learning and de novo generation of proteins are pivotal compu-
010
               tational biology tasks. Whilst natural language processing (NLP) techniques
011            have proven highly effective for protein sequence modelling, structure modelling
012            presents a complex challenge, primarily due to its continuous and three-dimensional
013            nature. Motivated by this discrepancy, we introduce an approach using a vector-
014            quantized autoencoder that effectively tokenizes protein structures into discrete
015            representations. This method transforms the continuous, complex space of protein
016            structures into a manageable, discrete format with a codebook ranging from 4096
017            to 64000 tokens, achieving high-fidelity reconstructions with backbone root mean
018            square deviations (RMSD) of approximately 1-5 Å. To demonstrate the efficacy
019            of our learned representations, we show that a simple GPT model trained on our
020
               codebooks can generate novel, diverse, and designable protein structures. Our ap-
               proach not only provides representations of protein structure, but also mitigates the
021
               challenges of disparate modal representations and sets a foundation for seamless,
022
               multi-modal integration, enhancing the capabilities of computational methods in
023            protein design.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The application of machine learning to large-scale biological data has ushered in a transformative era
029   in computational biology, advancing both representation learning and de novo generation. Particularly,
030   the integration of machine learning in molecular biology has led to significant breakthroughs (Sapoval
031   et al., 2022; Chandra et al., 2023; Khakzad et al., 2023; Jänes and Beltrao, 2024), spanning many
032   complex and inhomogenous data modalities, from sequences and structures through to functional
033
      descriptors and experimental assays, many of which are deeply interconnected and have attracted
      significant modelling efforts.
034
035   Currently, the deep learning landscape is increasingly converging towards a unified paradigm centered
036   around attention-based architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) and sequence modeling. This shift has
037   been driven by the impressive performance and scalability of transformers, and even accelerated since
038   treating non-standard data modalities as sequence-modeling problems has proven highly effective.
039
      Indeed, transformers-based models are leading methods in many machine learning domains, including
      image representation (Radford et al., 2021), image (Chen et al., 2020; Chang et al., 2022) and audio
040
      generation (Ziv et al., 2024), and for Reinforcement Learning (Chen et al., 2021; Boige et al., 2023).
041
      This trend has greatly benefited sequence-based biological models, allowing for the direct application
042   of NLP methodologies like GPT (Radford and Narasimhan, 2018) and BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)
043   with notable success (Ferruz et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023b).
044
045
      In particular, large multi-modal models (LMMs), leveraging transformer backbones, are emerging as
      a key tool with applications in various fields such as: ubiquitous AI (GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023),
046
      LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b), Gemini (Gemini et al., 2023), Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022)); text-
047
      conditioned generation of images (Parti (Yu et al., 2022b), Muse (Chang et al., 2023)) or sounds
048   (MusicGen (Copet et al., 2023)); and even reinforcement learning (Reed et al., 2022). LMMs are also
049   instantiated in biological settings, such as medicine (MedLlama (Xie et al., 2024), Med-Gemini (Yang
050   et al., 2024b) Med-PaLM (Tu et al., 2024)) and genomics (ChatNT (Richard et al., 2024)). Core to all
051   these LMMs is the use of pre-trained encoders as a mechanism for combining modalities in sequence
052   space. For instance, LLaVa (Liu et al., 2024b) and Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) used pre-trained
053   vision encoders, ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) and Normalizer Free ResNet (NFNet) (Alayrac
      et al., 2020) respectively, while Copet et al. (2023) leverages the the codec of Défossez et al. (2023).

054
      In general, training robust representations of specific modalities facilitates their incorporation into
055   state-of-the-art LMMs. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no established methodology
056   that readily allows the application of sequence-modelling to protein structures.
057
058
      Despite these advances in related domains, structure-based modeling of biological data such as
      proteins remains a formidable challenge. Unlike sequences, protein structures are inherently three-
059
      dimensional and continuous, which complicates the direct application of transformer models that
060
      primarily handle discrete data. Instead, structure-based methods often design bespoke geometric
061   deep learning methodologies to process Euclidean data; for example graph-neutral network encoders
062   (Dauparas et al., 2022; Krapp et al., 2023) and structurally-aware modules such as those in AlphaFold
063   (Jumper et al., 2021). Moreover, generative modelling of structures is typically performed with
064   methods designed for continuous variables; such as diffusion (Watson et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023)
065   and flow matching (Bose et al., 2024), rather than the discrete-variable models that have proved so
066   successful in sequence modelling.
067   In this work, we aim to address this gap by learning learning a quantized representations of protein
068   structures enabling to efficiently leverage sequence-based language models. The key objectives of
069   this this work are:
070
071            (i) To convert protein structures into the discrete domain We propose the transformation of
072                structural information of proteins into discrete sequential data, enabling seamless integration
073                with sequence-based models.
074           (ii) To learn a discrete and potentially low-dimensional latent space By learning a discrete
075                latent space through finite scalar quantization, we facilitate the mapping of continuous
076                structures to a finite set of vectors. This effectively builds a vocabulary for protein structures,
077                and can be pushed into low dimensions for applications with limited resources.
078           (iii) To achieve a low reconstruction error We aim to minimize the reconstruction error of the
079                 learned discrete representation, typically within the range of 1-5 Ångströms.
080
081   Our contributions are threefold. First, we introduce a series of quantized autoencoders that effectively
082   discretize protein structures into sequences of tokens while preserving the necessary information for
083   accurate reconstruction. Second, we validate our autoencoders through qualitative and quantitative
084
      analysis, and various ablation studies, supporting our design choices. Third, we demonstrate the
      efficacy and practicality of the learned representations with experimental results from a simple GPT
085
      model trained on our learned codebook, which successfully generates novel, diverse, and structurally
086
      viable protein structures. All source code for experiments is publicly available online1 .

      This representation learning task is similar to the traditional task of mapping point-clouds to sequences
129
      (Yang et al., 2024a; Boget et al., 2024). Inverse folding (Ingraham et al., 2019; Dauparas et al.,
130
      2022) is another example, that aims at estimating the sequence of amino acids corresponding to a
131   given a protein structure. Recently, ProteinMPNN has shown remarkable capacity at the inverse
132   folding task. We follow their design choices and parameterize our encoder using a Message-Passing
133   Neural Network (MPNN) (Dauparas et al., 2022). In addition, we introduce a cross-attention
134   mechanism, detailed in Appendix A.1 and Algorithm 2, allowing to effectively compress the structure
135   representation by a downsampling ratio r. We maintain locality using a custom attention masking
136   scheme in the downsampling layer (see Appendix A.1), ensuring that each down-sampled node
137   aggregates information from a small number of neighboring nodes in the original sequence space.
138   MPNN operates on a graph consisting of a set of vertices and edges. We follow Dauparas et al. (2022);
139   Ganea et al. (2022) and set as initial node features a positional encoding that reflects the residue’s
140   ordering within the sequence, while for the edge features, we use a concatenation of pairwise distance
141   features, relative orientation features and relative positional embeddings. Note that as positional
142   encoding, relative distance and orientation are invariant with respect to the frame of reference, the
143   input data fed to the model are invariant to rotation and translation. This invariant encoding of the
144   input structures guarantees the invariance of the learned representation regardless of the chosen
145
      downstream architecture. The encoding scheme is described in detail in Appendix A.2.

      Quantization The quantizer plays a crucial role in our work by discretizing all continuous latent
147
      representations into a sequence of discrete codes. Traditional methods typically involve direct
148
      learning of the codebook (van den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019). However, in line with the
149   literature, we suffered several drawbacks associated with these approaches. Indeed, explicit vector
150   quantization is particularly expensive as it involves computation of pairwise distances, particularly
151   problematic for long sequences and large codebooks. Moreover, training instabilities arising from
152   both the bias in the straight-through estimator and the under-utilization of the codebook capacity,
153   often referred to as codebook collapse, make learning a discretized latent representation a hard
154   optimization problem (Huh et al., 2023; Takida et al., 2024). To address these challenges, we leverage
155   the recent Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) framework (Mentzer et al., 2024), which effectively
156   resolves the aforementioned issues, notably by reducing straight-through gradient estimation error.
157   FSQ learns a discrete latent space by rounding a bounded low-dimensional encoding of the latent
158   representation. Consider zi ∈ Rc , the ith element of the continuous encodings z, and the quantization
159   levels L = [L1 , . . . , Ld ] ∈ Nd . Its discretized counterpart, denoted as z̃i ∈ Zd , typically with d ≤ 8,
160   is defined by:

171   Decoder The decoder module of our framework is tasked with estimating a structure p̂ from the
172   latent quantized representation z̃:
173                                                  N
174                                      dψ : z̃ ∈ R r ×c 7→ p̃ ∈ RN ×4×3                                    (3)
175   The task our decoder addresses formulates more broadly as a sequence to point-cloud task. A
176   paradigmatic example of such a task in biology is the protein folding problem, where the confirmation
177   of a protein is estimated given its primary sequence of amino-acids. Jumper et al. (2021) successfully
178   tackled this task proposing a novel architecture for point cloud estimation from latent sequence of
179   embeddings. Therefore, we use AlphaFold-2 structure module to parameterize our decoder and
180   learning the parameter from scratch.
181   Specifically, the structure module of AlphaFold parameterizes a point cloud using frames. A frame
182   is defined by a tuple T = (R, t) where R is the frames’ orientation (i.e. a rotation matrix) and t is
183   the frame center (i.e. a vector defining the translation of the center of the frame). The origin of the
184   frame is set to the Cα carbon, and the orientation is defined using the nitrogen and the other carbon
185   atom. For a thorough and mathematical description, we defer to Jumper et al. (2021). However, note
186   that AlphaFold’s structure module expects both a per-residue representation (si )i≤N and a pairwise
187
      representation (ki,j )i,j≤N between residues i and j. The per-residue representation si is constructed
      by mirroring the cross-attention mechanism described in Algorithm 2 used for downsampling in order
188
      to produce embeddings at the residue level by only varying the size of the initial input queries. The
189
      process for constructing the pairwise representation (ki,j )i,j≤N used for reconstruction is described
190   in Algorithm 3 and Appendix A.1.

194   Objective The Frame Align Point Error (FAPE) loss, introduced in Jumper et al. (2021), is a
195   function that enables the comparison between point clouds. Since there is no guarantee that the
196   coordinates provided by the decoding module are expressed in the same basis as the input structure,
197   direct comparison of the coordinates between the two point clouds becomes challenging. The core
198   concept behind the FAPE loss is to ensure that coordinates are expressed in the same global frame,
199   thereby enabling the computation of the mean squared error. To do so, given a ground-truth frame Ti
200
      and ground-truth atom position xj expressed in Ti , and their respective predictions Tp       p

211
      sample the data inversely proportional to the size of the cluster it belongs to when clustering the data
      by sequence similarity using MMseqs22 . We filter all chains shorter than 50 residues and crop the
212
      structures that have more than 512 residues by randomly selecting 512 consecutive amino acids in the

219   Model Hyperparameters For the encoder, we use a 3 layers message passing neural network
220   following the architecture and implementation proposed in Dauparas et al. (2022) and utilize the
221   swish activation function. The graph sparsity is set to 50 neighbours per residue. When the
222   downsampling ratio is r > 1, the resampling operation consists in a stack of 3 resampling layers as
223   described in Algorithm 2, the initial queries being defined as the positional encodings. We strictly
224   follow the implementation of AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021) regarding the structure module and use
225   6 structure layers.
226
227   Optimization and Training Details The optimization is conducted using AdamW (Loshchilov and
228   Hutter, 2019) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95 and a weight decay of 0.1. We use a learning rate warm-up
229
      scheduler, progressively increasing the learning rate from 10−6 to 10−3 , and train the model for 250
      epochs on 128 TPU v4-8 with a batch size 1024.

233
234   The primary focus of our work is to develop an effective method to encode and quantize 3D struc-
235   tures with high fidelity. In this section we first evaluate, both qualitatively and quantitatively, our
236   vector-quantized autoencoder by considering the compression and reconstruction performance and
237   demonstrate that our tokenizer indeed permits high-accuracy reconstruction of protein sequences. We
238
      then further highlight how this can be adapted to downstream tasks, by effectively training a de novo
      generative model for protein structures using a vanilla decoder-only transformer model trained on the
239
      next token prediction task.

      3.1   AUTOENCODER E VALUATION
242
243   Experiments We trained six versions of the quantized autoencoder; with small (K = 4096 codes)
244   and large (K = 64000) codebooks and increasing downsampling ratio (r) (from 1 to 4), and in
245   doing so, varying the information bottleneck of our model. For each codebook we evaluate the
246   reconstruction performance achieved on the held out test set, to understand the trade-offs between
247   compression and expressivity associated with these hyperparameter choices.
248
249   Metrics To assess the reconstruction performances of the models, we rely on standard metrics
250   commonly used in structural biology when comparing the similarity of two protein structures. The
251   root mean square distance (RMSD) between two structures is computed by calculating the square
252   root of the average of the squared distances between corresponding atoms of the structures after the
253
      optimal superposition has been found. The TM-score (Y. and J., 2005) is a normalised measure of
      how similar two structures are, with a score of 1 denoting the structures are identical. For context,
254
      two structures are considered to have similar fold when their TM-score exceeds 0.5 (Xu and Y., 2010)
255
      and a RMSD below 2Å is usually seen as approaching experimental resolution.
256
257

258   a downsampling of r = 1; our average reconstruction has 1.59 Å RSMD and a TM-score of 0.95.
259   For comparison, we also report the reconstruction performance of the exact same models without
260   latent quantization. Whilst increasing the model capacity may allow these scores to be improved
261   even further, this is already approaching the limit of experimental resolution (which is to say, the

375   of previously existing structures. To assess this, we measure the diversity and novelty of our generation.

394   TM-score as our similarity metric and consider a structure as novel if its maximum TM-score against
395   the reference dataset is below 0.5. As is commonly done, we only report the proportion of novel
396   samples in our generated dataset. Overall, we follow Yim et al. (2023) for the implementation of
397   these metrics and refer the reader to Appendix A.4.2 for more details on the validation pipeline.

400   Baselines To gauge how our structure generation GPT model fares against specifically designed
401   protein design methods, we compare our model with FrameDiff (Yim et al., 2023) and RFDiffusion
402
      (Watson et al., 2023). Both use bespoke SE(3) diffusion models specifically designed and trained
      the structure generation task. Note that, the state-of-the-art RFDiffusion leverages the extensive
403
      pre-training of RoseTTAFold (Baek et al., 2021) on a large dataset and requires considerable com-
404
      putational cost. On the contrary, our generation model is a standard GPT and the objective here is
405   to show how one can readily use the tokenized representation learned with our method for protein
406   design.

409   Results The results for the different metrics are given in Table 2 with the sampling strategy used for
410   each method described in Appendix A.4.3. It is noteworthy that while not specifically designed for
411   the protein structure generation task, even our simple GPT model is able to generate protein structures
412   of quality in par with a method specific to protein design such as FrameDiff. For comparison, and to
413   set an upper bound of what to expect in terms of the designability scores, we compute self-consistency

      Table 2. While our method shows competitive performance at generative designable domains, the

      domains that are structurally closer to the reference data set than the ones generated by the baselines.
417
      One explanation for this can be found in the sampling method where the chosen parameters favored

419   generating structures that are novel and diverse is desirable, structures that differ too much from

448   4    R ELATED W ORKS
449
450   Learning from Protein Structures. Learning from protein structure is thriving research field that
451   encompasses a wide variety of tasks crucial task. For instance, inverse folding (Ingraham et al., 2019;
452   Hsu et al., 2022; Dauparas et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2021) or binding site estimation (Krapp et al., 2023;
453   Ganea et al., 2022) are critically enabling for drug design (Scott et al., 2016). Others (Consortium,
454   2006; Robinson et al., 2023; Kucera et al., 2023; Gligorijević et al., 2021) focus on learning from
455
      protein structure to provide a better understanding of the role of the structure, hence pushing the
      knowledge frontier of biological processes.
456
457
458   Representation Learning of Protein Structures. ("Eguchi et al., 2022) learns a VAE using as
459
      inputs matrix distances and predicting 3D coordinates. The supervision is done by comparing distance
      matrices. Despite the straightforward nature of sampling from a VAE latent space, it often yields
460
      subpar sample quality, see (Kingma et al., 2016).
461
462   Discrete representation learning for protein structures has recently garnered increasing attention.
463   Foldseek (van Kempen et al., 2024) introduces a quantized autoencoder to encode local protein
464   geometry, demonstrating success in database search tasks. However, as it focuses solely on local
465
      features at the residue level, it lacks the capacity to provide global representation of protein structures.
      This limitation restricts its application in tasks like structure generation or binding prediction, where
466
      global information is critical (Krapp et al., 2023). Building on the 3Di-alphabet introduced by
467
      Foldseek, Su et al. (2024); Heinzinger et al. (2023) propose structure-aware protein language models
468   that integrate structure tokens with sequence tokens. Additionally, Li et al. (2024) combines a
469   structural autoencoder with K-means clustering applied to the latent representation of a fixed reference
470   dataset.
471
      More closely related to our work, Gao et al. (2023) adapts VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) for
472
      protein structures. However, its limited reconstruction performance constrains its applicability. Lin
473
      et al. (2023a) explores discrete structural representation learned by such VQ-VAEs (van den Oord
474   et al., 2017), while Liu et al. (2023) trains a diffusion model on the discrete latent space derived from
475   this approach. Similarly, and concurrently with our work, Liu et al. (2024a) combines finite scalar
476   quantization (Mentzer et al., 2024) with a specialized transformer-based autoencoder for proteins,
477   RNA, and small molecules.
478
      Very recently, efforts have emerged to combine quantized structural representation with discrete
479
      sequence representation, enabling multimodal generative models trained on a joint discrete latent
480   space. FoldToken (Gao et al., 2024b;a), is a concurrent approach that shares conceptual similarities
481   with our work but differs in key methodological and application-oriented aspects. FoldToken employs
482   joint quantization of sequence and structure, enabling integration across modalities, whereas our
483   method focuses exclusively on structural information. This decoupling allows for mode-specific
484   pretraining, aligning with strategies from subsequent works (Hayes et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024).
485   Methodologically, FoldToken introduces a series of improvements to existing VQ methods (van den
      Oord et al., 2017) aimed at enhancing reconstruction accuracy; whereas we adopt the FSQ framework,


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      which reduces the straight-through gradient estimation gap inherent to VQ methods while improving
487   codebook utilization. Furthermore, while FoldToken primarily emphasizes backbone inpainting and
488   antibody design (Gao et al., 2024b;a), our work considers de novo generation of complete structures.
489
490   Generation of Protein Structures. A substantial body of literature addresses the challenge of
491   sampling the protein structure space. Numerous studies have advocated for the use of diffusion-based
492   models Wu et al. (2024); Yim et al. (2023); Watson et al. (2023) or flow matching techniques (Bose
493   et al., 2024). While many of these works, such as those by (Yim et al., 2023; Watson et al., 2023;
494   Bose et al., 2024), employ complex architectures to ensure invariance to rigid-body transformations,
495   (Wu et al., 2024) opted for an alternative parameterization directly preserving symmetries allowing
496   the authors to capitalize on conventional architectures, yet working only on small protein crops.
497
      Recently, Wang et al. (2024b) employs a lookup-free quantizer (Yu et al., 2024) as a structure
      tokenizer and trains a diffusion protein language model (Wang et al., 2024a) on the concatenated
498
      sequence and structure tokens. Other works (Hayes et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024) simply uses VQ-
499
      VAEs (van den Oord et al., 2017) to tokenize the structures and train large language models (LLM)
500   on the combination of sequence and structure tokens.

      5   C ONCLUSION
504
505
      This work demonstrates a methodology for learning a discrete representation of a protein geometry;
      allowing the mapping of structures into sequences of integers whilst still recovering near native
506
      conformations upon decoding. This sequential representation not only simplifies the data format
507
      but also significantly compresses it compared to traditional 3D coordinates. Our belief is that the
508   primary contribution of our work lies in setting the stage for applying standard sequence-modelling
509   techniques to protein structures.
510
511
      A prerequisite for such development is the expressiveness of the tokenized representation, which must
      capture the necessary information to enable hight fidelity reconstructionf of the 3D structures. Both
512
      empirical and visual inspection confirm this to be the case for our proposed methodology. Indeed, as
513
      codebook collapse is effectively mitigated by the use of FSQ, larger codebook vocabularies and an
514   increased tokens usageprovide straightforward recipes for improving the reconstruction accuracy by
515   reducing the information bottleneck of the autoencoder
516
517
      The first step towards sequence-based modelling of structures is the proof-of-concept GPT model,
      trained on tokenized PDB entries, that serves as a simple de novo generating protein backbones. That
518
      the achieved results are competitive with some recent diffusion-based model underlines the promise
519
      of this paradigm. While a simple GPT does not yet match seminal approaches like RFDiffusion,
520   it is important to recognize that the performances of the latter stem from extensive developments
521   in 3D generative modeling. Given the remarkable performance of sequence-modelling algorithms
522   across a diversity of data modalities, equivalent efforts could also provide simpler and more powerful
523   treatments of protein structure. This represents a promising direction for future research based on
524   this work.
