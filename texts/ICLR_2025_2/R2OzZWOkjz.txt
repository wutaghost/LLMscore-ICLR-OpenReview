011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The knowledge embedded in Large Language Models (LLMs) is static, tied to the
014            time when the training data was collected. While Retrieval-Augmented Genera-
015
               tion (RAG) methods are widely used to introduce new knowledge, they simply
               rely on retrieved information for reasoning without integrating it into the model’s
016
               parameters. This limits the model’s ability for long-term knowledge retention and
017
               autonomous learning. To overcome this, in this work, we propose the Retrieval-
018            Augmented Editing Generation (RAEG) framework for open-domain question an-
019            swering (ODQA) tasks. RAEG enhances model generation performance by first
020            editing the retrieved paragraphs to inject necessary knowledge, followed by an
021            augmented generation phase. This dual mechanism—combining knowledge in-
022            jection and retrieval augmentation—provides complementary advantages in the
023            reasoning process. When the injected knowledge alone is insufficient for accu-
024            rate generation, the model can rely on the retrieved information to compensate,
025            and conversely, when retrieval yields suboptimal results, the injected knowledge
026
               ensures continuity and accuracy in the response. This interplay between internal-
               ized and externally sourced knowledge reinforces the model’s ability to produce
027
               correct answers, thereby enhancing overall task performance. We explore the im-
028
               pact of two key methods for knowledge injection: Knowledge Editing (KE) and
029            Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), and analyze how modifying the model’s
030            parameters influences its reasoning abilities and generation outcomes. To fur-
031            ther improve RAEG’s performance, we introduce a re-ranking mechanism to op-
032            timize the integration of external knowledge and apply parameter pruning to mit-
033            igate the potential drawbacks of parameter modifications during KE. Evaluations
034            on two authoritative ODQA benchmarks show that RAEG is able to further re-
035            place RAG as a competitive method. Our data and code will be available at
036            https://github.com/XXX/XXX.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Large-scale pre-trained language models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019; Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2022;
041
      Ouyang et al., 2022) leverage self-supervised learning on vast amounts of text to encode implicit
042   knowledge into their parameters, enabling high-quality language generation. However, the knowl-
043   edge embedded in these models is static, confined to the point in time when the training data was
044   collected. Leveraging the language understanding and generation capabilities acquired during pre-
045   training, large language models have achieved significant success across various practical applica-
046   tions. However, when confronted with more nuanced downstream tasks or unknown data knowledge,
047   relying solely on the internal knowledge reasoning of pre-trained language models often leads to is-
048   sues such as knowledge hallucination and knowledge gaps. These issues are primarily attributed to
049   the incompleteness, biases, and static nature of the training data. For example, in the open-domain
050
      question answering (ODQA) (Voorhees & Tice, 2000) task, to address these challenges, researchers
      adopted RAG-based (Lewis et al., 2020) fine-tuning techniques to generate high-quality responses
051
      with the help of external knowledge specific to the current task.
052
053   RAG is a widely adopted approach for handling open-domain, knowledge-intensive tasks. It works
      by retrieving relevant text paragraphs and incorporating them into the generation process to assist in

116   2.1     PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING (PEFT)
117
      End-to-end full fine-tuning, while being the simplest and most direct approach, becomes pro-
118
      hibitively expensive as the scale of pre-trained models increases. To address this, Parameter-Efficient
119
      Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Ding et al., 2023; Lialin et al., 2023) techniques have been proposed, which
120   aim to achieve performance comparable to full fine-tuning by adjusting only fewer parameters.
121   PEFT methods are primarily categorized into three types: Additive PEFT (Zhu et al., 2021; Lei
122   et al., 2023; Chronopoulou et al., 2023), which involves inserting adapter modules into Transformer
123   blocks for fine-tuning; Selective PEFT (Sung et al., 2021; Liao et al., 2023), which selectively fine-
124   tunes a subset of existing parameters; and Reparameterization PEFT, which transforms the model
125   architecture into an equivalent form for training, such as LoRa (Hu et al., 2021), which uses low-rank
126   matrices for adjustments. Although selecting an appropriate rank for LoRA has been a significant
127   challenge, various derivatives of LoRA, such as DyLoRA (Valipour et al., 2023), AdaLoRA (Zhang
128
      et al., 2023), and AutoLoRA (Zhang et al., 2024b), have emerged to address this issue. These tech-
      niques enhance the efficiency of parameter tuning in large pre-trained models.
129
130
131   2.2     K NOWLEDGE E DITING (KE)
132
133
      The KE methods of changing parameters mainly encompasses Meta-learning methods (De Cao
      et al., 2021; Hase et al., 2023; Mitchell et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2024), which involve training a
134
      hypernetwork to learn changes ∆W in model parameters, thus avoiding direct weight updates; and
135
      Location-then-Edit methods (Meng et al., 2022; 2023; Li et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023a), which
136   identify the locations within the model where knowledge is stored using causal traces (Meng et al.,
137   2022), and then perform edits on those specific regions. These approaches, by directly modifying
138   parameters of specific regions, enhance the model’s capability for knowledge updating.
139
140
      2.3     R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION (RAG)
141
142   RAG (Lewis et al., 2020), also known as the retrieval-reading architecture (Ma et al., 2023b), en-
143   hances language models by integrating external information through retrieval. The naive RAG
144   method relies on basic retrieval and generation processes, but often suffers from inaccurate re-
145   sponse (Wu et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024) due to irrelevant or similar information. Advanced
146   RAG addresses these issues by optimizing indexing and query processes (Gao et al., 2023; Peng
147   et al., 2024), and employing techniques such as re-ranking (Nogueira et al., 2020; Ju et al., 2021)
148
      and context compression (Xu et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024) to improve retrieval precision and
      response quality.

      3.1.2    M OTIVATION
178
179   This section primarily explores the feasibility of knowledge injection within RAG systems through
180   knowledge editing (KE) and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). It investigates whether targeted
181   adjustments to the model parameters can facilitate the integration of new question-answer pairs
182   while preserving the model’s original inferential capabilities, thereby enabling accurate derivation
183
      of answers from retrieved paragraphs.
184   Based on the above motivation, we propose the following research questions to explore the potential
185   impact of KE and PEFT on the model’s knowledge representation, reasoning abilities, and genera-
186   tion quality within the RAG framework.
187   RQ 1: In the RAG framework, the model relies on externally retrieved knowledge for genera-
188   tion. Can KE and PEFT shift the model’s reliance from external knowledge to internally embedded
189   knowledge by injecting edited information, and how might this affect the quality and consistency of
190   the generated output?
191
      RQ 2: Performing KE and PEFT on the original pre-trained model’s parameters will change the
192
      model’s parameters and alter its knowledge representation. While this may enhance the model’s
193   understanding of specific knowledge, does the alteration of parameters impact the model’s original
194   capabilities, particularly its ability to rely on external knowledge for reasoning during generation?
195   If the alteration of parameters impacts the model’s original capabilities, which method—KE or
196   PEFT—has a greater impact on the model?

233   We selected the MALMEN (Tan et al., 2024) method for knowledge editing, as it is more effective

239   context of the MALMEN method is to optimize the meta-learning hypernetwork H so that it can

254   3.3.2    F OR PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
255
      We employ Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) as our Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
256
      (PEFT) method. LoRA introduces trainable low-rank matrices into the pre-trained model, allow-
257
      ing us to fine-tune only a small subset of parameters while keeping the majority of the original
258   model frozen. For the task of Qsyn → Asyn (synthetic question-to-answer mapping), LoRA allows
259   efficient adaptation of the model by fine-tuning specific target modules. Specifically, the original
260   parameters W are kept frozen, and a small parameter matrix ∆W = A · B is introduced, where
261   A ∈ Rd×r and B ∈ Rr×d are both low-rank matrices. Here, d represents the output dimension
262   of the original weight matrix, and r is the rank of the low-rank matrices (typically r ≪ d). The
263   optimization process is as follows.
264
265                          ∆W = argmin∆W LCELF (−logP(W +=∆W ) [Asyn | Qsyn ])
266                             W ′ = W + α · ∆W                                                                                                                                     (5)
267
268   where α is a scaling factor used to balance the influence of the learned low-rank matrices with the
269   original model weights. By using LoRA, we efficiently fine-tune the model to integrate synthetic
      question-answer pairs while preserving the generalization ability of the pre-trained model.

365   although improving specific knowledge areas, can undermine the model’s reasoning performance.
366   This highlights the need for more robust and balanced KE techniques that do not compromise origi-
367   nal abilities of pre-trained model. In particular, developing more stable KE methods that generalize
368   effectively across diverse datasets and handle complex reasoning tasks could significantly enhance
369   the integration of edited knowledge into large models.

476   At pruning scales below 50%, magnitude-based pruning significantly outperforms random pruning.

      may remove some redundant parameters at higher pruning ratios, its inherent randomness still results

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523   In this work, we introduced a novel framework, Retrieval-Augmented Editing Generation (RAEG),
524   which combines knowledge injection from retrieved documents with RAG to enhance the accuracy
525   of answer generation. The dual strategy of first injecting knowledge and then performing retrieval-
526   augmented generation significantly improves model performance.
527
      A key contribution of our study is the investigation of the impact of two knowledge injection tech-
528   niques—Knowledge Editing (KE) and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)—on model reason-
529   ing abilities after parameter modification. Our experiments show that while KE effectively internal-
530   izes new knowledge, it severely disrupts the model’s prior reasoning capabilities. In contrast, PEFT,
531   which operates through global fine-tuning, preserves the model’s overall performance more effec-
532   tively and achieves better results on open-domain question-answering (ODQA) tasks. In finally, we
533   further enhanced RAEG by introducing a re-ranking mechanism to refine the selection of reliable
534   knowledge sources and by employing parameter pruning to mitigate the negative effects of KE on
535   model performance.
536   Future research should continue to explore and discuss the impacts of various editing techniques on
537   parameter updates of pre-trained language models across a broader range of NLP tasks. Addition-
538   ally, developing more robust components to counteract the unintended side effects of model editing
539   and its ripple effects (Cohen et al., 2024) remains a critical challenge. Our data and code have been
      made available to the community to support further advances in this research direction.
