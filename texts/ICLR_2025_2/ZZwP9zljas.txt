                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) are a class of Graph Neural Net-
012
               works (GNNs) that leverage the graph topology to propagate messages across
013            increasingly larger neighborhoods. The message-passing scheme leads to two dis-
014            tinct challenges: over-smoothing and over-squashing. While several algorithms,
015            e.g. DropEdge and its variants – DropNode, DropAgg and DropGNN – have suc-
016            cessfully addressed the over-smoothing problem, their impact on over-squashing
017            remains largely unexplored. This represents a critical gap in the literature as fail-
018            ure to mitigate over-squashing would make these methods unsuitable for long-
019            range tasks. In this work, we take the first step towards closing this gap by study-
020            ing the aforementioned algorithms in the context of over-squashing. We present
021
               novel theoretical results that characterize the negative effects of DropEdge on sen-
               sitivity between distant nodes, suggesting its unsuitability for long-range tasks.
022
               Our findings are easily extended to its variants, allowing us to build a comprehen-
023
               sive understanding of how they affect over-squashing. We evaluate these methods
024            using real-world datasets, demonstrating their detrimental effects. Specifically, we
025            show that while DropEdge-variants improve test-time performance in short-range
026            tasks, they deteriorate performance in long-range ones. Our theory explains these
027            results as follows: random edge-dropping lowers the effective receptive field of
028            GNNs, which although beneficial for short-range tasks, misaligns the models on
029            long-range ones. This forces the models to overfit to short-range artefacts in the
030            training set, resulting in poor generalization. Our conclusions highlight the need
031            to re-evaluate various methods designed for training deep GNNs, with a renewed
032
               focus on modelling long-range interactions.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Graph-structured data is ubiquitous – it is found in social media platforms, online retail platforms,
038   molecular structures, transportation networks, and even computer systems. Graph neural networks
039
      (GNNs) (Li et al., 2016; Scarselli et al., 2009) are powerful neural models developed for mod-
      elling graph-structured data, and have found applications in several real-world scenarios (Gao et al.,
040
      2018; Monti et al., 2017; Wale & Karypis, 2006; Ying et al., 2018; You et al., 2020a;b;c; 2022;
041
      Zheng et al., 2022; Zitnik & Leskovec, 2017). A popular class of GNNs, called message-passing
042   neural networks (MPNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017), recursively process neighborhood information us-
043   ing message-passing layers. These layers are stacked to allow each node to aggregate information
044   from increasingly larger neighborhoods, akin to how convolutional neural networks (CNNs) learn
045   hierarchical features for images (LeCun et al., 1989). However, unlike in image-based deep learn-
046   ing, where ultra-deep CNN architectures have led to performance breakthroughs (He et al., 2016;
047   Szegedy et al., 2015), shallow GNNs often outperform deeper models on many graph learning tasks
048   (Zhou et al., 2021b). This is because deep GNNs suffer from unique issues like over-smoothing
049   (Oono & Suzuki, 2020) and over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021), which makes training them
050
      notoriously difficult.
051   Over-smoothing refers to the problem of node representations becoming too similar as they are
052   recursively processed. This is undesirable since it limits the GNN from effectively utilizing the
053   information in the input features. The problem has garnered significant attention from the research
      community, resulting in a suite of algorithms designed to address it (Rusch et al., 2023). Amongst


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      these methods are a collection of random edge-dropping algorithms, including DropEdge (Rong
055   et al., 2020) and its variants – DropNode (Feng et al., 2020), DropAgg (Jiang et al., 2023) and
056   DropGNN (Papp et al., 2021) – which act as message-passing reducers.
057
058
      The other issue specific to GNNs is over-squashing. In certain graph structures, neighborhood size
      grows exponentially with distance from the source (Chen et al., 2018b), causing information to be
059
      lost as it passes through graph bottlenecks (Alon & Yahav, 2021). This limits MPNNs’ ability to
060
      enable communication between distant nodes, which is crucial for good performance on long-range
061   tasks. To alleviate over-squashing, several graph-rewiring techniques have been proposed, which
062   aim to improve graph connectivity by adding edges in a strategic manner1 (Alon & Yahav, 2021;
063   Black et al., 2023; Deac et al., 2022; Karhadkar et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023). In contrast, the
064   DropEdge-variants only remove edges, which should, in principle, amplify over-squashing levels.
065
      The empirical evidence in support of methods designed for training deep GNNs has been majorly
066
      collected on short-range tasks. That is, it simply suggests that these methods prevent loss of local
067   information, but it remains inconclusive if they facilitate capturing long-range interactions (LRIs).
068   Of course, on long-range tasks, deeper GNNs are useless if they cannot capture LRIs. This is
069   especially a concern for DropEdge-variants since evidence suggests that alleviating over-smoothing
070   with graph rewiring could exacerbate over-squashing (Giraldo et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023).
071
      Theoretical Contributions. In this work, we uncover the effects of random edge-dropping algo-
072
      rithms on over-squashing in MPNNs. By explicitly computing the expected sensitivity of the node
073   representations to the node features (Topping et al., 2022) (inversely related to over-squashing) in a
074   linear GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), we show that these methods provably reduce the effective recep-
075   tive field of the model. Precisely speaking, the rate at which sensitivity between distant nodes decays
076   is polynomial w.r.t. the dropping probability. Finally, we extend the existing theoretical results on
077   sensitivity in nonlinear MPNNs (Black et al., 2023; Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2018) to the
078   random edge-dropping setting, again showing that these algorithms exacerbate the over-squashing
079   problem.
080   Experimental Results. We evaluate the DropEdge-variants on node classification tasks using GCN
081   and GAT (Veličković et al., 2018) architectures. Specifically, we assess their performance on ho-
082   mophilic datasets – Cora (McCallum et al., 2000) and CiteSeer (Giles et al., 1998) – which represent
083   short-range tasks, and heterophilic datasets – Chameleon, Squirrel, TwitchDE (Rozemberczki et al.,
084   2021) – which correspond to long-range tasks. Our results indicate an increasing trend in test accu-
085   racy for homophilic datasets and a declining trend for heterophilic datasets as the dropping proba-
086   bility increases. Accordingly, we hypothesize that edge-dropping algorithms improve performance
087
      on short-range tasks, as has been reported earlier, by reducing the receptive field of the GNN and
      increasing model-dataset alignment, However, for long-range tasks, they decrease the model-dataset
088
      alignment, resulting in poor generalization.

137   Several variants of DropEdge have also been proposed, forming a family of random edge-dropping
138   algorithms for tackling the over-smoothing problem. For example, DropNode (Feng et al., 2020)
139   independently samples nodes and sets their representations to 0, followed by rescaling to make the
140   feature matrix unbiased. This is equivalent to setting the corresponding columns of the propagation
141   matrix to 0. In a similar vein, DropAgg (Jiang et al., 2023) samples nodes that don’t aggregate mes-
142   sages from their neighbors. This is equivalent to dropping the corresponding rows of the adjacency
143
      matrix. Combining these two approaches, DropGNN (Papp et al., 2021) samples nodes which nei-
      ther propagate nor aggregate messages in a given message-passing step. These algorithms alleviate
144
      over-smoothing by reducing the number of messages being propagated in the graph, thereby slowing
145
      down the convergence of node representations.

      2.3   OVER - SQUASHING
148
149   Over-squashing refers to the problem of information from exponentially growing neighborhoods
150   (Chen et al., 2018a) being squashed into finite-sized node representations (Alon & Yahav, 2021).
151   This results in a loss of information as it is propagated over long distances, disallowing MPNNs
152   from capturing long-range interactions (LRIs) and limiting their applications to short-range tasks.
153   Topping et al. (2022) formally characterized over-squashing in terms of the Jacobian of the node-
                                                          (L)
154   level representations w.r.t. the input features: ∥∂zi /∂xj ∥1 . Accordingly, over-squashing can be
155   understood as low sensitivity between distant nodes, i.e. small perturbations in a node’s features
156   don’t effect other distant nodes’ representations.
157   Several works have linked over-squashing in an MPNN with topological properties like Cheeger’s
158   constant (Giraldo et al., 2023; Karhadkar et al., 2023), curvature of edges (Liu et al., 2023; Nguyen
159   et al., 2023; Topping et al., 2022), effective resistance between nodes (Arnaiz-Rodríguez et al., 2022;
160   Black et al., 2023) and the expected commute time between them (Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Gio-
161   vanni et al., 2024). These results have inspired the design of several graph rewiring techniques that
      strategically add edges to improve the connectivity in the graph, thereby alleviating over-squashing.

514   5     C ONCLUSION
515
516   Our analysis points out a key assumption in algorithms designed for training deep GNNs: the idea
517   that if a deep GNN is trainable, it must be able to model LRIs. Our results suggest that this, in fact,
518   need not be true – we theoretically and empirically show that DropEdge-like algorithms exacerbate
519   the over-squashing problem in deep GNNs, and degrade their performance on long-range tasks. Our
520   results highlight a need for a thorough evaluation of methods employed when training deep GNNs,
521   with regards to their capacity to capture LRIs. This will allow us to reliably deploy them in real-life,
522   since we can be assured that the models did not simply overfit to short-range signals.
523   Limitations. While our theoretical analysis successfully predicts how DropEdge-variants affect test
524   performance on short-range and long-range tasks, it is based on several simplifying assumptions.
525   These assumptions, although standard in the literature, limit the generalizability of our conclusions
526   to other architectures. Specifically, our analysis focuses on certain classes of MPNNs, excluding
527   several GNN architectures specifically designed to enhance long-distance information propagation
528   (see Singh (2024) for a review).
529   Practical Considerations. Previous studies have shown that random edge-dropping algorithms
530   can effectively enhance generalization performance in short-range tasks, and our findings support
531   this conclusion. However, we have also demonstrated that such algorithms can negatively impact
532   over-squashing in MPNNs and harm test-time performance in long-range tasks. Therefore, we rec-
533   ommend exercising caution when using such methods, as careless application can result in models
534   that generalize poorly, which can be detrimental in critical applications.
535   Future Directions. This work focuses on node-classification tasks, but it is also important to un-
536   derstand the effect of random edge-dropping in other practical settings, e.g. link prediction, and
537   even graph-level tasks. In general, there is a need for a broader investigation into methods de-
538   signed for training deep GNNs. Specifically, analyzing various strategies designed for mitigating
539   over-smoothing (see Rusch et al. (2023) for a review), particularly in the context of over-squashing,
      could be invaluable for designing deep GNNs for long-range tasks.

813   A.1   M ETHODS FOR A LLEVIATING OVER - SMOOTHING
814
815   A popular choice for reducing over-smoothing in GNNs is to regularize the model. Recall that
816   DropEdge (Rong et al., 2020) implicitly regularizes the model by adding noise to the learning trajec-
817   tory (Section 2.2). Similarly, Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) drops entries from the input features
818
      to each layer, and DropMessage (Fang et al., 2023) drops entries from the message matrix (before
      the aggregation step). Graph Drop Connect (GDC) (Hasanzadeh et al., 2020) combines DropEdge
819
      and DropMessage together, resulting in a layer-wise sampling scheme that uses a different sub-
820
      graph for message-aggregation over each feature dimension. These methods successfully addressed
821   the over-smoothing problem, enabling the training of deep GNNs, and performed competitively on
822   several benchmarking datasets.
823
824
      Another powerful form of implicit regularization is feature normalization, which has proven crucial
      in enhancing the performance and stability of several types of neural networks (Huang et al., 2020).
825
      Exploiting the inductive bias in graph-structured data, normalization techniques like PairNorm
826
      (Zhao & Akoglu, 2020), Differentiable Group Normalization (DGN) (Zhou et al., 2020) and Node-
827   Norm (Zhou et al., 2021b) have been proposed to reduce over-smoothing in GNNs. On the other
828   hand, Energetic Graph Neural Networks (EGNNs) (Zhou et al., 2021a) explicitly regularize the
829   optimization by constraining the layer-wise Dirichlet energy to a predefined range.
830
      In a different vein, motivated by the success of residual networks (ResNets) (He et al., 2016) in
831
      computer vision, (Li et al., 2019) proposed the use of residual connections to prevent the smoothing
832
      of representations. Residual connections successfully improved the performance of GCN on a range
833   of graph-learning tasks. (Chen et al., 2020) introduced GCNII, which uses skip connections from
834   the input to all hidden layers. This layer wise propagation rule has allowed for training of ultra-
835   deep networks – up to 64 layers. Some other architectures, like the Jumping Knowledge Network
836   (JKNet) (Xu et al., 2018) and the Deep Adaptive GNN (DAGNN) (Liu et al., 2020), aggregate the
                                          (ℓ)
837   representations from all layers, {zi }L ℓ=1 , before processing them through a readout layer.
838
839
840   A.2   M ETHODS FOR A LLEVIATING OVER - SQUASHING
841
842   In this section, we will review some of the graph rewiring methods proposed to address the problem
843   of over-squashing. Particularly, we wish to emphasize a commonality among these methods – edge
844   addition is necessary. As a reminder, graph rewiring refers to modifying the edge set of a graph by
845   adding and/or removing edges in a systematic manner. In a special case, which includes many of
846   the rewiring techniques we will discuss, the original topology is completely discarded, and only the
847   rewired graph is used for message-passing.
848   Spatial rewiring methods use the topological relationships between the nodes in order to come up
849   with a rewiring strategy. That is the graph rewiring is guided by the objective of optimizing some
850   chosen topological properties. For instance, Alon & Yahav (2021) introduced a fully-adjacent (FA)
851   layer, wherein messages are passed between all nodes. GNNs using a FA layer in the final message-
852   passing step were shown to outperform the baselines on a variety of long-range tasks, revealing
853   the importance of information exchange between far-off nodes which standard message-passing
854   cannot facilitate. Topping et al. (2022) proposed a curvature-based rewiring strategy, called the
855
      Stochastic Discrete Ricci Flow (SDRF), which aims to reduce the “bottleneckedness” of a graph by
      adding suitable edges, while simultaneously removing edges in an effort to preserve the statistical
856
      properties of the original topology. Black et al. (2023) proposed the Greedy Total Resistance (GTR)
857
      technique, which optimizes the graph’s total resistance by greedily adding edges to achieve the
858   greatest improvement. One concern with graph rewiring methods is that unmoderated densification
859   of the graph, e.g. using a fully connected graph for propagating messages, can result in a loss of
860   the inductive bias the topology provides, potentially leading to over-fitting. Accordingly, Gutteridge
861   et al. (2023) propose a Dynamically Rewired (DRew) message-passing framework that gradually
862   densifies the graph. Specifically, in a given layer ℓ, node i aggregates messages from its entire ℓ-hop
863   receptive field instead of just the immediate neighbors. This results in an improved communication
      over long distances while also retaining the inductive bias of the shortest distance between nodes.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Spectral methods, on the other hand, use the spectral properties of the matrices encoding the graph
865   topology, e.g. the adjacency or the Laplacian matrix, to design rewiring algorithms. For example,
866   Arnaiz-Rodríguez et al. (2022) proposed a differentiable graph rewiring layer based on the Lovász
867   bound (Lovász, 1993, Corollary 3.3). Similarly, Banerjee et al. (2022) introduced the Random Local
868   Edge Flip (RLEF) algorithm, which draws inspiration from the “Flip Markov Chain” (Feder et al.,
869   2006; Mahlmann & Schindelhauer, 2005) – a sequence of such steps can convert a connected graph
870   into an expander graph – a sparse graph with good connectivity (in terms of Cheeger’s constant) –
871   with high probability (Allen-Zhu et al., 2016; Cooper et al., 2019; Feder et al., 2006; Giakkoupis,
872   2022; Mahlmann & Schindelhauer, 2005), thereby enabling effective information propagation across
873
      the graph.
874   Some other rewiring techniques don’t exactly classify as spatial or spectral methods. For instance,
875   Probabilistically Rewired MPNN (PR-MPNN) (Qian et al., 2024) learns to probabilistically rewire a
876   graph, effectively mitigating under-reaching as well as over-squashing. Finally, (Gabrielsson et al.,
877   2023) proposed connecting all nodes at most r-hops away, for some r ∈ N, and introducing posi-
878   tional embeddings to allow for distance-aware aggregation of messages.
879
880   A.3   T OWARDS A U NIFIED T REATMENT
881
      Several studies have shown that an inevitable trade-off exists between the problems of over-
882
      smoothing and over-squashing, meaning that optimizing for one will compromise the other. For
883
      instance, Nguyen et al. (2023); Topping et al. (2022) showed that negatively curved edges create
884   bottlenecks in the graph resulting in over-squashing of information. On the other hand, Nguyen
885   et al. (2023, Proposition 4.3) showed that positively curved edges in a graph contribute towards
886   the over-smoothing problem. To address this trade-off, they proposed Batch Ollivier-Ricci Flow
887   (BORF), which adds new edges adjacent to the negatively curved ones, and simultaneously removes
888   positively curved ones. In a similar vein, Giraldo et al. (2023) demonstrated that the minimum num-
889   ber of message-passing steps required to reach a given level of over-smoothing is inversely related to
890   the Cheeger’s constant, hG . This again implies an inverse relationship between over-smoothing and
891   over-squashing. To effectively alleviate the two issues together, they proposed the Stochastic Jost
892
      and Liu Curvature Rewiring (SJLR) algorithm, which adds edges that result in high improvement in
      the curvature of existing edges, while simultaneously removing those that have low curvature.
893
894   Despite the well-established trade-off between over-smoothing and over-squashing, some works
895   have successfully tackled them together despite only adding or removing edges. One such work
896   is Karhadkar et al. (2023), which proposed a rewiring algorithm that adds edges to the graph but
897   does not remove any. The First-order Spectral Rewiring (FoSR) algorithm computes, as the name
898
      suggests, a first order approximation to the spectral gap of Lsym , and adds edges with the aim of
      maximizing it. Since the spectral gap directly relates to Cheeger’s constant through Cheeger’s in-
899
      equality (Alon & Milman, 1985; Alon, 1986; Sinclair & Jerrum, 1989), this directly decreases the
900
      over-squashing levels. Moreover, Karhadkar et al. (2023, Figure 5) empirically demonstrated that
901   addition of (up to a small number of) edges selected by FoSR can lower the Dirichlet energy of
902   the representations. Taking a completely opposite approach, CurvDrop (Liu et al., 2023) adapted
903   DropEdge to remove negatively curved edges sampled from a distribution proportional to their cur-
904   vatures. CurvDrop directly reduces over-squashing and, as a side benefit of operating on a sparser
905   subgraph, also mitigates over-smoothing.
