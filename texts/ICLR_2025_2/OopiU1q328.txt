                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite being theoretically well-grounded, enforcing strict equivariance in deep
012            learning models has shown to be harmful in some cases. The problem is that most
013            available data does not follow mathematically precise rules, is noisy, and is not
014            strictly group-structured. While soft equivariance approaches attempt to address
015            these issues, they often struggle to maintain group structure and lack strong the-
016            oretical guarantees, potentially compromising the benefits of equivariance. Here
017            we introduce the concept of quasi equivariances, where group structure is main-
018            tained but the associated parameters become distributions, and implement it in
019            the proposed PowerNet architecture. Similar to CNNs, PowerNet is constructed
020
               by interlacing truncated matrix power series with non-linearities. We show how
               the base matrix used to define the power series can instill quasi-equivariance in a
021
               natural way. Finally, we provide results for augmented MNIST classification and
022
               transformation magnitude regression in addition to classification of CIFAR-10.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Although group equivariant convolutional networks (Cohen & Welling, 2016) can be limitlessly
028   expressive (Yarotsky, 2018), enforcing strict equivariance in neural networks has shown to be more
029   limiting than beneficial (Liu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022). Real-world data and tasks rarely
030   exhibit group-structured properties. Due to the closure property, group actions form inherently strict
031   algebraic structures and most datasets encountered in practice are not naturally distributed according
032   to known groups.
033   An exception can be made for tasks that are inherently mathematical or group structured, such as
034   molecules (Gilmer et al., 2017), PDE data (Brandstetter et al., 2023), dynamical systems (Yang et al.,
035   2024), or neural networks themselves (Navon et al., 2023; Kofinas et al., 2024). A typical approach
036   is to assume an underlying group structure and parameterize a distribution of the magnitudes of the
037   transformation on the orbits of the associated one-parameter group (Falorsi et al., 2019; Dehmamy
038   et al., 2021; van der Ouderaa & van der Wilk, 2022; Gabel et al., 2023). Mathematical results in
039
      group theory are usually only known for compact or linear groups, the contrary of the latter taking us
      beyond traditional (linear) representation theory, usually used in geometric deep learning. A natural
040
      extension of this observation is that groups are idealized abstractions of transformations, which data
041
      is then assumed to conform to, but often does not.
042
043   In this paper, we propose to revisit the recently proposed soft equivariance concept from a weight
044   sharing perspective. The connection between weight sharing and equivariance has been known
045
      since the introduction of CNNs (LeCun, 1989; Ravanbakhsh et al., 2017; Maron et al., 2019). Soft
      equivariance approaches aim to relax strict symmetry constraints, allowing models to handle real-
046
      world data that often doesn’t perfectly adhere to mathematical symmetries. However, these methods
047
      often struggle to maintain the underlying group structure and lack strong theoretical guarantees,
048   potentially compromising the benefits of equivariance while introducing additional computational
049   complexity. The motivation for studying a relaxed version of group equivariance, which we will
050   refer to as quasi-equivariance, is threefold. In mathematical settings, symmetry transformations
051   of a given task can often be described precisely by groups, whether as continuous (e.g., rotations)
052   or discrete group actions. However, real-world data is noisy, imprecise, and seldom exhibits such
053   exact group symmetries. Quasi-equivariance allows models to capture underlying patterns without
      committing to strict group-theoretic assumptions. Second, like soft equivariance, quasi-equivariance

054
      enables models to remain sensitive to probabilistic magnitudes of transformations, making the net-
055   work more adaptive to varying magnitudes of transformation in data. In real-world settings, where
056   robustness w.r.t. small deformations is often a sought-after property, this property can lead to vast
057   improvement in out-of-distribution performance. Third, learnable inductive biases or structural pri-
058   ors: By exploring equivariance through the lens of weight sharing, the model can learn inductive
059   biases more naturally.
060
      To illustrate our approach, we draw an analogy between Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
061   and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Both models employ weight-sharing principles derived from
062   symmetry assumptions, i.e., shift and permutation matrices respectively. With PowerNet, we pro-
063   pose a generalization for any base matrix, which enables more flexible structure learning. This
064   generalization does not inherently recover a basic multi-layer perceptron (MLP), however. To eval-
065   uate the effectiveness of our model, we report augmented MNIST classification and regression on
066   the magnitude of the parameters. Additionally, we ran our model on CIFAR-10 for classification.
067   This approach has promising implications for future research, particularly when combined with con-
068   tinual learning. A neural network equipped with learnable equivariances could dynamically update
069   its connectivity scheme as it processes new data, allowing it to adaptively learn new inductive biases
070   over time. Such a system could evolve its internal structure in response to experience, potentially
071   abstracting over consecutive inductive biases, whether in series (this work) or in parallel. The latter
072   of which we leave as a possibility for future work.
073   Contributions With this investigation of the PowerNet architecture, we introduce a number of
074   contributions to the field of geometric deep learning:
075
076          • A novel way of interpreting equivariant neural networks by relaxing the strict constraint
077            group actions place on the traditional group equivariant architectures considered in the
078            field,
079          • a connection between Laurent polynomials, weight sharing, and equivariant neural net-
080            works that could help with structural bias learning,
081          • the PowerNet architecture and library, which is fast, parameter efficient, open source, and
082            easy to use.
083
084   Limitations Besides the considerable low parameter count PowerNet is able to perform tasks such
085   as augemented MNIST classification and CIFAR with, it does not currently beat state-of-the-art on
086   the latter.
087   Reproducibility We provide all scripts and settings used for the experiments contained in this
088   paper. The PowerNet library will be made open-source in order to allow for further experimentation
089   and incorporation of the methods presented here.

      2.1   G ROUPS , G RAPHS , AND S ELF -ATTENTION
097
098   The operation of sharing weights in deep neural networks has been an intense area of study (Ravan-
099   bakhsh et al., 2017; Maron et al., 2019). It ranges from exploiting the symmetries of the weights in
100   neural networks in order to improve training Neural Fields (Navon et al., 2023; Kofinas et al., 2024)
101   to performing lifting operations in group convolutions that make the kernel mimic the group trans-
102   formation of interest such that the model becomes equivariant (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Kondor &
103   Trivedi, 2018; Bekkers et al., 2018; Weiler & Cesa, 2019). The connection between CNNs, group
104
      convolutions, and GNNs has been discussed in great detail (Bronstein et al., 2021). In a closely
      related work, matrix functions have been proposed for applications to graph-structured data (Batatia
105
      et al., 2024).
106
107   The success of transformer-based models has attracted interest from the geometric deep learning
      field. Group equivariant attention has been shown to be possible when applied to the positional en-

      codings (Romero & Cordonnier, 2021) and the relationship to wavelets in time-series for translation
109   and scale equivariance is also noteworthy (Romero et al., 2024).

111   2.2     I NDUCTIVE B IAS L EARNING
112
113   In the context of inductive bias learning, the irrelevancies of a given task, usually formalized math-
114   ematically as symmetry groups Knigge et al. (2022), can be exploited by geometric methods that
115   either lead to weight-sharing schemes within the neural network Zhou et al. (2020); Finzi et al.
116   (2021); van der Ouderaa et al. (2023) or overcompensate with additional computational operations
117   for when the input undergoes the expected transformation Kondor & Trivedi (2018); Bekkers et al.
118
      (2018); Romero & Lohit (2022).

120   3     T HE PowerNet A RCHITECTURE
121
122   We take inspiration from CNNs, in which convolutions can be rewritten as matrix multiplications
123   of a circulant matrix with the flattened, i.e., vectorized, image. First, we define the PowerLayer
124   and PowerBlock. Mathematically, we are interested in Laurent polynomials over the reals, a slight
125
      variation on the usual group-based exposition given in most works (Maron et al., 2019; Bronstein
      et al., 2021). The variable is a matrix that we will refer to as the base matrix.

135   Consider parametrizing the weight matrix of a neural network as a Laurent polynomial of a square
136   base matrix M ∈ Rd×d over R in  order to generalize the concept of a convolutional layer. Note, in
137   this case, P = P ∈ Π M , M −1 ⊂ Rd×d . In other words, we are interested in weight matrices
138   that can be written as a truncated power series of a matrix. We will refer to the linear mapping P x
139
      as a Laurent convolution.
140   Hence, we define a single PowerLayer as fθ (x|M ) = σ(P x + b), with feature vector and bias term
141   x, b ∈ Rd . The non-linear activation function is denoted by σ and the resulting feature vector is of
142   the same size as the input to the layer, namely fθ (x|M ) = y ∈ Rd . This parametrization of the
143   weight matrix as a Laurent convolution allows for a natural definition of kernel size, dilation, and
144   convolutions. In particular, if we let the powers range from −K to K, the number of parameters is
145
      2K + 1 ∼ O(K) where K ∈ N0 = {1, 2, 3, ...}. This gives PowerLayers a low parameter count,
      relative to most architectures.
146
147   Dilation of the kernel can easily be controlled: By analogy with CNNs, we simply multiply the
148   powers in the Laurent series by D ∈ N0 . Locality is easily relaxed by choosing a different base
149   matrix. Note that the layer still performs an affine transformation of the feature vector x.

169   3.3   L IE T HEORY AND THE S HANNON -W HITAKKER I NTERPOLATION
170
171   Clearly, we would like PowerNet to have the ability to encapsulate the usual group equivariance
172   pervasive in the geometric deep learning literature. Even though the group assumption has been re-
173
      laxed, here we show how PowerNet is still able to model group equivariance by choosing appropriate
      base matrices. The relevant kernels are the ones that mimic the group actions under consideration,
174
      this can be done by choosing a base matrix that permutes the pixels of the flattened image accord-
175
      ingly. Note that in most cases this will involve some aliasing effect. Since we are only interested
176   in quasi-equivariance, aliasing effects should not worry us here. The key assumption is that depth,
177   multiple kernels, pooling operations, and residual connections present in most deep learning models
178   are sufficient to solve the task.
179
      Using Lie theory, we can describe the problem as follows. The transformation of interest is defined
180
      by an element of a Lie algebra, and as long we can perform the exponential map, the result should
181
      be the group action we are interested in and a candidate for the base matrix of the PowerLayer. The
182   elements of the Lie algebra can be obtained by first defining the derivative operator, a matrix that
183   when exponetiated yields the shift matrix. One such approach involves using the Shannon-Whitakker
184   (SW) interpolation to calculate the derivative operator, as was done in previous works attempting
185   to parameterize Lie group in the context of machine learning (Rao & Ruderman, 1998; Dehmamy
186   et al., 2021; Gabel et al., 2023).
187

188   interpolation automatically assumes the function to be interpolated is periodic, although other inter-

190   transformations of low-resolution images, and forms one of the notable limitations of the current
191   model. One could investigate other choices, such as bicubic interpolation, although deriving the
192   differential operator for this scheme requires some additional analysis and is left for future work.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ET- UP
288
289   With this work, we provide code for the PowerNet mini-library. This library allows a user to
290   design custom quasi-equivariant deep neural networks for their own projects. The PowerNet archi-
291   tecture is characterized by the PowerLayer() class, which is defined by its base matrices.
292   Similar to CNNs, the user should provide kernel sizes (powers) in addition to the conventional
293   c in and c out values, which correspond to the number of input and output channels of the Pow-
294
      erLayer, respectively. To make the construction of deep architectures easier, the PowerBlock()
      class provides options for the strength of residual connections (rho), multiple layers per block
295
      (num layers), choice of non-linearities (non linearity), batch or layer normalization, etc.

311   4.2   AUGMENTED MNIST R EGRESSION AND CIFAR-10 C LASSIFICATION
312
313   In all experiments, BatchNorm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), residual connections and
314
      LeakyReLU(0.2) was used since it mainly sped up training.
315   The experimental setup was as follows: 4 blocks, 2 layers per block, 32 channels, kernel size of 5,
316   stride 1, with residual connection, batch size of 1024, average pooling, Adam optimizer (Kingma
317   & Ba, 2015), learning rate 0.01, and weight decay 0.0001. The base matrices were chosen from
318   rotation, scaling, and shift (in the “up” direction) and trained on rotated MNIST. The task was to
319   predict the transformation magnitude, in this case, the rotation angle. The test performance (MSE)
320
      on the augmented rotation angle in radians was 1.485 (shift), 1.456 (scale), 0.949 (rotation) radians.
      The disparate result clearly shows the improvement when the correct inductive bias is used. This
321
      model only has 28k parameters.
322
323   For augmented 2xMNIST classification, namely a double sized image with the digit rotated (full
      range), scaled (between 0.8 and 1.2), and translated (max. 10 pixels) the setup was: 4 base matrices,

      one for each transformation mentioned in the previous paragraph (shift up and shift right being two
325   separate base matrices), 5 blocks, 2 layers per block, [2, 4, 8, 16] channels, kernel size of 5, stride
326   1, max pooling, batch size of 64, Adam optimizer, learning rate 0.001, and weight decay 0.0001
327   the performance reached 84% (identical performance to a CNN baseline with the same channel and

334   5   C ONCLUSION
335
336   In this work, we introduced PowerNet, a neural network architecture that takes inspiration from
337   CNNs and GNNs by parameterizing the weight matrix by a truncated matrix power series. Mathe-
338
      matically, we draw connections to Laurent polynomials and the duality between equivariant models
      and weight sharing. We show equal performance to baselines on an augmented MNIST dataset, and
339
      decent performance on CIFAR-10 classification. This shows our implementation is a rewiring of the
340
      usual convolutional networks, with the added benefit of allowing for flexible filter choices. We look
341   forward to seeing how the community uses the PowerNet mini-library to incorporate different base
342   matrices for their use-cases.
