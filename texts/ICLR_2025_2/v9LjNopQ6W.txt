009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid advancement of Zero-Shot Text-to-Speech (ZS-TTS) technology has
012            enabled high-fidelity voice synthesis from minimal audio cues, raising significant
013            privacy and ethical concerns. In particular, the ability to replicate an individual’s
014            voice without consent poses risks, highlighting the need for machine unlearning
015            techniques to protect voice privacy. In this paper, we introduce the first machine
016            unlearning framework for ZS-TTS, Teacher-Guided Unlearning (TGU), designed
017            to ensure that the model forgets designated speaker identities while retaining its
018
               ability to generate accurate speech for other speakers. Unlike conventional un-
               learning methods, TGU leverages randomness to prevent consistent replication of
019
               forget speakers’ voices, ensuring unlearned identities remain untraceable. Addi-
020
               tionally, we propose a new evaluation metric, speaker-Zero Retrain Forgetting
021            (spk-ZRF), which measures the model’s effectiveness in preventing the repro-
022            duction of forgotten voices. The experiments conducted on the state-of-the-art
023            model demonstrate that TGU prevents the model from replicating forget speakers’
024            voices while maintaining high quality for other speakers. The demo is available at
025            https://speechunlearn.github.io/

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Significant advancements in Zero-Shot Text-to-Speech (ZS-TTS) (Le et al., 2024; Casanova et al.,
031   2022; Ju et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) have demonstrated ground-breaking performance, enabling
032   models to replicate and synthesize speech in any given speaker’s voice. Among the prominent
033
      methods in ZS-TTS, VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023) represents speech as discrete tokens to train a
      language model, while VoiceBox (Le et al., 2024) uses a masked prediction learning technique to
034
      effectively handle both ZS-TTS and audio-infilling tasks. Notably, these in-context based learning
035
      methods enable highly precise speech synthesis by cloning a specific voice with only a 3-second
036   audio cue.
037
038   Given that a person’s voice is a key biometric characteristic used for identification (Nautsch et al.,
039
      2019a;b), these rapid advancements in ZS-TTS raise significant ethical concerns, especially regard-
      ing the potential misuse of synthesizing speech from an individual’s voice without consent. These
040
      concerns are further amplified by regulations such as the European Union’s General Data Protection
041
      Regulation (GDPR) (Regulation, 2016) and the Right To Be Forgotten (RTBF) (Mantelero, 2013),
042   which emphasize the importance of protecting personally identifiable information.
043
044   As an approach to address these challenges, machine unlearning (MU) can serve as an effective
045
      solution by selectively removing certain knowledge through modifications to the model weights.
      Given that generative AI models are inherently capable of creating new content and thus particularly
046
      susceptible to privacy breaches (Panariello et al., 2024; Tomashenko et al., 2024), MU has been
047
      increasingly applied across various fields of generative AI to address these vulnerabilities. The
048   application of MU in computer vision has focused on removing and preventing the synthesis of
049   specific concepts (Gandikota et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024; Seo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), while
050   in natural language processing, it has been utilized to unlearn undesirable sequences and identity-
051   specific knowledge (Maini et al., 2024; Jang et al., 2023). However, despite the growing attention
052   to privacy concerns in speech-related tasks (Tomashenko et al., 2022; Yoo et al., 2020), there have
053   been no proposed methods that can effectively unlearn the ability to generate speech in a specific
      speaker’s voice.

054
      Unlearning in ZS-TTS presents unique challenges because the model can replicate speaker identities
055   in a zero-shot manner, even without direct training on specific speaker data. Therefore, traditional
056   unlearning approaches, which often rely on excluding data related to the forget speakers (i.e., Ex-
057   act Unlearning in Figure 1-top), fall short in effectively limiting a ZS-TTS model’s capability to
058   reproduce these voices. In addition, an ideal unlearned ZS-TTS model should avoid settling into
059   any specific voice style that could be traced back to the forget speakers’ identity. To achieve this,
060   the model needs to be trained to generate speech in random voice styles for forget speakers, using
061   aligned pairs of text and random voices.
062   To this end, this paper proposes the first machine unlearning framework for ZS-TTS, termed
063   Teacher-Guided Unlearning (TGU), which leverages the pre-trained teacher model as a guide to
064   generate speaker-randomized target outputs for the forget speakers (Figure 1-bottom). Unlike con-
065   ventional UL methods, TGU introduces randomness in voice styles when the model encounters
066   prompts related to the forget speakers, effectively guiding the model to unlearn these associations
067   and discouraging it from reproducing the forgotten voices. This approach allows the model to neu-
068   tralize its responses to forget speakers’ prompts while retaining the ability to generate high-quality
069
      speech for other speakers.
070   To evaluate the effectiveness of this unlearning process, we also introduce the speaker-Zero Retrain
071   Forgetting (spk-ZRF) metric. Unlike conventional evaluation metrics that only compare perfor-
072   mance between forget and retain sets, spk-ZRF measures the degree of randomness in the generated
073   speaker identities when handling forget speaker prompts. This provides a more comprehensive
074   assessment of how well the model has unlearned and mitigates the risk of reconstruction or manip-
075
      ulation of unlearned voices, ensuring enhanced privacy.
076   The main contributions are as follows:
077
078          • This paper is the first to address the challenge of implementing machine unlearning in ZS-
079            TTS, focusing on making the model ‘forget’ specific speaker identities while maintaining
080            its ability to perform accurate speech synthesis for retain speakers.
081          • We propose a novel framework, TGU, which guides the model to generate speech with
082            random voice styles for forget speakers, effectively reducing the ability to replicate their
083            identities.
084
             • Plus, we introduce a new metric, spk-ZRF, to evaluate the effectiveness of unlearning by
085            measuring the degree of randomness in synthesized speaker identities for forget prompts.

      2.1   M ACHINE U NLEARNING
091
092   Machine unlearning emerged as a process of making a model forget specific knowledge while main-
093   taining its overall performance (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) as
094   privacy concerns over personal data grew, such as RTBF (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017; Bertram
095   et al., 2019; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017). Early MU techniques focused on adjusting the pre-trained
096   model’s parameters to remove the influence of specific data within the training set (Guo et al., 2019).
097   Thus, Exact Unlearning, a method of retraining the model without data to forget from scratch, was a
098   predominant golden standard of MU methods (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
099
      2022a; Brophy & Lowd, 2021). Approximate unlearning, a method that removes the impact of spe-
      cific data without retraining, has gained prominence for its efficiency and proved particularly useful
100
      for large-scale and generative models (Golatkar et al., 2020; Thudi et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023;
101
      Warnecke et al., 2021; Heng & Soh, 2024). Research in computer vision (CV) and natural language
102   processing (NLP) has recently focused on ensuring that generative models like GAN or Diffusion
103   do not generate specific identities, data, words, or phrases (Zhang et al., 2024; 2023; Gandikota
104   et al., 2023; Seo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2022; Lynch et al., 2024). The impor-
105   tance of privacy is also emphasized in the audio domain, especially speech generation (Tomashenko
106   et al., 2024). While unlearning has been explored in natural language description generation through
107   concept-specific neuron pruning within the Audio Network Dissection (AND) framework (Wu et al.,
      2024), its effectiveness for more complex audio generation tasks like ZS-TTS remains untested and

136   Recently, there have been groundbreaking advancements in large-scale speech generative models,
137   allowing successful replication of a given voice with just a 3-second audio sample. VALL-E (Wang

228   3.3   P ROPOSED A PPROACH : T EACHER -G UIDED U NLEARNING
229
230   In line with the objectives outlined earlier, the synthesized output from an ideal unlearned ZS-TTS
231
      model must not only diverge from replicating the forget speaker’s style but should also avoid being
      fixed in any specific voice style. To achieve this, we can apply guided unlearning to make the model
232
      generate speech that targets a random and variable voice style, preventing it from settling into a
233
      consistent or identifiable pattern. However, to train the model to generate the given text y in a
234   random voice style, it requires a pair (xspk̸=f , y), where the speech audio xspk̸=f uttering y aligns
235   frame-wise with that of (xspk=f , y). Unfortunately, aligned pairs for truly random speakers cannot
236   be naturally obtained.
237
      As an alternative, for speakers in the remain set DR , we can extract an aligned pair (xr , y), and
238
      for speakers in the forget set, we can similarly extract (xf , y f ). Thus, a simple approach to tackle
239
      this challenge would be to concatenate those two pairs as if they form a single sample, then mask
240   the xr part and set this as the target for generation (Figure 2-(a)). However, the issue with this
241   naive Sample-Guided Unlearning (SGU) is that masking can only be applied to the entirety of xr ,
242   and not selectively in the middle of the concatenated speech. In the original VoiceBox framework,
243   the model uses both the preceding and succeeding audio contexts around the masked region to
244   perform infilling predictions. But in this case, the model would only have access to the unmasked
245   portion from the opposite side (xr ) for infilling, which severely limits its ability to leverage both
246   contexts. Moreover, if we attempt to mask in the middle of the concatenated speech, the model
247   may learn unnatural speech generation patterns due to the mismatches in tempo, rhythm, and other
248
      characteristics between the two speakers. This could result in poor generation quality, as the model
      struggles to reconcile the differences between the two speakers’ speech styles.
249
250   To address this, we propose a machine unlearning method for ZS-TTS, named Teacher-Guided Un-
251   learning (TGU), where we generate text-speech aligned target samples using the pre-trained teacher
252   model itself to guide the unlearning process effectively. Specifically, we suggest utilizing the fact
253   that when θ is conditioned solely on y, it generates speech with linguistic content based on y, but
254
      the resulting voice style varies depending on the initialization of x0 , i.e., Gaussian noise, leading
      to the synthesis of different voice styles. Using θ(y) as target guidance thus assures that at each
255
      initialization, the model generates varying voice styles, reducing the risk of reproducing identifiable
256
      information on forget speaker’s voice:
257
258                                            θ− (xf , y) ≈ θ(y).                                       (6)

      3.4   P ROPOSED M ETRIC : SPK -ZRF
282
283   Conventionally, evaluation methods on MU such as completeness (Wang et al., 2024), JS-
284   divergence, activation distance and layer-wise distance merely compare the performance gap be-
285   tween forget and remain set. However, a model exhibiting consistent patterns on the forget set
286   is not necessarily well unlearned, as these patterns can be exploited to reverse-engineer the forget
287   speaker’s voice. Therefore, such evaluations can be misleading, and an appropriate metric should
288   assess the extent to which the model exhibits random behaviors when generating speech for the
289   forget set. Epistemic Uncertainty, another existing metric in unlearning domain evaluates how little
290   information about the forget set is present in model parameters (Becker & Liebig, 2022). However,
291
      applying this method is not suitable when representations in model layers contain deeply entangled
      information. A low Epistemic Uncertainity in ZS-TTS models cannot indicate that the model has
292
      forgotten speaker-specific information instead of performance of audible speech generation. To this
293
      end, we suggest a novel metric to evaluate randomness in synthesized speech’s speaker identity
294   named speaker-Zero Retrain Forgetting metric (spk-ZRF), a metric that evaluates the degree of ran-
295   dom behavior of speech generation isolated from speech generative performance, inspired by Zero
296   Retrain Forgetting metric (Chundawat et al., 2023).
297
      Originally suggested Zero Retrain Forgetting metric utilizes a dumb teacher model initialized with
298
      random weights to generate outputs with random probability distribution. In the case of ZS-TTS
299
      unlearning, this is not directly applicable as we aim to randomize only on forget voices’ character-
300   istics, not the overall generated content. Thus, we modify the metric to measure randomness solely
301   on speaker identity by integrating usage of random speaker generation and a speaker verification
302   model.
303

304
305

306

      obtain this random probability distribution, speaker embeddings sθ(xsi ,yi ) and sθ(yi ) are extracted
307

308

309   of speaker embeddings is calculated as follows:

346   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
347
348
349   Dataset. We trained the original VoiceBox model on LibriHeavy(Kang et al., 2024), a speech corpus
350   consisting of 50,000 hours of data. LibriHeavy is derived from LibriLight(Kahn et al., 2020) and
351
      comprises English speech from 6,736 speakers, with accompanying transcriptions for each audio
      sample. For the forget set, we randomly selected 10 speakers from the LibriHeavy corpus, each
352
      having an average of 20 minutes of speech audio. For each speaker, 5 minutes of speech audio were
353
      randomly chosen for the evaluation set, with the remaining data used for the training set. To evaluate
354   zero-shot performance, we used unseen LibriSpeech test-clean set Panayotov et al. (2015). Please
355   refer to Appendix B for further detailed information.
356
357
      Baseline Methods. We applied four different approximate machine unlearning methods to the
      VoiceBox (Le et al., 2024) First, the Exact Unlearning method involves training a new model from
358
      scratch using only the DR . The Fine Tuning (FT) approach refines an existing pre-trained model
359
      through further training, utilizing only DR (Warnecke et al., 2021). The Negative Gradient (NG)
360   method adjusts the model parameters by reversing the gradient for the DF in (Thudi et al., 2022),
361   often referred to as Gradient Ascent (Fan et al., 2024). The selective Kullback-Libeler divergence
362   (KL) method applied in (Li et al., 2024; Chen & Yang, 2023) implements the pre-trained model
363   as a teacher and maximizes the KL divergence between predicted outputs when a forget speaker’s
364   sample is input, while minimizing for remain speakers.
365
      Model Configuration. As previously mentioned, we applied both baseline machine unlearning
366   methods and the proposed method to VoiceBox (Le et al., 2024), using the same configuration.
367   Please refer to Appendix B for more details on the training and inference settings for each baseline
368   method, the proposed method, the duration predictor, and the vocoder.
369
      Evaluation Metric. For quantitative evaluation, we used three metrics: Word Error Rate (WER),
370
      Speaker Similarity (SIM), and the proposed spk-ZRF method. WER was used to assess the accuracy
371
      of the generated content, utilizing a HuBERT-L model (Hsu et al., 2021) pre-trained on 60K hours of
372   LibriLight (Kahn et al., 2020) and fine-tuned on 960 hours of LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015).
373   To measure the similarity between the generated speech and the prompt speaker, we employed SIM.
374   As mentioned earlier, spk-ZRF was introduced to quantify the randomness in outputs for forget
375   speakers and the consistency for remain speakers. Both SIM and spk-ZRF were evaluated using the
376   WavLM-TDCNN speaker embedding model (Chen et al., 2022b). For qualitative assessment, we
377   used two additional metrics: Comparative mean opinion score (CMOS) for evaluating audio quality
      and Similarity MOS (SMOS) for comparing the similarity between prompt and generated audio.

387   inal model across both evaluation sets. These methods either completely exclude the forget set
388   during training or focus additional training on the remain set. This suggests that simply excluding
389   forget speakers from training is insufficient to protect voice style privacy, as the ZS-TTS model still
390   effectively replicates the speech style of unseen speakers.
391   For the NG method, training had to be limited to 9.5K steps to prevent instability, as the gradient for
392   the forget set became unbounded during extended training, causing the model to fail. Even with this
393   adjustment, the NG method performes poorly, showing high WER and low SIM scores on both sets,
394   likely due to the entanglement between speaker style and linguistic content in the VoiceBox training
395   process, which makes it challenging for this method to disentangle the two aspects effectively.
396   Among all methods evaluated, TGU consistently achieves the best results, aligning most closely
397   with our unlearning objectives. The SIM scores for the forget set with TGU fall within the range
398   of 0.169, which corresponds closely to the similarity scores observed between actual audio samples
399   from different speakers, demonstrating that TGU effectively generates voices distinct from the forget
400   speaker prompts. While SGU also exhibits some level of success in reducing similarity for the forget
401   set, it is significantly less effective than TGU, especially in maintaining performance on the remain
402   set. Notably, TGU maintaines an average SIM score of 0.631 for the remain set, showing only a
403
      2.8% decrease compared to the original model, indicating a high level of retention for the original
      speaker identity’s style. In contrast, SGU suffers a substantial drop of 21%, demonstrating that
404
      it struggles to preserve the model’s ability to replicate the prompt speaker’s voice. For detailed
405
      information on the ground truth SIM values, refer to Appendix C.
406
407   In terms of WER, both TGU and SGU achieve results comparable to the original model, indicating
408   that they do not compromise the correctness of speech generation. However, TGU outperforms SGU
409
      overall, proving to be the most effective unlearning method by balancing the dual goals of forgetting
      specific speaker identities while retaining the capability to generate high-quality speech for retain
410
      speakers. We also provide extensive experiments to measure model robustness in Appendix G.

511   5   L IMITATIONS
512
513   We applied machine unlearning to ZS-TTS, showing its effectiveness in restricting voice replication.
514   Despite the TGU unlearned model showing effective unlearning, performance drops exist. Overall,
515   TGU increases WER in both remain set and forget set. It can be inferred that introducing randomness
516
      compromises model’s abilities in generating correct and audible content. We also evaluated model’s
      performance across general tasks of ZS-TTS to evaluate how implementing randomness may affect
517
      overall performance in Appendix H. We believe this is due to removal of speaker identities and
518
      implementation of random behavior in model’s knowledge. Works that aim to preserve model’s
519   zero-shot capabilities and diversity should be pursued in future research of unlearning in ZS-TTS.
520
521   Moreover, as the number of forget speaker increases, the model’s overall performance declines sig-
522
      nificantly. Ideally, effective machine unlearning should be achievable in a zero-shot or few-shot
      manner, particularly in scenarios where access to the original training dataset is limited. However,
523
      both the baseline methods and TGU rely on partial of the original training data to maintain ZS-TTS
524
      performance while limiting the ability to replicate forget speakers.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we applied and analyzed machine unlearning techniques for the first time in the context
530   of Zero-Shot Text-to-Speech (ZS-TTS). Unlike in other generative AI domains, simply removing a
531
      speaker’s data during training is insufficient to protect the privacy of the speaker’s voice style in
      ZS-TTS. This highlights the need for techniques like machine unlearning to address this issue. Ad-
532
      ditionally, we proposed a novel framework called Teacher-Guided Unlearning (TGU). By leveraging
533
      a pre-trained model to guide the unlearning process, TGU effectively limits the model’s ability to
534   replicate the voices of forget speakers while maintaining the performance of the original ZS-TTS
535   system. Our experiments showed that TGU results in only a 2.6% decrease in speaker similarity
536   (SIM) for remain speakers, while maintaining competitive WER scores compared to the original
537   model. Furthermore, to assess the model’s ability to generate random voices for forget speakers
538   and prevent reverse engineering attacks that could reveal a speaker’s identity, we introduced a new
539   metric, spk-ZRF. This metric evaluates the degree to which the unlearned model generates speech
      independently of the forget speaker, thus enhancing privacy protection.

746   sampled 10 speakers whose audio durations fell within this range. For each selected speaker, ap-
747   proximately 300 seconds of audio was randomly chosen as the evaluation set, while the remaining
748   audio was designated for the unlearning training set. The selected speakers are: 789, 1166, 3912,
749   5983, 6821, 7199, 8866, 9437, 9794, and 10666.
750   To evaluate the performance of the existing ZS-TTS model, specifically its ability to replicate the
751   voices of unseen speakers, we used the LibriSpeech test-clean set ((Panayotov et al., 2015)). It is
752   important to note that there is no overlap between the speakers in the LibriSpeech test-clean set and
753   those in LibriHeavy (Kang et al. (2024)). Following the experimental setup outlined in the original
754   VoiceBox paper (Le et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023)), for both the forget and remain evaluation
755   sets, a different sample from the same speaker was randomly selected, and a 3-second segment was
      cropped to be used as a prompt.

      B.1   DATA PREPROCESSING
759
760   Speech is represented using an 80-dimensional log Mel spectrogram. The audio, sampled at 16 kHz,
761   has its Mel spectral features extracted at 100 Hz. A 1024-point short-time Fourier transform (STFT)
762   is applied with a 10 ms hop size and a 40 ms analysis window. A Hann windowing function is then
763   used, followed by an 80-dimensional Mel filter with a cutoff frequency of 8 kHz. We used the Mon-
764   treal Forced Aligner (MFA) (McAuliffe et al., 2017) to phonemize and force-align the transcripts,
765
      utilizing the MFA phone set, a modified version of the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA), while
      also applying word position prefixes.
766
767
768
      B.2   D URATION P REDICTOR A ND VOCODER
769
      We used the regression version of duration predictor proposed in Le et al. (2024). The duration
770   predictor has a similar model structure to the audio model, but with 8 Transformer layers, 8 attention
771   heads, and 512/2048 embedding/FFN dimensions. It is trained for 600K steps. The Adam optimizer
772   was employed with a peak learning rate of 1e-4, linearly warmed up over the first 5K steps and
773   decayed afterward. HiFi-GAN (Kong et al., 2020), trained on the LibriHeavy (Kang et al., 2024)
774   English speech dataset, is employed to convert the spectrogram into a time-domain waveform.
775
776   B.3   M ODEL C ONFIGURATIONS
777
778   The audio feature generator is based on a vanilla Transformer (Vaswani, 2017), enhanced with U-Net
779
      style residual connections, convolutional positional embeddings (Baevski et al., 2020), and AliBi
      positional encoding (Press et al., 2021). This model has 24 Transformer layers, 16 attention heads,
780
      and an embedding/feed-forward network (FFN) dimension of 1024/4096, with skip connections
781
      implemented in the U-Net style.
782
783
      B.4   P RETRAINING
784
785   Following Le et al. (2024), we trained the original Voice model for 500K steps. Each mini-batch
786   consisted of 75-second audio segments, and the Adam optimizer was employed with a peak learning
787   rate of 1e-4, linearly warmed up over the first 5K steps and decayed afterward. All training was
788   conducted using mixed precision with FP16.
789
790   B.5   T EACHER -G UIDED U NLEARNING
791
792   The Teacher-Guided Unlearning (TGU) model was trained for 145 K steps. Each mini-batch in-
793
      cluded 75-second audio segments. The Adam optimizer was employed with a peak learning rate of
      1e-4, which was linearly warmed up during the first 5 K steps and subsequently decayed throughout
794
      the remainder of the training. To facilitate the unlearning process, samples from the forget set xf
795
      were randomly selected with a 20% probability in each mini-batch.
796
797
      B.6   S AMPLE -G UIDED U NLEARNING
798
799   To apply SGU in the ZS-TTS system, we set up the training process such that when a forget sample
800   xf is provided, a random retain sample xr is selected as the target for training. To train VoiceBox,
801   both speech data and aligned text segments are required. However, as discussed in Section 3.3, it
802   is not naturally feasible to collect utterances from different speakers that share the same alignment.
803   To address this, the SGU training was set up as follows: Let y f and y r represent the corresponding
804   text segments for xf and xr , respectively. We generated a mask corresponding to the length of xr ,
805   training the model to predict xr based on this masked input. The text segments y f and y r were
806
      concatenated along the time axis and used as input, with the same process applied to the other input
      components, such as wf and wr .
807
808   During the training phase, the model was fine-tuned for 145K steps using the same configuration
809   as TGU. Additionally, forget samples xf and remain samples xr were selected and trained in a 2:8
      ratio.

      B.8    N EGATIVE G RADIENT
820
821   Implementation of Negative Gradient (NG) method follows that of (Thudi et al., 2022). On the
822   pre-trained VoiceBox model, we provide only the samples from the forget speaker set F . The loss
823   is inverted to counteract loss minimization previously occurred in the pre-trained model’s weights.
824   Given that approaches based on reversing the gradient often suffer from low model performance and
825   unstable training, we searched for learning rate with best evaluation score {1e-5, 1e-6, 1e-7, 1e-8}.
826   For evaluation, we use the checkpoint of 9.5K fine-tuned with Adam optimizer with a peak learning
827
      rate of 1e-8, linearly warmed up over first 5K steps and decayed after.

      B.9    S ELECTIVE K ULLBACK -L EIBLER D IVERGENCE
832
833   Numerous studies have adopted a loss function that focuses on utilizing a teacher-student framework
834   with selective Kullback-Leibler divergence loss (Li et al., 2024; Chen & Yang, 2023). We implement
835   this loss so the student model is fine-tuned to maximize KL-divergence between teacher and student
836   output when xf is given as input, and minimize when xr is given :
837
838
839              LKL = λDKL (θ(xr , y r )∥θ− (xr , y r )) − (1 − λ)DKL (θ(xf , y f )∥θ− (xf , y f ))   (13)
840
841
842   where λ is a hyper-parameter between 0 and 1 to balance the trade-off. Similar to NG, unbounded
843   reverted loss on KL-divergence is prone to low model performance. We searched for learning rate
844   with best evaluation score from {1e-5, 1e-6, 1e-7, 1e-8}, and λ from {0.5, 0.8}. For evaluation,
845   we use the checkpoint of 32.5K fine-tuned with Adam optimizer with a peak learning rate of 1e-8,
846   following warm up and decay of previous methods using λ = 0.5.

1101   H.1   T RANSIENT N OISE R EMOVAL
1102
1103   ZS-TTS can be applied in tasks where editing is required to remove undesired noise in speech
1104   datasets. To prevent having to go through repetitive and inefficient recording to obtain clean speech,
1105   ZS-TTS can generate clean audio for the noisy segment. We follow experimental settings of (Le
1106   et al., 2024) to analyze how TGU unlearned model performs on the task of transient noise removal.
1107   From LibriSpeech test-clean dataset samples of durations 4 to 10 seconds, we construct noise at a
1108   -10dB signal-to-noise ratio over half of each sample’s duration. Table 7 suggests that TGU provides
1109   comparable performances to that of the original model. While seemingly low, diminished model
1110   performances on transient noise removal is present relatively to the original model. We suggest that
1111   this is a trade-off from successful unlearning. While the model has unlearned to generate voice
1112   characteristics of the forget dataset, smaller knowledge-base and implemented randomness could
1113   have affected its reconstructing abilities.

1119
       Being able to generate diverse speech is also an important feature of ZS-TTS models as it ensures
       realistic and high-quality speech that resembles natural distributions. This is necessary in applica-
1120
       tions such as speech synthesis or generating training data for speech related tasks (e.g., Automatic
1121
       Speech Recognition). The diversity of generated speech samples is measured with Fréchet Speech
1122   Distance (FSD) as suggested in (Le et al., 2024). From generated speech samples, we extracted
1123   self-supervised features using 6th layer representation of wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020). The
1124   features were reduced to 128 dimensions with principle component analysis and used to calculate
1125   the similarity of distributions with real speech. High FSD indicates lower quality and minimal di-
1126   versity, while low FSD refers to high quality and more diversity. For this experiment, α is set to 0 to
1127   ensure more diversity. Ground truth FSD is obtained by partitioning the LibriSpeech test-other set
1128   into half while ensuring equal distribution of data per speaker across both subsets
1129   Experimental results in Table 8 show that FSD increases in TGU unlearned model. Because this task
1130   does not require input audio prompts, diverse speech sampling relies relatively heavier on datasets
1131   used to train the model. Implementing machine unlearning and thus inducing forgetting of specific
1132   speakers causes a trade-off in model’s diversity. Meanwhile, it is noticeable that TGU achieves
1133   a lower WER in this case. We can infer that TGU obtains robustness in relatively noisy dataset
       comparable to the Original model.
