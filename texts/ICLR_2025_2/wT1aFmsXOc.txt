009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Tabular data generation has attracted significant research interest in recent years,
012            with the tabular diffusion models greatly improving the quality of synthetic data.
013            However, while memorization—where models inadvertently replicate exact or
014            near-identical training data—has been thoroughly investigated in image and text
015            generation, its effects on tabular data remain largely unexplored. In this paper,
016            we conduct the first comprehensive investigation of memorization phenomena
017            in diffusion models for tabular data. Our empirical analysis reveals that mem-
018            orization appears in tabular diffusion models and increases with larger training
019
               epochs. We further examine the influence of factors such as dataset sizes, feature
               dimensions, and different diffusion models on memorization. Additionally, we
020
               provide a theoretical explanation for why memorization occurs in tabular diffusion
021
               models. To address this issue, we propose TabCutMix, a simple yet effective
022            data augmentation technique that exchanges randomly selected feature segments
023            between random training sample pairs. Experimental results across various datasets
024            and diffusion models demonstrate that TabCutMix effectively mitigates memo-
025            rization while maintaining high-quality data generation. Our code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TabCutMix-3F7B.

084   2   R ELATED W ORK
085
086
      Tabular Generative Models. Generative models for tabular data have gained attention due to
      their broad applicability. Early approaches like CTGAN and TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) leveraged
087
      Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020) and VAEs (Kingma, 2013)
088
      for handling imbalanced features. GOGGLE (Liu et al., 2023) advanced this by modeling feature
089   dependencies using graph neural networks. Inspired by NLP advancements, GReaT (Borisov et al.,
090   2023) transformed rows into natural language sequences to capture table-level distributions. More
091   recently, diffusion models, originally successful in image generation (Ho et al., 2020), have been
092   adapted for tabular data, as demonstrated by STaSy (Kim et al., 2023), TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al.,
093   2023), CoDi (Lee et al., 2023), TabSyn (Zhang et al., 2023a), and balanced tabular diffusion (Yang
094   et al., 2024b).
095
096   Memorization in Generative Models. Memorization has been widely studied in image and
097   language domains (van den Burg & Williams, 2021; Gu et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). In image
098   generation, reseasrchers (Somepalli et al., 2023a; Carlini et al., 2021) found that diffusion models,
099
      like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), memorize portions of
      their training data at varying levels. Concept ablation (Kumari et al., 2023) is proposed to mitigate
100
      memorization via fine-tuning of pre-trained models to minimize output disparity. AMG (Chen
101
      et al., 2024) uses real-time similarity metrics to selectively apply guidance to likely duplicates. For
102   text generation, text conditioning amplifies memorization risks, especially in large-scale language
103   models (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b; Huang et al., 2024). Goldfish loss (Hans et al., 2024) randomly
104   drops a subset of tokens from the training loss computation to prevent the model from memorizing.
105   Memorization prediction (Biderman et al., 2024), i.e., predicting which sequences will be memorized
106   before full-scale training, is investigated by analyzing the memorization patterns of lower-compute
107   trial runs for early intervention. Although these patterns are evident in image and text generation, the
      impact of memorization on tabular data remains underexplored.

109
110   Despite the development of numerous high-performing diffusion models for tabular data generation,
111   it remains unclear whether these models are susceptible to memorization. In this section, we
112   introduce a criterion for detecting and quantifying the intensity of memorization in tabular data.
113   Using this criterion, we explore memorization behaviors across various diffusion models under
114   different dataset sizes and feature dimensions. We choose two state-of-the-art (SOTA) generative
115   models: TabSyn (Zhang et al., 2023a) and TabDDPM (Kotelnikov et al., 2023) for our preliminary
116   memorization analysis. Furthermore four real-world tabular datasets—Adult, Default, Shoppers, and
117   Magic—each containing both numerical and categorical features are included. The details of the
118
      datasets can be found in Section 5. Additionally, we provide a theoretical analysis to explain the
      mechanisms behind memorization in tabular diffusion models.

236       3.5                T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
237
         In the previous section, we empirically investigate the memorization phenomenon in existing tabular
238
         diffusion models. However, the underlying cause of memorization in tabular diffusion models
239      remains unclear. To bridge the gap, we provide a theoretical analysis of why memorization occurs in
240      TabSyn (Zhang et al., 2023a), one of the SOTA tabular generative models.
241
242

243
         the latent space. The final synthetic data is generated via the decoder of VAE. For simplicity, we only
244
         consider latent diffusion in the analysis. Specifically, the following forward and backward stochastic
245      differential equations are adopted in the latent diffusion:

      4    M ETHODOLOGY
288
      Building on the memorization study presented in Section 3, we find that the issue of memorization
289
      in existing tabular diffusion models is significant but often overlooked in the literature. To address
290
      this problem and enhance the diversity of generated data, we propose a novel data augmentation
291   strategy, TabCutMix, specifically designed for tabular data generation. This approach is inspired by
292   the CutMix (Yun et al., 2019) data augmentation technique used in the image domain.
293
294
      We provide the details of TabCutMix data augmentation procedure. TabCutMix works by generating
      a new training sample (x̃, ỹ) to mitigate memorization, which is formed by combining two samples
295
      (xA , yA ) and (xB , yB ) that belong to the same class. In tabular data, ”same class” refers to instances
296
      that share the same categorical target label, ensuring that the generated sample remains consistent
297   with its original class and prevents label mismatches.
298
299   The feature swap within the same class increases data diversity and reduces the likelihood of
300
      memorization while preserving the integrity of the label. The newly generated sample is then used to
      train the diffusion model with its original loss function. The mix operation is defined as follows
301
302                                   x̃ = M ⊙ xA + (1 − M ) ⊙ xB ,                                          (6)
                          M
303   where M ∈ {0, 1} denotes a binary mask matrix indicating which features to swap between the
304   two samples, 1 is a mask filled with ones, and ⊙ represents element-wise multiplication. Similar
305   to existing data augmentation strategies, the portion of exchanged features λ is sampled from the
306   uniform distribution U(0, 1) In binary mask sampling, each element is independently sampled with
307   a value 1 based on Bernoulli distribution Bern(λ), i.e., the removal or fill-in of each feature is
308   independent in TabCutMix data augmentation. In each training iteration, we first sample the class
309
      index c ∈ {1, 2, · · · , C} using the class prior distribution and then randomly select two samples from
      that class. The pseudo code of our proposed algorithm is in Appendix C.
310
311   Moreover, we note that splitting highly correlated features during the augmentation process may
312   disrupt the inherent relationships in the original dataset, potentially leading to increased Out-of-
313   Distribution (OOD) Yang et al. (2024a) issues. To address this concern, we propose TabCutMixPlus,
314   a safer augmentation strategy designed to preserve the structural integrity of the data. In Tab-
315
      CutMixPlus, we first compute feature correlations to identify clusters of highly correlated features.
      Specifically, for numerical features, we use the Pearson correlation coefficient, for categorical features,
316
      we employ Cramér’s V Cramér (1999), and for numerical-categorical pairs, we calculate the squared
317
      ETA coefficient Richardson (2011). These measures allow us to group highly correlated features into
318   clusters, treating each cluster as an atomic unit during the swap operation. By ensuring that features
319   within a cluster are exchanged together, TabCutMixPlus avoids disrupting relationships among highly
320   correlated features, thereby mitigating the risk of introducing OOD issues. We evaluate the effective-
321   ness of TabCutMixPlus in Appendix 4, where we perform OOD detection experiments comparing
322   TabCutMix and TabCutMixPlus. The results demonstrate that TabCutMixPlus significantly reduces
323   OOD samples while maintaining the advantages of data augmentation, thereby providing a robust
      and reliable solution for training models on tabular data.

340   evaluate both generation quality and memorization performance for these models.
341
342
      Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate the performance of synthetic data generation from two perspec-
343
      tives: memorization and synthetic data quality. For memorization evaluation, we generate the same
344   number of synthetic samples as the training dataset and use Eq. (1) to calculate the distance between
345   the generated and real samples. The generated sample is considered memorized if its closest neighbor
346   in the training data is less than one-third of the distance to its second closest neighbor (Yoon et al.,
347   2023; Gu et al., 2023). For synthetic data quality evaluation, we consider 1) low-order statistics (i.e.,
348   column-wise density and pair-wise column correlation) measured by shape score3 and trend score
      4
349     ; 2) high-order metrics α-precision and β-recall scores measuring the overall fidelity and diversity
350   of synthetic data; 3) downstream tasks performance machine learning efficiency (MLE)5 , i.e., the
351   testing performance (e.g., AUC) on real data when trained only on synthetically generated tabular
352
      datasets. The reported results are averaged over 5 independent experimental runs. More details on
      evaluation metrics can be found in Appendic D.3.

361   Obs.1: TabCutMix consistently reduces the memorization ratio across all models and datasets.
362   The average reductions for the four datasets across different generative models range from 12.51%
363   to 15.62%. The average reductions for the three different generative models across four datasets

366   in synthetic data.
367   Obs.2: For data quality metrics, TabCutMix can preserve most of the original performance. For
368   example, MLE scores remain stable or show slight improvements with the application of TabCutMix,
369   suggesting that the reduction in memorization does not compromise model performance. The other
370   data quality metrics, such as α-Precision and shape Score, remain consistent across models with and

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this study, we first investigate memorization phenomena in diffusion models for tabular data using
      quantitative metrics. Our findings reveal the prevalent memorization behaviors in existing tabular
532
      diffusion models, with the memorization ratio increasing as training epochs grow. We further study
533
      the effects of the diffusion model instantiation, dataset size, and feature dimensions through the lens
534   of memorization ratio and observe the heterogeneous trend dependent on the dataset. The theoretical
535   analysis provides new insights into why memorization occurs within the SOTA model TabSyn. To
536   mitigate the memorization issue, we propose TabCutMix, a simple yet effective data augmentation
537   method. Our experiments demonstrate that TabCutMix significantly mitigates the memorization for
538   various diffusion models and preserves the data generation quality. We believe that our paper provides
539   a valuable contribution by not only drawing attention to the often-overlooked issue of memorization
      in tabular data generation but also offering an effective solution with TabCutMix.

1037   D.3.3       S AMPLE - LEVEL Q UALITY M ETRICS : α-P RECISION AND β-R ECALL
1038
1039   To rigorously evaluate the quality of synthetic data, we employ two complementary metrics proposed
1040   in work (Alaa et al., 2022): α-Precision and β-Recall. These metrics offer a refined approach to
1041
       assessing the fidelity and diversity of synthetic data samples by focusing on their relationship with
       the real data distribution.
1042
1043   • α-Precision. The α-Precision metric quantifies the fidelity of synthetic data by measuring the
1044     probability that a generated sample lies within the α-support of the real data distribution, denoted
1045     as Srα . The α-support includes the most representative regions of the real data, containing the
1046     highest probability mass. Therefore, a high α-Precision score ensures that the synthetic samples
1047     are realistic, falling within these high-density areas of the real data. This metric is particularly
1048     important because it distinguishes between synthetic samples that resemble real data in a typical
1049     way and those that might still be valid but are more akin to outliers. By focusing on the high-density
1050     areas, α-Precision ensures that the generated data looks both realistic and “typical” compared to
1051
         real-world data. Mathematically, this is expressed as:
1052                                           Pα = P(X̃g ∈ Srα ),   α ∈ [0, 1].                            (29)
1053
1054   • β-Recall. Conversely, β-Recall evaluates the coverage of synthetic data. It measures whether the
1055     synthetic data captures the entire real data distribution, particularly focusing on the β-support of
1056     the generative model, denoted as Sgβ . The β-support includes all regions of the real distribution,
1057     not just the frequent or typical areas. A high β-Recall score indicates that the synthetic data can
1058
         represent even the rare or low-density parts of the real distribution. This metric is crucial because it
         ensures that the synthetic data does not merely replicate the most common patterns but also spans
1059
         the broader diversity of the real data, capturing rare or edge cases. Mathematically, it is defined as:
1060
1061                                           Rβ = P(X̃r ∈ Sgβ ),   β ∈ [0, 1].                            (30)
1062
1063   Importance of α-Precision and β-Recall. The combination of α-Precision and β-Recall allows
1064   for a holistic assessment of synthetic data. While α-Precision ensures that the synthetic data aligns
1065   well with the most typical regions of the real data distribution (fidelity), β-Recall ensures that the
1066   synthetic data covers the full diversity of the real data (coverage). Together, these metrics provide
1067   insight into both the accuracy and diversity of the synthetic data. By sweeping through values of
1068
       α and β, one can gain a more dynamic understanding of how synthetic data aligns with different
       aspects of the real data distribution, offering a comprehensive evaluation of its quality.
1069
1070   In summary, α-Precision ensures the generated data looks realistic and falls within typical regions
1071   of the real distribution, while β-Recall ensures that the generated data covers the entire distribution,
1072   including rare cases. The complementary nature of these two metrics makes them essential for
1073   evaluating the fidelity and diversity of synthetic data.

1112
1113   The Classifier Two-Sample Test (C2ST) Zhang et al. (2023a) is used to evaluate how well synthetic
1114   data replicates the distribution of real data. This approach involves training a binary classifier to
1115
       distinguish between real and synthetic samples. If the synthetic data closely matches the distribution

1116

1117
       the classifier will achieve higher accuracy, indicating poor alignment. The C2ST score provides a
1118   quantitative measure of this alignment, offering insights into the quality of the synthetic data. A low
1119   C2ST score suggests that the synthetic data effectively captures the real data distribution, making it
1120   difficult for the classifier to distinguish between real and synthetic samples.

1282   F           L IMITATION D ISCUSSION
1283
1284   While this work makes significant contributions to augmenting tabular data with TabCutMix and its
1285   improved version, TabCutMixPlus, several limitations remain that warrant further exploration:
1286
1287                     • Assumptions About Feature Independence. TabCutMix assumes that features can be
1288                       swapped between samples independently without disrupting the data manifold. However,
1289                       this assumption does not hold for datasets with strongly correlated features or complex
1290                       interdependencies. For instance, in the Cardio dataset, the high feature correlation led to
1291
                           a relatively high OOD ratio (4.83%), highlighting a limitation of the current method in
                           preserving feature relationships during augmentation.
1292
1293                     • Challenges in Complex Domains. TabCutMix struggles in sensitive domains, such as health-
1294                       care or finance, where feature interactions often carry critical domain-specific meanings.
1295                       Arbitrary feature exchanges may result in implausible or nonsensical combinations, reducing
                           the utility of augmented data for downstream tasks. For example, relationships between

                features like age and medical diagnosis may be violated, leading to unrealistic augmented
1297            samples.
1298
              • Sensitivity to Outliers. The proposed mixed-distance metric, like many distance-based mea-
1299
                sures, is sensitive to outliers, particularly in numerical features, which can disproportionately
1300
                affect distance calculations and distort relationships between samples. While normalization
1301            mitigates feature dominance, it does not fully address the impact of extreme values. Future
1302            work could explore robust distance metrics, such as adaptive scaling or trimming, to reduce
1303            the influence of outliers and improve the reliability of distance-based approaches in tabular
1304            data modeling.
1305          • Lack of General Insights into Data-Centric Factors. While this work identifies the influence
1306            of data-centric factors, such as dataset complexity and feature interactions, on the effective-
1307            ness of TabCutMix, it does not provide a comprehensive framework for understanding or
1308            addressing these factors. A deeper investigation into these data-centric elements is necessary
1309            to fully realize the potential of TabCutMix and similar augmentation techniques.

       G     F UTURE W ORK
1312
1313
       While this study makes significant strides in addressing the issue of memorization in tabular data
1314   generation, several directions remain open for future exploration:
1315
1316   1. Theoretical Analysis of Factor Heterogeneity: A deeper theoretical investigation is valuable into
1317      how different factors, such as dataset size and feature dimensionality, influence memorization in
1318      heterogeneous ways. Specifically, understanding the nonlinear relationships between these factors
1319      and their combined effect on model memorization could provide further insights.
1320   2. Exploring Alternative Memorization Mitigation Techniques: Beyond data augmentation, future
1321      work could explore different strategies to mitigate memorization. These could include more
1322      advanced generative model training techniques, such as regularization methods or differential
1323
          privacy mechanisms that limit model overfitting to specific data points. Additionally, techniques
          like model pruning, weight clipping, or dropout variations could be explored for their potential
1324
          to reduce memorization during model training. The model architecture design by leveraging
1325
          architectures like variational autoencoders (VAEs), normalizing flows, or GAN variations with
1326      modified loss functions can be developed to mitigate memorization.
1327
       3. Evaluation Metrics for Memorization: Developing more comprehensive and practical evaluation
1328      metrics specifically tailored to detect memorization in tabular data models remains a key area
1329      for future work. These metrics could better assess the trade-off between generating high-quality
1330      synthetic data and avoiding overfitting to the training data.
1331   4. Real-World Applications and Use Cases: Applying these methods to a broader range of real-
1332      world use cases could provide valuable feedback and improvements. Specific industries such as
1333      healthcare, finance, and marketing, where tabular data is prevalent, would be ideal candidates for
1334      testing how well these approaches generalize and perform in production environments.
1335   5. Data-Centric Investigation for Tabular Diffusion Models: This study reveals that memorization
1336      in tabular diffusion models may be predominantly driven by dataset-specific factors such as feature
1337      complexity, sparsity, and redundancy, rather than model-specific architecture. A deeper exploration
1338      into these data-centric influences could provide valuable insights into the interplay between dataset
1339      properties and memorization behavior. Future work could focus on developing strategies to
1340
          quantify the impact of dataset characteristics on generative performance and proposing adaptive
          preprocessing, augmentation, or sampling methods tailored to diverse datasets. Such investigations
1341
          would enhance the robustness and applicability of diffusion models across a wide range of tabular
1342
          data scenarios.
