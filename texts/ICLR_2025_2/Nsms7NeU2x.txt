009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The leakage of benchmark data into the training data has emerged as a significant
012            challenge for evaluating the capabilities of large language models (LLMs). In this
013            work, we use experimental evidence and theoretical estimates to challenge the
014            common assumption that small-scale contamination renders benchmark evaluations
015            invalid. First, we experimentally quantify the magnitude of benchmark overfitting
016            based on scaling along three dimensions: The number of model parameters (up
017            to 1.6B), the number of times an example is seen (up to 144), and the number of
018
               training tokens (up to 40B). We find that if model and data follow the Chinchilla
               scaling laws, minor contamination indeed leads to overfitting. At the same time,
019
               even 144 times of contamination can be forgotten if the training data is scaled
020
               beyond five times Chinchilla, a regime characteristic of many modern LLMs. We
021            then derive a simple theory of example forgetting via cumulative weight decay. It
022            allows us to bound the number of gradient steps required to forget past data for any
023            training run where we know the hyperparameters of AdamW. This indicates that
024            many LLMs, including Llama 3, have forgotten the data seen at the beginning of
025            training. Experimentally, we demonstrate that forgetting occurs faster than what is
026            predicted by our bounds. Taken together, our results suggest that moderate amounts
027            of contamination can be forgotten at the end of realistically scaled training runs.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   A core principle of machine learning is that a model should not be trained on the test set used for
033
      evaluation (Donoho, 2017). For foundation models trained on Internet-scale data, there are increasing
      concerns that this principle is violated due to the leakage of benchmark evaluation data into the
034
      training data (Xu et al., 2024; Oren et al., 2024). Indeed, many LLM developers have found overlap
035
      between their training data and the benchmark questions used for evaluation (Brown et al., 2020;
036   Dubey et al., 2024). What is more, research on memorization (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021) shows that
037   text sequences from the training data are sometimes encoded within the model, including machine
038   learning datasets (Grynbaum & Mac, 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Nasr et al., 2023; Bordt et al., 2024).
039
      While the fact that data contamination can lead to invalid performance evaluations is now well-
040
      established (Magar & Schwartz, 2022; Li & Flanigan, 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024),
041
      little is known about the precise conditions under which this is the case. The main reason for this is
042   that the training data is usually unknown, and contamination identified via clever research designs
043   that work around this restriction (Golchin & Surdeanu, 2023; Oren et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024).
044   Because modern foundation models are sometimes trained for over a million gradient steps (Dubey
045   et al., 2024), it is unclear whether a single update on contaminated data at some point during training
046   necessarily impacts downstream evaluations. And indeed, there is quite some evidence that language
047   models need to see samples repeatedly to have any impact on the final model. For example, many
048   papers on memorization have found that it occurs only when a sample is frequently repeated in the
049   training data (Carlini et al., 2022; Biderman et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024b). The same is true for
050
      research on knowledge acquisition, where a fact needs to be paraphrased many times before it is
      finally remembered by the model (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023; Cao et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2024).
051
052   In this work, we study the impact of data contamination in a controlled setting. This means we train
053   language models from scratch on datasets where we explicitly insert contaminated examples (Jiang
      et al., 2024). We begin by quantifying how the overall magnitude of benchmark overfitting (or the


                                                        1

054
      cross-entropy loss of an observed sample) changes as we scale along three critical dimensions: (1)
055   the number of model parameters, (2) the number of training tokens, and (3) the number of repetitions
056   of an example in the training data (Section 4.1). Holding the other two dimensions fixed, we find that
057   the effect of scaling is monotone in each dimension. First, similar to many other works, we find that
058   the tendency of a model to overfit increases in the number of parameters (Goodfellow et al., 2016;
059   Zhang et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022). Second, and this is also expected, we find a clear scaling in
060   the number of repetitions, where more frequently repeated observations exhibit stronger overfitting
061   (Carlini et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024b). More surprisingly, we find that the effect of contamination
062   can vanish as we increase the number of training tokens, up to the point where 12 repetitions of an
063
      entire dataset in the training data have no impact on the downstream evaluation on that same dataset.
064   Our investigation reveals that the natural forgetting dynamics of gradient descent (Tirumala et al.,
065   2022; Jagielski et al., 2023) is the reason why increasing the number of tokens alleviates the impact
066   of contamination. Concretely, we show that training on five times Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022)
067   of clean data can cause a model to forget even 144 times repeated training examples (Section 4.2).
068   Forgetting the bulk of the impact of a training example can occur rapidly, a point that we demonstrate
069
      using OLMo-1B (Groeneveld et al., 2024) (Section 4.3). What is the reason for this rapid forgetting?
      We show that exposure to novel data is important. Interestingly, models tend to exhibit the strongest
070
      overfitting on examples seen repeatedly throughout training, even compared to those seen during the
071
      end (Section 4.2).
072
073   Because running pre-training experiments is expensive, we also ask to what degree forgetting can be
074   explained by the training dynamics of gradient descent. We show that the weight decay parameter
075
      and learning rate schedule of the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) play a key part in
      forgetting past training examples (Section 5). Concretely, we derive a simple theory of forgetting
076
      via cumulative weight decay (Section 5.1) and show that it provides an upper bound on empirical
077
      forgetting, which usually occurs faster (Section 5.2). The key point is that this approach allows us to
078   gauge the degree of forgetting present in any training run for which the optimization hyperparameters
079   are known. It even allows us to approximate how the final model parameters balance the gradient
080   updates from different stages of training. Overall, our analysis indicates that many LLMs, including
081   OLMo-7B and Llama 3 405B (Dubey et al., 2024), have forgotten the data seen at the beginning of
082   their training run.
083
      Taken together, our main contribution is to show that the impact of individual examples in the
084   training data depends on the precise characteristics of the setting. There are settings where the
085   effect can be significant; Chinchilla training is an important example (Section 4.1). However, there
086   are equally realistic settings where individual examples dont’t matter - including quite likely the
087   data-intensive training runs of many recent LLMs (Gemma Team, 2024).

090   2    R ELATED W ORK
091
092
      Data Contamination. The GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020) uses an n-gram-based approach to
093
      differentiate between “clean” and “dirty” benchmark questions. This approach has since been used in
094
      many LLM reports (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), including Llama 3 (Dubey et al.,
095   2024), where it is estimated that there might be a performance gain of up to 8 and 14 percentage
096   points on PiQA and HellaSwag, respectively. The GPT-4 technical report (Achiam et al., 2023)
097   remarkably concluded that “contamination overall has very little effect on the reported results”. This
098   has since given rise to a literature that aims to detect (Oren et al., 2024), mitigate (Li et al., 2024),
099   and estimate the effect of (Yang et al., 2023; Bordt et al., 2024) data contamination under various
100   assumptions, but crucially without access to the training data. This literature often challenges the
101   conclusion that contamination overall has little effect in GPT-4 (Xu et al., 2024).
102   Forgetting. In machine learning, the term forgetting is frequently associated with “catastrophic”
103   forgetting, where learning new tasks hurt the performance at previously solved tasks (Lopez-Paz &
104   Ranzato, 2017). In the context of LLMs, catastrophic forgetting can occur during fine-tuning (Luo
105   et al., 2023) or continual learning (Huang et al., 2024a). In contrast, this paper studies forgetting as
106   a potential “natural” phenomenon of learning (Toneva et al., 2019). Tirumala et al. (2022) study
107   forgetting in language modeling and find, similar to Toneva et al. (2019), that forgetting can be
      exponentially slow. In contrast, Jagielski et al. (2023) find that models empirically do forget examples


                                                         2

108
      over time. In concurrent work, Pagliardini et al. (2024) propose to add a second momentum term to
109   the AdamW optimizer, and show that this slows down the forgetting of past gradients.
110
111   Data Attribution. Data attribution methods (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ilyas et al., 2022; Park et al.,
112
      2023) aim to identify data points responsible for specific model behaviors. We ask how much a
      model’s benchmark performance is influenced by seeing the example during training, which broadly
113
      falls within this field (Grosse et al., 2023; Choe et al., 2024). Importantly, we directly measure
114
      the influence of contaminated examples through retraining, avoiding the approximation errors that
115   can occur when using data attribution methods (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ghorbani & Zou, 2019) for
116   large-scale models (Basu et al., 2021; Bae et al., 2022)

119   3     BACKGROUND AND M ETHODS
120
121   This Section gives additional details on the research questions and lays out our experimental setup.
122
123         Research question
124
125         How does the presence of a text in the training data influence the final model’s performance
126         on that same text?
127
128   While it is well known that an individual data point can be influential if the training data and model
129   are small (Koh & Liang, 2017), we are concerned with the large-data regime where the influence
130   of any individual example may vanish (Basu et al., 2021; Bae et al., 2022). To further clarify this
131   setup, Section 3.1 gives a brief overview of recent developments in scaling the training data of LLMs.
132   Section 3.2 details the used benchmarks and explains how we contaminate the training data. Section
133   3.3 discusses the problem of near-duplicate benchmark questions.
134   Models and Training Data. We train language models of up to 1.6B parameters using the architecture
135   and hyperparameters from the GPT-3 paper (Brown et al., 2020, Table 2.1). For this, we adopt the
136   llm.c codebase. The training data is the 100BT split of the FineWeb-Edu dataset (Lozhkov et al.,
137   2024). We also train OLMo-1B (Groeneveld et al., 2024) using the corresponding code and data
138   (Soldaini et al., 2024).
139
140   3.1    W E CONSIDER THE REGIME OF N - TIMES C HINCHILLA
141
142   According to the Chinchilla scaling law, for every doubling of model size, the number of training
143   tokens should also be doubled. The Chinchilla model itself has 70 billion (B) parameters and was
144   trained on 1.4 trillion (T) tokens; suggesting that the number of training tokens should be roughly
145   20x the number of model parameters (Hoffmann et al., 2022, Table 3). While the Chinchilla paper
146
      was highly influential, modern language models are trained on significantly more tokens (Sardana &
      Frankle, 2024). For example, the OLMo-7B model was trained on 2.46T tokens, 17.5x the amount
147
      suggested by Chinchilla (Groeneveld et al., 2024). Similarly, the Llama 3 70B model was reportedly
148
      trained on 15T tokens, at over 10x Chinchilla (Dubey et al., 2024; Meta AI, 2024). The same holds
149   for almost all recent LLMs at the 7B parameter scale (Gemma Team, 2024). In this paper, we count
150   the number of tokens a model is trained on as a multiple of its Chinchilla tokens.
151

161   PiQA: 19.7%, MMLU: 21.82%, BoolQ: 6.48%, ARC-Easy: 5.92%, and WinoGrande: 18.16%. A

181   3.3   W E FILTER N EAR -D UPLICATE B ENCHMARK Q UESTIONS
182
183   Our method requires that there are no side effects from contaminating the training data with one
184   question on the evaluation of another question. However, upon closer inspection, it turns out that all
185
      the commonly used benchmarks from the literature contain questions that are either near-duplicates
      or where the context of one question contains the answer to another question (for example, because
186
      the same text document was used to create multiple questions). This is illustrated in Figure 1, which
187
      depicts two near-duplicate questions on HellaSwag and questions from ARC-Easy and MMLU that
188   are cross-benchmark duplicates. To address this problem, we perform extensive filtering, removing
189   duplicate questions where the length-normalized Levenshtein distance falls below a certain threshold
190   (Levenshtein, 1966; Navarro, 2001). This is documented in Supplement A.3, where we also describe
191   an experiment to verify that our method is not invalidated by near-duplicate questions.

312   forgetting which have found that forgetting approaches a stable baseline Tirumala et al. (2022, Figure

314   observe that many previous works on forgetting have not trained on a continuous stream of data.
315   Instead, they have trained on the same training set for multiple epochs. Consequently, we modify our
316   forgetting experiment to repeatedly train on the same 100M tokens after the second epoch. The result
317   of this experiment is depicted in Figure 3d and should be compared to Figure 3a. Interestingly, this
318   simple modification causes the effect of forgetting to stabilize at a level strictly larger than zero. We
319   conclude that exposure to novel data is important for forgetting, an observation similar to Jagielski

469   give more weight to early versus late gradients.
470   While our analysis in this Section considers the mechanistic effect of individually decaying gradient
471   updates, it does not model any interactions between different gradient updates. For example, if the
472   weight updates at a later time step t2 were aligned with past updates at t1 , then the model might not
473   forget the information even if the effect of past updates vanishes from the sum. However, such complex
474   interactions are avoided if the model updates from contaminated samples are orthogonal. Formalizing
475   this observation leads to a more rigorous version of Proposition 1, presented in Supplement A.5. The
476   argument is that under suitable gradient orthogonality conditions, the decay of past gradients can
477
      guarantee forgetting.

499   6    D ISCUSSION
500
501   This work presents different experiments on data contamination and forgetting. We have seen that
502   the impact of contamination can vanish as the size of the training data increases – an aspect that has
503   largely been overlooked in the literature (Yang et al., 2023; Oren et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). We
504   have also shown that the hyperparameters of AdamW play an important part in forgetting – an insight
505   that might inform the parametrization of future training runs. Of course, it would be interesting to
506   study contamination and forgetting on a larger scale. Nevertheless, we have shown that the effects of
507   contamination can vanish even though the number of steps we could train the models for was limited.
508   We have studied data contamination with a focus on the leakage of benchmark questions into the
509   training data. This means that our work might be more informative about the topic than other
510   works that study contamination in different contexts. At the same time, one has to be careful when
511   extrapolating our results, especially to a privacy setup (Carlini et al., 2019; Jagielski et al., 2023). This
512   is because empirical forgetting might behave differently for random strings or otherwise uniquely
513   identifiable information (Carlini et al., 2021).
514   The “contamination problem” as studied in this paper has interesting connections to many areas
515   of machine learning, including privacy (Graves et al., 2021; Jagielski et al., 2023), data attribution
516   (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024), and generalization (Bousquet & Elisseeff, 2002; Hardt et al., 2016; Mania
517   et al., 2019). The connection to data attribution is especially relevant in light of our results. This is
518   because we demonstrate cases where the presence or absence of a datapoint in the training data is
519   irrelevant for the model behavior on that same datapoint, meaning it does not make sense to attribute
520
      model behavior to individual datapoints in this regime.

      A.1    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION OF DATA C ONTAMINATION A SSUMPTIONS AND S ETTING
813
814   Here, we discuss our data contamination approach in a bit more detail.
815
      In this paper, we consider only exact contamination. This means we contaminate the training data
816
      exactly with the text the model is later evaluated on. In the literature, it has been shown that non-exact
817   contamination (re-worded questions, translation into a different language) can affect benchmark
818   performance, too. For example, Yang et al. (2023) have shown that a 13B parameter Llama 2 Model
819   (Touvron et al., 2023) can achieve an accuracy increase of over 20 percentage points after training on
820   re-phrased benchmark questions. We decided against considering non-exact contamination for this
821   paper because the models we train from scratch are much smaller than those for which non-exact
822   contamination results have been shown. This means these models are less capable of making sense of
823   related information, potentially leading us to underestimate the effect of non-exact contamination for
824   realistic training runs.
825   In addition, we consider contamination with individual benchmark questions, inserted into the
826   training data at random positions. We consider this setup because we are interested in contamination
827   from the perspective of leakage, where individual benchmark questions may enter the training data
828   via different documents (for example, as quotes in Wikipedia articles, a case described in Brown
829   et al. (2020)). This contrasts with the setup where a dataset is present in the training data as a long
830   contiguous string, which we conjecture might have a similar impact but be easier detectable (Oren
831
      et al., 2024). The fact that we contaminate with benchmark questions also sets us apart from related
      works that study data contamination and memorization for random strings and uniquely identified
832
      objects (Carlini et al., 2019; 2021). It is worth highlighting that the results between these two setups
833
      might differ, especially considering the time it takes to forget an example.
834
835   We only consider pre-training.
