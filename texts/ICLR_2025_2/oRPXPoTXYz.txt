009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks heavily rely on the back-propagation algorithm for optimiza-
012            tion. Nevertheless, the global sequential transmission of gradients in the backward
013            pass inhibits its scalability. The Direct Feedback Alignment algorithm has been
014            proposed as a promising approach for parallel learning of deep neural networks,
015            relying on fixed random feedback weights to project the error on every layer in
016
               a parallel manner. However, it notoriously fails to train networks that are really
               deep and that include compulsory layers like convolutions and transformers. In this
017
               paper, we show that alternatives to back-propagation may greatly benefit from local
018
               and forward approximation of the gradient to better cope with the inherent and
019            constrained structure of such layers. This directional approximation allows us to de-
020            sign a novel algorithm that updates the feedback weights called GrAPE (GRadient
021            Aligned Projected Error). A first set of experiments are carried out on image classi-
022            fication tasks with feedforward and convolutional architectures. The results show
023            important improvement in performance over other backpropagation-free algorithms,
024            narrowing the gap with backpropagation. More importantly, the method scales
025            to modern and deep architectures like AlexNet, VGG-16 and Transformer-based
026            language models where the performance gains are even more notable.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   After many decades, the back-propagation algorithm (Rumelhart et al., 1986) is still a cornerstone in
031   training deep neural networks. Some important issues with this algorithm arose with the increase in
032   depth. For most of them, the proposed solutions have induced transformations in the architectures,
033   without modifying the algorithm itself. For instance, residual connections (He et al., 2016) along with
034   Batch and Layer Norm (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015; Ba et al., 2016) changed the archictecture design to
035   stabilize the training. However, with the exponential growth of architectures, the computational cost
036   of BP raises questions about its efficiency and the opportunity of exploring other approaches.
037   The memory footprint and the execution time clearly represent limitations when training modern
038   architectures, and the BP algorithm significantly contributes to the high energy consumption, when
039   training large or huge models. Reducing this burden is therefore an important challenge, as is using
040   specialized hardware with limited resources. This paves the way to exploring alternative algorithms
041   that better take into account environmental consequences and practical hardware considerations.
042   The study of the learning phase for biological neural networks emphasizes the differences with the BP
043   algorithm (Grossberg, 1987). First, the exact weight symmetry between the forward and backward
044   passes does not align with biological observations. Furthermore, the global transmission of errors
045   and the sequentiality of updates also make it highly implausible in reality (Lillicrap et al., 2020).
046   Although this is no critical issue for machine learning (ML), this mismatch may inspire other kinds
047   of learning algorithms by investigating new learning dynamics (Amato et al., 2019; Baydin et al.,
048   2022; Hinton, 2022; Lillicrap et al., 2020; Mostafa et al., 2018; Meulemans et al., 2020).
049   The BP essentially consists of two phases, the forward pass that computes the output and the
050   backward pass which propagates the error back through the network (also called feedback) to update
051   the parameters. Many recent approaches focus only on changing the the feedback phase. The goal is to
052   simplify the weight update process, namely how information about errors or targets is communicated
053   to each layer. Notably, the Feedback Alignment (FA) algorithm (Lillicrap et al., 2016) showed
      that random feedback paths could be used to train deep networks, without the need for symmetric

      weights. Nøkland (2016) even showed that this idea could be used to avoid sequentiality, leading to a
055   parallelized training of neural networks with the Direct Feedback Alignment (DFA) algorithm. These
056   algorithms, though simple, have shown promising results with simple feed-forward architectures,
057   but fail with more structured layers and therefore cannot be applied to deep convolutional networks
058   (Bartunov et al., 2018; Launay et al., 2019; Moskovitz et al., 2018; Crafton et al., 2019). Some
059   refinements have recently tried to reduce the gap of performance with the BP (Akrout et al., 2019;
060   Xiao et al., 2018; Lansdell et al., 2020; Guerguiev et al., 2020; Kunin et al., 2020).
061   A complementary line of work makes the opposite choice. An approximation of the gradients derives
062   from a modified forward pass using directional derivatives (Baydin et al., 2022; Silver et al., 2021;
063   Margossian, 2019; Fournier et al., 2023). These methods rely on the Jacobian-vector product during
064   the forward pass, reducing the backward pass to a straightforward update of the parameters. However,
065   the high variance of the estimation inhibits its scalability even when local losses are introduced as a
066   remedy (Ren et al., 2022; Belouze, 2022; Fournier et al., 2023).
067   Furthermore, recent developments propose a noteworthy alternative based on forward only algorithms,
068   for which a second and modified forward pass replaces the standard backward phase. Seminal works
069   in this field are the Forward-Forward algorithm (FF) and the Present the Error to Perturb the Input To
070   Modulate the Activity learning rule (PEPITA) proposed by Hinton (2022); Dellaferrera & Kreiman
071   (2022). These algorithms, though relatively new and not yet widely studied have exhibited promising
072   results and similarities with adaptive-feedback-alignment algorithms (Srinivasan et al., 2023).
073   In this paper, we draw inspiration from these promising approaches to design a new training algorithm
074   for deep neural networks and our contributions are the following:
075
                 • The Zoutendijk theorem on line search convergence allows us to reconsider the weakness of
076
                   previous feedback alignment methods and to design a method to align the random matrices
077                for different kind of architectures.
078
079
                 • The alignment of the feedback matrices uses a forward gradient estimate, which is carefully
                   used to avoid possible perturbation due to the high variance.
080
081              • We introduce a learning algorithm called GrAPE (GRadient Aligned Projected Error)
082                that can handle and scale to modern architectures like deep convolutional networks and
083
                   Transformer-based models.
084              • Empirical results show very promising results with models like VGG16 (Simonyan &
085                Zisserman, 2014), trained on CIFAR100. To the best of our knowledge, this the first time.
086              • GrAPE reaches training losses that are on par with BP. A convergence towards poorer
087                solutions could explain the lower test performance, leaving room for further improvement.

135   2.1      F EEDBACK A LIGNMENT (FA)
136
      Feedback Alignment (FA) introduces a paradigm-shifting and biologically plausible alternative to
137
      gradient back-propagation (Lillicrap et al., 2016). At the core of this method, random feedback
138   matrices drastically simplify the weight update process and break the symmetry. FA multiplies the
139   output error by the random feedback matrices in order to obtain the error at each layer, which is in
140   turn used to update the weights. The random matrices replace the term ∇Ll . However, the sequential
141   aspect of the update remains, as the error is still propagated from layer to layer through the random
142   feedback projections. With Bl the fixed random feedback matrix of the l-th layer, the update can be
143   estimated as follows:

147   ric weight transport during learning (Lillicrap et al., 2020), making this learning paradigm more
148   biologically plausible. Nøkland (2016) went even a step further when presenting Direct Feedback
149   Alignment (DFA). During training, the error signal is directly projected from the output layer to every
150   hidden layers without modification or intermediate calculations. With this additional simplification,
151   the updates can be easily parallelized.

      FA and DFA have been shown to perform reasonably well on certain tasks and architectures, especially
154
      when considering its profound shift from the backpropagation method. While they do not consistently
155
      outperform or even compete with backpropagation, their simplicity along with biological plausibility
156   stimulate research to scale up their use, as well as exploration to understand their key limitations.
157   Bartunov et al. (2018) for example, show empirically that FA variants perform significantly worse on
158   CIFAR-10 and ImageNet than BP, especially for convolutional networks. This is further analyzed by
159   Launay et al. (2019) in which they exhibit a bottleneck effect that prevents learning in narrow layers,
160   especially in the case of convolutional networks. As a workaround, some variants of FA showed
161   promising performances on deep CNNs (Moskovitz et al., 2018). A seminal work by Akrout et al.
      (2019) for example used weight mirroring to adapt the feedback matrices during training, matching

      architecture, including convolution and tranformer layers. The work of Launay et al. (2020) showed
167
      promising results for transformer models trained with DFA. However, this is done according two

173   allows us to better this proposition towards backpropagation-free training of modern architectures by
174   providing blocks and/or layers a more useful feedback signal.

248
249   In this section, we describe our main contribution: the GrAPE algorithm (Gradient Aligned Projection
250   Error). Our contribution relies on the efficient use of forward gradients of each layer to adapt the
251   feedback matrices. By combining both of these ingredients, the algorithm ensures the learning ability
252   on more challenging tasks and with more complex models.
253
254   3.1   L IMITATIONS OF F IXED R ANDOM F EEDBACK M ATRICES
255
256   In the context of line search algorithms, the angle between the search direction and the negative
257   gradient direction is a crucial factor. As mentionned in Section 2, the analogy for DFA is between the
258   feedback path with and the gradient. This standard optimization problem is also pivotal in FA-like
259
      methods but the standard methods do not have access to the necessary information regarding the
      gradient. In the case of convolutional layers, the operation can be written as a block Toeplitz matrix
260
      (d’Ascoli et al., 2019). This constrained structure is impossible to reproduce with a fixed randomly
261
      sampled feedback matrix. This argument, developed by Refinetti et al. (2021), explains the failure of
262   vanilla DFA on convolutional networks as shown by Launay et al. (2019), the convolutional weights
263   being unable to capture the projected information correctly. However, if the projected information
264   was aligned with the gradients of the corresponding weights, this issue could be resolved.
265

303   In this section, we discuss the validity of our algorithmic design choices and assumptions. The
304
      cornerstone concerns the gradients approximations using forward calculations. Baydin et al. (2022)
      and Ren et al. (2022) showed that an unbiased estimation of the gradients could be obtained using
305
      forward gradients calculated thanks to jacobian vector products. However, if we consider the original
306
      JVP formulation, the expectation of the forward gradient can be written as follows:
307
308                     E(ga,l ) = E((∇Ll Ul ) ⊗ Ul ) = E(Ul UlT )∇Ll = Cov(Ul )∇Ll .
309   For an unbiased estimate of the gradient, the covariance matrix of the perturbations must be the identity
310   matrix in the subspace that the gradients lie in, for every layer. The choice of usable perturbations is
311   thus rather limited. To meet this requirement, we draw perturbations from a multivariate standard
312   normal distribution for each layer. This allows us to ensure an identity covariance matrix for the
313
      perturbations in Equation 4.
314   The high variance of forward gradients is the second challenge to address. This is especially the case
315   for large networks as illustrated by Fournier et al. (2023). However, our method only relies on the
316   direction of the gradients and not on their magnitude, meaning that if the cosine similarity between
317   the forward gradient and the true gradient is high, our method should facilitate the learning. We
318   experimentally verify this assumption in Figure 5, showing that the cosine similarity between the
319
      forward gradients and the true gradients is high enough to get useful information about the direction.

320
      experiments when possible, downgrading to 128 for larger networks, due to computational constraints.
321
      We also mitigate this issue by averaging the gradients through the learning phase with a cumulative

       to BP, we believe it is critical for their development and future adoption to have a fair comparison.

373    to ensure the implementation does not contain any BP-related updates. We plan on releasing our
374    code upon acceptance. It has to be noted that even though our algorithm allows a parallelized
375    training of each layer, as modern deep learning frameworks are built for BP, the parallelization of the
376    whole training process is not yet possible. This implementation heavily relies on conv2d_input,
377    conv2d_weight, and conv2d_bias from the torch.nn.grad library to correctly adapt
       DFA and GrAPE to convolution related calculations.

      4.2   R ESULTS A NALYSIS
419
      The results reported in Tables 1, 2 and 3 show the superiority of our method over the other local and
420   parallel learning approaches. First of all, GrAPE clearly betters the performances of DFA when it is
421   possible on small networks, showing that the alignment of the feedback matrices with the forward
422   gradients is a promising way of improving the learning of FA-like algorithms. It also helps bridging
423   the performance gap of parallel learning algorithms with BP, beating DRTP, PEPITA and DFA on
424   every tasks. However our results show that we are still lagging behind BP even on small networks.
425
      Our method also shows promising results on larger networks, such as AlexNet, VGG-16 and even a
426   Transformer. The performance gap with DFA is here even more notable. The training of deep convo-
427   lutional networks like VGG-16, which was showed to be untrainable through DFA by Launay et al.
428   (2019) can be achieved with our method, unlocking new architectures for parallel learning algorithms.
429   On the other hand, the gap with BP is still important, showing room for major improvement. We note
430   a positive effect of batch normalization and the impact of the feedback initialization shows us that,
431   even though the effect of these operations are well-studied for standard BP, much work is yet needed
      to understand their way of functioning and optimize them for feedback learning.

436   found in the original paper and used the same setting to train with the GrAPE learning rule. The
437   results presented in Table 3 show a clear improvement of GrAPE over DFA when training modern
438   architectures, however still largely lagging behind BP in terms of validation performance.
439   We illustrate this in Figure 3. There is a wider gap between the training loss and the test loss for

445   such as activation functions, is also to be studied, which we leave for future work.

461   5    L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
462
463   Experimental results show that the alignment of the feedback matrices with the forward gradients is a
464   promising way of improving the learning of FA-like algorithms. This alignment allows the feedback
465   to follow directions nearly parallel to the gradient direction. This makes the difference between
466   GrAPE and previous work, when we consider deep architectures such as AlexNet and VGG16 on
467   CIFAR100 (Bartunov et al., 2018; Launay et al., 2019).
468   Our approach also performs better than PEPITA, even in its recent and augmented version (Srinivasan
469   et al., 2023). Borrowing ideas from the seminal work of Akrout et al. (2019), their method can be
470   considered as a tailored feedback-alignment algorithm with adaptive feedback weights. The major
471   difference with our work is that we have a feedback matrix for each layer, which enables a local
472   injection of the error signal. We can therefore use GrAPE on much deeper architectures, since this
473   prevents the error signal to become more and more distorted through the second forward pass.
474   However our method is still lagging behind BP, especially on larger networks. A couple of decades
475   were necessary to reach such performances with BP. Our line of work is much more recent and
476   requires further improvements to bridge this gap. For instance, the poor generalization we observed
477   in Figure 3 calls for the design of adapted regularization techniques. Furthermore, the high variance
478   of forward gradients increases with the number of neurons in the network (Ren et al., 2022). Hence,
479   the forward gradients differs from the true gradients, as showed in Section 3.4, thus perturbing the
480   learning direction. This could be mitigated either with iterative calculations of the forward gradients,
481   or with local losses Fournier et al. (2023), but at the expense of a higher computational cost. On
482
      the optimization side, it is worth noticing that the Zoutendijk theorem only ensures convergence
      towards a stationary point, which could be a saddle point. Our method could benefit from second
483
      order information, gathered for instance in the forward pass, to improve the convergence guarantee.
484
485   Lastly, we showed with this method that DFA could be adapted to take into account inherent gradient
      structure for particular calculations. In order to fully train modern architectures as Transformers with


                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      a parallel algorithm, it would thus be interesting to take into account the structure of some underlying
487   operations such as residual connections (He et al., 2016) and self-attention. The former was motivated
488   to address the vanishing gradient issue (due to BP), while the latter is dedicated to structured inputs.
489   We leave the adaptation of feedback-projection to these operations for future work. The presented
490   results on Transformers Table 3 also opens up new avenues for parallel training of networks, dividing
491   it into smaller chunks that would receive gradient-aligned feedback information.

      6    C ONCLUSION
494
495
      The GrAPE algorithm presented in this work emerges as a promising local alternative to conventional
496   BP. It successfully mitigates challenges posed to the DFA by using approximations of the gradient
497   calculated during the forward phase and enables parallel updates of all the parameters through the
498   network. We showed its ability to learn complex tasks with deep architectures. The adaptive feedback
499   allows the training algorithm to align its direction with the gradients. Hence with GrAPE, we can
500   reach training losses that are on par with BP, while the generalization capacity must be improved. By
501   turning the backward pass into a feedback projection, the information propagation in GrAPE follows
502   the forward-only paradigm. This interstingly brings our method closer to biological models of the
503   brain and the real-time constraints inherent to neuromorphic systems.
504   Our findings also show that in order to scale parallel learning algorithms to modern networks, a
505   theoretical and empirical work is still needed to understand how all the deep-learning ingredients that
506   interact with the BP recipe can be also used with DFA-like algorithms. These ingredients include
507   initialization schemes, architectural operations like Batch or Layer Normalization, as well as residual
508   connexions and dropout.
