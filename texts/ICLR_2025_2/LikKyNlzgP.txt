009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Iterative Markovian Fitting (IMF) procedure based on iterative reciprocal and
012            Markovian projections has recently been proposed as a powerful method for solv-
013            ing the Schrödinger Bridge problem. However, it has been observed that for the
014            practical implementation of this procedure, it is crucial to alternate between fitting
015            a forward and backward time diffusion at each iteration. Such implementation is
016            thought to be a practical heuristic, which is required to stabilize training and ob-
017            tain good results in applications such as unpaired domain translation. In our work,
018
               we show that this heuristic closely connects with the pioneer approaches for the
               Schrödinger Bridge based on the Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF) procedure.
019
               Namely, we find that the practical implementation of IMF is, in fact, a combina-
020
               tion of IMF and IPF procedures, and we call this combination the Iterative Pro-
021            portional Markovian Fitting (IPMF) procedure. We show both theoretically and
022            practically that this combined IPMF procedure can converge under more general
023            settings, thus, showing that the IPMF procedure opens a door towards developing
024            a unified framework for solving Schrödinger Bridge problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Diffusion models inspired by the Schrödinger Bridge (SB) theory, which connects stochastic pro-
029
      cesses with the optimal transport theory, have recently emerged as a powerful approach for numer-
      ous applications in biology (Tong et al., 2024; Bunne et al., 2023), chemistry (Somnath et al., 2023;
030
      Igashov et al.), computer vision (Liu et al., 2023a; Shi et al., 2023) and speech processing (Chen
031
      et al., 2023). Most of these applications are dedicated either to supervised translation, e.g., image
032   super-resolution and inpainting (Liu et al., 2023a) or unpaired domain translation, such as image
033   style-transfer (Shi et al., 2023) or single-cell data analysis (Tong et al., 2024).
034
035
      In this paper, we focus specifically on unpaired domain translation, where SB-based algorithms are
      typically used since they enforce two key properties: the similarity between input and translated
036
      object (referred to as the optimality property) and that the input domain is translated to the target
037
      domain (referred to as the marginal matching property).

      Early works (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Vargas et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021) on using Schrödinger
055   Bridge for unpaired domain translation employed the well-celebrated Iterative Proportional Fit-
056   ting (IPF) procedure (Kullback, 1968), also known as the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi & Doucet,
057   2014). The IPF procedure is initialized with a simple prior process that satisfies the optimality prop-
058   erty. It then refines this process iteratively through optimality-preserving transformations until the
059   marginal matching property is achieved. In each iteration, IPF decreases the forward KL-divergence
060   KL(q ∗ ||q) between the current approximation q and the ground-truth Schrödinger Bridge q ∗ . How-
061   ever, due to approximation errors, in practice, IPF may suffer from the ”prior forgetting”, where the
062   marginal matching property is achieved, but the optimality is lost (Vargas et al., 2024; 2021).
063   Recently, the Iterative Markovian Fitting (IMF) procedure (Shi et al., 2023; Peluchetti, 2023a;
064   Gushchin et al., 2024) was introduced as the promising competitor to IPF. Contrary to IPF, the
065   IMF starts from the stochastic process with the marginal matching property, while the optimality is
066   achieved during the IMF iterations. Unlike the IPF procedure, the IMF procedure at each iteration
067   decreases reverse KL-divergence KL(q||q ∗ ) between the current approximation q and the ground-
068   truth Schrödinger Bridge q ∗ . This iterative procedure can also be seen as a generalization of rectified
069
      flows (Liu et al., 2022) to stochastic processes, which is used (Liu et al., 2023b; Yan et al., 2024) for
      the modern foundational generative models such as Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024). In anal-
070
      ogy to IPF, the IMF procedure may also accumulate errors but in approximating data distributions
071
      due to a imperfect fit at each iteration, leading to losing marginal matching property.
072
073   In practice, the IMF is implemented as a bidirectional procedure alternating between learning for-
074   ward and backward processes either by diffusion-based models in the Diffusion Schrödinger Bridge
075
      Matching (DSBM) algorithm (Shi et al., 2023) or GANs in Adversarial Schrödinger Bridge Match-
      ing (ASBM) algorithm (Gushchin et al., 2024). This heuristic of alternating between learning for-
076
      ward and backward processes helps to stabilize the IMF training and overcome the accumulation of
077
      errors and loss of marginal matching property. In this work, we explore the theoretical aspects of
078   this heuristic approach and make the following key contributions.
079
080   Main contributions: We show that the heuristic bidirectional IMF procedure used in practice
081
      closely relates to IPF. Namely, we discover that it, in fact, secretly utilizes IPF iterations. Due to
      this, we propose to call this bidirectional IMF procedure Iterative Proportional Markovian Fitting
082
      (IPMF) and conjecture that it not only converges under more general settings than was previously
083
      thought but opens a promising way of developing a unified framework for solving the SB problem.
084
      1. Theory I. We prove that the IPMF procedure converges for 1-dimensional Gaussian distributions
085
         (M3.2). Even this proof is non-trivial and involves significant complexity, as IPF and IMF mini-
086
         mize different (forward and reverse KL) divergences, leading to interference. We also make and
087      motivate a conjecture about IPMF convergence for multivariate Gaussians (M3.2).
088
      2. Practice I. We experimentally support our conjecture for multivariate Gaussians (M4.1), using
089
         the closed form update formulas that can be derived in discrete time (M4.1).
090
      3. Practice II. We empirically validate through a series of standard experiments, including toy 2D
091
         setups (M4.2), the Schrödinger Bridge benchmark (M4.3), colored MNIST images and real-world
092
         image dataset - Celeba (M4.4), that IPMF procedure converges.
093
      These contributions show that the IPMF procedure has significant potential to combine many pre-
094
      viously introduced SB methods, including IPF and IMF-based, with both discrete (Gushchin et al.,
095   2024; De Bortoli et al., 2021) and continuous time (Shi et al., 2023; Peluchetti, 2023a; Vargas et al.,
096   2021), together with their online versions (De Bortoli et al., 2024; Peluchetti, 2024; Karimi et al.,
097   2024). Moreover, the forward-backward framework of IPMF could enable rectified flows to avoid
098   error accumulation, making them even more powerful in diffusion-based generative modeling.

112   2 BACKGROUND
113   This section recalls the Schrödinger Bridge (SB) problem (M2.1). Then, we describe procedures for
114   solving SB: Iterative Proportional Fitting (IPF, M2.2) and Iterative Markovian Fitting (IMF, M2.3).
115   We discuss the practical implementation of the IMF procedure called Bidirectional IMF in M2.4.
116   The Schrödinger Bridge problem can be considered both in discrete and continuous time, which
117   are equivalent. The discrete-time and continuous-time versions of IPF and IMF procedures are also
118   similar. In the main text, we operate only with the discrete-time setup to avoid a lot of repetitions,
119   which will harm the flow of the paper. Also, the discrete-time version provides the explicit formulas
120   for the projections used in IPF and IMF procedures, which makes it sufficiently easier to explain the
121   main idea of our paper. We present the analogical facts about the continuous setup in Appendix A.

      5 D ISCUSSION
521   Potential impact. The presented Iterative Proportional
522   Markovian Fitting procedure shows a potential to overcome
523   the error accumulation problem observed in distillation meth-
524
      ods like rectified flows (Liu et al., 2022; 2023b), which is
      used for the acceleration of the foundational image genera-
525
      tion models, e.g. StableDiffusion 3 in (Esser et al., 2024).
526
      These distillation methods are based on the one-directional
527   IMF procedure in the limit of Schrödinger Bridge hyperpa-
528   rameter ϵ = 0. However, the one-directional version is ob-
529   served to accumulate errors and may even lead to the diver-
530   gence (De Bortoli et al., 2024, Appendix I). Furthermore, us-
531   ing the limiting case of ϵ = 0 makes it impossible to use Figure 6: Convergence of models to target
532   the IPF procedure to restore marginals. The usage of a bidi-    distribution. FID plotted as a function of
533   rectional version along with ϵ > 0 should both correct the IPMF iterations for all the presented setups.
534   marginals and make trajectories of diffusion more straight to accelerate the inference of diffusion
535
      models. We believe that considering such distillation techniques from the IPMF point of view may
      help to overcome the current limitations of these techniques.
536
537   Limitations. While we show the proof of exponential convergence of the IPMF procedure for the
538   1-dimensional Gaussians in the continuous-time IMF and discrete-time IMF (with one inner point
539   N = 1), and present a wide set of experiments supporting this procedure, the proof of convergence
      of IPMF in the general case still remains a promising avenue for future work.

            The IPMF framework doesn’t require the starting process to have p0 , p1 marginals or to be a
            Schrödinger bridge. Then one can try other starting processes that would improve the performance of
1282
            the IPMF algorithm. Properties of the starting process that would be desirable are 1) q(x0 ) = p0 (x0 )
1283
            and q(x1 ) marginal to be close to p1 (x1 ) and 2) q(x0 , x1 ) to be close to a Schrödinger bridge. In
1284        the IMF or IPF we had to choose one of these properties because we can’t satisfy both of them
1285        completely.
1286
1287

            starting process for the IPMF procedure. Such a coupling would provide 1) q(x0 ) = p0 (x0 ) and

1292        prior. Various models can be used as an SDE prior, we explore two options: trainable and train-free.

            the CelebA female part. We then apply SDEdit with the Celeba male images as input to produce
