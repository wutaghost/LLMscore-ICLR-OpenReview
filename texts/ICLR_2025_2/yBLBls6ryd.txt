009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Many popular optimization methods can be united through fractional natural gra-
012            dient descent (FNGD), which pre-conditions the gradient with a fractional power
013            of the inverse Fisher: RMSprop and Adam(W) estimate a diagonal Fisher matrix
014            and apply a square root before inversion; other methods like K-FAC and Shampoo
015            employ matrix-valued Fisher estimates and apply the inverse and inverse square
016            root, respectively. Recently, the question of how fractional power affects opti-
017
               mization has moved into focus, e.g. offering trade-offs between convergence and
               generalization. Gaining deeper insights into this phenomenon would require going
018
               beyond diagonal estimations and using cheap and flexible matrix-valued Fisher
019
               estimators capable of applying any fractional power; however, existing methods are
020            limited by their expensive matrix fraction computation. To address this, we propose
021            a Riemannian framework to learn eigen-factorized Fisher estimations on the fly, al-
022            lowing for the cheap application of arbitrary fractional powers. Our approach does
023            not require matrix decompositions and, therefore, is stable in half precision. We
024            show our framework’s efficacy on positive-definite matrix optimization problems
025            and demonstrate its efficiency and flexibility for training neural nets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Many well-known adaptive methods, like SGD (Robbins & Monro, 1951), RmsProp (Tieleman &
030   Hinton, 2012) or Adam(W) (Kingma & Ba, 2015; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), can be framed as
031   fractional natural gradient descent (FNGD): Given the neural network (NN) parameters µ, the gradient
032   g and a curvature estimation S, FNGD applies µ ← µ − β1 S−1/p g using a learning rate β1 and
033
      subjecting the curvature approximation to a fractional power 1/p before inversion. S approximates
      the Fisher information matrix (Amari, 1998), e.g. through exponential averages of the empirical
034
      Fisher (Kunstner et al., 2019) or the gradient outer product (GOP, Kingma & Ba, 2015; Agarwal et al.,
035
      2019; Lin et al., 2024). FNGD’s matrix fractional power allows interpolating between NGD (Amari,
036   1998) with p = 1 and SGD as p → ∞; RMSprop/Adam(W) use p = 2.
037
038   While most adaptive optimization algorithms rely on a square root (p = 2), the fractional power’s
039
      role has recently garnered a lot of attention. Several works question the indispensable role of the
      square root and empirically demonstrate the usage of other fractions to trade off convergence and
040
      generalization (Chen et al., 2021), overcome the generalization gap between SGD and adaptive
041
      methods on convolutional neural nets (CNNs) observed by (Wilson et al., 2017), and to successfully
042   train transformers (Lin et al., 2024). Theoretically, Huh (2020) identify limitations of SGD and NGD
043   in terms of generalization, convergence, and stability when training deep linear networks. They argue
044   that FNGD with p ̸∈ {1, ∞} can offer the ‘best of both worlds’, i.e. NGD’s convergence speed with
045   SGD’s generalization.
046
      Applying other fractional powers is straightforward for methods with a diagonal curvature approxima-
047
      tion (e.g. PAdam from Chen et al., 2021) and does not add much computational cost. However, doing
048   so for methods with non-diagonal preconditioning matrices such as K-FAC (Martens & Grosse, 2015)
049   or Shampoo (Gupta et al., 2018; Anil et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023) is computationally and numerically
050   challenging. This is because computing matrix fractional powers is computationally intensive, and
051   must usually be done in high precision to avoid numerical instabilities (Anil et al., 2020; Shi et al.,
052   2023), preventing those methods from using fast, low-precision arithmetic (Micikevicius et al., 2018).
053   Making the root computation fast and stable in low-precision can further unleash the potential of
      non-diagonal fractional methods.

      To catalyze further investigations into the fractional power’s role, it would be desirable to have a
055   flexible and efficient framework for learning non-diagonal curvature approximations that can (i) apply
056   arbitrary fractional roots, and (ii) circumvent the numerical instabilities of matrix decompositions.
057
058
      We address this instability and inefficiency and present an update scheme to directly adapt the spectral
      factorization Bdiag(d)B⊤ of the curvature approximation S on the fly, which we term a spectral
059
      parametrization of S. Thanks to this factorization, we can apply any matrix fractional power to S by
060
      elementwise operation on the eigenvalues d. Our approach directly adapts eigenfactors and maintains
061   the factorization, and a practical version can operate without performing eigendecompositions. This
062   makes our scheme amenable to running in low precision because we do not use any unstable matrix
063   decomposition algorithm. However, the spectral factorization introduces several challenges as it
064   imposes constraints and ambiguities (i.e. B must be orthogonal and d sorted) that need to be dealt
065   with. These constraints make it more challenging to maintain the factorization on the fly. Our
066   contributions are:
067
             • We propose an update scheme to learn the spectral factorization Bdiag(d)B⊤ of a curvature
068
               matrix S on the fly and address how to account for the constraints, and resolve the ambigui-
069
               ties, imposed by this parameterization. We then show how to learn Kronecker-factorized
070                                                                                                   ⊤
071            spectral decompositions, i.e. S ≈ (S(K) ⊗ S(C) ) where S(i) = B(i) diag(d(i) )B(i) , which
072
               are crucial to scale the approach. The Kronecker factorization introduces new ambiguities,
               which we resolve by introducing a scalar α and demanding det(di ) = 1 (Section 2).
073
074          • Similar to Glasmachers et al. (2010); Lin et al. (2021; 2023), our approach views learning the
075
               curvature approximation as learning the covariance of a Gaussian variational distribution by
               performing Riemannian gradient descent on the manifold of dense or Kronecker-factorized
076
               positive-definite matrices. We extend these works by incorporating the new constraints
077
               arising from the spectral decomposition for the Fisher-Rao metric. (Section 3.)
078
079
             • Empirically, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach for a range of applications,
               including positive-definite matrix optimization (Pennec et al., 2006; Absil et al., 2009) and
080
               low-precision neural net training.

      2     FAST FNGD USING L EARNABLE S PECTRAL FACTORIZATIONS
109
110   Our goal is to design pre-conditioner update schemes that offer the flexibility to apply arbitrary matrix
111   roots at a low cost. The starting point is the observation that the pre-conditioner S can be interpreted
112   as the inverse covariance matrix of a Gaussian variational distribution (Lin et al., 2024), which can be
113
      learned on the fly via the update scheme in Equation (3). However, this parameterization complicates
      applying any fractional root to S since the root computation requires matrix decomposition.
114
115   Our contribution is to propose an update scheme for a new parameterization S = Bdiag(d)B⊤ ,
116   where B is an orthogonal square matrix and d is a vector with positive sorted entries, to learn d and
117   B. We call this parameterization a spectral parameterization, due to its connection to the spectral
118   decomposition of symmetric matrices. We empirically and theoretically establish its equivalence
119   to update rules that directly update S, implying one can enjoy efficient updates while retaining
120
      the behavior of traditional methods. Our spectral parametrization allows us to easily compute any
      fractional root S− /p = Bdiag(d− /p )B⊤ through elementwise roots on d, instead of matrix roots on
                        1               1

121
      S. We then extend it to Kronecker-factorized matrices, which is crucial for large-scale applications.
122
      Our update schemes are efficient as learning B and d does not involve any matrix decomposition.
123
124   In contrast to previous parameterizations, the spectral parameterization introduces new challenges,
125   such as satisfying the orthogonal constraint on B and handling parametrization ambiguities. We defer
126
      the technicalities how to handle these constraints to Section 3 where we derive the update scheme
      from scratch. This is mainly to avoid introducing technical Riemannian optimization concepts needed
127
      that are necessary for our derivation. In the following, our focus will be on presenting and empirically
128
      validating the update scheme, and its connections to existing methods.

      Challenges of Learning Spectral Parametrizations via RGD Our main idea is to learn a spectral
309
      parameterization/coordinate of S = BDiag(d)BT by solving a reparametrized Gaussian problem in
310
      Eq. 6. We then follow a similar procedure to convert an RGD step in the spectral coordinate into a
311
      root-free update scheme based on the spectral factorization. However, directly performing RGD in
312   a spectral coordinate is challenging because we have to (1) satisfy parameter constraints, (2) use a
313   non-singular metric (coordinate representation), and (3) analytically compute the metric inversion.

315
      Conflict between Simplification for RGD and Coordinate Transformation The simplification
      step (Step 3) turns a computationally expensive RGD step involving the metric inversion into a
316
      more efficient root-free adaptive update scheme. Without an analytical metric inversion, we cannot
317
      simplify the RGD step and explicitly express it as a root-free update scheme. When changing
318   coordinates, the metric representation has to be changed accordingly (Lee, 2018) to make RGD
319   invariant to coordinate transformation. However, the coordinate change of the metric complicates
320   the simplification. This is because we no longer analytically inverse this high-dimensional Fisher-
321   Rao metric, which is non-diagonal and singular in some coordinates like a spectral coordinate.
322   The simplification process is more challenging in Kronecker-factorized cases because additional
323   redundancy from Kronecker factorization renders the metric (coordinate representation) singular and
      complicates the simplification.

      Constraint Satisfaction and Metric Diagonalization via Local Coordinate Transformation
325   Inspired by general Riemannian (normal) local coordinates (Glasmachers et al., 2010; Lin et al., 2021;
326   2023) and Fermi coordinates (Manasse & Misner, 1963), we propose using local coordinates to tackle
327   these challenges. The main idea is to construct local coordinates that handle constraints and facilitate
328   the analytical metric inversion needed for the simplification. Using a local coordinate transformation
329   can locally diagonalize the metric at a single evaluation point. Given a global coordinate, such as a
330   spectral coordinate, we construct a local coordinate and its coordinate transformation map associated
331   with the global coordinate at every iteration. In our approach, the coordinate and its transformation
332   map should satisfy these conditions: (1) the map is differentiable and injective. (2) the coordinate
333
      should not have any coordinate constraint, and its origin represents a current iterate in the spectral
      coordinate. (3) it is common for the metric evaluated at the origin of the local coordinate to be an
334
      identity matrix (Lin et al., 2023). However, this can be loosened to be diagonal (Glasmachers et al.,
335
      2010) or even block-diagonal as long as the metric is easy to inverse. Our approach follows the
336   requirements for local coordinate construction (Lin et al., 2023). We propose new local coordinates
337   for spectral coordinates because the existing local coordinates do not account for spectral constraints.

343   straints using an unconstrained local coordinate. Because a spectral coordinate contains redundancies,
344   we remove them to simplify the inverse metric computation. This allows us to make the metric
345   diagonal in our local coordinates.

      Handling Redundancy due to Repeated Entries of d Recall that eigendecomposition is not
379   unique when having repeat eigenvalues. Our spectral parametrization is also not unique when d has
380   duplicated entries. In this case, the Fisher-Rao metric (coordinate representation) is singular. We
381   allow d to have repeated entries and address the singularity using the Moore–Penrose inversion (van
382   Oostrum et al., 2023). Computing the Moore–Penrose inversion is easy because we diagonalize the
383   metric at evaluation points. In practice, we also use this inversion to improve numerical stability if d
384   has very close entries.

533   5   C ONCLUSION
534   We present a Riemannian approach for learning spectral factorizations on the fly. Our method fixes
535   the instability and inefficiency of using a matrix fractional root for low-precision NN training and
536   enables matrix methods to use other fractional roots. An interesting direction is to evaluate our
537   methods in large-scale settings and investigate the potential benefit of using other fractional roots.

705   Moreover, this map is injective.

708   have det(I − N) = 0. Thus, N has an eigenvalue with 1. By the definition of the eigenvalue, there
709   exists a non-zero vector x ̸= 0 so that Nx = x. Notice that Given that N is skew-symmetric, we
710   have

      non-singular.
