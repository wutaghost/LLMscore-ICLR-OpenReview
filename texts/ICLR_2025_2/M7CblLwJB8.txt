011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013                Large language models are transforming the landscape of applications, with
014                their influence poised to expand. One important practical challenge is how to
015                selectively customize models to align with specific expectations, such as tone,
016                formality, or underlying biases. To solve this task, we develop AutoCustomiza-
017
                   tion. The key to our approach is leveraging the vast knowledge encoded in
                   modern language models to construct fine-tuning datasets focused on a specific
018
                   customization axis in contrast to prior methods, which depend primarily on
019
                   tediously constructed libraries of prompts. AutoCustomization demonstrates
020                several desirable properties. It is universally applicable to any bias axis (e.g.,
021                political, stylistic). It is efficient with small automatically generated datasets and
022                short fine-tuning. It allows for precise monitoring of the resulting bias change
023                with our BiasShift evaluation metric proven to be aligned with human perception,
024                generalizable to held-out aspects, and selective in preserving other model
025                capabilities. We verify AutoCustomization through human evaluation and show
026                that it outperforms existing prompting techniques while being simpler. Prompting
027                significantly degrades with increased context length—over 80% drop in the bias
028
                   strength for just 1,000 characters—and is susceptible to adversarial prompts,
                   with a 50% drop observed. In contrast, a model trained with AutoCustomization
029
                   maintained its bias adjustments in both scenarios.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have made significant advancements in recent years, powering
035
      a wide range of applications, including text and voice-based conversational agents (Zhong et al.,
036   2024; Foosherian et al., 2023). A key obstacle in deploying these models lies in a selective style
037   customization ensuring their language output aligns with specific expectations such as tone, formal-
038   ity, or underlying biases, including political or cognitive, as well as the scope of its taboos (Liu
039   et al., 2024; Neelakanteswara et al., 2024; Rozado, 2024a). These challenges often arise in practical
040   applications, necessitating a straightforward, computationally efficient method that does not rely on
041   labor-intensive datasets. Traditionally, prompting has been the primary method for achieving cus-
042   tomizations (Zheng et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), but it is often cumbersome and brittle, requiring
043   complex techniques and prompt libraries tailored to specific models and tasks .
044   To address this problem, we propose AutoCustomization, a novel framework that capitalizes on a
045   huge body of knowledge encoded in modern LLMs to automatically construct fine-tuning datasets
046   focused on a specific customization axis. Specifically, for a user-provided axis of adjustment (e.g.,
047   political bias between Republicans and Democrats), the LLM generates relevant subareas (e.g.,
048   gun ownership, welfare) and corresponding question-answer pairs. These pairs are then used for
049   fine-tuning to induce bias in one direction along the selected axis.
050   AutoCustomization has several desirable properties that we verify empirically. First universality,
051   our framework can be easily applied to any bias axis. Second efficiency, we demonstrate that even a
052   small auto-generated dataset and short fine-tuning is sufficient to shift the bias.1 Third, we introduce

      2    R ELATED WORK
078
079
      Personas & LLMs Large language models function as flexible agents capable of adopting various
080
      personas, influencing their interactions and responses. (Aher et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2024) show
081   that assigning socio-demographic personas leads to performance drops in reasoning tasks and intro-
082   duces biases, while (Li et al., 2024) demonstrate that persona assignment enhances steerability but
083   risks amplifying stereotypes. In addition, (Zheng et al., 2023) question the effectiveness of generic
084   roles like helpful assistant, and (Kong et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023) explore role-play and expert
085   prompting to improve reasoning and steerability in LLMs.
086
      Datasets & Generation Recent efforts to address challenges related to ideological bias, toxicity, and
087   personality expression in LLMs have led to the development of several benchmark datasets. (Chen
088   et al., 2024) introduced the IDEOINST dataset to study ideological manipulation, consisting of 6,000
089   opinion-eliciting instructions on sociopolitical topics, each paired with left-leaning and right-leaning
090   responses generated using GPT-4. In the field of toxicity, (Wang et al., 2024) developed the SafeEdit
091   dataset to assess LLM detoxification through knowledge editing. SafeEdit includes 540 harmful
092   questions, covering nine unsafe categories, generated using attack prompts based on OpenAI’s usage
093   policy. Additionally, (Mao et al., 2024) created the PersonalityEdit dataset to investigate personality
094   trait adjustments in LLMs. This dataset comprises 2,000 topics, with responses generated by GPT-
095
      4 tailored to neuroticism, extraversion, and agreeableness, ensuring high-quality data through a
      combination of automated filtering and manual verification. In this work, we present a universal
096
      method that is capable of generating adjustment datasets for all dimensions mentioned above.
097
098   Measuring Idologies & LLMs Measuring political ideologies has become more effective with re-
099   cent developments in LLMs. In (Kato et al., 2024), the authors use a fine-tuned BERT classifier
100   to extract opinion-based sentences from parliamentary speeches and map them onto an ideologi-
101
      cal spectrum, showing a close alignment with expert evaluations while reducing human interven-
      tion.(O’Hagan & Schein, 2024) employs LLMs to directly elicit numeric ideological scores, high-
102
      lighting the flexibility of LLMs in capturing subtle ideological shifts across various case studies.
103
      Finally, (Rozado, 2024b) investigates how embedded political biases in LLM responses can be mea-
104   sured using political orientation tests, revealing that LLMs can reflect ideological categories like
105   progressivism and conservatism.

      3     AUTO C USTOMIZATION METHOD
109
110
      AutoCustomization is a method for adjusting LLMs along a specific bias axis. Such axis is defined
111
      by two opposite stances A and B – keywords provided by the user, and will be further referred to
112   as the (A, B)-axis (e.g., the (Republican, Democrat)-axis). Our approach consists of two phases.
113   In the first one, a dataset D consisting of question-answer pairs grouped in sub-areas relevant to
114   the selected axis is generated . In the second phase, D is used to fine-tune an LLM. Additionally,
115   we utilize a second dataset, DN , which is static and independent of the (A, B)-axis. This dataset
116   is derived from selected areas of the MMLU dataset Hendrycks et al. (2021) and includes subjects
117   such as formal logic, global facts, and high school mathematics . It is essential to ensure that the
118   LLM retains its logical reasoning and general knowledge capabilities.
119   AutoCustomization can be applied to virtually any stylistic, political, or ideological bias axis. The
120   entire procedure is automated, with the only user input being the keywords and the desired strength
121   of the adjustment.
122
123   3.1    DATASET GENERATION
124
125
126   Algorithm 1: Dataset Generation Phase
      Input : Two opposite stances A, B (e.g., A = Republican, B = Democrat)
127
      Parameters:
128     Number of subareas N ;
        Number of questions per subarea K
129
      Output: Dataset D = {(Q, CA , CB )}
130   Required: A Large Language Model Lg capable of generating subareas and triplets;
131   Initialize
        D ← ∅;
132
      Step 1: Generate Subareas
133    S ← Lg .generate subareas(A, B, N ) ; // Generate a list of N subareas spanning the (A, B)-axis, e.g.,
134    gun ownership, immigration

135   Step 2: Generate Triplets for Each Subarea
       foreach s ∈ S do
136        for i = 1 to K do
                 Generate Question
137               Q ← Lg .generate question(s) ;    // Create a question relevant to subarea s, e.g., What should
138               be the relationship between law and gun ownership?
                 Generate Continuation for Stance A
139               CA ← Lg .generate continuation(Q, A) ;    // Generate continuation specific to stance A, e.g.,
140               protected by all cost
                 Generate Continuation for Stance B
141               CB ← Lg .generate continuation(Q, B) ;    // Generate continuation specific to stance B, e.g.,
142               tightly controlled
                 Add Triplet to Dataset
143               DS ← DS ∪ {(Q, CA , CB )};
144        end
           D ← D ∪ {DS };
145
      end
146   return D;

189   values sufficiently span the axis while minimizing overlap between subareas and questions. We
190   also provide an overlap detection procedure (see Figure 2), designed to guarantee the non-overlap

      3.2   T RAINING AND EVALUATION
202
203   In the second phase of AutoCustomization, we fine-tune the target LLM, Lf , to bias it towards one
204   side of the selected (A, B)-axis, say A. To achieve this, we use a mixture of the generated dataset
205   Dtrain and the neutral dataset DN . The fine-tuning loss is designed to increase the probabilities of
206   continuations A, decrease the probabilities of continuation B, and maintain the starting probabilities
207   of DN . The intuition behind this is that making A (resp. B) more (resp. less) likely will cause a bias
208   shift (if D is sufficiently large and diverse) while maintaining the original performance on DN will
209   protect the model’s neutral capacities. The dynamics of an example successful training is presented
210   in Figure 3.
211   To control the fine-tuning process, we introduce the BiasShift metric. It is at the core of our method,
212   intuitively it measures, how much the model has been adjusted towards the selected stance A (and
213   decreasing the intensity of B). It is defined as follow

248   4     E XPERIMENTAL EVALUATION
249
250   In this section, we present an experimental evaluation of our AutoCustomization method. We split it
251   into two parts: a detailed human evaluation-based analysis of a representative bias adjustment, and
252   a broader analysis, findings, and conclusions relating to other cases.
253
      The first part focuses on validating two elements: the alignment and precision of the auto-evaluation
254
      process (using BiasShift); and the stability and safety of the AutoCustomization. BiasShift is
255
      an inexpensive metric and human evaluation demonstrates that it is a superhuman indicator of
256   adjustment level for comparable adjusted models. At the same time, analysis of AutoCustomization
257   shows strong adjustment stability and resistance to ’prompt hacking’ compared to traditional
258   prompting techniques. In this part, we concentrate on the (Republican, Democrat)-axis.
259
      The second part presents examples of usage for other axes. It shows that AutoCustomization is a
260
      universal, well-generalizing, and efficient method of bias adjustment. Specifically, it can be easily
261
      applied to any stylistic bias axis (universality), the resulting bias properly manifests in the held-out
262   subareas (generalization) with small datasets and short fine-tuning being sufficient to achieve this
263   (efficiency).

      4.1.1   A LIGNMENT AND PRECISION OF AUTO - EVALUATION AND B IAS S HIFT METRIC
284
285   In this section, we validate that the BiasShift is well-aligned with human perception of adjustment
286   level. This property is crucial for the soundness of the training process, as the BiasShift metric
287   controls the stopping condition and the selection of the best checkpoint. Despite its simplicity, we
288   discover that the metric offers excellent quality, exceeding not only LLM models but also separate
289   human evaluators.
290   Specifically, we conducted the following evaluation. We selected checkpoints corresponding to
291   BiasShift values spanning its range (see checkpoints t0 to t4 of the left plot in Figure 3). We used
292   each of the selected checkpoints to generate 150 answers to questions relevant to the investigated
293   ideological axis. We presented these to human annotators and LLMs and asked them to rank the
294   responses according to the degree of ideological adjustment. For LLM evaluation we used the
295   prompt supplied in Appendix B.
296   We computed Kendall’s τ ranking agreement (Kendall, 1938) to score evaluators. Human labeler
297   scores were obtained through computing ordering agreement with other humans. The values for
298   the automated ranking methods, LLMs and BiasShift, were individually calculated as their ranking
299   agreement with human evaluators.
300   The results are described in described Table 1. We have found that the BiasShift metric has better
301   agreement with human evaluators than any of the LLMs. Moreover, it also aligns with human labels
302   better than human evaluations between themselves. We conclude that the BiasShift metric is a super-
303   human indicator of the level of adjustment. The BiasShift metric can then be used to easily select
304   models of different adjustment levels, which is very difficult using prompting.

306   4.1.2   AUTO C USTOMIZATION ’ S STABILITY AND RESISTANCE TO ’ PROMPT HACKING ’
307
308   Prompting remains the go-to method for LLM style and bias adjustments due to its apparent ease
309
      of use and effectiveness. However, there are several issues with prompting as a means of LLM-
      style control. In this section, we focus on two main problematic aspects. First is the stability
310
      of style control with respect to the amount of neutral information in the LLM’s system prompt
311
      or conversation window. The second is resistance to adversarial prompts, or so-called ’prompt
312   hacking’. Both are critical issues. Instability with respect to the amount of neutral data means it’s
313   easy to induce a desired bias in a ’test setup’, but impossible to maintain it in the application context,
314   where the dynamic size of the context is often unavoidable. This easily gives designers a false sense
315   of stylistic control that does not translate to good test-time performance. Lack of resistance to
316   prompt hacking on the other hand allows malignant users to overcome the desired style potentially
317   causing unwanted or toxic behaviors. We show empirically that AutoCustomization manifests an
318   order of magnitude stronger robustness than prompting in both of these aspects (see Figure 4).
319   We generated a set of ’padding’ datasets added to the system prompt of the style-adjusted model
320   (prompted and modified using AutoCustomization). We used GPT4 to generate 4000 token-long
321   texts containing knowledge from three domains: grammar, financial, and physics-related. Then
322   created summarizations of several desired lengths (0, 25, 75, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, and 4000 to-
323   kens), again using GPT4. We manually checked them and corrected them when the length had been
      adjusted imperfectly. We present a sample of this data in Appendix D.

396   5   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
397
398   Our work presents a practical and complete solution for the task of bias customization in LLMs.
399   The most important direction for future research is to understand its scope exhaustively. In the
400   preliminary tests, we observed that the method could be utilized with various models. However,
401   this part requires a more detailed investigation. We conjecture that multiple customizations along
402
      orthogonal axes may be possible, but this requires further research. Moreover, we believe that our
      method is compatible with parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (e.g., Lora Hu et al. (2021)).
403
      Having this would open interesting possibilities for the deployment of our method in real-world
404
      applications. Moreover, we conjecture that it might be possible to interpolate the bias strength
405   by smoothly interpolating the model’s parameters. Last but not least, in some of our preliminary
406   experiments, we observed that only a small number of parameters are needed to adjust the model.
407   If true, this opens further interesting research directions in the area of model compression and
408   parameter-efficient fine-tuning.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
411
412
      In this work, we present a practical method AutoCustomization. We verify that it is universal,
413   efficient, and easy to control. It outperforms the traditional prompting techniques, offering a more
414   reliable and robust way of adjusting the model’s bias. Having these in mind, and taking into account
415   its simplicity, we open source the code and propose AutoCustomization as a new standard for bias
416   customization in LLMs.
