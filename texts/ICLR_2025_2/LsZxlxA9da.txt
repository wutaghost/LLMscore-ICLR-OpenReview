                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               An agent trained within a closed system can master any desired capability, as long
               as the following three conditions hold: (a) it receives sufficiently informative and
011
               aligned feedback, (b) its coverage of experience/data is broad enough, and (c) it
012
               has sufficient capacity and resource. In this position paper, we justify these condi-
013            tions, and consider what limitations arise from (a) and (b) in closed systems, when
014            assuming that (c) is not a bottleneck. Considering the special case of agents with
015            matching input and output spaces (namely, language), we argue that such pure
016            recursive self-improvement, dubbed ‘Socratic learning,’ can boost performance
017            vastly beyond what is present in its initial data or knowledge, and is only limited
018            by time, as well as gradual misalignment concerns. Furthermore, we propose a
019            constructive framework to implement it, based on the notion of language games.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   On the path between now and artificial superhuman intelligence (ASI; Morris et al., 2023; Grace
025   et al., 2024) lies a tipping point, namely when the bulk of a system’s improvement in capabilities
026   is driven by itself instead of human sources of data, labels, or preferences (which can only scale
027
      so far). As yet, few systems exhibit such recursive self-improvement, so now is a prudent time to
      discuss and characterize what it is, and what it entails.
028
029   We focus on one end of the spectrum, the clearest but not the most practical one, namely pure
030   self-contained settings of ‘Socratic’ learning, closed systems without the option to collect new in-
031   formation from the external world. We articulate conditions, pitfalls and upper limits, as well as a
032   concrete path towards building such systems, based on the notion of language games.
033   The central aim of this position paper is to clarify terminology and frame the discussion, with an
034   emphasis on the long run. It is not to propose new algorithms, nor survey past literature; we pay no
035   heed to near-term feasibility or constraints. We start with a flexible and general framing, and refine
036   and instantiate these definitions over the course of the paper.
037
038   D EFINITIONS
039
040   Consider a closed system (no inputs, no outputs) that evolves over time (see Figure 1 for an illus-
041   tration). Within the system is an entity with inputs and outputs, called agent, that also changes over
042   time. External to the system is an observer whose purpose is to assess the performance of the
043   agent. If performance keeps increasing, we call this system-observer pair an improvement process.
044   The dynamics of this process are driven by both the agent and its surrounding system, but setting
045   clear agent boundaries is required to make evaluation well-defined: in fact an agent is what can be
046   unambiguously evaluated. Similarly, for separation of concerns, the observer is deliberately located
047   outside of the system: As the system is closed, the observer’s assessment cannot feed back into
048   the system. Hence, the agent’s learning feedback must come from system-internal proxies such as
049   losses, reward functions, preference data, or critics.
050   The simplest type of performance metric is a scalar score that can be measured in finite time, that
051   is, on (an aggregation of) episodic tasks. Mechanistically, the observer can measure performance
052   in two ways, by passively observing the agent’s behaviour within the system (if all pertinent tasks
053   occur naturally), or by copy-and-probe evaluations where it confronts a cloned copy of the agent
      with interactive tasks of its choosing.

074   Without loss of generality, the elements within an agent can be partitioned into three types: Fixed
075   elements are unaffected by learning, such as its substrate or unmodifiable code. Transient elements
076   do not carry over between episodes, or across to evaluation (e.g., activations, the state of a random
077   number generator). And finally learned elements (e.g., weights, parameters, knowledge) change
078   based on a feedback signal, and their evolution maps to performance differences (Lu et al., 2023).
079   We can distinguish improvement processes by their implied lifetime; some are open-ended and
080   keep improving without limit (Hughes et al., 2024), while others converge onto their asymptotic
081
      performance after some finite time.1

083   2       T HREE N ECESSARY C ONDITIONS FOR S ELF - IMPROVEMENT
084
085   Self-improvement is an improvement process as defined above, but with the additional criterion
086   that the agent’s own outputs (actions) influence its future learning. In other words, systems in which
087   agents shape (some of) their own experience stream, potentially enabling unbounded improvement
088   in a closed system. This setting may look familiar to readers from the reinforcement learning com-
089   munity (RL; Sutton, 2018): RL agents’ behaviour changes the data distribution it learns on, which
090   in turn affects its behaviour policy, and so on. Another prototypical instance of a self-improvement
091
      process is self-play, where the system (often a symmetric game) slots the agent into the roles of both
      player and opponent, to generate an unlimited experience stream annotated with feedback (who
092
      won?) that provides direction for ever-increasing skill-learning.
093
094   From its connection to RL, we can derive necessary conditions for self-improvement to work, and
095   help clarify some assumptions about the system. The first two conditions, feedback and coverage,
096   are about feasibility in principle, the third (scale) is about practice.
097
098   2.1       F EEDBACK
099
100
      Feedback is what gives direction to learning; without it, the process is merely one of self-
      modification. In a closed system where the true purpose resides in the external observer, but can
101
      not be accessed directly, feedback can only come from a proxy. This creates the fundamental chal-
102
      lenge for system-internal feedback is be aligned with the observer, and remain aligned throughout
103   the process. It places a significant burden on the system at set-up time, with the most common pitfall
104   being a poorly designed critic or reward function that becomes exploitable over time, resulting in
105   a process that deviates from the observer’s intent. RL’s famed capability for self-correction is not
106   applicable here: what can self-correct is behaviour given feedback, but not feedback itself. Addi-
107

      tionally, ideal feedback should be efficient, i.e., contain enough information (not too sparse, not too
109   noisy, not too delayed) for learning to be feasible within the time horizon of the system.

      2.2    C OVERAGE
112
113   By definition, a self-improving agent determines the distribution of data it learns from. To prevent
114   issues like collapse, drift, exploitation or overfitting, it needs to preserve2 sufficient coverage of the
115   data distribution everywhere the observer cares about. In most interesting cases, where performance
116   includes a notion of generalisation, that target distribution is not given (the test tasks are withheld),
117   so the system needs to be set up to intrinsically seek coverage, a sub-process classically called
118   exploration (Ladosz et al., 2022). Note that aligned feedback is not enough for this on its own:
119   even if a preferred behaviour is never ranked lower than a dispreferred one, that is not tantamount to
120
      guaranteeing that the agent will find the preferred behaviour.
121
122   2.3    S CALE
123
      The research field of RL has produced a lot of detailed knowledge about how to train agents, which
124
      algorithms work in which circumstances, an abundance of neat tricks that address practical concerns,
125
      as well as theoretical results that characterize convergence, learning dynamics, rates of progress,
126   etc. It would be futile to try and summarize such a broad body of work here. However, one general
127   observation that matters for our argument is that ‘RL works at scale’: in other words, when scaling
128   up experience and compute sufficiently, even relatively straightforward RL algorithms can solve
129   problems previously thought out of reach (high-profile examples include: Tesauro et al., 1995; Mnih
130   et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2016; 2018; Vinyals et al., 2019; AlphaProof & AlphaGeometry, 2024).
131   For any specific, well-defined practical problem, the details matter (and differ), and greatly impact
132   the efficiency of the learning dynamics; but the asymptotic outcome seems a foregone conclusion.
133   The ‘bitter lesson’ of Sutton (2019) argues a related point: betting on scaling up computation (as
134
      opposed to building in human knowledge) has consistently paid off in the history of AI. Hence, with
      an availability of compute that keeps expanding, the resource constraints of agents (memory and
135
      compute) may be a transient concern; not all inefficiencies need to be fixed fully.3

138   3     S OCRATIC L EARNING
139
140   The specific type of self-improvement process we consider here is recursive self-improvement,
141   where the agent’s inputs and outputs are compatible (i.e., live in the same space), and outputs become
142   future inputs.4 This is more restrictive but less mediated than the general case where outputs merely
143
      influence the input distribution, most commonly instantiated by a (complex) environment that maps
      agent outputs into inputs. This type of recursion is an attribute of many open-ended processes, and
144
      open-ended improvement is arguably a central feature of ASI (see Hughes et al., 2024). On the other
145
      hand, compatibility is less restrictive than homoiconic self-modification, see Section 6.
146
147   An excellent example of such a compatible space of inputs and outputs is language. A vast range
148   of human behaviours are mediated by, and well-expressed,5 in language, especially in cognitive
149
      domains (which are definitionally part of ASI). As argued by Chalmers (2024) and a few centuries
      of rationalists before him (Cottingham, 1988), language may well be sufficient for thinking and
150
      understanding, and not require sensory grounding. Plus, language has the neat property of being a
151
      soup of abstractions, encoding many levels of the conceptual hierarchy in a shared space (see also
152   Colas et al., 2022). A related feature of language is its extendability, i.e., it is possible to develop new
153

165   reason ‘manually’ through mathematical expressions when doing mental arithmetic, so can natural
166   language agents (OpenAI et al., 2024). And of course, it does not hurt that AI competence on
167   language domains has drastically improved recently, with a lot of momentum since the rise of LLMs.
168   Early instances of LLM-mediated recursive self-improvement can be glimpsed in the meta-prompts
169   of Fernando et al. (2023), the ‘action programs’ in Voyager’s skill library Wang et al. (2023), and
170   most recently, the self-reviewing, paper-generating ‘AI scientist’ (Lu et al., 2024).
171   For the remainder of the paper, we will use ‘Socratic learning’ to refer to a recursive self-
172   improvement process that operates in language space. The name is alluding to Socrates’ approach
173   of finding or refining knowledge through questioning dialogue and repeated language interactions,
174   but, notably, without going out to collect observations in the real world—mirroring our emphasis on
175   the system being closed. We encourage the reader to imagine an unbroken process of deliberation
176   among a circle of philosophers, maybe starting with Socrates and his disciples, but expanding and
177
      continuing undisturbed for millennia: what cultural artifacts, what knowledge, what wisdom could
      such a process have produced by now?6 And then, consider a question that seems paradoxical at
178
      first: In principle, how can a closed system produce open-ended improvement?

181       E XAMPLE
182       To help make these ideas more concrete, we describe a hypothetical but not a priori implausible
183       system (cf. Poesia et al., 2024). Consider the domain of mathematical statements (a subset
184       of language).a The observer’s performance metric is binary: has a proof for the Riemann
185       hypothesis been found? The agent reads and writes mathematical statements and proofs (which
186       are compatible input/output spaces). The system is closed, and contains the agent plus:
187               • a proof verifier (e.g., Lean)
188               • a collection C of theorems or conjectures.
189
                  • a proxy reward for the agent: +1 for each verified new proof of a statement in C.
190
                  • a second collection L of lemmas (or subgoals), initially empty.
191
192       The system allows the agent to produce proofs, verify them, formulate new statements, and
193       add those to L. Over time, the agent may learn to simplify and decompose existing theorems,
194       accumulate lemmas in L, learn to formulate lemmas that are more and more reusable, and
195
          increase the fraction of theorems in C for which it can produce valid proofs. It self-improves.
          At some point, the expanding frontier of verified mathematical knowledge reaches a proof of
196
          the Riemann hypothesis, and the observer, satisfied, stops the system.

      4     T HE F UNDAMENTAL L IMITS OF S OCRATIC L EARNING
203
      Among the three necessary conditions for self-improvement, two of them, coverage and feedback
204
      apply to Socratic learning in principle, and remain irreducible. To make their implications as clear
205
      as possible, we ignore the third (the scale, practicality and efficiency concerns, see Section 2.3) in
206   this section. We motivate this simplification by taking the long view: if compute and memory keep
207   growing exponentially, scale constraints are but a temporary obstacle. If not, considering a resource-
208   constrained scenario for Socratic learning (akin to studying bounded rationality) may still produce
209   valid high-level insights.
210
      The coverage condition implies that the Socratic learning system must keep generating (language)
211
      data, while preserving or expanding diversity over time. In the LLM age this has come to not
212   seem too far-fetched: We can envision a generative agent initialized with a very broad internet-like
213

      distribution that produces a never-ending stream of novel language utterances. However preventing
217   drift, collapse or just narrowing of the generative distribution in a recursive process may be highly
218   non-trivial (Lewis et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2024).
219
220
      The feedback condition requires the system to (a) continue producing feedback about (some subset
      of) the agent’s outputs, which structurally requires a critic that can assess language, and (b) that
221
      feedback remains sufficiently aligned with the observer’s evaluation metric (Christiano et al., 2018;
222
      Bai et al., 2022b). This is challenging for a number of reasons: Well-defined, grounded metrics
223   in language space are often limited to narrow tasks, while more general-purpose mechanisms like
224   AI-feedback are exploitable, especially so if the input distribution is permitted to shift. For example,
225   none of the current LLM training paradigms have a feedback mechanism that is sufficient for So-
226   cratic learning. Next-token prediction loss is grounded, but insufficiently aligned with downstream
227   usage, and unable to extrapolate beyond the training data. Human preferences are aligned by defini-
228   tion, but prevent learning in a closed system. Caching such preferences into a learned reward model
229   makes it self-contained, but exploitable and potentially misaligned in the long-run, as well as weak
230   on out-of-distribution data.
231   In other words, pure Socratic learning is possible, but it requires broad data generation with a robust
232   and aligned critic. When those conditions hold, however, the ceiling of its potential improvement
233   is only limited by the amount of resource applied. Current research has not established successful
234   recipes for this yet, so the next section endeavours to make a concrete but quite general proposal for
235   how to go about it.

      5       L ANGUAGE G AMES A RE A LL YOU N EED . . .
239
240   Fortunately, language, learning and grounding are well-studied topics. A particularly useful concept
241   for us to draw on is Wittgenstein’s notion of language games.7 For him, it is not the words that
242
      capture meaning, but only the interactive nature of language can do so. To be concrete here, define
      a language game as an interaction protocol (a set of rules, expressible in code) that specifies the
243
      interaction of one or more agents (‘players’) that have language inputs and language outputs, plus a
244
      scalar scoring function for each player at the end of the game.8
245
246   Language games, thus defined, address the two primary needs of Socratic learning; namely, they
247   provide a scalable mechanism for unbounded interactive data generation and self-play, while au-
248
      tomatically providing an accompanying feedback signal (the score). In fact, they are the logical
      consequence of the coverage and feedback conditions, almost tautologically so: there is no form of
249
      interactive data generation with tractable feedback that is not a language game.9 As a bonus, seeing
250
      the process as one of game-play immediately lets us import the potential of rich strategic diversity
251   arising from multi-agent dynamics (as spelled out in depth in Leibo et al., 2019; Duéñez-Guzmán
252   et al., 2023), which is likely to address at least part of the coverage condition. It also aligns with
253   our intuition that dynamic, social co-construction (e.g., the circle of philosophers) has an edge over
254   the self-talk of a single person that lives for millennia. Pragmatically too, games are a great way
255   to get started, given the vast human track record of creating and honing a vast range of games and
256   player skills (Berne, 1968); with Nguyen (2020) framing this richness as a demonstration of the flu-
257   idity of human agency and (local) motivations. Derrida might even argue that under the right lens,
258   discourse is already structured as a game.10 Colas et al. (2022) discuss a related set of ideas under
259
      the terminology of Vygotskian autotelic agents; while they do not assume a closed system, many
      of their ‘internalised social interactions’ could be cast as language games. A number of common
260
      LLM interaction paradigms are also well represented as language games, for example debate (Irving
261
      et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023), role-play (Vezhnevets et al., 2023), theory of mind
262   (Kim et al., 2023), negotiation (Lewis et al., 2017; FAIR et al., 2022), jailbreak defense (Zeng et al.,

      . . . I F YOU H AVE E NOUGH OF T HEM . . .
275
      Returning to our circle of deliberating philosophers: is there any one language game we could imag-
276
      ine them playing for millennia? Instead, maybe, they are more likely to escape a narrow outcome
277   when playing many language games. It turns out that Wittgenstein (him again) proposed this same
278   idea: he adamantly argued against language having a singular essence or function.11
279
280
      Using many narrow but well-defined language games instead of a single universal one resolves a
      key dilemma: For each narrow game, a reliable score function (or critic) can be designed, whereas
281
      getting the single universal one right is more elusive (even if possible in principle, as argued by
282
      Silver et al., 2021).12 From that lens, the full process of Socratic learning is then a meta-game,
283   which schedules the language games that the agent plays and learns from (which is an ‘infinite’
284   game as per Carse (2011)). We posit that in principle, this idea is sufficient to address the issue of
285   coverage (Section 2.2). Concretely, if a proxy of the observer’s distribution of interest is available
286   (e.g., a validation set of tasks), that can be used to drive exploration in the meta-game.

      . . . A ND YOU P LAY THE R IGHT O NES
289
290   Socrates was famously sentenced to death and executed for ‘corrupting the youth.’ We can take this
291   as a hint that a Socratic process is not guaranteed to remain aligned with external observers’ intent.
292   Language games as a mechanism do not side-step this either, but they arguably reduce the precision
293   needed: instead of a critic that is aligned at the fine granularity of individual inputs and outputs, all
294   that is needed is a ‘meta-critic’ that can judge which games should be played: maybe no individ-
295   ual language game is perfectly aligned, but what is doable is to filter the many games according to
296   whether they make an overall net-positive contribution (when played and learned about). Further-
297
      more, the usefulness of a game does not need to be assessed a priori, but can be judged post-hoc,
      after playing it for a while. Relatedly, a beneficial asymmetry is that it may be much easier to detect
298
      deviant emergent behaviour post-hoc than to design games that prevent it. All of these properties
299
      are forms of structural leniency that give the language games framework a vast potential to scale.
300
301   Stepping out of our assumption of the closed system for a moment: when we actually build ASI, we
302   will almost surely want to not optimistically trust that alignment is preserved, but instead continually
303
      check the process as carefully as possible, and probably intervene and adjust the system throughout
      training. In that case, explicitly exposing the distribution of games (accompanied by interpretable
304
      game descriptions and per-game learning curves) as knobs to the designer may be a useful level of
305
      abstraction.

308   6    H IGHER - LEVEL R ECURSIONS
309
310   So far, we discussed the minimal necessary form of recursion, a form of circularity that feeds (some
311   of) the agent’s outputs back to it. Within the framework of language games, two further types of
312   recursion come to mind. The first idea is to tell the agent which game it is playing, and give it the
313   choice to switch games, which game to switch to, and when to switch (Pislar et al., 2021). This is
314   related to hierarchical or goal-conditioned RL, providing the agent with more autonomy and a more
315   abstract action space. While shifting more responsibility into the agent, this setup could dramatically
316   improve outcomes, as compared to a hardwired game-selection process outside of the agent—but of
317
      course this extra freedom could introduce additional risks of collapse or misalignment.

      Second, as games are interaction protocols that can be fully represented as code, they can live in
325   a language agent’s output space. Consequently, the agent could learn to generate games for itself
326   to play.13 Initially, it could simply produce local variations of exiting games, which adapt the dif-
327   ficulty level of theme, later on crafting recombinations of games, and ultimately ending up with de
328   novo generation (Todd et al., 2024). This leads to second-order coverage concerns, in the space of
329   language games instead of the space of language, to be addressed with filtering, prioritization, or
330   curricula (Jaderberg et al., 2019; Parker-Holder et al., 2022).
331   The combination of both of these recursive extensions is an empowered agent that plays the full
332   meta-game of how to improve itself via game generation and play. While appealingly elegant, this
333   meta-game lacks the well-defined feedback mechanism of the inner language games, and it is an
334   open research question whether established proxy metrics like learning progress would be sufficient
335   to preserve both the coverage and alignment properties over time.

337   S ELF - REFERENTIAL S YSTEMS
338
339   The next and final step of recursion is recursive self-modification, that is, agents whose actions
340   change their own internals, not merely influencing their input stream. These methods live on a
341   spectrum characterized by the scope of what can be modified in such a way (and which elements
342   remain fixed), and what amount of introspection, or access to its own workings, is available to the
343
      agent (Schaul & Schmidhuber, 2010). At the extreme end, a fully self-referential agent can observe
      and modify any14 aspect of itself, without indirection. In principle, this type of agent has the highest
344
      capability ceiling; as asymptotic performance is capped by its fixed structure, unfreezing some of
345
      it and making it modifiable can only increase that upper bound—in particular, it is always possible
346   to set the newly-flexible parameters to how they were while frozen, to recover the performance of
347   the less-flexible agent (modulo learning dynamics that could get into the way). Past proposals for
348   how to design self-referential systems were not (intended to be) practical (e.g., Schmidhuber, 1993;
349   2003; Schmidhuber et al., 1997; Kirsch & Schmidhuber, 2022), but modern LLMs’ competence in
350   code comprehension and generation is changing the playing field and may soon move these ideas
351   from esoteric to critical.

      7        C ONCLUSION : O PEN - ENDED S OCRATIC L EARNING IS P OSSIBLE
354
355
      We set out to investigate how far recursive self-improvement in a closed system can take us on
356
      the path to AGI, and are now ready to conclude on an optimistic note. In principle, the potential
357
      of Socratic learning is high, and the challenges we identified (feedback and coverage) are well
358   known. The framework of language games provides a constructive starting point that addresses
359   both, and helps clarify how a practical research agenda could look like. We leave the fleshing out
360   of that roadmap to future work, but the overall direction is becoming apparent. In particular, an
361   understudied dimension is the breadth and richness of the many such language games. We think a
362   great place to start is with processes capable of open-ended game generation. And not without seeing
363   the irony, we propose all these ideas to scrutiny within an academic setting instead of resorting to
364   self-talk in a closed system.
