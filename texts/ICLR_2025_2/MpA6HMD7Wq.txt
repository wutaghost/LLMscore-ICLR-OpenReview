009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011                Until recently, behind every algorithmic advance in machine learning was a hu-
012                man researcher. Now, however, algorithms can be meta-learned automatically,
013                with little human input. However, to be truly useful, such algorithms must gen-
014                eralise beyond their training distribution. This is especially challenging in re-
015                inforcement learning (RL), where transferring algorithms between environments
016                with vastly different dynamics is difficult and training on diverse environments of-
017
                   ten requires prohibitively expensive large-scale data collection. Learned optimisa-
                   tion is a branch of algorithmic discovery that meta-learns optimiser update rules.
018
                   Learned optimisers can be classified into two groups: black-box algorithms, where
019
                   the optimiser is a neural network; or symbolic algorithms, where the optimiser is
020                represented using mathematical functions or code. While some claim that sym-
021                bolic algorithms generalise better than black-box ones (Chen et al., 2023), testing
022                such assertions is complicated by the fact that symbolic algorithms typically in-
023                clude additional hyperparameters, and thus their evaluation is done many-shot.
024                This is an unfair comparison with the zero-shot evaluation of black-box optimis-
025                ers. In this work, we build a pipeline to discover symbolic optimisers which are
026                hyperparameter-free, enabling a fair comparison of the generalisation of symbolic
027                optimisers with that of an open-source state-of-the-art black-box optimiser trained
028
                   for RL1 . Based on our analysis, we propose suggestions to improve the symbolic
                   optimiser discovery pipeline for RL, with an overall objective of reducing the need
029
                   for hyperparameter tuning to train an agent.

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Improvements to optimisation algorithms have driven machine learning to new heights over the
035   past few decades. The introduction of components like gradient momentum, second order momen-
036   tum (Nesterov, 1983; Kingma & Ba, 2017) and adaptive learning rates (Kingma & Ba, 2017; Zhuang
037   et al., 2020) has enabled swifter and more stable convergence, while learning rate annealing has im-
038   proved the fidelity of converged solutions. Recent evidence (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Chen et al.,
039   2021; Metz et al., 2022c; Chen et al., 2023; Goldie et al., 2024) suggests that the improvement of
040   optimisers could be automated via learned optimisation. In learned optimisation, developing new
041   optimisation algorithms is itself a meta-learning process based on data.
042   Approaches to learned optimisation fall into two camps. Most work (e.g., (Metz et al., 2022a; Kirsch
043   & Schmidhuber, 2022; Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Wichrowska et al., 2017; Goldie et al., 2024)) re-
044   places the optimiser, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017), with a black-box function using a neural
045   network. In this scenario, the weights of the network are updated in an outer loop to maximise the
046   performance of a trained model at the end of an inner loop. By contrast, some recent work (Chen
047   et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024a) focuses on discovering symbolic optimisation algorithms. In this
048
      case, the optimiser is represented by a set of mathematical equations or programming instructions.
      In general, interest in symbolic algorithm discovery has grown in the past couple of years (Romera-
049
      Paredes et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a) due to the advent of large language models (OpenAI et al.,
050
      2024; Dubey et al., 2024, LLMs). There are arguments in favour of both approaches: black-box al-
051   gorithms may be easier to work with (Goldie et al., 2024), while symbolic optimisers may generalise
052   better (Chen et al., 2023). However, there exists little study into the veracity of these claims.

      Furthermore, direct comparison between the approaches is complicated by the fact that they target
055   subtly different problems; black-box optimisers are typically evaluated zero-shot without any tune-
056   able hyperparameters, whereas symbolic optimisers such as Lion (Chen et al., 2023) tune hyperpa-
057   rameters per-task, making evaluation many-shot. Therefore, it is hard to compare these different
058   paradigms like-for-like based on current literature.
059
      The need for general optimisation algorithms is exacerbated in reinforcement learning (Sutton &
060
      Barto, 2018, RL) due to its many idiosyncratic issues which make optimisation challenging. In par-
061   ticular, RL is very sensitive to hyperparameters (Eimer et al., 2023) which can cause catastrophic
062   instability if they are not correctly tuned. This instability may stem from the fact that RL often
063   uses algorithms imported from supervised learning, motivating the development of RL-specific ap-
064   proaches (Henderson et al., 2018; Sarigül & Avci, 2017). For instance, many conventional optimis-
065   ers, like Adam Kingma & Ba (2017), are designed for stationary learning tasks and are thus ill-suited
066   for the non-stationarity of RL (Igl et al., 2021; Bengio et al., 2021). Learned optimisers tailored for
067   RL show promise in addressing these issues (Lan et al., 2024; Goldie et al., 2024).
068   However, simply relying on a large meta-task diversity to enable generalisation across RL is im-
069   practical. For anything beyond simple environments, sampling in RL is expensive. Therefore, find-
070   ing learned optimisation strategies which demonstrate generalisation, whilst maintaining a limited
071   meta-training cost, would significantly improve the practicality of RL. In this work, we compare
072   the generalisation capabilities of a pretrained, black-box optimiser for RL (Goldie et al., 2024) with
073   a roughly equivalent symbolic optimiser discovered using an evolutionary process based around
074   LLMs. We focus on a regime in which optimisers can only be learned from a small number of en-
075
      vironments; we believe this represents a scenario of greater interest than training in a distribution of
      gridworlds, which has been a previous focus for generalisation (Goldie et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024)
076
      but does not transfer well to the modern LLM-driven discovery pipeline. In doing so, we explore
077
      the question of whether black-box or symbolic optimisers are actually best for generalisation across
078   a number of axes, including to different environments and to longer training lengths. We use these
079   findings to recommend promising directions for future work in this field, thus providing a pathway
080   to unlock truly general learned optimisation algorithms.

082   2   BACKGROUND
083
084   Optimisation Optimisation is ubiquitous throughout machine learning. Given a general training
085   objective fθ (·), there is an extensive set of optimisation algorithms whose goal is to guide θ, a
086   model’s parameters, to the optimal θ∗ . Most fundamental of optimisers is gradient descent, where θ
087
      is updated iteratively towards negative gradient as θt+1 ← θt − η∇θ f (·), using a step-size η.
088   A number of augmentations are frequently applied to gradient descent to enable quicker conver-
089   gence, less noisy updates or improved asymptotic performance. For instance, modern optimisers
090   like Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) and RMSProp (Tieleman et al., 2012) use momentum, a time-
091   based moving average of gradients or updates which provides more consistent updates over training.
092   Similarly, learning rate annealing or warmup change the step size over time to provide closer con-
093
      vergence to the optimum by the end of training, or improved stability at the beginning of training,
      respectively (Robbins, 1951; Gotmare et al., 2018).
094

097   at each discrete timestep t, samples an action at ∈ A based on the current state st ∈ S (where
098   s0 ∼ ρ). After sampling an action, the agent transitions to the next state st+1 ∈ S according
099   to a transition distribution T (st+1 |st , at ) and receives a reward according to the reward function
100   R(st , at ). The policy is trained to maximise the discounted expected return, J π , based on the
101
      discount factor γ ∈ [0, 1), which is defined over a fixed length episode as

      Optimisation Difficulties in RL Goldie et al. (2024) discuss three optimisation difficulties present
109   in RL: plasticity loss (Lyle et al., 2023; 2022), a phenomenon in which neural networks lose the
110   ability to learn when given new data; exploration, where the optimiser must escape local optima from
111   the agent being trapped in a localised state-action space; and non-stationarity (Igl et al., 2021), which
112   arises as the input and output distributions in RL are continuously changing. OPEN incorporates a
113   number of features to tackle each individual problem. To be specific:
114           • For plasticity, OPEN conditions on neuron dormancy (Sokar et al., 2023), a metric which
115             measures what proportion of a layer’s activation comes from a specific neuron. Near-zero
116             dormancy neurons are dormant and need to be reactivated. OPEN also learns separate
117             update rules for each layer by conditioning on layer proportion.
118           • For nonstationarity, OPEN conditions on two timescales: batch proportion, or progress
119             through epochs with the current batch of data; and training proportion (Jackson et al.,
120             2023a), meaning how far through the training horizon optimisation is.
121           • To boost exploration, OPEN introduces stochasticity of a learned variance to the update.
122             This enables similar exploration behaviour to parameter space noise (Plappert et al., 2018)
123             or noisy nets (Fortunato et al., 2019) while also incidentally helping with dormancy.

125   3    R ELATED W ORK
126   Meta-Learning Algorithms Meta-learning intends to replace handcrafted algorithms with ones
127   learned from data. Though some approaches use meta-gradients which are backpropagated through
128   training episodes (e.g., (Lan et al., 2024; Oh et al., 2020)), this is impractical in our setting. Firstly,
129   meta-learning in RL requires long horizon rollouts, where untruncated backpropagation experiences
130   exploding or vanishing gradients but truncating biases towards greedy algorithms (Wu et al., 2018;
131   Metz et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2022b). Secondly, with a symbolic optimiser, it is not obvious how
132
      to project gradients on to the non-numerical symbols of our algorithm, requiring more complex
      techniques (e.g. (Kuang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024)).
133
134   Evolutionary methods (Rechenberg, 1973; De Jong, 2006) provide an alternative. These are
135   derivative-free optimisation methods which mutate and evaluate a populations of candidates. Com-
136   mon evolutionary methods include genetic algorithms (Such et al., 2018), covariance matrix adap-
137   tation (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), evolution strategies (Salimans et al., 2017) or, in the symbolic
138
      case, genetic programming (Koza, 1992). Evolution involves sequentially sampling population
      members, randomly changing their parameters and evaluating the final performance of the candi-
139
      date. By optimising based on the final evaluation, rather than backpropagating through the rollout,
140
      evolutionary methods avoid many of the issues with meta-gradients.
141
142   Since the advent of LLMs, a new form of symbolic evolution has emerged (Romera-Paredes et al.,
143   2024). Rather than applying random mutations, recent methods have replaced the evolutionary
144
      system with LLMs that suggest edits and reason about performance to guide search (Lu et al.,
      2024a; Meyerson et al., 2024; Lehman et al., 2022; Shojaee et al., 2024). This uses an LLM’s
145
      prior knowledge to make ‘intelligent’ changes, in effect limiting the search to reasonable if not
146
      limited edits. Despite its recent invention, this technique has led to impressive results in function
147   discovery (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) or solving symbolic regression tasks (Shojaee et al., 2024).
148
149
      Learned Optimisation Learning to optimise (Metz et al., 2020; 2022c;a; Chen et al., 2023; Goldie
150   et al., 2024, L2O) automates the discovery of better optimisers by meta-learning the algorithms.
151   Generally, L2O replaces the optimiser with a neural network which conditions on the gradient,
152   and potentially extra features, and outputs an update for each parameter in the training model.
153   This method has proven effective in supervised and unsupervised learning (Metz et al., 2022c), but
154   naı̈vely fails to transfer to RL. Due to the opportunity of learning specialised optimisation algo-
155   rithms, OPEN (Goldie et al., 2024) and Optim4RL (Lan et al., 2024) L2O directly for RL. This is
156   justified by many works suggesting RL-specific algorithms are warranted (Henderson et al., 2018;
157   Bengio et al., 2021; Sarigül & Avci, 2017). Whereas Optim4RL attempts to L2O in RL by con-
158
      straining the structure of the update, OPEN targets a number of difficulties present only in RL. Un-
      fortunately, while these works have demonstrated signs of life for generalisation, there is little work
159
      exploring whether black-box optimisation is the best route to discover truly generalist optimisers.
160
161   An alternative approach is Lion (Chen et al., 2023), an optimiser discovered by symbolic evolution.
      However, to enable comparison between black-box and symbolic optimisation, we make a number

170
      cently (Song et al., 2024b). FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) demonstrated the validity of
171
      this approach by prompting an LLM to write functions for specific tasks. Like FunSearch, many
172   works have synthesised the expressiveness of code with the creativity of LLMs: Hu et al. (2024) use

174   for preference optimisation in LLMs; and Lehman et al. (2022) incorporate Quality-Diversity ap-
175   proaches (Mouret & Clune, 2015) to produce different robot morphologies. While a common thread
176   exists between these works and ours – using LLMs as a mutation operator for evolution – our discov-

178   LLM can be used to handle additional inputs, defined by OPEN, with natural language descriptions.
179   Finally, we are approaching this setting from a purely analytical perspective.

181   4   M OTIVATION
182
      To motivate our study into the generalisation capabilities of symbolic and black-box optimisers, we
183
      briefly compare the two in terms of potential advantages, grounded in both literature and intuition.
184
185   Black-Box Optimisers Since black-box optimisers are principally neural networks, they have a
186   number of inherent advantages. Firstly, since they typically use small networks, they can easily be
187   trained with evolution (Salimans et al., 2017) to avoid issues of short-term bias from truncated meta-
188
      gradients (Wu et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2022b). This does, however, have the issue of high memory
      usage and training sample complexity since each meta-update needs a number of full training loops
189
      equal to the population size. Though GPU-vectorisation (Bradbury et al., 2018) helps speed up this
190
      training dramatically (Lu et al., 2022b), it can require both high-end hardware and easy-to-sample
191   environments which may not be practical.
192
193   Also, the simplicity of introducing additional inputs to black-box optimisers was demonstrated by
194
      OPEN, as well as an ease to learn interactions between input variables. This ability to easily scale
      with inputs may make black-box optimisers the best option in some settings.
195
196   Finally, due to their iterative meta-learning process, black-box optimisers can converge Goldie et al.
197   (2024). This is in contrast to symbolic optimisers, which may not converge due to the mechanisms
198   of symbolic evolution. This convergence can have advantages – training is predictable and usually
199   stable – but can also lead to the optimiser being trapped in subpar optima.
200   Symbolic Optimisers Though symbolic discovery of optimisers is relatively unexplored, it has a
201   number of potential advantages. It is worth noting, however, that we focus on a novel evaluation
202   regime which aligns symbolic and black-box optimisation. Whereas Lion (Chen et al., 2023) needed
203   tuning for its hyperparameters, black-box optimisers are applied zero-shot to new environments.
204   Therefore, we concern ourselves with symbolic algorithms which do not use hyperparameters.
205
      In this paper, we assess how black-box and symbolic optimisation algorithms generalise. Chen
206   et al. (2023) suggest, without justification, that symbolic algorithms should generalise better, which
207   seems intuitive. Symbolic optimisers are usually simpler; whereas Lion is 8 lines of code, OPEN
208   uses up to ∼ 4000 parameters, increasing the opportunity for overfitting. Also, symbolic optimisers
209   must start from something, meaning they can be initialised from pre-existing optimisers.
210
      A key advantage of symbolic algorithm discovery is that LLMs can interface into the discovery
211
      pipeline to improve the search efficiency, leaning on their vast knowledge-base to find new algo-
212   rithms Lu et al. (2024a); Romera-Paredes et al. (2024). This also gives a large amount of control to
213   the human-in-the-loop. As a researcher can describe design specifications in natural language, the
214   search can be biased towards algorithms based on design requirements. We find this can help with
215   including additional inputs to the algorithms, such as those from OPEN, even if the inputs are not
      included in the LLM’s training data.

      5      T HE S YMBOLIC O PTIMISER D ISCOVERY P IPELINE

      We design a symbolic discovery loop to enable like-for-like comparison with OPEN which incorpo-
237   rates all of the features proposed in OPEN and described in section 2. We focus our comparison on
238   the ‘Multi-Task Training’ setting from Goldie et al. (2024), where we meta-train on a small number
239   of environments from MinAtar (Young & Tian, 2019; Lange, 2022). We believe this scenario is par-
240   ticularly interesting due to its correspondence with learning from a small number of fast proxy-tasks
241   that approximate an ultimate objective.
242
      We use an LLM in place of standard mutation in our system for the reasons mentioned in section
243   4. This lets us describe the inputs from OPEN in natural language to direct the search to ‘reason-
244   able’ suggestions, avoiding a potentially more expensive and sample-inefficient random search, like
245   Lion (Chen et al., 2023). However, LLMs can be notoriously fickle (Anagnostidis & Bulian, 2024;
246   Gu et al., 2022). Therefore, we introduce a number of design decisions, described in this section, to
247   improve the system’s robustness. While we use GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024) in this work, we be-
248   lieve that our system should also maintain robustness for weaker, open-source models (e.g. (Dubey
249   et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024)). We report discovery hyperparameters in Appendix A.

251   5.1      OVERVIEW
252   Figure 1 shows our discovery pipeline, which is similar to a number of ‘LLM-Discovery’ meth-
253   ods (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a;b; Hu et al., 2024; Faldor et al., 2024), visually.
254   At the start of the process, an archive is initialised with a set of candidate optimiser functions. After
255   these are evaluated, one optimiser is selected for a generation of refinement, which involves itera-
256   tive mutation by an LLM, followed by evaluation and insertion to the archive, for N steps. After
257   refinement is complete, a new optimiser is sampled and the process repeats.
258   Below, we introduce high-level design decisions which are detailed in the remainder of section 5.
259
260   Initialising The Archive We follow DiscoPOP (Lu et al., 2024a) and Lion (Chen et al., 2023) by
261   initialising training from a small set of optimisers. However, whereas DiscoPOP use pre-established
262   loss functions, there is little precedent for hyperparameter-free optimisation. Therefore, we intro-
263   duce a small number of hyperparameter-less optimisers by hand. These are designed to be flexible,
264
      while ensuring they don’t fail catastrophically in the training environments.
265   Selection Our pipeline periodically samples a new optimiser to refine at each generation, align-
266   ing closely to traditional evolutionary computation. This contrasts with, say, DiscoPOP (Lu et al.,
267   2024a), which uses one long conversation with an LLM. By using the LLM more sparingly, this
268   approach has the added benefit of potentially letting our system operate with less powerful lan-
269   guage models. We select the best optimisers from the archive with probability p, and select random
      optimisers from the archive with an exploration probability 1 − p.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Mutation We split mutation over two LLMs: a thinker, which proposes a new idea based on
271   the current optimiser’s performance; and a coder, which implements the proposed changes. This
272   separation ensures faithful interpretations of ideas in the implementation and provides additional
273   user control with the different prompts. Our thinker prompt also includes examples of performant
274   optimisers in each environment.
275
276   Evaluation We evaluate optimisers on full-length RL environments at every refinement step. We
277
      track the final return and return area-under-the-curve of each optimiser for the thinker’s context
      to enable in-context reasoning. To sidestep the problem of score aggregation over multiple envi-
278
      ronments faced by OPEN, we simply give the LLM returns for all environments and prompt it to
279
      maximise performance in all.

281   5.2     I NITIALISATION
282
283
      Similar to recent works (Lu et al., 2024a; Faldor et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023),
      we initialise the archive of optimisers to a set of reasonable functions. However, given the scarcity
284
      of research on hyperparameter-free optimisation, the selection of initial optimisers is not straight-
285
      forward. To address this, we create a few sensible optimisers to kickstart learning. In most cases,
286   we write simple functions which have scaled relative changes to weights, though we also include a
287   simple LLM-proposed function for diversity.
288
289
      All optimisers follow the same design principles: they are simple, so that there are a large number
      of possible directions to improve them; they are diverse, so that they can lead to very different opti-
290
      misers after refinement; and they are hyperparameter-free, meaning that any values are fixed for all
291
      environments. Notably, our initial optimisers only depend on the parameter value and the gradient,
292   allowing the LLM to discover creative ways to use the additional inputs from OPEN without undue
293   bias. We include all of the initial optimisers in appendix B.

295   5.3     E VOLUTION
296
      For discovery, we blend LLM-based discovery algorithms with more conventional evolution
297
      (e.g. (Koza, 1992)). In doing so, we exploit the reasoning capabilities of LLMs to propose intel-
298   ligent in-context changes while leveraging population-based evolution. The process runs as follows:
299
300   At the start of a new generation, we sample an ‘initial’ optimiser (section 5.3.1) and set of context
301
      optimisers (section 5.3.3) and prompt the LLMs to make small optimiser edits for a fixed number
      of refinement steps, N . At each refinement step, we evaluate the optimiser on all RL environments
302
      after a full RL inner-loop. Like OPEN, we use PPO Schulman et al. (2017) as the RL algorithm.
303
      After each generation, we add all evaluated optimisers to the archive and sample a new initialisation
304   and context. Therefore, like Faldor et al. (2024), our archive grows over meta-training.
305
306   5.3.1    S AMPLING N EW O PTIMISERS
307
      We sample a new ‘base’ optimiser each generation. To balance exploration and exploitation in
308   our discovery process, we mostly sample good optimisers while occasionally selecting randomly to
309   promote diversity. However, the notion of good or bad is not black and white when considering
310   multiple environments of different reward scales. Naı̈vely averaging returns will prioritise environ-
311   ments which have a large reward scale, while normalising by, say, Adam’s (Kingma & Ba, 2017)
312   performance biases selection to environments where Adam underperforms (Goldie et al., 2024).
313
      Instead, we use the average of per-environment rankings, based on return, over the population to
314   measure how successful an algorithm is. In addition to scale-invariance, this has the benefit of
315   weeding out optimisers which overfit to one environment, aiding robustness. After calculating the
316   average rankings for the population, we select high-ranking optimisers with a probability p and sam-
317   ple from the full population with probability (1 − p). In this work, we set p = 0.8 to balance sample
318   efficiency (mostly starting from a performant optimiser) with diversity (occasionally sampling ran-
319   dom optimisers).
320
      5.3.2    M UTATION
321
322   We find that there is an occasional disparity between the proposal and implementation from LLMs
323   when prompted naı̈vely. This hurts interpretability; it is not possible to tell what changes the LLM
      is making purely by observing the conversation. Therefore, we augment our system into a 2-LLM


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      setup by dividing out thinking and coding. The thinker has the responsibility of suggesting changes
325   to the currently sampled optimiser and explaining why this change might be helpful. The coder has
326   the task of converting the proposed idea into a code edit and implementing a syntactically correct,
327   faithful python function. As an additional benefit, this allows different prompting strategies for each
328   operation, giving the user additional control over the discovery trajectory.
329
330   5.3.3   P ROMPTING
331   Different prompts can lead to vastly different results when using LLMs (Anagnostidis & Bulian,
332   2024; Gu et al., 2022). Here, we discuss the design decisions made in our prompting, and provide
333   examples of the actual prompts in Appendix C.
334
      Difficulties in RL To enable intelligent suggestions based on the problems of RL from OPEN,
335
      described in section 2, we provide a high level overview of each additional input variable and what
336
      typical values might mean.
337
338   Previous Performance To leverage in-context suggestion making, we condition the thinker on
339   the returns of the current optimiser and randomly sampled ‘context optimisers’, which perform well
340   in individual environments. To avoid issues highlighted in Goldie et al. (2024), where aggregating
341   scores between different environments proved difficult, we include final return values for all envi-
342   ronments into the prompt directly without averaging. This encourages the LLM itself to balance
343
      improvements between environments. To boost in-context reasoning further, we also provide values
      for the area-under-the-curve.
344
345   Separating Prompts To ensure fulfilment of their separate roles, we prompt the thinker and coder
346   LLMs differently. The thinker is prompted to produce a new idea based on previous performance
347   while the coder converts the idea into a code update. Whereas the thinker is prompted with a history
348   of optimisers for reasoning, the coder receives only the current optimiser and proposed change to
349   avoid obfuscating its task. Separating thinking and code has been shown to improve performance in
350   other work (Ye et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).

353   generalisation and the necessity of not introducing new hyperparameters. For the coder, we focus

391   7             A SSESSING G ENERALISATION
392
393   Our analysis centres on comparing symbolic discovered optimisers with OPEN to explore the dif-
394
      ference between in- and out-of-distribution behaviour of the two approaches. We focus on meta-
      training with a small number of environments, referred to as Multi-Task Training in Goldie et al.
395
      (2024). This differs to the scenario where one samples from a distribution of simple environments,
396
      such as gridworlds (e.g. (Oh et al., 2020; Jackson et al., 2023b; Goldie et al., 2024). We compare
397   against a pre-trained OPEN model which is available online, and Adam using a fixed standard learn-
398   ing rate of 1e-3. Following standard procedure in learned optimisation (Goldie et al., 2024; Metz
399   et al., 2022c; Lan et al., 2024; Metz et al., 2019) arising from the cost of meta-learning, we discover
400   optimisers from only one seed but run each experiment for multiple seeds. For all results, we report
401   the interquartile mean (IQM) with 95% stratified bootstrap confidence intervals calculated using rli-
402   able, a standard evaluation library (Agarwal et al., 2021). Hyperparameters for all experiments are
403   included in Appendix A. We consider a number of axes for generalisation, described and justified
404   below, which are inspired by the comparison of OPEN and Adam in Goldie et al. (2024).
405
406   Different Training Lengths Due to the cost of learned optimisation, one way to speed up meta-
407
      training could be to learn from shortened inner-loops and generalise to longer runs. However, due
      to the nonstationarity of the optimisers from their time-conditioning, their dynamic behaviour may
408
      not transfer between inner-training lengths.
409
410
      Different Architectures Prior work (Yang et al., 2022) suggests that hyperparameters often do not
411
      transfer between models with different architectures. As such, we explore the ability of the different
412
      optimisers to transfer between agents with different hidden dimensions and activation functions.
413
414
      Different Environments To ensure an optimiser is truly general purpose, it is important to test its
415   performance in unseen environments. This axis of generalisation explores how strongly an optimiser
416   overfits to the dynamics of its training environments.

492   9    A ROADMAP F OR THE F UTURE
493
494
      As demonstrated in Section 7, despite being occasionally outperformed in-distribution, the sym-
495   bolic optimisers were consistently better at generalising out of distribution, echoing the sentiments
496   of Chen et al. (2023). Empirically speaking, symbolic optimisers do not overfit as strongly to their
497   training distribution. Despite this, the drastic outperformance of Adam over the other optimisers
498   in Craftax suggests there is still significant room for improvement in discovering better optimisers.
499   As such, we believe exploring symbolic optimisation discovery is an important future direction for
500   research. In particular, we believe emphasis should be placed on discovering hyperparameter-free
501   optimisers, and evaluation should focus on generalisation to all of the axes discussed in section 7.
502   However, this begs the question: in a field increasingly dominated by LLM-driven discov-
503   ery (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024b), how can we best capitalise on these advance-
504   ments while incorporating components from preexisting black-box literature, such as the analysis
505   and inputs from OPEN. Our discovered optimisers exemplified this issue by failing to take advan-
506   tage of randomness which was beneficial in Goldie et al. (2024). Finding better ways to synthesise
507   these two lines of research may prove a very fruitful direction. We provide some possible directions
508   which may make this possible below.
509   An obvious future direction is to find ways to give additional feedback to the LLM and better cap-
510   italise on their capabilities for more intelligent decision making. For instance, while final return
511   may be the key metric, it offers little in diagnosing any problems with the current optimisation algo-
512   rithm. Instead, prompting with the trajectory of return over training may help an LLM reason about
513   what the shortfalls are with the current optimiser. To this end, more capable language models, like
514   o1-preview (OpenAI, 2024), could help take advantage and reason over these additional sources of
515
      data. Finally, finding better ways to include LLMs into evolutionary systems as intelligent muta-
      tion operators, rather than the LLM being the full algorithm, could ground discovery algorithms in
516
      evolutionary theory and produce more robust discovery algorithms.
517

      10    L IMITATIONS
520
521
522   Due to limited resources, we are only able to experiment with a single discovery run and a sin-
523
      gle learned black-box optimiser. Therefore, increasing the number of meta-seeds could robustify
      findings. Similarly, we are able to use only a single closed-source language model, GPT-4o (Ope-
524
      nAI et al., 2024), and thus exploring the effectiveness of different language models for discovery
525
      is still an open problem. Finally, we only consider the domain in which an optimiser is discovered
526   for a small set of environments rather than training from a distribution of gridworlds, which may
527   improve black-box generalisation (Goldie et al., 2024) but is impractical for symbolic discovery.
528   Meta-training on more environments, with varied training lengths and architectures, may aid gener-
529   alisation for both paradigms and overcome some issues of the black-box optimiser, in particular.

532   11    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we set out to contrast the generalisability of automatically discovered black-box and
535   symbolic optimisers. In doing so, we compare OPEN with symbolic optimisers given identical
536   inputs. We find that, while OPEN is able to outperform symbolic optimisers in-distribution, the
537   symbolic optimisers demonstrate significantly better scaling to larger networks or longer training
538   horizons, as well as performing better in a number of out-of-support environments. Based on these
539   findings, we make wide ranging recommendations for the future of learned optimisation to take
      advantage of ever-more capable LLMs without dismissing years of prior literature.
