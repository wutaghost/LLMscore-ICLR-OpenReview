                                                 A BSTRACT
033
034
               The exponential growth of the global makeup market has paralleled advancements
               in virtual makeup simulation technology. Despite the progress led by GANs, their
035
               application still encounters significant challenges, including training instability
036
               and limited customization capabilities. Addressing these challenges, this paper
037            introduces DreamMakup: a novel Diffusion model based Makeup Customiza-
038            tion, leveraging the inherent advantages of diffusion models for superior con-
039            trollability and precise real-image editing. DreamMakeup employs early-stopped
040            DDIM inversion to preserve the facial structure and identity while enabling exten-
041            sive customization through various conditioning inputs such as reference images,
042            specific RGB colors, and textual descriptions. Our model demonstrates notable
043            improvements over existing GAN-based frameworks, improved customization,
044            color-matching capabilities, and compatibility with textual descriptions or LLMs
045
               with affordable computational costs. Project page is available at here.

      So far, virtual face makeup modeling is mainly driven by generative adversarial networks (GANs)
      (Li et al. (2018); Jiang et al. (2019); Deng et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2022)).
080
      Despite its advancements, GAN-based frameworks face inherent instability in adversarial training,
081
      leading to several limitations. For instance, GANs in real-world applications demands extensive
082   collections of non-makeup and makeup facial images with diverse poses, expressions, styles, and
083   complex backgrounds. Furthermore, existing GAN-based methods are not fully customizable and
084   lack controllability. Specifically, most of these frameworks only support makeup transfer tasks,
085   inherently requiring reference target images. In many business contexts, users may seek to simulate
086   facial makeup with a more degree of freedom, e.g. test with specific RGB colors of new cosmetic
087   products, or linguistic descriptions such as ‘‘Glam makeup style with glossy lips".
088   In response to these challenges, this paper explores the adoption of diffusion models, recognized
089   for their superior controllability and real-image editing capabilities. Diffusion models offer several
090   advantages for facial beauty simulation. For instance, we can control the reverse sampling process
091   using the enriched text-conditions. Moreover, it supports various style customization using LoRAs
092   (Hu et al. (2021)) supported by the vibrant user community. By employing techniques such as
093   DDIM inversion (Song et al. (2020); Mokady et al. (2023)), diffusion models well preserve the
094   overall structure and subject identity of given facial images, while retaining rich editing capabilities.
095   To this end, we introduce DreamMakeup, a novel diffusion-based makeup customization distin-
096   guished by its advanced sampling guidance. This model is fully compatible with a variety of con-
097   ditionings to steer the makeup process, ranging from reference images and specific RGB colors to
098   textual descriptions of desired makeup looks. As shown in Fig. 2, given pre-trained latent diffusion
099   models (LDM), we commence by inverting facial images x into latents zt through early-stopped
100   DDIM inversion. Subsequently, we approximate the denoised estimate Erz0 | zt s and decode it back
101   into the pixel space, preserving the facial structure attributed to the inversion process. Then, we styl-
102
      ize these facial representations in pixel space through transformations such as histogram matching,
      RGB color matching, or warping, toward a targeted makeup style. Resuming the reverse sampling
103
      process from these transformed representations with proper makeup prompts yields harmonized fa-
104
      cial makeup outcomes. Our contributions are summarized as follows:

120   2   R ELATED W ORKS
121
122   Makeup transfer aims to modify a facial image to reflect a chosen makeup style, with numerous
123   approaches developed using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). Notably, BeautyGAN (Li
124   et al. (2018)) employed histogram matching to preserve color from the reference image. LADN (Gu
125
      et al. (2019)) used local discriminators for heavy makeup. PSGAN (Jiang et al. (2019)) enhanced
      style controllability through matrices and addressed pose misalignments with attention mechanisms.
126
      SCGAN (Deng et al. (2021)) tackled pose issues with style codes. RamGAN (Xiang et al. (2022))
127
      improved makeup transfer with regional attention, and EleGANt (Yang et al. (2022)) offered flexible
128   control over arbitrary regions using attention mechanisms, reducing computational demands.
129
130   However, GAN-based methods typically require large datasets of makeup and no-makeup images
131
      for training and reference images for inference, limiting application diversity and customizability.
      DreamMakeup overcomes these limitations by utilizing foundational diffusion models and open-
132
      source LoRAs for image generation, enabling replication of makeup styles from references or mod-
133
      ification via RGB values or textual prompts. This approach enhances controllability and versatility
134   without relying on extensive datasets.

173   4     D IFFUSION - BASED M AKEUP C USTOMIZATION
174
175   Given an input non-makeup human facial image x0 , our main goal is to (a) customize the makeup
176   style with coarse (e.g. RGB color) to fine (e.g. reference makeup image) level information, while
177   (b) preserving the overall facial structure and subject identity.
178
      To achieve this, DreamMakeup leverages various user preferences for conditional guidance, e.g. tar-
179
      get color, reference image, and textual make-up description. As shown in Fig. 2, the customization
180
      process consists of three main phases: (1) early-stopped DDIM inversion to impose structural con-
181   sistency, (2) pixel-space optimization to guide the sampling process towards target makeup style,
182   and (3) reverse sampling with cross attention composition to accommodate various textual makeup
183   descriptions simultaneously.
184
      One of our primary contributions is to integrate a pixel-space makeup customization during reverse
185
      sampling process given the decoded intermediate estimates x̂0 ptq “ Dpẑ0 ptqq. While makeup
186
      customization demands delicate control over low-level visual appearance, such features like color
187   and edges are often encoded nonlinearly in low-dimensional latents, making direct customization
188   challenging in latent space. To address this, we hijack the latents to the pixel-domain, where low-
189   level visual features can be fully identified and guided. This one-step fine-grained customization in
190   the pixel-domain is a significant departure from existing multi-step latent/attention-space guidance
191   (Hertz et al., 2023; Kwon & Ye, 2022; Mokady et al., 2023). Moreover, we empirically demon-
192   strate that DreamMakeup can be integrated with Large Language Models (LLM) or facial classi-
193   fiers, paving a new path for virtual makeup pipeline design. Additional experimental details and
194   pseudo-code are available in the appendix.

303   4.3   M AKEUP HARMONIZATION IN CROSS - ATTENTION LAYERS
304
      After pixel-space guidance, the output x̂new preserves facial structural consistency while coarsely
305
      following the desired makeup style in the local facial attributes, e.g. lips, eye shadow, etc. Our next
306   goal is to stylize x̂new with text guidance to harmonize such local variations with a fine-grained
307   consistent global aesthetic style, e.g. “Korean K-Pop style”, “Nude makeup”, etc. Moreover, since
308   the local transfer is based on coarse masks, it contains some unnatural discontinuity (x̂new in Figure
309   3). Thus, the following refinement process aims to: (a) align x̂new with specified textual descriptions
310   and (b) enhance overall image quality.

      We further evaluate DreamMakeup against other baselines using quantitative metrics for both color
467
      makeup and makeup transfer tasks. For the makeup transfer task, we assess LPIPS and CLIP image

      similarity to measure identity preservation. Additionally, we gathered user feedback from 10 expert
469   makeup artists and 24 non-expert participants, asking them to rank 10 randomly selected output
470   images generated by different models. The evaluation criteria focused on two key aspects: how
471   closely the makeup style in the output matched the target image (visual details) and the overall
472   makeup quality. Ratings were provided on a scale of 1 to 5. As shown in Table 1a, DreamMakeup

512   5.3     I NTEGRATION WITH LLM
513
514   DreamMakeup can demonstrate improved performance by integrating with Large Language Models
515   (LLMs) for personalized makeup recommendations. By harnessing the exceptional inference capa-
516   bilities of LLMs, DreamMakeup selects makeup colors that are harmonious with the characteristics
517
      of the source image. We provide a pipeline in Fig. 10. This pipeline involves an initial extraction
      of facial attributes such as skin tone and facial structure from the source image via a classifier. This
518
      information is then conveyed to the LLM, which determines the most appropriate makeup colors for
519
      various facial regions, including the skin, eyes, and lips. DreamMakeup subsequently utilizes these
520   recommendations to generate the final makeup-enhanced image.
521
522   These components (classifier, LLM, and DreamMakeup) operate sequentially during inference but
523
      are trained independently. The classifier was trained using ResNet50 on a dataset of 1,000 artificially
      generated images of Asian women, annotated with facial information. The LLM is trained on the
524
      dolly-v2-3b model with a specialized QnA dataset from beauty professionals. As illustrated in Fig-
525
      ure 10(b), the LLM adeptly matches skin, eye shadow, and lip colors to the source image, facilitating
526   the application of these colors by DreamMakeup. The process ensures that the selected colors are
527   well-suited to the source image, leading to an effectively applied makeup look. This demonstrates
528   the potential of integrating classifiers and LLMs in DreamMakeup to provide customized makeup
529   solutions based on in-depth analysis of facial features, thereby enhancing the personalization and
530   effectiveness of makeup applications.

534
535   In this paper, we introduced DreamMakeup, a novel approach to customizing facial makeup uti-
536   lizing the diffusion model. Our method leverages RGB colors or textual descriptions with pixel-
537   space sampling guidance, ensuring precise makeup customization. Additionally, we demonstrated
538   DreamMakeup’s effectiveness in makeup transfer tasks through the application of a facial structure
539   classifier and LLM. Notably, our approach is computationally efficient, paving the way for practical
      makeup customization solutions.
