009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in state-space models (SSMs) have showcased effective
012            performance in modeling long-range dependencies with subquadratic complexity.
013            However, pure SSM-based models still face challenges related to stability and
014            achieving optimal performance on computer vision tasks. Our paper addresses
015            the challenges of scaling SSM-based models for computer vision, particularly the
016            instability and inefficiency of large model sizes. To address this, we introduce a
017            Modulated Group Mamba layer which divides the input channels into four groups
018
               and applies our proposed SSM-based efficient Visual Single Selective Scanning
               (VSSS) block independently to each group, with each VSSS block scanning in one
019
               of the four spatial directions. The Modulated Group Mamba layer also wraps the
020
               four VSSS blocks into a channel modulation operator to improve cross-channel
021            communication. Furthermore, we introduce a distillation-based training objective to
022            stabilize the training of large models, leading to consistent performance gains. Our
023            comprehensive experiments demonstrate the merits of the proposed contributions,
024            leading to superior performance over existing methods for image classification on
025            ImageNet-1K, object detection, instance segmentation on MS-COCO, and semantic
026            segmentation on ADE20K. Our tiny variant with 23M parameters achieves state-of-
027            the-art performance with a classification top-1 accuracy of 83.3% on ImageNet-1K,
028            while being 26% efficient in terms of parameters, compared to the best existing
029
               Mamba design of same model size. Our code and models will be publicly released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Various context modeling methods have emerged in the domains of language and vision understanding.
034
      These include Convolution (He et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2022), Attention (Vaswani et al., 2017), and,
035
      more recently, State Space Models Gu et al. (2022); Gu & Dao (2023). Transformers with their multi-
036   headed self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) have been central to both language models
037   such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) and vision models such as Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al.,
038   2021; Liu et al., 2021). However, challenges arose due to the quadratic computational complexity
039   of attention mechanisms particularly for longer sequences, leading to the recent emergence of State
040   Space models such as S4 (Gu et al., 2022).
041
      While being effective in handling extended input sequences due to their linear complexity in terms
042   of sequence lengths, S4 (Gu et al., 2022) encountered limitations in global context processing in
043   information-dense data, especially in domains like computer vision due to the data-independent nature
044   of the model. Alternatively, approaches such as global convolutions-based state space models (Fu
045   et al., 2023b) and Liquid S4 (Hasani et al., 2022) have been proposed to mitigate the aforementioned
046   limitations. The recent Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) introduces the S6 architecture which aims to
047   enhance the ability of state-space models to handle long-range dependencies efficiently. The selective-
048   scan algorithm introduced by Mamba uses input-dependent state-space parameters, which allow for
049   better in-context learning while still being computationally efficient compared to self-attention.
050   However, Mamba, specifically the S6 algorithm, is known to be unstable for e.g., image classification,
051   especially when scaled to large sizes (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024). Additionally, the Mamba model
052   variant used in image classification, generally called the VSS (Visual State Space) block, can be
053   more efficient in terms of parameters and compute requirements based on the number of channels.
      The VSS block includes extensive input and output projections along with depth-wise convolutions,

102   Convolutional Neural Networks (ConvNets) have been the popular choice for computer vision tasks
103   since the introduction of AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). The field has rapidly evolved with several
104   landmark ConvNet architectures (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; Szegedy et al., 2015; He et al., 2016;
105   Howard et al., 2017; Tan & Le, 2019). Alongside these architectural advances, significant efforts
106   have been made to refine individual convolution layers, including depthwise convolution (Xie et al.,
107   2017), group convolution (Cohen & Welling, 2016), and deformable convolution (Dai et al., 2017).
      Recently, ConvNeXt variants (Liu et al., 2022b; Woo et al., 2023) have taken concrete steps towards

      modernizing traditional 2D ConvNets by incorporating macro designs with advanced settings and
109   training recipes to achieve on-par performance with the state-of-the-art models.

111   In recent years, the pioneering Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) has significantly
112
      impacted the computer vision field, including tasks such as image classification (Touvron et al., 2021;
      Liu et al., 2021; 2022a; Fan et al., 2021), object detection (Carion et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021;
113
      Meng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022), and segmentation (Cheng et al., 2022; Shaker et al., 2024;
114
      Kirillov et al., 2023). ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) introduces a monolithic design that approaches
115   an image as a series of flattened 2D patches without image-specific inductive bias. The remarkable
116   performance of ViT for computer vision tasks, along with its scalability, has inspired numerous
117   subsequent endeavors to design better architectures. The early ViT-based models usually require
118   large-scale datasets (e.g., JFT-300M (Sun et al., 2017)) for pretraining. Later, DeiT (Touvron et al.,
119   2021) proposes advanced training techniques in addition to integrating a distillation token into the
120   architecture, enabling effective training on smaller datasets (e.g., ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009)).
121   Since then, subsequent studies have designed hierarchical and hybrid architectures by combining
122   CNN and ViT modules to improve performance on different vision tasks (Srinivas et al., 2021; Maaz
123
      et al., 2022; d’Ascoli et al., 2021; Shaker et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2021). Another line of work is to
      mitigate the quadratic complexity inherent in self-attention, a primary bottleneck of ViTs. This effort
124
      has led to significant improvements and more efficient and approximated variants (Wang et al., 2020;
125
      Shaker et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2022; Mehta & Rastegari, 2023; Kitaev et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2021;
126   Tu et al., 2022), offering reduced complexity while maintaining effectiveness.
127
128   Recently, State Space Models (SSMs) have emerged as an alternative to ViTs (Vaswani et al., 2017),
129
      capturing the intricate dynamics and inter-dependencies within language sequences (Gu et al., 2022).
      One notable method in this area is the structured state-space sequence model (S4) (Gu et al., 2022),
130
      designed to tackle long-range dependencies while maintaining linear complexity. Following this
131
      direction, several models have been proposed, including S5 (Smith et al., 2023), H3 (Fu et al.,
132   2023a), and GSS (Mehta et al., 2022). More recently, Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) introduces an
133   input-dependent SSM layer and leverages a parallel selective scan mechanism (S6).
134
135
      In the visual domain, various works have applied SSMs to different tasks. In particular for image
      classification, VMamba (Liu et al., 2024b) uses Mamba with bidirectional scans across both spatial
136
      dimensions in a hierarchical Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) style design to build a global
137
      receptive field efficiently. A concurrent work, Vision Mamba (Vim) (Zhu et al., 2024), instead
138   proposed a monolithic design with a single bidirectional scan for the entire image, outperforming
139   traditional vision transformers like DeiT. LocalVMamba (Huang et al., 2024) addresses the challenge
140   of capturing detailed local information by introducing a scanning methodology within distinct
141   windows (inspired from Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021)), coupled with dynamic scanning
142   directions across network layers. EfficientVMamba (Pei et al., 2024) integrates atrous-based selective
143   scanning and dual-pathway modules for efficient global and local feature extraction, achieving
144   competitive results with reduced computational complexity. These models have been applied for
145   image classification, as well as image segmentation (Liu et al., 2024a; Ma et al., 2024; Ruan & Xiang,
146
      2024; Gong et al., 2024), video understanding (Yang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024),
      and various other tasks (Guo et al., 2024b; He et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024a; Liang
147
      et al., 2024). Their wide applicability shows the effectiveness of SSMs (Gu et al., 2022; Smith et al.,
148
      2023; Fu et al., 2023a; Mehta et al., 2022), and in particular Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), in the visual
149   domain. In this paper, we propose a Modulated Group Mamba layer that mitigates the drawbacks
150   of the default vision Mamba block, such as lack of stability (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024) and the
151   increased number of parameters with respect to the number of channels.

200   State-Space Models: State-space models (SSMs) like S4 (Gu et al., 2022) and Mamba (Gu & Dao,
201   2023) are structured sequence architectures inspired by a combination of recurrent neural networks
202   (RNNs) and convolutional neural networks (CNNs), with linear or near-linear scaling in sequence
203   length. Derived from continuous systems, SSMs define and 1D function-to-function map for an input
204   x(t) ∈ RL → y(t) ∈ RL via a hidden state h(t) ∈ RN . More formally, SSMs are described by the
205   continuous time Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) in Equation 1.
206                                           h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t),
207                                                                                                          (1)
                                               y(t) = Ch(t),
208
209   where h(t) is the current hidden state, h′ (t) is the updated hidden state, x(t) is the current input, y(t)
210   is the output, A ∈ RN ×N is SSM’s evolution matrix, and B ∈ RN ×1 , C ∈ RN ×1 are the input and
211
      output projection matrices, respectively.
212   Discrete State-Space Models: To allow these models to be used in sequence modeling tasks in
213   deep learning, they need to be discretized, converting the SSM from a continuous time function-to-
214   function map into a discrete-time sequence-to-sequence map. S4 (Gu et al., 2022) and Mamba (Gu &
215   Dao, 2023) are among the discrete adaptations of the continuous system, incorporating a timescale
      parameter ∆ to convert the continuous parameters A, B into their discrete equivalents A, B. This

      3.3.2    G ROUPED M AMBA O PERATOR
271
272   Considering the motivation presented earlier in Section 3, we aim to design a variant of the Mamba (Gu
273   & Dao, 2023) that is both computationally efficient and can effectively model the spatial dependencies
274   of the input sequence. Given that Mamba is computationally inefficient on large number of channels C
275   in the input sequence, we propose a grouped variant of the operator, inspired by Grouped Convolutions.
276   The Grouped Mamba operation is a variant of the VSSS block presented in Section 3.3.1, where the
277
      input channels are divided into groups, and the VSSS operator is applied separately to each group.
      Specifically, we divide the input channels into four groups, each of size C4 , and an independent
278
      VSSS block is applied to each group. To better model spatial dependencies in the input, each of
279
      the four groups scans in one of four directions across the token sequence: left-to-right, right-to-left,
280   top-to-bottom, and bottom-to-top, as outlined in Figure 2 (d).
281
282   Let G = 4 be the number of groups representing four scanning directions: left-to-right, right-to-left,
283
      top-to-bottom, and bottom-to-top. We form four sequences from the input sequence Xin , namely
      XLR , XRL , XTB , and XBT , each of shape (B, H, W, C4 ), representing one of the four directions
284
285
      specified earlier. These are then flattened to form a single token sequence of shape (B, N, C4 ), where
      N = W × H is the number of tokens in the sequence. The parameters for each of the four groups
286
      can be specified by θLR , θRL , θTB , and θBT , respectively, for each of the four groups, representing the
287
      parameters for the VSSS blocks.

      3.4   D ISTILLED L OSS F UNCTION
325
326   As mentioned earlier in the motivation in Section 3, the Mamba training is unstable when scaled to
327   large models (Patro & Agneeswaran, 2024). To mitigate this issue, we propose to utilize a distillation
328   objective alongside the standard cross-entropy objective. Knowledge distillation involves training
329   a student model to learn from a teacher model’s behavior by minimizing a combination of the
330   classification loss and distillation loss. The distillation loss is computed using the cross-entropy
331   objective between the logits of the teacher and student models. Given the logits (Zs ) from the student
332
      model, logits (Zt ) from a teacher model (RegNetY-16G (Radosavovic et al., 2020) in our case), the
      ground truth label y, and the hard decision of the teacher yt = argmaxc Zt (c), the joint loss function
333
      is defined as shown in Equation 10.
334
335
336                              Ltotal = αLCE (Zs , y) + (1 − α)LCE (Zs , yt ).                        (10)
337
      where LCE is the cross-entropy objective and α is the weighting parameter. We experimentally show
338
      in Section 4 that training with this distillation objective stabilizes training, leading to consistent
339
      performance gains on larger model variants.

527   5         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   In this paper, we tackle the computational inefficiencies and stability challenges associated with
530   visual SSMs for computer vision tasks by introducing a novel layer called the Modulated Group
531   Mamba. We also propose a multi-directional scanning method that improves parameter efficiency by
532   scanning in four spatial directions and leveraging the Channel Affinity Modulation (CAM) operator
533   to enhance feature aggregation across channels. To stabilize training, especially for larger models, we
534   employ a distillation-based training objective. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed
535   GroupMamba models outperform recent SSMs while requiring fewer parameters.
536   Our research has focused on image classification, object detection, instance segmentation, and
537   semantic segmentation. To further validate and extend the generalization ability of our method,
538   we aim to explore additional downstream tasks, such as video recognition and time-series data
539   applications. Evaluating the Modulated Group Mamba layer in these contexts will help to uncover its
      potential benefits and limitations, providing deeper insights and guiding further improvements.
