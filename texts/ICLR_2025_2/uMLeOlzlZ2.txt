011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Reducing hallucination of Large Language Models (LLMs) is imperative for use
014            in the sciences, where reliability and reproducibility are crucial. However, LLMs
015
               inherently lack long-term memory, making it a nontrivial, ad hoc, and often bi-
               ased task to fine-tune them on domain-specific literature and data. Here we in-
016
               troduce LLaMP, a multimodal retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework
017
               of hierarchical reasoning-and-acting (ReAct) agents that can dynamically and re-
018            cursively interact with computational and experimental data from the Materials
019            Project (MP) and run atomistic simulations via high-throughput workflow inter-
020            face. Without fine-tuning, LLaMP demonstrates strong tool-usage ability to com-
021            prehend and integrate various modalities of materials science concepts, fetch rel-
022            evant data stores on the fly, process higher-order data (such as crystal structure
023            and elastic tensor), and streamline complex tasks in computational materials and
024            chemistry. We propose a metric combining uncertainty and confidence estimates
025            to evaluate the self-consistency of responses by LLaMP and vanilla LLMs. Our
026
               benchmark shows that LLaMP effectively mitigates the intrinsic bias in LLMs,
               counteracting the errors on bulk moduli, electronic bandgaps, and formation ener-
027
               gies that seem to derive from mixed data sources. We also demonstrate LLaMP’s
028
               capability to edit crystal structures and run annealing molecular dynamics simu-
029            lations using pre-trained machine-learning interatomic potentials. The framework
030            offers an intuitive and nearly hallucination-free approach to exploring and scaling
031            materials informatics, and paves the way for future agentic scientific workflows
032            and knowledge-grounded LLMs.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The generation of convincing yet unreliable information poses a pressing challenge to large language
038   model (LLMs), particularly to their application in the sciences. LLMs are prone to hallucination–
039
      providing outright false information with high confidence (Bang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). This
      issue is particularly concerning for knowledge-intensive tasks, where users rely on chatbots and
040
      other AI systems to provide accurate guidance (Lewis et al., 2020). LLMs often lack up-to-date
041
      factual knowledge on topics outside their training data, requiring rigorous verification against trusted
042   external sources (Mallen et al., 2023). In the scientific community, where the integration of insights
043   and data accuracy is already complex, the proliferation of generative models may exacerbate the risk
044   of misinformation. This trend accentuates the importance of scrutinizing and ensuring the reliability
045   of information sources.
046
      Current approaches to enhance LLM accuracy in domain-specific knowledge often involve fine-
047
      tuning pre-trained models (Dagdelen et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2022) or tailored prompt engineering
048   techniques (Yang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). While these models are easy to deploy, they
049   suffer from diminished reproducibility and data adherence due to the absence of a memory base, un-
050   traceable fine-tuning history, or opaque extraction processes. Even though fine-tuning can encode a
051   certain amount of domain-specific knowledge into LLMs, it is constrained by scalability and intrin-
052   sic memory capacity. Fine-tuned LLMs struggle to retain in the long term the knowledge they were
053   trained on as the training progresses, nor can they be aware of the recent events and data beyond pre-
      training. Prompt engineering, while effective, also compromises the generalizability, thus limiting


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the overall power and flexibility of LLMs. Therefore, a more sensible approach involves equipping
055   LLMs with external data sources, allowing them to generate holistic responses via few-shot adapta-
056   tion to factual information (Lewis et al., 2021) that can reliably support real-world scientific research
057   and decision-making.
058
      In this work, we propose LLaMP, a multimodal retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) framework
059
      leveraging hierarchical reasoning-and-acting (ReAct) agents to interact with Materials Project (MP),
060
      arXiv, Wikipedia, and atomistic simulation tools. The framework serves as a safeguard against
061   LLM hallucination by grounding them in high-fidelity material informatics from large-scale mate-
062   rial databasederived from various sources, including computational data from quantum-mechanical
063   first-principles calculations and expert-curated material synthesis recipes, and further enables the
064   capabilities of complex downstream tasks. The hierarchical planning of supervisor and assistant
065   ReAct agents improves self-correcting tool-usage performance and enhances the self-consistency
066   in final responses. The new capabilities emerge—such as multi-modal searching, tensor and 3D
067   crystal structure retrieval and operation, and language-driven simulation. The frameworkThrough
068   hierarchical planning of multiple ReAct agents, we demonstrate that LLaMP not only can correctly
069
      retrieve high-fidelity, higher-order materials datahigher-order materials data such as tensors and
      3D crystal structures but also can combine different modalities to perform complex, knowledge-
070
      intensive inferences and operations essential for real-world materials science applications.
071
072   Our contributions are as follows: (1) we introduce a multimodal RAG framework employing hierar-
073   chical ReAct agents that dynamically interact with the Materials Project, enabling LLMs to access
074   high-fidelity materials informatics; (2) we propose a statistical metric to assess the self-consistency
075
      of LLM responses in high-precision, reproducibility-critical settings; (3) we evaluate the perfor-
      mance of LLaMP and standard LLMs in predicting key material properties, including bulk moduli,
076
      electronic bandgaps, formation energies, and magnetic orderings; (4) we showcase real-world appli-
077
      cations in materials science, such as inorganic synthesis and crystal structure generation and editing;
078   (5) we enhance LLaMP with high-throughput atomistic simulation workflows and pre-trained uni-
079   versal ML force fields, lowering the entry barriers to computational materials and chemistry.

082   2   BACKGROUND
083
084   Materials Project (MP) The Materials Project is a multi-institution effort to explore and com-
085   pute the properties of all known inorganic materials (Jain et al., 2013) and molecules (Spotte-Smith
086   et al., 2023). The initiative leverages high-throughput electronic structure calculations (Kresse and
087   Furthmüller, 1996; Shao et al., 2015) based on density functional theory (DFT), providing large-
088
      scale open-source database and analysis algorithms, with the ultimate goal to drastically reduce the
      time and cost required for materials discovery by focusing experiments on the promising candidates
089
      from computational screening. Most of the atomic structures are selected from the Inorganic Crystal
090
      Structure Database (ICSD) (Zagorac et al., 2019) and undergo standardized relaxation procedures,
091   followed by post-processing or additional calculations for higher-order material properties such as
092   electron and phonon bandgaps, elastic tensors, dielectric tensors, and more. MP provides these
093   calculated material properties through API endpoints.
094
095
      NLP and LLM in materials science Natural language processing (NLP) has found extensive
096   application in extracting valuable information from scientific publications, with notable instances
097   involving text-to-text or more recent image-to-text summarization techniques (Gupta et al., 2022;
098   Radford et al., 2021; Tshitoyan et al., 2019). For summarizing crystal structures in textual form,
099   Ganose and Jain (2019) introduced the robocrystallographer, a toolkit designed for the analysis
100   and generation of descriptions for crystalline materials. Their method condenses atomic structures
101   into descriptive JSON representations that encompass coordination statistics, connectivity motifs,
102   geometric features, and dimensionality. MP leverages robocrystallographer to generate human-level
103   descriptions for 130K compounds which are accessible through MP website and API.
104   Recent efforts have curated datasets (Zaki et al., 2023) and benchmarks (Song et al., 2023) to better
105   evaluate the limitations of LLMs in question answering within the materials science domain. Zhang
106   et al. (2024) further curated instruction data to fine-tune Llama for material science-specific tasks.
107   These works focus on general (undergraduate-level) question answering instead of factual grounding
      on expert-curated database and downstream agentic workflow. In a complementary aspect, other

109   as tables and unstructured texts (Hira et al., 2024; Schilling-Wilhelmi et al., 2024). This motivates
110   us to augment LLM’s knowledge base with MP—one of the most authoratative materials database
111   of stable crystal structures, high-fidelity DFT calculations, inorganic solid-state synthesis recipes,

      3     R ELATED W ORK
116
117
      Prompting and fine-tuning in domain science Prompt-based methods have been used as ef-
      fective tools for automating data extraction process from the literature. Polak and Morgan (2023)
118
      employ a prompt workflow to extract the cooling rates of metallic glasses and yield strengths of high
119
      entropy alloys. Zheng et al. (2023) implement a ChatGPT metal-organic framework (MOF) synthe-
120   sis assistant through embedding and searching on preselected papers. StructChem (Ouyang et al.,
121   2024) leverages step-by-step reasoning, and iteratively refines results to solve college-level chem-
122   istry questions. Yang et al. (2023) use GPT-4 to extract experimentally measured bandgaps to train
123   a graph neural network for accurate bandgap prediction from crystal structures. Despite the success
124   in the specific data extraction tasks, prompt-based methods face challenges in reproducibility when
125   the used prompts are fine-grained to work for specific edge cases. They are also still prone to hal-
126   lucination and less generalizable to combine different data sources due to the deliberately designed
127   prompt.
128   Several other knowledge-grounded, domain-specific language models lean on the fine-tuning ap-
129   proach against pre-selected data and literature. For instance, ChemGPT (Frey et al., 2022) in-
130   volves fine-tuning GPT-neo on self-referencing embedded strings (SELFIES) representations of
131   small molecules. Jablonka et al. (2024) demonstrated GPT-3 fine-tuned against online corpora could
132   outperform purpose-trained models on classification, regression, and inverse design of high-entropy
133   alloys and molecules. Dagdelen et al. (2024) fine-tuned GPT-3 on ∼500 prompt-completion pairs to
134
      enhance LLM’s capability to extract useful information on materials chemistry from text paragraphs.
      However, the fine-tuned models without augmentation inherently lack awareness of the up-to-date
135
      results and any data only available after their training. Moreover, fine-tuned LLMs still suffer from
136
      limited memory retention and are prone to forget during continual training (Wang et al., 2023).
137
138
      LLM function calling and tool usage An emerging class of LLM applications, including this
139
      work, take advantage of LLM text completion and instruction following capability for function
140   calling. This approach extends LLMs with expert-curated tools to improve the quality of control for
141   downstream applications. Coscientist (Boiko et al., 2023) combines tools such as search engines,
142   Python, and document index for autonomous chemical research. ChemCrow (M. Bran et al., 2024)
143   gathers multiple molecule and safety tools to enhance organic chemistry experiment and molecule
144   design. Concurrently, Zhang et al. (2024) develop retrieval based agentic framework on their curated
145   dataset. Ghafarollahi and Buehler (2024) propose AtomAgents for alloy design and analysis.
146   However, most prior works adopt flat planning strategy, where a single agent accesses all the avail-
147   able tools, resulting in a lack of self-correcting tool usage capabilities. This often leads to premature
148   reasoning stop and summarization when the agent encounters tool usage errors. We mitigate this
149   through hierarchical planning of multiple ReAct agents (see Section 4.1).

186   Supervisor agent The supervisor agent acts as a router and decision-maker, handling abstract
187   logic between user requests and assistant agents. Here, we adopt ReAct on GPT-4 (Yao et al., 2023)
188   to augment the agent’s action space A with a language space L to create an expanded action space
189   of Â = A ∪ L. This expanded action space empowers the agent to take action ât ∈ L in language
190   space that facilitate the collaboration with assistant agents to retrieve domain specific information
191   and achieve complex downstream tasks such as molecular dynamics simulations.
192
193   Assistant agent The efficient function calling in LLMs is often hindered by the need to process
194   complex API schemas, which can consume a significant portion of the context window. To address
195   this, we assign a specialized ReAct agent for each specific tool or API endpoint. It reduces con-
196   text window consumption, as each agent handles only the relevant schema for its task, avoiding
197   unnecessary schema parsing. Additionally, the use of ReAct agents enables them to refine their API
198   calls based on feedback, significantly improving task completion rates through ReAct’s iterative
199
      self-correcting mechanism.
200   The full list of agents and tools are defined in A.1. Each MP assistant agent employs a self-correcting
201   ReAct mechanism, enabling agents to refine their API calls and improve task completion rates.
202   The framework’s modularity enable a seamless integration of new assistant agents, allowing for
203   extensibility to various materials discovery methods and experimental techniques (Luo et al., 2023;
204   Pilania et al., 2017; Wen et al., 2023; 2024; Zeni et al., 2024).

236   5.1   M ULTIMODAL R E ACT AUGMENTATION
237
      Materials design often involves multi-objective property optimization. These properties span a
238
      Pareto front where optimizing one factor incurs deterioration in others. To succeed in such tasks,
239   combining different modalities of materials properties is necessary. LLaMP achieves this through
240   the hierarchical orchestration of multiple ReAct agents (Yao et al., 2023). For the example ques-
241   tion “What’s the stiffest material with the lowest formation energy in Si-O system?” (Figure A.1),
242   when a query requires multimodal information and compound logic, the supervisor agent decom-
243   poses the query into multiple subtasks, delegates them to assistant agents (MPThermoExpert and
244   MPElasticityExpert) for information retrieval, and in the final stage of reasoning integrates in-
245   formation from both modalities, drawing on the context in episodic memory retrieved from the
246   assistant agents (Figure 1). This enables LLaMP to achieve various tasks step-by-step by combining
247
      multiple data sources from the Materials Project (MP) (e.g. 3D crystal structures, thermodynamic,
      mechanical, magnetic properties, and more listed in Appendix A.1) in a single query.

      5.2   P ERFORMANCE B ENCHMARKS
251
      Response quality and consistency We evaluate the performance of LLaMP, StructChem (Ouyang
252   et al., 2024), Darwin (Xie et al., 2023), and vanilla LLMs (gpt-4, llama3-8b, gemini-1.0-pro)
253   on material properties such as bulk modulus, formation energy, and bandgap (Figure 2, Table 1).
254   Performance is assessed through Precision, CoP, SCoR, and MAE metrics, as defined in Section 4.2.
255   We argue that any useful LLM agents to be included in the scientific workflow should have high
256   SCoR and low error on the materials properties. Notably, LLaMP consistently outperforms other
257   models, achieving the highest SCoR and the lowest errors across material properties, making it
258   highly suitable for scientific workflows. StructChem, despite extensive prompting strategies, often
259   fails due to a lack of necessary domain knowledge, resulting in high refusal rates when it cannot
260
      validate outputs.
261   For bulk modulus prediction, vanilla LLMs, particularly Llama 3-8b, frequently rely on low-fidelity
262   online data, leading to significant deviations for elements like Cr, Mn, and Fe, compared to MP
263   theoretical values. Interestingly, Llama 3-8b usually cites spurious reference in the responses despite
264   largest response variance but occasionally agrees with MP values. In contrast, LLaMP outperforms
265   vanilla LLMs and reduces the MAE from around 40 to 14.57 GPa.
266   Our results demonstrate that vanilla LLMs fail to provide accurate formation energy predictions,
267   with low SCoR and high MAE ranging from 1.5 to 5.5 eV, which is impractical for material dis-
268   covery requiring meV-level precision. This is not unexpected, since accurate formation energy
269   prediction requires the computation of multiple energetics (energies of the compound itself and its
      elemental constituents).

      mation have shown the capability to generate stable crystals under the constraints of atomic positions
      and charges (Gruver et al., 2023). In this context, we delve into the examination and comparison
472
      of the crystal generation capabilities between LLaMP and GPT-3.5, without resorting to fine-tuning
473
      or tailored prompt messages in previous work. Figure 4 showcases the structures generated by
474   LLaMP and vanilla GPT-3.5 without RAG, both instructed to insert one lithium atom at the tetrahe-
475   dral site of the diamond cubic silicon structure (Table B6.11). Notably, both LLaMP and GPT-3.5
476   place an additional Li atom at fractional coordinate (0.5, 0.5, 0.5). However, the Si structure re-
477   trieved by LLaMP adheres to the MP convention, positioning two Si bases at (0.125, 0.125, 0.125)
478   and (0.875, 0.875, 0.875). This causes the inserted Li atom to be hexagonal interstitial instead of
479   tetrahedral interstitial.
480   GPT-3.5 locates the Li atom at the tetrahedral site given the “luckily chosen” Si bases at (0, 0, 0) and
481   (0.25, 0.25, 0.25); however, the resulting cell volume and shape are highly distorted, and the Si Si
482   bond length and Si Si Si angle deviate significantly from the ground truth (Table 4), highlight-
483   ing the limitations in the intrinsic encoding of LLMs for atomistic information and the challenges
484   associated with zero-shot generation of crystal structures. In contrast, the LLaMP-retrieved MP
485   structure serves as a robust prior, anchoring the lattice parameters of the generated structure to the
      correct values.

      Summary We present a hierarchical agentic framework, LLaMP, based on ReAct to extract and
541   manipulate material informatics through few-shot generalization. By grounding thoughts and ac-
542   tions with high-fidelity information, LLaMP showcases the ability to integrate various modalities of
543   material properties and perform logical inferences to accomplish assigned tasks, all without the need
544   for fine-tuning. In essence, the proposed LangChain framework holds the potential to expand its ap-
545   plicability to multiple data sources, encompassing both theoretical computations and experimental
546   data, knowledge graph databases (Venugopal and Olivetti, 2024; Ye et al., 2024), and real-world lab-
547   oratories by incorporating additional assistant agents for data retrieval and robot control (Fei et al.,
548   2024). LLaMP functions as a knowledge-aware agent, empowering users to navigate and manipulate
549
      complex materials informatics. In the context of self-driving labs (Boiko et al., 2023; Szymanski
      et al., 2023), LLM agents with multimodal data sources, sensors, and actors may improve their de-
550
      cision making and operation (Miret and Krishnan, 2024). As new tools continue to emerge, there
551
      is an exciting avenue for further exploration to ascertain if this framework can effectively facilitate
552   scientific hypothesis generation and guide data-driven experiments.
