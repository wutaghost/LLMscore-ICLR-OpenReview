009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge distillation (KD) aims to transfer useful information from a large-
012            scale model (teacher) to a lightweight model (student). Classical KD focuses on
013            leveraging the teacher’s predictions as soft labels to regularize student training.
014            However, the exact match of predictions in Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence could
015            be somewhat in conflict with the classification objective, given that the distribution
016
               discrepancies between teacher-generated predictions and ground-truth annotations
               tend to be fairly severe. In this paper, we rethink the role of teacher predictions from
017
               a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) perspective and transfer knowledge by introducing
018
               teacher predictions as latent variables to reformulate the classification objective.
019            This MoE strategy results in breaking down the vanilla classification task into
020            a mixture of easier subtasks with the teacher classifier as a gating function to
021            weigh the importance of subtasks. Each subtask is efficiently conquered by distinct
022            experts that are effectively implemented by resorting to multi-level teacher outputs.
023            We further develop a theoretical framework to formulate our method, termed MoE-
024            KD, as an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and provide proof of the
025            convergence. Extensive experiments manifest that MoE-KD outperforms advanced
026            knowledge distillers on mainstream benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Deep learning has shown its significance by boosting the performance of various real-world tasks such
031   as computer vision (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2018), and
032   reinforcement learning (Silver et al., 2016). However, it is worth mentioning that the effectiveness of
033   deep learning generally comes at the expense of huge computational complexity and massive storage
034   requirements. This greatly restricts the deployment of large-scale models (teachers) in real-time
035   applications where lightweight models (students) are preferable due to limited resources. Under this
036   context, with the primary goal of improving the student’s performance for the task at hand, knowledge
037   distillation (KD) (Gou et al., 2021; Wang & Yoon, 2021) is introduced as a de facto standard to
038   transfer knowledge from a teacher model to a student model.
039   The rationale behind KD can be explained from an optimization perspective: there is evidence that
040   high-capacity models can find good local minima due to over-parameterisation (Du & Lee, 2018;
041   Soltanolkotabi et al., 2018). This motivates KD to use such models to facilitate the optimization
042   of lower-capacity models (i.e., the student) during training. Classically, KD is approached by
043   minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between predictive distributions of the teacher
044   and student (Hao et al., 2024; Hinton et al., 2015), the motivation behind which is to leverage the
045
      teacher’s predictions as soft labels to regularize the student training (Müller et al., 2019; Yuan et al.,
      2020). However, the efficacy of classical KD is challenged by counter-intuitive observations (Cho &
046
      Hariharan, 2019; Stanton et al., 2021). Specifically, a larger teacher does not necessarily increase a
047
      student’s accuracy compared to a relatively smaller teacher. This can be attributed to the capacity
048   gap between the two models which makes the discrepancy between their predictions significantly
049   large (Huang et al., 2022a). On the one hand, some methods (Dong et al., 2023; Mirzadeh et al.,
050   2020; Park et al., 2021; Son et al., 2021) develop student-friendly teachers to tackle the poor learning
051   issue of the student model. Unfortunately, such methods suffer from complex distillation procedures
052   and heavy computational costs for re-training the teacher model, therefore not being applicable in
053   practice. On the other hand, ATS (Li et al., 2022b) separately applies a higher/lower temperature
      to the correct/wrong class by finding that more complex teachers are more likely to assign a larger


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      score for the correct class or less varied scores for the wrong classes while KD-Zero (Li et al., 2024)
055   develops automated searches for distillers without manual architecture modification and KD design.
056
057   Despite remarkable progress, people tend to overlook the fact that there could be a significantly
058
      large discrepancy between ground-truth labels and teacher-generated labels. In particular, whether
      the temperature is high or low, the teacher would produce imbalanced predictive distributions even
059
      though it is trained on a balanced dataset (Niu et al., 2022). Given the fact that classical KD typically
060
      calculates the cross-entropy loss between the ground-truth label and the student’s prediction in
061   addition to the KL divergence between the teacher’s and student’s predictions, this kind of transfer
062   gap makes it ill-prosed to simultaneously align the student’s predictive distribution with those mutually
063   exclusive targets, which greatly undermines the power of classical KD.
064
      To get out of this dilemma, this paper rethinks knowledge distillation from a mixture-of-experts (MoE)
065
      perspective. The heart of our method, termed MoE-KD, lies in leveraging the teacher’s predictions
066
      as latent variables to rewrite the classification objective. In this way, we arrive at decomposing the
067   student classifier as a convex combination of conditional models. Namely, each of the conditional
068   models, referred to as an expert, learns to classify a subset of samples, where an input-dependent
069   gating function partitions the dataset into subsets by allocating weights among experts.
070
      To address the nontrivial learning problems, we formulate MoE-KD as an Expectation-Maximization
071
      (EM) algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977), where we iteratively estimate the Bayes-optimal poste-
072
      rior distribution of the latent variables given the observed data (in the E-step) and maximize the
073   evidence lower bound (ELBO) of the reformulated classification objective (in the M-step). We
074   theoretically prove that the ELBO is upper-bounded and our proposed EM algorithm contributes to
075   the convergence of the ELBO (see Section 4.3). Empirically, our proposed MoE-KD achieves state-
076   of-the-art performance in various distillation settings regarding teacher-student pairs (homogeneous
077   and heterogeneous) and training datasets (coarse-grained and fine-grained).

      2.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
083
084
      Knowledge distillation is the process of using a teacher model to improve the performance of a student
085   model. In its classical form, one trains the student to fit the teacher’s predictive distribution. (Hinton
086   et al., 2015) popularizes this solution by formulating it as logit matching. MLD (Jin et al., 2023)
087   extends logit matching not only at the instance level but also at the batch and class levels, DKD (Zhao
088   et al., 2022) decouples classical KD into distilling target and non-target class knowledge, and
089   WSLD (Zhou et al., 2021) provides a bias-variance trade-off perspective for the KL term. Besides,
090   the teacher’s knowledge can also be distilled in the form of features. One line of feature-based
091   distillation is to mimic the intermediate representations of the teacher network in terms of Euclidean
092   distance (Romero et al., 2014), mutual information (Fu et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2019), Wassertein
093
      distance (Chen et al., 2021a), and maximum mean discrepancy of the network activations (Huang &
      Wang, 2017) respectively. Another line of feature-based distillation occurs to explore transferring the
094
      relationship between features rather than the actual features themselves, where the feature correlation
095
      can be captured by the Gram matrix (Yim et al., 2017), Taylor series expansion (Peng et al., 2019),
096   graph (Liu et al., 2019), or quantized visual word space (Jain et al., 2020).
097
098   The transfer gap between the teacher and the student is an emerging topic in KD. To mitigate the
099
      feature-level transfer gap, MasKD (Huang et al., 2022b) distils the valuable information from receptive
      regions that contribute to the task precision; NORM (Liu et al., 2023) conducts feature matching in a
100
      many-to-one manner, and DiffKD (Huang et al., 2023) explicitly denoises and matches features using
101
      diffusion models. When it comes to the logit-level transfer gap, DIST (Huang et al., 2022a) relaxes
102   the KL divergence in logit-based distillation with a correlation-based loss; TAKD (Mirzadeh et al.,
103   2020) introduces multiple middle-sized teaching assistant models to guide the student; DGKD (Son
104   et al., 2021) improves TAKD by densely gathering all the assistant models, and SFTN (Park et al.,
105   2021) provides the teacher with a snapshot of the student during training. Different from these
106   prior works, our method is motivated by the observations that teacher predictions and ground-truth
107   labels indeed behave differently (Niu et al., 2022), arguing that this largely overlooked transfer gap
      makes it problematic for classical KD to encourage student predictions to simultaneously mimic

108
      the ground-truth labels and teacher predictions. Facing this dilemma, this paper proceeds from a
109   mixture-of-experts perspective by rethinking the role of teacher predictions as latent variables.

      2.2       M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
112
113   The MoE model was initially proposed by (Jacobs et al., 1991) as a technique to combine a series of
114   sub-models and perform conditional computation (Bengio et al., 2015; 2013; Cho & Bengio, 2014)
115   that aims at activating different subsets of a network for different inputs. To increase the model
116   capacity in dealing with complex data, (Ahmed et al., 2016; Gross et al., 2017) extend the MoE
117   structure to the deep neural networks by proposing a deep MoE model composed of multiple layers of
118   routers and experts. Recently, (Shazeer et al., 2017) simplifies the MoE layer by making the output of
119
      the gating function sparse for each example, which greatly improves the training stability and reduces
      the computational cost. Since then, the MoE layer with different base neural network structures has
120
      achieved tremendous success in scene parsing (Fu et al., 2018), multi-task learning (Gupta et al.,
121
      2022), deep clustering (Chazan et al., 2019; Kopf et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2017),
122   domain generalization (Dai et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022a), data generation (Xia et al., 2022) and
123   question answering (Dai et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2022).
124
125
      KD and MoE tend to evolve mostly independently in the literature. To the best of our knowledge,
      the only exceptions are (Dai et al., 2022a; Xue et al., 2022). In essence, (Dai et al., 2022a; Xue
126
      et al., 2022) exploit the benefits of KD to overcome over-fitting problems (Fedus et al., 2022; Wu
127
      et al., 2022) of MoE models on downstream tasks with limited data. On the contrary, this paper
128   formulates the student’s classifier as a lightweight MoE layer with the teacher’s knowledge to enhance
129   the efficiency of knowledge transfer from the teacher to the student.

476   6     C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we study knowledge distillation from a novel mixture-of-experts perspective, where we
479
      leverage the teacher’s knowledge from outputs and learned parameters to tackle the classification
480   within a partition-and-classify principle. Our method comprises an input-dependent gating function
481   that distributes subtasks to one or a few specialized experts, and multiple experts that classify the
482   subset of samples based on sample-level student representations and class-level teacher representa-
483   tions. Moreover, by deriving the ELBO, our model can be formulated as an expectation-maximization
484   algorithm and trained without requiring any other loss or regularization terms. Extensive experiments
485   show that our method is empirically effective in not only consistently boosting the student model
      classification performance in various distillation settings but also improving the feature transferability.
