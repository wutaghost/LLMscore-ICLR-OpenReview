011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional scaling laws suggest that performance metrics of language models improve
014            predictably with increases in model or dataset size. However, recent works display sub-
015            scaling growth for large language models, where performance improvements decelerate
016            as the dataset or model size increases. This study aims to systematically investigate the
017
               sub-scaling law phenomenon through an extensive empirical analysis involving over 400
               models, ranging from 20 million to 7 billion parameters, with varying datasets and training
018
               strategies. Our findings indicate that sub-scaling laws arise primarily from high data density
019
               and non-optimal training resource allocations. Specifically, we observed that both factors
020            contribute more significantly to performance deceleration than previously anticipated. We
021            examine the sub-scaling phenomenon from two perspectives: data density and training
022            strategy. High data density leads to diminishing marginal gains in performance, while
023            optimal resource allocation is crucial for sustaining performance improvements. Further,
024            we propose a sub-optimal scaling law that generalizes the Chinchilla scaling law to better
025            predict performance and loss in sub-scaling regimes.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The rapid advancement in natural language processing (NLP) has been significantly driven by the development
031   of increasingly large language models. These models, such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), Chinchilla (70B)
032   (Hoffmann et al., 2022), Gopher (280B) (Rae et al., 2021), and Megatron-Turing NLG (530B) (Smith et al.,
033
      2022), have set new benchmarks across a variety of linguistic tasks. There is also a growing body of research
      on scaling strategies (McCandlish et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2022; 2023), which could be beneficial for large
034
      language models (LLMs). The conventional wisdom suggests that augmenting model size and corresponding
035
      training data generally results in enhanced performance. This trend has led to the popularization of a ’bigger
036   is better’ paradigm within the field. This scaling up has been driven by the empirical observation that larger
037   models trained on vast amounts of data tend to perform better on various natural language processing tasks
038   (Brown et al., 2020; Komatsuzaki, 2019; Hernandez et al., 2022a).
039
      However, recent empirical studies Hernandez et al. (2022a); Hu et al. (2023); Porian et al. (2024); Muennighoff
040
      et al. (2024) have observed deviations from this expected trend, particularly in the context of exceptionally
041
      large language models. These deviations manifest as sub-scaling growth, where the rate of performance
042   improvement decelerates as model or dataset size continues to increase. Specifically, Hernandez et al. (2022a);
043   Muennighoff et al. (2024) observe that sub-scaling occurs in scenarios involving repeated training data,
044   leading to diminishing returns in performance. Hu et al. (2023) highlight that sub-scaling is particularly
045   pronounced in tasks requiring complex reasoning or multi-step processes. Furthermore, Porian et al. (2024)
046   find that sub-scaling exists under non-optimal training strategies with sub-optimal hyper-parameters. Figure 1

      This study aims to systematically investigate the sub-scaling law phenomenon through an extensive empirical
      analysis involving over 400 models, ranging from 20 million to 7 billion parameters, with varying datasets and
121
      training strategies. Our findings indicate that sub-scaling laws arise primarily from high data density and non-
122
      optimal training resource allocations. Specifically, we observed that both factors contribute more significantly
123   to performance deceleration than previously anticipated. We examine the sub-scaling phenomenon from two
124   perspectives: data density and training strategy. High data density leads to diminishing marginal gains in
125   performance as shown in Figure 2, while optimal resource allocation is crucial for sustaining performance
126   improvements as shown in Figure 3. Further, we propose a sub-optimal scaling law that generalizes the
127   Chinchilla scaling law Hoffmann et al. (2022) to better predict performance and loss in sub-scaling regimes.
128   Our analysis reveals that the quality and diversity of training data are paramount, often outweighing the
129   benefits of mere scale in model size. Key findings from our study include:
130   1. Sub-Scaling Law Phenomenon: Traditional scaling laws fail to predict performance improvements for very
131   large models and datasets. The performance gains decelerate, leading to sub-scaling growth, especially in
132   high data density scenarios and with non-optimal resource allocation.
133
      2. Impact of Data Density: High data density causes sub-scaling due to diminishing returns from redundant
134
      data. Low-density datasets, with more diverse data, align more closely with traditional scaling laws.
135
136   3. Optimal Resource Allocation: Efficient computational resource allocation is crucial to mitigate sub-scaling
137   and sustain performance improvements.
138   4. Sub-Optimal Scaling Law: We proposed a Sub-Optimal Scaling Law that generalizes the Chinchilla scaling
139   law to better predict performance and loss in sub-scaling regimes. This new framework accounts for the
140   quality and diversity of training data, emphasizing that these factors often outweigh the benefits of mere scale.

160   In this section, we investigate the phenomenon of sub-scaling law from two perspectives: data density and
161   training strategy. Our analysis is based on extensive empirical evaluations involving over 400 models, ranging
162   from 20 million to 7 billion parameters, trained on various datasets with different training strategies.

164   2.1   A NALYSIS 1: T HE P ERSPECTIVE OF DATA
165
166   Previous works Abbas et al. (2024); Sachdeva et al. (2024); Sorscher et al. (2022) have used data density
167   to measure the quality and diversity of datasets. By focusing on density, these methods aim to identify and
168
      remove redundant data points within high-density clusters, effectively reducing redundancy and ensuring a
      wide range of topics, genres, and linguistic structures are covered. As shown in Figure 4, we observed that
169
      high data density often leads to diminishing returns in performance improvements. This phenomenon can be
170
      understood through the lens of Information Theory, where redundant information in high-density datasets
171   contributes less new information to the model, thus reducing the marginal gains from additional data.
172

175

246   In information theory, the amount of new information (or entropy) introduced by additional data plays a
247   critical role in model learning Sachdeva et al. (2024); Abbas et al. (2024). High-density datasets often
248   contain redundant information, while low-density datasets provide more diverse and unique data points. This
249
      distinction directly impacts the law governing performance improvement: whether it follows a scaling law or
      a sub-scaling law.
250
251   Low-density datasets, which contain more diverse and less redundant information, exhibit a more linear
252   relationship between the number of samples and performance improvement. Each new sample provides
253   relatively new information, maintaining a steady rate of performance gain. The information gain I(n) for
254   low-density datasets can be expressed as a linear function: I(n) = I0 · n where I0 is a constant representing
255
      the information gain per  sample. The performance P (n) in a low-density dataset context is given by:
      P (n) = P0 1 − e−β·I(n) . Given the linear information gain, this simplifies to: P (n) = P0 1 − e−β·I0 ·n .

256
      For smaller n, this can be approximated as a linear function:

260   In contrast, high-density datasets, characterized by a significant level of redundancy, tend to exhibit diminish-
261   ing returns as more data is added. Redundant information increases the mutual information between samples,
262   leading to a lower incremental gain from each additional sample, which leads to the sub-scaling law.
263   The information gain I(n) for high-density datasets can be modeled as: I(n) = I0 · n−α where I0 represents
264   the initial information gain, and α is a positive constant indicating the rate of diminishing returns. This
265   formulation suggests that as the number of samples n increases, the additional information gained decreases.
266
      The performance P (n) as a function of the number of samples n follows: P (n) = P0 1 − e−β·I(n) .

300   Optimal allocation of computational resources between model size and training data is paramount for
301   achieving the best performance while avoiding sub-scaling effects. Striking the right balance ensures that
302   computational resources are used most efficiently, thereby maximizing performance improvements and
303   mitigating the diminishing returns associated with sub-scaling laws. We delve into the impact of different
304
      allocation strategies, focusing on the effects of non-optimal allocation especially on over-training (i.e., using
      more tokens than optimal relative to the model size).
305
306   The theoretical foundation for optimal resource allocation is encapsulated by the compute budget relationship:
307   FLOPs(N, D) ≈ 6N D, where N is the model size (number of parameters), D is the number of training
308   tokens, and the compute budget (FLOPs) is kept constant. The challenge lies in distributing this budget
309   optimally between increasing the model size and augmenting the dataset.
310   Over-training occurs when the number of training tokens significantly exceeds the optimal allocation for
311   a given model size. This imbalance results in diminishing returns and a divergence from the expected
312   performance improvements based on traditional scaling laws. We quantify over-training using the Over-
313   Training Ratio (OTR): OTR = DDopt where D is the total number of training tokens, and Dopt represents the
314   optimal number of tokens required for the model size N .
315

354   3.1   A PPROACH 1: E STIMATING S UB -O PTIMAL S CALING L AW VIA DATA D ENSITY
355
356   To understand how data density impacts performance, we estimated the power-law relationship for datasets
357   with similar densities. The performance P (n) was modeled using:
358
359
                                             P (n) = P0 1 − e−βI(n) .                                      (6)
360
      For high-density datasets where information gain diminishes, we model the information gain I(n) as:
361
      I(n) = I0 · n−α . In high-density datasets, redundancy is prevalent. This means that as more data is added,
362   the amount of new, unique information gained decreases. This redundancy leads to diminishing returns,
363   where each additional sample contributes less to the overall performance improvement of the model. The
364   decay factor RD allows the model to more accurately represent this non-linear relationship and the reduced
365   effectiveness of additional data. Thus, we use decay factors RD to show the influence of data density for
366   fitting sub-optimal scaling:
367                                                P = RD · λ · C α ,                                         (7)
368   where λ and α are empirically determined coefficients. Given this equation, we could fit different density
369   datasets with varying RD values to account for the influence of data density on performance. The fitting

462
      This study systematically investigated the sub-scaling law phenomenon in large language models (LLMs),
463
      where performance improvements decelerate as model or dataset size increases. Through extensive empirical
464
      analysis of over 400 models, we identified key factors contributing to sub-scaling: high data density and
465   non-optimal training resource allocations. Our findings highlight that high data density leads to diminishing
466   marginal gains in performance, while optimal resource allocation is crucial for sustaining improvements.
467   We proposed a Sub-Optimal Scaling Law that better predicts performance and loss in sub-scaling regimes
468   by accounting for data quality and diversity. Future research should refine this sub-optimal scaling law and
469   explore its applicability to other models and tasks.

629   A.1    DATASET
630
631   For all datasets utilized in our study, we adhered to a systematic preprocessing routine to ensure consistency
632   and optimize training efficiency similar to previous works (Xie et al., 2024; Hoffmann et al., 2022).
633
      To enhance the training process, we organized the examples by domain. This organization allows for
634   hierarchical sampling, where a domain is first selected according to predefined weights, and subsequently, an
635   example is randomly chosen from the selected domain. This method ensures that the model is exposed to
636   a diverse range of text styles and content during training, which is critical for developing robust language
637   models.
638
      Furthermore, to reduce the number of padding tokens, which can adversely affect model training efficiency
639
      and performance, we employed a packing strategy. In this strategy, examples, potentially from different
640   domains, are packed together into the same sequence. This approach not only reduces padding but also
641   introduces a degree of domain variability within the same batch, potentially enhancing the model’s ability to
642   generalize across different types of text.
643
      The primary dataset used in our experiments is The Pile (Gao et al., 2020), a comprehensive text corpus that
644
      consists of approximately 800GB of text spanning 22 different domains. The Pile is well-regarded in the
645
      language modeling community for its diversity and volume, making it an ideal choice for studying the effects
646   of over-training. The default sampling weights for The Pile were determined based on heuristic methods to
647   balance the representation of each domain adequately.
648
649
      By meticulously preparing and preprocessing our datasets, we ensure that the models are trained under
      optimal conditions, allowing us to accurately assess the effects of over-training and the validity of the newly
650
      proposed scaling laws.

      or a linear function—we can look at it from the perspectives of Information Theory. Here, we’ll explore
712
      potential formulas and theoretical explanations for these phenomena.
713
714   Information Theory Perspective. From Information Theory, we can quantify the amount of new information
715   (or entropy) gained from each additional sample. The key concept here is the redundancy of information in
716   high-density datasets versus the diversity in low-density datasets.
717   High-Density Datasets (Power-Law Growth).
718
      Redundant Information: When a dataset has many duplicates, the mutual information between samples
719
      increases, leading to redundancy. The additional information gained from each new sample decreases as the
720
      number of samples increases.
721
722   Diminishing Returns: The concept of diminishing returns can be described by a power-law function. If I(n)
723   represents the information gained after seeing n samples, it can be modeled as:
724                                                    I(n) = I0 · n−α
725   where I0 is a constant representing the initial information gain and α is a positive constant indicating the rate
726   of diminishing returns.
727
728
      Performance Growth: - Performance P (n) as a function of the number of samples n can be expressed as:
729                                               P (n) = P0 · (1 − e−β·I(n) )
730   where P0 is the maximum achievable performance and β is a constant scaling the information gain to
731   performance.
732
733
      Low-Density Datasets (Linear Growth)
734   Diverse Information: In low-density datasets, each sample provides more unique information, leading to a
735   more consistent information gain.
736
      Consistent Returns: The information gain in a low-density dataset can be approximated as a linear function:
737
738
                                                         I(n) = I0 · n
739   where I0 is a constant representing the information gain per sample.
740   Performance Growth: Performance P (n) in a low-density dataset can be modeled as:
741
742                                               P (n) = P0 · (1 − e−β·I(n) )
743   Given I(n) is linear, this simplifies to:
744                                               P (n) = P0 · (1 − e−β·I0 ·n )

746
747                                                 P (n) ≈ P0 · β · I0 · n

      information. This can be modeled using:

762
      training efficiency and effectiveness, it does not significantly impact sub-scaling phenomena. Non-optimal
763
      hyper-parameters show poor performance from the beginning of the training process, rather than causing a
764   deceleration in performance improvement as the dataset or model size increases. Our findings demonstrate

766   The power-law relationships established through empirical data provide a solid foundation for setting these
767   hyper-parameters efficiently across different model sizes and training conditions. By optimizing these
768   hyper-parameters, we can achieve better performance while minimizing the computational expenditure and
769   mitigating the effects of over-training.
770   In the context of training large language models, managing the balance between sufficient training and
771   over-training is crucial for achieving optimal performance. Over-training can lead to diminished returns and
772   wasted computational resources. To address these challenges, we propose an optimal hyper-parameter search
773   method that is robust to the effects of over-training.
774
      Hyper-parameters play a critical role in the training of machine learning models. They control the learning
775
      process and significantly influence the model’s performance. However, the traditional hyper-parameter
776   optimization process does not take into account the potential for over-training, leading to selections that may
777   not be optimal under over-training conditions. Our proposed method focuses on identifying hyper-parameters
778   whose performance relationship remains stable and unaffected by over-training. This stability ensures that the
779   chosen hyper-parameters continue to yield the best possible performance even as the amount of training data
780   significantly exceeds the optimal level.

1029   Language Models Recently, a variety of large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Hoffmann
1030   et al., 2022; Rae et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2023; Reid et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024)
1031   have emerged, demonstrating excellent performance in the field of language processing. However, during the
1032   exploration in this field, the constraints of existing computational resources make training LLMs exhausting.
1033   Notably, the proposed scaling law suggests that the performance of smaller models can be extrapolated to

                                                                         22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1034
       larger ones (Kaplan et al., 2020), highlighting the significance of Small Language Models (SLMs). SLMs
1035
       are generally defined as models smaller in scale compared to well-known LLMs like Chinchilla (Hoffmann
1036   et al., 2022), typically not exceeding 7 billion parameters (Hu et al., 2024). Nonetheless, many factors impact
1037   performance improvement for SLMs, such as the availability of training tokens (Muennighoff et al., 2023). In
1038   this paper, we focus on the performance of LLMs under over-training conditions.
1039
       Scaling Laws for Large Language Models The development of large language models (LLMs) has sparked
1040
       significant interest in understanding their scaling laws due to the huge costs associated with their training.
1041
       Recent studies (Bahri et al., 2021; Kaplan et al., 2020) suggest a power-law relationship between the loss
1042   and the number of non-embedding parameters, dataset size, and compute budget for autoregressive language
1043   models (LM) across various scales. However, another study (Hoffmann et al., 2022) adjusted training settings,
1044   including training tokens and learning rate schedules, and concluded that model size and training tokens
1045   should be scaled equally, contrary to the findings in (Kaplan et al., 2020). Moreover, research (DeepSeek-AI
1046   et al., 2024) explores the scaling laws of batch size and learning rate relative to non-embedding FLOPs/token
1047   M , rather than model scale. This study presents an allocation strategy for scaling up models and data.
1048   Additionally, it investigates the impact of pre-training data quality on model performance. Furthermore, their
1049   results show that with the same compute budget, the optimal parameter space varies slightly, which has been
1050
       attributed to the selection of hyperparameters and training dynamics. Recent empirical studies Hernandez
       et al. (2022a); Hu et al. (2023); Porian et al. (2024); Muennighoff et al. (2024) have observed deviations
1051
       from this expected trend, particularly in the context of exceptionally large language models. These deviations
1052
       manifest as sub-scaling growth, where the rate of performance improvement decelerates as model or dataset
1053   size continues to increase. Specifically, Hernandez et al. (2022a); Muennighoff et al. (2024) observe that
1054   sub-scaling occurs in scenarios involving repeated training data, leading to diminishing returns in performance.
1055   Hu et al. (2023) highlight that sub-scaling is particularly pronounced in tasks requiring complex reasoning or
1056   multi-step processes. Furthermore, Porian et al. (2024) find that sub-scaling exists under non-optimal training
1057   strategies with sub-optimal hyper-parameters.
1058
       Compared to recent works (Kaplan et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022; Henighan et al., 2020; Ghorbani et al., 2021;
1059   Hernandez et al., 2021), which focus on general scaling laws, our study provides a detailed examination
1060   of sub-scaling laws under specific conditions. Previous research (Gadre et al., 2024) has also highlighted
1061   sub-scaling under over-training conditions, and some works (Muennighoff et al., 2024; Hernandez et al.,
1062   2022b) have explored scaling laws in data-constrained regimes. However, there is a lack of systematic
1063   research on the sub-scaling behavior of large language models (LLMs).
1064
       Language Model Behaviour under Over-training As the computational resources required to train larger
1065   models increase, over-training a smaller language model can be a more efficient strategy to achieve perfor-
1066   mance on par with larger models. It has been observed that smaller models can sometimes outperform larger
1067   ones. For instance, the 2B model MiniCPM (Hu et al., 2024) demonstrates capabilities comparable to those of
1068   larger models such as Llama2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mistral-7B. This underscores the importance of
1069   Small Language Models (SLMs) (Hu et al., 2024) and the influence of over-training conditions (Gadre et al.,
1070   2024). However, there is a lack of thorough investigation into scaling laws under over-training conditions
1071   for SLMs. Previous work (Gadre et al., 2024) focuses more on the optimal allocation of tokens and model
1072   size rather than its impact on the extrapolation of the scaling law. This work focuses on the performance
1073
       degradation or improvement caused by over-training and its impacts on the scaling law.
