009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The training and sampling of diffusion models have been exhaustively elucidated
012            in prior art (Karras et al., 2022; 2024b). Instead, the underlying network architec-
013            ture design remains on a shaky empirical footing. Furthermore, in accordance with
014            the recent trend of scaling law, large-scale models make inroads into generative
015            vision tasks. However, running such large diffusion models incurs a sizeable com-
016
               putational burden, rendering it desiderata to optimize calculations and efficiently
               allocate resources. To bridge these gaps, we navigate the design landscape of ef-
017
               ficient U-Net based diffusion models, stemming from the prestigious EDM2. Our
018
               exploration route is organized along two key axes, layer placement and module in-
019            terconnection. We systematically study fundamental design choices and uncover
020            several intriguing insights for superior efficacy and efficiency. These findings cul-
021            minate in our redesigned architecture, EDM2+, that reduces the computational
022            complexity of the baseline EDM2 by 2× without compromising the generation
023            quality. Extensive experiments and comparative analyses highlight the effective-
024            ness of our proposed network architecture, which achieves the state-of-the-art FID
025            on the hallmark ImageNet benchmark. Code will be released upon acceptance.

      In recent years, diffusion models have swept the field of generative modeling, catalyzing a plethora
030
      of applications to image (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024), video (Ho
031   et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023), and 3D shape generation (Poole et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023)
032   in the realm of visual synthesis. Dating back to a decade ago, the advent of diffusion models relies
033   on a plain Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architecture (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015). The
034   embarrassingly simple architecture might have posed a hindrance to the immediate blossoming of
035   diffusion models. During the following period, one has witnessed a meteoric rise of Generative
036   Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) in yielding photorealistic imagery (Karras
037   et al., 2018; 2019; 2020b; 2021). In the meantime, the development of diffusion models is sluggish
038   but has never stood still. Until 2020s, Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al.,
039
      2020) resurges, sparking a new wave of deep generative modeling. In this seminal work, DDPM,
      the introduction of U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) backbone in tandem with a few modern ar-
040
      chitectural components (e.g., Group Normalization (Wu & He, 2018), self-attention, and position
041
      embedding (Vaswani et al., 2017)) unleashes the potential of diffusion models in producing high-
042   quality images comparable to other types of generative models. Henceforth, diffusion models make
043   tremendous strides forward within the ambit of visual generative modeling.

116   2     R ELATED W ORK
117
118   We provide a skim-through of several important aspects revolving around diffusion models in prior
119   literature, spanning from training and sampling to network architecture design. We also clarify their
120   similarities and differences compared with our work.
121
122   2.1   D IFFUSION T RAINING
123
124   Drawing inspiration from nonequilibrium thermodynamics (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), diffusion
125   models decompose the entire generative process into progressive denoising transitions from standard
126
      Gaussian noise to clean images. In stark contrast to other explicit likelihood-based models (e.g.,
      Variational AutoEncoder (Kingma & Welling, 2014), Autoregressive models (van den Oord et al.,
127
      2016; Salimans et al., 2017), and Non-Autoregressive models (Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023))
128
      or implicit likelihood-based models (e.g., GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014)), diffusion models pose
129   the generation task as a supervised learning scheme, greatly enhancing training stability and thus
130   scalability. In practice, the likelihood-induced Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) is simplified to an ℓ2
131   regression learning objective. Depending on this realization, different regression targets, including
132   image (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), noise (Ho et al., 2020), and velocity (Salimans & Ho, 2022),
133   simply translate to different loss function weights (Kingma & Gao, 2023). In consequence, scaling
134   up diffusion models to billions of parameters and web-scale training data becomes more frictionless
135   compared to the previous prevalent GANs (Kang et al., 2023), incubating a bunch of text-to-image
136   commercial products, such as Stable Diffusion series (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2024;
137
      Esser et al., 2024), DALL·E 2&3 (Nichol et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023),
      and Imagen series (Saharia et al., 2022; Imagen-Team-Google et al., 2024). In the present work, we
138
      inherit the EDM (Karras et al., 2022) preconditioning framework for training due to its well-behaved
139
      training dynamics.
140
141
      2.2   D IFFUSION S AMPLING
142
143   For diffusion models, the sampling procedure typically demands thousands of consecutive steps to
144   synthesize a high-quality image. Theoretically, the diffusion backward process could be interpreted
145   as a reverse Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) or the corresponding Probability Flow Ordinary
146   Differential Equation (PF-ODE) (Song et al., 2021b). Therefore, there naturally exists a trade-off
147   between the discretion error and step size. The straighter the sampling trajectory, the larger step size
148   can be tolerated. As such, much endeavor has been devoted to straightening the trajectory (Song
149   et al., 2021a; Karras et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) and inventing advanced ODE solvers (Liu et al.,
150
      2022a; Lu et al., 2022; Zhang & Chen, 2023). In parallel, a growing body of diffusion distillation
      techniques (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Meng et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024) is proposed
151
      to reduce the number of sampling steps. In addition, the overall sampling cost could also be re-
152
      duced by cutting down the model latency per step in the denoising trajectory. Our work goes along
153   this research vein, contributing to a compact yet high-performing model via reworking the network
154   structure from scratch. Hence, our work distinguishes itself from post-hoc pruning (Li et al., 2023b),
155   quantization (Li et al., 2023a) and cache (Wimbauer et al., 2024) methodology but is complementary.
156
157   2.3   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE E NGINEERING
158
159   U-Net is ubiquitously applied to low-level vision tasks, including visual segmentation (Chen et al.,
160   2018), generation (Kingma et al., 2016) and so on. Skip connections in the network always play an
161   instrumental role in transmitting the high-resolution signal to the output end for detailed refinement,
      either in CNN or Transformer (Bao et al., 2023). Building upon a U-Net backbone, DDPM (Ho et al.,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2020) interleaves convolution blocks with self-attention modules (Vaswani et al., 2017), effectively
163   gathering long-range pixel dependence. iDDPM (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) extends single-head
164   self-attention to multi-head ones and widens its usage over a broader range of feature resolutions.
165   Adaptive Group Norm (AdaGN) is involved as well, resembling AdaIN (Huang & Belongie, 2017).
166   ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) additionally steals the network topology and scaled residual con-
167   nections from the GAN literature (Brock et al., 2019; Karras et al., 2020b). Several hyperparameters
168   are ablated here, including the network depth/width and the number of attention heads. EDM2 (Kar-
169   ras et al., 2024b) emphasizes standardizing the magnitudes of network weights, activations, gradi-
170   ents, etc., in the same spirit of pioneering magnitude-focusing image recognition networks (Brock
171
      et al., 2021a;b). Diffusion Transformers (DiT) position themselves as appealing alternatives to the
      de facto standard U-Net, attracting enthusiasm from both academia and industry (Peebles & Xie,
172
      2023; Hoogeboom et al., 2023). Subsequently, DiffuSSM (Yan et al., 2024) supplants the attention
173
      mechanism of DiT with the State Space Model (SSM) (Gu et al., 2022) blocks to promote the effi-
174   ciency. Our work takes root in a hybrid architecture, EDM2, that alternates convolution blocks with
175   attention modules and manifests as an outstanding denoiser architecture.

      3     D ESIGN ROADMAP
179
      We first give a brief recap on the network design of preeminent diffusion models. Next, we provide
180
      our intuition about model design and present the roadmap to an efficient architecture while preserv-
181
      ing the generation quality, in which the design path could be split into two branches, layer placement
182   in the denoising network and its synergy with the embedding network.
183
184
      3.1   P RELIMINARY
185
186   EDM1 and EDM2 commonly employ the encoder-decoder paradigm in the denoising network, into
187   which a noise-perturbed image is fed and from which a reparameterized denoised image is retrieved.
188   Each network block stacks two 3 × 3 regular convolutions for feature extraction or mixing. Differ-
189   ently, Group Normalization (Wu & He, 2018) and SiLU nonlinearity (Ramachandran et al., 2018)
190   precede convolution in EDM1 while EDM2 revokes such normalizations and substitutes SiLU with
191   MP-SiLU. A skip connection is indispensable to facilitate smooth training (He et al., 2016). For-
192
      mally, a network block either in the encoder or decoder of EDM2 could be formulated as
193                                  o = conv(φ(conv(φ(x)))) + γ(x),                                     (1)
194
      where φ denotes the MP-SiLU activation function and γ refers to a potential linear projection that
195   compresses channels only in the decoder part (otherwise it is identity in the encoder part). o and x
196   enumerate the input and output feature tensor of the considered network block. Moreover, for con-
197   ditional generation, a condition embedding is utilized to rectify the midway representation, written
198   as
199                               o = conv(φ(conv(φ(x)) × ϕ(c))) + γ(x),                                 (2)
200   where c defines the condition information and ϕ symbolizes the mapping network that transforms
201   it into a high-dimensional embedding. A self-attention module is suggested to append to this con-
202   volution block in case that the feature resolution is low, for example, 1/4 and 1/8 of the input noisy
203   image resolution. Since self-attention does not occupy a majority of the computation in our context,
204   we omit to discuss it in the sequel.
205   Reminiscent of the classical Progressive GAN (Karras et al., 2018), EDM2 draws a wealth of lessons
206   from it: standard normal weight initialization, constant input channel concatenation, pixel normal-
207   ization, and magnitude-preserving learned layers (aka equalized learning rate). EDM2 also echoes
208   certain modern architecture design philosophies: pixel norm analogous to RMSNorm (Zhang &
209   Sennrich, 2019), magnitude-preserving layers to weight standardization (Qiao et al., 2020) and co-
210   sine attention to QK Normalization (Dehghani et al., 2023). In turn, EDM2 now serves as a starting
211   point for our redesigned architecture. To hit our ultimate design, we also additionally tap wisdom
212
      from other celebrated model architectures.

214   ploration journey in Table 1 and 2. Concretely, the revisions are enabled one by one on top of the
215   baseline EDM2 and we delineate the stepwise quality metric accompanied by the model parameters

243   3.2   D ENOISING N ETWORK D ESIGN
244
245   Our overarching goal is to slim the model architecture without prejudice to the generation quality.
246
      Recall that previous lightweight network designs usually resort to a couple of depthwise and point-
      wise convolutions, supporting spatial and channel information mixing respectively. On this premise,
247
      we further posit that at the heart of a visual synthesis task is not only spatial pattern mixing or re-
248
      finement but also composing semantically meaningful components for a high-fidelity imagery. In
249   essence, this task is complicated by reasoning from the interaction between scene and objects, which
250   cannot be solely reflected from superficial spatial patterns. Thus, once within a tight computational
251   budget, we advocate trading the spatial representation mixing for stronger semantic representation
252   learning in the channel dimension.
253

254

255   capacity. Then, each building block can be derived as

      3.4     E MBEDDING N ETWORK D ESIGN
348
349   The condition embedding is the crux of injecting external condition signals into the main stream of
350   the denoising network. The condition information might be a timestamp (Ho et al., 2020) or a noise
351   level (Song & Ermon, 2019), a class label (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) or a more verbose textual
352   description (Rombach et al., 2022). In this work, we operate on the noise level and the class label
353   information, since our exploration is set out on the ImageNet benchmark for class-conditioned image
354   synthesis. Typically, an input numeral, representative of the condition information, is appropriately
355   scaled and mapped to a high-dimensional space using non-learnable Fourier feature (Tancik et al.,
356   2020) or sinusoidal embedding, in concert with a (few) learnable linear projection layer(s). This
357
      stack of neural layers is collectively dubbed as the embedding network.
358   It is trendy that the embedding network is gradually minimized, exemplified by the shallower map-
359   ping network in StyleGAN2-ADA (Karras et al., 2020a) or StyleGAN3 (Karras et al., 2021) and the
360   trimmed embedding network in EDM2 (Karras et al., 2024b). The model capacity of this tiny net-
361   work is presumably sufficient to extract semantic information from a single scalar condition, while a
362   shorter path here permits the denoising network to be better informed of the condition information.
363
      Provided the network depth is extremely truncated by design, we are particularly interested in how
      to maximize its cooperation with the denoising network from other factors, for instance, whether
364
      the network width of their junction makes a difference to the generation quality and parameter effi-
365
      ciency.
366

370

371
      flow. To substantiate our hypothesis, we reposition the embedding network after the last pointwise
372   convolution, constructing an “embed bottleneck” in CONFIG G∗ (similar for CONFIG F∗ )

406
407
      4.1      DATASETS
408
      We adopt ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) pixel-space diffusion at 64×64 resolution as the benchmark.
409   Before training, we follow ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) protocol to pre-process the raw Ima-
410   geNet dataset for a fair comparison to previous works. Specifically, the images are resized along the
411   short edge and then cropped at the center to a desired square shape. No data augmentation is applied
412   during training, since a large-scale dataset like ImageNet is deemed to be challenging enough to fit
413   for most visual generation models.
414
415   4.2      E VALUATION
416
417   The evaluated checkpoints are constructed post-hoc through power-function Exponential Moving
418   Average (EMA) over a group of snapshots with the recommended length by EDM2. The evaluation
419   metric is the widely recognized FID (Heusel et al., 2017). It compares the distribution statistics
420
      of 50K synthesized samples against all the 1,281,167 real images in the training dataset, in line
      with common practice. The class labels for the 50K synthesized images are drawn from a uniform
421
      distribution. For feature extraction, we use the pre-trained Inception-v3 (Szegedy et al., 2016) model
422
      provided by StyleGAN3 (Karras et al., 2021). Limited by the computational resource, we compute
423   FID only once, which may even put us at a disadvantage in comparison to EDM2 (because EDM2
424   computes FID three times and reports the minimum).

527   5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
528
529   This work invests effort into the rapidly evolving arena of diffusion model architectures, via un-
530   dertaking a systematic exploration and unraveling practical guidelines for efficient network design.
531   The valuable discoveries, pinpointing the significance of layer placement and module interconnec-
532   tion, are leveraged to deliver a model family named EDM2+. Our presented EDM2+ architecture
533   achieves pronounced efficiency gains against the EDM2 counterpart and redefines the state-of-the-
534   art performance of generative modeling.
535   Despite promising, the practical runtime is expected to be further optimized for real-time deploy-
536   ment. Probing more fine-grained architecture design options, such as the preference discrepancies
537   between the network encoder and decoder, or even the per-block design regime, is intended as the
538   next step in our research agenda. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) (Zoph & Le, 2017; Zoph et al.,
539   2018) is a plausible avenue to reach this goal. Marrying our EDM2+ architecture to the latent-space
      diffusion for high-resolution image synthesis is also left as our future work.
