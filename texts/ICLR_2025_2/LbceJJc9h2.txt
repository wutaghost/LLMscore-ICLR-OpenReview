                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               In-context learning (ICL) describes a language model’s ability to generate outputs
010
               based on a set of input demonstrations and a subsequent query. To understand this
011
               remarkable capability, researchers have studied simplified, stylized models. These
012            studies have consistently observed long loss plateaus, during which models exhibit
013            minimal improvement, followed by a sudden, rapid surge of learning. In this work,
014            we reveal that training on multiple diverse ICL tasks simultaneously shortens the
015            loss plateaus, making each task easier to learn. This finding is surprising as it
016            contradicts the natural intuition that the combined complexity of multiple ICL
017            tasks would lengthen the learning process, not shorten it. Our result suggests that
018            the recent success in large-scale training of language models may be attributed not
019            only to the richness of the data at scale but also to the easier optimization (training)
020
               induced by the diversity of natural language training data.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      In-context learning (ICL), first reported by Brown et al. (2020) with GPT-3, describes a language
057   model’s ability to generate outputs based on a set of input demonstrations and a subsequent query.
058   In ICL, the model discerns the task implied by the context of these demonstrations without explicit
059   descriptions, indicating that the model may internally implement an algorithm or engage in a rea-
060   soning process. To understand this remarkable capability that emerges in language models trained
061   on complex real-world language data, researchers such as Garg et al. (2022) have studied simplified,
062   stylized models. In these studies, transformers are trained from scratch to learn simple functions in
063   context, such as linear regression. We thoroughly review this prior work in Section 1.1.
064   An intriguing phenomenon observed in these works is the long loss plateau in training for ICL.
065   Throughout these plateaus, models display minimal performance improvement, followed by a sud-
066   den, rapid surge of learning—deviating from the typical smooth reduction in training loss. In this
067   work, we reveal that training on multiple ICL tasks simultaneously shortens the loss plateaus
068   as illustrated in Figure 1. This finding is surprising as it contradicts the natural intuition that the
069   combined complexity of multiple ICL tasks would lengthen the learning process, not shorten it.
070   In the language of multi-task learning, our findings present an instance where multi-task learning
071   is easier than single-task learning in the sense of training dynamics. While previous research has
072   primarily focused on the statistical benefits of task diversity in multi-task learning, our findings
073   reveal optimization benefits. This insight suggests that the recent success in large-scale training of
074   language models may be attributed not only to the richness of the data at scale but also to the easier
075   optimization (training) induced by the diversity of natural language training data.
076
077   Organization. Section 2 describes the experimental setup for training for multiple ICL tasks. Sec-
078
      tion 3 articulates our main claim that task diversity shortens the ICL plateau and presents experi-
      mental evidence with transformers and state-space models trained on synthetic and natural language
079
      ICL tasks. Section 4 investigates the underlying reasons for this phenomenon. Section 4.1 charac-
080
      terizes the model’s behavior during the plateau, which we refer to as the no-context learning regime.
081   Section 4.2 shows that there is a common structure shared across the ICL tasks and that task diver-
082   sity accelerates the learning of this shared structure. Section 5 presents further experimental details.
083   Section 6 concludes the paper.

085   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
086
087   In-context learning. In-context learning (ICL) abilities of pretrained Large Language Models
088   have gained significant attention since first investigated by GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020). Follow-
089   ing this, a large body of empirical studies have explored ICL in Large Language Models (Min et al.,
090   2022a;b; Liu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023). Given the complexity
091
      of real-world data, researchers have explored ICL in more stylized and simplified setups. Garg et al.
      (2022) formalized an approach to studying transformer’s performance to learn simple function class
092
      in context. Building on this work, several works have investigated the ability of models to learn
093
      stylized function classes in various settings, including boolean functions (Bhattamishra et al., 2024),
094   regular language (Akyürek et al., 2024), as well as task mixtures (Tripuraneni et al., 2023), and the
095   ability of Mamba (Park et al., 2024; Grazzi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). For more comprehensive
096   overview, please refer to the survey by Dong et al. (2024).
097
      While these studies have scrutinized ICL abilities across various setups, most have not delved into
098
      the in-context algorithms that are implemented by models. Xie et al. (2022) suggested that the ICL
099
      process can be interpreted as Bayesian inference. A number of works have argued, both theoretically
100   and empirically, that transformers implement gradient descent to learn linear regression in-context
101   (Akyürek et al., 2023; von Oswald et al., 2023; Mahankali et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Ahn
102   et al., 2023). Beyond these works, many have sought to uncover the internal ICL procedure of
103   transformers, in more complex algorithms (Fu et al., 2023; Giannou et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024;
104   von Oswald et al., 2024; Lin & Lee, 2024); when handling more intricate tasks (Wang et al., 2024;
105   Guo et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024).

144   trained models to outperform in downstream tasks, alongside a large body of research across various
145   domains: supervised learning (Tripuraneni et al., 2020; 2021; Du et al., 2021; Maurer et al., 2016;
146   Crawshaw, 2020; Ruder, 2017), reinforcement learning (Zhang et al., 2023a; Jin et al., 2020; Hu
147
      et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Collins et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2022), and natural
      language processing (Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2020;
148
      Song et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2019; Gunasekar et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023). These works
149
      mostly focused on statistical benefits, whereas our finding emphasize on the optimization benefits.

190   3    TASK DIVERSITY SHORTENS ICL PLATEAUS
191
192   Long loss plateaus have been commonly reported in the various setups for training sequence models
193   from scratch to perform ICL, including simple in-context functions (Garg et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
194   2024; Li et al., 2024; Bhattamishra et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024), image datasets (Fu et al., 2024;
195   Kirsch et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2024; Reddy, 2024), and language datasets (Akyürek et al., 2024;
196   Olsson et al., 2022). In this section, we present the following claim:
197
198       Claim: Task diversity shortens the ICL plateau, making each task easier to learn.
199
200
      Here, task diversity refers to learning multi-task ICL on a mixture of distinct function classes. The
      claim that multi-task ICL is easier to learn than single-task ICL is surprising as it contradicts the
201
      natural intuition that the combined complexity of multiple ICL tasks would lengthen the learning
202
      process, not shorten it.
203
204   We provide comprehensive experimental evidence to support this claim. Table 1 summarizes our
205   experimental results on transformers. Figure 3 shows the results of state space models, specifically
206
      Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) and Hyena (Poli et al., 2023). Across the hundreds of task combinations
      in these setups, we consistently observed that task diversity enables training to escape plateaus
207
      more quickly. Figure 7 shows results in natural language ICL tasks reinforcing our claim. Refer to
208
      Appendix A for experimental details and Appendix B for additional tables.
209
210   However, not all ICL tasks mutually reduce the duration of plateaus. For instance, we consider
211   Regbench task (Akyürek et al., 2024), which is generated by a random automata. Our experiments
212
      in Appendix C.2 show that combining Regbench with the ICL tasks we consider does not reduce the
      plateau of Regbench, but does reduce the plateau of the other ICL tasks.
213
214   Therefore, the claim should be understood as a description of a general tendency rather than a
215   universal law. Nevertheless, our finding is broadly and robustly observed, as borne out by our
      extensive experiments.

349   4.2     C OMMON STRUCTURE ACROSS ICL TASKS
350                                                                               Sk
351   Consider a multi-task ICL setup, where a model is trained on a set of tasks m=1 Tm . Suppose there
352   exists a “common structure” C shared across tasks. Denoting the remaining part of each task as Im ,
353   we can decompose each task as Tm = C + Im for m = 1, . . . , k. Thus, multi-task ICL training is
354
      decomposed into two sub-problems: [learning C] and [learning I1 , . . . , Ik ]. We argue that the main
      claim of Section 3 can be explained by the following hypotheses:
355
356
      (1) There exists a common structure shared across the multiple ICL tasks.
357
358   (2) The ICL plateau arises from the difficulty of learning this common structure.
359   (3) Training multiple tasks jointly with a shared structure makes it easier to learn that structure.
360
361   In the following, we present evidence supporting these hypotheses. Section 4.2.1 demonstrates (1)
362   and (2). Section 4.2.2 elucidates (3) with a toy experiment on feature learning.
363

416   4.2.3   T HE COMMON STRUCTURE IS NOT JUST AN INDUCTION HEAD
417
418
      So then, what specifically is this common structure? We believe it must involve some algorithmic
      component, as all of the ICL tasks necessitate an internal algorithm to identify the specific function
419
      being demonstrated by the in-context demonstrations.
420
421   A plausible candidate is the induction head, a circuit that searches over the sequence for previous
422   instances of a current token and predicts the same completion again. Indeed, Olsson et al. (2022)
423   argued that the development of an induction head coincides with the escape from the training plateau.
424
      To test this idea, we designed the following Retrieval ICL task, inspired by the prior ICL tasks from
      Park et al. (2024); Singh et al. (2024); Reddy (2024).
425
426   Retrieval. Sample 1024 5-tuples of one key and four values: {(ki , vi1 , vi2 , vi3 , vi4 )}1024
                                                                                                 i=1 . The ki s and
427   vij s are independently sampled from DK and DV , respectively. To generate prompts, we uniformly
428   sample (n − 1) 5-tuples without replacement and uniformly choose one vi per 5-tuple, resulting

                     i=1 . Next, we sample p ∼ Unif ({1, . . . , n − 1}) and set q = kp . Finally, given
430   Pn = (k1 , v1 , . . . , kn−1 , vn−1 , q), the task is to predict vp . We consider two Retrieval tasks:
431   Gaussian Retrieval with (DK , DV ) = (N (0, Id ), N (0, 1)) and Boolean Retrieval with (DK , DV ) =
      (Unif {±1}d , Unif {±1}).

      Note that the induction head is precisely the mechanism for solving this Retrieval task. We conduct
433   a checkpoint experiment for the Retrieval tasks, allowing the models to learn the induction head. To
434   train continuous ICL tasks, we use a checkpoint model pre-trained with Gaussian Retrieval. To train
435   Boolean ICL tasks, we use a checkpoint model pre-trained with Boolean Retrieval. We find that the
436   checkpoint does not significantly shorten the ICL tasks’ plateaus. This result demonstrates that the
437   common structure shared by other ICL tasks is not just an induction head. (It is unclear whether an
438   induction head is useful for our ICL tasks at all.) For further details, please refer to Appendix D.2.
439   Presently, the problem of characterizing the common structure with any specificity remains unre-
440   solved. We defer further investigation of this matter to future work.

475   6   C ONCLUSION
476
477   In this work, we identify that training on a diverse set of multiple ICL tasks is surprisingly easier
478   than training for a single ICL task in the sense of a more favorable optimization landscape. This
479   observation aligns with the “blessing of dimensionality/scale” seen in the modern era of deep learn-
480   ing. Indeed, LLM training via next-token prediction can be thought of effectively as a highly diverse
481   multi-task learning, requiring a wide range of reasoning skills for a wide range of text types, and the
482
      success of LLM training may be attributed not only to the richness of the data at scale but also to the
      easier optimization (training) induced by the diversity of natural language training data.
483
484   This insight opens new avenues for future work. It may be that explaining and understanding the
485   success of large-scale deep learning requires considering not just the large data, large network, and
      large compute but also the large (effective) task diversity.

766   employs 24 layers, doubling the number of layers to match the model parameter size as is standard

      for Mamba. All models contain input and output projection heads that map input to the hidden
