009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                       The recent development of Video-based Large Language Models (VideoLLMs),
012                       has significantly advanced video summarization by aligning video features—and,
013                       in some cases, audio features—with Large Language Models (LLMs). Each of
014                       these VideoLLMs possesses unique strengths and weaknesses. Many recent meth-
015                       ods have required extensive fine-tuning to overcome the limitations of these mod-
016                       els, which can be resource-intensive. In this work, we observe that the strengths
017                       of one VideoLLM can complement the weaknesses of another. Leveraging this
018                       insight, we propose a novel video summarization framework inspired by the Mix-
019
                          ture of Experts (MoE) paradigm, which operates as an inference-time algorithm
                          without requiring any form of fine-tuning. Our approach integrates multiple Vide-
020
                          oLLMs to generate comprehensive and coherent textual summaries. It effectively
021
                          combines visual and audio content, provides detailed background descriptions,
022                       and excels at identifying keyframes, which enables more semantically meaningful
023                       retrieval compared to traditional computer vision approaches that rely solely on
024                       visual information, all without the need for additional fine-tuning. Moreover, the
025                       resulting summaries enhance performance in downstream tasks such as summary
026                       video generation, either through keyframe selection or in combination with text-
027                       to-image models. Our language-driven approach offers a semantically rich alter-
028                       native to conventional methods and provides flexibility to incorporate newer Vide-
029                       oLLMs, enhancing adaptability and performance in video summarization tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
      In recent day, the proliferation of video content across various platforms has led to an overwhelm-
      ing amount of information, making it challenging for users to efficiently access and digest the key
058
      information. As a result, video summarization has emerged as a crucial task, enabling the efficient
059
      extraction of key segments from lengthy videos. The goal of video summarization is to condense
060   extensive video content into textual summaries, short video clips, or a collection of representative
061   images. Given the vast amount of video data generated daily, effective summarization not only
062   enhances user experience by reducing the time required to access essential information but also
063   supports efficient content management and retrieval across platforms. Additionally, video sum-
064   marization has significant applications in areas such as surveillance, education, entertainment, and
065   multimedia indexing, making it a vital tool for navigating and leveraging the vast expanse of video
066   data available today.
067   The success of Visual Language Models (VLMs) (Liu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Alayrac
068   et al., 2022) has paved the way for the development of Video LLMs, such as VideoLLaMA (Zhang
069   et al., 2023), VideoChat (Li et al., 2023a), and VideoLLaVA (Lin et al., 2023a). These VideoLLMs
070   leverage human-annotated data for instruction tuning, and they propose different methods to align
071   video features with the LLM feature space. Each model exhibits distinct strengths: for example,
072   PG-Video-LLaVA (Munasinghe et al., 2023) demonstrates pixel-grounded capabilities for captur-
073   ing detailed scenes, Video-LLaMA adopts a multi-branch cross-modal framework that incorporates
074
      audio information in addition to video content, and LLaMA-VID (Li et al., 2023b) excels in captur-
      ing background scene details. However, despite these strengths, existing VideoLLMs have inherent
075
      shortcomings and lack coherent methods to address them. For instance, Video-LLaVA and LLaMA-
076
      VID are unable to retrieve audio signals, while Video-LLaMA lacks the grounding abilities required
077   for retrieving fine-grained details. Additionally, LLMs within these VideoLLMs often suffer from
078   hallucination issues. To overcome these limitations, previous approaches typically resort to fine-
079   tuning or retraining models, which can be computationally expensive. Our observation, however,
080   suggests that the limitations of one VideoLLM can often be mitigated by the strengths of another.
081   This leads us to ask: What if we could utilize existing VideoLLMs collaboratively, instead of re-
082   sorting to costly fine-tuning or retraining of a new model?
083   In this work, we draw inspiration from the Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Shen et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
084   2024) paradigm, which is designed to enhance performance in processing large and complex tasks
085   by leveraging multiple expert sub-models. Specifically, our approach employs multiple VideoLLMs
086   for video summarization, integrating the concept of LLM cooperation to combine the outputs from
087   these video “experts” through our proposed inference-time algorithm. This method allows us to
088   address the limitations of individual VideoLLMs by compensating with the strengths of other expert
089   VideoLLMs. Furthermore, since our framework does not require fine-tuning or retraining, it can
090   seamlessly adapt to incorporate new or updated VideoLLMs as additional expert models.
091   Overall, we propose a novel video summarization method that follows a unique path of language-
092   based semantic understanding. By proposing an inference-time algorithm, we can generate compre-
093   hensive textual summaries that capture not only visual content but also audio information, providing
094   detailed descriptions of background scenes to offer users a more holistic view of the original videos.
095   Additionally, with our comprehensive textual summaries, we can perform various downstream video
096   summarization tasks, such as identifying keyframes and generating images and videos, thereby sur-
097
      passing the capabilities of existing VideoLLMs.
098   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
099
100          • We propose an inference-time algorithm that leverages the capabilities of LLMs to combine
101            the output summaries of multiple VideoLLMs into a single, coherent, and unbiased sum-
102            mary. This approach provides more detailed and comprehensive information, enhancing
103            the overall quality of video summarization.
104          • Additionally, our comprehensive and coherent summaries enhance keyframe retrieval with
105            a simple keyframe selection algorithm, surpassing the performance of existing approaches.
106
107          • Our proposed method is both flexible and general. The components of our framework can
               be easily replaced with more powerful models. Moreover, it is general enough to support

               extended video applications that can leverage our intermediate outputs, such as textual

112   2   R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Large Language Models. Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of nat-
115   ural language processing (NLP) and artificial general intelligence (AGI) with their exceptional ca-
116   pabilities in language generation, in-context learning, and reasoning. The historical evolution of
117   these models began with foundational architectures such as BERT (Devlin, 2018), GPT-2 (Rad-
118   ford et al., 2019), and T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), which set the stage for subsequent advancements.
119
      The introduction of GPT-3 (Brown, 2020), with its 175 billion parameters, marked a significant
      breakthrough, showcasing remarkable performance across a wide spectrum of language tasks. This
120
      progress spurred the development of an array of other influential LLMs, including Megatron-Turing
121
      NLG (Smith et al., 2022), Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022), PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2023), OPT
122   (Zhang et al., 2022), BLOOM (Le Scao et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), MOSS (Sun
123   et al., 2024), and GLM (Zeng et al., 2022). These models, characterized by their scale and open-
124   source availability, have become invaluable for both training large models and fine-tuning them for
125   specific applications.
126
127   Visual Language Models. With the emergence of LLMs, recent works (Liu et al., 2024; Wang
128   et al., 2023b; Alayrac et al., 2022) have increasingly explored their use in processing visual inputs,
129   giving rise to Visual Language Models (VLMs). The central idea behind this line of work is to
130   align visual features with the textual features of LLMs by utilizing a common framework. This
131
      framework typically involves a pretrained visual encoder to extract visual features, a projection
      layer to map these visual representations into the text latent space of LLMs, and the pretrained
132
      LLM to generate responses, thereby enabling the powerful capabilities of LLMs to be applied to
133
      vision tasks. Video-based Large Language Models (VideoLLMs) extend the capabilities of VLMs
134   by incorporating temporal and/or audio features, allowing for richer video-language understanding
135   through human-video dialogue interactions. For instance, methods such as VideoChatGPT (Maaz
136   et al., 2024) and Valley (Luo et al., 2023) use pooling over visual tokens to obtain compact visual
137   representations. VideoChat (Li et al., 2023a) employs pretrained video foundation models and Q-
138   Former (Zhang et al., 2024) from BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2022) to aggregate video representations. Video-
139   LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023) introduces a Video Q-Former and an Audio Q-Former for multimodal
140   video comprehension. Furthermore, MovieChat (Song et al., 2024) proposes an advanced memory
141   management mechanism for reasoning over extended video content.
142
143   LLM Evaluator. The field of Natural Language Generation (NLG) evaluation has evolved con-
144
      siderably over the years, launching from traditional metrics to more advanced methodologies, par-
      ticularly with the advent of LLMs. Early metrics, such as ROUGE (Lin, 2004) and BLEU (Papineni
145
      et al., 2002), have been foundational in assessing the quality of generated text by comparing it to ref-
146
      erence texts based on n-gram overlap. However, these methods have limitations in capturing deeper
147   semantic nuances. To address this, embedding-based metrics like BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019)
148   were introduced, measuring the semantic similarity between texts using word and sentence embed-
149   dings. With the rise of LLMs, evaluation methods have further advanced. LLM-based evaluators,
150   such as GPTScore (Fu et al., 2023), G-Eval (Liu et al., 2023a), and UniEval (Zhong et al., 2022),
151   leverage the comprehensive understanding and generation capabilities of LLMs to provide deeper
152   insights into NLG quality. Recognizing the inherent limitations of these early approaches, subse-
153   quent studies concentrated on enhancing factual accuracy (Min et al., 2023), ensuring interpretability
154   (Lu et al., 2024), reducing position bias (Wang et al., 2023a), and aligning evaluation more closely
155
      with human judgment standards (Liu et al., 2023b). These efforts represent a significant shift toward
      more robust and human-aligned evaluation methods in NLG.

185   3.1      V IDEO S UMMARIZATION
186
187   We perform inference on the given input video using multiple VideoLLMs. To fully leverage the
188   capabilities of these models, we design and employ prompts specifically tailored to the architecture
189   of each VideoLLM. This approach results in four unique summaries, each capturing different aspects
190   of the input video and reflecting the strengths of each model.
191
192   3.1.1      D ENOISE - AND -C OOPERATE
193
194   There are two primary challenges in utilizing the generated summaries from these VideoLLMs.
195   First, each VideoLLM exhibits varying degrees of the “hallucination” issue, which can mislead
196   users and make us difficult to identify the inaccuracies specific to each model. Second, effectively
197
      integrating and combining the “strengths” of each model from the resulting summaries is a complex
      task. To address these challenges, we propose the following strategies:
198
199   Filter Outliers. We propose two outlier filtering strategies to remove the summaries that deviate
200   from the others, that is, from the four distinct summaries generated in the previous step, we identify
201   and exclude the summary that exhibits the lowest similarity to the other three, considering it an
                                                                                                                           Fi l t er Out l i er s


202   outlier. For the first strategy, we reference the scoring method from Open-Sora1 to evaluate the
203
      summaries generated by each VideoLLM. By calculating the matching score between each summary
      and the middle frame of the video, we identify and remove the summary with the lowest score. As for
204
      the second strategy, we aim to enhance the video-text alignment between the generated summaries
205
      and the input video, our implementation is outlined in Algorithm 1. This involves calculating the
206   average CLIP score across the summaries and discarding the one with the lowest score.
207
208   Cooperate. After filtering outliers, we leverage the capabilities of state-of-the-art LLMs, to combine
209
      the remaining summaries into a single coherent paragraph. We propose three distinct strategies for
      this synthesis: Merge, Find Common Ground, and Select.
210
211
212
      • Merge: This strategy integrates all information from the VideoLLM summaries into a comprehen-
        sive single summary, capturing the full spectrum of details provided by each model. The resulting
213
        summary aims to be inclusive and detailed.

      3.2   K EYFRAME R ETRIEVAL
239
240   After obtaining our coherent summary from the Video Summarization module, previous methods
241   either prompt the VideoLLM to generate short segments most relevant to the summary (Qian et al.,
242   2024; Huang et al., 2024), which is an area where current VideoLLMs often underperform, or train-
243   ing a model specifically to encode visual and textual features (Lin et al., 2023b; Moon et al., 2023).
244   The latter approach often employs a sliding window technique to capture and align temporal infor-
245   mation, enabling the accurate identification and retrieval of relevant video segments that correspond
246   to the summary. However, this method is computationally expensive and can sometimes result in
247
      redundant information.
248   Given that our textual summary is highly informative, we propose an alternative approach that avoids
249   the need for training a new model. Instead, we utilize a fixed joint embedding space, combined with
250   a similarity metric, to guide the keyframe retrieval. Specifically, we encode the input video frames at
251   two-second intervals, following a sampling technique inspired by Moment-DETR (Lei et al., 2021),
252   alongside our textual summary. Both the text and video frames are encoded using CLIP (Radford
253
      et al., 2021). We then calculate the cosine similarity between the text embeddings (whole summary)
      and the individual frame embeddings, sorting the similarity scores in descending order to identify
254
      the top-k video frames as keyframes.

      3.3   E XTENDED A PPLICATIONS
258
      Our method extends beyond existing video summarization, offering practical real-world applica-
259   tions that leverage both our coherent textual summary (from Section 3.1) and retrieved keyframes
260   (from Section 3.2). These include visual manual generation for instructional videos, aiding product
261   manufacturers in creating efficient user guides, and privacy-preserving content generation, which
262   produces short video clips and representative images that capture the essence of the original video
263   without revealing sensitive content. These applications demonstrate our method’s versatility, ad-
264   dressing challenges in content creation, information dissemination, and privacy protection across
265   various domains, thus surpassing the capabilities of existing VideoLLMs.

      Effect of filtering outliers. To assess the impact of our “Filter Outliers” component, we compare
451
      our framework’s performance with and without this feature. In both scenarios, we utilize GPT-
452
      4-Turbo to synthesize summaries from individual VideoLLMs using the “Find Common Ground”
453   strategy. The key difference lies in the input to this fusion process: with outlier filtering, we exclude
454   the detected outlier, while without it, all four summaries are included. As demonstrated in Table
455   4, the inclusion of outlier filtering led to a significant improvement in performance, enhancing both
456   mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) and Recall metrics by at least 2%. This consistent improve-
457   ment across metrics underscores the effectiveness of our outlier filtering approach in refining the
458   quality of the final summary.
459
460   Effect of different cooperation strategies with different LLMs. We examine the impact of dif-
461   ferent cooperation strategies and LLMs on our framework’s performance. We compare two coop-
462
      eration strategies, Merge and Find Common Ground, implemented with two distinct LLMs: the
      open-source Llama-3-8b-Instruct and the closed-source GPT-4-Turbo, and the prompt template we
463
      apply is provided in the Appendix (cf. A.1). We present the results in Table 5. Our analysis reveals
464
      that the choice of LLM and cooperation strategy has only marginal effects on the overall perfor-
465   mance. However, all combinations demonstrate substantial improvements over utilizing only the
466   individual VideoLLM summaries, as shown in Table 3. Our results strongly suggest that our method
467   of combining and refining summaries from multiple VideoLLMs produces more comprehensive and
468   accurate textual representations, which in turn lead to improved keyframe selection.
469
470   Effect of audio information. To assess the influence of audio information, we conduct experi-
471   ments with and without audio input, noting that some VideoLLMs, such as Video-LLaMA, incor-
472   porate audio information, others like Video-LLaVA and LLaMA-VID do not include this modality
473   in their frameworks. For Video-LLaMA, we remove the audio branch to simulate scenarios without
474   audio information. In the case of PG-Video-LLaVA, we deactivate the audio branch in our default

      We propose a holistic video summarization framework that leverages multiple VideoLLMs to gen-
489
      erate comprehensive textual summaries that capture the detail of the given video without fine-
490   tuning. Our extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in downstream tasks
491   like keyframe retrieval and extended applications such as visual manual generation and privacy-
