009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Soft labels can improve the generalization of a neural network classifier in many
012
               domains, such as image classification. Despite its success, the current literature
013
               has overlooked the efficiency of label smoothing in node classification with graph-
014            structured data. In this work, we propose a simple yet effective label smoothing for
015            the transductive node classification task. We design the soft label to encapsulate
016            the local context of the target node through the distribution of the neighborhood
017            label. We apply the smoothing method for seven baseline models to show its ef-
018            fectiveness. The label smoothing methods improve the classification accuracy in
019            10 node classification datasets in most cases. In the following analysis, we find
020            that incorporating global label statistics in posterior computation is the key to the
021            success of label smoothing. Further investigation reveals that the soft labels miti-
022
               gate overfitting during training, leading to better generalization performance. Our
               code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PosteL.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Adding a uniform noise to the ground truth labels has shown remarkable success in training neural
029
      networks for various classification tasks, including image classification and natural language pro-
030   cessing (Szegedy et al., 2016; Vaswani et al., 2017; Müller et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021a). Despite
031   its simplicity, label smoothing acts as a regularizer for the output distribution and improves gener-
032   alization performance (Pereyra et al., 2017). More sophisticated soft labeling approaches have been
033   proposed based on the theoretical analysis of label smoothing (Li et al., 2020; Lienen & Hüllermeier,
034   2021).
035
      In the graph domain, soft labels have been employed to improve the performance of node classifi-
036   cation tasks. Based on the homophilic assumption, where the nodes with the same label are likely
037   to be connected, previous studies often employed neighborhood labels to soften the ground truth
038   labels (Wang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023). Despite the success of these approaches, their perfor-
039   mance on heterophilic graphs, where nodes tend to connect with others that are dissimilar or belong
040   to different classes, still remains questionable (Zhu et al., 2021; Luan et al., 2022; Chanpuriya &
041   Musco, 2022).
042   In this work, we propose a simple yet effective smoothing method for transductive node classification
043   that can be used for both homophilic and heterophilic graphs. Inspired by the previous work sug-
044   gesting predicting the local context of a node (Hu et al., 2019; Rong et al., 2020), such as subgraph
045   prediction, helps to learn better representations, we propose a smoothing method that can potentially
046   reflect the local context of the target node. To encode the neighborhood information into the node
047   label, we propose to relabel the node with a posterior distribution of the label given neighborhood
048   labels.
049   Under the assumption that the neighborhood labels are conditionally independent given the label
050   of the node to be relabeled, we factorize the likelihood into the product of conditional distributions
051   between two adjacent nodes. To compute the posterior, we estimate the conditionals and prior from
052   a graph’s global label statistics, making the posterior incorporate the local structure and global label
053   distributions. Since the posterior obtained in this way does not preserve the ground truth label, we
      finally interpolate the posterior with the ground truth label, resulting in a soft label.

054
      The posterior, however, may pose high variance when there are few numbers of neighborhood nodes.
055   To mitigate the issue with the sparse labels, we further propose iterative pseudo labeling to re-
056   estimate the likelihood and prior based on the pseudo labels. Specifically, we use the pseudo labels
057   of validation and test sets to update the likelihood and prior, along with the ground truth labels of
058   the training set.
059
      We apply our smoothing method to seven different baseline neural network models, including MLP
060
      and variants of graph neural networks, and test its performance on ten benchmarks, including ho-
061   mophilic and heterophilic graphs. Our empirical study finds that the soft label with iterative pseudo
062   labeling improves the accuracy in 76 out of 80 cases despite its simplicity. We analyze the cases
063   where the soft label decreases the accuracy and reveals characteristics of label distributions with
064   which the soft labeling may not work. Further analysis shows that using local neighborhood struc-
065   ture and global label statistics is the key to its success. Through the loss curve analysis, we find that
066   the soft label prevents overfitting, leading to a better generalization performance in classification.

      2     R ELATED WORK
070
071   In this section, we introduce previous studies related to our method. We begin by discussing various
072   node classification methods, followed by an exploration of the application of soft labels in model
073
      training.
074
075   2.1   N ODE CLASSIFICATION
076
077   Graph structures are utilized in various ways for node classification tasks. Some studies propose
078   model frameworks based on the assumption of specific graph structures. For example, GCN (Kipf
079   & Welling, 2016), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), and GAT (Veličković et al., 2017) aggregate
080
      neighbor node representations based on the homophilic assumption. To address the class-imbalance
      problem, GraphSMOTE (Zhao et al., 2021), ImGAGN (Qu et al., 2021), and GraphENS (Park
081
      et al., 2022) are proposed for homophilic graphs. H2 GCN (Zhu et al., 2020) and U-GCN (Jin
082
      et al., 2021) aggregate representations of multi-hop neighbor nodes to improve performance on
083   heterophilic graphs. Other studies concentrate on learning graph structure. GPR-GNN (Chien et al.,
084   2020) and CPGNN (Zhu et al., 2021) learn graph structures to determine which nodes to aggregate
085   adaptively. Besides, research such as ChebNet (Defferrard et al., 2016), APPNP (Gasteiger et al.,
086   2018), and BernNet (He et al., 2021) focus on learning appropriate filters from the graph signals.
087
088
      2.2   C LASSIFICATION WITH SOFT LABELS
089
090   Hinton et al. (2015) demonstrate that a small student model trained using soft labels generated by
091   the predictions of a large teacher model shows better performance than a model trained using one-
092   hot labels. This approach, known as knowledge distillation (KD), is recognized as effective for
093   compression or performance improvement (Liu et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2020; Tang & Wang, 2018).
094
      On the other hand, simpler alternatives to generate soft labels are considered. The label smoothing
095
      (LS) (Szegedy et al., 2016) generates soft labels by adding uniform noise to the labels. The benefits
096   of LS have been widely explored. Müller et al. (2019) show that LS improves model calibration.
097   Lukasik et al. (2020) establish a connection between LS and label-correction techniques, revealing
098   LS can address label noise.
099
      While label smoothing has been widely adopted in computer vision (Zhang et al., 2021a; Lukov
100
      et al., 2022; Vasudeva et al., 2024) and NLP (Vaswani et al., 2017; Song et al., 2020; Guo et al.,
101
      2021), the efficiency of smoothing in the graph domain has been less explored. To the best of
102   our knowledge, there are two papers that propose label smoothing methods for node classification.
103   SALS (Wang et al., 2021) proposes a method for smoothing node labels to make them more similar
104   to the labels of neighboring nodes. Similarly, ALS (Zhou et al., 2023) generates soft node labels by
105   aggregating neighborhood labels and applying adaptive label refinement. Both methods rely on the
106   homophilic assumption that connected nodes should have similar labels, which may negatively im-
107   pact performance on heterophilic graphs (Zhu et al., 2021; Luan et al., 2022; Chanpuriya & Musco,
      2022).

      and provide a new training strategy that iteratively refines the soft labels via pseudo labels obtained
133   from the training procedure.

198   3.2   I TERATIVE PSEUDO LABELING
199
200   Posterior relabeling is a method used to predict the label of a node based on the labels of its neighbor-
201
      ing nodes. However, in transductive node classification tasks where train, validation, and test nodes
      coexist within the same graph, the presence of unlabeled nodes can hinder the accurate prediction of
202
      posterior labels. For instance, when a node has no labeled neighbors, the likelihood becomes one,
203
      and the posterior only relies on the prior. Moreover, in cases where labeled neighbors are scarce,
204   noisy labels among the neighbors can significantly compromise the posterior distribution. Such
205   challenges are particularly prevalent in sparse graphs. For example, 26.35% of nodes in the Cornell
206   dataset have no neighbors with labels. In such scenarios, the posterior relabeling can be challenging.
207
      To address these limitations, we propose to update the likelihoods and priors through the pseudo
208
      labels of validation and test nodes. We first train a graph neural network with the soft labels obtained
209
      via Equation (3) and predict the labels of validation and test nodes to obtain the pseudo labels. We
210   choose the most probable label as a pseudo label from the prediction. We then update the likelihood
211   and prior with the pseudo labels of the validation and test nodes while keeping the ground-truth
212   labels of the training nodes. This process re-calibrates the posterior smoothing and soft labels.
213   By repeating training and re-calibration until the best validation loss of the predictor no longer
214   decreases, we can maximize the performance of node classification. We assume that if posterior
215   label smoothing improves classification performance with a better estimation of likelihood and prior,
      the pseudo labels obtained from the predictor can benefit the posterior estimation as long as there are

      4.1   N ODE CLASSIFICATION
229
230   In this section, we assess the enhancements in node classification performance across a range of
231   datasets and backbone models. Our aim is to validate the consistent efficacy of our method across
232   datasets and backbone models with diverse characteristics.
233
234   Datasets We assess the performance of our method across 10 node classification datasets. To
235   examine the effect of our method on diverse types of graphs, we conduct experiments on both ho-
236   mophilic and heterophilic graphs. Adjacent nodes in a homophilic graph are likely to have the same
237   label. Adjacent nodes in a heterophilic graph are likely to have different labels. For the homophilic
238   datasets, we use five datasets: the citation graphs Cora, CiteSeer, and PubMed (Sen et al., 2008;
239
      Yang et al., 2016), and the Amazon co-purchase graphs Computers and Photo (McAuley et al.,
      2015). For the heterophilic datasets, we use five datasets: the Wikipedia graphs Chameleon and
240
      Squirrel (Rozemberczki et al., 2021), the Actor co-occurrence graph Actor (Tang et al., 2009), and
241
      the webpage graphs Texas and Cornell (Pei et al., 2020). Detailed statistics of each dataset are
242   illustrated in Appendix D.
243
244   Experimental setup and baselines We evaluate the performance of PosteL across various back-
245   bone models, ranging from MLP, which ignores underlying structure between nodes, to seven
246   widely used graph neural networks: GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GAT (Veličković et al., 2017),
247   APPNP (Gasteiger et al., 2018), ChebNet (Defferrard et al., 2016), GPR-GNN (Chien et al., 2020),
248   BernNet (He et al., 2021), and OrderedGNN (Song et al., 2023). We follow the experimental setup
249   and backbone implementations of He et al. (2021). Specifically, we use fixed 10 train, validation,
250   and test splits with ratios of 60%/20%/20%, respectively, and measure the accuracy at the lowest
251   validation loss. The model is trained for 1,000 epochs, and we apply early stopping when validation
252
      loss does not decrease during the last 200 epochs. We report the mean performance and 95% confi-
      dence interval. The detailed experimental setup, including the search spaces of the hyperparameters,
253
      is provided in Appendix E.
254
255   We compare our method with two domain-agnostic soft labeling methods, including label smoothing
256   (LS) (Szegedy et al., 2016) and knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015), along with two
257   label smoothing methods tailored for node classification, SALS (Wang et al., 2021) and ALS (Zhou
258
      et al., 2023).

535   In this paper, we proposed a novel posterior label smoothing method, PosteL, designed to enhance
536   node classification performance in graph-structured data. Our approach integrates both local neigh-
537   borhood information and global label statistics to generate soft labels, thereby improving generaliza-
538   tion and mitigating overfitting. Extensive experiments across various datasets and models demon-
539   strated the effectiveness of PosteL, showing significant performance gains compared to baseline
      methods despite its simplicity.

882   C     A CASE STUDY COMPARING SMOOTHING METHODS AND P OSTE L
883
884
      In this section, we aim to provide an in-depth explanation of the main differences between PosteL
885   and other label smoothing methods to offer insight into why PosteL performs well, especially on
886   heterophilic graphs.
887
888
      C.1   T HE EFFECT OF UTILIZING GLOBAL STATISTICS
889
890   PosteL leverages global statistics, specifically P̂ (Yj = m|Yi = n, (i, j) ∈ E) and P̂ (Yi = m),
891   to generate soft labels. In contrast, SALS (Wang et al., 2021) only utilizes information from 1-
892   hop neighbors. The use of global statistics in ALS (Zhou et al., 2023) is questionable due to the
893   presence of learnable component in their soft label. Figure 7 shows an example of the soft labels
894   on a binary node classification task with a bipartite graph. The toy example highlights the key
895   differences between existing methods and ours, which will be elaborated further below.
896

898   ability of a label given the neighborhood label. We analyze the conditional distribution in balanced
899   binary classification. Let us define node-wise individual homophily pi and class homophily ck as:

      A simple analysis shows that PosteL assigns a high probability to the ground truth label regardless of
919
      whether the graph is homophilic or heterophilic. The presence of the global label statistics P̂ (Yj =
920
      m|Yi = n, (i, j) ∈ E) plays an important role in these cases.
921
922
      SALS (Wang et al., 2021) SALS interpolates the ground truth label with the neighborhood labels
923   to generate a soft label. Hence, when the graph is heterophilic, the soft label is likely to be dominated
924   by the negative labels. Figure 7 shows the example of SALS, where the soft label is dominated by
925   the negative label.
926
927   ALS (Zhou et al., 2023) The label smoothing of ALS P       consists of three processes: 1) ALS ag-
928   gregates the neighborhood labels using the formula |N1(i)| j∈N (i) ej , 2) the aggregated labels are
929   interpolated with the ground truth label, and 3) the interpolated labels are transformed via a linear
930   transform parameterized by learnable weight matrix W followed by softmax.
931
      Analyzing the deterministic behavior of ALS (Zhou et al., 2023), as done previously for PosteL
932   and SALS, is non-trivial due to the presence of a learnable component. However, there are explicit
933   cases where ALS fails to distinguish nodes with different labels. When pi = 0, ALS generates a
934   soft label as softmax([1, 1]W), regardless of the value of yi . In such cases, ALS adds the same
935   noise regardless of the characteristics of nodes, which is identical to uniform smoothing. Figure 7
936   illustrates an example of ALS, where it assigns the same label to all nodes.
937
938   Global label distribution and imbalanced datasets Next, P̂ (Yi = m) := |{u|y|V|        u =m}|
                                                                                                    , repre-
939   sents the proportion of label m across all nodes. This enables PosteL to account for the overall label
940   distribution in the graph. When a label has a low proportion, PosteL assigns it a lower probabil-
941   ity. Reflecting the label distribution across the entire graph can be advantageous for graphs with
942   imbalanced labels. For example, PosteL demonstrates consistent performance improvement on the
943   imbalanced Computers dataset.
944
945   Discussion Based on these differences, we argue that PosteL is significantly distinguished from
946   other smoothing methods. This difference comes from the use of global statistics, P̂ (Yj = m|Yi =
947   n, (i, j) ∈ E), so we conjecture that it is the main factor behind PosteL’s superior performance. This
948   aligns with the results in Table 2 of our paper, which show that replacing global statistics with local
949   statistics decreases performance.
950
951   C.2   T HE ADVANTAGE OF UTILIZING PSEUDO LABELING STRATEGY
952
      Another distinction is the pseudo labeling strategy. SALS and ALS cannot work on sparse graphs,
953
      as there will be no labeled neighborhoods. Our pseudo labeling strategy enables smoothing using
954   neighborhood label information even on sparse graphs. To the best of our knowledge, PosteL is the
955   first label smoothing approach in node classification to address sparse label scenarios.
