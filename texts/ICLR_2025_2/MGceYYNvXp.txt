               There exists an extremely wide array of LLM benchmarking tasks, whereas of-
012
               tentimes a single number is the most actionable for decision making, especially
013
               by non-experts. No such aggregation schema exists that is not Elo based, which
014            could be costly or time consuming. Here we propose a method to aggregate per-
015            formance across a general space of benchmarks, nicknamed Project “MPG”, here
016            dubbed Model Performance and Goodness, in addition referencing a metric widely
017            understood to be an important yet inaccurate and crude measure of car perfor-
018            mance. Here, we create two numbers: an “Goodness” number (answer accuracy),
019            and a “Fastness” number (cost or QPS). We compare models against each other
020            and present a ranking according to our general metric as well as subdomains. We
021            find significant agreement between the raw pearson correlation of our scores and

               thosee of LMSys, even improving on the correlation of the MMLU leaderboard to
               LMSys.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Miles Per Gallon (MPG) has long been useful as a standardized, one-dimensional measure of vehicle
029
      fuel efficiency. Although the limitations of MPG are well documented — particularly its inability
030   to capture the full spectrum of a vehicle’s performance and environmental impact—its utility lies
031   in providing a single, generalized measure that simplifies comparisons between vehicles while re-
032   taining some accuracy, making MPG a standard bearer in consumer decision-making and various
033   regulatory contexts. We endeavor to apply the same sort of principled aggregation techniques to
034   Large Language Models (LLMs).
035
      There exist a vast array of benchmarks for LLMs (i.e. logic (Kil et al., 2024), math (Liu et al., 2024),
036   law (Guha et al., 2024), linguistic understanding (Narayan et al., 2018), factual recall (Hendrycks
037   et al., 2020), general performance ((bench authors, 2023), etc.) yet in many cases, decision-makers
038   require a single, unified metric to facilitate model selection. In this paper, we introduce a novel
039   aggregation approach, dubbed Project MPG – which nods both to Miles Per Gallon and also Model
040   Performance and Goodness, a more accurate description of our focus.
041
      Project MPG generates two primary metrics: a “Goodness” score, representing general answer ac-
042   curacy, and a “Performance” score, reflecting queries per second (QPS). These metrics are derived
043   from the aggregation of various open benchmarks, designed to: (1) be representative of a general-
044   ized, real-world use cases by focusing on key domains where benchmarks correlate, (2) maintain
045   relational distances between models, similar to those captured by existing intelligence and latency
046   evaluations, and (3) be quick to compute and financially efficient. Please see Figure 1 for the calcu-
047   lation of Goodness vs Performance that we will further define throughout our paper.
048   Our target audience includes resource-constrained developers - such as engineers at smaller com-
049   panies or universities — who lack access to human evaluations, large-scale compute, or public
050   ratings. By providing a lightweight evaluation approach, we enable these users to select models
051   that align with their specific requirements for quality and latency. Additionally, our approach may
052   be of interest to teams that need to rapidly evaluate internal model versions to quantify incremental
053   improvement, or test that fine-tuning efforts have not caused general capabilities to decrease. This
      framework would serve many developing or deploying large language models.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
085
086
087
      Evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs) has become increasingly important as their usage ex-
      pands across diverse applications. One approach that has gained traction is LLM as a judge (Zheng
088
      et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024b), where models are used to evaluate other models by scoring gener-
089
      ated outputs. Indeed, several benchmarks which employs LLM-as-Judges, such as Arena-Hard-Auto
090   (Li et al., 2024) and AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024a). LLM-as-a-judge raises questions about
091   biases and objectivity, as LLM judges may have similar myiopias to the LLMs that they are judging.
092
093
      Most non-LLM-as-judge benchmarks are thus static and ground-truth-based (e.g., multi-choice
      question answering). They cover a wide range of domains, including math, science, coding, and
094
      reasoning. Common ones include MMLU Hendrycks et al. (2020), MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021),
095
      GSM-8K Cobbe et al. (2021), HumanEval Chen et al. (2021), BigBench bench authors (2023), Hel-
096   laSwag Zellers et al. (2019), AGIEval Zhong et al. (2023), as well as comprehensive collection such
097   as HELM Liang et al. (2023).
098
099
      However, one recent development is LLM-Sys Elo ratings inspired by the Elo rating system used in
      competitive games. This method evaluates LLMs by having them compete in pairwise comparisons,
100
      allowing models to be ranked dynamically based on their performance against others in specific
101
      tasks, and has been implemented on many scales (Luo et al., 2024). However, there are critiques to
102   Elo rankings (Boubdir et al., 2023). Namely, (1) there is difficulty representing a suitable breadth
103   of questions; as different model matchups are served different questions, rankings are created in
104   opaque and non-standard ways. (2) Each matchup’s winner isn’t actually reflective of good quality:
105   one matchup featuring two similarly bad responses may look the same to the ranking as a matchup
106   featuring similarly good responses. (3) These flaws may only be resolved with rather extreme com-
107   putational or human cost, with Chatbot Arena featuring O(10k) votes per top model. (4) An Elo
      ranking thus has difficulty in comparing a model’s change over time; a fixed benchmark may be run

108
      more routinely, is less opaque, and is better for understanding. The backbone of the well known
109   Chatbot Arena, although competition based, is Bradley Terry (Chiang et al., 2024).
110
111   DyVal 2, or Dynamic Evaluation, both proposes a grouping of benchmark questions into different
112
      psychometric domains and a method by which benchmark questions may be kept uncontaminated
      through heuristic strategies, like shuffling multiple choice answers or adding incorrect answers –
113
      strategies that meaningfully test whether the LLM is memorizing order or wording (Zhu et al., 2024;
114
      Lin et al., 2024). Together, these approaches represent a shift toward more nuanced and adaptive
115   methods for evaluating LLMs, highlighting the need for evaluation systems that keep pace with the
116   rapid advancements in model development.

      3     B ENCHMARK M ETHODOLOGY

      3.1   B ENCHMARK S ELECTION
121
122   To determine which benchmarks to assign under specific hierarchies, we ensure comprehensive
123   coverage LLM benchmark domains as measured in the work of Ilic 2023 (Ilić & Gignac, 2024).
124
      Ilic et al. highlight that the primary benchmarks in LLMSys show varying degrees of cross-
125
      correlation; a model’s strong performance in certain benchmarks often predicts success in related
126
      ones. By analyzing distinct clusters within their pairwise correlation matrix, we selected represen-
127   tative benchmarks from each cluster: the MMLU-redux global facts, MMLU college mathematics
128   and computer science, BigBench ambiguous and disambiguous benchmarks in sexuality, race, and
129   socioeconomic status, and ARC-C-Challenge. We included some additional benchmarks beyond
130   those in the cross correlation matrix for the sakes of representing famous benchmarks: SQuAD-2
131   (Rajpurkar et al., 2018), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), and Cli-
132   mate Fever (Diggelmann et al., 2020). This targeted selection captures a broad spectrum of LLM
133   capabilities while minimizing redundancy.
134   Having selected benchmarks, we move on to scoring and aggregating them. For multiple choice
135   questions, which compromise the majority of our dataset, we prepare the prompt in the following
136   way:
137
138            You are a succinct and smart LLM who answers questions parsimoniously. Here
139
               is your question: ... And here are your options: (A:..., B:..., C:..., D:...). Please
               answer with the letter corresponding to the choice, only!
140
141   We score multiple choice questions by performing an 1-gram lookup of the correct letter.
142
143
      For boolean questions, we prepare the prompt with the same prefix:
144            You are a succinct and smart LLM who answers questions parsimoniously. Here
145            is your question:... Answer in a True/False only!
146
147   And simply score the answer using an XOR with the correct response. Please see Figure 5 for a
148   description of the relevant benchmark domains.

150   3.2   B ENCHMARK G ROUPING
151
      In line with psychometric traditions, we categorize our MPG subdomains into three primary areas:
152
153         1. Factual Recall: This subdomain assesses the model’s domain knowledge, particularly in
154            relation to global facts, science, and climate change, which are known to correlate with
155            other factual datasets. The benchmarks used in this category include BoolQ (developed
156            by the Google AI Language team) (Clark et al., 2019), the Stanford Question Answering
157            Dataset (SQuAD) (Rajpurkar et al., 2018), MMLU Global Facts (Hendrycks et al., 2020),
158            and the ClimateFever dataset (Diggelmann et al., 2020).
159         2. Linguistic Capability and Social Understanding: This area focuses on the model’s sensi-
160            tivity to social biases. Specifically, we evaluate the model using BigBench’s benchmarks on
161            sensitivity to LGBT identity and race, which are known to be cross-correlated with broader
               social sensitivities (bench authors, 2023).

163            employ the MMLU College-level Computer Science and Math to evaluate problem-solving

189   3.3   S CORE AGGREGATION
190
191   We consider each node i in this tree as a beta distribution with shape Beta(αi , βi ), and each collec-
192   tion of children under a parent to be overlapping samples from a similar space. Thus, our goal in
193
      aggregation is to use observed data from the leaf nodes to resolve the latent posterior beta distribu-
      tions representing a model’s capabilities on subdomains that we do not observe directly. The mean
194
      and 95% coverage of these latent aggregates become the scores that we present in Figure 1 and 6.
195
196   The score of the model’s answers on each benchmark question is an observation which can be mod-
197   eled by a binomial likelihood function. As a reminder to the reader, a beta distribution is conjugate
198   with a binomial likelihood function; therefore, when defining the prior to be non-informative; that
199
      is, a lima,b→0 Beta(a, b), the posterior beta distributions is computed by setting the distributions’ pa-
      rameters to Beta(#scores, Ni − #scores). Here, Ni is the number of questions in each benchmark.
200
      We propose a form of a Monte-Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) to simulate latent questions from the

      aggregate beta distributions.

231
232   In order to evaluate models, we used a RunPod console to inference six open source models on
233   A100 GPUs: yi-1.5-34b-chat, llama-3.1-70b-Instruct, quen2-72b-Instruct, phi-3-small-8k-instruct,
234   gemma-2-9b-it, gemma-2-27b-it, and qwen2-72b-instruct, and the following five proprietary models
235   on their own public facing APIs: GPT-4o-2024-05-13, Gemini 1.5 Pro, Mistral-large 2, Claude 3.5
236   Sonnet 2024-06-20, and Claude 3 Opus 2024-02-29.
237   We measured an average Queries-per-Second (QPS) by simply timing the response rate of every
238   prompt that was sent to the external servers for our specific benchmark questions. Please note that
239   another set of benchmark questions, including longer and multimodal questions, may have garnered
240   a different QPS ordering.

      5.3   S OCIAL S ENSITIVITIES
348
349
      In the social sensitivity benchmarks, LLMs are presented with two individuals who have different
350   social characteristics. They are then asked questions, some of which are intentionally ambiguous,
351   where no specific answer is expected, while others include clear factual details, and the goal is for
352   the LLM to accurately recognize and respond to those details. (As a reminder to the reader, these
353   questions are part of a classic benchmark, BigBench (bench authors, 2023).)
354
      We found a substantial difference in the probability that a model would answer ambiguous questions
355
      correctly relative to unambiguous. We read this finding in the context of responsible AI develop-
356   ment, finding that many major language models have improved in this ratio relative to the original
357   BigBench findings. For example, the Gemini Pro, Claude Sonnet and Opus, and Phi-3 models
358   avoided generating harmful responses 100% of the time. However, we caution to the reader that
359   more further study is warranted.
360
      We note as well that the pattern of consistent differences between scores is some hedge against data
361
      contamination. Were these datasets fully contaminated, we would expect the most competent models
362   to get all or most questions correct. Instead, we often find quite consistently lower performance on
363   types of questions.

366   6     C ONCLUSION
367
368   In this work, we introduce IQ, a benchmarking framework that aggregates a minimal set of bench-
369   marks in order to efficiently generalize an agent’s capabilities. Our approach prioritizes factual,
370   falsifiable questions, such as “What is the height of the Eiffel Tower?” over more subjective prompts
371   like “compose a beautiful haiku.” We intend our focus on factuality to ensure reproducibility and
372   enable objective, quantifiable evaluation metrics, with an eye towards consistent performance as-
373   sessments.
374   Our target audience includes resource-constrained stakeholders, such as modeling managers at
375   smaller companies or universities, who may lack access to extensive human evaluations, large-scale
376   testing, or public ratings like those solicited in LLMSys. By providing a lightweight evaluation
377   approach, we enable such users to select models that align with their specific requirements in terms
      of quality and latency. Additionally, this framework serves as a guide for those in the early stages
