009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce Functional Group-Aware Representations for Small Molecules
012            (FARM), a novel foundation model designed to bridge the gap between SMILES,
013            natural language, and molecular graphs. The key innovation of FARM lies in
014            its functional group-aware tokenization, which directly incorporates functional
015            group information into SMILES, enriching SMILES with detailed chemical con-
016
               text. For example, instead of using “O” to represent all oxygen atoms, we use
               specific tokens like “O ketone” and “O hydroxyl” to differentiate oxygen atoms
017
               belonging to distinct functional groups. This tokenization expands the chemical
018
               lexicon, thereby more effectively bridging SMILES and natural language, ulti-
019            mately enhancing the model’s ability to predict molecular properties. FARM also
020            represents molecules from two perspectives: by using masked language model-
021            ing to capture atom-level features and by employing graph neural networks to
022            encode the whole molecule topology. FARM leverages contrastive learning to
023            aligns these two views of representations into a unified molecular embedding. We
024            rigorously evaluate FARM on the MoleculeNet dataset, where it achieves state-
025            of-the-art performance on 11 out of 13 tasks. These results highlight FARM’s
026            potential to improve molecular representation learning and demonstrate its strong
027
               transfer learning capabilities, paving the way for promising applications in drug
               discovery and pharmaceutical research.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Artificial intelligence (AI) has emerged as a transformative tool in accelerating scientific discovery,
033   particularly in drug development. It is increasingly employed for tasks such as molecular prop-
034   erty prediction, drug-target interaction prediction, and quantitative structure-activity relationship
035   (QSAR) modeling (Chen et al., 2016; Wen et al., 2017; Shen & Nicolaou, 2019; Walters & Barzi-
036   lay, 2020; Achary, 2020; Wang et al., 2022a; Edwards et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a; Nguyen
037   et al., 2024a; Edwards et al., 2024b;a). However, one of the central challenges in this field is the
038   scarcity of large labeled datasets required for traditional supervised learning methods. This has
039
      shifted the focus towards self-supervised pre-trained models that can extract meaningful patterns
      from vast amounts of unlabeled molecular data (Shen & Nicolaou, 2019). As a result, the devel-
040
      opment of robust foundation models for molecular representations is now more critical than ever.
041
      Despite significant advancements in other domains, such as natural language processing (NLP) and
042   computer vision, there is still no dominant foundation model tailored to molecular representation in
043   drug discovery (Zhang et al., 2023b). This paper begins to address this pressing gap by introduc-
044   ing an innovative approach that leverages functional group (FG)-aware tokenization in the context
045   of both sequence-based and graph-based molecular representations. Analogous to what has been
046   seen with prior studies, aligning these two representations generates a unified and comprehensive
047   molecular embedding that effectively captures both atom-level features and the structural topology
048   of molecules. The innovation here is that the expansion in tokenization granularity in a way that
049   is intentionally interfaced with key drivers of functional properties (i.e., functional groups) enables
050
      this form of molecular embedding to better promote models’ capacity to understand and predict
      molecular functions. Figure 1 shows an overview of our molecular representation learning model.
051
052   Molecular structures are commonly represented either as sequences, like SMILES or SELFIES, or as
053   molecular graphs. However, relying solely on one type of representation—whether sequence-based
      or graph-based—limits the ability to capture the full complexity of molecular structures. Sequence

079   accepted definitions in the literature, and their usage varies across different studies, we clarify that
080   functional groups can be considered a subset of these molecular concepts, with a more rigorous

082
      In this work, we integrate functional group information into molecular representations. In the litera-
083
      ture, terms such as “motifs,” “fragments,” “substructures,” and “building blocks” are often used inter-
084
      changeably with “functional groups.” However, in this paper, we use the term “functional groups” to
085   represent a subset of these molecular concepts, with a more rigorous definition grounded in chem-
086   ical principles. A functional group refers to a chemically meaningful portion of a molecule that
087   significantly influences its properties and behavior. This can include simple functional groups, such
088   as hydroxyl (-OH), as well as more complex molecular substructures, such as ring systems, which
089   serve distinct functional roles within the molecule. Functional groups (FGs) play a crucial role in de-
090   termining a molecule’s properties, as illustrated in Figure 2. It presents the example of salicylic acid
091   and aspirin, two molecules that share the same core structure but differ by just one functional group.
092   This minor modification has profound implications, leading to large differences in their chemical
093
      properties and biological activities.

097   molecular representation with chemically meaningful context. The FG-aware tokenization and frag-
098   mentation directly incorporate functional group information into the representation of each atom,
099
      embedding chemical semantics into the molecular representation. This approach addresses a major
      limitation of sequence-based models, which typically focus only on individual atom types while
100

101

      chemical semantics. This larger, more nuanced vocabulary allows the model to better capture the

152   In recent years, there has been growing recognition that incorporating functional group (FG) infor-
153
      mation into molecular representations can significantly enhance model performance in downstream
      tasks. Approaches in this domain can be broadly classified into two categories: those leveraging
154
      language models (LMs) (Li et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023) and those utilizing graph neural networks
155
      (GNNs) (Zhang et al., 2020; 2021; Yu & Gao, 2022; Yang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Wu et al.,

157   functional group detection, relying on predefined chemical rules to identify FGs, or adopt unsuper-
158   vised strategies that infer substructures or motifs from the data. Regardless of the approach, these
159   models consistently demonstrate that enriching molecular representations with functional group in-
160   formation leads to improved performance across a wide range of molecular property prediction
161   tasks (Fang et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). This underlines the importance of
      functional group awareness in achieving accurate and generalizable molecular representations.

200   In studies such as Zhang et al. (2021); Han et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2022), the
201   BRICS algorithm (Degen et al., 2008) is employed to fragment molecules, with some extending the
202   approach through additional rules to achieve finer-grained segmentation. However, BRICS relies
203   on 16 predefined bond-breaking rules rooted in retrosynthetic reactions, which, while useful for
204   synthesis planning, often result in coarse-grained fragmentation. This method focuses on general
205   reaction-based bond breaking, rather than targeting functional group-specific characteristics, and
206
      therefore may overlook the detailed structural nuances needed for more precise molecular analysis.
      Additionally, while BRICS groups atoms into fragments, it does not explicitly identify or label
207
      functional groups, limiting its utility in tasks that require functional groups-specific information.
208
209   Other works (Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) leverage RDKit (Landrum, 2010) for functional group
210   detection. While RDKit is effective in identifying common functional groups, its capabilities are
211   limited to well-known groups and do not extend to detecting more complex functional groups, such
212
      as ring systems that often dominate molecular datasets and play a critical role in molecular function.
      Hence, RDKit may overlook less frequent functional groups or larger, more intricate structures that
213
      are essential for achieving a comprehensive molecular representation in diverse chemical datasets.
214
215   In Wang et al. (2023), the authors propose an unsupervised motif-based graph representation learn-
      ing technique. This approach can capture local structural motifs but is highly sensitive to the choice

219   In this work, we present a functional group detection algorithm that employs rule-based methods to
220

      Unlike frequent subgraph mining (FSM) and motif-based tokenization, which often yield subgraphs

224   rate and highly relevant to chemistry.

      Given a molecule, we can assign each atom to a functional group, ensuring that every atom is asso-
      ciated with a functional unit. This allows us to directly inject functional group information into each
227
      atom in SMILES, enriching it with a richer chemical context. By doing so, we can effectively lever-
228
      age language models (LMs) to learn molecular representations. To the best of our knowledge, we
229   are the first to directly incorporate functional group information into SMILES, providing enhanced
230   chemical context and enabling language models to learn more accurate and meaningful molecular
231   representations.

233   2.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING -BASED M OLECULAR R EPRESENTATIONS
234
235   Contrastive learning, a self-supervised learning technique, aims to learn representations by maxi-
236   mizing agreement between positive pairs while distinguishing them from negative samples. In the
237   context of molecular representation, Wang et al. (2022b) applies this technique by augmenting each
238   molecular graph to create slightly different versions, which are then treated as negative examples to
239
      enhance the learning process. In Pinheiro et al. (2022), the authors use a molecular graph encoder
      and a SMILES encoder to encode both molecular graphs and SMILES, employing contrastive loss
240
      to maximize the agreement between these embeddings. This method enriches SMILES with topol-
241
      ogy information and the molecular graph with sequence context, making the final embeddings of
242   molecules more robust. In Zhang et al. (2020), the authors minimize the distance between the rep-
243   resentation of a molecule and the representations of its constituent substructures. Collectively, these
244   works highlight the versatility of contrastive learning in unifying diverse molecular representations,
245   leading to improved downstream performance in molecular tasks. Our method extends this approach
246   by using contrastive learning to maximize the agreement between FG-enhanced SMILES represen-
247   tations and FG graphs, thereby enhancing the alignment between sequence-based and graph-based
248   representations, ensuring a more comprehensive and chemically informed embedding of molecules.

251
252   In this section, we present our methodologies for enhancing molecular representation learning. Sec-
253   tion 3.1 introduces FG-aware tokenization and fragmentation, which injects detailed chemical con-
254   text into both sequence and graph representations. Section 3.2 delves into the masked atom pre-
255   diction task, a self-supervised technique that enables the model to learn atom-level representations.
256   In Section 3.3, we describe our method for capturing the core molecular structure. Finally, Sec-
257   tion 3.4 presents contrastive learning, which aligns FG-enhanced SMILES strings with FG graph
258   embeddings to achieve a comprehensive, unified molecular representation.

260   3.1   F UNCTIONAL GROUP -AWARE T OKENIZATION AND F RAGMENTATION
261
      We propose an FG-aware tokenization and fragmentation method that embeds detailed functional
262
      group information into molecular representations, tailoring it for both SMILES and graph-based
263
      models. This method defines a set of functional groups and employs an algorithm to detect them
264   within the molecular graph. The algorithm traverses the graph, evaluating each atom based on
265   criteria such as atom type, neighboring atom types and corresponding bonds, number of neighbors,
266   atom charge, and bonded hydrogen atoms to identify the functional group. This approach ensures
267   precise detection of functional groups within molecular graphs. For instance, a carbon atom with
268   a charge of 0 and three neighbors, including a double-bonded oxygen atom, is classified as part of
269   a ketone group (RCOR’), with the oxygen also contributing to the group. Additionally, we address
      cases where a functional group may be a subset of another. The algorithm first checks for the


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      presence of the larger functional group. If it’s not identified, the algorithm then checks for smaller
271   functional groups, ensuring correct identification even in complex structures. Appendix B.1 provides
272   a full list of non-ring-containing functional groups.
273
274
      After identifying conventional functional groups, such as hydroxyl (-OH) and carboxyl (-COOH),
      the molecule is further analyzed to detect rings and fused ring systems, which are also treated as
275
      functional groups. These ring systems are highly diverse and make up a significant portion of all
276
      functional groups. For atoms that cannot be assigned to any predefined functional group—typically
277   rare atoms like Ag or Fe—their chemical symbols are used to represent the functional group. This
278   ensures that all atoms are included in the functional group representation, even if they do not fit
279   into standard categories. Once all functional groups are identified, the molecule is segmented at
280   bonds connecting these groups, preserving the functional groups’ integrity during fragmentation.
281   The results of the FG detection algorithm are utilized for two distinct processes:
282   • FG-aware tokenization: The molecular graph, where each node (atom) is assigned to a specific
283     functional group, is converted back into a SMILES string that incorporates functional group in-
284     formation. For example, a ketone-containing group originally depicted in SMILES as *C(=O)*
285     is transformed into an FG-enhanced SMILES string like *C ketone(=O ketone)*. This FG-
286     enhanced SMILES embeds additional chemical context directly into the molecular representa-
287     tion while remaining fully compliant with traditional SMILES rules (if the FG information is
288     removed, the FG-enhanced SMILES reverts to its standard SMILES form).
289   • FG-aware fragmentation: Once functional groups are identified, the molecule is segmented
290     based on the bonds connecting these groups, as illustrated in Figure 3(b). These bonds are repre-
291
        sented as edges in a graph, where the nodes correspond to the functional groups. This structure,
        known as the functional group graph, conveys the molecule’s structural information.

      3.4    M OLECULAR S TRUCTURE I NTEGRATION VIA C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
403
      To integrate FG-enhanced SMILES representations with molecular structure information, we em-
404   ploy contrastive learning to align these representations with FG graph embeddings. This method
405   captures both the atom-level and topological aspects of molecular structures, allowing the model to
406   develop a unified representation that integrates chemical context with overall molecular architecture.
407
      In this framework, each molecule is treated as a pair of representations: the FG-enhanced SMILES
408
      and its corresponding FG graph. The contrastive learning task encourages the embeddings of these
409
      two representations to be as similar as possible for the same molecule, while pushing apart the
410   representations of different molecules. This allows the model to capture both local chemical features
411   (from the FG-enhanced SMILES) and global molecular topology (from the FG graph).
412
413
      To enhance the learning process and make it more robust, we generate negative examples by aug-
      menting the FG graph. We apply two types of augmentations: (1) node deletion, where one or more
414
      functional groups are removed from the graph, and (2) node swapping, where functional groups
415
      are randomly exchanged with one another. Figure 3(d) illustrates how these augmentations are ap-
416   plied to generate negative examples from a FG graph. These augmentations create harder negative
417   examples that force the model to better understand the correct structure and connectivity between
418   functional groups, making it more effective at learning meaningful molecular representations.
419
      Specifically, given a positive pair (hMLM , hpos ), where hMLM is the atom-level representation derived
420
      from a pretrained BERT model and hpos is the corresponding structure representation from a graph
421
      neural network (GNN), and a negative pair (hMLM , hneg ), where hneg is a augmented FG-graph, the
422   contrastive loss can be written as:
423

425                 N i=1

427   where γ is the margin parameter and N is the number of training examples (or contrastive pairs).
428   The final objective function for integration is:
429                                   LIntegration = λMLM · LMLM + λCL · LCL

431   where λMLM and λCL are hyperparameters that control the contribution of each loss to the overall
      objective. Details on the implementation of contrastive training can be found in the Appendix D.4

439   4    P RE - TRAINING DATA C OLLECTION AND D IVERSITY A SSESSMENT
440
441
      The performance and generalizability of a machine learning model is heavily dependent on the qual-
442
      ity and diversity of its training data. Given the vastness of chemical space, it is vital that the training
443
      data represents a sufficiently representative subset of this space. Traditional approaches assess the
444   diversity of datasets based on criteria such as the number of chemical elements and the number
445   of atoms per molecule. However, to the best of our knowledge, no previous studies have system-
446   atically evaluated the diversity of large chemical datasets like ZINC (Irwin & Shoichet, 2005) or
447   ChEMBL (Gaulton et al., 2011) in terms of functional groups. This gap is significant, as functional
448   groups provide deep insights into the structural and functional complexity of molecules.
449
      In this work, we introduce a novel criterion to assess dataset diversity by using the size of the
450   FG-enhanced SMILES lexicon as a metric. By analyzing the size of this lexicon, we can assess
451   whether the dataset captures a comprehensive range of chemical functionalities, which is crucial for
452   building robust molecular foundation models. Table 1 presents the size of the FG-enhanced SMILES
453   lexicon for the ZINC15 and ChEMBL25 datasets, as well as the dataset we collected for training our
454   foundation model. The table shows that despite its widespread use for training foundation models
455   in small molecule representation, ZINC15 is significantly less diverse compared to ChEMBL25.
456   This is largely due to the fact that ZINC15 is primarily designed to include molecules that adhere
457   to Lipinski’s Rule of Five for drug-likeness, excluding more exotic or less common elements that
458
      are less relevant to pharmaceutical chemistry. This limited diversity may negatively impact the
      model’s performance on out-of-distribution datasets, which contain a broader range of atom types
459
      and functional groups not present in ZINC15. In contrast, ChEMBL25 is far more diverse and thus
460
      better suited for training foundation models that can be fine-tuned for various downstream tasks.
461   Based on this insight, we collected a dataset that includes the entire ChEMBL25 database, libraries
462   from chemical drug suppliers, and a subset of ZINC15 to ensure a more comprehensive coverage of
463   chemical space. Detailed information about the collected data can be found in the Appendix A.

523   6    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525   In summary, FARM demonstrates robust performance across various MoleculeNet classification
526   tasks, outperforming or matching baseline models. The integration of functional group information
527   and the alignment of FG-enhanced SMILES representations with FG graph embeddings through
528   contrastive learning significantly enhance its effectiveness. This approach underscores FARM’s ver-
529   satility and strength as a pre-trained model, capable of improving molecular structure understanding
530
      and predictive accuracy for a wide range of downstream tasks.
531   While FARM shows strong performance, there are two main limitations that should be addressed in
532   future work. First, the current model does not incorporate a full 3D molecular representation, which
533   is critical for capturing stereochemistry and spatial configurations that affect molecular properties.
534   Incorporating 3D information Yan et al. (2024) could further enhance the model’s predictions. Sec-
535   ond, the model faces challenges when dealing with rare fused ring systems due to out-of-vocabulary
536
      issues. A potential solution to this limitation is to extend the training dataset, covering a broader
      portion of chemical space to include more diverse and complex molecular structures.
537
538   Looking ahead, our ultimate goal is to develop a pre-trained atom embedding that parallels the ca-
539   pabilities of pre-trained word embeddings in NLP. This would enable a richer and more nuanced un-
      derstanding of molecular properties and behaviors at the atomic level. Similarly, we aim to achieve


                                                                      10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      molecule-level representations that are as expressive and versatile as sentence-level embeddings in
541   NLP, capturing both local and global molecular features. By bridging the gap between atom-wise
542   embeddings and holistic molecule representations, FARM paves the way for more accurate, gener-
543   alizable molecular predictions across a variety of tasks.

781   B.2    NAMING FUNCTIONAL GROUPS WITH R INGS IN F USED R ING S YSTEMS
782
      Fused ring systems are a diverse and prevalent class of functional groups, accounting for 99.37% of
783
      the total functional groups in our dataset (147,564 out of 148,507 FGs). Despite their importance,
784
      many of these systems lack standardized nomenclature. To address this, we propose a systematic
785   approach to naming these ring systems based on their ring sizes and core structures.
786
787   Each ring in a fused ring system is named according to its size. For instance, a six-membered
788
      aromatic ring like benzene is named ring 6. This straightforward approach provides a clear identifier
      for individual rings within a system. For systems composed of multiple fused rings, we use the
789
      following naming convention:
790
791            • Identification: Determine the smallest atom index for each ring within the system.
792
               • Sorting: Arrange the rings by increasing atom indices.
793
794            • Construction: Combine the ring sizes in ascending order. For example, a fused system
795
                 with a six-membered ring and a five-membered ring would be named ring 5 6.
796
      This systematic naming helps in identifying and categorizing complex fused ring systems by focus-
797
      ing on their core structure. The core structure is defined as the central framework of interconnected
798   rings that forms the fundamental backbone of the molecule. The core structure of a ring system
799   is important because it influences the molecule’s reactivity, stability, and biological activity. In
800   SMILES notation, which uses lowercase characters to indicate atoms within aromatic rings, we can
801   enhance the representation by combining the atom symbol (uppercase or lowercase) with the core
802   structure, thereby providing a comprehensive depiction of the ring system. Figure 6a illustrates an
803   example of naming a fused ring system based on the rules described above, and Figure 6b shows
804   how FG-aware tokenization is applied.

       masked tokens in the input sequence effectively, and LCL denotes the contrastive loss, which aligns
1097   the SMILES and structural representations. The coefficients λMLM and λCL are hyperparameters that
1098   control the contribution of each loss to the overall objective. By tuning these coefficients, we can
1099   balance the learning process between the two tasks, allowing the model to learn rich and meaningful
1100   representations from both the sequential and structural aspects of the molecular data.
1101
       This combined loss function enables the model to leverage the strengths of both masked language
1102
       modeling and contrastive learning, fostering a more comprehensive understanding of molecular rep-
1103   resentations that can enhance performance in downstream tasks such as property prediction, molec-
1104   ular generation, and structure-based drug discovery.
1105
1106
       In our contrastive learning model, we set the margin γ = 0.5 and the weights λM LM = 1.0 and
       λCL = 0.5. We train the contrastive BERT model using a batch size of 126 for a total of 5 epochs.
1107
       This training configuration mirrors the setup used for learning atom representations with the BERT
1108
       model, as described in Section D.1.

1113   FARM and pair it with a GRU head for both classification and regression tasks. For classification,
1114   we use cross-entropy as the loss function, while for regression, we employ mean squared error.
1115   The Adam optimizer is applied with a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a cosine annealing learning rate
1116   schedule with a period of 20 epochs. The training process spans 100 epochs with a batch size of 16,
1117   using an 80-10-10 train-validation-test split with scaffold splitting. To address imbalanced datasets,
