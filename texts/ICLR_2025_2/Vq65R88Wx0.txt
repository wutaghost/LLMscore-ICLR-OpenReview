009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Most knowledge distillation (KD) methodologies predominantly focus on teacher-
012            student pairs with similar architectures, such as both being convolutional neural
013            networks (CNNs). However, the potential and flexibility of KD can be greatly
014            improved by expanding it to novel Cross-Architecture KD (CAKD), where the
015            knowledge of homogeneous and heterogeneous teachers can be transferred flex-
016            ibly to a given student. The primary challenge in CAKD lies in the substantial
017
               feature gaps between heterogeneous models, originating from the distinction of
               their inherent inductive biases and module functions. To this end, we introduce an
018
               assistant model as a bridge to facilitate smooth feature knowledge transfer between
019
               heterogeneous teachers and students. More importantly, within our proposed de-
020            sign principle, the assistant model combines the advantages of cross-architecture
021            inductive biases and module functions by merging convolution and attention mod-
022            ules derived from both student and teacher module functions. Furthermore, we
023            observe that heterogeneous features exhibit diverse spatial distributions in CAKD,
024            hindering the effectiveness of conventional pixel-wise mean squared error (MSE)
025            loss. Therefore, we leverage a spatial-agnostic InfoNCE loss to align features
026            after spatial smoothing, thereby improving the feature alignments in CAKD. Our
027            proposed method is evaluated across some homogeneous model pairs and arbitrary
028
               heterogeneous combinations of CNNs, ViTs, and MLPs, achieving state-of-the-art
               performance for distilled models with a maximum gain of 11.47% on CIFAR-100
029
               and 3.67% on ImageNet-1K. Our code and models will be released.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Knowledge Distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015; Romero et al., 2015) has been demonstrated as
035   a powerful method to transfer knowledge from a pre-trained and cumbersome teacher model to a
036   compact and efficient student model. Compared to the model trained from scratch, the performance
037   of the student model distilled by appropriate teachers usually improves significantly. Commonly,
038   knowledge transferred is derived from either the output logits (logits-based KD (Sun et al., 2024))
039
      or the intermediate features (feature-based KD (Romero et al., 2015)) of the teacher model. There-
      fore, it is intuitive to understand different teachers have different knowledge (logits or features)
040
      determined by their unique architectures (Liu et al., 2021a).
041
042   Most existing KD approaches focus on similar-architecture distillation (Romero et al., 2015; Tian
043   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023) (called SAKD), i.e., optional teachers are restricted to a limited scope
044   with structures similar to the student model. However, this homogeneous distillation presents two
045
      principal limitations: (1) Limited Potential: Compared to the broader range of arbitrary teachers
      (including homogeneous and heterogeneous ones), the restricted scope of teachers in SAKD may
046
      fail to include the optimal knowledge necessary to enhance the performance of certain students.
047
      For instance, as OFA (Hao et al., 2023) demonstrated, distilling knowledge from a heterogeneous
048   ViT-Base to ResNet50 yields superior student performance compared to using a ResNet152 as the
049   homogeneous teacher. (2) Limited Flexibility: The emergence of new models (Liu et al., 2022;
050   Tolstikhin et al., 2021) or the scarcity of perfectly tuned homogeneous teachers in domain-specific
051   tasks (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Li et al., 2024) poses significant challenges in obtaining suitable
052   homogeneous teachers, thereby impeding the applicability of SAKD. Thus, this paper expands KD
053   to cross-architecture KD (CAKD), investigating methods to distill knowledge from both homoge-
      neous and heterogeneous teachers to students (Hao et al., 2023). By broadening the pool of optional

068   In CAKD, the main challenge is that heterogeneous teachers and students have significant repre-
069   sentation gaps as detailed in feature analyses based on CKA alignment in OFA (Hao et al., 2023).
070   These gaps stem from inherent differences in (1) inductive biases (Raghu et al., 2021) and (2) mod-
071   ule functions (Liu et al., 2023). (1) Inductive biases: As depicted in Fig. 3 (a), convolutional-
072   neural networks-based models (CNNs) (He et al., 2016; Sandler et al., 2018) exhibit hard induc-
073   tive biases, specifically locality and translation-equivariance. Consequently, CNN-generated fea-
074   tures are derived from local pixels within local receptive fields in Fig. 1 (a,b). In contrast, as
075   shown in Fig. 3(b,c), multi-head-self-attention-based models (MSAs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
076
      and multilayer-perception-based models (MLPs) (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) have soft inductive bi-
      ases (Bachmann et al., 2024; Park & Kim, 2021; Raghu et al., 2021), i.e., patchify and long-distance
077
      dependency. Hence, as shown in Fig. 1(c,d) and Appendix C, features of most MSA/MLP models
078
      are generated by exchanging globally the information of all patches (Raghu et al., 2021). (2) Mod-
079   ule functions: Varied module functions generate different features at different stages. For instance,
080   features of shallow and deep layers in ViT have higher similarity than hierarchical CNN (Park &
081   Kim, 2021; Raghu et al., 2021) in Fig. 1(a,c). Therefore, heterogeneous T.-S. pairs in CAKD ex-
082   hibit significantly different inductive biases and module functions compared to SAKD, leading to
083   substantial representation gaps that impede effective feature transfer. While OFA (Hao et al., 2023)
084   attempts to address heterogeneous feature gaps by projecting features into the logit spaces, it is
085   suboptimal due to substantial damage to feature-specific knowledge. This dilemma leads to a nat-
086   ural question: Can we get the best use of different inductive biases and module functions, thereby
087
      reducing heterogeneous representation gaps and making feature transfer promising in CAKD?
088   To alleviate heterogeneous feature gaps in CAKD, we introduce a Teacher-Assistant-Student dis-
089   tillation paradigm (T.-A.-S.1 , called TAS) by incorporating a hybrid assistant model as a bridge to
090   facilitate smoother knowledge transfer. More importantly, our assistant model adheres to a novel de-
091   sign principle: “the assistant model should be composed of merging CNN and MSA/MLP modules
092   derived from teacher and student models”.
093   Our design is well-motivated by the following popular beliefs: (1) Why use a hybrid model as our
094   assistant, not a pure CNN/MSA/MLP model? As demonstrated in (Park & Kim, 2021; Li et al.,
095   2023a;b), CNNs and MSAs/MLPs are complementary. A hybrid model that uses CNNs in the early
096   stages and MSAs/MLPs in the later stages can benefit from both local and global inductive biases.
097   Compared to existing KD like Fig. 2 (a-e), our proposed hybrid assistant model in Fig. 2 (f) projects
098   heterogeneous features to a common space by merging CNN and MSA/MLP modules, thereby re-
099
      ducing distillation gaps attributed to inductive biases. (2) Why is our assistant model composed of
      student and teacher modules, not an externally introduced hybrid model (Li et al., 2023b)? As
100
      demonstrated in (Liu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; 2022b), the disparity between heterogeneous
101
      features is also from module functions, i.e., how the models will read, decode, and process the input
102   features. Unlike an externally introduced model, a hybrid assistant model comprising student and
103   teacher modules not only optimizes the functional similarity (Liu et al., 2023) between heteroge-
104   neous T.-S. pairs, but also introduces minimal additional learnable parameters in Appendix G. (3)
105   How do we align heterogeneous features spatially? Widely used mean square error loss (MSE)
106   aligns the features pixel-by-pixel, which is suitable for features that have similar spatial distribution,

139   2.1     TAXONOMY OF OUR METHODS
140
141
      As shown in Fig. 2, the majority of existing KD methodologies concentrate on homogeneous distil-
      lation by using a single projector (e.g., single linear layer) to align the output logits (Hinton et al.,
142
      2015; Huang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024), intermediate features (Romero et al., 2015; Chen et al.,
143
      2021; Liu et al., 2023), feature embeddings (Tian et al., 2020), and module functions (Liu et al.,
144   2023) of T.-S. pairs, thanks to the highly-similar features between homogeneous T.-S. pairs. How-
145   ever, they fall short in addressing the complexities of heterogeneous distillation, where the distinct
146   features between heterogeneous T.-S. pairs pose significant challenges. Although OFA Hao et al.
147   (2023) achieves consistent improvements for arbitrary T.-S. pairs, it does so at the expense of sacri-
148   ficing feature information to logits. In this paper, our method dives into the nature of heterogeneous
149   feature gaps (i.e., caused by inductive bias and module functions) and introduces a hybrid assistant
150   to facilitate smoother feature transfer between heterogeneous T.-S. pairs.
151   Additionally, several other works are pertinent to our method: (1) We note that some methods at-
152   tempt to distill the knowledge between CNNs and MSAs (Zhao et al., 2023), but they are tailored
153   to specific T.-S. pairs rendering them impractical for our arbitrary T.-S. CAKD. (2) Certain methods
154   apply progressive distillation to transfer the knowledge via a middle model (Mirzadeh et al., 2020;
155   Cao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021a), but they are progressive training strategies that are not train-
156   ing algorithms designed for transferring knowledge between heterogeneous T.-S. pairs. (3) Lastly,
157   while some logits-based methods can be easily applied to CAKD (Hinton et al., 2015; Sun et al.,
158
      2024; Hao et al., 2023), they are suboptimal because they overlook the significance of feature-based
      knowledge (Romero et al., 2015). Conversly, our method (1) is easily applied arbitrary T.-S. pairs
159
      with two important design principles, (2) employs three-level joint optimization algorithms, and (3)
160
      utilizes a generic feature-based method for both homogeneous and heterogeneous distillation.
161
      In this paper, our method focuses on feature-based CAKD with a three-level distilling paradigm.

      2.2   H YBRID M ODEL
180
181   As illustrated in Fig. 3, different models exhibit different inductive biases and module functions.
182   (Raghu et al., 2021) investigates the internal representation structures of ViT and CNN models,
183   revealing significant differences between their heterogeneous features. (Park & Kim, 2021) further
184   provides some fundamental explanations for this phenomenon. Specifically, CNNs are data-agnostic
185   and channel-specific high-pass filters, while MSAs are data-specific and channel-agnostic low-pass
186   filters. Therefore, researchers (Park & Kim, 2021) think CNNs and MSAs are complementary,
187
      which inspires them to design a hybrid model following the rules of “alternately replacing CNN
      blocks with MSA blocks from the end of a baseline CNN model”. The hybrid model outperforms
188
      CNNs in both large and small data regimes (Park & Kim, 2021). Furthermore, the architecture of
189
      MLP models (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) is notably similar to ViTs not CNNs, so ConvMLP (Li et al.,
190   2023a) also achieves advanced performance in basic visual tasks by the co-design of CNNs and
191   MLPs. In a nutshell, hybrid CNN-MSA/MLP models improve performance and efficiency through
192   the combination of different inductive biases and module functions.
193
      Inspired by the design of the hybrid model, we mitigate the heterogeneous feature gaps by introduc-
194
      ing a hybrid assistant model between cross-architecture T.-S. pairs.

199   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
200
201
      Existing KD methods perform well in homogeneous distillation, but they may fail in heteroge-
      neous teachers and students. The primary reasons stem from fundamentally distinct feature and
202
      logit spaces, caused by different inductive biases and module functions of heterogeneous models.
203
204   Inductive Bias and module functions. Inductive bias refers to the set of assumptions that a model
205   uses to make predictions on unseen data (Ren et al., 2022). Module functions describe how a model
206   reads, encodes, decodes, and processes the data (Liu et al., 2023). As shown in Fig. 3, heteroge-
207
      neous models exhibit different inductive biases and module functions. (1) CNN models (He et al.,
      2016) slide a set of learnable local kernels across the pixel-level image, focusing on local receptive
208
      fields. The weight-sharing kernels are applied across the entire image, providing the network with
209
      translation-equivariance to recognize an object regardless of location. (2) MSA models (Dosovit-
210   skiy et al., 2021) split the input image into patches, which are then projected into Query, Key, and
211   Value. The attention modules calculate the scores between the Query and Key to generate attention
212   maps, which are then used to weight the corresponding Value. This process, capturing long-distance
213   dependency, allows the model to consider the global information from all patches. (3) MLP mod-
214   els (Touvron et al., 2022) also begin by dividing the input image into patches. It then mixes global
215   information along all patches’ spatial and channel dimensions. In a nutshell, different inductive
      biases and module functions determine different distribution of generated features.

232   3.2   T HREE -L EVEL D ISTILLATION PARADIGM
233
234   As shown in (a-e) of Fig. 2, existing methods usually apply a two-level paradigm in SAKD (Hinton
235   et al., 2015; Romero et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2023), i.e., T.-S. scheme, to transfer directly the knowl-
236   edge of teachers to students. Besides, some works apply progressive training strategy (Mirzadeh
237
      et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a) to transfer multi-teacher knowledge to a single student model in
      SAKD, but they are also a two-level paradigm for each T.-S. distillation. Despite these two-level
238
      paradigms being better suited for SAKD, transferring knowledge directly without any transition
239
      mechanism is challenging for our CAKD due to the spatial diversity of features in Fig. 1.
240
241   In the human learning process, the role of teaching assistants greatly reduces the information gaps
242   between teachers and students. Motivated by this, as illustrated in Fig. 4, this paper introduces
243
      an assistant model as a middle bridge, where the knowledge is transferred by training a teacher-
      assistant-student scheme (TAS) as follows:

      The following considerations drive the design of our assistant model: (1) Inductive biases of CNNs
262
      and MSAs/MLPs are complementary and hybrid CNN-MSA/MLP models demonstrate good per-
263
      formance (Raghu et al., 2021; Park & Kim, 2021; Li et al., 2023a). Therefore, we replace the CNN
264   blocks at the end of a baseline CNN model with MSA/MLP blocks as our assistant model which
265   can benefit from both the local features in the early stages and the global information exchanges in
266   the last stages. For example, final feature appearances of the assistant model are converted from the
267   local CNN features to global receptive fields in Fig. 5. (2) Different models have different mod-
268   ule functions, which can be aligned implictly by connecting different module functions in a single
269   pipeline (Liu et al., 2023). Therefore, we form our assistant model by using CNN/MSA/MLP mod-
      ules derived from students and teachers. As shown in Fig. 4, our assistant model is mainly composed

      Loss Function. As shown in Fig. 4, we only transfer the final features after average pooling and
277   the logits for the following reasons. (1) Due to the weight-sharing between our assistant model
278   and T.-S., it combines actually different inductive biases only in the final features, not early and
279   middle features. (2) As shown in Fig. 5 and Fig. 1, the final features of different models are also
280   very different in spatial, so we smooth them by average pooling to mitigate the spatial gaps between
281   different features. The knowledge in Eq. (1) is formulated by Ki = {fi , pi }, i = t, a, s, where fi
282   and pi denote the final features embeddings after average pooling and the output logits.
283   In this paper, we use spatial-agnostic InfoNCE loss LInfoNCE (He et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2020)
284   and OFA loss LOFA to supervise the transfer of features and logits respectively, motivated by the
285   following observations. (1) Wildly used MSE loss computes the pixel-wise metrics that are suit-
286   able for features having similar spatial information, but it will fail when the features are very spa-
287   tially different (e.g., FitNet with MSE loss gets only 24.06% top-1 accuracy when the teacher is
288   ConvNeXt-T and the student is Swin-P in CIFAR100 Tab. 1). Consequently, we use a contrastive
289   loss InfoNCE (He et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2020) to transfer the structural information of feature em-
290   beddings (Tian et al., 2020), which captures complex interdependencies of features without spatial
291
      information. (2) As demonstrated in (Hao et al., 2023), the different inductive bias leads models to
      variant logit spaces. For example, local CNN models are more suitable for small objects, but global
292
      MSA/MLP models are more suitable for large objects. Therefore, LOFA enhances the information
293

294   student learning from being disturbed by irrelevant information of the teacher. In a nutshell, our

406   Lastly, OFA (Hao et al., 2023) yields significant and consistent improvements under all settings.

410   more independent (Tian et al., 2020). In contrast, our framework bridges the cross-architecture rep-

415   Results on ImageNet-1K. We also conduct extensive experiments on 14 combinations of cross-
416   architecture T.-S. models on the large-scale ImageNet-1K dataset. Here, we observe that feature-
417
      based methods perform well when handling MSA/MLP students, for instance, utilizing Fit-
      Net (Romero et al., 2015) when the teacher model is ResNet50 and the student model is DeiT-T.
418
      This is opposite to our observations on CIFAR100. We argue that this discrepancy arises due to
419
      the data-hungry nature of MLP/MSA models and additional linear feature projectors (Park & Kim,
420   2021), which are better suited for training on large-scale datasets. Even so, traditional feature-based
421   methods still have negative impacts in some other situations, e.g., FitNet yields 70.45% (-1.72%)
422   when the teacher is ConvNeXt-T and the student is DeiT-T. In a nutshell, even in training with
423   large-scale data, simple feature projectors are not sufficient to transfer the features of students into
424   arbitrary cross-architecture teacher spaces.
425
      In this paper, besides the feature projectors L2G, our assistant model also includes modules derived
426   from students and frozen teachers. In other words, our assistant model achieves a more important
427   task, i.e., aligning the knowledge of student functions to match the frozen teacher functions. There-
428   fore, our TAS leverages more information and constraints than linear feature projectors in existing
429   feature-based methods, leading to superior and stable performance on extensive combinations of
430   cross-architecture models in the large-scale dataset. Besides, compared to leveraging four interme-
431   diate features of SOTA (Hao et al., 2023), our assistant achieves more competitive performance by
      only leveraging the final features.

461
462   The architecture of the assistant model. In Tab. 4(A-B), we remove the module S4m in (B) and
463   the MSA module in (A), the performance of different teacher-student pairs drops significantly. This
464   demonstrates the power of combining the inductive biases by adding MSA modules following the
465
      CNN modules and combining the module functions by adding S4m . Besides, different MSA blocks
      have different functions for different T.-S. pairs (details in Appendix F), so we use the Swin block
466
      as our MSA block in L2G for simplicity.
467
468   The components of the three-level paradigm. (1) In Tab. 4 (C), we remove the supervision from
469   the teacher to our assistant model, i.e., LTAS (Kt , Ka ), which makes the assistant model learn no
470   correct knowledge from the teachers and then transfers the incorrect knowledge to the students.
471
      So the distilled students have poor performance. (2) We remove the supervision from the assistant
      model to students in Tab. 4 (D), i.e., LTAS (Ka , Ks ), which is the same as the traditional T.-S. scheme.
472
      Due to the gaps between heterogeneous students and teachers are not mitigated by our assistant
473
      model, the final performance of students is poor too. (3) We remove the supervision from the
474   teacher model to students in Tab. 4 (E), i.e., LTAS (Kt , Ks ). In this case, although the performance
475   of distilled students is good in some situations, it is not optimal compared to our TAS. This is
476   because the assistant model inevitably damages some knowledge from the teachers, and some easy
477   knowledge is more suitable to transfer by the T.-S. scheme without a middle bridge. Totally, the
478   novel paradigm is powerful for heterogeneous knowledge transfer in cross-architecture distillation.
479
      Feature and Logit Loss. We remove the feature loss LInfoNCE in Tab. 4 (F) and the logit loss LOFA
480   in Tab. 4 (G), and different teacher-student pairs have different performance drops on CIFAR100
481   and ImageNet-1K. For instance, in CIFAR100, LOFA is more important when the teacher is Swin-T
482   and the student is ResNet18, while LInfoNCE is more important when the teacher is ConvNeXt-T
483   and the student is Swin-P. In other words, our LInfoNCE and LOFA are complementary for different
484   teacher-student pairs, so utilizing them jointly will enhance the effectiveness of various distillations.
485   Besides, we demonstrate that MSE loss is not suitable for any T.-S. pairs compared to our INFONCE
      loss (Tian et al., 2020) in Sec. 4.2 and Appendix E.

      5      C ONCLUSION
525
      Limitations and Future Works. (1) It is noteworthy that for certain specific models, such as
526
      the extensively studied ResNet18, the performance resulting from distillation by a heterogeneous
527
      teacher is inferior to that achieved by a homogeneous teacher. Although our current focus is on the
528   generalizability of various heterogeneous models, yielding significant performance improvements in
529   CAKD, a promising avenue for future research could involve additional prior when specific teacher-
530   student pairs are predefined. (2) Our TAS may disrupt heterogeneous features’ spatial alignments.
531   This limitation could be mitigated by aligning extra spatial-level distributions (rather than pixel-
532   level). (3) We have validated our methodology on widely used CV baselines and datasets. While our
533   approach naturally extends to other domains, such as NLP, validating our method across a broader
534   range of domains remains a subject for future investigation.
535   Conclusion. This paper introduces a novel Teacher-Assistant-Student (TAS) scheme designed to
536   enhance the efficacy of heterogeneous distillation. TAS integrates diverse inductive biases and mod-
537   ule functions by introducing a hybrid assistant model comprising CNN/MSA/MLP modules derived
538   from students and teachers, thereby improving the feature transfer among heterogeneous models.
539   Extensive experiments demonstrate our TAS is more powerful than other homogeneous and hetero-
      geneous methods on CIFAR100 and ImageNet-1K.

730   B     C OMPARISONS WITH OTHER METHODS
731
732   We compare the differences between some similar methods and our TAS in Fig. 2. Firstly, to the best
733   of our knowledge, our TAS is the first to apply three-level teacher-assistant-student scheme, which
734   provides more flexible designs for knowledge transfer than existing two-level scheme. Secondly,
735   our assistant model bridges the representation gaps between cross-architecture students and teachers
736   by combining different inductive biases and module functions, making our TAS more suitable for
737   cross-architecture distillation. Thirdly, as demonstrated in (Hao et al., 2023), the LOFA enhances the
738   target information and hinders the transfer of incorrect information from the teacher by a modulating
739   parameter γ (Hao et al., 2023), which is more suitable than LKL in cross-architecture distillation.
740
      Lastly, the LMSE aligns the features in a pixel-by-pixel manner, which is not reasonable for spatially
      different heterogeneous features, e.g.(A) and (E) in Fig. 6. Thus, as demonstrated in Tab. 10, we
741
      get the better performance by smoothing the features in spatial and apply contrastive learning by
742
      LInfoNCE to align the feature embeddings of cross-architecture models.
743
744   There are some works to input the student features to teachers in similar-architecture distillations,
745   e.g., ReviewKD (Chen et al., 2021), FCFD (Liu et al., 2023), and so on (Chen et al., 2022b; Li
746
      et al., 2020). However, they are all designed for teacher-student pairs with similar architectures,
      suggesting different motivations and designs compared to our TAS for cross-architecture distillation.
747
      For example, FCFD (Liu et al., 2023) has the best performance and is the most similar method to
748
      our TAS, but some important designs of our TAS are very different from FCFD.
749
750   Firstly, FCFD (Liu et al., 2023) is designed for CNN students and teachers, which needs to be
751   modified seriously if we apply it to heterogeneous distillations. Besides, as demonstrated in Tab. 8,
752
      FCFD is not suitable for any cross-architecture teacher-student models. But our TAS is generic for
      any teacher-student pair.
753
754   Secondly, although FCFD (Liu et al., 2023) also combines different module functions, the connec-
755   tions between students and teachers are random and mutual, which makes it hard to converge
      to the optimal spaces and brings huge training costs, especially for cross-architecture distillations.

789   C    HETEROGENEOUS FEATURES
790
791   Fig. 6 shows heterogeneous features, demonstrating some important observations in our main paper.
792   Firstly, heterogeneous models have different inductive biases. For example, CNN models (He et al.,
793   2016) have the inductive bias of “locality”, thereby making the features local like (A-B) in Fig. 6.
794   Differently, the features of MSA and MLP models (Liu et al., 2021b; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Tol-
795   stikhin et al., 2021) are global because of their global inductive bias, e.g., (C-F) in Fig. 6. Therefore,
796   combining different inductive biases helps mitigate the gaps between heterogeneous models like our
797   assistant.
798   Secondly, heterogeneous models have different module functions. For example, the architec-
799   tures/functions of ResNet (He et al., 2016) and Swin models (Liu et al., 2021b) are hierarchical.
800   They gradually expand the receptive fields and upsample the features, e.g., (A-D) in Fig. 6. Dif-
801   ferently, MLP models (Tolstikhin et al., 2021) and most MSA models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
802   are uniform. The features of shadow and deep layers have higher similarity than the hierarchical
803   CNN, e.g., (E-F) in Fig. 6. Therefore, combining different module functions helps mitigate the gaps
804   between heterogeneous models like our assistant.
805   Thirdly, features of heterogeneous models have different spatial distributions in different channels.
806   For example, the different channels of CNN models have similar spatial localizations (such as the
807   right figures of Fig. 6 (A-B)). Conversely, the features of MSA and MLP models in different channels
808   are more diverse, e.g., the right figures of Fig. 6(C-F). Besides, as demonstrated in (Park & Kim,
809   2021; He et al., 2016; Tolstikhin et al., 2021), spatial smoothing is useful for the predictions of
      CNN/MSA/MLP models (e.g., average pooling). Therefore, we smooth the features and replace

902
903   As shown in Tab. 11, when we replace the Swin block (Liu et al., 2021b) with ViT block (Dosovit-
904   skiy et al., 2021) in our L2G, the performance on different teacher-student pairs has different rises
905   and falls. Thus, the MSA block in L2G is also important and is worth exploring in future works.
906   Our L2G includes a patch embedding for dimension alignments of features and an MSA block for
907   global information exchange.
908
909
      Why do we use a patch embedding? The feature shape of a CNN model with the size (N, C, H,
      W), while that of an MSA/MLP model is denoted as (N, L, D). N indicates the batch size, and C,
910
      H, and W refer to the channel, height, and width of the CNN model’s feature map respectively. L
911
      and D denote the patch number and embedding dimension of the ViT/MLP model’s feature map. In
912   our assistant model, to connect the features of CNN and MSA/MLP models, we need to transform
913   the feature map of the CNN model into the MSA/MLP-style (shape) feature through a ”patchify”
914   operation. Besides, the effectiveness of “divide image to patches” has been demonstrated in CNN
915   models (Trockman & Kolter, 2022), MSA models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021b), and
916   MLP models (Li et al., 2023a; Touvron et al., 2022; Tolstikhin et al., 2021). Therefore, we use a
917   patch embedding to process the CNN features. More importantly, patch embedding is a kind of
      spatial smoothing that is beneficial to align heterogeneous features.
