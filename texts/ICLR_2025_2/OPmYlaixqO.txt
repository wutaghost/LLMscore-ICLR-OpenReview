009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Arctic sea ice performs a vital role in global climate and has paramount impacts
012            on both polar ecosystems and coastal communities. In the last few years, multiple
013            deep learning based pan-Arctic sea ice concentration (SIC) forecasting methods
014            have emerged and showcased superior performance over physics-based dynami-
015            cal models. However, previous methods forecast SIC at a fixed temporal gran-
016
               ularity, e.g. sub-seasonal or seasonal, thus only leveraging inter-granularity in-
               formation and overlooking the plentiful inter-granularity correlations. SIC at vari-
017
               ous temporal granularities exhibits cumulative effects and are naturally consistent,
018
               with short-term fluctuations potentially impacting long-term trends and long-term
019            trends provides effective hints for facilitating short-term forecasts in Arctic sea
020            ice. Therefore, in this study, we propose to cultivate temporal multi-granularity
021            that naturally derived from Arctic sea ice reanalysis data and provide a unified
022            perspective for modeling SIC via our Sea Ice Foundation Model. SIFM is del-
023            icately designed to leverage both intra-granularity and inter-granularity informa-
024            tion for capturing granularity-consistent representations that promote forecasting
025            skills. Our extensive experiments show that SIFM outperforms off-the-shelf deep
026            learning models for their specific temporal granularity.

      1   INTRODUCTION
029
030
      Arctic sea ice has a profound influence on both local and global climate systems. The near-surface
031   air temperature of Arctic regions has increased at a speed that is two to nearly four times faster
032   than the global average from 1979 to 2021, a phenomenon known as “Arctic amplification” (Screen
033   & Simmonds, 2010; Rantanen et al., 2022). This accelerated temperature rise performs a key role
034   in the unprecedented rapid diminishing of Arctic sea ice which has extensive consequences that
035   could transcend the polar area. For example, the accelerated reduction of Arctic sea ice could not
036   only jeopardize the survival of species residing in polar regions but also pose adverse effects on local
037   communities whose livelihoods and well-being depend on those animals; it could substantially affect
038   mid-latitude summer weather by weakening the storm tracks (Vavrus, 2018); and it will bring new
039
      opportunities for marine transportation and new access to natural resources like fossil fuels (Vincent,
      2020).
040
041   Due to its vital role in coastal communities, global climate, and potential impacts on the world’s
042   economy, numerical and statistical models have been proposed to forecast pan-Arctic sea ice con-
043   centration (SIC) ranging from sub-seasonal to seasonal scale (Johnson et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
044   2019). However, numerical and statistical models usually rely on high-performance computing on
045
      CPU clusters and often lead to complex debugging processes and uncertain parameterization, which
      limits their performance in forecasting long-term SIC changes. With the advent of deep learning
046
      models and their powerful capability in capturing complex patterns within high dimensional data,
047
      recent studies have developed end-to-end SIC forecasting models based on deep learning approaches
048   and have presented a promising performance that exceeds previous numerical and statistical meth-
049   ods (Andersson et al., 2021; Ren et al., 2022). Although the intrinsic annual cyclic trend and intra-
050   seasonal predictability of Arctic sea ice (Wang et al., 2016) contains rich information both between
051   and within temporal scales, existing deep learning-based methods mainly focus on predicting SIC
052   at a specific temporal granularity, e.g., 7 days or 6 months’ averages, which leads to potential ne-
053   glect of intrinsic correlation between different time scales and limits the performance of forecasting
      models. Since the Arctic sea ice extent (SIE, where SIC value is larger than 15%) has been ob-

080   Based on the above-mentioned motivations, we propose the transformer-based Sea Ice Foundation
081   Model (SIFM) that unifies the temporal granularity of interest to boost overall performance on
082
      forecasting SIC in pan-Arctic region. Unlike previous approaches (as demonstrated in Figure 2),
      we propose to independently tokenize spatial features, explicitly extract sequential information and
083
      jointly model three granularities: daily, weekly average, and monthly average. Specifically, SIFM
084
      first embeds SIC from each temporal granularity into independent spatial tokens and sequentially
085   concatenated to represent temporal fluctuations within each granularity. Then, we treat these inde-
086   pendent sequences as correlated granularity variates and utilize the attention mechanism in conjunc-
087   tion with the feed-forward network (FFN) for extracting both intra-granularity and inter-granularity
088   correlations. By incorporating multi-granularity representation, SIFM could simultaneously gener-
089   ate future SIC in different temporal scales and boost overall performance. Our contributions are
090   three folds:
091
092          • We revisit the potentially overlooked inter-granularity information by previous methods for
093
               Arctic SIC forecasting and explore the effectiveness of independent spatial tokens repre-
               sentation of SIC for facilitating accurate predictions.
094
095          • We propose SIFM that leverages independent spatial tokenization of SIC and effectively
096            unifies three temporal granularities that cover from sub-seasonal to seasonal scale for better
097            overall representation and improved forecasting performance.
098          • The comprehensive experiments demonstrate that by adopting the approach of multi-
099            granularity fusion, our SIFM achieves state-of-the-art on prediction in each granularity,
100            which advances toward a more practical Arctic sea ice forecasting system.

142   2.2       M ULTI - SCALE REPRESENTATIVE LEARNING
143
144
      The multi-scale phenomenon is common in vision tasks, while it is always overlooked in sea ice
      modeling. To exploit the information in multi-scale sources, multi-scale features are commonly
145
      exploited by using spatial pyramids (Lazebnik et al., 2006), dense sampling of windows (Yan et al.,
146
      2012), and the combination of them (Felzenszwalb et al., 2008) in the vision community. The
147   learning of CNN-based multi-scale representations is typically approached in two ways: utilizing
148   external factors like multi-scale kernel architectures and multi-scale input architectures (Reininghaus
149   et al., 2015), or designing internal network layers with skip and dense connections (Lin et al., 2016).
150   Recently, there has been a surge of interest in applying transformer-based architectures to computer
151   vision tasks, with the Vision Transformer (ViT) being particularly successful in balancing global
152   and local features compared to CNNs (Dosovitskiy, 2020). When revisiting the task of forecasting
153   sea ice concentration, its multiscale features stem from different temporal resolutions. Existing
154   methods focus on a single scale, such as daily, weekly, or monthly. However, different temporal
155
      resolutions are inherently connected, and treating them as a single scale for modeling would increase
      the complexity of network learning.

195   3.1           S EA ICE CONCENTRATION TOKENIZATION
196
197
      Existing mainstream deep learning-based methods for SIC forecasting adopt U-Net architectures and
198   leverage 2D convolution to perform channel-wise expansion and downsampling that extracts both
199   spatial features and temporal dependencies. However, since U-Net-based models are not specifically
200   designed for sequence modeling (Azad et al., 2024), the joint spatial-channel fusion of SIC and
201   implicit sequence modeling could be ill-posed properties for spatio-temporal forecasting tasks. In
202   this regard, we propose to independently tokenize spatial features at first, which could disentangle
203   the above ill-posed problem and be beneficial for SIC forecasting.
204   Independent spatial embedding. Since we aim to simultaneously model SIC derived from three
205   temporal granularities, encoding their spatial features into shared embedding space not only yields
206   consistent representation but also reduces the number of trainable parameters. Inspired by prior
207   works (Hu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), we utilize Swin Transformer V2 (Liu et al., 2022) as the
208   backbone for both shared spatial encoder and decoder.
209
      Specifically, each SIC input is independently fed into the shared spatial encoder and partitioned by a
210   non-overlapped window to generate patch representation (Dosovitskiy, 2020) with 32 spatial chan-
211   nels (the original SIC data has only one channel). To preserve local SIC information, we choose the
212   smallest 2 by 2 window size for the patch partition. Then, the patch tokens are further transformed
213   by the first two Swin Transformer blocks. The multi-scale spatial features are extracted through
214   the subsequent hierarchical encoder layers which comprise a patch merging operation and two Swin
215   Transformer blocks. The patch merging operation first concatenates the spatial feature of each group
      of 2 by 2 adjacent patch representations from the previous encoder layer. The calculation of each

243   3.2   M ULTI - GRANULARITY FUSION
244
      We propose to jointly model three granularities that cover sub-seasonal to seasonal scale, i.e., 7
245
      days, 8 weeks averages, and 6 months averages, and explicitly capture inter-granularity correlation
246
      and intra-granularity sequential information.
247
248   Modeling granularity variates. As mentioned in Section 3.1 the shared spatial encoder trans-
249   forms each SIC into independent 1D tokens. These individual spatial tokens are then concatenated
250
      sequentially based on their granularity respectively and utilized to form the multi-granularity rep-
      resentation. As each granularity incorporates a different time span, the dimensions of concatenated
251
      granularity sequences are mismatched. Considering that both the weekly average and monthly av-
252
      erage are derived from daily SIC values, we choose to tokenize those sequences further and align
253   their feature dimensions with the length of daily input using a linear transformation. The generated
254   multi-granularity variates are subsequently fed into the sequential modeling backbone. Encouraged
255   by prior work (Liu et al., 2023), we propose to adopt an encoder-only Transformer architecture as the
256   sequential modeling backbone for multi-granularity fusion in Figure 3 that: (1) applies multi-head
257   self-attention on the embedded granularity variate tokens to explicitly capture inter-granularity cor-
258   relations; (2) each granularity variate is independently processed by FFN to extract intra-granulairty
259   information (as depicted in Figure 4(a)). As for the conventional usage of vanilla Transformer in se-
260   quence prediction, the attention mechanism is applied on embedded temporal tokens which comprise
261
      variate information collected from the same time step (as in Figure 4(b)). The vanilla Transformer is
      challenged in forecasting series with larger lookback windows due to performance degradation and
262
      computation explosion. Furthermore, the temporal token embeddings incorporate multiple variates
263
      that represent distinct physical measurements, which may struggle to capture variate-specific repre-
264   sentations and potentially lead to the generation of incoherent attention maps. However, in sea ice
265   modeling, each dimension of the tokenized granularity variate incorporates SIC features that come
266   from a different time span. This could lead to poor representation of sequential SIC features and
267   restrict the effective modeling of inter-granularity correlations. Experimentally, we will show in
268   Section 4.4 that by adopting our sequential modeling, the overall performance is superior to alter-
269   native backbones. After each SIC granularity variate token is properly fused and encoded, the final
      prediction of future granularity variate features is generated through a linear projection layer.

      4.1           DATASETS
312
      We evaluate our proposed SIFM framework on the G02202 Version 4 dataset from the National
313   Snow and Ice Data Center (NSIDC). It records daily SIC data starting from October 25th 1978 and
314   provides the coverage of the pan-Arctic region (N:-39.36◦ , S:-89.84◦ , E:180◦ , W:-180◦ ). Each daily
315   SIC data is formed of 448 x 304 pixels and each pixel corresponds to the area of a 25km x 25km grid.
316   The SIC data has a range of 0% to 100% and areas where SIC value is greater than 15% indicate the
317   SIE. We choose data from October 25th 1978 to the end of 2013 as the training dataset, the years
318   2014 and 2015 are selected as validation set, and data collected from 2016 to 2023 are used to test
319   models.

531   5      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533
      In this paper, we propose SFIM, a transformer-based sea ice foundation model that unifies multi-
534   granularity covering from sub-seasonal to seasonal scale to enhance the sea ice concentration fore-
535   casting. Specifically, we propose to explore the independent spatial tokens representation of SIC
536   to exploit the inter-granularity information. These spatial tokens will be concatenated within their
537   own granularity and go through multi-granularity fusion to explicitly model their inter-granularity
538   correlations. Experiments demonstrate that our SIFM fulfills skillful forecasting in each granularity
539   compared with single-granularity methods. Since our SIFM is a versatile framework, the multi-
      granularity climate information could also be incorporated easily in future work.

678
      20 epochs. For each temporal granularity, SIFM outputs the same length of input data as forecasting

679
      window with a length of 30 days to generate averaged SIC in monthly granularity. The dimensions
680
      of embedding features of shared spatial encoder and decoder are set to 32. They both comprise of 4
