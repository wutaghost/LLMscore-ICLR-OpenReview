009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Backdoor attacks in machine learning create hidden vulnerability by manipulating
012            the model behaviour with specific triggers. Such attacks often remain unnoticed
013            as the model operates as expected for normal input. Thus, it is imperative to
014            understand the intricate mechanism of backdoor attacks. To address this challenge,
015            in this work, we introduce three key requirements that a backdoor attack must meet.
016            Moreover, we note that current backdoor attack algorithms, whether employing
017            fixed or input-dependent triggers, exhibit a high binding with model parameters,
018            rendering them easier to defend against. To tackle this issue, we propose the
019
               Key-Locks algorithm, which separates the backdoor attack process into embedding
               locks and employing a key for unlocking. This method enables the adjustment of
020
               unlocking levels to counteract diverse defense mechanisms. Extensive experiments
021
               are conducted to evaluate the effective of our proposed algorithm. Our code is
022            available at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/KeyLocks-FD85

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep neural networks (DNNs) require extensive data and training resources for a decent performance,
028
      which are not available for most companies (Jeon et al., 2019). As a result, many organizations
029   resort to outsourcing model training or using public models (Chen et al., 2022). However, verifying
030   a model as free from backdoor attacks with stealthy triggers is challenging. These triggers remain
031   dormant, allowing the model to perform normally, but causing it for incorrect results when activated.
032   Based on (Gu et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2017; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Wang et al., 2022a; Nguyen &
033   Tran, 2020; Li et al., 2021b), in this paper, we first systematically delineate three requirements for a
034   successful backdoor attack:
035   Requirement 1: An effective backdoor trigger cannot affect the semantic information of the original
036   image.
037
038
      Requirement 2: The trigger should be able to manipulate the model for incorrect outputs.
039   Requirement 3: Victim model must operate normally in the absence of the backdoor trigger.
040
      Requirement 1 emphasises the backdoor stealth, as a big disruption of the the semantic information
041
      would make it more vulnerable to defense. Requirements 2 and 3 ensure the trigger’s efficacy and
042   prevent the backdoor from interfering with regular task execution. This is crucial for maintaining the
043   concealment. Backdoor attack methods should meet three requirements without compromising model
044   integrity. We further analyze how these requirements influence the defense process in Section 3.2.
045
      Normally the backdoor attack algorithms are depicted as fixed trigger backdoors (Gu et al., 2017;
046
      Chen et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2022a; Nguyen & Tran, 2021) and input-dependent backdoors (Nguyen
047
      & Tran, 2020; Li et al., 2021b). In Figure 1, we note that fixed trigger backdoors exhibit high binding
048   with the model parameters. Specifically, to satisfy the three requirements, the parameters responsible
049   for activating these backdoors are sensitive and fragile, making them susceptible to existing defense
050   algorithms. Although the input-dependent backdoor algorithms can adaptively generate triggers as
051   needed, they are also highly bound to the generator parameters. Interestingly, it is this high binding
052   nature that is crucial for the success of backdoor defenses. Defenders can weaken or eliminate the
053   backdoor effect by adjusting model parameters or generator parameters, thereby reducing the success
      rate of backdoor attacks.

      Generally, backdoor attacks incorporate a backdoor into the model, tightly coupled with a specific
076
      trigger. This tight coupling, referred to as high binding, is a primary factor exposing backdoor attacks
077   to defensive measures. A detailed discussion on the association between the three requirements of
078   backdoor attacks and high binding, as well as the reason why such design leads to high binding, is
079   provided in Section 3.2. High binding implies that changes in the model parameters or the backdoor’s
080   operational context can render the trigger ineffective.
081
      To circumvent the limitations imposed by high binding, we introduce a more flexible and less
082
      detectable approach that involves embedding a component within the model that is responsive to a
083   broad range of trigger conditions (locks). We use Appendix A to show the structure of our approach.
084   Furthermore, a mechanism capable of generating a range of triggers (keys) is developed. These
085   triggers are designed to interact with the embedded component, effectively triggering the backdoor
086   across various scenarios. The operational details of the backdoor are primarily encoded within these
087   triggers. Consequently, the embedded component’s sole function is to respond to the presence of
088   trigger information, without the need to store or memorize specific backdoor details. Therefore,
089   compared to other backdoor attack strategies, our method offers enhanced evasion of existing defense
090   mechanisms.
091   Overall, our contributions are: •We summarise three requirements for backdoor attacks, elucidate
092   the high binding nature between backdoor attack algorithms and model parameters. We analyse how
093   backdoor defense leverages high binding property. •Our Key-Locks (K&L) algorithm decouples
094   the attack algorithm from model parameters, successfully penetrating nearly all existing backdoor
095   defense mechanisms. • We introduce a new metric: the Accuracy-ASR Curve (AAC). Extensive
096   experiments validate the performance of the K&L algorithm, demonstrating its effectiveness against
097
      nearly all current backdoor defense strategies.

102   2.1   BACKDOOR ATTACKS
103
104   We firstly discuss existing backdoor attack methods that compromise DNNs by inserting stealthy
105   triggers or altering training data, leading to incorrect outputs under specific conditions. We feature
106   several methods such as BadNet (Gu et al., 2017), Blended (Chen et al., 2017), WaNet (Nguyen &
107   Tran, 2021), Bit-per-pixel (Bpp) (Wang et al., 2022a), Input-Aware (Nguyen & Tran, 2020), and
      Sample-specific Backdoor Attack (SSBA) (Li et al., 2021b).

108
      BadNet introduces malicious behavior by inserting backdoor samples during training, such as a
109   specific pattern on an object. Blended seamlessly integrates a key pattern into inputs, creating
110   poisoned samples by subtly blending the pattern. The key pattern is intensified as a stronger presence
111   associated with a higher likelihood to trigger the backdoor. WaNet uses image warping to inject
112   backdoors, making the images more natural and harder to detect. We will analyse how the fixed-
113   trigger methods result in a high binding with the model parameters in Section 3.2. Bpp attack employs
114   image quantization and dithering to create stealthy Trojan triggers, which reduces the color palette
115   and uses contrastive learning and adversarial training to inject the Trojan. The model parameters must
116   remember the attack pattern. Input-Aware attack generate unique triggers for each input, however the
117
      generator is highly related to the model parameters. SSBA embeds attack strings into benigh images
      to create imperceptible noise as backdoor triggers, bypassing different backdoor defenses.

      2.2   BACKDOOR D EFENSES
121
      Here we review classic and state-of-the-art defense algorithms that mitigate backdoor threats, starting
122   with testing-time defenses such as Strong Intentional Perturbation (STRIP). STRIP (Gao et al.,
123   2019) defends against backdoor attacks by perturbing input images with a set of clean images and
124   monitoring the entropy of prediction outputs. High entropy in predictions indicates a robust response
125   to potential backdoor triggers, making STRIP an effective preliminary defense mechanism. Following
126   this, we delve into strategies from neuron pruning to advanced techniques like attention distillation.
127   Adversarial Neuron Pruning (ANP) (Wu & Wang, 2021) prunes sensitive neurons directly, avoiding
128   extensive retraining and requiring minimal data. Similarly, Batch Normalization Statistics-based
129   Pruning (BNP) (Zheng et al., 2022b) considers the altered BN layer statistics, utilizing divergence for
130
      pruning. Channel Lipschitzness based Pruning (CLP) (Zheng et al., 2022a) uses Channel Lipschitz
      Constant as a metric to identify and prune channels most affected by trigger patterns. Implicit
131
      Backdoor Adversarial Unlearning (I-BAU) (Zeng et al., 2021) removes embedded triggers using
132
      a minimax optimization with implicit hypergradients, streamlining the unlearning process. Neural
133   Attention Distillation (NAD) (Li et al., 2021a) realigns the compromised network’s attention through
134   a teacher-student paradigm, akin to knowledge distillation. These methods collectively form a defense
135   ecosystem that addresses different aspects of the backdoor threat, from direct intervention to subtle
136   attention realignment, catering to a variety of defense scenarios.
137
      Enhancing the credibility of AI systems through interpretability tools can also detect backdoor
138
      attacks (Selvaraju et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2023). This paper employs the Boundary-based Integrated
139   Gradient (BIG) (Wang et al., 2022b), an attribution interpretability tool that aggregates gradients
140   along a path from input to the nearest decision boundary, leveraging local boundary information for
141   more precise explanations. BIG is used here to investigate the detectability of backdoor samples
142   generated by various attack techniques.

      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
147
148   A backdoor attack on a neural network (NN) classifier aims to create a new model f (:; W ) that
149   behaves normally on the standard input distribution but misclassifies inputs that contain a specific
150   pattern τ to a target class ct . D is the training dataset. The poisoned samples are created by applying
151   a perturbation τ to a subset of D, yielding a poisoned dataset B. The model f (:; W ) is trained using
152   a dataset DB which includes both clean and poisoned samples.
153   f (x; W ) represents a mapping Rn → Rc for an input x to output over C classes. For an input-
154   dependent backdoor attack, the progress of adding a trigger is defined by a perturbation function
155   Tτ : Rn → Rn which embeds a backdoor trigger τ into the benign input x, and a target label ct , by
156   optimizing model parameters W :
157                                       f (Tτ (x); W ) = ct   ∀x ∈ B,                                    (1)
158
159                                        f (x; W ) ≈ p(y|x) ∀x ∈ D,                                     (2)
160   where p(y|x) denotes the true probability distribution over labels given input x, and ct is the target
161   label specified by the attacker. Eq. 1 embodies Requirement 2, ensuring that the backdoored model
      misclassifies any input containing the trigger τ to the target class ct . Eq. 2 aligns with Requirement


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      3, asserting that the model’s behavior on clean inputs x approximates the true label distribution
163   p(y|x). Requirement 1 stipulates that the benign input x and its triggered counterpart Tτ (x) should
164   share similar semantics, a condition that can be quantitatively expressed using the L2 norm such that
165   ∥x − Tτ (x)∥2 < ϵ, where ϵ is a small constant. It ensures the stealth of the trigger, preventing easy
166   detection while preserving the original semantics of the input as much as possible.

      3.2   R ESEARCH P ROBLEM
170
      Q1: What makes the fixed-trigger backdoor susceptible to effective defense?
171
172   Fixed trigger backdoor attacks typically involve inserting identical triggers into an image, and to
173   maintain semantic similarity before and after the attack (Requirement 1), these triggers are often
174   designed to be imperceptible, such as being extremely small or nearly transparent. Consequently, the
175   model needs to meticulously memorize the characteristics of these triggers. Hence, any parameters
176
      changes can lead to a loss of memory regarding these triggers, effectively neutralizing the backdoor
      attack. This principle underlies the rationale of most backdoor defense mechanisms.
177
178   Under the premise that Requirement 3 is used to ensure normal model function, due to Requirements
179   1 and 2, these parameters must be highly sensitive and fragile. The model is expected to trigger with
180   only a minimal presence of harmful features without impacting its semantic information. We interpret
181   this sensitivity and fragility as high binding between the backdoor attack and model parameters.
182
      Any trigger addition method that does not rely on model parameters is highly bound to the
      parameters, the reason being that the model’s parameters must memorize these triggers or the
183
      methods of adding these triggers. This high binding is precisely why fixed trigger methods are
184
      susceptible to data-free defense methods like Clip, which detects and eliminates parameters that
185   deviate from normal, often characterized by their high sensitivity.
186
187   Additionally, defense methods like ANP, RNP, FP, FT, I-BAU, and NAD use clean datasets to fine-
188
      tune or distill the model. The conspicuous sensitivity of anomalous parameters after gradient descent
      engenders a shift in the original backdoor trigger conditions, rendering conventional backdoor attack
189
      methods useless.
190
191   Q2: What factors contribute to the defensibility of current input-dependent backdoor attacks?
192   Input-aware backdoor attacks serve as a paradigmatic instance of input-dependent backdoor strategies.
193   This approach entails the concurrent training of a Generator alongside the primary model, with the
194   objective of fabricating a distinct Trigger for each input sample. To comply with Requirement
195   1, which necessitates minimal perturbation to the original input features, the perturbation of the
196   generated Trigger must be rigorously regulated. Fundamentally, the input-aware methodology
197   transitions the binding from a fixed trigger and model parameters to a dynamic association between
198   the model parameters and those of the Generator. For instance, a trigger generated by the Generator
199   for a given input image is a one-off creation, thus establishing a pronounced binding between this
200
      trigger and the model parameters. Consequently, any modification to the model could potentially
      misalign the specifically tailored trigger, leading to the nullification of the backdoor, signifying
201
      that the Generator exhibits a high binding to the model parameters. The Bpp algorithm embodies
202
      input-dependence and Requirement 1 through methods such as image quantization and dithering.
203   Nonetheless, these techniques are inherently sensitive and decoupled from the model parameters,
204   resonating with the high binding scenario posited in Question 1, where Trigger generation is not
205   contingent on model parameters.
206
      Definition of high binding: A backdoor attack method that exhibits high binding to model parameters
207
      must meet at least one of the conditions: 1. A fixed Trigger is generated. 2. The process of generating
208
      the Trigger is independent of the model’s parameters. 3. The Trigger generation process is parametric
209   and is trained in conjunction with the model.
210
211   Q3: What properties should a backdoor attack possess to surpass current defensive strategies?
212   (a)Initially, it is expected to conform to all requirements 1-3. (b) The approach must adhere to an
213   Input-dependent condition while avoiding high binding to the model parameters. Specifically, the
214   generation of Triggers should be parameter-dependent, with the Trigger addition method not resulting
215   from joint training with the model.(c) Compromising the effectively embedded backdoor results in
      affecting the model’s performance on standard tasks.

286   3.4   D IFFERENTIATING K&L FROM A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
287
288   Adversarial attacks aim to perturb inputs in a manner that deceives the target model without altering
289   its parameters. These attacks typically introduce minimal perturbations to the input data, denoted
290   by δ, leading to an adversarially modified input xadv = x + δ. The objective is to cause the model
291   f (·; W ) to misclassify xadv , such that f (xadv ; W ) ̸= y, where x is the original input, W represents
292
      the model parameters, and y is the true label.
293   In contrast, our approach involves embedding a backdoor into the model during the training phase
294   by adjusting its parameters W to induce a high binding phenomenon. This is achieved through a
295   dual-objective optimization process, where the model is trained to minimize the loss on clean inputs
296   while simultaneously ensuring that inputs containing a specific pattern or trigger τ are misclassified
297   to a target class ct . The optimization can be formalized as:
298                               W ∗ = arg min L(x, y; W ) + λL(x′ , ct ; W ),                             (7)
                                               W
299
300   where L denotes the loss function, λ is a regularization term that balances the performance on clean
301
      and poisoned inputs, and x′ represents the function embedding the backdoor trigger into a benign
      input x. The goal is to find optimal model parameters W ∗ that ensure the model performs accurately
302
      on legitimate inputs while classifying inputs with trigger as the target class ct .
303
304   Our method’s distinction from adversarial attacks lies in its focus on modifying the model param-
305   eters during training to embed a backdoor, as opposed to crafting input perturbations at inference
306   time. This high binding backdoor strategy not only renders the backdoor more difficult to detect and
307
      remove but also potentially increases the model’s susceptibility to adversarial attacks, fundamentally
      altering the model’s response to specific input patterns.

      5    C ONCLUSION
503
504
      In this work, we present the key requirements for successful backdoor attacks and address the high
505
      binding nature prevalent in existing methods. Our novel Key-Locks Backdoor Attack algorithm
506   effectively circumvents this challenge, proving resilience against nearly all current defense methods
507   while maintaining minimal perturbation. Extensive experiments validate our approach, showcasing
508   the K&L algorithm’s effectiveness in penetrating backdoor defense methods. However, The process
509   of the K&L attack requires keeping the key at hand. Although other backdoor attack algorithms may
510   also store the trigger or the trained model, the K&L approach increases the demands of computing
511   resources, which is directly related to the model size. Despite this, we believe the effectiveness of the
512   attack justifies the additional cost.

719   C     T HREAT M ODEL

721
      Suppose an attacker’s goal is to implant a backdoor into an artificial intelligence model used in an
722   automated image recognition system. This backdoor would trigger incorrect behavior or outputs
723   when specific image features are detected. The attacker has the ability to access or influence the
724   model’s training data and possesses sufficient permissions and opportunities to participate in the
725   training to implant a backdoor. By embedding this backdoor, AI model will perform predetermined
726   erroneous actions, such as misclassification, when it detects images containing specific triggers. The
727   attacker utilizes our Key and Locks (K&L) attack method to generate triggers that are difficult for
728   the human eye to detect, achieving a more covert attack. A successful backdoor attack could lead
729   to the system making incorrect decisions in practical applications, such as a security monitoring
730
      system failing to correctly identify threats, resulting in security vulnerabilities. The hypothesis is
      feasible, as many companies currently do not possess the resources to train models themselves, and
731
      therefore, they tend to outsource model training to third parties. This situation provides opportunities
732
      for attackers. Additionally, there are numerous platforms for open-source models, such as Hugging
733   Face, where attackers could upload their backdoored models. These factors make the threat model
734   feasible.

737   D     E VALUATION METRICS
738
739   D.1    B ENIGN ACCURACY (BA) AND ATTACK S UCCESS R ATE (ASR)
740
741   BA, calculated as the ratio of correct predictions on clean test instances, reflects the model’s normal
742
      operation, ensuring the backdoor does not impair primary task performance. ASR, measuring the
      rate at which backdoored samples are misclassified into a targeted class, assesses the backdoor’s
743
      effectiveness. High ASR indicates effective trigger recognition, while BA assures the attack’s
744
      stealth by demonstrating unaltered performance on regular inputs. Both metrics are crucial for a
745   comprehensive assessment of the backdoor attack’s impact and stealth.

      D.2    ACCURACY-ASR C URVE (AAC)
748
749
      To facilitate the evaluation of backdoor attack algorithms, we introduce a new metric: Accuracy-ASR
750   Curve (AAC). The y-axis represents the percentage of the attack method penetrating the defense
751   algorithms, while the x-axis corresponds to the ASR threshold for considering a defense successful.
752   A higher ASR on this curve signifies a more lenient criterion for successful defense. As shown
753   in Figure 3, the AAC metric requires setting a parameter for the permissible loss in accuracy. We
754   employ AAC3 to denote the defense scenario where an accuracy loss of up to 3% is allowed. We
755   compute the area under the AAC and term it the Accuracy-ASR Value (AAV); a higher AAC value
      implies a more effective attack method under the defined accuracy loss constraint.
