009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Theorem Provers (NTPs) present a promising framework for neuro-
012            symbolic reasoning, combining end-to-end differentiability with the interpretabil-
013            ity of symbolic logic programming. However, optimizing NTPs remains a sig-
014            nificant challenge due to their complex objective landscape and gradient sparcity.
015            On the other hand, Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) methods offer smooth
016            optimization with well-defined learning objectives but often lack interpretability.
017            In this work, we propose several strategies to integrate the strengths of NTPs and
018            KGEs. By incorporating KGE objectives into the NTP framework, we demon-
019
               strate substantial improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Deep Learning (DL) methods have recently achieved tremendous progress in various tasks such
025   as language modeling (Touvron et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) and content generation Rombach
026   et al. (2022); Kerbl et al. (2023). However, when compared with symbolic systems, they are still
027
      limited by the long-lasting problems of the lack of interpretation, out-of-domain generalizability and
      reasoning abilities.
028
029   To address the above challenges, the concept of Neuro-Symbolic AI (NeSy) has been proposed
030   to integrate DL and symbolic AI into one end-to-end differentiable system. A popular approach
031   for such integration is to embed discrete symbols into continuous vector space to enable end-to-
032   end differentiability (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017; Minervini et al., 2019; Badreddine et al., 2022).
033
      Neural Theorem Prover (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017) (NTP) is a representative of such approach.
      It introduces the concept of soft unification during backward chaining process, where the unification
034
      operation is on the learnt embedding space instead of between discrete symbols. Subsequent works
035
      Greedy Neural Theorem Prover (GNTP) (Minervini et al., 2019) and Conditional Theorem Prover
036   (CTP) (Minervini et al., 2020) implement top-k rule retrieval and rule reformulation to improve
037   NTP’s scalability and performance.
038
039
      Although NTP has been shown to be effective on various datasets, it is known to be hard to opti-
      mize (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017; Minervini et al., 2019; Maene & Raedt, 2023; de Jong & Sha,
040
      2019). Specifically, as NTPs adopt the fuzzy min-max semiring for unification score aggregation,
041
      only a fraction of embedding parameters will receive gradient updates. The model optimization
042   is thus heavily dependent on the initialization, and can get stuck in local minima (de Jong & Sha,
043   2019), resulting in under-explored and unregularized embedding space. DeepSoftLog (Maene &
044   Raedt, 2023) addresses the above limitation by using differentiable probabilistic semiring instead
045   of fuzzy semiring, along with other proposed properties to smooth out the back-propagation pro-
046   cess. However, as it requires additional modules for knowledge compilation (Darwiche, 2011) and
047   requires all possible proofs to be considered during training (as opposed to k-best approximation), it
048   is intrinsically hard to scale to larger datasets.
049   On the other hand, Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE)s (Bordes et al., 2013; Lin et al., 2015;
050   Yang et al., 2015; Trouillon et al., 2016; Dettmers et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2019) are state-of-the-art
051   (SOTA) methods for modeling Knowledge Graphs (KGs). KGEs learn mappings from symbols into
052   their corresponding vector representations by maximizing scores for positive triplets while mini-
053   mizing for the negatives, based on some predefined score functions. KGEs enjoy well-defined loss
      functions, smooth optimization process, and have shown SOTA performances on KG tasks such as

054
      link prediction. However, as KGEs are purely sub-symbolic algorithms, they lack the interpretability
055   as compared to NTPs.
056
057   Motivated by the complementary properties of NTPs and KGEs, we conduct the first systematic
058
      study for integrating KGEs into the NTP framework. The rest of the paper is arranged as follow: in
      Section 3 we provide brief definition and introduction for NTPs and KGEs, and discuss the hardness
059
      of training NTP from an embedding perspective 3.4; In Section 4 we explain four strategies for
060
      integrating KGEs with NTPs, and conduct detailed experiments in Section 5. Finally, we provide
061   ablation studies to examine important components during the integration between KGEs and NTPs.
062   We wish our work can serve as a first step to future studies on such integration and to improve upon
063   existing differentiable provers.
064
      Contribution:
065
066
067        1. We provide the first systematic study for integrating KGEs into NTPs and propose four
068           integration strategies, with two focusing on performance, and the other two on efficiency.
069
070        2. We show that the integration noticeably improve the baseline NTP by a large margin and
071           achieve SOTA results on multiple datasets. We also show that by leveraging the properties
072           of KGEs we could drastically improve the inference and evaluation efficiency.
073
074        3. We provide detailed ablations to examine the learnt embedding of NTPs and key factors
075           in the integration. Interestingly, we find NTPs can achieve superior results with pure KGE
076           objectives under several datasets, suggesting the synergy between the two distinct methods.

082   Differentiable Logic Programming algorithms can be roughly divided into two categories. (1)
083   Disentangled perception+reasoning (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023).
084   This line of works train a neural network to output a probability distribution over symbols, which is
085   then consumed by a differentiable logic solver. For example, DeepProbLog Manhaeve et al. (2018)
086   guides a neural network with probabilistic circuits constructed by Sentential Decision Diagram (Dar-
087
      wiche, 2011) (SDD). Scallop (Huang et al., 2021) scales up DeepProbLog by only considering top-k
      possible worlds. NeurASP (Yang et al., 2023) adopts the same strategy, but replace SDD with a An-
088
      swer Set Programming solver. Under this regime, the neural component is completely separated
089
      from the reasoning module. (2)Soft logic programming (Cohen, 2016; Badreddine et al., 2022;
090   Yang et al., 2017; Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017). This line of works are a continuous relaxation on
091   top of logic programming, by learn a mapping from symbols and logic operations into latent embed-
092   dings and differentiable tensor operations. Logic Tensor Network (Badreddine et al., 2022) extends
093   First-Order Logic (FOL) with fuzzy semantics. N EURAL LP (Yang et al., 2017) is a rule-based
094   learning algorithm that extends TensorLog (Cohen, 2016) by learning to soft select and compose
095   rules. Besides, Neural Theorem Prover (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017) learns latent embeddings for
096   symbols following backward chaining algorithm. Greedy Neural Theorem Prover (Minervini et al.,
097   2019) and Conditional Theorem Prover (Minervini et al., 2020) improve the scalability of NTP by
098
      incorporating top-k retrieval and soft rule reformulation.
099   Knowledge Graph Embedding (KGE) are SOTA methods for link prediction tasks over large-
100   scale KGs. T RANS E (Bordes et al., 2013) and its extensions (Wang et al., 2014; Xiao et al., 2015)
101   are translation-based KGEs which minimize distance between subject and object, translated by the
102   predicate. On the other hand, RESCAL (Nickel et al., 2011), C OMPL E X (Trouillon et al., 2016),
103   T UCK ER (Balazevic et al., 2019) etc. use multi-linear maps to combine subject, relation and object
104
      for score calculation.
105   Path-based KG Algorithms explicitly learn the multi-hop paths over KGs. They can be applied
106   directly on top of KGEs by handling multi-hop relation paths as compositions over embedding
107   space such as in (Lin et al., 2015), or can be formulated as path-searching algorithms, optimized by
      Reinforcement Learning objectives such as in (Das et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2018).

      3.1       N EURAL T HEOREM P ROVER
111
112   In this section we define the syntax and briefly introduce the SLD resolution and NTP algorithm.
113   We refer the reader to (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017) for a more in-depth explanation.
114
115   Syntax A term t can be either a constant c or a variable X1 . An atom is defined as a combination
116   of a predicate symbol and a list of terms. Rules are in the form of H :– B, where the head H is an
117   atom, and the body B is a list of atoms connected by conjunctions. A rule with no free variables is
118   called a ground rule, and a ground rule with an empty body is called a fact. A substitution, denoted
119   as ψ = {X1 /t1 , . . . , XN /tN } , assigns variables Xi to terms ti , and applying a substitution to an
120   atom replaces each occurrence of Xi with the corresponding term ti . In this work, we only consider
121
      atoms with binary predicates in the form of (s, r, o), where s, r and o denote subject, predicate
      (relation) and object respectively.
122
123
124
      Backward Chaining Given the goal, backward chaining works backward to find supporting facts
      and rules from the Knowledge Base (KB). It can be seen as an iterative process of applying AND/OR:
125
      the OR operation looks for all rules with matching head to perform unification. The AND module is
126
      subsequently called to iteratively prove all atoms in the unified rule’s body, where the OR module is
127   again called recursively.
128
129
      NTP and Soft Unification NTPs provide a continuous relaxation of backward chaining by intro-
130   ducing soft unification. It calculates a unification score γ = ϕNTP (ci , cj ) over the embeddings of
131   two symbols, where ϕNTP refers to the predefined similarity function, ci and cj denotes two constant
132   terms to be unified. In case of NTP, a Gaussian kernel is usually adopted for ϕNTP . The unification
133   score γ at each proof state are then aggregated following the min/max fuzzy semiring, also known
134   as the Gödel t-norm. Specifically, the AND module performs min aggregation as all sub-goals have
135   to be proved for the given rule, and OR perform max aggregation, since we only need one proof to be
136   true to prove the goal. During training, given a KG G, each fact (s, r, o) ∈ G is corrupted to obtain
137   negative samples (s′ , r, o), (s, r, o′ ) and (s′ , r, o′ ) ̸∈ G. The learning objective is then defined as the
138
      negative log likelihood of the aggregated unification score:
139

144   3.2       KG S AND E MBEDDING M ETHODS
145
146   A Knowledge Graph (KG) G is a directed multi-graph, represented as a collection of triplets (facts)
147   (s, r, o) ⊆ E × R × E, where E and R denote the set of entities and relations in G. A KGE model
148   defines a function that maps triplets to scores ϕKGE : E × R × E → R. This score function ϕKGE
149   can be translation-based as in T RANS E (Bordes et al., 2013): ϕT RANS E (s, r, o) = −||s + r − o||,
150   or similarity-based using a multi-linear function (Trouillon et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2015). For in-
151
      stance, C OMPL E X (Trouillon et al., 2016) defines the score function as ϕC OMPL E X = Re(⟨s, r, o⟩),
      where ⟨·, ·, ·⟩ denotes the tri-linear product, Re denotes the real part of the complex number, and ·
152
      denotes the complex conjugate. KGEs are traditionally interpreted as energy-based models (EBMs),
153
      where the score is interpreted as the negative energy of triplets, and are trained with contrastive ob-
154   jectives and negative log likelihood loss, similar to LNTP . Besides treating KGEs as EBMs, existing
155   works (Joulin et al., 2017; Lacroix et al., 2018; Ruffinelli et al., 2020) have shown that KGEs can
156   be effectively trained using cross-entropy loss to predict missing object over E, given subjects and
157   predicates, i.e. by maximizing:

      3.3         NTP S AS M EMORY-AUGMENTED PATH - BASED A LGORITHM
172
      Inspired by Conditional Theorem Prover (CTP) (Minervini et al., 2020) we can implement NTP as a
173
      memory-augmented path-based algorithm. Instead of searching for all rules in the KB, CTP extends
174   NTP by learning a goal transformation module that directly transforms each goal predicate to a list
175   of predicates following pre-set rule templates (e.g. transitivity), thereby forming the proof paths.
176   Given a (sub)goal, the model steps through each atom formed by the transformed goal predicate
177   until it reaches the end of the path. The above procedure is instantiated recursively for each atom
178   (sub-goal) along the path until it reaches the depth limit. This formulation gives us more flexibility
179   for integrating KGE methods comparing to original NTP. In Figure 1 we show a simple example of
180   CTP with depth = 1 and one transitive rule template of length n. At each step, the process can be
181   viewed as sampling k plausible objects given the subject and predicate o ∼ P(s, r), which shares
182
      similar formulation as in formula 2.

184   3.4         H ARDNESS IN T RAINING NTP S
185
186   Previous works (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017; Maene & De Raedt, 2023; de Jong & Sha, 2019)
187   have primarily focused on analyzing and addressing the limitations of NTPs from the perspective of
188   unsmooth optimization, particularly in relation to the sparse gradient problem. However, attempts
189
      to mitigate this issue often introduce additional computational overhead. For example, DeepSoft-
      Log (Maene & De Raedt, 2023) tackles the sparse gradient problem in NTP training by employing
190
      differentiable probabilistic semantics, combined with a knowledge compilation step for probabilistic
191
      inference, and evaluates the entire proof tree (as opposed to using a top-k approximation) to ensure
192   accurate gradient calculation. While this approach yields improved accuracy and provides a more
193   interpretable probabilistic framework, it struggles to scale beyond small KBs.

      In contrast to previous works, we try to view the hardness in NTP training from the embedding
      perspective. Unlike KGEs, which compute triplet scores based on interactions between entities and
208
      predicates, NTPs derive embeddings solely from pairwise unification scores. This results in embed-
209
      dings in NTPs being less structured. Furthermore, while KGEs typically sample a large number of
210   negative examples (e.g., 256) to learn the distribution of entities given a subject/object and relation:
211   (o ∼ P(s, r)) or (s ∼ P(r, o)), NTPs generally sample only a single negative example per entity and
212   retrieve only the top-k facts from the KB for unification, where k ≪ |E|. As a result, semantically
213   similar embeddings in NTPs may end up in vastly different regions of the embedding space if they
214   are never unified or do not receive gradient updates due to the fuzzy min/max operations. In Figure 2
215   we show the distribution of pairwise unification scores between entities, and we could observe the

      In this section we discuss the four variants we considered for integrating KGEs with NTPs. In
241
      Table 1 we summarize how each variant are implemented on top of the original CTP framework.
242
243
      KGE as an auxiliary loss model The most straightforward strategy for leveraging KGEs to sup-
244   port NTP training is to use KGE as an auxiliary model for loss calculation. The overall loss for
245   training NTPs then becomes:
246
247                                              X
248       L = λLNTPG + (1 − λ)                                −y log(KGEθ ((s, r, o)) − (1 − y) log(1 − KGEθ ((s, r, o))
                         θ
249                                         ((s,r,o),y) ∈ G
250
251   where λ is a hyper-parameter controlling the weight for the mixture. We denote this variant as
252
      CTP1 . Note that using KGE as an auxiliary loss term was briefly mentioned in the original NTP
      paper (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017). However, it was not further examined nor was it used in the
253
      subsequent works in GNTP and CTP.
254
255
      KGE as an auxiliary score function Similar to CTP1 , we again consider utilizing KGE score
256   function. But rather than appending it as a loss term at the very end, here we inject KGE score
257   function ϕKGE into NTPs as an auxiliary score ϕmixed = λϕNTP + (1 − λ)ϕKGE . In this way, we
258   could provide additional regularization at each proof step, and force the model to learn interactions
259   between entities and predicates along the proof path. We refer to this variant as CTP2 . Despite the
260   simplicity, we find this variant to bring the most consistent improvement across most experiments.
261
      KGE for stepping through For translation-based KGEs such as T RANS E and ROTAT E, the tail
262
      object o can be efficiently calculated given (s, r). To leverage this translational property, we con-
263
      sider replacing the topk retrieval with a translation-based operation to improve inference efficiency.
264   Specifically, given a (s, r) pair, we use translational KGE to obtain corresponding object and then
265   retrieve the closest k facts for score calculation. During inference we skip the retrieval and score
266   calculation. This variant, referred to as CTP3 , is designed to improve the efficiency of NTPs. In
267   this case, for each proof path, CTP3 is very similar to the path-based KGE method PT RANS E (Lin
268   et al., 2015). However, they differ in that 1) PT RANS E follow KGE training strategy, and utilize
269   additional prior for spurious relations, while 2) CTP3 calculates unification scores along the proof
      path, and uses the original NTP’s retrieval-based score calculation for each proof path.


                                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      KGE for final score calculation We consider applying KGE at the final step at each proof path.
271   One drawback on NTPs’ efficiency is their evaluation speed. During evaluation of a link prediction
272   task, in order to rank all the entities in the KG, the model retrieves top-k facts for each combination
273   of the missing entity and the known predicate-object/subject pair, followed by the unification score
274   calculation between two tensors of shape (|E|, k, 3d) where k is the retrieved k facts, and d is the
275   embedding dimension. For example, WN18RR dataset contains 40,943 entities. With k = 10 we
276   need to compute the pairwise distance with the Gaussian kernel between two matrices of shape
277   (40, 943 × 10, 3d). This is done at the end of every proof path, leading to extremely slow evaluation
278   compared to KGEs. Therefore, we try to replace the last proof step with KGEs, while keeping the
279
      previous steps with NTP. In this way, we wish to leverage the multi-hop reasoning ability of NTPs
      while using KGEs for local ranking at the final step. We refer to this variant as CTP4 .
280
281   While it is trivial to combine any variants together, we do not find performance gain by doing so.
282   Therefore we leave them separated for the sake of clarity.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
287
288   Dataset We conduct experiments on popular link prediction datasets including Nations, UMLS
289   and Kinship (Kemp et al., 2006). Following GNTP (Minervini et al., 2019) we also experiment on
290   FB122 (Guo et al., 2016a) and WN18RR (Dettmers et al., 2018). FB122 consists of two test split:
291   Test-I and Test-II, where Test-II contains the set of triplets that can be inferred via logic rules, and
292   Test-I denotes the other triplets. We follow the same evaluation protocol as in GNTP and CTP, and
293   report Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) and HITS@m under the filtered setting.
294
295   Baseline We compare our work with the previous NTPs: GNTP and CTP. Following GNTP, we
296
      also compare with additional neuro-symbolic systems: N EURAL LP (Yang et al., 2017) and MIN-
      ERVA (Das et al., 2018) on Kinship, Nations and UMLS datasets. N EURAL LP extends on T EN -
297
      SOR L OG (Cohen, 2016) by also learning rules; MINERVA deploys REINFORCE algorithm. For
298
      FB122 and WN18RR, we also compare against popular KGE methods C OMPL EX and D IST M ULT.
299
300
      Implementation We conduct our experiments primarily on CTP (Minervini et al., 2020). Since
301
      the original CTP did not evaluate on large-scale dataset FB122 and WN18RR, we perform hyper
302   parameter tuning to obtain the CTP baseline for these two datasets. By default, we use C OMPL EX
303   for CTP1 , CTP3 and CTP4 , and ROTAT E for CTP2 as we observe best overall performance under
304   these settings. During training, we obtain negative samples by corrupting subject, entity, and both,
305   each with n times, resulting in 3n negative samples generated for each triplet. These negative
306   samples will receive negative label y = 0, and the model is trained according to the NTP objective
307   (Eq. 1). For CTP1 and CTP2 we use λ = 0.5 as the default weight for combining KGE and NTP.

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper we propose to leverage KGE methods to improve NTP performance by enhancing
527
      NTP’s embedding space to be better structured and regularized. We explore four variants CTP1 -
528   CTP4 for integrating KGEs into the NTP, and find that by injecting KGEs into NTP’s score calcula-
529   tion (CTP2 ) we can improve upon the baseline NTP by a large margin and achieve SOTA results on
530   multiple datasets. We also show that we could drastically improve NTP’s inference and evaluation
531   performance by substituting computationally intensive NTP components with lightweight KGE op-
532   erations. Finally, we conduct detailed ablations and analysis on key components of the integration.
533
534   Limitation We also recognize limitations and directions for future work. First, while we show
535   noticeable performance gain by integrating KGEs into NTPs, we also demonstrate in our ablations
536   sections 5.3 and 5.3 that KGEs do not naturally reconcile with NTPs, where further analysis is
537   required to examine the synergy between the two methods. Second, the efficiency of NTPs are still
538   a concern. Although in CTP3 and CTP4 we reduce the inference and evaluation time drastically,
539   it however comes with performance degradation, and is still lagging behind KGE methods. This
      hinders the usage of NTPs in real-world scenarios.

718   7.1   DATASET I NFORMATION
719
720   We conduct experiments on three small-scale link prediction datasets: Kinship, Nations and
721   UMLS (Kemp et al., 2006), as well as two large-scale Knowledge Graph (KG) datasets: FB122 (Guo
722   et al., 2016a) and WN18RR (Dettmers et al., 2018). FB122 is a subset of Freebase (Bollacker et al.,
723
      2007) containing facts of people, location and sports. Its test set is splitted into two subsets, Test-I
      and Test-II, where Test-I contains all triplets that cannot be derived by deductive logic inference,
724
      and Test-II denotes all the rest triplets. WN18RR is derived from WordNet (WN18) (Miller, 1995),
725
      where test triplets that can be obtained by inverting triplets in the training set are removed. In Table 7
726   we summarize the statistics of these datasets.

729
      7.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
730
      Rule templates CTP defines a number of rule templates for the model to explore. The template is
731
      defined as number of steps – how many steps to hop from the head to the tail entity, and whether
732   it is a reverse relation, indicated by r, i.e. stepping from tail to head entity. For example, rule = 0
733   means the model will try to directly unify the goal with facts in the KB. rule = 2 means two
734   steps from the head to the tail entity, e.g. R(s, o) :– R1 (s, z), R2 (z, o). rule = 1R means a reverse
735   relation: R(s, o) :– R1 (o, s). For Kinship, Nations and UMLS we follow the setting in CTP with
736   Kinship={0, 1, 1r}, Nations={0, 2, 1r}, and UMLS={0, 2}. For FB122 and WN18RR we both use
737   {0, 1, 2, 1r}.
738   Training For hyper-parameters we follow CTP (Minervini et al., 2020) on Kinship and UMLS
739   datasets for all the experiments. Specifically, we use embedding size=50, top-k=4, batch size=8,
740   learning rate=0.1, trained 100 epochs with Adagrad optimizer. For each triplets we sample 3 nega-
741   tive sample per entity (a total of 9 negative samples per triplet). For Nations we use batch size=256
742   with AdamW optimizer for the CTP2 variant, and the same as CTP for the rest of models. For
743   FB122 and WN18RR we mostly follow the setting from GNTP (Minervini et al., 2019), with em-
744   bedding size=100, top-k=10, and 1 negative sample per entity. We use Adagrad optimizer and train
745   100 epochs.
746   For baseline CTP we find freezing the model entities in the first 25 epochs work well, while for all
747   our CTP variants we receive better results by not freezing the model from the beginning. We also
748   explore different score aggregation operations for aggregating scores along one proof path (AND
749   operation). For baseline CTP and CTP1 we find the original min generally work well, while mean
750   and multiplication work better for CTP2 , CTP3 and CTP4 . besides, we considered using cosine
751   similarity as the scoring metric, with using addition instead of concatenation for obtaining the em-
752
      bedding for the whole triplets. However, we do not observe it to perform better than using the
      Gaussian kernel.
753
754   Incorporating KGE objectives To ensure KGE score lies within 0 and 1 we add a Sigmoid function
755   to its negative score function. To avoid small negative scores being pushed to zeros after Sigmoid,
      we first subtract the mean from the negative scores.

773   7.4   P SEUDO - CODE IMPLEMENTATION
774
775   Neural Theorem Prover implements backward chaining algorithm by recursively instantiating
      AND / OR modules, where OR is called to prove each goal by unifying with each rule head in the
776
      KB. Then, the AND module is called to prove the rule body, where for each atom in the body the
777
      OR is recursively called, until the algorithm reaches depth limit d. The pseudo-code for NTP can be
778
      found in 1.

783   predicting plausible rules given goals. The pseudo-code for CTP can be found in 2.

879       if max_depth == 0:
              return unify(s, r, o)
880       else:
881           score = None
              for d in range(max_depth):
882
                  level_score = None
883               for rule_path in rule_templates:
884
                      path_score = None
885                   for step_ind, rule_transform in rule_path:
886                       if is_inverse_relation:
887                           latent_score, s = step(o, r, s, max_depth - 1)
                          else:

889                       if path_score is None:

                          else:
891                           # min aggregation -- all proofs need to be hold.
                              path_score = min(path_score, latent_score)
892
893                       if step_ind == len(rule_path):

894                           path_score, _ = max(path_score, dim=-1)
895                   if level_score is None:
896                       level_score = path_score
                      else:
897                       # max aggregation -- only one proof path needs to be hold.
                          level_score = max(level_score, path_score)
898
899           if score is None:
                  score = level_score
900           else:
                  # max aggregation -- only one proof path needs to be hold.
901               score = max(score, level_score)

903
          if o is not None:
904           topk_ind = topk([s, r, o])
905       else:
              topk_ind = topk([s, r])
906           o = KB[topk_ind][-1]
