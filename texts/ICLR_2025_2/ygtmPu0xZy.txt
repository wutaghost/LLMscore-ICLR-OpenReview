010

016            ensemble coupling, Ensemble++ decouples mean and uncertainty estimation by

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Sequential decision-making under uncertainty is a fundamental challenge in machine learning, with
      critical applications in reinforcement learning, contextual bandits, recommendation systems, au-
036
      tonomous systems, and healthcare (Russo et al., 2018). In these domains, agents must balance the
037
      dual objectives of exploration—gathering new information about the environment—and exploita-
038   tion—maximizing rewards based on current knowledge. This balance is particularly challenging
039   in high-dimensional, complex environments where computational resources are limited and data
040   collection is costly.
041
      A key requirement for effective decision-making in such settings is the accurate estimation and uti-
042
      lization of epistemic uncertainty, which arises from incomplete knowledge about the environment.
043
      Traditional methods like Thompson Sampling (TS) (Thompson, 1933) provide a principled approach
044   to balancing exploration and exploitation by sampling from the posterior distribution over model
045   parameters. While TS achieves optimal regret bounds in simple settings with conjugate priors, it be-
046   comes computationally infeasible in high-dimensional environments involving complex models like
047   neural networks, where exact posterior updates and sampling are intractable (Russo et al., 2018).
048
      Approximate methods have been proposed to overcome these computational challenges. Random-
049
      ized Least Squares (RLS) (Osband et al., 2019) leverages neural function approximation but requires
050   retraining or resampling historical data at each decision step, leading to unbounded computational
051   complexity over time. Ensemble-based methods, such as Bootstrapped Thompson Sampling (Os-
052   band & Van Roy, 2015; Osband et al., 2016) and Ensemble+ (Osband et al., 2018; 2019), maintain
053   multiple models to approximate the posterior distribution and support incremental updates, avoiding
      the need for full retraining. However, these methods face significant limitations:

                                                        1

054
      • Computational Overhead: Maintaining multiple models increases computational and memory
055     costs, especially in high-dimensional settings.
056
      • Ensemble Coupling in Shared-Layer Architectures: To reduce computational load, ensemble
057
        members often share layers (Osband et al., 2016; 2018; 2019; Lee et al., 2021). This introduces
058     gradient coupling across ensembles during training, leading to ineffective exploration.
059
      These challenges highlight a critical gap in the literature: the need for scalable exploration meth-
060
      ods that can handle high-dimensional neural function approximation, maintain bounded per-step
061
      computational complexity, and achieve effective exploration by accurately estimating epistemic un-
062   certainty. In this paper, we propose Ensemble++, a novel method designed to address the challenges
063   of scalable exploration in high-dimensional environments through architectural and algorithmic in-
064   novations that enhance both computational efficiency and exploration diversity.
065
      Our main contributions are:
066
067   1. Scalable Exploration Algorithm Design: We introduce Ensemble++, which mitigates ensemble
068
         coupling by:
069      • Decoupled Optimization: Separating mean and variance estimation into distinct modules with
070        a symmetrized optimization objective, and employing a stop-gradient operator to enable inde-
071        pendent learning across ensemble components.
072      • Ensemble++ Sampling: Introducing richer exploration dynamics through linear combinations
073
           of ensemble components with carefully designed random weights.
074   2. Theoretical Analysis and Guarantees: We provide rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating
075      that Ensemble++ matches the regret bounds of exact Thompson Sampling under linear function
076
         approximation while achieving a scalable per-step computational complexity of O(d3 log T ).
         This exponentially improves over prior approximate Thompson Sampling methods (Xu et al.,
077
         2022; Qin et al., 2022), offering the first rigorous analysis showing ensemble sampling as a
078
         scalable and effective approximation to Thompson Sampling.
079
080
      3. Empirical Validation: In Figure 1, Section 6, and Appendix I.2, we empirically show that En-
         semble++ outperforms existing methods in both regret performance and computational efficiency
081
         across a range of nonlinear bandit environments, including language-input contextual bandits
082
         using a GPT backbone. We also perform extensive simulations in linear bandit environments
083      (Appendix I.1) to validate our theoretical guarantee.
084
085   By bridging the gap between computational efficiency and exploration quality, Ensemble++ offers
086   a robust framework for real-time adaptation in complex environments. Our method is particularly
087   suitable for applications where both computational and data collection budgets are constrained.

      2    BACKGROUND
116
117
      In this section, we formalize the sequential decision-making problem and introduce ensemble meth-
118   ods using standard notation.
119
120   Sequential Decision Making We consider a sequential decision-making problem over a discrete
121   time horizon T . At each time step t, the agent observes an action set At ⊆ A, selects an action
122   At ∈ At based on the history Ht = {A1 , A1 , Y1 , . . . , At−1 , At−1 , Yt−1 , At }, and receives a reward
123   Yt = f ∗ (At )+ϵt , where f ∗ is the unknown reward function and ϵt is zero-mean noise. The objective
                                                      PT
124   is to minimize the cumulative regret: R(T ) = t=1 (maxa∈At f ∗ (a) − f ∗ (At )).
125
126   Ensemble Sampling Ensemble sampling methods (Lu & Van Roy, 2017) , including Bootstrapped
127   DQN (Osband et al., 2016) and Ensemble+ (Osband et al., 2018; 2019), approximate the posterior
128   distribution of f ∗ by maintaining multiple models, each representing a hypothesis about f ∗ based
129   on historical data. An ensemble consists of M models with parameters θt = {θt,m }M     m=1 . At each
130   time step t, the agent samples an ensemble member mt uniformly from {1, 2, . . . , M } and selects an
131
      action: At = arg maxa∈At fθt,mt (a), where fθt,mt (a) is the prediction of ensemble member mt for
      action a. After observing the reward Yt , each ensemble member m updates its parameters θt+1,m
132
      by performing stochastic gradient descent on the loss starting from previous iterate θt,m :

137   Here, D = Ht and Zs,m are independent random perturbations added to encourage diversity among
138   ensemble members, and Ψ(θt+1,m ) is a regularization term. This perturbed training procedure en-
139   sures that each ensemble member captures different aspects of the uncertainty in f ∗ , representing
140   different plausible hypotheses consistent with the history. The random perturbations Zs,m are inde-
141   pendent across time index s and model index m. Once realized, Zs,m are fixed throughout the rest of
142
      the training, enabling incremental updates for real-time adaptation. This is a key computational fea-
      ture compared to methods like Randomized Least Squares (RLS) (Osband et al., 2019) or Perturbed

      History Exploration (PHE) (Kveton et al., 2020a). In RLS and PHE, fresh independent perturba-
144
      tions for all historical data are introduced at each time t, and the model requires full retraining from

148   3    E NSEMBLE C OUPLING AND S EQUENTIAL D EPENDENCY
149
150   Ensemble methods are powerful tools for approximating the posterior distribution of an unknown
151
      function f ∗ in sequential decision-making tasks. By maintaining multiple models, each providing
      independent estimates, ensembles help mitigate sequential dependency. However, when models
152
      are updated incrementally and ensemble members share layers, an ensemble coupling issue arises,
153
      leading to reduced ensemble diversity, reintroduction of sequential dependency, and, ultimately,
154   linear regret. In this section, we analyze this phenomenon and explain why it occurs.
155
156   Sequential Dependency in Incremental Updates In sequential decision-making, agents aim to
157   make optimal decisions under uncertainty by continuously updating their models based on new
158   observations and selecting actions accordingly. Incremental updates, such as those in recursive
159   methods, cause the parameters at time t to depend on those at time t − 1, creating a chain of depen-
160   dencies. This introduces sequential dependency, wherein the model parameters and action selection
161   become intertwined over time. In the context of Randomized Least Squares (RLS) (Osband et al.,
      2018; 2019), fresh independent perturbations at each time step are crucial to ensure that the sampled


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      parameters match the posterior distribution. Naive recursive implementations introduce sequential
163   dependency because the action at time t depends on past perturbations through the updated pa-
164   rameters, violating the independence required for accurate posterior approximation, i.e., epistemic
165   uncertainty estimation. A detailed analysis of this issue is provided in Appendix B.2.
166
167   Ensemble Methods and the Problem of Coupling To address sequential dependency while al-
168   lowing incremental updates, ensemble methods like Ensemble Sampling (ES) have been proposed
169   (Osband & Van Roy, 2015; Osband et al., 2016; Lu & Van Roy, 2017). ES maintains multiple inde-
170   pendent models, each with its own perturbations, and selects actions by randomly choosing a model
171   at each time step. This approach reduces sequential dependency over time by decoupling model
172   updates from action selection. Connections between RLS, Recursive RLS (RRLS), and ensemble
173
      methods are discussed in Appendix B.3.
174   However, when ensemble members share layers—a common practice in neural network implemen-
175   tations due to computational efficiency—an ensemble coupling issue emerges. In shared-layer en-
176   sembles, ensemble members share the hidden representations but have separate output layers. The
177   shared parameters are updated based on the aggregated gradients from all ensemble members, which
178   leads to several problems. First, gradient interference occurs because the random perturbations in-
179
      troduced to encourage diversity cause each ensemble member to have different loss landscapes.
      Their gradients with respect to the shared parameters may point in conflicting directions, resulting
180
      in destructive interference during updates. This interference hampers the effective learning of shared
181
      features that support diverse hypotheses about f ∗ . Second, there is a homogenization of ensemble
182   members. Since all ensemble members share the same feature extractor, the hidden representations
183   become similar across members. The shared feature extractor tends to capture common patterns
184   favored by the majority, reducing the uniqueness of individual ensemble members’ internal repre-
185   sentations. Consequently, the ensemble members produce correlated predictions, leading to reduced
186   ensemble diversity. The variance among ensemble outputs decreases, undermining the ensemble’s
187   ability to represent uncertainty effectively. This homogenization reintroduces sequential dependency
188   because the shared parameters w depend on the entire history of actions and observations, creating
189   a feedback loop between the agent’s actions and the model’s parameters. Detailed mathematical
190
      analysis of this phenomenon is provided in Appendix B.4.
191
192
      Ineffective Exploration and Linear Regret The reduced diversity and reintroduction of sequen-
      tial dependency have significant impacts on the agent’s performance. The ensemble fails to represent
193
      a wide range of plausible hypotheses about f ∗ , leading to ineffective exploration of the action space.
194
      As ensemble members suggest similar actions, the agent tends to exploit known actions and fails
195   to discover better ones. This results in persistent suboptimal decisions. Moreover, the cumula-
196   tive regret R(T ) grows linearly with time T because the agent consistently misses opportunities
197   to find better actions through exploration. This linear regret indicates that the agent’s performance
198   does not improve adequately over time, which is undesirable in sequential decision-making tasks
199   that require learning from experience. Experiments demonstrate that shared-layer ensembles exhibit

      4       M ETHODS
225

      In this section, we introduce Ensemble++, a novel method designed to address the challenges of
227   ensemble coupling and sequential dependency in shared-layer ensembles.
228
229   Key Design Principles Ensemble++ is built upon the following key design principles: 1. De-
230   coupling mean and uncertainty estimation by separating the base network (mean estimator) and
231   ensemble components (uncertainty estimators). 2. Introducing symmetric auxiliary variables al-
232   lows the training objective to decouple into separate loss terms for the base network and ensemble
233   components, facilitating independent learning. 3. Applying the stop-gradient operator to the shared
234   representation ensures that gradients from the ensemble components do not affect the shared layers,
235   further preventing coupling. 4. Using continuous index vectors for random linear combinations of
236   ensemble components enables richer exploration dynamics without increasing computational costs.
237
238   Architecture As illustrated in Figure 2 (c), the architecture of Ensemble++ consists of a shared
239
      feature extractor, a base network for mean estimation, and ensemble components for capturing un-
      certainty. Let x ∈ X denote the input, and h(x; w) be the shared feature extractor parameterized
240
      by w. The extracted features are denoted by x̃ = h(x; w). The base network ψ(x̃; b), parameter-
241
      ized by b, estimates the mean prediction based on the shared features. The ensemble components
242   {ψ(sg(x̃); θm )}Mm=1 , parameterized by θm , is designed to capture the uncertainty in the prediction.
243   The stop-gradient operator sg(·) prevents gradients from flowing through x̃ when computing gra-
244   dients with respect to θm , effectively decoupling the ensemble components from the shared layers.
245   Put all together, θ = {w, b, θ1 , . . . , θM } are the model parameters1 .

      Technical Innovation The primary technical challenge stems from the sequential dependence be-
368
      tween the random perturbation vectors and the high-dimensional random variables in the decision-
369
      making process. To overcome this, we introduce two key innovations: (1) a variance-aware dis-

      influences the reward associated with an action. Formally, at each time step t, the agent observes a
440
      context Xt ∈ X and selects an action At ∈ At (Xt ), where the action set At (Xt ) ⊆ A may depend
441
      on the observed context, i.e., At = A(Xt ). It has wide applications ranging from recommendation
442   systems to online content moderation as discussed in Appendix H. We conduct comprehensive ex-
443   periments on nonlinear contextual bandits to validate our main theoretical insights. Additionally, we
444   provide detailed findings on linear contextual bandits in Appendix I.1.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
507
508
      In this work, we presented Ensemble++, a novel method designed to address the challenges of
509
      scalable exploration in high-dimensional sequential decision-making tasks. By introducing archi-
510   tectural and algorithmic innovations—including the decoupling of mean and uncertainty estimation
511   via a symmetrized loss function, the use of the stop-gradient operator to prevent gradient coupling,
512   and enhanced exploration through continuous index sampling—Ensemble++ effectively mitigates
513   the ensemble coupling issue inherent in shared-layer ensembles.
514
      Our theoretical analysis demonstrates that Ensemble++ achieves regret bounds comparable to ex-
515
      act Thompson Sampling under linear function approximation, while maintaining a scalable per-step
516   computational complexity of O(d3 log T ). This establishes Ensemble++ as the first approximate
517   Thompson Sampling method to offer both provable scalability and near-optimal regret across vari-
518   ous decision set configurations. These theoretical results validate the effectiveness of our algorithmic
519   design schemes, including the separation of heads, the symmetrized loss function, and the continu-
520   ous index sampling.
521
      Empirical evaluations on a diverse set of nonlinear bandit tasks, including language-input contextual
522   bandits using a GPT backbone, confirm the practical efficacy of Ensemble++. Ensemble++ consis-
523   tently outperforms state-of-the-art methods such as Ensemble+, EpiNet, and LMC-TS, achieving
524   superior regret performance and computational efficiency.
525
526   Future Directions While Ensemble++ shows significant promise, several avenues for future re-
527   search remain. Extending Ensemble++ to more complex environments, such as reinforcement learn-
528   ing with delayed rewards, multi-agent systems, and continuous action spaces, could further demon-
529   strate its versatility. Investigating alternative index distributions and sampling strategies may yield
530   additional improvements in exploration efficiency and theoretical guarantees. Moreover, integrating
531   Ensemble++ with modern foundation models like large language models opens up possibilities for
532
      scalable exploration, multi-step reasoning, and planning in even more complex domains.
533   In summary, Ensemble++ represents a significant advancement in ensemble-based uncertainty esti-
534   mation for sequential decision-making, providing scalable exploration suitable for real-time appli-
535   cations.

      A     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
865
866
      Thompson Sampling (TS) (Thompson, 1933) is a classical method renowned for achieving near-
867   optimal regret bounds in sequential decision-making tasks by leveraging Bayesian principles (Russo
868   et al., 2018). TS samples from the posterior distribution over model parameters to balance explo-
869   ration and exploitation. However, in high-dimensional settings involving complex models like neu-
870   ral networks, maintaining and sampling from exact posterior distributions becomes computationally
871   infeasible due to the intractability of posterior updates (Osband et al., 2019).
872
      To address these computational challenges, approximate Bayesian methods have been developed.
873   Randomized Least Squares (RLS) (Osband et al., 2019) approximates the posterior by adding ran-
874   dom perturbations to least squares estimates. While effective in simple settings, RLS requires re-
875   training or resampling historical data at each decision step, leading to unbounded computational
876   complexity over time. Perturbed History Exploration (PHE) (Kveton et al., 2020a) introduces per-
877   turbations to historical data, scaling linearly with decision time horizons, but it is limited to linear
878   bandit settings and does not generalize well to neural network-based environments. Langevin Monte
879   Carlo Thompson Sampling (LMC-TS) (Xu et al., 2022) incrementally updates posterior approxima-
880   tions using stochastic gradient Langevin dynamics but incurs growing computational costs due to its
881
      gradient update schedule, making it less practical in long-horizon tasks.
882   Ensemble-based methods approximate posterior distributions by maintaining multiple models, each
883   representing a plausible hypothesis about the environment. Bootstrapped Thompson Sampling (Os-
884   band et al., 2016) and Ensemble+ (Osband et al., 2018) support incremental updates and avoid
885   the computational overhead of full retraining required by RLS. Ensemble sampling methods have
886   been applied across various domains, including recommendation systems (Lu et al., 2018; Zhu &
887
      Van Roy, 2023), robotics (Lee et al., 2021), and large language models (Dwaracherla et al., 2024).
      However, they face significant challenges. Maintaining multiple independent models increases com-
888
      putational and memory costs, especially in high-dimensional settings. To reduce computational load,
889
      ensemble members often share layers, but this introduces ensemble coupling during training, lead-
890   ing to homogenization of ensemble members, reduced diversity, and ineffective exploration. EpiNet
891   (Osband et al., 2023) introduces an MLP that takes a hidden representation and random noise as
892   input to estimate epistemic uncertainty. While innovative, EpiNet lacks theoretical grounding and
893   suffers from large model parameter sizes due to its architecture, discussed in Appendix J. Empir-
894   ical studies have shown that it demonstrates suboptimal exploration performance in practice (Li
895   et al., 2024). Despite these advances, key challenges remain. Existing methods struggle to main-
896   tain bounded computational complexity while performing effective exploration in high-dimensional
897   neural function approximation.
898   Ensemble++ addresses these challenges by introducing architectural and algorithmic innovations
899   and achieves scalable exploration with theoretical guarantees, closing a long-standing gap in scalable
900   exploration methods.

1102   B.4.2   I MPACT ON E NSEMBLE D IVERSITY
1103
1104   Updating the shared parameters w using gradients aggregated from all ensemble members introduces
1105   several issues:
1106
1107   • Gradient Interference: The random perturbations Zs,m lead to ensemble members having dif-
1108     ferent loss landscapes. As a result, their gradients with respect to w may point in conflicting
1109
         directions, causing destructive interference during updates. This interference hampers the effec-
         tive learning of shared features that could support diverse hypotheses.
1110
1111   • Homogenization of Features: Since all ensemble members share w, the feature representations
1112     x̃s become similar across members. The shared feature extractor tends to capture the common
1113     patterns favored by the majority of ensemble members, reducing the uniqueness of individual
1114     members’ internal representations.
1115   • Reduced Diversity in Predictions: With similar features and coupled updates, ensemble mem-
1116     bers produce correlated predictions. The variance among ensemble outputs decreases, which un-
1117     dermines the ensemble’s ability to represent uncertainty about f ∗ .
1118
1119   B.4.3   R EINTRODUCTION OF S EQUENTIAL D EPENDENCY
1120
1121   Ensemble coupling through shared parameters w reintroduces sequential dependency for the fol-
1122
       lowing reasons:
1123
1124
       • Dependence on Entire History: The shared parameters w are updated using all past data
         {(As , Ys )}ts=1 and perturbations {Zs,m }. Consequently, w becomes a function of the entire train-
1125
         ing history and the randomness injected into all ensemble members.
1126
1127   • Feedback Loop in Action Selection: At time t, action At is selected based on the current en-
1128     semble predictions, which depend on w. Since w encapsulates information from past actions
1129     and perturbations, the action selection process becomes entangled with historical data, creating a
1130
         feedback loop.
1131   • Violation of Independence Assumptions: The independence between the agent’s intrinsic ran-
1132     domness (e.g., from ensemble perturbations) and the history Ht is crucial for accurate posterior
1133     approximation and uncertainty estimation. Ensemble coupling violates this independence, leading
         to biased estimates and compromised exploration strategies.

       B.4.4                           C ONSEQUENCES ON E XPLORATION AND R EGRET
1135
1136   The reintroduction of sequential dependency and reduction in ensemble diversity have significant
1137   impacts:
1138
1139   • Ineffective Exploration: With reduced diversity, ensemble members tend to suggest similar ac-
1140     tions. The agent explores a narrower region of the action space, diminishing the likelihood of
1141
         discovering optimal or near-optimal actions.
1142   • Suboptimal Uncertainty Estimation: The lack of independent hypotheses leads to overconfi-
1143     dent predictions. The agent underestimates the uncertainty in f ∗ , affecting the balance between
1144     exploration and exploitation.
1145   • Linear Regret Growth: Ineffective exploration results in the cumulative regret R(T ) growing
1146     linearly with time T . The agent consistently fails to improve its policy by not adequately exploring
1147     less-known actions, leading to poor long-term performance.
1148
1149   Empirical studies confirm that shared-layer ensembles exhibit linear regret in problems requiring
1150
       exploration, such as contextual bandits and reinforcement learning tasks.

1678   We start by providing a general analytical framework for agent, potentially randomized, operating
1679   in the generic bandit environments. Let us introduce a few necessary definitions to facilitate the
1680   understanding and analysis. The confidence bound is used for uncertainty estimation over the ture
1681   function f ∗ given the history Ht .
1682   Definition 6 (Confidence bounds). Confidence bounds are a sequence of real-valued Ht -
1683   measurable functions Lt (·) and Ut (·) for t ∈ [T ] such that, w.p. at least 1 − δ, the joint event
1684   E = ∩t∈[T ] Et holds, where Et := {f ∗ (a) ∈ [Lt (a), Ut (a)] , ∀a ∈ At }.
1685
1686   The agent may not perform well unless it is well-behaved, defined by reasonableness and optimism.
1687   Intuitively, an agent that explores too much or too little will incur a high regret. Reasonableness and
1688
       optimism are the mechanisms for controlling these potential flaws respectively.
1689   Definition 7 (Reasonableness). Given confidence bounds Lt (·) and Ut (·) for t ∈ [T ], an (random-
1690   ized) agent is called reasonable if it produces a sequence of functions (f˜t (·), t ∈ [T ]) such that w.p.
1691   at least 1 − δ, the joint event Ẽ = ∩t∈[T ] Ẽt holds, where Ẽt := {f˜t (a) ∈ [Lt (a), Ut (a)] , ∀a ∈ At }.
1692
1693   In short, reasonableness ensures that the chosen action according to f˜t is close to the best action
1694   which ensures agent does not explore actions unnecessarily. The following optimism guarantees the
1695   agent sufficient explores.
1696   Definition 8 (p-optimism). Let p be a sequence of positive real number (pt , t ∈ [T ]). We say an
1697   (randomized) agent is p-optimistic when it produces a sequence of functions (f˜t (·), t ∈ [T ]) such
1698   that for all t ∈ [T ], f˜t (·) is pt -optimistic, i.e., P(maxa∈At f˜t (a) ≥ maxa∈At f ∗ (a) | Ht ) ≥ pt .
1699
1700   The generic agent satisfying the conditions on reasonableness and optimism has desired behavior.

2611
                     over the unit ball. This environment is utilized as the testbed in Zhou et al. (2020); Xu et al.

2616
                     dataset, we construct context vectors for N arms, such as a(1) = (x, 0, · · · , 0), · · · , a(N ) =
2617                 (0, 0, · · · , x) ∈ RN d . Only the arm a(j) where j matches the correct class of this data x
2618
                     has a reward of 1, while all other arms have a reward of 0.

       In all environments, the original reward r is disrupted by Gaussian noise ϵ drawn from N (0, 0.1).

       In this section, we provide a detailed comparison between Ensemble++ and EpiNet (Osband et al.,
2757   2023), focusing on architectural differences, parameter counts, and computational efficiency.
2758
2759
       J.1    OVERVIEW OF E PI N ET A RCHITECTURE
2760
2761   EpiNet is designed to estimate epistemic uncertainty in neural networks by incorporating an epis-
2762   temic index z ∈ RDz into the model. The EpiNet architecture consists of a base network µζ and an
2763   epinet σηL , which together produce predictions that account for uncertainty.
2764
2765
       The epinet σηL is typically implemented as a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with Glorot initialization:
2766
2767                                    σηL (x̃, z) := mlpη ([x̃, z])⊤ z ∈ RC ,
2768
2769   where:
2770
       • mlpη is an MLP parameterized by η with output dimensions RDz ×C .
2771
2772   • x̃ is the shared feature representation of the input x.
2773   • [x̃, z] denotes the concatenation of x̃ and the epistemic index z.
2774   • C is the number of output classes.
2775
2776   The final prediction of EpiNet is given by combining the base network and the epinet:
2777
2778
                                   fEpiNet (x, z) = µζ (x) + σηL (x̃, z) + σ P (x̃, z),
2779
2780   where σ P is a fixed prior function that introduces additional uncertainty modeling.
2781
2782
       J.2    PARAMETER C OUNT C OMPARISON
2783
2784   We analyze the parameter counts of both EpiNet and Ensemble++ to highlight the differences in
2785   computational efficiency.
2786
       Consider the following components in EpiNet:
2787
2788   • Base Network µζ : A shared feature extractor with input dimension Din , output dimension D, and
2789     parameters θµ .
2790
       • Epinet σηL : An MLP that takes [x̃, z] as input and outputs RDz ×C .
2791
2792   • Fixed Prior σ P : An ensemble of Dz small MLPs, each producing logits for the C classes.
2793
2794   The parameter count for the epinet σηL is:
2795                              ParamsσηL = (D + Dz ) × H + H × (Dz × C),
2796
2797   where H is the hidden layer width of the epinet MLP.

       The fixed prior σ P consists of Dz small MLPs. If each MLP has parameters ParamsσMLP

                                                                 P .

2802

2803
2804                               Total ParamsEpiNet = ParamsσηL + ParamsσP .

2807
       • Base Network ψ(x̃; b): Similar to the base network in EpiNet, with parameters b.

2842   J.3   C OMPUTATIONAL E FFICIENCY
2843
       Ensemble++ offers computational advantages over EpiNet:
2844
2845
       • Simpler Ensemble Components: Ensemble++ uses linear layers for ensemble components, lead-
2846
         ing to faster computations during both forward and backward passes.
2847
2848   • Stop-Gradient Operator: By applying the stop-gradient operator to the shared features in En-
2849     semble++, we reduce the computational cost of backpropagation, as gradients do not flow through
2850     the shared layers from the ensemble components.
2851   • No Additional Inputs: Ensemble++ does not concatenate the epistemic index with the shared
2852     features, avoiding the increase in input dimensionality that occurs in EpiNet.
2853
2854   In contrast, EpiNet’s architecture introduces additional computational costs due to:
2855
2856   • Higher Input Dimensionality: Concatenating z with x̃ increases the input dimension of the
2857     epinet MLP from D to D + M .
2858
       • Complex MLP Structure: The epinet MLP with hidden layers and larger output dimensions
2859
         requires more computations, both in the forward pass and during backpropagation.
2860
2861   • Fixed Prior Computations: The fixed prior σ P involves additional MLPs, adding to the compu-
         tational burden.

       J.4   P RACTICAL I MPLICATIONS
2863
2864   The architectural simplicity of Ensemble++ translates to practical benefits:
2865
2866   • Faster Training and Inference: Fewer parameters and simpler computations lead to faster model
2867     updates and predictions.
2868   • Scalability: Ensemble++ scales better to larger ensemble sizes M and higher feature dimensions
2869     D, making it suitable for high-dimensional tasks.
2870   • Effective Exploration: Despite its simplicity, Ensemble++ effectively captures epistemic uncer-
2871     tainty and facilitates efficient exploration, as demonstrated in our empirical evaluations.
2872
2873   In contrast, EpiNet’s more complex architecture may hinder its performance in practice:
2874
2875   • Increased Computational Load: The larger parameter count and complex computations can
2876     slow down training and inference, especially in resource-constrained environments.
2877   • Diminished Performance: Empirical studies have shown that EpiNet may demonstrate subop-
2878     timal exploration performance in practice (Li et al., 2024), potentially due to the challenges in
