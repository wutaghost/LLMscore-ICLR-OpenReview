                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Wasserstein distance is a key metric for quantifying data divergence from a distri-
010
               butional perspective. However, its application in privacy-sensitive environments,
011            where direct sharing of raw data is prohibited, presents significant challenges. Exist-
012            ing approaches, such as Differential Privacy and Federated Optimization, have been
013            employed to estimate the Wasserstein distance under such constraints. However,
014            these methods often fall short when both accuracy and security are required. In this
015            study, we explore the inherent triangular properties within the Wasserstein space,
016            leading to a novel solution named TriangleWad. This approach facilitates the
017            fast computation of the Wasserstein distance between datasets stored across dif-
018            ferent entities, ensuring that raw data remain completely hidden. TriangleWad not
019
               only strengthens resistance to potential attacks but also preserves high estimation
               accuracy. Through extensive experiments across various tasks involving both image
020
               and text data, we demonstrate its superior performance and significant potential for
021
               real-world applications.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Optimal Transport (OT) is one of the representative approaches that provides a geometric view
027   that places a distance on the space of probability measures Villani et al. (2009). Specifically, it
028   aims to find a coupling matrix that moves the source data to the target data with smallest cost,
029   thereby inducing the Wasserstein distance, a metric used to measure the divergence between two
030   distributions. Due to its favorable analytical properties, such as computational tractability and the
031   ability to be computed from finite samples, the Wasserstein distance has been applied in various
032   domains, including document similarity measurement Kusner et al. (2015), domain adaption Courty
033
      et al. (2016; 2017), geometric measurement between labelled data Alvarez-Melis & Fusi (2020),
      generative adversarial networks Arjovsky et al. (2017), dataset valuation and selection Just et al.
034
      (2023); Kang et al. (2024).
035
036   However, calculating the Wasserstein distance often requires access to raw data, which restricts its
037   use in privacy-sensitive environments. In Federated Learning (FL), for instance, multiple parties
038   collaboratively train a model without sharing raw data, while their data are usually non-independently
039
      and identically distributed (Non-IID) Li et al. (2022). In this context, the Wasserstein distance can
      be used to measure data heterogeneity, cluster clients with similar distributions, filter out out-of-
040
      distribution data, and ultimately improve FL model performance. However, since raw data cannot
041
      be accessed in the FL setting, direct computation of the Wasserstein distance becomes infeasible.
042   Similarly, in a data marketplace, buyers seek to acquire training data from multiple sellers to build
043   models for specific predictive tasks. However, sellers are often reluctant to grant access to their
044   data prior to transactions due to the risk of data being copied, while buyers are hesitant to make
045   purchases without first assessing the data’s value, quality, and relevance Lu et al. (2024). In this case,
046   a promising approach to aligning the interests of data sellers and buyers is to compute the Wasserstein
047   distance between datasets in a privacy-preserving manner.
048   Recently, FedWad Rakotomamonjy et al. (2024) takes the first step to approximate the Wasserstein
049   distance between two parties via triangle inequality. However, its applicability is limited to scenarios
050   involving only two parties, making it unsuitable for data marketplaces with multiple data sellers, where
051   the Wasserstein distance between aggregated training data (from multiple sellers) and validation data
052   (held by the buyer) is required. Moreover, privacy concerns arise due to the shared information used
053   to facilitate FedWad calculations, which unintentionally exposes raw images from both parties. By
      exploiting optimization conditions, it is even possible to reconstruct “clean’ images from the shared


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      data. Privacy risks are even more pronounced when dealing with textual data, as shared information
055   in the embedding space can reveal most of the original raw words. All of the aforementioned risks
056   are undesirable for high-sensitive parties and make FedWad unacceptable in real-world applications.
057   FedBary Li et al. (2024b) extends the previous work and addresses the task of noisy data detection
058   based on shared information, but it suffers from an asymmetry in detection capabilities: only clients
059   in FL or sellers in the data marketplace know exactly which data points are noisy, while the server in
060   FL or data buyers lack this information. This asymmetry becomes particularly problematic when data
061   sellers or clients are not trusted. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a privacy-enhanced approach
062   to Wasserstein distance computation that ensures efficiency, accuracy, and symmetry in detection
063
      capabilities, especially in settings involving sensitive data and multiple parties.
064   This paper aims to develop a faster and more secure method for approximating the Wasserstein
065   distance without sacrificing much accuracy. Our approach is based on geometric intuition derived
066   from the intercept theorem associated with geodesics: by constructing two similar triangles, we
067   establish a proportional relationship between their corresponding sides. This enables the direct
068   approximation of the Wasserstein distance between two data distributions through the distance of
069
      their parallel segments. With our approach, accurate estimation can be achieved in just one round
      of interaction, significantly reducing computational costs. Moreover, as we reduce the interactions
070
      and change the optimization condition, our approach mitigates the privacy concerns associated with
071
      previous methods, which will be discussed in detail later. Thanks to its scalability, efficiency, and
072   effectiveness, this solution addresses various real-world challenges. These include calculating client
073   contributions in FL, performing clustering in FL, filtering out corrupt data points before training,
074   assessing data relevance in data marketplaces, and any other privacy-sensitive contexts that require
075   measuring distributional similarity.
076
      Our major contributions: (1) We conduct a comprehensive theoretical analysis of geometric
077
      properties within the Wasserstein space, design a distributional attack against FedWad and introduce a
078   novel approach, TriangleWad; (2) TriangleWad is simple, fast, accurate and enhances privacy.
079   It also significantly improves the detection of noisy data from the server side in FL, better aligning
080   with real-world requirements; (3) We conduct extensive experiments on both image and text datasets,
081   covering a range of applications such as data evaluation, noisy data detection, and word movers
082   distance, demonstrating its strong generalization capabilities.

086   2.1   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Private Wasserstein Distance There are very few efforts to provide privacy guarantees for computing
089   Wasserstein distance when raw data is forbidden to be shared. The first attempt is to apply Differential
090   Privacy (DP) Lê Tien et al. (2019) with Johnson-Lindenstrauss transform. However, this approach
091   is used for domain adaptation tasks, where only the source distribution is perturbed while the
092   target distribution remains unchanged. Additionally, it does not have geometric property, and has
093
      inaccurate estimation empirically. The following work Rakotomamonjy & Liva (2021) considers DP
      for Sliced Wasserstein distance, and Jin & Chen (2022) uses DP for graph embeddings. Recently
094
      proposed FedWad Rakotomamonjy et al. (2024) develops a Federated way to approximate distance
095
      iteratively based on geodesics and interpolating measures, and FedBary Li et al. (2024b) extends this
096   approach to approximate data valuation and Wasserstein barycenter, which could further be used for
097   distributionally robust training Li et al. (2024a). It is worthy to note that one latest work Wasserstein
098   Differential Privacy Yang et al. (2024) focuses computing privacy budgets through Wasserstein
099   distance, which is not related to our applications.
100
      Data Evaluation in FL and Data Marketplace Data quality valuation has gained more attentions
101
      in recent years since it has impact on the trained models and downstream tasks. Due to privacy
102   issue, e.g. Federated Learning, only model gradients are shared for evaluation. Therefore, Shapley
103   value (SV) Song et al. (2019); Jia et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2021a;b) is mainly
104   used to measure client contributions as it provides marginal contribution score. Recently, based
105   on Rakotomamonjy et al. (2024), FedBary Li et al. (2024b) uses Wasserstein distance to measure
106   dataset divergence as the score of client contribution, and it leverages sensitivity analysis to further
107   select valuable data points. In this paper, we focus on the horizontal FL, where clients’ data shares
      the same feature space. Data evaluation with privacy guarantees is also applied in data marketplaces,

166   Our goal is to compute the Wasserstein distance among different datasets distributed on separate
167   parties, with the constraint that raw data is not shared. Without loss of generality, we start with the
168   case to calculate the Wasserstein distance between two measures, which can be easily extended to
169   measuring the divergence among multiple measures. We consider the 2-Wasserstein distance W2 (·, ·)
170   in this paper, while our proposed approach can be generalized to other p cases.

382   4.2     P RIVACY A NALYSIS
383
384   In this section, we will discuss two privacy benefits of our proposed approach.
385
      (1) Sec 4.2.1: Attackers lack important pieces of information to infer raw data: Attackers can
386
      only infer raw data µ when they know ηµ (t0 ), the OT plan π(µ, γ) and the value of push-forward
387   parameter t0 , while the later two terms are kept private. And approximating the OT plan is an NP-hard
388   problem; Compared to the previous approach, our approach involves only one round of interaction,
389   which limits the available common information and hinders any attempts at approximation.
390
      (2) Sec 4.2.2:Setting a large t0 could help protect privacy: from a geometric perspective, it controls
391
      how much the interpolating measure is pushed closer to random Gaussian noise. From a statistical
392
      perspective, it introduces more substantial noise to the raw data. Consequently, a larger t0 results in a
393   greater Wasserstein distance between ηµ (t0 ) and µ, indicating higher dissimilarity between them.
394
395   4.2.1    D EFENSE TO ATTACKS
396
397   Traditional general attacks cannot be directly applied in our setting, as our approach does not involve
398   any model training, and the shared information is insufficient to train a model. We consider two types
399   of attack tailored to this research from both geometric and statistical views. Suppose an attacker tries
400
      to infer µ̂ based on available information, there are two potential attacks:
401   (1) Distributional attack: Whether W2 (µ̂, µ) < ϵ for a very small ϵ?
402   (2) Reconstruction attack: Whether ∥µ̂ − µ∥2 < ϵ for a very small ϵ?
403
      The first attack is the distributional attack designed for FedWad. TriangleWad does not calculate
404   any interpolating measure between µ and ν, so the attacker can not use available information to
405   identify the distribution of the raw data. For the second attack, the attacker knows the structure
406                                                                    1
      of equation 5, ηµ ,γ. The attacker might approximate µ̂ = 1−t      (ηµ − tγ) while the groundtruth is
407     1
      1−t0 (ηµ −t0 π(µ, γ)γ). In the worst case that t0 becomes public information, it is also challenging for
408
      the attacker to reconstruct raw data, as private information π(µ, γ) has m + k − 1 non-zero elements
409   with non-identical value, which is impossible to exactly approximate. Therefore, without knowing the
410   exact value of both t0 and π(µ, γ), µ̂ and µ will have a significant gap in both the Euclidean distance
411   and Wasserstein distance, making the attack fail. We visualize this result in Figure 2 (lower right
412   panel) in the experiments, and find each element in ηµ (Local IM) and µ̂ (Attack) are uninformative.
