010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            The success of visual instruction tuning has accelerated the development of large
013            language and vision models (LLVMs). Following the scaling laws of instruction-
014            tuned large language models (LLMs), LLVMs have also further increased in size,
015
               with examples including 26B, 34B, and even 80B parameters. While this increase
               in model size has yielded significant performance gains, it demands substantially
016
               more hardware resources for both training and inference. Consequently, there
017
               naturally exists a strong need for efficient LLVMs that achieve the performance
018            of larger models while being smaller in size. To achieve this need, we present
019            a new efficient LLVM family with model sizes of 0.5B, 1.8B, 3.8B, and 7B pa-
020            rameters,     Phantom, which significantly enhances learning capabilities within
021            limited structures. By temporarily increasing the latent hidden dimension dur-
022            ing multi-head self-attention (MHSA), we make LLVMs understand much more
023            vision-language knowledge on the latent, without substantially increasing physi-
024            cal model sizes. To maximize its advantage, we introduce Phantom Optimization
025            (PO) using both autoregressive supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and direct preference
026
               optimization (DPO)-like concept, which effectively follows correct answers while
               eliminating incorrect and ambiguous ones.       Phantom outperforms numerous
027
               larger open- and closed-source LLVMs, positioning itself as a leading solution in
028
               the landscape of efficient LLVMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, artificial general intelligence (AGI) has increasingly become a part of daily life,
034
      significantly enhancing our convenience. This trend is largely attributed to technical advancements
035
      of large language models (LLMs) and their impressive generalization performance, facilitated by
036   instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022). Building on this momentum, instruction
037   tuning has expanded its realm into visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023c), integrating both
038   language and vision as a format of text and image, under the use of pretrained LLMs. Based on them,
039   numerous large language and vision models (LLVMs) have continuously emerged as multimodal
040   LLMs and they have shown outstanding vision-language performances.
041
      In terms of open-to-public regarding model architectures and their trained parameters, LLVMs can
042   be categorized into open-source and closed-source models. For example, there are representative
043   closed ones: GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini-Pro (Team et al., 2023), and Qwen-VL-Plus (Bai
044   et al., 2023a;b), all of which are renowned for their remarkable vision-language performances, large
045   model sizes, and extensive number of dataset samples. In response, open-source LLVMs have tried
046   to narrow the performance gap with their closed-source performances, by following the similar
047   strategies the closed ones used, such as scaling up model sizes (Liu et al., 2024a; McKinzie et al.,
048   2024; Li et al., 2024d) (e.g., 26B, 34B, and 80B) and curating larger number of visual instruction
049   tuning samples (Hu et al., 2024a; Fang et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024) (e.g., 4M, 6M, and 10M).
050   Along with them, several modules have focused on image-level understanding by leveraging numer-
051   ous types of vision encoders (Kar et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Goncharova et al., 2024; Ranzinger
052   et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024d) and multiple computer vision models (Chen et al.,
053   2024a; Wang et al., 2024c; Jiao et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024c;d). Additionally, a series of projectors
      have been employed alongside various vision encoders to improve fine-grained understanding (Li

      However, these efforts — summarized as (a) scaling up model size, (b) curating larger datasets, and
072
      (c) incorporating additional modules and projectors — may not be regarded as a primary key to ba-
073   sically improve their own learning capabilities of LLVMs. In other words, there remains unexplored
074   potential in fully utilizing LLVMs to align vision knowledge with language one and embed much
075   more vision-language knowledge within limited structures, without relying on external modules
076   and projectors. Beyond their limited learning capabilities, specifically, (a) and (b) bring in strik-
077   ing computational burdens during training, necessitating high-end GPUs with substantial VRAM.
078   This (a) more becomes a critical drawback in devices with limited GPU resources, such as mobile
079   phones and embedded boards. Furthermore, the high computational inference costs, associated with
080   larger model sizes, exacerbate these issues, particularly for real-time applications such as augmented
081
      reality (AR) systems. As a result, deploying and operating LLVMs in such resource-constrained on-
      device environments becomes a major challenge.
082
083   To meet the two needs of maintaining model sizes while achieving superior performance, we present
084   an efficient LLVM family,      Phantom, which stimulates enlarging vision-language learning capa-
085   bilities within limited structures. When conducting multi-head self-attention (MHSA),          Phan-
086   tom temporarily increases the latent hidden dimension and understand much more vision-language
087
      knowledge. Without significantly increasing the physical model size, we get an effect of increas-
      ing the dimension in query, key, and value, which we now call as Phantom Dimension. In order
088
      to maximally boost this advantage, we introduce Phantom Optimization (PO), inspired by RLHF
089
      and DPO (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2024;
090   Hong et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2024). Unlike traditional preference-based methods, PO is designed
091   to minimize the generation of incorrect and ambiguous answers. Since autoregressive supervised
092   fine-tuning (SFT) primarily focuses on producing correct answers, PO provides         Phantom with
093   additional guidance to avoid confusing answers by borrowing the recent DPO formulation (Meng
094   et al., 2024).
095
      To do so, we first need a collection of incorrect and ambiguous answers. These are generated and
096   filtered through GPT-4o(-mini) and human review from 2.8M visual instruction tuning samples cov-
097   ering diverse capabilities (details in Section 3). This process resulted in the curation of 2M Phantom
098   triples including question, its correct answer, and the corresponding incorrect and ambiguous an-
099   swers (see Appendix A). By using the triple,          Phantom is trained with the two training steps,
100   where we train vision projector and Phantom Dimension in the first step with the pretrained LLM
101   frozen. In the second step, all components are trained together. Notably, PO utilizes SFT together
102   with DPO-like concept throughout first training step, making          Phantom have an ability that fol-
103   lows correct answers while eliminating incorrect and ambiguous ones. In the experiment section,
104
      we demonstrate that handling the latent hidden dimension and using PO enhances vision-language
      performances by a large margin. As a result, we release an efficient LLVM family               Phantom
105
      with 0.5B, 1.8B, 3.8B, and 7B model sizes, which outperform open- and closed-source LLVMs,
106
      establishing a leading solution in the realm of efficient LLVMs.

      2       R ELATED W ORKS
131
132
      Large Language and Vision Models. To bridge the performance gap with closed-source LLVMs,
      open-source LLVMs have adopted three primary strategies: scaling up model size, curating larger
133
      datasets, and incorporating additional modules or projectors. For instance, LLaVA-NeXT (Liu et al.,
134
      2024a), MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024), Yi-VL (Young et al., 2024) and MiniGemini (Li et al., 2024d)
135   build model variants with parameters up to 34B. Concurrent to these efforts, mPLUG-Owl (Hu
136   et al., 2024a), VILA2 (Fang et al., 2024), and Cambrian-1 (Tong et al., 2024) curate high-quality
137   visual instruction tuning datasets specialized for diverse visual capabilities. Lastly, recent works
138   have leveraged various vision encoders (Kar et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Goncharova et al., 2024;
139   Ranzinger et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024d) and integrated external computer vision
140   modules (Chen et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024c; Jiao et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024c;d) to expand
141   LLVMs’ perception capabilities. Alongside using extra vision encoders, several works utilize pro-
142   jectors to extract hierarchical features of images (Li et al., 2024d; Tong et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024a;
143
      Chen et al., 2024c; Yao et al., 2024) or to improve real-world knowledge comprehension such as
      document analysis, chart interpretation, and mathematical reasoning (Lee et al., 2024b).
144
145   While these approaches enhance downstream task performance, they do not address the core chal-
146   lenge of improving the intrinsic learning capabilities of LLVMs. Scaling up model size or employing
147   larger instruction tuning datasets leads to substantial computational burdens. In addition, relying on
148   extra visual encoders or computer vision modules brings in external visual knowledge, but they
149
      mainly focus on visual perception-related capabilities and their additional parameters may also lead
      the burden. This underscores the need for developing more efficient LLVMs with enhanced inherent
150
      capabilities that do not depend on such resource-intensive strategies.
151
152
153
      Efficient Modeling. In an effort to enhance the fundamental capabilities of LLMs while maintain-
      ing model size, several works for natural language processing has increasingly focused on devel-
154
      oping smaller model sizes (Thawakar et al., 2024; Mehta et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c), network
155
      pruning (Ma et al., 2023; Men et al., 2024; Ashkboos et al., 2024), and quantization (Li et al.,
156   2023c; Shao et al., 2024a; Park et al., 2024a). These approaches primarily aim to accelerate training
157   speed and reduce inference time while retaining performance, rather than boosting performances or
158   improving LLVMs’ embedding capabilities of vision-language knowledge within the limited struc-
159   tures. While efficient modeling has been extensively explored for LLMs, the design of efficient
160   vision-language models (LLVMs) remains underexplored. A recent work, TroL (Lee et al., 2024a),
161   uniquely introduces a layer traversing technique that reuses layers in a token-wise manner to po-
      tentially embed more vision-language knowledge. However, it faces significant challenges, such as

180   In response to the need for efficient yet high-performing LLVMs, we introduce a new efficient LLVM
181   family,    Phantom, which enhances the embedding capability of vision-language knowledge by
182   temporarily increasing the latent hidden dimension during multi-head self-attention (MHSA). This

      avoiding confusion, is expected to pave the way for more efficient LLVMs in both training and
      inference and to represent a crucial first step in advancing the field.

188   3              P HANTOM

190   Existing LLVMs and         Phantom. Figure 3(a) depicts the commonly used training paradigm for
191   building LLVMs, where pretrained LLMs are fine-tuned to acquire visual understanding and handle
192   vision-language tasks using both text and image inputs. This approach directly modifies the orig-
193
      inal latent dimensions derived from the their parameters to accommodate the new vision-language
      capabilities. In contrast, Figure 3(b) introduces a new concept of expanding the latent dimension,
194
      which we call as Phantom Dimension, which trains only the added latent space without fine-tuning
195
      the entire pretrained LLMs. It enables for pretrained LLMs to have a room to embed the new knowl-
196   edge, which effectively integrates vision language knowledge without overwritting the parameters
197   and altering or compromising the original knowledge. In other words, Phantom Dimension injects
198   vision language knowledge to the pretrained-LLMs directly with the enlarged hidden dimension,
199   where it can be considered as another hidden room of internal model architectures. By leverag-
200   ing this approach, we build a new LLVM family of          Phantom and in order to extremely boost
201   its vision language performances, we curate incorrect and ambiguous answers and present a way
202   utilizing them, called as Phantom Optimization. Next, we will explain model architecture of
203   Phantom, and the realization of Phantom Dimension. In addition, the process of creating incorrect
204
      and ambiguous answers, as well as strategies for effectively utilizing them, will be discussed.
205
206   Overview of Model Architecture. As shown in Figure 4(a), the architecture of               Phantom
207
      model consists of vision encoder, vision projector, and a multimodal language model including
      word embedding and language model head, which follows a common configuration used in open-
208
      source LLVMs (Liu et al., 2023c;b; Bai et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023a; McKinzie et al., 2024).
209
      Specifically, we utilize InternViT-300M (Chen et al., 2023b) as the vision encoder instead of CLIP-
210   L-428M (Radford et al., 2021), due to its superior ability to align text-to-image representations
211   through contrastive learning with large language models (LLMs). The vision projector is constructed
212   using two fully connected layers, where GELU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) activation function
213   is interleaved with each layer. For multimodal LLM component, we initialize it using pretrained
214   LLMs across various sizes, selected for their state-of-the-art performance within their respective
215   size: Qwen2-0.5B (Yang et al., 2024), InternLM2-1.8B (Cai et al., 2024), Phi3-mini-3.8B Abdin
      et al. (2024), and InternLM2.5-7B (Cai et al., 2024).

242   Realization of Phantom Dimension. For better understanding, Figure 4(b) represents the simple
243   overview of how Phantom Dimension works. We utilize start of sequence (sos) token that will serve
244   as a key in enhancing the latent hidden dimension for the query, key, and value components in multi-
245   head self-attention (MHSA) layers. The latent feature on the location of sos token is propagated into
246   QKV linear function, and we denote its outputs as Q∗l ∈ Rdq , Kl∗ ∈ Rdkv , and Vl∗ ∈ Rdkv at each
247   layer l. Note that d denotes the latent hidden dimension. Q∗l , Kl∗ , and Vl∗ ∈ Rdkv are supposed to
248   inject into the multi-head cross-attention (MHCA) module. A natural question arises: Why inject
249   these features into the cross-attention module? The reason lies in the dynamic length N of user input
250
      tokens, which varies with the question length. Therefore, these features need to have dimension
      Q∗l ∈ RN ×dq , Kl∗ ∈ RN ×dkv , and Vl∗ ∈ RN ×dkv since sos token only represents a single token.
251

344   RLHF (Christiano et al., 2017) and DPO are designed to optimize towards human or AI-driven pref-
345   erences, PO is tailored to follow correct answer and reduce incorrect and ambiguous answers during
346   training. To reduce the computational complexity of incorporating an additional reference model,
347   we adopt the loss formulation from SimPO (Meng et al., 2024). Similar to ORPO (Hong et al.,
348   2024a), we simultaneously use autoregressive supervised fine-tuning (SFT). This enables      Phan-
349   tom to effectively reinforce correct answers y + while eliminating incorrect and ambiguous ones y −
350   in response to a given prompt x. This formulation can be expressed as follows:
351
                                                   β                     β

                                                                 +                  −
352            min LPO = LSFT − ED log σ                log πθ (y |x) − − log πθ (y |x) − γ ,         (4)
                 θ                               |y + |                |y |
353
354   where θ represents the trainable parameters and LSFT denotes the supervised fine-tuning loss for
355   question-answer pairs. Note that, D is a dataset for Phantom triples (x, y + , y − ) ∼ D, σ denotes
356   sigmoid function, β and γ means the hyper-parameter used in SimPO (Meng et al., 2024), where if
357   β increases, then the gap of the probability between positive answer and negative answer will be at
358
      large margin. In addition, γ is a empirical reward margin to make the training stable. We implement
      a two-step training strategy. In the first step, which focuses on vision and language alignment, the
359
      parameters of the pretrained LLM are frozen. We then train the parameters of vision projector and
360
      the components related to Phantom Dimension (MHCA and the functions f and g). In the second
361   step, we unfreeze all parameters and train them all at once. We apply PO throughout the first training
362   step only, not to interrupt multimodal LLM’s own text generation ability because the positive and
363   negative answers y + /y − are mostly generated by closed-source LLVMs instead of instruction fine-
364   tuned self model, which is totally different strategy from RLHF and DPO. For verification, we show
365   the performance degradation in experiment section when using PO in the second training step.

      RTX 3090 24GB GPUs,          Phantom’s training and inference processes take place. To conduct effi-
456
      cient training, each step undergoes a single epoch of training using 8-bit quantization and bfloat16
457   data format (Kalamkar et al., 2019) for every backbone multimodal LLM. Following bit quantiza-
458   tion, we apply QLoRA (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2023) to both vision encoders and backbone
459   multimodal LLMs across all linear layers, using 256 rank and 256 alpha parameters.
460
461   (d) For Phantom Optimization, we choose equal hyperparameters used in SimPO (Meng et al.,
462   2024): β = 2 and γ = 0.5. For training, AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) is applied,
463   and cosine annealing adjusts the learning rate from 1e-5 to 1e-6 throughout each training step. For
464   multimodal LLM, gradient checkpointing (Sohoni et al., 2019) is employed to manage memory
465   efficiently. A gradient accumulation of 4 leads to batch sizes totaling 128 for each training step, with
466   each step taking roughly two to five days depending on model size. For inference efficiency,
467   Phantom is validated using the same quantization level in training, and we make Phantom Dimension
468   cache: Q∗l , Kl∗ , and Vl∗ in each layer to get speedy inference like kv-cache technique, where we use
469
      deterministic beam search (Freitag & Al-Onaizan, 2017) (n = 3). Memory-efficient scaled dot
      product attention (SDPA) and FlashAttention2 (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023) accelerates multi-head
470
      self-attention (MHSA) computation for Phantom Dimension, benefiting from its hardware-aware
471
      ability to mitigate the overhead from the increased latent hidden dimension.

      Discussion and Limitation. The development of high-performing LLVMs increasingly depends
516   on combining diverse models (Lu et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024c;d;b; Zong et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
517   2024) and refining existing architectures (Liu et al., 2024c; Lee et al., 2024a), as many aspects of
518   these systems remain unexplored. However, such structural modifications often leads to substan-
519   tial low-level programming when addressing both development and production-level demands. In
520   response, we will do comprehensive exploration of significantly larger open-source LLVMs, with-
521   out additional architectural changes. Although there has been a growing trend toward open-source
522   LLVMs, much of the research continues to focus on closed-source LLVMs such as GPT-4V and
523   Gemini-Pro. We either had used GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o. Therefore, we believe there is untapped
524
      potential not only in utilizing the textual outputs of larger open-source LLVMs but also in accessing
      deeper insights, such as layer-wise features or full parameter sets across layers. Moving forward,
525
      we plan to investigate layer-wise distillation methods, which go beyond traditional distillation, to
526
      transfer knowledge into models with entirely different architectures using human-understandable
527   language. This direction promises to open up exciting possibilities in a more easier way to develop
528   efficient LLVMs, such as transferring knowledge across heterogeneous structures.

532
533
      We present an efficient LLVM family        Phantom with significantly enhanced learning capabilities
      within limited model sizes. By introducing Phantom Optimization (PO) that leverages both autore-
534
      gressive supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and DPO-like concept, it effectively learns and boosts vision-
535
      language performances. Remarkably, despite being smaller than many high-performing LLVMs
536   with larger model sizes,     Phantom demonstrates comparable or even superior performance, mak-
537   ing it a promising solution for resource-constrained environments. Our results underscore the power
538   of latent space optimization in boosting both efficiency and performance, offering a pathway toward
539   more efficient LLVMs for various applications.

           Question: ( 2 ^ 2 + 4 ^ 2 + 6 ^ 2 + . . . . . + 14 ^ 2 ) = ?

           Answer:      To find the sum of the squares of the even numbers from 2 to 14, we can calculate each square and then add them
978                     together.
979
                        Here are the squares of the even numbers from 2 to 14:
980
981                     2^2 = 4
982                     4^2 = 16
                        6^2 = 36
983                     8^2 = 64
984                     10^2 = 100
                        12^2 = 144
985                     14^2 = 196
986
987                     Now, let's add them together:

988                     4 + 16 + 36 + 64 + 100 + 144 + 196 = 560
989
                        So, the sum of the squares of the even numbers from 2 to 14 is 560..

992        Rejected: To find the sum of some numbers, we focus on the pattern of even numbers leading to the total. If we only consider
993                  the first few:
994                     2^2 = 4
995                     4^2 = 16
996                     6^2 = 36
                        8^2 = 64
997
998                     Now, instead of continuing, let’s just quickly check if this looks like it’s adding up correctly:
999                     4 + 16 + 36 + 64 = 120, but that might not include everything we need here.
