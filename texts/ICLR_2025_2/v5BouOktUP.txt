011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The analysis of multivariate time series (MTS) presents a complex yet crucial task
014            with substantial applications in areas such as weather forecasting, policy formu-
015            lation, and stock market prediction. It is important to highlight three key charac-
016            teristics of MTS that contribute to the challenging and multifaceted nature of their
017            analysis: (i) their interrelationships are represented through causal relationships
018
               rather than mere similarities; (ii) they convey information across multiple inde-
               pendent factors; and (iii) their dynamics often arise from inherent temporal depen-
019
               dencies. While conventional time series analysis frameworks often fail to capture
020
               one or more of these aspects, resulting in incomplete or even misleading conclu-
021            sions, we propose an end-to-end trainable Series Prediction model Augmented by
022            Causality Estimation (SPACE) to address these limitations. This model effectively
023            incorporates temporal dependencies and causal relationships, featuring a tempo-
024            ral embedding and a transfer entropy-based Cross-TE module designed to enhance
025            predictions through causality-augmented mechanisms. Experiments demonstrate
026            that SPACE achieves state-of-the-art results on challenging real-world time series
027            prediction tasks, showing its effectiveness and versatility. Code is available at
028            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SPACE-D448/.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Time series forecasting (TSF) is an inherently difficult problem. A large part of this is due to the
034   overall structural complexity of time series information. This is especially true for time series that
035   reflect real-world data, for example those that record usage statistics of the electrical grid, local
036   temperature variations over a specific time window, or market values of stocks. On the one hand,
037   it is understood that the diverse length scales and temporal dynamics underlying each of these sys-
038   tems is the main reason for their richness and insight; conversely, they are also the reason for the
039
      intractability of many real-world time series. In the examples listed above, the frequency and dis-
      tribution of electrical grid value fluctuations are greatly influenced by temporal and seasonal trends,
040
      sudden changes in demand (e.g., during a popular sporting event, when a large number of users tune
041
      in to the sports broadcast; or during an unexpected heat wave or cold snap), and the spatial location
042   and role of possible malfunctioning grid nodes, which affect supply to a subset of users. Similarly,
043   the changes in stock values also governed by a multitude of factors, both overt and latent, and these
044   often interact in a variable and nonlinear manner. The analysis of these examples is most naturally
045   expressed in the language of causality.
046
      In contrast, most current TSF frameworks Liu et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2021); Oreshkin et al. (2020)
047
      approach the problem of forecasting from the perspective of similarity and multivariate dependen-
048   cies; in other words, at the base level, these approaches are mainly focused on learning the correlative
049   weights between time series. This is especially the case for SOTA attention-based models Liu et al.
050   (2023); Chen et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b), as these models focus specifically on learning at-
051   tention weights between different preprocessed time series. All these different approaches excel at
052   capturing different facets of complex time series, but they are all predicated upon the same idea of
053   correlation and similarities. This presents a singularly one-dimensional view of conventional time
      series analysis and hence hampers comprehensive and in-depth understanding of this information.

      As mentioned previously, this unsatisfactory situation with regards with current SOTA TSF models
      can traced to a key omission: the neglect of causal relationships. They play an essential role in
072
      understanding the causes and effects influencing temporal behavior. They have been shown Moraf-
073
      fah et al. (2021); Runge et al. (2023) to provide essential perspectives into the temporal dimension
074   and, therefore, should contain invaluable insights for a comprehensive understanding of time series.
075   In fact, the idea of precedence in a time series and causality are inextricably linked together and it
076   is misleading to analyze temporal information without considering causal relations Wikipedia con-
077   tributors (2024). Although there is a significant body of work on TSF models that do Kong et al.
078   (2024); Nichani et al. (2024); Börjesson & Singull (2020); Nauta et al. (2019); Cheng et al. (2024);
079   Löwe et al. (2022); Chikahara & Fujino (2018); Dhaou et al. (2021) take causal information into
080   account, their focus is usually misplaced, in that their contributions tend to either focus exclusively
081   on temporal causal relationships, or treat causality as another aspect of time series to be studied.
082
      The first type of models Börjesson & Singull (2020); Nauta et al. (2019); Cheng et al. (2024) mainly
      emphasizes analysis of time series from the sole perspective of causality, while discarding other,
083
      equally important, features. The other type of approach Kong et al. (2024) looks at causality as
084
      merely one facet of time series data, and hence is susceptible to de-emphasizing its importance.
085
086   We take an integrative approach which promotes the causal aspect to a level which places it on
087   the same footing as other time series features; in other words, we propose an integrative approach
088
      that takes both temporal dependencies and causal information into account. We believe that the in-
      tractability of naturally-occurring time series can be understood within the all-encompassing context
089
      of causality. By starting from an intuitive understanding of causality as the study of cause and ef-
090
      fect relationships, we further leverage causality as the guiding principle according to which we can
091   comprehend the above-mentioned complexities of real-world time series. In our framework, we ex-
092   tract temporal causal information in the form of embedding vectors, which we integrate via a spatial
093   causal module to facilitate downstream forecasting tasks. For this purpose, we propose SPACE, a
094   model which is able to comprehensively take both these aspects of time series into account. SPACE
095   is inspired by the modular design of the original transformer and graph neural networks, augmented
096   by several main elements:
097
098          • A Sequence Enhancer, whose role is to compute correlational coefficients between the
099
               patched and projected raw data from the embedding step, using an attention mechanism;
100          • A Cross TE module which gets causality information by computing transfer entropy (TE)
101            self-causal relationships between time series using fast-pTE algorithm promoted by us;
102          • A Causal Graph Neural Network (CGNN) which integrates information by taking causal-
103            ity matrix from cross-TE modules as its adjacency matrix, in order to present a causally-
104            consolidated embedding vector for the final downstream tasks;
105
106   We integrate the above-mentioned modules in a conventional attention framework, which enables
107   our workflow to function as a drop-in replacement for attention modules. In short, our contribution
      in this work are as follows:

126   2.1   T RANSFORMER - BASED T IME S ERIES A NALYSIS
127
      Time series analysis techniques are very well-developed. State-of-the-art time series analysis mod-
128
      els incorporate the most recent advances in sequential data analysis, including various modifications
129
      to the basic transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2017). Liu et al. (2023) inverts the traditional
130   time series embedding for attention models, such that they now emphasize the attentive correlation
131   between variates, taking into account the full set of timepoints for each variate into account. Zhang &
132   Yan (2023) utilizes cross-dimensional dependencies between related variates to enhance time-series
133   prediction accuracy. Nie et al. (2023) further emphasizes the importance and advantages of patch-
134   ing for time series forecasting, and interprets each patch as semantic, making it to provide a new
135   perspective on its function. Wu et al. (2021) is an approach which actively decomposes the time se-
136   ries into logically coherent substructures (long-term trends, intermediate scale fluctuations, etc) and
137   uses these simpler substructures to enhance predictive power of the model. Lin et al. (2023) includes
138
      learnable placeholders in the input embedding to the transformer encoder, thus achieving increased
      accuracy and reduced model complexity. Zhang et al. (2024) takes multiscale data into account by
139
      extending patch-based TS transformers with attention mechanisms that learn multiresolution repre-
140
      sentations; Finally, Wang et al. (2024b) incorporates exogenous variables into the learning process,
141   hence taking into account the effect of such variables on the dynamics of the time series process.
142   unlike most attention-based models described here, Wang et al. (2024b) is able to explicitly reason
143   about dynamics of a specific time series contingent on possible influencing factors.
144
145   2.2   G RANGER C AUSALITY VIA T RANSFER E NTROPY
146
147   In recent years, causality has become a well-studied and essential component of time-series analysis.
148   The original proposal, by Granger Granger (1969), was actually meant to analyze “precedence“, in
149   the sense that, for two series X and Y , Y is said to be forecast by X, if there exists a Granger-
150   causal relationship between them. In most applications involving causal relationships, Granger-
151
      causality is inferred from the transfer entropy Schreiber (2000), which is a non-parameteric statistic
      originating from the physics literature. It is an information theoretic measure that quantifies the
152
      amount of information transfer between two random processes Hlaváčková-Schindler et al. (2007).
153
      It has been shown Barnett et al. (2009) that Granger causality and transfer entropy are the same for
154   a stream of normal-distributed random variables. Although conditioning on the distribution restricts
155   the applicability of transfer entropy as a causality surrogate, it has been widely utilized in this context
156   because of its ease of computation.

197   3                    M ETHODOLOGY
198
199   In time series forecasting, it is common to encounter with occasion when multivariate data is used for
200   prediction. Given historical observation with N dimension X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xL } ∈ RN ×L where
201   L is the look back window length, we predict future T time steps Y = {xL+1 , xL+2 , ..., xL+T } ∈
202   RN ×T . It is worth noting that there may be a causal relationship between time series of different
203   dimensions in the same set of data, that is, if the time series y is caused by x, then introducing x
204   when predicting y will definitely help improve the accuracy of the model results.

      3.1.1                  P REPROCESSOR
212
213   Before we start our discussion about embedding, we would like to review the two methods previ-
214   ously used by mainstream multivariate time series prediction models, and contrast these with our
215   own approach towards time series embedding. The method is cross-sectional, i.e., all the data points
      occurring at the same time are turned into a column vector Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2021) for


                                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      embedding. The limitation of this method is obvious, as it only focuses on obtaining the depen-
217   dencies in the time dimension, and the dependencies across the sequences are learnt only through
218   embedding and subsequent linear projection, which limits the ability to help predict between dif-
219   ferent sequences while adding a lot of noise to the prediction of a single time series. The second
220   method is cross-temporal, i.e., each individual time series is seen as a vector. The whole time series
221   is either treated as a token for embedding Liu et al. (2023) or patchified Nie et al. (2023); Zhang &
222   Yan (2023).
223   While the former approach absorbs the idea that linear projection is capable of learning cross-time
224   dependencies alone without any help from other structure including attention mechanism Zeng et al.
225   (2022); Li et al. (2023), in our point of view, the latter approach considers the properties of time
226   series from the perspective of the time period, rather than being restricted to individual points in
227   time, which can be utilized to lower the high uncertainty in single time point, improving overall per-
228   formance of modules such as our Cross-TE. To illustrate, given raw data ∀xi ∈ X, we first patchify
229   them into a 2D tensor hi ∈ RPN ×PL , where PN is the number of patches and PL refers to patch
230   length. The patchified data will then be mapped to latent space of dimension d via trainable linear
231
      projection. After embedding, series are inputted into our Sequence Enhancer module, which serve
      as the method to share information and patterns from other patches, through which we believe can
232
      enhance a sequence for latter process since it can better utilize the characteristic of auto-regressivity
233
      in time series. In this module, we adopt merely a multi-head attention block, with input-token linear
234   mapping with Wi , bi ∈ Rd×d and {Q, K, V } = H · Wi + bi , i ∈ {q, k, v}. Then through output
235   linear mapping, subsequently followed by LayerNorm and residual connection, we get H as the
236   output that should be forwarded to encoder layers. It is worth noting that with attention and residual
237   connection, we not only enhance the series but also preserve its time steps information, which is of
238   great importance for the computation of transfer entropy.

290   3.1.3   C AUSAL G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK
291
      Information between series and dependencies across time plays a vital role in time series forecasting
292
      tasks. The question of how to perfectly aggregate this information, as well as how to take advantage
293   of the temporal property, is of great importance. Therefore, we propose the Causal Graph Neural
294   Network (CGNN) in order to take full account in both in cross-temporal and cross-dimensional
295   situations.
296
      Our transfer entropy matrix as computed in the Cross-TE module is a powerful tool for tackling
297
      problems in terms of aggregating information from others beyond a series itself, since causality re-
298
      veal their essential relation. However, causality is a highly abstract concept, and a simple summation
299   of values following linear mapping in transformer encoders will give misleading results. In addition,
300   information among patches in each series needs to be shared for the same reason, for which a single
301   linear mapping along hidden dimension is insufficient. These limitations underscores the need to
302   apply graph neural networks to more accurately capture the intricate relationships among series.
303
      Previous works that use transfer entropy in graph neural network simply adopt primitive graph neural
304
      networks, such as GCN and GIN, propagating information layer by layer through the graph structure
305   embedded in the adjacency matrix to gradually integrate more global information Duan et al. (2022).
306   They tend to calculate transfer entropy matrices before training models, which are then converted to
307   sparse adjacency matrices by max (thresh, T0 ) where thresh is the minimum causality the model
308   tend to consider, and T0 is the entropy score. This method offers the advantage of simplifying
309   calculations by uniformly and equivalently treating strongly causal sequences, but nevertheless falls
310   short in distinguishing differences among them. So an all-pair message passing graph network with
311   weights is called for. To simplify our description, we will only discuss the process in a single
312   head. The transfer entropy score T0 ∈ RN ×N calculated by Cross-TE module will be normalized
313
      by SoftMax to T , and forwarded to CGNN. A function f , which is flexible to choose, will act on
      input data H 0 ∈ RN ×t×d from Sequence Enhancer, where t and d represent number of time state

      4.2   M AIN R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
354
355   The comprehensive forecasting results are listed in Table 1 with the best in red and second best in
356   blue and underlined. SPACE shows the best performance in comparison with the baseline mod-
357   els across many real-world datasets and prediction length settings. Specifically, there are 69 first
358   place and 10 second place rankings out of 90 comparison points. Hence we can conclude that the
359   integration of causal information is essential to improving forecasting performance. On the pub-
360   lic dataset, SPACE’s performance exceeds those of the attention-based baselines by a considerable
361   margin. The improvements in MSE and MAE for different prediction lengths S are not linearly
362   correlated with the baselines’ performances, indicating a more fundamental departure in the design
363
      of SPACE, compared to the baselines’ architectures, than a simple difference in depth or width of
      network architecture, which are all changes in degree rather than a change in form. This difference
364
      implies that the improvements we see in our results cannot be reproduced simply by modifying the
365
      degree complexity of the models. This departure is entirely due to the tight integration of causal
366   modules with the conventional attention-based logic for time series forecasting.
367
368   In addition to the differences in computed metrics (MSE and MAE), results obtained on the real-

      world datasets show that causality enhances interpretability of time series forecasting as well. We

475   5     C ONCLUSION
476
477   Conventional time-series forecasting models, such as the recent ones based on the attention mech-
478
      anism, predominantly learn correlative information between time series data. On the other hand, it
      has been shown in previous work that the analysis of time series based on the learning of causative
479
      factors yields better results than models based on correlative ones. Drawing on this fact, we design
480
      and implement SPACE, a time-series analysis model which learns causative information and uses
481   this for downstream forecasting tasks. We introduce several novel modules which significantly sim-
482   plify the computation, as well as organizes and aggregates this information. We perform extensive
483   experiments which validates our approach. Our experimentations show that:
484
485           • SPACE is able to outperform a number of SOTA baseline models in for both the MSE and
                MAE metrics, and hence is shown to be superior for general forecasting tasks;

      B.1   A LGORITHM
705
706   Pseudo transfer entropy (pTE) is a rigorous algorithm that requires precise alignment of each
707   observation in the two sequences according to their temporal order, i.e., given two time series
708   x = {x1 , x2 , ..., xT } and y = {y1 , y2 , ..., yT }, each xi and yi should occur at the same time point,
709   while real-world data cannot be so precise.
710
      Moreover, pTE can sometimes make mistakes due to the complex dynamic property of time se-
711   ries, which can be limited by the sampling of series data, and fails to capture information flows on
712   multiple time scales.
713
714
      Therefore, we choose to calculate TE on time steps, i.e., on the patches instead of the initial time
      points. This method have two advantages: (1) It enables the model to learn causality in a hidden
715
      state with higher dimension, allowing its flexibility and stronger capability. (2) It is more robust and
716
      less easy to be influenced by the precision of measurement than pure TE computation method. (3)
717   With linear projection on each patch in previous steps, it can make use of the multi-scale dynamics
718   of time series. We shall prove the third one in next section.
719
720

      tleneck for our training process. To state it more clearly, we consider the random process X follows

790   B.2   M ULTI - SCALE NATURE OF C ROSS TE M ETHOD
791
792   Previous works correspond to multi-scale transfer entropy mainly use moving average as their way
793   to take more data points, i.e., different scales, into account. However, this approach has limited the
794   capability of detecting multi-scale dynamics since if we view it from the perspective of interpolation,
795   it merely considers the middle point of a range of time points. It can be inferred that a linear
796   mapping is a stronger way to capture the multi-scale dynamics, as we extrapolate the average to
797
      linear interpolation.
798   Consider linear map W = [w1 , w2 , wd ]⊤, and the patches before embedding is P = [p1 , p2 , ..., pT ],
799   where wi ∈ R1×n and pj ∈ Rn×1 , which will then be embedded as p̃ij = wi · pj . Hence if we
                                  wi
800   normalise the wi to w̃i = |w  i|
                                       , we can re-write embedding function p̃ij = |wi |w̃i · pj , where w̃i · pj
801   is a linear interpolation. In addition, ∀i, j ∈ {1, 2, ..., T }, pki and pkj are mapped with the same
802   wj , preserving the time order for p̃ki and p˜kj . Therefore, with the linear interpolation that enlarge
803   the horizons depends on different value of wi while perpetuate the temporal order, this method can
804   theoretically acceptable in calculating multi-scale transfer entropy.
805   Back to our model, except for embedding, there are several other steps that have been implemented,
806   which could potentially undermine the aforementioned advantages. However, by retaining the resid-
807   ual connections, we ensure that the original information is preserved throughout the calculations,
808   thereby mitigating this issue. To be more specific, there are two non-linear mapping in the steps that
809   might be happen earlier than a Cross TE module, such as Feed-Forward layer and 2dMixer. Admit-
      tedly, it will break the linear interpolation which we discussed above, but in each step we use the
