009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Linguistic style encompasses a range of dimensions, including sensorial language
012            as well as traditional stylistic features (represented using LIWC features). While
013            these dimensions of linguistic style have been studied independently, relation-
014            ships between the different dimensions, particularly between sensorial style and
015            traditional stylistic features, remain understudied. This paper introduces a novel
016            approach to model this interaction and tests it across a diverse set of texts. In
017            particular, we propose using a Reduced-Rank Ridge Regression (R4) to model
018            low-rank latent relationships between LIWC-based stylistic features and senso-
019            rial language features. We find that compared to the full LIWC feature set
020
               (r = 74), its low-dimensional latent representations (r = 24) effectively cap-
               ture stylistic information relevant to sensorial language prediction. Based on our
021
               results, we propose Stylometrically Lean Interpretable Models (SLIM-LLMs) —
022
               dimensionality-reduced LLMs that model the non-linear relationships between
023            these two major dimensions of style. We evaluate SLIM-LLMs on the ability
024            to predict sensorial language (the actual sensorial words used) in five text genres:
025            business reviews, novels, song lyrics, advertisements, and informative articles.
026            Results show that SLIM-LLMs augmented with low-rank style features consis-
027            tently outperform baseline models. These SLIM-LLMs approach the performance
028            of full-scale language models while using significantly fewer parameters (up to
029            80% reduction).

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Salient and persistent structural patterns in language use constitute linguistic style. This includes tra-
035   ditional stylistic features like sentence length, language complexity, sentiment, and syntactic struc-
036   ture as well as patterns in the language used to describe sensory experiences — sensorial style. Sen-
037   sorial style is a relatively new area of research and is informed by ideas from sensorial linguistics.
038   Research into sensorial style holds significant potential for providing insights into human cognition.
039
      Stylometrics, the study of linguistic style, has largely overlooked patterns in the use of sensorial
040   language — words pertaining to sense and perception. While standard stylometric lexicons, such
041   as LIWC, include some sensorial terms, these are generally distributed across different LIWC sub-
042   categories and their coverage of the sensorial language space is sparse. Moreover, there has been
043   no focused investigation of the relationship between traditional stylometrics and sensorial language.
044   Thus, we do not know for example if these two major dimensions of linguistic style are independent
045   of each other or related to some degree. Our goal is to investigate this relationship.
046   Our motivation for studying this relationship stems from theories in cognitive science. The inter-
047   action between different dimensions of linguistic style can be modeled using cognitive frameworks
048   similar to the ‘mental lexicon’ proposed by Levelt (1992), which posits a central repository of lin-
049   guistic knowledge that mediates various aspects of language processing. We extend this idea to
050   propose a Central Language Processing Unit (CLPU) that coordinates interactions between differ-
051   ent representations of linguistic style.

      This model of interactions within linguistic style also aligns with the grounded cognition theory
055   (Barsalou, 2008), which suggests that linguistic processes are closely tied to the brain’s perceptual,
056   motor, and introspective systems. This theory implies that how we articulate sensory and bodily
057   states influences our language use. However, previous research in this area has been limited to
058   small-scale studies (Zwaan & Madden, 2005; Pulvermüller, 2013). In this context, our work aims
059   to bridge this gap in stylometric research by computationally modeling the relationship between
060   traditional style features and sensorial style across large and diverse text collections. We propose
061   a novel approach to modeling this relationship, drawing from stylometrics, sensorial linguistics as
062   well as cognitive sciences.
063   Our work makes the following contributions to the field:
064
065          • We model the interactions within traditional LIWC-style and sensorial style using Reduced-
066            Rank Ridge Regression (R4). We use R4 to identify low-rank group structures within
067            LIWC-style.
068          • We introduce Stylometrically Lean Interpretable Models (SLIM-LLMs), which provide a
069            more interpretable lens to study the relationship between traditional linguistic style and
070            sensorial style.
071          • We conduct large-scale analysis across diverse text genres, providing empirical support for
072            theoretical claims about the interaction between different aspects of linguistic style.

075   2     R ELATED W ORKS
076
077   The study of sensorial style is a relatively new area of research. There are no directly comparable
078   studies examining sensorial style and its relation to traditional styles. Instead, we review works from
079
      allied fields — stylometry and sensorial linguistics — that intersect with our work.
080
081   2.1   S TYLOMETRY
082
      Stylometry focuses on analyzing linguistic style use through various computational and statistical
083
      techniques. While much of stylometric research has centered on author attribution (Overdorf &
084
      Greenstadt, 2016), more recently stylometrics have been used to analyze emotional and psycholog-
085   ical dimensions of language use.
086
087   One of the primary stylometric methods that focus on psycholinguistics is Linguistic Inquiry and
088
      Word Count (LIWC) (Pennebaker et al., 2007). LIWC measures various linguistic features, includ-
      ing emotional tone, cognitive processes, and personal concerns. It has been widely used for tasks
089
      ranging from author attribution to modeling psychological states such as depression (De Choudhury
090
      et al., 2013).
091
092   Similarly, ANEW (Bradley & Lang, 1999), provides a set of normative emotional ratings for around
093   1000 English words. More recently VADER (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014) has emerged as a rule-based
094
      sentiment analysis tool that combines a lexicon and rule-based approach to measure sentiment.
095   In addition to these emotion-focused measures, stylometric features have traditionally included a
096   range of measures like Readability and n-gram usage (Potthast et al., 2017) that represent different
097   dimensions of linguistic style.
098   Recently Large Language Models (LLMs) have been increasingly utilized to represent linguistic
099   style. Li et al. (2019) and Sousa et al. (2019) have demonstrated the effectiveness of LLMs like
100   BERT, in modeling various aspects of linguistic style, including sentiment. However, while these
101   LLM-based approaches have shown impressive results, they often lack interpretability. Additionally,
102   there has been a limited focus on understanding sensorial style in these approaches.
103
104   2.2   S ENSORIAL L INGUISTICS
105
106   Sensorial linguistics investigates the relationship between sensory perception and language, studying
107   how different experiences and perceptions are represented using linguistic units (Winter, 2016). The
      field has traditionally focused on the five classical senses: visual, auditory, olfactory, gustatory, and


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      haptic. However, recent research has expanded this model to include interoception as a sixth sense
109   (Lynott et al., 2020).
110
111   Winter et al. (2018) analyzed the distribution of sensorial language across different parts of speech
112
      (nouns, adjectives, and verbs) and found that visual language dominates across all categories. This
      aligns with Viberg (1983)’s proposed universal hierarchy of the senses, with vision at the top, fol-
113
      lowed by hearing, touch, smell, and taste.
114
115   Lynott et al. (2020) introduced the Sensorimotor Lexicon, a comprehensive resource containing
116   sensory ratings for around 40,000 concepts across six sensory dimensions, including interoception.
117
118   2.3   S ENSORIAL S TYLE
119
120   While traditional stylometry has focused on analyzing patterns in language use through various
121   computational and statistical techniques, it has largely overlooked sensorial language. Stylomet-
122   ric measures like LIWC and VADER are not specifically designed to capture patterns in sensorial
123   language use.
124
      Recently, methods have been proposed to analyze sensorial style. Kernot et al. (2016) proposed a
125   method to analyze sensorial style by measuring the use of sensory adjectives. Khalid & Srinivasan
126   (2022) introduced a method to measure sensorial style based on synaesthesia, or the propensity to
127   replace one sensorial modality with another.
128
      Prior works have focused on analyzing traditional stylometry and sensorial style independently,
129
      but there remains a gap in understanding how these two aspects of linguistic style interact. Our
130
      work aims to bridge this gap by proposing a novel approach that models the relationship between
131   traditional linguistic style (as captured by LIWC features) and sensorial style.

134   3     M ETHODS
135
136
      3.1   R EPRESENTING S ENSORIAL S TYLE
137
138   Sensorial style is modeled and represented across a range of granularities. A synaesthesia-based
139   approach has been used to model sensorial style at a high level (Khalid & Srinivasan, 2022). A
140   high-level approach to modeling sensorial style focuses on patterns of sensory language-use across
141   broader linguistic units or entire texts, rather than on individual words. In contrast, we model sen-
142   sorial style at the word-level, which focuses on individual sensorial words and their relationships to
143   other linguistic style features.
144   We represent a sensorial sentence as a one-hot encoding of the sensorial vocabulary. Khalid &
145   Srinivasan (2022) have defined the sensorial vocabulary V as a subset of 18,749 words from the
146   Lancaster Sensorimotor Lexicon (Lynott et al., 2020). They consider a sentence to be sensorial if
147   it has one or more sensorial words in it. We use this criterion and consider a sensorial sentence to
148   have just one sensorial term. For example, ‘it is a noisy room’ has two sensorial words, the auditory
149   ‘noisy’ and the visual ‘room’. Assuming ‘noisy’ and ‘room’ are the second and fourth words in the
150   sensorial vocabulary, this sentence constitutes two sensorial sentences represented as [0, 1, 0, 0, . . . ,
151   0] for ‘noisy’ and [0, 0, 0, 1, . . . , 0] for ‘room’. The length of the two vectors equals the size of our
152
      sensorial vocabulary; that is, |V | = 18, 749.
153   We formalize the previous idea as follows. Let V = {w1 , w2 , ..., wn } be the sensorial vocabulary
154   of size n. For a given sensorial word w in a sentence, we represent it as a vector y ∈ {0, 1}n , where
155   yi = 1 if w = wi and 0 otherwise. A sentence S with m sensorial words is represented as a set of m
156   n-vectors and S = {y1 , y2 , ..., ym }, where yj (j = 1, . . . , m) corresponds to the one hot encoding
157   of the jth sensorial sentence.
158   We represent each sensorial sentence as a vector based on the LIWC-style (Pennebaker et al., 2015).
159   Let X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xm } be the set of m LIWC categories. For a given sensorial sentence S, we
160   exclude the sensorial term ws and represent the style of the remaining sentence as a vector s ∈ Rm .
161   Each element si of this vector corresponds to the proportion of words in S excluding ws that belong
      to the ith LIWC category xi : si = ( {w ∈ S \ {ws } : w ∈ xi } )/(|S| − 1).

      For example, given the sentence ‘it is a noisy room’ with two sensorial words ‘noisy’ and ‘room’,
163   we create two style vectors. For ‘room’, the style vector will be based on [‘it’, ‘is’, ‘a’, ‘noisy’],
164   and for ‘noisy’ the style vector will be based on [‘it’, ‘is’, ‘a’, ‘room’]. This is comparable to the
165   BERT masked language model setup, where each sensorial word is treated as the target word to be
166   predicted, and the embedding is calculated from the remaining words in the sentence.

168   3.2   L INEAR M ODELS FOR S TYLE I NTERACTIONS
169
170   We use regression to model the relation between traditional style and sensorial style. Let the style
171   features of a sentence S be the LIWC vector x = (x1 , . . . xm ) and let y = (y1 , y2 . . . yn ) be the
172   one-hot sensorial vector of the sentence, where m is the number of style features and n is the size of
173
      the sensorial vocabulary S (Lynott et al., 2020). Then, y⊤ = x⊤ B+e⊤ models the relation between
      linguistic style x and sensorial language use y, with e denoting the errors independent of x. The
174
      regression coefficient matrix is B ∈ Rm×n , and its (i, j)the element bij is the mean increase in the
175
      sensorial word yj for a unit increase in style feature xi , given other features in x remain unchanged.
176   The linear regression model is equivalent to a sensorial-word-prediction problem, where we predict
177   the sensorial word ws in a sentence from the linguistic style of the remaining text. This method is
178   analogous to the masked word prediction task used to train LLMs like BERT Devlin (2018).
179
      We fit the regression model to the training data as follows. For a set of k sentences, the ith sentence
180
      has sensorial vector yi = (yi1 , . . . , yin ), and its corresponding style vector is xi = (xi1 , . . . , xim ).
181
      The training data are represented as the k × n matrix Y = [y1 , . . . , yk ]⊤ and k × m matrix
182   X = [x1 , . . . , xk ]⊤ . For a sufficiently large k, the least squares estimate of B is (X⊤ X)−1 X⊤ Y
183   (Qian et al., 2022). Previous works have shown that LIWC features have a low-rank structure (Geng
184   et al., 2020). However, the standard least squares approach fails to capture this structure and the
185   latent dependencies between the sensorial features and LIWC-style features, which correspond to
186   the columns of Y and X. This limitation is particularly significant because not all LIWC features
187   capture the same amount of information. For example, the function category words are more infor-
188   mative than categories like fillers. The word categories have group behavior. For instance, in the
189   LIWC features, first person singular is a subcategory of personal pronouns, whereas the ingestion
190
      category contains words like ‘eat’ that also belong to the verb category.
191
192   3.3   R EDUCED -R ANK R IDGE R EGRESSION
193
      We circumvent the previous limitations by assuming that B is a low-rank matrix. This assumption
194   implies that the previous linear model becomes a reduced-rank regression model (Anderson, 1951),
195   which assumes that B has a rank r and r ≪ min{m, n}. In a sparse B, a large fraction of the
196   entries are 0, where bij = 0 denotes that xi and yj are not associated. Similarly, a row sparse B has
197   bij = 0 for j = 1, . . . , n for many is. If the ith row of B is zero, then xi is not associated with any
198   sensorial word. To model a rank-r B, we set B = UV⊤ , where U = (u1 , u2 . . . ur ) ∈ Rm×r and
199   V = (v1 , v2 . . . vr ) ∈ Rn×r . By assuming row sparsity of B, we can effectively select a subset of
200   LIWC features that have the strongest associations with sensorial words across different contexts.
201   This assumption is more appropriate for our goals of identifying the most influential LIWC features
202   that contribute to sensorial language use.
203   Consider a reduced-rank model for regressing Y on X. For a rank r, Chen & Huang (2012) propose
204   a (row) sparse reduced-rank regression (SRRR) of B via U and V estimates as

228   3.4   M ODELING N ON -L INEAR S TYLE I NTERACTIONS
229
230   The R4 model in equation 2 assumes a linear association between LIWC-style and sensorial style.
231   The associations, however, are nonlinear from linguistic and cognitive perspectives. We model
232
      the relationship between LIWC-style and sensorial style as a phenomenon mediated by a Central
      Language Processing Unit (CLPU), using Large Language Models (LLMs) as a proxy for the of
233
      the CLPU. The CLPU is a similar construct to Levelt (1992)’s ‘mental lexicon’. LLMs, trained on
234
      vast corpora of human language, encapsulate general language norms and patterns. They capture
235   the complex interactions mediated by our broader linguistic knowledge and cognitive processes
236   (Manning et al., 2020).
237
238
      To model this interaction, we represent traditional stylistic features of a sentence using our LIWC-
      based representation. We then use an LLM for a masked language modeling task on the original
239
      sentence, with the sensorial words masked. Finally, we use the LLM’s predictions for masked
240
      sensorial words, combined with the LIWC-style, to predict sensorial style. Formally, let S be the
241   original sentence, and m(S) be the sentence with sensorial words masked. Let f be the function
242   represented by the LLM that takes the masked sentence m(S) and returns the encoder embedding
243   representation of the masked word. Then, the model relating sensorial words and LLM’s encoder
244   embeddings of the masked word is

251   3.5   S TYLOMETRICALLY L EAN I NTERPRETABLE M ODELS (SLIM-LLM S )
252
253
      LLMs like BERT are often overparameterized (Matton & de Oliveira, 2019). This can obscure the
      relationship between LIWC-style and sensorial style due to redundancies in the model’s training. To
254
      address this, we propose using dimensionality reduction techniques to create Stylometrically Lean
255
      Interpretable Models (SLIM-LLMs). SLIM-LLMs are reduced versions of standard LLMs that aim
256   to reveal the underlying relationships between LIWC-style and sensorial style more clearly. We
257   create SLIM-LLMs using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). Let E ∈ Rk×d be the encoder
258   embedding matrix of our LLM, where d is the dimension of the hidden state and k is the number of
259   sentences in our dataset.
260
      The SLIM-LLM retain only the top r singular values and their corresponding singular vectors for
261
      the SVD of E and are denoted as Eslim . Specifically, let E = UΣV⊤ be the SVD of E, where
262   U ∈ Rk×k and V ∈ Rd×d are the left and right orthonormal matrices. Then, Eslim = Ur Σr Vr⊤ ,
263   where Ur ∈ Rk×r , Σr ∈ Rr×r , and Vr ∈ Rd×r . The nonlinear classification model relating
264   sensorial words and LLMs in equation 3 is now rewritten for SLIM-LLMs as

      5   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      Our results demonstrate that SLIM-LLMs, such as SLIM-BERT, and LIWC-style features capture
489   complementary aspects of sensorial style across various language genres. The combination of these
490   two representations consistently outperforms either representation alone, supporting our hypothe-
491   sis of a mediated interaction between LIWC-style and sensorial style through a Central Language
492   Processing Unit (CLPU).
493
      For example, in the case of Articles and Advertisements, we observe that the combination of SLIM-
494
      BERT and Latent LIWC features achieves higher accuracy than the sum of their individual perfor-
495   mances. Specifically, for Articles, SLIM-BERT+Latent LIWC with r = 240 achieves an accuracy
496   of 0.48, compared to SLIM-BERT (0.41 at r = 240) and LIWC-style (0.06) alone.
497
498
      While we focused on LIWC-style features in this work, our approach can be extended to incorpo-
      rate other stylometric features such as ANEW, VADER, and measures of linguistic complexity like
499
      Readability and Hapax Legomenon. Such extensions would let us not only study the relationships
500
      between these features and sensorial style, but also the interactions with the rest of the stylometric
501   features.
502
503   One limitation of this study is its focus on English language texts. However, the dimensionality
504
      reduction technique used to create SLIM-LLMs is not inherently language-specific and is only lim-
      ited by the underlying LLM’s training data. This approach can be extended to other languages by
505
      creating SLIM versions of language-specific or multilingual models, such as SLIM-BETO for Span-
506
      ish (based on the BETO model (Cañete et al., 2020)) or SLIM-mBERT (based on the multilingual
507   BERT model).
