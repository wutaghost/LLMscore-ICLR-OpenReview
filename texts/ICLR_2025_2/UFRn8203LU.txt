009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Overparameterization in neural networks has demonstrated remarkable advan-
012            tages for both memorization and generalization, particularly in models trained
013            with gradient descent. While much of the existing research focuses on the inter-
014            play between overparameterization and gradient-based methods, we explore its
015            influence on the loss landscape of ℓ2 supervised regression problems, independent
016            of any specific optimizer. By leveraging the Nyquist-Shannon-Whittaker sampling
017            theorem, we establish a theoretical link between sampling theory and overparam-
018
               eterized neural networks. Our findings reveal that overparameterization not only
               exponentially increases the number of global minima but also expands the dimen-
019
               sionality of loss valleys for various ℓ2 regression problems modelled with feed-
020
               forward neural networks. We empirically validate these theoretical insights across
021            multiple supervised ℓ2 regression tasks, trained with both gradient-based and non-
022            gradient-based optimization algorithms. These results offer fresh perspectives on
023            the advantages of overparameterization in neural network design, independent of
024            the chosen learning algorithm.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Overparameterization has demonstrated remarkable benefits for both memorization and general-
030   ization, particularly when training with gradient descent. Traditional learning theory suggests that
031   models with excessive capacity are prone to overfitting. However, modern deep learning research has
032   shown that overparameterized models can perfectly fit or memorize training data while still general-
033
      izing well to unseen data (Zhang et al., 2021). This memorization effect is particularly pronounced
      when using gradient descent, which efficiently navigates high-dimensional parameter spaces to lo-
034
      cate global minima of the loss function, even in highly overparameterized networks (Arora et al.,
035
      2019; Zhang et al., 2021). The neural tangent kernel (NTK) theory has revealed that with suffi-
036   cient overparameterization, gradient descent (flow) closely mirrors the behavior of kernel regression
037   (Jacot et al., 2018; Bietti & Mairal, 2019; Huang et al., 2020). This insight highlights the critical
038   role of overparameterization in understanding the dynamics of gradient descent in neural networks.
039   Moreover, gradient descent exhibits an implicit bias toward finding solutions with minimal norm in
040   overparameterized models, such as those with ReLU activations. This bias has been associated with
041   improved generalization properties, even when the model can perfectly memorize the training data
042   (Du et al., 2018; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019). This balance between memorization and generalization
043   underscores the effectiveness of overparameterization in modern deep learning.
044   While these works reveal a deep connection between overparameterization, memorization, and gen-
045   eralization, they focus on the context of gradient descent as the learning algorithm. In this article,
046   we seek to understand whether overparameterization offers inherent benefits for the loss landscape
047   associated with ℓ2 supervised regression problems, independent of any particular optimizer.
048   Our approach builds on the sampling theory of Nyquist-Shannon-Whittaker (NSW) (Nyquist, 1928;
049   Shannon, 1948; Whittaker, 1915) a foundational result in signal processing, providing the conditions
050   under which a continuous signal can be perfectly reconstructed from a discrete set of samples. It
051   states that if a signal is band-limited, meaning its frequency components are restricted to a maximum
052   frequency ωmax , then the signal can be fully recovered from its samples, provided the sampling rate
053   is at least twice the highest frequency present—this rate is known as the Nyquist rate. Specifically, if
      the sampling interval T satisfies T1 ≥ 2ωmax , the original signal can be reconstructed using a sum of

056   process as long as the Nyquist criterion is satisfied.

058
      We build on these insights to establish a connection between sampling theory and supervised regres-
      sion problems. Our first main result focuses on sinc-activated feedforward networks for modeling
059
      ℓ2 regression problems and demonstrates how overparameterization leads to an exponential increase
060
      in global minima around the origin of the parameter space. What is particularly interesting about
061   this result is that it is independent of any optimizer, implying that for such networks, overparame-
062   terization provides a significant benefit for the loss landscape that should help any optimizer. Our
063   second main result shows how sampling theory with the triangular function offers a new perspective
064   on understanding ReLU feedforward networks. We mathematically prove that overparameterization
065   results in an increase in the dimension of global minima that manifest as loss valleys in the parameter
066   space.
067   Both theorems present a novel viewpoint on the benefits of overparameterization, going beyond
068   what has been previously studied in the literature. To validate that our theoretical results provide
069   practical insights into ℓ2 supervised regression problems, we conduct a series of experiments for
070   both sinc and ReLU-activated feedforward networks using first-order gradient-based optimizers,
071   second-order gradient-based optimizers, and non-gradient-based genetic optimizers. In each case,
072   our results support our theoretical findings. We believe that the insights offered by sampling theory
073   will lead to a deeper understanding of overparameterization and its effects on deep learning.
074   Our main contributions are:
075
076         1. Theoretical results explaining how overparameterization alters the global minima of the
077            loss landscape for a supervised ℓ2 regression problem modelled with a sinc or ReLU-
078            activated feedforward network that is independent of any optimizer.
079         2. A comprehensive validation of our theoretical results across a variety of supervised ℓ2
080            regression problems trained with different optimizers.

102
103   Research on overparameterization has enhanced our understanding of how large models achieve
104   both memorization and generalization. While overfitting was once a concern, studies like Zhang
105   et al. (2021) showed that overparameterized networks can still generalize well, despite perfectly
106   fitting training data. The neural tangent kernel (NTK) framework (Jacot et al., 2018) explained
107   how gradient descent in these regimes resembles kernel regression, with Arora et al. (2019) and
      Huang et al. (2020) demonstrating that overparameterization smooths the loss landscape, leading

      to multiple global minima. Additionally, Allen-Zhu et al. (2019) and Du et al. (2018) explored
109   how gradient descent’s bias toward minimal-norm solutions improves generalization of a network.
110   Recent work by Belkin et al. (2019) and Nakkiran et al. (2021) introduced the ”double descent” phe-
111   nomenon, showing that increasing model size beyond the interpolation threshold further enhances
112   performance. Despite this focus on gradient-based optimization, less attention has been given to
113   overparameterization’s impact on alternative optimizers.
114
      The sinc function has been applied to neural networks in tasks such as audio sampling (Ravanelli
115   & Bengio, 2018b;a) and dynamical systems (Ramasinghe et al., 2023; Saratchandran et al., 2024).
116   Saratchandran et al. (2024) also established a universal approximation theorem for sinc-based net-
117   works. While these works focus on signal processing applications, this paper takes a different ap-
118   proach, using sinc networks to provide new insights into overparameterization.

190   theorem demonstrates that increasing the number of neurons, either by adding width or depth, leads

199   or depth of the network, leads to a growth in the dimensionality of the global minima, which man-
200   ifest as loss valleys. We provide a precise quantitative characterization of how this dimensionality
201   increases. These results further highlight the significant benefits of overparameterization, regardless

211   Definition 5.1. Let L2 : Rparam → R denote the ℓ2 loss function associated to a neural network as in
212   equation 4. Let Λ denote a collection of points in Rparam such that each θ ∈ Λ is a global minimum
213   of L2 . We say Λ defines a global minimum valley if for each point θ ∈ Λ there exists an r > 0
214   such that Λ ∩ Br (θ) is homeomorphic to Rk for some 0 < k ≤ param. The dimension of the global
215   minimum valley is k. We say a point θ∗ in Rparam is an isolated global minimum of L2 if there exists
      an r > 0 such that Br (θ∗ )\{θ∗ } does not contain any global minima of L2 .

522   7     L IMITATIONS
523
524   Our results in Theorems 5.3 and A.14 apply to signals in L2 (R), as they are rooted in the Nyquist-
525   Shannon-Whittaker sampling theorem, which pertains to such signals. An interesting extension
526   would be to explore whether bounds outside the training data can be established for higher-
527   dimensional signals in L2 (Rk ) for k > 1. We believe this direction could be linked to the mul-
528   tidimensional sampling theorem by Petersen & Middleton (1962), potentially offering new insights
529   into the role of network depth and its impact on generalization. We aim to take this up in a future
530
      project.

532   8     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we demonstrated that overparameterization, viewed through the lens of sampling the-
535   ory, provides valuable insights into the structure of the loss landscape for ℓ2 supervised regression
536   problems. Our theoretical findings reveal that both sinc and ReLU activated feedforward networks,
537   when overparameterized, significantly increase the number of global minima for the ℓ2 loss func-
538   tion, regardless of the optimizer used. Empirical validation with various optimizers reinforces these
539   results, highlighting the pivotal role of overparameterization. We hope these insights inspire new
      approaches to understanding neural networks and the loss functions used to train them.

       A.2.1    E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
1535
1536   In this section we discuss the experimental hyperparameters we used for each of the experiments.
1537   For each of the four experiments we used the optimizers SGD, Adam, OnePlusOne and L-BFGS.
1538
1539   SGD: For this optimizer we used the standard PyTorch implementation with a learning rate of
1540   1e-3 for all experiments. We found we obtained similar results with learning rates of the form 1e-2
1541   and 1e-4 (and lower) but that 1e-3 was the best and seemed to be the commonly used learning rate
1542   for SGD in the literature. We observed that with different learning rates overparameterization still
1543
       led to better train PNSRs and test PSNRs.
1544
       Adam: For this optimizer we used the standard PyTorch implementation with a learning rate of
1545
       1e-4 for all experiments which is what the literature used. We found we obtained similar results with
1546
       learning rates of the form 1e-2 and 1e-3 (and lower) but that 1e-4 was the best. We observed that
1547   with different learning rates overparameterization still led to better train PNSRs and test PSNRs.
1548
1549   OnePlusOne: This optimizer operates through an iterative process involving “parents” and “off-
1550   spring.” The algorithm starts with a single solution, referred to as the parent. At each iteration, a new
1551   solution, the offspring, is generated by introducing a random mutation to the parent. This mutation
1552   typically follows a Gaussian distribution. The offspring is then evaluated based on the objective
1553   function. If the offspring achieves a better result than the parent, it replaces the parent; otherwise,
1554   the parent remains unchanged. This process continues until convergence or a stopping criterion is
1555   met, gradually improving the solution with each iteration. We implemented this optimizer based on
1556
       the facebook research code available at https://facebookresearch.github.io/nevergrad/. The hyperpa-
       rameter that needs to be fixed is the number offspring the optimizer sends out to compare against the
1557
       parent. We found that anywhere between 10 to 30 offspring did the best and thus fixed 20 offspring
1558
       as our hyperparameter. For each hyperparameter we noticed the trend that oveparameterization
1559   yielded better train and test PSNRs.
1560
1561   L-BFGS: For this optimizer we used the standard PyTorch implementation with a learning rate
1562   of 1e-3 for all experiments. We found we obtained similar results with learning rates of the form
1563   1e-2 and 1e-4 (and lower) but that 1e-3 was the best. We observed that with different learning
1564   rates overparameterization still led to better train PNSRs and test PSNRs. We also noticed that
1565   the optimizer struggled for the larger experiments image super resolution and the binary occupancy
       field. This was also found in the literature in work of Saratchandran et al. (2023).
