009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               With the proliferation of machine learning services, the risk of privacy breaches
012            has never been higher, owing to the need for collecting – sometimes by any means
013            necessary – valuable, yet sensitive training data. When an unsanctioned data access
014            occurs, it may become apparent after the fact, in the predictive models that have
015            been trained on compromised data. This calls for effective membership inference
016            methods, enabling an evaluator to identify privacy breaches. Distinct from tradi-
017            tional membership inference attacks (MIAs), which focus on determining whether
018            individual data records were used in training, this study centers on the evaluation
019            of sets of records, particularly when only a small proportion of the set are training
020
               members. In this scenario, traditional MIAs often suffer from non-ideal evaluation
               reliability. To address this issue, from a privacy evaluator’s perspective, we propose
021
               a novel approach for membership inference, applicable not to individual records
022
               but to sets thereof. It relies on a non-parametric two-sample test, which leverages
023            the differences between high-level representation to infer membership. Based
024            on extensive experiments, our proposed High-level Representation-based MMD
025            (HR-MMD) test exhibits high sensitivity in distinguishing between the training
026            and non-training sets, with ideal type I error, making it a powerful membership
027            detection tool. Our study offers insights into an alternative privacy breach detec-
028            tion scenario and opens up a promising avenue for privacy evaluation based on
029            membership inference tests.

033
034
      Machine learning (ML) models have become indispensable in critical domains such as healthcare,
035
      genomics, and image recognition, playing a pivotal role in tasks like disease diagnosis, genetic pattern
036   analysis, and image classification (Xiong et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Miotto et al., 2018). These
037   models, particularly deep neural networks, excel at extracting complex patterns and correlations from
038   diverse datasets, enabling them to make accurate predictions (Song et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2019;
039   Zhang et al., 2021). However, this proficiency comes with a potential drawback – the models can
040   inadvertently memorize training data, introducing a potential vulnerability to various privacy attacks.
041
      As a vulnerability, this propensity of models to memorize data facets has been explored extensively,
042   with studies revealing that attackers can exploit model memorization through several methodologies,
043   including model extraction, attribute inference, property inference, and, crucially, membership
044   inference (whether a given data record may have been used to train a model or not) (Fredrikson
045   et al., 2015; Tramèr et al., 2016; Shokri et al., 2017b; Ganju et al., 2018). The last one, being the
046   most related to the focus of our discussion, stands out for specifically targeting the confidentiality of
047   training data points (Shokri et al., 2017b; Yeom et al., 2018b; Salem et al., 2019b; Chen et al., 2021).
048   But what is mostly regarded as a vulnerability in this context can also be exploited as an opportunity
049   for privacy evaluators to detect unsanctioned data accesses in hindsight, from predictive models that
050   have been trained on compromised data. For example, a clinical trial for a common disease may be
051   conducted across multiple hospitals, with each hospital recruiting its patients from a large population,
052   following a uniform recruitment strategy. Although clinical records are highly sensitive, different
053   hospitals may be more or less able to protect them. Some data may be exposed to data theft, and
      may then make its way into training datasets for third-party predictive models (for that disease). By

075   The breach of privacy is a general and profound concern in the current machine learning landscape,
076   prompting urgent discussions around user data protection. Recognizing this, legislative bodies have
077   enacted robust privacy protection laws such as the general data protection regulation (GDPR) in
078   the European Union, the california consumer privacy act (CCPA) in California, and the personal
079   information protection and electronic documents act (PIPEDA) in Canada, which have legally consol-
080   idated the privacy protection. In particular, these laws provide individuals with the legal framework
081
      to protect their membership privacy, recognizing the risk of breaches through membership inference
      from ML models, calling for privacy-aware learning approaches. However, when unsanctioned data
082
      accesses do occur, and when compromised data does make its way, later on, into training datasets for
083
      third-party predictive models, membership inference can also be an effective tool for privacy auditors
084   / evaluators, allowing them to detect data breaches from such models.
085
086   There are however essential differences between the well-studied membership inference problem,
087
      viewed from an attacker’s perspective, and a membership inference problem reframed from a privacy
      evaluator’s perspective, in terms of objectives and data granularity, as discussed next.
088
089   Membership inference attacks (MIAs) are commonly used to discern whether a given data record
090   is used to train a target model (member) or not (non-member) (Hayes et al., 2017; Pyrgelis et al.,
091   2017; Nasr et al., 2018; Rahman et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2018; Yeom et al., 2018b; Truex et al.,
092   2018; Jia et al., 2019; Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Song & Marn, 2020; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020b;
093
      Rahimian et al., 2020; Song & Mittal, 2021; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
      2022; Ye et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022). However, by their positioning and objectives, traditional
094
      MIAs often suffer from high false positive rate when aiming for high true positive rate, even with
095
      the currently considered state-of-the-art Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA) (Carlini et al., 2022). This
096   drawback persists in scenarios where the subject being tested are sets of records (Maini et al., 2021;
097   Kandpal et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024), yet only a small proportion are training members (as seen in
098   Figure 1 (a)). This renders them unreliable as a tool serving a privacy evaluator – i.e., using them
099   directly to evaluate third-party models that are potentially trained on compromised (leaked) data. An
100   evaluation’s reliability when using MIAs approaches directly would therefore not be sufficient in
101   practice, as it would return a prediction with a high false positive rate or low true positive rate. Such
102   an evaluation may not provide enough evidence to impose penalties, and the third-party owning the
103   model could argue that their samples were mis-classified by the evaluation.
104   To address the issue of non-ideal evaluation reliability, we turn to the domain of two-sample tests and
105   we adopt a membership test working at a coarser granularity, for sets of records. Two-sample tests are
106   hypothesis tests that determine if two sets of samples come from the same distribution. Traditional
107   methods such as t-tests and Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests are effective only on data in extremely
      low-dimensional spaces and require strong parametric assumptions about the distributions being

      studied. Recent work in statistics and machine learning has focused on relaxing these assumptions,
109   resulting in methods either generally applicable or specific to various complex domains (Gretton et al.,
110   2012a; Székely & Rizzo, 2013; Heller & Heller, 2016; Jitkrittum et al., 2016; Chen & Friedman,
111   2017; Ghoshdastidar et al., 2017; Ramdas et al., 2017; Li & Wang, 2018; Gao et al., 2018).
112
      A popular class of non-parametric two-sample tests is based on kernel methods (Smola & Schölkopf,
113
      2001). Such tests construct a kernel mean embedding (Berlinet & Thomas-Agnan, 2004; Muandet
114
      et al., 2017) for each distribution, and measure the difference in these embeddings. For any char-
115   acteristic kernel, two distributions are the same if and only if their mean embeddings are the same,
116   and the distance between mean embeddings is the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton
117   et al., 2012a). Tests based on checking for differences in mean embeddings evaluated at specific
118   locations (Chwialkowski et al., 2015; Jitkrittum et al., 2016) and the kernel Fisher discriminant
119   analysis (Harchaoui et al., 2007) are also related methods. These non-parametric tests work well for
120   samples from simple distributions when using appropriate kernels and can be applied to machine
121   learning problems such as domain adaptation, generative modeling, and causal discovery (Gong et al.,
122   2016; Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2017; Binkowski et al., 2018; Stojanov et al., 2019).
123   The detection problem we address can be framed as the statistical membership inference for privacy
124   breach detection. In short, given a well-trained classifier fˆ on ST , the problem we are concerned
125   with is determining if an incoming data set SY has the same distribution as a non-training set SX and
126   contains no elements from the training set ST . Although previous two-sample tests can readily solve
127   the former challenge, they are incapable of addressing the latter. This is because the raw features of
128   both the non-training and training sets originate from the same underlying distribution. Therefore, in
129   this paper, we focus on solving the latter issue. Our motivation stems from studies on the overfitting
130   phenomenon (Shokri et al., 2017a; Long et al., 2018; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020a), which reveal that
131
      neural networks inevitably capture noise, leading to varying degrees of overfitting. This, in turn,
      results in the generalization gap between non-training and training data. Through our investigation,
132
133
      we have discovered that the high-level representations extracted by the well-trained classifier fˆ exhibit
      significant differences between the non-training and training data (e.g., as graphically illustrated in
134
      Figure 1 (b-c)). This inherent disparity in high-level representations forms the foundation of our
135
      proposed method, which leverages these differences to effectively distinguish between members and
136   non-members. In this study, we broaden the scope of our analysis on high-level representations by
137   incorporating a wider array of statistics derived from these representations, including confidence
138   scores and several widely-recognized metrics within the MIA literature, such as loss value (Yeom
139   et al., 2018b), entropy (Salem et al., 2019a), and likelihood ratio (Carlini et al., 2022).
140
      In summary, we propose a novel and effective test that employs a high-level representation-based
141
      kernel, termed High-level Representation-based MMD (HR-MMD), to assess whether a given privacy-
142   evaluation dataset contains elements from the training set. Unlike existing MIAs, which focus on
143   distinguishing individual data records as members or non-members and often exhibit non-ideal
144   evaluation reliability, our approach is designed to operate at a broader granularity, distinguishing
145   between entire sets of records. Our objective is to achieve high test power and an ideal type I
146   error rate. By developing a non-parametric two-sample test for membership inference, our goal
147   is to establish a robust statistical methodology capable of accurately detecting privacy breaches
148   in hindsight when compromised records have been used to train a third-party model. Extensive
149   experiments across image and non-image datasets demonstrate that our HR-MMD test successfully
150
      meets these objectives, exhibiting high sensitivity in distinguishing between training and non-training
      records.
151
152   Our contribution lies in being the first to introduce a non-parametric two-sample test for membership
153   inference, and we provide theoretical analysis on the asymptotics of HR-MMD to further substantiate
154   its robustness. We emphasize that existing two-sample test methods cannot effectively address the
155   problem of statistical membership inference for privacy breach detection. The effectiveness of our
156
      HR-MMD test stems from two key aspects: the superior performance of high-level representations and
      the novel deep kernel design. Our utilization of these high-level representations and the incorporation
157
      of a high-level representation-based deep kernel in the context of MIAs distinguishes our work from
158
      existing literature. As membership privacy is one of the most established methods for quantifying
159   privacy risks in ML models (Murakonda & Shokri, 2020), we believe our contribution offers valuable
160   insights into an alternative scenario for privacy breach detection and, in the long run, opens up a
161   promising avenue for privacy evaluation based on membership inference tests.

      as the training set ST . The model is then built through a training process that aims to minimize a

      2.2   N ON - PARAMETRIC TWO - SAMPLE TEST
176
177   Let X ⊂ Rd and P, Q be Borel probability measures on X . Given IID samples SX = {xi }ni=1 ∼ Pn
178   and SY = {yj }m          m
                     j=1 ∼ Q , in the two-sample test problem, we aim to determine if SX and SY come
179   from the same distribution, i.e., if P = Q.
180
      We use the null hypothesis testing framework, where the null hypothesis H0 : P = Q is tested
181
      against the alternative hypothesis H1 : P ̸= Q. We perform a two-sample test in four steps: (i)
182
      select a significance level α ∈ [0, 1], (ii) compute a test statistic t̂(SP , SQ ), (iii) compute the p-value
183
      p̂ = PrH0 (T > t̂), i.e., the probability of the two-sample test returning a statistic as large as t̂ when
184   H0 is true, and (iv) finally, reject H0 if p̂ < α.

210   (Chwialkowski et al., 2015), amplitude modulated signals (Gretton et al., 2012b), and challenging
211   image datasets, e.g., the MNIST and the CIFAR-10 (Liu et al., 2020b).

224   3    P ROBLEM S ETTING
225
226   We aim to address the following problem of statistical membership inference for privacy breach
227   detection.
228   Problem 1. Let X be a subset of Rd and P be a Borel probability measure on X , and let ST =
229   {x′i }ni=1 ∼ Pn be IID observations from P. Assume that the privacy evaluator can obtain (i) a
230   well-trained classifier fˆ trained on ST , and (ii) IID observations SX from P, where ST ∩ SX = ∅ (a
231   validation dataset for fˆ). For reasonably large m values, we aim to determine if a given privacy-
232   evaluation dataset SY = {yi }m   i=1 contains elements from the training set ST .
233
234   In Problem 1, if SY does not contain elements from the ST , given a significance level α, we aim to
235   accept the null hypothesis H0 (i.e., SY are not the members of the training set of the classifier fˆ)
236   against the alternative hypothesis H1 (i.e., SY contains members of the training set of the classifier fˆ)
237   with the probability 1 − α. Conversely, if SY contains elements from the ST , we aim to reject the null
238   hypothesis H0 with a probability near to 1. The probability of not committing a type II error is called
239   the power of the hypothesis test. Achieving a high test power requires sufficient samples, which can
240
      accurately reflect the sample distribution. Unsurprisingly, the test power is influenced by the sample
      size (denoted as m in Problem 1). A minimum sample size M can be set to prevent an inadequately
241
      small sample size, leading to unreliable hypothesis testing. More details on this aspect can be found
242
      in our analysis on the test power of our hypothesis under varying sample sizes. Additionally, we
243   discussed the scenario where SY contains out-of-distribution samples in Appendix D. The used
244   important notations can also refer to Table 1.
245
246
      In our framework, we rely on the following assumptions: I. The evaluator has access to the target
      model, which essentially follows the setting of black-box MIAs (see Appendix A for a more detailed
247
      literature review). We believe this is a realistic assumption, whenever the target black-box model can
248
      be queried freely, e.g., in the setting of Machine Learning as a Service (MLaaS), where query results
249   can be stored locally by the evaluator; II. The evaluator has a validation set (SX in Problem 1),
250   which is also a realistic assumption as validation sets are widely used in machine learning (e.g., the
251   local shadow dataset in (Chen et al., 2021)); III. A generalization gap (test error minus training
252   error) holds. Note that a generalization gap has been observed by practitioners during the training
253   of deep learning models (Song et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2019; Murakonda & Shokri, 2020; Zhang
254   et al., 2021). It is a common assumption in MIAs and many works (Shokri et al., 2017b; Yeom et al.,
255   2018b; Salem et al., 2019b; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020b; Chen et al., 2020) have pointed out that
256   overfitting of the target ML models is the main factor contributing to the success of MIAs; IV. All
257
      the data mentioned in Problem 1 are independent and identically distributed (IID) observations.

259   4    L IMITATIONS OF P RIOR R ESEARCH FOR P RIVACY B REACH D ETECTION
260
261   There is a consensus in established computer security practices (Lazarevic et al., 2003; Vangelis et al.,
262   2006; Kolter & Maloof, 2006; Kantchelian et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2017) that designing methods should
263   focus on achieving low false positive rate (FPR). This viewpoint is also supported by previous state-of-
264   the-art MIAs (Carlini et al., 2022), which argue that membership inference should prioritize the true
265   positive rate (TPR) at exceedingly low FPRs, often approaching zero. Such stringent requirements
266
      are essential to ensure the reliability and credibility of privacy breach detections, thereby preventing
      unjustified accusations that could have severe repercussions for stakeholders.
267
268   In alignment with these security standards, our research is premised on the assertion that any detection
269   method exhibiting a non-ideal FPR cannot be deemed successful for evaluating privacy breaches. Our
      objective is to develop privacy breach detection techniques that achieve an ideal TPR of 100% while

289   However, existing MIAs and two-sample tests fall short of meeting these stringent criteria. Traditional

294
      members within a dataset. Nonetheless, this approach proves unreliable because these methods are
295
      constrained by their heuristic foundations, which lack the robust statistical guarantees necessary for
296   high-stakes privacy evaluations.
297
298   Moreover, the inherent design limitations of existing MIAs render them incapable of providing the
299
      ideal FPR required for dependable privacy breach detection. These methods, which rely heavily
      on heuristic motivations—such as the assumption that samples with lower loss values are more
300
      likely to be members—fail to offer the same level of statistical rigor as our proposed HR-MMD
301
      test. Consequently, they invariably result in non-ideal FPRs, which undermine their utility in
302   practical scenarios where false positives must be minimized. Empirical evaluations, detailed in
303   Appendix H.1, demonstrate that existing MIAs including Loss (Yeom et al., 2018b), Entropy (Salem
304   et al., 2019a), Attack R (Ye et al., 2022), and Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA) (Carlini et al., 2022) fail
305   to simultaneously achieve the desired TPR and FPR under conditions where non-training members
306   predominate the dataset. Specifically, as illustrated in Figure 1 (a), these traditional MIA methods are
307   unable to attain an ideal TPR of 100% and an FPR of 0%, primarily due to their disproportionate
308   focus on the characteristics of non-training members, who constitute the majority in such settings.
309   Additionally, while two-sample tests provide a robust statistical framework for assessing whether two
310   datasets originate from the same distribution and for maintaining an ideal FPR, they are unsuitable
311   for addressing the specific challenge of statistical membership inference in privacy breach detection.
312   This limitation is particularly pronounced when both members and non-members are drawn from
313   identical distributions. Such inadequacies reveal a significant gap in existing privacy breach detection
314   methodologies, underscoring the necessity for innovative approaches that can operate effectively at
315   the granularity of record sets while ensuring both high sensitivity and reliability.

      where L(·, ·) denotes the loss function. The condition g > 0 signifies that there is a discrepancy in
335
      the error distributions between the training set (members) and the test set (non-members).
336
337   Based on how machine learning models work, this divergence in error distributions implies that the
338   difference may also exist in other high-level representations. Empirical evidence supporting this phe-
339   nomenon is illustrated in Figures 1 (b-c) and 2, where t-SNE (Maaten & Hinton, 2008) visualizations
340
      of the high-level representations extracted from a well-trained deep neural network (e.g., ResNet-18)
      reveal distinct clustering patterns between members and non-members. Notably, while the raw input
341
      features of these datasets may appear similarly distributed within their respective semantic classes,
342
      the high-level representations exhibit clear separations attributable to the underlying generalization
343   gap. Additional visualizations across different network layers are provided in Appendix F.
344
345   Motivated by these observations, we propose to develop a statistical testing method to distinguish
346
      members from non-members by leveraging the differences in high-level representations. Unlike
      traditional MIAs that rely on specific metrics such as loss values, our high-level representation-based
347
      approach explores a broader spectrum of high-level representations to enhance the discriminative
348
      power between training and non-training sets, and previous statistics like loss, entropy, and likelihood
349   ratio can also be easily included in our framework. This flexibility is particularly advantageous in
350   real-world scenarios where certain information may be unavailable or deliberately obfuscated. For
351   instance, in settings where true labels are inaccessible, making loss-based metrics infeasible, our
352   framework can leverage alternative high-level representations like confidence scores. Furthermore,
353   adversarial strategies such as MemGuard (Jia et al., 2019) introduce perturbations to confidence
354   scores to thwart membership inference, rendering loss-based and confidence score-based methods less
355   effective. In such cases, our HR-MMD test can employ other high-level representations, such as those
356   from the penultimate layer, to maintain robust membership inference capabilities. By accommodating
357
      a diverse array of high-level representations, HR-MMD extends the evaluative toolkit for privacy
      breach detection, ensuring adaptability and resilience across varying operational conditions.

      7                       C ONCLUSION
525
526
      The risk of unsanctioned data accesses, leading to sensitive data being used in third-party predictive
527
      models cannot be ignored. Such accesses may become apparent after the fact, in the models that
528   have been trained on compromised data. This calls for effective membership inference methods,
529   enabling an evaluator to identify privacy breaches1 . Existing membership inference attacks (MIAs) –
530   on whether a given data record may have been used to train a model or not – suffer from non-ideal
531   evaluation reliability. Therefore, a change both in perspective and in approach in necessary, compared
532   to the MIAs’ setting, to enable privacy evaluators to do membership inference for sets of records.
533   In this paper, we propose a novel non-parametric two-sample test, which leverages the differences
534   between high-level representations, from training data and non-training data, to detect whether a set
535   of samples was used for training a given model. Our experiments show that the proposed HR-MMD
536
      test exhibits remarkably high sensitivity in distinguishing between the training and non-training sets,
      with the ideal type I error, making it a powerful membership inference tool for detecting data breaches
537
      from machine learning models.

920
      A.1    P RIVACY ATTACKS IN M ACHINE L EARNING
921
922   Machine learning (ML) models, owing to their deep integration in critical domains like health-
923   care (Miotto et al., 2018), genomics (Xiong et al., 2015), and image recognition (He et al., 2016),
924   hold immense responsibility in safeguarding sensitive information. The success of these models
925   primarily depends on extensive datasets and computational resources (Bengio et al., 2021). However,
926   their capability to memorize training data (Song et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021)
927   opens avenues for various privacy attacks, highlighting the crucial need for robust defenses against
928   privacy breaches.
929   Privacy attacks targeting ML models can be broadly categorized into model extraction attacks (Tramèr
930   et al., 2016), attribute inference attacks (Fredrikson et al., 2015), property inference attacks (Ganju
931   et al., 2018), and membership inference attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017b). These attacks differ
932   in their objectives and methodologies. While model extraction attacks replicate the target model’s
933   functionality, attribute and property inference attacks aim at deducing sensitive information or global
934   properties of the training data, respectively. MIAs, conversely, ascertain whether a particular record
935
      was part of the model’s training data, thereby posing severe privacy risks.
936   MIAs specifically target the data used in the training phase of ML models. The confidentiality
937   breach through MIAs is so significant that it’s recognized by institutions like the National Institute of
938   Standards and Technology (NIST) (Tabassi et al., 2019) and regulations such as the General Data
939   Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Wikipedia, 2021). The genesis of MIAs traces back to the work of
940   Homer et al. (Homer et al., 2008), evolving over time to affect various ML models including, but
941
      not limited to, classification (Shokri et al., 2017b), regression (Gupta et al., 2021), and generation
      models (Hayes et al., 2019). The threat model typically assumes black-box access, where the attacker
942
      only has the model’s prediction outputs.
943
944   Concurrently, research has proliferated around defensive mechanisms aiming to mitigate the risks
945   posed by MIAs, balancing between preserving data privacy and maintaining model utility. These
946   proposed defenses span across diverse methodologies, indicating the depth and complexity involved in
947
      securing ML models against MIAs. As machine learning continues to permeate sensitive applications,
      understanding and mitigating privacy attacks, especially MIAs, become paramount.
948
949
950   A.2    M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE ATTACKS
951
      Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) in machine learning (ML) are pivotal in assessing privacy
952
      risks, where the attacker aims to determine if a specific data instance was used in training an ML
953
      model. Central to MIAs is the exploitation of the model’s learned parameters and predictions.
954   The efficacy of these attacks is closely linked to the adversary’s knowledge, which encompasses
955   insights into the training data distribution and details of the target model, including its architecture
956   and parameters. The classification of MIAs into white-box and black-box attacks hinges on this
957   knowledge spectrum, ranging from full access to the model’s internals to limited information based
958   on prediction outputs (Shokri et al., 2017b).
959
      In binary classifier-based MIAs, the attack employs a classifier trained to distinguish between training
960   data (members) and non-training data (non-members). This classifier’s training involves shadow
961   models to simulate the target model’s behavior, a concept introduced by Shokri et al. (2017b). These
962   shadow models, varying in the amount of accessible information, help construct datasets for training
963   the attack classifier. The sophistication of the attack model differs in white-box and black-box settings,
964   with the former having complete access to the target model, including intermediate computations and
965   gradients, and the latter limited to prediction vectors.
966   Metric-based MIAs, a less complex alternative, infer membership status by calculating metrics on
967   prediction vectors of data records against a predetermined threshold. These MIAs are categorized into
968   several types based on the metric used, such as prediction correctness, loss, confidence, and entropy-
969   based attacks, each with its implications and effectiveness in inferring membership (Yeom et al.,
970   2018b; Salem et al., 2019b). Song & Mittal (2021) improved upon traditional entropy-based MIAs
971   by incorporating ground truth label information into the entropy calculation, making the inference
      more accurate. Recently, advanced MIAs such as Attack R (Ye et al., 2022) and the Likelihood Ratio

988    In addition to instance-level MIAs, there is a growing interest in set-based membership inference

990    Maini et al. (2021) introduced Dataset Inference, providing strong statistical evidence to detect if a
991    given model is derived from its private training data. The key intuition behind their method is that
992    classifiers maximize the distance of training examples from the model’s decision boundaries, while
993    test examples are closer to these boundaries since they have no impact on the model weights.
994    In the context of large language models (LLMs), Maini et al. (2024) introduced dataset inference
995    methods to detect datasets used for training. They utilized adaptively weighted statistics but relied
996    on weak t-tests for distinction. Similarly, Kandpal et al. (2023) proposed User Inference attacks
997    targeting sets of samples to determine if data from specific users were included in the training set.
998    This approach is akin to applying Attack R (Ye et al., 2022) in the context of LLMs. However, in
999    non-large model scenarios, these methods exhibit significant limitations, as the statistical methods
1000   employed may not provide sufficient discriminatory power.
1001   Overall, MIAs represent a significant privacy concern in ML, underscoring the necessity for effective
1002   defense mechanisms. The success of these attacks depends on the adversarial knowledge and the
1003   chosen methodology, ranging from sophisticated binary classifier-based attacks to simpler metric-
1004   based approaches, and extending to set-based attacks in the context of large models. This broad
1005   spectrum of attack strategies and defense considerations in MIAs reflects a dynamic area of research,
1006   essential for safeguarding privacy in the evolving landscape of machine learning and artificial
1007
       intelligence (Shokri et al., 2017b; Song et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2019; Nasr et al., 2019; Murakonda
       & Shokri, 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Song & Mittal, 2021; Ye et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022; Maini

       et al., 2021; Kandpal et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024).

1013   Two-sample tests aim to check whether two datasets come from the same distribution. Traditional
1014   tests such as t-test and Kolmogorov-Smirnov test are the mainstream of statistical applications, but
1015   require strong assumptions on the distributions being studied. Researchers in statistics and machine
1016   learning have been focusing on relaxing these assumptions, with methods specific to various real-
1017
       world domains (Sugiyama et al., 2011; Yamada et al., 2011; Kanamori et al., 2012; Gretton et al.,
       2012a; Jitkrittum et al., 2016; Sutherland et al., 2017; Chen & Friedman, 2017; Ghoshdastidar et al.,
1018
       2017; Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2017; Li & Wang, 2018; Kirchler et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020b). In
1019
       order to involve distributions with complex structure such as images, deep kernel approaches has
1020   been proposed (Sutherland et al., 2017; Wenliang et al., 2019; Jean et al., 2018), the foremost study
1021   has shown that kernels parameterized by deep neural nets, can be trained to maximize test power in
1022   high-dimensional distribution such as images (Liu et al., 2020b). They propose statistical tests of
1023   the null hypothesis that the two distributions are equal against the alternative hypothesis that the two
1024   distributions are different. Such tests have applications in a variety of machine learning problems
1025   such as domain adaptation, covariate shift, label-noise learning, generative modeling, fairness and
       causal discovery (Binkowski et al., 2018; Fang et al., 2020a; Gong et al., 2016; Fang et al., 2020b;

       A.4    M ACHINE U NLEARNING
1030
1031   The right to be forgotten is a legal principle that allows individuals to request the deletion of their
1032   personal data from entities that store it. This right has gained significant attention in recent years due
1033   to concerns over privacy and data protection. To implement the right to be forgotten in the context
1034   of machine learning, it is necessary to ensure that any influence that the data sample may have on
1035   the original model is removed (Cao & Yang, 2015; Villaronga et al., 2018). This process, known as
1036
       machine unlearning, has gained momentum both in academia and industry (Neel et al., 2021; Cao &
       Yang, 2015; Xue et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2018; Du et al., 2019; Ginart et al., 2019; Sommer et al.,
1037
       2020; Golatkar et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020c; Baumhauer et al., 2020; Izzo et al.,
1038
       2021; Neel et al., 2021; Bourtoule et al., 2021).
1039
1040   In practice, implementing machine unlearning can be challenging. The most legitimate approach
1041   is to remove the data sample requested to be deleted (referred to as target data) and retrain the
1042
       machine learning model from scratch. The model generated by this approach is referred to as the
       retrained model. However, this approach can incur high computational overhead. To mitigate this,
1043
       several approximate approaches to machine unlearning have been proposed. The models generated
1044
       by these approaches are referred to as the unlearned models. These techniques aim to minimize the
1045   computational overhead while ensuring that the outputs of the unlearned model and the retrained
1046   model are statistically indistinguishable (Izzo et al., 2021; Cao & Yang, 2015; Baumhauer et al., 2020;
1047   Bourtoule et al., 2021).
1048
       In addition to the approaches of machine unlearning, it is increasingly necessary to conduct research
1049
       on their evaluation. Legislation such as the general data protection regulation (GDPR) in the
1050
       European Union, the california consumer privacy act (CCPA) in California, and the personal
1051   information protection and electronic documents act (PIPEDA) in Canada have legally solidified
1052   the right to be forgotten. These laws provide individuals with the legal framework to exercise
1053   their right to request the deletion of their personal data, which makes the effective evaluation of
1054   machine unlearning approaches necessary. For example, as the regulator, we need to inspect the
1055   privacy protection effectiveness of machine unlearning of artificial intelligence service providers.
1056   Alternatively, as an artificial intelligence service provider who has employed a contractor to complete
1057   the machine unlearning service, we need to inspect the contractor’s performance. Although statistical
1058   indistinguishability is a good measure of machine unlearning approaches in previous research (Izzo
1059
       et al., 2021; Cao & Yang, 2015; Baumhauer et al., 2020; Bourtoule et al., 2021), it is not applicable
       in real-world scenarios where the retrained model is unavailable for evaluators.

1211   As outlined in the main paper, our primary objective is to evaluate whether SY contains elements
1212   from the training set ST , under Assumption IV presented in Section 3 that SY consists solely of
1213   in-distribution samples. In this section, we extend our analysis to encompass scenarios where the
1214   dataset SY may include out-of-distribution (OOD) samples.
1215
1216   D.1    A SSUMPTION OF I N -D ISTRIBUTION S AMPLES
1217
       Initially, our analysis operates under the assumption that SY does not contain any OOD samples.
1218
       This simplification enables the direct application of our proposed HR-MMD test to ascertain whether
1219
       SY contains elements from ST , thereby detecting potential privacy breaches through statistical
1220   membership inference. Under this assumption, traditional two-sample tests (TST) are unnecessary, as
1221   the focus remains exclusively on membership verification within a consistent data distribution. There
1222   is also a common assumption in the field of membership inference (Hayes et al., 2017; Pyrgelis et al.,
1223   2017; Nasr et al., 2018; Rahman et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2018; Yeom et al., 2018b; Truex et al.,
1224   2018; Jia et al., 2019; Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Song & Marn, 2020; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020b;
1225   Rahimian et al., 2020; Song & Mittal, 2021; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
1226   2022; Ye et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022).
1227
1228   D.2    I NCLUSION OF O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION S AMPLES
1229
1230
       Previous MIA methods have exclusively conducted experiments on samples from the same distribution
       to ascertain whether a sample is a training member. However, in practical applications, SY may
1231
       inadvertently include OOD samples. To address this more complex scenario, we propose a two-step
1232
       approach:
1233
1234   •   Detection of OOD Samples: When there is a possibility that SY contains OOD samples, the
1235       initial step involves employing traditional two-sample tests (TST) to evaluate whether SY deviates
1236       from the distribution of SX . This preliminary detection is crucial to ensure the integrity of
1237       subsequent membership inference analyses.
1238   •   Membership Inference on In-Distribution Samples: If the TST confirms that SY does not
1239       include OOD samples, we proceed to apply our HR-MMD test to determine whether SY contains
1240       any elements from ST . This sequential approach ensures that membership inference is conducted
1241       solely on in-distribution data, thereby mitigating the risk of adversarial outcomes arising from the
           presence of OOD samples.

1273   the deliberately generated out-of-distribution non-members successfully deceived the MIAs into

1277   membership inference techniques. By doing so, we can ensure that our HR-MMD test operates under
1278   the correct assumptions, thereby maintaining the integrity and effectiveness of privacy evaluations in
1279   diverse and potentially adversarial environments.

       E    D ISCUSSION ON THE U SE OF H IGH -L EVEL R EPRESENTATIONS
1282
1283
       Unlike traditional MIAs that rely on specific metrics such as loss values, our approach leverages
1284   high-level representations to enhance the discriminative power between training and non-training sets.
1285   Additionally, previous statistics like loss, entropy, and likelihood ratios can be seamlessly integrated
1286   into our framework. This flexibility is particularly advantageous in real-world scenarios where certain
1287   information may be unavailable or deliberately obfuscated.
1288
       For instance, in settings where true labels are inaccessible, making loss-based metrics infeasible, our
1289
       framework can utilize alternative high-level representations such as confidence scores. Furthermore,
1290   adversarial strategies like MemGuard (Jia et al., 2019) introduce perturbations to confidence scores
1291   to thwart membership inference, thereby reducing the effectiveness of loss-based and confidence
1292   score-based methods. In such cases, our HR-MMD test can employ other high-level representations,
1293   such as those from the penultimate layer of a neural network, to maintain robust membership inference
1294   capabilities. By accommodating a diverse array of high-level representations, HR-MMD extends the
1295   evaluative toolkit for privacy breach detection, ensuring adaptability and resilience across varying
       operational conditions.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       Our approach distinctly differs from previous metric-based MIAs (Yeom et al., 2018a; Salem et al.,
1297   2018; Song & Mittal, 2021) in several key aspects. It does not rely on predefined functions and preset
1298   thresholds. Instead, we propose a comprehensive and reliable hypothesis testing framework coupled
1299   with a high-level representation-based deep kernel. Crucially, our method does not merely select an
1300   existing kernel; it innovatively learns one by optimizing its parameters during training. To the best of
1301   our knowledge, we are the first to introduce such a non-parametric two-sample test into the field of
1302   membership privacy.
1303   Our ablation study, presented in Figure 7, demonstrates that using a non-deep kernel-based method
1304   with high-level representations (HR+MMD-G) does not achieve the same effectiveness as our pro-
1305   posed HR-MMD approach. This underscores the superiority of our deep kernel learning methodology
1306   in enhancing membership inference performance.
1307
       While many studies have highlighted the utility of the penultimate layer of Deep Neural Networks
1308
       (DNNs) for tasks such as out-of-Distribution (OOD) detection and adversarial detection (Lee et al.,
1309   2018), our high-level representation-based MMD test offers significant implications beyond mem-
1310   bership inference. Specifically, the objective of OOD detection is to verify whether input testing
1311   data is out of distribution, and the goal of adversarial detection is to identify whether input testing
1312   data comprises adversarial samples. Our work reveals that even when input testing samples are
1313   neither out of distribution nor adversarial, differences in the high-level representations of members
1314   and non-members relative to the same model still exist. This insight has been largely overlooked by
1315   previous detection research.
1316   As membership privacy remains one of the most established methods for quantifying privacy risks
1317   associated with machine learning models, our contribution offers valuable insights into an alternative
1318   scenario for privacy breach detection. By demonstrating that high-level representations can effectively
1319   distinguish between members and non-members, our work provides a robust foundation for enhancing
1320   privacy protection mechanisms in various machine learning applications.

1462   CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) CIFAR-10 is a widely-used dataset consisting of 60,000
1463   color images, each with a resolution of 32 × 32 × 3. The images are evenly distributed across 10
1464   distinct classes, which represent various objects such as airplanes, automobiles, birds, and more.
1465   In our preprocessing, we follow the standard procedure recommended in the PyTorch ResNet
1466   documentation2 , which involves normalizing the pixel values so that the dataset has a mean of zero
1467   and a standard deviation of one. This normalization aids in stabilizing the training process of deep
1468
       neural networks and ensures faster convergence.

       CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) CIFAR-100 shares the same structure and resolution as

1471
       challenge, as it is divided into 100 classes, each containing 600 images. Similar to CIFAR-10, we
1472
       preprocess CIFAR-100 using mean subtraction and standardization, normalizing the pixel values to
1473   achieve a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. This ensures consistency across the datasets
1474   and helps improve model training performance, particularly for complex models like ResNet.

1477   600,000 digit images captured from real-world street views. Each image has a resolution of 32×32×3

1479   backgrounds, and digit orientations. For preprocessing, we apply normalization similar to CIFAR-10

1485   combines CIFAR-10 images with a subset of ImageNet images, providing a larger and more diverse

1492   product categories, with 80,000 samples allocated for training and 20,000 samples designated for

       G.5    C HOICES OF HR-MMD T EST S ERIES
1553
1554   Our approach leverages high-level representations to enhance the discriminative power between
1555   training and non-training sets. Additionally, statistics such as loss value, entropy, and likelihood
1556   ratio can be seamlessly integrated into our framework. This flexibility is particularly advantageous in
1557   real-world scenarios where certain information may be unavailable or deliberately obfuscated.
1558
       For instance, in settings where true labels are inaccessible, rendering loss-based metrics infeasible, our
1559
       framework can utilize alternative high-level representations such as confidence scores. Furthermore,
1560   adversarial strategies like MemGuard (Jia et al., 2019) introduce perturbations to confidence scores
1561   to thwart membership inference, thereby reducing the effectiveness of loss-based and confidence
1562   score-based methods. In such cases, our HR-MMD test can employ other high-level representations,
1563   such as those from the penultimate layer of a neural network, to maintain robust membership inference
1564   capabilities. By accommodating a diverse array of high-level representations, HR-MMD extends the
1565   evaluative toolkit for privacy breach detection, ensuring adaptability and resilience across varying
       operational conditions.

1622   In our experiments comparing the false positive rates and true positive rates between prior Membership
1623   Inference Attacks (MIAs) and our proposed HR-MMD test, we conducted comparisons with four
1624   typical MIA methods: Loss Attack (Yeom et al., 2018b), Entropy Attack (Salem et al., 2019a), Attack
1625   R (Ye et al., 2022), and the Likelihood Ratio Attack (LiRA) (Carlini et al., 2022).
1626   We focused on predicting membership risks for datasets where only a small proportion (10%) of
1627   the records are training members. For the LiRA attack, we selected its offline attack version to save
1628   computational cost. For model training, we followed the process in (Carlini et al., 2022), training
1629   a ResNet-18 model to 60% accuracy on 25,000 CIFAR-10 examples, denoted as ST , which were
1630   randomly selected from the original CIFAR-10 training set. We assumed that the adversary (evaluator)
1631   has access to a non-training set Snon containing the remaining 25,000 examples from the original
1632   CIFAR-10 training set, which were used to train 64 shadow models.
1633   For the baseline MIAs, which output decisions for individual data points, we aggregated their outputs
1634   to the dataset level by computing average statistics such as loss, entropy, or likelihood ratio over
1635   the input test dataset SY , and setting thresholds to make set-level decisions, similar to the approach
1636   used in set-based attacks like (Kandpal et al., 2023; Maini et al., 2024). In the evaluation, when
1637   assessing the false positive rate, we selected SY of size 1,000 from the original CIFAR-10 testing
1638   set (10,000 samples), containing no training members. When assessing the true positive rate, we
1639   selected SY of size 1,000, where 90% of the samples were from the CIFAR-10 testing set and 10%
1640
       were from the training set ST , thereby containing training members. We repeated this full process

1641
       the average statistics. When the average statistics such as loss for one dataset SY was no greater than
1642
       the threshold, we considered that SY contained training members. When the average statistics was
1643   greater than the threshold, we considered that SY contained no training members.

1764   In this section, we demonstrate the applicability of our HR-MMD test within the context of large
1765   language models (LLMs), focusing specifically on dataset inference scenarios as discussed in cutting-

1772   The first involves aggregating features using Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs). Specifically, the

1775   to train a linear regression model, which learns the importance weights for different MIA features to
1776   predict membership status. The second component performs dataset inference using statistical t-tests
1777   to determine whether the suspect set was utilized during the model’s training.

1779   adhere strictly to their experimental setup, utilizing the weighted results from the linear regression
1780   model as our high-level representations. Specifically, we replace the feature extractor ϕp of our
1781   deep kernel in Eq.(5) with these weighted MIA features. Unlike traditional t-tests, our deep kernel,
       equipped with a learning process, effectively captures distributional differences, providing a more

       In this section, we delve into the robustness of our HR-MMD test when confronted with advanced
       membership inference defenses. Our aim is to demonstrate that our method maintains high efficacy
1840
       even when models employ sophisticated techniques to obfuscate membership information. The
1841
       experimental setup aligns with the details provided in Appendix G.6, ensuring consistency and
1842   comparability across our evaluations. To rigorously assess the resilience of our HR-MMD test, we
1843   implemented three prevalent membership inference defense strategies in training ResNet-18 models
1844   on the CIFAR-10 dataset:
1845
       Knowledge Distillation. We utilized a teacher-student framework where the student model learns
1846
       from the softened outputs (soft labels) of a pre-trained teacher model. This approach is intended to
1847
       enhance generalization and obscure membership information by transferring knowledge in a manner
1848   that reduces overfitting to the training data.
1849
1850   L1 Regularization. We incorporated an L1 regularization term into the loss function during training.
1851
       By penalizing the absolute values of the model weights, L1 regularization encourages sparsity in
       the parameters, which can mitigate overfitting and, consequently, reduce the leakage of membership
1852
       information.
1853
1854   Differential Privacy (DP-SGD). We implemented the Differentially Private Stochastic Gradient
1855   Descent (DP-SGD) algorithm (Abadi et al., 2016), setting the privacy budget to ε = 1. DP-SGD
1856   introduces carefully calibrated noise to the gradients during training, providing formal privacy
1857
       guarantees that limit the influence of any individual training sample on the model parameters.
1858   The datasets used in these experiments were partitioned as per Section G.6. Specifically, the training
1859   set ST comprised 25,000 images from CIFAR-10, while the non-training set Snon consisted of 5,000
1860   images disjoint from ST . The input test dataset SY was a size of 1,000 for the main experiments,
1861   with samples drawn either from the training set (for test power assessments) or the testing set (for
1862   type I error evaluations). Our experimental procedure adhered to Algorithm 1, with the evaluation
1863
       phase repeated 100 times to obtain reliable assessments. The rejection rates were calculated based on
       the proportion of times the null hypothesis H0 was rejected across these repetitions.
1864
1865   As shown in Figure 8, The HR-MMD test consistently demonstrated superior performance compared
1866   to traditional two-sample tests across all defense mechanisms. Under knowledge distillation and L1
1867   regularization, our method maintained high test power, effectively detecting the presence of training
1868   members in the input dataset SY . Notably, even under the stringent privacy guarantees of DP-SGD
1869
       with ε = 1, our HR-MMD test achieved significant detection rates, indicating robustness against
       differential privacy defenses. These findings validate the HR-MMD test as a robust and reliable
1870
       method for membership inference in adversarial settings where models employ advanced defense
1871
       strategies. The ability to effectively detect membership information underlines the practical relevance
1872   of our method in privacy risk assessments and reinforces its standing as a state-of-the-art benchmark
1873   in this domain.

1987   In this section, we explore the application of our HR-MMD test to the task of estimating the fraction
1988   of training members within a given input test dataset SY . This task extends beyond traditional binary
1989   membership inference, requiring precise statistical modeling to determine the proportion of members
1990   present in the dataset.
1991

1992   rejection rates that vary with different membership fractions f . In such cases, we can estimate the
1993   fraction directly by mapping the observed rejection rates to the corresponding membership fractions.
1994   This mapping is derived from a calibration curve based on validation data, allowing for accurate
1995   fraction estimation. However, in scenarios where the model is trained without membership inference
1996   defenses, the HR-MMD test tends to be highly sensitive, achieving a 100% rejection rate even at
1997   low membership fractions. In these cases, the rejection rate does not provide granular information
       correlated with f , necessitating an alternative approach for fraction estimation.

2026   In practical applications, datasets often contain out-of-distribution (OOD) samples that deviate
2027   from the data distribution on which a model was trained. The presence of such OOD samples
2028   poses significant challenges for membership inference methods, potentially leading to inaccurate
2029   assessments or false conclusions. To enhance the robustness of our HR-MMD test in these scenarios,
2030   we propose an extended approach capable of effectively managing OOD data.
2031
       Our method involves a two-step process designed to first detect and then mitigate the influence
2032   of OOD samples within the input test data SY . Initially, we employ a traditional two-sample test
2033   between SY and the non-training data Snon to identify any significant deviations from the expected
2034   data distribution. This preliminary detection is crucial for ensuring that subsequent membership
2035   inference analyses are not compromised by the presence of OOD samples.
2036
       Upon detection of OOD samples, we proceed to isolate in-distribution subsets from SY . This can be
2037
       achieved by selecting subsets of SY that are statistically similar to Snon according to the two-sample
2038   test, thereby filtering out the OOD samples. By focusing on these in-distribution subsets, we can
2039   apply the HR-MMD test more effectively to assess the presence of training members without the
2040   confounding effects of OOD data.
2041
       This two-step approach ensures that the influence of OOD samples does not compromise the accuracy
2042
       of the membership inference. By first detecting and then isolating in-distribution data, our method
2043
       maintains reliable detection capabilities even when OOD samples constitute a significant portion of
2044   SY . This enhances the applicability of the HR-MMD test in real-world scenarios, where datasets
2045   may not be clean or fully representative of the training distribution.
2046
2047
       Effectively managing OOD scenarios reinforces the utility of the HR-MMD test as a robust tool for
       privacy risk assessment. By addressing the complexities inherent in real-world data, our method
2048
       provides a more comprehensive and reliable evaluation of membership inference risks, ensuring that
2049
       privacy assessments remain accurate and meaningful even in the presence of diverse data conditions.
