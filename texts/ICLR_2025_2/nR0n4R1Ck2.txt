011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Training and fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) demand significant
014            computational resources and time due to their large model sizes and optimizer
015
               states. To mitigate these challenges and improve accessibility, several memory-
               efficient methods have been developed. Methods such as Low-Rank Adaptation
016
               (LoRA) optimize model weights within a low-rank subspace, while Gradient Low-
017
               Rank Projection (GaLore) projects gradients into a lower-dimensional space to de-
018            crease memory footprint. In this paper, we propose Gradient Subspace Tracking
019            (SubTrack-Grad), a method that confines optimization to a compact core subspace
020            of the gradient matrices and dynamically tracks its changes using the geometry of
021            Grassmannian manifolds. SubTrack-Grad efficiently updates its subspace estima-
022            tion by leveraging estimation errors and previously identified subspaces. Specifi-
023            cally, SubTrack-Grad reduces wall-time by up to 20.57% on GLUE tasks (15%
024            average reduction) and up to 65% on SuperGLUE tasks (22% average reduc-
025            tion). Notably, for a 3B parameter model, GaLore incurred a substantial 157%
026
               increase in wall-time compared to full-rank training, whereas SubTrack-Grad ex-
               hibited only a 31% increase—representing a 49% reduction in wall-time com-
027
               pared to GaLore. Additionally, the memory required for storing optimizer states
028
               in SubTrack-Grad is equivalent to GaLore’s, and it exhibits only a minimal in-
029            crease in peak memory consumption.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved state-of-the-art performance across various tasks
035   and are rapidly growing in popularity. However, their training and fine-tuning demand substantial
036   resources, such as hardware and time, making them impractical for many applications and contribut-
037   ing to a larger carbon footprint (Zhao et al., 2024; Jaiswal et al., 2024; Muhamed et al., 2024; Miles
038   et al., 2024; Modoranu et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). As a result, there is an acute
039
      need to develop memory- and time-efficient methods to democratize their use and mitigate environ-
      mental impact. Various techniques have been proposed to reduce memory usage, such as gradient
040
      checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016) and memory offloading (Rajbhandari et al., 2020). In this con-
041
      text, several Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) approaches aim to reduce memory usage by
042   optimizing a subset of model parameters or operating within a lower-dimensional space (Dettmers
043   et al., 2024; Yaras et al., 2024; Lialin et al., 2023; Renduchintala et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2024; Miles
044   et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2021). Notably, the well-known method LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) decomposes
045   weight matrices into two low-rank trainable matrices, optimizing network parameters within a small
046   subspace, which significantly reduces the memory footprint.
047
      Memory requirements extend beyond trainable parameters, with a significant portion consumed by
048   optimizers for storing element-wise states and parameters (Zhao et al., 2024). To address this, re-
049   cent efforts have focused on reducing the memory footprint of optimizer parameters (Li et al., 2023;
050   Anil et al., 2019; Lv et al., 2024; Dettmers et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024; Modoranu et al., 2024;
051   Zhao et al., 2024; Muhamed et al., 2024). GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024) reduces the memory usage
052   of optimizers by projecting gradient matrices into a low-rank subspace and tracking changes via
053   periodic Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to obtain a rank-r approximation. However, this ap-
      proach faces several challenges. First, SVD is computationally expensive, and if the gradient does

081   To address these challenges, we propose Gradient Subspace Tracking (SubTrack-Grad), a
082

      updates. SubTrack-Grad leverages information from the orthogonal complement to improve sub-
083
      space estimation through simple linear algebra operations, which are computationally more efficient
084
      than GaLore as they avoid periodic SVD on the main gradient matrices. Furthermore, SubTrack-
085   Grad dynamically adapts to changes in the gradient subspace, reducing abrupt shifts in subspace
086   updates for faster convergence. Our main contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We introduce SubTrack-Grad, a computationally and memory-efficient method that
089            projects gradients onto a core subspace and dynamically adjusts this subspace using Grass-
090            mannian manifold geometry, leveraging estimation errors as a signal for adjustment.
091          • We demonstrate that SubTrack-Grad achieves performance comparable to or better than
092            GaLore, with a significant reduction in runtime and minimal memory overhead.
093
             • We show that tracking the gradient subspace helps reduce abrupt changes in the optimiza-
094
               tion process, thereby accelerating convergence.
095
096          • We prove that our method aligns with GaLore’s convergence guarantees while enabling
097            more frequent subspace updates by exercising greater control over subspace adjustments
098
               through the use of prior knowledge and errors from the orthogonal space.

103
104   Several works aim to improve the efficiency of training and fine-tuning LLMs, addressing a growing
105   demand as their popularity rapidly increases. LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), a widely recognized method
106   for reducing the number of trainable parameters, projects model weights into a lower-dimensional
107   space, resulting in two trainable low-rank matrices. This approach optimizes the matrices and sig-
      nificantly reduces memory requirements for fine-tuning large models. Dettmers et al. (2024) builds


                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on LoRA by employing quantization techniques and paged optimizers to further reduce memory us-
109   age. Additionally, Yaras et al. (2024) introduces Deep LoRA, which uses deep matrix factorization
110   for low-rank optimization, addressing overfitting issues and reducing the need for precise tuning
111   of the rank parameter. Several other works have also extended LoRA to enhance the efficiency
112   of training and fine-tuning large models (Lialin et al., 2023; Renduchintala et al., 2024; Xia et al.,
113   2024; Pan et al., 2024). Miles et al. (2024) proposes compressing intermediate activation vectors
114   and reconstructing them during backpropagation to enhance memory efficiency. Additionally, Hao
115   et al. (2024) demonstrates that full-parameter fine-tuning is feasible by using random projections
116   on the gradient matrix, showing that LoRA essentially performs a down-projection of the gradient.
117
      BAdam (Luo et al., 2024) leverages the block coordinate descent framework to achieve low memory
      consumption while maintaining optimization capabilities comparable to Adam.
118
119   Several approaches aim to reduce memory consumption in optimizers, as optimizers like Adam
120   (Kingma & Ba, 2017) account for a significant portion of memory usage due to their storage of
121   element-wise states to improve the optimization process (Li et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2019; Lv et al.,
122   2024; Dettmers et al., 2022). MicroAdam (Modoranu et al., 2024) tackles this issue by compressing
123
      the gradient space for optimization and utilizing the resulting compression error through feedback
      loops to improve the optimization process. Adam-mini (Zhang et al., 2024) partitions model param-
124
      eters into blocks, assigning a single learning rate to each block. This design achieves a significant
125
      reduction in memory usage while maintaining performance. Gur-Ari et al. (2018) suggests that a
126   substantial portion of gradients lies within a small, largely consistent subspace, a finding also re-
127   ported by other studies, including Schneider et al. (2024); Yaras et al. (2023). GaLore (Zhao et al.,
128   2024) leverages this property to reduce the memory requirements of optimizers by projecting gra-
129   dients into a lower-dimensional subspace and then projecting them back for complete parameter
130   tuning. This approach has been effectively integrated with other methods to further reduce memory
131   usage during training and fine-tuning of LLMs (Li et al., 2024). However, not all layers’ gradients
132   evolve within a stable low-rank subspace. Jaiswal et al. (2024) identifies layers with constantly
133   changing gradients where low-rank projection may be inefficient for tuning. By analyzing the dis-
134
      tribution of singular values across different layers, they select those that evolve within a small sub-
      space for fine-tuning while freezing the remaining layers. Gradient Structured Sparsification (Grass)
135
      (Muhamed et al., 2024) further reduces memory usage by applying sparse projection matrices to the
136
      gradient, transforming the gradient matrix into a sparse vector space. This approach leverages sparse
137   representations to significantly decrease the memory footprint. In Ramesh et al. (2024) the authors
138   propose an approach that dynamically selects and updates a small subset of parameters, leading to
139   faster and more memory-efficient training without altering the model’s structure.
140
      When working with high-dimensional data, a common approach is to project the data into a lower-
141
      dimensional space, and many studies focus on tracking these subspaces as they evolve over time.
142
      Balzano et al. (2011) introduces an incremental method for updating subspaces on the Grassmannian
143   manifold when data is partially observed. Zhang & Balzano (2016) and Kasai (2017) address the
144   challenges posed by noise in data streams and evolving environments, proposing methods to handle
145   such noise. Additionally, Blocker et al. (2023) presents a method for time-varying data based on
146   geodesics in Grassmannian space to track changes and update the subspace effectively.

      (Balzano et al., 2011). However, a decreasing step size can impair the ability to accurately track and
364
      adapt to subspace changes. Consequently, SubTrack-Grad uses a constant step size during training
365
      and fine-tuning to effectively adjust subspaces. This approach does not hinder convergence, as
366   proved in Theorem 4.1, which guarantees convergence as long as changes are controlled to maintain
367   the stable subspace assumption.

      6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
526
527
      We proposed a computationally efficient method that projects gradients into a lower-dimensional
528   subspace, and preserves the previously computed subspace to incorporate gradient components from
529   the orthogonal complement to perform rank-1 updates. This approach reduces the frequency of
530   abrupt transitions between iterations and leverages the available information effectively.
531
      In some cases, the extent of changes in the subspace may require updates of rank greater than 1;
532
      the pre-training of Llama-based architectures is a clear example of this need. During our experi-
533
      ments, we observed that applying updates as per equation 7, using the full-rank SVD of the tangent
534   vector from equation 6, can hinder convergence if the singular values of the tangent vector become
535   very small. Furthermore, simultaneously updating vector spaces associated with different singular
536   values caused convergence issues in some cases. Therefore, we restricted updates to rank-1 in this
537   paper, as this approach still enabled SubTrack-Grad to achieve performance comparable to or better
538   than GaLore, with reduced runtime. In future work, we plan to explore increasing the rank of up-
539   dates without compromising convergence. Additionally, dynamically selecting the step size could
      eliminate the need for manual tuning as a hyperparameter and further enhance convergence.
