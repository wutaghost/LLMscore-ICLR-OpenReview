009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose Riemannian Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (RDDPMs)
012            for learning distributions on submanifolds of Euclidean space that are level sets
013            of functions, including most of the manifolds relevant to applications. Existing
014            methods for generative modeling on manifolds rely on substantial geometric in-
015            formation such as geodesic curves or eigenfunctions of the Laplace-Beltrami op-
016
               erator and, as a result, they are limited to manifolds where such information is
               available. In contrast, our method, built on a projection scheme, can be applied to
017
               more general manifolds, as it only requires being able to evaluate the value and
018
               the first order derivatives of the function that defines the submanifold. We provide
019            a theoretical analysis of our method in the continuous-time limit, which elucidates
020            the connection between our RDDPMs and score-based generative models on man-
021            ifolds. The capability of our method is demonstrated on datasets from previous
022            studies and on new datasets sampled from two high-dimensional manifolds, i.e.
023            SO(10) and the configuration space of molecular system alanine dipeptide with
024            fixed dihedral angle.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Diffusion models are generative models that learn data distributions by gradually perturbing data into
029
      noise and then reconstructing data from noise using stochastic processes. Two primary frameworks
030   of diffusion models are Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs;Ho et al. (2020)), where
031   the models are trained to minimize the variational bound in variational inference, and Score-based
032   Generative Models (SGMs;Song & Ermon (2019); Song et al. (2021b)), where the models are trained
033   to learn the score function (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005). Both frameworks have achieved remarkable
034   success in various applied fields.
035
      In recent years, there has been a growing interest in developing generative models for data on mani-
036   folds (De Bortoli et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2023; Chen & Lipman, 2024; Jo & Hwang, 2024). However,
037   existing methods on manifolds rely on substantial geometric information, e.g. geodesics (De Bortoli
038   et al., 2022), heat kernel or its approximations (De Bortoli et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2023), or eigen-
039   functions and (pre)metrics (Chen & Lipman, 2024). As a result, their applications are restricted to
040   manifolds where such information can be obtained.
041
      In this work, we introduce Riemannian Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (RDDPMs), an
042   extension of DDPMs to Riemannian submanifolds. A key ingredient is the projection scheme used
043   in Monte Carlo methods for sampling under constraints (Zappa et al., 2018; Lelièvre et al., 2022),
044   which allows us to develop Markov chains on manifolds with explicit transition densities. The main
045   advantages of our method over existing methods are summarized below.
046
047
      • Our method is developed for submanifolds that are level sets of smooth functions in Euclidean
        space. This general setting includes most of the often studied manifolds such as spheres and
048
        matrix groups. More importantly, it fits well with applications where constraints are involved, e.g.
049
        applications in molecular dynamics and statistical mechanics.
050
051
      • Our method only requires the computation of the value and the first order derivatives of the func-
        tion that defines the submanifold. Therefore, it is applicable to more general manifolds.
052
053   • We present a theoretical analysis for the loss function of our method in the continuous-time limit,
        elucidating its connection to the existing methods (De Bortoli et al., 2022). This analysis also

          shows the equivalence between loss functions derived from variational bound in variational infer-

057   We successfully apply our method to datasets from prior works, and to new datasets from the special
058   orthogonal group SO(10) and from alanine dipeptide with fixed dihedral angle, both of which are
059   challenging for existing methods due to their geometric complexity.

128   3.1   P ROJECTION SCHEME
129
130   We recall a projection scheme from Monte Carlo sampling methods on manifolds (Ciccotti et al.,
131   2008; Zappa et al., 2018; Lelièvre et al., 2022), and we show that it allows us to construct Markov
132   chains on M with tractable transition densities.
133   Given x ∈ M and a tangent vector v ∈ Tx M that is drawn from the standard Gaussian distribution
134   on Tx M, we compute the intermediate state x′ = x + σ 2 b(x) + σv ∈ Rn , where σ > 0 is a
135   positive constant and b : Rn → Rn is a smooth function. In general, x′ does not belong to M. We
136   consider the projection y ∈ M of x′ onto M along an orthogonal direction in the column space of
137   ∇ξ(x). Precisely, the projected state y is found by (numerically) solving the constraint equation for
138   c ∈ Rn−d
139                     y = x + σ 2 b(x) + σv + ∇ξ(x)c, such that ξ(y) = 0 ∈ Rn−d .                         (5)
140   The choice of b will affect the final invariant distribution and the convergence rate to equilibrium (see
141   Section 3.5 for further discussion). There are n − d constraints in equation 5 with the same number
142
      of unknown variables. In particular, when ξ is scalar-valued, i.e. n − d = 1, solving equation 5
      amounts to finding a root of a (nonlinear) scalar function.
143
144   In general, it is possible that for some vectors v there are either no solution or multiple solutions to
145   equation 5. When multiple solutions exist, we assume that the numerical solver finds one solution
146   in a deterministic way. This is true as long as a deterministic solver is adopted with fixed initial
147   condition c = 0. Let Fx,σ be the set of v for which a solution can be found and denote by ϵ(x; σ) =
148
      P(v ∈ / Fx,σ ), i.e. the probability that no solution can be found. Since ϵ(x; σ) = 0 when σ = 0
      (c = 0 is a solution for any v), we can expect that ϵ(x; σ) → 0 as σ decreases to zero. However, we
149
      do not require this assumption in deriving our method. We denote Mx,σ the set of all states in M
150
      that can be reached from x by solving equation 5 with certain v ∈ Fx,σ .
151
152   To derive the transition density of y from x, we notice that, by applying the orthogonal projection
153   matrix P (x) to both sides of equation 5 and using the fact that P (x)∇ξ(x) = 0, we have the relation

      3.5        A LGORITHMIC DETAILS
234
235   The algorithms for training and data generation are summarized in Algorithms 1 and 2, respectively.
236   Algorithm for sampling the forward process, which is similar to Algorithm 2, and algorithm for
237   solving constraint equations are presented in Algorithms 3 and 4 in Appendix B. In the following,
238   we discuss several algorithmic details of our method. Further details are left in Appendix B.
239
240   Generation of trajectory data The optimal parameter θ is sought by minimizing the objective in
241   equation 14, for which trajectory data of the forward process is required as training data. We sample
242   trajectories of the forward process in a preparatory step before training, and train the model with
243   min-batches sampled from this trajectory dataset. The trajectory dataset is updated by re-sampling
244   trajectories every several training epochs (see line 2 and lines 10–12 in Algorithm 1).
245
246   Choice of N , σk , and βk+1 The total number of steps N should be large enough such that the for-
247   ward Markov chain is able to reach equilibrium (approximately) starting from the data distribution.
248   While larger σk , βk+1 allow the Markov chains to make larger jumps, their size should be properly
249   chosen (depending on the manifold) such that the solution to the constraint equations 9 and 11 can
250
      be found with high probability.
251
252   Method for solving constraint equations 9 and 11 As in Monte Carlo sampling methods on
253   submanifolds (Ciccotti et al., 2008; Zappa et al., 2018; Lelièvre et al., 2022), we employ Newton’s
254
      method to solve the constraint equations 9 and 11. This method has quadratic convergence (locally)
      and its implementation is simple. In most cases, a solution with high precision can be found within
255
      a few iteration steps (e.g. less than 5 steps). When no solution is found, one can re-generate the state
256
      or the entire trajectory. Our implementation of Newton’s method is summarized in Algorithm 4.
257
258
      Choices of b and sampling of the prior p(x(N ) ) For relatively simple manifolds, we can for
259
      simplicity set b = 0 and choose the prior (see line 2 in Algorithm 2) as the uniform distribution on
260
      M. When M is non-compact or when the convergence of Markov chain to equilibrium is slow with
261   b = 0, we can choose non-zero b such as b = −∇V , i.e. the (full space) gradient of a function
262   V : Rn → R in the ambient space. In this case, sampling the prior can be done by simulating
263   a single long trajectory of the forward process. See Section 6.4 and Appendix B.4 for a concrete
264   choice of nonzero b in practice.

342   5   R ELATED WORK
343
344   Denoising diffusion probabilistic models DDPMs (Ho et al., 2020) employ a forward Markov
345   chain to perturb data into noise and a reverse Markov chain to incrementally recover data from noise.
346   The models are trained to minimize a variational bound on the negative log-likelihood. DDPMs
347   have made remarkable achievement in generative modeling on Euclidean space. However, to our
348   knowledge, prior to this study there was no successful extension of DDPMs to manifolds.
349
350   Diffusion models on manifolds Riemannian Score-based Generative Models (RSGMs;De Bortoli
351   et al. (2022) extend SGMs to Riemannian manifolds. A major difficulty of RSGMs is due to the fact
352   that the denoising score-matching objective involves heat kernel, which is not known analytically
353   except for very special manifolds. In addition, RSGMs also require sampling of geodesic curves
354   on manifolds. De Bortoli et al. (2022) and Lou et al. (2023) proposed to approximate heat kernel
355   by eigenfunction expansion or Varadhan’s approximation, but these approximations bring in extra
356   errors. In contrast, our method requires neither geodesic curves nor heat kernel.
357   The Riemannian Diffusion Model (RDM;Huang et al. (2022)) adopts a variational diffusion model
358   framework on Riemannian manifolds. Similar to our method, RDM framework considers submani-
359   folds embedded in an Euclidean space and it also utilizes a variational upper bound on the negative
360   log-likelihood as loss function. However, RDM requires to sample SDEs on manifolds using closest-
361   point projection, which in general may be a difficult task. In contrast, the projection adopted in our
362   method is applicable to general submanifolds and the computational cost is lower.
363
364   Flow-based generative models on manifolds Riemannian continuous normalizing flow models
365   (Lou et al., 2020; Mathieu & Nickel, 2020; Rozen et al., 2021; Ben-Hamu et al., 2022; Chen &
366   Lipman, 2024) extend the continuous-time flow framework (Chen et al., 2018) to manifolds. In
367   particular, the methods proposed in Rozen et al. (2021); Ben-Hamu et al. (2022); Chen & Lipman
368   (2024) are simulation-free for manifolds with simple geometry. The method in Chen & Lipman
369   (2024) can deal with manifolds with general geometry, but it is not feasible for high-dimensional
370   manifolds, where eigenfunctions of the Laplace-Beltrami operator are typically difficult to compute.
371   Our method requires sampling trajectories in order to evaluate loss function. However, the cost for
372
      trajectory simulation can be alleviated by working with a pre-prepared trajectory set that is updated
      during training with a tunable frequency. Our method does not use eigenfunctions nor metrics and
373
      it can be easily applied to high-dimensional manifolds.
374
375
376   Markov Chain Monte Carlo on submanifolds Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) under
377   equality constraints has been studied in several works (Zappa et al., 2018; Lelièvre et al., 2019;
      2022). We adopt the same projection scheme from these works to develop our method.

      We point out a general issue regarding model evaluation on these datasets. We notice that in each
391   dataset there are a few data points (i.e. isolated points) whose vicinity contains no other points,
392   and the size of dataset is relatively small compared to its complex distribution. For such datasets,
393   standard dataset splitting for cross-validation results in non-overlapping training/validation/test sets,
394   whose empirical distributions are considerably different. As a consequence, computing the NLL on
395   the test set either results in an overconfident assessment of the model (when the test set contains
396   no isolated points) or requires evaluating the model on isolated points that are completely unseen
397   during training (when the test set contains isolated points).
398   We argue that the phenomenon discussed above is general and should appear regardless of the meth-
399   ods employed. As a simple solution, we propose to identify the isolated points in validation and
400   test sets and include them into the training set. In particular, evident improvement is achieved when
401   isolated points are included in training set, as shown in the last line of Table 1. Further discussions
402   are provided in Appendix B.1.

533
534   We have proposed Riemannian Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models for generative modeling
535   on submanifolds. Our method does not rely on sophisticated geometric objects on manifold and it is
536   applicable to high-dimensional manifolds with nontrivial geometry. We have provided a theoretical
537   analysis of our method in the continuous-time limit, which elucidates its connection to Rieman-
538   nian score-based generative models. We have demonstrated the strong capability of our method on
539   datasets from previous studies and from high-dimensional manifolds that can not be easily studied
      by existing methods.
