011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Statistical data heterogeneity is a significant barrier to convergence in federated
014            learning (FL). While prior work has advanced heterogeneous FL through better
015            optimization objectives, these methods fall short when there is extreme data het-
016
               erogeneity among collaborating participants. We hypothesize that convergence
               under extreme data heterogeneity is primarily hindered due to the aggregation of
017
               conflicting updates from the participants in the initial collaboration rounds. To
018
               overcome this problem, we propose a warmup phase where each participant learns
019            a personalized mask and updates only a subnetwork of the full model. This per-
020            sonalized warmup allows the participants to focus initially on learning specific
021            subnetworks tailored to the heterogeneity of their data. After the warmup phase, the
022            participants revert to standard federated optimization, where all parameters are com-
023            municated. We empirically demonstrate that the proposed personalized warmup
024            via subnetworks (FedPeWS) approach improves accuracy and convergence speed
025            over standard federated optimization methods.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Federated learning (FL) is a distributed learning paradigm where participants collaboratively train a
031   global model by performing local training on their data and periodically sharing local updates with
032   the server. The server, in turn, aggregates the local updates to obtain the global model, which is
033
      then transmitted to the participants for the next round of training (McMahan et al., 2017). While
      FL preserves data confidentiality by avoiding collating participant data at the server, statistical
034
      heterogeneity between local data distributions is a significant challenge in FL (Kairouz et al., 2021).
035
      Several attempts have been made to tackle heterogeneity via federated optimization algorithms (Wang
036   et al., 2019; Khaled et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020c;b; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Tupitsa et al., 2024;
037   Sadiev et al., 2022; Beznosikov et al., 2021), dropout (Horvath et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2022), and
038   batch normalization (Li et al., 2021d).
039
      Consider the scenario where multiple hospitals collaborate to learn a medical image classification
040
      model that works across imaging modalities and organs, where the data from each hospital pertains
041
      to a different modality (e.g., histopathology, CT, X-ray, ultrasound, etc.) and/or organ (e.g., brain,
042   kidney, colon, etc.). Most of the existing heterogeneous FL algorithms fail when there is such
043   extreme data heterogeneity among collaborating participants, especially when the model is learned
044   from scratch (with random initialization). The main reason for this failure is the high degree of
045   conflicts between the local updates during the initial collaboration rounds. While enforcing a strong
046   regularization constraint on the local updates (Li et al. (2020b)) can partially alleviate this problem, it
047   dramatically slows down local learning and hence, convergence speed.
048   In this work, we explore an alternate approach to minimize the initial conflicts between heterogeneous
049   participants by allowing participants in FL to initially train a partial subnetwork using only their
050   local datasets. This warmup phase enables the participants to focus first on learning their local data
051   well before engaging in broader collaboration. Thus, our proposed approach can be summarized as
052   follows (see Figure 1). Initially, each participant uses a personalized binary mask tailored to their
053   data distributions, allowing them to first learn their local data distributions and optimize their local
      (sparse) models. During this warmup phase, participants transmit only their masked updates to the

089   2      R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Collaborative Learning. FL is a distributed learning paradigm that addresses data confidentiality
092   concerns (Kairouz et al., 2021), particularly in environments where data can not be centralized due
093   to regulatory or practical reasons (Albrecht, 2016). One of the seminal FL algorithms, FedAvg
094   (McMahan et al., 2017), involves participants training models locally on their data and periodically
095   transmitting their model parameters to a central server. The server averages these parameters to update
096   the global model, which is then redistributed to the participants for further local refinement. FedAvg
097   has inspired a plethora of variants and extensions aimed at enhancing performance (Karimireddy
098   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020b; Mishchenko et al., 2022), scalability (Guo et al., 2023; Al-Shedivat
099
      et al., 2021), communication efficiency (Ullah et al., 2023; Rahimi et al., 2023; Isik et al., 2023),
      privacy/confidentiality (Tastan & Nandakumar, 2023; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Ullah et al., 2023),
100
      robustness (Li et al., 2019), and fairness (Xu et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2023; Tastan et al., 2024).
101
      For example, strategies such as weighted averaging or adaptive aggregation have been proposed
102   to accommodate the non-i.i.d. nature of distributed data sources − a scenario where data is not
103   identically distributed across all participants, which can significantly hinder model performance (Li
104   et al., 2020b; Wang et al., 2020b; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021d; Wang et al., 2020a).
105   Specifically, FedProx (Li et al., 2020b) addresses data heterogeneity by integrating a proximal term
106   into the FedAvg framework. There is also a body of work that focuses on addressing the heterogeneity
107   problem through personalization-based approaches, utilizing local-centric objectives (Gasanov et al.,
      2022; Hanzely et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021c).

      Independent Subnet Training. Independent subnet training (IST) is a variant of distributed learning
109   that focuses on enhancing model personalization and reducing communication overhead by training
110   separate subnetworks for different participants (Yuan et al., 2022). IST distributes neurons of a fully
111   connected neural network disjointly across different participants, forming a group of subnets. Then,
112   each of these subnets is trained independently for one or more local SGD steps before synchronization.
113   In every round, after broadcasting the server weights, each participant gets updated neurons to focus
114   on, and the local subnet training continues. This approach led to different works along the line of
115   using subnetwork training for efficiency (Horvath et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2021;
116   Nader et al., 2020; Alam et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021a; Mozaffari et al., 2021) in FL. In our work, we
117
      adopt IST’s core principle of selecting neurons rather than focusing on weight values, which in turn
      narrows the search space. A key distinction between our method and IST lies in how the neurons are
118
      selected and the necessity of covering all neurons. Whilst IST typically involves random sampling
119
      of masks in each training round by the server and full coverage of neurons, we do not randomly
120   sample neurons; instead, we use a learnable mask for each participant that is trained along with the
121   parameters, and we relax the assumption of full coverage of neurons.
122
123   Finding Subnetworks in FL. Another relevant idea is the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) (Frankle
124   & Carbin, 2019), which attempts to identify subnetworks within a larger network. LTH is a model
125   personalization technique, which focuses on sparsifying the network to create a smaller-scale version
126   that improves per-round communication efficiency. In contrast to LTH, our method is directed towards
127   training a shared global model and simultaneously improving convergence speed (reducing number of
128   communication rounds). After LTH, there has been a growing interest in finding sparse and trainable
129   networks at initialization (Mellor et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020a). Recently, in (Isik et al.,
130   2023), sparse networks were found inside the main model to increase communication efficiency in
131
      FL. The proposed FedPM method focuses on finding a subnetwork by freezing the model weights
      and training for masks on a weight level, in contrast to IST, which works on a neuron level. FedPM
132
      utilizes the sigmoid function to obtain probability values from unbounded mask scores and then uses
133
      Bernoulli sampling to obtain binary masks. We use a similar approach in our FedPeWS algorithm to
134   learn the neuron-level personalized masks.

206   In the FedAvg algorithm, the number of communication rounds necessary to achieve a certain
207   precision is directly proportional to the heterogeneity measure (Li et al., 2020c). Notably, this
208   relationship holds true in convex settings; however, in non-convex scenarios, the algorithm may
209   either not converge or may converge to a suboptimal solution. Stemming from this observation, our
210
      objective is to reduce the number of requisite communication rounds to achieve convergence, while
      simultaneously achieving a better solution.

      Identification of personalized subnetworks: It is not straightforward to directly optimize for the
250   personalized binary (discrete) masks mi in Equation 3. Hence, we make the following design choices.
251   Firstly, personalized masks are learned at the neuron-level and then expanded to the parameter-level.
252   Following IST (Yuan et al., 2022), masks are specifically applied only to the hidden layer neurons,
253   while the head and tail neurons remain unaffected. However, unlike IST, the neuron-level masks
254   are not randomly selected in each collaboration round. Instead, we learn real-valued personalized
255   neuron-level mask score vectors si ∈ Rh , which in turn can be used to generate the binary masks.
256   Here, h denotes the number of hidden neurons in the classifier M and h ≪ d. A higher value of
257   element si (ℓ), ℓ ∈ [h], indicates that the ℓth neuron is more likely to be selected by participant i. Let
258
      G : Rh → {0, 1}d be the mask generation function that generates the binary parameter-level masks
      mi from neuron-level mask score vectors si , i.e., mi = G(si ). G consists of three steps. Firstly, we
259
      convert si into probabilities by applying a sigmoid function, i.e., θi = σ(si ), where θi ∈ [0, 1]h is the
260

261   sampling from a Bernoulli distribution with parameter θi , i.e., m̃i (ℓ) ∼ Bernoulli(θi (ℓ)), ∀ ℓ ∈ [h].
262   Finally, these binary neuron masks can be directly mapped to the binary parameter-level mask mi ,
263   i.e., if a neuron is selected, all the weights associated with the selected neuron are also selected. Thus,

533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we introduced a novel concept called personalized warmup via subnetworks for
536   heterogeneous FL − a strategy that enhances convergence speed and can seamlessly integrate with
537   existing optimization techniques. Results demonstrate that the proposed FedPeWS approach and
538   achieves higher accuracy than the relevant baselines, especially when there is extreme statistical
539   heterogeneity. Limitations of FedPeWS include the need to tune two additional hyperparameters (no.
      of warmup rounds and mask diversity weight) and the lack of theoretical convergence analysis.

960   column corresponds to different fully connected layers (FC1 to FC4) in the network. Observations
961   are as follows:
962
963         1. Before switching (t ≤ W ): for Participant i, subnetwork i shows higher activation patterns
964            in all given FC layers, i ∈ [1, 2], while the other subnetwork exhibits a minimal activation.
965         2. After switching to full mask (t > W ): (i) there is a noticeable increase in activations for
966            both participants upon switching to using full masks, (ii) Participant 1 with its originally
967            initialized subnetwork 1, shows a substantial increase in activations compared to subnetwork
968            2 across all layers. The same pattern is obesrved for Participant 2 with subnetwork 2.
969

971   follow when specific data points are fed into the network. This supports the superiority of our method
