009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Model merging based on task vectors, i.e., the parameter differences between
012            fine-tuned models and a shared base model, provides an efficient way to integrate
013            multiple models without retraining. This approach can be used to combine task-
014            specific models into a multitask model, improve generalization, or address model
015            deficiencies. One of the significant challenges faced by model merging is the con-
016            flicts between task vectors. Existing works aim to mitigate these conflicts through
017            sparsification; however, two issues observed in our experiments significantly limit
018
               their performance: high parameter overlap and unbalanced weight distribution.
               To address these issues, we propose a simple yet effective framework called CABS
019
               (Conflict-Aware and Balanced Sparsification), consisting of Conflict-Aware Spar-
020
               sification (CA) and Balanced Sparsification (BS). CA can reduce parameter over-
021            lap by applying masks during sequential pruning, ensuring that each task vector
022            retains distinct, non-overlapping parameters. BS leverages n:m pruning to pre-
023            serve critical weights while maintaining an even distribution across layers. Our
024            comprehensive experiments demonstrate that CABS outperforms state-of-the-art
025            methods across a range of diverse tasks and model sizes. Notably, in experiments
026            with 7B-parameter language models, CABS surpasses the average performance
027            of an “ideal” model, a virtual model that selects the highest score from individual
028            fine-tuned models for each task (CABS: 76.50 vs. Ideal Model: 76.30 vs. Base-
029
               line: 76.02 vs. Fine-tuned Model: 75.86). Our results highlight the importance
               of addressing both high parameter overlap and unbalanced weight distribution to
030
               achieve robust and high-performance model merging.

      Model merging has gained increasing attention in the deep learning community, particularly in the
036   context of using task vectors for model merging in large language models (LLMs) (Ilharco et al.,
037   2022; Li et al., 2023; Wortsman et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Matena & Raffel, 2022; Singh & Jaggi,
038   2020; Akiba et al., 2024). This technique has become especially popular for merging homologous
039   models—those fine-tuned from the same base models—to create better-performing models. Many
040   top-performing models on the LLM leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023) are built by fine-tuning base
041   models and subsequently merging them to optimize task-specific performance. Additionally, major
042   enterprises have employed model merging techniques in the development of pretraining models,
043   such as LLaMA3 (Dubey et al., 2024) and Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024), to enhance
044
      generalization capabilities and improve performance across a range of tasks.
045   Recent studies have further shown that sparsifying task vectors before merging can mitigate pa-
046   rameter conflicts between different task vectors, leading to measurable improvements in merging
047   performance (Yu et al., 2024; Yadav et al., 2024; Davari & Belilovsky, 2023; He et al., 2024). These
048   conflicts can be categorized into two types: (a) conflicts due to redundant parameters, where pa-
049   rameters that contribute little to performance are unnecessarily retained, and (b) conflicts due to
050
      overlapping parameters, where task vectors retain parameters that overlap, potentially with signifi-
      cantly different magnitudes or signs. These overlaps make the merging process less efficient.
051
052   Sparsifying can be achieved by selectively or randomly dropping part of a task vector. This process
053   is similar to one-shot pruning, with the former aiming to reduce conflicts in model merging and
      the latter targeting model compression. Magnitude-based pruning (Liang et al., 2021) is one of the

      Our research explores the reasons behind this discrepancy, especially in a setting where magnitude-
      based pruning is expected to perform well. Addressing these issues is key to advancing model
087
      merging and developing high-performance merged models. Specifically, by analyzing the weight
088

089   tified two key factors contributing to the underperformance of magnitude-based pruning:
090
091   High Parameter Overlap: After magnitude-based pruning, the retained weights of different task
092
      vectors often exhibit significant overlap, particularly compared to random methods like DARE. This
      leads to increased conflicts between task vectors during model merging, ultimately affecting the
093
      resulting model performance.
094
095   Unbalanced Weight Distribution: Magnitude-based pruning tends to distribute retained weights
096   unevenly across the model’s weight matrices, with some regions retaining significantly more weights
097   than others. After pruning, the model merging process applies a uniform rescaling factor globally
098
      across the model to restore performance. However, this process amplifies the existing imbalance,
      ultimately leading to suboptimal performance. In contrast, random pruning methods like DARE can
099
      avoid this problem, which maintain better balance across the model by distributing weights more
100
      uniformly.

142   2   R ELATED W ORK
143
144   Model merging has become a vital strategy for combining multiple fine-tuned models into a single
145   multitask model without requiring additional training. Fine-tuned models from the same pre-trained
146   model often share part of the optimization trajectory, making them suitable for merging. This pro-
147   cess can enhance performance on target tasks, improve out-of-domain generalization, and support
148   applications such as federated learning, model compression, and continual learning.
149
      The simplest merging technique involves directly averaging the model parameters (Izmailov et al.,
150   2018; Wortsman et al., 2022). However, this naive approach often fails to account for task-specific
151   variations, leading to suboptimal performance. A more refined approach, Task Arithmetic (Ilharco
152   et al., 2022), was introduced as a pioneering method in the realm of task vector-based merging. In
153   Task Arithmetic, task vectors—computed as the difference between fine-tuned model parameters
154   and their initial pre-trained values—are combined using weighted sums to create a multitask model.
155   However, it struggle with issues such as parameter redundancy and sign conflicts.
156   To address some of these issues, TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2024) introduces a more sophisticated
157   approach that operates in two key ways: first, by pruning parameters that are not significantly im-
158   pactful, thereby reducing the influence of redundant parameters; and second, by resolving sign con-
159   flicts during the merging process. This dual approach minimizes interference between task vectors
160   and ensures that the most critical parameters are preserved and properly aligned during the merge.
161   DARE (Yu et al., 2024), a technique inspired by Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), reveals the high
      redundancy in task vectors by randomly dropping 90% of the parameters and rescaling the remaining


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      ones. Using random pruning, DARE has been shown to outperform magnitude-based pruning meth-
163   ods in model merging. However, DARE does not fully explain the reasons for this improvement.
164   Our analysis suggests that DARE helps mitigate some of the overlap and imbalance. Nevertheless,
165   the random nature of the approach may potentially sacrifice precision. Other categories approaches,
166   such as Evolutionary Model Merge(Akiba et al., 2024) and Pack of LLMs(Mavromatis et al.,
167   2024), are detailed in the AppendixA.17.
168
      Network pruning techniques, particularly magnitude pruning (Zhu & Gupta, 2018), have been
169   extensively studied for their role in optimizing model performance and reducing computational
170   costs (Liu et al., 2019; Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Gale et al., 2019; Zhu & Gupta, 2018). Magni-
171   tude pruning retains parameters based on their magnitude, assuming that larger magnitudes corre-
172   spond to more critical information (Kovaleva et al., 2021; Puccetti et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2023).
173   However, when applied in the context of model merging, this approach can lead to an unbalanced
174   distribution of retained weights, which exacerbates conflicts during the merging process and results
175   in suboptimal performance.
176   To address this issue, while n:m pruning (Zhou et al., 2021; Xia et al., 2022)was originally de-
177   signed for structured pruning and inference acceleration, we discovered that it can be repurposed
178   to control the balance of sparsified task vectors in model merging. Although n:m pruning may not
179   perform as well as unstructured pruning in traditional scenarios, our findings demonstrate that it
180   effectively mitigates weight imbalance, leading to improved performance in merged models. This
181   insight forms a key contribution of our work, highlighting the potential of n:m pruning in enhancing
182   model merging outcomes.
183   Our proposed CABS method builds upon prior works by introducing CA, a novel approach designed
184   to eliminate parameter overlap during model merging. Additionally, it repurposes the existing n:m
185   pruning technique to mitigate unbalanced weight distribution. Together, CABS effectively enhances
186   the stability and performance of model merging.

243   To address the aforementioned issues, we propose the CABS (Conflict-Aware and Balanced Spar-
244
      sification) framework. As illustrated in Figure 1, CABS resolves parameter conflicts and ensures

245
      integrates two core strategies: Conflict-Aware Sparsification (CA) and Balanced Sparsification (BS),
246
      which will be detailed in the following sections. Algorithm 1 demonstrates how these strategies are
247   implemented in CABS.

260   4.2   C ONFLICT-AWARE S PARSIFICATION (CA)
261
262   Motivation. During model merging, overlapping task vectors can lead to performance degradation
263   of the merged model because different task vectors may update the same parameters inconsistently,
264   often with differing magnitudes or signs.By minimizing these overlaps, it is expected to enhance the
265   stability and performance of the merged model.
266   Sequential Pruning and Mask Application. CA aims to eliminate parameter overlap during model
267   merging by employing a sequential pruning strategy. The process begins with the first vector τA
268   being pruned, producing a mask maskA that marks the positions of the retained weights. This mask
269   is then used to guide the pruning of the second task vector τB , ensuring that there is no overlap
      between the parameters of τA and τB .


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      For the second task vector τB , the prior mask maskA is applied in an inverted form to determine the
271   remaining weights that do not overlap with the first pruned task vector. Specifically, the remaining
272   weights of τB are calculated as:
273
274
                                       τB remaining = τB ⊙ (1 − maskA ).
275   This ensures that only the non-overlapping weights in τB are retained in the subsequent pruning
276   process. Afterward, a second round of pruning is performed on τB remaining , generating a new sparse
277   mask maskB , which can then be merged with the prior pruned task vector without any parameter
278   overlap.
279   Minimizing Overlap When Sparsity Limits are Exceeded. When the sum of the sparsity levels
280   across all task vectors exceeds 1 (e.g., when each vector retains 75% of its parameters), it becomes
281   impossible to achieve zero overlap. In such cases, the objective shifts from eliminating overlap to
282   minimizing it as much as possible. Additional pruning steps are applied selectively to reduce the
283   extent of overlap between task vectors. For the detailed implementation of this process, please refer
284   to Appendix A.3.
285
286   4.3   BALANCED S PARSIFICATION (BS)
287
      Motivation. While CA can effectively reduce overlap, it does not address the imbalance in weight
288
      distribution that can arise within task vectors. These imbalances often lead to suboptimal perfor-
289
      mance in the merged model, affecting both its stability and efficiency. To mitigate this problem, we
290   propose the Balanced Sparsification (BS) strategy, which enhances CA by addressing these imbal-
291   ances and further improving the model’s overall performance.
292
293
      Balanced Sparsification. In BS, the weight matrix is divided into disjoint blocks of m consecutive
      weights, and within each block, the n weights with the largest absolute magnitude are retained,
294
      while the rest are pruned. This strategy is applied uniformly across all layers to ensure a more
295
      even weight distribution within each task vector. Minimizing imbalances prevents performance
296   degradation of the merged models. For a more detailed discussion about the differences between
297   Balanced Sparsification (BS) and n:m pruning, please refer to Appendix A.4.
298
299
      We also discuss the flexibility and efficiency of the CABS framework in Appendix A.5. CABS can
      be integrated with other model merging techniques, where CA and BS can be applied independently
300
      or combined with other approaches to further enhance model merging. Additionally, our analysis
301
      of computational cost shows that CABS introduces virtually no additional overhead compared to
302   standard merging methods, making it an efficient and adaptable solution for various model merging
303   scenarios.

      6   C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this work, we identified the issues of high parameter overlap and unbalanced weight distribution
530   in task vector sparsification. We then proposed the CABS framework to address these challenges in
531   model merging. CABS effectively reduces overlap and ensures a more balanced distribution of re-
532   tained weights, resulting in improved performance across various tasks and model sizes. The CABS
533   framework can be integrated into existing model merging techniques. Extensive experiments on both
534   small- and large-scale models demonstrated CABS’s effectiveness in improving model performance
535   and maintaining model generalization. We also conducted a detailed analysis of CABS’s compo-
536   nents, providing insights into its robust handling of sparsification challenges in model merging. For
537   a discussion on limitations and future work, see Appendix A.15.

705
706   The overlap rate between two task vectors is a metric used to quantify the extent to which the
707   same parameters are retained after pruning. This metric is particularly useful in understanding how
708   pruning strategies impact the sharing of model parameters across different tasks, which can lead to
709   conflicts during model merging.
710   The overlap rate is calculated as follows: Given two task vectors τA and τB , the overlap rate is
711   defined as the ratio of the number of shared non-zero parameters to the total number of non-zero
712   parameters in the first task vector τA . Mathematically, this can be expressed as:
713
714
                                                                                                                                                                    |τA ∩ τB |
                                                                                                                  Overlap Rate =
715                                                                                                                                                                    |τA |
716   where |τA ∩τB | represents the count of non-zero parameters that are common to both vectors τA and
717   τB , and |τA | denotes the total count of non-zero parameters in vector τA . This calculation shows the
718   extent of overlap between two task vectors. A higher overlap rate means more shared parameters,
719   increasing the potential for conflicts during model merging.

      In this section, we provide the detailed algorithm for Low-Overlap Sparsity in Algorithm 2, designed
834   to minimize direct conflicts during the model merging process. The algorithm sequentially applies
835   sparsification to task vectors, ensuring that the non-overlapping portions of the task vectors are
836   prioritized, thereby reducing coupling and conflict between different tasks in the final merged model.
837
838
      Algorithm 2 CABS Implementation:minimize overlap rate
839
840   Input: Task vectors τA , τB , base model Wbase , sparsity level n , m, rescale factors λA , λB
841
      Output: Merged model parameters Wfinal
       1: Apply n:m pruning to τA and compute maskA                                            // include BS
842
       2: Compute initial maskB = 1 − maskA , retaining non-overlapping regions of τB
843
       3: If initial maskB retains less than n ÷ m of weights, update maskB by including additional
844       weights from the overlapping region maskA ⊙ τB until the target sparsity n ÷ m is reached
845    4: Merge the pruned vectors with the base model:

849        5: return Wfinal

      A.4                      C OMPARISON OF N : M PRUNING AND BS
854
      Although both n:m pruning and BS employ the same operation—selecting the top n values out of
855
      m consecutive weights based on magnitude—their goals and use cases differ:
856
857   - Goal: The primary goal of n:m pruning is to achieve model compression and acceleration by
858   reducing computational and memory costs. In contrast, BS is designed to maintain a balanced
859   distribution of task vectors while minimizing conflicts between them during merging, not to merely
860
      discard unimportant weights.
861   - Result: n:m pruning is typically used for structured pruning in models, aiming to reduce inference
862   time and memory usage. On the other hand, BS is applied specifically to task vectors. After the task
863   vectors are merged with a base model, the resulting model remains dense, meaning that the practical
      computation and memory savings are not realized, but the model gains improved capacity.


                                                                                                                                      16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      - Sparsity Ratios: n:m pruning often uses configurations like 2:4 or 4:8, where the sparsity level is
865   generally around 50%. In contrast, the sparsification of task vectors under BS can involve much
866   higher sparsity levels, as can be seen in Table 11 (Appendix A.13), with configurations such as
867   64:256 at 75% sparsity.
868
      - Effectiveness: Typically, n:m pruning yields lower performance compared to magnitude pruning
869
      in compression tasks, as the more strict uniform distribution of sparsity across blocks (e.g., every
870
      4 weights) tends to hurt performance. However, in model merging, n:m sparsity can outperform
871   row-wise or layer-wise magnitude pruning due to its more balanced distribution.

      A.5   F LEXIBILITY, I NTEGRATION , AND E FFICIENCY
874
875   Flexibility and Integration of CABS. The CABS framework offers flexibility in its integration with
876   existing model merging techniques. CA and BS can be applied independently or in combination
877   with other approaches. For instance, CA can be combined with other sparsification strategies (e.g.,
878   DARE, magnitude) to minimize parameter overlap, while BS can implement n:m sparsity using
879   different importance metrics beyond magnitude. Additionally, at lower sparsity levels, CABS can
880   be effectively combined with techniques like Ties-merging to solve sign conflict, making it adaptable
881   to various merging scenarios.
882   Efficiency and Complexity. Model merging, as implemented in toolkits like MergeKit (Goddard
883   et al., 2024), inherently has low computational overhead since it bypasses full model retraining. In
884   CABS, we introduce minimal additional cost to the merging process. The Conflict-Aware (CA)
885   strategy modifies the pruning process from parallel to sequential, with the addition of a mask inver-
886   sion and element-wise product to avoid overlap between task vectors. These operations introduce
887   negligible computational overhead, especially given that most modern sparsification frameworks,
888   including MergeKit, already adopt a sequential approach.
889   For Balanced Sparsification (BS), while extreme n:m pruning ratios (e.g., 32:128) may not benefit
890   from hardware-level acceleration available for smaller ratios like 2:4, BS remains efficient in terms
891   of time complexity. Here, N represents the total number of parameters in a given layer of the model.
892   Instead of performing a full sorting operation across the entire layer as in layer-wise magnitude
893   pruning (which has a time complexity of O(N log N )), BS operates by selecting n weights within
894   smaller fixed windows of size m. This process involves sorting each window of size m, resulting
895
      in a time complexity of O(N log m), which is more efficient than the global sorting required for
      layer-wise pruning.
896
897   In conclusion, while CABS introduces additional steps to improve weight distribution and miti-
898   gate overlap, these steps have minimal impact on the overall computational cost, ensuring that the
899   merging process remains efficient.

       In our small-scale experiments, we employed a two-step grid search strategy to determine the opti-
1017
       mal rescale factor λ that maximizes average performance across multiple tasks.
1018
1019   Grid Search Strategy As the sparsity level increases, the range of potential optimal λ values broad-
1020   ens, and performance typically follows a pattern of increasing and then decreasing with respect to λ.
1021   To address this, we first performed a manual search with a 0.1 interval, identifying the broader re-
1022
       gion where the optimal λ is likely to reside. Based on the results of this initial search, we conducted
       a more fine-grained search using a 0.01 interval, focusing on the region identified in the first step.
1023
1024   Unlike a fixed-range search, this adaptive approach allowed us to zero in on the most effective rescale
1025   factors for each sparsity level, ensuring more precise performance optimization. The performance
       values presented in the main text correspond to the optimal λ found through this two-step process.

1156   A.15                          L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1157
1158   General Limitations. Like other task vector-based methods, our approach is limited to models with
1159   identical architectures due to the element-wise operations used in merging model weights. This con-
1160
       straint restricts the generalization of the framework to models with homogeneous structures. Addi-
       tionally, the reliance on manual tuning of the parameter λ remains a common challenge, especially
1161
       for large language models, requiring trial and error to optimize model performance.
1162
1163   Limitations Specific to CABS. CABS introduces two new hyperparameters—the sparse sequence
1164   and the n:m ratios—unique to its design, as discussed in Appendix A.12 and A.13. While these hy-
1165   perparameters were not particularly sensitive in our experiments, they add complexity and increase
1166
       computational cost. Furthermore, while CA and BS improve performance across various tasks, their
       effectiveness is reduced in scenarios where task vectors have minimal overlap or where models
1167
       exhibit significant weight imbalances prior to sparsification. Additional experiments, especially at
1168
       extreme sparsity levels or with heavily imbalanced models, are necessary to better understand these
1169   limitations.
1170
1171   Future Work. Several directions could help overcome these limitations. Expanding model merging
1172
       techniques to include heterogeneous architectures or models trained from scratch represents a key
       area for future research. Additionally, improving the performance of merged models in multi-task
1173
       settings—where current approaches do not yet match the performance of original single-task mod-
1174
       els—remains a priority. Automating the search for optimal hyperparameters, particularly λ, would
1175   reduce complexity and improve usability, especially in large-scale applications.
