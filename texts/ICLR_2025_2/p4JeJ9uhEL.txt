009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            We have been witnessing remarkable success led by the power of neural networks
012            driven by a significant scale of training data in handling various computer vision
013            tasks. However, less attention has been paid to monitoring the camouflaged an-
014            imals, the masters of hiding themselves in the background. Robust and precise
015            segmentation of camouflaged animals is challenging even for domain experts due
016
               to their similarity to the environment. Although several efforts have been made
               in camouflaged animal image segmentation, to the best of our knowledge, limited
017
               work exists on camouflaged animal video understanding (CAVU). Biologists of-
018
               ten prefer videos for monitoring and understanding animal behaviors, as videos
019            provide redundant information and temporal consistency. However, the scarcity of
020            labeled video data significantly hinders progress in this area. To address these chal-
021            lenges, we present CamoVid60K, a diverse, large-scale, and accurately annotated
022            video dataset of camouflaged animals. This dataset comprises 218 videos with
023            62,774 finely annotated frames, covering 70 animal categories, which surpasses all
024            previous datasets in terms of the number of videos/frames and species included.
025            CamoVid60K also offers more diverse downstream tasks in computer vision, such
026            as camouflaged animal classification, detection, and task-specific segmentation
027
               (semantic, referring, motion), etc. We have benchmarked several state-of-the-art
               algorithms on the proposed CamoVid60K dataset, and the experimental results
028
               provide valuable insights for future research directions. Our dataset serves as a
029
               novel and challenging benchmark to stimulate the development of more powerful
030            camouflaged animal video segmentation algorithms, with substantial room for
031            further improvement.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The continuous evolution of neural networks (e.g., Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (He
037   et al., 2016) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020)) has provided powerful and
038   efficient tools for visual understanding based on captured images and videos. Enhancements in both
039
      data and algorithm have led to significant progress and success in the field. Large-scale datasets
      (e.g., COCO (Lin et al., 2014), ADE20K (Zhou et al., 2017) and Object365 (Shao et al., 2019))
040
      with supervised annotations serve as essential stimuli for developing powerful visual perception
041
      algorithms (Xie et al., 2022) and benchmarking them to reveal future research directions. However,
042   most existing datasets mainly contain everyday objects (e.g., 80 categories in COCO). This work
043   focuses on camouflaged animals, a less explored area of research. In addition, monitoring and
044   understanding camouflaged animals is crucial for biodiversity conservation (Rands et al., 2010; Soofi
045   et al., 2022), as it helps protect species that are otherwise difficult to detect and are at risk of unnoticed
046   population declines. Furthermore, studying camouflaged animals provides insights into evolutionary
047   biology and adaptation mechanisms, enriching our scientific understanding of natural selection.
048   However, unlike everyday objects, collecting images and videos of camouflaged animals is more
049   challenging, and annotation procedures usually involve domain experts. Segmentation, which involves
050   generating precise masks for objects of interest, is a fundamental task in computer vision. Camou-
051   flaged animal segmentation helps accurately identify and isolate these animals from their backgrounds
052   in images, facilitating detailed study and analysis. The resulting masks aid in gathering precise data
053   on their behavior, habitat, and population dynamics, enhancing our overall understanding of their
      ecology (Troscianko et al., 2017; Lv et al., 2021). Recently, several efforts Xie et al. (2022); Cheng

120   2.1   C AMOUFLAGED S CENE U NDERSTANDING
121
      Camouflaged scene understanding (CSU) is a hot computer vision topic aiming to learn discriminative
122   features that can be used to discern camouflaged target objects from their surroundings (Fan et al.,
123   2023). CSU tasks can be divided into image-level and video-level categories. Image-level CSU
124   tasks include five main types: camouflaged object counting (Sun et al., 2023), camouflaged object
125   localization (Lv et al., 2021; 2023), camouflaged object segmentation (Fan et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2023;
126   He et al., 2023a), camouflaged instance ranking (Lv et al., 2021; 2023), and camouflaged instance
127   segmentation (Pei et al., 2022; Le et al., 2021). These tasks can be further categorized based on
128   their semantic focus: object-level and instance-level. Object-level tasks focus on identifying objects,
129   while instance-level tasks aim to differentiate various entities. Additionally, camouflaged object
130
      counting is considered a sparse prediction task due to its nature, while the other tasks are classified
      as dense prediction tasks. In addition, CSU video-level task includes video camouflaged object
131
      segmentation (Ji et al., 2014; Xie et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2022b) and video camouflaged object
132
      detection (Lamdouar et al., 2020; 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022; Meunier et al., 2022;
133   Kowal et al., 2022). Overall, the progress of video-level CSU has been somewhat slower than image-
134   level CSU, primarily because the process of collecting and labeling video data is labor-intensive and
135   time-consuming.
136
137   2.2   V IDEO C AMOUFLAGED O BJECT D ETECTION AND S EGMENTATION
138
139   We review two kinds of perception tasks for camouflaged animal videos: detection (Lamdouar
140   et al., 2020; 2021; Yang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022; Meunier et al., 2022; Kowal et al., 2022) and
141
      segmentation (Ji et al., 2014; Xie et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2022b; Lamdouar et al., 2023). The former
      video camouflaged object detection (VCOD) yields bounding box sequences for the camouflaged
142
      animals, while the latter video camouflaged object segmentation (VCOS) generates dense pixel-level
143
      masks. MoCA Lamdouar et al. (2020) proposed the first large-scale moving camouflaged animals
144   video dataset with bounding box annotations and additional optical flows to boost the detection of
145   camouflaged animals. Further work Lamdouar et al. (2021) incorporated visual appearance from a
146   static scene as additional clues to promote the ability of the model to detect camouflaged animals.
147   However, the bounding box annotations could not accurately describe camouflaged objects’ pose,
148   appearance, and patterns. To address this issue, Xie Xie et al. (2019) proposed a novel pixel-trajectory
149   RNN to cluster fore-ground pixels and generate dense segmentation masks for object discovery in
150   videos. MoCA-Mask Cheng et al. (2022b) proposed the first large-scale dataset and benchmark with
151   pixel-level handcrafted ground truth masks for camouflaged animal videos. However, MoCA-Mask
152
      provides bounding boxes and pixel-wise masks for only every 5 frames, totaling just 4,691 frames,
      which is insufficient for deep learning approaches. In contrast, our dataset offers annotations for
153
      every frame, resulting in 62,774 annotated frames (13 times larger). This substantial increase can
154
      significantly enhance the performance of various downstream tasks. Our dataset and benchmark pave
155   the way for future exploration and a deeper understanding of camouflaged animal analysis.

180   3.1    DATA C ONSTRUCTION AND P ROCESSING
181   Data Sources and Pre-Processing. We built our dataset by incorporating previous datasets (Table 1)
182   and crawling additional videos from the internet to cover a variety of camouflaged animals. We
183   initially collected 1,929 videos, then manually checked and filtered out any blurry or irrelevant videos,
184   retaining those with clear depictions of animals. Next, we extracted every frame of each video
185   (instead of every 5 frames as proposed in existing datasets, see Table 1) before annotating them. At
186   the end, our dataset comprises 218 videos with 62,774 frames of 70 animal species. We provide more
187   details in Appendix A.1.
188
189   Bounding Box and Mask Annotation. We utilized the annotation tool from (Zheng et al., 2023a),
190   which is heavily based on the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) for mask
191   initialization and bounding box creation, and XMem (Cheng & Schwing, 2022) for mask and
192
      bounding box propagation. We then manually checked and refined every frame to provide accurate
      bounding boxes and segmentation masks. In addition, we adopted the perceptual camouflage score
193
      (Sp ) from (Lamdouar et al., 2023) to quantify the effectiveness of animals’ camouflage, i.e., how
194
      successfully an animal blends into its background. Based on this score, we retained videos with a
195   score higher than a threshold (e.g., Sp > 0.3).
196
197   Note that, due to the nature and characteristics of camouflaged animals and also the low resolution of
198
      videos, some frames or videos may contain errors or mislabeling at the boundaries between animals
      and the background. We will continue improving the quality of the mask annotations and also provide
199
      rotated bounding boxes (RBbox) in the next version. RBbox excels over traditional axis-aligned
200
      bounding boxes in three main areas: better localization (accurate fit for elongated and rotated objects),
201   reduced overlap of different objects or instances, and improved isolation of objects (capturing the
202   proper aspect ratio and containing fewer background pixels).
203
204   Pseudo-label Optical Flow Annotation. Previous optical flow datasets, such as Flying
205   Chairs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015), KITTI (Menze & Geiger, 2015), Sintel (Butler et al., 2012),
206   and FlyingThings3D (Mayer et al., 2016), utilized either simulation software or real images with
207   additional heavy sensor information (depth, LiDAR, etc.) and algorithms to create optical flow ground
208   truth. This process is time-consuming and requires significant effort. Recently, with the development
209   of deep learning techniques, many methods (Teed & Deng, 2020; Wang et al., 2023) can produce
210   accurately estimated optical flow. Therefore, we utilized these methods for our pseudo-label optical
211   flow annotation, using the algorithm shown in Algorithm 1. We used the pre-trained model of RAFT
212
      on FlyingThings3D (Mayer et al., 2016) and the pre-trained DINO model of ViT-B architecture.
213   Note that, even though our processing pipeline for optical flow annotation produces accurate and
214   dense optical flow, it is still estimated optical flow. Therefore, it is reasonable and suitable to use
215   as additional input to boost performance for other tasks such as motion segmentation. It is not
      recommended to use it as ground truth for evaluation.

456   5.2   B ENCHMARKS AND D ISCUSSIONS
457   Comparison with Image-Based and Video-Based Motion Segmentation Methods. Table 2
458   compares the performance of our method with other approaches. Compared to image-based methods,
459   our approach demonstrates significantly superior performance due to the incorporation of temporal
460   information. When evaluated against video-based methods, our approach also surpasses MG (Yang
461   et al., 2021), which relies solely on estimated optical flows as input. However, compared to the
462   recent state-of-the-art method SLT-Net (Cheng et al., 2022b), our method performs worse on certain
463   metrics. This is because SLT-Net excels at modeling both short-term dynamics and long-term
464   temporal consistency from videos, allowing for joint optimization of motion and camouflaged object
465
      segmentation through a single optimization target.
466
467   Comparison with Object Detection Methods. As shown in Table 3, our proposed model demon-
468
      strates performance comparable to other specialized methods, owing to its dual capabilities in object
      detection and motion segmentation. Specifically, our method significantly outperforms conventional
469
      CNN-based methods. This advantage stems from dual optimizations in the detection and segmen-
470
      tation streams, along with the integration of additional optical flow information. However, when
471   compared to DETR-like methods (Carion et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023), our approach shows mixed
472   results. It surpasses the standard DETR model (Carion et al., 2020), yet falls short of DINO (Zhang
473   et al., 2023), an advanced variant of DETR. DINO enhances performance through several innovative
474   techniques: it employs contrastive denoising training to refine one-to-one matching, a mixed query
475   selection method to better initialize the queries, and a ‘look forward twice’ method that utilizes
476   gradients from subsequent layers to adjust parameters more accurately.
477
478   Additional Analysis and Discussions. As shown in Table 4, optical flow plays a crucial role in
479   the motion segmentation of camouflaged animals. By analyzing the motion vectors between frames,
480   optical flow can detect subtle movements, distinguishing moving animals from static backgrounds.
481   This capability is particularly useful in identifying the slight movements of camouflaged animals.
482   State-of-the-art methods, including foundation models trained on large datasets such as CLIP (Radford
483   et al., 2021), UniCL (Yang et al., 2022), and K-LITE (Shen et al., 2022), struggle with zero-shot
484   image classification of camouflaged animals, as shown in Table 5. This is due to the subtle and
485   complex patterns of camouflaged animals, the lack of specific training data, and the difficulty in
      generalizing across different backgrounds and lighting conditions. Improving these methods involves

496   6    C ONCLUSION
497
      In this paper, we introduced CamoVid60K, a large-scale video dataset for camouflaged animal
498
      understanding, aiming to foster further research on camouflaged animals. This dataset provides a
499   significant benchmark for camouflaged animal video understanding tasks, enabling the evaluation
500   of various algorithms and methods. We also plan to scale up our dataset and utilize it to build
501   foundational models for studying camouflaged animals.
502
503   Limitations and Future Work. As mentioned in Section 3, the annotation quality in some cases is
504   suboptimal. We plan to enhance these annotations and introduce more types of annotations in the
505   future. Additionally, our current pipeline requires images and pre-computed optical flow as inputs,
506
      which restricts the generation of new data due to the necessity of pre-computed optical flow. To
      address this limitation, we will propose a learnable module to estimate the implicit optical flow field.
507
508   New Benchmark. CamoVid60K is a diverse and comprehensive benchmark curated from publicly
509   accessible datasets and the internet to enhance the assessment and exploration of camouflaged animal
510   understanding. It includes various camouflaged animals across different environments, providing a
511   robust framework for testing and developing new models.
512
513   Impact on Animal Studies. By providing detailed and varied data on camouflaged animals, the
514   CamoVid60K dataset significantly contributes to studying animal behavior, ecology, and evolution.
515
      Researchers can utilize this dataset to explore how different species employ camouflage in their
      natural habitats, leading to deeper insights into predator-prey interactions and survival strategies.
516
      Furthermore, this dataset can aid conservation efforts by improving the detection and monitoring of
517
      endangered species in their natural environments (Troscianko et al., 2017; Norouzzadeh et al., 2018;
518   Beery et al., 2018; Simões et al., 2023).
519
520   Broader Impact. The study of camouflaged objects has several important applications, such as
521   identifying and safeguarding rare animal species, preventing wildlife trafficking, detecting medical
522   conditions like polyps or lung infections, and aiding in search-and-rescue operations. Our dataset
523   deliberately excludes any military or sensitive scenes, ensuring its focus remains on benign and
524
      beneficial applications. Besides the significant applications mentioned, our work advances the
      understanding of video content in the presence of distorted motion information, contributing to the
525
      broader field of video analysis and computer vision.

722   A.1   DATA C URATION
723
724   We built our dataset from published datasets (Camouflaged Animals Dataset (CAD) (Pia Bideau,
725
      2016), Moving Camouflaged Animals (MoCA) (Lamdouar et al., 2020), MoCA-Mask (Cheng et al.,
      2022b), Marine Video Kit (MVK) (Truong et al., 2023)) and crawled video from internet.
726
727   The CAD dataset includes nine short video sequences obtained from YouTube videos. Hand-labeled
728   ground truth masks are provided for every 5 frames.
729   The MoCA dataset comprises approximately 37,000 frames extracted from 141 YouTube video
730   sequences. Most videos are presented at an image resolution of 1280 × 720 and 3840 × 2160 pixels,
731   and the videos have a frame rate of 24 FPS. This dataset includes 67 distinct species of animals in
732   locomotion within their native habitats, although it contains a few instances of animals with less
733   camouflaged characteristics.
734
      The MoCA-Mask dataset is built upon the MoCA dataset with some modifications. This new subset
735   consists of 87 video sequences with 22,939 frames. It offers precise human-labeled segmentation
736   masks for every 5 frames. Consequently, the ground truth (GT) is available in two formats: 4,691
737   bounding box annotations and 4,691 pixel-level masks.
738
      The MVK dataset comprises 1,379 underwater videos recorded at 36 unique geographical sites during
739
      various seasons. These videos exhibit a broad duration spectrum, ranging from as short as 2 seconds
740
      to almost 5 minutes, with a total duration slightly above 12 hours. On average, the videos are roughly
741   29.9 seconds long, with a median length of around 25.4 seconds. Notably, the dataset presents videos
742   recorded under different conditions, such as variable light levels, points of view, water clarity, and
743   environmental conditions. They also offer approximately 40,000 frames (extracted at one FPS or
744   every 30 frames) with associated referring expression annotations.
745
      To crawl videos from the internet, we curated a list of animal names that potentially have camouflage
746
      abilities. We then created a template for searching and downloading videos: “video of camou-
747   flaged/concealed + animal’s name”. Combining these with the videos from the above datasets, we
748   collected 1,929 videos.

      visibility) by penalizing the boundary pixels that are predicted as contours in both the image’s contour
      (C) and the ground truth animal’s contour (Cgt ) using the F1 metric:
823
824
825
                                   SB (I, mS ) = 1 − F1(mb ⊙ Cgt , mb ⊙ C),                                (5)
826
827
828
      where mb = dilate(mS ) − erode(mS ).
829   We used the same parameter values as in (Lamdouar et al., 2023), specifically α = 0.35 and λ = 0.2.
830   In addition, we illustrate the difference between low-ranking and high-ranking camouflage in Figure 8
831   and Figure 9.

833   A.3    R EFERRING E XPRESSION A NNOTATION
834
835   Referring expression annotations are used for the Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS)
836   task. RVOS differs from traditional Video Object Segmentation (VOS), where a mask is provided
837
      for the first frame, and the model predicts the segmentation for the remaining video frames. In
      RVOS, the initial frame mask is replaced with a referring expression (i.e. a sentence) that accurately
838
      describes the target object throughout the entire video, e.g. “the yellow fish swimming toward the
839
      camera.” This approach also differs from Referring Image Segmentation (RIS), which uses different
840   referring expressions for each image. Referring expression annotations can be utilized for various
841   video understanding tasks, such as RVOS (Seo et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024), video retrieval systems
842   with semantic understanding (Ha et al., 2023), video grounding (Mu et al., 2024), etc.
