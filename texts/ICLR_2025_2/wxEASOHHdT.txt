               Similar to Vision Transformers, this paper identifies artifacts also present within
010
               the feature maps of Vision Mamba. These artifacts, corresponding to high-norm
011
               tokens emerging in low-information background areas of images, appear much
012            more severe in Vision Mamba—they exist prevalently even with the tiny-sized
013            model and activate extensively across background regions. To mitigate this issue,
014            we follow the prior solution of introducing register tokens into Vision Mamba.
015            To better cope with Mamba blocks’ uni-directional inference paradigm, two key
016            modifications are introduced: 1) evenly inserting registers throughout the input
017            token sequence, and 2) recycling registers for final decision predictions. We term
018            this new architecture Mamba® . Qualitative observations suggest, compared to
019            vanilla Vision Mamba, Mamba® ’s feature maps appear cleaner and more focused

022

023            with 341M parameters), attaining a competitive accuracy of 83.6% (84.5% if fine-

025            segmentation task also supports Mamba® ’s efficacy.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent advances in State Space Models (SSMs) have showcased their considerable potential in
031   sequence modeling tasks. In contrast to Transformers’ quadratic computational complexity with
032   respect to sequence lengths, SSMs operate with linear computational complexity, offering significant
033
      efficiency advantages in managing extended sequences. One exemplary instantiation of SSMs is
      the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023), which employs selective scan techniques alongside a
034
      suite of hardware-optimized designs. This innovation facilitates the efficient training and inference
035
      of recurrent models with linear computational complexity and memory overhead. Furthermore, a
036   comprehensive body of recent research (Gu & Dao, 2023; Behrouz et al., 2024; Lieber et al., 2024)
037   substantiates that the Mamba architecture is able to achieve competitive performance levels, on par
038   with Transformers, particularly in processing natural language and audio.
039
      Furthermore, the Mamba architecture has also been successfully extended to a variety of visual
040
      tasks (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024). The motivation for this
041
      expansion mainly arises from the computational challenges presented by processing high-resolution
042   images and videos. These data types often lead to long input sequences that conventional models
043   struggle to handle effectively or efficiently—e.g., for long-length input, traditional models such
044   as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) suffer from relatively small receptive fields, and Vision
045   Transformers (ViTs) contend with high computational and memory costs. Yet, Vision Mamba (Vim)
046   architectures have shown the potential to mitigate these limitations—recent works demonstrate that
047   they not only manage computational and memory demands more efficiently but also deliver strong
048   performance across a variety of generic visual tasks, including classification, segmentation, and
049   image generation (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2024).
050   Despite the competitive benchmark performance, our observations reveal that Mamba’s internal
051   modeling exhibits significant issues when processing visual inputs. This issue is similar to the prior
052   observation of ViTs (Darcet et al., 2024), where some outlier tokens located in the less semantic
053   background unexpectedly contain rich global information (showing as high attention scores in the
      feature map). These unusual feature activations are termed artifacts. In this work, we reveal that

083   2) at the end of the Vision Mamba, concatenate the register tokens to form a comprehensive image
084   representation for the final prediction. We name this enhanced architecture Mamba® .
085

086
      cleaner feature maps displayed in the 3rd and 6th columns of Figure 1, Mamba® significantly sup-
087
      presses artifacts, with responses now more focused on visually meaningful content. Meanwhile, as
088   visualized in Figure 6, registers can well capture object-related semantic information for building
089   high-quality image representations. Quantitatively, the improvements in benchmarks are equally
090   compelling. For example, Mamba® -Base achieves an accuracy of 83.0% on ImageNet, notably
091   outperforming the 81.8% accuracy of Vim-Base, which is a vanilla Vision Mamba architecture.
092   Furthermore, Mamba® successfully expands the scaling capabilities of Vision Mamba—whereas
093   previous models were limited to configurations no larger than 90M parameters (Zhu et al., 2024;
094   Liu et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024), Mamba® can be effectively trained with up to 341M parame-
095   ters, reaching an impressive 83.6% accuracy on ImageNet; this accuracy can be further boosted to
096
      84.5% by enlarging the image input size to 384 × 384. For the ADE20k (Zhou et al., 2019) seman-
      tic segmentation benchmark, our Mamba® attains a 49.1% evaluation mIoU, which significantly
097
      outperforms Vim’s best result of 44.9% mIoU (Zhu et al., 2024).

102   Generic Visual Backbone Architectures. Modern computer vision predominantly relies on two
103   types of backbone architectures: CNNs that excel in extracting hierarchical features and ViTs
104   that are effective in modeling long-range dependencies. Since the advent of CNNs (LeCun
105   et al., 1998), their structure and scale have undergone a series of significant innovations in recent
106   decades (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; He et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017;
107   Tan & Le, 2019; Liu et al., 2022). Unlike CNNs that build spatial dependencies through convo-
      lutional operations, ViTs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) attain a global receptive field by utilizing the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), leading to state-of-the-art performance in a series
109   of downstream visual tasks. Based on this architecture, extensive research has been dedicated to im-
110   proving its model design (Yuan et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021a; Liu et al., 2021), enhancing training
111   strategy (Touvron et al., 2021; 2022), and advancing self-supervised pretraining frameworks (Chen
112   et al., 2021b; Caron et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2022; He et al., 2022).
113
      State Space Models. The concept of State Space Models (SSMs) can be dated back to the 1960s in
114
      control systems where it was used to process continuous inputs (Kalman, 1960). With advancements
115   in discretization strategies (Tallec & Ollivier, 2018; Gu et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2022; Gu et al.,
116   2023), SSMs have recently been introduced into the field of deep learning, modeling sequential
117   information such as language and speech (Gu et al., 2022; 2021; Smith et al., 2022). Broadly defined,
118   SSMs can refer to any recurrent models with a latent state such as RNNs and the variant architectures
119   such as Linear Attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), RetNet (Sun et al., 2023), and RWKV (Peng
120   et al., 2023). More recently, Gu & Dao (2023) introduced a selective SSM block, namely Mamba,
121   that incorporates structured SSMs with hardware-aware state expansion, leading to a highly efficient
122   recurrent architecture that is competitive to Transformer.
123   Mamba Models in Vision. Building upon the Mamba block, a series of follow-up studies have
124   explored the application of SSMs in computer vision. For example, Zhu et al. (2024) propose a
125   straightforward vision Mamba model by sequentially stacking the Mamba blocks, attaining superior
126   performance than Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021) in both tiny
127   and small model sizes. Liu et al. (2024b) presents a hybrid architecture that combines Mamba with
128   2D convolution, showcasing significant results in a series of vision tasks. The study of Mamba-based
129
      architectures is continuously explored (Lieber et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a).

135   The original definition of SSM is a Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) system that projects the input stim-
136   ulation x(t) ∈ RL to the output response y(t) ∈ RL through a hidden state h(t) ∈ CN . For the
137
      continuous inputs, the system can be formulated by a group of linear ordinary differential equations
      as follows:
138
139                                         h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t)

140                                          y(t) = Ch(t) + Dx(t),

143   By discretizing this ordinary differential equation group, the continuous-time SSMs can be inte-
144   grated to process discrete inputs such as language, speech, and image pixels. To this end, the model
145   can be solved by an analytic solution and then approximated by Zero-Order Hold (Gu & Dao, 2023),
146   leading to a discrete model:
147
                                              ht = Aht−1 + Bxt

149                                           yt = Cht + Dxt ,

176   To adapt Mamba for visual tasks, images are first processed into sequential inputs through patch
177   embedding as in ViT. However, the standard Mamba is a unidirectional model where each token in
178   the sequence can only access information from preceding tokens. This characteristic, while working
179   well with 1-D language signals, significantly constrains the model’s capacity to gather contextual
180   information inherently from 2-D visual signals. To overcome this limitation, a common solution is
181   to reconfigure Mamba blocks for bidirectional scanning. Specifically, the sequence is scanned once
182   from start to end and again from end to start, with the outputs from both scans subsequently averaged
183   to obtain a comprehensive representation (Zhu et al., 2024). We follow this scanning design in all
184
      subsequent experiments.

186   3.2   F EATURE A RTIFACTS OF V ISION M AMBA
187
188   In ViT, an interpretable feature map can be obtained by visualizing the activation scores in their self-
189
      attention blocks. Ideally, under appropriate pre-training paradigms, these feature maps are expected
      to display high attention scores in the informative foreground regions of images and relatively low
190
      scores in less semantic background areas. Nevertheless, a considerable amount of outliers often ap-
191
      pear in these feature maps, which position-wise correspond to low-information background regions
192   yet exhibit anomalously high attention scores. A recent study (Darcet et al., 2024) has termed these
193   outliers as feature artifacts. Specifically, this study reveals that the artifact tokens always possess
194   high normalization values and, during inference, they tend to discard their local information in favor
195   of retaining global features, thereby ‘compromising’ the quality of the feature map.
196
      This work identifies a similar issue in Vision Mamba models. First, by computing the ℓ2 distances
197
      between vanilla Vision Mamba’s global and local outputs, we observe a considerable amount of
198   activations in background areas (shown in Figure 1). Further analysis of their normalization reveals
199   that these background activations also exhibit high normalization values, akin to the artifacts ob-
200   served in ViTs. For example, by visualizing the ℓ2 normalization of vanilla Vision Mamba’s local
201   outputs in Figure 2, we can observe a significant presence of high-norm tokens in the background,
202   even blurring the distinction between foreground and background regions. Quantitatively, we plot
203   the norm distributions of vanilla Vision Mamba in Figure 3a, where it clearly displays a number of
204   outliers with high normalization, confirming consistency with previous findings in ViTs as discussed
205   in (Darcet et al., 2024).
206   Furthermore, it is equally noteworthy that these artifacts in Vision Mamba function similarly to
207   those in ViTs in retaining global representations (Darcet et al., 2024). As reported in Table 1, the
208   vanilla Vision Mamba model can obtain 81.0% ImageNet accuracy by merely using the average of
209   top 5% high norm tokens as a global feature, which is only 0.1% lower than that of pooling all local
210   tokens. Increasing this threshold to top 10% or 20% high norm tokens further enables the model to
211   match the accuracy of using global pooling. In contrast, relying on the remaining 80% of relatively
212
      low-norm tokens results in a performance drop to 79.3%.
213   Yet differently, we observe that the artifact issues are considerably more severe in Vision Mamba than
214   in ViTs: these artifacts appear more prevalent in the background areas and exhibit higher normal-
215   ization values than those observed in ViTs. As shown in Figure 3a, the average norm of the outlier
      tokens increases rapidly with the depth of layers, reaching over 4000 by the 23rd layer. Compared

      3.3              M AMBA® : V ISION M AMBA WITH R EGISTERS
257
258   Following the solution of removing artifacts in ViT (Darcet et al., 2024), we propose to address this
259   issue by introducing register tokens into Vision Mamba. We term our new architecture Mamba® .
260   Unlike the previous method (Darcet et al., 2024) which only appends register tokens at one end of
261   the input sequence, we hypothesize that by distributing the register tokens more densely throughout
262   the sequence, our method can 1) better address the more pervasive artifact issue that is unique to
263   vision mamba; and 2) helps capture global representation that is often missed in vision mamba due
264   to its uni-directional nature. The framework of Mamba® is illustrated in Figure 5. Overall, we
265   follow the backbone architecture of vanilla Vision Mamba (Vim) (Zhu et al., 2024), where the input
266
      image is first decomposed into a sequence of non-overlapping patches and then fed into a stack of
      bi-directional Mamba blocks. Based on this plain architecture, we make the following two simple
267
      yet very effective modifications to build our Mamba® .
268
269   Sparsely distributed register tokens. The input sequence of Mamba® is composed of m image
      tokens produced by patch embedding and n register tokens evenly inserted between them. Contrary

314   mechanism, this property could have the potential to offer a valuable dimension for interpreting
315   Mamba’s feature representations.

477
478
      In this paper, we explored the nature of artifacts within the feature maps of Vision Mamba, con-
479
      trasting these with those observed in Vision Transformers. Specifically, we implemented a novel ar-
480   chitecture named Mamba® , incorporating registers strategically to enhance image processing. Our
481   empirical assessments demonstrate that, qualitatively, Mamba® not only reduces the presence of
482   artifacts but also sharpens the focus on semantically relevant areas within images, leading to cleaner
483   and more effective feature maps. Quantitatively, Mamba® not only surpasses its predecessors in
484   accuracy but also exhibits superior scalability, handling larger model sizes with competitive accu-
485   racy. We hope this work can establish a solid backbone architecture for future research in optimizing
      Mamba architectures in vision.
