011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly impacted the field of Math Word
014            Problems (MWPs), transforming how these problems are approached and solved,
015
               particularly in educational contexts. However, existing evaluations often focus on
               final accuracy, neglecting the critical aspect of reasoning capabilities. This work
016
               addresses that gap by evaluating LLMs’ abilities to detect and correct reasoning
017
               mistakes. We present a novel dataset, MWP-MISTAKE, containing MWPs with
018            both correct and incorrect reasoning steps generated through rule-based methods
019            and smaller language models. Our comprehensive benchmarking of state-of-the-art
020            models such as GPT-4o and GPT4 uncovers important insights into their strengths
021            and limitations. While GPT-4o excels in mistake detection and rectification, gaps
022            remain, particularly in handling complex datasets and novel problems. Additionally,
023            we identify concerns with data contamination and memorization, which affect LLM
024            reliability in real-world applications. While OpenAI’s O1 model demonstrates
025            90% accuracy in reasoning and final answers on complex tasks, it remains weak
026
               in mistake detection. Our findings highlight the need for improved reasoning
               evaluations and suggest ways to enhance LLM generalization and robustness in
027
               math problem-solving.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed artificial intelligence applications across diverse
033   domains, including healthcare, agriculture, and education (OpenAI, b;a). Their remarkable capabili-
034   ties in natural language understanding, question answering, and mathematical problem-have shown
035   potential to revolutionize various human endeavors (Liu et al., 2024b). Recent advancements have
036   fueled extensive research into applying LLMs to interpret and solve a wide array of mathematical
037   tasks, from basic arithmetic to complex algebraic equations and calculus problems (Hendrycks et al.,
038   2021; Zhang et al., 2024a). Math Word Problems (MWPs) involve interpreting narrative scenarios
039
      to extract mathematical concepts and apply reasoning for solutions (Srivatsa & Kochmar, 2024).
      Studies (Xu et al., 2024; He-Yueya et al., 2023; Deb et al., 2023) show LLMs can convert text into
040
      mathematical expressions and generate accurate results, but a critical element mathematical reasoning
041
      is often underexplored.
042
043   Despite achieving remarkable accuracy rates exceeding 90% on datasets like GSM-8K (Grade School
044   Math dataset with linguistically diverse word problems) (Cobbe et al., 2021a), foundational LLMs
045
      such as Claude-3-Opus (noa, a), Gemini Ultra (Team et al., 2024), and OpenAI GPT-4 (OpenAI
      et al., 2024) reveal a significant gap in our understanding of their capabilities in mathematical
046
      reasoning (Deb et al., 2023). Current research predominantly focuses on evaluating the final accuracy
047
      of MWPs (Luo et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024), neglecting the intricate reasoning processes necessary to
048   derive solutions. We argue that the reasoning steps play a pivotal role, and it is imperative to assess
049   them to comprehensively analyze the foundational capabilities of these models. This necessity is
050   further underscored by the increasing utilization of LLMs in domains such as education (Gan et al.,
051   2023), where they serve as personalized tutors for students, aiding in teaching concepts and solving
052   mathematical problems. Simply deriving the final answer is insufficient; the ability to guide students
053   through correct steps, identify errors in their reasoning, and provide corrective guidance is paramount
      for such applications.

068   are threefold: firstly, to comprehensively evaluate LLMs’ capabilities in mathematical reasoning,
069
      with a particular emphasis on mistake detection and correction; secondly, to identify the specific
      strengths and weaknesses of these models in handling various types of mathematical challenges; and
070
      thirdly, to propose potential directions for enhancing LLM capabilities in this domain.
071
072   To achieve this comprehensive evaluation, we have developed our own mistake dataset, designed to
073   include errors in the reasoning steps. This dataset allows the assessment of models’ proficiency not
074   only in providing correct solutions but also in detecting and correcting mistakes within the reasoning
075
      steps. We evaluate 12 different foundational models including large, small and fine-tuned on math,
      language models on our curated dataset MWP-MISTAKE. We are releasing this dataset for further
076
      evaluation and benchmarking1 .
077
078   Our analysis reveals several key insights into the performance of LLMs on MWPs. Firstly, detecting
079   mistakes, even trivial ones remains a significant challenge for these models. Secondly, LLMs often
080   derive correct answers despite this difficulty in mistake detection. This can be attributed to data
081
      memorization and potential contamination in training datasets, where models may have encountered
      similar/same problems before. However, the ability to recover from or correct errors in the reasoning
082
      process is generally poor across most models. Our contributions to this paper are as follows:
083
084   1. We collect and release to the research community MWP-MISTAKE, a dataset containing MWPs
085      with both correct and incorrect reasoning obtained from state-of-the-art MWP datasets such as
086      SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021), GSM-8K (Cobbe et al., 2021b), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021),
087
         MATHBENCH (Liu et al., 2024a), and JEEBENCH (Arora et al., 2023). Incorrect reasoning is
         derived through meticulously crafted rules to alter the reasoning steps and using smaller models,
088
         leveraging their inherent limitations in solving MWPs.
089
      2. We provide benchmark results for our dataset to evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs
090      such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, a), GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), GPT-3.5Turbo (noa, b), Claude (noa,
091      a), as well as smaller language models like Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), Phi (Abdin et al.,
092      2024a), and Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024) and also models fine-tuned on Math datasets. Our analysis
093      demonstrates that all SOTA LLMs struggle with mistake detection and correction.
094   3. Through meticulous evaluation and comparison of different LLMs, we offer a detailed analysis of
095      their strengths and weaknesses in handling mathematical reasoning tasks. We also provide early
096      preliminary evaluations with OpenAI o1 models, which still does not excel in mistake detection.

      2.1    M ETICULOUSLY C RAFTED RULES TO P ROGRAMMATICALLY I NJECT E RRORS
121
122
      These rules are motivated and derived from common mistakes observed in educational settings,
123   ensuring the errors introduced are realistic and representative of actual student errors.
124
125   1. Shuffle numerical values: Numerical values are shuffled among themselves to verify if models
126
         can correctly understand the question and select appropriate numerical values from the question.
      2. Replace numerical values: Numerical values are replaced with random numbers ranging from 0
127
         to 100. It identifies if the model can correctly pick the numerical values present in the question.
128
      3. Shuffle operations: We randomly swap operators with other operators to test the model’s ability
129      to perform numerical operations.
130   4. Insert random reasoning steps: A random reasoning step is added at a random position to test
131      the model’s ability to identify incorrect reasoning.
132   5. Shuffle reasoning steps: The reasoning steps are shuffled to introduce ambiguity in the thought
133      process. This tests whether the model can identify changes in reasoning order.
134   6. Delete reasoning steps: One reasoning step is deleted in solutions that have two or more steps.
135      This helps to identify if the model can spot omissions in the reasoning process.
136   These rules mimic real-world student behavior by reflecting tendencies to get the order of steps wrong,
137   skip steps, misinterpret numerical values, use incorrect numbers, apply the wrong mathematical
138   operations, and add irrelevant steps in problem-solving. While rules #5 and #6 do not introduce
139   explicit errors in reasoning, they are considered mistakes in our dataset to prompt the model to
140   identify scenarios lacking clarity. Table 1 shows the number of questions selected from each of
141   the five datasets to which these six rules are applied to curate incorrect reasoning. Thus, for every
142   question selected, we created seven variations of reasoning steps (one correct + six incorrect).

165   3     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
166
      Task Details. Our aim is to assess the performance of LLMs on MWPs, focusing on their ability to
167   detect and correct mistakes within the reasoning steps. We have two task variants to accomplish this:
168
169   1. Task-1 (T1): Given a question and its reasoning steps, the model must identify correctness, rectify
170
         mistakes if needed and compute the final answer.(Figure 1).
      2. Task-2 (T2): The model only needs to identify whether the reasoning steps provided are correct
171
         or incorrect and provide the final answer. No correction of reasoning steps is explicitly required.
172
173   In essence, T1 evaluates the model’s ability to detect mistakes, rectify them, and derive the correct
174   answer, while T2 focuses solely on detecting mistakes and solving MWP correctly. Both tasks operate
175   under few-shot settings, with specific prompt details provided in Appendix D.
176   Models. To evaluate LLMs’ mathematical reasoning capabilities, we utilize foundational LLMs,
177   SLMs and math-finetuned SLMs.
178
      1. LLMs: We utilize 6 LLMs that have shown tremendous performance in MWPs such
179
         as GPT-4o, GPT-4, GPT-3.5Turbo, Claude-3-Opus, Llama-2-70b (Touvron et al., 2023),
180      Llama-3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024).
181   2. SLMs. Additionally, we evaluate six popular SLMs—Phi-3-mini (Abdin et al., 2024b),
182      Mixtral-8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024), Llama-2-7b-chat (Touvron et al., 2023), Qwen2-7B (Yang
183      et al., 2024), Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), and Llama-3-8b-finetuned (Chen & Li, 2024)
184      trained on high-quality data to assess their reasoning capabilities. Appendix E provides the details
185      of the models, including their last training date.
186   Metrics. We compute the F1 score for all experiments as follows: for mistake detection, the model
187   outputs either "yes" (indicating correct reasoning) or "no" (indicating incorrect reasoning). The
188   ground truth labels are similarly "yes" for correct reasoning and "no" for incorrect reasoning, and the
189   model’s predictions are compared against these labels to calculate the F1 score. For performance
190   evaluation, the generated final answer is compared to the ground truth final answer to compute the F1
191   score for accuracy.

236   4.2   C AN LLM S E FFECTIVELY I DENTIFY M ISTAKES IN R EASONING S TEPS ?
237
238   We evaluate the ability of various models to detect mistakes in the reasoning steps of MWPs, with F1
239   scores across five datasets, as shown in Table 3 for both default (D) and smaller models (SM).
240   GPT-4o Performance. GPT-4o is the top performer, outperforming models like GPT-4,
241   GPT-3.5Turbo, and several smaller models. However, despite an overall F1 score of 87% across
242   all datasets, it struggles with consistent mistake detection, particularly on simpler datasets such as
243   SVAMP, and on more complex datasets like JEEBENCH, where its performance notably declines.
244   For instance, on JEEBENCH, GPT-4o ’s F1 score drops by 6% compared to its performance on
245   GSM-8K. This shows that while GPT-4o excels in many areas, its precision for comprehensive
246   mistake detection is still lacking, especially when faced with varying levels of complexity.
247   Rule-based vs. SLM-generated Mistakes. One notable observation is that GPT-4o and other models
248   detect SLM-generated mistakes with higher accuracy compared to rule-based mistakes. For instance,
249   GPT-4o achieves an F1 score of 94% for SLM errors versus 80% for rule-based errors across all
250   datasets. This discrepancy suggests potential exposure to SLM-generated data during GPT-4o ’s
251   training, giving it an advantage in detecting these mistakes. This is an important insight into how
252   training data might influence the model’s effectiveness in mistake detection.
253   Performance of GPT-4 vs. GPT-3.5Turbo. While GPT-3.5Turbo performs similarly to GPT-4 and
254   even surpasses it on certain datasets like GSM-8K, it struggles with errors generated by smaller
255   models. On these, GPT-4 handles mistakes more effectively, likely due to potential data contamination
256   or overfitting during its training. For instance, GPT-4 ’s F1 score for smaller model-generated mistakes
257   is 86%, compared to GPT-3.5Turbo ’s 56%.
258   Smaller Models and Fine-tuning. Smaller fine-tuned models, such as Llama-3-8b-finetuned, demon-
259   strate a competitive performance close to that of GPT-4. Llama-3-8b-finetuned achieves an F1 score
260   of 76%, outperforming other SLMs and even rivaling GPT-4 (77%) in certain cases. This highlights
261   the effectiveness of domain-specific fine-tuning, especially for mathematical tasks, where tailored
262   training significantly improves mistake detection accuracy.
263
      Challenges with Newer Datasets. All models, including GPT-4o, face significant challenges with
264   newer and more complex datasets such as MATHBENCH and JEEBENCH. For instance, GPT-4o
265   ’s F1 score drops by 6% on JEEBENCH compared to its performance on GSM-8K. This stark
266   decline across models shows that their reasoning capabilities do not generalize well to unseen and
267   more complex problem types. While GPT-4o still leads the pack, its limitations on these datasets
268   underscore the need for better generalization in handling deeper reasoning challenges. Appendix F
269   shows additional detailed results showcasing the F1 score analysis on different types of rule-based
      reasoning mistakes across different models.

334   4.4   E XPLORING DATA C ONTAMINATION AND M EMORIZATION E FFECTS IN M ATH
335         R EASONING TASKS
336
337   In our analysis of LLMs’ mathematical reasoning performance, we’ve identified potential instances
338
      of data contamination and memorization, both of which can significantly impact the effectiveness of
      these models. Data contamination, characterized by the presence of test data from downstream tasks
339
      in LLMs’ training data, poses a major challenge in accurately assessing their real-world performance.
340
      Meanwhile, memorization occurs when models replicate solutions from training data without grasping
341   the underlying principles, thereby hindering their ability to generalize to new problems.
342
343   The presence of data contamination is evident in instances of unexpectedly high performance on
344
      certain datasets. For example, GPT-3.5Turbo’s superior performance over GPT-4 on the GSM-8K
      dataset raises concerns about biases in GPT-4’s training data. Similarly, the comparable performance
345
      between smaller and larger models suggests the potential presence of memorization. These findings
346
      underscore the critical need for rigorous evaluation to mitigate the impacts of memorization, ensuring
347   the reliability and effectiveness of LLMs in real-world applications.
348
349   Investigating data contamination and memorization poses challenges due to restricted pre-training
350
      data access and computational limitations. To tackle this, we employ an approach outlined in (Golchin
      & Surdeanu, 2024), utilizing an LLM to replicate individual instances of the dataset. This involves
351
      guiding the LLM with instructions containing unique identifiers from the source dataset, like dataset
352
      name, partition (e.g., train, test, or validation), and a fragment of the reference instance. By instructing
353   the LLM to complete these partial instances, we can evaluate contamination and memorization.
354
355   To detect contamination, a heuristic is applied comparing the average overlap score between generated
356
      completions and reference instances using ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004). This comparison is made between
      guided instructions (including dataset and partition identifiers) and general instructions (lacking
357
      such identifiers). If the overlap score is significantly larger with guided instructions, it suggests
358
      contamination. This method relies on the premise that the only distinction between the two instructions
359   is the inclusion of dataset and partition names in guided instructions, implying any improvement can
360   be attributed to contamination (Appendix I for more details).
361
362
      Figure 4 illustrates the difference in ROUGE-L scores between guided and general instructions across
      all datasets for various models. The results highlight notable discrepancies, providing early evidence
363
      of data contamination, particularly among the larger models.
364
365   GPT-4o Performance. GPT-4o exhibits the highest ROUGE-L scores across all datasets, suggesting
366   a significant level of data contamination. This is consistent with its earlier performance, where
367   it excelled in simpler tasks but struggled with more complex datasets, likely due to reliance on
368
      memorized data rather than true reasoning capabilities.
369   Comparative Contamination Across Models. Following GPT-4o, both GPT-4 and GPT-3.5Turbo
370   show progressively lower ROUGE-L scores, though they still indicate some level of contamination.
371   This pattern reinforces the earlier performance trends, where these models performed well but not
372   as dominantly as GPT-4o, suggesting that their performance may also benefit from memorized data
373   (especially on GSM-8K), albeit to a lesser degree.
374   SLMs’ Minimal Contamination. In contrast, smaller language models (SLMs) such as Llama and
375   Phi display negative ROUGE-L scores, suggesting minimal to no contamination. These models seem
376   to rely more on reasoning rather than memorization, as their performance is not inflated by exposure
377   to the test data during training. However, their lower overall performance on complex tasks highlights
      that they lack the advanced reasoning capabilities needed to match the larger models.

404   4.5   C AN LLM S C ORRECTLY R ECTIFY M ISTAKES IN R EASONING S TEPS ?
405
406   In Task 1, the model not only detects mistakes but also attempts to rectify them to arrive at the correct
407   answer. We evaluate the model’s ability to rectify mistakes once detected by examining the number
408   of questions where mistakes were identified and calculating how many times the model produced the
409   correct answer after rectification. The assumption is that if the model reaches the correct final answer
410   after detecting a mistake, it has successfully rectified the incorrect reasoning step. For instance, if the
411   model identifies mistakes in 90 out of 100 questions and rectifies them in 45 cases (resulting in final
412
      correct answer), the rectification score would be 50% (45/90).
413   Table 5 illustrates the performance of different models in rectifying reasoning steps and producing
414   the correct final answer across various datasets.
415
      GPT-4o shows high proficiency in rectifying mistakes, achieving an overall rectification score of 78%
416   across all datasets. It outperforms GPT-4 by 11% and exceeds other models, including SLMs, by over
417   35%. Specifically, GPT-4o excels in correcting mistakes caused by rule-based reasoning compared to
418   those induced by SLMs. However, its ability to fix mistakes decreases with more complex datasets
419   like MATHBENCH and JEEBENCH. On simpler datasets, such as GSM-8K, MATH, and SVAMP,
420   GPT-4o demonstrates high accuracy in rectification, potentially due to either data contamination (as
421   discussed earlier) or the simpler nature of rule-based mistakes.
422   As observed earlier, Claude-3-Opus performs comparably to GPT-4o in rectifying mistakes. Other
423   models, however, exhibit poorer rectification abilities, with scores ranging between 30-50%. Notably,
424   Llama-3-70B achieves performance similar to GPT-4, indicating strong rectification capabilities.
425
      To delve deeper into the rectification process, we also compute the percentage of questions where the
426
      model rectified the reasoning steps but still arrived at incorrect answers. Across the MWP-MISTAKE
427
      dataset, GPT-4o failed to derive the correct answer in 17% of cases after correcting the reasoning,
428   while other models like GPT-4, GPT-4, Llama-2-7b-chat, Mixtral-8x7B, and Phi-3-mini resulted in
429   30%, 43.5%, 80.9%, 40.2%, and 55.6% incorrect answers, respectively. Additionally, we evaluated
430   the rectified reasoning steps by comparing them with ground-truth reasoning steps to assess the
431   effectiveness and alignment of the rectification process across models (detailed in Appendix G and H).
      This comparison is quantified using traditional NLP metrics such as BERTScore.

      OpenAI recently introduced the O1 model, designed to enhance reasoning capabilities by spending

436

437   steps, 56 rule-based mistakes, and 26 SLM-generated mistakes), several key insights emerged. O1
438   consistently outperforms GPT-4O, particularly in deriving correct final answers, showcasing its

      Rule-based mistake identification. both O1 and GPT-4O perform similarly, with F1 scores of

443   becomes apparent when comparing their ability to derive the correct final answer despite the mistakes.
444   While GPT-4O manages an F1 score of only 0.43, O1 excels with a final answer F1 score of 0.8277,
445   showing a notable improvement in reasoning capabilities. O1’s ability to achieve such high final
446   accuracy, despite similar mistake detection rates, underscores its advanced reasoning abilities, which
447   may benefit from improved rectification strategies or more sophisticated handling of mistakes during
448   the reasoning process.
449   SLM-generated mistakes. When analyzing SLM-generated mistakes, both models achieve 100%
450   mistake detection accuracy, reflecting strong capabilities in identifying these more complex errors.
451   However, the models diverge significantly in their ability to correct these mistakes and derive the
452   correct final answer. O1 reaches a final answer F1 score of 0.9, while GPT-4O lags significantly
453   behind with a score of only 0.62. This stark contrast highlights O1’s substantial advancement not
454   only in detecting mistakes but also in rectifying them to produce the correct final answer, showcasing
455   its enhanced reasoning and generalization capabilities on more challenging datasets.
456   In summary, while both models are comparable in terms of mistake identification, O1 demonstrates
457   a clear advantage in final answer generation and rectification, particularly on SLM-based mistakes.
458   These results illustrate the superior reasoning capabilities of O1 over GPT-4O, making it a more effec-
459   tive model for handling intricate reasoning tasks. However, issues like potential data contamination
460   and inefficiencies in processing time and token usage with O1 remain areas for further optimization.

            I MPROVING M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
465
466
      1. GPT-4o’s Performance Strengths and Limitations: GPT-4o is the top performer across all
         datasets, achieving an overall F1 score of 87%. Its foundational capabilities enable strong
467
         performance in both mistake detection and rectification, particularly on simpler datasets like
468
         GSM-8K. However, its performance drops significantly on more complex datasets such as
469      JEEBENCH and MATHBENCH, indicating limitations in handling highly complex or novel
470      problems and highlighting the need for improved reasoning capabilities.
471   2. Data Contamination and Overfitting Concerns: GPT-4o’s unexpectedly high performance on
472      datasets like GSM-8K and SVAMP suggests possible data contamination and overfitting, with
473      models benefiting from memorized examples. To ensure fair evaluation, cleaner datasets and
474      rigorous training methods are needed to assess true reasoning abilities rather than memorization.
475   3. Challenges with Smaller Language Models (SLMs): There is a notable performance gap
476      between smaller models (SLMs) and larger models like GPT-4o. While some SLMs, like
477
         Llama-3-8b-finetuned and Llama-3-70B, show competitive results, this may stem from similar
         contamination issues. Enhancing SLMs’ reasoning abilities, is a key area for improvement.
478
      4. Generalization Difficulties Across Datasets: The performance decline on newer datasets such as
479
         MATHBENCH and JEEBENCH points to a generalization issue in LLMs. While models perform
480      well on familiar datasets, they struggle with novel problems. Addressing this requires improved
481      training strategies and more diverse datasets to broaden models’ reasoning skills.
482   5. Inconsistent Rectification Abilities: Despite strong mistake detection, GPT-4o shows inconsis-
483      tent rectification performance, especially in complex datasets. Its ability to correct errors drops
484      significantly between simple tasks (like rule-based errors) and more challenging ones (SLM-
485      generated mistakes). This highlights the need for more robust error correction capabilities in
         diverse reasoning scenarios.


                                                        9
486
      Future research should prioritize cleaner datasets and techniques to reduce data contamination and
487   overfitting, enabling better generalization to new tasks. Improving error rectification and enhancing
488   smaller models through fine-tuning are key, as is advancing models’ ability to handle complex
489   rule-based reasoning for better performance on structured problems.
490
491   6    R ELATED W ORK
492   Current research on large language models (LLMs) for solving math word problems (MWPs)
493   primarily emphasizes generating correct answers, often focusing on overall accuracy rather than
494   evaluating the underlying reasoning processes. Studies like MathPrompter (Imani et al., 2023) and
495   WizardMath (Luo et al., 2023) showcase impressive results in solving MWPs by generating complex
496   reasoning steps. However, their focus remains heavily centered on achieving the correct answer
497   without rigorously evaluating the correctness, relevance, or verification of the individual reasoning
498   steps. For instance, works such as (Liu et al., 2024b; Yuan et al., 2023; Schulman et al., 2017) focus
499   primarily on enhancing LLMs’ ability to reach accurate answers but do not delve into assessing
500
      whether the reasoning process itself is correct or aligned with logical problem-solving paths.
501   Several recent works have begun shifting their attention toward reasoning quality, but these efforts
502   remain limited in scope. Studies like (Sawada et al., 2023) evaluate reasoning by comparing the
503   similarity of generated and reference reasoning, while others, such as (Xia et al., 2024), introduce the
504   idea of assessing reasoning steps through metrics like validity and redundancy. ROSCOE(Golovneva
505   et al., 2023) takes this further by offering a suite of unsupervised metrics that evaluate various aspects
506
      of reasoning quality, such as semantic consistency and logicality, rather than just the final answer.
      While these methods attempt to scrutinize reasoning steps, they often fall short of addressing the
507
      detection and rectification of specific reasoning mistakes within MWPs, leaving a gap in understanding
508
      how well LLMs can manage flawed reasoning.
509
510   A third significant gap in the literature pertains to the limited exploration of LLMs’ foundational
511   reasoning abilities, particularly in mistake detection and rectification. While some works propose
512
      LLMs as verifiers for their own reasoning (Zhang et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2023), they typically
      assess reasoning correctness without tackling the deeper issue of identifying and correcting logical
513
      mistakes. Moreover, studies like (Olausson et al., 2024) demonstrate that LLMs struggle to find and
514
      correct their own reasoning errors, especially in tasks involving code generation. Recent works such
515   as Alice in Wonderland (Nezhurina et al., 2024) breakdown the function and reasoning capabilities
516   of LLMs and show that even small variations in such common sense tasks has drastic performance
517   reduction. However, there remains a lack of rigorous benchmarking for mistake detection and
518   correction in MWPs, especially for foundational models.
519
      Our work addresses this gap by introducing the MWP-Mistake dataset, which includes diverse,
520
      systematic reasoning mistakes in MWPs. Unlike prior research, our analysis focuses not only on
521   models’ ability to detect mistakes but also on their ability to rectify these errors and generate correct
522   answers. We provide a comprehensive benchmark, comparing state-of-the-art models on both simple
523   and complex datasets. Through this work, we aim to provide a clearer understanding of current
524   models’ limitations in handling reasoning mistakes and propose a framework for evaluating LLMs’
525   true reasoning abilities, rather than relying on answer accuracy alone.
526
527   7    C ONCLUSIONS
528
      This study evaluates large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4o, GPT-4, GPT-3.5Turbo, along-
529
      side smaller models like Llama-2-7b-chat, Mixtral-8x7B, and Phi-3-mini, on their ability to detect
530
      and correct errors in mathematical reasoning. Using our MWP-MISTAKE dataset, which includes
531   incorrect reasoning steps generated through both rule-based methods and smaller models, we compre-
532   hensively assess LLMs’ performance in error detection and rectification. While GPT-4o outperforms
533   other models, there remains a gap in its ability to consistently detect mistakes, as it struggles with
534   several simple problems and its performance degrades on more complex tasks. We also uncover
535   issues of data contamination and overfitting, especially in GPT-4’s performance on GSM8K, and
536   observe a performance drop on newer datasets like MATHBENCH and JEEBENCH, highlighting
537   generalization challenges. Addressing these limitations—such as enhancing generalization and mini-
538   mizing data contamination—is essential for making LLMs more reliable and applicable to real-world
539
      mathematical problem-solving. Future research should focus on refining training processes and
      strengthening models’ reasoning abilities to meet these challenges.

693   A    MWP-MISTAKE DATASET
694
695   MWP-MISTAKE dataset is curated using 4 different types of well-known datasets. Below are the
696   details of each of the datasets.
697          • SVAMP Patel et al. (2021): SVAMP is a MWP dataset created by applying a carefully
698            chosen variations over examples sampled from existing datasets, AsDiv-A Miao et al. (2020)
699            and MAWPS Koncel-Kedziorski et al. (2016).
700          • GSM-8K Cobbe et al. (2021b):GSM-8K is a dataset of diverse grade school math word
701            problems created by human writers, involving basic arithmetic operations. Released in
               November 2021.


                                                       13
702
              • MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021): The MATH dataset is divided into seven categories, each
703             with five difficulty levels. For our study, we used levels 1, 2, and 3 from the algebra
704             and counting and probability categories. Released in November 2021. We focused on
705             Levels 1 to 3 because the problems in Levels 4 and 5 are more complex, requiring specific
706             notations, symbols, and equations. Injecting reasoning mistakes into such complex problems
707             is non-trivial and would require expert knowledge to ensure accuracy in the reasoning chains.
708           • MATHBENCH Liu et al. (2024a): MATHBENCH is a recent dataset with questions divided
709             by educational stages, from basic arithmetic to college levels. For our experiment, we chose
710             middle and high-school-level single-choice multiple-choice questions. Released in May
711
                2024.
              • JEEBENCH Arora et al. (2023): JEEBENCH is a challenging benchmark dataset for
712
                evaluating LLM problem-solving abilities, containing 515 pre-engineering math, physics,
713
                and chemistry problems from the IIT JEE-Advanced Exam. For our experiment, we chose
714             mathematics single-choice questions only. Released in October 2023.
