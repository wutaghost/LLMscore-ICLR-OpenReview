009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) have shown out-
012            standing advancement in various multi-modal understanding tasks by leveraging
013            the powerful knowledge of large language models (LLMs). Extending MLLMs to
014            text-video retrieval enables handling more complex queries with multiple modal-
015            ities beyond simple uni-modal queries for traditional search engines. It also pro-
016
               vides a new opportunity to incorporate search into a unified conversational sys-
               tem, but MLLM-based text-video retrieval has been less explored in the liter-
017
               ature. To this end, we investigate MLLMs’ capabilities in text-video retrieval
018
               as a generation task, namely, generative retrieval, in two directions. An intu-
019            itive direction is content generation that directly generates the content given a
020            query. Another direction is query generation, which generates the query given
021            the content. Interestingly, we observe that in both text-to-video and video-to-text
022            retrieval tasks, query-generation less suffers from the bias and significantly out-
023            performs content-generation. In this paper, we propose a novel framework, Bidi-
024            rectional Text-Video Generative Retrieval (BGR), that handles both text-to-video
025            and video-to-text retrieval tasks by measuring the relevance using two genera-
026            tion directions. Our framework trains MLLMs by simultaneously optimizing two
027
               objectives, i.e., video-grounded text generation (VTG) and text-grounded video
               feature generation (TVG). At inference, our framework ensembles predictions by
028
               both generation directions. We also introduce a Prior Normalization, a simple
029
               plug-and-play module, to further alleviate the prior bias induced by the likelihood
030            of uni-modal content data that often overwhelms the relevance between query and
031            content. Our extensive experiments on multi-modal benchmarks demonstrate that
032            BGR and Prior Normalization are effective in alleviating the prior bias, especially
033            the text prior bias from LLMs’ pretrained knowledge in MLLMs, achieving state-
034            of-the-art performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In recent years, large language models (LLMs) (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023;
      Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2022; Team, 2023) have gained attention
040
      due to their powerful knowledge obtained during pretraining. To leverage LLMs’ knowledge in
041
      understanding visual recognition, LLMs have been extended into Multi-modal large language mod-
042   els (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Driess et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024b; Zhu
043   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c;b; Liu et al., 2024) and they have demonstrated remarkable capabil-
044   ities in comprehensive image and video understanding. Despite the success of MLLMs in various
045   multi-modal understanding tasks, adapting those models to text-video retrieval (i.e., text-to-video
046   and video-to-text), which aims to find the most relevant video (or text) content among content can-
047   didates given a text (or video) query as in Fig. 1a, has been less explored in the literature.
048   In general, MLLMs are trained to generate a text sequence given the image or video, enabling
049   them to follow complex human instructions regarding visual information. Therefore, integrating
050   the retrieval task into MLLMs can be easily extended to instruction-based retrieval beyond standard
051   retrieval benchmarks with simple captions, in real-world chatting systems. Specifically, MLLMs can
052   be applied to process more complex multi-modal queries to retrieve the most relevant multi-modal
053   content as in Fig. 1b. The versatility, in addition to MLLMs’ powerful knowledge, motivates our
      work to integrate the retrieval task into MLLMs by mainly exploring the Text-Video Retrieval task.

073   In this paper, we formulate the Text-Video Retrieval task as a generation task by directly leveraging
074   MLLMs’ generation capabilities, i.e., generative retrieval (Tay et al., 2022). Intuitively, when adapt-
075   ing the generative retrieval to Text-Video Retrieval, one might want to generate a content modality
076   given a query modality, i.e., content-generation. However, the query-generation model, which gen-
077
      erates the query given the content, significantly outperforms the content-generation model. For ex-
      ample, in text-to-video retrieval (T2V), the query-generation model which generates the text query
078
      given the video content surpasses the content-generation model which generates the video content
079
      by a margin of 28.9 on R@1 in Fig. 1c. This is attributed to an undesirable bias towards the prior
080   probability of the content, i.e., prior bias. Specifically, on video-to-text retrieval (V2T), the content-
081   generation model generating text content predominantly retrieves the caption with the highest text
082   prior probability in Fig. 2a, i.e., RANK-1 based on the text prior probability P (t)). In fact, this
083   caption is retrieved for approximately 777 out of 1,003 videos, accounting for 77% of the total. This
084   implies that MLLMs provide suboptimal retrieval results in V2T ignoring the relevance between the
085   video query and the text content. We also observe that the text prior bias in V2T is more severe than
086   the video prior bias in T2V due to the LLMs’ strong pretrained knowledge. Such text prior bias is
087   prevalent even in other multi-modal tasks like visual question answering and captioning (Niu et al.,
088
      2021; Ramakrishnan et al., 2018; Cadene et al., 2019; Leng et al., 2024). Overall, both text prior
      bias and video prior bias are exhibited in MLLMs with the content-generation model for generative
089
      retrieval.
090
091   To handle both video and text retrievals, we propose a novel framework, Bidirectional Text-Video
092   Generative Retrieval (BGR), which measures the relevance between the video and text using two
093   generation directions. Our framework trains MLLMs by simultaneously optimizing two objectives,
094
      i.e., video-grounded text generation (VTG) and text-grounded video feature generation (TVG).
      At inference, BGR ensembles predictions by both generation directions. For instance, in text-to-
095
      video retrieval, BGR bolsters its predictions from content generation (video) by query generation
096
      (text), which is more accurate and robust to prior bias as in Fig. 1c. We also propose a plug-
097   and-play module, Prior Normalization, which is a simple yet effective training-free score cali-
098   bration method to further reduce the prior bias at retrieval inference. We showcase the effective-
099   ness of our BGR equipped with Prior Normalization on four popular Text-Video Retrieval bench-
100   mark datasets (DiDeMo (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017), ActivityNet (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015),
101   LSMDC (Rohrbach et al., 2017), and MSRVTT (Xu et al., 2016)). Our in-depth analyses show-
102   case that Prior Normalization can be extended to a Prior Normalized Decoding for a wide range of
103   multi-modal understanding tasks beyond retrieval.
104   To sum up, our contributions are as follows:
105

107            knowledge, this paper is the first work that studies prior bias induced by the likelihood of
               uni-modal content data, which is severer in content generation than qeury generation.

123   2.1   P RELIMINARY
124
      Text-Video Retrieval consists of two sub-tasks, i.e., text-to-video retrieval (T2V) and video-to-text
125
      retrieval (V2T), aiming to find the most relevant video (or text) content given a text (or video) query.
126   Previous approaches have conducted the retrieval task by employing video-text contrastive (VTC)
127   and video-text matching (VTM) loss functions to align video and text features. The VTC loss
128   focuses on aligning uni-modal video and text representation by maximizing the mutual information
129   with the symmetric contrastive loss (Bain et al., 2021). Given the global features of video and text
130   from each uni-modal encoder, ṽ ∈ RD and t̃ ∈ RD , the VTC loss is defined as:

202   3.1   MLLM BASELINE FOR T EXT-V IDEO R ETRIEVAL
203
204   We here introduce a simple baseline of MLLM for Text-Video Retrieval by applying common
205   retrieval objectives, VTC and VTM. MLLMs are generally composed of a Vision Encoder, Pro-
206   jection Layer, and LLM. The visual tokens are first extracted from the vision encoder and then
207
      projected into the LLM’s embedding space via the projection layer. Those tokens are concate-
      nated to the text tokens and fed to the LLM to perform multi-modal tasks. We fine-tune pretrained
208
      VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024b) which adopts the Q-Former and a linear layer as the projection layer
209
      and LoRA to fine-tune an LLM, as illustrated in Fig. 3a. In addition to the projected Lv video tokens
210   v = [v1 , . . . , vLv ] ∈ RLv ×D and Lt text tokens t = [t1 , . . . , tLt ] ∈ RLt ×D with the hidden dimen-
211   sion D, we append two additional special tokens vvtc ∈ RD and tvtc ∈ RD that indicate the end of
212   the video and text, respectively. These special tokens aggregate information of each modality and
213   their output representations (ṽvtc and t̃vtc ) are used to calculate the VTC loss in Eq. (1). The cross-
214   modal attention is masked to prevent information leakage from the other modality when obtaining
215   ṽvtc and t̃vtc . Similarly, one special token zvtm is appended to calculate the VTM loss. We update the
      parameters only in the linear projection layer and LoRA for parameter-efficient fine-tuning.

349
350   In this section, we evaluate the benefits of our method on four popular Text-Video Retrieval bench-
351   mark datasets and seven image and video understanding benchmarks. We first show an extensive
352   analysis of BGR and then verify the effectiveness of Prior Normalization and Prior Normalized
353   Decoding. Finally, we compare our model with the state-of-the-art models.

356   diverse-length video and caption pairs. For a comprehensive evaluation of multi-modal understand-

505   5   R ELATED W ORKS
506
507   Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs). In recent years, there has been a significant
508   surge in the development of large language models (LLMs) (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Brown et al.,
509   2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Chung et al.,
510   2022). Following the achievements, integrating a visual encoder into such LLMs has propelled
511   progress in the vision-language multi-modal domain, known as multi-modal large language models
512   (MLLMs) (Alayrac et al., 2022; Driess et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023b; 2024b; Zhu
513
      et al., 2023). A notable MLLM includes Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), which aligns the pretrained
      vision encoder and the language model and handles a sequence of text tokens interleaved with the
514
      visual content. More recently, both image MLLMs (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023b; Liu
515
      et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023) and video MLLMs (Li et al., 2023c; 2024b; Maaz et al., 2023; Zhang
516   et al., 2023a) are further trained by visual instruction tuning to enhance visual understanding and
517   reasoning. For example, VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024b) has achieved state-of-the-art performances
518   on various video multi-modal tasks by 3-stage pretraining with diverse instruction-tuning data.
519
      Text-Video Retrieval. Text-Video Retrieval is one of the popular multi-modal tasks that aims to find
520
      the most relevant video based on text or vice versa. Early studies (Luo et al., 2022; Gorti et al., 2022;
521
      Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023) have focused on leveraging pretrained CLIP (Rad-
522   ford et al., 2021) for joint embedding space learning between video and text. For example, Wu et al.
523   (2023) have proposed Cap4Video by utilizing captions to enhance video-text alignment based on
524   CLIP. However, such studies have shown unsatisfactory performances due to the limitation of shal-
525   low fusion of the dual encoders. Recently, large-scale multi-modal video foundation models (Cheng
526   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a; Li et al., 2023d) have boosted the performance by implementing a
527   cross-modal fusion, which learns the fine-grained interactions between the two modalities. Those
528   models have adopted a hybrid inference approach which efficiently retrieves the top-k candidates by
529   VTC and then computes their pairwise scores by VTM for more accurate retrievals.
530
531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we observe that content-generation with MLLMs tends to retrieve the incorrect caption
534   from a given video (and vice versa) due to the prior bias in Text-Video Retrieval. Therefore, we
535   propose a Bidirectional Text-Video Generative Retrieval (BGR) to address the over-reliance on the
536   prior by ensembling predictions from both directions, i.e., content-generation and query-generation.
537   Moreover, our simple plug-and-play score calibration module, Prior Normalization, further improves
538   the performances along with BGR in Text-Video Retrieval by reducing dependence on the prior. Our
539   experimental results highlight the potential of Prior Normalized Decoding applied to MLLMs in a
      more balanced consideration of both textual and visual information in various multi-modal tasks.

834   A.1   T EXT-V IDEO R ETRIEVAL
835
      DiDeMo (Anne Hendricks et al., 2017). Distinct Describable Moments (DiDeMo) contains 10K
836
      videos which are divided into 5-second segments. It has a total of 26K moments whose descriptions
837
      are detailed and contain camera movement, temporal transition indicators, and activities. We follow
838   the previous works Bain et al. (2021); Lei et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023); Cheng
839   et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023d) by concatenating all captions of one video and solving the task
840   as a paragraph-video retrieval task. The number of training and test samples is 8,394 and 1,003,
841   respectively.
842
      ActivityNet (Caba Heilbron et al., 2015). ActivityNet dataset contains 19K videos from YouTube,
843
      which are categorized into 200 different types of activities. On average, each category has 137
844   videos and each video has 1.41 activities which are annotated with temporal boundaries. Similar to
845   DiDeMo, we also concatenate all the captions of a video to form a paragraph-video retrieval task on
846   the ‘val1’ split by following Zhang et al. (2018); Gabeur et al. (2020); Luo et al. (2022); Cheng et al.
847   (2023); Li et al. (2023d). Therefore, the number of training and test samples is 10,009 and 4,917,
848   respectively.
849
      LSMDC (Rohrbach et al., 2017). Large Scale Movie Description Challenge (LSMDC) contains
850   118K short video clips from 202 movies with captions from the movie script or from transcribed
851   DVS (descriptive video services) for the visually impaired. Our model is trained with 101,055
852   videos and evaluated on 1,000 videos.
853
      MSRVTT (Xu et al., 2016). Microsoft Research Video to Text (MSRVTT) contains 10K video
854
      clips from 20 categories, with each video clip annotated with 20 sentences. There are 29K unique
855
      words in all captions. Following the literature Yu et al. (2018); Luo et al. (2022); Miech et al. (2019);
856   Gabeur et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023d), we train our model
857   with 9,000 × 20 training samples and 1,000 test samples.

      soning, numerical calculation, code reasoning, etc.

      SeedBench (Li et al., 2023a). SeedBench aims at a comprehensive assessment of generative models

      BGR details. Our BGR is built upon VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024b) and is further fine-tuned on
893   each Text-Video Retrieval dataset. Specifically, VideoChat2 consists of a visual encoder, Q-Former,
894   a linear projection layer, and a LLM. The visual encoder and LLM are initialized with UMT-L (Li
895   et al., 2023d) and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023), respectively. We freeze parameters
896   in the visual encoder, Q-Former, and LLM, and only update parameters in the linear projection layer
897   and LoRA for parameter-efficient fine-tuning, resulting in 48M trainable parameters among 7B total
898   parameters (0.7%). We accumulate gradients from the TVG and VTG loss and update the trainable
899   parameters at once.

       G     D ICUSSION ON C OMPUTATIONAL C OST
1147
1148
       Since traversing all video candidates requires intensive computational cost, we employ a re-ranking
1149   approach to efficiently obtain the retrieval scores among top-k candidates. The VTG and TVG
1150   scores generally demonstrate superior performance compared to VTC scores but require a larger
1151   computational cost. Therefore, we adopt a hybrid approach which efficiently retrieves the top-k
1152   candidates by VTC loss, and then computes more accurate scores using VTG (or TVG) for all pairs
1153   of the top-k candidates. This re-ranking approach among top-k candidates significantly reduces the
1154   inference time complexity of the single-tower model from O(mn) to O(k(m + n)) given m videos
1155   and n texts, where k ≪ m, n. As a result, the inference time is 307 times faster than traversing all
1156   candidate videos on ActivityNet (97 hours → 19 minutes).
1157   Furthermore, although our final model, BGR∗ , requires an ensemble of output scores optimized for
1158   three different objectives (i.e., VTM, VTG, and TVG), the overall inference process remains efficient
1159   due to the shared feature extraction stage across all objectives. In Tab. 3, ensembling VTM, VTG,
1160   and TVG boosts R@1 significantly, e.g., 82.4 on DiDeMo, while achieving a 19% improvement in
1161   inference speed compared to processing each objective separately.
1162   Finally, Tab. 7 demonstrates the additional inference time overhead of Prior Normalized Decoding
1163   on the benchmarks in Tab. 2. Since these benchmarks consist of multi-choice questions, the number
1164   of newly generated tokens by the model is less than 10 tokens. This implies that Prior Normalized
1165   Decoding introduces only a marginal increase in inference time. In Tab. 7, the average performance
1166   is improved by 16.3 while the additional inference time is only increased by 4.9%. On the other
1167   hand, the inference time might be increased if the number of newly generated tokens becomes large.
