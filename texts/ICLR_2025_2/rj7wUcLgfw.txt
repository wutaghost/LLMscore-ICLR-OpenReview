011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Techniques that explain the predictions of black-box machine learning models are
014            crucial to make the models transparent, thereby increasing trust in AI systems. The
015            input features to the models often have a nested structure that consists of high- and
016            low-level features, and each high-level feature is decomposed into multiple low-
017            level features. For such inputs, both high-level feature attributions (HiFAs) and
018            low-level feature attributions (LoFAs) are important for better understanding the
019
               model’s decision. In this paper, we propose a model-agnostic local explanation
               method that effectively exploits the nested structure of the input to estimate the
020
               two-level feature attributions simultaneously. A key idea of the proposed method
021
               is to introduce the consistency property that should exist between the HiFAs and
022            LoFAs, thereby bridging the separate optimization problems for estimating them.
023            Thanks to this consistency property, the proposed method can produce HiFAs and
024            LoFAs that are both faithful to the black-box models and consistent with each
025            other, using a smaller number of queries to the models. In experiments on image
026            classification in multiple instance learning and text classification using language
027            models, we demonstrate that the HiFAs and LoFAs estimated by the proposed
028            method are accurate, faithful to the behaviors of the black-box models, and pro-
029            vide consistent explanations.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The rapid increase in size and complexity of machine learning (ML) models has led to a growing
035
      concern about their black-box nature. Models provided as cloud services are literal black boxes,
036   as users have no access to the models themselves and the training data used. This opacity raises
037   numerous concerns, including issues of trust, accountability, and transparency. Consequently, tech-
038   niques to explain the predictions made by those black-box models have been attracting significant
039   attention (Danilevsky et al., 2020; Došilović et al., 2018; Saeed & Omlin, 2023).
040
      Various model-agnostic local explanation methods have been proposed to explain the predictions
041
      of black-box models. The representative methods are, for example, local interpretable model-
042   agnostic explanation (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016) and kernel Shapley additive explanations (Kernel
043   SHAP) (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), which estimate the feature attributions of the individual prediction
044   by approximating the model’s behavior with local linear surrogate models around the input.
045
      In LIME and Kernel SHAP, the input to the model is generally assumed to be a flat structure, where
046
      the input features are treated as independent variables. In many realistic tasks for various domains,
047
      such as image, text, geographic, e-commerce, and social network data, however, the input features
048   have a nested structure that consists of high- and low-level features, and each high-level feature is
049   decomposed into multiple low-level features. A typical task with such nested features is multiple
050   instance learning (MIL) (Ilse et al., 2018) where the model is formulated as set functions (Kimura
051   et al., 2024). In MIL, the input is a set of instances, the high-level feature is an instance in the set,
052   and the low-level features represent the features of the instance. In addition, even if the input is not
053   represented with a nested structure when it is fed into the model, it may be more natural to interpret
      it with the nested structure. For example, although a text input is usually represented as a sequence

      The two-level features enable us to understand the model predictions with two types of feature
072
      attributions that have different levels of granularity in explanation, which we name high-level feature
073   attributions (HiFAs) and low-level feature attributions (LoFAs), respectively. Figure 1 shows an
074   example of the prediction for a nested structured input and its corresponding HiFAs and LoFAs. The
075   HiFAs represent how much each of the high-level features contributes to the prediction. These are
076   also referred to as instance attributions in the MIL literature (Early et al., 2022; Javed et al., 2022),
077   which are used to reveal which instances strongly affected the model’s decision. On the other hand,
078   the LoFAs represent how much each of the low-level features contributes to the prediction, providing
079   a more fine-grained explanation of how the components of the instances affected the prediction.
080   Both the HiFAs and LoFAs are important for understanding the model’s decision. However, existing
081
      studies have focused on estimating either-level attributions, and no study has addressed estimating
      the HiFAs and LoFAs simultaneously.
082
083   For the estimation of the HiFAs and LoFAs, two naive approaches can be applied. One is to estimate
084   the HiFAs and LoFAs separately by applying existing model-agnostic local explanation methods
085   to the high- and low-level features, respectively. The other is to estimate the LoFAs first, as with
086   the former approach, and then estimate the HiFAs by aggregating the LoFAs. However, these ap-
087
      proaches have two rooms for improvement in terms of using the nested structure of the input. First,
      even though the queries to the black-box model are often limited in practice due to the computational
088
      time and request costs, the input structure is not utilized to reduce the number of queries in the esti-
089
      mation. Second, the former approach can produce inconsistent explanations between the HiFAs and
090   LoFAs, for example, the most influential high-level feature and the high-level feature to which the
091   most influential low-level feature belongs may not match.
092
093
      To address these issues, we propose a model-agnostic local explanation method that effectively ex-
      ploits the nested structure of the input to estimate the HiFAs and LoFAs simultaneously. A key idea
094
      of the proposed method is to introduce the consistency property that should exist between the HiFAs
095
      and LoFAs, thereby bridging the separate optimization problems for them. We solve a joint opti-
096   mization problem to estimate the HiFAs and LoFAs simultaneously with the consistency constraints
097   depicted in Figure 1 based on the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) (Boyd et al.,
098   2011). The algorithm is a general framework that can also introduce various types of regularizations
099   and constraints for the HiFAs and LoFAs, such as the ℓ1 and ℓ2 regularizations and non-negative
100   constraints, which lead to the ease of interpretability for humans.
101
      In experiments, we quantitatively and qualitatively assess the HiFAs and LoFAs estimated by
102   the proposed method on image classification in the MIL setting and text classification using lan-
103   guage models, compared with estimating them separately and using a recent attribution method for
104   MIL (Early et al., 2022). The experimental results show that the HiFAs and LoFAs estimated by
105   the proposed method 1) satisfy the consistency property, 2) are faithful explanations to the black-
106   box models even when the number of queries to the model is small, 3) can accurately guess the
107   ground-truth positive instances and their features in the MIL task, and 4) are reasonable explana-
      tions visually.

      The contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
109
110   • This study is the first to propose a model-agnostic local explanation method to estimate the two-
111     level nested feature attributions simultaneously, which satisfies the consistency property between

113   • In the experiments on practical tasks, we demonstrated that the proposed method could produce

      2     R ELATED W ORK
118
119
120
      Numerous methods for explaining the individual predictions of black-box models have been pro-
      posed in the literature (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2018; Petsiuk
121
      et al., 2018; Plumb et al., 2018). A versatile approach is to explain feature attributions estimated by
122
      approximating the model predictions with surrogate models around the input, such as LIME (Ribeiro
123   et al., 2016) and Kernel SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017). The proposed method is in line with this
124   type of approach.
125
126
      Set data is one of the nested input features, which treats a set of multiple instances as a single
      input. Set data appears in various ML applications, such as point cloud classification (Guo et al.,
127
      2021), medical image analysis (Cheplygina et al., 2019), and group recommendation (Dara et al.,
128
      2020), and the explainability on those applications has also been studied in the literature (Tan &
129   Kotthaus, 2022; van der Velden et al., 2022). Unlike our work, most such studies focus only on
130   estimating instance attributions corresponding to those of high-level features. For example, Early et
131   al. proposed to estimate instance attributions by learning surrogate models with MIL-suitable kernel
132   functions (Early et al., 2022).
133
      Several studies have addressed estimating feature attributions effectively by leveraging group in-
134
      formation of input features. In the natural language processing literature, some studies estimated
135   sentence- and phrase-level feature attributions by grouping words in the same sentence and phrase
136   together and regarding them as a single feature (Zafar et al., 2021; Mosca et al., 2022). In addi-
137   tion, Rychener et al. showed that word-level feature attributions can be improved by generating
138   perturbations at a sentence level, mitigating the issues of out-of-distribution for the model and high-
139   dimensional search space (Rychener et al., 2023). In the official SHAP library (shap (Github), 2024),
140   by grouping input features by hierarchical clustering in advance and generating perturbations at the
141   group level, one can reduce the number of queries to the model.
142   If we consider high-level features as nodes and low-level features as the features of the nodes and
143   then somehow put edges between the nodes, we can think of an input as a graph. By doing so,
144   model-agnostic explanation methods for graphs, such as GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) and
145   GraphLIME (Huang et al., 2023), can be applied to our task. However, since this approach highly
146   relies on the graph structure, additional information is required to create appropriate edges.

      The model f to be explained is a trained black-box model that takes an arbitrary input, such as
      tabular, image and text, x ∈ X , and outputs a prediction y = f (x) ∈ [0, 1]C where X is the input
154
      space and C is the number of classes. The input x is made of two-level nested features, referred to as
155
      high-level and low-level features, and the high-level feature is decomposed into multiple low-level
156   features. In particular, the input x is represented as a set or sequence of J high-level features, i.e.,
157   x = {xj }Jj=1 where xj ∈ RDj is the Dj -dimensional low-level feature vector representing the j-th
158   high-level feature. One example of such input appears in image classification under the MIL setting.
159   In this setting, the input is a bag of images, the high-level feature is an image in the bag, and the
160   low-level features correspond to super-pixels in the image. Another example appears in a document
161   classification where the input is a sequence of sentences, the high-level feature is a sentence in the
      sequence, and the low-level features correspond to the words in the sentence.

249   the HiFAs and LoFAs accurately with small NH and NL is crucial. In the experiments in Section 4,
250   we demonstrate that the proposed method can estimate high-quality HiFAs and LoFAs even when
251   NH and NA are small. A detailed discussion on the computational time complexity is provided in

      Dataset. We constructed an MIL dataset from the Pascal VOC semantic segmentation dataset (Ev-
310
      eringham et al., 2015) that allows us to evaluate the estimated HiFAs and LoFAs with the ground-
311
      truth instance- and pixel-level labels. With the training subset of the dataset, each sample (bag)
312   has from three to five images (high-level features) drawn at random from the training subset of the
313   Pascal VOC. Here, low-level features correspond to regions (super-pixels) of each image, which are
314   obtained by the quick shift algorithm (Vedaldi & Soatto, 2008). Each bag is labeled positive if at
315   least an image in the bag is associated with “cat” label and negative otherwise. Also, each image
316   pixel is labeled positive if the pixel is associated with “cat” label and negative otherwise. We used
317   the instance- and pixel-level supervision only for evaluation. Similarly, we constructed validation
318   and test subsets whose samples contain images from the training and test subsets of the Pascal VOC,
319   respectively. The number of samples in training, validation, and test subsets is 5,000, 1,000, and
320
      2,000, respectively, and the positive and negative samples ratio is equal.
321
322   Black-box Model. We used DeepSets permutation-invariant model (Zaheer et al., 2017) with
323   ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) as black-box model f to be explained. We describe the implementation
      details of the model in Appendix C.1. Here, the test accuracy of the model was 0.945.

      Quantitative Evaluation. We assessed the estimated HiFAs and LoFAs in terms of correctness,
325   faithfulness, and consistency. The correctness is evaluated using the ground-truth instance- and
326   pixel-level labels. Following the evaluation in the MIL study (Early et al., 2022), we evaluated the
327   estimated HiFAs with normalized discounted cumulative gain (NDCG). For the estimated LoFAs,
328   as with the evaluation of the LoFAs for single image classification (Sampaio & Cordeiro, 2023), we
329   evaluated them as the predictions of the pixel-level labels by the area under ROC curve (AUROC)
330   in the binary semantic segmentation manner. In the faithfulness evaluation, we assessed whether
331   the estimated HiFAs and LoFAs are faithful to the behaviors of the model f based on insertion and
332   deletion metrics. The insertion and deletion metrics evaluate the change in the predictions of the
333
      model f when features deemed important in the LoFAs are gradually added and removed from the
      sample, respectively (Petsiuk et al., 2018). In our experiments, we gradually add and remove the
334
      low-level features across all the high-level features in descending order of their LoFAs. Also, for
335
      the HiFAs, we add and remove the high-level features instead of the low-level ones, respectively.
336   In terms of the consistency evaluation, we used the following two metrics. The first one is the
337
      consistency between the estimated HiFAs and LoFAs, which is calculated with ∥α − M β † ∥2 used
338   to calculate the penalty for the consistency constraints in (7). The second one is the agreement of
339   the most important high- and low-level feature (MIHL), which is calculated by the ratio that the
340   high-level feature of the highest HiFA is identical to the one associated with the low-level feature of
341   the highest LoFA.
342
      We evaluated the above metrics using only the samples with the positive bag label because we could
343
      not evaluate the correctness of those with the negative bag label. We ran the evaluations three times
344   with different random seeds and reported the average scores and their standard deviation.

397   Another practical application of the proposed method is to explain the attributions of sentences and

400   Dataset. For evaluation, we constructed a dataset whose validation and test subsets are made of

      500 and 1,000 product review texts extracted randomly from the training and test subsets of the

402
      multiple sentences regarded as high-level features, where each sentence is represented as a sequence

      of words regarded as low-level features, and the sample label represents the review’s polarity, posi-
404   tive or negative.

409
      accuracy of the model is 0.947. When masking a word in the input to generate perturbed inputs, we
      replaced the word with the predefined mask token [MASK]. Similarly, when masking a sentence,
410
      we replaced all the words in the sentence with the mask token.
411
412   Quantitative Evaluation. Because no ground-truth labels for HiFAs and LoFAs are available in

      Our work contributes to improving the transparency of black-box models. However, it should be
487   noted that high-quality feature attributions may give hints about stealing the information that the
488   model’s providers want to hide, such as the training data and the model’s decision-making process.
489   To prevent such risks, it is essential to establish guidelines that ensure that the feature attributions
490   are not used for malicious purposes.

      6    C ONCLUSION
493
494
      We proposed a model-agnostic local explanation method for nested structured inputs, which explains
495
      two-level feature attributions, referred to as HiFAs and LoFAs, simultaneously. We hypothesized
496   that the consistency property naturally derived from the characteristics of the surrogate models is
497   essential to produce explanations that are accurate, faithful and consistent between HiFAs and LoFAs
498   with a smaller number of queries to the model. Then, we presented an optimization algorithm
499   that estimates the HiFAs and LoFAs while forcing them to ensure the consistency property. We
500   demonstrated that the proposed method can produce high-quality explanations query-efficiently in
501   the experiments on image classification in multiple instance learning and text classification using
502   large language models. In future work, we will expand the applicability of the proposed method
503   by extending it to tasks with three or more levels of nested features, such as multi-multi instance
504
      learning (Tibo et al., 2020).
