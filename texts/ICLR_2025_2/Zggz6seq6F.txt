011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           Large vision-language models (LVLMs) have made significant strides in address-
014           ing complex video tasks, sparking researchers’ interest in their human-like mul-
015           timodal understanding capabilities. Video description serves as a fundamental
016           task for evaluating video comprehension, necessitating a deep understanding of
017           spatial and temporal dynamics, which presents challenges for both humans and
018           machines. Thus, investigating whether LVLMs can describe videos as comprehen-
019           sively as humans—through reasonable human-machine comparisons using video
020
              captioning as a proxy task—will enhance our understanding and application of
              these models. However, current benchmarks for video comprehension have no-
021
              table limitations, including short video durations, brief annotations, and reliance
022
              on a single annotator’s perspective. These factors hinder a comprehensive assess-
023           ment of LVLMs’ ability to understand complex, lengthy videos and prevent the
024           establishment of a robust human baseline that accurately reflects human video
025           comprehension capabilities. To address these issues, we propose a novel bench-
026           mark, FIOVA (Five In One Video Annotations), designed to evaluate the differ-
027           ences between LVLMs and human understanding more comprehensively. FIOVA
028           includes 3,002 long video sequences (averaging 33.6 seconds) that cover diverse
029           scenarios with complex spatiotemporal relationships. Each video is annotated by
030           five distinct annotators, capturing a wide range of perspectives and resulting in
031
              captions that are 4 ∼ 15 times longer than most existing benchmarks, thereby
              establishing a robust baseline that represents human understanding comprehen-
032
              sively for the first time in video description tasks. Using the FIOVA benchmark,
033
              we conducted an in-depth evaluation of six state-of-the-art (SOTA) LVLMs, com-
034           paring their performance with humans. To enhance this evaluation, we proposed
035           FIOVA-DQ, a novel event-based metric that incorporates weighted event impor-
036           tance derived from human annotations. Results show that while current LVLMs
037           demonstrate some perception and reasoning capabilities, they still struggle with
038           information omission and descriptive depth. Moreover, we found significant dis-
039           crepancies between LVLMs and humans in complex videos, particularly where
040           human annotators exhibited substantial disagreement, whereas LVLMs tended to
041           rely on uniform strategies for challenging content. These findings underscore the
042
              limitations of using a single human annotator as the groundtruth for evaluation
              and highlight the need for new evaluation perspectives. We believe this work of-
043
              fers valuable insights into the differences between LVLMs and humans, ultimately
044
              guiding future advancements toward human-level video comprehension.

      2.2      C APTION Q UALITY A SSESSMENT
202
203   In Section 2.1, we provided descriptions from five different annotators for each video, capturing
204   diverse human perspectives to establish a robust human baseline. In addition to this diversity, a con-
205   solidated human description was generated as the final groundtruth, serving as a refined summary
206   for video captioning evaluation. To create the groundtruth, we used GPT-3.5-turbo to evaluate de-
207
      scriptions across five key dimensions, following methods similar to those in Video-ChatGPT (Maaz
      et al. (2023)) and Tarsier (Wang et al. (2024a)). Following VideoLLaMA2 (Cheng et al. (2024)),
208
      these dimensions are: (1) Consistency: Whether the description is logically coherent and aligned
209
      with the video content. (2) Context: Whether the description accurately captures scene changes
210   and relationships between events. (3) Correctness: Whether the information is accurate and free
211   from misleading content. (4) Detail Orientation: Whether the description captures critical details,
212   such as people, objects, scenes, and events. (5) Temporality: Whether the description follows the
213   chronological order of events without skipping or over-summarizing. GPT-3.5-turbo assigned scores
214   ranging from 1 to 10 for each caption across five dimensions (see Appendix D.1.1). This scoring
215   allowed us to comprehensively analyze the quality of each annotator’s description and identify those
      with the highest consistency and accuracy.

248   2.3 G ROUNDTRUTH G ENERATION
249   We used the GPT-3.5-turbo model to synthesize the five human-provided descriptions into a single,
250   comprehensive video description that serves as the final groundtruth (see Appendix D.1.2). During
251   this synthesis, the model integrates key elements from each of the five descriptions, balancing the
252   diversity of perspectives with consistency and coherence. This ensures that the final groundtruth
253   captures the most salient and informative aspects of the video while maintaining logical flow and
254   completeness across all relevant dimensions, as illustrated in Fig. 4.
255   Using GPT-3.5-turbo for synthesis provides a systematic way to combine multiple viewpoints, re-
256   ducing subjective bias and ensuring that no crucial detail is omitted. Each synthesized groundtruth
257   represents a consolidated understanding of the video, balancing detail orientation, contextual rele-
258   vance, and temporal accuracy. By combining the strengths of multiple human annotations, the gen-
259   erated groundtruth not only supplements individual descriptions but also sets a higher standard of
260   quality, serving as a more stringent and standardized benchmark for evaluating model performance.

303   The video descriptions generated by the
304   LVLMs in the previous section are suitable for
305   evaluation using traditional metrics. However,
306   the recently proposed AutoDQ (Wang et al.
307

      tion approach by extracting events from both
308
      reference and model-generated captions, en-
309
      abling fine-grained assessments based on event
310   matching. While AutoDQ has demonstrated its
311   effectiveness in aligning model-generated de-

313   account for the cognitive importance of differ-
314   ent events as perceived by human annotators. To address this limitation, we propose FIOVA-DQ, an
315   extended evaluation metric that incorporates human cognitive weights into the event-based evalua-
316   tion process. By assigning weights to events based on their importance across multiple annotators,

323   across the five annotators. These weights, normalized to sum to one, reflect the cognitive emphasis
      placed on different events by human annotators (see Fig. 4). This weighting mechanism enables

334   4.1   E VALUATION M ETHODS
335
      Traditional metrics like BLEU (Papineni et al. (2002)) have limitations in evaluating detailed and
336
      longer video descriptions, often failing to capture the semantic nuances and contextual accuracy
337
      required for a comprehensive assessment. Recent studies have attempted to use models such as
338   ChatGPT for content rating (Maaz et al. (2023); Achiam et al. (2023)), but the lack of interpretability
339   in score assignment remains a challenge (see Appendix A.3). Therefore, we adopted AutoDQ (Wang
340   et al. (2024a)), which extends traditional metrics like BLEU, GLEU, and METEOR by integrating
341   text and semantic similarity, providing a more holistic evaluation of the alignment between LVLM-
342   generated captions and human annotations.
343
      To further enhance the evaluation process, we propose FIOVA-DQ, which builds upon AutoDQ
344   by incorporating cognitive weights derived from human annotators. At first, events are extracted
345   from both the groundtruth caption (E gt i) and the LVLM-generated caption (E r ij), as described
346   in Section 3.2. For AutoDQ, two ratios are computed: (1) the ratio of events in E gt i that are
347   also present in E r ij (i.e., recall), and (2) the ratio of events in E r ij that are also present in E gt i
348   (i.e., precision). For FIOVA-DQ, these ratios are adjusted using weights assigned to each event in
349   Eigt based on their cognitive importance as perceived by annotators. Then, the harmonic mean of
350   weighted precision and recall (i.e., weighted F1 score) is calculated to provide a balanced measure of
351   model performance. This adjustment ensures that critical events are given more emphasis, aligning
352   the evaluation process more closely with human judgment.
353   Finally, we employed a combination of traditional metrics (BLEU, GLEU, and METEOR),
354   AutoDQ-based metrics (F1, Precision, and Recall), and the newly proposed FIOVA-DQ metrics
355   (weighted F1, weighted Precision, and weighted Recall) for evaluation. These metrics collectively
356   enable two main evaluation tasks: (1) Overall evaluation: Assigns quality scores to each gener-
357   ated caption, assessing whether LVLMs can describe videos at a level comparable to humans using
358   all metrics. (2) Batch evaluation: Evaluates the relative performance of multiple model outputs,
359   providing a nuanced understanding of the models’ ability to produce human-like descriptions.
360

      Tarsier’s success can be attributed to its high lexical overlap with the groundtruth, as its generated

      4.4 S UMMARY
506
507   Based on the above results, we conclude that existing LVLMs exhibit notable perception and rea-
508
      soning capabilities, enabling reasonably accurate video descriptions. However, most models face
      challenges with information omissions, limiting their ability to generate semantically comprehen-
509
      sive captions. Among the six evaluated models, Tarsier achieved the best overall performance,
510
      effectively leveraging temporal relationships to handle complex videos. Nevertheless, it requires
511   improvements in descriptive precision and minimizing irrelevant content.
512
513   Compared to human-generated captions, LVLMs show significant discrepancies in simpler videos,
514
      often missing subtle nuances that human annotators readily capture. In contrast, for complex videos,
      LVLMs demonstrate greater consistency and stability, likely due to uniform strategies adopted under
515
      challenging scenarios. For videos of moderate complexity, LVLMs perform comparably to humans,
516
      balancing accuracy and completeness. However, issues such as hallucinations and redundancy re-
517   main prominent in some models, as illustrated in Fig.A23, Fig.A22, and Fig. A24. While all six
518   models perform well in simple scenarios, such as Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu practice, their performance
519   declines significantly when handling spatiotemporal inconsistencies or frequent scene transitions.
520   These findings highlight the need for substantial improvements in processing complex video scenes
521   with intricate temporal dynamics.
522
      The experiments also reveal the limitations of traditional metrics in assessing open-ended video
523   descriptions. These metrics rely on lexical matching, making them inadequate for capturing the
524   semantic richness, fluency, and contextual relevance of captions, particularly for tasks involving
525   diverse content and nuanced understanding. To address these limitations, new evaluation metrics
526   are urgently needed. Future metrics should emphasize semantic alignment, linguistic fluency, and
527   content relevance to provide a more comprehensive and accurate evaluation of LVLMs’ capabilities.
528
529   5   C ONCLUSIONS
530
      This paper proposes FIOVA, a new benchmark designed to evaluate the judgment capabilities of
531   LVLMs in video captioning across different evaluation settings and to assess their consistency with
532   human judgments. Our findings indicate that while Tarsier performs well in terms of precision and
533   temporal utilization, it often generates brief captions that lack detail, limiting comprehensiveness. In
534   contrast, ShareGPT4Video, although comparable to GPT-4V in its claimed understanding, suffers
535   from hallucinations and redundancy in its outputs. The FIOVA benchmark provides a complex
536   environment for comparing LVLMs to human assessments, offering insights into their respective
537   strengths and limitations across diverse video scenarios. Our results also emphasize the need for
538   improved LVLMs that can effectively balance accuracy, comprehensiveness, and content relevance,
539
      particularly in complex settings. We hope that FIOVA will support further research in advancing
      video description and understanding.

      A.1   LVLM S FOR V IDEO C APTION
707
708   In recent years, research on Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) has seen a notable surge, with
709   some models even claiming to achieve performance on par with GPT-4V (Achiam et al. (2023))
710   in handling general video tasks such as visual question answering and video description. These
711   advanced models aim to bridge the gap between visual and linguistic understanding, allowing for
712   more sophisticated interactions with video content.
713   One of the standout models in this domain is Tarsier (Wang et al. (2024a)), which employs CLIP-
714   ViT to encode individual video frames and leverages a Large Language Model (LLM) to model
715   the temporal relationships between these frames. Through a carefully crafted two-stage training
716   process, Tarsier demonstrates superior capabilities in generating video descriptions compared to
717   existing open-source models, making it a leading player in this rapidly evolving space.
718
      Building on earlier innovations, VideoLLaMA2 (Cheng et al. (2024)) advances video captioning by
719   improving on its predecessor, VideoLLaMA (Zhang et al. (2023)). It introduces a custom-designed
720   Spatio-Temporal Convolution (STC) connector that effectively captures the complex interplay be-
721   tween spatial and temporal elements in video data. This enhancement enables the model to generate
722   more accurate and context-aware video descriptions and address broader video understanding tasks.
723
      Another notable development comes from ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al. (2024a)), which advances
724
      video understanding in LVLMs and video generation in text-to-video models (T2VM) to new lev-
725   els. By generating dense, detailed, and precise captions, ShareGPT4Video achieves state-of-the-art
726   (SOTA) performance across three advanced video benchmarks, significantly enhancing the quality
727   of video descriptions and the overall understanding of complex video content.
728
      Video-LLaVA (Lin et al. (2023)) further pushes the boundaries of foundational LLMs by align-
729
      ing visual representations with the language feature space, working towards a more unified LVLM
730
      architecture. This alignment is critical in enhancing the model’s ability to understand and gener-
731   ate coherent, contextually appropriate captions that seamlessly integrate both visual and linguistic
732   elements.
733
734
      VideoChat2 (Li et al. (2023)) stands out for its impressive capabilities in spatio-temporal reasoning,
      event localization, and causal reasoning. By integrating a video backbone with a large language
735
      model via a learnable neural interface, VideoChat2 excels in tasks that require a deeper understand-
736
      ing of temporal sequences and the causal relationships between events in video data. This makes it
737   particularly effective in scenarios that demand detailed analysis and interaction with dynamic video
738   content.
739
740
      The emergence of these models has prompted researchers to ask a fundamental question: “Can
      video-based LVLMs describe videos like humans and exhibit human-level understanding?” This
741
      question forms the basis of our work. We selected these state-of-the-art models as evaluation sub-
742
      jects and conducted a comprehensive comparison of human and machine video understanding using
743   the FIOVA benchmark.

745   A.2   V IDEO C APTION DATASET
746
747   As the field of video understanding continues to evolve, researchers have introduced a growing
748   number of video description datasets that cater to various levels of complexity and diversity in video
749   content. These datasets play a crucial role in advancing video captioning models by providing
750   training and evaluation materials that reflect real-world challenges.
751   One of the well-known datasets in this field is YouCook-II (Zhou et al. (2018)), which comprises
752   2,000 cooking videos evenly distributed across 89 distinct recipes. These videos, sourced from
753   YouTube, encompass a wide range of cooking techniques and present various challenges typical of
754   open-domain videos. The dataset features variations in camera angles, camera movement, lighting
755   conditions, and background changes, making it an excellent resource for testing models on dynamic
      and complex scenarios.

      The Microsoft Video Description (MSVD) (Chen & Dolan (2011)) dataset offers another founda-
757   tional benchmark for video captioning tasks. It includes 1,970 short video clips from YouTube, each
758   paired with human-annotated sentences that provide natural language descriptions of the video con-
759   tent. This dataset is widely used for training and evaluating models, given its open-domain nature
760   and the diversity of content it covers.
761
      Further expanding the scope, the MSR-Video to Text (MSR-VTT) (Xu et al. (2016)) dataset offers
762
      a larger and more diverse collection of open-domain videos for captioning tasks. It consists of
763   7,180 videos subdivided into 10,000 clips, organized into 20 distinct categories that encompass a
764   broad range of scenarios, from sports to news events, and more. The MSR-VTT dataset serves as a
765   benchmark for evaluating a model’s capability to handle diverse, real-world video content, making it
766   an important resource for researchers seeking to enhance the generalization abilities of their models.
767
      Currently the largest dataset in the field, Panda-70M (Chen et al. (2024b)), features an astounding
768
      70 million videos paired with high-quality text captions. This extensive dataset has significantly
769   accelerated the development of video understanding by providing a vast array of training examples
770   that capture a wide spectrum of real-world video content. Its scale and diversity allow researchers
771   to train more robust models capable of handling complex, open-world scenarios.
772
      Notably, FIOVA stands out as the only dataset that provides multiple annotations for each video,
773
      offering richer insights into how different viewers perceive and describe the same content. Addi-
774
      tionally, the length of the video descriptions in FIOVA is considerably longer than in other datasets,
775   providing more detailed and nuanced explanations of the video content. This makes FIOVA an
776   exceptional resource for testing the ability of models to generate comprehensive, contextually rich
777   descriptions, pushing the boundaries of what video captioning systems can achieve.

      A.3   V IDEO C APTION E VALUATION
781
782
783
      In the early stages of video description research, the primary focus was on pretraining video-
      language models, followed by fine-tuning on specific datasets for video captioning tasks. The
784
      performance of these models was typically assessed using well-established metrics such as BLEU
785
      (Papineni et al. (2002)), GLEU (Mutton et al. (2007)), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie (2005)), and
786   CIDEr (Vedantam et al. (2015)). These metrics, while useful for measuring the quality of gener-
787   ated descriptions based on syntactic and semantic alignment, often led to models that could achieve
788   impressive results on specific datasets. However, a significant limitation was that these models fre-
789   quently struggled to generalize well beyond their training data, especially when confronted with
790   more diverse or open-world videos (Wang et al. (2024a)).
791
      To address this challenge, recent research efforts have shifted towards developing models capa-
792   ble of zero-shot video description (Tewel et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2019b); Zhou et al. (2024)).
793   These models aim to generate accurate captions for unseen videos without requiring fine-tuning
794   on task-specific datasets. Although promising, the simplicity of many standard video description
795   benchmarks limits their ability to fully evaluate these models’ capabilities. These benchmarks often
796   focus on straightforward, short videos with basic actions, which fails to stress-test models on more
797   complex, nuanced content.
798   As the complexity of videos increases—whether in terms of length, visual diversity, or intricate
799   narrative structure—traditional evaluation metrics struggle to reflect the true quality and relevance
800   of the generated captions. This mismatch highlights the need for more sophisticated evaluation
801   methods. In response, researchers have recently proposed using advanced language models, such
802   as ChatGPT, for automatic evaluation (Sottana et al. (2023)), which has gained popularity for tasks
803   like open-ended question answering. While this approach offers more flexibility in evaluating the
804   nuances of video descriptions, directly assigning a numerical score to an entire video description
805   often lacks interpretability, with the meaning of each score level being ambiguous and inconsistent
806
      (Maaz et al. (2023)).
807   To overcome the limitations of traditional evaluation metrics, we adopted AutoDQ (Wang et al.
808   (2024a)), a recently proposed approach for automatic scoring. AutoDQ offers significant advantages
809   over traditional methods, as it combines both text similarity and semantic similarity to evaluate
      the alignment between the LVLMs’ video captions and human-generated captions. This approach


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      enables a more comprehensive evaluation of both the lexical accuracy and the semantic integrity of
811   the descriptions, making it better suited for assessing the quality of detailed, nuanced video captions.
812
813   The AutoDQ evaluation process involves two main stages. First, events are extracted from both
814
      the groundtruth and the LVLM-generated captions. In the next stage, these events are compared to
      calculate two key metrics: recall, which measures how much of the groundtruth’s events are cap-
815
      tured by the model-generated caption, and precision, which evaluates how accurately the generated
816
      content aligns with the events present in the groundtruth. Finally, the F1 score—a balanced measure
817   of precision and recall—is used to provide an overall assessment of the model’s performance. This
818   method allows for a more nuanced understanding of how effectively a model captures the content of
819   a video, considering both completeness and accuracy.
820
      In our evaluation of LVLMs using the FIOVA benchmark, we employed both traditional metrics
821
      (such as BLEU, GLEU, and METEOR) and the advanced AutoDQ approach. By combining these
822
      evaluation methods, we aim to provide a more comprehensive analysis of model performance, cap-
823   turing both the lexical alignment and the deeper semantic relationships that are crucial for effective
824   video comprehension. This combined approach ensures a scientifically rigorous comparison be-
825   tween LVLMs and human-generated video captions, particularly in complex video scenarios.

       B.2   H UMAN A NNOTATION RULES
1027
1028
1029
        Annotation Scheme and Standards.
1030
1031    1. Annotators should label based solely on the visual content of the video, without referring
1032    to the audio content or any subtitles in the video, except for the text that appears naturally in
1033    the scene (such as store signs, road signs, etc.). Annotators can choose to use this information or
1034    not, based on their judgment.
1035    - Example: If a news image appears with the title “Earthquake Report,” this text can be
1036    referenced. However, if text appears in the form of movie subtitles at the bottom of the video, it
1037
        should not be used.
1038
        2. Annotators should describe each video using a few simple declarative sentences to form
1039
        a paragraph. The number of sentences depends on the changes in events and scenes in the
1040    video, and the content can be appropriately enriched.
1041
1042    3. Introduce simple and observable scene information, such as time (morning, noon, evening,
1043    late night), location (e.g., on a basketball court, beside a highway, in a bar), and the main objects
1044    and their positions in the scene (e.g., a truck overturned in the middle of the road, spectators
1045    filling the stands around the stadium). Avoid using overly literary descriptions.
1046
1047    4. Do not include the names of public figures in the video, such as Obama, Clinton, Sun Yang,
1048
        Yao Ming, Yang Mi, etc.Use third-person references such as “a man,” “a woman,” “a boy,” “a
        girl,” “he,” “she,” etc., instead.
1049
1050
        5. Optionally include observable details of characters, such as clothing, hairstyle and color,
1051    age, etc., e.g., “A basketball player wearing a white jersey dribbled past another player wearing
1052    a black jersey.”
1053
1054    6. Describe the behaviors and actions of individual characters as well as interactions
1055    between them. For interactions between multiple people, use references such as “this person,
1056    that person,” “one person, another person,” “the one on the left, in the middle, on the right,” or
1057    “this group, that group” to refer to different entities. There are no strict requirements for the
1058    specific language used, but the relationships and actions must be clearly and concisely described.
1059
        7. Do not use emotionally biased words (mostly adjectives or adverbs), such as “pitiful,”
1060
        “disgusting,” “joyfully,” etc.
1061
1062    8. Do not use idioms (e.g., “a dime a dozen”), proverbs (e.g., “No pain no gain”), or internet
1063    slang (e.g., “imho (in my humble opinion)”).
1064
1065    9. Do not use overly literary descriptions or speculate on the psychological state of characters.
1066    For example: “As she thought of her youth slipping away, a faint sorrow appeared on her face.”
1067
1068    10. Do not use subjective inference terms, such as “obviously.” Sentences should be concise;
1069    use shorter phrases where possible, e.g., replace “at the same time” with “meantime.”
1070
        11. Do not use unnecessary conjunctions if there is no causal relationship between events in
1071
        the video.
1072
1073    12. Avoid redundant or conversational language. For example: instead of “Just after Andy
1074    rode his bike home, he immediately ran out again,” simplify to “After a boy rode home, he ran
1075    out again” or “A boy rode home and then ran out again.”
1076
1077    13. The events in the video must be described in the order in which they occur, without
1078    skipping ahead or using summarizing language.

       To ensure the quality and robustness of the annotations in the FIOVA dataset, a carefully designed
1081   annotator arrangement strategy was implemented. Below, we describe the approach taken and its
1082   contributions to the diversity and representativeness of the dataset.
1083
1084
       Annotator Assignment. Unlike some datasets annotated by a fixed group of individuals, the anno-
       tation of FIOVA involved multiple groups of annotators. Specifically:
1085
1086         • Dynamic Annotator Groups: Each video was independently annotated by five annotators;
1087           however, the annotators assigned to different videos varied.
1088
             • Training and Standardization: All annotators were required to undergo rigorous training
1089
               to ensure a thorough understanding of the annotation guidelines and the ability to deliver
1090           consistent, high-quality annotations.
1091
1092   Diversity in Annotations. The use of multiple annotator groups was a deliberate choice aimed
1093   at enhancing the diversity, coverage, and adaptability of the GT. The key benefits of this approach
1094   include:
1095
1096
             • Diverse Descriptive Perspectives: Allowing different annotators to work on the dataset
               brought varied linguistic styles and perspectives, minimizing bias that might arise from
1097
               relying on a fixed annotator group.
1098
1099         • Comprehensive Semantic Coverage: The involvement of diverse annotators improved
1100
               the coverage of video details, capturing nuanced aspects of the scenes and events depicted.
1101         • Enhanced Robustness: The diversity in annotators’ perspectives enabled the GT to bet-
1102           ter generalize and adapt to various evaluation scenarios, ensuring that the dataset remains
1103           applicable across diverse use cases.
1104
       Quality Control Measures. While annotator diversity introduces variability in descriptive styles,
1105
       robust quality control measures were implemented to ensure the reliability and consistency of the
1106   annotations. These measures include:
1107
1108         • Standardized Guidelines: A unified set of annotation instructions was provided to all
1109           annotators, ensuring consistency across annotations.
1110         • Post-Annotation Review: All annotations underwent a quality review process to verify
1111           their alignment with video content and eliminate errors.
1112
             • Semantic Integration: Using GPT-3.5-turbo, the annotations from five annotators were
1113           integrated into a single, cohesive description, balancing consistency with the retention of
1114           diverse perspectives.
1115
1116   Through these measures, the FIOVA dataset provides a robust, diverse, and high-quality GT that
1117   supports the evaluation of LVLMs in long-video description tasks.

2133   In Table A3, it is observed that comparing model outputs with GPT-summarized human captions
2134   (aggregated GT) results in higher metric scores than directly comparing model outputs with single
2135   human captions. Below, we provide an analysis and explanation for this phenomenon:
2136   Improved Information Coverage by GPT-Summarized Descriptions. Each video in the FIOVA
2137   dataset is annotated by five independent annotators who watched the full video before providing
2138   detailed descriptions. Due to their differing focuses, each annotator’s description may emphasize
2139   various aspects, such as:
2140
2141          • Action Details: Certain annotators might prioritize characters’ actions and their sequences.
2142          • Contextual Information: Others may focus on the environment, background, or sec-
2143            ondary events.
2144
2145   GPT-3.5-turbo aggregates these descriptions, effectively integrating multi-perspective information
2146   from all five annotators into a comprehensive and diverse GT. By synthesizing multiple viewpoints,
2147
       the aggregated GT captures a broader spectrum of video content, ensuring improved coverage com-
       pared to single human descriptions. For instance, as shown in Fig. A7, certain annotators emphasize
2148
       the actions of a child, while others document background details. The aggregation process ensures
2149
       that both types of information are represented in the GT, enhancing its overall comprehensiveness.
2150
2151   Reasons for Higher Metric Scores. The higher scores observed when comparing model outputs
2152   with aggregated GT can be attributed to two main factors:
2153
2154

2155
                quently:
2156
2157                 – Model outputs are more likely to match specific details captured by at least one anno-
2158                   tator.
2159                 – The inclusion of diverse content reduces the chance of missing critical information,
                       resulting in improved BLEU and METEOR scores.

             • Limitations of Single Annotator Descriptions: Single annotators may provide descrip-

2163           leading to relatively lower metric scores.

       E.4.2    P OTENTIAL C AUSE A NALYSIS
3295
3296           • Architectural Limitations.
3297               – Cross-modal alignment issues: Current LVLMs face significant challenges in ef-
3298                 fectively aligning video-text data. For instance, Tarsier processes each frame using
3299                 separate visual encoders, while VideoLLaMA2 adopts a shared visual encoder for
3300                 all frames. These varying alignment strategies directly impact the models’ ability to
3301                 interpret and understand video content comprehensively.
3302               – Insufficient long-sequence modeling: Handling long videos with multiple events re-
3303                 quires robust attention mechanisms to ensure coherence and completeness. However,
3304
                     many LVLMs struggle in this aspect. For example, Video-LLaVA’s descriptions often
                     prioritize initial scenes while neglecting subsequent parts of the video.
3305
3306           • Training Data Bias.
3307               – Inconsistent or insufficient data diversity: Training data with limited diversity can
3308                 lead to biased outputs. For example, Video-LLaVA shows significant difficulty in
3309                 recognizing martial arts scenes (Fig. A23) compared to other LVLMs, suggesting gaps
3310                 in its training dataset.
3311               – Hallucination issues: Noisy or incomplete training data may propagate hallucinated
3312                 content. In Fig. A20, VideoChat2 misidentifies players and spectators in a baseball
3313                 stadium as children, illustrating a severe misalignment between the output and actual
3314
                     video content.
3315           • Generation Strategy Issues.
3316               – Simplistic generation strategies: Using basic generation techniques, such as
3317                 beam search, often results in repetitive or incoherent descriptions. For instance,
3318                 ShareGPT4Video, while utilizing high-quality training data, demonstrates repetitive
3319                 descriptions due to inadequate constraints during generation.
3320               – Weak constraints during generation: Insufficient semantic constraints in generation
3321                 processes can lead to hallucinated content or semantic errors.

       E.4.3    S UGGESTIONS FOR I MPROVEMENT AND O PTIMIZATION
3349
3350           • Model Optimization.
3351               – Enhancing detail capture: Refining attention mechanisms to focus on key events and
3352                 details can significantly improve the comprehensiveness of video descriptions. Hier-
3353                 archical attention mechanisms for long-sequence modeling, as demonstrated by Vide-
3354                 oLLaMA2’s STC Connector, offer a promising direction for enhancing spatiotemporal
3355                 continuity in descriptions.
3356               – Improving semantic alignment: Incorporating cross-modal alignment constraints,
3357                 such as visual-language consistency checks, can reduce semantic discrepancies and
3358
                     hallucination issues. Models like LLaVA-NeXT-Video emphasize the importance of
                     maintaining alignment consistency throughout the comprehension process.
3359
3360
                   – Implementing deduplication strategies: Introducing mechanisms to detect and
                     eliminate repetitive content during generation can improve description coherence and
3361
                     reduce redundancy.
3362
3363           • Training Data Optimization.
3364               – Enhancing data diversity: Expanding training datasets to include diverse scenarios,
3365                 particularly complex events in long videos, can mitigate bias and improve generaliza-
3366                 tion.
3367               – Data cleaning: Removing hallucinated or erroneous examples from training corpora
3368                 enhances data quality. For instance, ShareGPT4Video demonstrates notable improve-
3369                 ments through high-quality video-text data, though further refinements remain neces-
3370
                     sary.
3371           • Evaluation Method Enhancement.
3372               – Fine-grained error categorization: Incorporating detailed error categorization
3373                 mechanisms within the FIOVA framework can help identify model weaknesses more
3374                 precisely. For example, when calculating FIOVA-DQ, event similarity between anno-
3375                 tators’ descriptions and LVLM outputs could aid in detecting specific error types.
