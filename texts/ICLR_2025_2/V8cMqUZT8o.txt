011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Text-Attributed Graphs (TAGs) seamlessly integrate textual data with graph struc-
014            tures, presenting unique challenges and opportunities for jointly modeling text and
015            graph information. Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have
016
               significantly enhanced the generative and predictive capabilities of text modeling.
               However, existing graph models often fall short in capturing intricate node relation-
017
               ships, as their edge representations are typically limited to scalar values. In this
018
               paper, we introduce SheaFormer , a novel method that encodes rich and complex
019            relational information between nodes as edge vectors. During the message-passing
020            phase, SheaFormer aggregates both neighbor node representations and edge vectors
021            to update the central node’s representation, eliminating the need to fine-tune the
022            LLMs on the text-attributed graph. Specifically, for a given TAG, SheaFormer is
023            trained to minimize the prediction errors of the LLM in forecasting the next word
024            in node text sequences. Furthermore, we enhance SheaFormer ’s performance by
025            incorporating prompt-based fine-tuning techniques. Once trained, SheaFormer can
026            be seamlessly adapted to various downstream tasks. Extensive node classification
027
               experiments across multiple domains demonstrate that SheaFormer consistently
               achieves state-of-the-art performance, validating its effectiveness in capturing com-
028
               plex relationships within TAGs. Additionally, we conduct ablation studies and
029
               scalability analyses to ensure the robustness and applicability of our approach.

034
      Graph structures are pervasive in real-world applications Berge (2001). In numerous practical
035
      scenarios, nodes within a graph are enriched with textual features, resulting in TAGs Yang et al.
036   (2021). Examples include paper titles and abstracts in citation networks Hu et al. (2020a) or webpage
037   content in hyperlink networks Chen & Liu (2023). In TAGs, nodes encapsulate both textual and
038   structural data, reflecting their intrinsic attributes. Leveraging the rich information embedded in
039   graph topologies and their associated textual attributes has led to significant advancements in graph
040   representation learning Zhang et al. (2024). TAGs are widely utilized in applications such as fact
041   verification Zhou et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019b), recommendation systems Zhu et al. (2021), and
042   social media analysis Li et al. (2022).
043   Recent studies have focused on enhancing node representations in TAGs by either utilizing features
044   generated by lightweight pre-trained language models (PLMs) Yang et al. (2021); Chien et al. (2021);
045   Zhao et al. (2022b); Dinh et al. (2023); Duan et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024) (e.g., Sentence-BERT
046   Reimers & Gurevych (2019)) or refining raw text using the extensive knowledge of LLMs He et al.
047   (2023b); Zeng et al. (2023). LLMs are primarily designed for modeling sequential text, leading
048   researchers to initially process text independently using PLMs or LLMs, followed by aggregating the
049   results through graph neural networks (GNNs) to form final node embeddings. This representation
050
      paradigm has been widely adopted across various research domains Zhu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021);
      Hu et al. (2020c); Zhou et al. (2019).
051
052   Despite these advancements, predefined graph structures do not always reflect the true correlations
053   between nodes. Existing approaches often treat graph structures as mere topological information,
      considering them as uniform and single-faceted relationships, thus overlooking the rich semantic

118   2.1   P RE - TRAINED L ANGUAGE M ODELS
119
      Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) are multi-layer Transformer encoder-based systems that process
120   tokenized text data. PLMs are trained using autoregressive pre-training tasks, modeling the joint
121   probability distribution of token sequences. The model outputs hidden states for each token, which
122   can be used to represent sentences either by using the first token ([CLS]) or mean pooling. The
123   PLM training objective is to maximize the likelihood of predicting the next token given the previous
124   tokens, using cross-entropy loss. This allows the model to learn contextual representations of text. To
125   address the discrepancy between pre-training and downstream tasks, prompt-based methods have
126   been introduced. These methods insert task-specific prompts into the original text, helping the model
127   extract task-relevant semantics. The hidden state of the last token in the prompted sequence is used
128
      as the sentence representation, effectively integrating prompt information with the original sentence.
      This prompt-based approach has been shown to bridge the gap between PLMs and downstream tasks,
129
      improving performance by maximizing the utilization of knowledge learned during pre-training. It
130
      allows for better adaptation to specific tasks without extensive fine-tuning.

      3     M ETHOD
163
164   Motivation. In TAGs, many structural semantics are challenging to infer solely from textual
165   context. For example, two documents may share rich relational information that traditional Graph
166   Neural Networks (GNNs) struggle to model effectively because their edge representations are
167   scalar. To address this, we propose SheaFormer , which enhances node feature fusion by integrating
168   LLMs with Sheaf . Specifically, LLMs extract detailed relational information between nodes, while
169   Sheaf incorporates this information into node features to better represent and understand complex
170   relationships. Essentially, Sheaf acts as an adapter for the frozen LLM, merging structural information
171   with PLMs and pre-training it on semantic understanding tasks within TAGs. This integrated approach
172
      not only enhances the model’s ability to encode textual relationships but also significantly improves
      its performance on downstream tasks by effectively capturing and leveraging relational information
173
      between textual nodes.

      3.1   DATASET C OMPOSITION
176
177   Unlike traditional TAG methods, SheaFormer requires additional edge information to capture complex
178   relationships between nodes more effectively. This approach leverages LLMs and prompt techniques
179   to generate relational textual descriptions. Specifically, given two adjacent nodes, SheaFormer inputs
180   node information into the LLM using tailored prompts to obtain relational textual descriptions.
181
      The dataset construction process involves the following steps:
182
183         1. Node Information Preparation: Each node represents a paper, containing the paper’s title
184            and abstract as textual features. For adjacent node pairs, we extract their corresponding
185            textual descriptions Si and Sj .
186         2. Relational Description Generation: For each adjacent node pair (i, j), we input their
187            textual descriptions into a pre-trained LLM using specific prompt templates to generate a
188            relational textual description Rij . For example, for nodes i and j, the prompt template is:
189              Given the title and abstract of paper i: [TITLE_i, ABSTRACT_i] and paper j: [TITLE_j,
190                        ABSTRACT_j], describe the relationship between these two papers.
191         3. Dataset Construction: The final dataset comprises nodes enriched with each paper’s title
192            and abstract, and edges annotated with the generated relational textual descriptions Rij .
193
194   Implementation Details:
195          • LLM Selection: We utilize the GPT-4 model for generating relational descriptions due to
196            its superior understanding and generation capabilities. However, our framework is agnostic
197            to the choice of LLM and can be adapted to other models such as LLaMA or GPT-3 based
198            on resource availability.
199          • Prompt Engineering: Extensive experiments were conducted to design prompt templates
200            that maximize the quality and relevance of the relational descriptions. We ensured that
201            prompts are clear, concise, and contextually appropriate to extract meaningful relationships.
202          • Edge Description Length: To maintain computational efficiency, we limit the generated
203            relational descriptions to a maximum of 50 tokens. This balance ensures sufficient detail
204            without overwhelming the model with excessive information.
205
206
      In the field of natural language processing, pre-training is a widely adopted strategy to enhance
      language models’ semantic understanding through self-supervised learning, such as autoregressive
207
      pre-training (e.g., GPT-2/3 Radford et al. (2019); Brown et al. (2020), Llama 2 Touvron et al. (2023))
208
      and autoencoding pre-training (e.g., BERT Yang & Cui (2021), RoBERTa Liu et al. (2019a)). Based
209   on our motivation, SheaFormer employs the same pre-training tasks as these PLMs. Specifically, we
210   utilize autoregressive pre-training, which we refer to as language-structure pre-training, as it uses
211   contextual semantics to supervise structural learning.

282
283   As illustrated in Figure 2, SheaFormer is pre-trained using token-level semantic understanding tasks.
284   To fully leverage the knowledge acquired during pre-training, we introduce a prompt-based fine-
285   tuning method. Prompts are inserted into the textual data to obtain task-specific sentence embeddings
286   for each node, transforming various downstream tasks into next-token prediction tasks. For instance,
287   a node classification task can be reformulated as follows:
288
        [Context], Task: There are the following categories: rule learning, neural networks, case-based,
289     genetic algorithms, theory, reinforcement learning, probabilistic methods. Which category does
290                                          this paper belong to?
291
292   During pre-training, SheaFormer has learned to utilize structural information captured by Sheaf to
293
      enhance next-token predictions. Therefore, transformed downstream tasks can better exploit the
      knowledge acquired during pre-training.
294
295   Formally, given the textual data Si of node i, we append a series of task-specific prompt tokens to the
296   textual data, resulting in Si|P = {si,0 , . . . , si,n } ∪ P. We then obtain its sentence hidden state hi|P
297   through the PLM’s Transformer:
298                                        hi|P = Transformer(Si|P ).                                        (10)
299
300   The resulting hidden state is fused with the node’s structural representation to form the node repre-
301   sentation for the specific downstream task:
302                                        ri|P = Fusion(hi|P , zi ).                                  (11)

305

      4.5   P ERFORMANCE E NHANCEMENT A NALYSIS
413
      In TAGs, traditional GNNs often operate under the homophily assumption, which posits that con-
414   nected nodes tend to share the same labels. While effective in many scenarios, this assumption can
415   lead to performance degradation in complex or diverse relational networks. SheaFormer overcomes
416   this limitation by introducing edge encoding, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to capture so-
417   phisticated relationships within the graph. The following key aspects contribute to SheaFormer ’s
418   performance improvements:
419
      Rich Edge Information Encoding. In SheaFormer , edges encapsulate more than mere connectivity;
420   they include rich attribute information, such as textual descriptions of relationships. This design
421   allows the model to understand not only the existence of connections but also the semantic nature of
422   these connections. For example, two papers may be connected due to discussing the same technical
423   issue but belong to different categories. SheaFormer can utilize edge attributes to make nuanced

      In this paper, we present SheaFormer , a novel graph representation learning framework that effec-
      tively captures and leverages complex relationships in TAGs by integrating GNNs with LLMs through
478
      the mathematical construct of Sheaf . SheaFormer addresses the limitations of traditional GNNs
479
      under the homophily assumption by introducing rich edge encoding and deep semantic understanding.
480   Experimental results across multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that SheaFormer surpasses
481   existing state-of-the-art methods, showcasing its superior ability to understand semantic connections
482   and model heterophilic relationships in textual graphs. Additionally, SheaFormer includes mecha-
