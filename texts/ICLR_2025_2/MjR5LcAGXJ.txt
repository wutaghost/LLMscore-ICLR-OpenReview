009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance in
012            various tasks such as multi-document QA, summarization, and text classification.
013            This has been achieved in part by recent advancements in prompt engineering
014            and in-context learning (ICL), enabling LLMs to consume tens of thousands of
015            input tokens as the supported context for the given query. However, this creates
016            higher computational costs, longer latency, and potential performance degrada-
017            tion. To address these issues, we propose a task-agnostic and efficient approach
018            called “Fast RAG Inspired Prompt Evaporator”, or FRAPPE, to significantly re-
019
               duce LLMs’ latency, memory requirement, and computation by compressing in-
               put tokens. Unlike many other proposed approaches for prompt compression, our
020
               method does not rely on any large model for computing conditional probabilities,
021
               and data preparation is fast with negligible memory requirements. In particu-
022            lar, our approach first pre-processes the input data, categorizes and ranks phrases
023            based on their informativeness, and finally selects the highest-ranked phrases to
024            generate highly compressed and extractive input. We show the efficacy of our ap-
025            proach through a comprehensive set of experiments on public datasets and bench-
026            marks. For instance, on the summarization task of the MeetingBank dataset, at
027            a compression rate of 70%, our proposed approach achieves performance sim-
028            ilar to the full context while performing compression up to 4 times faster than
029            the contemporary state of the art compression algorithms. We extend FRAPPE
030
               to create the Context-Aware FRAPPE algorithm, which incorporates task-specific
               information when ranking phrases, which further improves performance of down-
031
               stream tasks using compressed text. Additionally, we demonstrate that the use
032
               of FRAPPE can reduce toxicity by close to 50% relative to the original text by
033            removing extraneous vitriolic phrases, in contrast to other compression methods,
034            which often increase toxicity.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Large Language Models (LLMs) have progressed to exhibit strong performance across various tasks
040   such as multi-document QA, information retrieval, in-context learning and reasoning, code comple-
041   tion, and document summarization (Brown et al., 2020). These LLMs, which are based on the
042   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) architecture, have achieved such strong performance thanks
043   to intelligent architectural and training choices. Moreover, recent prompting techniques, including
044   In-context Learning (ICL) (Dong et al., 2022), Chain-of-Thought (COT) (Wei et al., 2022), and Re-
045   trieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) have enabled LLMs to handle complex
046
      queries thanks to the supporting context provided by lengthy inputs with tens of thousands of tokens.
      However, there are inherent constraints on LLM performance, such as limited context window size1
047
      and the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism. Long prompts increase the computational
048
      challenge for LLMs, resulting in longer processing times and potentially inferior LLM performance
049   (Xiong et al., 2023). Similarly, as the window size of an LLM increases, it may be less sensitive
050   to the related information in the query (Shi et al., 2023). These restrictions have spurred many at-
051   tempts to reduce the memory and computation cost of the Transformer models using architectural
052

      optimization such as sparse attention (Child et al., 2019), local dense attention (Beltagy et al., 2020),
055   grouped attention (Burchi & Vielzeuf, 2021), and Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022).
056
057   An alternative approach to increasing LLM efficiency is reducing the input token length through
058
      prompt compression, which prunes irrelevant or non-informative tokens without sacrificing perfor-
      mance. Here, we focus on this latter viewpoint. Prompt compression can be task-aware, where non-
059
      informative tokens are pruned based on specific downstream tasks, often improving performance.
060
      One canonical example of this kind of approach is in question answering. However, this can be inef-
061   ficient for RAG-based applications, as it requires designing multiple compression schemes per task.
062   A more efficient option is task-agnostic compression, which removes tokens without considering the
063   task or query. This approach leverages the redundancy in human languages(Shannon, 1951), which
064   might not be useful for LLMs to generate texts. Proposed task-agnostic compression methods typi-
065   cally consider some notion of information-theoretical measures such as surprisal (self-information)
066   or perplexity provided by a smaller language model (Li et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023a). A poten-
067   tial problem with this measure is that it may be sub-optimal and model-dependent. Moreover, using
068   causal LMs to compute the measures is limited to one direction in the context, which is not aligned
069
      with many tasks, requiring a full context.
070   This paper proposes a task-agnostic prompt compression method inspired by RAG, called “Fast
071   RAG Inspired Prompt Evaporator”, or FRAPPE. The method chunks input text into smaller phrases,
072   removing common repetitions and fillers. Using embedding vectors, it prunes phrases aligned with
073   low-information phrases. The remaining phrases are then ranked by saliency, with the top ones
074   forming the compressed text.
075   We summarize our contribution as follows:
076
077          • Inspired by the RAG pipeline, we propose FRAPPE, a fast, modular, and task-agnostic
078            compression algorithm up to four times faster than state-of-the-art methods.
079          • Unlike contemporary algorithms, FRAPPE does not use an information-theoretical mea-
080            sure or LLM to compute the conditional probabilities and data preparation, improving
081            computational efficiency.
082          • Comprehensive experiments on public datasets for tasks like summarization, multi-
083            document QA, conversation, in-context reasoning, and code completion show FRAPPE’s
084            superior or comparable performance against strong baselines and full context prompts.
085          • We demonstrate an extension of FRAPPE using task-specific context for phrase pruning.
086
             • We present a study that shows that FRAPPE compression can reduce text toxicity.

092   increase in the time to generate responses by LLMs, increasing computational costs. ATo ad-

094

095
      tention (Child et al., 2019), local dense attention (Beltagy et al., 2020), grouped attention (Burchi

097   windows restrict the use of prompt engineering methods like Chain-of-Thought (COT) (Wei et al.,
098   2022), and Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020). Recently, data-centric
099   methods have emerged, focusing on selecting diverse, informative examples for efficient learning.
100

101

102   strategy in task-aware or task-agnostic ways. The former methods are tailored to a specific down-

104   of multiple compression schemes for every single task, which may increase the complexity of algo-
105   rithm deployment. On the other hand, task-agnostic compression methods remove tokens without
106   considering the query and/or downstream task, and they are more generalizable to multiple tasks.
107   However, they can yield sub-optimal performance and model-dependent results. Moreover, using

      This section details FRAPPE which treats a prompt as a tuple of instructions, context (aka demon-
147
      stration), and queries. We use τ = 1 − τ to denote the target compression rate we want to achieve
148
      (fraction of pruning tokens), and τ to denotes the fraction of remaining tokens. Our method is
149   inspired by the RAG pipeline, in which context is first chunked, indexed, and stored using an em-
150   bedding model, and non-informative phrases are pruned by comparing them against a database of re-
151   dundant words and phrases. As illustrated in Figure 1, our approach has two phases: Pre-processing
152   and Compression. In the Pre-processing phase, the input prompt is chunked into smaller pieces (i.e.,
153   phrases), and those ones found in the pre-defined database of phrases, which we call redundancies,
154   are removed from each piece. The Compression phase itself has two components. First, all phrases
155   closely aligned with the redundancies are identified and deleted using a similarity approach. Sec-
156   ond, the remaining phrases in the input are ranked by their saliency, and the top-ranked phrases are
157
      selected according to the rate τ . Thus, the input prompt has been compressed in an extractive way.
      The compressed input can now be sent to a target LLM for accomplishing the desired task2 . We now
158
      provide more details of each step in our approach.

      3.1       I NPUT C HUNKING AND R EDUNDANCY R EMOVAL
175
176   The input prompt is segmented into smaller chunks using five punctuation marks: ”.”, ”,”, ”;”, ”!”,
177   ”?”. These chunks, or phrases can be sentences or other expressions comprising of one or more
178   words, resulting in N number of tokens. We then remove articles (e.g., a, an, the) from all phrases3 .
179   Next, repeated phrases and those included in one of our defined redundancy groups are cleaned out.
180   Figure 2 shows four defined redundancy groups with some examples. This step is crucial for com-
181   pressing transcribed speech, conversations, or meeting transcripts.The redundancy groups— Phatic
182   Expressions, Filler-Utterances, Connectives, and Stop Words were created by prompting GPT-4 and
183
      curated through several iterations for a comprehensive collection. Now, we provide more details
      about creating each redundancy group (please section A.7 for more details:
184
185   Phatic Expressions. These phrases facilitate social interaction rather than conveying information.
186   We started by asking GPT-4 for a list of Phatic Expressions and expanded it with variations and
187   examples for greetings and conversation closures. After careful curation, we compiled around 90
188   expressions, diversifying the list with examples from online resources.
189   Filler Utterances. The second category involved asking GPT-4 for a comprehensive list of Filler
190   Utterances in English, targeting redundancies in casual conversations. We compiled about 30
191   words/phrases like ”huh”, ”mmm”, ”uhm”, and ”ah”.
192
      Connectives. We prompted GPT-4 to generate a list of connective words, conjunctions, and tran-
193   sitional phrases in English. This produced an extensive list, highlighting groups like Comparative
194   (e.g., ”similarly”), Additive (e.g., ”and”), Contrastive (e.g., ”but”), and others including Conditional,
195   Summarize, Illustrative, and Time categories.
196
      Stopwords. We have utilized the NLTK Library (Bird et al., 2009). for this category.
197
198   After removing all phrases in the redundancy groups, we end up with Nc tokens pruned from N
199   initial tokens; hence, the compression rate by the end of this stage is given by τc = NNc .
200
201   3.2       A LIGNED R EDUNDANCY R EMOVAL
202
203   Each redundancy group contains phrases that are variations conveying similar information. For
204
      instance, in ”Phatic Expressions,” phrases like ”How are you doing?” and ”How you doing?” are
      semantically alike. Since it’s impractical to list all variations, we consider a subset of common
205
      examples in each category (some of which are shown in Figure 2). This forms the redundancy
206
      database. We then use an embedding model to extract the embedding vectors of all phrases remain-
207   ing after the prior stage. We can compute the cosine similarity of the remaining phrases to those
208   phrases included in our redundancy database4 . Phrases too similar to redundant ones are pruned at
209   this stage, resulting in a clean input prompt.
210
      This approach is similar to the RAG retrieval mechanism for finding related contexts to a query.
211
      Using phrase embeddings is cheaper and faster than token-level embeddings because there are
212
      fewer phrases than tokens, as noted by (Li et al., 2023b). Moreover, we observe that eliminating

224   3.3     P HRASE R ANKING AND S ELECTION
225
226   At the final stage, we compress the remaining phrases by ranking and selecting the most informative
227   ones, resulting in Ne pruned tokens, where τe = N −NNce−Nr . Putting all together, the total number
228   of pruned tokens at the end will be given by τ N = Nc + Nr + Ne . Here, we have used a ranking
229   algorithm to sort the phrases in terms of their importance or informativeness in the input. Since our
230   main goal is to compress the input tokens with a low time complexity while producing high-quality
231   results, we focus on fast and efficient graph-based ranking algorithms. In particular, the phrases
232
      are represented as a set of vertices V in a weighted graph G = (V, E), where E denotes the set of
      edges, i.e., Edge eij from node Vi to node Vj is weighted by the similarity score (cosine similarity
233
      between embedding vectors of phrases) from the previous stage. Thus, the entries of the adjacency
234
      matrix or similarity matrix (SM) is given by SMij = wij = CosSim(Vi , Vj ). Having constructed
235   the similarity matrix, we can now rank the nodes in the graph and select the top ones. To calculate
236   nodes’ (or phrases’) salience, we use the concept of Node Centrality.
237
238
      Different methods have been proposed for calculating the centrality of nodes. This includes algo-
      rithms such as TextRank or LexRanks algorithms (Mihalcea & Tarau, 2004; Erkan & Radev, 2004)
239
      (which are adapted from PageRank (Brin & Page, 1998)), and more recently, PACSUM (Zheng &
240
      Lapata, 2019), FAR (Liang et al., 2021), STAS (Xu et al., 2020), and HipoRank (Dong et al., 2021).
241   In the TexRank-type of algorithms, an undirected graph G is considered, and the importance score
242   of nodes is iteratively updated based on the combination of the current importance scores and the
243   values of edges until no significant changes are observed. This can be seen as finding the station-
244   ary distribution of the Markov chain where the transition matrix is defined based on the similarity
245   matrix and a damping factor to ensure the underlying graph is irreducible. Hence, the nodes’ Cen-
246   trality is computed by finding the leading left eigenvector (corresponding to the largest eigenvalue).
247   Experiments show that methods such as PACSUM, FAR, STAS, and HipoRank exhibit similar per-
248   formance (Xu et al., 2020)6 . These methods establish a directed underlying graph G as they account
249
      for the order of phrases in a text. As a result, asymmetric centrality of node Vi defined as

401   The experiments demonstrate that FRAPPE outperforms other algorithms in speed. However,

404
      The left plot in Figure 3 shows the total time taken to compress our test set for 5 different compres-

      sion rates on both a CPU-only machine and on a machine with an NVIDIA A100 GPU (Choquette
406   et al., 2021). While the compression rate has little impact on the four algorithms, FRAPPE’s speed
407   is notable. Interestingly, the only algorithms that are faster than FRAPPE on a CPU are FRAPPE
408   on a GPU and LLMLingua-2 Small on a GPU. The right plot in Figure 3 shows the dependence of
409   compression time on text length. Each line in this figure represents 269 distinct length-time mea-
410   surements (one for each example in the dataset) for the given algorithm and environment. First, one

412   algorithms and both CPU-only and GPU-enabled configurations.
413   Another aspect shown in this plot is the stability of the algorithms. The LLMLingua-2 algorithms
414   show consistent lines with minimal deviations from their linear dependence on text length. Selective
415   Context exhibits more variability in timing for texts of similar lengths. FRAPPE is mostly stable,
416   with only a few instances of slow convergence due to the power iteration algorithm used for comput-
417   ing the leading eigenvector of the similarity matrix. Despite these occasional slowdowns, FRAPPE
418   remains the fastest algorithm on average.

420   4.3    C ONTEXT-AWARE FRAPPE
421
422   Frappe is effective for summarization but can miss query-specific details due to its centrality-based
423   compression. This oversight is due to the diverse nature of queries, which may not always align with
424   the central theme of a document.
425
      Context-Aware (shown as Cont-Aware in the following tables) FRAPPE addresses this issue by
426   including the query prompt with the input text, ensuring phrases similar to the query are retained
427   during redundancy removal. The algorithm calculates a query similarity vector between the query
428   and phrases, using it as initial weights in a Personalized PageRank algorithm. Unlike Frappe, which
429   solely focuses on centrality, Context-Aware FRAPPE generates a new centrality score that considers
430   the relevance to the specific query. We evaluate the performance of the Context-Aware FRAPPE
431   algorithm with Uncompressed text, Frappe, and the LLMLingua2-small model, using the GPT 3.5-
      Turbo Language Model, with a 70% compression rate for all algorithms.

496   6    T OXICITY
497
498   To assess the impact of compression on toxicity we tested our model on the Toxigen dataset from Mi-
499   crosoft (Hartvigsen et al., 2022), which includes 250,000 samples with implicitly toxic and benign
500   sentences about 13 minority groups. We used the popular detoxify (Hanu & Unitary team, 2020)
501   to evaluate toxicity, employing both its ”original” and ”unbiased” versions. The detoxify models
502   returns a probability of toxicity and classifications such as “severely toxic (sev toxic)”, “obscene”,
503   “threat”, “insult” and “identity attack (Id attack)”. After establishing a baseline toxicity for each
504   sample, we ran the samples through Frappe to compress the prompts and then re-evaluated their tox-
505
      icity. In all categories, toxicity was significantly reduced, confirming that toxic language does not
      usually contribute anything meaningful to the conversations and can be effectively removed through
506
      compression. Frappe does not eliminate all toxic content, as the dataset contains highly concentrated
507
      toxic information and Frappe is not specifically trained for detoxification. However, across the board
508   toxicity scores decreased, suggesting that the more inflammatory remarks were found to not be cen-
509   tral to the underlying position. We aim to further study toxicity aware compression as if we explicitly
510   do not want certain content in the outputs it may promise to be a prime compression candidate. Fur-
511   thermore, we study the effects of of compression on toxicity and find that Frappe reduces toxicity
512   by 50%. We also find that this is not the case with other compression algorithms, as some methods
513   ”distil” toxicity equating to a net overall increase in toxicity scores in their compressed outputs.

526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   Inspired by RAG, we proposed a simple yet efficient prompt compression method. Our approach,
529
      called FRAPPE, is task-agnostic and doesn’t rely on any LLMs to generate data and compute condi-
      tional probabilities. In particular, FRAPPE first chunks the input prompt into phrases and removes
530
      some common uninformative phrases called “redundancies”. Next, phrases closely aligned with
531
      these redundancies are also pruned from the prompt. This is done by measuring the similarity of
532   their embedding vectors and removing those with a similarity above a threshold. Finally, using
533   a graph-based ranking algorithm, the importance of the remaining phrases is computed, and the
534   top ones are selected as the compressed input prompt. Comprehensive experiments show that the
535   FRAPPE is up to 4 times faster than SOTA compression methods and often yields higher perfor-
536   mance in a variety of downstream tasks.

742   A.2   D ETAILS OF E XPERIMENTS
743
744   In all the experiments, if the input’s length exceeded the LLM’s context length for uncompressed
745   and compressed scenarios, we simply cut it off to fit it in the context window. We afforded the LLM
746
      a maximum of 400 tokens for its abstract.
747   Summarization. Given a text, the task is to generate a summary that captures the text’s main
748   points. The input prompt includes a short instruction for summarization, and the context and query
749   are typically the input text. We use different evaluation metrics, including BLEU (Papineni et al.,
750   2002), Rouge-1/2/L (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and BertScore-F1 (Zhang
751   et al., 2019).
752   Single/Multi-Document QA. The goal of this task is to evaluate the understanding of a model given
753   a set of questions and the provided information in the document to generate the desired answers.
754   The input prompt includes an instruction, a single or multi-document as the context, and a set of
755   questions as the query. We use the F1 and Rouge-L scores provided in the LongBench benchmark
      (Bai et al., 2023) benchmark.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Conversation. Here, we want to generate an answer to a query given a previous conversation history.
757   The above summarization scores are used to evaluate this task.
758
759   In-context Reasoning. This task measures the reasoning power of a model on complex tasks,
760
      including math and science. For this task, we use the Exact Match (EM) score, defined as the
      string’s exact match between the prediction and the reference texts, to evaluate the compression
761
      method.
762
763   Few-Shot, Synthetic Tasks, and Code Completion. These tasks are from the LongBench bench-
764   mark. The few-shot is to accomplish a task (e.g., answering a question) given a few examples. F1
765   and Rouge- scores L are used as the evaluation metrics. The Synthetic tasks are similar to QA, e.g.,
766
      What is the total number of different paragraphs in a given essay? Accuracy is the standard metric
      for this task. The code completion task predicts the next line of code given one or several pieces
767
      of code. The evaluation metric is Edit Similarity (similarity of two strings based on the number of
768
      insertions, deletions, and substitutions).

      ccdv/arxiv-summarization9 . The LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) is a benchmark for multi-
774
      task assessment of long context understanding capabilities of LLMs. It comprises tasks including
775   summarization, single/multi-document QA, few-shot learning, synthetic tasks, and code completion.
776   Moreover, we have used the ZeroScrolls dataset (Shaham et al., 2023), consisting of ten different
777   datasets and associated tasks. The datasets vary significantly in length, and the tasks span text sum-
778   marization, query-based summarization, question answering, multiple-choice question answering,
779   and aggregation. Each dataset and task pair has an associated metric, and to create a single “Zero-
780   SCROLLS Score” for a particular algorithm, one takes the average of the results of the ten metrics
781   across the ten datasets. We use GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and BBH (Suzgun et al., 2022) datasets
782   for the reasoning task. GSM8k (Grade School Math 8K) is a dataset of 8.5K high-quality linguis-

      tically diverse grade school math word problems. BBH (Big Bench Hard) consists of a suite of 27

941   Redundancy is a common occurrence in natural languages that tends to diminish the semantic signif-
942   icance of the data. Consequently, eliminating such redundancies can increase information density,
943   thereby enhancing the overall quality and relevance of the input prompt. To address this issue, we
944   have identified and established four categories of redundancy, as illustrated in Fig.2: Phatic Expres-
945   sions, Filler Utterances, Connectives, and Stopwords.
946   Phatic Expressions. These comprise conversational phrases that primarily aim to foster or sustain
947   social relationships rather than to relay specific information. We have initiated the process by asking
948   GPT-4 for a comprehensive list of unique Phatic Expressions with the following specific prompts:
949
950          • Can you provide a comprehensive list of non-repetitive Phatic Expressions?
951          • Can you provide some more variations?
952          • Please provide a comprehensive list of Phatic expressions used for greeting in conversa-
953            tions.
954
             • Please provide a comprehensive list of Phatic expressions used at the end of conversations.
955
956   To expand on this, we further prompted the model to provide additional variations and specific ex-
957   amples of Phatic expressions used in greetings and conversation closures. Some examples include
958   “Hello”, “Hi”, “Hey”, “how are you doing?”, “Have a good one”. After careful curation and veri-
959   fication, we compiled a list of approximately 90 Phatic expressions. To diversify this list, we have
960   also sourced examples from online resources.
961   Filler Utterances. The second category involved prompting GPT-4 with the query, “Can you pro-
962   vide a comprehensive list of Filler Utterances in English?” This category aims to capture and ad-
963   dress linguistic redundancies often found in casual conversations and discourse. We have compiled
964   a list of approximately 30 words/phrases (both lower and upper case) like: “huh”, “mmm”, “whu”,
965   “uhm”, “ah”, “em”, “umh”, “eh”, “um”, “ha”, “heh”, “uh”, “uhs”, “wha”, “mhm”, “hum”, “hmm”,
966   “humm”, “oh”, “uh-oh”, “er” , ”errr”, “well”, , “like”, “actually”, “basically”, “seriously”, “liter-
967   ally”, “totally”, “clearly”, “you see”, “you know”, “I mean”, “you know what I mean?”, “at the end
968
      of the day”, “believe me”, “I guess”, “I suppose”, “or something”, “so”, “right”.
969   Connectives. For this category, we have prompted GPT-4 with several queries aimed at generating
970   a list of connective words, conjunctions, and transitional phrases in English. This exercise produced
971   an extensive list of words, highlighting diverse groups such as Comparative (e.g., “similarly”, “in
      the same way”, “likewise”), Additive (e.g., “and”, “also”, “as well as”, “moreover”, “additionally”),
