009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the function of individual units in a neural network is an important
012            building block for mechanistic interpretability. This is often done by generating a
013            simple text explanation of the behavior of individual neurons or units. However,
014            for these explanations to be useful, we must understand how reliable and truthful
015            they are. In this work we unify many existing explanation evaluation methods un-
016            der one mathematical framework. This allows us to compare and contrast existing
017            evaluation metrics and understand the evaluation pipeline with increased clarity.
018
               We propose two simple sanity checks on the evaluation metrics and show that
               many commonly used metrics fail these tests and do not change their score after
019
               massive changes to the concept labels. Based on our experimental and theoretical
020
               results, we propose guidelines that future evaluations should follow and identify
021            good evaluation metrics such as correlation.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep learning models have achieved great success on a wide range of tasks, but they are very difficult
027   to understand and often perceived as black-boxes. To address this challenge, the field of mechanis-
028   tic interpretability has recently emerged, aiming to provide a clearer understanding of the internal
029   mechanism of deep neural networks.
030
      Providing natural language explanations for small components of a neural network is an important
031   part of mechanistic interpretability. Classic work in this area includes Network Dissection [1] and
032   other works explaining individual neurons in deep vision models [2; 3; 4; 5; 6; 7; 8; 9]. Other
033   examples include automated neuron explanations [10; 11] for large language models, as well as
034   explaining features of sparse autoencoders [12; 13].
035
      Despite the introduction of various approaches for generating neuron explanations, these methods
036
      often use completely different metrics to evaluate how good their descriptions are, and it is not clear
037   how they compare to each other. In addition, many evaluation metrics used have problems, as shown
038   by [14] for example. To ensure that unit explanations are reliable and trustworthy, it is crucial to
039   establish a standardized framework for evaluation.
040
      Motivated by the need for a standardized approach, in this work we unify many existing evaluation
041
      methods under a single mathematical framework, which provides much needed conceptual clarity to
042
      the topic of explanation evaluation. This framework allows us to clearly compare and contrast of cur-
043   rent evaluation techniques and provides a more transparent understanding of the evaluation pipeline.
044   Through the framework, we rigorously analyze and identify several failure modes in commonly-
045   used metrics. Additionally, we introduce two sanity tests to validate the metrics, revealing that most
046   commonly used evaluations fail at least one of these basic tests. This helps understand which metrics
047   are not suitable for reliable interpretation and should not be used.
048
      In summary, in this paper we:
049
050          • Formalize the task of evaluating individual unit explanations.
051
             • Unify existing evaluation methods under a single mathematical framework.
052
053          • Propose two sanity checks for evaluation metrics: Missing Labels test and Extra labels test,
               and show that many commonly used metrics fail at least one of these basic tests

             • Experimentally compare different evaluation metrics, and identify good metrics to use,
055            such as Correlation.

             • Discover that using biased top-and-random sampling makes a good evaluation metric such

062   2.1   W HAT IS A INDIVIDUAL UNIT IN A NEURAL NETWORK ?
063
064   In this paper we are focused on individual unit explanations. By a unit, we mean a smaller part of a
065   neural network that can have an independent meaning. The simplest form of such units is a single
066
      neuron, or a single channel of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), but a unit can be any scalar
      function of network inputs. Other interesting units that fit in our framework are linear combinations
067
      of neurons (i.e. directions in activation space), which are considered to correspond to a specific
068
      interpretable concept. These are used in studies such as TCAV [15], Concept Bottleneck Models
069   [16], Linear Probing [17] or steering vectors [18]. Finally, a unit could be a feature of a Sparse
070   Autoencoder [19; 12] trained to disentangle a layer’s activations into interpretable individual com-
071   ponents. While other, larger units such as entire layers or attention heads of a model are sometimes
072   of interest, we do not study these in this paper as they have complicated non-scalar activations and
073   require different methods to analyze than our units of interest.

      2.3   W HAT ARE THE GOALS OF AN EXPLANATION ?
121
122   Neuron explanations are typically generated to improve our understanding of the model, which can
123   then help for example improve safety and reliability of the models. However, this is vague and hard
124   to measure in a general way. We believe a more precise definition of the goals of an explanation is
125   essential for thinking clearly about how to evaluate them.
126   For clarify, it is useful to divide the neural network f (x) into two parts, f 0:l and f l+1:L , where 0
127   corresponds to the input layer, f i:j represents the i through j’th layers of the neural network f (x),
128   l is the layer of the neuron we are interested in and L is the total number of layers in the network.
129   Here we assume the units are neurons for notational simplicity. Then:
130
                                         f (x) = f l+1:L (fk0:l (x), f¬k
                                                                      0:l
                                                                          (x)),                             (4)
131
132   where fk0:l (x) is the activation of neuron of interest k in layer l, while f¬k
                                                                                   0:l
                                                                                       (x) is the activations of
133   all the other neurons in layer l.
134   Most unit explanations can be seen as a simple interpretable approximation of one (or both) of the
135   following functions:
136
137          • Function 1: Input → Unit Activation
138            This corresponds to explaining the function: x → fk0:l (x). A good explanation should be
139            able to describe which inputs cause a high unit activation, and which do not.
140          • Function 2: Unit Activation → Output
141            This corresponds to explaining the function z → f l+1:L (z, f¬k  0:l
                                                                                    (x)), where z is a real
142            number (e.g. intervened value). This function describes how changes in the unit activation
143            change the final network output.
144
145
      As we can see from the above notations, these are different problems, and may require different
      methods to solve and evaluate. While there is often a connection between these two problems,
146
      they are often conflated in existing work, and we believe recognizing the difference can improve
147
      our understanding. In particular, we should not expect a single text explanation to describe both
148   functions, especially if the neuron is used in an unexpected way. For example, we can imagine
149   a simple image classification model that was trained on biased data, where the cat class has only
150   images of black and grey cats, while the class table has several images of red cats laying on tables.
151   We could now find a neuron that only activates when there is a red cat in an image, and could be
152   described as a red cat neuron for Function 1. However, the activations of this neuron increase the
153   likelihood of the table class, while decreasing the likelihood of the cat class, requiring a different
154   description for its effects (Function 2). This can partially explain findings like [14] finding that
155   the neuron explanations of [10] have little to no effect on an intervention based evaluation, as the
156
      intervention based evaluation corresponds to Function 2, while the explanations of [10] were created
      to explain fk0:l (x)(Function 1).
157
158   In our paper, we focus on evaluating explanations of Function 1 only, i.e. fk0:l (x), as this is more
159   common in existing evaluations and can be applied more generally, for example to explain linear
160   combinations of neurons that don’t have a direct effect on the output such as TCAV [15]. While
161   evaluating Function 2 is also important, this requires different methods and is outside the scope of
      this work.

      3     A U NIFIED E VALUATION F RAMEWORK
163
164   In this section, we propose a principled framework based on the following insight – Almost all
165   existing methods for evaluation E can be formalized as a function of two vectors: neuron activations
166   ak of neuron k and concept activations ct of concept t, where
167
168          • ak : denotes the activations of neuron k on probing data xi ∈ D. I.e. [ak ]i = fk0:l (xi )
169          • ct : denotes the presence of concept t on the inputs xi ∈ D. I.e. [ct ]i = P (t|xi ).
170
171
      For notational convenience, we may use aki to denote [ak ]i and cti for [ct ]i in this paper. Within this
      framework, the evaluation score s is a function M (ak , ct ), i.e. s = E(D, f, k, l, t) = M (ak , ct ),
172
      where M is the metric chosen to measure similarity between these vectors. Concept activations
173
      ct can be gathered from different sources such as existing labels, pseudo-labels from a model or a
174   crowdsourced human evaluation. The main focus of this paper is analyzing and comparing different
175   choices of metric M , and showing how many commonly used metrics have problematic properties.
176

      3.2     E XISTING W ORK AS S PECIAL CASES
244
245   As summarized in Table 1, we show how existing evaluation work fits into our evaluation framework.
246   We note that that existing evaluation methods differ from each other on four key ways:
247
248          (i) Evaluation metric M : This is the main focus of our paper, to analyze which evaluation
249              metrics are good choices.
250         (ii) How is the concept vector ct determined? There are many choices for the concept vector
251              ct . These include, but are not limited to: labels from a labeled dataset, using a model
252              to create pseudo-labels, using a human evaluator, or generating new inputs and using the
253              prompts as labels.
254         (iii) What is the granularity of the vectors? The simplest case is full input level activations,
255               i.e. |D| = |ak | = |ct | = n. These can also be more specific, for example pixel-level as
256               is the case in Network Dissection, or token level as is often the case for language model
257               explanations.
258
            (iv) What is the probing dataset D used? This is an important choice and has a significant
259              effect on the outputs. Typical choices include the training/validation data the model was
260              trained on, a special labeled dataset designed for probing, or different datasets for different
261              concepts. Importantly, the dataset used for evaluation should be disjoint from the dataset
262              used for explanation generation to avoid overfitting.

      4.1     I DENTIFYING FAILURE CASES
300
301   (I) Failure Case of Recall. Recall corresponds to looking at the inputs that activate a neuron the
302   highest, and measuring what fraction of them contain concept ct . This is essentially how most
303   crowd-sourced evaluations are currently done. It is known that only measuring recall could be prob-
304   lematic in binary classification as it ignores performance on negative inputs. In our case, using this
305   metric for evaluation will favor explanations with more generic concepts. As an extreme example,
306   consider a very generic description where the concept ct = 1, where 1 ∈ Rn is a vector of all
307   ones. This could be a concept like “image” or “entity” which can be a valid description for almost
308   all images. Plugging these into equation (7), we get:
309                                                       B(ak ) · 1    ||B(ak )||1
310                         M (ak , ct ) = M (ak , 1) =               =             = 1,                   (12)
                                                          ||B(ak )||1   ||B(ak )||1

312   since B(ak )i ≥ 0, ∀ i. We see that with the maximally generic concept ct = 1, precision is always

413   and Accuracy in red color, while methods that only fail one of these are colored orange.
414   In addition, we run a theoretical version of the Missing/Extra labels test on hypothetical neurons
415   whose activations perfectly match a concept, which we discuss in detail in Appendix B. We find
416   that our theoretical results closely match our empirical observations, and that failure in these tests is
417   closely associated with concept imbalance, with failing metrics performing particularly poorly with
418   imbalanced data where concepts are only rarely positive.
419   Surprisingly, we can see that most metrics fail at least one of these tests:
420
421            • Accuracy and Spearman Correlation perform poorly in both directions as their score is
422              largely determined by the majority of inputs that neither activate the neuron nor have the
423              concept.
424            • Along with Recall, Balanced accuracy and AUC fail the Extra Labels test and are biased
425              towards generic concepts.
426            • Precision, Inverse Balanced Accuracy, Inverse AUC and MAD fail the Missing Labels Test
427              and are biased towards specific concepts.
428
429   The only methods that pass both tests are F1-Score/IoU, Correlation, Cosine, Cosine Cubed and
430   AUPRC. Since a good evaluation metric should be able to pass these tests, we believe metrics beside
431   these should not be relied on by themselves when evaluating unit explanations. This aligns with our
      interpretation is caused by poor handling of imbalanced data, as metrics known to work worse for

      imbalanced data like accuracy and AUC fail the tests, and metrics designed for imbalanced data like
433   F1-score and AUPRC pass the tests. Finally we analytically studied the expected change in scores
434   for different binary metrics under missing and extra labels conditions in Appendix D, where our
435   results largely agree with the empirical findings.
436
      Top-and-random sampling: In addition to metrics described in Section 3.1, we also tested a version
437
      of correlation and Spearman correlation that uses top-and-random sampling, where the inputs are
438
      evaluated on a random sample of consisting of 50% highly activating inputs and 50% randomly
439   sampled inputs as done by [10; 12]. This is in contrast to the default setting of evaluating on the
440   entire dataset or a uniform random sample. For our top-and-random experiments we sampled 25
441   inputs from the top 0.2% highest activating inputs and 25 random inputs. We can see that while
442   correlation passes both tests when evaluated on full data, top-and-random sampling makes it fail
443   the extra labels test. This makes sense, as greatly oversampling highly activating inputs makes the
444   method more similar to Recall that only focuses only highly activating inputs. This also explains
445   why [14] found explanations from [10] with very high correlation(top-and-random) scores to have
446   relatively low F1-scores.
447   Generative ct : Interestingly, when using generative models for ct it may sometimes be necessary to
448   use methods that fail the Missing Labels test, as the generative labels themselves are missing labels.
449   See Appendix A.4 for further discussion on this phenomenon.

523   6   C ONCLUSIONS
524
525   In this paper, we have created a unified mathematical framework for different evaluation metrics
526   and clarified the definitions of around evaluating unit explanations. We have also performed several
527   sanity tests and experiments to answer the following question:
528
529   Which Evaluation Metric should you use to evaluate explanations? Considering all the evi-
530
      dence from our experiments study, we lean towards Correlation (with uniform sampling) as the
      best overall metric for evaluating neuron descriptions. While cosine similarity performed similarly
531
      in Table 3, unlike other metrics its outputs depend on the mean of neuron activations, which can
532
      cause significant errors when explaining neurons whose average activation is different from zero as
533   we show in Appendix F.2. Other good metrics include cosine cubed and AUPRC. F1-score or IoU
534   can also be a good choice, but requires choosing an activation cutoff α and it is unclear how to best
535   make this choice. Our most important recommendation is that evaluations should not rely on a
536   single metric that doesn’t pass our missing or extra labels test. Combinations of such metrics can
537   still be useful, and for example F1-score could be efficiently evaluated by combining a crowdsourced
538   Recall evaluation with evaluating Precision using a generative model.

705   A.1   L IMITATIONS
706
707   Framework Limitations:
708
709   First, not every evaluation of neuron descriptions can fit into our framework. Below we split these
710
      into few separate cases and discuss whether each case represents a limitation of the framework or
      not:
711
712   Evaluating Multiple Inputs at once: Our evaluation framework assumes that the presence of a
713   concept is estimated separately for each input. Many human study based evaluations (e.g. [1], [4])
714   instead evaluate a group of inputs at once, asking questions like ”How well does concept match this
715   group of images?”. However we believe this is simply a less precise/less objective way of asking
716
      whether the concept matches each input separately and does not in general represent a significant
      limitation for the framework.
717
718   Comparing similarity to ”correct” explanation: Another approach to evaluate neuron descrip-
719   tions is to compare how close they are to a ”correct” description, typically in a text-embedding
720   space. For example, this is the main evaluation used by MILAN [3], where they generate ”correct”
721   explanation by asking Mechanical Turk workers to describe neurons based on their most highly ac-
722
      tivating inputs. We do not think this a very reliable way to evaluate explanations, because it relies on
      the assumption that there exists a single ”correct” text-based explanation for each neuron (and that
723
      we have some way of finding it), and we do not think this is the case for many real neurons because
724
      of issues like polysemanticity and non-verbal concepts like specific graphical patterns. For these
725   hard-to-interpret neurons it is better to just measure how well our explanation matches the neuron
726   like the metrics in our framework do.
727
728
      Non-text based explanations: While we focus on text based concepts t in our paper, the framework
      works on non-textual concepts just as well, as long as we have some way of generating a concept
729
      vector ct for that concept. For example, the evaluation of [21] uses a group of highly activating
730
      inputs as the concept, and then asks workers whether a new input is similar to those inputs or not.
731   Despite this difference, it can be described neatly within our framework.
732
733   Function 2: Activation → Output: Probably the most significant limitations of our framework is
734
      that it is focused on evaluating function 1 only (Input → Unit Activation) as discussed in Section
      2.3, and we believe measure Function 2 is equally important. While currently our framework is
735
      meant for function 1 only, we believe many of the ideas and metrics we discussed could be useful in
736
      evaluating function 2. For example, in a generative model we could use the same metrics to measure
737   similarity between unit activation and the presence of a specific concept in the output. However in
738   function 2 there are additional considerations and thing like measuring difference in outputs when
739   changing the unit activation are likely more important. We believe extending this framework or
740   creating a similar one for function 2 evaluations is an important direction for future work.
741
      Experimental Result Limitations:
742
743   Overall we are quite confident in the generality of our results on the missing/extra labels test (Table
744   2) as they are consistent across final/hidden layer neurons and different datasets, and more impor-
745   tantly we showed theoretically that they are caused by poor metric performance on imbalanced data.
746
      Importantly this theoretical result is independent of the data domain, type of concepts or the type of
      unit in question.
747
748   However, it is important to note that passing these sanity checks does not guarantee that the metric
749   is a good metric, but failing them does indicate a metric should not be relied on. This is similar to
750   sanity checks proposed by [24], which have been quite influential in the field of saliency maps/input
751   importance estimation.
752   On the other hand, our comparison results in Table 3 are mostly focused on final layer neurons or
753   other units where we have ground truth available such a concept neurons inside a CBM. While they
754   consistently prefer certain metrics, it is possible that these final layer neurons are systematically
755   different from other units we are interested in such as hidden layer neurons, and these results should
      not be relied on too strongly.

      A.2   S IMULATION VS C LASSIFICATION
759
760   Simulation: In section 3.1 we define the neuron activation ak as the observed ground truth vari-
761   able, and ct as the predicted variable. This corresponds to seeing the explanation from a simulation
762   point of view, i.e. our goal is to predict how the neuron activates, based on the neurons explanation
763   and current inputs. This gives us binary classification metric definitions that are aligned with those
764   of [14].
765
766   Classification: However this is an arbitrary choice, and we could just as well define ct as the
767   observed ground truth variable and and ak as the prediction. This corresponds to a classification
768   view, where our goal is to use neuron k as a classifier for concept ct . In terms of metrics, this
769   doesn’t change the definitions for True Positive (TP) or True Negative (TN), but it switches the
770
      places of False Positive and False Negative, i.e.
771
772                                      TPcls = B(ak ) · B(ct ) = TPsim
773
                                         FPcls = B(ak ) · B(ct ) = FNsim
774
775                                      FNcls = B(ak ) · B(ct ) = FPsim

                                         TNcls = B(ak ) · B(ct ) = TNsim
777
778   This change in framing also affects for metrics which are not symmetric in terms of False Posi-
779   tives and False Negatives. For example, Recall(simulation) = Precision(classification), and Preci-
780   sion(simulation) = Recall(classification).
781
782                                      TPsim           TPcls

                                     TPsim + FNsim   TPcls + FPcls

785   The other binary metric that is sensitive to this framing is balanced accuracy. The metric we called
786   Balanced Accuracy in Section 3.1 corresponds to the simulation version of Balanced Accuracy,
787   so for completeness sake we also included Inverse Balanced Accuracy, which is the classification
788   version.
789   Finally, the AUC we define in Section 3.1 is AUC(simulation), while we also include AUC in the
790   classification framing we called Inv AUC.
791
      Some related works use the simulation framing, while others use the classification definition, which

      sometimes causes conflicting definitions of metrics like Recall.

837   ct from Generative Models: Evaluation methods that use generative models to generate new data
838   and the concept vector ct actually naturally have missing labels similar to our missing labels test.
839
      This is because the generated inputs serve as positive labels for ct , but the negative inputs are often
      taken to just be all the existing inputs, even though some of actually do have the concept t. So the ct
840
      we get from generative models is actually randomly missing a potentially large portion of the labels.
841
      Because of this, most evaluation methods using generative ct in tabel 1 use methods that fail the
842   missing labels test such as Inverse AUC and MAD. In fact, this is desirable as the missing labels
843   in ct do not affect the evaluation score for these metrics. However, these metrics alone should not
844   be relied for evaluation score, and instead the best way to use generative models in evaluation is to
845   combine them with a another evaluation that doesn’t fail the missing labels test, as is done by [14]
846   who measure Precision using generated data, and measure Recall on existing data with model based
847   pseudo-labels and combine these results into F1-score.

      In this section we analyze the effects of missing/extra labels test on a simpler toy setting, where
867   neuron k’s activations ak perfectly match the concept labels of concept tk , i.e. ak = ctk . In
868   this setting, we assume binary neuron and concept activations, i.e. the neuron’s activation is 1 if
869   concept is present on the input, and 0 if its not. Consequently, we do not need to perform additional
870   binarization of concept activations with top α like we did in previous sections.
871
      In this simplified setting, our missing and extra labels tests correspond to being able to differentiate
872
      between three concepts as defined in section 4:
873
874         1. ctk : The perfect predictor for neuron ak , with precision=1 and recall=1.
875
            2. c−
                tk : (Missing labels) This concept has Precision=1 since whenever a concept is present, the
876
               neuron is also active, and Recall of 0.5 since only half to inputs where the neuron activate
877            now have the concept.
878
            3. c+
                tk : (Extra labels) Inverse from above, this concept has Precision=0.5 and Recall=1.
879
880   We then measure whether a metric passes the test by measuring the score difference across:
881
882          • Missing labels test: M (ak , c−
                                             tk ) − M (ak , ctk ) < −ϵ
883
             • Extra labels test: M (ak , c+
                                           tk ) − M (ak , ctk ) < −ϵ
884
885   , where ϵ is a small positive number (0.01 in our case). A good metric should be able to reliably
886   differentiate between these concepts. Interestingly we find that the ability of most metrics to differ-
887   entiate greatly depends on whether the data is balanced or not.
888
      Since the neuron activations perfectly align with concept t and are binary, the only parameter that
889   can effect the results of our missing and extra labels test is the activation frequency of concept t, i.e.
890   what fraction of inputs x ∈ D contain concept t. Following the notation in section D, we denote
891   this fraction as γ. Note technically it should be γ + η, but η = 0 in this case with perfect match
892   between concept and neuron. We then test whether a metric passes the test on different values of
893   γ, using simulated data on tables 4 and 5. Each number is the average result from 1000 evaluations
894   with 500,000 datapoints each. In addition, in Section D, we derived a closed form solution to the
895   binary metrics under missing or extra labels as a function of γ and other parameters. This simplifies
896   nicely when we consider an ideal neuron with ak = ct , and we can derive the expected result of
897
      missing/extra labels test as a simple function of γ alone in Table 9. These theoretical results perfectly
      agree with our simulated results.
898
899   Results: In tables 4 and 5 we ran the simulated results on a variety of different activation sparsi-
900   ties. We can see most metrics (expect from recall and precision) perform well on balanced data
901   (γ = 0.499 and γ = 0.1). However, their performance often starts to drop with score difference
902   approaching 0 as the data becomes more and more unbalanced. We can see that practically all the
903
      metrics that failed our experimental missing/extra labels tests cannot differentiate between perfect
      and inperfect concept, specifially on inbalanced data, highlighting that likely the root cause of the
904
      failure on these test is that the metrics performs poorly on inbalanced data. This is also aligned with
905
      conventional knowledge that metrics such as accuracy and AUC are a poor choice to rely on when
906   your data is heavily inbalanced. On the other hand, metrics that passed the tests are insensitive to
907   activation fequency γ and converge to a nonzero constant as γ decreases.
908
909
      We defined a metric as passing the test as having a significant score diff < 0.01 on all the evaluated
      activation frequencies. The results in terms of passing are almost identical to our experimental
910
      results in section 4. The only differences were WPMI which passes the theoretical extra labels test
911
      but fails the experimental one. We believe this has to do with hyperparameter(α, λ) choices and
912   that WPMI can in principle pass the test but with poor hyperparameters it will not, leading us to
913   overall recommend against using it in practice as hyperparamter choice is challenging in the real
914   world. An interesting case is AUPRC and particularly Inverse AUPRC. These metrics are known to
915   be preferable for inbalanced data and work well in that domain, but actually peform worse when the
916   data is balanced, in particular Inverse AUPRC fails the test when data is perfectly balanced. This
917   indicates caution should be used if relying on them, in case you have some neurons with extremely
      common concepts.

1408   Concept extraction. Extracting interpretable concepts from a learned representation is a common
1409   challenge and relevant for finding individual units to evaluate in our framework. This can either
1410   be supervised as proposed by [15] where the concepts are specified by human and labels are pro-
1411   vided. This approach is also used by linear probing based work such as [17]. Later, a series of
1412   works[25; 26; 27] was proposed to automatically extract concepts from model activations, without

1415

1416

1417

1421   general form of attribution methods. However this is typically a local explanation while our frame-
1422   work is focused on global explanations and it is more related to Function 2 defined in Sec. 2.3, i.e.
1423   how the unit affects the output.

1425   Human-centered evaluation on concept-based models. Human-centered evaluation of model
1426   explanation[29; 30] has drawn attention from the XAI community. Recently, [31] collected human
1427   evaluation of XAI explanations as a benchmark for explanation methods. These works provide
1428   important techniques and inspiration for evaluating explainability, but almost all existing work is
1429
       focus on local feature importance explanations, which is very different from our work on global
       neuron-level explanations.

       F.2   FAILURE CASE OF COSINE : A DDING A CONSTANT ACTIVATION
1540
1541   In this section we dicuss a failure case of cosine similarity as an evaluation metric. The main idea
1542   is that cosine similarity outputs are not independent of the mean of the neuron’s activation, while all
1543   other evaluation metrics are. This causes it to associate neurons with large average activation values
1544   with very generic concepts that are almost always active. As an example, we added a constant (1) to
1545   all activations of concept neurons in a Concept Bottleneck Model [16] trained on CUB200, and ran
1546   our argmax generation test. All other evaluation metrics are invariant to this change, but the accuracy
1547   of Cosine drops from 99.07% to 0.93% with this small change, as shown in Table 13. We believe
1548
       this is a flaw that points against using cosine similarity, as the average activation of a hidden layer
       neuron is not functionally important, and could be absorbed into biases of the next layer. Instead
1549
       we recommend using Pearson correlation which is identical to cosine similarity after normalizing to
1550
       mean 0, as we show in Section A.3.
