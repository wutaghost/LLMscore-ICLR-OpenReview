011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013             Recent advances in retrieval-augmented generation have significantly improved the
014             performance of question-answering systems, particularly on factoid ‘5Ws’ ques-
015             tions. However, these systems still face substantial challenges when addressing
016
               ‘1H’ questions, specifically how-to questions, which are integral to decision-making
                processes and require dynamic, step-by-step answers. The key limitation lies in
017
                the prevalent data organization paradigm, chunk, which divides documents into
018
                fixed-size segments, and disrupts the logical coherence and connections within
019             the context. To overcome this, in this paper, we propose T HREAD, a novel data
020             organization paradigm aimed at enabling current systems to handle how-to ques-
021             tions more effectively. Specifically, we introduce a new knowledge granularity,
022             termed ‘logic unit’, where documents are transformed into more structured and
023             loosely interconnected logic units with large language models. Extensive exper-
024             iments conducted across both open-domain and industrial settings demonstrate
025             that T HREAD outperforms existing paradigms significantly, improving the success
026             rate of handling how-to questions by 21% to 33%. Moreover, T HREAD exhibits
027
                high adaptability in processing various document formats, drastically reducing the
                candidate quantity in the knowledge base and minimizing the required information
028
                to one-fourth compared with chunk, optimizing both efficiency and effectiveness.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Question answering (QA) is a foundational research topic in human-machine interaction (Allam
034   & Haggag, 2012). Among the most advanced techniques, Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
035   organizes external documents into fixed-size chunks and retrieves relevant knowledge to enhance QA
036   systems (Shao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023). This routine is
037   particularly effective in handling the ‘5Ws’ questions, which are typically factoid requiring factual
038   information about specific details (Yang et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2019; Kwiatkowski et al., 2019;
039   Stelmakh et al., 2022), such as ‘When is Shakespeare’s birthday?’. Specifically, the data organization
040   paradigm only needs to provide chunks containing the relevant knowledge, e.g. triples or documents
041   about the topic entity, to the RAG systems to facilitate the ‘5Ws’ questions. However, the ‘1H’
042
      questions, derived from Aristotle’s Nicomachean Ethics (in this paper as ‘how-to’ questions, Crisp
      2014), remain largely underexplored1 . These questions are in high demand in practical applications
043
      such as teaching us how to write code to achieve specific goals.
044
045   Central to problem-solving (Polya & Pólya, 2014) and human learning in cognitive science (Learn,
046   2000), how-to questions are inherently more complex, often involve processes that require interpre-
047   tation and analysis (Deng et al., 2023b). For example, correctly answering the question shown in
048
      Figure 1 ‘How to diagnose and fix a performance issue in a web application?’ involves a multi-step
      decision-making process, i.e., firstly checking the server load and response time, then optimizing
049
      server configuration or indexes and queries with user feedback. Such a dynamic nature necessitates
050
      the RAG systems incrementally guide users through each step, adapting to specific circumstances
051   and providing precise and logical information. However, the prevalent data organization paradigm,

068   chunk2 (Splitter, 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023) leaves current RAG systems (Asai et al.,
069   2023; Shao et al., 2023) struggling to solve such questions. This chunk-based paradigm disrupts
070   the logical flow and coherence of the content due to its inability to effectively represent the internal
071   logic. Consequently, it returns excessive information during inference, making it difficult to maintain
072   continuity between sequential steps, leading to fragmented responses for how-to questions and posing
073
      significant challenges for users. Overcoming these limitations necessitates a paradigm shift that
      accounts for the logical structure and stepwise nature of how-to questions, enabling more precise and
074
      coherent information retrieval tailored to specific needs.
075
076   In this paper, we propose T HREAD, a new logic-based data organization paradigm designed to
077   enhance the handling of how-to questions. The name T HREAD evokes the idea of ‘Pulling on the
078   thread, the whole mystery started to unravel like a sweater.’ (Garcia & Stohl, 2011). Specifically,
079
      we introduce a new knowledge granularity named ‘logic unit’, comprising five key components and
      four different types (see §3.1 and §3.2). We employ a two-stage process depicted in §3.3 to extract
080
      logic units (LUs) from documents. The first, optional stage is reformulating the original documents
081
      depending on their format and style, and the second focuses on extracting and merging logic units. In
082   this way, T HREAD captures connections within the documents, breaking them into more structured
083   and loosely interconnected logic units. When answering how-to questions, the system integrated with
084   T HREAD enables a dynamic interaction manner. First, it retrieves relevant LUs based on their indexed
085   headers. Then, the body of the selected LU provides the necessary content to generate responses for
086   the current step. With user feedback, the linker in LU dynamically connects to other LUs, allowing
087   the system to adapt its responses until the how-to question is comprehensively addressed.
088   We evaluate the effectiveness of T HREAD by conducting experiments in two open-domain, web
089   navigation (Deng et al., 2023a) and Wikipedia Instructions (Koupaee & Wang, 2018), and one
090   industrial setting, Incident Mitigation (Shetty et al., 2022). Experimental results demonstrate that
091   while existing paradigms struggle with how-to questions, T HREAD excels at handling consecutive
092   steps and consistently outperforms them, especially in real-world incident mitigation scenarios, with
093   an improvement of Success Rate ranging from 21.05% to 33.33%. In addition, T HREAD demonstrates
094   great efficiency and effectiveness in processing various document formats, significantly reducing the
095   number of retrieval units to 20% and the length of tokens needed for generation to 22.60%. The main
096
      contributions of this paper include:
097   • We highlight the challenges faced by current RAG systems in addressing how-to questions. To
098     address the limitation of the chunk-based paradigm, we propose T HREAD, a novel data organization
099     paradigm that transforms original documents into structured, interconnected logic units.
100   • Integrated with T HREAD, our system follows a dynamic interaction mode, guiding users incremen-
101     tally through each step and adapting to their specific circumstances. Our system also brings more
102     possibilities for an automation pipeline, solving how-to questions more efficiently.
103
      • Experimental results show that T HREAD significantly outperforms existing data organization
104
        paradigms across three scenarios. Additionally, T HREAD requires less information, while effec-
105     tively handling various document formats and substantially reducing the knowledge base size.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Data Organization Paradigm in RAG The data organization process is a critical pre-stage of
111   RAG methods where documents are processed and segmented following certain data organization
112   paradigms. The most common data organization paradigm is splitting documents into retrieval
113   units (Gao et al., 2023). These retrieval units vary in granularity such as phrases, sentences, propo-
114   sitions (Chen et al., 2023), chunks (Kamradt, 2024), etc. Coarser-grained units contain more
115   information but introduce redundant noise, while finer-grained units have lower semantic integrity
116   and often require retrieving more units to gather comprehensive information. However, the chunk-
117   based data organization paradigm ignores the logical and relational connections between chunks,
118   potentially disrupting the inherent logic flow in documents. Another paradigm constructs documents
119
      into knowledge graphs (KG), where retrieval units include entities, triplets, etc. (Gaur et al., 2022;
      Sen et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Edge et al., 2024). These approaches emphasize
120
      semantic/lexical similarities between retrieval units, their success in factoid questions is limited
121
      when applied to how-to questions. This limitation arises because how-to questions demand logical
122   connections between retrieval units that extend beyond mere semantic or lexical similarities.
123
124
      Information Retrieved by RAG The effectiveness of RAG methods depends on the generator’s
125   ability to utilize retrieved information and the quality and quantity of that information. Insufficient
126   question-relevant information can cause hallucinations in LLM-based generators (Li et al., 2023;
127   Zhang et al., 2023), making it crucial to improve the retrieval process. Traditional one-round retrieval
128   methods (Guu et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020) often fail to gather all necessary information due
129   to their reliance on the similarity between query and retrieval units (Gan et al., 2024). Advanced
130   RAG methods use query rewriting and expansion (Shao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023; Kim et al.,
131   2023) or iterative retrieval (Shao et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2023) to collect more
132   information. However, these approaches still struggle with how-to questions, which require making
133
      next-step decisions based on the current retrieved units, unless the current retrieved units contain
      clues that lead to the next step. The main issue is the lack of connections between retrieval units,
134
      which prevents effective retrieval and the gathering of sufficient information.

143   3.1   L OGIC U NIT: R ETRIEVAL U NIT OF T HREAD
144
145   We propose a new knowledge granularity called ‘logic unit’. Different from the chunk-based
146
      paradigm, LU comprises specially designed components, especially for bridging consecutive steps
      when addressing how-to questions, considering the internal logic and coherence of documents.
147
148   Prerequisite. The prerequisite component acts as an information supplement, providing the necessary
149   context to understand the LU. For example, an LU may include domain-specific terminology such
150   as entities or abbreviations. The prerequisite explains these terms and can generate new queries
151   to retrieve LUs with more detailed information. Without this context, passing these LUs to an
152
      LLM-based generator could lead to hallucinations. Additionally, the prerequisite can function as an
      LU filter, containing constraints that must be met before the LU is considered in answer generation.
153
      This filtering ensures only relevant LUs are retrieved. As shown in step LU in Figure 2, accessing the
154
      server monitor is a prerequisite for checking the server load.
155
156   Header. The header summarizes the LU or describes the intention it aims to address, depending on
157   the type of LU (refer to §3.2). For example, the header could be the name of a terminology if LU
158
      describes a terminology; if the LU describes actions to resolve a problem, the header describes the
      intent or the problem LU aims to resolve. Different from chunk that indexes the entire content, we
159
      use the header for indexing which serves as the key for retrieving the LU based on a query.
160
161   Body. The body contains detailed information on the LU, which is the core content fed into the
      LLM-based generator to generate answers. It includes specific actions or necessary information such

212
      actions that need to be completed before executing the current actions. The prerequisite is especially
213   crucial for identifying the entry point of a solution. For example, when facing a problem, there exist

231   3.3   T HREAD : LU- BASED K NOWLEDGE BASE
232
233   In practice, documentation is often unstructured and varies in format and style. Our approach to
234
      converting documentation into T HREAD involves a two-stage process to obtain LUs, as shown in the
      left part of Figure 2. We believe that with advancements in LLMs, this process could be streamlined
235
      into a single stage, enabling high-fidelity LU conversion with minimal hallucination.
236
237   Documentation Reformulation (Optional). This stage is optional, depending on the quality of
238   the documentation. For example, in software engineering, Troubleshooting Guides (TSGs) often
239   vary in format, include diverse types of information, and lack readability and detail, negatively
240
      impacting productivity and service health (Shetty et al., 2022). Due to such format, where some are
      clearly outlined and others are disordered, we avoid directly extracting LUs from original documents.
241
      Instead, we first reformulate these documents into structured formats. Leveraging LLMs for this
242
      task, we enhance the LLMs’ in-domain understanding by providing search capabilities and domain-
243   specific context. This is followed by a refinement step to prevent overlooking details or hallucinating
244   information. Figure 2 (a) shows the reformulation stage and the right represents with symbols. But it
245   is unnecessary for well-written documents like product help docs, which typically follow a linear
246   how-to format. We illustrate the process in Table 9 by example and provide prompts in Appendix C.1.
247
      LU Extraction and Merge. After reformulation, multiple LUs of varying types can be extracted
248
      from a single structured document (shown in Figure 2 (b)). Unlike chunk-based data organization
249   commonly with fixed chunk sizes, LU granularity depends on content. For example, solutions to
250   linear how-to questions typically form a single path from start to completion, with interconnected
251   steps and no multiple execution outcomes encapsulated in one LU, such as an FAQ LU. However, for
252   dynamic how-to questions with multiple possible outcomes, it is better to have one step per LU (Step
253   LU), with Linkers navigating to the next LUs. Note that in dynamic how-to questions, not every step
254   has multiple execution outcomes. If only one next step exists, the LUs can be merged to include both
255   current and subsequent steps. Additionally, LUs with similar Headers and Bodies should be merged,
256   extending the Prerequisite and Linker. More details about merging are provided in Appendix C.2.
257   LU update. In industry, documentation is often updated with each product version release. When this
258   happens, we redo the above steps for the updated documentation, identifying LUs in T HREAD with
259   their Meta Data and replacing outdated LUs.
260
      As we extract and merge LUs, the collection of LUs from all documents forms the knowledge base,
261   T HREAD. This LU-based knowledge base serves as an essential component compatible with the
262   current RAG system and even makes it possible for automation.

533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we address the overlooked category of handling how-to questions in QA systems by
536   proposing T HREAD, a novel data organization paradigm that captures logical connections within
537   documents. By introducing a new knowledge granularity called ‘logic unit’, T HREAD restructures
538   documents into interconnected logic units that are compatible with RAG methods. Extensive exper-
539   iments show that T HREAD significantly outperforms existing paradigms, improving performance
      while reducing the knowledge base size and minimizing the information needed for generation.

      A        C URRENT DATA O RGANIZATION PARADIGM
703
704
      From Gao et al. (2023), current data organization paradigms can be categorized into phrases, sentences,
705
      propositions, chunks, and so on. In our paper, we choose chunks 10 and propositions to compare with
706   our proposed T HREAD11 .
707
708   Recursive Chunk. This chunking method splits the original documents using a list of separators,
709
      then reassembles them according to specified chunk sizes and overlap sizes. In our experiment, we
      use different chunk sizes for each dataset: 1000 for Mind2Web, 2000 for IcM, and 300 for WikiHow.
710
      The chunk overlap sizes also vary 50 for Mind2Web, 100 for IcM, and 30 for WikiHow.
711
712   Entire Document. This method sends the entire document directly into the model, constrained by
713   the document’s length and structure.
714   Semantic Chunk. Kamradt (2024) proposes splitting chunks based on semantic similarity. The
715   hypothesis is that semantically similar chunks should be grouped together. By comparing the semantic
716   similarity between adjacent sentences, the method identifies “break points”. If the similarity in the
717   embedding space exceeds a certain threshold, it marks the start of a new semantic chunk.
718
      Agentic Chunk (Proposition). Chen et al. (2023)12 introduces the concept of the Proposition
719   Paradigm, which involves extracting independent propositions from original documents. The Agentic
720   Chunk method is based on this paradigm. It first splits the documents into paragraphs, then extracts
721   propositions from each paragraph, and at last merges similar propositions into chunks.
722
      In all our experiments, we set the temperature of LLMs to 0 and top_p to 1 for results reproduction.

       F    L IMITATIONS
1189
1190
       This work focuses on evaluating the effectiveness of T HREAD by designing how-to questions in three
1191   specific scenarios, covering both linear and dynamic how-to questions. However, there are several
1192   limitations that point to future directions. First, as our logic-based knowledge base can coexist with
1193   the original chunk-based knowledge base, we do not extend our method to factoid questions, such as
1194   multi-hop or long-form questions. Second, the experiments primarily use ChatGPT and LLaMA as
1195   the backbones. Future work should include evaluations with other LLMs and alternative retrievers
1196   such as Contriever to further validate the effectiveness of T HREAD. Lastly, while extracting logic
1197   units involves an initial cost in terms of LLM usage, this is a one-time process. Once the knowledge
1198   base is constructed, it provides significant advantages for industrial applications, particularly in terms
1199
       of subsequent updates and maintenance.
