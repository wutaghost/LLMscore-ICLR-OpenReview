009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Compressive adaptation approaches, such as QLoRA, are widely popular alter-
012            natives for reducing memory requirements during fine-tuning of large language
013            models (LLMs) while producing models capable of handling various downstream
014            tasks. The key idea is to employ a “two-tower” architecture: compressing pre-
015            trained LLM parameters into compact representations and fine-tuning the additive
016
               full-precision adapter, which typically has few tunable parameters in low-rank
               format. However, the strict algebraic assumptions, such as low-rank assumption,
017
               and the complexity of composing two-tower architectures are some of the known
018
               shortcomings, resulting in a poor accuracy-efficiency trade-off. In response to
019            these known limitations, we propose SpaLLM (Sketched Parameter Adaptation
020            of LLMs), a novel compressive adaptation approach for LLMs. This method is
021            also the first to illustrate parameter-sharing compression methods for LLM fine-
022            tuning, which, unlike QLoRA, are free from strict low-rank algebraic assumptions
023            on adapters. Furthermore, our proposal unifies model compression and adaptation
024            into a single, streamlined process, eliminating the need for two-tower architec-
025            tures. SpaLLM sketches pre-trained LLM weights into lookup tables and directly
026            fine-tunes the values in these tables. This approach simplifies LLMs’ compres-
027
               sive adaptation workflow, potentially improves multi-user serving efficiency, and
               delivers significantly better accuracy for both natural language understanding and
028
               generation tasks. Moreover, by avoiding the “two-tower” architecture, our frame-
029
               work only requires one compressed matrix multiplication per layer during infer-
030            ence, demonstrating superior inference efficiency compared to previous methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional perfor-
036   mance in Natural Language Processing (NLP), enabling a broad spectrum of downstream applica-
037   tions. LLMs have demonstrated impressive generalization abilities across many downstream tasks in
038   a zero-shot manner. However, compared to training-free methods such as in-context learning (Dong
039
      et al., 2022; Rubin et al., 2021) and few-shot prompting (Brown, 2020; Song et al., 2023), fine-tuning
      on these LLMs is often the ideal method to achieve optimal performance on a specific downstream
040
      task (Ding et al., 2023). Clearly, full-precision fine-tuning on these LLMs are often impractical
041
      due to the massive requirement of high-performance computing devices such as GPUs. As a re-
042   sult, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning methods (PEFT), such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu
043   et al., 2022), emerged as a less resource-intensive approach to fine-tuning while achieving reasonable
044   accuracy in NLP applications. Clearly, there is a trade-off between accuracy and efficiency. Nev-
045   ertheless, these PEFT methods still heavily relies on significant compute and memory resources,
046   especially when the base model is large, due to their “two-tower” architecture.
047
      Compressive adaptation of LLMs. This paper studies the compressive adaptation of LLM, where
048   the limited hardware resources cannot even afford the full-precision storage of LLM parameters. In
049   this case, we have to compress LLM parameters to lower precision, and then perform adaptation.
050   PEFT on top of a compressed model has become a popular compressive adaptation approach to ad-
051   dress the high resource expense (Dettmers et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; 2024b; Qin
052   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023) associated with LLM adaptations. Most of these approaches all use vari-
053   ations of LoRA-based adapters to perform the fine-tuning. For example, QLoRA (Dettmers et al.,
      2023) first compress a model to 4-bit precision, then add a set of LoRA adapters that are fine-tuned


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      by back-propagating through the compressed weights. LoftQ (Li et al., 2023) finds an initializa-
055   tion for the LoRA adapters that approximates the full-precision parameters before quantization, thus
056   reducing the performance gap.
057
058
      Challenges of two-tower compressive adaptation. Two-tower approaches with compress-finetune
      strategies face two challenges: 1) Strict algebraic assumption: The existing methods for com-
059
      pressive adaptation are built on strict algebraic assumptions about the adapters Dong et al. (2022);
060
      Liu et al. (2023). For instance, all the LoRA-based compressive adaptation approaches assume
061   that the difference between fine-tuned model parameters and base model parameters forms a low-
062   rank matrix. However, studies have shown that the difference in weights between fully fine-tuned
063   and base parameters can be high-rank (Liu et al., 2024a). Additionally, the compression process is
064   lossy, potentially requiring the adapters to exert extra effort to compensate for the loss. As a result,
065   two-tower methods underperform when base parameters are compressed to lower bit levels, such
066   as 3-bits or fewer (Yin et al., 2023). 2) Difficulty in implementation: The two-tower compressive
067   adaptation approaches demand complex implementation during both training and inference. If not
068   carefully managed, the distinct two-path structure introduces additional overhead in both computa-
069
      tion and memory usage. For example, in the QLora formulation, the first path dequantizes low-bit
      pre-trained weights and uses them to perform matrix multiplication with the input. In the second
070
      path, the input undergoes matrix multiplication with full-precision low-rank adapter parameter ma-
071
      trices. The results from both paths are then combined to produce the final output. Since these paths
072   involve multiplications at different precision levels, they must be handled as separate operations,
073   requiring significant system-level optimizations. As a result, finding methods that mitigate the two
074   problems mentioned above and provide better efficiency-accuracy trade-offs for LLM fine-tuning
075   remains an active area of research.
076
      Our proposal: unified adaptation directly on compressed parameters. This paper proposes a
077
      unified approach for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) that is both parameter- and memory-
078   efficient. The proposed method fine-tunes directly on compressed model parameters, without adher-
079   ing to strict algebraic assumptions. This flexibility allows for improved model performance, es-
080   pecially when heavy compression is needed to meet hardware resource constraints. By applying
081   matrix multiplication-based updates directly on the compressed parameters, the approach circum-
082   vents the need for extensive system-level optimizations, such as quantization and dequantization.
083   This not only simplifies the process but also reduces computational costs by eliminating the need for
084   dequantization and the use of complex “two-tower” architectures.
085   SpaLLM: Sketched Parameter Adaptation of LLMs. In this paper, we propose SpaLLM, denoted
086   as Sketched Parameter Adaptation of LLMs, a novel compressive adaptation approach for LLMs.
087   Our method is the first to apply sketching-based parameter-sharing techniques to LLM fine-tuning
088   without relying on algebraic assumptions, which are common in methods like QLoRA and LoftQ.
089   SpaLLM streamlines the process by combining model compression and adaptation into a single
090   workflow, eliminating the need for complex two-tower architectures. SpaLLM involves transform-
091   ing pre-trained LLM weights into lookup tables and directly fine-tuning these values, simplifying
092
      the adaptation process. This not only enhances the efficiency of serving multiple users but also
      improves accuracy for both natural language understanding and generation tasks. Additionally, by
093
      avoiding the two-tower structure, SpaLLM requires just one compressed matrix multiplication per
094
      layer during inference, significantly boosting inference efficiency compared to previous methods.

098
099   In this section, we introduce our method, SpaLLM (Sketched Parameter Adaptation of LLMs),
100   emphasizing how sketching algorithms facilitate parameter sharing. This approach enables scalable
101   and unified compressive adaptation of LLMs.
102
103   2.1   PARAMETER S HARING AS U NIFIED C OMPRESSIVE A DAPTATION .
104
105   Parameter sharing involves techniques that allocate a fixed set of ML model parameters and reuse
106   them across various parts of the model. For example, in LLMs, this often involves sharing weights
107   between various functional components. A common, though simplistic, form of parameter sharing
      in LLMs is group query attention (GQA) (Ainslie et al., 2023), where different attention heads with


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      distinct key and value projections share the same query projections. The parameter sharing approach
109   is particularly useful in LLMs, where parameter redundancy can quickly lead to increased memory
110   and computational demands. In this work, we propose that beyond improving efficiency, parameter
111   sharing also enables a unified, compressive adaptation of LLMs across different downstream tasks.
112
      Parameter sharing for more compression. In the context of model compression, parameter shar-
113
      ing offers an effective way to reduce model size with minimal impact on performance. While tradi-
114
      tional structural parameter sharing methods like GQA are widely used, recent research highlights the
115   advantages of element-wise parameter sharing (Chen et al., 2015; Desai & Shrivastava, 2022; De-
116   sai et al., 2022; 2023; Desai & Shrivastava, 2023), which goes beyond conventional techniques like
117   pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) and quantization (Frantar et al.,
118   2022). In this approach, model parameters are categorized into two types: virtual parameters and
119   trainable parameters. Virtual parameters correspond to specific locations within the model archi-
120   tecture where a parameter is required. Trainable parameters, on the other hand, represent the values
121   that are updated during the training process. The number of trainable parameters is significantly
122   smaller than the number of virtual parameters. Element-wise parameter sharing maps each virtual
123
      parameter to a corresponding trainable parameter, with multiple virtual parameters often sharing the
      same trainable parameter. During training, gradients are first calculated for each virtual parameter.
124
      These updates are then aggregated for the shared trainable parameters, which may receive gradients
125
      from several virtual parameters. The trainable parameters are updated by averaging these gradi-
126   ents. Empirical evidence suggests that this element-wise parameter-sharing approach opens up new
127   possibilities for compressing machine learning models effectively.
128
129
      Parameter sharing as regularization. Beyond compression, parameter sharing can also serve as
      a form of regularization in post-training processing of LLMs. For a pre-trained LLM, we treat all
130
      parameters as virtual and map each virtual parameter to a trainable one. During this process, a
131
      single trainable parameter may receive values from multiple virtual parameters, and these values
132   are averaged. This averaging acts as a regularization technique for the original parameters of the
133   LLM. By requiring different virtual parameters to rely on the same trainable parameter, the model is
134   constrained, reducing over-fitting by limiting its flexibility (Shakerinava et al., 2024). This ensures
135   that the shared parameters capture more general, robust information from the training data.
136
      Regularize, then adapt: parameter sharing for unified compressive adaptation. We propose pa-
137
      rameter sharing as a valuable method that combines the benefits of compression and regularization.
138   Starting with a pre-trained LLM, we apply parameter sharing to compress the model while simul-
139   taneously introducing regularization. Following this, we fine-tune the trainable parameters while
140   keeping the mapping between virtual and trainable parameters fixed, maintaining the regularization
141   effect during fine-tuning. This integrated approach not only reduces the model’s size but also allows
142   for task-specific fine-tuning, ensuring the model remains both efficient and adaptable. It has proven
143   effective in reducing the memory footprint of LLMs while preserving their generalization ability,
144   making it a practical solution for deploying large models in environments with limited resources.
145
146   2.2   S KETCHING FOR PARAMETER S HARING
147
148   We propose sketching algorithms to enable efficient parameter sharing for unified compressive adap-
149
      tation of LLMs. Our approach involves three key steps. First, we apply row-wise parameter sketch-
      ing, where each row of the model parameters is compressed into sketched parameters. Next, we
150
      introduce a weighted version of Lloyd’s algorithm to optimize this sketching process. Finally, we
151
      implement lookup tables to store the sketched parameters and their corresponding mappings, en-
152   abling efficient computation.
153
154   Row-wise parameter sketching. We perform parameter sketching for each row of the parameter
155
      matrix independently. As shown in Figure 1, given a parameter matrix Θ ∈ Rn×d in the LLM, we
      perform parameter sketching in every row θ ∈ Rd of Θ. Formally, we approximate θ as:
156
157                                                  θ̂ = Πw,                                             (1)
158
159   where w ∈ Rk is the sketched parameter and Π ∈ Rd×k is a one-hot sketching matrix. Every row
160   of Π is a one-hot vector with only one nonzero value as 1. In this formulation, every entry in θ is
161   mapped to an entry in w, and multiple entries in θ can be mapped to the same entry in w. We note
      that k < d so we compress the θ with one-hot mapping Π and sketched parameter w.

176   Weighted Lloyd’s algorithm for learning sketched parameters. Common sketching methods,
177   such as Bloom filters (Bloom, 1970) and Count Min Sketch (Cormode & Muthukrishnan, 2005), of-
178   ten employ randomized hashing as the sketching matrix, to minimize the impact of collisions on the
179   sketching quality. However, since LLMs parameters encapsulate rich pre-trained knowledge and are
180
      sensitive to perturbations (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023), randomized sketching would greatly degrade
      the quality of the model. Moreover, a randomized-hashing-based sketching is good for preserving
181
      heavy hitters, but LLM parameters do not exhibit heavy-hitter patterns (Xiao et al., 2023). There-
182
      fore, we propose to learn the sketch by taking inspirations from existing quantization approaches
183   (Zhang & Shrivastava, 2024). We perform Lloyd’s algorithm (Lloyd, 1982) to learn a set of k cen-
184   troids w ∈ Rk for the row of parameters    θ, which are inversely weighted by the Hessian diagonals
      diag(H−1 ) = diag (XX⊤ )−1 . Here, X ∈ Rs×d is the sample input matrix to the layer, whose

185
186   inner product with θ is the partial layer output, with s being the sample size. The learned centroids
187   w then become the sketched parameters of the parameter row θ.
188
      Learning the sketching matrix. Once the centroids w have been established, we learn the sketching
189   matrix by employing the iterative loss-error-based quantization framework (Zhang & Shrivastava,
190   2024). We iteratively round each parameter in θ to the nearest centroid in w, while updating the
191   not-yet-rounded parameters in θ to compensate for errors introduced by rounding according to the
192   update rule in Frantar et al. (2022). This process maps each parameter in θ to a single entry in
193   the sketched parameters w. As a result, the mapping becomes our sketching matrix Π, which is
194   row-wise one-hot.
195   Scaling up learning power by increasing sketched parameters. As we increase the number of
196   sketched parameters, the learning power of the adapted LLM increases. One potential way of in-
197   creasing the number of sketched parameters is to increase the size of the centroids w. However,
198   this would increase the size of the sparse sketching matrix Π. We instead increase the number of
199   sketches by dividing each row of parameters into contiguous groups, where each group keeps its
200   own sketch. This keeps the size of sketching matrices Π constant while increasing the number of
201   sketched parameters. We use groups per row (GPR) to scale up the learning power of our compres-
202   sive adaptation method.

      2.4   S PA LLM VS QL O RA: R ETAINS A LL N ICENESS W HILE E LIMINATING THE L OW-R ANK
            A SSUMPTION
246
247
      From Figure 2, we can clearly see that both QLoRA and SpaLLM are parameter-efficient, with the
248   number of trainable parameters being significantly smaller compared to the total number of param-
249   eters. This allows for efficient storage and processing on the adapters. Furthermore, like QLoRA,
250   SpaLLM only requires memory equivalent to the quantized weights. This is especially critical when
251   fine-tuning large LLMs, where parameter memory dominates overall memory requirements. As a
252   result, SpaLLM preserves all the known advantages of QLoRA.
253
      On the advantage side, SpaLLM makes no low-rank assumptions and can even handle full-rank
254   updates. Many full-rank matrices can be compressed using the shared-parameter scheme without
255   losing information. A simple identity matrix offers a perfect illustration. An identity matrix is
256   full rank and can be compressed perfectly with only two values (0 and 1) using shared-parameter
257   compression. At the same time, it is well known that there is no effective low-rank approximation
258   for an identity matrix; thus, enforcing a low-rank assumption can lead to information loss compared
259   to a shared-parameter assumption. In light of experimental results presented in(Liu et al., 2024a)
260   establishing that the weight differences between fully fine-tuned and base parameters tend to be
261   high-rank, shared-parameter adaptation seems like a more natural assumption for enabling PEFT.

417
      precision, r = 64). The results indicate a clear improvement in accuracy as the number of groups
      increases, demonstrating that finer granularity in sketched parameter groups enhances the retention
418
      of information. On the LLaMA-2-7B model, SpaLLM surpasses the baselines at 8 groups per row
419
      and achieves comparable performance with just 4 groups per row, while using half the number of
420   trainable parameters. Similarly, on the LLaMA-2-13B model, SpaLLM outperforms the baselines
421   starting from 1 group per row, utilizing only 1/5 of the trainable parameters. These findings highlight
422   the scalability of SpaLLM and its ability to efficiently utilize trainable parameters for knowledge
423   retention during fine-tuning.

431   in LoRA-based adaptations, resulting in lower memory usage and faster throughput by performing
      only a single pass during inference.

463   4   R ELATED W ORKS
464
465   Compressive adaptation of LLMs. The adaptation of LLMs to specific tasks while maintain-
466   ing computational efficiency has been an active area of research. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
467   (PEFT) methods have been created to mitigate the high cost of full fine-tuning on LLMs. Among
468   these these methods, there has been two major tracks of work: low rank based adaptation meth-
469   ods, include LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), DoRA (Liu et al., 2024b); and structural sparse adaptation
470   methods, including OFT (Qiu et al., 2024) and BOFT (Liu et al., 2023). Compressive adaptation
471
      methods aim to further reduce the memory and computational footprint of fine-tuning large models
      without sacrificing performance. Fine-tuning after quantization has become a popular approach.
472
      QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) introduced the NormalFloat data type for performing quantiza-
473
      tion while fine-tuning additional LoRA adapters to mitigate the knowledge lost during quantiza-
474   tion. LoftQ (Li et al., 2023) reduce the error between the compressed, fine-tuned model and the
475   original full-precision model by approximating the base weights using the quantized weights and
476   LoRA adapter during quantization. IR-LoRA (Qin et al., 2024). However, these existing compres-
477   sive adaptation methods, due to their use of the two-tower approach (compressed base weights and
478   adapters), face difficulties in managing complex implementation. Without careful management, the
479   two distinct path during inference introduce additional overhead in compute and memory usage.
480   Parameter Sharing in Machine Learning Advances in machine learning have led to the explo-
481   ration of parameter sharing as a means to reduce the memory footprint and computational costs
482   typical of large models. HashedNets(Chen et al., 2015) applies compression before training by us-
483   ing a hash function to group weights into hashed buckets. Similarly, Desai et al. (2022) introduced
484   ROBE, which compresses embedding tables through randomized hashing for more efficient stor-
485   age and access. ROAST(Desai et al., 2023) extends these ideas by utilizing global weight sharing
      to improve both training and inference speed. STABLE-RPS (Desai & Shrivastava, 2023) refines


                                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the ROAST method, introducing a gradient-scaling technique to enhance stability during training
487   and improve model accuracy. However, applying these methods to LLMs poses challenges, as they
488   rely on compression before training. This pre-training compression constrains the model’s learning
489   ability, which has led to limited success in applying parameter-sharing approaches to LLMs.
490

      5   C ONCLUSION
493
      In this paper, we introduced SpaLLM, a novel compressive adaptation approach for LLMs that ef-
494
      fectively addresses the limitations of existing methods like QLoRA. By eliminating the need for
495
      low-rank assumptions and unifying model compression and fine-tuning into a streamlined process,
496   SpaLLM offers a more efficient solution for fine-tuning LLMs on resource-constrained hardware.
497   The proposed sketching-based parameter-sharing mechanism allows SpaLLM to maintain high per-
498   formance even under heavy compression, while significantly reducing the computational and mem-
499   ory overhead typically associated with two-tower architectures. Through extensive experiments, we
500   demonstrated that SpaLLM not only outperforms state-of-the-art compressive adaptation methods
501   in terms of accuracy but also achieves superior inference efficiency, making it an ideal choice for
502   large-scale LLM deployments. Our approach’s ability to adapt seamlessly across a variety of natural
503   language understanding and generation tasks further highlights its versatility and robustness.
