009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models still struggle in challenging scenarios that leverage
012            structured data, complex reasoning, or tool usage. In this paper, we pro-
013            pose Source2Synth: a new self-augmentation approach for teaching LLMs new
014            skills that can be leveraged in low data regimes without relying on costly human
015            annotations. . Source2Synth takes as input a custom data source and produces syn-
016            thetic data points with intermediate reasoning steps grounded in real-world sources.
017            Source2Synth improves the dataset quality by discarding low-quality generations
018
               based on their answerability. We demonstrate the generality of this approach by
               applying it to two challenging domains: we test reasoning abilities in multi-hop
019
               question answering (MHQA), and tool usage in tabular question answering (TQA).
020
               Our method improves performance by 25.51% for TQA on WikiSQL and 22.57%
021            for MHQA on HotpotQA compared to the fine-tuned baselines.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Devlin et al., 2019; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020;
027   Vaswani et al., 2017) have risen to popularity due to their remarkable ability to digest and generate
028   human-like text (Radford et al., 2018). However, they still struggle with more complex tasks such as
029   multi-step reasoning, tool use and manipulating or processing structured data. For many of these tasks
030   there exists source data - for example, existing structured data on the web -, but little information of
031   how to use these data to solve a task. In principle, one can achieve performance improvements during
032
      fine-tuning by enriching the data with human annotations collected for specific tasks. However, this
      is an expensive and time-consuming process (Touvron et al., 2023) subject to human-errors and bias.
033
034   In this paper, we propose Source2Synth, a general approach to produce synthetic data grounded
035   in external real-world sources. Basing the data generation process on real-world sources steers
036   the examples to be more realistic, diverse, and factually correct. We showcase our method on
037   two challenging tasks: multi-hop questions based on sources from the web and tabular question
038
      answering using SQL as a tool. In both cases, models trained following Source2Synth’s pipeline
      achieve improved performance without relying on human annotations, resulting in a scalable data
039
      generation method for complex tasks, and present increased abilities at tackling corner cases.
040
041   Source2Synth consists of three stages: Dataset Generation, Dataset Curation, and Model Finetuning,
042   see Figure 1. At the Dataset Generation stage, we start by selecting a data source (such as tables on
043   the web or Wikipedia articles) to ground our synthetic generation in realistic information. Then, our
044
      method selects a seed topic to trigger the generation and condition all its components - for example a
      specific entity in a Wikipedia article or a factual statement about a table. Given the seed topic, the
045
      method then produces the full example: the instruction (e.g., question), the reasoning chain to arrive
046
      at the answer (e.g., the steps of multi-hop question answering, or tool use) and the answer itself.
047
048   At the Data Curation stage, the constructed synthetic dataset is split into two slices: the first half is
049   used to fine-tune the LLM. We use this intermediate model to curate the second half of the synthetic
050
      dataset via an imputation and a filtering step by rejection sampling. For imputation, we blank some
      parts of the given example in order to get a more natural and cohesive entry. For filtering, we reject
051
      examples that cannot produce the correct answer in k = 3 tries. This provides a higher quality curated
052
      dataset for the second fine-tuning stage, resulting in a final better performing model on a given task.
053
      To demonstrate the generality of our approach, we apply it to two different domains:

062          • We introduce a curation method based on filtering and imputation which yields higher

065   2     R ELATED W ORK
066
067   Synthetic Data Generation using LLMs A number of works propose different strategies to generate
068   synthetic datasets leveraging pre-trained language models. Some of these works rely on knowledge-
069   probing by first providing a prompt and letting the model either generate the continuation of a prefix
070   or predict missing words in a close-style template (Schick & Schütze, 2020; Schick & Schütze, 2021;
071   Petroni et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2019). Other works introduce a variety of ways to improve the quality
072   of synthetic data by using model-based or human filtering (Schick & Schütze, 2021; Liu et al., 2022;
073
      Li et al., 2024; Thoppilan et al., 2022). Our method however does not rely on human annotations, and
      we improve the quality of the synthetic data by leveraging the LLM itself. Furthermore, our selection
074
      of the seed topic is automated and we use real data as a starting point. We note that some recent
075
      work also leverages real-world data for specific cases, such as a corpus from the web to construct
076   high-quality synthetic data (Nguyen et al., 2024) or open-source code snippets to generate diverse
077   instruction data for code generation (Wei et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). In our case, we proposes
078   a general framework which can be applied across tasks and we do not require a back-translation
079   approach or an initial finetuning to come up with the seed.
080   See Liu et al. (2024) for a thorough overview of synthetic data research and references therein.
081
      Teaching LLMs to Use Tools Enabling LLMs to use different tools can extend their abilities towards
082   manipulating structured data, retrieving information from external sources, or interacting with APIs.
083   Even though the goal of our work is not specifically to teach models to use tools, but to develop a
084   general synthetic data generation approach, we consider this to be a by-product. As an example, we
085   demonstrate how our method can be applied so that LLMs use SQL. Various works augment LLMs
086   with general tools or API calls (Parisi et al., 2022; Schick et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023), while some
087   propose to interweave intermediate reasoning steps with API calls (Gao et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024;
088   Paranjape et al., 2023) which improves performance on more complex tasks. Finally, handling unseen
089   tools at test time has also been tackled (Paranjape et al., 2023; Mekala et al., 2024). See Mialon et al.
090
      (2023) and Qin et al. (2023) for an in-depth review of augmented tool-use.
091   Teaching LLMs to use SQL The above approaches usually tool usage is restricted to inputs that are
092   strings or numbers. However, using structured data (like tables and graphs) during post-training can
093   be useful to enhance the LLM’s capabilities in complex tasks. A particular tool of interest is SQL
094   since it enables aggregating information from tabular data. There exist a variety of benchmarks that
095   have been proposed to assess LLMs abilities to generate SQL as well as their performance on tabular-
096
      based question answering leveraging SQL tasks (Li et al., 2023a; Zhong et al., 2017). Alternatively,
      handling tabular data directly by LLMs has also been tried (Herzig et al., 2020; Gemmell & Dalton,
097
      2023), and tabular question answering benchmarks have been proposed (Pasupat & Liang, 2015).

139   Seed In order to create a given example of our new synthetic dataset, we first generate a seed topic as
140   the initial trigger for the generation process, which is chosen conditioned on a randomly selected
141   portion of the source data. The seed inspires the creation of the entry and dictates how the source
142   data will be used. In addition, the randomness of the seed ensures variety in the generated data.
143   Dataset construction In order to tackle complex tasks, LLMs can leverage a step-by-step ap-
144   proach (Wei et al., 2022) that divides reasoning into smaller sub-tasks plus instructions on how to
145   merge back each step into the final one. In Source2Synth, we leverage the seed to build synthetic data
146   step-by-step, decomposing into such intermediate steps in order to arrive at an answer for a given
147   question. This reasoning chain can then be used as supervision by providing it as the target in the
148   synthetically generated training examples.
149
150   3.2   DATASET C URATION
151
152   During curation, LLMSynth is then used to improve the quality of the second slice of the dataset
153   using imputation plus a filtering step. We observe that tasks that require compositionality during the
154   construction of the synthetic entry benefit from imputation since it allows the LLM to reformulate
155   into something of higher likelihood under the model’s distribution. For example, in MHQA, the
156   merging of two sub questions might produce a multi-hop question that sounds artificial. Following
157   the effort in aligning LLMs with human preferences for a more natural use, we observe by looking
158
      at the perplexity of imputation that partially reconstructing Q leads to more human-like questions..
      After these steps, we obtain the final curated dataset (shown in purple in Figure 1).
159
160   Data filtering During filtering, LLMSynth is used to predict the output of the given synthetic example
161   using k = 3 tries. If the output cannot be predicted at least once, it is assumed the example is low
      quality and is not included in the final curated dataset.

336   5     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
337
338   We test our method on two domains: tabular question answering and multi-hop question answering.
339   For each, we use Source2Synth to generate and curate a high quality dataset suitable for fine-tuning,
340   and compare our method to a number of baselines.
341
342   5.1     M ULTI -H OP QA S ETUP
343
344   Data To evaluate Source2Synth on MHQA, we evaluate it on HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018): a
345   benchmark based on Wikipedia containing 113,000 examples of multi-hop QA pairs, split in train,
346   test, and validation sets.
347   A comparison question entails comparing the same concept between n objects (e.g. "Who is the
348   tallest student in class?"), while a bridge question builds on a logical and/or causal link and requires
349   deriving statements to get to the answer (e.g. "What is the height of the student that topped the
350   entry exam?" - this requires first identifying the student that topped the exam). The hop length is the
351   number of comparison objects for comparison questions or the number of links for bridge questions.
352   In our case, we chose n = 2 to be consistent with HotpotQA . The test set consists of 7,405 entries,
353   split evenly between bridge and comparison questions. We only generate synthetic data for bridge
354
      questions, since they pose a bigger challenge to current LLMs and to counterbalance this disparity,
      we include 500 comparison questions from HotpotQA ’s training dataset in our fine-tuning dataset.
355
356   Metrics We measure the performance using soft exact match (soft-EM) as the metric. Soft-EM is 1 if
357   the generated output contains the golden answer and 0 otherwise.
358   Model In MHQA experiments we chose Llama-2 70B-Chat and we fine-tune Source2Synth and
359   various other baseline methods initializing from this model. Source2Synth is trained with 1250
360   synthetic examples, unless noted otherwise, in addition to the 500 HotpotQA examples above.
361
      Baselines We compare Source2Synth to the following baselines:
362
363
              • Instruction-tuned LLM: using LLama 2 70B-Chat for the task in a zero-shot manner.
364
365           • Fine-tuned LLM (HotpotQA only): fine-tuning from the base model on 500 HPQA examples
366
                from the training split.
367           • LLMSynth (Synthetic dataset only): training our model with 1250 synthetic examples from
368             Slice 0 (see Figure 1), without the data curation step.
369           • LLMSynth (Synthetic and HotpotQA ): training with the uncurated synthetic data in addition
370             to the 500 HPQA examples.
371
372   For all the models listed, we tested them using two prompting methods: a zero-shot and a three-shot
373   CoT prompt, see the Appendix E for details.

504
505
      We report the experimental results for Tabular question answering in Table 3. Firstly, we see that
      providing no context about the table when prompting the instruction-tuned StarChat language model
506
      has very poor performance (first row), with an EM metric of 0.25%. This is expected, since questions
507
      in WikiSQL require information contained in the table to answer, while the model does not have any
508   other information except for the general knowledge stored in its parameters. However, even if we
509   pass the table as part of the prompt, the performance does not improve much due to its difficulties
510   to digest structured data. For example, passing in a zero-shot fashion (second row) only has an EM
511   metric of 1.83%. While passing an example of table usage in a one-shot fashion (third row) improves
512   the soft-EM metric, the EM metric is still very low (2.03%). Hence, this is still very challenging for
513   the model. Thirdly, the performance increases once we provide a one-shot example containing the
514   relevant table and SQL query (fourth row), with an EM of 12.3%. The ability to use the SQL tool
515   improves performance markedly.
516   We obtain a significant increase in performance when we fine-tune the StarChat model using the
517   Source2Synth curated data (last row), with an EM of 34.5%. Our full method performs significantly
518   better than fine-tuning the StarChat language model using synthetic data without curation, LLMSynth
519   (second to last row) which has an EM of 23.86%, although that still outperforms the other baselines
520   by a large margin as well, indicating the utility of our Source2Synth synthetic data generation scheme.

522   7     L IMITATIONS
523
524   In this paper, our applications use a single seed or table per query to derive questions. However,
525   Source2Synth can be extended to more complex scenarios e.g. multiple hops or queries that require
526   multi-table tool-use. This can be done by looping the dataset generation steps and feeding the result
527   of the previous step as input to the next one. Our method could also be improved with more clever
528   sampling techniques. We consider this to be an interesting avenue of future research.
529
530   8     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduce Source2Synth, a new method for generating and curating high-quality
533   synthetic data grounded in real data sources. We demonstrate its utility on two tasks that pose
534   significant challenges for LLMs: multi-hop reasoning and tabular question answering with SQL. Our
535   work could also be beneficial in other low-data regimes and on other tasks and in diverse fields.

834   to adapt it for a new task: prompts, seed, and data source. If the new task builds on document- or
835   table-use, some parts of the two applications showcased in the main body of the paper can be reused.
836   We proceed illustrating how to adapt prompts, seed, and data source in case of the following new
837   task: generating code (Python functions) to compute statistics on spreadsheets. Since this task builds
838   on tables, we integrate TQA in it by leveraging its seed generation system. We believe that this is a
839
      meaningful and diverse example as it showcases a shorter tasks that does not produce a QA dataset

842                This can be a custom dataset, or a public one (like WikiSQL).;

                   the user. For example since we would like to compute statistics on the tables in the dataset,
