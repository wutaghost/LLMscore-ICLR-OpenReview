009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Hypergraph neural networks are a class of powerful models that leverage the
012            message passing paradigm to learn over hypergraphs, a generalization of graphs
013            well-suited to describing relational data with higher-order interactions. However,
014            such models are not naturally interpretable, and their explainability has received
015            very limited attention. We introduce SHypX, the first model-agnostic post-hoc ex-
016            plainer for hypergraph neural networks that provides both local and global expla-
017            nations. At the instance-level, it performs input attribution by discretely sampling
018            explanation subhypergraphs optimized to be faithful and concise. At the model-
019
               level, it produces global explanation subhypergraphs using unsupervised concept
               extraction. Extensive experiments across four real-world and four novel, synthetic
020
               hypergraph datasets demonstrate that our method finds high-quality explanations
021
               which can target a user-specified balance between faithfulness and concision, im-
022            proving over baselines by 25 percent points in fidelity on average.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Relational data in the form of graphs arises naturally in social networks (Fan et al., 2019), natural
028
      sciences (Zhang et al., 2021; Cranmer et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021), traffic dynamics (Jiang &
029
      Luo, 2022), and knowledge databases (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018). The neural approach (Kipf &
030   Welling, 2016) has enjoyed exciting successes, setting new state-of-the-art and expanding the reach
031   of machine learning to new modalities (Ektefaie et al., 2023; Battaglia et al., 2018).
032
033
      However, graphs can only describe pairwise relationships. This is insufficient to model real world
      systems that depend crucially on multi-way or group-wise interactions (Benson et al., 2016; Agarwal
034
      et al., 2005; Estrada & Rodrı́guez-Velázquez, 2006). A data structure that is well-suited to capturing
035
      higher-order correlations is the hypergraph. Whereas each edge in a graph joins two nodes, each
036   hyperedge in a hypergraph joins an arbitrary number of nodes. Message passing principles extended
037   to hypergraphs give rise to hypergraph neural networks (hyperGNNs) (Feng et al., 2019).
038
039
      Unfortunately, graph neural networks (GNNs) and hyperGNNs share a key concern with all black-
      box neural models: their lack of explainability. In response, many post-hoc explainers (Ying et al.,
040
      2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021; Magister et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2020) and interpretable-
041
      by-design architectures (Zhang et al., 2022b; Magister et al., 2023) have been developed for GNNs.
042   However, the literature for hyperGNN explainability remains exceedingly sparse, with the hyper-
043   graph modality posing new challenges as the space of possible explanations is substantially larger
044   than the graph counterpart.
045
      In this work, we introduce SHypX(Subhypergraph-based HyperGNN eXplainer), the first post-hoc
046
      hyperGNN explainer that produces explanations both at the instance level and global level. Our ex-
047
      planations take the form of subhypergraphs. Its core idea is to approximate subhypergraph sampling
048   with a collection of independent Gumbel-Softmax samplers, and use gradient feedback from a loss
049   function to obtain good explanation as per user specifications. This instance-level optimization is
050   combined with concept extraction to produce global explanations, where concepts represent signif-
051   icant, recurring subhypergraphs. The design choices of our explainer are guided by several con-
052   siderations: ensuring explanations are faithful to the hyperGNN under study, keeping explanations
053   concise and legible, and avoiding the introduction of another black-box model in the explanation
      method.

      To the best of our knowledge, this is the first global explainer designed for hypergraphs. For
055   instance-level explanations, the only existing hypergraph explainer (Maleki et al., 2023) relies on
056   learning an attention map to attribute the importance of each node-hyperedge link and induce the
057   explanation subhypergraph. However, it remains contentious whether attention provides a valid ex-
058   planation (Jain & Wallace, 2019; Wiegreffe & Pinter, 2019; Bibal et al., 2022). In contrast, SHypX
059   is simple, effective, and doesn’t rely on additional black-box networks to explain the hyperGNN.
060
      In addition to introducing an effective hypergraph explainer, we also propose a set of synthetic
061   datasets, designed to better assess the quality of hypergraph explanations along with suitable met-
062   rics for evaluation. As our experiments show, the current real-world datasets used in the previous
063   work (Maleki et al., 2023) barely take into account the hypergraph structure, making it difficult to
064   properly evaluate explainers. We believe that our datasets, which entirely depend on the higher-order
065   structures, have the potential to speed up the advancements in the field of hypergraph explainability.
066
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
067
068         1. We develop a model-agnostic post-hoc explainer for hyperGNNs that finds salient sub-
069            hypergraphs for both instance-level and global-level explanations.
070
071
            2. The instance-level explainer alleviates the need for black-box attention mechanisms
               used in the previous work. We integrate our instance-level explainer with unsupervised
072
               concept extraction to produce a global-level explanation – a novelty in the field of hy-
073
               pergraph explainability.
074
075         3. We introduce the first hypergraph explainiability benchmark containing four synthetic
076            datasets which are highly structure-dependent and thus offer a challenging testbed for ex-
077            plainability. Moreover, we generalize the fidelity metric for explanation faithfulness,
078
               making it more sensitive to deviations induced by the explanation subhypergraph.
079         4. We conduct extensive evaluations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that
080            our explainer obtains coherent explanations for each class, outperforming existing methods.

085   Hypergraph neural networks. HyperGNNs operate over hypergraphs, taking inspiration from the
086   message-passing paradigm of GNNs. HGNN (Feng et al., 2019), HyperGCN (Yadati et al., 2019),
087
      and HNHN (Dong et al., 2020) generalize GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) to hypergraphs. HCHA

088
      tention mechanisms for hypergraphs to dynamically learn the incidence matrix, analogous to GAT
089
      (Veličković et al., 2017). UniGNN (Huang & Yang, 2021) proposes leveraging GNN architectures
090   for updating node representations. AllSet (Chien et al., 2021) and EDHNN (Wang et al., 2023) use
091   universal approximators to learn multiset functions for node and hyperedge updates. Our work pro-
092   poses a model-agnostic explainer, producing hypergraph explanations regardless of the architectural
093   choice.
094
      GNN explainers. The majority of GNN explainers are local, finding an explanation subgraph per-
095
      taining to a specific input instance. Pope et al. (2019) and Sanchez-Lengeling et al. (2020) apply
096   gradient-based attribution techniques from vision and language to graphs to produce local explana-
097   tions. GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) learns fractional edge weights and thresholds them to pro-
098   duce explanation subgraphs; this framework is extended by PGExplainer (Luo et al., 2020), which
099   learns a second neural network to predict edge weights. SubgraphX (Yuan et al., 2021) finds the sub-
100   graphs instead by Monte Carlo Tree Search. GraphLIME (Huang et al., 2022) and PGMExplainer
101   (Vu & Thai, 2020) learn explainable surrogates of the original GNN. In contrast, global explainers
102   like XGNN (Yuan et al., 2020) and GCExplainer (Magister et al., 2021) produce explanations rep-
103   resentative of a class: XGNN generates explanation graphs with policy gradients and GCExplainer
104
      with unsupervised concept extraction.

106   2023). It optimizes an attention-based network with InfoNCE to assign importance weights to node-
107   hyperedge links to produce local explanations. However, there is ongoing debate about whether
      attention mechanisms offer valid explanations (Jain & Wallace, 2019; Wiegreffe & Pinter, 2019;

156   Problem statement. Consider the task of node classification over a hypergraph. (Our explainer
157   is more general, but we defer this discussion to Appendix A.) Let f be a hyperGNN that outputs
158
      for each node v a probability distribution f (G, X, v) over the classes. Our proposed model obtains
      both local and global explanations that are architecture-agnostic and fully post-hoc.
159
160   The goal of a local hypergraph explainer is, for each instance, to find which parts of the input hy-
161   pergraph are most important to determining f ’s output. Accordingly, the explanation artefact is a
      subhypergraph. A good explanation subhypergraph Gexpl = (Vexpl , Eexpl ) should be able to repro-

171   Given a trained hyperGNN f , a hypergraph G, and a node instance v in G, our goal is to produce
172   an explanation subhypergraph that is both faithful and concise. To achieve this, we formulate these
173   desiderata as a joint objective and optimize the explanation subhypergraph against this objective by
174   discrete sampling. Figure 1(top) gives an overview of the local explainer.
175
176   Objective function. We can quantify the faithfulness of the explanation by the Kullback-Leibler
177   divergence between the original class probabilities predicted by f over G, and when f is restricted
178   to the explanation subhypergraph. We can quantify concision by the L1 norm of the incidence
179
      matrix, which is equivalent to the number of node-hyperedge links. We denote this size measure on

229   challenging loss landscape of this discrete problem. We discard disconnected components to pro-
230   duce a smaller and more legible explanation artefact, and grant the same advantage to the baselines
231   in our evaluations.

233   4.2   G LOBAL EXPLAINER
234
235   The local explainer returns an explanation subhypergraph for a single node instance. How can
236   we leverage this to obtain a global explanations at the class-level? While global explanation for
237   hyperGNNs is an unexplored area of research, several methods were proposed for GNNs. However,
238   creating class prototypes by graph alignment (Ying et al., 2019) is NP-hard, and graph generation
239   with reinforcement learning (Yuan et al., 2020) requires expensive policy gradients optimization.
240
      We desire a global explainer whose computation costs do not scale with the increased combinatorial
      possibilities of the hypergraph space.
241
242
243   Concept extraction and visualization. We propose to obtain global explanations using unsuper-
244
      vised concept extraction, inspired by Magister et al. (2021). Concepts are higher-level units of
      information, more accessible for humans than low-level neural network constructs (Ghorbani et al.,
245
      2019). Similar to the GNNs domain (Magister et al., 2021), we find that concepts may be identified
246
      with clusters in the hyperGNN’s activation space. We then visualize each concept by finding the
247   local explanation subhypergraph of its representative node.
248
249   Stated more precisely, a hyperGNN f learns latent node representations zv , ∀v ∈ V . We train a
250
      k-means model with k centroids on {zv }v∈V , and use it to map each node v onto one of k concepts,
      KMeans(zv ) = cv . To obtain a concept-level explanation for concept c, we take the node closest to

295   5.1   S YNTHETIC HYPERGRAPHS
296
297   Motivation. Synthetic graph datasets for GNN explainability have driven substantial progress in
298   the field. However, no such dataset exists for hypergraphs. We argue that synthetic (hyper)graph
299   datasets are valuable because they guarantee the primacy of structure for solving the task. For many
300   real world hypergraphs like those in the benchmarks of Chien et al. (2021), competitive performance
301   is already achieved by MLPs, which do not account for the hypergraph’s structure. Accordingly, we
302   find that node-level explanations obtained for such datasets typically comprise a “trivial” subhy-
303
      pergraph containing just the node itself. While valid explanations, they suggest the dataset fails to
      provide a challenging and discriminating testbed for evaluating hyperGNN explainability. Our syn-
304
      thetic hypergraphs ensure that labels depend critically on the hypergraph structure by construction,
305
      complementing evaluation on real world datasets.
306
307
308
      Dataset construction. Our synthetic hypergraphs are inspired by the synthetic graphs of Ying
      et al. (2019), which have served as a core benchmark in graph explainability. Each hypergraph com-
309
      prises a “base” component that is either random or a deterministic “hyper-binary-tree“ (Figure 2a-b),
310
      to which various “motifs” (Figure 2c-e) are attached using a single hyperedge. Additionally, we ran-
311   domly add degree-2 hyperedges as perturbations. Figure 2e shows an example of a hypergraph
312   constructed in this manner. The task is to classify nodes based on their positions in the base or
313   motif. See Appendix B for details.
314

327   datasets (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Magister et al., 2021). Not only is this approach

329   is unprincipled and potentially misleading. The implanted motifs reflect human reasoning, but are
330   not necessarily faithful to the neural network, which may instead rely on a variant or correlate of
331   the motif. Rather, a good explanation should provide users information about the hyperGNN’s

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      Explainability for hyperGNNs is an under-explored topic, but essential for their responsible de-
534   ployment in critical applications. We introduce SHypX, a model-agnostic post-hoc explainer, and
535   demonstrate its efficacy with extensive evaluations. At the instance-level, our method finds ex-
536   planation subhypergraphs that can target a desired tradeoff between explanation faithfulness and
537   concision. At the model-level, we are the first to extend our instance-level method with concept
538   extraction to efficiently derive concise global explanation subhypergraphs. Additionally, we design
539   novel synthetic hypergraph datasets and propose more general fidelity metrics, which together allow
      for a challenging and sensitive evaluation of hyperGNN explainers.

729   edge and graph level. In regression, we simply replace the KL divergence in L with MSE, since f
730   outputs regression targets instead of class probabilities. For hyperedge- and hypergraph-level tasks,
731   Gcomp may be more complex than a d-hop neighborhood, depending on the architecture, or even
732   equal to G. Additionally, we allow disconnected components if they contribute to the final predic-
733   tion, e.g. for hypergraph-level tasks formulated with a global pooling layer. The overall pipeline is
734   otherwise unchanged.

832
833          • Random. The importance score of each node-hyperedge link is randomly assigned as a
834            random variable drawn from U (0, 1). That is, we get the subhypergraph induced by n
835            uniformly random node-hyperedge links.
836          • Gradient. We compute the gradient of the logit on the predicted class of the node being
837            explained, with respect to the hypergraph edge index. We then get the subhypergraph
838            induced by the n node-hyperedge links with the largest non-zero gradients by absolute
839            value.

841            ilar saliency and integrated gradients baselines constructed by Maleki et al. (2023). Our
842            gradient baseline computes gradients on the hyperedge index, and thus selects a set of
843            node-hyperedge links. Their gradient baselines compute gradients over the input nodes,
844            and thus selects a set of nodes.

905   C.3   C ONCEPT EXTRACTION AND CONCEPT COMPLETENESS
906
907   We extract concepts by k-means clustering, as described in Section 4.2. The quality of concept
908   extraction is quantified by concept completeness, the accuracy of a decision tree classifier that opti-
909   mally maps the set of concepts onto the set of class labels (Magister et al., 2021). Optimal is defined
910   such that each node instance, featurized only by its concept label, is mapped to a class label with
911   high accuracy. Since the concept label is a discrete class, the decision tree classifier is optimized
912   by performing majority vote within each concept, as proposed in Section 4.2. We consider the con-
913   cept extraction successful if its concept completeness is close to the task accuracy, since this overall
914
      procedure relies on the latent representations learned by the hyperGNN.
915   Table 5 shows that across all datasets, the latents are indeed such that we can successfully extract
916   meaningful concepts that score well on concept completeness (i.e. within a few percentage points
917   of the task accuracy). We find that k = 10 suffices to achieve this condition on all datasets, but that
      it is beneficial to increase to k = 15 for H-C OMM H OUSE.

1073

1078   prediction. This can be seen with a simple intuition: when the explanation subhypergraph is concise
1079   – that is, all of its parts are necessary, as desired – the complement is large. The complement is
       therefore likely to include a large number of hyperedges and neighbors directly incident to the node
