009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Random matrix theory has proven to be a valuable tool in analyzing the general-
012            ization of linear models. However, the generalization properties of even two-layer
013
               neural networks trained by gradient descent remain poorly understood. To under-
               stand the generalization performance of such networks, it is crucial to character-
014
               ize the spectrum of the feature matrix at the hidden layer. Recent work has made
015
               progress in this direction by describing the spectrum after a single gradient step,
016            revealing a spiked covariance structure. Yet, the generalization error for linear
017            models with spiked covariances has not been previously determined. This paper
018            addresses this gap by examining two simple models exhibiting spiked covariances.
019            We derive their generalization error in the asymptotic proportional regime. Our
020            analysis demonstrates that the eigenvector and eigenvalue corresponding to the
021            spike significantly influence the generalization error.
022
023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024   Significant theoretical work has been dedicated to understanding generalization in linear regression
025   models (Dobriban & Wager, 2018; Advani et al., 2020; Mel & Ganguli, 2021; Derezinski et al.,
026   2020; Hastie et al., 2022; Kausik et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). In an effort to extend this under-
027   standing to two-layer neural networks, researchers have explored various approximations, including
028   the random features model (Mei et al., 2022; Mei & Montanari, 2021; Jacot et al., 2020), the mean-
029   field limit of two-layer networks (Mei et al., 2018), the neural tangent kernel (Jacot et al., 2018;
030   Adlam & Pennington, 2020), and kernelized ridge regression (Barzilai & Shamir, 2024; Liang et al.,
031   2020; Xiao et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024).
032   For the random features approximation, the first layer of the neural network is considered fixed, and
033   only the outer layer is trained. Concretely, consider a two-layer neural network

086   Contributions This paper considers linear regression where the data x has a spiked covariance
087   model. The main contributions of the paper are as follows.
088     (i) We consider two linear regression problems that capture some of the challenges present in the
089         spiked covariance model from Moniri et al. (2023). These regression problems extend prior
090         models from Hastie et al. (2022) and Li & Sonthalia (2024). In particular, we introduce two
091         regression targets, providing a more comprehensive understanding of how the learned features
092         interact with the target function.
093
        (ii) We derive closed-form expressions for the generalization error for both models (Theorem 3
094          and Theorem 4), offering precise quantification of the generalization error.
095
096    (iii) We show that the risk can be decomposed into the asymptotic risk for the unspiked case plus
097
             a correction term that depends on the eigenvector and eigenvalue corresponding to the spike.
             We show that for finite matrices, if the variance for the distribution of the bulk eigenvalues is
098
             small enough, then the correction is significant.
099
100   Other Related Works Spiked covariance models have gotten significant attention. Prior works
101   such as Benaych-Georges & Nadakuditi (2012); Baik & Silverstein (2006) have examined the largest
102   eigenvalue and its corresponding eigenvector in the asymptotic limit. Spiked covariances can be
103   seen when denoising low rank signals (Nadakuditi, 2014; Sonthalia & Nadakuditi, 2023; Kausik
104
      et al., 2024). Additionally, recent work Li & Sonthalia (2024) also considers two particular spiked
      covariance models and shows interesting double descent phenomena. See Couillet & Liao (2022)
105
      for more applications where spiked covariance models appear.
106
107   Recent work has also sought to understand the features of the spectrum beyond a single step (Wang
      et al., 2024b) and for three layer neural networks (Wang et al., 2024c; Nichani et al., 2023).

      Proof Idea In this section, we provide a brief discussion of the proofs of the two theorems, par-
487   ticularly focusing on how we handle the spike in the covariance matrix. The proof relies on the
488   asymptotic limiting spectrum but only for the noise matrix A, not for A + Z. This is why we cannot
489   let ╧ДAtrn , ╧ДAtst go to zero. The proof builds upon ideas from Sonthalia & Nadakuditi (2023); Li &
490   Sonthalia (2024). The main idea is that the solution ╬▓so or ╬▓spn is of the form:
491
                                                X тАа y = (Z + A)тАа y.
492
493   Here, instead of using the spectrum of Z + A to quantify the error, we expand (Z + A)тАа using the
494   result from Meyer (1973) into sums of terms where we only invert A, not A + Z. Thus, we only
495   care about the Stieltjes transform of A.
496   Let ╬▓0 be the solution to the signal-only problem when ╧Д╬╡2trn = 0. Then we see that
497
498                    ╬▓so = ╬▓0 + (Z + A)тАа ╬╡trn ,       and   ╬▓spn = ╬▓so + (Z + A)тАа A╬▓тИЧ .
499   We compute a bias-variance type decomposition for the risk. For example, for the signal-plus-noise
500   problem, we decompose it as
501
                  тИеZtst ╬▓тИЧ тИТ Ztst ╬▓spn тИе2F + тИеAtst ╬▓spn тИе2F + тИеAtst ╬▓тИЧ тИе2F тИТ 2╬▓тИЧT ATtst Atst ╬▓spn .
502
503   Then we show that each of these can be expressed as the product of dependent quadratic forms
504   that are mostly of the form ╧Й1T (ATtrn Atrn )тАа ╧Й2 or ╧Й1T (ATtrn Atrn )тАа (ATtrn Atrn )тАа ╧Й2 for some vectors
505
      ╧Й1 , ╧Й2 . We use the almost sure weak convergence of the spectrum of A to express these as m(тИТ┬╡2 ),
      i.e., the Stieltjes transform of the limiting e.s.d for A at тИТ┬╡2 . Additionally, we show that these

      As such, we can estimate the expectation of the product using the product of the expectations. We

512   5    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
513
      While this work takes an important step in understanding the generalization error for data with
514
      spiked covariances, significant work remains to be done.
515
516   Discrepancies with Moniri et al. (2023) There are a few discrepancies between the model con-
517   sidered here and the spiked covariance model from Moniri et al. (2023). Specifically:
518        (i) The distribution of the spectrum for F0 versus that of A.
519
520        (ii) The dependency between F0 (for us A) and X╠Гw (for us Z).
521   We believe the distribution of the spectrum of F0 is solvable using the techniques presented here. We
522   need to use the appropriate Stieltjes transform, which has been studied in prior work (P├йch├й, 2019).
523   The dependency between F0 and X╠Гw also appears tractable but would introduce some additional
524   quadratic forms that would need bounding. While these two problems are approachable, they require
525   significant work and are avenues for future research.
526   Multiple Spikes and Steps This paper only considers the model where there is a singular spike.
527   However, depending on the step size, we may see multiple spikes. We believe that the manner in
528   which Kausik et al. (2024) generalizes Sonthalia & Nadakuditi (2023) from rank one to generic low
529   rank could be adapted to study the problem with multiple spikes. Additionally, we only consider the
530   case where we take one step.
531
532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
      The feature matrix of a two-layer neural network has been shown to have spiked covariance with
534   finitely many spikes. However, these spikes cannot be detected when we look at the asymptotic
535   proportional limit. Nevertheless, the spikes are crucial as they arise due to the feature learning
536   capabilities of neural networks. This paper considers linear regression with data that has a simplified
537   spiked covariance. We show that the models here are natural extensions of prior work and quantify
538   the generalization error. We show that for the signal-plus-noise model, the spike has an effect for
539   finite matrices, but this effect disappears in the asymptotic limit. For the signal-only problem, we
      show that the dependence on the spike appears even in the asymptotic limit.
