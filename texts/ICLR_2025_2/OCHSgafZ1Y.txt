011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Mixed-precision quantization (MPQ) aims to identify optimal bit-widths for lay-
014            ers to quantize a model. On the other hand, zero-shot quantization (ZSQ) aims
015            to learn a quantized model from a pre-trained full-precision model in a data-free
016
               manner, which is commonly done by generating a synthetic calibration set used
               for quantizing the full-precision model. While it is intuitive that there exists in-
017
               herent correlation between the quality of the generated calibration dataset and the
018
               bit allocation to the model’s layers, all existing frameworks treat them as separate
019            problems. This paper proposes a novel method that jointly optimizes both the cali-
020            bration set and the bit-width of each layer in the context of zero-shot quantization.
021            Specifically, we first propose a novel data optimization approach that takes into
022            consideration the Gram-Gradient matrix constructed from the gradient vectors of
023            calibration samples. We then propose a novel scalable quadratic optimization-
024            based approach to identify the model’s bit-widths. These proposals will then be
025            combined into a single framework to jointly optimize both the calibration data
026            and the bit allocation to the model’s layers. Experimental results on the ImageNet
027
               dataset demonstrate the proposed method’s superiority compared to current state-
               of-the-art techniques in ZSQ.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The impressive performance of deep learning models across various fields and applications has
      spurred considerable interest in their applications on resource-constrained devices. As a result,
034
      model acceleration and memory optimization for deep neural networks (DNNs) have become an
035
      increasingly crucial problem. Among the prevalent network compression techniques such as pruning
036   (He et al., 2017; Molchanov et al., 2019), knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2014; Romero et al.,
037   2015), and quantization (Courbariaux et al., 2015; Rastegari et al., 2016), network quantization
038   stands out as one of the most effective approaches. Network quantization aims to produce smaller
039   models by representing full-precision parameters (32 bits) with significantly smaller bit-widths, e.g.,
040   1, 2, or 4 bits), while still achieving performance comparable to full-precision models (Chen et al.,
041   2021; Dong et al., 2019; Yang & Jin, 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Nagel et al., 2020).
042   Zero-shot quantization is a quantization approach when there is no access to any part of the orig-
043   inal data. By leveraging information from the full-precision model, ZSQ generates a small set of
044   synthetic data and exploits it as calibration data for the quantization process. Prominent ZSQ meth-
045   ods (Choi et al., 2021; Jeon et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023) take advantages of statistics
046   from the batch normalization (BN) layers of the full-precision model to generate synthetic samples
047   such that the feature distributions of generated samples match the BN statistics. Other approaches
048   focus on boundary information, generating data near the decision boundary of the full-precision
049   model (Choi et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023a).
050   Instead of using the same bit-width for all layers, mixed-precision quantization (MPQ) seeks to
051   enhance performance by allocating higher bit-widths to crucial network layers. MPQ methods can be
052   generally divided into two main categories: search-based approaches and sensitivity-based methods.
053   Search-based approaches, such as those proposed by (Wang et al., 2018; Deng et al., 2023), employ
      reinforcement learning (RL) to optimize bit-widths for network layers. In contrast, sensitivity-based

054
      methods, including (Cai et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2019; 2020; Chen et al., 2021), use layer sensitivity
055   as a proxy measure to evaluate the contribution of each layer to the final model performance. These
056   techniques determine sensitivity scores for layers using predefined heuristics and then formulate an
057   integer programming problem to allocate bit-widths based on these sensitivity scores, while adhering
058   to computational and memory constraints. However, none of the aforementioned methods consider
059   the impact of data quality during the mixed-precision identification process.
060
      Despite substantial research on mixed-precision quantization and zero-shot quantization, these top-
061   ics are often treated as separate problems. Given that most existing mixed-precision methods em-
062   ploy data-based heuristics, it is intuitive to assume that the quality of the calibration set influences
063   the bit-width selection in mixed-precision settings, and vice versa. In this paper we propose a novel
064   approach for zero-shot mixed-precision quantization in which the data generation and bit allocation
065   are jointly optimized, thereby enhancing the efficiency of quantized models. To our best knowledge,
066   this paper is the first work that jointly optimize the data generation and the bit allocation in the con-
067   text of zero-shot mixed-precision quantization. Furthermore, we propose a novel data generation
068   approach that is based on the Gram-Gradient matrix of the generated calibration data. Specifically,
069
      we firstly show that the optimization of the training set is equivalent to maximizing its gradient
      matching with the validation set during the training process. We then theoretically show that this
070
      optimization is equivalent to matching the Gram-Gradient matrices of the two sets. In addition, we
071
      propose a scalable quadratic optimization approach to optimize the model bit-widths, that takes into
072   account the impact of different layer bit-widths to the model’s gradient.
073
074   The contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:
075   ❶ To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first one that proposes a mechanism to jointly
076   optimize both the data generation and the bit allocation for zero-shot mixed-precision setting. ❷ We
077   propose a novel approach for optimizing the calibration data based on the Gram-Gradient matrix of
078   that calibration set. ❸ We propose a scalable quadratic optimization approach that takes into account
079   the impact of bit-widths setting to the gradient of the model for bit allocation optimization. ❹ Exper-
080   imental results demonstrate that our novel zero-shot mixed-precision quantization (ZMPQ) method
081
      outperforms the state-of-the-art ZSQ methods under low-bit quantization settings. In addition, al-
      though our proposed ZMPQ approach does not require real calibration data, our method achieves
082
      competitive results compared to mixed-precision methods that require real data for the quantization
083
      process.

      2.2   Z ERO - SHOT QUANTIZATION
109
110   As the solution for scenarios where the original data is not available, Zero-shot quantization (ZSQ)
111   approaches attempt to generate synthetic data by leveraging information from the full-precision
112   models. A conventional method for synthesizing such data is to minimize the cross-entropy loss
113   based on the full-precision model’s prediction and the synthetic label. One of the pioneering works
114   in ZSQ, ZeroQ (Cai et al., 2020), identified that there exists distributional mismatch between the
115   synthetic data and real data, which leads to substantial performance degradation. In order to combat
116   this problem, ZeroQ proposes to take advantage of the batch normalization (BN) statistics derived
117   from the full-precision model, resulting in significant performance enhancements. Subsequently,
118
      advanced ZSQ data generation techniques have emerged, taking into account this idea and additional
      elements for data generation. For instance, Genie (Jeon et al., 2023) proposes a framework that
119
      learns both the generator and its inputs concurrently. Qimera (Choi et al., 2021) and HAST (Li et al.,
120
      2023) propose to generate boundary-supporting samples with different approaches. While Qimera
121   produces samples within the decision boundary between classes by combining class embeddings,
122   HAST incentivizes samples based on their loss uncertainty. DSG (Qin et al., 2021) highlights the
123   limited heterogeneity in synthetic samples resulting from BN statistics optimization and suggests
124   introducing a margin threshold when minimizing BN statistics mismatch, to enhance sample variety.
125   On the other hand, AdaDFQ (Qian et al., 2023a) seeks to optimize both boundary information and
126   data diversity.
127

      2.3   M IXED - PRECISION Q UANTIZATION
129
130
      Mixed-precision quantization techniques can be broadly categorized into two main categories:
131
      search-based and sensitivity-based approaches. Search-based methods, exemplified by HAQ (Wang
132   et al., 2018) and AutoQ (Lou et al., 2019), utilize reinforcement learning for bit-width optimiza-
133   tion of network layers. The performance of the mixed-precision quantized network serves as the
134   reward signal, while their action space consists of all possible bit-width configurations. A notable
135   search-based MP method recently is EMQ (Dong et al., 2023), which introduces a proxy search
136   framework through evolving algorithms that automatically generate proxies to guide the mixed-
137   precision process. On the other hand, sensitivity-based methods such as ZeroQ (Cai et al., 2020),
138   HAWQ (Dong et al., 2019), HAWQ-V2 (Dong et al., 2020) and MPQCO (Chen et al., 2021) lever-
139   age layer sensitivity as a surrogate metric to estimate the impact of quantization on overall model
140
      performance. This family of technique assess layer sensitivity scores for all layer with predefined
      heuristics, before formulating an integer programming problem that allocates layer bit-width accord-
141
      ing to their sensitiveness, under memory and computational constraints. A recent sensitivity-based
142
      mixed-precision quantization method, CLADO (Deng et al., 2023), successfully take into account
143   the inter-dependency between network layers when selecting their bit-width. Unfortunately, simi-
144   lar to conventional mixed-precision quantization techniques, CLADO (Deng et al., 2023) requires
145   non-trivial time complexity for their bit allocation. In order to address the computational bottleneck
146   problem, OMPQ (Ma et al., 2023), a prominent mixed precision quantization method, introduces
147   an orthogonality metric to measure the correlation between layer orthogonality. This metric is used
148   to determine the optimal bit-width configuration for different layers by assigning more bit to layers
149   with stronger orthogonality. However, most of the prior mixed-precision quantization works does
150   not consider the impact of data quality to the bit allocation algorithm.

388   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
389
      Dataset and network architectures. We evaluate our methodology using the ImageNet (Rus-
390
      sakovsky et al., 2015) dataset, a common benchmark in zero-shot quantization studies. We validate
391
      our approach on ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016), ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016), and MobileNetV2 (San-
392   dler et al., 2018) architectures.
393
394
      Quantization setting. We follow the approach outlined in Genie (Jeon et al., 2023), starting with
395
      a standard uniform quantization framework that incorporates an adaptive rounding variable, as de-
396   tailed in section 2.1. The weight bit-widths of both the initial and last layers are set to 8 bits for
397   initialization. Additionally, following BRECQ (Li et al., 2021), we set the activation bit-widths for
398   the second and last layers to 8 bits, while other layers adopt the uniform activation bit setting. The
399   model’s bit budget, Bbudget , is equal to the size of the initial model.
400
401   Implementation details. To warm up the synthetic dataset, we utilize a generator and 256-
402   dimensional embedding vectors for each mini-batch of generated images using Genie model (Jeon
403   et al., 2023). Initially, we set the learning rate for the generator to 0.1 and for the embeddings
404   to 0.01. We set k to 10, and use the 10th order Gram-Gradient matrix for our data optimization.
405   The parameter λ in Eq. (12) is set to 0.02, while η in Eq. (8) is set to 2, 1 and 1 for ResNet-18,
406   ResNet-50 and MobileNetV2, respectively. We adopt the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014)
407
      for both the generator and the data embeddings, and we employ ExponentialLR and ReduceLRon-
      Plateau learning rate schedules for the generator and the data embeddings, respectively. Throughout
408
      all experiments, we use a batch size of 128 during data generation and a batch size of 32 during the
409
      quantization process. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we benchmark our
410   model against different SOTA zero-shot quantization models on various low-bit settings. Following
411   previous works (Jeon et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023a) and incorporating an additional ultra-low-
412   bit setting (2/2), we employ three distinct quantization configurations for the zero-shot ImageNet
413   experiments: 2/2, 3/3, and 4/4 bit-widths. We then compare our proposed approach with recent
414   leading zero-shot quantization models, i.e., Qimera (Choi et al., 2021), Genie (Jeon et al., 2023),
415   AdaDFQ (Qian et al., 2023a), IntraQ (Zhong et al., 2022) and AdaSG (Qian et al., 2023b). For a fair
416   comparison, we generate a total of 1, 024 images, following Genie (Jeon et al., 2023). Additionally,
417   we also present the performance of the current leading mixed-precision quantization models (Ma
418
      et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023; Yang & Jin, 2020; Cai et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Choi et al., 2018)
      under different model size constraints {4.0, 4.5, 5.81}.

      quantization settings and model budgets confirm the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
