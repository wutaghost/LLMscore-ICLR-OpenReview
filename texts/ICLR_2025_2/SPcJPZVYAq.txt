009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become essential tools for analyzing struc-
012            tured data across various domains. In GNNs, sampling is critical for reducing
013            training latency by limiting the number of nodes processed during training, es-
014            pecially for large-scale applications. However, as the demand for better pre-
015            diction performance increases, existing sampling algorithms become more com-
016            plex, introducing significant overhead in the training process. To address this
017            issue, we introduce YOSO (You-Only-Sample-Once), an algorithm designed to
018
               achieve highly efficient training while preserving prediction accuracy in down-
               stream tasks. YOSO proposes a compressed sensing-based sampling and recon-
019
               struction framework, where nodes are sampled once at the input layer, followed by
020
               a lossless reconstruction at the output layer during each epoch. This approach not
021            only avoids costly computations, such as orthonormal basis, but also guarantees
022            high-probability accuracy retention, equivalent to full node participation. Exper-
023            imental results on both node classification and link prediction tasks demonstrate
024            the effectiveness and efficiency of YOSO, reducing GNN training by an average
025            of around 75% compared to state-of-the-art methods, while maintaining accuracy
026            on par with top-performing baselines.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017; Veličković et al.,
032   2017; Chen et al., 2018; Chiang et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2019) have become pivotal in modeling
033
      structured data across various domains, such as social network analysis (Guo & Wang, 2020), pro-
      tein interactions (Réau et al., 2023), and transportation systems (Liu et al., 2021a). As graphs rapidly
034
      grow, long training time in GNNs becomes a crucial factor impeding the wide utilization of GNNs.
035
      To mitigate the issue, various sampling strategies such as node-wise (Hamilton et al., 2017; Chen
036   et al., 2017), layer-wise (Chen et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2018), and subgraph-based
037   methods (Chiang et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2019) have been developed. These techniques facilitate
038   mini-batch training, which reduces the amount of memory required to sustain the training process
039   and potentially speeds up convergence. However, with the increasing complexity of sampling algo-
040   rithms, GNNs have struggled to maintain training efficiency in large-scale applications (Gong et al.,
041   2023) and large graph datasets (e.g., OGB (Hu et al., 2020) and IGB (Khatua et al., 2023)).
042   Theoretically, the challenge in sampling stems from the biases and variances introduced when al-
043   tering the data distribution during training (Huang et al., 2018). Unlike the inherently unbiased and
044   variance-free GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Huang et al., 2018) that utilize all training nodes, easily
045   computable sampling methods struggle to accurately estimate both graph structure and features or
046   embeddings (Jin et al., 2020), potentially degrading the learning outcomes. To achieve more precise
047   estimates, such as ensuring unbiasedness and variance reduction, subsequent methods have become
048   increasingly complex, focusing predominantly on reducing variance to improve accuracy but often
049   at the expense of increased computational demands. However, this contradicts the initial goal of
050
      sampling–to reduce computational load–and highlights a significant gap in current research: finding
      a method that achieves both high accuracy and efficiency. This gap is represented by the difference
051
      between the target goal (i.e., the seven-pointed red star) and other sampling schemes in Figure 1(a).
052

      ations for state-of-the-art (SOTA) sampling schemes with Reddit dataset (Hamilton et al., 2017)(de-

084   To address these inefficiencies and the identified research gap, we propose a novel approach, YOSO
085   (You-Only-Sample-Once), which innovatively applies compressed sensing (Candes & Tao, 2006)
086   (CS) to GNN sampling. YOSO reimagines the feature or embedding matrix as multi-channel signals,
087   utilizing adapted compressed sensing techniques to reduce the amount of computation involved in
088   the training by transferring the feature matrix to another domain with high sparsity. This approach
089   enables training with only a fraction M of nodes from a graph with N nodes (where M ≪ N ), and
090
      reconstructs the training effect as if all N nodes were used. The nearly lossless reconstruction feature
      of YOSO guarantees that model performance closely aligns with zero bias and variance. Moreover,
091
      the sampling process in YOSO is designed to occur only once at the beginning of the training. This
092
      involves determining the sampling set and sampling matrix based on the specific characteristics of
093   the graph. Subsequently, the reconstruction process takes place after each forward propagation.
094   This is done by utilizing the loss generated from the reconstructed embedding matrix to guide the
095   backward propagation. This innovative approach not only streamlines the entire training process
096   by eliminating the need for continuous resampling but also ensures that every step of learning is
097   informed by an optimally reconstructed data state, significantly enhancing both the efficiency and
098   efficacy of the model training. We summarize our contributions below.
099   • We proposed a novel approach named YOSO that significantly reduces GNN training time while
100     maintaining strong prediction accuracy across various downstream tasks by performing only-once
101     sampling for the entire training process.
102   • YOSO eliminates the need for expensive computations typically associated with combining com-
103     pressed sensing with GNN sampling (e.g., determining the orthonormal basis and sampling ma-
104     trix), thereby making the sampling process highly efficient.
105   • Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of YOSO on both node classification and link
106     prediction tasks. Specifically, YOSO significantly reduces overall training time by an average of
107
        around 75% while preserving model accuracy. Ablation studies further reveal that YOSO achieves
        near-zero bias and variance, effectively reconstructing the embedding matrix with minimal error.

110   2.1   S CHEMES FOR LARGE - SCALE GNN TRAINING
111
112   To address the efficiency issue of large-scale GNN training, different schemes have been proposed
113   at the algorithmic level (Zhang et al., 2023), such as Historical Embedding (Chen et al., 2017; Fey
114   et al., 2021), Linearization (Frasca et al., 2020; Abu-El-Haija et al., 2021), Graph Condensation &
115   Distillation (Zheng et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2022), and sampling-based methods. For the first three
116   schemes, please refer to Appendix A for a detailed description, where we discuss their differences
117
      and connections with sampling-based methods. The scope of this paper focuses on sampling-based
      methods.
118
119   A widely accepted criterion (Liu et al., 2021b) divides current different sampling methods into three
120   categories: node-wise sampling, layer-wise sampling, and subgraph-based sampling, depending on
121   the granularity of the sampling operation during mini-batch generation.
122   Node-wise Sampling: This fundamental approach, pioneered by works such as GraphSage (Hamil-
123
      ton et al., 2017) and others (Ying et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017; Dai et al., 2018), involves sampling
      at the individual node level. Each node’s neighbors are selected according to specific probabilities.
124
      For example, GraphSage samples k−hop neighbors at varying depths with the sampling sizes, for
125
      each depth tailored to optimize model performance. This approach, while simple and effective, has
126   been criticized for its exponential increase in sampling time complexity as the number of GNN lay-
127   ers grows.
128   Layer-wise Sampling: Developed to address the exponential growth in computational complexity
129   as GNNs depth increases in node-wise sampling, this method samples multiple nodes simultane-
130   ously in one step. Techniques like FastGCN (Chen et al., 2018) reframe GNN loss functions as
131   integral transformations and utilize importance sampling and Monte-Carlo approximation to man-
132   age variance. Further developments, such as AS-GCN (Huang et al., 2018) and LADIES (Zou et al.,
133   2019), focus on maintaining sparse connections between sampled nodes to aid convergence. How-
134
      ever, these methods tend to introduce additional complexity and computational cost.
      Subgraph-based Sampling: This approach forms mini-batch training subgraphs using expensive
135
      graph partitioning algorithms. Cluster-GCN (Chiang et al., 2019) partitions the full graph into clus-
136
      ters, sampling these clusters to create subgraphs for training batches. GraphSAINT (Zeng et al.,
137   2019) dynamically estimates sampling probabilities for nodes and edges to form subgraphs over
138   which the full GNN model is trained. While these techniques typically improve model accuracy,
139   they also lead to longer training time.

141   2.2   C OMPRESSED S ENSING
142
143   CS (Candes & Tao, 2005; 2006) is a framework that enables perfect recovery of data from a signif-
144   icant small number of measurements. Assuming a signal x ∈ RN can be sparsely represented as
145
      x = Ux̂, where x̂ ∈ RK is sparse, i.e., ∥x̂∥0 ≪ K. U ∈ RN ×K usually is a known transformation,
      the measurement process is modeled as y = Φx = ΦUx̂, with Φ ∈ RM ×N and M ≪ N . Φ is
146
      called sensing matrix (Candes & Tao, 2006) or sampling matrix (Anis et al., 2016). Subsequently,
147
      CS uses y ∈ RM instead of the original data x ∈ RN for processes, such as computation (Shi et al.,
148   2019) or network transmission (Haupt et al., 2008). Since M ≪ N , the cost of using y is signifi-
149   cantly lower than directly using x. After the process step is completed, the original data x needs to
150   be reconstructed from y. The Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) provides a necessary condition for
151   successfully recovering x from y, which guarantees that ΦU should preserve:
152                               (1 − δk )∥x̂∥22 ≤ ∥ΦUx̂∥22 ≤ (1 + δk )∥x̂∥22 ,
153   where δk ∈ (0, 1) is a constant. If the chosen Φ and U satisfy the RIP, then x̂ can be reconstructed
154   perfectly from y by solving an ℓ1 -minimization problem

236
       Obstacle I. Working with Unknown U and Universal Φ. Given the high computational cost of
237
       determining U(l) , we need to satisfy or approximate CS’s necessary and sufficient condition with-
238
       out explicitly knowing U(l) . Without U(l) , identifying the support and determining essential nodes
239
       for reconstruction becomes challenging, complicating the construction of Φ(l) . Since Φ(l) is layer-
240
       specific, calculating it for each layer is impractical. Thus, we require a method that works with an
241    unknown U using a universal sampling matrix Φ, ensuring Φ remains adaptable to any U while
242    satisfying compressed sensing conditions.
243    Obstacle II. Balancing computational efficiency with the need for accurate reconstruction. If
244    we sample once at the input layer and use these results throughout the GNN computation, followed
245    by reconstruction only at the output layer, this approach requires just one sampling and reconstruc-
246    tion step for the entire training process. Although it may introduce some accuracy loss due to
247    reduced intermediate layer information, it remains efficient if this loss is controllable with a known
248    upper bound, allowing a balance between computational efficiency and model accuracy.
249    We address the Obstacle I in Section 5.2 (Unknown U) and Section 5.3 (Univerisal Φ), respectively,

372   5.3   C ONSTRUCTION OF S AMPLING M ATRIX Φ
373
374   When the orthonormal basis U remains unspecified prior to training, a key challenge arises in com-
375   puting T(0) = ΦUX̂ in Equation (7), as the lack of knowledge about U complicates the design of
376   Φ. In traditional CS, U maps data into a sparse domain, where the support (i.e., the indices of non-
377   zero rows) is explicitly identifiable, and these non-zero rows contain the crucial information. This
      clarity allows Φ to be tailored to the supports. However, without prior knowledge of U, designing a


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Φ that effectively captures the essential information becomes significantly more difficult. Thus, the
379   core challenge lies in designing an robust and universal sampling matrix Φ that accurately captures
380   the key characteristics of the graph data while remaining compatible with any U without violating
381   the RIP.
382   To address this challenge, we propose an approach that integrates the design of a matrix Ŝ ∈ RM ×N ,
383   derived from the graph structure, with the construction of the sampling matrix Φ, i.e., Φ = Ŝ ⊗ Σ,
384   where Σ ∈ RM ×N is a random matrix and ⊗ is element-wise production. The matrix Ŝ is de-
385   termined once during the preprocessing phase and remains fixed throughout training. Its design
386   is based on graph-structure for two reasons: (1) the graph structure is invariant, and (2) it reflects
387   the importance of certain nodes, which is crucial for the GNN message-passing process. For the
388   sampling matrix Φ, maintaining a row full rank property is essential. Intuitively, Φ combines node
389   features or embeddings linearly, using weights corresponding to the indices of the support (non-zero
390
      rows). If Φ is row over-ranked, it introduces redundancy, if row under rank, it results in information
      loss. Thus, ensuring that Φ is crucial for effectively capturing the necessary information.
391
392
      Construction of Ŝ. Considering the normalized Laplacian matrix Â = I − D−1/2 AD−1/2 where
      D and A denote the degree matrix and adjacency matrix, respectively. The N nodes correspond to
393
394
      N eigenvalues from the spectral decomposition of Â, denoted as {λ1 , . . . , λN } with λi ≥ 0 for any
      i. These eigenvalues capture important structural properties of the graph, where larger eigenvalues
395
      correspond to more influential nodes. To construct the sampling probability distribution, we define
396
      P (i) = PNλi λ , assigning node i a sampling probability proportional to its eigenvalue relative to
397              j=1   j

398   the total eigenvalue sum. Using this distribution, we sample M times to form the M rows of Ŝ. If
399   node i is sampled, the corresponding row in Ŝ includes the 1-hop neighbors of node i. Assume node
400   i has N (i) neighbors, each neighbor is randomly sampled with a probability of N1(i) . This construc-
401   tion ensures that Ŝ will not contain any all-zero rows, due to the self-loop added by the normalized
402   Laplacian. Consequently, the matrix Φ = ŜΣ is row full rank (detailed proof in Appendix D.2),
403   avoiding any row rank deficiency issues.
404   Construction of Σ. Randomness has been shown to play a critical role in achieving the RIP (Bara-
405   niuk et al., 2008). Therefore, we define Σ as a random matrix. Intuitively, in the absence of precise
406   support knowledge, we estimate the support by randomly sampling M nodes based on eigenvalue
407   weights. The matrix Σ must represent the contribution level of each node i to the non-zero rows
408   (the support). For instance, if node k contributes to both nodes i and j, Σ should quantify k’s con-
409   tribution to each. This is crucial for ensuring accurate reconstruction and satisfying the RIP. For
410   any column j in Ŝ, assume it contains g(j) non-zero elements. The corresponding elements in Σ
                                                                                 1
411   are assigned random values drawn from a Gaussian distribution N (0, g(j)     ). This design effectively
412   captures contribution levels, ensuring compliance with the Restricted Isometry Property.(detailed
413   proof in Appendix D.3).

588
      In this paper, we introduce YOSO (You Only Sample Once), a novel algorithm aimed at significantly
      enhancing the efficiency of GNN training without sacrificing prediction accuracy. By leveraging a
589
      compressed sensing-based sampling and reconstruction framework, YOSO performs node sampling
590
      only once at the input layer, followed by a lossless reconstruction at the output layer during each
591   training epoch. Our experimental results demonstrate that YOSO can achieve up to 75% reduction of
592   existing state-of-the-art methods while achieving accuracy comparable to top-performing baselines.

      A    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS AND D ISCUSSION
757
758
      Graph Condensation&Distillation: Graph Condensation Gao et al. (2024) and Graph Distilla-
759
      tion Tian et al. (2023) are methods designed to enhance computational efficiency. They achieve this
760   by shrinking large-scale graphs into smaller ones while preserving essential structural and feature
761   information. Alternatively, they replace complex GNN models with approximate and computation-
762   ally simpler models, such as MLPs (Ramchoun et al., 2016). However, these kinds of processes
763   introduce additional computational overhead and may result in the loss of important information,
764   potentially leading to a decrease in model performance. For example, GCond (Jin et al., 2021)
765   leverages a gradient matching framework to condense large graphs into significantly smaller syn-
766   thetic graphs. It optimizes node features as free parameters and models synthetic graph structures as
767   functions of these features, ensuring that training trajectories on the condensed graph mimic those
768
      on the original graph. Another work, GC-SNTK Wang et al. (2024), reformulates graph condensa-
      tion as a Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR) task, replacing computationally intensive GNN training
769
      with a Structure-based Neural Tangent Kernel (SNTK). This approach captures both node feature
770
      interactions and structural relationships, enabling efficient graph condensation while maintaining
771   strong generalization across GNN architectures.
772   Historical Embedding. This class of methods is not independent of sampling. Instead, they are of-
773   ten integrated with existing sampling strategies to improve specific aspects of sampling performance,
774   such as estimated variance (Chen et al., 2017), or expressiveness (Fey et al., 2021). For example,
775   VR-GCN (Chen et al., 2017) utilizes historical embeddings within node-wise sampling. GNNAu-
776   toScale (Fey et al., 2021) incorporates the concept of historical embeddings within subgraph-based
777   sampling. Although historical embedding can be effective in terms of accuracy, it often comes with
778   high computational complexity.
779
      Linearization. This stream of works (Abu-El-Haija et al., 2021; Frasca et al., 2020) aims to simplify
      the training and inference processes by removing the nonlinear components (e.g., activation func-
780
      tions or deep iterative propagation) inherent in traditional GNN models. This simplification achieves
781
      computational efficiency while preserving essential graph structure and feature information through
782   linear transformations, i.e., SIGN Frasca et al. (2020) or precomputations, i.e., iSVD (Abu-El-Haija
783   et al., 2021). Linearization techniques often involve precomputing graph-based transformations
784   (e.g., matrix products or embeddings) and applying efficient optimization methods (e.g., truncated
785   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) or matrix factorization) to enable scalable training, particu-
786   larly for large graphs.
