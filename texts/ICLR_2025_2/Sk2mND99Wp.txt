009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Iterative optimization-based methods have dominated the field of stereo matching
012
               with extraordinary precision and speed. However, these methods still suffer from
013            low iteration efficiency and insufficient correlation volume with low utilization
014            rates. As the countermeasure, we propose grouped correlation aggregation with
015            propagation, aka., GCAP- STEREO, a novel stereo matching method inspired by
016            traditional methods. We design an efficient updater to improve the performance
017            of single iteration optimization. To alleviate the problems of correlation volume, a
018            novel grouped window shifting mechanism and a contour-aware aggregation mod-
019            ified from semi-global matching (SGM) have been introduced. Our method out-
020            performs all methods in zero-shot generalization and ranks 1st on ETH3D among
021
               published works. Additionally, we conducted targeted inference optimization on
               the video stream and demonstrated the improvement in frame rate without sacrific-
022
               ing accuracy through experiments on the simulator. Finally, a real-world binocular
023
               system is deployed to qualitatively demonstrate the practicality of our method.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Stereo matching is a vital task in computer vision that has numerous practical fields such as 3D
030   reconstruction, autonomous driving, and AR/VR (Jamiy & Marsh, 2019; Fan et al., 2018). It aims to
031   obtain the pixel-level matching relationships between two images captured by the calibrated binoc-
032   ular system, namely the disparity.
033   Many popular traditional methods have demonstrated significant effectiveness in both theory and
034   practice. Semi-Glocal Matching (SGM) (Hirschmüller, 2005) uses mutual information to evaluate
035   matching cost, and approximates a global two-dimensional smoothing constraint by aggregating
036   one-dimensional constraints. PatchMatch Stereo (Bleyer et al., 2011) randomly initializes disparity,
037   then propagates and optimizes disparity between pixels, gradually obtaining a high-quality disparity
038   map. However, due to the lack of parallelism and insufficient perception of image information, these
039   methods cannot meet the real-time and accuracy requirements in practical scenarios.
040   With the development of deep neural networks, learning-based methods have demonstrated abso-
041   lute advantages in the field. CNN-based methods such as PSMNet (Chang & Chen, 2018) use an
042   amount of convolutions to complete information extraction and the matching cost calculation. These
043   methods has improved computational efficiency and accuracy, but they still cannot meet the require-
044   ments for high pixel-level tasks due to the high memory and computing power demands. Moreover,
045   the iterative optimization-based method adopts a storage instead of computation approach to store
046   the matching cost volumes between all pixels of images and uses lightweight convolutional recur-
047
      rent neural network (convRNN) units for iterative updates. It can balance accuracy and time by
      dynamically setting the number of iterations, and the memory overhead is also relatively small.
048
049   However, there are still some points to consider in optimization-based methods. Firstly, a single
050   iteration update is not efficient enough and the methods require a sufficient number of iterations to
051   achieve the required accuracy. Secondly, the matching cost volume only considers the matching
052   cost between single pixels, which leads to frequent occurrences of noise points and matching errors.
053
      Finally, the methods store the matching relationships of all pixels, but a large part of the relationships
      are not accessed at all and occupy memory.

      When deep neural networks were first used in the field of stereo matching, they demonstrated sig-

143

144

145   ture extraction on each image, calculates the correlation, and then performs multi-layer convolution
146   operations. Subsequently, deep neural networks were incorporated into other traditional algorithm

148   tion phase. AAnet (Xu & Zhang, 2020) introduces deformable convolution in calculating matching
149   cost and cost aggregation. Stereonet (Khamis et al., 2018) uses multi-stage hierarchical refinement
150   from coarse to fine, making the network more lightweight while maintaining good accuracy. Hit-

152   disparity to have two gradients and achieves sub-pixel level accuracy. DeepPruner (Duggal et al.,
153

154

159   based on this approach emerged and continued to optimize the accuracy. DLNR (Zhao et al., 2023)
160   holds detailed information in feature maps using a decoupled Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
161   and achieves remarkable performance. CREStereo (Li et al., 2022) designs a coarse-to-fine network
      and a special stacked cascaded architecture for inference to improve accuracy. IGEV-Stereo (Xu

      3.2   G ROUP W INDOW S HIFTING
217
218   Although the APC designed by RAFT-Stereo (Lipson et al., 2021) contains amounts of information,
219   many matching relationships will never be used in iterative updates. As shown in fig. 4, for an APC∈
220   RB×H×W ×W , only about 10%−30% of the matching relationships are potential candidate matching
221   points while other matching relationships may potentially affect the accuracy of the method and
222   occupy unnecessary memory. The grouped window shifting mechanism (GWS) is illustrated in
223   the fig. 5. Specifically, for an APC, we set a designed window size ws and group size L that can
224   contain all potential matching relationships and discard the vast majority of useless information.
225
      The shifting mechanism can be formulated as:
226
                                           ∆s = (W − ws)L/((W − D))                                      (1)
227
228   where W is the width of image and D is the preset max disparity. For each group which contains L
229   rows, we select ws elements to be candidate points. After selecting each group, the selected window
230   will be shifted by ∆s pixels until it reaches the margin. Note that ws will be set to a value larger
231   than d (usually 1.5 or 2 times) to ensure that potential candidates will not lost.

282
283   For previous RAFT-Stereo based work, the procedure on multiple iterations to optimize disparity
284   can be summarized as the following formula,
285                                      ∆f, hi+1 = R(f ; cor; hi )                               (6)
286                                                 ′

287                                                  f = f + ∆f                                            (7)
288   where f is the disparity of the current state, corr is the correlation matrix obtained based on its
289   searching in the correlation pyramids, and hi is the current hidden layer. Based on these parame-
290   ters, a new hidden layer hi+1 and the corresponding increment of flow will be output through the
291
      RNN network R(·; ·; ·). By continuously changing the flow and generating new hidden layers, the
      disparity will be iteratively optimized. As discussed above, we consider that such a single iteration
292
      is not efficient, mainly due to the selection of searching points. Therefore, inspired by PatchMatch
293
      Stereo (Bleyer et al., 2011), we have introduced a new iterative update method called propagation
294   updater (P-Updater). As illustrated in fig. 6, if the orange area is the valid candidate point area, for
295   each point, RAFT updater (Zhang et al., 2024) will select several points on both sides, namely the
296   green area, for searching. This not only lacks perception of image information, but also makes it
297   easier to obtain many invalid candidate points. Contrarily, the propagation updater directly prop-
298   agates with neighbors, searching within a small range of these neighboring points, which can be
299   more targeted and reduce the possibility of selecting invalid candidate points. Considering that mul-
300   tiple iterations of a single level can easily trap the updater in local optima, we adopted a multi-level
301   update approach, where each scale is swapped with different neighbors and two types of updaters
302
      are utilized alternately for updates. Finally, the first two low levels updaters use standard bilin-
      ear interpolation for upsampling, while the final 1/4 resolution employs the convex combination in
303
      RAFT-Stereo (Lipson et al., 2021).

      a lighter coarse-grained updater, the accuracy did not decrease but instead increased. This is because
408
      the propagation method at multi-level are different, and compared to a single-level fixed propagation
409
      method, the receptive field is larger, which can make it easier for the final result to be out of local
410   optima and achieve better results.
411
412   Effectiveness of CA and GWS. As shown in the table 2, by approximating the SGM equation and
413   using deformable convolution for calculation, the accuracy can be further improved with little cost.
414   This is because the CA is not limited to small-area neighbor convolutions, but rather extends as
415   much as possible around the object contour boundary like CenterNet (Zhou et al., 2019), resulting
416   in more abundant information obtained through aggregation. Furthermore, GWS can greatly reduce
417
      the volume of the correlation, thereby reducing the computational cost brought by CA and make the
      searching area more concentrated and targeted. The combination of the two makes our method more
418
      effective.
419
420   Effectiveness of single optimization. Our method can still perform well at a low number of itera-
421   tions. As shown in table 2, we report the bad 1.0 score with different iterations. In the case of only 8
422   iterations, the accuracy of RAFT-Stereo will sharply decrease, but our method can still maintain sta-
423   bility, indicating that our single iteration is more efficient and robust. Moreover,in just 8 iterations,
424   even with the addition of only one single-level P-Updater to our method, it has already surpassed
425   RAFT-Stereo, which has undergone 32 iterations.

531
      To solve the common problems in iterative networks for stereo matching, we propose Grouped
      Correlation Aggregation with PropagationI (GCAP-Stereo), a new solution for stereo matching. The
532
      efficiency of single iteration optimization has been improved by introducing a new propagation-
533
      based updater. Through improving traditional algorithms, targeted modifications have been made
534   to the correlation volume to make it more robust and accurate. Finally, targeted optimization was
535   carried out on the inference of the video stream. GCAP-Stereo ranks 1st on ETH3D two-view stereo
536   benchmarks and achieves competitive performance on KITTI 2012/2015 and Middlebury among
537   published methods. Moreover, our method demonstrates excellent performance advantages in video
538   stream testing and zero-shot generalization, which has superior cross-domain generalization and
539   real-time performance. We believe our work will be an important technique empowering the high-
      precision binocular vision system.
