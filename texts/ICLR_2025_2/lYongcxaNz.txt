                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            The intriguing in-context learning (ICL) abilities of deep Transformer models
013            have lately garnered significant attention. By studying in-context linear regression
014
               on unimodal Gaussian data, recent empirical and theoretical works have argued
               that ICL emerges from Transformers’ abilities to simulate learning algorithms like
015
               gradient descent. However, these works fail to capture the remarkable ability of
016
               Transformers to learn multiple tasks in context. To this end, we study in-context
017            learning for linear regression with diverse tasks, characterized by data covariance
018            matrices with condition numbers ranging from [1, κ], and highlight the importance
019            of depth in √
                           this setting. More specifically, (a) we show theoretical lower bounds of
020            log(κ) (or κ) linear attention layers in the unrestricted (or restricted) attention
021            setting and, (b) we show that multilayer Transformers can indeed solve such tasks
022            with a number of layers that matches the lower bounds. However, we show that
023            this expressivity of multilayer Transformer comes at the price of robustness. In
024            particular, multilayer Transformers are not robust to even distributional shifts as
025
               small as O(e−L ) in Wasserstein distance, where L is the depth of the network. We
               then demonstrate that Looped Transformers —a special class of multilayer Trans-
026
               formers with weight-sharing— not only exhibit similar expressive power but are
027
               also provably robust under mild assumptions. Besides out-of-distribution general-
028            ization, we also show that Looped Transformers are the only models that exhibit
029            a monotonic behavior of loss with respect to depth.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
035
      Transformer-based language models (Vaswani, 2017) have demonstrated remarkable in-context
      learning abilities (Brown et al., 2020), thereby bypassing the need to fine-tune them for specific
036
      tasks. This is a desirable feature for large models because fine-tuning them is often expensive. In
037
      particular, given just a few samples of a new learning task presented in the context, the model is able
038   to meta-learn the task and generate accurate predictions without having to update its parameters.
039   In an attempt to study this phenomenon, recent work has shown that Transformers can demonstrate
040   such in-context learning abilities on a variety of learning tasks such as linear or logistic regression,
041   decision trees (von Oswald et al.; Garg et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2021) and, perhaps surprisingly, even
042   training Transformers themselves (Panigrahi et al., 2023). This has sparked significant interest to
043   theoretically understand the in-context learning phenomenon. From a more theoretical perspective,
044   there are two crucial aspects to this in-context learning ability: (1) the Transformer architecture is
045   powerful enough to implement iterative algorithms, including first order optimization methods such
046
      as gradient or preconditioned gradient descent (Li et al., 2024; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Akyürek
      et al., 2022), and (2) Transformers with appropriate optimization algorithm can indeed learn to
047
      simulate such algorithms (Ahn et al., 2024a; Gatmiry et al., 2024). These results provide further
048
      insights into how in-context learning could arise within Transformer models. Recent papers (e.g
049   (Ahn et al., 2024a; Gatmiry et al., 2024)) have studied both these aspects for Transformers on a
050   single task — linear regression setting where the data is sampled i.i.d Gaussian.
051
052   One of the mysterious features of recent foundation models, which separates them from traditional
053   learning systems, is their ability to handle a spectrum of tasks, from question answering within a
      broad range of topics, to reasoning or in-context learning for various learning setups. The emer-

054
      gence of this ability to handle task diversity naturally necessitates the training data to incorporate a
055   diverse datasets, which inherently makes the training distribution highly multimodal. Consequently,
056   foundation models need more capacity to be able to solve such diverse tasks. However, recent work
057   on in-context learning theory focus on unimodal Gaussian data distribution. In such settings, even
058   constant number of layers suffice to achieve any arbitrarily small error. Thus, while interesting,
059   these works fail to capture the remarkable ability of Transformers to learn multiple tasks in context
060   and explicate the role of depth in these settings.
061
062
      To this end, in this paper we introduce a new in-context learning setting, task-diverse linear re-
      gression, which specifically examines how well Transformers can in-context learn linear regression
063
      problems arising from fairly diverse data. More specifically, in task-diverse linear regression, the
064
      data can be sampled from multiple Gaussians where the eigenvalues of its covariance matrix can
065   range from [1, κ]. For this setting, we ask the following important question:
066
067   For multilayer Transformer, what is the role of depth in task-diverse linear regression? Is it possible
068   to establish upper and lower bounds that depend on task diversity?
069
070   In this paper, we resolve this problem by showing lower bound of log κ and matching upper bounds
071
      — indicating a fundamental limit on the depth required to solve the task-diverse linear regression
      setting. Furthermore, in the special
                                      √ case of attention layers considered in (Von Oswald et al., 2023),
072
      we obtain a lower bound of Ω( κ) instead. Correspondingly, we also show a matching upper bound
073
      in this special setting, thereby; providing a comprehensive picture of the representation power of
074   multilayer Transformers in various important settings with task diversity.
075
076   While the above results paint a powerful picture of multilayer Transformers, it remains unclear
077   if this comes at any cost. In this paper, we show that this is indeed the case and the price we
078   pay is robustness. First, diverging significantly from prior works, we study the problem of out-of-
079   distribution generalization on a very general class of distributions (see 2). Under this general class of
080   distributions, we show that the out-of-distribution generalization of multilayer Transformer can get
081
      exponentially worse with depth. Thus, the strong representation power of multilayer Transformers
      indeed comes at the price of robustness. This begs an important question: is it possible to achieve
082
      both robustness and expressivity at the same time?
083
084   Recently, there has been interest in the special class of Transformers, called Looped Transformers,
085   which have empirically shown to have much more robust out-of-distribution generalization (Yang
086   et al., 2023). Looped Transformers are essentially multilayer Transformers that share weights across
087   layers. It is natural to ask if Looped Transformers exhibit similar representation-robustness tradeoff
088   as multilayer Transformers. Perhaps surprisingly, we provide a negative answer to this question.
089   In particular, we show that Looped Transformers can match the lower bound of log(κ) on the rep-
090   resentative power, similar to multilayer Transformers. However, this does not come at the cost of
091
      robustness. In fact, Looped Transformers can achieve very good out-of-distribution generalization.
      In light of this discussion, we state the following main contributions of our paper.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
118
119
120
      Transformers in Task Diversity. Raventós et al. (2024) observe a connection when the level of task
      diversity in the pretraining distribution is above a threshold, and the emergence of strong in-context
121
      learning abilities for linear regression, by switching from a Bayesian estimator to ridge regression.
122
      Garg et al. (2022); Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023) observe that Transformers can
123   extrapolate to new test distributions for in-context learning. Min et al. (2022) discover surprising
124   latent task inference capabilities in language models, even when using random labels for in-context
125   demonstrations. Kirsch et al. (2022) empirically show the benefits of task diversity for in-context
126   learning in classification tasks. Chan et al. (2022) investigate which aspects of the pretraining dis-
127   tribution enhance in-context learning and find that a more bursty distributions proves to be more
128   effective than distributions that are closer to uniform. The impact of task diversity has also been
129   studied in the context of meta learning (Yin et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2023; Tripuraneni et al.,
130   2021) or transfer learning and fine-tuning (Raffel et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2020). In the current work,
131
      we use task diversity to study the expressivity of Transformers and the role of depth.
132
      Transformers and Looping as Computational Models. Transformers are known to be effec-
133   tive computational models and Turing-complete (Pérez et al., 2019; 2021; Giannou et al., 2023).
134   Garg et al. (2022); Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2022) argue that
135   Transformers can implement optimization algorithms like gradient descent by internally forming
136   an appropriate loss function. Allen-Zhu and Li (2023) observe that Transformers can generate sen-
137   tences in Context-free Grammers (CFGs) by utilizing dynamic programming. Other work focuses on
138   how training algorithms for Transformers can recover weights that implement iterative algorithms,
139   such as gradient descent, for in-context learning, assuming simplified distributions for in-context
140   instances Ahn et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2024); Mahankali et al. (2023). Notably, while most
141
      of these studies focus on the single-layer case, recent work by the authors in Gatmiry et al. (2024)
      provides a global convergence result for training deep Looped Transformers.

      2.2   TASK D IVERSITY & ROBUSTNESS
163
164
      To handle task diversity, we need a model that not only makes accurate predictions for a fixed
165
      distribution, but is also robust to a class of distributions. Here, we focus on the notion of robustness
166   with respect to various distributions on the covariance matrix Σ of the linear regression instance. It is
167   unreasonable to expect the model to behave well for all possible distributions, so we focus on the set
168   of instances where the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix are in the range (α, β) for 0 < α < β.
169   Definition 1. We define the set Sα,β of (α, β) “normal” linear regression instances as the set in-
170
      stances with a well-conditioned covariance matrix X ⊤ X:
171
                                  Sα,β = (X, w∗ , y, xq ) : αI ≼ XX ⊤ ≼ βI .

172
173
174   In order for the model to handle task diversity on the set of instances in Sα,β , the loss needs to be
175   small for any distribution Pα,β that is supported on covariances αI ≼ Σ ≼ βI, which means the
176   model has to be accurate for all the instances in Sα,β . In this work, we are interested in understand-
177   ing the ability of Transformers to handle task diversity from two angles: (1) expressiveness of the
178   Transformer architecture is in representing task-diverse models (Section 3) and (2) robustness of
179
      these models in terms of out-of-distribution generalization. We first formally define the models used
      in this paper before exploring these questions.

      In the previous section, we studied expressivity of multilayer Transformers for task-diverse linear
      regression. In Section 4.1, we discuss how even though Looped Transformers are more restricted
332
      compared to multilayer Transformers, their expressive power is just as good for in-context linear
333
      regression. While expressivity is important for task-diversity of our model, we also want the model
334   to be robust to distribution shifts from the training distribution. In Section 4.2, we show that mul-
335   tilayer Transformers are not very robust to even very tiny perturbations to the training distribution.
336   In contrast, Looped Transformers are provably more robust. Finally, motivated by the fact that early
337   stopping is a desirable property for neural nets, in Section 4.3 we study the monotonicity behavior
338   of multilayer Transformers with respect to depth and interestingly find that they cannot behave
339   monotonic with respect to all covariance matrices unless the weights of different layers are equal.

459   4.3   N ON - MONOTONICITY OF THE LOSS IN M ULTILAYER T RANSFORMER
460
461
      In Section 3.3, we discussed how one can adaptively pick a depth based on problem difficulty. A
462
      related idea is that of early-exiting (Teerapittayanon et al., 2016), where the goal is to exit the model
463
      at an earlier layer for easier example. This naturally provides inference efficiency. An important
464   consideration in the early exit literature is monotonicity with respect to layers, i.e., the model’s
465   error decreases as the layer index increases. This has been studied in detail (Baldock et al., 2021;
466   Laitenberger et al.) and there is also work on enforcing such monotonicity (Schuster et al., 2022;
467   Jazbec et al., 2024). In the linear regression incontext learning setting considered in this paper,
468   we make an intriguing observation: if a multilayer model has monotonically decreasing error with
469   depth for a diverse set of distributions, then it must be a looped model. This suggests that looped
470   models are naturally suited for early-exiting strategies. We believe this phenomenon deserves further
471   exploration in future work. The following theorem formalizes this idea.
472

504
505
506
      In this section, we run experiments on the in-context learning linear regression problem to validate
507
      the theoretical results. In particular, we would like to demonstrate: (1) role of depth (in both
508   multilayer and Looped Transformers) with increasing task diversity and (2) robustness of looped
509   Transformer for out-of-domain generalization. We use the codebase and experimental setup from
510   (Ahn et al., 2024a) for all our linear regression experiments. In particular, we work with d = 10
511   dimensional inputs and each input instance consists of n = 20 pairs of (x, y) in the context. We
512   train with L attention layer models for multilayer training and 1 layer attention model looped L
513   times, as described in Section 2.3. For simplicity we use the restricted linear attention setting. In all
514   experiments, we follow the same data distribution as described in Section 4.2. For all experiments,
515   the covariances are sampled from training and test distributions as follows: train distribution is
516
      Σ = sId , s ∼ Dtrain and test distribution: Σ = sId , s ∼ Dtest . In each experimental setting we will
      specify Dtrain and Dtest .
517
518   Role of Depth. We demonstrate the importance of depth in presence of task diversity. Consider the
519   following train and test distributions Dtrain = Dtest = Unif([1, κ]). Task diversity is controlled by
520   varying κ ∈ [2, 4, 8], with higher value corresponding to more diverse tasks. In Figure 1, we see that
521   that more diverse tasks (larger κ) require more layers (or loops) in order to achieve low loss. This
522   aligns with the theoretical results from Section 3.1. Furthermore, we find that looped model with L
523   loops can be very competitive to multilayer model with L layers even in the most diverse setting.
524
525
      Out-of-distribution generalization. For this set of experiments, we train models using a distribu-
      tion comprising of k covariances. The precise form the train and test distributions are:
526
527
528    • Train distribution: Dtrain = Unif({s1 , . . . , sk }) where Sk = {s1 , . . . , sk } is selected to be k
529      values uniformly spread out in the range [1, 8].
530
531    • Test distribution: Dtrain = Unif(∪ki=1 [si − w, si + w]) for some deviation w ∈ R.
532
533
534
      Note that the train distribution is a mixture of k tasks, whereas the test distribution has a slight de-
      viation using a window size of w around the training covariances. For evaluations, w is varied from
535
      0, corresponding to in-distribution evaluation, to a max value of 0.5. In Figure 2, we plot the robust-
536
      ness of multilayer and looped model for different training distributions corresponding to k = 2, 5
537   and 9. Consistent with our theory in Section 4.2, we observe that looped Transformers have much
538   better out-of-distribution generalization compared to multilayer Transformers. This also aligns with
539   the empirical results from Yang et al. (2023) on various incontext learning problems. Furthermore,
      as the number of train covariances increase, the looped model becomes perfectly robust.

      6   C ONCLUSION
541
542   In this paper, we study a more realistic in-context setting than prior works — task-diverse linear
543   regression, which we believe is more reflective of the incontext abilities of Transformer based foun-
544   dation models. For this setting, we provide lower bounds on the depth and show Transformers match
545   these bounds; thereby, providing a comprehensive picture about number of layers required to solve
546   the problem. While multilayer Transformers demonstrate this powerful representation power, we
547   show they have weak out-of-distribution generalization, which gets exponentially worse with re-
548   spect to depth. Surprisingly, we show the Looped Transformers exhibit similar representative power
549   with much better robustness. We validate our theoretical findings through experiments on linear
550
      regression setting. Finally, we touch upon aspects like adaptive depth and monotonicity of loss in
      layers, and we believe that these are very interesting future directions to pursue to understand the
551
      power of looped models. While the incontext linear regression setting is quite simplified, it already
552
      provides a lot of interesting insights in the role of depth and task diversity. Extending this study to
553   other settings, like reasoning, is a very interesting future direction.

      A    R ELATED WORK
703
704
705   Transformers in Task Diversity. Raventós et al. (2024) empirically study the relationship between
706
      task diversity in the pretraining distribution and the emergence of in-context learning abilities for
      linear regression. They observe an intriguing phase transition, where as task diversity increases, the
707
      learned model shifts from acting as a Bayesian estimator to implementing ridge regression. Garg
708
      et al. (2022) investigate in-context learning across various tasks, where both the inputs and the link
709   function for the in-context instances are sampled from an underlying distribution. For in-context
710   linear regression, Garg et al. (2022); Akyürek et al. (2022); Von Oswald et al. (2023) further observe
711   that transformers are capable of extrapolating to new test distributions, both in terms of the inputs
712   and the regressor. Min et al. (2022) discover surprising latent task inference capabilities in language
713   models; even when using random labels for in-context demonstrations (i.e., in the test distribution),
714   the model can still produce accurate predictions, provided the input distribution in the test instances
715   is similar to that of the pretraining data. The impact of task diversity has also been studied in the
716   context of meta learning Yin et al. (2019); Kumar et al. (2023); Tripuraneni et al. (2021). Kirsch et al.
717
      (2022) empirically demonstrate the benefits of task diversity for in-context learning in classification
      tasks. Chan et al. (2022) investigate which aspects of the pretraining distribution enhance in-context
718
