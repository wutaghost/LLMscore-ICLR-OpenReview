009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) has emerged as a promising technique in SLAM
012            due to its rapid and high-quality rendering capabilities. However, its reliance on
013            discrete Gaussian ellipsoid primitives limits its effectiveness in capturing essen-
014            tial geometric features crucial for accurate pose estimation. To overcome this
015            limitation, we propose a novel dense RGB-D SLAM system that integrates an
016
               implicit Truncated Signed Distance Function (TSDF) hash grid to constrain the
               distribution of Gaussian ellipsoids. This innovative approach enables precise es-
017
               timation of the scene’s geometric structure by smoothing the discrete Gaussian
018
               ellipsoids and anchoring them to the scene’s surface. Acting as a low-pass filter,
019            the implicit TSDF hash grid mitigates the inductive biases inherent in traditional
020            3DGS methods while preserving rendering quality. Our geometrically constrained
021            map also significantly enhances generalization capabilities for depth estimation in
022            novel views. Extensive experiments on the Replica, ScanNet, and TUM datasets
023            demonstrate that our system achieves state-of-the-art tracking and mapping accu-
024            racy at speeds up to 30 times faster than existing 3DGS-based systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Visual Simultaneous Localization and Mapping SLAM (VSLAM), which parallels human visual
029
      perception, has garnered significant attention within the research community. Although traditional
030   VSLAM systems (Mur-Artal et al., 2015; Mur-Artal & Tardós, 2017; Qin et al., 2018; Campos
031   et al., 2021) achieve high tracking accuracy, their map representations often fall short for down-
032   stream tasks. Recently, various VSLAM systems have adopted Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) by
033   (Mildenhall et al., 2020) or 3DGS by (Kerbl et al., 2023), both based on differentiable rendering, as
034   mapping solutions due to their high-quality rendering capabilities. Compared to NeRF, 3DGS offers
035   faster rendering speeds and higher-quality rendering results, making it more suitable for real-time
036   applications. Recent studies have paved the way for integrating 3DGS into VSLAM.
037   A key challenge in previous works (Matsuki et al., 2024; Keetha et al., 2024) is utilizing Gaussian
038   ellipsoids to accurately represent the geometric structure of the scene, which significantly influences
039   the accuracy of pose estimation. Although discrete Gaussian ellipsoids provide high rendering qual-
040   ity, their discrete nature leads to poor representation of scene geometric structures, as 3D recon-
041   struction from multi-views is an underconstrained problem (Barron et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024).
042   Therefore, the continuous, implicit representation of NeRF provides a potential solution to solve the
043   inaccurate geometric representation of 3DGS.
044   Another challenge stems from the increasing number of Gaussian ellipsoids required as the scene
045   expands, complicating their management and optimization(Deng et al., 2024). Since rendering an
046   image requires only a subset of the total Gaussian ellipsoids, this highlights the importance of uti-
047   lizing a submap of ellipsoids. Moreover, the strategy for densifying Gaussian ellipsoids is critical to
048   system performance (Chen & Wang, 2024). Adding too many ellipsoids increases the computational
049   burden while adding too few results in an inadequate scene representation.
050   To address these challenges, we propose a novel system that combines the strengths of implicit and
051   explicit representations. Our approach optimizes an implicit multi-resolution hash encoding (Müller
052   et al., 2022) to predict TSDF (Azinović et al., 2022) values, which are then converted into opacities
053   for each Gaussian ellipsoid. This hybrid mapping imposes geometric constraints on the unstructured
      Gaussian ellipsoids, enhancing the learning and generalization of scene geometric structures while

097   Here, we briefly introduce representative VSLAM systems. For a more detailed review, please refer
098   to traditional SLAM surveys (Cadena et al., 2016; Macario Barros et al., 2022) and differentiable

101
      or density of their map reconstructions. Sparse reconstruction systems (Davison et al., 2007; Mur-
102   Artal et al., 2015; Engel et al., 2017; Mur-Artal & Tardós, 2017; Campos et al., 2021) functioned
103   at higher speeds and primarily focused on camera tracking. However, the maps these systems pro-
104   duced often needed more detail for recognition tasks or other downstream applications due to their
105   sparse nature. On the other hand, dense mapping VSLAM systems, while incurring higher compu-
106   tational costs due to dense reconstructions, have gained popularity in recent years for applications
107   in Augmented Reality (AR) and robotics, where detailed environmental representation is essential.
      KinectFusion (Newcombe et al., 2011a), a real-time RGB-D SLAM algorithm for 3D reconstruction

140   NeRF-based SLAM. NeRF-based SLAM systems (Zhu et al., 2023a; Deng et al., 2023) represent a
141
      class of learning-based SLAM methods that utilize implicit map representations through volumetric
      rendering. One way to classify NeRF-based systems is by the method for pose estimation. Besides
142
      systems (Sucar et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; 2024a) leveraging volumetric render-
143
      ing to directly optimize camera poses, others (Kong et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023; Rosinol et al.,
144   2023; Zhang et al., 2023) incorporated traditional SLAM tracking modules to enhance performance.
145   Another way to classify NeRF-based SLAM systems is by their map representations. Beyond the
146   original NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Sucar et al., 2021), various structures have been explored,
147   including Multi-MLP (Kong et al., 2023), Voxel Grid (Zhu et al., 2022; 2023b), Octree (Yang et al.,
148   2022), Triplane (Chan et al., 2022; Johari et al., 2023), Hash Grid (Müller et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
149   2023; Li et al., 2024a), and Neural Point Cloud (Sandström et al., 2023; Liso et al., 2024). Despite
150   the rapid advancements in NeRF-based methods, their reliance on implicit map representations im-
151   poses limitations on rendering and training speeds.
152   Among NeRF-based SLAM systems, the early pioneers, iMAP (Sucar et al., 2021) and NICE-
153   SLAM (Zhu et al., 2022) introduced NeRF into SLAM using MLP and Voxel Grid, respectively.
154   Building on this, Co-SLAM (Wang et al., 2023) and ESLAM (Johari et al., 2023) adopted the In-
155   stantNGP (Müller et al., 2022) and Tri-plane (Chan et al., 2022), significantly improving both map-
156   ping and tracking accuracy, as well as speed. Recent systems like Go-SLAM (Zhang et al., 2023)
157   and Loopy-SLAM (Liso et al., 2024) incorporated loop closure techniques, demonstrating superior
158
      performance over extended image sequences. However, with the emergence of 3DGS based on ex-
      plicit Gaussian ellipsoids, the research focus of differentiable rendering-based SLAM has gradually
159
      shifted from NeRF to 3DGS, which enables fast and high-quality rendering.

      3.3.1    M APPING
307
308   We iteratively optimize our hybrid map and the poses of selected keyframes. Our mapping loss is
309   composed of the following components:
310                      Lmap = λc Lc + λd Ld + λd¯Ld¯ + λr Lr + λin Lin + λout Lout ,                    (9)
311
312   where λ(·) represents the weight for each loss and Ld¯ denotes the L1 loss of depth for implicit
313   rendering. In implicit rendering, we render the depths of selected pixel points by sampling pixels
314
      and spatial points as previously described and then calculate the implicit depth L1 loss.
315   Bundle adjustment. During mapping, we optimize the pose concurrently. We select the top Nk
316   most relevant frames from the previous keyframe pool based on the current keyframe’s pose, opti-
317   mizing both the map and the poses.
318   Gaussian ellipsoid initialization. We initiate gaussian ellipsoids according to two criteria. For
319   areas where the cumulative opacity is less than τo or where the depth significantly exceeds the
320   actual depth, we initialize the relevant gaussian ellipsoids at the depth location. Additionally, after
321   a certain number of iterations, we sample spatial points within a small random area and use our
322   implicit map to predict their TSDF. For every spatial point whose TSDF is less than τs , we search
323   its nearest gaussian ellipsoid and calculate the distance d. We initialize a new gaussian ellipsoid on
      the location of the spatial point with d larger than τd .

516   5     C ONCLUSION
517
518   We propose a 3DGS-based RGB-D SLAM system with a hybrid map representation. Our system
519   combines the strengths of both implicit and explicit map types—leveraging the continuous geo-
520   metric constraints from implicit maps alongside the rapid, high-quality rendering capabilities of
521   explicit maps—this results in state-of-the-art reconstruction and tracking accuracy and enhanced
522   generalization across novel views. Our refined strategies for processing gaussian ellipsoids, select-
523   ing keyframes, and BA significantly boost our SLAM system’s speed and accuracy. Extensive and
524   meticulous experiments corroborate the efficacy of our approach.
525   Our work utilizes complementary fusion methods between explicit discrete 3DGS and implicit con-
526   tinuous NeRF, exploring a new way for accurately representing scene geometric information. Look-
527   ing ahead, we aim to further enhance the scalability of our system for larger and more complex
528   environments by exploring advanced geometric constraints in expansive scenes. Additionally, we
529   plan to investigate the integration of efficient loop closure detection with 3DGS to improve the ro-
530   bustness and accuracy of SLAM systems, particularly in large-scale and dynamic environments.
531
      These future directions will ensure our approach remains at the SLAM research and applications
      forefront.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
757
758
759
      The experiments were conducted on a desktop platform with an Intel Core i7-12700 CPU and an
      NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. For the explicit map optimization, we computed the Jacobian of each
760
      Gaussian ellipsoid concerning the pose being optimized, leveraging the CUDA implementation from
761
      Gaussian Splatting SLAM (Matsuki et al., 2024). To enable joint optimization with the implicit
762   map, we manually implemented the Jacobian for the pose perturbations within the implicit map.
763   The learning rate for the Gaussian ellipsoid features was set to 0.0025, while the learning rates for
764   the scale and quaternions were set to 0.001. The learning rate for the position was determined as
765   0.000016 multiplied by the scene radius. For the implicit optimization, we used two-layer fully
766   connected networks with a hidden feature size of 32 as decoders for predicting the TSDF. The Hash
767   Grid level was set to 16 for the Replica dataset (Straub et al., 2019), with learning rates for the
768   decoder and grid set to 0.01. The optimization learning rates for rotational and translational poses
769   were set to 0.001. For the Replica dataset (Straub et al., 2019), we set Nu to 32 and Nd to 11. For
770
      the ScanNet dataset (Dai et al., 2017), Nu was set to 96 and Nd to 21. On the TUM dataset (Sturm
      et al., 2012), Nu was set to 64 and Nd to 21.
771
772   In the tracking thread for implicit maps, we use a constant velocity assumption to compute the
773   initial pose of the tracking frame. We iterate 15 times, sampling 2000 pixels in each iteration. In
774   the mapping thread, the number of points sampled on the rays corresponding to pixels varies across
775   different datasets. For the Replica (Straub et al., 2019) dataset and ScanNet(Dai et al., 2017) dataset,
776
      we sample 4000 pixels each time. For the TUM dataset, we sample 2000 pixels each time.
777   When calculating reconstruction metrics, we followed the methodology of SplaTAM (Keetha et al.,
778   2024), which involves computing relevant reconstruction metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, Depth L1)
779   every five frames. We refer to the metrics calculated in this manner as “Train View” metrics. Our
780   ablation studies calculate reconstruction metrics under “Novel View” conditions. We efficiently
781   generated depth maps from new poses using the Replica dataset’s ground truth mesh files. We
782
      selected 100 images from these new poses to test the depth estimation in Novel View settings. This
      demonstrated the continuity and generalizability of the implicit TSDF Hash Grid regarding depth
783
      estimation. When computing poses, we adhere to the methodology used by Co-SLAM (Wang et al.,
784
      2023). For non-keyframes, we estimate and store their relative poses to adjacent keyframes. We
785   store their estimated poses for keyframes and optimize them within the backend using joint Bundle
786   Adjustment (BA). In the final calculation of the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), we reconstruct
787   the poses of all non-keyframes based on the poses of keyframes and their relative poses. We then
788   calculate the final RMSE metric based on these reconstructed poses.
