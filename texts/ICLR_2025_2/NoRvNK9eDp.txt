009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            3D shape analysis has been largely focused on traditional 3D data of point clouds
012            and meshes, but the discrete nature of these data makes the analysis susceptible
013            to variations in input resolutions. Recent development of neural fields brings in
014            level-set parameters from signed distance functions as a novel, continuous, and
015            numerical representation of 3D shapes, where the shape surfaces are defined as
016            zero-level-sets of those functions. This motivates us to extend shape analysis
017
               from the traditional 3D data to these novel parameter data. Since the level-set
               parameters are not Euclidean like point clouds, we establish correlations across
018
               different shapes by formulating them as a pseudo-normal distribution, and learn
019
               the distribution prior from the respective dataset. To further explore the level-
020            set parameters with shape transformations, we propose to condition a subset of
021            these parameters on rotations and translations, and generate them with a hyper-
022            network. We demonstrate the potential of the novel continuous representation in
023            pose-related shape analysis through applications to shape classification, retrieval
024            under arbitrary poses, and 6D object pose estimation. Code and data in this re-
025            search are anonymously provided at this github link.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029   3D surfaces are traditionally represented as point clouds or meshes on digital devices for visualiza-
030   tion and geometry processing. This convention results in the current predominance of those data
031   in 3D shape analysis, though the discrete nature of point clouds and polygon meshes can make the
032   analysis approaches susceptible to variations in data resolutions (Qi et al., 2017a; Wang et al., 2019d;
033   Li et al., 2018; Lei et al., 2020; Hanocka et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2022). The recent advancements
034   in neural fields enable manifold surfaces to be continuously represented by the zero-level-set of
035   their signed distance functions (SDFs) (Park et al., 2019a; Sitzmann et al., 2020b; Xie et al., 2022).
036
      Specifically, SDFs compute a scalar field by mapping each coordinate x ∈ R3 to a scalar v ∈ R
      using a deep neural network, where the scalar v indicates the signed distance of a point to its closest
037
      point on the surface boundary. Let θ be the optimized parameters of the neural network. The shape
038
      surface can be numerically represented as θ, which we refer to as the level-set parameters. A typical
039   example of 3D surface representation with level-set parameters could be [nx , ny , nz , d] for a 3D
040   plane of n⊺ x + d = 0, where n = [nx , ny , nz ]⊺ and ∥n∥ = 1. The level-set parameters bring in
041   novel 3D data for continuous shape analysis.
042
      To initiate the novel shape analysis, we first have to construct the level-set parameters for each shape
043
      independently and with good accuracy in the dataset of interest. This differs from most methods in
044
      the SDF research domain, which are reconstruction-oriented and use a shared decoder together with
045   various latent codes to improve the surface quality (Park et al., 2019a; Mescheder et al., 2019; Erler
046   et al., 2020; Chen & Zhang, 2019; Peng et al., 2020; Chibane et al., 2020). However, the level-set
047   parameters do not conform to Euclidean or metric geometry, unlike 3D point clouds. This presents
048   a critical challenge for establishing good correlations in the parameter data of different shapes. Our
049   solution is to formulate those parameters in high dimension as a pseudo-normal distribution with
050   expectation µ and identity covariance matrix I, that is,
051
                                                  θ = µ + ∆θ.                                             (1)
052
053   We associate ∆θ with each individual shape, while the parameter µ is shared by all shapes to (i)
      align different shapes in the parameter space, and (ii) initialize the SDF networks of individual


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      shapes. Previous works train per-category SDF initializations using all training shapes (Sitzmann
055   et al., 2020a) or a particular shape (Erkoç et al., 2023) in the category, which are either computation-
056   intensive or dependent on the specific shape chosen. In contrast, we seek an initialization that
057   generalizes to all categories of the dataset. Therefore, we take the trade-off of Sitzmann et al. (2020a)
058   and Erkoç et al. (2023) to use a few shapes from each category to learn µ. Other than learning an
059   initialization, there are also methods employing identical random settings to initialize (Luigi et al.,
060   2023), which we empirically show yield insufficient correlations among shapes.
061   Level-set parameters have rarely been considered as a data modality in shape analysis. Luigi et al.
062   (2023) had to rely on the traditional data (i.e., point cloud and meshes) to extract shape seman-
063   tics from level-set parameters using an encoder-decoder, due to their suboptimal SDF initializations.
064   Erkoç et al. (2023) utilized a small SDF network for tractable complexity in shape diffusion, whereas
065   a small SDF network can undermine the representation quality of level-set parameters for complex
066   shapes. More importantly, both methods are limited to shape analysis in the reference poses, ignor-
067   ing the important shape transformations such as rotation and translation. In contrast, we explore the
068   level-set parameters with transformations and extend the shape analysis to be pose-related.
069   Usually, transformations of shapes represented by level-set parameters only affect a typical subset
070   of the parameters (e.g., those in the first layer of SDF). We propose to condition those subset param-
071   eters on rotations and translations and generate them with a hypernetwork to facilitate the analysis.
072   The inherent separation of pose-dependent and pose-independent subsets in level-set parameters en-
073   ables classification of continuous shapes in arbitrary poses to be simple and outperform equivariant
074   neural networks for point clouds (Deng et al., 2021; Chen & Cong, 2022). Our work focuses on
075
      demonstrating the viability of level-set parameters as an independent data modality for continuous
      shape analysis. This contrasts with existing approaches that leverage neural fields to learn invariant
076
      features from discrete data for semantic analysis (Kwon et al., 2023).
077
078   To acquire experimental data, we construct level-set parameters for shapes in the ShapeNet (Chang
079   et al., 2015) and Manifold40 (Hu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2015) datasets. We demonstrate the potential
080   of the proposed data through applications in shape classification of arbitrary poses, shape retrieval,
081
      and 6D object pose estimation. In pose estimation, we consider the problem of estimating shape
      poses from their partial point cloud observations, given that the level-set parameters are provided.
082
      This is similar to a partial-to-whole registration task (Dang et al., 2022). The main contributions of
083
      this work are summarized as below:
084
085          • We introduce level-set parameters as a novel data modality for 3D shapes, and demonstrate
086            their potential with pose-related shape analysis. A new hypernetwork is contributed to
087            transform the shapes in the level-set parameter space to facilitate the analysis.
088          • We present an encoder that is able to accept the high-dimensional level-set parameters as
089            inputs and extract the shape semantics in arbitrary poses for classification and retrieval.
090          • We propose a correspondence-free registration approach that estimates the 6D object poses
091            from their partial point cloud observations based on the SDF reconstruction loss.
092          • We opensource our code and level-set parameter data on github.

      2.1 S HAPE A NALYSIS WITH T RADITIONAL 3D DATA
096
097   Semantic Analysis. The traditional 3D data, point clouds and polygon meshes, represent shape sur-
098   faces discretely in Euclidean space. Point clouds, as orderless collections of points, result in early-
099
      stage MLP-based neural networks (Qi et al., 2017a; Klokov & Lempitsky, 2017; Qi et al., 2017b)
      to introduce permutation-invariant operations for semantic learning from such data. Graph convolu-
100
      tional networks (Lei et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019c; Wu et al., 2019; Thomas et al., 2019) enable
101
      point clouds to be processed with convolutional operations which handle spatial hierarchies of data
102   better than MLPs. Transformer-based networks (Zhao et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022)
103   treat each point cloud as a sequence of 3D points, offering another approach. Point clouds can also
104   be voxelized into regular grids and processed by 3D convolutional neural networks (Wu et al., 2015;
105   Maturana & Scherer, 2015; Riegler et al., 2017; Graham et al., 2018). Polygon meshes, which are
106   point clouds with edge connections on the shape surface, provide more information about the shape
107   geometry. Different approaches have been proposed to learn shape semantics from meshes (Hanocka
      et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2022; Smirnov & Solomon, 2021; Lei et al., 2023).

108
      Furthermore, a variety of equivariant networks have been presented to incorporate rotation and trans-
109   lation equivariance for the method to maintain its effectiveness when the discrete data are trans-
110   formed (Deng et al., 2021; Chen & Cong, 2022; Esteves et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2018; Thomas
111   et al., 2018; Poulenard & Guibas, 2021). In contrast, we study shape semantics from their continuous
112   representations using level-set parameters, requiring no equivariant modules for transformations.
113
      Geometric Analysis. 6D object pose estimation is crucial for various applications such as robotics
114
      grasping (Tremblay et al., 2018), augmented reality (Marchand et al., 2015), and autonomous driv-
115   ing (Geiger et al., 2012). It requires accurately determining the rotation and translation of an object
116   relative to a reference frame. Despite the predominance of estimating object poses from RGB(D)
117   data (Wang et al., 2019a;b; Peng et al., 2019; Park et al., 2019b), this task can alternatively be defined
118   as a registration problem based on point cloud data. It involves registering the partial point cloud
119   of an object (observation) to its full point cloud (reference) (Dang et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023).
120   We are interested in the potential of level-set parameters in the geometric analysis of 3D shapes
121   and therefore represent the reference objects using level-set parameters instead of full point clouds.
122   Leveraging the SDF reconstruction loss, we propose a registration method that requires no training
123
      data (Zeng et al., 2017), correspondences (Choy et al., 2019; Wang & Solomon, 2019; Huang et al.,
      2021; Ao et al., 2023) or global shape features (Huang et al., 2020; Aoki et al., 2019). We compare
124
      it with other optimization-based registration algorithms that also require no training data, includ-
125
      ing ICP (Besl & McKay, 1992), FGR (Zhou et al., 2016), and TEASER(++) (Yang et al., 2020).
126   Go-ICP (Yang et al., 2015) is excluded here due to its high time complexity.

128   2.2   N EURAL F IELDS FOR 3D R ECONSTRUCTION
129
      Neural fields utilize coordinate-based neural networks to compute signed distance fields (Park et al.,
130   2019a; Sitzmann et al., 2020b) or occupancy fields (Mescheder et al., 2019) for 3D reconstructions.
131   Many methods in this domain employ a shared decoder to reconstruct 3D shapes across an entire
132   dataset or a specific category using different shape latent codes (Park et al., 2019a; Mescheder et al.,
133   2019; Erler et al., 2020; Chen & Zhang, 2019; Atzmon & Lipman, 2020; Gropp et al., 2020). The
134   modelling function can be denoted as fθ : R3 ×Rm →R, with z ∈ Rm being the shape latent code,
135   which is learned by an encoder from various inputs (Mescheder et al., 2019), or randomly initialized
136   and optimized as in DeepSDF (Park et al., 2019a). Some works extend the concept of latent codes
137   from per-shape to per-point for improved surface quality (Peng et al., 2020; Chibane et al., 2020).
138
      In contrast, SIREN (Sitzmann et al., 2020b) applies an SDF network to instance-level surface recon-
      struction using periodic activations, which does not involve latent codes. It employs an unsupervised
139
      loss function, eliminating the need for ground-truth SDF values as in DeepSDF. We note that neural
140
      radiance fields (NeRFs) (Martin-Brualla et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Yariv et al.,
141   2021) reconstruct 3D surfaces with entangled neural parameters for surface geometry and photom-
142   etry, where the latter is view-dependent. We therefore focus on surface geometry and utilize the
143   level-set parameters from SDFs in our study. Our SDF network is adapted from the common 8-layer
144   MLP utilized by others (Park et al., 2019a; Atzmon & Lipman, 2020; Gropp et al., 2020).
145
146   2.3   L EVEL -S ET PARAMETERS AS 3D DATA
147   Few works have studied the level-set parameters as an alternative data modality for 3D research,
148   and each has utilized a different SDF network. Luigi et al. (2023) initialized the SDF network of
149   SIREN (Sitzmann et al., 2020b) with identical random settings and trained the parameters indepen-
150   dently for each shape, resulting in insufficient shape correlations in the parameter space. To extract
151   shape semantics from the level-set parameters, theyo utilized an encoder-decoder architecture with
152   additional supervision from traditional data. In contrast, our proposed method constructs the pa-
153
      rameter data with improved shape correlations, enabling the learning of shape semantics using an
      encoder without relying on traditional data. We note that the periodic activation functions of SIREN
154
      cause undesired shape artifacts in empty spaces compared to ReLU activations (Ben-Shabat et al.,
155
      2022). Erkoç et al. (2023) utilized a much smaller SDF network with ReLU activations for continu-
156   ous shape generation. They initialized the network using overfitted parameters of a particular shape
157   and trained the parameters of each shape within the same category independently. Yet, small SDF
158   networks cannot represent complex shapes with good accuracy. Dupont et al. (2022) aimed to ex-
159   plore the parameters of diverse neural fields as continuous data representations, but instead resorted
160   to their modulation vectors (Mehta et al., 2021; Chan et al., 2021) for simplicity. They exploited the
161   strategy of meta-learning (Sitzmann et al., 2020a; Finn et al., 2017; Tancik et al., 2021) to construct
      those modulation vectors.

165   3    DATASET OF L EVEL -S ET PARAMETERS
166
167   Preliminaries. We adopt the well-established SDF architecture from previous research (Park et al.,
168
      2019a; Atzmon & Lipman, 2020), which comprises an 8-layer MLP with a skipping concatenation
      at the 4th layer. We utilize 256 neurons for all interior layers other than the skipping layer which
169
      has 253 neurons due to the input concatenation. Note that shape latent codes are not required.
170
      We employ smoothed ReLU as the activation function and train the SDF network f (x; θ) with the
171   unsupervised reconstruction loss of SIREN (Sitzmann et al., 2020b), i.e.,
172
173                              LSDF = λ1 Lpdist + λ2 Lndist + λ3 Leik + λ4 Lpnorm .                     (2)
174   Lpdist , Lndist are the respective distances of positive and negative points to the surface. Leik is the
175   Eikonal loss (Gropp et al., 2020) and Lpnorm imposes normal consistency. The constants λ1∼4 balance
176   different objectives.
177
      The SDF network only provides level-set parameters for each shape in their reference poses. How-
178   ever, pose-related shape analysis requires surface transformations to be enabled in the level-set pa-
179   rameter space. To address this, we propose a hypernetwork that conditions a subset of the SDF
180   parameters on rotations and translations in SE(3) in § 3.1.
181
182   3.1 S URFACE T RANSFORMATION
183   We introduce a hypernetwork hϕ conditioned on rotations R and translations t to generate weights
184   and biases for the first SDF layer. Let m∈[256] be the row index, and n∈[4] be the column index,
185   where [i]={1, 2, . . . , i}. We index each parameter to be generated for the first SDF layer as θ1mn .
186   The trainable parameters ϕ in the hypernetwork are composed of two components, including (1) the
187   neural parameters η of all fully connected layers, (2) the small latent matrices {Ymn ∈RI×J } for
188
      each θ1mn in the first SDF layer. We use I=2 and J=8 as dimensions of Ymn in our experiments.
      The computation of each θ1mn from the hypernetwork is expressed as
189
190                                         θ1mn = h(R, t; η, Ymn ).                                      (3)
191
      Instead of generating the first layer parameters directly as h(R, t; η), we introduce the latent ma-
192   trices {Ymn } to guarantee that our generated SDF parameters satisfy the geometric initializations
193   recommended by SAL (Atzmon & Lipman, 2020). This is critical for the network convergence and
194   good performance.
195
      Generally, the hypernetwork calculates a compact matrix B=h(R, t; η) of size 256×4×I×J ac-
196
      cording to R, t, which contains the pose-dependent coefficient matrices Bmn associated with each
197
      latent matrix Ymn . Each pair of matrices Bmn and Ymn is combined to compute a vector zmn ,
198   normalized into ẑmn to satisfy the standard normal distribution. In the last layer of hϕ , two branches
199   of fully connected layers accept zmn and ẑmn , respectively, to initialize the biases and weights in
200   {θ1mn } accordingly. See appendix for the details. We note that the tanh activation in the final com-
201   putation of B (see Fig. B) helps to constrain all of its values within the range of [−1, 1].
202
      By incorporating this hypernetwork hϕ into the SDF network, we obtain a transformation-enabled
203
      SDF architecture, referred to as HyperSE3-SDF. It can transform the surface in the level-set param-
204   eter space by adaptively modifying {θ1mn } in θ. Although we can also apply the formulas
205
206                                   W′ = WR−1 , b′ = −WR−1 t + b,                                       (4)
207   to the weights W and biases b in the 1st and 5th layers of the utilized SDF network for simplified
208   surface transformations, the Euclidean nature of these computations yields transformed parameters
209   with shape semantics that are incomparable to those produced by HyperSE3-SDF. We show this
210   empirically with experiments. Derivations of the formulas are provided in the appendix.

      4.2 R EGISTRATION - BASED 6D P OSE E STIMATION
304
305   We train the SDF network to estimate shape poses, which entails incorporating the pose parameters
306
      R, t into the optimizable parameters of the plain SDF network. To achieve, we can either (1) gener-
      ate the 1st layer parameters with the hypernetwork, or (2) compute the pose-dependent parameters
307
      in the 1st and 5th layers based on Eq. (4). We provide estimation results for both options in our
308
      experiments.
309
310   Problem setting. Given a partial point cloud observation of a shape and the level-set parameters θ
311   representing the shape in its reference pose, we estimate the pose of the observation by optimizing
312
      the pose-dependent level-set parameters for the point cloud. This process follows the standard train-
      ing procedures of the SDF network. During training, we maintain the reference level-set parameters
313
      θ frozen. The pose parameters are trained using the distances of points to surface, i.e., the Lpdist loss
314
      in Eq. (7) for SDF reconstruction. We consider all points as samples on the surface of the shape.
315
316   Pose initialization. We define the rotation parameters using Euler angles ω = (α, β, γ). The pose
317   estimation involves 3 parameters for rotation and 3 parameters for translation. In the context of SDF,
318
      we consider only small translations in the registration. Therefore, we always initialize the translation
      as t = 0. For the rotation angles, we uniformly partition the space of [0, 2π]×[0, 2π]×[0, 2π] into
319
      distinct subspaces, and initialize ω with the centers of each subspace. This results in a total of T 3
320                                           2πt     2πt   2πt

479   6    C ONCLUSION
480   This paper extends shape analysis beyond traditional 3D data by introducing level-set parameters as
481   a continuous and numerical representation of 3D shapes. We establish shape correlations in the non-
482   Euclidean parameter space with learned SDF initialization. A novel hypernetwork is proposed to
483   transform the shape surface by modifying a subset of level-set parameters according to rotations and
484   translations in SE(3). The resulting continuous representations facilitate semantic shape analysis in
485   SO(3) compared to the Euclidean-based transformations of continuous shapes. We also demonstrate
      the efficacy of level-set parameters in geometric shape analysis with pose estimation. The SDF

531
      Limitations. Level-set parameters summarize the overall topology of 3D shapes and can be applied
532
      to global feature learning of continuous shapes. However, they are not suitable for learning local
533
      features of 3D shapes, as there is no correspondence between the local structures of the shape and
534   subsets of the level-set parameters. Researchers have explored local modulation vectors (Bauer
535   et al., 2023) to improve the image classification performance of continuous representations, but
536   with limited success. Mixtures of neural implicit functions (You et al., 2024) may offer enhanced
537   local encoding in the continuous representations. Besides, distinguishing the level-set parameters
538   into pose-dependent and independent subsets, and making them learnable from arbitrarily posed
539   point clouds, could be important for continuous shape representations and analysis in the future.
      Extending continuous representations from shapes to real-world surfaces also merits exploration.

938   C I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
939   Hypernetwork hϕ . In the first stage of our dataset construction, we train HyperSE3-SDF with a
940   batch size of 50 for 50000 epochs. We utilize the Adam Optimizer [80] with an initial learning rate
941   of 0.001, which exponentially decays at a rate of γ=0.998 every 30 epochs to train the model. This
942   training process takes 3 days on a GeForce RTX 4090 GPU.
943   Sampling Transformations. We consider Euler angles in the range of [0, 2π] and translations in
944   the subspace of [−0.1, 0.1]3 in the context of SDF. For dataset construction, we randomly sample
945   150 pairs of (R, t), including the reference pose (I, 0), which are fixed and shared across all epochs.
946   Additionally, in each epoch, we randomly sample another 150 (R, t) on-the-fly. These fixed and on-
947   the-fly transformations are collectively utilized to train the HyperSE3-SDF network. This strategy
948   facilitates satisfactory convergence and generalization of the model.
949   Augmentation of Level-Set Parameters. In the semantic shape analysis with level-set parameters,
950   we apply scaling operations to the level-set parameters using normal distributions with a major sigma
951   of σ1 =0.2 and a minor sigma of σ2 =0.05 for small perturbations of different θ. We also perturb
952   the level-set value c=0 using normal distributions with σc =0.1, and apply positional encoding to c,
953   denoted as PE(c). The resulting PE(c) is concatenated with Θ3 for feature extraction from the
954   corresponding branch. We set the level of PE(c) to 10 for ShapeNet and 16 for Manifold40. In the
955   transformer layer, we apply dropout with a rate of 0.3.
956   Other Details. We use 500 shapes from each of the five categories: airplane, chair, lamp, sofa,
957   table, in the experiments of § 5.1.
