009                                                                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011                            The rapid growth of camera-based Internet of Things (IoT) devices demands the
012                            need for efficient video compression, particularly for edge applications where
013                            devices face hardware constraints, often with only 1 or 2 MB of RAM and unstable
014                            internet connections. Traditional and deep video compression methods are designed
015                            for high-end hardware, exceeding the capabilities of these constrained devices.
016                            Consequently, video compression in these scenarios is often limited to Motion-
017
                               JPEG (M-JPEG) due to its high hardware efficiency and low complexity. This
                               paper introduces MCUCoder, an open-source adaptive bitrate video compression
018
                               model tailored for resource-limited IoT settings. MCUCoder features an ultra-
019
                               lightweight encoder with only 10.5K parameters and a minimal 350KB memory
020                            footprint, making it well-suited for edge devices and Microcontrollers (MCUs).
021                            While MCUCoder uses a similar amount of energy as M-JPEG, it reduces bitrate by
022                            55.65% on the MCL-JCV dataset and 55.59% on the UVG dataset, measured in MS-
023                            SSIM. Moreover, MCUCoder supports adaptive bitrate streaming by generating a
024                            latent representation that is sorted by importance, allowing transmission based on
025                            available bandwidth. This ensures smooth real-time video transmission even under
026                            fluctuating network conditions on low-resource devices. Source code available at
027                            [Link removed due to double-blind policy, code submitted in ZIP].

091
092
       Approach: To address these challenges, we introduce MCUCoder, an adaptive bitrate deep video
       compression model tailored for resource-limited IoT devices. Our approach focuses on creating an
093
       "asymmetric" compression model that features an ultra-lightweight encoder designed to be both
094
       computationally efficient and memory-friendly. Also, MCUCoder produces an "adaptive bitrate"
095    bitstream. Specifically, in MCUCoder, we train the encoder using stochastic dropout such that,
096    instead of explicitly detecting the important parts, it produces latent channels that are sorted based on
097    importance. Afterward, based on the available internet bandwidth, the encoder transmits the first k
098    channels to the decoder; see Fig. 1. This approach is beneficial for low-power MCUs since it shifts the
099    complexity of identifying important data to the training phase rather than the inference phase. Also,
100    by employing stochastic dropout training, the decoder can reconstruct the frame even with partial data
101    availability, which is essential for maintaining smooth and uninterrupted video transmission in real-
102    time applications, where network conditions can vary. Additionally, MCUCoder’s encoder is INT8
103
       quantized, allowing it to utilize Digital Signal Processor (DSP) and CMSIS-NN (ARM-software,
       2024) accelerators for faster processing and reduced power consumption.

140   2.1                   T RADITIONAL AND NN BASED VIDEO COMPRESSION
141
      Video compression is a field that has been evolving for decades. Beyond traditional codecs like H.264
142
      (Wiegand et al., 2003), H.265 (Sullivan et al., 2012), and H.266 (Bross et al., 2021), deep learning-
143   based approaches often replace conventional modules such as motion compensation (Agustsson et al.,
144   2020; Yang et al., 2020), transform coding (Zhu et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2021), and entropy coding
145   (Xiang et al., 2023; Mentzer et al., 2022). Also, some work has been done regarding the end-to-end
146   optimization of video compression models (He et al., 2020; Van Rozendaal et al., 2021; Khani et al.,
147   2021).Lu et al. (2019) introduce DVC, the first end-to-end deep video compression model. Hu et al.
148   (2022; 2021) extend DVC to operate in both pixel and feature domains. Li et al. (2021) and Liu
149   et al. (2020) reduce bitrates by modeling probabilities over video frames using conditional coding.
150   Also, in recent years, there has been growing interested in using implicit neural representations for
151
      video compression (Kwan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2021). However, due to their substantial hardware
      requirements, these models are unsuitable for deployment on low-resource IoT devices.
152

161   exploring alternative software-based models: Veluri et al. (2023) employ M-JPEG on the encoder to
      capture black-and-white and colorized frames at two different resolutions and uses super-resolution

195   3     MCUC O D E R
196
      In this section, we introduce MCUCoder, an adaptive bitrate asymmetric video compression model,
197   specifically designed for IoT settings. We begin by detailing the asymmetric encoder-decoder
198   architecture of MCUCoder, including the customized quantization processes. Then, we present the
199   stochastic dropout training method, which trains the encoder of MCUCoder to store information in
200   its channels based on importance.
201
202   3.1    A SYMMETRIC C OMPRESSION
203
204   MCUs are characterized by highly constrained hardware resources, such as limited RAM, CPU,
205   FLASH, and power availability. Additionally, existing MCU-specific NN frameworks like TFLite
206   Micro support only a limited set of NN layers (Hu et al., 2020). To address these constraints, we
207
      propose an asymmetric (Yao et al., 2020) encoder-decoder architecture optimized for constrained
      devices. Due to hardware constraints, MCUCoder encodes each frame independently, as inter-frame
208
      compression is not feasible. The encoder contains only 10.5K parameters, while the decoder utilizes
209
      approximately 3M parameters and leverages SOTA image decompression blocks; see Fig. 3. The
210   encoding process begins by passing input frame ft through three convolutional layers. To maximize
211   the data range for subsequent quantization, no activation function is applied in the final encoder layer,
212   avoiding the negative truncation caused by ReLU. Afterward, each channel of the latent is quantized
213   into INT8 individually, followed by a further reduction to 5-bit precision to enhance compression
214   efficiency. For the decoder, inspired by He et al. (2022), we integrate a combination of attention
215   blocks (Cheng et al., 2020) and residual bottleneck blocks (He et al., 2016) to reconstruct the frame;
      see Fig. 3.

312   4.1   S ETTINGS
313
314
315
      We train MCUCoder on the 300K largest ImageNet images (Deng et al., 2009) and apply noise-
      downsampling preprocessing (He et al., 2021; Ballé et al., 2018). We use Adam with an initial
316
      learning rate of 10−4 and a batch size of 16, and train for 1M iterations, lowering the learning
317
      rate to 10−5 in the final 50K iterations (He et al., 2022). To address quantization effects, we add
318   random noise to the latent. Since MCUCoder is specifically designed for IoT environments, where
319   the structure of the output is more critical than fine details, we use MS-SSIM as the loss function.
320   We also quantize inputs, weights, and activations to INT8 for RAM efficiency and to leverage DSP
321   and CMSIS-NN accelerators (ARM-software, 2024) in MCUs. We use post-training quantization
322   existing in TFLite-Micro (TensorFlow, 2023) to reduce latency, processing power, and model size
323   with minimal degradation in model accuracy. For all comparisons, we report performance metrics for
      both the FLOAT32 and INT8 models.

      4.2    Q UANTITATIVE RESULTS
346
347   Due to the limited hardware resources of MCUs, inter-frame compression is not practically feasible.
348   As a result, in such devices, video compression is limited to M-JPEG where each frame is compressed
349   independently. Therefore, in addition to evaluating MCUCoder and its baselines from the perspective
350   of video compression, we also assess its performance on image compression datasets. Given the
351   lower resolution commonly encountered in IoT scenarios, we resize all the videos and images to
352   224 × 224.
353   Video compression: We evaluate MCUCoder on the UVG (Mercat et al., 2020) and MCL-JCV
354   (Wang et al., 2016) datasets, comparing its performance to M-JPEG , see Fig. 7. For additional context,
355   we include comparisons with traditional video codecs such as H.264 (Wiegand et al., 2003) and
356   H.265 (Sullivan et al., 2012), even though these codecs are impractical for deployment on MCUs due
357   to their significant computational and hardware demands. Also, we report the Bjøntegaard Delta (BD)
358   rate (Bjøtegaard, 2001) for both datasets in Table 1. The results indicate that MCUCoder achieves a
359
      significantly higher MS-SSIM per bit compared to M-JPEG, highlighting its ability to deliver better
      video quality at lower bitrates. This is especially valuable for IoT applications, where achieving high
360
      compression rates with minimal computational overhead is crucial due to limited hardware resources.
361
      Additionally, MCUCoder has 12 "stacked" channels in its latent space, which provides 12 levels of
362   quality that can be dynamically adjusted based on the available network bandwidth. In Fig. 9, we
363   illustrate the bpp and MS-SSIM for each frame in a video from the UVG dataset for all 12 levels of
364   quality. The results show that using more channels for decoding leads to a higher MS-SSIM, which
365   verifies the effectiveness of the proposed stochastic dropout training.
366
      Image compression: To assess the image compression capabilities of MCUCoder, we conduct
367
      experiments on the CLIC (cli, 2020) and KODAK (Eastman Kodak, 1993) datasets, see Fig. 7.
368   The results in Table 1 show that MCUCoder achieves an impressive average bitrate reduction of
369   55.75% on the KODAK dataset and 49.54% on the CLIC dataset, compared to JPEG. As previously
370   mentioned, MCUCoder is specifically designed for IoT environments, where preserving the structural
371   integrity of the output is more important than capturing fine detail, leading to its optimization for
372   MS-SSIM. In contrast, JPEG is more focused on optimizing PSNR (Wang et al., 2004), which
373   explains why M-JPEG performs slightly better in PSNR at higher bitrates, see Fig.8.

419   MCUCoder demonstrates a low memory footprint, consuming 360 KB of RAM on the STM32F7
420   and 344 KB on the nRF5340, which is significantly efficient for such constrained devices. This
421   compact memory usage highlights the suitability of MCUCoder for low-power, resource-constrained
422   IoT applications. To assess the energy efficiency of MCUCoder, we conducted a comparative

      optimized JPEG encoder for the Cortex-M series1 on the nRF5340 platform; see Fig.10. The results

426
      slower processing performance compared to the STM32F7 for both MCUCoder and M-JPEG. This
427   discrepancy suggests that while the nRF5340 is energy-efficient, its lower computational capabilities
428   make it more appropriate for event-driven applications rather than real-time streaming tasks, where
429   the STM32F7 excels.

      5   L IMITATIONS
449
450   The design of MCUCoder is inherently motivated by the resource constraints typical of IoT devices;
451   however, these constraints also constitute its limitations. One significant limitation arises from the
452
      restricted RAM available on most IoT devices, which prevents the incorporation of intra-frame
      compression techniques. Consequently, MCUCoder exhibits a performance drop when compared
453
      to more computationally demanding video compression models, such as the H.26X series, which,
454
      however, require significant hardware resources far beyond the capabilities of MCUs with only one or
455   two MB of RAM. Furthermore, limited RAM also constrains the resolution of input frames processed
456   by MCUCoder, which can negatively impact the visual fidelity of the compressed video, particularly
457   in applications requiring higher detail. Additionally, the low clock speeds of MCU’s processors,
458   necessitated by battery conservation needs, result in prolonged encoding times for MCUCoder. This
459   increased encoding duration ultimately leads to lower fps during video processing, which can hinder
460   real-time performance and responsiveness in streaming applications. However, these limitations are
461   not unique to MCUCoder. The state-of-the-art video compression model used in such constrained
462   devices, M-JPEG, faces similar issues. M-JPEG does not utilize intra-frame compression either
463
      and requires more RAM to achieve higher resolutions, impacting visual fidelity. Like MCUCoder,
      M-JPEG’s reliance on the low clock speeds of MCU processors results in longer encoding times
464
      and reduced fps. Nonetheless, despite all of these limitations, MCUCoder significantly outperforms
465
      M-JPEG in both image and video compression datasets.

468   6   C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we presented MCUCoder, an open-source, ultra-lightweight video compression model
471   designed specifically for resource-constrained IoT devices. With only 10.5K parameters and a
472   350KB memory footprint, compared to M-JPEG, MCUCoder demonstrates significant bitrate re-
473   ductions—55.65% on the MCL-JCV dataset and 55.59% on the UVG dataset—while maintaining
474   hardware efficiency similar to M-JPEG. Furthermore, MCUCoder supports adaptive bitrate stream-
475   ing, enabling real-time video transmission under variable network conditions. These features make
476   MCUCoder a promising solution for video compression in edge applications where both hardware
477
      and bandwidth are limited.
