009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-Modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) recently demonstrated strong
012            capabilities in both instruction comprehension and responding, positioning them as
013            promising tools for human-computer interaction. However, the inherent ambiguity
014            of language poses a challenge, potentially leading models astray in task implemen-
015            tation due to differing interpretations of the same text within varying contexts. In
016
               multi-modal settings, visual information serves as a natural aid in disambiguating
               such scenarios. In this paper, we introduce the first benchmark specifically de-
017
               signed to evaluate the performance of MLLMs in Ambiguous contexts (MMA).
018
               This benchmark employs a multiple-choice visual question-answering format and
019            includes 261 textual contexts and questions with ambiguous meaning. Each ques-
020            tion is linked to a pair of images that suggest divergent scenarios, thus leading
021            to different answers given the same question. These questions are stratified into
022            three categories of ambiguity: lexical, syntactic, and semantic, to facilitate a de-
023            tailed examination of MLLM performance across varying levels of ambiguity. By
024            evaluating 24 proprietary and open-sourced MLLMs, we find that: (1) MLLMs
025            often overlook scenario-specific information provided by images to clarify the
026            ambiguity of texts. When presented with two different contextual images and
027
               asked the same question, MLLMs achieved an accuracy rate of only 53.22% in
               answering both correctly, compared to human performance at 88.97%. (2) Among
028
               the three types of ambiguity, models perform best under lexical ambiguity and
029
               worst under syntactic ambiguity. (3) Open-sourced models generally perform
030            significantly lower than proprietary MLLMs, with an average performance gap of
031            12.59%, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, emerges as the top model, achieving 74.32% accuracy.
032            These findings firstly underscore the current limitations of MLLMs in integrating
033            visual information to clarify textual ambiguities and highlight critical areas for
034            future improvements. The codes and benchmark data are available.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Our interaction with the world is inherently multimodal, involving the reception and processing of
039
      information across modalities (Turk, 2014). By training on large-scale datasets, multimodal large
      language models (MLLMs) built-up on transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Tsai et al., 2019; Xu
040
      et al., 2023), such as GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2024b), Gemini (Team et al., 2023) and LLaVA (Liu et al.,
041
      2024), have demonstrated strong understanding, reasoning, and even coding ability across vision and
042   language modalities. With visual and language understanding abilities, the realization of MLLM-
043   based agents has become feasible, sparking the potential for a variety of innovative applications, such
044   as mobile-operation (Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024; You et al., 2024) and graphics design
045   (Cheng et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). These applications highlight the transformative potential of
046   MLLMs in future human-computer interaction (Gao et al., 2024; Bahmani; Yang et al., 2024).
047
      However, clarity during interactions is not always guaranteed. Ambiguity, which refers to cases where
048   an expression conveys multiple denotations (Wasow et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024),
049   is inherently present in human interactions (Norris, 2004). For examples shown in Figure 1, lexical
050   ambiguity can be seen in “I saw her duck,” where "duck" can mean either the bird or the action of
051   lowering one’s head. Syntactic ambiguity is illustrated by the sentence “The chicken is ready to eat,”
052   which can mean either the cooked chicken is ready to be eaten or the live chicken is ready to eat food.
053   Another example is “What a good job,” which can either be genuine praise or sarcasm, illustrating
      semantic ambiguity. Without sufficient context, it is difficult to determine the meaning of ambiguous

092

101

129   Visual question answering Since the introduction of the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task
130

      et al., 2016; Krishna et al., 2017; Goyal et al., 2017; Hudson & Manning, 2019; Li et al., 2019;
131
      Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). However, the challenge of accurately interpreting this combined data still
132
      remains. The VQA v2 dataset (Goyal et al., 2017) tackles these complexities by utilizing balanced
133   image pairs to enhance detailed visual analysis. Studies like (Stengel-Eskin et al., 2022) created a
134   VQA dataset featuring ambiguous examples where images provide just enough information to answer
135   the questions but do not resolve the inherent ambiguities within the questions themselves. Unlike
136   many VQA datasets that primarily rely on straightforward visual cues for answering questions, our
137   benchmark requires a deeper understanding of the nuanced interplay between text and visual contexts.
138   This approach focuses on clarifying ambiguities that arise from the combination of text and images,
139   where the contextual information from the images is crucial for disambiguating the textual content.
140   Datasets for ambiguity The field of ambiguity resolution in machine learning has been explored
141   through various specialized datasets, each targeting specific aspects of ambiguity. For example,
142   WiC (Pilehvar & Camacho-Collados, 2019) and CoNLL-2012 (Pradhan et al., 2012) focus on word
143
      sense disambiguation and coreference resolution, respectively, addressing text-based ambiguities in
      linguistic contexts. Datasets like SemEval-2018 Task 7 (Buscaldi et al., 2017) , AmbiEnt (Liu et al.,
144
      2023a) , AmbigQA (Min et al., 2020b) , AmbigMT (Pilault et al., 2023) , and AmbiCoref (Yuan
145
      et al., 2023b) further this work by tackling different forms of textual ambiguities, from semantic
146   similarity to natural language inference and machine translation. While these datasets offer valuable
147   insights, they are largely limited to single-modal, text-based tasks, each focusing on a specific type of
148   ambiguity. The advent of multimodal datasets, such as LAVA (Berzak et al., 2015) , MM-Star (Chen
149   et al., 2024a) , and MMMU (Yue et al., 2023) , represents significant progress by integrating both
150   visual and textual data, challenging models to resolve ambiguities across modalities. However, these
151   multimodal datasets often remain confined to specific tasks or ambiguity types. Existing works have
152   several limitations: (1) they primarily focus on text ambiguities and lack multimodal datasets; (2)
153   they are limited to disambiguation within specific scenarios and tasks; and (3) they often address only
154
      one particular type of ambiguity. Our approach aims to overcome these limitations by incorporating
      multimodal data and encompassing a wide range of ambiguity types to explore ambiguity issues in a
155
      more general context.

184   3.1       OVERVIEW OF OUR MMA BENCHMARK
185
      In order to systematically explore the capability of MLLMs to perceive and resolve ambiguities of
186
      varying complexities, we categorize ambiguities into lexical, syntactic, and semantic types based on
187
      the linguistic characteristics (detailed in Section 3.2). The benchmark tasks are structured as multiple-
188   choice VQA scenarios, a format that simplifies the evaluation process, where the meaning of each
189   question is ambiguous, and they are associated with multiple images that provide varying contexts,
190   allowing the same question to elicit different correct responses based on the visual information
191   provided as shown in Figure 2. This design forces the MLLMs to adeptly integrate and interpret both
192   textual and visual data to select the most accurate answer, reflecting the true potential and challenges
193   of deploying such models in diverse, ambiguity-filled environments.
194
195   3.2       T YPES OF AMBIGUITIES
196
197   We divide the ambiguity into the following types and design questions according to each different
198
      ambiguity type. Each category is designed to evaluate specific aspects of how well models integrate
      and interpret complex linguistic and visual information to resolve ambiguities. For a more detailed
199
      understanding, we provide examples of each type in Table 2.
200
      Lexical ambiguities. Lexical ambiguity mainly evaluates the ambiguity caused by polysemy in
201
      sentences. We considered the ambiguity caused by nouns, adjectives, and verbs. The verb category
202   includes both the ambiguity of polysemy and the ambiguity of different emotions it may evoke.
203
      Syntactic ambiguities. Syntactic ambiguities occur when sentence structures allow for multiple
204   interpretations. There are three main types: (a) Attachment Ambiguity: This occurs when a
205   modifying phrase, usually a prepositional phrase or clause, can logically attach to more than one
206   part of the sentence. (b) Coordination Ambiguity: This happens when adjectives, adverbs, or other
207   modifiers can ambiguously apply to one or more nouns in a series, creating uncertainty about whether
208   the modifiers apply to all or just some elements. (c) Structural Ambiguity: This arises when verbs
209   can be used in both transitive and intransitive forms, leading to different meanings.
210   Semantic ambiguities. Semantic ambiguities involve the broader meanings of text and their
211   interaction with visual elements : (a) Idiomatic Ambiguity: This occurs with idiomatic expressions
212   that can be interpreted both literally and metaphorically. (b) Pragmatic Ambiguity: This arises from
213   interpreting a sentence in different contexts provided by visual cues, affecting how the listener or
214
      viewer understands the relevance and expected response.

248   3.3    DATA COLLECTION
249
      To effectively evaluate the ability of MLLMs to resolve ambiguity in multimodal contexts, we
250
      constructed a benchmark dataset based on a multiple-choice question (MCQ) format. This format
251
      enables standardized automatic evaluation, allowing for a quantitative assessment of model accuracy
252   in handling complex scenarios involving both visual and textual cues. The multiple-choice format also
253   ensures consistent and objective scoring across test cases, facilitating direct performance comparisons
254   between different models.
255   Question selection. The dataset focuses on three primary types of ambiguity: lexical, semantic,
256   and syntactic. We began by compiling a list of ambiguous words and phrases representing each type,
257   drawing from resources like the Oxford English Dictionary, Google search, and idiom lists. For each
258   ambiguous term, we crafted grammatically correct sentences designed to be interpretable in multiple
259   plausible ways without visual context. These sentences formed the basis of our ambiguous questions.
260
261   Image selection. Each ambiguous sentence was paired with two images representing different
262   interpretations of the ambiguity. These images were either sourced from Google or, when necessary,
263   generated using text-to-image, e.g., Stable-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and Dall-E (OpenAI,
264
      2024a). All images underwent rigorous human review to ensure clarity, relevance, and accurate
      portrayal of the intended scenarios.
265
      Option design. Each MCQ in MMA includes a strategically designed set of answer options: One
266
      correct answer per image: Reflecting the scenario depicted and the intended interpretation of
267
      the ambiguous question. Multiple potential interpretations: Representing plausible but incorrect
268   interpretations, revealing model biases. Visual bias distractors: Based on image elements unrelated
269   to the question, testing susceptibility to visual bias. Linguistic bias distractors: Derived from the


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      question text but unsupported by images, testing susceptibility to linguistic bias. This multi-faceted
271   option design allows us to identify potential biases in how models process information and understand
272   how they integrate different information sources in practical applications.

352
353   As shown in Table 3, the mean ambiguity accuracy (Amb_A) of MLLMs varies significantly across
354   different ambiguity types, highlighting challenges in handling structural and pragmatic ambigui-
355   ties. However, a clear gap remains when comparing these models to human performance, which
356   significantly outperforms the MLLMs.
357   Proprietary models, such as Claude 3.5 Sonnet (74%), achieve the best overall performance on
358   Amb_A and excel at handling lexical ambiguities (83%). Among open-source models, InternVL-
359   Chat-V1-5 (69.7%) shows strong performance, particularly in lexical categories (82%), achieving
360   nearly comparable performance to Claude 3.5 Sonnet.

      Despite these advancements, the best-performing models like Claude 3.5 Sonnet and GPT-4o still
362   show a substantial gap when compared to human performance. Claude 3.5 Sonnet achieves an
363   overall accuracy of 74%, which is 15% lower than the human benchmark of 89%. Similarly, GPT-4o
364   performs 19% lower than human performance with an overall accuracy of 70%. Gemini-1.5 pro
365   and InternVL-Chat-V1-5 also underperform humans by 23% and 19%, respectively, with overall

      4.3.4    S YNTACTIC AMBIGUITY AND SEMANTIC AMBIGUITY ARE MORE CHALLENGING THAN
               LEXICAL AMBIGUITY
448
449   For both Amb_A, all models (except MiniCPM) perform better on lexical ambiguity and worse on
450   syntactic and semantic ambiguities (Figure 5). Lexical ambiguity, which involves straightforward
451   word meanings, is easier for models to handle. For example, InternVL-Chat-V1-5 achieves an
452   accuracy of 82% on lexical ambiguities, significantly higher than its performance on syntactic (43%)
453   and semantic (62%) ambiguities. This trend is consistent across most models; for instance, GPT-4o
454   shows 82% accuracy on lexical ambiguities but drops to 31% and 69% on syntactic and semantic
455   ambiguities, respectively.
456   Syntactic ambiguities present a unique challenge because they involve the relationships between
457   components within a sentence. Often, even a short modifier can introduce ambiguity, making it
458   difficult for models to resolve these cases without fine-grained analysis. To effectively handle
459   syntactic ambiguities, models need not only a more granular approach to language processing but also
460   the capability to accurately recognize positional relationships and details in images. This requires a
461   higher level of precision compared to lexical and semantic ambiguities. Models like Cogvlm2 and
462
      VILA-3b, for example, perform poorly in this category, with accuracies of 19% and 10%, respectively.
463   Similarly, semantic ambiguities, which involve nuanced meanings and context, are also difficult for
464   models to resolve. For instance, VILA-40b achieves only 49% accuracy on semantic ambiguities,
465   despite a higher performance on lexical (79%).

467   4.3.5    S CALING LAW ON MMA
468
      To comprehend whether the parameter number affects performance on the MMA benchmark, we
469
      conducted experiments on the same series of models with varying sizes, all trained on similar data.
470   As Figure 4 shows, there is a clear improvement in ambiguity accuracy as the parameter count
471   increases across different ambiguity types. For instance, larger models like VILA1.5-40B consistently
472   outperform smaller ones such as VILA1.5-3B. The larger models show significant improvements
473   in handling lexical, syntactic, and semantic ambiguities, demonstrating that increased parameters
474   enhance the model’s ability to understand and disambiguate complex, multi-modal inputs. This trend
475   indicates a positive correlation between model size and performance on the MMA benchmark.

477   5   L IMITATION
478
479   Data collection Due to constraints on the number of participants, the dataset size is limited in
480   certain categories. Despite this limitation, we emphasize that the quality and representativeness of
481   the dataset are more crucial for establishing a meaningful benchmark than merely the number of
482   samples. As demonstrated in Table 3, the considerable performance discrepancy between human
483   participants and MLLM responses underscores the benchmark’s effectiveness in highlighting the
484   current challenges that MLLMs face, particularly their inability to adequately utilize visual context to
485   resolve textual ambiguities. Moving forward, we are committed to expanding the dataset in future
      iterations of the benchmark, aiming to broaden its scope and enhance its representational validity.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Question design In our benchmark, both images and texts are designed to provide context informa-
487   tion to model the multi-modal real-world cases. Due to the paper presentation problem, how to present
488   some questions naturally presents certain challenges. We conducted experiments with text-only input
489   and found that MLLMs demonstrate high accuracy, ranging from 83% to 90% (as Table 4 shows).
490   However, when errors occurred, models consistently chose the same incorrect answers (as Table 5
491   shows). These results clearly indicate MLLMs have a strong bias towards text-based information and
492   a failure to effectively incorporate visual context.
493
494   Real-world likeness Some of images used in our benchmark are generated by generative models.
495   The images in our benchmark are specifically chosen to provide the necessary context to clarify
496   ambiguities in the accompanying texts. Due to the current limitations of search engines, which
497
      struggle with semantic search, it is challenging to find suitable images that naturally align with the
      required context (This doesn’t mean that these images don’t exist.). Therefore, using generated
498
      images is the most effective approach. They are instrumental in simulating the diverse and often
499
      unconventional situations that MLLMs encounter in real applications. MLLMs are expected to
500   perform comparably to humans in these scenarios, regardless of the variability in inputs. However,
501   our human study shows that humans can achieve approximately 90% accuracy on this benchmark
502   without any additional interactions. This sharply contrasts with the average accuracies of 58% for
503   closed-source models and 47% for open-source models.
504
505
      6   F UTURE W ORK
506
507
      Additional Modalities The world is multimodal rather than just bimodal. For instance, audio plays
508
      an important role in daily life, and there are some ambiguities caused by audio. For example, the
509   phrases "He’s a great rapper" and "He’s a great wrapper" sound similar but refer to completely
510   different things. With a concrete scene provided, the meaning of a segment of audio can be uniquely
511   determined.
512
513   Additional Languages Language-specific features and rhetorical devices vary widely, influencing
514   how information is processed and understood. For instance, the use of ’Huwen’ in ancient Chinese
515   literature requires an understanding of how meanings are intricately split and reconnected across
516   sentences. Expanding MLLMs to accommodate the linguistic structures and subtleties of various
517   languages could improve their applicability and accuracy in global communication contexts. This
518
      development would necessitate models that are not only multilingual but also sensitive to cultural and
      contextual nuances within languages.
519
520
      Multiple Images per Sentence Lexical ambiguities can extend beyond dual interpretations, with
521
      some words or phrases having multiple meanings. Current models often limit context to one or
522
      two visual representations per sentence. By providing multiple images that correspond to each
523   potential meaning of a sentence, MLLMs can be trained to discern finer distinctions in word usage
524   and context. This enhancement would allow models to handle more complex scenarios where multiple
525   interpretations are valid, reflecting the true complexity of human language and cognition.

      7   C ONCLUSION
529
      This paper introduces MMA, the first benchmark designed specifically to evaluate the ability of
530
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to understand and respond to ambiguous queries.
531
      MMA leverages a multiple-choice visual question-answering format, presenting MLLMs with a
532   question and two images depicting contrasting scenarios that lead to different correct answers.
533   Our evaluation of 16 MLLMs, including both limited-access and open-sourced models, reveals a
534   significant performance gap compared to human performance. While humans achieve an accuracy
535   of 88.97%, the MLLMs average only 50.59% accuracy. This indicates a fundamental challenge
536   for current MLLMs: effectively integrating scenario-specific visual information to disambiguate
537   questions and arrive at the correct answer. Even the top-performing model, GPT-4o and Claude3.5-
538   Sonnet, attains only about 70.00% accuracy, highlighting considerable room for developing MLLMs
539   that can effectively leverage visual information to clarify the textual ambiguity and capable of
      human-level understanding and reasoning in complex, real-world scenarios.

      A.4      A BLATION STUDY
846
847   Same images with lexical or semantic questions To understand why MLLMs perform better on
848   lexical ambiguity compared to semantic ambiguity, we explored how changing the question type on
849   noun ambiguity impacts their performance. We created two versions of questions for noun categories:
850   the first being the most direct, “What’s the meaning of <Noun>?”, and the second incorporating
851   reasoning into the question. For example, given an image of a table, a synonym question for lexical
852   ambiguity might be “What is the meaning of table?” where the model identifies “table” as a piece
853   of furniture. In contrast, a reasoning question for semantic ambiguity would be “How can we best
854
      utilize the space on this table?” which requires the model to consider various uses of the table. This
      type of question tests the model’s ability to perform object grounding and higher-order reasoning,
855
      areas where MLLMs often show weaker performance due to their reliance on pattern recognition
856
      rather than true comprehension. More examples are given in Appendix.
857
858   As Figure 7 shows, GPT-4 Vision performs well on noun word ambiguity with a score of 90% but
859   drops to 59% on noun reasoning ambiguity. Similarly, Gemini-1.5 shows a significant drop from
860
      83% in noun word ambiguity to 63% in noun reasoning ambiguity. Intern-VL-Chat-V1-5, while
      achieving 92% in noun word ambiguity, sees a decline to 75% in noun reasoning ambiguity. These
861
      examples highlight the challenges MLLMs face in understanding and reasoning about more complex
862
      and context-dependent scenarios.
