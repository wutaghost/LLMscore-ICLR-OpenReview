009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As AI systems, particularly Large Language Models (LLMs), rapidly advance
012            towards surpassing human cognitive capabilities, ensuring their alignment with
013            human values and safety standards emerges as a formidable challenge. This study
014            addresses a crucial aspect of superalignment by investigating the decision-making
015            capabilities and adversarial vulnerabilities of LLMs, focusing on GPT-3.5, GPT-
016
               4 and Gemini-1.5, within structured experimental settings that mimic complex
               human interactions. We applied an adversarial framework to two decision-making
017
               tasks—the two-armed bandit task and the Multi-Round Trust Task (MRTT)—to
018
               test the vulnerabilities of LLMs under adversarial conditions. In the bandit task,
019            the adversary aimed to induce the LLM’s preference for the predefined target
020            action with the constraint that each action must be assigned an equal number
021            of rewards. For the MRTT, we trained two types of adversaries: one aimed
022            at maximizing its own earnings (MAX) and the other focused on maximizing
023            fairness (FAIR). GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5 showed a bias toward exploitation in the
024            bandit task, prioritizing early-established strategies, which made them predictable
025            and vulnerable to manipulation. GPT-3.5, while more exploratory in the bandit
026            task, demonstrated more risk-seeking behavior in the MRTT, leading to increased
027
               vulnerability in interacting with the MAX adversary. Notably, Gemini-1.5 excelled
               in the MRTT, adapting effectively to adversaries and outperforming both GPT-3.5
028
               and GPT-4 by balancing risk and cooperation with its adversaries. By presenting
029
               a specific set of tasks that characterizes decision-making vulnerabilities in LLM-
030            based agents, we provide a concrete methodology for evaluating their readiness
031            for real-world deployment. The adversarial framework proved a powerful tool for
032            stress-testing LLMs, revealing the importance of ensuring that AI models are both
033            robust against adversarial manipulation and responsive to fairness cues in complex,
034            dynamic environments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In recent years, the landscape of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has been reshaped by the emergence of
      Large Language Models (LLMs) such as Generative Pre-trained Transformers (GPT). These models
040
      have demonstrated remarkable performance across various applications, from natural language
041
      processing to complex problem-solving tasks. As LLMs are increasingly integrated into decision-
042   making processes in sectors such as healthcare (Karabacak & Margetis, 2023), finance (Krause,
043   2023), and autonomous systems (Sha et al., 2023), they augment human capabilities in data analysis,
044   forecasting, and strategic planning. However, as LLMs advance toward cognitive capabilities that
045   may surpass human functions, ensuring that their operations are aligned with human values and safety
046   standards becomes crucial. This challenge, often referred to as "superalignment", is particularly
047   critical in domains with significant ethical and practical implications. Achieving superalignment
048   necessitates a deep understanding of the decision-making processes of LLMs, including the factors
049   influencing these decisions and potential biases they barbor (Rahwan et al., 2019). Insights into these
050
      processes are essential for mitigating risks and ensuring that AI systems operate in a way that is
      predictable, reliable, and aligned with ethical standards.
051
052   While significant efforts have focused on improving LLM architectures and optimizing hyperparame-
053   ters, there is growing recognition that evaluating these models’ decision-making capabilities requires
      a more interdisciplinary approach that goes beyond traditional performance metrics. By employing

054
      methodologies from cognitive psychology and game theory, researchers can treat LLMs as active
055   participants in structured psychological experiments, providing a more comprehensive assessment
056   of their cognitive abilities compared to human norms (Hagendorff, 2023). For instance, the use of
057   psychology-inspired tests has revealed cognitive biases and different problem-solving approaches
058   of LLMs that extend beyond traditional performance-based metrics, highlighting their limitations
059   in deeper reasoning and causal understanding. A pioneering study by Binz et al. (Binz & Schulz,
060   2023) assessed GPT-3’s cognitive abilities through a series of cognitive experiments, revealing that
061   while GPT-3 can generate superficially appropriate responses, its decision-making falters with deeper
062   reasoning or causal understanding. Subsequent studies have evaluated LLMs’ cognitive performance
063
      from different aspects, such as analogical reasoning, theory of mind, and problem-solving (Webb
      et al., 2023; Kosinski, 2023; Orrù et al., 2023). Hagendorff et al. (Hagendorff et al., 2022; 2023)
064
      explored intuitive and deliberative thinking (System 1 and System 2 processes) in assessing LLMs’
065
      behaviour and reasoning biases. This series of studies identified a significant evolution in LLM capa-
066   bilities from pattern recognition to human-like reasoning and decision-making. The exploration has
067   been extended into social exchange scenarios where strategic thought and game-theoretic reasoning
068   are required. It was found that while LLMs can learn and apply strategies, they struggle with complex
069   strategies like forgiveness and deception, and generalizing across different contexts (Fan et al., 2024;
070   Akata et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024).
071
      This line of research has shown that, despite LLMs’ advancements, they still struggle with human-like
072   strategic reasoning, particularly in complex social and decision-making scenarios . The application
073   of psychological approaches in these studies highlights the potential of interdisciplinary research in
074   advancing AI. Leveraging principles of human cognition could enhance LLM behavior and uncover
075   susceptibilities to biases and manipulations (Yao et al., 2024). Building on these insights, this
076   paper proposes to use a novel adversarial framework (Dezfouli et al., 2020) specifically designed
077   to assess decision-making vulnerabilities in LLMs. Rather than focusing solely on performance
078   in specific tasks, this framework enables systematic probing of LLMs’ decision-making processes
079   under adversarial interactions, providing a structured way to assess how LLMs adapt—or fail to
080
      adapt—when faced with dynamic, strategic opponents.
081   We demonstrate the utility of the adversarial framework through experiments on GPT-3.5, GPT-4,
082   and Gemini-1.5 across two decision-making tasks: the two-armed bandit task and the Multi-Round
083   Trust Task (MRTT). These experiments validate the framework’s ability to uncover model-specific
084   vulnerabilities and provide insights into LLM decision-making mechanisms. Designed to be adaptable,
085   this framework is applicable to a wider range of decision-making scenarios and LLM architectures,
086
      offering a versatile tool for future AI safety research. Notably, the aim of this paper is not to
      comprehensively assess all LLMs but to present a novel approach for evaluating decision-making
087
      alignment in intelligent agents. The primary contributions of this paper are as follows:
088
089          • Adversarial Framework for LLM Evaluation. We introduce a structured adversarial
090            framework to reveal vulnerabilities in LLM decision-making, highlighting biases such as
091            exploitation and risk-seeking behaviors under adversarial influence.
092
             • Superalignment and Ethical Decision-Making. This study addresses the challenge of
093
               aligning advanced LLMs with human values and safety standards by applying cognitive and
094            game-theoretic insights into LLM decision-making processes.
095
096
             • Model-specific vulnerabilities. Our experiments reveal specific vulnerabilities in the
               models, such as exploitation bias in GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5 during the bandit task and
097
               risk-seeking behavior in GPT-3.5. Gemini-1.5 outperformed the other models in the MRTT,
098
               showing better adaptability and balance between risk and cooperation, which suggests that
099            certain LLMs can be trained to maintain fairness and adaptability in dynamic interactions.

      2.2   T HE T WO - ARMED BANDIT TASK
173
174
      We applied the framework to develop adversaries for GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 on two decision-making
175   tasks: the two-armed bandit task and the (MRTT). The bandit task is a repeated, two-alternative
176   forced-choice task based on the bandit task introduced by Dan & Loewenstein (2019). The task
177   includes 100 trials, where the LLM selects between two options and receives instant feedback
178   indicating a reward or no reward after each decision. The adversary assigns rewards to both potential
179   actions with the constraint that each action receives an equal number of potential rewards (25 times).
180   This experiment aims to subtly influence GPT’s preferences and evaluate the adversary’s effectiveness
181   under these constraints.
182
183   2.3   T HE M ULTI -ROUND T URST TASK
184
185   The MRTT is designed as a structured interaction between two participants: the "investor" and the
186   "trustee" (Brooks King-Casas et al., 2005; McCabe et al., 2003). The task contains 10 sequential
187   rounds, with the investor initially receiving 20 monetary units at the outset of each round. The
188
      investor decides how much of this endowment to allocate to the trustee. The experimenter triples the
      invested amount and sends it to the trustee, who can then return any portion of the received amount
189
      as repayment. Cumulative earnings for each participant are calculated by summing their respective
190
      gains from all rounds.

195   3.1   T HE T WO - ARMED BANDIT TASK
196
197   The objective of this experiment was to examine how the LLMs respond to rewards based on their
198   choices in the two-armed bandit task and to assess if an adversary can manipulate their preferences
199   toward a predetermined target action. Data were generated from GPT-3.5, GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5 by
200   providing prompts (as illustrated in Fig 2A) corresponding APIs. The system message established the
201
      context for the LLM’s behaviour and decision-making process within the simulation. In this scenario,
      the LLM plays the role of a space explorer deciding between visiting two planets, X or Y, to find
202
      gold coins. Each trial’s prompt asks the model which planet to visit, with responses and outcomes
203
      from previous trials included. GPT-3.5 and Gemini-1.5 were simulated 200 times, and GPT-4 was
204   simulated 100 times, with each simulation consisting of 100 trials. The reward probability for the
205   two options were the same, both of which were 25%, and the target option was defined as Planet X.
206   We used the dataset provided by Dan & Loewenstein (2019) as a benchmark for human performance
207   on the two-armed bandit task.

305   Adversarial Analysis Simulated data from GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and Gemini-1.5 were used to train a
306   learner model for each LLM. We then trained deep Q-learning models as adversaries to exploit these
307   learners (see Appendix for details of the training parameters). The adversary’s goal was to induce the
308   learner to select a predetermined target action, with a constraint limiting rewards to 25 per action.
309
      The trained adversaries were then evaluated against the LLMs (Fig. 1 D), based on the proportion of
      trials in which the chosen action aligned with the target action (Planet X) before and after adversarial
310
      influence. As shown in Fig. 3B, GPT-3.5 and Gemini-1.5 initially favored the non-target action
311
      (Planet Y), but adversarial influence increased their target selection from 30% to 68% and from 38%
312   to 94%, respectively. GPT-4 showed a mixed preference for either option, averaging a 56% target
313   selection rate, which was increased to 93% under adversarial manipulation. These increases indicate
314   effective adversarial control of LLM decision-making.
315
      Finally, we sought to understand the strategy used by the adversaries and the responses of the LLMs’
316
      when subjected to adversarial strategies. Fig. 4 presents sample adversarial strategies and LLM
317
      responses. The green shaded area represents the probability of the learner model choosing the
318   target option. A higher green area across trials implies the adversary consistently influences the
319   learner model (and hence the LLMs) towards the target action. GPT-3.5’s adversary started by
320   rewarding the target action and then “burned” non-target rewards when it predicted the non-target
321   option would not be chosen. When GPT-3.5 chose the non-target action, the adversary realigned
322   preferences by rewarding the target action. Despite the initial stability, GPT-3.5 began switching
323   actions when target rewards were depleted. This exploratory action suggested a robust decision-
      making characteristic of GPT-3.5 that integrates new information continuously, assessing the potential

      benefits of diverging from established preferences. For GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5, their adversaries
325   assigned disproportionately rewards to the target action at the start to establish baseline preference,

327   to wane. Unlike GPT-3.5, GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5 consistently preferred the target action once

      3.2   M ULTI -ROUND T RUST TASK
333
334
      In the MRTT, the LLM plays the investor and the adversary plays trustee. The adversary’s decisions,
      representing the proportion of money sent back to the investor, are categorized into five actions
335
      (0, 25, 50, 75, and 100%). The objective of the adversary was to influence the LLMs’ investment
336
      decisions to align with its goals. We developed and trained two types of adversaries for each LLM:
337   MAX and FAIR. The MAX adversary aimed to maximize its total gain over 10 rounds, adopting a
338   competitive strategy. The FAIR adversary sought to distribute earnings evenly between itself and
339   the LLM, adopting an equitable strategy. This dichotomy in objectives allowed us to examine how
340   the LLMs respond to different adversarial strategies, revealing their capabilities in complex social
341   exchanges and decision-making processes.
342
      Behavioural Analysis We firstly collected data from GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and Gemini-1.5 playing
343   against with a random trustee (also called as random adversary in the following, i.e. the trustee selects
344   repayment action uniformly at random). The prompts for interacting with the LLM are shown in
345   Fig. 5 A. The system message sets the scenario for the LLM and decision-making process. In each
346   round, the LLM received a summary of previous rounds, including the amount the LLM invested,
347   the consequential action the trustee took, and the total earnings from the transaction in each round.

448   4    D ISCUSSION
449
450
      Our experiments reveal important insights into the decision-making processes of the LLMs compared
451   to humans, expecially in their responses to adversarial strategies in dynamic environments. These
452   findings highlight both the vulnerabilities and strengths of LLM decision-making, with significant
453   implications for real-world applications. The adversarial framework employed in this study was
454   central to understanding the LLMs’ responses to changing conditions and adversarial tactics. By
455   placing LLMs in scenarios where they interacted with adversaries in the bandit task and the MRTT,
456   we were able to observe how different models reacted in situations that involves choice engineering
457   and social exchanges. The adversarial setup revealed nuanced patterns of behavior that would not be
458   apparent through traditional testing.
459   In the bandit task, most human participants exhibited a balanced approach between exploration and
460   exploitation, dynamically adjusting their strategies to capitalize on rewards more effectively (Wilson
461   et al., 2014). In contrast, GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5 showed a stronger tendency to exploit a single
462   option, likely driven by algorithmic optimization of past rewards Binz & Schulz (2023); Nguyen &
463   Satoh (2024). The adversarial framework revealed how this computational bias toward exploitation
464   made their decision-making highly predictable, exposing their vulnerability to manipulation when
465
      interacting with adversarial strategies. While such exploitation minimizes risk, it limits these models’
      ability to adapt to changing conditions, leading to potential inefficiencies in dynamic environments.
466
      GPT-3.5, on the other hand, demonstrated greater flexibility, as the adversarial framework exposed its
467
      tendency to test alternative strategies, especially in response to non-rewarding outcomes. However,
468   this exploratory behavior also made GPT-3.5 more vulnerable to exploitation in the MRTT, where its
469   risk-seeking approach was exploited by the MAX adversary, leading to the largest earnings gap. The
470   framework helped clarify this trade-off: exploration opens opportunities in uncertain environments but
471   can also increase the risk of exploitation as it may lead to testing riskier strategies, while exploitation
472   biases provide stability but reduce adaptability.
473
      In real-world applications—such as finance, healthcare, and autonomous systems—AI systems must
474   strike a careful balance between exploration and exploitation to thrive in dynamic, unpredictable
475   environments. AI models that favor exploitation, as seen with GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5, are prone
476   to predictable behavior, limiting their ability to respond effectively to adversarial tactics or new
477   opportunities. Conversely, while GPT-3.5’s exploratory tendencies allowed it to engage more flexibly
478   with its environment, the framework revealed its susceptibility to adversarial exploitation. These
479   insights emphasize the value of adversarial testing in stress-testing AI decision-making.
480   The MRTT further demonstrated the power of the adversarial framework in uncovering differences in
481   LLM behavior when navigating complex economic exchanges (Xie et al., 2024). The FAIR adversary
482   aimed to foster cooperation, but the conservative strategy adopted by GPT-4 limited its ability to
483   engage fully, despite the adversary’s efforts to encourage greater investment (Rafailov et al., 2024).
484   In contrast, Gemini-1.5’s balanced approach allowed it to adapt dynamically to both the MAX and
485   FAIR adversaries, capitalizing on reciprocal fairness while avoiding excessive exploitation. This
      adaptability enabled Gemini-1.5 to outperform both GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 in maximizing gains while


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      remaining resilient against exploitation. In adversarial settings like cybersecurity or competitive
487   business environments, the ability of AI systems to adjust dynamically is critical for success. For
488   instance, models like GPT-4, which prioritize stability over flexibility (Akata et al., 2023; Huang
489   et al., 2024), risk missing opportunities for reciprocal benefits in contexts that require long-term trust
490   and cooperation, such as negotiations and business partnerships. Additionally, the ethical implications
491   of these findings are significant (Coeckelbergh, 2020). By leveraging the adversarial framework, we
492   showed that AI models must be both robust enough to withstand adversarial manipulation and flexible
493   enough to recognize fairness cues and respond accordingly. The framework’s ability to simulate
494   adversarial and cooperative scenarios enables testing of how AI systems will perform in real-world
495
      contexts where trust, fairness, and adaptability are critical for both success and ethical alignment.
496   In summary, the adversarial framework offers a novel and effective way to assess the strengths and
497   weaknesses of LLM decision-making. It allows researchers to probe how AI systems balance risk and
498   reward, exploration and exploitation, and stability and adaptability in complex, real-world situations.
499   The findings from both the bandit task and the MRTT illustrate the importance of developing AI
500   systems that can dynamically adjust to new information while safeguarding against adversarial
501
      manipulation, which is essential for ensuring the success and ethical alignment of AI systems in
      diverse applications.

504   5    L IMITATION
505
506
      While our study provides valuable insights into the decision-making processes and adversarial
507
      vulnerabilities of three well-known LLMs, several limitations must be acknowledged. The controlled,
508
      structured environments used in our simulations may not fully represent the complexity of real-world
509   scenarios. We focused on two specific tasks, the two-armed bandit task and the MTT, which do
510   not cover all potential decision-making contexts for LLMs. Additionally, the adversarial strategies
511   employed, while effective, might not encompass the full range of real-world tactics. These limitations
512   suggest the need for further research with more diverse scenarios, additional tasks, and broader
513   adversarial strategies to enhance the robustness and applicability of our findings, particularly in the
514   context of AI safety.

      6    C ONCLUSION
518
519   This study employed an adversarial framework to investigate the decision-making behaviors of
520   three LLMs, revealing model-specific strengths and vulnerabilities. In the bandit task, GPT-4 and
521
      Gemini-1.5 exhibited a bias toward exploitation, which made them predictable and susceptible
      to manipulation. GPT-3.5, while showing a more balanced approach between exploration and
522
      exploitation, was still prone to exploitation in adversarial settings due to its risk-seeking behavior.
523
      Gemini-1.5, while showing exploitation tendencies in the bandit task, excelled in the MRTT, where
524   it adapted effectively to both MAX and FAIR adversaries, outperforming the other models in both
525   adversarial and cooperative settings. The adversarial framework proposed in this paper proved
526   to be a powerful tool for testing AI resilience and adaptability in real-world applications such as
527   cybersecurity, finance, and autonomous systems. The findings revealed by the adversarial framework
528   underscore the need for AI models to be not only robust against adversarial manipulation but also
529   flexible enough to respond to fairness cues and changing conditions. They should motivate AI
530   engineers to investigate how to develop better and more robust decision making capabilities which
531   have better strategic flexibility Additionally, integrating interdisciplinary approaches from cognitive
532
      science and game theory will be crucial to developing AI systems that not only perform effectively
      but also align with human values, expectations and ethical standards. By fostering AI that can
533
      dynamically adjust strategies and recognize manipulative patterns, we can ensure safer and more
534
      reliable applications in critical sectors like healthcare and finance.

671   The Deep Q-learning algorithm was used for training the adversary in both the bandit task and the

      the gains of trustee and investor over the whole task. The adversary neural network has three fully
676   connected layers with 128, 128, and 4 units, employing ReLU activation functions for the first two
677   layers and a liner activation function for the final layer. Replay buffer sizes of 200,000 and 400,000
678   were considered. The ϵ-greedy method was used for exploration with ϵ ∈ 0.01, 0.1, 0.2. Learning
679   rates 10−3 , 10−4 , 10−5 were considered for training the adversary using the Adam optimizer. The
680   adversary was simulated against the learner model 200 times and the average bias was calculated
681   to evaluate the performance of the adversary. The optimized combination of parameters for each
682   experiment is shown in Table 2.
