009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Multimodal large language models achieve satisfying performance in complex
013
               reasoning tasks, while still suffers from high model complexity in deployment
               especially for resource-limited devices. In this paper, we propose an automatic
014
               pruning method of large vision-language models for efficient multimodal reasoning.
015
               Conventional methods leverage the training data of the original model to select the
016            proper pruning ratio for different network components, while they are infeasible for
017            large vision-language models due to the unbearable search cost caused by web-scale
018            training corpus. On the contrary, we only use a few samples to search the desired
019            pruning policy by maximizing its generalization ability on the unknown training
020            data despite of the model accuracy, so that the optimal accuracy-efficiency trade-off
021            can be obtained for large vision-language models. Specifically, we formulate the
022            generalization gap for the pruning policy based on the structural risk minimization
023            principle. With the task performance and the generalization ability, we iteratively
024
               search for the optimal pruning policy in the given search space and optimize the
               vision projector to evolve the search space with higher upper bound of performance.
025
               We conduct extensive experiments on ScienceQA, Vizwiz, MM-vet and LLaVA-
026
               Bench datasets for the task of visual question answering. With only 64 samples for
027            pruning policy search, our method achieves 83.05% accuracy on ScienceQA and
028            ×1.47 speedup compared to the dense LLaVA-v1.5-7B model.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
035   Multimodal large language models have been widely adopted in complex reasoning tasks such as
036
      visual question answering (Shao et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023), embodied task planning (Huang et al.,
      2022; Mendez-Mendez et al., 2023) and dialogue systems (Li et al., 2018). Although they achieve
037
      excellent performance in the high-level tasks with rich commonsense, the inference stage of large
038
      vision-language models (LVLMs) in deployment requires large memory footprint and long latency
039   because of the tremendous neurons. In order to deploy the powerful LVLMs on mobile devices such
040   as cellphones, navigation robots and autonomous vehicles with strict computational resource limit,
041   we are required to reduce the model complexity without obvious degradation on the performance.
042
      Network pruning aims to remove unimportant components including layers, channels, tokens and
043
      neurons to reduce the storage and computational complexity without harming the task performance,
044
      which have been widely studied for computer vision (Burtsev et al., 2018; Kong et al., 2022), natural
045   language processing (Sanh et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2023) and system control (Tang et al., 2020). The
046   components importance can be defined as the l1 and l2 magnitude of weights and activations (Sun
047   et al., 2023), and the Jacobian and Hessian matrix regarding the loss functions (Qiao & Yoo, 1999).
048   Since different components usually have various sensitivity to pruning in the downstream tasks,
049   automatic pruning assigns the optimal pruning ratio to each component in order to achieve higher
050   accuracy-efficiency trade-off. In conventional methods, the training data of the original model is
051   leveraged to evaluate the sampled pruning policy based on the accuracy and the model complexity,
052   which provides feedback to the search algorithm to select better candidates. However, LVLMs require
053
      extensive training corpus, and the search cost associated with existing automatic pruning methods is
      prohibitively high.

      Large Vision-language Models: Large Vision-language Models(LVLMs) fuse visual and linguistic
091   modals via bridging the gap between different modalities that achieved outstanding performance
092   such as in-context predictions (Liu et al., 2023; Salewski et al., 2024), multi-image and chain-of-
093   thought reasoning (Yang et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023). To utilize learnable connector module
094   projecting visual information into language modal space, token-level fusion and feature-level fusion
095   are presented for encoding multimodal information to LLMs. For token-level fusion, LLaVA (Liu
096   et al., 2024b) series utilized linear MLPs to align the image tokens with language tokens, finetuning
097   LVLMs with visual instruction samples to enhance zero-shot capabilities. MM1 (McKinzie et al.,
098   2024) compared the significance of varied architectural aspects and found the number of visual
099
      tokens and input resolution was more important than the design of projector. For feature-level fusion,
      Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) inserted extra cross-attention layers to connect pre-trained vision-only
100
      and language-only model, significantly boosting learning efficiency. CogVLM (Wang et al., 2024)
101
      integrated a visual expert module into every Transformer layer to fuse the vision and language
102   features. Existing methods usually focus on the performance of LVLMs, ignoring the real-world
103   deployment particularly in environments with limited resources. Despite light-weight LVLMs such
104   as MobileVLM (Chu et al., 2023), TinyLLaVA (Zhou et al., 2024) and quantinized methods such as
105   GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) have reduced the size of LMMs, the cost still
106   exceeds the resource constraint of mobile devices and they fail to consider the intermodal interaction.
107   Differently, our method focus on pruning the pre-trained LVLMs to find the optimal trade-off between
      accuracy and efficiency with few-shot proxy samples.

      Automatic Pruning: Automatic pruning(Luo & Wu, 2020; Lin et al., 2020) removes unnecessary
      connections, neurons, and layers to reduce the model size, while the model accuracy remains high. The
132
      early work of automotic pruning AMC (He et al., 2018) utilized reinforcement learning to effectively
133
      explore the design space automatically, thereby enhancing the quality of model compression. To
134   achieve fine-grained pruning across samples, dynamic pruning is proposed to predict an instance-wise
135   pruning strategy for each input sample. AutoPrune (Xiao et al., 2019) pruned the network through
136   optimizing a set of trainable parameters instead of original weights. DynamicVit (Rao et al., 2021)
137   progressively and adaptively removed unnecessary tokens based on the input due to the sparse
138   attention. AutoCompress(Liu et al., 2020) introduces an automatic structured pruning framework
139   that improves model compression and inference speed using ADMM-based pruning and heuristic
140   search, achieving high pruning rates. When pruning large pre-trained neural networks, the training
141   set for the original model cannot be acquired. To address this, post-training pruning(Kwon et al.,
142
      2022; Lazarevich et al., 2021) derives the sensitivity of each component to pruning by first-order and
      second-based gradient information. Dynamic Context Pruning (Anagnostidis et al., 2024) utilized a
143
      learnable mechanism that selects uninformative tokens to be removed from the context at various
144
      stages of the generation process. However, traditional automatic pruning methods are not satisfied for
145   LVLMs due to ignoring the generalization gap between training data and few-shot proxy samples.
146   Different from the previous works, our method evolve the search space to find the optimal pruning
147   policy maximizing the generalization ability.

      3      A PPROACH
151
      In this section, as illustrated in Figure 2, we first introduce the preliminary of automatic pruning for
152
      LVLMs. We then formulate the generalization gap between the few-shot proxy samples for pruning
153
      policy search and the overall data distribution, and search the pruning policy with generalizability
154   maximization in given search space. Finally, we evolve the search space to improve the upper bound
155   of accuracy and generalizability ability for all policies. We ensure that the search space remains
156   flexible and evolves dynamically with each iteration, improving the overall pruning performance.

161   network and computational complexity, while minimizing the impact on performance. Automatic

183   Evaluating the true network objective for searched pruning policies is very challenging because of the
184   limited samples compared with the entire training data. Therefore, we maximize the generalization
185   ability of the pruning policy via the structural risk minimization (SRM) principle, which can be
186   bounded by the empirical risk and the unseen data distribution. Structural Risk Minimization (SRM)
187
      is a principle from statistical learning theory that seeks to balance model complexity with training
      data performance, minimizing the risk on both known and unseen data. The optimal trade-off between
188
      complexity and model performance ensures that our approach can be applied effectively in various
189
      real-world applications. For a dataset containing N samples in pruning policy search, the structural
190   risk is written in the following with the probability at least 1 − ϵ:

348   4.2     A BLATION S TUDY
349
350   Effects of generalizable automatic pruning: Automatic pruning determines optimal pruning ratios
351   for various components to achieve the ideal trade-off between accuracy and efficiency. In order
352
      to minimize the generalization gap between the provided samples and training data distribution,
      we significantly enhance the generalization ability of the pruning policy via the structural risk
353
      minimization (SRM) principle. The use of SRM allows us to balance the pruning policy across
354
      layers, taking into account both the accuracy on the proxy samples and the unseen data. This leads to
355   the optimal pruning policy, especially on challenging tasks where overfitting to the proxy samples
356   could otherwise degrade performance. We compare our pruning method with uniform pruning
357   policies (Uniform) and that without considering the generalization ability (Auto w/o Gen). Table 1
358   shows the average pruning ratio of the pruned model and the corresponding accuracy on ScienceQA,
359   where both the automatic pruning and generalization improvement significantly contribute to the
360   accuracy-efficiency trade-off on the acquired pruning policy. Uniform pruning policy ignores the
361   layer-wise importance variance of pre-trained LVLMs, and searching the pruning policy on limited
362   proxy samples without considering the generalization gap substantially degrades the performance of

524   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526
      In this paper, we present SlimLLaVA, an automatically pruning method for LVLMs with limited
527
      sampled data, to achieve low latency and unaffected task performance. We institute the generalization
528   gap of the pruning policy between the calibration samples and the unknown training set for the
529   pruning policy using the structural risk minimization principle. By estimating via the Euclidean
530   distance of the candidate policy set, we enhance the upper bound of the generalization ability for all
531   policies in the pruning space. The pruning policy candidates evolve with the goal of optimizing task
532   performance and generalization by refining the vision projector. SlimLLaVA focuses on minimizing
533   the generalization gap, allowing it to generalize across various scenarios after a single search, without
534   the need for retraining or task-specific fine-tuning. Extensive experiments on question-answer datasets
535   demonstrate that SlimLLaVA achieves competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art pruned
536
      LVLMs.
537   While SlimLLaVA has significantly improved generalization ability and maintained the performance
538   of pruned LVLMs, there are still areas for future work. We aim to develop more efficient search
539   algorithms to reduce the search cost and design a pruning policy that reduces the activation matrix to
      achieve higher compression ratios, further improving inference time.
