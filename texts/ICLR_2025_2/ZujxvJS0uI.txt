011                                          Abstract
012
013           The adoption of machine learning techniques within the domain of drug
014           design provides an opportunity of systematic and eﬀicient exploration of
015           the vast chemical search space. In recent years, advancements in this do-
016
              main have been achieved through the application of deep reinforcement
              learning (DRL) frameworks. However, the scalability and performance of
017
              existing methodologies are constrained by prolonged training periods and
018
              ineﬀicient sample data utilization. Furthermore, generalization capabilities
019           of these models have not been fully investigated. To overcome these lim-
020           itations, we take a multi-objective optimization perspective and introduce
021           SMORE-DRL, a fragment and transformer-based multi-objective DRL ar-
022           chitecture for the optimization of molecules across multiple pharmacolog-
023           ical properties, including binding aﬀinity to a cancer protein target. Our
024           approach involves pretraining a transformer-encoder model on molecules
025           encoded by a novel hybrid fragment-SMILES representation method. Fine-
026           tuning is performed through a novel gradient-alignment-based DRL, where
027
              lead molecules are optimized by selecting and replacing their fragments with
              alternatives from a fragment dictionary, ultimately resulting in more desir-
028
              able drug candidates. Our findings indicate that SMORE-DRL is superior
029
              to current DRL models for lead optimization in terms of quality, eﬀiciency,
030           scalability, and robustness. Furthermore, SMORE-DRL demonstrates the
031           capability of generalizing its optimization process to lead molecules that
032           are not present during the pretraining or fine-tuning phases.

035   1   Introduction
036
037   Successfully developing a drug is a tremendously time-consuming, expensive and diﬀicult
038   endeavour. On average, it takes 10-15 years and costs $1–2 billion USD to deliver a new
039
      drug to market (Sun et al., 2022). The objective of drug design is to identify molecules that
      exhibit multiple pharmacological properties characteristic of pharmaceutical-grade drugs,
040
      ensuring they are safe and eﬀicacious. Drug design thus can be modelled as a multi-objective
041
      optimization (MOO) problem. One of the main challenges of drug design is effectively
042   navigating the immense chemical search space, which is estimated to contain 1020 and 10200
043   possible drug-like molecules (Brown, 2015).
044
045
      Machine learning methods, including deep reinforcement learning (DRL), offer a promising
      solution to this problem (Al-Jumaily et al., 2023; Goel et al., 2021; Gottipati et al., 2021;
046
      Kim et al., 2021; Pereira et al., 2021; Popova et al., 2018; Ståhl et al., 2019; Tang et al.,
047
      2023; Wang & Zhu, 2024; Yang et al., 2021). Molecular optimization is a process that
048   requires a model to perform minor modifications on a lead molecule to improve its drug-like
049   qualities while preserving structural similarity. As molecules with comparable structures
050   are anticipated to exhibit similar behaviours, this approach aims at preventing the model
051   from producing unrealistic or undesirable molecules. This differs from molecular generation,
052   where a model’s task is to generate novel and diverse compounds from scratch (Ståhl et al.,
053   2019). Additionally, present methodologies are hindered by lengthy training requirements
      and sub-optimal use of training data, resulting in impaired scalability and performance.


                                                   1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In this work, we present SMORE-DRL (Scalable Multi-Objective Robust and Eﬀicient
055   Deep Reinforcement Learning), a gradient-alignment-based multi-objective DRL (MODRL)
056   framework for molecular optimization. The key contributions of this research include: (1)
057   molecular optimization is modelled naturally as a Pareto-based multi-objective reinforce-
058   ment learning problem where the challenges of gradient dominance and conflict are addressed
059   with gradient alignment inspired by a study from multi-task learning; (2) a novel molecular
060   tokenization strategy is proposed to represent the a molecule as a hybrid of fragments and
061   SMILES, enabling eﬀicient policy learning and effective representation of any new molecules;
062   and (3) a synergistic integration of gradient alignment, hybrid fragment-SMILES representa-
063
      tion, contrastive learning, and a transformer-encoder allows for scalability and generalization
      capability superior to existing MODRL methods. Moreover, SMORE-DRL demonstrated
064
      its ability to effectively scale and generalize its optimization process to new molecules after
065
      fine-tuning. This is a particularly notable aspect of our work, as existing DRL methods lack
066   scalability and their generalization capacities are under-explored in current literature.

069   2     Related Work
070
071   The fundamental techniques of MODRL and aligned multi-task learning, closely related to
072
      this study, are reviewed below. In addition, see Appendix A.1 for a review of transformer-
      encoder architectures and MLM.
073
074
075   2.1   Deep Reinforcement Learning
076
      Reinforcement learning (RL) agents learn through a trial-and-error process guided by the
077
      Markov decision process (MDP). See Appendix A.2 for an introduction to basic RL concepts.
078   When the RL task entails exploring a vast state or action space, as is often the case in drug
079   design, learning an exact optimal policy or value function can become computationally
080   intractable. Thus, DRL is used to approximate policies or value functions (Arulkumaran
081   et al., 2017). The actor-critic framework approximates both and has been leveraged by
082   various drug development frameworks (Al-Jumaily et al., 2023; Goel et al., 2021; Gottipati
083   et al., 2021; Pereira et al., 2021; Popova et al., 2018; Ståhl et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2023;
084   Wang & Zhu, 2024; Yang et al., 2021). The actor model is responsible for learning a
085   parameterized policy πθA . This is guided by feedback known as temporal difference (TD)
086
      error from the critic model, which evaluates the actor’s actions based on the state. One
      approach to this is by learning the advantage function Aπ (s, a) = Qπ (s, a) − V π (s), which
087
      measures the desirability of taking action a compared to alternative actions available from
088
      state s (Graesser & Keng, 2019).
089
090
      2.2   Multi-Objective Deep Reinforcement Learning
091
092   MODRL is a domain within machine learning and also a family within MOO focused on
093   simultaneously optimizing two or more objectives (Liu et al., 2015). In an MODRL setting,
094   the reward function is extended to a vector of size K, which represents K different objectives
095   (Nguyen et al., 2020). MODRL approaches have been developed for molecular design. Zhou
096   et al. (2019) developed Molecule Deep Q-Networks (MolDQN), a multi-objective molecular
097   optimization framework that implements double deep Q-learning (DDQN) and randomized
098   value functions. For the optimization task, an episode starts with a seed lead molecule and
099
      in each timestep, MolDQN optimizes the molecule through one of the following actions: (1)
      atom addition, (2) bond addition, and (3) bond removal. A linear weighted sum method
100
      is used for MOO. Deep Fragment-based Multi-Parameter Optimization (DeepFMPO), in-
101
      troduced by Ståhl et al. (2019), is an actor-critic multi-objective method for molecular
102   optimization. In this work, a library of fragments is derived from a set of lead molecules
103   and fragments are encoded using a balanced binary tree such that similar molecules have
104   similar binary encoding. One modification step involves replacing a fragment in the lead
105   molecule with a similar fragment from the fragment library. A constrained reward function
106   is used, where a molecule is either assigned a constant positive reward for each objective
107   achieved or a reward of zero. If all objectives are met, the reward is doubled. A dynamic
      reward mechanism is also implemented, where the model is penalized if it begins to under-


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      perform compared to previous epochs. Bolcato et al. (2022) expand DeepFMPO to include
109   3D-shape and electrostatics in the similarity measurements. This extension was applied
110   because a seemingly minor alteration to a SMILES string can significantly impact its 3D
111   structure. Consequently, the revised representation of fragments is suggested to achieve a
112   more precise similarity measure. When recognizing the existence of other RL approaches
113   for molecular optimization, the three aforementioned MODRL approaches representatively
114   form the benchmarks to compare with our proposed framework.

116   2.3   Aligned Multi-Task Learning
117
118   Two potential issues that arise when solving an MOO problem directly using gradient descent
119   are dominating and conflicting gradients. A dominating objective gradient is characterized
120
      by the largest magnitude, which leads to a bias in the solution favouring the corresponding
      task (Senushkin et al., 2023). When two objective gradients are conflicting, an increase
121
      in the solution towards one objective decreases the solution for the conflicting objective.
122
      Conflicting gradients are characterized by having a negative cosine similarity (Yu et al.,
123   2020). To address these challenges in the context of multi-task learning, Senushkin et al.
124   (2023) propose aligned-multi-task learning (AMTL). Let Lk (θ) represent the objective of
125   task k, where there are K > 1 tasks that are associated with a set of model parameters θ.
126   The training objective is to converge to a set of θ ∗ defined as follows:

131   To mitigate conflicting and dominating gradients, AMTL aligns the principal components
132   of an initial linear system of gradients. This process can be interpreted as re-scaling the
133   axes of a coordinate system that is determined by the principal components, such that the
134
      minimal singular value of the gradient matrices is identified and all other singular values
      are adjusted to match it, resulting in aligned gradients. Subsequently, these aligned gra-
135
      dients are combined into a common gradient (Senushkin et al., 2023). Inspired by AMTL
136
      which formulates a multi-task learning problem to an MOO problem, we integrate the same
137   gradient alignment technique to solve MODRL for drug design in this study. A detailed
138   description of AMTL-based MODRL can be found in Appendix A.3.

141   3     Methods
142
143   In this section, we discuss the data pre-processing, pretraining, and fine-tuning processes
144
      carried out in our SMORE-DRL framework.
145
146   3.1   Data Preparation: Fragments-SMILES Hybrid Tokenization Strategy
147
      The dataset used for transformer-encoder pretraining is the MolGen task of the Therapeutics
148
      Data Commons (TDC) (Huang et al., 2021), a set of the ChEMBL, MOSES, and ZINC-250K
149
      datasets. We canonicalized all SMILES strings, and only kept strings with a maximum of
150   100 characters, resulting in a pretraining dataset of 4 million molecules. We then fragmented
151   each molecule using the fragmentation method from HierVAE by Jin et al. (2020), which
152   breaks single bonds extending from ring atoms (Ståhl et al., 2019). This method is also
153   used by DeepFMPO (Ståhl et al., 2019) and DeepFMPOv3D (Bolcato et al., 2022).
154
      While fragmentation is a good technique for reducing the chemical search space, it may
155
      result in a vast token dictionary size. Figure 4 in Appendix A.4 is a fragment frequency
156   chart based on the 4-million molecule dataset, which shows that nearly 68,000 fragments
157   were extracted. Most of these fragments are rarely encountered in the dataset, with 95%
158   appearing fewer than 100 times. Moreover, building a token dictionary solely from the
159   pretraining dataset will create obstacles during fine-tuning tasks. Given the sparse nature
160   of fragment occurrences, it is likely that molecules used for fine-tuning will contain fragments
161   not present in the dictionary, especially if the fine-tuning dataset differs from the one used
      for pretraining.


                                                     3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To reduce the dictionary size while still representing fragments that are absent or infre-
163   quently encountered, we propose a novel hybrid tokenization strategy that uses both frag-
164   ments and SMILES. Following the construction of a fragment dictionary from the pretraining
165   dataset, we append tokens for SMILES and exclude all fragments that appear less than twice.
166   This reduces the dictionary size from 68,000 to approximately 41,130 tokens. As a result, if
167   a molecule contains a fragment not found in the reduced dictionary, that fragment is repre-
168   sented atom by atom. See Appendix A.4 for a diagram of the hybrid tokenization strategy.
169   As part of our ablation studies in Section 4.2, we demonstrate that further reducing the
170   token dictionary by retaining only the most frequently encountered fragments (resulting in
171
      molecules being primarily represented by SMILES atoms) hinders training performance.
172
173   3.2     Pretraining
174
175   SMORE-DRL utilizes a transformer-encoder model inspired by architectural aspects of the
176   Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) model introduced in
177   MTL-BERT by Zhang et al. (2022), a multi-task learning model pretrained on SMILES
178   strings and fine-tuned for downstream ADMET tasks. Rather than representing molecules
179
      by SMILES atom tokens as was done in MTL-BERT, we adopt our hybrid fragment token
      representation. SMORE-DRL employs a combination of two pretraining tasks: MLM and
180
      contrastive learning.
181
182
183   3.2.1    Masked Language Model (MLM)
184
      Given a training batch of molecules, they are fragmented and encoded into their token
185
      representations. Unlike the static masking technique used for the MLM in the original BERT
186
      model, where sequences are masked once and reused throughout training, we employ the
187   dynamic approach introduced by Liu et al. (2019) in Robustly Optimized BERT Pretraining
188   Approach (RoBERTa). In each training batch, 20% of the tokens are randomly selected for
189   masking, with 90% of those selected tokens end up being masked. If a selected token
190   is part of a sequence of atom tokens representing a fragment that does not exist in the
191   token dictionary, 20% of that fragment’s atom token sequence is also masked. The masked
192   molecule token sequence is then passed into the encoder model, which attempts to accurately
193   reconstruct the original values of the masked tokens. See Appendix A.5 for a diagram of
194   the MLM process.
195
196   3.2.2    Contrastive Learning
197
198   We further refine the SMORE-DRL’s contextual understanding of molecules by allowing it
199
      to align its representations of similar molecules. This is particularly valuable during fine-
      tuning, where the model is tasked with optimizing lead molecules over multiple timesteps
200
      while ensuring that the optimized molecules in the earlier timesteps retain chemical sim-
201
      ilarity to the original lead molecule. To accomplish this, we introduce a straightforward
202   contrastive learning technique that builds upon the MLM approach. Rather than directly
203   masking tokens in the fragment sequence as previously described, a “separation” token is
204   inserted at the end of the sequence, followed by an augmented version of that sequence.
205   Augmentation involves randomly selecting a token to replace with a fragment token from
206   the token dictionary. If the selected token belongs to a sequence of atom tokens represent-
207   ing a fragment that is not in the token dictionary, the entire SMILES atom sequence for
208   that fragment is replaced with a randomly selected fragment token. The masking process
209   consists of keeping the original molecule sequence fully visible to the model, while masking
210
      25% of the augmented sequence using the same technique described earlier. See Appendix
      A.5 for a diagram of the contrastive learning process.

253   3.3.2   Reward System
254
255
      To assess a molecule’s potential as a drug candidate, we use the following three properties:
      (1) logarithm of partition coeﬀicient (ClogP), which impacts a drug’s administration, ab-
256
      sorption, transport and excretion, (2) synthetic accessibility score (SAS), which measures
257
      the diﬀiculty of synthesizing a molecule, and (3) binding aﬀinity score (BAS) to LPA1,
258   which quantifies the binding capability of a drug to a target protein (Brown, 2015; Ertl &
259   Schuffenhauer, 2009; Li et al., 2019). However, the number and type of properties can be
260   tailored to any specific optimization task. RDKit is used for ClogP and SAS calculations,
261   and QuickVina2-GPU-2.1 (Tang et al., 2024) was used to calculate BAS. Lysophosphatidic
262   acid receptor 1 (LPA1/LPAR1), a bioactive lipid mediator primarily derived from membrane
263   phospholipids, is chosen as the target protein for BAS. LPA Receptors (LPARs) have been
264   found to be over-expressed in multiple types of cancer, with LPA1 specifically expressed in
265   ovarian cancer, breast cancer, liver cancer, gastric cancer, pancreatic cancer, lung cancer,
266
      glioblastoma and osteosarcoma. LPA1 promotes metastasis and tumor motility, making it a
      natural choice for targeting in efforts to inhibit cancer spread and cell movement (Lin et al.,
267
      2021).

414   To examine scalability, SMORE-DRL was trained for 70 epochs using 10,000 molecules-5,000
415   from the DrugBank database (Wishart et al., 2018) and 5,000 from the Collection of Open
416   Natural Products (COCONUT) database (Sorokina et al., 2021). COCONUT molecules
417
      were included to attempt to test the model’s robustness, as they are different from those
      typically encountered during pretraining. All lead molecules were optimized over 4 timesteps
418
      per epoch, with those from the final timestep of the last epoch used for comparisons. While
419
      the baseline model was run five times, ablation studies were also conducted. These included:
420   (1) without the use of AMTL, (2) with a weight emphasis on the BAS reward (0.25 for
421   ClogP, 0.25 for SAS, and 0.5 for BAS), (3) with the freezing of encoder weights for all
422   agents, (4) with the use of pretraining on the 100-frequency dictionary, and (5) with the use
423   of pretraining on the 1000-frequency dictionary. Figure 3 demonstrates that freezing encoder
424   weights and pretraining on the 100-frequency and 1000-frequency dictionaries significantly
425   impair the model’s learning progress. As such, these experiments were limited to a single
426   run of 25 epochs and excluded from further analysis. To evaluate the impact of omitting
427   AMTL and placing greater emphasis on the BAS reward, these model variations were run
428
      three times for 70 epochs, while the baseline model ran five timess. Presented results are
      based on run averages.
429
430   As displayed in Figure 3, incorporating AMTL improves training stability and enhances the
431   overall quality of optimized molecules. Findings in Table 3 support this by demonstrating
      that omitting AMTL significantly impairs most properties, namely uniqueness.

      5   Conclusion
518
519   In this work, we present SMORE-DRL, a scalable gradient-alignment-based MODRL frame-
520   work for molecular optimization. A novel hybrid fragment-SMILES representation to depict
521   molecules enables SMORE-DRL to selects and replace fragments in the lead molecules with
522   alternatives from the fragment dictionary, resulting in improved drug candidates. This is
523   achieved by using three agents: a masker, actor and critic, all pretrained on MLM and con-
524   trastive learning tasks. SMORE-DRL excelled as a lead molecular optimizer, significantly
525   outperforming other MODRL models while demonstrating scalability. Furthermore, when
526   evaluated on new molecules post fine-tuning, SMORE-DRL effectively generalized its opti-
527
      mization process. The next development of SMORE-DRL will include additional measures
      to encourage the model to produce molecules that are as effective as those in the current ver-
528
      sion, but with greater similarity to lead compounds. The implementation of SMORE-DRL
529
      is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SMORE-DRL-F38B.

775   A.3   AMTL-based MODRL Algorithm
776
777   For the MODRL training, we aim to use the gradient modulation method AMTL (Senushkin
778   et al., 2023) for policy learning. AMTL specifically addresses the multi-task optimization
779   challenges, i.e., gradient dominance and gradient conflicts, by aligning principal components
780   of a gradient matrix. The existence of conflicting or dominating gradients disrupts the
781   stability of the training process and leads to a deterioration in overall performance.
782   It is acknowledged that the gradient dominance can be measured with a gradient magni-
783   tude similarity (Yu et al., 2020), and a cosine distance between vectors can measure the
784   gradient conflicts (Liu et al., 2021). However, the two metrics cannot offer a comprehensive
785   assessment if taken in isolation. One of the key components of AMTL is the proposal of the
786   condition number, a stability criterion that can indicate the presence of both challenges.

      values of the corresponding matrix. Minimizing the condition number of the linear system

       A.10   Discussion
1351
1352   In this paper, we introduce SMORE-DRL, a novel transformer-based MODRL model for
1353   molecular optimization. Three sets of experiments were conducted to evaluate the model’s
1354   performance: (1) a comparative study against DeepFMPO, DeepFMPOv3D, and MolDQN,
1355   three MODRL molecular optimization models, tasked with optimizing 1,000 lead molecules,
1356   (2) a scalability study, where the model was tasked with optimizing 10,000 lead molecules,
1357   and (3) a generalization study to assess how well the model, after training in the scalability
1358
       study, can optimize 40,000 lead molecules in a test scenario.
1359   SMORE-DRL demonstrated outstanding performance in all experiments. In the compara-
1360   tive study, it significantly outperformed all other models. In the scalability study, SMORE-
1361   DRL performed eﬀiciently, optimizing a set of lead molecules that did not achieve all proper-
1362   ties so that one third of produced molecules satisfied all properties. Additionally, SMORE-
1363   DRL’s robustness allowed it to successfully generalize its optimization approach to unseen
1364
       molecules. With just two modification steps, it improved the lead molecules from 0% to 19%
       achieving all target properties. The inclusion of AMTL has proven to be a vital component
1365
       of SMORE-DRL, enhancing training stability and improving the overall performance.
1366
1367   As discussed, the primary objective of molecular optimization is developing a novel molecule
1368   similar to a lead molecule, aiming to have both molecules exhibit comparable qualities. As
1369   such, the progression of SMORE-DRL’s optimized molecules were analyzed by comparing
1370
       their similarity to lead molecules and their corresponding rewards across all timesteps. Fig-
       ure 15 depicts the average similarity and reward for each of the four optimization timesteps
1371
       performed on 1,000 molecules during the scalability study. To measure similarity, we utilize
1372
       the method described in DeepFMPO (Ståhl et al., 2019), which employs a combination
1373   of maximum common substructure Tanimoto similarity and Levenshtein distance. A sim-
1374   ilarity score greater than or equal to 0.7 indicates high similarity, while a score between
1375   0.5 and 0.7 is considered medium similarity (Loeffler et al., 2024). While SMORE-DRL
1376   does not achieve high similarity, it still presents strong results. As seen in Figure 15, there
1377   is an inverse correlation between average similarity and average sum of rewards across all
1378   objectives, where as similarity decreases, reward increases. This represents a trade-off: re-
1379   stricting the optimization process to minimal modifications of a lead molecule may result in
1380   high similarity, but will likely restrict exploration and hinder the development of superior
1381
       candidates. Nonetheless, the next iteration of SMORE-DRL should balance exploration
       with maintaining similarity to lead molecules, aiming to generate high-quality compounds
1382
       without sacrificing similarity. One possible approach involves incorporation of a dynamic
1383
       similarity component into the reward function, allowing for exploration in the initial training
1384   epochs while penalizing molecules with low similarity to lead molecules in the later epochs.
