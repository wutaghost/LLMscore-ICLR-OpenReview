011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            There has been a growing interest in the ability of neural networks to solve al-
014            gorithmic tasks, such as arithmetic, summary statistics, and sorting. While state-
015
               of-the-art models like Transformers have demonstrated good generalization per-
               formance on in-distribution tasks, their out-of-distribution (OOD) performance is
016
               poor when trained end-to-end. In this paper, we focus on value generalization, a
017
               common instance of OOD generalization where the test distribution has the same
018            input sequence length as the training distribution, but the value ranges in the train-
019            ing and test distributions do not necessarily overlap. We propose that using fixed
020            positional encodings to determine attention weights – referred to as positional at-
021            tention – enhances empirical OOD performance while maintaining expressivity.
022            We support our claim about expressivity by proving that Transformers with posi-
023            tional attention can simulate parallel algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are versatile models used in various applications, including vi-
028
      sion (Yuan et al., 2021; Khan et al., 2022; Dehghani et al., 2023) and natural language processing
029
      (Wei et al., 2022b; Touvron et al., 2023). Their effectiveness in complex tasks is particularly no-
030   table in Large Language Models (LLMs) (Wang et al., 2018; Hendrycks et al., 2021), where they
031   excel at generating coherent text and understanding context. This strong performance has led to an
032   increased interest in understanding the Transformer architecture as a computational model capable
033   of executing instructions and solving algorithmic reasoning problems.
034
      In this context, Pérez et al. (2021); Wei et al. (2022a) show that Transformers are Turing Complete,
035
      and Giannou et al. (2023); Back De Luca & Fountoulakis (2024); Yang et al. (2024) demonstrate
036   that Transformers can effectively encode instructions to solve linear algebra and graphs problems.
037   Additionally, it has been shown that Transformers can perform reasoning tasks using far fewer layers
038   than the number of reasoning steps (Liu et al., 2023), indicating a connection between Transformers
039   and parallel algorithms. To this end, Sanford et al. (2024) further demonstrates that Transformers
040   can simulate the Massively Parallel Computation (MPC) model (Andoni et al., 2018), which is based
041   on the MapReduce framework for large-scale data processing (Dean & Ghemawat, 2008).
042   Complementing this theoretical framework, empirical studies have demonstrated the capabilities of
043   Transformers, among other models, in executing algorithms (Veličković & Blundell, 2021). No-
044   table applications include basic arithmetic (Lee et al., 2024), sorting (Tay et al., 2020; Yan et al.,
045   2020), dynamic programming (Dudzik & Veličković, 2022; Ibarz et al., 2022b), and graph problems
046   (Veličković et al., 2022; Cappart et al., 2023).
047
      Despite promising empirical results, these approaches rely on additional supervision, such as inter-
048   mediate labels of existing algorithms (Veličković et al., 2022) or self-supervised learning techniques
049   to incorporate algorithmic information (Rodionov & Prokhorenkova, 2023). While extra supervision
050   helps guide the model toward solutions with some degree of OOD generalization, it is not only more
051   expensive to train but also requires knowledge of the underlying algorithmic solution to the problem.
052   Furthermore, this additional supervision can limit the model’s ability to derive alternative solutions
053   independently, reducing their functionality to simply simulating a predetermined algorithm. On the
      other hand, training models like Transformers end-to-end, without additional supervision, on algo-

077   It has been shown both theoretically and empirically that aligning the model’s architecture with
078   the algorithm improves generalization (Xu et al., 2020). While it is well-known that Transformers

083   Regarding OOD generalization, we focus on settings where the input lengths are the same, but the
084   values in the test set have a different or larger magnitude than those in the training set. We call this
085   type of OOD generalization value generalization and provide a formal definition in Section 3. Value
086   generalization is particularly important since, when learning to solve an algorithmic task, the model
087   is expected to be able to perform such a task on a range of numbers that it might not have seen during
088   training. This should also serve as an indication that the model learns to solve the problem.
089   Our contributions: We examine Transformers with positional attention (positional Transformers)
090   from the perspective of expressivity and OOD value generalization in algorithmic reasoning tasks.
091
092       1. (OOD generalization) We empirically demonstrate an average 1000× improvement (ranging
093          from 400× to 3000×) in OOD value generalization for positional Transformers compared to
094          traditional Transformers during end-to-end training on various algorithmic tasks (Section 6).
095       2. (Expressivity) We prove that positional Transformers can simulate any algorithm defined in
096          a parallel computation model, defined in Section 5, which we call Parallel Computation with
097          Oracle Communication (PCOC).

100   2    R ELATED WORK
101
102   Empirical: Several studies focus on the empirical aspects of training neural networks to execute
103   algorithms, achieving promising results in OOD generalization. Notable examples include Yan
104   et al. (2020); Ibarz et al. (2022a); Diao & Loynd (2023); Bevilacqua et al. (2023); Engelmayer
105   et al. (2023); Rodionov & Prokhorenkova (2023). However, all these works rely on some form of
106   additional supervision during training, with some differing in the type of supervision employed.
107   Specifically, Rodionov & Prokhorenkova (2023) leverages problem-specific information within a
      self-supervised training framework, whereas the other studies utilize intermediate labels to guide


                                                          2
108
      the learning process. For instance, Engelmayer et al. (2023) demonstrates that using intermediate
109   labels derived from parallel algorithms leads to improved performance for parallelizable tasks.
110
111   From the perspective of OOD generalization, most works focus on length generalization (Veličković
112
      et al., 2022; Minder et al., 2023), i.e., testing on longer inputs, with a few exceptions address-
      ing graphs with different connectivity distributions (Georgiev et al., 2023) as well as graphs with
113
      varying sizes, edge weights, and connectivity patterns (Tang et al., 2020). In the context of value
114
      generalization, (Klindt, 2023) shows how simple neural network models fail to learn to sum two
115   numbers in a way that generalizes to larger values. In length generalization, some studies employ
116   digitization schemes, such as binary numbers or tokenization (Kaiser & Sutskever, 2016; Lee et al.,
117   2024; Shen et al., 2023; Ruoss et al., 2023), which can also imply value generalization. However,
118   these operations are typically restricted to processing only two numbers, and the values involved
119   are all integers. In contrast, our work operates on real numbers and supports processing multiple
120   elements simultaneously instead of just two.
121   While some research, such as Kazemnejad et al. (2023), investigates the role of different positional
122   encodings in length generalization, to the best of our knowledge, no existing work examines the use
123   of positional attention within the context of neural algorithmic reasoning. The closest formulation
124   to the positional Transformer architecture presented in Section 4 is the position-based attention
125   proposed by Schmidt & Di Gangi (2023), but it is used in the context of neural machine translation.
126
      Theoretical: From a theoretical perspective, the most closely related work to ours is Sanford et al.
127   (2024), which presents simulation results for Transformers within the Massively Parallel Compu-
128   tation (MPC) model. This approach uses local computations over the input data to determine the
129   destinations for communication between machines. In contrast, our method encodes this communi-
130   cation information directly within the network parameters, eliminating the need for destinations to
131   depend on the input. Consequently, we adopt a different parallel computational model.
132
      Other relevant studies demonstrate the expressive power of neural networks through simulation re-
133   sults. For instance, Siegelmann & Sontag (1995) establishes the Turing completeness of recurrent
134   neural networks (RNNs), while Hertrich & Skutella (2023) presents specific RNN constructions that
135   solve the shortest paths problem and provide approximate solutions to the Knapsack problem. Ad-
136   ditionally, other simulation results focused on Transformers have shown their Turing completeness
137   (Pérez et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022a) as well as demonstrated constructive solutions to linear algebra
138   and graph-related problems (Giannou et al., 2023; Back De Luca & Fountoulakis, 2024; Yang et al.,
139   2024). In our work, we are also motivated by the concept of algorithmic alignment (Xu et al., 2020),
140   demonstrating that further aligning the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) with parallel
141
      algorithms can lead to better empirical performance.

      We denote by N = {1, 2, 3, . . . } the set of natural numbers. We use [n] to refer to the set
146
      {1, 2, . . . , n} for n ∈ N. For a set S we denote by P(S) its power set (i.e. the set containing
147   all subsets of S).
148
149   Out-of-distribution generalization. Out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization broadly refers to
150
      the ability of a supervised learning model to “perform well” when evaluated on data that are drawn
      from a distribution that is different from the one used to generate the training data. Two quantitative
151
      measures of OOD generalization are given below.
152
153   Definition 1 (OOD risk). Let X be the feature space, Y be the set of labels, and let h : X → Y be
154   the hypothesis returned by a supervised learning algorithm where the training data are sampled from
155   a distribution Dtrain on X × Y. Let Dtest be a different distribution on X × Y. The out-of-distribution
156
      risk of h with respect to Dtest is defined as RDtest (h) = E(x,y)∼Dtest [ℓ(h(x), y)].
157   Definition 2 (Empirical OOD   Pnrisk). Using the same setting as Definition 1 we define the Empirical
158   OOD risk as RS (h) = n1 i=1 ℓ(h(xi ), yi ), where S = ((x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )) are drawn i.i.d.
159   from the distribution Dtest .
160
161   Models that achieve low (empirical) OOD risk are said to OOD-generalize. We now define value
      generalization, which is the type of OOD generalization that we are concerned with in this work.


                                                         3
162
      Definition 3 (Value generalization). We use the term value generalization to refer to the following
163   particular case of OOD generalization. The feature space is Euclidean, i.e., X ⊆ Rk for some
164
      k ∈ N. There exists a ground-truth labeling function ĥ which maps every x ∈ X to its true label
165
      y = ĥ(x) ∈ Y, i.e., Dtrain and Dtest are completely characterized by their marginalization onto X ,
166
      denoted by Dtrain (X ) and Dtest (X ), respectively. We say that a model value-generalizes from Dtrain
167   to Dtest if it achieves low OOD risk and supp(Dtest (X ))\supp(Dtrain (X )) ̸= ∅.1
168
169   Note that the quantities used to measure OOD generalization (and value generalization in partic-
170   ular) do not assume anything regarding the overlap between the training and test distributions. In
171   the context of learning, this can lead to artificially low OOD risk when there is significant overlap
172   between Dtrain and Dtest . Therefore, the interesting cases are those where samples from Dtest are
173
      unlikely to have been sampled from Dtrain . In the context of value generalization, this translates to
      Px∼Dtest (X ) (x ∈ supp(Dtrain (X ))) being low. That is, the probability that a test sample lies in the do-
174
      main of the training distribution should be small. In our experiments, this probability is sufficiently
175
      small (see Appendix D for details), so a low test error indicates “true” value generalization.
176
177   In the context of neural algorithmic reasoning, good value generalization (with minimal overlap
178   between test and training distributions) provides a strong indication that a model has learned to
179
      execute an algorithm. This is explained by the fact that an algorithm consists of a fixed sequence of
      instructions that does not change when the input values change.

184   We now define the positional Transformer, as an adaptation of the Transformer model (Vaswani
185   et al., 2017). The motivation for positional Transformers stems from the observation that in many
186   real-world parallel algorithms, communication between machines is independent of the specific data
187   being processed. Analogously, since the mixture of data is handled by attention, we hypothesize that
188
      two factors contribute to poor scale generalization in Transformers: (i) the inclusion of input values
      in the computation of the attention weights and (ii) the use of positional encodings in the input
189
      matrix. To address this, we decouple input values from attention weight computation and remove
190
      positional encodings from the input matrix, leading to positional attention. In practice, we validate
191   our empirically validate our hypothesis, demonstrating that positional Transformers achieve better
192   scale generalization within specific algorithmic reasoning tasks.

223   5     E XPRESSIVITY OF P OSITIONAL T RANSFORMERS
224
225   We prove that our architecture can simulate algorithms within a parallel computational model, which
226   we refer to as Parallel Computation with Oracle Communication (PCOC). We first describe the main
227   features of PCOC, followed by its definition, and then discuss its limitations. It is important to note
228   that the simulation result is theoretical. In practice, the model could converge to a parameter setting
229
      that does not correspond to an interpretable algorithm. However, the simulation result is significant
      as it demonstrates the minimal capabilities of our architecture in theory. Such theoretical approaches
230
      have been employed in previous works. For example, see Sanford et al. (2024); Loukas (2020).
231
232
      5.1     PARALLEL C OMPUTATION WITH O RACLE C OMMUNICATION (PCOC)
233
234   The PCOC model consists of two steps at each round. The first step is communication, where
235   machines send and receive data from other machines. The communication pattern can change at
236   every round. The second step is computation, where all machines perform some local computation
237   on data stored in their local memory.
238
      Oracle communication. For each length n and input data, we assume the existence of an oracle
239   that provides the destination for each machine and message at each round. The oracle executes
240   a communication pattern agnostic to the data being processed. This contrasts with other parallel
241   models, such as the Massively Parallel Computation (MPC) model (Andoni et al., 2018), where it is
242   assumed that the processed data can influence communication patterns. At first glance, introducing
243   such an oracle might not seem particularly useful, especially because it is fixed for each input data,
244   where the data can be real-valued, which implies potentially unaccountably many communication
245   oracles for a particular task. However, its importance lies in the fact that for a variety of parallel
246   algorithms, the communication pattern at each round depends only on the length of the input, and it
247
      is independent of other input data. For example, in algorithms that compute basic statistics such as
      the sum or minimum, or tasks like sorting a list of numbers, the communication between machines is
248
      determined not by their values but by their positions. This means that if the values at each machine
249
      change, the communication pattern established between machines for a given task and input length
250   remains the same. In Appendix A, we illustrate communication patterns for some of these tasks,
251   which are also addressed in the experiments in Section 6.
252
253
      The observation that, for many algorithms, communication is agnostic to the data also informs the
      design of our architecture. On the other hand, since the proposed positional attention does not
254
      rely on input data, simulating a model that determines destinations based on input data might be
255
      inefficient. Such cases reflect algorithms where the communication pattern varies as a function of
256   the input values. In these instances, PCOC may not be the most appropriate model to consider, a
257   point we further explore in the limitations section below.
258

292   5.2     L IMITATIONS OF PCOC
293
294   While PCOC offers great flexibility in executing parallel tasks, it should be noted that the oracle
295   communication scheme can be limiting. The computational model in PCOC is subjected to a given
296   oracle. In contrast, in other models, like MPC, the same computational model can execute different
297
      communication patterns based on the input data. Although it is possible to simulate this property
      within PCOC, it requires more rounds, machines, or memory than with a model such as MPC. We
298
      further discuss the differences between the two models in Appendix A.

301   5.3     P OSITIONAL T RANSFORMERS CAN SIMULATE PCOC
302
303
      Having established the PCOC model, we now show that the positional Transformer model in Equa-
      tion (1) can simulate it. More specifically, our results show that a R-round PCOC protocol can be
304
      simulated by a positional Transformer with R layers. We first present the corresponding theorem,
305
      followed by a proof overview. The details of the proof are presented in Appendix B
306
307   Theorem 1. Consider a PCOC instance P with R rounds, N machines with local memory s, and
308
      data type T = R. Let M be a model following the architecture in equation 1 with n = N + 1 nodes,
      R layers and s attention heads. Then, for any instance P with Borel measurable local functions,
309
      there exists a configuration of M that approximates P to any desired degree of accuracy.
310
311
      Proof overview: The proof starts by demonstrating that a single layer of the positional Transformer
312   can simulate each individual round of PCOC. The constructive proof can be further divided into two
313   main components: communication and computation.
314
315
      Communication: The communication stage leverages the oracle RCVn,Data to specify the subsets of
      machines and local memory positions from which each machine receives information. These subsets
316
      can be transformed into binary encodings, which are represented by distinct attention heads, one for
317
      each local memory position, for a total of s positional attention heads. This part of the proof relies
318   on the capability of attention to represent binary patterns, which is shown in Appendix B.1. It is
319   important to note that the number of nodes exceeds the number of machines by one, as an additional
320   node is necessary to represent unsent messages. In the computation of attention, no attention matrix
321   can contain a row of all zeros, implying that a machine is not receiving any information. Due to such
322   cases, we introduce an additional sink node to account for information not directed to any machine.
323   Therefore, in this framework, we can show that any communication pattern defined by the oracle
      can be effectively represented by s attention heads across N + 1 nodes.


                                                         6
324
      Computation: This stage accounts for the local computations executed by each machine. To this
325   end, we invoke the universal approximation results of multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) (Cybenko,
326   1989; Hornik et al., 1989) to establish that, in each round, the local computations initiated by each
327   machine can be approximated by Φℓ as detailed in Equation (1). One important consideration is the
328   inclusion of unique node identifiers in the input to ensure the injectivity of the MLP approximation.
329   Even if two input rows may have the same input values, the unique identifiers guarantee that each
330   row corresponds to a distinct local function. Furthermore, the node identifiers must be preserved at
331   every layer to maintain this injectivity. This is achieved by the MLP when processing both the output
332   of the attention heads and the residual input X, ensuring that identifiers are consistently retained.
333   Therefore, by demonstrating that our architecture can approximate any oracle and local functions,
334   we show its ability to simulate any algorithm in PCOC. In practice, finding an oracle and local func-
335   tions for a specific task can be posed as a learning problem. Our proposed architecture adopts this
336   approach and can learn to execute parallel algorithms using fixed positional encodings in the atten-
337   tion mechanism. As our experiments illustrate, this approach helps mitigate OOD generalization
338   issues. In our experiments we do not assume access to explicit supervision for communication or
339
      computation, both are learned indirectly through ground truth, as all models are trained end-to-end.

      We compare the standard Transformer, which utilizes the attention mechanism in Equation (3), and
379   the positional Transformer, which employs the attention defined in Equation (2). Both variants share
380   the same number of layers and dimensional configurations, with any specific differences explicitly
381   noted. In all configurations, the total number of layers is set to ⌈log2 n⌉ + 1, where n denotes the
382   maximum input length, and each layer uses 2 attention heads. Along with each input sequence, we
383   also append an empty scratchpad entry. This extra entry does not count toward the total number of
384   layers and is not used to compute the loss. It is included solely to aid in the computation of the tasks.
385   For the function Φ(ℓ) , we employ a 2-layer MLP with ReLU activation functions. The embedding
386   dimension of the encoder and the hidden dimensions of the MLP are both set to 64.
387   We use one-hot encoded vectors of dimension n for positional encodings, where the non-zero entry
388   corresponds to the node position. Consequently, the embedding dimensions of WQ and WK are
389   set to n. A key difference between the models is that standard Transformers concatenate positional
390   encodings to the input, whereas positional Transformers supply positional information exclusively
391   through the matrix P . Therefore, in positional Transformers, input values are solely encoded in the
392   input, and positional information is exclusively encoded in the positional encoding matrix.
393   Both models are trained end-to-end using the squared loss between the predicted and target vectors
394   of size n, with no intermediate supervision. We train models with Adam, starting with a learning
395   rate of 5 · 10−4 and a learning rate scheduler for a total of 2000 epochs.

      Our training data consists of samples from the range [−2, 2]. To ensure diversity in the data, for each
397
      input sample, we first select lower and upper bounds γl and γu uniformly in [−2, 2], and then for
398   each of the n elements of the input sample, we select its value uniformly from the interval [γl , γu ].
399   We employ a similar sampling strategy for testing but extend the value range to [−2c, 2c], where
400   c > 1 is the OOD scale factor. Additionally, during the test sampling process, we apply a rejection
401   step to ensure that either γl < −2 or γu > 2, while maintaining −2c ≤ γl ≤ γu ≤ 2c. This
402   ensures that, with high probability, a test sample does not lie in the domain of the training data.
403   Our sampling strategy for the test data does not guarantee that every test sample will be outside
404   the domain of the training distribution. However, as we show in Appendix D, the probability of
405   generating a test sample that lies inside the domain of the training distribution is at most O(1/nc2 ).
406
      In fact, it turns out that the vast majority of the test instances in our test data do not lie in the domain
      of the training distribution.2 This implies that our test results reflect the “true” value generalization
407
      performance of both architectures.

511   7                   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
512
513   Our present work shows strong evidence that the positional attention mechanism is a better alterna-
514   tive to standard attention in the context of neural algorithmic reasoning. However, more research
515   is needed to uncover the true potential of this mechanism. We identify three main future research
516   directions which we believe are important.
517
518                   1. OOD generalization theory: It is very often the case that existing OOD generalization
519                      bounds are not tight (see Appendix F for an extended discussion). For specific tasks, there
520
                         is often a gap between what theory says about the worst-case performance and what one ob-
                         serves empirically. This highlights the need for a more fine-grained analysis that will be able
521
                         to capture the difference in OOD generalization capabilities among different architectures.
522
523                   2. Length generalization capability: Our current proposal uses fixed positional encodings,
524                      making it difficult to test it on bigger length inputs. Designing positional encodings that can
525                      work with arbitrary input lengths will allow us to explore the length generalization capabili-
526                      ties of positional attention.
527
528                   3. Complementary tasks: Testing the positional attention mechanism on complementary tasks,
529
                         such as graph algorithms, requires special treatment. In particular, graph algorithms require
                         that the model effectively process graph connectivity rather than merely treating it as input
530
                         for the data matrix. Adopting positional attention in various architectures that use graph
531
                         attention is an exciting future work.
532
533
      A final potential research direction is understanding why the standard Transformer fails to OOD-
534   generalize even when trained on (seemingly) simple tasks. In Appendix E we discuss some potential
535   sources of issues that might be the underlying causes of poor OOD generalization in standard self-
536   attention. For example, a potential problem seems to be related to the stability of self-attention
537   weights against OOD data. In particular, the weights can be very sensitive to the scale of input
538   values. We observe a dramatic change in attention weights as soon as we give the model the same
539   input but scaled so that the values lie outside the domain of the training data. However, more research
      is needed before we can conclusively answer this question.

718   A.1     M ASSIVELY PARALLEL C OMPUTATION (MPC)
719
720   The MPC model is a computational model of the MapReduce framework (Dean & Ghemawat,
721   2008), widely used for computations over massive datasets. It defines a parallel computing model
722   that jointly executes a function across multiple machines, each constrained by limited memory ca-
723   pacity. The MPC model is capable of representing various parallel algorithms (Im et al., 2023) and
724   is more powerful than other established parallel models, such as parallel random-access machine
725
      (PRAM) (Andoni et al., 2018).
726   For completeness, we provide a simplified version of the definition of the MPC protocol by Andoni
727   et al. (2018), which makes the connection to our PCOC model more apparent.
728   Definition 5 (MPC protocol, Def. I.1 (Andoni et al., 2018), simplified). Let s be a parameter. There
729   are p ≥ 1 machines (processors), each with local memory of size s. The input is distributed on the
730   local memory of some of the machines. The computation proceeds in rounds. In each round, each
731   machine computes the data in its local memory and sends messages to other machines at the end of
732   the round. The total size of messages sent or received by a machine in a round is bounded by s. In
733   the next round, each machine only holds the received messages in its local memory. At the end of
734   the computation, the output is in the memory of some machines.
735
736   A.1.1    R ELATION BETWEEN PCOC AND MPC
737
      Why we do not use the MPC model. We describe two main differences between MPC and PCOC,
738   which justify introducing the latter. First, the communication within the MPC model may depend on
739   the processed data. Our agnostic model removes this dependency by decoupling communication and
740   computation. In particular, we assume that information required for communication (i.e. message
741   destinations) is provided by an oracle, which, in practice, can be realized as a learning problem.
742   Second, the original MPC definition contains assumptions about the relations between the memory
743   size s, the input size, and the number of machines p, as well as an assumption about the number
744   of machines to which the input gets distributed. Although these assumptions make sense when
745   implementing algorithms for MPC in practice, and we could consider such assumptions for our
746
      simulation results, it is unclear what additional value they provide within the context of neural
      algorithmic reasoning. Thus, they are not part of the definition of the PCOC model.
747
748   PCOC can simulate MPC. For a given task, length n and input Data, it is easy to observe that
749   PCOC can simulate an algorithm on the MPC model that does not utilize the memory and processor
750   restrictions mentioned above. In such cases, PCOC allows two different simulation approaches for
751   an MPC protocol.
752   First, assuming the existence of an oracle RCVn,Data which has information about the communica-
753   tion at each round of a specific R-round MPC algorithm as well as the destinations of each element
754   in the memories of the machines at each round, a PCOC algorithm on n machines and R rounds
755   with the aforementioned oracle RCVn,Data can simulate an R round MPC protocol on n machines.
      At each round, the oracle essentially routes all data according to the underlying MPC algorithm’s

879   B.2.1    E NCODING
880
      Input matrix: In alignment with the PCOC model, the input matrix X represents N machines,
881
      where each machine is denoted by a row in the matrix, and its local memory, MEMi ∈ Ts , is rep-
882
      resented by the corresponding columns. The maximum size of data that any machine can send or
883   receive is s bits, with each bit corresponding to a column in X.
884
885   However, the actual number of rows and columns in X differs from the number of machines and the
886
      local memory size for two reasons:
887         1. Sink node: A dummy node is introduced to facilitate all possible communication patterns
888            in PCOC using positional attention. This is necessary because PCOC allows for the possi-
889            bility of information not being sent to any receiving machine. This scenario is incompatible
890            with the softmax formulation, which requires at least one non-zero entry. The dummy node
891            serves as a sink, collecting all messages that do not have a destination. Consequently, the
892            number of rows in X is n = N + 1.
893         2. Unique node identifier: Each machine also requires a unique identifier to enable element-
894            wise local computations. To achieve this, we encode a unique scalar for each node in the
895            last column of X, resulting in a feature dimension of dX = s + 1 for the input matrix.
896
      As discussed in Section 5, in PCOC, routing is set by an oracle that decides how packets of data
897   should be routed at each round. Under this framework, routing must be performed to prevent multi-
898   ple data from being sent to the same destination. Since our construction relies on matrix operations,
899   this leads to the following assumption:
900   Assumption 1. (No-collision). For any layer ℓ ∈ [L], no two different machines i1 , i2 ∈ [N ]
901   should route data to the same destination i3 ∈ [N ] for the same column j ∈ [s], where each column
902   represents a bit of local memory across the nodes.
903
904
      Note that this assumption does not limit the generality of our PCOC model. It only defines how
      data should be stored in the memory of each receiving machine, and any valid PCOC routing has
905
      a corresponding no-collision configuration of bits that realizes it due to the restriction on the total
906
      size of received data. As demonstrated in the constructive proof, this directly influences the sparsity
907   pattern generated by each attention head.
908
909   Positional encodings: As previously mentioned, although the connectivity at each layer may vary,
910
      the positional encodings remain consistent across all layers. Our architecture simulates MPC using
      node positional encodings with dimension dP = n by setting P = In , with each positional encoding
911
      functioning as a standard basis vector.

       C.6      A RELATIONAL TASK WITH MIXED - TYPE INPUTS
1405
1406   In this section, we evaluate the OOD performance of standard and positional Transformers on a
1407   relational task that involves numerical and textual data. We begin by presenting an example of a
1408   training and a testing sample for this task and then proceed with a formal description.
1409
       Training sample:
1410
1411   Input = [’Cat2’, 3.45, ’Cat5’, 1.23, ’Cat7’, 0.65, ’Cat8’, 2.23, ’Cat11’,
       4.10, ’Cat13’, 1.10, ’Cat14’, 0.10, ’Cat20’, 2.75, ’Find min of Cat5, Cat8,
1412
       Cat11 and Cat20’]
1413
       Output = 1.23
1414
1415   Test sample:
1416   Input = [’Cat23’, 7.28, ’Cat24’, 33.5, ’Cat28’, 9.17, ’Cat30’, 55.90,
1417   ’Cat31’, 23.70, ’Cat33’, 12.47, ’Cat34’, 8.45, ’Cat40’, 1.50, ’Find min of
1418   Cat28, Cat31, Cat33 and Cat40’]
1419   Output = 1.50
1420   Note that the categories and the range of values change between the training and test data. Additionally, we
1421   experiment with the following tasks: minimum (min), sum, and a multi-tasking task that combines minimum
1422   (min) and maximum (max). For multi-tasking, we choose minimum and maximum since they are opposites.
1423   This task requires pattern matching, since the categories between the train and test data are different. It requires
1424   conditional reasoning, since the queries are about a sub-set of the categories in the samples. Finally, it requires
1425   algorithmic reasoning to compute the output.
1426
1427
       Task description: Let n, k ∈ N with n ≥ k. The input to the model is a list consisting of text and
       numerical values of the following form:
1428
1429
1430    [’Cati1 ’, vi1 , ’Cati2 ’, vi2 , ..., ’Catin ’, vin , ’Find {type} of Catj1 , Catj2 ,
1431                                   ..., Catjk−1 and Catjk ’]3
1432
       where i1 , . . . , in ∈ N, vi1 , . . . , vin ∈ R≥0 , j1 , . . . , jk ∈ {i1 , . . . , in }, and {type} is one of ’min’, ’max’,
1433
       ’sum’. Notice that this setting allows for one model to potentially process multiple query types. In fact, we
1434   present one such experiment later. The answer to the query is either the minimum, maximum, or sum of the set
1435   {vj1 , . . . , vjk } depending on {type}. As a real-world use case, the categories could correspond to the various
1436   types of expenses of a company, and the model could be used by data analysts to perform common aggregation
1437   tasks.
1438
1439
       OOD generalization: For this task, we measure OOD generalization in two ways (simultaneously):
1440            1. We test using category identifiers (i.e i1 , i2 , . . . , in ) that the models haven’t encountered during train-
1441               ing, and
1442            2. The range of values v1 , v2 , . . . , vn used for testing is larger than the range used for training.
1443
1444   Experimental setting: We fix n = 8 and k = 4. Both standard and positional Transformers consist of 3
1445   layers, two attention heads per layer, and an embedding dimension of 32. We employ 2-layer MLPs with hidden
1446   dimension 32. All characters of the non-numeric parts of the input are tokenized and passed through an em-
1447
       bedding layer, while the numeric ones are passed through a linear layer. The category identifiers i1 , i2 , . . . , i8

       C.8                     ACCURACY M EASURES
1662   Given the regressive nature of the tasks considered in this work, the notion of model accuracy is not prop-
1663   erly defined. However, we propose the following transformation strategies that assign binary labels (“cor-
1664   rect”/“incorrect”) to the models’ outputs allowing us to measure their accuracy (with respect to these transfor-
1665   mations):
1666
                               • Rounding transformation: We evaluate the model on lists containing integers (while training is still
1667
                                 done using real numbers) and round the model’s output to the nearest integer (or nearest 0.5 for the
1668                             median task). A prediction is considered “correct” if the rounded and ground truth lists are the same.
1669                             The value generalization accuracies for all tasks in the main paper using this transformation strategy
1670                             are presented in Figure 27.
1671                           • Closeness transformation: We evaluate the model on lists of real numbers, considering a prediction
1672                             “correct” if each entry in the predicted list is within an absolute precision of 0.05 and a relative
1673                             precision of 5% compared to the corresponding entry of the ground truth list. The value generalization
                                 accuracies for all tasks in the main paper using this transformation strategy are presented in Figure 28.


                                                                                                                              31
1674
       It is important to note that the above metrics are quite unforgiving, as even a single element in the predicted list
1675   failing to meet the corresponding criterion results in the entire list being classified as “incorrect”. It is therefore
1676   expected that increasing the OOD scale factor causes the model’s accuracy to decrease rapidly. Nevertheless,
1677   the positional Transformer significantly outperforms the standard Transformer in terms of accuracy in all five
1678   tasks.

       E    P OTENTIAL REASONS FOR FAILURE IN SELF - ATTENTION
1858
       In this section, we discuss potential reasons for the shortcomings of standard Transformers in algorithmic
1859   tasks. While it is more straightforward to elicit reasons for the success of positional attention – motivated by
1860   the algorithmic alignment (Xu et al., 2020) between positional Transformers and parallel computational models
1861   – it is considerably more challenging to pinpoint the causes of failure in standard Transformers.
1862   Firstly, Transformers can simulate parallel algorithms, as demonstrated by Sanford et al. (2024). Intuitively,
1863   a single layer of self-attention should be more powerful than positional attention, as it leverages attention
1864   beyond positional encodings and allows for a more flexible structure in response to input variations. However,
1865   as discussed in Section 5, executing parallel algorithms does not require using anything beyond positional
1866
       information in attention.
1867   Assuming that standard Transformers should adopt positional information to effectively execute parallel algo-
1868   rithms, the operations required by standard Transformers become increasingly difficult than positional Trans-
1869
       formers for two main reasons:
1870
             1. Self-attention layers must learn to ignore input values and exploit positional information.
1871
             2. Transformer layers must preserve positional encodings for subsequent layers.
1872
1873   Namely, these desirable properties of positional Transformers present two significant challenges for standard
1874   Transformers. The first challenge arises naturally from the differences between standard and positional attention
1875   mechanisms. The second challenge highlights the compositional structure of attention layers, which can be
1876   detrimental during training. Specifically, the operations performed by each attention and MLP layer can degrade
1877
       the inputs of subsequent layers.
1878   This issue is further emphasized in Remark 2, where we state that while positional Transformers can represent
1879   any softmax pattern at any layer, standard Transformers may fail to do so due to potential degradation of
1880
       the attention inputs. Although residual connections can mitigate this issue by preserving input information,
       they must ensure that no overlaps hinder the use of positional encodings in subsequent layers. Moreover, this
1881   problem compounds across layers, making training more difficult as errors in earlier layers adversely affect
1882   subsequent computations.
1883
       Nevertheless, these remain speculative reasons for the observed failure of standard Transformers. Determin-
1884   ing the exact causes and the difficulty of achieving the two aforementioned goals through training requires
1885   a thorough analysis of the training dynamics, which is inherently challenging. Future in-depth work within
1886   the mechanistic interpretability framework (Nanda et al., 2023) can potentially shed light on these issues by
1887   inspecting network parameters at convergence, thereby uncovering the underlying reasons for the failure of
1888
       standard Transformers.
1889   Along this direction, we present some empirical evidence, in Figure 30 and Figure 31, that self-attention layers
       in a trained Transformer model can be highly sensitive to input values. In particular, the attention weights


                                                                   35
1890
       change dramatically when the input values of the test data do not necessarily lie in the domain of the training
1891   data. This suggests that self-attention potentially overfits the training data and, therefore, offers a plausible
1892   explanation for why the standard Transformers exhibit such poor value generalization in our experiments.

             B OUNDS AND FUTURE WORK
1947
       The topic of OOD generalization has been studied extensively from a theoretical perspective. Researchers often
1948   focus on bounding the risk on the test distribution using a mixture of terms that go to zero as the number of
1949   samples increases, plus a divergence term that does not necessarily go to zero depending on the distributions
1950   and the hypothesis class. For an extensive survey, we refer the reader to Redko et al. (2022). Although, in
1951   general, such bounds offer valuable intuition, they might not be tight for our particular setting. In particular,
       we examine a popular type of bound found in Mansour et al. (2009) which can be large even if the difference
1952
       in the support of the train and test distributions is the smallest it can be. Note that there are more types of
1953   bounds in Redko et al. (2022) than the one found in Mansour et al. (2009). Although we have not conducted
1954   an in-depth analysis of all cases, we note that all of them depend in a worst-case on the hypothesis class. We
1955   believe that to improve upon such generic bounds, one must consider the dynamics of the training procedure
1956   for deep positional attention architectures. We find this topic extremely interesting, but we leave it for future
       work, as it is highly non-trivial.
1957
1958   In what follows, we use a popular example of one of these bounds Mansour et al. (2009) and illustrate why
1959   it is not tight for a simple task of interest. Briefly, the main issue with this particular OOD bound is that it
       depends in a worst-case manner on the hypothesis class. We demonstrate this issue using the task of computing
1960
       the minimum over an array of length n. We assume that n is even. For simplicity, we do not work with the
1961   cumulative version of the minimum problem, as we did in the main paper. Therefore, the ground truth is simply
1962   the minimum of the input array. Dtrain is the train distribution over arrays of length n, where each component
1963   of the array is sampled independently and uniformly at random from integers in the range 0 to LDtrain , where
1964   LDtrain is a constant. Dtest is the test distribution over arrays of length n, where each component of the array
       is sampled independently and uniformly at random from integers in the range 0 to LDtrain + z, where z ≥ 0
1965
       is a constant integer. We use an equal number of samples from the train and test distributions, denoted by m.
1966   The loss function is ℓ(h(x), y) = |h(x) − y|, where h is a hypothesis, x is an input array of length n, and
1967   y = min(x), which is the minimum function over the array x.
1968
       The hypothesis class H is the architecture in equation 1 with log2 n layers, 2 heads per layer, and WO and WV
1969   as the identity matrices for all layers. For positional vectors in general position with dimension n, Lemma 1
1970   implies that there exist key and query matrices of size n × n that can represent any attention pattern at each
1971   layer. The MLP at each layer consists of 2 layers with a hidden dimension equal to 4. We use the ReLU
1972   activation function in all MLPs. This allows the MLP to represent the minimum and maximum functions on
       two input values exactly. This is because the minimum and maximum functions can be written using ReLUs
1973
       and linear operations:

2087
2088   We carried out additional experiments to explore variations in the input data format and the placement of
2089   positional encodings. This leads to the following 7 architectural choices (including 2 from the main paper and
       5 additional variations):
2090
2091         1. Standard Transformers: Input numbers and positional encodings are fed to MLPs, value, query, and
2092            key matrices.
2093         2. Standard Transformers with positional input (but no positional encodings): Input numbers are placed
2094            in one-hot positions, that is, the input is Diag(X) where X is the list of input numbers we give to
2095            other architectures. No additional positional encodings are used.
2096         3. Positional Transformers: Input numbers are fed to the MLPs and value matrix; positional encodings
2097            are fed to query and key matrices.
2098         4. Misaligned Positional Transformers: Input numbers are fed to the MLPs and value matrix; positional
2099            encodings are fed to the MLPs, value, query, and key matrices. That is, compared with Positional
2100            Transformers, we add positional encodings to the input.
2101         5. Input-regularized Standard Transformers: Input numbers are fed to the MLPs, value, query, and key
2102            matrices; positional encodings are only used in query and key matrices.
2103         6. No Positional Encodings: input numbers are fed to the MLPs, value, query, and key matrices. No
2104            positional encodings are used.
2105         7. Using RoPE Only: Input numbers are fed to MLPs, value, query, and key matrices, removing absolute
                positional encodings, and using only RoPE in standard transformers.

2154    Graph Neural Networks are a popular choice for solving algorithmic tasks on graphs. We tested the performance
2155    of Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) and Graph Attention Network (GAT) in solving the cumulative sum
2156
        and sorting tasks. Since the tasks tested have no underlying native graph, we tested these models on complete
        and star graphs. Notably, the original GAT architecture on a complete graph is similar to a standard transformer
2157    but differs in that the value, key, and query weights are shared in GAT. In Table 1 we present results reporting
2158    the median MSE loss. As the results indicate, neither GCN nor GAT works very well even for in-distribution
2159    data (OOD scaling factor = 1), let alone achieving OOD generalization. We believe this is because these tasks
        are inherently unsuitable for standard message-passing architectures.
