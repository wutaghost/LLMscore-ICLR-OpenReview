009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, large language models have exhibited impressive performance and sur-
012            prising emergent properties. However, their abilities remain constrained by the
013            preset context window of the Transformer architecture, and they continue to strug-
014            gle with length generalization. In this work, we propose a new perspective on
015            length generalization by focusing on the output distribution rather than the in-
016            put, as most prior studies have done (e.g., through positional encodings or data
017            structure). First, through case studies on simple synthetic tasks, we highlight the
018
               importance of output alignment—the consistency of output distributions across
               sequences of varying lengths. We then extend this observation to natural language
019
               tasks and introduce a metric named Long-Short Misalignment to quantify output
020
               alignment, ﬁnding a strong correlation between this metric and length general-
021            ization performance. Based on these insights, we propose a regularization loss
022            during training that improves output alignment. Extensive experiments conﬁrm
023            the effectiveness of this approach. Overall, our work provides a novel perspective
024            for understanding and enhancing length generalization in large language models.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive abilities in various tasks such as nat-
030   ural language generation, reading comprehension, code synthesis, instruction-following, and com-
031   monsense reasoning (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron
032   et al., 2023). Their performance has consistently improved by scaling both model and dataset sizes
033
      (Kaplan et al., 2020). However, the ability to generalize from smaller training context sizes to
      larger ones, commonly known as length generalization, is a major challenge for Transformer-based
034
      language models (Anil et al., 2022). This issue persists even in larger Transformers (Liu et al.,
035
      2024). With larger context sizes, a model can beneﬁt from more in-context learning examples, an
036   increased number of reasoning steps, or longer text generation (Li et al., 2024; Huang & Chang,
037   2023). However, training a Transformer with a larger context size is often excessively slow and
038   memory-intensive. Therefore, understanding the mechanism of length generalization and enhancing
039   the length generalization ability of these models is urgently needed.
040
      There exist two main approaches to understanding and improving length generalization. The ﬁrst
041
      is to understand and design different positional encoding (PE) (Press et al., 2021; Su et al., 2024;
042   Kazemnejad et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Yang, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). PE
043   plays a crucial role in the length generalization of Transformers, as the model must systematically
044   encode tokens in all possible positions. By designing PE that reduces the gap between shorter
045   training sequences and longer test sequences, length generalization can be improved to some extent
046   (Kazemnejad et al., 2023). The second approach is to understand the fundamental mechanisms of
047   Transformer models (Zhou et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023; Veličković & Blundell, 2021; Nogueira
048   et al., 2021; Deletang et al., 2022). This includes studying which algorithms Transformers can
049   represent and how they can generalize better by designing more effective tasks. However, in this
050
      work, we ﬁnd that the previous works ignore a component, the output space of the model, which we
      found to be quite crucial in the length generalization tasks.
051
052   We begin by investigating length generalization on synthetic tasks: predicting the mean value and
053   the length of binary sequences. Both empirical and theoretical results reveal that while Transformers
      generalize well in the mean prediction task, they struggle in the length prediction task. The key dis-


                                                        1

054
      tinction lies in the output distribution’s support set: in the mean prediction task, it remains consistent
055   as input sequences grow, whereas in the length prediction task, the support set shifts with increasing
056   sequence lengths. We hypothesize that this misalignment in the output distribution leads to poor
057   length generalization in the length prediction task. To verify this hypothesis, we propose a repa-
058   rameterization technique named OutRep that explicitly aligns the output distribution across different
059   sequence lengths in the length prediction task. Our empirical and theoretical analyses conﬁrm that
060   this approach signiﬁcantly enhances length generalization which supports our hypothesis.
061   Building on this insight, we extend our ﬁndings to natural language tasks. Although the model out-
062   put in natural language tasks is a vector (unlike the scalar output in synthetic tasks), which makes
063   it difﬁcult to directly apply the same analysis, similar output misalignment issues still arise. Specif-
064   ically, for two sequences with the same ending but slightly different lengths, the models output is
065   expected to remain consistent. However, we ﬁnd that models with poor length generalization tend
066   to produce divergent outputs when conditioned on these sequences. To explore the quantitative re-
067   lationship between output alignment and length generalization, we introduce a metric—long-short
068   misalignment—using symmetrical cross-entropy loss to measure the divergence between the out-
069
      puts of such sequences. Both empirical and theoretical results demonstrate that this metric strongly
      correlates with a models long-context performance, making it a more reliable predictor of length
070
      generalization than traditional training loss. Consequently, we incorporate this metric as a regular-
071
      ization loss in training, and extensive experiments on both length generalization and long-context
072   modeling show that our proposed training loss signiﬁcantly improves performance.
073
074   The main contributions of this work are as follows:
075
076          • We identify the crucial role of output alignment in achieving length generalization. Both
077            empirical and theoretical analyses show that misalignment in the output distribution across
078
               varying input sequence lengths leads to poor generalization performance.
079          • Building on this insight, we introduce a long-short misalignment metric to quantify output
080            misalignment and demonstrate its strong correlation with long-context modeling perfor-
081            mance both empirically and theoretically.
082
083
             • To further improve generalization, we integrate this metric as a regularization loss into
               the training process. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of this approach in
084
               boosting performance.

      Length Generalization on Synthetic Tasks. Our paper is related to the line of work that seeks
090
      to understand the capabilities and limitations of Transformer models when it comes to algorithmic
091   reasoning (Veličković & Blundell, 2021). Speciﬁcally, we focus on simple tasks and study length
092   generalization on the standard Transformer architecture with causal structure. Related to this, Lee
093   et al. (2023) study how well transformers trained from scratch can learn simple arithmetic tasks,
094   and ﬁnd that no length generalization is observed. Nogueira et al. (2021) ﬁnd that partial length
095   generalization on addition is observed only when models reach 3B parameters and when the addi-
096   tion questions are presented in reverse order. Jelassi et al. (2023) study models trained on addition
097   and ﬁnd strong generalization performance when using a few examples of longer sequences. Zhou
098   et al. (2024) proposes the RASP Generalization Conjecture that Transformers tend to learn a length-
099
      generalizing solution if there exists a short RASP-L program that works for all input lengths. Liu
      et al. (2023) discovers that the Transformer will learn shortcuts through the study of various syn-
100
      thetic tasks. Besides these explorations on speciﬁc tasks, some works study the impact of differ-
101
      ent positional encodings on the length generalization of math reasoning tasks. Alibi adds a linear
102   bias on the attention score to achieve better length generalization performance (Press et al., 2021).
103   Ontanon et al. (2022) studies different settings of positional encodings and identiﬁes Transformer
104   conﬁgurations that generalize compositionally signiﬁcantly better in a diverse set of compositional
105   tasks. Kazemnejad et al. (2023) systematically studies the role of no positional encoding (NoPE) in
106   Transformer with causal structure. Ruoss et al. (2023) proposes a randomized positional encoding
107   scheme that simulates the positions of longer sequences and randomly selects an ordered subset to
      ﬁt the sequence’s length.

      Long-context Modeling on Natural Language Tasks. A series of works (Sun et al., 2023; Chi
136
      et al., 2022; 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024; Yang, 2023; Chen et al.,
137   2023a) aim to extend the context size of Transformer-based models during ﬁne-tuning, primarily by
138   modifying positional encodings. For example, Zhang et al. (2024b) introduces a novel extension to
139   RoPE (Su et al., 2024) which combines adjusting RoPEs base frequency and scaling the attention
140   logits to help LLMs efﬁciently adapt to a larger context window. Chen et al. (2024) generalizes the
141   positional encoding scaling approaches to model the continuous dynamics by ordinary differential
142   equations over the length scaling factor. (Chen et al., 2023a) proposes to extend the context length
143   by slightly modifying RoPE via Position Interpolation (PI) and ﬁne-tuning on a small amount of
144   data. Our approach, in contrast, focuses on the model’s output space, which identiﬁes the crucial
145
      role of output alignment in length generalization. Wang et al. (2024) proposes a novel approach
      designed to narrow the generalization gap by reﬁning the interpolation of RoPE features for OOD
146
      positions and provides length extrapolation analysis on the feature gap.

149   3    A C ASE S TUDY ON S YNTHETIC TASKS : H OW O UTPUT A LIGNMENT
150        A FFECTS L ENGTH G ENERALIZATION ?
151
152   Length generalization refers to the ability to generalize from smaller training context sizes to larger
153   ones. Previous research has explored various factors critical to successful length generalization,
154   such as the task type (Zhou et al., 2024; Jelassi et al., 2023; Nogueira et al., 2021) and the positional
155   encoding (Ontanon et al., 2022; Kazemnejad et al., 2023; Ruoss et al., 2023). However, in this work,
156   we propose a new perspective, identifying output distribution alignment across different positions as
157   another important factor.
158   Mean Prediction v.s. Length Prediction. We start from a case study on synthetic tasks: in the
159   mean prediction task, the prediction target is the mean value of the sequence, while in the length
160   prediction task, the target is the length of the sequence. We focus on binary input sequences, where
161   each position in the sequence is ﬁlled with 0 or 1 with the same probability, and the decoder-only
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), a model widely used in both synthetic tasks (Zhou et al., 2024;


                                                                    3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Jelassi et al., 2023) and LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024), which utilizes a causal
163   mask in the self-attention module to enable auto-regressive generation. More model details can be
164   found in Appendix A. We train the model on sequences with a maximum length of ltrain = 10
165   and test it on sequences with a maximum length of ltest = 50. Figure 1a and Figure 1b display
166   the test results for both tasks. We observe that regardless of the positional embedding used, the
167   test loss of the length prediction task dramatically increases when the test sequence length exceeds
168   10, the maximum training length. Furthermore, the test loss continues to rise as the test sequence
169   length grows, indicating that the model demonstrates very low length generalization ability in the
170   length prediction task. In contrast, the model exhibits strong generalization ability in the mean
171
      prediction task, as the test loss on longer sequences remains nearly consistent with the loss on
      shorter sequences. We provide a theoretical analysis of this observation in the Appendix B.1.
172
173   From the results above, it is evident that while the mean and length of a sequence all convey global
174   information, the model’s length generalization ability varies across these tasks. A key distinction
175   lies in the differences in output distribution for each task. In the mean prediction task, where the
176   model generalizes well, the output remains within the ﬁxed range of [0, 1], regardless of sequence
177
      length. However, in the length prediction task, where generalization is poor, the support set of
      the output distribution shifts to a single-point set {l} as the sequence length increases to l. This
178
      distinction between the two types of tasks motivates us to consider the importance of the alignment
179
      in the output distribution for better length generalization ability.
180
181   Explicit output alignment helps length generalization. We propose OutRep, a reparameterization
182   technique to explicitly improve output distribution alignment in synthetic tasks, thereby enhancing
183
      the model’s length generalization ability. In the length prediction task, the output distribution for
      sequences of certain lengths is known. Leveraging this prior knowledge, during training, we apply
184
      a reversible function f : R → R to map the support sets of output distributions for sequences of
185
      varying lengths into more aligned sets. Instead of using the original target y(x), we train the model
186   on the transformed target f (y(x)). At test time, we apply the reverse function f −1 to the output to
187   recover the original prediction. This approach aligns the output distributions across different lengths,
188   which is expected to√improve length generalization. We consider      the following reparameterization
                                                                   √
189   functions: f (x) = x, f (x) = log(x) and f (x) = 1/ x. We show the experiment results in
190   Figure 1c. It can be observed that all three reparameterization functions successfully relieve the
191   poor length generalization
                           √      ability in the length prediction task. Speciﬁcally, the reparameterization
192   function f (x) = 1/ x has a nearly perfect generalization ability when the length is no more than
193   35. The rising trend when the test sequence length becomes longer is still slow. These results
194
      verify our conjecture on the output alignment that better output alignment leads to improved
      length generalization ability. We add more theoretical results and discussions in Appendix B and
195
      Appendix C. In the next section, we will extend these ﬁndings to the more practical natural language
196
      tasks.

199   4     O UTPUT A LIGNMENT IN NATURAL L ANGUAGE TASKS
200
201   In the previous section, we observed a positive correlation between length generalization ability and
202   output distribution alignment in synthetic tasks. Motivated by this ﬁnding, in this section, we aim to
203
      extend this investigation to natural language tasks. First, we introduce a metric to quantify output dis-
      tribution alignment in sequence modeling and demonstrate its strong correlation with performance
204
      on long-context benchmarks. Building on this insight, we propose incorporating this metric as a
205
      regularization loss during training to improve output alignment, which can lead to the performance
206   gains detailed in Section 5.
207
208
      4.1   L ONG -S HORT M ISALIGNMENT: Q UANTIFYING O UTPUT A LIGNMENT OF L ANGUAGE
209
            M ODELS
210
211   In synthetic tasks, we measure the discrepancy between the support sets of output distributions to
212   capture the differences in output across varying sequence lengths. However, in natural language
213   tasks, the model output is a vector gθ (x) ∈ R|V| , where the dimension is the size of the vocabulary
214   |V|. This makes it challenging to directly apply the same analysis from synthetic tasks to natural
215   language tasks. Despite this, similar output misalignment issues can still be observed in natural
      language tasks. Speciﬁcally, for a sequence x and its sufﬁxes x[−l1 :] and x[−l2 :] , where l1 and l2


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      are two lengths and x[−li :] means the last li tokens of x (i = 1, 2), the model’s output is expected
217   to remain consistent when l1 and l2 are similar, because the two sufﬁxes share large overlap in
218   tokens, resulting in similar contextual information. However, we ﬁnd that models with poor length
219   generalization tend to produce distant output distributions when conditioned on these sequences.
220
221   4.1.1   M ETRIC
222
223
      To quantitatively explore the relationship between output distribution alignment and length general-
      ization ability, we want to ﬁrst design a metric to evaluate output alignment. We propose to utilize
224
      symmetrical cross-entropy (SCE) loss (Wang et al., 2019) to measure the divergence between out-
225
      put distributions conditioned on two distinct sequences. Consider two input sequences, x and x′
226   with corresponding model predictions y = gθ (x) and y′ = gθ (x′ ). The SCE loss between these
227   predictions is deﬁned as:
228
                                LSCE (y, y′ ) = − (⟨y′ , log(y)⟩ + ⟨y, log(y′ )⟩) ,                          (1)
229
230
      where ⟨·, ·⟩ denotes the inner product and the log function is applied element wise. A lower SCE
      loss between the two predictions indicates better alignment. To assess overall output distribution
231
      alignment, we compute the expectation over sequence lengths l1 and l2 for a given input x:
232
233                          Lmisalign (gθ ) = Ex,l1 ,l2 LSCE (gθ (x[−l1 :] ), gθ (x[−l2 :] )).              (2)
234   We refer to this metric as the long-short misalignment, where a lower value signiﬁes better output
235   alignment across different sequence lengths. An illustration of this metric is shown in Figure 2a. In
236   practice, we sample l1 and l2 from the interval [ltrain /2, ltrain ], where ltrain represents the maximum
237
      context length used during training.

      4.1.2   R ESULTS
239
240   To evaluate the model’s length generalization ability, we use the perplexity on long validation sets
241   (16k length) and the LongBench-E score (Bai et al., 2023b). For perplexity evaluation, we select a
242   subset from the RedPajama-Book corpus (Computer, 2023), following the protocol in (Chen et al.,
243   2024). LongBench-E is a multitask benchmark that comprehensively evaluates large language mod-
244   els’ ability to understand long contexts, with task lengths averaging between 5k and 32k tokens.
245   Empirical Results. Table 1 shows the long-short misalignment metric Lmisalign for the mod-
246   els along with their corresponding long-context evaluation results. Additionally, we include the
247   training loss Ltrain for each model on the RedPajama-Book corpus, which reﬂects the perplex-
248   ity on sequences with the maximum training length ltrain . Interestingly, while the training loss
249   (i.e., log of perplexity on sequences of length ltrain ) shows a moderate correlation with long-
250   context performance metrics—indicating that lower training loss can contribute to improved length
251   generalization—the long-short misalignment metric Lmisalign demonstrates a much stronger corre-
252
      lation with long-context performance, as evidenced by its higher absolute correlation coefﬁcient.
      These ﬁndings suggest that Lmisalign is a promising indicator of length generalization ability. How-
253
      ever, it is important to note that we do not claim any causal relationship based solely on these
254
      observations. We will elaborate more on this relationship in Section 5.
255

257   autoregressive modeling (Zhang et al., 2024a) with a theorem:
258   Theorem 1 (Generalization guarantees for the natural language task). Under some model as-

260   sum of training loss Ltrain (gθ ) and misalignment metric Lmisalign (gθ ), i.e.,

      4.2     L ONG -S HORT M ISALIGNMENT M ETRIC AS R EGULARIZATION L OSS
306
307   Since both empirical and theoretical results indicate a strong correlation between the proposed long-
308   short misalignment metric and long-context benchmark performance, we incorporate this metric as
309   a regularization loss into the training loss, resulting in the new training loss deﬁned as:
310
311                                                     L∗train (gθ ) = Ltrain (gθ ) + α · Lmisalign (gθ ),                                                                          (4)
312
313   where Ltrain is the original cross-entropy training loss and α is the regularization coefﬁcient. Cal-
314   culating these two losses separately during training can be time-consuming, as the computation of
315
      Lmisalign requires forward propagation through two distinct sequences. To address this, we propose
      an efﬁcient implementation for L∗train . We ﬁrst sample an integer lextra from [1, ltrain /2] and then
316
      sample a sequence of length ltrain + lextra . The ﬁrst ltrain tokens form the ﬁrst input sequence,
317
      while the last ltrain tokens form the second input sequence. Both sequences can be used to compute
318   Ltrain . The overlap between the two sequences starts at token lextra + 1 and continues to token
319   ltrain , resulting in an overlap of ltrain − lextra tokens. We can calculate the long-short misalignment
320   loss in the overlapping positions. This implementation requires only two forward propagations for
321   the two sequences, resulting in minimal additional time and resource costs compared to calculating
322   the original train loss Ltrain . A detailed Pytorch-like algorithm is provided in Appendix D and an
323   overall illustration can be found in Figure 2b. We will conduct experiments using the proposed
      regularization loss in the next section to show its effectiveness.

529
530
531
      In this work, we focused on length generalization tasks and introduced a new perspective by examin-
      ing the output space. We ﬁrst identiﬁed the critical role of output alignment in length generalization,
532
      demonstrating both empirically and theoretically that output distribution misalignment across dif-
533
      ferent input sequence lengths leads to poor length generalization. Building on this insight, we
534   proposed a reparameterization technique, OutRep, to align the output space, and conﬁrmed its effec-
535   tiveness through empirical and theoretical validation. We extended this approach to natural language
536   tasks, introducing a metric called Long-Short Misalignment to quantify output alignment, which
537   showed a strong correlation with length generalization performance. Based on these ﬁndings, we
538   proposed a regularization loss during training to improve output alignment. Extensive experiments
539   further validated the effectiveness of this approach. Overall, our work offers a novel perspective for
      understanding and enhancing length generalization in large language models.

      We focus on the decoder-only Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), a model widely used in both
705   synthetic tasks (Zhou et al., 2024; Jelassi et al., 2023) and LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Peng et al.,

709   Since recent works found that by removing the positional encoding, Transformers can trained to be
710   well generalized on length (Deletang et al., 2022; Kazemnejad et al., 2023), we adopt this setting
711   (i.e. NoPE) by default. To provide a clear signal for the model to accomplish the tasks, we add
712   both the begin-of-sentence (BOS) token and the end-of-sentence (EOS) token in the sequence. We
713
      apply a feed-forward neural network on the hidden state of the last token to generate an output of
      a real number. We train all of our models on the train distribution from scratch to convergence if
714
      possible. For all tasks, the length of training examples is sampled uniformly from length 1 up to the
715
      max training length ltrain . We select hyper-parameters such as the learning rate for each task based
716   on what is required to ﬁt the training set. At test time, the length of the examples will traverse from
717   1 to the max testing length ltest .
