009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Prototype-Based Federated Learning (PBFL) has gained attention for its com-
012
               munication efficiency, privacy preservation, and personalization capabilities in
013
               resource-constrained environments. Despite these advantages, PBFL methods
014            face challenges, including high communication costs for high-dimensional pro-
015            totypes and numerous classes, privacy concerns during aggregation, and uniform
016            knowledge distillation in heterogeneous data settings. To address these issues, we
017            introduce three novel methods, each targeting a specific PBFL stage: 1) Class-
018            wise Prototype Sparsification (CPS) reduces communication costs by creating
019            structured sparse prototypes, where each prototype utilizes only a subset of repre-
020            sentation layer dimensions. 2) Privacy-Preserving Prototype Aggregation (PPA)
021            enhances privacy by eliminating the transmission of client class distribution infor-
022
               mation when aggregating local prototypes. 3) Class-Proportional Knowledge Dis-
               tillation (CPKD) improves personalization by modulating the distillation strength
023
               for each class based on clients’ local data distributions. We integrate these three
024
               methods into two foundational PBFL approaches and conduct experimental eval-
025            uations. The results demonstrate that this integration achieves up to 10× and 4×
026            reductions in communication costs while outperforming the original and most
027            communication-efficient approaches evaluated, respectively.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as an innovative paradigm in distributed machine learning,
034   enabling collaborative model training across decentralized devices while preserving data privacy
035   (McMahan et al., 2017). However, FL faces significant challenges, primarily due to heterogeneous
036   data distributions (Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022) and diverse model architectures across clients (Li
037   & Wang, 2019; Lin et al., 2020), which often lead to performance degradation. Researchers have
038   proposed various personalized and heterogeneous federated learning approaches to address these
039
      challenges. For data heterogeneity, approaches include model interpolation (Li et al., 2021; Deng
      et al., 2020; Lee & Choi, 2024), clustering (Sattler et al., 2020; Ghosh et al., 2020; Briggs et al.,
040
      2020; Duan et al., 2021), and multi-task learning (Mills et al., 2021; Hanzely & Richtárik, 2020;
041
      Huang et al., 2021). To tackle model heterogeneity, researchers have developed strategies such as
042   logit or representation exchange on public datasets (Li & Wang, 2019; Lin et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
043   2021c) and partial model (Liang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021) or auxiliary model sharing (Wu et al.,
044   2022; Zhang et al., 2022).
045
      Despite the progress made by these approaches, many existing methods are not communication-
046
      efficient, as they involve sharing large amounts of model parameters or logits on a public dataset.
047
      This makes them unsuitable for resource-constrained devices, especially those with limited band-
048   width. In response to these limitations, Prototype-Based Federated Learning (PBFL) has emerged as
049   a promising alternative (Tan et al., 2022a). PBFL significantly reduces communication overhead by
050   transmitting only prototypes between the server and clients, with the communicated data size limited
051   to the prototype dimension multiplied by the number of classes. Moreover, PBFL enhances privacy
052   protection by design because prototypes represent averages of local models’ representations. Fur-
053   thermore, PBFL naturally facilitates personalization by allowing local models to distill knowledge
      exclusively from global prototypes corresponding to classes in their local datasets.


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Despite these advantages, PBFL still needs to overcome several challenges that limit its effective-
055   ness in specific scenarios. While generally more communication-efficient than other approaches,
056   PBFL can still incur high communication costs when the dimension of the prototype is very high
057   or the number of classes is vast. Additionally, some existing PBFL methods, such as (Tan et al.,
058   2022a), often require the server to know each client’s class distribution when aggregating local pro-
059   totypes, potentially compromising privacy (Zhang et al., 2024). Another challenge is that the uni-
060   form knowledge distillation of global prototypes without considering data heterogeneity can hinder
061   effective personalization, potentially leading to suboptimal performance.
062   To address these challenges and fully realize PBFL’s potential in resource-constrained environments,
063   we propose three novel methods that can be applied to existing PBFL frameworks. Class-wise Pro-
064   totype Sparsification (CPS) enforces structured sparse prototypes per class by assigning specific
065   representation dimensions to each prototype, zeroing out others. By transmitting only non-zero
066   dimensions, CPS significantly reduces communication costs. Privacy-Preserving Prototype Aggre-
067   gation (PPA) performs weighted averaging of local prototypes without requiring the server to know
068   clients’ class distributions, thereby enhancing privacy. Finally, Class-Proportional Knowledge Dis-
069
      tillation (CPKD) distills knowledge from global prototypes by weighting the distillation process
      based on local class distributions. This approach facilitates effective adaptation to each client’s
070
      unique data characteristics, thus improving personalization.
071
072   Our three methods have been evaluated using heterogeneous lightweight models. Experimental
073   results demonstrate that when applied to two established PBFL approaches (FedProto and FedTGP),
074   our methods significantly reduce communication costs while outperforming the original and several
075
      data-free FL approaches.

      2.1   H ETEROGENEOUS F EDERATED L EARNING
081
      Heterogeneous Federated Learning (HtFL) has emerged as a response to the challenge of hetero-
082
      geneity in real-world federated settings. HtFL strategies can be broadly classified into two cate-
083   gories: those dependent on public data and those that operate without such reliance. Public data-
084   dependent approaches leverage shared or globally accessible datasets to facilitate knowledge transfer
085   across heterogeneous clients. Knowledge Distillation (KD) based methods are notable examples in
086   this category (Li & Wang, 2019; Zhang et al., 2021b; Yu et al., 2022). Data-free approaches can
087   be categorized based on what is shared among the server and clients: partial model parameters,
088   auxiliary model parameters, or prototypes. Partial model sharing strategies, such as LG-FedAvg
089   (Liang et al., 2020) and FedGen (Zhu et al., 2021), partition client model architectures. By shar-
090   ing only upper layers while allowing lower layers to vary, these approaches aim to balance model
091
      customization with knowledge sharing. Alternatively, auxiliary model-based techniques like FML
      (Shen et al., 2020) and FedKD (Wu et al., 2022) train and share a compact auxiliary model through
092
      mutual distillation. An auspicious direction in data-free HtFL is the use of prototype-based methods
093
      that share condensed class representations (Jeong et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2022a;b; Huang et al., 2023;
094   Zhang et al., 2024). By focusing on essential class-level information, prototype-based methods aim
095   to strike a delicate balance between effective knowledge sharing and privacy preservation.
096
097
098
      2.2   P ROTOTYPE -BASED F EDERATED L EARNING
099
      (Jeong et al., 2018) employs class-wise averaged logits for knowledge transfer, which can pose
100
      privacy risks by exposing the number of classes and each class’s logit distribution. To address
101
      this, FedProto (Tan et al., 2022a) introduced a more privacy-safe approach by exchanging local
102   prototypes of the decision layer instead of logits. Building upon these foundations, several works
103   have further refined PBFL techniques. FedTGP (Zhang et al., 2024) enhances performance through
104   Adaptive-margin-enhanced Contrastive Learning (ACL), which refines global prototypes. To im-
105   prove efficiency, FedPCL (Tan et al., 2022b) leverages both class prototypes and pre-trained models,
106   effectively reducing computational and communication costs. Addressing the challenge of domain
107   shift in federated learning, Federated Prototypes Learning (FPL) (Huang et al., 2023) develops clus-
      ter and unbiased prototypes, offering rich domain insights and a balanced convergence objective.

192   4.1   A DAPTATION OF P ROTOTYPES WITH S TRUCTURED S PARSITY
193
194   Representation layers often exhibit sparsity when using the ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) activation
195
      function, which can lead to ‘dead’ hidden units. A dead unit is defined as a hidden unit that outputs
      zero for all input patterns in the training set (Lu et al., 2019), effectively not contributing to learning
196
      or inference. Our observations reveal that in the decision layer of a deep network, nearly half of
197
      the hidden units can be dead per class. Figure 1a illustrates this phenomenon, displaying a heatmap
198   of 500-dimensional local prototypes for 20 clients (L0-L19) and the global prototype for class #2
199   of the CIFAR-10 dataset after completing FL with FedProto. In this visualization, colored features
200   indicate non-zero values, while blank areas represent zeros (dead units). Notably, some clients show
201   zero prototype vectors, indicating the absence of class #2 in their local dataset. Several clients (L1,
202   L3, L4) utilize only partial feature dimensions.
203
      Intriguingly, despite these sparse representations, deep networks maintain high performance. This
204   resilience can be attributed to the networks’ substantial capacity and robust generalization capabil-
205   ities (Arpit et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021a; Kawaguchi et al., 2022). Given these observations,
206   one might hypothesize that the sparsity in a decision layer (prototype, feature embedding) could be
207   advantageous, potentially reducing communication requirements between the server and clients if
208   the sparse locations were consistent across clients. However, in PBFL scenarios, the locations of
209   dead units typically vary among clients, as evident in Figure 1a due to the heterogeneity of models
210   and data across clients.
211   Class-wise Prototype Sparsification (CPS) To leverage sparsity benefits in PBFL, we propose
212   Class-wise Prototype Sparsification (CPS). This method imposes structured sparsity per class, ensur-
213   ing consistency in zero locations across clients. CPS implementation is straightforward, involving
214   sharing predetermined sparse locations in prototypes. We introduce class-specific binary masking
215   vectors mj ∈ {0, 1}d , which determine which prototype vector elements are set to zero, creating
      a ‘structured sparse prototype.’ We omit superscripts and subscripts for simplicity, representing a

473   6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
474
475   Our study presents quantitative evidence on the effects of three methods on FedProto and FedTGP.
476   However, the mechanisms by which these methods operate remain partially unclear. For instance,
477   while applying CPS alone to FedProto generally improves accuracy, it does not yield similar ben-
478   efits for FedTGP (Table 1). We investigated the effect of sparsity proportion but did not observe a
479   consistent relationship between the cosine similarities of global prototypes and performance (Figure
480   2, Tables 1 and 2). This lack of consistency suggests a trade-off between prototype distances and
481   deep network capacity for efficient training on different sizes of decision layers.
482   Our evaluation results indicate that while PBFL approaches offer advantages regarding communi-
483   cation cost, privacy, and personalization, they tend to underperform compared to several existing
484   data-free FL approaches when used alone. However, by incorporating our methods, which can be
485   easily integrated into each stage of PBFL, we have enhanced the original PBFL approaches. In
      resource-constrained environments, these enhanced PBFL approaches may prove more practical.

       Our proposed components are designed for seamless integration into existing PBFL algorithms. To
1029   demonstrate this, we will outline the key modifications needed to incorporate these components into
1030   vanilla PBFL (FedProto). The first major change involves the server generating masking vectors
1031   and distributing them to clients, as shown in Line 1 and 8 of Algorithm 1. The second modification
1032   utilizes these vectors for exchanging compressed prototypes between the server and clients (Lines 5
1033   and 11), followed by their reconstruction into structured sparse prototypes (Line 9). PPA and CPKD
1034   can be directly applied to FedProto by replacing its aggregation and distillation components. This
1035   integration process is similarly adaptable to other PBFL algorithms, such as FedTGP, showcasing
1036   the versatility of our methods across various PBFL frameworks.
1037   When integrating PPA with FedTGP, careful attention to prototype scaling is crucial. FedTGP em-
1038   ploys local prototypes to train a trainable prototype prior to aggregation. Applying PPA to FedTGP
1039   scales each local prototype by ni,j , as shown in Eq. (11), which can lead to training loss divergence.
1040   To address this, we introduce a compensatory scaling factor. Specifically, we re-scale each local
1041   prototype by Kn · |Nj |, where Nj denotes the set of clients possessing samples from class j. This
1042   adjustment ensures stable training while preserving the benefits of both PPA and FedTGP.
