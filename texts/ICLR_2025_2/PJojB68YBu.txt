009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We propose an alternative computational unit for feedforward supervised learning
012            architectures, called Generative Matching Units (GMUs). To understand GMUs,
013            we start with the standard perceptron unit and view it as an undirected symmetric
014            measure of computation between the weights W = [w1 , w2 , ..wd ] and each input
015            datapoint X = [x1 , x2 , .., xd ]. Perceptrons forward W T X + b, which is usually
016            followed by an activation function. In contrast, GMUs compute a directed asym-
017            metric measure of computation that estimates the degree of functional dependency
018            f of the input elements xi of each datapoint to the weights wi in terms of latent
019
               generative variables θ, i.e, f (wi , θ) → xi . In order
                                                                   P to estimate the functional de-
               pendency, GMUs measure the minimum error (f (wi , θ) − xi )2 incurred in the
020
               generation process by optimizing θ for each input datapoint. Subsequently, GMUs
021
               map the error into a functional dependency measure via an appropriate scalar
022            function, and forward it to the next layer for further computation. In GMUs, the
023            weights [w1 , w2 , .., wd ] can therefore be interpreted as the generative weights. We
024            first compare the generalization ability of GMUs and multi-layered-perceptrons
025            (MLPs) via comprehensive synthetic experiments across a range of diverse set-
026            tings. The most notable finding is that when the input is a sparse linear combi-
027            nation of latent generating variables, GMUs generalize significantly better than
028            MLPs. Subsequently, we evaluate Resnet MLP networks where the first feedfor-
029            ward layer is replaced by GMUs (GMU-MLP) on 30 tabular datasets and find that
030
               in most cases, GMU-MLPs generalize better than the MLP baselines. We also
               compare GMU-MLP to a set of other benchmarks, including TabNet, XGBoost,
031
               etc. Lastly, we evaluate GMU-CNNs on three standard vision datasets and find
032
               that in all cases they generalize better than the corresponding CNN baselines. We
033            also find that GMU-CNNs are significantly more robust to test-time corruptions.

      We consider the supervised classification problem, where the objective is to predict the out-
      put category y ∈ {1, .., c}, given the input X ∈ Rd , and we are given the sample S =
040
      {(X1 , y1 ), .., (Xm , ym )} ∼ P (X, y). Most supervised learning approaches tackle this problem
041
      via feedforward architectures. A key building block of most feedforward learners has been the per-
042   ceptron unit, which involves a linear map W T X + b followed by some activation function a(.),
043   resulting in the outcome a(W T X + b), where X, W, b represent the inputs, weights and biases,
044   respectively. This computational unit has been quite ubiquitous across various modern architec-
045   tures, including transformer variants Lin et al. (2022) and convolutional network variants Li et al.
046   (2021). Even networks which don’t specifically have the perceptron as a unit of computation, end
047   up using similar feedforward concepts, such as in Capsule networks Patrick et al. (2022) or in the
048   recently proposed Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks Liu et al. (2024). Multi-layered perceptrons are
049   also proven universal approximators, so given enough complexity and data, they can indeed learn
050
      the underlying function to an arbitrary degree of accuracy Hornik et al. (1989).
051   Mechanistically, if each input dimension of X represents causal variables which impact the output
052   label in a feedforward manner, then the feedforward perceptron unit could potentially end up closely
053   reflecting the actual mechanism involved in yielding the output. This is because MLPs are good
      function approximators, and thus if the underlying mechanism is indeed a function that maps the

      input to the output, they should be preferred. However, what if the underlying mechanism is not
055   feedforward?

057   Consider scenarios where the actual causes of the data are hidden, and furthermore, where the
058
      mechanism of the data generation is related to the output category y. More specifically, suppose
      each datapoint has been generated as Xi = f (θi , Wyi ), where θi ∈ Rk , k < d, f : Rk → Rd
059
      and Wyi ∈ Rk×d represents the generative weights corresponding to the ground truth label yi for
060
      Xi . θi can be interpreted as the latent generating variables for the instance Xi . Note that we will
061
      have a total of c generative weights W1 , W2 , .., Wc which corresponding to each output label from
062   {1, 2, .., c}. The function f represents the structural mechanism via which the hidden variables θi
063   interact with the generative weights Wyi . Note that in this scenario, the generative mechanism for
064   the input X is dependent on its ground truth label y and the latent generating variables θ. If the form
065   of f is known, we can construct a network with c output units and corresponding learnable weights
066   W1′ , .., Wc′ as follows: for j = 1, 2, · · · c, vj = minθ ∥X − f (θ, Wj′ )∥, and the output label esti-
067   mate is ŷ = arg minj∈{1,···c} vj . As the underlying generative weights W1 , .., Wc are unknown, we
068   would need to optimize Wj′ via gradient descent on some loss function, so that the network would
069   ideally converge to the case where the weights Wj′ = Wj . This represents the intuitive idea behind
070   generative matching units (GMU) which we propose, in this paper, as an alternative computational
071   unit for feedforward supervised learning network architectures.
072   The previous example illustrates an alternative means to create computational units for supervised
073   learning, from the perspective of generative error, using a set of generative weights. This can be
074   beneficial in scenarios where the causal variables that generate the data distribution are hidden, and
075   also directly relate to the ground truth label. Furthermore, for the optimization minθ ∥X −f (θ, Wj′ )∥
076   to be fully determinable, it needs to hold that dim(θ) < dim(X), as otherwise there will be more
077   unknowns than equations. In fact, when the data is high dimensional but exists in low dimensional
078   manifolds (dim(θ) << dim(X)), we can impose the constraint that k << d, which imparts a
079   natural dimensionality bias in the unit.

095   1. Feedforward computation from a generative perspective: We rethink feedforward computa-
096      tion from a generative standpoint, where each unit attempts to generate the given input datapoint
097      using its associated weights and outputs a function of the error incurred in the generation process.
098      This ensures that our units are primarily computing from a top-down perspective, which is helpful
099      in scenarios where the latent variables that generate the data also relate to the output class.

104      in contrast to the perceptron unit which can be roughly interpreted as the projection of the input

176   2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
177
178   This brings us to the overall contributions of our work. We outline them as follows.
179
180   • We propose a new computational unit for feedforward supervised learning architectures, called a
181     generative matching unit. We propose several differentiable variants of GMUs and showcase the
182
        uses of each form on different types of datasets.
183   • GMUs consider a generative approach to computation, and we show theoretically and empirically
184     that they can convey more information in high-dimensional spaces, leading to more expressivity.
185     Much smaller GMUs can be highly expressive and learn the underlying function when the low-
186     dimensional generative assumptions are met.
187   • We show that GMU-based networks and multi-layered perceptrons have their own scenarios
188     where they generalize better than the other. Specifically, in the sparse linear structure predic-
189     tion problem, and the structure regression problem, we find that GMUs generalize significantly
190     better than MLPs. Similarly, we find that when the ground truth classifier is Naive Bayesian with
191     linear dependencies, MLPs outperform GMUs.
192   • We conduct an exhaustive comparison of performance between GMU-MLPs and MLPs on 30
193     tabular datasets. Here, GMU-MLPs refer to the architecture that results after replacing the first
194     layer of the MLP (Resnet) with GMUs. We find that in the majority of cases GMU-MLPs gen-
195     eralize better than MLPs, and in some cases they yield state-of-the-art results, when compared to
196     well-known benchmarks.
197   • We test GMU-MLP and GMU-CNN variants on three standard vision datasets, focusing on other
198     aspects of performance in addition to test accuracy. Specifically, we find that GMU-MLP and
199     GMU-CNNs significantly outperform their vanilla counterparts in terms of robustness to test-time
200     corruptions.

      5.5   OVERALL TAKEAWAYS
479
      Overall, we find that the GMU variants show significantly better generalization, especially to out-
480   of-distribution test data. Also, we observe that in the cases where the inputs are structured via a
481   common causal framework, such as the sparse and tree structure prediction experiments, more input
482   dimensionality becomes a blessing rather than a curse. This is simply because of the law of large
483   numbers. When high dimensional inputs all share the same cause, with the correct assumptions
484   one can obtain a more accurate estimate of the underlying generating variables, unlike the low-
485   dimensional case. Therefore, it is notable that we see larger dimensionality help performance in the
      structure prediction experiments, while doing the opposite in the Gaussian experiment.

      Our work demonstrates the potential advantages of an alternative computational unit that computes
      from a generative perspective, imposing a low-complexity constraint on the generation process.
531
      Generative Matching Units showcase better generalization and demonstrates a significantly higher

      ability to identify dynamic causal structures in the inputs. On real tabular datasets, Resnets replaced

534   remain open for incorporating GMUs in larger networks across other domains, and also find ways
