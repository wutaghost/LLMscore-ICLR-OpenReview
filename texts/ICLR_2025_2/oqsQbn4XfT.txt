009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) has accentuated the need for diverse,
012            high-quality pre-training data. Synthetic data emerges as a viable solution to the
013            challenges of data scarcity and inaccessibility. While previous literature has fo-
014            cused predominantly on the quality and quantity of real data, our work enables the
015            measurement of diversity in synthetic data and explores its impact on LLM perfor-
016            mance. We study the downstream effects of synthetic data diversity during both
017            the pre-training and fine-tuning stages by introducing a new diversity metric, LLM
018
               cluster-agent, designed to evaluate the diversity of synthetic datasets. Through a
               series of controlled experiments with models of 350M and 1.4B parameters, we
019
               demonstrate that the proposed cluster-based LLM scoring of diversity correlates
020
               positively with both pre-training and supervised fine-tuning performance. Our
021            findings also reveal that synthetic data diversity in pre-training affects supervised
022            fine-tuning more significantly than pre-training itself, even for smaller models. We
023            hope this study advances our understanding of the optimal use of synthetic data in
024            LLM training and opens new avenues for efficient data generation processes.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   A common hypothesis behind the success of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2019;
030   Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023a;b; Touvron et al., 2023b) is the scaling law of computing, model
031   size, and, perhaps the most important, high-quality pre-training data (Kaplan et al., 2020a; Wei et al.,
032   2022; Muennighoff et al., 2024). The most capable LLMs these days often have been pre-trained on
033
      trillions of tokens (Bai et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023b). Acquiring such massive
      amounts of high-quality data has become more challenging (Villalobos et al., 2022).
034
035   As a remedy, synthetic data have been widely adopted in training LLMs, which are relatively easier
036   to obtain with more controllable quality (Bauer et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Long et al., 2024a).
037   For example, Phi series (Gunasekar et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023; Javaheripi et al., 2023; Abdin
038   et al., 2024) used a large amount of textbook-style synthetic data with real data in pre-training,
039
      empowering the promising performance of smaller-scale LLMs. Synthetic data for programming
      and math have also been adopted to improve the coding and reasoning abilities of LLMs (Guo et al.,
040
      2024; Yu et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024). Previous studies have also focused on synthetic data for
041
      supervised fine-tuning (Zelikman et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Eldan & Li, 2023;
042   Chen et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024), instruction tuning (Wang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Li
043   et al., 2024c; Wang et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a;b; Wu et al., 2024), downstream
044   transferring (Meng et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022), and evaluation (Zhu et al., 2023; 2024a;b).
045
      Despite the wide usage of synthetic data, understanding what aspect of and how the synthetic data
046
      affect the performance of LLMs still remains largely unexplored, especially for pre-training. In the
047
      past, many studies have shown that both the quality and quantity of real data matters for LLM pre-
048   training (Kaplan et al., 2020a; Sorscher et al., 2022). While the effectiveness of quantity of real data
049   has been extensively verified on LLMs as the scale of training tokens increases (Radford et al., 2019;
050   Brown et al., 2020; Computer, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023b; Dubey et al., 2024), the quality of real
051   data, affected by various factors such as corruption (Elazar et al., 2023), bias (Gallegos et al., 2024),
052   toxicity (Bender et al., 2021), duplication (Lee et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2024), and diversity (Tirumala
053   et al., 2023b), to name a few, is more difficult to validate due to the co-functioning of these factors
      (Kreutzer et al., 2022; Longpre et al., 2023b). Some recent research studied different quality factors

      In this paper, we propose to study the diversity, as one of the most important quality factors (Tirumala
069
      et al., 2023b; Sachdeva et al., 2024), of the pre-training synthetic data. Existing studies on synthetic
070   data in pre-training either only present methods of creating them (Allal et al., 2024b;a) or provide
071   findings that are restricted to relatively small scales (Wu et al., 2022; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023b; Ye
072   et al., 2024; Zhu & Li, 2023; Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023a; Yang et al., 2024b), with limited understanding
073   on how exactly diversity of the synthetic tokens affect the training of LLMs. However, studying the
074   diversity of synthetic data presents two main challenges. First, the lack of an effective metric for
075   measuring the diversity of text data (Lee et al., 2023; Shaib et al., 2024a; Tirumala et al., 2023a;
076   Ankner et al., 2024), and second, the difficulty of conducting controlled large-scale experiments with
077   synthetic tokens due to the high cost of generation and various aspects influencing their diversity.
078   To overcome the obstacle, we propose a diversity measure pipeline by automatically directing LLMs
079   to perform a clustering of text corpus, termed LLM Cluster-agent. Specifically, we design prompts
080   that guide LLMs to summarize the characteristics from randomly sampled data points that can best
081   capture the underlying diversity in the corpus and then perform clustering based on the character-
082   istics with a self-verification mechanism. An LLM cluster score is computed from the clustering
083   results as a measure of text diversity. The proposed pipeline is wrapped as a diversity metric toolkit,
084
      and we showcase its effectiveness, consistency, and scalability with different LLMs on large-scale
      synthetic data, where traditional diversity metrics fail and produce significantly inconsistent results.
085
086   To perform controlled experiments on synthetic data diversity, we extract 620,000 topics from
087   Wikipedia and then use them to seed the synthetic generation. With the proposed LLM Cluster-agent
088   pipeline, we use synthetic datasets with various levels of diversity from different perspectives, in-
089   cluding the underlying distribution, prompts and models of synthetic generation, and ratios between
090
      synthetic and real tokens. As the first large-scale study on synthetic data diversity, we pre-train a set
      of language models of 350M and 1.4B parameters on the combination of 34B real and the generated
091
      synthetic tokens and supervised fine-tune them to study the downstream effects. We show that:
092
093   • LLM cluster score positively correlates with both the pre-training and supervised fine-tuning per-
094     formance of LLMs, as shown in Fig. 1. It thus shows great potential to be applied in practical and
095     large-scale LLM synthetic data pre-training and predict the performance in the future.
096   • The underlying distribution of synthetic data, in terms of the number of topics and the number of
097     generations per topic, matters for LLM performance. In Section 3.3, we show that more unique
098     topics usually present better diversity, and too large the number of generations per topic may
099     introduce redundancy in synthetic data generation, thus hurting the performance.
100   • Prompts incorporating different text styles and various targeted audiences for synthetic data gen-
101     eration can significantly boost the diversity and thus the LLM performance. In Section 3.4, we
102     show that models trained on synthetic data with different styles and personas present the best
103     performance and outperform models trained on Cosmopedia v0.1 and v0.2 (Allal et al., 2024b;a).
104   • Better LLMs-generated synthetic data present more diversity in synthetic generation. In Sec-
105     tion 3.5, we show that the diversity and performance of trained models with GPT-4o generated
106     synthetic data is better than GPT-3.5, and 8B instruct Llama-3.1 is better than 7B instruct Mistral.
107   • More balanced ratio between real and synthetic tokens benefits LLMs the most, and over-weighted
        synthetic tokens may hurt performance due to diversity deterioration, as shown in Section 3.6.

136   2                    M ETRICS FOR M EASURING S YNTHETIC DATA D IVERSITY
137
138   Measuring the diversity in large-scale text data is very challenging due to the complex nature of
139
      language (Lee et al., 2023; Shaib et al., 2024a). Different metrics have previously been used to
      measure the diversity of text data, and we broadly categorize them into two types: heuristic-based
140
      and model-based. Heuristic-based metrics, such as vocabulary size, n-gram diversity (Li et al.,
141
      2022a; Meister et al., 2023), and self-repetition score (Salkar et al., 2022), often provide a very
142   limited view, focusing only on statistical variations within the text without capturing deeper semantic
143   nuances. Model-based methods such as K-means clustering (Abbas et al., 2023) and homogenization
144   score (Lin & Och, 2004; Shaib et al., 2024a) struggle with large-scale and context-rich datasets, as
145   they rely on predefined features, which can oversimplify the true diversity present in the data. These
146   limitations are further compounded in synthetic text data generated by LLMs due to similar patterns
147   in part-of-speech tagging and syntax often present in them (Rosenfeld & Lazebnik, 2024; Shaib
148   et al., 2024c), making it difficult to assess diversity accurately. This motivates us to address the gap
149   by proposing an LLM-based metric to uncover the intricate and latent structures within the data.
150
151   2.1                   LLM C LUSTER - AGENT
152
      Given a text corpus X = {xi } with in total |X| text samples, to allow LLMs to measure their diver-
153
      sity, we propose to originate the measure from the principle of entropy, i.e., capture the underlying
154   distribution of clusters and cluster sizes. However, there are two challenges that prevent LLMs from
155   performing clustering directly. First, it is difficult to define the proper criteria for LLMs to cluster
156   that captures the true distribution. Second, due to the limited context length of LLMs1 , one cannot
157   directly feed the entire text corpus to LLMs for clustering as in traditional clustering methods.
158
      We thus introduce LLM Cluster-agent, a diversity measure pipeline that leverages LLM’s abilities
159
      to interpret semantic meanings and to understand rich contexts of text samples for clustering. To

      overcome the above challenges, we design LLM Cluster-agent to perform an iterative clustering
179
      based on K text samples each time, according to the clustering criteria that are also summarized by
180   the LLM. More specifically, our method includes the following steps, as shown in Fig. 2.
181
182   Metadata and metric generation. We first design two types of clustering criteria: metadata and
183
      metrics. The metadata are used to guide LLM to summarize the detailed attributes of the text samples
      and the metrics are used for scoring the samples and reasoning behind the clustering. Due to the
184
      massive amount of the text corpus, a metadata and metric generation prompt is used to extract 3-5
185
      metadata and metrics from the randomly selected J samples of the corpus and repeat the process
186   M times. A metadata and metric gathering prompt is then designed to individually collect and
187   summarize the most frequent ones from the multi-round generation. The collected metadata and
188   metrics are used for clustering criteria. We find that it is beneficial to highlight the criteria at the top
189   of our clustering prompt in the next step to emphasize the focus of clustering, and thus we exploit
190   another criteria summary prompt to summarize the high-level definition of the gathered metrics.
191
      Cluster generation and verification. After obtaining a set of metadata and metrics and their def-
192   inition of high-level criteria, we design a clustering prompt. Due to the context limit of LLMs, we
193   similarly randomly select K samples from the corpus and prompt LLMs to group the K samples
194   into different clusters according to the attributes defined by the metadata and scoring rules defined
195   by metrics. We also include instructions for LLMs to give the reasoning for each cluster. After
196   obtaining the clusters, we use a cluster verification prompt to inspect whether the reasoning and the
197   samples in the cluster are valid. We find that this additional verification step is very essential in
198   removing some unreasonable clusters. We repeat this process N times, and each generation will
199   produce a result of the number of clusters C and the number of samples per cluster S from these K
200
      samples. Eventually, we define LLM Cluster score as the diversity measure by averaging the cluster
                                                        PN
201   results from the N times generation: D = N1 i=1 SCii , where D denotes the diversity score, and
202   Ci and Si are the number of clusters and the number of samples per cluster in the i-th generation.
203   This approach enables the identification of diverse themes, topics, or stylistic variations within the
204
      synthetic dataset. The full prompts used for each step are shown in Appendix D. We also present the
      ablations of the pipeline design, prompt design, and the parameters in Section 3.8 and Appendix B.3.

207   2.2     BASELINE M ETRICS
208
      We include several commonly used heuristic-based and model-based diversity metrics as baselines
209
      (Shaib et al., 2024a). Context Length (CL) measures the average token length of the text corpus. Self-
210   Repetition Score (SRS) quantifies the repetition of tokens within sentences, while N-Gram Diversity
211   Score (NDS) measures the proportion of unique n-grams. Compression Ratio (CR) compares the
212   g-zip compressed size of the dataset to its original size. Perplexity measures the uncertainty of a pre-
213   trained model in predicting the next token and Perplexity Gap calculates the perplexity difference
214   between a larger and a smaller model. K-means Clustering utilizes feature embeddings from a pre-
215   trained model to cluster the data. A summary of the diversity metrics is shown in Table 1 and we
      further describe these diversity metrics in Appendix C. Apart from our baseline measures to quantify

      3     S YNTHETIC DATA D IVERSITY IN P RE - TRAINING
224   With the proposed LLM Cluster-agent metric, we conduct a series of controlled experiments by
225   generating synthetic data with various levels of diversity and training models on them. We reveal a
226   linear correlation between the LLM Cluster Score and training performance from the perspectives of
227   underlying distribution, prompts and models for generation, and ratio of real and synthetic tokens.
228
229   3.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
230
231   Pre-training. We adopt the Llama architecture (Touvron et al., 2023b) with a context length of 2,048
232
      and the Codegen-Mono (Li et al., 2023; Nijkamp et al., 2022) tokenizer with a vocabulary size of
      50,304. We primarily use 350M and 1.4B models and pre-train all models on the combination of real
233
      and synthetic data, except for the baselines on real data only. For real data, we use filtered web data,
234
      consisting of the Wikipedia subset and part of the C4 (Raffel et al., 2019) subset of Dolma (Soldaini
235   et al., 2024), code data, consisting of the filtered the Stack (Kocetkov et al., 2022), StackOverflow,
236   and Code Contest (Li et al., 2022b) as in Phi-1.5 (Li et al., 2023), and math data from the filtered
237   OpenWebMath (Paster et al., 2023) subset of Dolma. The real data in total contain 34B tokens,
238   where the ratio of web, code, and math tokens is 4:1:1. For synthetic data, we generate variants with
239   different underlying distributions, prompts, and models for generation (more details in the following
240   sections). Our experiments mainly involve two ratios of real (web) and synthetic tokens: 4:1 for
241   smaller synthetic data experiments, and 1:1 for larger ones, following Phi-1.5. More ratios are also
242   studied. We train 350M and 1.4B models for a total of 50B and 150B tokens, respectively.
243   Supervised Fine-tuning. In addition to pre-training, we also conduct supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
244   to study the effect of diversity in pre-training data inherited to downstream performance (Chen et al.,
245   2024a). After pre-training the models, we supervised fine-tune them for 3 epochs on the combination
246   of GPT-4 filtered version of the Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) and FLANv2 (Longpre et al., 2023a).
247   The learning rate of the AdamW optimizer for fine-tuning is set to 2e-5 and weight decay to 0.
248   Benchmark Evaluation. To evaluate the performance of both the pre-trained model and supervised
249   fine-tuned model, we use WinoGrande (Pı̂rtoacă et al., 2019), ARC-Easy (Pı̂rtoacă et al., 2019),
250   ARC-Challenge (Ferré, 2021), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), SIQA (Bauer & Bansal, 2021), PiQA
251   (Bisk et al., 2020), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), and COPA (Roemmele et al., 2011). We report
252   the zero-shot accuracy using LM-Eval Harness (Gao et al., 2021) for both pre-trained and supervised
253   fine-tuned models. We utilized a system prompt consistent to fine-tuning to evaluate tuned models.
254   Diversity Evaluation. To effectively evaluate the diversity of the large-scale synthetic corpus, we
255   employ bootstrapping to obtain robust results. Specifically, we randomly select one million text
256   samples from the corpus and run the baseline diversity metrics and our proposed LLM cluster metric
257   on this subset. We repeat the process for 10 rounds with different random seeds and report the
258   average results and the corresponding error bar. For the model-based metrics, we use BERT-L
259   (Devlin, 2018) embeddings for K-means clustering, and GPT-2-L and GPT-2-XL (Radford et al.,
260   2019) to calculate perplexity and perplexity gap. For K-means clustering, we set the number of
261
      clusters to 10K, which we find as a good trade-off between speed and accurate measurement. We
      set K = 10 and N = 5K for the proposed LLM Cluster-agent. We also find J = 5 and M = 100
262
      is good enough to obtain meaningful clustering criteria, as we show in Appendix B.3. We use non-
263
      uniform scale and mainly compare the relative trend to measure the diversity. More details of the
264   model architecture, training parameters, and evaluation datasets are shown in Appendix A.

502   4            R ELATED W ORK
503
      Principled scaling (Kaplan et al., 2020b) of language models both in terms of model and data size
504   has resulted in powerful systems (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Jiang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Yang
505   et al., 2024a; AI et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024). However, high-quality training data are still finite
506   and expected to be consumed entirely in the near future (Villalobos et al., 2022). To overcome
507   this limitation, synthetic data generated from advanced LLMs are used for per-taining (Gunasekar
508   et al., 2023b; Ben Allal et al., 2024; Allal et al., 2024b; Long et al., 2024b), post-training, fine-
509   tuning, or alignment (Wang et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). In addition to scaling
510   models and data sizes, the quality of pre-training data plays an equally critical role in determining
511   the overall performance of language models (Sachdeva et al., 2024; Penedo et al., 2024). High-
512
      quality data, particularly when it exhibits diversity, is essential for achieving strong downstream
      task performance (Miranda et al., 2024; Tirumala et al., 2023a; Chung et al., 2023). As a result,
513
      accurately measuring the quality of pre-training data has become a focus of research, since low-
514
      quality or noisy data can degrade model performance on downstream tasks (Penedo et al., 2024).
515   Several studies have explored the relationship between data quality and performance, demonstrating
516   that improvements in data quality directly affect downstream results (Penedo et al., 2024).
517
518
      Further, there exists a variety of strategies to carefully select high-quality data from large corpora
      while maintaining model performance. For example, (Sachdeva et al., 2024) show that even simple
519
      approaches, such as using large language models to filter and select data. Other methods, including
520
      perplexity-based data selection and diversity-aware sampling techniques, have also proven effective
521   in curating high-quality data from expansive datasets without sacrificing model performance(Ankner
522   et al., 2024; Tirumala et al., 2023b; Tan & Wang, 2024b; Longpre et al., 2023b). Recent studies have
523   focused on evaluating data quality using metrics such as perplexity, factuality, and alignment with
524   human judgment to ensure that models are trained on meaningful and representative datasets (Shaib
525   et al., 2024b; Montahaei et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020). Among the many important characteristics of
526   high-quality pre-training data, diversity stands out as a critical factor (Tirumala et al., 2023b). Var-
527   ious methods have been developed to quantify diversity (Shaib et al., 2024b), but these approaches
528   have been applied mainly to natural data sources and present limitations, as we showed earlier.
529
530   5            C ONCLUSION
531
      In this study, we investigated the impact of synthetic data diversity on the performance of LLMs.
532
      We proposed and validated a new metric, LLM Cluster-agent, to quantify the diversity of synthetic
533
      data. Our experiments demonstrated that increased diversity correlates positively with model per-
534   formance, particularly in downstream fine-tuning tasks. Moreover, the choice of generation seeds,
535   the prompt template, the generation model, and the ratio between real and synthetic tokens all sig-
536   nificantly influence both the data diversity and model performance. Although the scale of models
537   in this study is mainly restricted up to 1.4B due to computational limits, we demonstrated that the
538   results in this study may present the potential to be applied on a larger scale. These results suggest
539   that diverse, high-quality synthetic data is essential for the training of robust and effective LLMs,
      paving the way for future improvements in the generation and utilization of synthetic data.

       C       D IVERSITY M ETRICS
1232
1233
       Context Length refers to the average length of the sequences in the dataset. Longer contexts can
1234
       indicate more complex data structures and richer narratives. By analyzing context length, we can
1235   infer the ability of the synthetic data to capture long-term dependencies and intricate patterns.
1236
1237   Self-repetition Score quantifies how often sequences or phrases are repeated within the dataset.
1238
       Lower scores suggest higher diversity, as the model generates more varied outputs rather than reit-
       erating the same phrases. High self-repetition can indicate overfitting or a lack of creativity in the
1239
       synthetic generation process.
