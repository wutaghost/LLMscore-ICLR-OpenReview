                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Numerical instability, such as gradient explosion, is a fundamental problem in
011
               practical deep reinforcement learning (DRL) algorithms. Beyond anecdotal de-
               bugging heuristics, there is a lack of systematic understanding of the causes for
012
               numerical sensitivity that leads to exploding gradient failures in practice. In this
013
               work, we demonstrate that the issue arises from the ill-conditioned density ratio
014            in the surrogate objective that comes from importance sampling, which can take
015            excessively large values during training. Perhaps surprisingly, while various policy
016            optimization methods such as TRPO and PPO prevent excessively large policy
017            updates, their optimization constraints on KL divergence and probability ratio
018            cannot guarantee numerical stability. This also explains why gradient explosion
019            often occurs during DRL training, even with code-level optimizations. To address
020            this issue, we propose the Vanilla Policy Gradient with Clipping algorithm, which
021            replaces the importance sampling ratio with its logarithm. This approach effectively
022
               prevents gradient explosion while achieving performance comparable to PPO.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has demonstrated its effectiveness in various domains (Mnih
028   et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2017; Vinyals et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022). Despite these successes,
029   the reliability and numerical stability of DRL algorithms remain fundamental limitations that hinder
030   their application in real-world environments. In particular, DRL algorithms are often observed to
031   have numerical instability issues, such as exhibiting the major problem of gradient explosion that
032   destroys the learning progress. Existing work on the brittleness of DRL methods attributes this to
033
      either the high variance in actions (Fujita & Maeda, 2018), the gradient estimator (Liu et al., 2020),
      or the sensitivity to hyperparameters (Henderson et al., 2018).
034
035   In this paper, we perform a rigorous analysis of the root cause of numerical instability in policy
036   optimization algorithms such as Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) and Proximal Policy
037   Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2015a; 2017). Gradient explosion is a consequence of overflows
038   caused by computations involving excessively large numbers that exceed the limits of floating-point
039
      arithmetic. We show that the long horizons and large value variance are not the core reason for
      overflow, especially under action clipping as commonly used in standard DRL implementations (Duan
040
      et al., 2016; Raffin et al., 2021; Bou et al., 2024). Another popular explanation is that the covariance
041
      matrix of the stochastic policy can become singular during training, leading to gradient explosion.
042   However, our experiments in Appendix B show that arithmetic overflows can occur even when the
043   standard deviations are lower-bounded. Instead, our analysis indicates that the root cause is the
044   importance sampling steps in TRPO and PPO (Schulman et al., 2015a; 2017), where the probability
045   ratio can take exponentially large values, especially in the case of Gaussian distributions. Specifically,
046   we analyze the condition number of the probability ratio, and demonstrate that the probability ratio is
047   exponential with respect to a line integral in the policy space, which can lead to arithmetic overflows
048   and cause gradient explosion.
049   In particular, we demonstrate that optimization constraints proposed in TRPO/PPO, such as limiting
050   the KL-divergence and clipping, do not prevent numerical instability. We show that the probability
051   ratio in importance sampling can grow exponentially fast, ultimately causing arithmetic overflows,
052   even while the KL divergence remains small. This indicates that various policy optimization methods
053   that prevent excessively large policy updates do not prevent numerical problems. Indeed, the
      probability ratios are not guaranteed to stay small, especially when the policy network’s output is

054
      further clipped in the action space, leading to excessively small values in the probability density
055   functions and large condition numbers in the density ratio.
056
057   Given that importance sampling is the root cause of the exploding gradient issue, we propose an
058
      algorithm called Vanilla Policy Gradient with Clipping, a modified version of the PPO algorithm
      in which the importance sampling ratio is replaced with its logarithm. We conduct experiments on
059
      continuous-control benchmarks to evaluate its effectiveness. Specifically, we find that this algorithm
060
      performs comparably to PPO on small policy networks where no gradient explosion occurs and
061   maintains good performance on large policy networks where PPO encounters numerical issues.
062   Additionally, the influence of other code-level techniques, such as reward scaling and learning rates,
063   is discussed in Appendix B.
064
      Overall, our findings suggest that the TRPO/PPO loss is inherently ill-conditioned due to importance
065
      sampling and action clipping, which directly contribute to the numerical instability of deep policy
066
      gradient methods. This issue should not be viewed merely as a hyperparameter tuning problem but as
067   a fundamental limitation of DRL algorithms that requires more attention.

072
      Deep policy gradients in practice. It has been reported that implementation details and code-level
      optimizations fundamentally impact the performance of deep policy gradient algorithms such as
073
      TRPO and PPO (Henderson et al., 2018; Engstrom et al., 2020; Andrychowicz et al., 2021). In
074
      particular, empirical studies have shown that the most significant discrepancies between theory and
075   implementation arise in gradient estimation, value prediction, and optimization landscapes (Ilyas
076   et al., 2020). These findings were later supported by theoretical studies, which indicated that they
077   stem from the fractal structures present in both value and policy landscapes (Wang et al., 2023). Our
078   work contributes to this line of research by providing an in-depth study on the numerical stability of
079   deep policy gradient methods.
080
081   Exploding gradients in deep learning. Many neural network architectures have been reported to
082   suffer from the gradient explosion issue, including deep multi-layer networks (Bengio et al., 1994;
083   Glorot & Bengio, 2010) and residual neural networks (RNNs) (Pascanu et al., 2013). This problem
084
      is closely linked to the depth of the network architecture (Philipp et al., 2017; Schoenholz et al.,
      2017), which can result in a long chain of Jacobian multiplications. In the context of reinforcement
085
      learning (RL), Liu et al. (2018) points out that the product of density ratios over a long horizon can
086
      grow exponentially, leading to exploding variance in off-policy estimation. Also, Khorasani et al.
087   (2023) proposes to use second-order information in the form of Hessian vector products to bypass
088   importance sampling weights in policy gradient. In this work, we demonstrate that even a single
089   density ratio can take excessively large values and cause numerical overflow, and propose an algorithm
090   that drops the importance sampling from PPO objective while achieving comparable performance.
091   Wang et al. (2024) investigates gradient explosion in the context of generative adversarial imitation
092   learning (GAIL), suggesting that a large reward function can cause exploding gradients. However,
093   our empirical results in Section B indicate that this does not fully explain gradient explosion in policy
094   gradient algorithms, as scaling down the reward does not completely overcome the issue.

319      in deep policy gradient methods like TRPO and PPO, which incorporate the probability ratio into

         the numerical issues as they are supposed to.

501   7   C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
502
503   In this work, we demonstrate that ill-conditioned density ratios in importance sampling are the
504
      fundamental cause of numerical instability in deep policy gradient methods. While this issue cannot
      be effectively addressed through optimization constraints alone, code-level techniques are necessary
505
      to fully eliminate or mitigate its effects. To address this issue, we find that directly dropping
506
      the importance sampling term yields the best performance, and we therefore advocate for further
507   investigation in this direction in future studies.
508
509   According to our analysis, larger neural networks make bigger updates in each optimization step,
510
      resulting in larger density ratios in importance sampling and thus increasing the risk of gradient
      explosion. Therefore, we question whether deep policy gradient methods are numerically stable and
511
      robust when optimizing complex policy networks. This is particularly important for the scalability of
512
      deep policy gradient methods. Although the experiments in this work focus on MuJoCo continuous-
513   control environments, the limitations discussed are inherent to the algorithms and may apply to other
514   scenarios as well. We believe that a better understanding of these issues can undoubtedly enhance the
515   effectiveness of deep reinforcement learning in real-world applications.
