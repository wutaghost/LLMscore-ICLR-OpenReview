009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Herein the topics of (natural) gradient descent, data decorrelation, and approxi-
012            mate methods for backpropagation are brought into a common discussion. Natural
013            gradient descent illuminates how gradient vectors, pointing at directions of steep-
014            est descent, can be improved by considering the local curvature of loss landscapes.
015            We extend this perspective and show that to fully solve the problem illuminated
016            by natural gradients in neural networks, one must recognise that correlations in
017            the data at any linear transformation, including node responses at every layer of a
018            neural network, cause a non-orthonormal relationship between the modelвҖҷs param-
019            eters. To solve this requires a method for decorrelating inputs at each individual
020
               layer of a neural network. We describe a range of methods which have been pro-
               posed for decorrelation and whitening of node output, and expand on these to
021
               provide a novel method specifically useful for distributed computing and com-
022
               putational neuroscience. Implementing decorrelation within multi-layer neural
023            networks, we can show that not only is training via backpropagation sped up sig-
024            nificantly but also existing approximations of backpropagation, which have failed
025            catastrophically in the past, benefit significantly in their accuracy and convergence
026            speed. This has the potential to provide a route forward for approximate gradient
027            descent methods which have previously been discarded, training approaches for
028            analogue and neuromorphic hardware, and potentially insights as to the efficacy
029            and utility of decorrelation processes in the brain.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The method of gradient descent is as popular as it is intuitive. This method, of stepping in the
035
      direction of steepest descent of a function, is applied successfully across the engineering sciences
036   as well as in the modern AI revolution to find (local) optima of arbitrary functions. Alternative
037   optimization methods have proven largely unsuccessful in being generally applied to continuous
038   functions of arbitrary form, with second-order methods being largely brittle when applied to non-
039   convex functions. Nonetheless, methods for speeding-up optimizations are not only interesting but
040   have huge potential economic and environmental impact.
041
      In 1998 it was proposed that there might be a perspective beyond typical gradient descent, called
042   natural gradient descent (Amari, 1998), which might overcome some elements of skew and scale
043   in the updates produced by gradient descent. Natural gradient descent has since been explored at
044   the edges of the field of optimization, and deep neural network training (Bernacchia et al., 2018;
045   Martens & Grosse, 2015; Desjardins et al., 2015; Heskes, 2000), with sometimes greater stability
046   than traditional second order methods (Dauphin et al., 2014), though recently developed second
047   order methods show significant promise (Gupta et al., 2018; Ren & Goldfarb, 2021; Vyas et al.,
048   2024). Regardless, the principles of natural gradients are less widely understood, less applied, and
049   less intuitive than they could be.
050   Simultaneous to this line of development, the principles behind learning in natural biological systems
051   and potential algorithms for learning in distributed systems have been under investigation (Lillicrap
052   et al., 2020). From these fields have sprung a whole range of approximate methods for gradient
053   descent which promise to explain how learning might occur in brains or how it might be enabled in
      analogue hardware (neuromorphic, analogue, or otherwise).


                                                        1
054
      We aim to bring together these lines of research, contributing to each individually while also pro-
055   viding a common space for impact. Specifically,
056
057         1. We demonstrate that correlations in data (between features) at the input and hidden lay-
058            ers of deep networks are one half of natural gradients, and that they contribute to a non-
059            orthonormal basis in parameters,
060
            2. We explore and expand the efficacy of methods for removing correlations from deep neural
061            networks to better align gradient descent with natural gradients, and
062
063         3. We show that decorrelating mechanisms not only speed up learning by backpropagation,
064
               but that decorrelation can also enable alternatives approximations to gradient descent.
065
      This paper is organised in an unconventional format due to the multiple sub-fields within which it is
066
      embedded and to which it contributes. Therefore, one should consider each of the coming sections
067   as descriptions of a particular contribution, insight, or result embedded within a larger narrative. The
068   titles of each section represent their core contribution to this narrative.
069
070
      The intention of this work is to bring insight to those who wish for more efficient gradient descent
      and excitement to those interested in approximate gradient descent methods whether for explanation
071
      of learning in biological systems or implementation in physical/neuromorphic systems.

251   3   S IMPLE DECORRELATION MECHANISMS CAN RID NETWORKS OF DATA
252       CORRELATIONS
253
254
      Above we have shown that data correlations impact the relationship between parameters at linear
255
      transformations. Thereby, the direction of gradient descent is skewed. We now go on to show how
256
      this can be corrected for.
257
258   There are multiple potential routes for correction of the gradient descent direction. One may measure
259   correlation structure and directly invert and apply this inversion to the gradient updates to move
260
      toward a natural gradient descent update rule. However, the data being fed into oneвҖҷs parameters is
      still correlated and therefore continues to contribute to a non-orthonormal basis.
261
262   Alternatively, we here describe methods for modifying neural network models such that the neural
263   outputs at each layer are decorrelated via some operation, no matter whether we are doing inference
264   or training. Regular gradient descent in such a case is now closer in its update to natural gradients
265   and furthermore our parameters can now relate in an orthogonal basis. This is explained in greater
266
      detail in Section 3.3.
267   Note that hereafter we make use of gradient descent to update models and attempt to remove data
268   correlations within our models. Notably, we do not attempt to remove gradient correlations, the
269   better known aspect of natural gradients (left most component of Equation 1). Thus performance is
      potentially left available via that route.

      3.1   E XISTING METHODS FOR DECORRELATION
271
272   Data correlations can be removed from every layer of a deep neural network via a number of meth-
273   ods. These correlations must be undone continuously to keep up with changing correlation structures
274   within deep neural networks while they are trained. Two main approaches exist to tackle the issue
275   of removing data correlations: measurement of the correlation and inversion (via matrix decom-
276   position) or a direct, continually updated, estimate of a matrix which can achieve a decorrelated
277   outcome.
278   A number of works exist for directly taking the inverse-square-root of the correlation matrix of some
279   data. Desjardins et al. (2015) did so by measuring the correlation matrix of data at every layer of a
280   neural network (at pre-defined checkpoints) and thereafter carried out a matrix decomposition and
281   an inverse (as did Luo (2017)). Bernacchia et al. (2018) did this at a minibatch-level, measuring
282   correlations within a mini-batch and inverting these individually. Batch-normalization has also been
283   extended to whitening and decorrelation for deep neural network training in a similar fashion (Huang
284   et al., 2018; 2019), and this principle has been extended to much deeper networks, though also while
285
      stepping away from the theoretical framing of natural gradients. These works went so far as to apply
      decorrelation methods to extremely deep networks (101-layer) networks (Huang et al., 2018; 2019).
286
287   Few methods have considered iteratively computing a decorrelation matrix directly (i.e. without
288   inversion or matrix decomposition) (Ahmad et al., 2022; Dalm et al., 2024; Sussillo & Abbott,
289   2009). Some methods optimise a matrix for decorrelation alongside the regular weight matrices
290   in a neural network by construction of an appropriate loss function capturing how data should be
291
      modified for correlation reduction (Ahmad et al., 2022; Dalm et al., 2024). Other methods (Sussillo
      & Abbott, 2009) instead do not carry out any decorrelation within a network but instead store a
292
      disconnected matrix containing an interatively updated inverse correlation matrix and use this for
293
      parameter updating with the goal of achieving recursive least squares optimization. Regardless,
294   these methods were thus far not theoretically linked to natural gradients.
295
296   From computational neuroscience however, a number of methods for dynamic and recurrent removal
297
      of data correlations have been proposed from the perspective of competitive learning and inhibitory
      control (FoМҲldiaМҒk, 1990; Pehlevan et al., 2015; Oja, 1989; Vogels et al., 2011). FoМҲldiaМҒk (1990); Pehle-
298
      van et al. (2015); Oja (1989); Vogels et al. (2011) describe, in work spanning almost two decades,
299
      a set of learning rules between nodes which, via linear recurrent dynamics, push neural activities
300   toward decorrelated states at fixed points of these systems. The rules proposed by all four examples
301   rely upon a simple updating scheme in which recurrent connections within populations of nodes are
302   updated by an вҖҳanti-hebbianвҖҷ parameter update, in short with parameter gradients proportional to
303   node-output correlations. They each, however, contribute a unique perspective on how such an up-
304   date can be useful, from iterative learning of PCA dimensions (FoМҲldiaМҒk, 1990; Oja, 1989), through
305   alternative subspace constructions such as multi-dimensional scaling (MDS) extraction (Pehlevan
306   et al., 2015), all the way to an implementation in spiking neural networks which explains the de-
307   velopment of real inhibitory synaptic connection structures in neurons (Vogels et al., 2011). Inves-
308
      tigation into even more detailed methods to describe decorrelational inhibitory dynamics continues
      into contemporary work Lipshutz & Simoncelli (2024). These methods all provide neat and easily
309
      implementable dynamical systems for competition and decorrelation, however in contrast to the ma-
310
      chine learning examples above, these methods have all been developed in the context of single layer
311   networks, for unsupervised learning purposes, with little consideration for efficient implementation
312   or application to gradient descent.
313
314
      Here we present a method which bridges across all of the above, allowing fast and stable decorre-
      lation while having two equivalent formulations: one via a single weight matrix multiplication, and
315
      another as the fixed-point of a recurrent system of dynamics.

397   implementation and one should not consider it efficient for applications unless applied in a cus-
398   tom neuromorphic, analogue, or other exotic hardware solution (e.g. nervous systems). The exact
399   computational cost of decorrelation is, for all of these reasons, highly dependent upon network ar-
400   chitecture as well as implementation. Therefore itвҖҷs utility must be examined on a case-by-case
401   basis.

492   5   D ISCUSSION
493
494   Herein we were able to link the concepts of natural gradients and decorrelation to show how, why,
495   and to what degree decorrelating node activities at every layer of a neural network can massively
496   boost performance of approximate gradient descent methods. The two approximate gradient descent
497   algorithms treated herein are by no means the only algorithms which might be enabled by a decorre-
498   lation mechanism, and if one is interested in training of neural networks upon exotic hardware, one
499   might consider combining such a distributed decorrelation mechanism with a number of alternative
500   learning algorithms such as direct feedback alignment (Lillicrap et al., 2016; NГёkland, 2016), sur-
501
      rogate gradient learning Neftci et al. (2019) and many more. One must remain aware, however, that
      adding decorrelation to neural network architectures requires an additional weight matrix per neural
502
      network layer and induces additional computational overheads.
503
504   Outside of theoretical treatments and application spheres, decorrelation has been presented in neuro-
505   scientific work as an explanation of the filtering which happens at multiple levels of nervous systems.
506   The center-surround processing which takes places at the earliest stage of visual processing (at reti-
507
      nal ganglion cells), for example, has been proposed as an aid for decorrelation of visual input to
      the brain (Pitkow & Meister, 2012). At a more general scale, inhibitory plasticity appears to be
508
      learned in a manner which also leads to spatial decorrelation (He & Cline, 2019), something which
509
      has also been modelled (Vogels et al., 2011). Thus it appears that decorrelation might be active in
510   real nervous systems and could therefore aid in whatever form of optimization is taking place.
511
512   Beyond this, simulation work in computational neuroscience has also shown that via decorrelation of
513
      unit activities, competitive learning can be established to learn subspaces and carry out unsupervised
      feature extraction in a distributed and вҖҳlocalвҖҷ fashion (Bell & Sejnowski, 1997; FoМҲldiaМҒk, 1990; Oja,
514
      1989; Zylberberg et al., 2011). Thus, it appears that decorrelational processes may also have utility
515
      unsupervised competitive learning approaches.
516
517   However, in real nervous systems nearby neurons can have significantly correlated activities - a
518   feature which may be required for redundancy and robustness to cell death. Thus, as strict a decor-
519
      relational process as presented herein seems unlikely. Nonetheless, we point toward a promising
      method by which local and distributed learning can be enabled, and it remains to be investigated as
520
      to how this could be combined mapped more directly to real neural systems.
521
522   We find that our decorrelation approach has a combination of benefits: increased convergence speed
523   (per epoch) along with increased generalization performance, most notably for BP. One question
524   which we address only shortly in this work is the relative tradeoff of decorrelation vs whitening pro-
525
      cesses. Wadia et al. (2021) demonstrate that whitening approaches must be regularized to maintain
      generalization performance. Given our results, we propose that decorrelation might be a sensible
526
      tradeoff, where signal correlations are removed but remaining signal (or noise) is not excessively
527
      rescaled.
528

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work we present an integration of a set of research directions including natural gradient
533
      descent, decorrelation and whitening, as well as approximate methods for gradient descent. Notably
534   we illustrate and describe how one component of natural gradients is often overlooked and can be
535   framed as correlations in feature data (at all layers of a neural network) bringing parameters into
536   a non-orthonormal relationship. We show that data correlation at every layer of a neural network
537   can be removed, in a similar fashion to neural competition, to enable orders of magnitude faster
538   training. These results and insights together suggest that failures of вҖҳbiologically-plausibleвҖҷ learning
539   approaches and learning rules for distributed computing can be overcome through decorrelation and
      the return of parameters to an orthogonal basis.

      In this work we limit our emphasis on whitening and focus more upon decorrelation. The reason
744   for this is two-fold. First, the off-diagonal elements of the decorrelation matrices cause the greatest
745   impact in skewing gradient descent. The diagonal elements simply scale up/down the gradient vector
746   and this can be trivially dealt with by modern optimizers (e.g. with the Adam optimizer (Kingma
747   & Ba, 2014)). Second, for data in which features are often zero or extremely sparse, normalizing
748   for unit variance can result in extremely large valued activations (distributions with extremely long
749   tails) or unstable updates in the stochastic updating regime.
750   Wadia et al. (2021) pointed out that the restriction to whitened data can restrict the space of gener-
751   alization unless properly regularized. This issue is one to keep in mind when developing methods
752   which speed up training of models significantly and generalization performance should be a point
753   of concern. However, as also described by Wadia et al. (2021), it may be that a regularized form
754   of whitening/decorrelation would in fact be optimal for training for maximum generalization per-
755   formance. Ultimately, we opt to avoid enforcing strict whitening and rely on decorrelation as an
      alternative and find it to be performative in practice.
