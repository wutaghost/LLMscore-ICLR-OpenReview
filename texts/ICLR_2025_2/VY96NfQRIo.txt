                                                        A BSTRACT
013
014
                   Inverse folding models play an important role in structure-based design by pre-
                   dicting amino acid sequences that fold into desired reference structures. Models
015
                   like ProteinMPNN, a message-passing encoder-decoder model, are trained to re-
016
                   liably produce new sequences from a reference structure. However, when applied
017                to peptides, these models are prone to generating repetitive sequences that do not
018                fold into the reference structure. To address this, we fine-tune ProteinMPNN to
019                produce diverse and structurally consistent peptide sequences via Direct Prefer-
020                ence Optimization (DPO). We derive two enhancements to DPO: online diversity
021                regularization and domain-specific priors. Additionally, we develop a new under-
022                standing on improving diversity in decoder models. When conditioned on Open-
023                Fold generated structures, our fine-tuned models achieve state-of-the-art structural
024                similarity scores, improving base ProteinMPNN by at least 8%. Compared to stan-
025
                   dard DPO, our regularized method achieves up to 20% higher sequence diversity
                   with no loss in structural similarity score.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Engineering biopolymers that fold into desired 3D structures, a computational challenge known
032
      as inverse protein folding problem, has broad applications in drug discovery and material science
      (Yang et al., 2023; Dill et al., 2008; Abascal & Regan, 2018). Several approaches for inverse folding
033
      have been adopted over the past decades, from molecular dynamics simulations to machine learning
034
      approaches (Dauparas et al., 2022b; Shanker et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2022a; Yi et al., 2023; Correa,
035   1990). In the standard machine learning approach, a molecular backbone chain serves as input, and
036   a model generates sequences that adopt folding topologies compatible with the reference backbone.
037   Sequences do not necessarily share sequence homology, as multiple diverse sequences can fold into
038   similar structures (Hsu et al., 2022a; Yue & Dill, 1992; Godzik et al., 1993).
039
      Peptides, which are small biopolymers comprising 2-50 residues, are interesting targets for inverse
040
      folding given their role in diverse biological functions, acting as hormones, neurotransmitters, sig-
041   nalling molecules, or nanostructures assemblers (Chockalingam et al., 2007; Torres et al., 2019;
042   Copolovici et al., 2014; Ulijn & Smith, 2008). Only about 225,000 protein structures have been
043   experimentally determined1 and made available via the Protein Data Bank (PDB). Training inverse-
044   folding machine learning models in a supervised fashion is a challenging task, due to the complexity
045   of the problem and the limited amount of experimental data. The challenge is aggravated in the
046   peptide domain as fewer than 3.5% PDB structures contain 50 residues or less. In fact, applying
047   the SCOP classification filter in the PDB to display structures labelled as “Peptide” reveals only 509
048   entries, circa 0.2% of all experimentally determined structures available.
049   In addition to the lack of data, sequences are subject to composition bias. The incidence of certain
050   amino acids may differ depending on the sequence length , as longer proteins have more options
051   for accommodating multiple secondary structures and folding loops (Tiessen et al., 2012). Popular
052   models like ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022b), PiFold (Gao et al., 2022) and ESM-IF1 (Hsu
053

      et al., 2022b) are primarily trained on data derived from longer proteins, leading to poor perfor-
055   mance for peptide design tasks. Additionally, shorter sequences fold into simpler structures. Indeed,
056   Milner-White & Russell (2008) argue that short peptides are notoriously “structureless” and tend to
057   flicker between conformations. For example, a structural conformation of a single alpha helix or
058   beta sheet – or a combination of two or three of them – is not necessarily stable and can fluctuate.
059
      Existing inverse folding models optimize sequence residue-recovery accuracy and structural sim-
060
      ilarity via template modeling (TM) score (Zhang & Skolnick, 2004). However, they often suffer
061   from low sampling diversity (Gao et al., 2023b). Ideally, the inverse folding model generates maxi-
062   mally diverse candidate sequences, as additional design filters, such as synthesizability and thermal
063   stability, reduce the number of potential hits downstream of the design process.
064
      To address these issues, we apply Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023), a
065
      fine-tuning method, to improve inverse-folding methods for peptide design. To the authors’ knowl-
066
      edge, we are the first to apply DPO to this task. We propose several enhancements to DPO to address
067   specific problems that arise in inverse folding. Particularly, we forward fold generated sequences
068   and derive an online regularized algorithm for optimizing structural similarity to the reference and
069   sequence diversity simultaneously. We show empirically that this algorithm targets the differential
070   entropy in log-probability space. Furthermore, we present a simple reward scaling approach to in-
071   corporate scalar reward information, showing that reward scaling adaptively selects a KL divergence
072   penalty (Kullback, S. and Leibler, R. A., 1951) to improve performance on harder structures.

075   2    P RELIMINARIES
076
077   Inverse folding is the problem of inferring the sequence of amino acids y that fold into a
078   given protein structure. This protein structure is represented by a set of coordinates x =
079   (xi , yi , zi ) | i = 1, . . . , n ∈ R3n ,where each (xi , yi , zi ) represents the 3D position of a backbone
080   atom and n the number of atoms. The inverse folding problem is underconstrained; there may be
081
      many solutions y that fold into structures similar to x (Koehl & Levitt, 1999). Prior research is pri-
      marily concerned with recovering the ”ground-truth” sequence yx from the experimental (reference)
082
      structure (Dauparas et al., 2022a; Hsu et al., 2022b; Jing et al., 2021b;a). Recently, forward folding
083
      models like AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021), ESMFold (Lin et al., 2022), and OpenFold (Ahdritz
084   et al., 2022), have made it possible to estimate the structural similarity between generated sequences
085   and the reference structure. In this work, we focus on measuring structural similarity of generated
086   sequences to the reference structure via the self-consistency TM-score (sc-TM) (Gao et al., 2023b).
087
      ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022a) is a popular inverse-folding method that produces full pro-
088
      tein sequences from backbone features (distances and orientations between backbone atoms, back-
089
      bone dihedral angles, accounting for a virtual Cβ atom). ProteinMPNN is a 6-layer encoder-decoder
090   message-passing neural network based on the ’Structured Transformer’ (Ingraham et al., 2019). Un-
091   like autoregressive methods like ESM-IF1 (Hsu et al., 2022b), ProteinMPNN decodes residues in a
092   random decoding order, as opposed to a fixed left-to-right order from N-terminus to the C-terminus.
093   ProteinMPNN is trained on examples from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) to determine the most
094   likely residues for a given protein backbone. On native protein backbones, it achieves a sequence
095   recovery of 52.4%, compared to 32.9% for Rosetta (Leman et al., 2020). In this work we build upon
096   ProteinMPNN by proposing methods for adapting it to peptide design.
097   DPO for inverse folding. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) is a popular method for aligning
098   Large Language Models to a dataset of human-produced preference assignments, that discrimi-
099   nate amongst the responses grouped by prompt (Rafailov et al., 2023). We adapt DPO to fine-tune
100   inverse-folding models by replacing human preference labels on generated sentences with TM-score
101   rankings on generated sequences. Specifically, we generate a dataset of sequences conditioned on a
102   set of reference structures, and score each sequence with the TM-score computed between its pre-
103   dicted structure and the reference structure. These scores define preference pairs over the generated
104
      sequences, for each reference structure.
105   Let r(x, y) be the TM-score between structure x and the predicted fold of sequence y, and πref
106   be a conditional distribution over y given x. Given a dataset D mapping structures (“prompts”)
107   to K generated sequences per structure (“responses”), DPO is derived within the KL-constrained
      reward-maximization objective (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023):

134   3     P REFERENCE OPTIMIZATION FOR PEPTIDE DESIGN
135
136   In this section, we consider designing fine-tuning methods well-suited for peptide design. While
137   DPO in its original formulation is useful for fine-tuning in biology (Park et al., 2023; Widatalla
138   et al., 2024; Mistani & Mysore, 2024), we consider how it may be improved to tackle specific
139
      problems arising in inverse-folding for peptides (i.e., poor generation diversity and lack of peptide
      data in initial training). First, we derive a diversity-optimized DPO loss by incorporating an online
140
      diversity penalty directly into the top-level Reinforcement Learning objective. Next, we propose
141
      an ad-hoc modification to the DPO reward that incorporates scalar TM-scores instead of solely
142   preference pairs, and address the distribution shift between peptides and longer-length proteins.

      3.2   E NTROPY IN DIVERSITY OPTIMIZATION
196
197   Here, we consider the mechanism behind diversity regularization, showing that diversity improves
198   due to randomness in the token decoding order during sampling. Particularly, we develop a new
199   understanding of ProteinMPNN based on random decoding order. ProteinMPNN performs random-
200   order token decoding during sampling and all forward passes. So, for a fixed policy π, sequence y,
201   structure x, we highlight that log π(y | x) is a random variable, depending on a distribution over
202   token decoding orders. Let ℓ(π,x,y) = log π(y | x) denote this random variable, and let fπ (d)
203   be a function parameterized by π that takes decoding orders d to log-probabilities s. In practice,
204
      fπ is a forward pass through ProteinMPNN, and d is a uniformly chosen permutation over indices
      {1, 2, . . . , |y|}. For a decoding order d ∼ U sampled from the uniform decoding order distribution
205
      U, we can compute a log-probability s = fπ (d). For this s, ℓ(π,x,y) (s) gives the probability, over all
206
      possible d, that the log-probability of y|x under π is s.
207
208   Revisiting training with random log-probabilities. This new viewpoint allows us to re-evaluate
209   the training of random-order decoder models, like ProteinMPNN. The implicit rewards in Eqs.3
210   and 7, from which a MLE loss is derived, hide the stochastic nature of log π(y|x). In the explicit
211
      decoding order notation, we can write Eq. 3 as

234   3.3   L EVERAGING DOMAIN - SPECIFIC PRIORS
235
236   Aligning train set and base model. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) is a standard part of the DPO
237   pipeline (Rafailov et al., 2023),where a gradient-based optimizer maximizes the log-probability of
238   a target sequence prior to applying DPO. The left part of Fig. 1 shows that the distribution of to-
239   kens sampled from base ProteinMPNN and the peptide training dataset differs significantly. SFT
240   assuages the token distribution problem by improving alignment with training distribution, and en-
241
      sures consistency with previous research, where DPO has been applied to related biological tasks
      (Park et al., 2023; Widatalla et al., 2024; Mistani & Mysore, 2024).
242
243   Incorporating scalar rewards. Given multiple responses from a single prompt, DPO is derived
244   assuming access to pairwise preferences. However, since we measure the reward of a sequence
245   generation by the TM-score between the original structure and the generated sequence’s predicted
246   structure, we have access to a total ordering over generations through scalar rewards for each re-
247
      sponse. Widatalla et al. (2024) derives weighted DPO to incorporate scalar rewards, but it does not
      substantially outperform standard DPO.
248
249   We consider a simple ad-hoc method to incorporate these scalar scores. Given a structure x, K
250   generated sequences yk | x, and K corresponding TM-scores rk | x, we scale the log-probabilities of
251   πref by the average of rk | x. To see the effect of this scaling, consider the modified implicit reward:

255                   = βR(x) [log πθ (y | x) − log πref (y | x)] + (β − βR(x)) log πθ (y|x) + β log Z(x)

260   When R(x) is large, the first term (mirroring the standard DPO reward, Eq. 3) has larger weight, and
261   the second term (a penalty on the entropy H(πθ ) = −E [log πθ (y | x)]) has smaller weight. Since
262   the weight on the DPO reward controls the strength of KL divergence regularization (Rafailov et al.,
263   2023), large R(x) corresponds to less aggressive optimization and a lower-entropy policy.
264   When R(x) is near 1.0, the data-generating policy (i.e., base ProteinMPNN) already produces high-
265   quality sequences on structure x. Therefore, we perform less aggressive optimization when R(x) is
266   large, and vice versa. Effectively, per structure, this reward scaling adapts the KL divergence penalty
267   to the strength of the dataset-generating policy. The right part of Fig. 1 shows that this reward scaling

269   Scaling log-probabilities improves their ability to rank sequences by TM-score, which provides a

286
287   Here we present results on benchmarks across a suite of inverse folding models evaluated on peptide
288   design tasks, as well as an exploration into the behavior of the two proposed algorithm enhance-
289   ments (diversity regularization and reward scaling). We find that diversity regularization is effective
290   in improving sampling diversity, and provide justification as to how this improvement happens. Ad-
291
      ditionally, we find reward scaling produces a small improvement in TM-score, enabling fine-tuned
      ProteinMPNN to reach SOTA performance in some situations.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL OVERVIEW
295
      Datasets. We fine-tune trained ProteinMPNN from Dauparas et al. (2022a) on a set of 211,402 dedu-
296   plicated peptide structures with length up to 50 amino acids, derived from the ColabFold database
297   (Mirdita et al., 2022) by filtering for predicted local distance difference test (pLDDT) ≥ 80. Each
298   structure was used to generate 4 candidate sequences using pretrained ProteinMPNN with T = 0.1.
299   Each sequence, including the true reference sequence from the structure, was folded with OpenFold
300   (Ahdritz et al., 2022). TM-scores were computed to create a ranking       over generated sequences for
      every structure prompt. Each structure therefore contributed 42 = 6 chosen-rejected pairs for DPO,

301
302   for a total of 1,268,412 training pairs. Details of the folding process can be found in Appendix A.1.
303   Benchmarks. We consider two non-overlapping benchmarks. First, we take 50 sequences from
304   the OpenFold set, enforcing a sequence identity threshold of 0.4 from the train set and filtering for
305   sequence length L ≤ 50. Next, we take the CATH 4.3 test split, as used in Hsu et al. (2022b), filter
306   for sequence identity threshold of 0.4 and L ≤ 60, and use the resulting 173 peptides as a second
307   benchmark. The OpenFold set contains structures resolved via OpenFold, while the CATH split has
308   structures from the PDB. For evaluation, we sample 4 sequences per benchmark structure at T = 0.
309   We compute diversity, TM-score, and recovery metrics as in Gao et al. (2023b). Following Section
310   3.1, we define diversity as the average fraction of non-identical amino acids, computed pairwise
311
      across all sampled sequences for the same structure.
312   Hyperparameters. Supervised fine-tuning was run for 2 epochs with Adam through the PyTorch
313   implementation (Paszke et al., 2019; Kingma & Ba, 2014), with structures’ true sequence as the
314   target. DPO was run for 20 epochs with default Adam hyperparameters on pairs of generated se-
315   quences. We vary β based on the method used to facilitate a fair comparison across similar KL
316   budgets. For more details, see Appendix A.1.
317   Sweeps. We run DPO with diversity penalties α = {0.0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5} and DPO with/without reward
318   scaling, all on the same training set. All training is done with per-GPU batch size of 32 on a single
319   node with 8xA100 80GB GPUs. Each run takes about 8 hours wall-clock time.

420   4.4   D IVERSITY REGULARIZATION TARGETS DIFFERENTIAL ENTROPY
421
422   We now consider the explanation for diversity improvement presented in Section 3.2. Naively, we
423
      would expect diversity regularization to directly increase the entropy of the sampling distribution
      H(π) = −Ex∼D,y∼π [log π(y|x)]. In the middle part of Fig. 2, we show that this is not the case.
424
      Apart from β = 0.5 (an outlier as discussed in Section 4.3), increasing diversity does not increase
425
      entropy, which seems contradictory. However, this is expected under the differential entropy for-
426   mulation in Section 3.2, which argued that diversity optimization increases differential entropy in
427   the continuous log-probability space. To test this, we draw 128 samples from ℓ(π,x,y) for all (x, y)
428   structure-sequence test pairs, i.e., compute log-probabilities with 128 different decoding orders. In
429   the right part of Fig. 2, we show that the estimated Hd increases with larger α, as expected under
430   this theory. This agrees with our intuition, since Hd controls variability in model log-probabilities,
431   which decide the next token during sampling. That is, larger Hd leads to greater log-probability
      variability, leading to greater diversity.

462
      In this section, we consider how diversity optimization affects ProteinMPNN’s ability to recover
463
      native sequences from structures, and accurately predict the quality of generated sequences.
464
465   First, we explore whether sequence recovery (i.e., the ability to recover the conditioning structure’s
466   sequence) is still possible with diversity-regularized fine-tuning. Intuitively, it may seem that models
467
      with higher sampling diversity have lower native sequence recovery rate. In the left half of Fig. 4,
      it seems like this is the case, with sequence recovery rates decreasing as α increases across all
468
      temperatures. However, for small alpha (α = 0.1), the drop in sequence recovery compared to
469
      standard DPO is small. Additionally, this recovery gap disappears if we allow for more compute
470   during inference time. In the right side of Fig. 4, we take N samples from each model and compute
471   the best-of-N sequence recovery rate. As N grows, the gap between standard DPO recovery rate
472   and α = 0.1 recovery rate shrinks to nearly zero. Therefore, optimizing for diversity does not
473   significantly impact sequence recovery rate, particularly as more sequences are sampled.
474
      Gao et al. (2023b) claims that log-probabilities may correlate with sequence quality, e.g. TM-score.
475
      Given that diversity regularization increases log-probability entropy as shown in Section 4.4, one
476   might expect this correlation to be less strong under more diverse models. As shown in the middle
477   of Fig. 4, this is not the case. Indeed, across all models, log-probabilities do not correlate with
478   TM-score.

      5   L IMITATIONS
516
517   While we illustrate an empirical connection between diversity optimization and differential entropy,
518   we do not establish a mathematical framework for how this optimization happens. Furthermore,
519   during the derivation of diversity-regularized DPO, we approximate π ⋆ with the latest iteration of
520   gradient descent. Exploring the bounds on this approximation, and establishing a theory-first per-
521   spective for the differential entropy framework, are promising directions for future research.
522   Since DPO and the variants proposed here are model-agnostic, they can be used to fine-tune any
523   inverse-folding model. Applying DPO to other inverse-folding models may produce even better
524   results compared to ProteinMPNN Gao et al. (2023b). For example, it may be possible to further
525   push the frontier of peptide sequence design by fine-tuning stronger base models like PiFold (Gao
526   et al., 2022) or KW-Design (Gao et al., 2023a).

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We fine-tuned ProteinMPNN, a widely adopted inverse folding model, for diverse and structurally
532
      consistent peptide sequence generation and proposed diversity-regularized DPO with an online sam-
533
      pling term to accurately estimate and encourage diversity. Domain-specific priors were also incorpo-
534   rated into our methodology to account for peptides’ residue distribution and dynamically control the
535   strength of KL divergence regularization. Our approach results in improvements on sampling diver-
536   sity, sequence recovery and structural similarity of the generated peptide sequences. Furthermore,
537   we give additional intuition on the impact of diversity regularization on differential and discrete
538   entropy. While our results are reported using ProteinMPNN as a base model for fine-tuning, our
539   proposed methods are agnostic to the inverse folding model, setting the grounds for future research
      in peptide design via fine-tuning.
