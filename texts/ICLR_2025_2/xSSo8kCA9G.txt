009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the increase in the number of parameters in large language models, the
012            process of pre-training and fine-tuning increasingly demands larger volumes of
013            GPU memory. A significant portion of this memory is typically consumed by the
014            optimizer state. To overcome this challenge, recent approaches such as low-rank
015            adaptation (LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)), low-rank gradient projection (GaLore (Zhao
016            et al., 2024a)), and blockwise optimization (BAdam (Luo et al., 2024)) have been
017            proposed. However, in all these algorithms, the effective rank of the weight updates
018
               remains low-rank, which can lead to a substantial loss of information from the
               gradient. This loss can be critically important, especially during the pre-training
019
               stage. In this paper, we introduce FRUGAL (Full-Rank Updates with GrAdient
020
               spLitting), a new memory-efficient optimization framework. FRUGAL leverages
021            gradient splitting to perform low-dimensional updates using advanced algorithms
022            (such as Adam), while updates along the remaining directions are executed via state-
023            free methods like SGD or signSGD (Bernstein et al., 2018). Our framework can be
024            integrated with various low-rank update selection techniques, including GaLore
025            and BAdam. We provide theoretical convergence guarantees for our framework
026            when using SGDM for low-dimensional updates and SGD for state-free updates.
027            Additionally, our method consistently outperforms concurrent approaches across
028            various fixed memory budgets, achieving state-of-the-art results in pre-training and
029
               fine-tuning tasks while balancing memory efficiency and performance metrics.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT (OpenAI, 2023) and LLaMA-3
034
      Dubey et al. (2024) have demonstrated remarkable performance across various disciplines (Brown,
035
      2020b; Yang et al., 2024; Romera-Paredes et al., 2024). However, a critical factor in achieving
036   these results is the size of these models (Hoffmann et al., 2022). A larger number of parameters not
037   only increases computational cost but also significantly raises memory requirements. For instance,
038   training an 8 billion parameter LLaMA model in a 16-bit format necessitates each parameter to
039   occupy 2 bytes, resulting in 16GB for storing the parameters and an additional 16GB for gradients.
040   Utilizing the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014), which is standard for pre-training and fine-tuning
041   LLMs, adds a further 32GB of memory to store the m and v statistics, resulting in 64GB total amount
042   of memory. Furthermore, to achieve higher-quality results, training in pure 16-bit format is often
043   insufficient (Zamirai et al., 2020). This necessitates storing master weights and optimizer statistics in
044
      32-bit format, leading to total memory demands that exceed the capacity of cutting-edge graphics
      cards, such as the A100-80GB.
045
046   Numerous research projects have been aimed at reducing these significant costs. These approaches
047   include engineering solutions like gradient checkpointing Chen et al. (2016) and memory offloading
048   (Rajbhandari et al., 2020), which do not change the training trajectory. There are also methods
049   that adjust the training algorithm by decreasing the number of trainable parameters (Frankle &
050
      Carbin, 2018; Wang et al., 2023; Sreenivasan et al., 2022; HorvaŐĀth et al., 2024) or their bit precision
      (Wortsman et al., 2023), as well as optimizer statistics (Dettmers et al., 2021; Shazeer & Stern, 2018;
051
      Zhang et al., 2024c). In this work, we concentrate on the latter category.

      However, PEFT methods have a fundamental limitation: parameter updates always lie in a low-
      dimensional subspace L, which prevents the use of these methods for pre-training (Lialin et al., 2023)
075
      and may constrain their capabilities in fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2024a).
076
077   Recent works, such as GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a), ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2023), BAdam (Luo
078   et al., 2024) and BlockLLM (Ramesh et al., 2024), offer a solution to this problem. These methods
079   enable higher-dimensional full-parameter learning by periodically changing the optimizable low-rank
080
      subspace L. However, even though these methods result in overall parameter changes that are
      high-dimensional, the updates in each step remain low-dimensional. The dimensionality of the frozen
081
      subspace dim M = dim L‚ä• significantly exceeds dim L. The remaining information contained in
082
      the gradient is not utilized for parameter updates. Nevertheless, this information can still be leveraged
083   to train the model.
084
085   We present the FRUGAL framework, designed to bridge this gap. Our approach stems from a crucial
086
      observation: although memory constraints prevent using optimizers with auxiliary optimizer state ‚ÄĒ
      such as Adam (Kingma, 2014) ‚ÄĒ in the remaining subspace M , one still can update M using state-
087
      free optimization algorithms like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) or signSGD (Bernstein et al.,
088
      2018). This solution allows for high-dimensional updates, which provides additional opportunities to
089   explore the parameter space and improves convergence. We will further refer to subspaces L and M
090   according to the types of optimizers used for their updates - state-full and state-free.

112   2                R ELATED WORK
113
114
      Memory-efficient full-parameter learning. Recent research has focused on reducing the memory
      footprint of LLMs by decreasing the size of optimizer states while maintaining their performance.
115
      Low-rank adaptation methods, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), inject trainable rank decomposition
116
      matrices into each layer of the model, reducing memory requirements by optimizing only a few
117   learnable adapters. ReLora (Lialin et al., 2023) builds upon this by merging low-rank adaptations
118   into the main model weights during training, potentially increasing the total rank of the update.
119   BAdam (Luo et al., 2024) leverages Block Coordinate Descent for full-parameter training by switch-
120   ing active blocks during fine-tuning. MicroAdam (Modoranu et al., 2024) compresses gradient
121   information before feeding it into the optimizer state, significantly reducing memory footprint while
122   enabling full parameter learning through error feedback mechanisms. GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a)
123   maintains full parameter learning by projecting gradients onto a low-rank subspace using truncated
124   SVD decomposition, storing optimizer states in this reduced space. Notably, GaLore achieves good
125
      results in pre-training, with performance close to that of Adam. However, while these methods
      effectively reduce memory overhead, they all perform low-rank updates at each iteration. In contrast,
126
      our approach utilizes all available gradient information to perform full-dimensional updates at each
127
      optimizer step, offering a novel perspective on memory-efficient optimization for LLMs.
128
129   Other memory-efficient optimization. Several other methods have been proposed to reduce the
130   memory footprint of optimizers. AdaFactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) attempts to mimic Adam‚Äôs
131
      behavior while reducing memory usage through factorization of the variance matrix v. Adam-
      mini (Zhang et al., 2024c) further reduces memory by storing only one value v per block. Dettmers
132
      et al. (2021) and Li et al. (2024) decrease memory footprint by quantizing optimizer states to the
133
      lower-precision representations. Lv et al. (2023) proposed to reduce weight gradient memory by
134   fusing the backward operation with the optimizer update. Notably, these approaches are orthogonal
135   to our method FRUGAL and can be combined with it for further memory efficiency.
136

139
      2015a). In recent years, a specific instance of BCD, known as layer-wise learning, has been applied
140   to deep learning. Notable examples include (Luo et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024), which leverage
141   this approach for LLM fine-tuning. To the best of our knowledge, our work presents the first
142   theoretical analysis of an extended BCD framework (Section 5) where the remaining coordinates are
143   also updated using a different algorithm. This novel approach extends traditional BCD techniques,
144   opening new avenues for full model optimization in deep learning.

      In this section, we present empirical evidence that motivates our approach. First, we show that
188
      access to the whole parameter space is crucial during training. Then, we show how utilizing full-rank
189   updates can significantly improve model performance.

191   3.1       T HE IMPORTANCE OF EXPLORING THE ENTIRE SPACE DURING THE TRAINING PROCESS
192
193   In recent work, Zhao et al. (2024a) proposed GaLore, an optimization method based on projecting the
194   gradient matrix G of each Linear layer2 onto a low-dimensional subspace. To obtain the projection
195   matrix P , they use SVD decomposition of Gt , which is recomputed with frequency T . The vectors
196   or rows of G are projected onto the first r left or right singular vectors, respectively. This approach
197   has theoretical foundations: the first r singular vectors correspond to the first r singular values and,
198
      therefore, should better utilize information from the spectrum of G.
199   The authors pointed out that calculating the SVD decomposition results in extra computational
200   overhead, which can be as much as a 10% increase as the hidden size of the model grows. To
201   minimize this cost and examine the significance of using SVD decomposition, one may wonder about
202   the possibility of employing a random semi-orthogonal projection matrix R instead of projecting
203   onto the first r singular columns with P . Surprisingly, while SVD decomposition provides better
204
      initial performance, random projection proves superior in long-term training, yielding significant
      improvements. As an illustration, we took the pre-training3 of a 130M model with LLaMA-like
205
      architecture on the C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2020). The results are presented in the first part
206
      of Table 1 (Optimizes state-free subspace = No), where we compare SVD and Random
207   projections. The ranks of both projections P and R are equal to 192.
208
209   To investigate this phenomenon, we pre-trained the LLaMA-60M model and collected gradients Gt
210
      from different iterations t for examination. Following the setup from GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a), we
      computed SVD decompositions and extracted projections Pt with a rank of 128. We evaluated the
211
      similarity of the projection matrices by calculating the principal angles between different projections
212
      Pt at different steps. Similarly to the observations in Q-Galore (Zhang et al., 2024d), we found that
213   these projections show minimal change during the training period; see Figure 2.

228   3.2    A DVANTAGE OF THE F ULL -R ANK U PDATES
229
230   The insight from the Section 3.1 suggests that the training of language models performs significantly
231   better when the entire parameter space is utilized during the training process. Given the importance
232   of updating parameters in all directions, this poses the question: Is it optimal to use low-rank
233
      updates, as employed by methods such as GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a), ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2023),
      and BAdam (Luo et al., 2024)? Using low-rank updates means the effective rank of the update is
234
      significantly smaller than the full dimensionality of the parameter space, inevitably leading to a loss
235
      of valuable information contained in the gradient.
236
237   However, the method to leverage the full-rank gradient for updating parameters is not readily obvious.
238   Using algorithms like Adam (Kingma, 2014) is not an option due to the memory overhead they
239
      introduce, which is precisely what we aim to avoid. An alternative approach is to use state-free
      optimizers such as SGD or signSGD (Bernstein et al., 2018). Unfortunately, SGD have been shown
240
      to be ineffective for training transformer models, as shown in Zhang et al. (2020); Pan & Li (2023).
241
242   Nevertheless, a recent study Zhao et al. (2024b) suggests a promising methodology: while SGDM
243   doesn‚Äôt generally work well with transformers, using SGDM for the majority of parameters and Adam
244   for a selected subset can lead to effective training. This raises the question: could a hybrid approach
245
      using SGD or signSGD instead of SGDM be viable? If the key subset of parameters is handled by
      advanced algorithms, can the other parameters be trained effectively with state-free optimizers?
246
247   To address this question, we conducted an experiment on LLaMA-130m, where we utilized the
248   Adam (Kingma, 2014) for state-full parameters and signSGD (Bernstein et al., 2018) for state-
249   free parameters. A detailed description of the experimental setup can be found at Appendix A.1.
250   Once again we used Random projection and highlighted the result in the second part of Table 1
251
      (Optimizes state-free subspace = Yes). Full-rank updates significantly enhance per-
      formance, approaching the efficiency of the memory-intensive Adam optimizer, which serves as a
252
      upper bound in terms of performance. These findings underscore the potential of state-free algorithms
253
      for updating a substantial portion of the parameter space, paving the way for efficient, scalable
254   optimization methods that deliver high performance without the significant memory costs traditionally
255   associated with state-of-the-art optimizers.

      ‚ÄĘ Training Duration. The training approach in Zhao et al. (2024a) aligns with the empirical rule
395
        from scaling laws (Hoffmann et al., 2022), which suggests using approximately 20 times the model
396
        size in tokens for training. However, this number of tokens is far from achieving convergence. In
397     practice, models are typically trained for significantly longer periods (Touvron et al., 2023b; Zhang
398     et al., 2024b). One reason for this discrepancy is that the original scaling laws do not account for
399     the inference of the model after training. Adjustments to scaling laws considering this parameter
400     are discussed, for example, in (Sardana & Frankle, 2023). For our experiments we chose 200k
401     steps for the 60M and 130M models, 240k for 350M model and 300k for the 1B model.
402   ‚ÄĘ Learning Rate. The authors of GaLore suggested using different learning rates for fixed un-
403     projectable parameters (Embeddings, RMSNorms (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019), Logits) and the
404     remaining projectable parameters (attention and FFN weights modules weights). However, in-
405     troducing additional hyperparameters complicates the use of the algorithm. Since both sets of
406     parameters are state-full and trained using the same optimization algorithm, we always used the
407     same learning rate for them in FRUGAL and BAdam. For GaLore learning rate see Section 6.1.
408   ‚ÄĘ Mixed Precision instead of the pure bfloat16 training. Pure 16-bit training has been shown to
409     potentially compromise model convergence and accuracy (Zamirai et al., 2020). This degradation
410     stems from storing both the model weights and optimizer statistics in reduced precision formats
411     such as float16 or bfloat16. However, these formats often lack sufficient precision in representing
412
        floating-point numbers. Consequently, mixed precision training has become a more common
        approach for training language models (Le Scao et al., 2023; Almazrouei et al., 2023)). While
413
        training in pure 16-bit format is also possible, stochastic rounding (Gupta et al., 2015; Zamirai
414
        et al., 2020) is often employed to mitigate the aforementioned issue. Given that the goal of this
415     research is to identify the optimal optimization algorithm, we deemed it more appropriate to
416     compare optimizers in a transparent and stable setup that does not require auxiliary tricks. Hence,
417     we primarily used Mixed Precision training for its illustrative value in understanding each method‚Äôs
418     potential. However, for completeness, we also conducted experiments in pure bfloat16 format,
419     detailed in our ablation study Section 6.1.2.

533   7      C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we introduce a new memory-efficient optimization framework, FRUGAL. Within this
536   framework, the optimization space is divided into two subspaces: the first is updated using a state-full
537   algorithm such as Adam, while the second is updated using a state-free algorithm such as signSGD.
538   We prove theoretical convergence guarantees for our framework with SGDM serving as the state-full
539   algorithm and SGD as the state-free algorithm. In experiments involving pre-training and fine-tuning
      of language models, FRUGAL outperforms other approaches while using the same or smaller memory.

      A     E XPERIMENTAL SETUPS
757
758
      This section describes the main setups used in the experiments and presents additional experiments.
759
760   To begin, we introduce the hyperparameter density ŌĀ. This hyperparameter represents the fraction of
761   the total space in Linear layers that is updated with a stateful optimizer. For GaLore, this parameter
762   is equal to ŌĀ = r/h, where r is the projection rank, and h is the hidden size of the model. For
763
      RandK projection, this parameter can be expressed as 1 ‚ąí s, where s means sparsity. For BAdam and
      FRUGAL with the blockwise update, this parameter denotes the ratio of the number of active blocks
764
      ablock to the total number of blocks p, i.e., ŌĀ = ablock /p. When using FRUGAL with the column-wise
765
      update, as in Section 6.2, ŌĀ is equal to the ratio of the number of active columns acolumn to their total
766   number h, i.e., ŌĀ = acolumn /h.
767

      A.1    P RE - TRAINING SETUP
769
770   We adopt a LLaMA-based architecture with RMSNorm and SwiGLU (Wang, 2018) activations on the
771   C4 dataset. Following Zhao et al. (2024a), we trained using a batch size of 512 sequences, sequence
772   length of 256, weight decay of 0, and no gradient clipping. We used T5 tokenizer, since it also was
773   trained on C4 with dictionary size equal to 32k. The update frequency T is set to 200.
774
      Since, unlike GaLore, we consider not only matrix projections, we decided to generalize the concept

776   state-full subspace. Thus, for SVD-like projection as in GaLore, the density equals ŌĀ = r/h, where h
777   denotes the hidden dimension of the model. We also should point out that similarly to Zhao et al.
778   (2024a), we keep Embeddings, RMSNorms (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019), and Logits in the state-full
779   subspace throughout the training and don‚Äôt reset the optimizer state for them.

925   C    O PTIMIZER STATE MANAGEMENT
926
927   In this section, we would like to propose some modifications to the GaLore algorithm. These
928   modifications are also used in our framework as SVD projection.
929   Specifically, we want to consider the projection of the state when changing the active subspace. In
930   GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a), when updating the projection, the optimizer states M and V do not
931   change. This results in new projected gradients and old M and V being in different subspaces. This
932   implementation has little effect on the result with large values of update frequency T , as the values
933   of M and V from the previous subspace decay exponentially quickly. However, more frequent
934   changes T significantly affect the result. We hypothesize that this is why in Zhao et al. (2024a) the
935   model quality degraded so significantly when T was decreased, while as seen in Table 10, FRUGAL
936   experiences much less degradation.
937   There are two different ways to overcome this obstacle: either project the state back to full-rank space
938   or reset the state before a new round. However, the first option may be challenging in the case of
939   arbitrary projection. Specifically, while it‚Äôs possible to project momentum back to full-rank space (see
940   Alg. 2 in Hao et al. (2024)), the same cannot be easily done with variance because its values depend
941   quadratically on the projection matrix. However, the projection of variance will also be trivial if the
942   set of basis vectors for the projection is fixed, which is true, for example, for coordinate projection
943
      with RandK.

       In this section we present additional experiments.
1542
1543   Connection between density and type of the projection. First, we present the results of the
1544   experiments that follow the setup of Table 1 but explore different density ŌĀ values: 0.0625, 0.125,
1545
       0.333, and 0.5 (while in Table 1 we use ŌĀ = 0.25). The results, presented in Table 15, align with
       our findings from Table 1. Specifically, training with random projection significantly outperforms
1546
       SVD projection when training without optimizing the state-free subspace. When state-free subspace
1547
       optimization is employed, SVD projections marginally outperform their Blockwise counterparts.
1548
1549   Different state-full and state-free optimizers. Next, we conducted experiments for other state-full
1550   and state-free optimizers. We explored two variations: 1. replacing AdamW with Lion (Chen
1551
       et al., 2024) as the state-full optimizer, and 2. substituting signSGD with SGD as the state-free
       optimizer. We pre-trained LLaMA-130M on C4 for 200k steps with the hyperparameters specified
1552
       in Appendix A.1. We approached the Lion experiments in the same way as the Adam experiments:
1553
       first finding the optimal learning rate for the original algorithm through grid search, then using that
1554   same learning rate for both GaLore and FRUGAL. For SGD experiments, we kept state-full Adam‚Äôs
1555   learning rate constant while only adjusting the learning rate for the state-free optimizer.
1556
1557
       The results are presented in tables Table 16 and Table 17. As observed, the results for Lion are similar
       to those obtained with AdamW - the additional optimization of the state-free subspace significantly
1558
       improves performance, resulting in FRUGAL significantly outperforming GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024a).
1559
       While training with SGD as the state-free optimizer shows somewhat lower performance compared
1560   to signSGD, it still significantly outperforms both GaLore and BAdam (Luo et al., 2024). However,
1561   we would like to note that unlike signSGD, hyperparameter tuning for SGD training is considerably
1562   more challenging. This is because, unlike signSGD, whose update magnitudes approximately equal
1563   to those of popular Adam-like algorithms, the magnitude of updates (essentially, gradients) in SGD
1564   differs substantially, necessitating learning rates that deviate significantly from those used with the
1565   state-full optimizer. Furthermore, successful training with SGD absolutely requires gradient clipping,
       while the absence of such clipping is not a critical impediment for signSGD.
