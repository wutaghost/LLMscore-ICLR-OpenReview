011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The brain possesses highly efficient learning algorithms that have not been fully
014            understood. The gradient backpropagation (BP) serves as a powerful tool for
015
               training artificial neural networks, but it diverges from the known anatomical and
               physiological constraints of the brain. Conversely, biologically plausible learning
016
               algorithms have efficiency limitations in training deep neural networks. To bridge
017
               this gap, we introduce a perturbation-based approach called low-rank cluster or-
018            thogonal (LOCO) weight modification. Theoretical analysis shows that LOCO
019            provides an unbiased estimate of the BP gradient and achieves low variance in
020            gradient estimation. Compared with some brain-inspired algorithms, LOCO keeps
021            mathematical convergence guarantees and improves the efficiency. It can train the
022            deepest spiking neural networks to date without gradient backpropagation, achiev-
023            ing state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark datasets and exhibiting the
024            ability to overcome catastrophic forgetting. These findings suggest that biolog-
025            ically feasible learning methods can be substantially more efficient than previ-
026
               ously believed. Furthermore, avoiding gradient backpropagation allows LOCO
               to achieve O(1) time complexity for weight updates. This opens a promising av-
027
               enue for developing distributed computing systems that are more efficient than
028
               BP-based counterparts.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Brain is a highly efficient parallel learning system. Its learning algorithms are capable to configure
034   large network quickly to perform various tasks thereby enabling us to adapt to diverse environments.
035   In addition, brain’s algorithms can largely mitigate the problem of catastrophic forgetting, allowing
036   us to learn continually. So far, the key components underlying brain’s efficient learning is still not
037   fully understood. Artificial neural networks provide a useful opportunity to study the capability and
038   limitation of various learning algorithms. Currently, mainstream learning algorithms can be broadly
039
      categorized into two major classes: Backpropagation (BP)-based and nonBP-based Lillicrap et al.
      (2020); Bellec et al. (2020); Perich et al. (2018). BP and its variants achieve impressive results in a
040
      variety of machine intelligence tasks Sejnowski (2020); Pei et al. (2019); Ma et al. (2022). However,
041
      they rely on learning rules that are not biologically plausible Illing et al. (2019). Specifically, BP re-
042   quires exactly symmetric forward and backward connections to allow the gradients to be propagated
043   back, which also lead to weight transport problem Lillicrap et al. (2020) and update-locking prob-
044   lem Jaderberg et al. (2017). These requirements limit the parallelization in distributed computing
045   systems, preventing the optimization from being performed in a truly parallel manner.
046
      NonBP-based algorithms include Hebb-based, scalar feedback and vector feedback Lillicrap et al.
047
      (2020). Many of them are inspired by the brain and therefore are biologically plausible and suitable
048   for parallel computing Flower & Jabri (1992); Crafton et al. (2019); Payeur et al. (2021). Neverthe-
049   less, current nonBP-based algorithms exhibit notable limitations in training neural networks. Specif-
050   ically, these rules demonstrate satisfactory convergence only in shallow networks Adrien Journé
051   (2023), often with difficulties in training deeper architectures Amato et al. (2019); Legenstein et al.
052   (2005). Notably, node perturbation (NP) Widrow & Lehr (1990) is a biologically plausible algo-
053   rithm with convergence guarantees. It adjusts the connection weights by multiplying information
      from the pre- and postsynaptic membranes with a global reward signal Fiete et al. (2007); Kornfeld

054
      et al. (2020); Miconi (2017); Bouvier et al. (2018); Aronov et al. (2008); Ali et al. (2013). Although
055   NP has the advantage of mathematically guaranteed convergence in learning, its efficiency decreases
056   rapidly as the variance increases with the growing number of neurons in the network Malladi et al.
057   (2024); Hiratani et al. (2022). Therefore, understanding the brain’s high efficiency in learning re-
058   mains an open question. Solving this could not only provide insights into brain’s operation, but also
059   be instrumental for designing AI systems that can learn more efficiently and, at the same time, avoid
060   the limitation associated with BP, i.e., imperfect for large scale distributed computing.
061   Recently, empirical studies about neural networks in the brain have revealed two significant phe-
062   nomena, including 1) that neural representation that needs to be separated are often organized or-
063   thogonal to each other Flesch et al. (2022); Xie et al. (2022), suggesting that synaptic modifications
064   may be aimed at minimizing interference between different tasks Zeng et al. (2019); Duncker et al.
065   (2020); and 2) brain dynamics are often manifested in low-dimensional manifolds Flesch et al.
066   (2022); Perich et al. (2018); Goudar et al. (2023), suggesting that the learning happens in a low-rank
067   parameter space. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has incorporated low-rank and or-
068   thogonality constraints into non-BP algorithms. Additionally, there has been no theoretical analysis
069
      investigating the impact of orthogonality and low-rank constraints on learning efficiency.
070   In this work, we demonstrate that, within a low-rank setting, orthogonality constraints significantly
071   reduce the variance of gradient estimates, increase the upper bound of the learning rate, and ul-
072   timately enhance convergence efficiency. We proposed the LOCO (Low-rank Cluster Orthogonal
073   Weight Modification) algorithm, which is a NP-based learning rule that incorporates the low-rank
074   and orthogonal learning principles. As a result, LOCO ensures 1) synaptic modifications occur
075
      within a low-dimensional subspace spanned by historical input information. Thus, rather than being
      modified independently, synaptic weights are adjusted in a highly structured and correlated manner;
076
      and 2) different subspaces are task-specific and mutually orthogonal. We analytically show that
077
      LOCO can converge more quickly than the conventional NP algorithm. In addition, we demon-
078   strated empirically that LOCO is capable of training deeper spiking neural networks compared to
079   other plasticity-based mechanisms, resulting in the state-of-the-art results on several benchmark
080   datasets.
081
      Our results demonstrate that the efficiency of biologically plausible learning algorithms can be sub-
082
      stantially improved by implementing brain-inspired constraints, providing potential explanation for
083
      brain’s efficient learning capability. In addition, as a learning rule that avoids gradient backprop-
084   agation, LOCO is better suited for efficient training on large-scale distributed computing systems
085   compared to BP.

090   2.1   L OW- RANK C LUSTER O RTHOGONAL W EIGHT M ODIFICATION (LOCO)
091
092   LOCO is a NP-based learning algorithm. NP was proposed as a local optimization method that is bi-
093   ological feasible and can be implemented by distributed computing hardware Lillicrap et al. (2020).
094   Similar to BP, NP calculates local derivatives of a global loss function to individual network con-
095   nections, thereby executing the learning process. However, different from directly computing the
096   derivatives in BP, in NP, small perturbations of activity are introduced to each node in the network
097   to estimate the derivatives. Specifically, the derivatives of each weight are estimated by the product
098   of 1) the equivalent changes in connection weights that would lead to the same activity perturbation,
099
      and 2) the changes in the loss function. i.e., the reward, caused by the perturbation. As a result,
      if a perturbation improves the performance, the weights are modified to steer the network activity
100
      towards the direction of the perturbation, and vice versa. The strength of NP resides on 1) it requires
101
      only a global reward signal and information of local activity, which is similar to the brain’s learn-
102   ing mechanisms and suitable for parallel computing systems, 2) it is a unbiased estimation of the
103   derivatives in BP, which guarantees that the learning process of NP can convergence anywhere BP
104   can be implemented. However, due to the large variance in the gradient estimates caused by random
105   activity perturbation, the NP algorithm exhibits poor convergence efficiency Malladi et al. (2024),
106   leading to very slow learning speed even in shallow networks. Importantly, the convergence effi-
107   ciency deteriorates as the network depth increases, making it less suitable for training more complex
      networks.

145   To solve the efficiency problem, here we propose the LOCO algorithm that can significantly reduce
146   the variance in the learning process. In brief, LOCO introduces two extra constraints to the NP algo-
147
      rithm, i.e., the low-rank and orthogonality in the weights modification space, to greatly improve the
      efficiency of the learning process, while preserving the strength of local computation and provable
148
      convergence. Below we introduce LOCO and validate its effectiveness in Spiking Neural Networks
149
      (SNN) first and then show it can also be used to train Artificial Neural Networks (ANN).
150
151   In SNNs, we deployed a multilayer architecture constituted with Leaky Integrate and Fire (LIF)
152   neurons, as illustrated in Fig. 1A, with the number of layers varying from three to ten. The learning
153
      process involves two forward propagation runs. The first run was the standard one. The second run
      introduced a random perturbation to the membrane potential of individual hidden layer neurons. The
154
      perturbations caused minor difference in the network’s output values and the loss function compared
155
      to the first run. We name the difference in the loss function between the two runs as the temporal
156   difference (TD) error, which was broadcasted to the hidden layer of the network to guide the learning
157   (see Appendix for details). Specifically, based on the average firing rates of neurons in each layer
158   and the TD error, the weight modification ∆WlNP was obtained using the NP algorithm (Fig. 1B,
159   details about mathematical basis of NP is listed in Appendix). Additionally, the average firing
160   rates of neurons were also used to calculate LOCO constraints, which is a projection operator Pl
161   that restricts the weight modification to a subspace (Fig. 1C), resulting in ∆WlLOCO as the actual
      weight modification.

165   LOCO imposes two constraints: 1) low-rank and 2) orthogonality to the space of weights modifi-
166   cation (Sw ), which were achieved by project the weights modification vector calculated by the NP
167   algorithm ∆WlNP to a subspace using the orthogonal projector Pl (Eq. 1).
168   We note that for the NP algorithm, ∆WlNP is always pointing to the same direction as the input
169   vector X (Eq. 9). Thus, we can impose two constraints by reducing the space spanned by input
170   vectors for category/task i (Sxi ), and then applying Pl to Sxi to obtain the direction of ∆WlLOCO .
171   Specifically, to impose the low rank constraint, the original Sxi was reduced to low dimensions,
172   such as three dimensions of PC1-3, which were calculated using the principal component analysis
173   (Fig. 1D upper row, see also Eq. 6). As a result, the variance of gradient estimates is reduced. We
174   provide analytical prove that it enhances the convergence efficiency compared to the original NP
175   method (see Appendix for details).
176   Orthogonal constraint reduces interference between different categories/tasks, improving the stabil-
177   ity of the network’s learning process and also contributing to increase convergence efficiency. To
178   impose the orthogonal constraint when learning the category/task i, we first find the input vector xk
179   for k-th category/tasks by k-means clustering, and then calculate the space orthogonal to the space
180   spanned by all xk (k ̸= i) except the xi (Fig. 1D lower row, see also Eq. 5). Finally, projecting the
181   low dimension space Sxi onto this orthogonal space yielded the weight modification space under the
182   LOCO constraints (Eq. 6). The weight modification space constraint is represented as orthogonal
183   projector Pl . Taken together, by greatly reducing the parameter space for searching compared to the
184
      NP method, the LOCO algorithm lowers the variation in the training process, and limits interference
      between tasks. As a result, these effects increase the speed of convergence (Fig. 1E).

      jection Matrix (PlCO ) is computed. Then, the principal components of the input space are calculated
211

212
      jection matrix PlLOCO under the LOCO constraint.

      3.3   LOCO LEARNING IN THE ORTHOGONAL SPACE
455
456
457   The orthogonal constraint introduced to the LOCO method aims to learn new categories with mini-
458
      mal disturbance to existing mappings. It is achieved by the orthogonal projector P , which restricts
      the weight modifications to the orthogonal space of the principal components of other categories
459
      (see Methods for the details regarding the construction of P ). In the following analysis, we verify
460
      the orthogonal constraint empirically in the networks trained to preform the MNIST task.
461
462   First, we examines whether the weight modifications of the first category is orthogonal to others, by
463   measuring the cosine similarity between the two. Fig. S1A shows that the LOCO algorithm indeed
464
      lead to smaller similarity compared to the NP algorithm, indicating that the weight modifications of
      other categories were mainly occur in the complement space of the first category.
465
466   The small cosine similarity of weight modifications between first and other categories is a reason-
467   able result, indicating that weight modifications occur within the complement space of first category.
468   These results suggest that the LOCO algorithm indeed constrains weight modifications to be orthog-
469   onal to the weight modification spaces of other categories during the learning process, fulfilling the
470
      orthogonal constraint. In contrast, the NP algorithm’s weight modifications highly overlap with the
      spaces of other categories, leading to interference between categories.
471
472   Furthermore, we analyze the most sensitive weight when learning particular category and observe
473   their changes across different categories. Fig. S1B shows that the weights most sensitive to a given
474   category are uninvolved when learning other categories, suggesting the task specificity of weights
475   and the orthogonal relationship between weight modifications across different categories.
476   The effects of the orthogonal constraint in alleviating the interference across different categories can
477   be intuitively seen by looking at the learning curve of the system. Fig. S1C shows the accuracy
478   in recognizing digit ”1” along the training process. Compared with NP algorithm, LOCO indeed
479   significantly reduces the adverse effect caused by learning other digits.
480   Finally, we analyze whether there is an orthogonal relationship at the level of neuronal activity,
481   which has been reported in biological neural networks. Specifically, we compare the average cosine
482   similarities, defined as r, to measure orthogonality at the activity level between different categories
483   over the course of learning. Fig. S1D, E show that r decreases with learning progress, suggesting that
484   neural activities become orthogonal to each other between different categories. A direct visualization
485   of activities of digit ”0”, ”1”, and ”2” also illustrate the orthogonality in activities across different
      categories (Fig. S1F), similar to that has been observed in the biological brain Flesch et al. (2022).

      3.4   LOCO LEARNING IN THE LOW RANK SPACE
487
488
489   The low-rank constraint introduced to the LOCO method aims to reduce the parameter search space
490   during learning, thereby enhancing convergence efficiency. It is achieved by restricting the weight
491   modifications to a low-dimensional subspace spanned by the first k principal components of in-
492   put vectors from the individual categories (k ≤ 20 in the current study), which is much smaller
493   compared to the original dimensionality of the original parameter search space, i.e., the number of
494
      neurons in the hidden layer (up to 500 in the current study). We provide the analytic proof that the
      low-rank constraint we introduced improves convergence speed compared to the NP method (see
495
      Appendix for details). In the following analysis, we empirically verify that the weight modifications
496
      are indeed constrained to the low-dimensional space in the networks trained to preform the MNIST
497   task.
498
499   Fig. S2A shows that the rank of weight modification matrix during LOCO learning is much lower
500
      than that during the standard NP training, indicating that LOCO’s weight modifications occur in a
      low-dimensional space. Analyzing the distribution of the ranks of weight modifications (Fig. S2B)
501
      during training also reveals that the LOCO ranks predominantly distribute in low values. Modifying
502
      weights in a low-dimensional space also reduces interference between different categories during
503   training. Fig. S2C shows that modifying in low-rank space reduces the variation of network weights
504   while maintaining accuracy, indicating that maintaining network stability has a significant impact
505   on learning effectiveness. Concurrently, smaller magnitudes of weight adjustments imply that com-
506   putational hardware can achieve a reduction in energy consumption. Fig. S2D shows that the weight
507   modification of different categories reside in distinct subspaces. It can be proven that low-rank
508   weight modification is also associated with low-rank network activity (see Methods for details), and
509   the latter has been well documented in biological experiments Flesch et al. (2022).

      4     D ISCUSSION
514
515
516
517   There is a significant explanatory gap between the extraordinary learning capability of the brain
518   and the low performance of brain-inspired, non-BP-based learning rules in training deep neural
519
      networks, which are often necessary to perform complex tasks.
520   Non-BP-based learning rules are effective only for networks with relatively fewer, e.g., no more
521   than 5, layers. For instance, the Hebbian learning rule Pogodin & Latham (2020); Scellier & Bengio
522   (2017); Millidge et al. (2022) is effective in shallow networks, faces challenge in maintaining stabil-
523   ity and learning efficiency in deeper networks Amato et al. (2019); Legenstein et al. (2005). In a re-
524   cent study, Zhang et al. proposed the Spiking Backpropagation (SBP) algorithm, extended the depth
525
      of optimizable layers in spiking neural networks from 2 to 4 in SNN Zhang et al. (2021a). Journé
      et al. proposed the SoftHebb, allowing effective training for up to five hidden layers Adrien Journé
526
      (2023). Although vector feed back learning rules have been applied to train networks with multiple
527
      linear hidden layers Frenkel et al. (2019), as multiple linear hidden layers are equivalent to a single
528   linear layer, the networks trained in this way cannot achieve the same capability as deep non-linear
529   networks Lillicrap et al. (2014). In this work, we proposed LOCO introducing the orthogonality and
530   low-dimensionality in the process of training artificial neural networks. The relationship between
531   LOCO and biological mechanisms is detailed in the Appendix.
532
      LOCO suggests a promising path to develop efficient and reliable brain-inspired learning algorithms.
533
      By narrowing the explanatory gap between the brain’s highly efficient learning and less efficient
534   training of DNNs with non-BP methods, it shows that algorithms using simple scalar feedback for er-
535   ror signals can achieve efficient training of complex networks by introducing appropriate constraints
536   on weight modifications. Removing the burden of complex back propagation procedure in calculat-
537   ing gradients makes local learning rules like LOCO better suited for efficient distributed computing.
538   Furthermore, time complexity of feedback run during the LOCO training is O(1), meaning that all
539   weights can be modified simultaneously (see Appendix). Consequently, the feedback running time
      does not increase with the scale of the network, which is ideal for training large models.

1407   Recent empirical studies have revealed that neural activities in the brain are restricted to low-
1408
       dimensional manifolds and activity patterns exhibit orthogonal characteristics across different tasks,
       leading to the suggestion that these features may be the key to the brain’s efficient learning.
1409
1410   Flesch et al. Flesch et al. (2022) explored how neural networks effectively encode multiple tasks
1411   through orthogonality, which is manifested by projecting the representations of different tasks onto
1412   mutually orthogonal low-dimensional manifolds. Theoretically, such orthogonal manifold designs
1413   allow different tasks to be independent in neural representation, enhancing learning efficiency in
1414
       multitasking and reducing interference in learning different tasks. Indeed, functional Magnetic Res-
       onance Imaging (fMRI) results show that human brains employs similar orthogonal representation
1415
       patterns when processing different tasks, suggesting that the brain optimizes information process-
1416
       ing using orthogonality to minimize interference between tasks. In addition, Libby and Buschman
1417   Libby & Buschman (2021) discovered that the brain reduces interference between sensation and
1418   memory of the same auditory stimuli by rotating sensory representations into orthogonal memory
1419   representations. Recording of neural activities in the auditory cortex of mice showed that neural
1420   populations represent sensory input and memory along two orthogonal dimensions. This transfor-
1421   mation process, facilitated by ”stable” neurons (maintaining selectivity) and ”switching” neurons
1422   (reversing selectivity over time), effectively converts sensory input into memory. Model simulation
1423   also confirmed that the rotation dynamic and orthogonal representations can protect memory from
1424   sensory interference. These studies suggest that orthogonal representation play a significant role in
1425
       reducing interference between tasks.
1426   In the study by Flesch et al. Flesch et al. (2022), another discovery is the existence of low-
1427   dimensional, task-specific representations in human brains, particularly in the prefrontal areas. Neu-
1428   ral encoding along task-irrelevant dimensions is compressed, yet this compression still retains the
1429   original space of the inputs. It suggests that the brain can handle multitasking and complex sce-
1430   narios by encodes tasks through low-dimensional representations. In addition, the low-dimensional
1431
       dynamics of neural population activities have been well documented in the areas of motor learning
       and motor execution. For example, Sadtler et al. (2014) investigated neural activity patterns in the
1432
       primary motor cortex of rhesus monkeys during learning, revealed intrinsic constraints in the form
1433
       of low-dimensional manifolds. Another study Perich et al. (2018) found that the brain uses low-
1434   dimensional neural population activities for rapid behavioral adaptation. In the premotor (PMd) and
1435   primary motor (M1) cortices , despite high-dimensional complexity in neuronal activities, the core
1436   functional connectivity remains stable. As a result, the activity of hundreds of motor neurons is rep-
1437   resented in a low-dimensional manifold that reflects the covariance across the neuronal population.
1438   Pandarinath et al. (2018) discovered co-activation patterns among neurons. This means that even
1439   without observing all neurons, the brain’s computational processes can be understood by analyzing
1440   a few key latent factors. This led to the proposal of the Latent Factor Analysis Dynamic Systems
1441   (LFADS) method. Applying this method to M1 and PMd also led to the discovery that the activities
1442
       of a large number of neurons can be described by low-dimensional dynamics. In addition, Goudar
       et al.’s study Goudar et al. (2023) explored the acceleration of learning speed in neural networks
1443
       when learning similar problems. They found that network activity patterns in the learning process
1444
       are formed within the low-dimensional subspace of neural activity, and efficiency in learning similar
1445   tasks can be enhanced by restricting parameter exploration within this low-dimensional subspace.
