                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010            This paper presents a novel approach to aligning large language models (LLMs)
011            with individual human preferences, sometimes referred to as Reinforcement
012            Learning from Personalized Human Feedback (RLPHF). Given stated preferences
013            along multiple dimensions, such as helpfulness, conciseness, or humor, the goal
014
               is to create an LLM – without completely re-training – that best adheres to this
               specification. Starting from specialized expert LLMs, each trained for one such
015
               particular preference dimension, we propose a black-box method that merges their
016
               outputs on a per-token level. We train a lightweight Preference Control Model
017            (PCM) that dynamically translates the preference description and current context
018            into next-token prediction weights. By combining the expert models’ outputs at
019            the token level, our approach dynamically generates text that optimizes the given
020            preference. Empirical tests show that our method matches or surpasses existing
021            preference merging techniques, providing a scalable, efficient alternative to fine-
022            tuning LLMs for individual personalization.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools for content genera-
029   tion and personal assistants; however, they must be closely aligned with human preferences to ensure
030   safety and meet users’ expecations. Methods like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
031   (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) align models with general human preferences, however, with the
032   widespread adoption of LLMs comes the need to for alignment with respect to individual prefer-
033
      ences. For example, an LLM used by a child should be easy to understand and contain safeguards,
      whereas the one used by an IT professional should generate far more technical details.
034
035   Prior work (Jang et al., 2023) has pioneered breaking down preferences into individual dimensions
036   along bases of preferences, e.g. harmful, helpful, concise, or funny. Such a breakdown is simple
037   and intuitive, while allowing for a user-friendly framework to define any specific preference as a
038   combination along these bases dimensions. However, model fine-tuning within such a framework
039
      is nontrivial due to the curse of dimensionality: The number of possible combinations increases
      exponentially with the number of dimensions—rendering existing adaptation methods based on fine-
040
      tuning via RLHF intractable very quickly.
041
042   To this end, Jang et al. (2023) and others (Wang et al., 2024a; Guo et al., 2024) explored Reinforce-
043   ment Learning from Personalized Human Feedback (RLPHF). Starting with a pre-trained LLM, they
044   create multiple copies and fine-tune each one with respect to a single preference dimension, i.e., one
045
      expert model for humor, another for conciseness, etc. During the fine-tuning process the updates are
      kept low-rank, using LoRA (Hu et al., 2021). Given any user specific preference—a combination
046
      of dimensions such as helpful and funny—they create a new LLM by directly merging the LoRA
047
      weights of the experts corresponding to the target preference. Although highly innovative and suc-
048   cessful, one of the main shortcomings of these approaches is that the weight merging is independent
049   of the context. For example, if the user wants to generate a humorous, harmless, non-technical,
050   poem about tulips, the humorous expert alone might generate up to specification. By averaging its
051   model weights with the harmless and non-technical experts, the humor can be washed out and lost.
052   Further limitations are that each expert model must be fine-tuned from the exact same architecture,
053   the user must have access to the model weights, and that model weights must be swapped out to
      service multiple users.

054
      In this paper we introduce Merged Preference Dimensions (MPD), a novel approach to RLPHF
055   that dynamically weighs and combines the outputs of expert LLMs on the fly instead of merging
056   their model parameters. Notably, we compute different weights for each token, depending on the
057   preceding context and the user’s preference description. Similar to prior work, MPD also starts with
058   pre-trained expert LLMs. However, unlike prior work based on weight-merging, it is a black-box
059   method that does not require access to expert model weights. All it requires access to is the top
060   output logits (or probabilities) of each expert.
061   In order to dynamically compute the weights to merge the outputs of the expert LLMs, we train a
062   lightweight preference control model (PCM) that takes as input the current context and preference
063   description and outputs the weights for merging probabilities of the next token. During inference
064   we combine the posterior distributions of the individual expert models through a weighted average,
065   where the PCM produces the weight for each expert. For training, we leverage a reward model for
066   each preference dimension and use the online Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithm REBEL (Gao
067   et al., 2024) to train the PCM to maximize the average reward of the dimensions specified in the per-
068   sonal preference. By merging model outputs at the token-level rather than the models’ parameters,
069
      our approach is trivially parallelizable during inference and requires no re-training when preferences
      change (e.g. a new user with different requirements appears).
070
071   Empirically, we demonstrate that the MPD performance surpasses the performance of prior pref-
072   erence merging techniques, despite making far fewer assumptions on the models and their archi-
073   tectures. We evaluate our method with the Tulu-7B (Wang et al., 2023) LLM on two multifaceted
074   preference datasets Koala (Geng et al., 2023) and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023), showing that
075
      our method achieves higher pairwise win-rates (measured by both GPT4 and humans) over all other
      methods averaged across all preference combinations. Further, we demonstrate that, due to more
076
      effective parallelization, MPD is faster than prior weight merging work on most modern computing
077
      resources.

080   2   R ELATED W ORK
081
082   Alignment of Language Models to Human Preferences. Alignment of language models to hu-
083   man preferences has arguably begun with prompting (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019;
084   Chowdhery et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023). However, without any finetuning, these models
085
      sometimes produce outputs that are not well-aligned with human values or preferences (Gehman
      et al., 2020; Ousidhoum et al., 2021; Cho et al., 2022); recent works study how to improve their
086
      alignment with a general human preference with additional fine-tuning. Many current methods fol-
087
      low the Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) paradigm, popularized by Ouyang
088   et al. (2022) and leveraged across a myriad of tasks (Stiennon et al., 2020; Nakano et al., 2021;
089   Thoppilan et al., 2022), to first learn a reward function to model human preference before opti-
090   mizing the language model on it. Other recent directions include direct policy optimization (DPO)
091   (Rafailov et al., 2023) and reward-ranked tuning (Lu et al., 2022), which bypasses learning a reward
092   from human preference and instead directly optimizes the policy. In general, such works rely on
093   the reinforcement learning framework to optimize over a single, average human preference. In con-
094   trast, a recent line of works Jang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024a) explores individual or case-based
095   preferences fine-tuning; however, such methods rely on merging or fine-tuning model weights and
096
      is applicable only in white-box model settings whereas our approach treats these fine-tuned models
      as black-box models.
097

      process towards composite, often conflicting objectives (Hayes et al., 2022). Recent works ex-
100

101
      2022a;b), Relevance-Correctness-Completeness (Wu et al., 2023), and Expertise-Informativeness-
102   Style (Jang et al., 2023). Wu et al. (2023) propose a PPO-based MORL framework where multiple
103   objectives are combined in the reward model, thus achieving superior performance to traditional
104   RLHF models in long-form question answering tasks. Other works devise similar reward-merging
105   techniques for supervised fine-tuning (SFT)-DPO pipelines (Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a) or
106   train an additional encoder-decoder network to combine multiple outputs from individually-trained

166   3.1   MPD I NFERENCE
167
168   The MPD inference setting assumes that the user provides an instruction x such as “Give me a
169   5 day itinerary for Switzerland” as well as a preference ξ (e.g., “I want a helpful, concise, and
170   funny response!”) that consists of n individual preference dimensions {p1 , ..., pn }, where n = 3
171   in Figure 1: helpfulness, conciseness, and humour. We are also provided with n black-box LLMs
172
      {M1 , ..., Mn }, each specialized along the corresponding preference dimension pi only. For example,
      M3 is an LLM optimized to be humorous. We refer to these models as relevant expert models
173
      (REM). Given a partial response generated so far y<t (e.g. “Sure! Here’s a Swiss”), we want to
174
      construct a next token probability distribution in order to decode the subsequent token yt .
175
176   Since we aim to achieve effective multi-objective personalization, we introduce a trainable neural
177   network called the preference control model (PCM), parametrized by θ. PCM takes as input the
178
      instruction x, the partial response y<t , and the preference vector ξ, then outputs a weight vector with
      length n (one weight for each REM) whose entries are non-negative and sum to 1. Assuming we
179
      already have a well-trained PCM, the inference of MPD is very similar to the standard autoregressive
180
      decoding mechanism of LLMs. Specifically, we can construct the following next token probability
181   distribution as a weighted sum of all experts:
182                                                  n
                                                     X
183                            πθ (yt |x, y<t , ξ) =    αθ (x, y<t , ξ)i πMi (yt |x, y<t )                 (1)
184                                               i=1
185   where πMi (yt |x, y<t ) is the next token probability distribution from expert Mi and αθ (x, y<t , ξ) is
186   the output of the PCM. As each individual πMi () outputs a probability distribution and πθ is a convex
187   combination of them, it itself is also a well-defined distribution over all tokens in the vocabulary.
188   As each Mi is specialized for one specific preference dimension, the weights given by αθ (·) specify
189   how much importance should be given to each dimension pi at time step t. Individual experts Mi
190   are frozen and can be treated as black-box models since only their output probabilities are needed.
191   Various decoding methods such as greedy or temperature sampling can be used to decode the next
192   token yt from the distribution πθ . As the PCM only outputs a distribution over n dimensions instead
193
      of the vocabulary size, it can be a relatively small model that effectively orchestrates each of the
      large models Mi .

      3.2   MPD T RAINING
197   An overview of the MPD training procedure is shown in Figure 2. Our framework uses online RL
198   algorithms such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and REBEL (REgression to RElative REward Based
199   RL) (Gao et al., 2024) to train the PCM αθ . Similar to prior work (Jang et al., 2023), we assume
200   we have access to a black-box reward model for each individual dimension (e.g., a reward model
201   that can quantify the level of helpfulness). Alternatively, one can also train such reward models
202   from existing pairwise comparison data such as Cui et al. (2023). We define y ∼ πθ (·|x, ξ) as the
203   method for generating a response y (a sequence of tokens) following MPD inference procedure.
204   Furthermore, πθ (y|x, ξ) represents the probability ofQgenerating the response y. Note that since
205
      our models are autoregressive, we have πθ (y|x, ξ) = t πθ (yt |x, ξ, y<t ), i.e., the likelihood of the
      whole response is the product of the likelihood of each token. Below we give our formulation for
206
      the reward modeling for RL training.
207
208
      Reward modeling using Bradley-Terry. In the multi-objective personalization setting, instead of
209
      having a single reward model for the entire preference, we have access to black-box reward models
210   for individual dimensions (e.g., a reward model for conciseness and a different reward model for
211   humorousness). For our purpose, individual reward models can either be off-the-shelf classifiers,
212   APIs or trained from existing human-labeled data. Given an instruction x and ξ = {p1 , ..., pn },
213   for each response y MPD generates, we obtain a vector of reward values [rp1 (x, y), ..., rpn (x, y)]
214   from the corresponding reward models. Here rpi corresponds to the reward value of the preference
215   dimension pi . This corresponds to the helpfulness, conciseness, and humour rewards on the right-
      hand side of Figure 2.

275   4.1      DATASETS AND E VALUATION
276
277   We assess how effectively both baseline and MPD satisfy diverse preferences in open-ended gen-
278   eration tasks. Building on the work of Jang et al. (2023), we define 8 distinct preferences across 6
279   dimensions: elementary, knowledgeable, concise, informative, friendly, and unfriendly. These di-
280   mensions are grouped into 3 pairs, where each pair consists of opposing qualities (e.g., A and B). A
281   preference is created by selecting one dimension from each group. Detailed preference dimensions
282
      and corresponding instructions are provided in Table 1. For evaluation, we use the same subset of 50
      instances from the Koala dataset (Geng et al., 2023) as in Jang et al. (2023), as well as an additional
283
      50 instances from the Ultrafeedback dataset (Cui et al., 2023), yielding a total of 800 prompts for
284
      performance evaluation1 .
285

287   Table 1: Preference dimensions and preference instructions used in our experiments. Eight
288   preferences are formed by drawing one dimension from each of the three groups.
289    Preference Dimension                                         Preference Instruction
290      Elementary (1A)            Generate a response that can be easily understood by an elementary school student.
291     Knowledgeable (1B)          Generate a response that only a PhD Student in that specific field could understand.
           Concise (2A)                 Generate a response that is concise and to the point, without being verbose.
292      Informative (2B)        Generate a response that is very informative, without missing any background information.
293        Friendly (3A)            Generate a response that is friendly, witty, funny, and humorous, like a close friend.

296   We use LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023) framework that evaluates responses from all methods in
297   a pairwsie fashion. We use the same win rate calculation method as Jang et al. (2023). Specifically,
298   for a pair of responses yA , yB , we use GPT4 to simulate human judge and evaluate each dimension
299   separately. GPT4 can assign either WIN, TIE, or LOSE to the pairwise comparison, which translates
300   to a numerical score of 1, 0, and -1. To evaluate the overall performance for the pair of generations,
301   we sum the numerical scores across individual dimensions. yA is considered to be better / equally
302
      good / worse than yB if the overall score is greater / equal / less than 0. Finally, we calculate
      the overall win rate of two methods from all pairwise comparisons that do not lead to TIE. To
303
      further validate the response quality, we additionally conduct a human evaluation to ensure that our
304
      results are well aligned to the true human preferences. We follow the same protocol of computing
305   win-rates, and obtain simulated individual preference by instructing the evaluators to rate based on
306   the specified preference dimension. More details can be found in Appendix A.2. We use greedy
307   decoding to generate responses from all methods.

337
      Vanilla Prompting. To highlight the importance of personalization, we simply prompt the base
338
      model (the instruction fine-tuned Tulu-7B model) just with the instruction but without any preference

343

345   model by uniformly merging the parameters of the experts that belong to the preference. After

348   MPD (Uniform). To see if MPD can already achieve strong personalization without training a PCM

489   Inference efficiency. Since MPD merges different expert outputs at the token level throughout
490   generation, MPD’s compute cost is proportional to the number of preference dimensions specified
491   by the user. In contrast, parameter merging methods (Ramé et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023) includ-
492   ing Personalized Soup merges expert parameters before generation and seems to only require one
493   forward pass. This naturally raises a question: is MPD, or more broadly, output merging based
494   personalization approaches much less efficient compared to their parameter merging counterparts?
495   Surprisingly, output merging approaches can actually be faster due to better parallelism. Intuitively,
496   for two distinct preferences requested, parameter merging approach cannot batch them and has to
497
      process them separately since different merged model weights are needed for different preferences.
      As the number of preference dimension increases, the number of preferences increase exponentially,
498
      making parameter merging less scalable. Output merging, however, can batch on the individual pref-
499
      erence dimensions. That is, if two preferences share any preference dimensions, those preference
500   dimensions can be processed together. For example, the expert model will take both requests of
501   humorous preferences and do forward pass in a batched fashion. To empirically verify this, under
502   our experimental setting, we simulate a batch of 32 simultaneous requests with randomly selected
503   preferences and benchmark the average time taken between Personalized Soup and MPD. The av-
504   erage time needed per request for Personalized Soup and MPD are 13.25s and 10.48s respectively,
505   indicating output merging approaches are indeed faster. More details about this experiment and a
506   coarse theoretical analysis can be found in Appendix A.3.

508   4.6   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSION
509
510
      The training of preference control module in MPD requires enumerating on all preference combi-
      nations. We note that in order to achieve strong personalization performance, other related work
511
      (Ramé et al., 2023) also perform training on all preference combinations. However, because MPD
512
      only updates the preference control module and does not backpropagate through individual experts,
513   the training of MPD is more lightweight than other multi-objective training approaches.
514
515   Another limitation but also an exciting future research direction is how to handle the introduction of
516
      new preferences under multi-objective preference optimization. Existing work (Zhou et al., 2023;
      Wang et al., 2024b) do not focus on the introduction of new preferences. In this work, we make the
517
      assumptions that changes in preference within a population are relatively slow, and new dimensions
518
      of preference do not emerge frequently. For MPD, the preference control module will need to re-train
519   to output the weights for the new dimension, since it directly outputs the mixture coefficients over
520   a set of preferences. However, because the preference control module is small, it should generally
521   take less time to re-train.

      5     C ONCLUSION
525
      In this work, we explore the problem of LLM personalization, specifically under the scenario where
526
      we assume black-box expert models with only access to its output probability. Towards this task,
527   this work introduces Merged Preference Dimensions (MPD), a method that approaches this task
528   by merging outputs from relevant expert models via a learned composition. Our method leverages a
529   smaller, lightweight preference control model to achieve multi-objective personalization, benefitting
530   both deployment, privacy, and practicality. Empirically, MPD achieves a new state-of-the-art per-
531   formance result, without the need to access model weights of individual expert models. Future work
532   include exploring preference optimization for implied user preferences. More broadly, we suggest
533   future work to explore other domains with compositionality, beyond simple preference dimensions
534   and instruction following.
