009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Anomaly detection is crucial yet challenging in industrial production, especially
012            in multi-class scenarios. Existing high-performance unsupervised methods of-
013            ten suffer from low efficiency and high model complexity. While lightweight
014            discriminator-based detectors have been proposed, they are typically designed
015            for single-class detection and exhibit significant performance degradation when
016
               extended to multi-class tasks. To address these limitations, we propose a novel
               Contrastive Learning-based multi-class Anomaly Detection (CLAD) method. Our
017
               approach first encodes multi-class normal images to generate normal samples in the
018
               feature space, then synthesizes anomalous samples in this encoded space. We then
019            employ an adapter network to compress the samples and leverage contrastive learn-
020            ing to effectively cluster normal and anomalous samples across multiple classes.
021            Finally, a discriminator network is used for anomaly classification and score predic-
022            tion. By leveraging anomaly sample generation and a two-stage training process,
023            our framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on the MVTec and VisA
024            datasets under the discriminator-based paradigm. Our key contributions include a
025            novel framework for multi-class anomaly detection, efficient sample generation
026            techniques, and a comprehensive evaluation of model configurations.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Anomaly detection is a critical task in modern industrial production, serving as a key component
031   for ensuring product quality and safety. In practice, detecting anomalies in complex manufacturing
032   processes involves identifying rare, unseen, and often subtle deviations from the expected behavior.
033   Unsupervised anomaly detection methods have gained popularity due to their ability to learn from
034   unlabeled data, making them particularly suitable for real-world applications where obtaining an-
035   notated samples is costly and time-consuming. However, the majority of existing approaches are
036   predominantly designed for single-class anomaly detection, which significantly limits their practical
037   applicability in multi-class scenarios where the detection task involves distinguishing between a
038   diverse set of normal and anomalous conditions. Current unsupervised methods can be broadly catego-
039
      rized into two types: reconstruction-based and embedding-based approaches. Reconstruction-based
      methods, such as UniAD You et al. (2022) and DiAD He et al. (2024), rely on learning to reconstruct
040
      normal samples accurately, identifying anomalies based on reconstruction errors. While effective
041
      in single-class settings, these methods often struggle with multi-class detection due to their limited
042   generalization capabilities across different normal classes. On the other hand, embedding-based
043   methods like GLASS Chen et al. (2024) and SimpleNet Liu et al. (2023) aim to capture the feature
044   representations of normal samples, yet they are seldom explored in multi-class contexts and often fail
045   to distinguish between complex patterns of normal and anomalous samples.
046
      To bridge this gap, we introduce a novel framework called Contrastive Learning-based Anomaly
047
      Detection (CLAD), which leverages contrastive learning to enhance feature embedding and distin-
048   guish between normal and anomalous samples across multiple classes. Unlike traditional methods
049   that rely solely on the reconstruction or simplistic feature embedding strategies, CLAD employs
050   a two-stage training process. The first stage utilizes contrastive learning to learn discriminative
051   feature representations by contrasting normal samples against synthesized anomalies, specifically
052   tailored for each class. The second stage refines these learned representations through fine-tuning,
053   effectively adapting the model to multi-class anomaly detection tasks. Our approach addresses two
      critical challenges in multi-class anomaly detection. First, we tackle the issue of efficiently generating

092   Multi-class Anomaly Detection. Current research in multi-class anomaly detection (MUAD)
093
      focuses on utilizing diffusion models to generate reference images and applying Vision Transformer
      (ViT) models for improved performance in multi-class tasks. Specifically, AnoDDPM is one of the
094
      earliest models to apply diffusion models for anomaly detection in medical imaging, leveraging their
095
      superior image generation and reconstruction capabilities to effectively recover complex features
096   across multiple classes. This allows the model to handle different types of anomalies rather than
097   solely learning features for a single class. Additionally, DiffusionAD enhances anomaly detection
098   through an anomaly synthesis strategy, generating abnormal samples and combining denoising with
099   segmentation networks. However, these methods still face challenges when reconstructing large-scale
100   defects, particularly in handling complex backgrounds or extensive anomalies. DiAD improves this
101   by employing a semantic-level image generation strategy that preserves the semantic information of
102   images, enabling better reconstruction of complex and large anomaly regions, and thereby increasing
103   detection accuracy. This multi-class anomaly detection approach offers new insights into addressing
104
      the limitations of existing diffusion models in anomaly localization.
105   ViT processes the entire image using self-attention mechanisms, capturing long-range dependencies
106   between image patches. This ability allows it to better identify distinguishing features across multiple
107   categories. Traditional anomaly detection methods typically rely on pyramidal structures (multi-
      resolution) to extract multi-level features, while ViT, with its straightforward architecture, extracts


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      rich multi-scale features at each layer, making it well-suited for addressing complex anomalies in
109   multi-class scenarios.
110
111   For instance, methods like InTra and AnoVit have applied ViT in image reconstruction and anomaly
112
      detection, typically capturing image features through ViT encoders to detect anomalies. However,
      most of these methods utilize standard Transformer architectures and lack in-depth exploration of the
113
      specific advantages of Transformers. Recent studies indicate that using a pure ViT architecture for
114
      multi-class anomaly detection yields significant performance improvements. For example, DINO
115   pre-trained ViT features perform exceptionally well in multi-class anomaly detection tasks, effectively
116   capturing anomalies of various scales within images, thus reducing information leakage. Furthermore,
117   ViT’s hierarchical query decoder allows it to handle both global and local anomalies simultaneously,
118   enhancing robustness and precision in detecting complex anomalies.
119
      Despite their successes, these methods often suffer from complex model designs, resulting in time-
120
      consuming inefficiencies. The Runtang Model addresses this issue with a simple contrastive architec-
121   ture based on convolutional models.
122
123   Efficient Anomaly Detection Methods. EfficientAD exemplifies the focus on high efficiency rather
124
      than single-class detection. This method improves performance through various strategies while
      maintaining a manageable model complexity.
125
126   GLASS integrates Global Anomaly Synthesis (GAS) and Local Anomaly Synthesis (LAS) to syn-
127   thesize anomalies at both feature and image levels, thereby enhancing detection capabilities across
128   a wider range of anomaly types. GAS uses gradient ascent-guided Gaussian noise for subtle de-
129   fect detection, while LAS overlays distinct abnormal textures on normal images to manage more
130
      pronounced anomalies and increase synthesis diversity.
131   However, these methods involve complex model designs or optimization strategies. Recent works,
132   such as SimpleNet, present a new direction with a simpler discriminator-based architecture.
133
      SimpleNet combines unsupervised learning and synthetic anomaly generation, using normal samples
134   for training while generating diverse abnormal samples to enhance model performance. This model
135   excels in inference speed and detection accuracy, demonstrating strong adaptability for effective
136   surface defect detection in industrial applications.
137
      Contrastive Anomaly Detection Methods. ReConPatch employs a contrastive learning framework
138
      to extract patch features from pre-trained models, constructing distinguishable feature representations.
139
      By training a linear transformation instead of the entire network, ReConPatch effectively adjusts
140   feature representations, making them more targeted for anomaly detection tasks.

      MVTec-AD dataset. The MVTec-AD dataset Bergmann et al. (2019) is designed for unsupervised
379   anomaly detection in industrial scenarios. It contains 5,354 high-resolution images across 15 cate-
380   gories (5 textures, 10 objects) from various industrial domains. The data set is divided into a training
381   set of 3,629 anomaly-free images and a test set of 1,725 images with normal and abnormal samples.
382   In the test set, pixel-level annotations for anomalies are provided, allowing the evaluation of both
383   detection and localization tasks. This comprehensive dataset fills a critical gap in industrial anomaly
384   detection research, offering a standardized benchmark for algorithm development and assessment in
385   realistic production environments.
386   VisA dataset. The VisA (Visual Anomaly) dataset, introduced by Zou et al. (2022), is a compre-
387   hensive resource for visual anomaly detection research. It comprises 10,821 high-resolution images,
388   including 9,621 normal and 1,200 anomalous samples featuring 78 types of anomalies. The dataset
389   is structured into 12 subsets, each representing a distinct object. These objects are categorized into
390   complex structures, multiple instances, and single instances. This diverse composition allows for
391   a thorough evaluation of anomaly detection algorithms in various complexities and scenarios of
392   objects. VisA is valuable for developing and testing robust visual inspection methods in industrial
393
      and research applications.
394   Evaluation Metrics for AD. Similar to (Deng & Li, 2022; Zavrtanik et al., 2021; Bergmann et al.,
395   2020), we use threshold-independent measures, including mean Area under the Receiver Operating
396   Curve (mAU-ROC) to evaluate binary classification ability and mean Area Under the Per-Region-
397   Overlap Bergmann et al. (2020) (mAU-PRO) to weigh regions of different sizes equally. Note that
398   mAU-ROC is used in image-level (anomaly classification) and pixel-level (anomaly segmentation)
399
      evaluations. The maximum pixel-level value is regarded as the image-level anomaly score Deng &
      Li (2022); You et al. (2022). The models are evaluated ten times evenly for all methods, and the
400
      result corresponding to the maximum pixel-level mAU-ROC value is taken as the final result. We
401
      demonstrate and emphasize using all metrics for evaluation.
402
403   Comparision Methods. As MUAD is a relatively new task, we mainly evaluate the published
404   UniAD You et al. (2022) methods. We also compare with the latest augmentation-based DRAEM
405
      Zavrtanik et al. (2021), reconstruction-based RD Deng & Li (2022), and Embedding-based Sim-
      pleNet Liu et al. (2023). Since the above methods only report results under the SUAD setting, we
406
      retrain them to obtain MUAD results by official codes.
407
408   Training. In this study, we employ WideResNet50 as the backbone network for CLAD. The input
409   images are resized to 256x256, followed by center-cropping to 224x224, without applying any data
410   augmentation. We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate set to 0.0002. The training is
411
      divided into two stages: In the first stage, a batch size of 8000 patch features is used, and the model is
      trained for 200 epochs. The best-performing model from this stage is then fine-tuned in the second
412
      stage, with a batch size of 100,000 patch features for 2000 epochs. All images are trained together,

      5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
487
488
      In this work, we are the first to introduce a feature embedding-based discriminative approach into
489   multi-class anomaly detection. By leveraging dimensionality reduction and contrastive learning,
490   we propose the CLAD method, which achieves state-of-the-art performance using only an MLP.
491   Our results demonstrate the effectiveness of feature embedding-based approaches, offering a novel
492   perspective distinct from reconstruction-based multi-class anomaly detection methods. This provides
493   a new pathway for advancing the development of multi-class anomaly detection algorithms.
