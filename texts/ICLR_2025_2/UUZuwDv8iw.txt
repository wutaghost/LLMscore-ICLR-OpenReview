011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Sparsely activated Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) has shown promise in scaling up
014            the learning capacity of neural networks. However, vanilla SMoEs have issues
015            such as expert redundancy and heavy memory requirements, making them ineffi-
016            cient and non-scalable, especially for resource-constrained scenarios. Expert-level
017            sparsification of SMoEs involves pruning the least important experts to address
018            these limitations. In this work, we aim to address three questions: 1 What is the
019
               best recipe across multiple plausible recipes to identify the least knowledgeable
               subset of experts that can be dropped to achieve a desired sparsity level? 2 How
020
               should we perform expert dropping (one-shot or iterative), and what correction
021
               measures can we undertake to minimize its drastic impact on SMoE subnetwork
022            capabilities? 3 What capabilities of full-SMoEs are severely impacted by the
023            removal of the least dominant experts, and how can we recover them? Firstly,
024            we propose MoE Experts Compression Suite (MC-Suite), which is a collection
025            of some previously explored and multiple novel recipes to provide a comprehen-
026            sive benchmark for estimating expert importance from diverse perspectives, as
027            well as unveil numerous valuable insights for SMoE experts. Secondly, unlike
028            prior works with a one-shot expert pruning approach, we explore the benefits of
029            iterative pruning with the re-estimation of the MC-Suite criterion. Moreover, we
030
               introduce the benefits of task-agnostic fine-tuning as a correction mechanism dur-
               ing iterative expert dropping, which we term MoE Lottery Subnetworks. Lastly,
031
               we present an experimentally validated conjecture that, during expert dropping,
032
               SMoEs’ instruction-following capabilities are predominantly hurt, which can be
033            restored to a robust level subject to external augmentation of instruction-following
034            capabilities using k-shot examples and supervised fine-tuning.

      In parallel to well-studied techniques that address memory and compute bottlenecks using weight
073   sparsity (Jaiswal et al., 2023c; Lee et al., 2019; Frankle & Carbin, 2019; Yin et al., 2023b; Liu et al.,
074   2023a) and quantization (Liu et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023; Frantar et al.,
075   2022; Lin et al., 2023), SMoEs architecture design facilitates a unique opportunity for expert-level
076   sparsification that aims to compact the SMoE model by retaining fewer but more knowledgeable
077   experts. For instance, Figure 1 illustrates that the existence of some experts is critically important
078   (dominant) and dropping them could lead to an abrupt performance drop, while some experts are
079   notably redundant with negligible impact when removed. Recently, a few works1 have proposed
080   expert importance estimation techniques such as token reconstruction loss (Lu et al., 2024) and
081
      heavy-hitters counting (Muzio et al., 2024), illustrating the potential of expert dropping. However,
      a comprehensive benchmarking of possible task-agnostic recipes to select the best recipe is still
082
      missing. At this point, one key question arises: What is the best recipe to identify less knowledgeable
083
      experts that can be dropped without sacrificing the vital knowledge and capabilities of the SMoE?
084
085   In this work, we present MoE Experts Compression Suite (MC-Suite), a comprehensive collec-
086   tion of potential recipes for expert importance estimation2 which studies “clues” from four broad
087
      and diverse perspectives: a expert & router weight dynamics, b expert inference behavior dynam-
      ics, c intermediate activation properties, and d expert gradient properties. In addition to expert
088
      importance, MC-Suite unveils numerous valuable insights across experts: dominant experts tend
089
      to have lower stable-rank (i.e., pretraining knowledge is well compressed (Jaiswal et al., 2024))
090   which is favorable for additional compression using low-rank factorization; intermediate activation
091   and gradients corresponding to dominant experts tend to have higher entropy indicating better infor-
092   mation quantity and conducive finetuning abilities for downstream adaptation (Zhang et al., 2024;
093   Zhao et al., 2024); among many others as outlined in Section 3. It is important to note that dropping
094   experts involves deleting its entry in the router gating function, which leaves the MoE subnetwork
095   in a sub-optimal state (i.e., increased skewness in load distribution across retained experts, abrupt
096   drop in performance with high dropping ratio). Most existing prior works (Lu et al., 2024; He et al.,
097   2024; Muzio et al., 2024) adopt one-shot criterion estimation for expert removal that alleviates the
098
      impact incurred due to sparsification in the form of load imbalance and abrupt performance drop.
099   In this work, we systematically illustrate that extending one-shot pruning to iterative pruning with
100   re-estimation of importance criterion in k-rounds3 , leads to identifying a better subset of experts
101   for dropping. Moreover, motivated by lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2018), we pro-

      To unveil the true merits of expert-level sparsification, in this work we ask an interesting question:
114
      Given the existence of redundancy across experts, during expert-level sparsification, what capabil-
115   ities of full-MoE are severely impacted? We hypothesize that during expert-level sparsification of
116   well-trained MoEs, instruction following capabilities are notably hurt while the derived MoE sub-
117   network still retains the pretraining knowledge and reasoning abilities to a great extent. Our work
118   design controlled experiments from zero-shot setting to k-shot setting and supervised finetuning
119   (SFT) using instruction-tuning dataset, to augment instruction following capabilities into derived
120   MoE subnetwork. Our experimental results indicate that external instruction-following support can
121   impressively minimize the performance drop due to expert-level sparsification on complex reasoning
122   downstream tasks. Our key contributions can be briefly summarized as:
123          • We present MoE Experts Compression Suite (MC-Suite), to re-look the expert importance
124            estimation and facilitate a comprehensive benchmark from a multi-dimensional perspec-
125            tive. Our extensive experiments show that activation & gradient-guided importance esti-
126            mation criterions that take into account both input tokens and weight parameters, identifies
127            a superior subset of least dominant experts which can be dropped with minimal impact.
128          • We explore the potential of iterative estimate-prune-finetune procedure in context of
129            expert-level sparsification. Our experiments illustrate that a fairly limited amount of task-
130            agnostic finetuning facilitate not only improved performance of resultant subnetwork but
131            overcome the skewness in load distribution incurred due expert dropping.
132          • Our extensive experiments across multiple downstream dataset (e.g., MMLU, ARC-c,
133            ARC-e, HellaSwag, and WinoGrande) surprisingly found that MoE subnetworks, even at
134            non-trivial sparsity ratios (e.g., ≥50% with ≥1.27× speedup and ≤0.55× memory usage)
135            can achieve robust performance subjected to external augmentation of instruction follow-
136            ing capabilities using k-shot examples or supervised finetuning.

      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture has been recently gaining enormous attention for the scaling
142
      up of LLMs while maintaining roughly constant FLOPs. By incorporating multiple expert networks
143   and employing a sparse gating mechanism, MoE achieves efficient computation, enabling the de-
144   velopment of larger models within the constraints of limited computational resources (Fedus et al.,
145   2022; Jiang et al., 2024). Despite its advantages, MoE suffers from extensive memory costs, which
146   hinder its practical deployment and widespread adoption. For example, the Mixtral-8×7B MoE
147   model takes around 180GB memory while only 28GB parameters are activated for each input to-
148   ken5 . In parallel to conventional model compression techniques like weight sparsity, quantization,
149   and distillation; the architecture design of MoEs facilitates a unique opportunity for expert-level
150   sparsification which involves identifying and removing the least important experts or connections.
151   Figure 1 presents the wikitext perplexity of Mixtral-8×7B by dropping a single expert e from layer
152   l. It can be clearly noted that some experts tend to have an abrupt impact on the performance of the
153   pre-trained checkpoint compared to others6 . Therefore, it is critically important to carefully identify
154   the subset of least important experts, which are pruned to match the desired sparsity level with
155   minimal impact on performance. In this section, we present MoE Experts Compression Suite (MC-
156   Suite), a first comprehensive benchmark to investigate expert importance using a wide spectrum of
157
      novel and previously explored (e.g., expert usage frequency) criterions broadly categorized in four

314   Expert-level sparsification of SMoEs involves identifying r experts with the least importance using
315   criterions outlined in Section 2 and discarding them to reduce exorbitant memory requirements
316   of loading n experts. Dropping experts require explicit handling of the routing gate function by
317   removing the entry corresponding to dropped experts. In our work, we found that gating function
318
      is highly sensitive to any modification and an ad-hoc deletion of r entries from the router matrix

320
      heavier load on few among remaining n − r experts. Prior works have limited exploration of one-

321

484   reasoning abilities or instruction-following abilities? We conjecture that when we drop the least

      In this paper, we provide a detailed investigation of multiple expert importance estimation tech-
533
      niques (MC-Suite) to identify the best recipe for selecting the least knowledgeable experts that can
534   be dropped without sacrificing the vital knowledge and capabilities of the SMoE. We propose to
535   adopt a iterative pruning strategy with task-agnostic finetuning as a correction measure to minimize
536   the drastic impact on SMoE capabilities. We present and experimentally validate an interesting con-
537   jecture that during expert dropping, SMoE instruction following capabilities are predominantly hurt,
538   and SMoE performance can be notably recovered with a few-shot demonstration or supervised fine-
539   tuning. In our future work, we plan to investigate and disentangle the instruction-following abilities
      and pretraining knowledge across the parameters of SMoE experts.

812   A.1    R ELATED W ORK
813
814   SMoE and Its Superiority. It is widely acknowledged that scaling model size benefits per-
815   formance by enhancing learning capacity and generalization ability (Brown et al., 2020; Ka-
816   plan et al., 2020). To achieve more efficient model scaling, Sparsely activated Mixture-of-
817   Experts (SMoE) (Shazeer et al., 2017b; Zoph et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022) has emerged as a
818   widely adopted approach, enabling the training of larger models with negligible additional com-
819
      putational overhead (Jiang et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024). Given the
      predominance of Transformer architectures in NLP, numerous research efforts have focused on in-
820
      corporating MoE layers within the feed-forward neural networks of these models. In pursuit of
821
      enhanced SMoE models, various iterations of the standard MoE architecture have been proposed.
822   For example, DeepSeek-MoE (Dai et al., 2024; DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024) utilizes a large number
823   of finely segmented experts, designating a subset as shared experts to capture common knowledge.
824   More recently, Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024) has demonstrated that SMoE can achieve performance
825   comparable to full-parameter LLMs while utilizing significantly fewer active parameters.
826
827   Compression for LLMs and SMoEs. LLMs have demonstrated remarkable success. However,
828   their substantial memory and computational requirements pose deployment challenges. Numerous
829   model compression techniques have been proposed to address this issue. Algorithmically, these
830   methods can be classified into three main categories: 1 Quantization, which converts float32
831
      weights or activations to lower-bit representations(Lin et al., 2023; Frantar et al., 2022; Jaiswal et al.,
      2022; Xiao et al., 2024); 2 Pruning, which eliminates less critical components, such as weights,
832
      neurons, or layers (LeCun et al., 1989; Han et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2023); 3 Knowledge distillation,
833
      which transfers knowledge from a larger model to a smaller one (Gou et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024b;
834   Rajbhandari et al., 2022). In this study, we concentrate on model pruning for compression, which
835   is generally divided into structured and unstructured approaches. Structured pruning methods (Liu
836   et al., 2017; Molchanov et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023) eliminate entire struc-
837   tured components of a network, facilitating straightforward GPU acceleration. Existing techniques
838   primarily rely on weight or activation statistics of neurons (Dubey et al., 2018; Molchanov et al.,
839   2017). Unstructured methods (Han et al., 2015; Paul et al., 2022; Hoang et al., 2023) operate at
840   the individual weight level, preserving performance at higher sparsity levels but typically requiring
841   additional effort to enable GPU speedups (Mishra et al., 2021).
842   SMoE architectures enable the scaling of LLMs but necessitate substantial memory to host experts
843   while exhibiting expert redundancy. To address these challenges, numerous studies have also fo-
844   cused on developing SMoE-model-specific compression techniques. Initial approaches (Chen et al.,
845   2022; Kim et al., 2021; Koishekenov et al., 2023; Sarkar et al., 2024) propose expert pruning based
846   on utilization metrics; however, these methods often resulted in diminished performance. Subse-
847   quent research (Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Fedus et al., 2022; Artetxe et al., 2022) explores the cre-
848
      ation of smaller models, either dense or SMoE-based, with reduced layer counts through knowledge
      distillation (KD). While effective, this approach demands significant computational resources and
849
      fails to address the inherent redundancy among experts. More recently, MC-SMoE (Li et al., 2024b)
850
      dynamically merges experts during inference time, though it is limited to specific tasks. Besides
851   pruning-based methods, there are also a few works that specifically study quantization in SMoE
852   models Li et al. (2024a).
853

      finetuning will be sufficient to achieve a reasonable performance gain facilitated by it?
