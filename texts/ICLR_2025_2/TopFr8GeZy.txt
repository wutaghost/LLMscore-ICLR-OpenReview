010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            In imitation learning, when the learning agent is at a state that is outside the
013            demonstration of the expert, it could be difficult for her to choose an action. To
014
               overcome this challenge, inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) learns a parame-
               terized reward function based on which we can generalize the expert’s behavior to
015
               those states that are unseen in the demonstration. However, on the one hand, there
016
               could be multiple reward functions that can explain the expert’s behavior, lead-
017            ing to reward ambiguity in IRL. On the other hand, though we often consider the
018            transition kernel of the expert to be known to the agent, sometimes the transition
019            kernel of the agent is different from the expert’s and is unknown, leading to tran-
020            sition kernel ambiguity in IRL. Drawing on the notion of soft-robust optimization,
021            we build a target-oriented soft-robust IRL (SRIRL) model where the performance
022            of the output policy strikes a flexible balance between risk aversion and expected
023            return maximization towards reward uncertainty in IRL. Moreover, by employing
024            the robust satisficing framework, our SRIRL is also robust to transition kernel am-
025
               biguity in IRL. In our target-oriented SRIRL, we keep a target for the performance
               of the output policy that balances expected return and risk, and we minimize the
026
               constraint violation incurred by the difference between the ambiguous transition
027
               kernel and the empirical one. We derive tractable reformulation for SRIRL, and
028            we design tailored first-order methods for SRIRL. Numerical results showcase the
029            soft robustness towards reward uncertainty and the robustness against transition
030            kernel ambiguity of SRIRL, as well as the stronger scalability of our first-order
031            methods compared to a state-of-the-art commercial solver.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In imitation learning (IL) (Hussein et al., 2017; Osa et al., 2018), we train an agent to imitate the
037   behavior of an expert based on her demonstration, via either directly mimicking the behavior of the
038   expert as in behavior cloning (Pomerleau, 1991) or inferring the reward function of the expert as in
039
      inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016; Kalman, 1964; Ng et al., 2000).
      For behavior cloning, it could be challenging to imitate the behavior of the expert in situations
040
      that are not considered in the expert demonstration. IRL addresses this challenge by learning the
041
      (parameterized) reward function from the expert demonstration that does not necessarily consider all
042   the situations/states in the environment (Golmisheh & Shamaghdari, 2024; Zeng et al., 2024). After
043   learning the parameters of the reward function, one can generalize the behavior of the expert to the
044   unobserved states (by learning a policy that is optimal under the learned reward function). Other
045   than its application in IL, IRL is also an important approach where estimating the rewards of the
046   expert is our main interest, for example, in reward discovery for animal behavior study (Hirakawa
047   et al., 2018; Pinsler et al., 2018; Yamaguchi et al., 2018).
048   IRL comes with inevitable uncertainty in the rewards, and we observe that the risk attitude towards
049   reward uncertainty should vary and depend largely on application. For example, in autonomous
050   driving (Alozi & Hussein, 2024; Huang et al., 2023; You et al., 2019), when an autonomous vehicle
051   meets an object that has never been detected before, it should take a highly risk-averse attitude and
052   thus should keep away from this object. On the contrary, for a robot vacuum cleaner, the risk of
053   handling an unseen object would be much smaller than that in autonomous driving, and always
      staying away from unseen objects ensues low efficiency. Motivated by this observation, in this


                                                       1

054
      paper, we adopt the notion of soft robustness (Ben-Tal et al., 2010; Lobo et al., 2020) under which
055   the decision maker optimizes a convex combination of the mean and percentile performances. Soft-
056   robust IRL has been popular in recent years (Brown et al., 2020b; Javed et al., 2021; Javed, 2022),
057   via which the decision maker strikes a flexible balance between the maximization of expected return
058   and minimization of risk towards reward uncertainty in IRL. By setting a large (respectively, small)
059   weight parameter for the expected return in the objective function of our model, the output policy
060   (exploited by the agent) tends to be less (respectively, more) risk-averse.
061   In addition to the flexibility in taking a different risk attitude towards reward uncertainty, our model
062   is also robust to potential transition kernel ambiguity in IRL. Traditional IRL approaches often as-
063   sume that the learning agent and the expert share the same transition kernel (Levine et al., 2011;
064   Lindner et al., 2022; Ramachandran & Amir, 2007; Ratliff et al., 2006; Ziebart et al., 2008). Un-
065   fortunately, we observe that this assumption may fail to hold in practice. For example, in robot
066   learning, the real dynamics model of the robot may differ from the empirical one, leading to pos-
067   sible deterioration or even failure of the trained policies (Brunke et al., 2022). To hedge against
068   transition kernel ambiguity (of the learning agent), we apply the robust satisficing approach (Long
069
      et al., 2023) in building our model—target-oriented soft-robust IRL (SRIRL), where we prescribe
      in the constraint that the performance of the output policy should reach a user-specified target. We
070
      minimize the violation of the Bellman flow constraints (incurred by the deviation of the ambiguous
071
      transition kernel from the empirical one) in our SRIRL, where a smaller target comes with a smaller
072   violation, thus corresponding to stronger robustness against transition kernel ambiguity. Note that
073   the performance of the output policy of SRIRL here is taken as a weighted sum of the expected
074   return and some risk measure of return, reflecting its soft robustness towards reward uncertainty.
075
      We summarize our contributions as follows. (i) We propose the target-oriented soft-robust inverse
076
      reinforcement learning framework by which we not only achieve a balance between risk aversion
077
      and expected return maximization in the face of reward uncertainty but also perform robustly against
078   transition kernel ambiguity in IRL. (ii) We provide a tractable reformulation of SRIRL (as a conic
079   program) for computing its output policy. (iii) We propose tailored first-order methods for solving
080   SRIRL that is more scalable than the Gurobi solver (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2022), and thus
081   could be preferable in large-scale problems. (iv) In experiments, we compare our SRIRL with other
082   benchmarks in a simulation study and a quadruped robot navigation application. Results showcase
083   that our SRIRL achieves not only soft robustness towards reward uncertainty but also robust perfor-
084   mance against transition kernel ambiguity.
085   We organize the remainder of this paper as follows. We provide necessary preliminaries in Section 2.
086   In Section 3, we study SRIRL and provide its tractable reformulation. Tailored first-order methods
087   for SRIRL are introduced in Section 4. Numerical experiments are conducted in Section 5. A
088   conclusion is drawn in Section 6. Due to page limit, we conduct a survey on related work on
089   soft-robust Markov decision processes (MDPs), robust satisficing, dual formulation of MDPs, and
090   imitation learning in Appendix A.

102   2.1   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESSES
103
104   We model our environment by a Markov decision process (Puterman, 2014) that is represented by
105   the tuple < S, A, r, p, γ, d >. The state and action spaces are S = [S] and A = [A], respectively.
106   The rewards are condensed to the vector r ∈ RS·A , in which the agent receives a reward rs,a if
107   she takes action a ∈ A at state s ∈ S. The transition kernel p ∈ (∆S )S·A records the transition
      probabilities of the agent, where ps,a,s′ is the probability of the agent being transitioned to the next

109   to denote the discount factor and d ∈ RS++ to denote the distribution of the initial state.

      2.2   L INEAR R EWARD F UNCTIONS
112
113   Reward functions can often be expressed as a linear combination of (known) feature vectors (Abbeel
114   & Ng, 2004; Sadigh et al., 2017; Ziebart et al., 2008) or deep neural networks (Fu et al., 2017; Ho
115   & Ermon, 2016). We follow the former and assume that the rewards r ∈ RS·A are expressable as
116   r = F w, where F ∈ RS·A×K is a feature matrix storing the K feature vectors as its columns, and
117   w ∈ RK are the (reward) weights. Therefore, the reward uncertainty can be equivalently treated as
118   uncertainty in weights. The distribution of the reward uncertainty can be a prior distribution learned
119   via previous tasks (Xu et al., 2019) or a posterior one learned given expert demonstration (Brown
120
      et al., 2020a; Ramachandran & Amir, 2007; Sadigh et al., 2017) or human-specified proxy reward
      (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017; Ratner et al., 2018). Note that this distribution is often not directly
121
      accessible and only approximated via samples drawn by approaches such as Markov chain Monte
122
      Carlo sampling (Brown et al., 2020a; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017; Ramachandran & Amir, 2007).

130   distribution (Pflug, 2000; Rockafellar et al., 2000). Value-at-risk with a risk threshold ε, as implied
131   by its definition, coincides with the lower ε-percentile of the random input, and the conditional
132   value-at-risk is essentially the conditional expectation of the random input given that the random
133   input is no larger than its VaR (with a same risk threshold ε). Although being a popular choice

137
      account for the severity of losses beyond the VaR threshold. Such an ignorance of the potential
      impact could be problematic for those applications where rare but catastrophic events are possible
138
      to happen. In contrast to VaR, maximizing CVaR results in a convex optimization problem. CVaR
139
      takes the magnitude of losses below the VaR threshold into consideration, and it is a lower bound
140   of VaR. As will be introduced soon, we can obtain an equivalent reformulation of our SRIRL that is
141   equipped with CVaR as a convex optimization problem.

454   5.2                A PPLICATION : Q UADRUPED ROBOT NAVIGATION
455
      In robotics, a fundamental task is navigation, which involves controlling a robot to reach a target
456
      position. For quadruped robots, the dynamics model is overly complex, and so navigation is often
457
      handled using a two-layered framework: the upper layer is a decision-making module to compute
458   the command speed, and the lower layer is a locomotion control module used to execute the speed
459   commands from the upper layer and control the robot’s motors. However, since it is difficult for the
460   upper-level decisions to fully account for the complex dynamics of lower-level motion control, the
461   navigation performance is often suboptimal. This section aims to apply BROIL and SRIRL to train
462   the upper-level policy and then compare their policies on the robot. The environment is a 2-D plane,
463   the state is the location of the robot (with the ranges for the x and y coordinates both being [0, 2.5]),
464   and the action is the desired velocity. The goal of the robot is to reach the center of the state space
465   (i.e., location (1.25, 1.25)). For the lower-level motion policy, we used a neural network controller.
466
      This controller can output motor control commands based on input speed to track the robot’s desired
      speed. Ideally, the tracking error of this neural network controller should be minimal. However, due
467
      to the facts that (i) the dynamics of a quadruped robot could be far more complex than 2D point-
468
      mass kinematics, and that (ii) the neural network motion controller may fail to perfectly achieve the
469   desired speed, the realistic transition kernel of the robot may deviate from the empirical one. This
470   motivates us to apply SRIRL in pursuit of robustness against such transition kernel ambiguity. See
471   Appendix G for detailed settings of the environment.
472
      We train BROIL and SRIRL and deploy their output policies on a Unitree A1 Robot in the PyBullet
473
      simulation environment. Results show that SRIRL can deliver performance that is more robust than
474
      that of BROIL: as shown in Figure 3, the robot under BROIL falls during its navigation, while SRIRL
475   successfully navigates to the target point. The difference in navigation performance between SRIRL
476   and BROIL is due to the mechanical structure of the A1 robot, which could result in a significant
477   deviation (of the true transition kernel) from the ideal 2D point mass motion model. In particular,
478   the optimal policy of BROIL is even unable to keep the robot balanced. In contrast, our SRIRL
479   approach takes into account the transition kernel ambiguity, resulting in a more robust strategy.

      6                C ONCLUSION
531
      We propose target-oriented SRIRL whose output policy is soft-robust towards reward uncertainty
532
      and robust against transition kernel ambiguity in IRL. In SRIRL, the performance of the output pol-
533
      icy is taken as a weighted sum of the average and CVaR of return, and this soft-robust performance
534   is constrained to reach a user-specified target. This constraint is strictly imposed under the empirical
535   transition kernel, and softly imposed under all other possible ones. The violation of the Bellman
536   flow constraints is minimized in SRIRL, and a smaller target ensues stronger robustness of SRIRL.
537   We reformulate SRIRL as a min-max problem where we design scalable tailored first-order methods
538   for an efficient solution. Experiments demonstrate the soft robustness and robustness of SRIRL, as
539   well as the strong scalability of our tailored algorithms. A promising avenue for future work would
      be extending SRIRL to the setting of continuous state and action spaces.

      A    R ELATED W ORK

      Soft-robust MDPs are a popular line of research motivated by the notion of soft robustness that was
      first introduced by Ben-Tal et al. (2010). There are two different ways to model soft robustness in
773
      MDPs: (i) optimizing a weighted average of expected return and percentile performance (Brown
774
      et al., 2020b; Javed et al., 2021; Javed, 2022; Lobo et al., 2020). (ii) Optimizing the expected
775   return with the constraint that the percentile performance is larger than some user-specified lower
776   bound (Chow & Ghavamzadeh, 2014; Prashanth, 2014). Our SRIRL follows the first way to achieve
777   soft robustness but is fundamentally different from existing works. First, those models that are
778   soft-robust towards reward uncertainty (Brown et al., 2020b; Javed et al., 2021; Javed, 2022) are not
779   robust against transition kernel ambiguity, while our SRIRL also performs robustly against transition
780   kernel ambiguity. Second, compared to Lobo et al. (2020) where the soft-robustness is towards
781   transition kernel uncertainty and where obtaining the exact solution is NP-hard, our SRIRL is soft-
782   robust towards reward uncertainty and allows an exact solution via solving a conic program, and is
783
      also robust against transition kernel ambiguity via the robust satisficing framework.
784   There has been a surge in the study of the robust satisficing (RS) framework in recent years (Liu
785   et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023; Ramachandra et al., 2021; Sim et al., 2022). In comparison to the
786   robust optimization (Ben-Tal et al., 2009; Ben-Tal & Nemirovski, 2002; Bertsimas et al., 2011;
787   Gorissen et al., 2015; Bertsimas et al., 2018) framework where the decision maker optimizes the
788   worst-case costs by considering the worst-case realization of the uncertainty from a user-specified
789
      ambiguity set, robust satisficing is free from the specification of such a set and minimizes the con-
      straint violation (incurred by the deviation of the uncertainty from its estimated value) directly. The
790
      robustness of the RS model is adjusted by varing the target of the costs (serving as a targeted upper
791
      bound for the costs), where a higher target comes with stronger robustness. The successful applica-
792   tions of the robust satisficing framework includes those in contextual Bayesian optimization (Saday
793   et al., 2024), energy system (Keyvandarian & Saif, 2023), hub location problem (Hu et al., 2024),
794   and resource pooling (Cui et al., 2023).
795
      To solve nominal MDPs (Puterman, 2014), one approach is to express them as a linear program in
796
      primal and dual forms. Our SRIRL and BROIL (Brown et al., 2020b) are both based on the dual
797
      formulation. In recent years, there has been a notable increase in the development of new reinforce-
798   ment learning models based on the dual formulation, driven by its interpretability. For example,
799   in the face of reward uncertainty, Delage & Mannor (2010) optimizes the value-at-risk (VaR) of
800   expected return, while Brown et al. (2020b) maximizes a weighted average of the conditional value-
801   at-risk (CVaR) and expected return. Confronted with transition kernel ambiguity, Lobo et al. (2020)
802   also optimizes a weighted average of CVaR and expected return, Ruan et al. (2023) propose a robust
803   satisficing MDPs that hedge against the transition kernel ambiguity by applying the robust satisfic-
804   ing framework to the dual formulation. Our SRIRL is different from these existing models in that
805   it not only achieves a flexible balance between risk aversion and expected return maximization but
806
      also hedges against transition kernel ambiguity, both of which are important features in IRL.
807   Behavior cloning directly imitates the policy of the expert, ensuing a quadratic regret (Ross & Bag-
808   nell, 2010). DAgger has a sublinear regret that is achieved by making frequent queries to the expert
809   for her action taken at different states. Its safe variants Hoque et al. (2021); Oh & Matsubara (2024);
      Zhang & Cho (2016) also rely on unlimited access to extra expert demonstration. Unlike these

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      approaches, inverse reinforcement learning learns the expert’s behavior indirectly by inferring the
811   reward function employed by the expert (Adams et al., 2022; Arora & Doshi, 2021; Metelli et al.,
812   2021; 2023). However, many inverse reinforcement learning methods only infer a single estimate
813   of the demonstrator’s reward function (Brown et al., 2019; 2020c; Fu et al., 2017; Ziebart et al.,
814   2008). To hedge against reward uncertainty, a number of studies on safe imitation learning using
815   IRL consider an adversarial reward function (Chang et al., 2021; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017; Ho &
816   Ermon, 2016; Huang et al., 2018; Regan & Boutilier, 2012; Syed et al., 2008), which could output
817   overly conservative policies in practice (Brown et al., 2020b). We focus on hedging against epis-
818   temic risk coming from reward uncertainty in IRL, yet there is another line of research focusing
819
      on the aleatoric risk stemming from transition probabilities (Lacotte et al., 2019; Majumdar et al.,
      2017; Santara et al., 2018). Though we consider the case with demonstration provided by a Boltz-
820
      mann rational expert, some previous works consider cases where the expert(s) provide suboptimal
821
      demonstration in IRL (Brown et al., 2019; Choi et al., 2019; Poiani et al., 2024; Shiarlis et al., 2016;
822   Zheng et al., 2014).

2248   Ideally, the tracking error of the neural network controller should be minimal, so the robot’s motion
2249   dynamics in a 2D plane can be modeled as:
2250
                                                                   xt+1 = xt + vxt ∆t,      yt+1 = yt + vyt ∆t.                                                               (46)
2251
2252
2253   where xt and yt represent the robot’s x and y coordinates, and vxt and vyt represent the robot’s
2254   velocity. To model this problem as an MDP, we treat xt and yt as states, and vxt and vyt as actions,
2255
       while equation (46) serves as the (deterministic) transition kernel. The initial position of the robot
       is uniformly distributed, and the navigation target is set as the center of the state space.
2256
2257   It is worth noting that Equation (46) is not entirely realistic for an actual quadruped robot due to:
2258   (i) the dynamics of a quadruped robot being far more complex than 2D point-mass kinematics,
2259   and (ii) the neural network motion controller not being able to perfectly achieve the desired speed.
2260   Therefore, although (46) is an efficient description of the robot’s motion transition kernel, it is not
2261
       accurate. Similar to the lava corridor experiment, we pollute Equation (46) as follows:
2262
                                                         xt+1 = xt + (vxt + wx )∆t,         yt+1 = yt + (vyt + wy )∆t.                                                        (47)
2263
2264   where wx and wy are parameters used to pollute the original deterministic transition kernel, and
2265   they are used to compute the ambiguity set P in SRIRL (2). We remark that, the polluted motion
2266   dynamics (47) is only for the purpose of the construction of the support set P in the SRIRL. Neither
2267   the motion dynamics as described in (46) nor the one in (47) are an accurate description of the
       realistic dynamics of a quadruped robot.
