011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have spurred numerous
014            attempts to apply these technologies to embodied tasks, particularly focusing on
015            high-level task planning and task decomposition. To further explore this area,
016            we introduce a new embodied task planning benchmark, ET-Plan-Bench 1 , which
017
               specifically targets embodied task planning using LLMs. It features a controllable
               and diverse set of embodied tasks varying in different levels of difficulties and
018
               complexities, and is designed to evaluate two critical dimensions of LLMs’ appli-
019
               cation in embodied task understanding: spatial (relation constraint, occlusion for
020            target objects) and temporal & causal understanding of the sequence of actions in
021            the environment. By using multi-source simulators as the backend simulator, it
022            can provide immediate environment feedback to LLMs, which enables LLMs to
023            interact dynamically with the environment and re-plan as necessary. We evaluated
024            the state-of-the-art open source and closed source foundation models, including
025            GPT-4, LLAMA and Mistral on our proposed benchmark. While they perform
026            adequately well on simple navigation tasks, their performance can significantly
027            deteriorate when faced with tasks that require a deeper understanding of spatial,
028
               temporal, and causal relationships. Thus, our benchmark distinguishes itself as a
               large-scale, quantifiable, highly automated, and fine-grained diagnostic framework
029
               that presents a significant challenge to the latest foundation models. We hope it
030
               can spark and drive further research in embodied task planning using foundation
031            models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Embodied planning refers to an agent’s ability to formulate plans and execute tasks within a phys-
037   ical environment. Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) have
038   demonstrated significant advancements in vision understanding, natural language comprehension, and
039   generation. Although LLMs and VLMs are not inherently designed for embodied planning, there is
040   potential for these models to contribute to this field. LLMs and VLMs possess an extensive repository
041   of knowledge derived from their training data, which enables them to comprehend and generate
042   contextually relevant advice and strategies. Additionally, they have the capability to translate complex
043   tasks into step-by-step instructions through interactions with humans. Furthermore, they can refine
044   plans based on feedback from the environment or human interventions. In the context of embodied
045
      planning, LLMs focus on the decomposition of complex tasks. The LLM acts both as a translator and
      a mediator. Each sub-task, such as navigating to a specific location or grabbing a particular object,
046
      can be executed using advanced Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods. However, currently, LLMs
047
      and VLMs face some challenges in understanding the physical world (Jia et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
048   2024; Jain et al., 2023), including spatial understanding. Effective spatiotemporal reasoning often
049   necessitates the integration of knowledge from multiple domains, such as physics and human behavior.
050   These domains may not be adequately represented in the training data of existing foundational models.
051   To further explore this area, we introduce a new embodied task planning benchmark, ET-Plan-Bench,

055   evaluate tasks with spatial and temporal understanding of the environment.
056
057   For spatial understanding, our benchmark aims to assess the LLMs’ ability to complete tasks with
058
      relational, size, and occlusion constraints between objects, which are pivotal for effective interaction
      within a given space. In our benchmark, the spatial aspect is currently not associated with the
059
      understanding of left and right directions. In terms of temporal understanding, we focus on evaluating
060
      LLMs’ ability to understand the causality and sequence between preceding and subsequent actions
061   during the execution of embodied tasks. This aspect tests how well the models can plan and execute
062   actions that depend on each other in a meaningful sequence.
063
064
      Compared to existing benchmarks in the field of embodied AI, our approach offers unique features
      that allows a thorough evaluation on existing foundation models for their embodied task planning
065
      ability. Distinguishing itself from the standard benchmarks that often rely on manual curation for
066
      embodied tasks (Puig et al., 2018; 2020; Shridhar et al., 2020a; Li et al., 2023; Wan et al., 2022), our
067   framework employs LLMs to autonomously generate a wide array of embodied tasks with different
068   spatial and temporal constraints in a systematic way, incorporating tasks with variety of objects,
069   scene and room layouts in different level of difficulties. Another important category of benchmarks
070   is embodied question answering (EQA), which is designed to evaluate the capability of LLMs to
071   answer questions based on first-person or third-person videos instead of task decomposition and
072   long-horizon planning (Jia et al., 2022; Majumdar et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). However, most of
073   these EQA datasets depend on videos and lack interaction with the physical or vitual environment,
074   while our benchmark features end-to-end (long-horizon) task planning through interaction with a
075
      dynamic environment.
076   This comprehensive embodied benchmark aims to identify both the capabilities and limitations of
077   current foundation models in navigating and interacting within complex, dynamic environments. Our
078   findings are intended to provide deeper insights into the practical applications of these models in
079   real-world settings. The main contributions of our work state as follows:
080   Embodied task generation with various difficulties We have developed an embodied task generation
081   pipeline which can produce different levels of difficulties and complexities through introducing spatial
082   and temporal constraints. It supports automatic task generation and task success criteria generation,
083   allowing automatic task planning evaluation in an end-to-end manner.
084
      Benchmark: Using the task generation pipeline, we introduce a benchmark focusing on various
085   aspects of spatial (relation constrain, object occlusion and global layout map) and temporal under-
086   standing (actions dependency and optimal moving path for robot). It provides a detailed and thorough
087   diagnostic assessment of existing foundation models.
088
      LLM agent baseline comparison: We provide a comprehensive set of our proposed LLM agent
089
      baseline results, featuring both recently released LLMs such as GPT-4 and open-source foundation
090
      model like LLAMA (Touvron et al., 2023) and Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024). Particularly, we
091   demonstrates that a supervised fine-tuned (SFT) small-size LLAMA-7B model using our benchmark
092   EQA data can match the state-of-the-art closed-source model GPT-4, highlighting the effectiveness
093   of our benchmark in enhancing understanding of embodied environments.

209   3.1   B ENCHMARK TASK DEFINITION
210
211   In our benchmark, we mainly provide three task categories: navigation tasks with or without spatial
212   constraints, navigation and manipulation tasks with or without spatial constraints, and navigation
213   and manipulation tasks with temporal constraints. For each task category, we divide it into several
214   sub-task categories based on different aspects that require spatial and temporal understanding for the
215   embodied agents. We introduce higher levels of task difficulty by combining the basic tasks. We
      further discuss the 5 key aspects that will impact the task difficulties in Appendix A.5.

216
      Navigation Navigation tasks are common in everyday household scenarios, such as locating items.
217   We define navigation tasks more specifically as object navigation, where the agent is required to
218   navigate to an instance of a specified object category in unseen environments.
219
220
      Navigation with layout map In real-world scenarios, robots often possess knowledge about certain
      environmental priors, such as a layout map which contains the locations of large pieces of furniture
221
      (e.g., sofas and refrigerators), which are less frequently moved compared to smaller objects. This
222
      information enables the robot to perform its navigation tasks more efficiently.
223
224   Navigation with spatial relation constraint This task requires the robot to identify a specific target
225   object that meets given criteria, such as locating a box on the wall self. This spatial relation constraint
226
      could assess the LLM agent’s perception ability of relative positions between objects. In general,
      these tasks are more challenging than navigation tasks without constraints.
227
228   Navigation with occlusion due to small object size ’Occlusion’ refers to situations where large
229   objects or barriers obstruct the view of target items, challenging an agent to provide strategies to
230   uncover or reach these hidden target objects. This scenario is common in small object navigation,
231   where the object will not be directly visible for the robot and complicating the robot’s visual field and
232
      increase the difficulty of the tasks.
233   Navigation with occlusion due to distant initial position We designed tasks that target objects
234   located far from the robot’s initial position. The robot is then required to locate the target object over
235   a greater distance. This setup poses greater challenges for the agent as it navigates through the room,
236   with objects more likely to be obscured by large pieces of furniture.
237   Navigation and manipulation The most common embodied tasks, typically involve moving and
238   then organizing items. For example, one might pick up one object and relocate it to a designated
239   area, which could be a container or a furniture surface. Other task categories, such as navigation and
240   manipulation with spatial constraints or occlusions, are similarly described in the earlier description.
241
      Navigation and manipulation with temporal and causal constraints Compared to simply combin-
242   ing tasks that are order-invariant, temporal constraints involve actions that must be performed in a
243   strict sequence. For example, an agent might need to prepare a meal where each step is executed in a
244   specific order to ensure the dish is correctly completed. Alternatively, tasks may require the agent to
245   optimize the order of actions to minimize the number of steps needed to complete a task. Additionally,
246   some tasks feature Dependency Chains, where certain actions can only start once previous steps have
247   been completed, such as needing to unlock and open a door before entering a room. Our designed
248   navigation and manipulation tasks include both temporal and causal constraints. For example, the
249   agent must sequentially place object A into container B, and then place object A and container B
250
      together into container C, in the correct order. Alternatively, the task requires the agent to sequentially
      find the target objects, grasp them, locate the recipient area, and place the object in the designated
251
      spot in the correct order. These tasks are crucial for assessing the agent’s ability to understand the
252
      temporal and causal effects of environmental states and the actions it performs.
253
254   Navigation and manipulation multiple objects in an optimal path with two arms In navigation
255   and manipulation tasks, a typical scenario involves preparing various items to achieve specific goals.
256
      This requires the robot to handle multiple objects simultaneously, such as gathering a pen and a
      notebook for writing. Virtual Home supports this by equipping the robot with two arms, allowing
257
      it to use either one or both arms to complete tasks. We design embodied tasks that specifically
258
      exploit these unique features. Using both arms can often be more efficient and optimal, for example,
259   by reducing the moving distance and the number of exploration plan steps. We offer two versions
260   for navigating and manipulating multiple objects: one-arm and two-arm configurations, enabling a
261   comparison of their performance. We consider the two-arm version to be the optimal path.

308   Generating task template. The first step is to define a task template for a specific type of task.
309   Each template includes a task description and completion criteria. The description accounts for the
310   number of objects involved, their properties, and any additional spatial or temporal constraints. This
311   information is integrated as placeholders into various phrasings of the task. The completion criteria
312   comprise the necessary state of relations between the elements involved at specific points during
313   task execution. For example, for a task involving finding an apple and placing it in a fridge, the final
314
      success criteria would be (CLOSE, robot, fridge) & (INSIDE, apple, fridge).
315   Gathering environment information from the simulator. Given the specific task template, we
316   gather related information about the simulation environments through its scene graph. We select all
317   possible object candidates that satisfy the required constraint from the task template. For example, if
318   the task template specifies finding objects with certain relational constraints such as “INSIDE”, we
319   will fetch the target objects and their corresponding objects that satisfy the “INSIDE” relation.
320   LLMs assist task generation After retrieving the relevant information from the environment, we
321   shortlist all possible item combinations that meet the requirements of the commission template. For
322   instance, the task of placing object A into container B necessitates retrieving both object A and
323   container B from the environment. We subsequently employ LLMs to generate tasks adhere to
      principles of logical common sense and are rephrased in diverse ways to increase the diversity of

      Ground truth planning label generation We generate the ground truth planning sequence using
349
      the scene graph in each layout. We find the shortest path between the robot and the target items. A
350
      detailed elaboration of the ground truth action sequence generation is included in Appendix A.6.
351
352   Human evaluation is essential for ensuring the quality of generated tasks. To assess this, we randomly
353   selected 5 simulation environments and chose 50 tasks from each environment. Human annotators
354
      then evaluated the reasonableness of these complex tasks. Our findings indicate that approximately
      4% to 8% of the tasks were deemed unreasonable. The primary cause of the LLM’s failure to correctly
355
      classify these tasks was ambiguity or misinterpretation in the descriptions of the objects involved. For
356
      instance, a task such as "Find the face cream tube and put it into the folder" might pass the LLM’s
357   post-checking process because the term "folder" could sometimes refer to a larger container. Overall,
358   LLMs perform well in identifying unreasonable tasks.

      object and the anchor object will be prioritized. If the anchor object is limited to a single room, only
375
      that room needs to be explored.
376
377   Navigation-object After arriving at a designated room, a 360-degree scan is conducted, and the
      objects within the field of view are identified by the perception module. If the target object is not

      5     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      Our study has some limitations worth noting. The evaluation was conducted in two virtual envi-
      ronments. While we can benefit from controlled experimentation, it might not fully capture the
526
      complexities of real-world settings, potentially leading to a sim-to-real gap. Furthermore, there are
527
      areas within the perception module that could benefit from further exploration and improvement.
528
529   In this work, we present an automatic embodied planning task generation and an LLM agent
530   baseline for benchmark evaluation. We introduce spatial and temporal constraints in navigation
531
      and manipulation tasks, which are barely touched by existing embodied planning benchmarks.
      The method is scalable and can generate infinite number of diverse embodied planning data. The
532
      experiment results demonstrate that addition of spatial and temporal constraints, which are common
533
      in real world embodied tasks, poses significant challenge to the agent with the most advanced LLM
534   GPT-4, because the success rate dramatically reduces for the tasks involving spatial or temporal
535   constraints. Through supervised finetuning, the much smaller model like LLAMA-7B can match
536   or even slightly outperform GPT-4. Given that we merely introduced an LLM agent as a baseline
537   for benchmark evaluation, there remains considerable potential for baseline improvement in future
538   research. Overall, our proposed benchmark can assist researchers in evaluating the performance of
539   their LLM agents in completing complex embodied planning tasks that involve spatial and temporal
      constraints.

1055   A.3.1    E VALUATION OF F OUNDATION M ODELS FOR H OUSEHOLD E MBODIED P LANNING
1056
1057   LLMs are used in task planning for their generalization capabilities across various tasks(Brown
1058   et al., 2020; Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022). Recent works show significant advances in using
1059
       LLMs for long-horizon planning (Zhao et al., 2024), but challenges like hallucination and poor
       spatial reasoning remain (Valmeekam et al., 2024; Dziri et al., 2024). An automatic evaluation of
1060
       LLM-based planners is crucial, yet performance comparison is complicated by factors such as prompt
1061
       construction and model selection. Despite advances in task planning with LLMs, related benchmarks
1062   and automatic evaluations remain limited.
1063
1064   Evaluating LLM-based task planners requires datasets and simulators that include goal conditions of
1065
       tasks, natural language instructions, and a high-level API that facilitates validation using the simulator.
       ActivityProgram (Puig et al., 2018) is a dataset of household activity descriptions in Virtual Home
1066
       (Puig et al., 2018). However, it does not include goal conditions, leaving the evaluation of task success
1067
       to human annotators. Watch-And-Help (Puig et al., 2020) (WAH), another dataset based on Virtual
1068   Home (Puig et al., 2018) focusing on human-robot interactions, lacks natural language descriptions
1069   of the household tasks. LoTa-BENCH (Choi et al., 2024) proposes WAH-NL, an extension to the
1070   Watch-And-Help dataset that includes natural language descriptions. However, all tasks in both
1071   datasets are generally about object rearrangement and only have a similar level of complexity with
1072   short sequences of actions.
1073
       LLM-MCTS (Zhao et al., 2024) attempts to create more complex tasks using the WAH dataset by
1074   defining a complex task as a composition of multiple simple tasks. However, combining simple
1075   tasks does not necessarily increase the difficulty of planning, and this approach does not represent
1076   the complexity of household tasks in real-world cases. EgoPlan-Bench (Chen et al., 2023) also
1077   provides hierarchical reasoning for tasks, claiming that the goal can be broken down into subgoals
1078   and secondary subgoals. However, it still follows the idea that combining simpler tasks creates more
1079   complex tasks, ignoring the potential constraints and obstacles that can make tasks more challenging
       in a real-world setting.


                                                                20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Instead of generating tasks, Behaviour (Srivastava et al., 2022) takes household activities from
1081   the American Time Use Survey, focusing on the diversity and complexity of tasks. However, the
1082   generation of data is not automatic, rendering it hard to scale. HandMeThat (Wan et al., 2022)
1083   proposes another benchmark dataset to add ambiguity to the generated tasks, forcing the agent to
1084   retrieve more implicit information from the task. Like WAH (Puig et al., 2020), HandMeThat (Wan
1085   et al., 2022) also includes a watch step where the agent observes actions performed by humans. They
1086   generate their tasks by instantiating them from the human-annotated tasks in Behaviour (Srivastava
1087   et al., 2022). However, they only interact with a text-based environment.
1088   Datasets such as ALFRED (Shridhar et al., 2020a) are also generated to benchmark embodied
1089   planning tasks using AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2017) or ALFWORLD (Shridhar et al., 2020b) by
1090   generating more visually complex scenes of partially observable environments. RoboGen (Wang
1091   et al., 2023) adopts a generative approach to generate diverse tasks, simulation scenes, and training
1092   supervisions. They leverage Large Language Models for generating tasks, scene configurations, and
1093   task decomposition and finally deploy the generated scenes in a generative simulation engine.
1094   In this research, we focus on household activity planning, which is a combination of object navigation
1095   and manipulation. There is other research more focused on lower-level manipulation tasks that we
1096   did not mention here, for example ManiSkill2 (Gu et al., 2023), which is out of the scope of high
1097   level task planning.

1099   A.3.2    E MBODIED Q UESTION A NSWERING
1100
       Ego-centric Visual Question Answering (EgoVQA) is a crucial task for many robotic applications.
1101
       EgoVQA (Fan, 2019) is a benchmark dataset focusing on first-person VideoQA tasks instead of third-
1102
       person videos. They collected and manually annotated the egocentric VideoQA pairs. EgoTaskQA (Jia
1103   et al., 2022) is a dataset of question-answer pairs for ego-centric videos of human activities; however,
1104   it specifically focuses on descriptive, predictive, explanatory, and counterfactual questions, attempting
1105   to understand spatial, temporal, and causal relationships in the tasks. OpenEQA (Majumdar et al.,
1106   2024) is another benchmark that includes human-generated questions about the environment with
1107   open-vocabulary answers. The questions are categorized into two main types: those where the agent
1108   needs to use episodic memory, such as video, to extract the answer, and those where the agent needs to
1109   explore the environment to find the answer. They leverage LLMs as evaluators to score the answers.
1110   The task of Embodied Question Answering (EQA) has also influenced task planning. EgoPlan-Bench
1111   (Chen et al., 2023) designs the planning task as a multiple question-answering problem, and EgoCOT
1112   (Mu et al., 2024) constructs the task planning data by generating questions about how the task must
1113   be planned. In this work, we focus on task planning using different levels of complexity rather than
1114   answering questions about the environment. However, we continue to employ a question-and-answer
1115   format to prompt the Large Language Model to generate a plan for the task.

1184   A.5   FACTORS THAT I MPACT E MBODIED TASK D IFFICULTIES
1185
1186   Based on tasks described in Table 2 of the main paper, we categorize them into various difficulty
1187   levels according to 5 aspects: action sequence length, prior knowledge, spatial relation constraints,
       occlusion, and temporal constraints.


                                                          22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       Action sequence length Based on the action sequence length, we categorize our benchmark dataset
1189   into three sub-datasets: 1. Navigation tasks with or without spatial constraints: These are the simplest
1190   tasks as they only require the agent to find the target object. Reaching the target object requires at
1191   least one step. 2. Navigation and manipulation tasks with or without constraints: These tasks are
1192   more challenging as they necessitate not only finding the target object and the recipient location, but
1193   also moving the target object to the recipient location. Completing the task requires at least four steps:
1194   walking to the target object, grabbing the target object, walking to the recipient location, and placing
1195   the target object at the recipient location. 3. Navigation and manipulation multiple objects: These
1196   are the most complex tasks, requiring the agent to locate multiple target objects and the recipient
1197
       location, move all objects to the recipient location sequentially, and determine whether the recipient
       location can accommodate both objects simultaneously.
1198
1199   Prior knowledge Prior knowledge is categorized into three levels of environmental semantic infor-
1200   mation: visible information, visible information supplemented with a layout map, and comprehensive
1201   environmental information with full scene graph. "Visible information" implies that the agent can only
1202   obtain details about the environment through its embodied vision capabilities, simulating a real-world
1203
       scenario in which a robot explores an entirely unknown environment from scratch. This scenario
       presents the most challenging task for the robot to complete. In another real-world scenario, the robot
1204
       might possess knowledge of certain environmental details, such as the locations of large, immovable
1205
       furniture. This additional information makes it relatively easier for the robot to complete the task.
1206   In the final scenario, the robot has detailed information about the entire environment including the
1207   location of all small objects, making it the easiest task to complete, as the robot can navigate directly
1208   to its destination without the need for any exploration.
1209
       Spatial relation constraints Tasks with spatial relation constraints are more challenging than those
1210
       without because verifying the spatial relationship between two objects requires advanced scene
1211
       understanding abilities of the agent. However, in some scenario, these constraints also provide
1212   additional information, facilitating more efficient exploration.
1213
1214   Occlusion As discussed in section 3.1, tasks involving occlusion are more challenging, either because
1215
       the size of the object is small or because the initial distance between the robot and the target object is
       far, compared to those without occlusion.
1216
1217   Temporal constraints Tasks involving the navigation and manipulation of multiple objects with
1218   temporal constraints are more challenging than those without, as the robot must adhere to a specific
1219   sequence of steps. Unlike tasks with completion criteria only at the final stage, these tasks include
1220   various intermediate completion criteria due to their causal dependencies.
1221

1223   A.6    G ROUND T RUTH G ENERATION
1224
1225   For each task, we are able to generate ground truth data directly from the scene graph of the layout
1226   provided in the simulator, where objects are represented as nodes and the relationships between
1227   them are represented as edges. For example, in order to find an apple located inside a fridge in the
1228   kitchen, the shortest path between the robot and the apple can be extracted from the scene graph:
1229   kitchen-fridge-apple. Then the path is translated into sequence of actions which could be executed in
1230   the simulator. If the action list can be successfully executed and the goal conditions are met, the data
1231   is saved as ground truth that represents the shortest action list to complete the task. The tasks that
1232
       cannot be completed in this way are removed from the dataset. This happens mostly due to inherent
       bugs of the simulator.
1233
1234   The environment may contain multiple instances of objects with the same name. For tasks involving
1235   spatial constraints, the target object meeting the specified requirements is identified. Utilizing the
1236   unique object ID, the shortest path to the specific target object is then determined. For the tasks that
1237   require object manipulation, the actions of "grab" or "place" are added to the target object or the
1238
       recipient location after navigation.
1239   The tasks with temporal constraints, such as firstly placing object A inside object B then placing
1240   object B that contains A into object C, can be decomposed into two sub-tasks: (1) find object A and
1241   put it in object B; (2) grasp object B and put it into object C. The ground truth for each sub-task can
       be generated using the aforementioned logics.


                                                          23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       For tasks involving navigation and manipulation of multiple objects (e.g., placing both object A
1243   and object B at location C), the single-arm approach generates the ground truth by moving the two
1244   target objects to the destination one by one. It is a straightforward combination of two navigation &
1245   manipulation tasks involving a single object. In contrast, the two-arm approach involves first locating
1246   both objects, then proceeding to the destination while carrying one object in each hand, and finally
1247   placing them down.

1273   The LLM agent’s exploration strategy involves locating the target object using its vision, navigation,
1274   and manipulation capabilities. Virtual Home offers an API to access the visible graph, which details
1275
       the objects within the field of view and their relationships from a first-person perspective. It also
       enables navigation through the execution of the WALK action, specified by the object name and ID.
1276
       Additionally, the robot has various manipulation skills such as OPEN, CLOSE, and GRAB during its
1277
       exploration. The core algorithm governing the robot’s exploration logic is detailed in Algorithm 1.
1278   The primary idea involves systematically exploring rooms and potential large visible objects one
1279   by one in a 360-degree manner until either the target object is found or the maximum exploration
1280   steps are reached. The prompt template for ranking rooms is presented in Table 12, while the prompt
1281   template for the selection of large objects is shown in Table 13. In Virtual Home, each TURNRIGHT
1282   action corresponds to a 30-degree right turn. Therefore, we perform the TURNRIGHT action 12
1283   times to simulate a complete 360-degree exploration. The LLM is involved in ranking rooms and the
1284   selection of large objects, thereby enhancing the agent’s cognitive capabilities.
1285   In tasks involving navigation with spatial constraints, the main concepts are similar to those in
1286   Algorithm 1, with two key differences: 1. After locating the target object, the robot must verify if
1287   the object meets the spatial constraint criteria. 2. If the robot observes only the target object or the
1288   constrained object, it should proceed to investigate one of these objects further, instead of exploring
1289   other large pieces of furniture. Detailed steps of this algorithm are provided in Algorithm 2.
1290   The LLM agent for evaluating navigation and manipulation tasks is detailed in Algorithm 3. This
1291   task can be broken down into four subtasks: navigating to the object to be manipulated, grasping
1292   the object, navigating to the recipient location, and placing the object at the recipient location. The
1293   grasping and placing actions can be executed using the Virtual Home GRAB, PUT, or PUTIN actions.
1294   The navigation to the object to be manipulated follows the procedure outlined in Algorithm 1. During
1295   the navigation process, all visible objects are stored in "memory". If the recipient location is already
       in memory, the robot can navigate there directly, avoiding redundant exploration. Otherwise, the robot

1388
1389
       Given our automated quantitative evaluation pipeline, we utilize various metrics to assess results
1390
       across different levels of tasks.
1391
1392   Success Rate The criteria for determining the success of a sample vary slightly depending on the
1393   task. For navigation tasks, a task is deemed successful if it finds the target object. For navigation
1394
       tasks with spatial constraints, the robot must find a specific object that meets spatial relation criteria.
       For example, if the task is to find an apple in the fridge but the robot finds an apple on the table, it is
1395
       not considered as a success. Success rate is calculated as the number of successfully completed cases
1396
       divided by the total number of cases, with a higher success rate being more favorable.
1397
1398   Sequence Length Another key metric is the sequence length, which is the number of action steps
1399   required to complete the task. For failed tasks, we use the predefined maximum number of steps as
1400
       the sequence length. We then average the sequence lengths across all tasks. Shorter sequence lengths
       are desirable, as they indicate quicker task completion.
1401

1403   similarity between the agent’s exploration actions and the ground truth actions. Typically, in the most
       successful cases, the length of the longest common sequence matches the length of the ground truth
