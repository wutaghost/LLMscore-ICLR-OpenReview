009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL), which aims to capture representations from
012            unlabeled graphs, has made significant progress in recent years. In GCL,
013            InfoNCE-based loss functions play a crucial role by ensuring that positive node
014            pairs—those that are similar—are drawn closer together in the representational
015            space, while negative pairs, which are dissimilar, are pushed apart. The primary
016            focus of recent research has been on refining the contrastive loss function, partic-
017            ularly by adjusting the weighting of negative nodes. This is achieved by changing
018
               the weight between negative node pairs, or by using node similarity to select the
               positive node associated with the anchor node. Despite the substantial success
019
               of these GCL techniques, there remains a belief that the nodes identified as pos-
020
               itive or negative may not accurately reflect the true positives and negatives. To
021            tackle this challenge, we introduce an innovative method known as Positive Min-
022            ing Graph Contrastive Learning (PMGCL). This method consists in calculating
023            the probability of positive samples between the anchor node and other nodes us-
024            ing a mixture model, thereby identifying nodes that have a higher likelihood of
025            being true positives in relation to the anchor node. We have conducted a com-
026            prehensive evaluation of PMGCL on a range of real-world graph datasets. The
027            experimental findings indicate that PMGCL significantly outperforms traditional
028            GCL methods. Our method not only achieves state-of-the-art results in unsuper-
029
               vised learning benchmarks but also exceeds the performance of supervised learn-
               ing benchmarks in certain scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Manessi et al., 2020)
035
      have emerged as a powerful class of models for learning representations from graph-structured data.
036   GNNs often demonstrate remarkable performance across various domains by aggregating neighbor-
037   hood information multiple times, including node classification, link prediction, and graph classifi-
038   cation tasks. Traditional GNNs are primarily built upon supervised or semi-supervised approaches,
039   inherently relying on large amounts of high-quality labeled data. However, in practical applications,
040   acquiring an abundance of graph labels requires considerable resources and time (Dai et al., 2022;
041   Shi et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2022; 2021b; Zheng et al., 2022). Consequently, unsupervised learning
042   remains a challenging endeavor.
043   Contrastive learning (CL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for unsupervised representation
044   learning. Unlike traditional supervised learning methods that rely on labeled data, contrastive learn-
045   ing leverages the inherent structure and relationships within the data to learn meaningful represen-
046   tations without the need for explicit labels. It has gained a lot of attention and achieved impressive
047   results in various fields, including Computer Vision (CV) (Zhu et al., 2020), Natural Language
048   Processing (NLP) (Aberdam et al., 2021), and more recently Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL)
049   (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020; You et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020; You et al., 2020), which combines
050
      CL with GNN to learn rich information from unlabeled graph data.
051   A similar process is adopted in existing GCL methods. First, different graph augmentation meth-
052   ods are used to generate various views, such as node dropping (You et al., 2020), edge perturbation
053   (Veličković et al., 2018), attribute masking (Zhu et al., 2021), subgraph sampling (Yang et al., 2022)
      and graph noise injection (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020). Then use the same GNN encoder or a

085
      in Figure 1(a) and 1(b), by creating different augmentation views, each anchor forms a positive
086
      pair, InfoNCE treats all other distinct nodes of different views as negative pairs, while NT-Xent
087   treats all distinct nodes in the same and different views as negative pairs. Based on this, many GCL
088   methods produce different loss functions by adjusting the weights between negative pairs. This
089   causes nodes belonging to the same classes to be pushed away from the anchor. However, according
090   to Contrastive Learning theory and empirical analysis, samples of the same class should be close
091   to each other, not pushed apart (Tian et al., 2020). As shown in Figure 1(c), NCLA (Shen et al.,
092   2023) solves this problem to some extent. According to the homogeneity hypothesis (McPherson
093   et al., 2001), interconnected nodes usually belong to the same class. Therefore, NCLA takes the
094   neighbor nodes of the anchor as positive samples. In fact, there are many false positives in the
095
      neighbor nodes, which will push the positives away inappropriately. Choosing more and the right
      negatives remains a challenge. To remedy the aforementioned limitations, we propose a new GCL
096
      method, called PMGCL, which we believe can distinguish between true and false positives by fitting
097
      a two-component (true-false positives) beta mixing model (BMM) (Gupta & Nadarajah, 2004; Ji
098   et al., 2005). With BMM, we can obtain more suitable positives according to the probability of true
099   positives of the anchor, and the loss function is different from the contrast loss function originally
100   proposed by CV (such as InfoNCE and NT-Xent), which only takes one positive pair. We allow
101   multiple positive pairs obtained from BMM. As shown in Figure 1(d), node color represents the
102   probability of being directly associated with the anchor, with red representing the highest probability
103   and blue the lowest probability. The difference from Figure 1(c) is that in our method, neighbor
104   nodes can be negative and non-neighbor nodes can be positive. Our contributions can be summarized
105   as follows:
106
107        1) We propose that using BMM to estimate the probability of other nodes being true positive
              to the anchor is a more suitable method for selecting positive pairs.

118   2.1   G RAPH C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING (GCL)
119
120   Contrastive learning, as an effective unsupervised learning paradigm, can get rid of the constraints
121   of artificial labels (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Tan et al., 2021) (Xia et al., 2021a). Initially, DGI
122
      (Veličković et al., 2018) applies the idea of Deep InfoMax (DIM) to the graph, encoding the lo-
      cal neighborhood of each node and encoding the global graph to learn the representation of nodes.
123
      Inspired by DGI, InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019) uses information sharing between local features of
124
      nodes and the global structure of graphs to improve node representation learning. Similarly, GMI
125   (Peng et al., 2020) works by maximizing mutual information between input and output graphs. MV-
126   GRL (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020) proposes to learn node-level and graph-level representations by
127   node diffusion and comparing nodes with representations of augmentation graphs. Later, GraphCL
128   (You et al., 2020) proposed different combinations of graph augmentations, including random node
129   drop, feature masking, edge perturbation, subgraph sampling and graph noise injection. To make
130   GraphCL more flexible, JOAO (You et al., 2021) automatically selects combinations of different
131   random graphs for augmentation. Recently, GCL has focused on fully parametric graph augmenta-
132   tion, with AutoGCL (Yin et al., 2022) building a learnable graph generator that learns a probability
133
      distribution to help adaptive drop nodes and mask features. SimGRACE (Xia et al., 2021a) even
      simplifies GCL by removing data augmentations. In this paper, we consider how to select positives
134
      to further improve the effectiveness of positive selection in node-level contrastive learning.
135
136
137   2.2   C ONTRASTIVE O BJECTIVE
138
      Common contrast modes in GCL are graph-graph, graph-node, node-node (Liu et al., 2022). In the
139
      node-node GCL method, the positives are close to each other and the negatives are far away from
140
      each other. For example, DGI (Veličković et al., 2018) and GMI (Peng et al., 2020) contrasts the
141   neighborhood characteristics and hidden representations of each node. Recently, proposed ProGCL
142   (Xia et al., 2021b), the weight of negative samples is reassigned by mining hard negatives. However,
143   we believe that these methods are all similar to InfoNCE and NT-Xent, taking only one positive and
144   the rest of the nodes are negative, and then pushing it away from the anchor. However, this is not
145   desirable in terms of graph domain, as it may push nodes of the same label away as well. In a recent
146   study, NCLA (Shen et al., 2023) and gCool (Li et al., 2022) considered different approaches to
147   defining positives. NCLA considers all of the anchor’s neighbors as positives, and gCool considers
148   all of the nodes in the same community as positives which is not quite appropriate in the real graph
149
      data. There are still a large number of negative nodes in the neighborhood or community nodes,
      resulting in mistakenly pulling the negative nodes closer to the anchor. In this paper, we calculate
150
      the probability that nodes and anchors are positives to obtain more reasonable positives and increase
151
      the number of positive pairs.

      3.4   T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
325
326   In the training process, using BMM to estimate the probability takes a slight time, but we only
327   need to fit the BMM once    in the whole training process, instead of fitting each epoch once, and
328   we obtain M M << N 2 similarities to fit the BMM by random sampling method. Therefore, the
329   time complexity of fitting BMM by EM algorithm is O(IM ). I is the number of iterations that fit
330   the BMM. The time complexity of neighbor contrastive learning is O(N 2 F ′ ) where N is the number
331   of nodes. F is the number of input features and F ′ is the embedding dimension. Thus, the total time
332   complexity is O(IM + N 2 F ′ ).

      4.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
337
338   Datasets. We evaluate our method using seven widely used datasets, Cora, Citeseer, and PubMed
339   from the Plantoid (Kipf & Welling, 2016), Photo and Computers from the Amazon (McAuley
340   et al., 2015), a co-authorship network Coauthor-CS (Shchur et al., 2018), a reference network from
341   Wikipedia WikiCS (Mernyei & Cangea, 2007). More details are in the appendix A.
342
      Baselines. We primarily compare our PMGCL with classical GSSL algorithms: DeepWalk (Perozzi
343
      et al., 2014) and Node2vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016). Additionally, we also consider other recent
344   GSSL baselines: BGRL (Thakoor et al., 2021), GRACE (Zhu et al., 2020), GCA (Zhu et al., 2021),
345   MVGRL (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020), DGI (Veličković et al., 2018), GBT (Bielak et al., 2022),
346   ProGCL (Xia et al., 2021b) and PiGCL (He et al., 2024). We also compare PMGCL with super-
347   vised counterparts including GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) and Graph Attention Networks (GAT)
348   (Veličković et al., 2017).
349
      Detailed Setup. Based on previous work (Veličković et al., 2018), we trained the model in an
350   unsupervised manner. We test our PMGCL on classification and clustering tasks. For classification
351   task, we adopt a two-layer GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016) as transduction study of encoder. We follow
352   the GRACE test (Zhu et al., 2020). Specifically, we use 10% of the data to train the downstream

463   5     C ONCLUSIONS
464
465   To address the challenges of difficult positive selection in GCL and the insufficiency of positives
466
      in contrastive loss, PMGCL has been proposed. On one hand, PMGCL can more accurately select
      positive nodes by fitting the BMM. On the other hand, instead of directly adopting the contrastive
467
      loss from computer vision, PMGCL improves upon it by transitioning from a single positive to
468
      multiple positives. Consequently, PMGCL significantly enhances the utilization of positive nodes.
469   Extensive experiments in node classification and clustering demonstrate that the PMGCL method
470   can identify a greater number of more accurate positive nodes and can achieve superior performance
471   across multiple tasks.
