009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The mainstream federated learning algorithms only communicate the first-order
012            information across the local devices, i.e., FedAvg and FedProx. However, only
013            using first-order information, these methods are often inefficient and the impact
014            of heterogeneous data is yet not precisely understood. This paper proposes an
015            efficient federated Newton method (FedNewton), by sharing both first-order and
016            second-order knowledge over heterogeneous data. In general kernel ridge regres-
017            sion setting, we derive the generalization bounds for FedNewton and obtain the
018
               minimax-optimal learning rates. For the first time, our results analytically quan-
               tify the impact of the number of local examples, the data heterogeneity and the
019
               model heterogeneity. Moreover, as long as the local sample size is not too small
020
               and data heterogeneity is moderate, the federated error in FedNewton decreases
021            exponentially in terms of iterations. Extensive experimental results further vali-
022            date our theoretical findings and illustrate the advantages of FedNewton over the
023            first-order methods.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Owing to the great potential in privacy preservation and in lowering the computational costs, feder-
029   ated learning (FL) McMahan et al. (2017); Li et al. (2020a); Zhang et al. (2021) becomes a promising
030   framework in processing large-scale tasks. However, federated learning is facing massive challenges
031   from the heterogeneous data Zhao et al. (2018); Zhou et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023), including both
032   the data heterogeneity and the model heterogeneity. The data heterogeneity comes from that inputs
033
      across devices are usually sampled from heterogeneous distributions, while the model heterogeneity
      measures the response shift due to inconsistency between local models and the global model.
034
035   First-order approaches, including FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) and FedProx Li et al. (2020a),
036   share the first-order information rather than the data across devices and tolerate the heterogeneity
037   in federated learning, while Newton-type FL methods Ghosh et al. (2020); Gupta et al. (2021); Sa-
038   faryan et al. (2022); Islamov et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Dal Fabbro et al. (2024); Li et al. (2023)
039
      utilized second-order information for updating federated model. To the best of our knowledge, most
      of existing learning guarantees for FL methods are derived in the context of optimization and fo-
040
      cused on in-sample predictive errors only, i.e., the convergence analysis (optimization) of first-order
041
      FL Li et al. (2020b); Karimireddy et al. (2020); Pathak & Wainwright (2020); Glasgow et al. (2022)
042   and Newton-type FL Ghosh et al. (2020); Safaryan et al. (2022); Qian et al. (2022). However,
043   beyond the optimization, the generalization guarantees (out-sample predictive performance) are of
044   great practical and theoretical interests for FL. Despite recent efforts and progress on the general-
045   ization for first-order algorithms Mohri et al. (2019); Yagli et al. (2020); Su et al. (2021); Yuan et al.
046   (2022), the generalization guarantees for Newton-type FL algorithms remain elusive, especially on
047   heterogeneous data and localized models. Therefore, a challenging problem in FL is how to quantify
048   the impact of heterogeneity from the generalization perspective?
049   In this paper, motivated by sharing second-order information, we propose a second-order federated
050   optimization method, named FedNewton. It approximates the global predictor on the entire data
051   by utilizing the global gradient and local Hessians, improving the predictive accuracy in an efficient
052   communications framework. We then study the statistical properties of FedNewton, and derive the
053   generalization bounds with the minimax optimal rates. We conclude with experiments on simulated
      data and publicly available tasks that complement our theoretical results, exhibiting the computa-

      tional and statistical benefits of our approach. Due to the length limit, we leave the experiment part
055   in the appendix. We summarize our contributions as below:
056
057   1) On the algorithmic front. We propose a fast second-order federated learning algorithm, which
058
      improves the approximation of the centralized model while only requiring similar computational and
      communication costs as the first-order methods. The convergence of FedNewton is exponentially
059
      fast and a few communications, for example, t → 2, can approximate the global model well.
060
061   2) On the statistical front. To our best knowledge, in presence of both data heterogeneity and
062   model heterogeneity, we present the optimal generalization guarantees for the first time. Our results
063   further analytically quantify the impacts of the local sample size, the data heterogeneity, and the
064
      model heterogeneity. Especially, the federated error decreases exponentially fast in benign cases,
      i.e., a sufficient number of local examples and moderate data heterogeneity.

480   In this paper, we present an efficient second-order optimization method for FL. We derive gener-
481   alization bounds with the optimal rates, which quantify the impacts of local sample size, the data
482   heterogeneity, and the model heterogeneity. In benign cases, the federated error convergence expo-
483   nentially fast, and thus communications can be small. Our theoretical findings fill the gap between
484   optimization and generalization for federated learning, rather than focusing on one of them. Overall,
485   the techniques presented here highlight new ways for designing efficient algorithms and analyzing
      both generalization and optimization for FL.
