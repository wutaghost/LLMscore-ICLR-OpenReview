011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Random Feature Model (RFM) with a nonlinear activation function is instrumen-
014            tal in understanding training and generalization performance in high-dimensional
015
               learning. While existing research has established an asymptotic equivalence in
               performance between the RFM and noisy linear models under isotropic data as-
016
               sumptions, empirical observations indicate that the RFM frequently surpasses lin-
017
               ear models in practical applications. To address this gap, we ask, “When and how
018            does the RFM outperform linear models?” In practice, inputs often have addi-
019            tional structures that significantly influence learning. Therefore, we explore the
020            RFM under anisotropic input data characterized by spiked covariance in the pro-
021            portional asymptotic limit, where dimensions diverge jointly while maintaining
022            finite ratios. Our analysis reveals that a high correlation between inputs and la-
023            bels is a critical factor enabling the RFM to outperform linear models. Moreover,
024            we show that the RFM performs equivalent to noisy polynomial models, where
025            the polynomial degree depends on the strength of the correlation between inputs
026
               and labels. Our numerical simulations validate these theoretical insights, confirm-
               ing the performance-wise superiority of RFM in scenarios characterized by strong
027
               input-label correlation.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110
111   The RFM has emerged as a vital tool in the realm of machine learning, initially introduced as a
112
      randomized approximation to kernel methods (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). Its significance has grown in
      parallel with the development of neural networks, particularly in how both the RFM and the Neural
113
      Tangent Kernel (NTK) offer distinct linear approximations of two-layer neural networks (Ghorbani
114
      et al., 2021). While empirical evidence shows that neural networks often outperform both the RFM
115   and NTK (Ghorbani et al., 2020), analyzing the RFM provides critical insights into the behavior of
116   nonlinear models, serving as a bridge toward understanding more complex architectures.
117
118
      A substantial body of research has focused on the equivalence between the RFM and noisy lin-
      ear models with regards to their performances (Montanari et al., 2019; Gerace et al., 2020; Goldt
119
      et al., 2020; 2022; Dhifallah & Lu, 2020; Mei & Montanari, 2022). For instance, Hu & Lu (2023)
120
      demonstrated this equivalence in terms of training and generalization performance using Linde-
121   berg’s method. Additionally, Montanari & Saeed (2022) showed that nonlinear features could be
122   substituted with equivalent Gaussian features in empirical risk minimization settings. However,
123   these studies mainly examined the RFM under isotropic data assumptions, which limits their appli-
124   cability to real-world scenarios characterized by more complex data structures. Our work extends
125   these findings by investigating the RFM under anisotropic data conditions, thereby addressing a sig-
126   nificant gap in the literature. Recent works have indicated that equivalent Gaussian features may
127   not consistently provide equivalent training and generalization errors for nonlinear models (Gerace
128   et al., 2022; Pesce et al., 2023). This discrepancy has led researchers to explore alternative feature
129
      representations, including Gaussian mixtures (Dandi et al., 2023b). However, our study diverges
      from this line of inquiry by concentrating on identifying equivalent activation functions rather than
130
      seeking alternatives to Gaussian features.
131
132   In light of the RFM’s limitations in surpassing linear models, recent research has turned to neural
133   networks trained with one gradient step as a potential solution to bridge this performance gap (Ba
134   et al., 2022; Damian et al., 2022). Studies by Ba et al. (2023) and Mousavi-Hosseini et al. (2023)
135
      have explored one-step gradient descent techniques in relation to sample complexity under spiked
      covariance data assumption. Furthermore, Moniri et al. (2023) explored neural networks trained
136
      with one gradient step in an isotropic data setting and showed that the update can be characterized
137
      by the appearance of a spike in the spectrum of the feature matrix. Dandi et al. (2023a); Cui et al.
138   (2024) studied the one-step gradient descent with a higher learning rate in comparison to Moniri
139   et al. (2023). Ba et al. (2022); Moniri et al. (2023); Dandi et al. (2023a); Cui et al. (2024) also stated
140   Gaussian equivalence results in terms of training and generalization errors when studying the one
141   gradient step technique under isotropic Gaussian data. Note that although the aforementioned works
142   considered feature learning in two-layer neural networks, feature learning for three-layer neural
143   networks is shown to have better sample complexities than the two-layer case (Nichani et al., 2023;
144   Wang et al., 2024). While these works primarily focus on surpassing the performance of linear
145   models through feature learning in neural networks, our research aims to clarify when and how the
146
      RFM can outperform linear models without relying on feature learning.
147   Additionally, there is an ongoing exploration into deep random features that integrate multiple
148   layers of random projections interleaved with nonlinearities (Schröder et al., 2023; Bosch et al.,
149   2023). Research by Zavatone-Veth & Pehlevan (2023) has also examined deep random features
150   with anisotropic weight matrices using statistical physics techniques. Moreover, studies have inves-
151   tigated the RFM under polynomial scaling limits where k, n, m → ∞ while maintaining specific
152
      ratios between these parameters, i.e., k/nc1 , m/k c2 ∈ (0, ∞) for some constants c1 , c2 > 0 (Hu
      et al., 2024), further providing equivalence results for the RFM under the polynomial scaling limit.

      In this section, we describe our main theoretical results, significantly enhancing the understanding
219   of RFM under structured data conditions. In Section 4.1, we introduce a new “universality” theorem
220   that establishes the performance-wise equivalence of the RFM when utilizing two distinct activa-
221   tion functions, σ and σ̂. This theorem asserts that if the first two statistical moments of the pairs
222   (σ(Fx), y) and (σ̂(Fx), y) are matched, the training and generalization performance of the RFM
223   will remain equivalent across both functions. Building on this foundation, Section 4.2 explores
224   the scenario where σ̂ is represented as a finite-degree Hermite expansion of σ, demonstrating that
225   the RFM retains equivalent performance regardless of the chosen activation function. Notably, we
226   reveal that the degree of this expansion is intricately tied to the input-label correlation, offering in-
227
      sights into how data structure influences model performance. In Section 4.3, we establish conditions
      for the equivalence between the RFM and a noisy linear model, providing a crucial corollary to our
228
      earlier findings. When these conditions are not satisfied, we identify that a high-order polynomial
229
      model serves as an equivalent to the RFM, a topic further elaborated in Section 4.4.

238   and the loss function, their findings were constrained by an isotropic data assumption. To address
239   these limitations, we propose a new universality theorem tailored for scenarios involving spiked

      covariance data, as outlined in equation (6).

528   6   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In conclusion, this study explored the random feature model within the context of spiked covariance
531   data, establishing a significant “universality” theorem that underpins our findings. We demonstrated
532
      that a strong correlation between inputs and labels is essential for the RFM to surpass linear models
      in generalization performance. Additionally, we established that the RFM achieves performance
533
      equivalent to noisy polynomial models, with the polynomial degree influenced by the magnitude of
534
      the input-label correlation. Our numerical simulations corroborated these theoretical findings, high-
535   lighting the RFM’s superior effectiveness in environments with strong input-label correlation. This
536   work highlights the critical role of data modeling in understanding and enhancing model perfor-
537   mance. Moving forward, we believe that extending the data model presents a promising avenue for
538   future research, allowing for deeper exploration of complex relationships and further optimization
539   of high-dimensional learning strategies.

1539   G    D ETAILS FOR CIFAR-10 E XPERIMENTS
1540
1541   In our experiments, we focus on binary classification between airplanes and automobiles using the
1542   CIFAR-10 dataset (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) to demonstrate how our findings translate to real-world
1543   applications. We randomly select 2,000 samples from each class for training, while a separate set
1544   of 2,500 samples (distinct from the training set) is used to calculate the test (generalization) error.
1545   To prepare the input samples, we normalize the pixel values to achieve zero mean and unit variance
1546   for each color channel (R, G, B). Specifically, the pixel values are first scaled to the range [0, 1]
1547   by dividing by 255, followed by subtracting channel-wise means and dividing by their respective
1548   standard deviations. The images are then flattened into vectors for input into the model.
1549   For the feature matrix F, we sample entries independently from a normal distribution
1550   N (0, 1/Tr(E[xxT ])), ensuring that E[(fiT x)2 ] = 1 for all i, where fi denotes the i-th row of F.
1551
       Our results are presented in two scenarios: first with the original normalized inputs (Figure 4a),
1552
       and second after adding standard Gaussian noise (variance of one for each feature) to create a more
1553   isotropic covariance structure (Figure 4b). This addition of noise allows us to assess the performance
1554   equivalence between the linear model and RFM under conditions of weak input-label correlation.
1555
1556
       For labels, we use {−1, 1} for the first class and the second class, respectively. To control input-label
       correlation, we introduce a label flipping mechanism with probability p, where p = 0 corresponds
1557
       to true labels (maximum correlation) and p = 0.5 represents random labels (minimum correlation).
1558
       By varying p within the range [0, 0.5], we interpolate between these two extremes and analyze how
1559   this affects model performance. The training errors for these experiments are illustrated in Figure 7.
