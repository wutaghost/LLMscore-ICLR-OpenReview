007                                               A BSTRACT
008
009            The scientific computing for large deformations of elastic-plastic solids is critical
010            for numerous real-world applications. Classical numerical solvers rely primarily on
011            local discrete linear approximations, which are constrained by an inherent trade-off
012            between accuracy and efficiency. Recently, Deep Learning models have achieved
013            impressive progress in solving PDEs. While previous models have explored various
014            architectures and constructed coefficient-solution mappings, they are designed for
015
               general instances without considering specific problem properties and hard to
               accurately handle with complex elastic-plastic solids involving contact, loading
016
               and unloading. In this work, we take stretch bending, a popular metal fabrication
017
               technique, as our case study and introduce LaDEEP, a deep learning-based surrogate
018            model for Large Deformations of Elastic-Plastic Solids. We encode the partitioned
019            regions of the involved solids into a token sequence to maintain their essential
020            order property. To characterize the physical process of the solid deformation, a
021            two-stage Transformer-based module is designed to predict the deformation with
022            the sequence of tokens as input. Empirically, LaDEEP achieves five magnitudes
023            faster speed than finite element methods with a comparable accuracy, and gains
024            20.47% relative improvement on average compared to other deep learning baselines.
025            We have also deployed our model into a real-world industrial production system,
026
               and it has shown remarkable performance in both accuracy and efficiency.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The scientific computing for large deformations of elastic-plastic solids (Bathe & Ozdemir, 1976) is
032   essential in continuum mechanics, which is widely used in various areas such as civil engineering
033
      (AbouRizk & Hajjar, 1998), aerospace (Phanden et al., 2021), and nuclear materials (Allen et al.,
      2012). The deformation of a solid typically results from the application of external loads and
034
      constraints. Large deformations occur when the extent of deformation becomes significant enough
035
      to invalidate the assumptions of infinitesimal strain theory (Bower, 2009). Metals are particularly
036   important subjects of study due to their distinct elastic and plastic behaviors. In the elastic regime,
037   metals tend to return to their original shape after deformation, but only up to a certain threshold.
038   Beyond this limit, when sufficient load is applied and the material enters the plastic state, permanent
039   deformation occurs. Upon the release of the load, the elastic component of the deformation will
040   recover, while the plastic deformation remains, causing the material to rebound slightly and assume
041   its final shape. An accurate and efficient solver for such complex task is in urgent demand.
042   Figure 1 illustrates a practical example known as stretch bending (Clausen et al., 2000), one of the
043   most widely used metal fabrication techniques (Murr et al., 2012). This process consists of two
044   distinct stages: loading and unloading. During the loading phase, the metal workpiece is positioned
045   on the machine and securely held by two working arms. These arms move and rotate, applying force
046   to the workpiece, causing it to lengthen and curve around a mold, which acts as a constraint. In the
047   unloading phase, the applied loads are released, allowing the metal to rebound and assume its final
048   shape. Our primary interest lies in predicting the final shape of the workpiece given the influence of
049   the applied loads and constraints.
050   This fabrication technique involves large deformation of elastic-plastic solids, governed by partial
051   differential equations (PDEs). The workpiece corresponds to the solution domain, the mold represents
052   the boundary condition, and the loads from the movement of the working arms are modeled as source
053   terms. Traditional methods, such as Finite Element Methods (FEM) (Reddy, 2019), depicted in
      Figure 2a, rely on local discrete linear approximations. The material is subdivided into smaller

072

073

074   et al., 2020; 2023) and DeepONet (Lu et al., 2019), learn the mappings between infinite-dimensional

079   a compact latent space in linear time. Similarly, Transolver (Wu et al., 2024) introduces physics-
080   attention to adaptively partition the discretized domain into a series of learnable slices, capturing
081   the underlying physical states. While these methods are generally effective for problems in fluid
082
      mechanics or simple solid mechanics, they tend to underperform in our problem, which involves
      non-smooth boundary conditions (solid contacts) and multi-stage processes (unloading and loading)
083
      due to the ignorance of characteristics of large deformation and staged modeling.

      To address these challenges, we begin by analyzing the inherent properties of the task and make a pio-
100
      neering attempt to apply deep learning to the large deformations of elastic-plastic solids. Focusing on
101
      a highly relevant industrial scenario, the stretch bending, we introduce LaDEEP as a surrogate model
102   to approximate the complex multi-stage process. The model follows an Encoder-Processor-Decoder
103   procedure (Battaglia et al., 2018). We develop several specialized modules to encode property-aware
104   token sequences, which represent sequentially partitioned regions corresponding to solids that retain
105   essential order properties. Then, a two-stage Transformer-based module, the Deformation Predictor
106   (DP), is proposed as a processor to characterize the loading and unloading process. By applying
107   attention mechanisms to the token sequences , DP effectively captures the complex underlying
      interactions between objects (see Figure 5). To validate our approach, we first create a new dataset

130   Physics-Informed Neural Networks. These approaches formulate the PDEs, including governing
131   equations, source items, initial conditions, and boundary conditions, as loss functions within DL
132   models (Raissi et al., 2019). During training, the output of the model progressively conforms to the
133   PDE constraints, ultimately providing an accurate approximation of the PDE solution. However,
134   These approaches require the precise formula of PDEs, making them difficult to apply to real-world
135   scenarios with incomplete observations. Additionally, they are typically limited to solving a single
136   problem instance, and any change in parameters requires retraining the models.
137   Neural Operators. The idea of neural operators is to learn mappings between two infinite-
138   dimensional function spaces. The most prevailing models are FNO (Li et al., 2022), which ap-
139   proximates integration with linear projection in the Fourier domain. Building on this, various variants
140   (Wen et al., 2022; Rahman et al., 2022; Gupta et al., 2021; Tran et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; 2023;
141   2024b; Bonev et al., 2023) have been proposed with crafted architectures to improve the accuracy,
142   efficiency and application extensions. DeepONet (Lu et al., 2019) is also a prevalent model for
143
      operator learning which is designed based on the Universal Approximation Theorem for Operator
      (Chen & Chen, 1995). However, as shown in our experiments, they tend to degenerate in our case,
144
      which involves non-smooth boundary conditions (solid contacts) and multiple stages (unloading and
145
      loading) due to the lack of consideration over the properties of solids and staged modeling.
146
147   Transformer-based PDE Solvers. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have been employed to
148   solve PDEs. HT-Net (Ma et al., 2022) combines Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) with multigrid
149
      methods (Wesseling, 1995) to capture multiscale spatial relationships. FactFormer (Li et al., 2024a)
      enhances efficiency by leveraging a low-rank structure with multidimensional factorized attention.
150
      LSM (Wu et al., 2023) is introduced to address the high-dimensional complexity of PDEs by utilizing
151
      spectral methods (Gottlieb & Orszag, 1977) within a learned latent space. Transolver (Wu et al.,
152   2024) introduces physics-attention to dynamically partition the discretized domain into learnable
153   slices that capture the underlying physical states. However, these approaches struggle to handle our
154   case due to the lack of the inherent property of the large deformations of elastic-plastic solids.

183   The formats of the considered objects in our case are unstructured and unsuitable for deep learning
184   models. We first need to preprocess these objects to organize their structure, reduce redundancy, and
185   ensure completeness. In practice, most workpieces are slender with a constant cross-sectional shape
186
      along their length. This means the cross-section remains the same when cut parallel to certain faces
      of the workpiece, allowing it to be recorded as a 2D image. We define curves along the length of the
187
      workpiece as “characteristic lines”, which contain curvature information and describe how the cross-
188
      section expands. A 2D cross-section and a characteristic line can fully represent the 3D workpiece.
189   For the 3D mold, its cross-section is designed based on the workpiece, so it can be represented simply
190   by its characteristic line. We sample each characteristic line (for both the workpiece and the mold)
191   as a point set p = {pi |i = 1, · · · , M }, consisting of M points where each point pi is a position
192   vector of (x, y, z). Regarding the motion of each working arm, it applies loads on the workpiece
193   through moving and rotating, controlled by 6 degrees of freedom (DoFs) {ux , uy , uz , rx , ry , rz },
194   where {ux , uy , uz } represent spatial displacements, and {rx , ry , rz } are rotations around each axis.
195   In practice, most of the products are symmetric and the middle of the workpiece are kept static when
196   processing. For simplicity and without violating the physics, we only consider the half of the system.

       image with shape 1×H×W . We use the Signed Distance Function (SDF) (Guo et al., 2016) to provide
221
       a low-redundancy representation of the shape and structure for cross-section, omitting unnecessary
222
       brightness, spectrum and semantic information. Specifically, a contour set Con in a domain Ω ⊂ R2
223    is defined as Con = {(i, j) ∈ R2 : g(i, j) = 0}, where g is the sign function indicating the point’s
224    position relative to the contour: g(i, j) = −1 when (i, j) ∈ Ω and g(i, j) = 1 when g(i, j) ∈ ∁R2 Ω.
225    The SDF D(i, j) is formulated as D(i, j) = min(i′ ,j ′ )∈Con |(i, j)−(i′ −j ′ )|·g(i, j), which measures
226    the distance of a given point (i, j) to the nearest contour point with a sign indicting the relative
227    position. After getting the SDF representation s ∈ R1×H×W , a frozen pre-trained ResNet (He et al.,
228    2016) is utilized as a backbone to extract features, commonly applied in Computer Vision (CV)
229    (Voulodimos et al., 2018). Since the backbone is trained on natural images and may not be suitable
230    for the SDF, we add several convolutional layers with trainable parameters for greater flexibility. The
231    cross-section of the workpiece is taken as input, resulting in the flattened output feature sw ∈ R1×C .
232     Object Feature Fusioner (OFF). Recall that a workpiece is described by two separated features,
233     the cross-section sw ∈ R1×C and the characteristic line xw ∈ RN ×C . We then fuse them to obtain
234     complete workpiece representation. We repeat the feature sw by N times and add it into xw to form
235     the fused result zw ∈ RN ×C . In this way, on the one hand, we can ensure that each token in xw
236     contains cross-sectional information, preserving the knowledge of constant cross-section. On the
237     other hand, it does not disrupt the inherent order property within the feature of characteristic line xw .
238    Motion Parameter Encoder (MPE). As claimed before, the loads applied on the workpiece are
239    caused by the movement of each working arm, which is controlled by 6 DoFs. We use multiple
240    tokens to capture the comprehensive influence of the movement effect along each degree of freedom.
241    Specifically, the DoF of each axis is embedded with size C and then separately projected into
242    N = Y × 6 tokens in total by 6 distinct linear layers (N is set as an integer multiple of 6). Each DoF
243    is represented by adjacent Y tokens, distributing the effects across different tokens for better learning.

       The final output are the tokens ym ∈ RN ×C , representing the motion parameters.

323   After getting the high-dimensional representations of the workpiece, we develop several decoders to
      map high-dimensional representations back to the original space and calculate losses.

      Characteristic Line Decoder (CLD). We roughly reverse the structure of the Characteristic Line
325   Encoder (CLE) as Characteristic Line Decoder (CLD) to decode the high-dimensional representation
326   back to the original space. Take zb ∈ RN ×C from unloading module as input, we first use N
327   distinct linear layers to decode each token to adjacent M/N points. Then, a linear layer is utilized to
328   de-embed these points back to the Euclidean space. We decode za ∈ RN ×C from loading module in
329   the same way. The final outputs for za and zb are pa ∈ RM ×3 and pb ∈ RM ×3 , respectively.
330
      Cross-Section Decoder (CSD). Given that the cross-section has a significant impact on the forming
331   of the workpiece (Yu et al., 2018), we establish a Cross-Section Decoder (CSD) to recover SDFs,
332   ensuring the extracted features by Cross-Section Encoder (CSE) are effective. We refer to the structure
333   of VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2022) and utilize deconvolution layers and interpolation operations to
334   construct CSD. Take sw ∈ R1×C from CSE as input, the output is sr ∈ R1×H×W , matching the
335   shape of ground truth s ∈ R1×H×W .

337   the cross-section, and the prediction loss lossp , for the workpiece. The lossp is calculated twice,
338   once for the workpiece after loading and once after unloading. We use 3 optimizers to train different
339   parts of the network with corresponding   losses. For the reconstruction loss, we measure the Mean
                                      Pn
340   Square Error (MSE) lossr = n1 1 (s − sr )2 . The function lossp measures the discrepancy between
341   the characteristic lines. Due to imbalanced value distributions across different coordinate axes of
342   the 3D characteristic line, normalizing all axes equally could lead to incorrect shifts. To address
343   this, we emphasize axes with more significant orders of magnitude by employing a coordinated
344   L2 loss defined as: lossp = λx ∥∆x∥2 + λy ∥∆y∥2 + λz ∥∆z∥2 , where λx , λy and λz are weights
345
      corresponding to 3D axes, computed based on the data range along each axis. The terms ∆x, ∆y and
      ∆z denote the differences between the ground truth p̃a and p̃b and the prediction pa and pb along
346
      each respective axis.

523   4     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
524
525
      In this paper, we firstly attempt to apply deep learning into large deformations of elastic-plastic solids.
526
      We propose LaDEEP, a novel deep learning-based framework tailored for an industrial task, stretch
527
      bending. We design several modules to encode sequential property-aware tokens and propose a
528   two-stage, Transformer-based module, the Deformation Predictor (DP) to approximate the two-stage
529   solutions. We generate a dataset to support our approach and fill the data gap in this area. LaDEEP
530   achieves five magnitudes faster speed than FEM with a comparable accuracy, and gains 20.47%
531   relative improvement on average compared to other deep learning baselines.
532
      Limitation. We are well aware that there exists a limitation of the application scenarios of LaDEEP.
533
      However, due to the general insights of exist models, directly applying them into the reality-related
534   complex large deformations of the elastic-plastic solids without problem-specific property would be
535   hard to capture the correlations among solids accurately. Hence, we take the industrial technique,
536   stretch bending, as a good start point to explore the problem properties and incorporate them into
537   the models to learn effective inductive biases. More complex problems and general solvers would
538   be studied in future works. We believe that the framework of LaDEEP is a meaningful modeling
539   approach for solids involving large elastic-plastic deformations and contacts, and has great potential
      of being adapted to other complex industrial applications.

705   A.1   L A DEEP F RAMEWORK
706
707   The detailed overview of LaDEEP framework is illustrated in Figure 10. The whole framework
708
      contains three main components.
709   Data Preprocess. In this process, we need to process the data to represent the assembly model
710   without redundant information. For a given stretch-bending problem, the inputs include a 3D-shaped
711   workpiece, a 3D-shaped mold, and the motion parameters of the working arms. In practice, most
712   of the products are symmetric. For simplicity and without violating the physics, we only consider
713   the simulating the half of the system and keep the middle of the workpiece static. We use the
714
      combination of the cross-section and the characteristic line to represent the workpiece. The mold is
      only represented by the characteristic because its cross-section is related to the cross-section of the
715
      workpiece. The movement of the working arms is depicted by a vector with size of 6, representing 6
716
      degree-of-freedom motion.
717
718   Deep Learning-based Surrogate Model. The detailed structure of the model is depicted in Fig-
719   ure 11.Take the processed representations as inputs, the model use encoders (CLE, CSE, MPE) to
720
      extract the high-dimensional features. Then a fusioner (OFF) is utilized to fuse the two features
      that represent the cross-section and characteristic line of the workpiece, respectively. The feature of
721
      the cross-section is reconstructed by a decoder(CSD). Then the result is used to calculate the loss
722
      function lossr and optimize the part of the model (CSE and CSD). Subsequently, the Deformation
723   Predictor (DP) is composed of two-stage modules, loading and unloading. The outputs of the loading
724   module and unloading module are projected back to the original space through decoders. Then the
725   outputs are used to calculate the losses lossp to train corresponding modules. See Appendix A.2 for
726   detailed training strategy.

753   trace or sweep out volume (a solid) that may be represented by the moving set and its trajectory. More

755   the end. In the sweep process, the cross-section is perpendicular to the tangent of the characteristic

896
897   A.4.1       C LASSICAL FEM M ODELS
898
899   The considered problem involves complex large deformation of elastic-plastic solid, nonlinear solid
900   contacts, three-dimensional motion, and nonlinear rebound. Due to the complexity and computational
901   demands, developing a custom FEM solver from the ground up would be impractical and time-
902   consuming. Therefore, we utilize Abaqus (Khennane, 2013; Abaqus, 2011), a robust and widely
903
      adopted industrial simulation software based on FEM, to generate our data. Each assembly model
      is divided into around 20,000 elements. And as a comparable baseline, we use scale factors {0.8,
904
      0.6, 0.4, 0.2} to reduce the number of meshes in the highest resolution assembly models. During the
905
      loading stage, we employ the explicit dynamic algorithm (Barbero, 2023) for forward calculations
906   based on dynamic equations. For the unloading stage, we use the implicit static algorithm (Barbero,
907   2023), iteratively solving the problem with Newton’s method (Kelley, 2003).
908
909
      Since the explicit dynamic algorithm in Abaqus does not support GPU computation, we leverage a
      32-core CPU server for parallel processing using MPI (Walker & Dongarra, 1996). We also assess
910
      the impact of different core counts on computation speed and find that 32 cores nearly reach the
911
      maximum acceleration for our single assembly model. Increasing the core count further lead to
912   diminishing returns due to higher communication overhead. We recognize the importance of ensuring
913   a fair comparison between the FEM and Deep Learning models. Given the different nature of
914   parallelization (CPU vs. GPU) and the inherent computational requirements of the FEM method, we
915   take all reasonable steps within the constraints of our available tools and hardware to optimize the
916   FEM simulations. We believe these measures demonstrate a conscientious approach to achieving
917   a fair and balanced comparison, considering the specific capabilities and limitations of the FEM
      method within the industrial context of our study.

       A.8      T WO -L OOP M OLD D ESIGN PARADIGM
1205
1206   Errors in mold design process are mainly categorized into simulation error (SE), application error
1207   (AE) and production error (PE). The SE is the discrepancy between the result of simulation and the
1208   target shape, defined within the virtual space. However, there is often a gap between simulation
1209   results and their practical application due to various sources of factors arise at different stages of
1210   process. For example, mold production inherently involves certain tolerances which are introduced by
1211   the manufacturing machines. Installation tolerance arises when installing the mold onto the machine.
1212
       The motion parameters of working arms may exhibit zero-point drift, and the properties of workpiece
       change over time, among other factors. The AE is defined as the error generated when simulation
1213
       results are directly implemented in production, within 10mm. This gap is inevitable due to the
1214
       inherent complexities. Then on-site adjustments will be carried out. On-site adjustment methods
1215   include machining of the mold by tools directly, as well as fine-tuning the parameters of working
1216   arms. By employing these methods, the final PE will be kept below 1mm for production.
