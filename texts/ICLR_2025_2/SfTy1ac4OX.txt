009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                With the rapid development of generative models, detecting generated images to
012                prevent their malicious use has become a critical issue recently. Existing methods
013                frame this challenge as a binary image classification task. However, such methods
014                focus only on visual space, yielding trained detectors susceptible to overfitting
015                specific image patterns and incapable of generalizing to unseen models. In this
016                paper, we address this issue from a multi-modal perspective and find that fake
017                images exhibit more distinct discrepancies with corresponding captions compared
018
                   to real images. Upon this observation, we propose to leverage the Image-Text
                   Discrepancy (TIDY) in joint visual-language space for universal fake image detec-
019
                   tion. Specifically, we first measure the distance of the images and corresponding
020
                   captions in the latent spaces of CLIP, and then tune an MLP head to perform the
021                usual detection task. Since there usually exists local artifacts in fake images, we
022                further propose a global-to-local discrepancy scheme that first explores the discrep-
023                ancy on the whole image and then each semantic object described in the caption,
024                which can explore more fine-grained local semantic clues. Extensive experiments
025                demonstrate the superiority of our method against other state-of-the-art competitors
026                with impressive generalization and robustness on various recent generative models.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent years have witnessed the rapid development of generative models, such as generative adver-
032   sarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2018; 2019; Brock et al., 2018; Park
033   et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2017) and diffusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Nichol et al., 2021;
034
      Rombach et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022). These generative models enable users
      to create high-quality synthetic images at very low cost. However, this accessibility also presents
035
      a double-edged sword, as perpetrators can easily generate fake images for malicious use, such as
036
      using Deepfakes 1 to mislead the public, defame celebrities, and even fabricate evidence, leading to
037   severe social, privacy, and security concerns (Suwajanakorn et al., 2017; Devlin & Cheetham, 2023).
038   Therefore, developing general and effective fake image detectors has become a critical issue.
039
040
      A common approach to tackling the fake image detection problem is to frame it as a binary image
      classification task, discriminating between real and fake images. Typically, a dataset of real and
041
      fake images is used to train a binary classifier (Wang et al., 2020; 2023), but this approach often
042
      leads to overfitting on specific image patterns, limiting the model’s generalization capability. For
043   instance, some detectors rely on artifacts introduced by specific model architectures (Tan et al., 2024),
044   which constrains their effectiveness to those particular architectures. In contrast, universal detection
045   methods (Ojha et al., 2023) leverage the vision encoder of Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training
046   (CLIP)(Radford et al., 2021) to improve the generalization of visual representations through its
047   zero-shot abilities. However, these methods focus solely on visual cues, neglecting the language
048   component, which is a key driver of CLIP’s strong generalization performance.
049   Given these limitations, we pose the following question: Can we develop a universal fake image
050   detector that generalizes to all generated images, regardless of model architectures or hyperparame-
051   ters? While existing detectors rely solely on visual clues, we propose to tackle this challenge from
052   a multimodal perspective using pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs). We first investigate

      Fake image detection. With the rapid development of generative models, such as GAN (Goodfellow
101
      et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2018; 2019; Brock et al., 2018; Park et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2017) and
102   diffusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Nichol et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Gu et al.,
103   2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), a variety of detectors have been proposed to combat the malicious use of
104   AI-generated fake images. Some methods focus on the visual artifacts or traces left by generative
105   models in fake images, such as the noise residual (Yu et al., 2019), face boundaries (Li et al., 2020),
106   patch-level artifacts (Chai et al., 2020), compression traces (Agarwal & Farid, 2017) and frequency
107   clues (Qian et al., 2020). To train the classifier, other methods design specific representations or
      augmentations, such as (Wang et al., 2020) where pre- and post-processing with data augmentation

108
      are carefully designed to build a universal GAN detector. To detect diffusion-generated images,
109   DIRE (Wang et al., 2023) introduces reconstruction error. To boost generalization, recent methods
110   have exploited pretrained models , such as UniFD (Ojha et al., 2023) that utilizes a pre-trained
111   CLIP-ViT (Radford et al., 2021) model to learn the general image representation for detection.
112
      These methods, however, focus only on the difference on low-level visual image patterns, which may
113
      lead to limited generalization on unseen generative models. Although some existing methods tried
114
      to use the vision language models (VLMs), such as UniFD (Ojha et al., 2023), they also explored
115   only in visual space. Whereas, we find that there exists significant discrepancy between the images
116   and corresponding captions in a joint vision-language space at semantic level. Hence, we propose to
117   leverage the image-text discrepancy to achieve universal fake image detection.
118
      Visual-language models. Recent studies have demonstrated the great potential of vision-language
119
      models (VLMs) in learning general visual representation and aligning visual and text concepts Liu
120
      et al. (2024); Li et al. (2022a). The pre-trained VLMs have been proven to have impressive transferring
121   ability to a variety of downstream tasks (Radford et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021).
122   The CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021) could be a milestone of VLMs, as it employs transformer-
123   based architecture (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) with a contrastive pre-training strategy (Chen et al.,
124   2020) for both image and text representation learning. There are already some works (Ojha et al.,
125   2023; Cozzolino et al., 2023) that use pre-trained VLMs, such as CLIP, to learn image representation
126   for detection. These methods, however, use only the visual space of VLMs, which could still lead to
127   overfitting image patterns and cause insufficient learning without fully exploring VLMs’ multi-modal
128   potential. Whereas, we fully explore the multi-modal potential of VLMs by exploiting the distance
129
      between the images and corresponding captions in joint visual-language space at the semantic level,
      thus avoiding the overfitting of the visual-only image patterns and achieving improved generalization.

135   3.1   I MAGE -T EXT D ISCREPANCY R EPRESENTATION
136
      To exploit the discrepancy between image and text modalities, we first need to model the representa-
137
      tion of these two modalities in a given visual-language latent space. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) has
138
      been a milestone that optimizes an aligned vision-language space via contrastive learning. Hence, we
139   propose to exploit the joint vision-language space of CLIP to learn the representation of image and
140   text modality and explore their discrepancy.
141
142
      For a given image x and its corresponding caption prompt p, we feed the (x, p) into CLIP’s image
      and text encoder, respectively, to obtain the visual and language representation (I, T), which can be
143
      formulated as follows:
144
145                                           (I, T) = CLIP(x, p),                                        (1)
146
147   where we use CLIP:ViT-L/14 with 768 output dimensions as our joint visual-language space.
148   Then we design a distance D to measure the discrepancy of (I, T) in the joint latent space. As the
149   pre-training objective of CLIP is the cosine similarity between two modalities, we propose to use
150   the subtraction of the two representations as their distance. The reason behind this design is that the
151   subtraction of two representations is coherent with CLIP’s objective, the cosine similarity, and our
152   designed distance could provide higher dimensional information in latent space than one similarity
153   score, such as direction, which should also contain informative clues for measuring discrepancy. We
154   can formulate our image-text discrepancy representation as:
155
156                                               D = |I     T|,                                          (2)
157
158
      where D is our designed distance to measure the discrepancy of image and text modalities. Based on
      our observation in Fig. 1 (b), the D for fake images should be higher than the real images.
159
160   Thus, for a given image x, we can measure its discrepancy distance D with the corresponding
161   caption by first using a caption model to generate its caption p. The distance serves as a clue for
      discriminating between fake and real images.

181   3.2    G LOBAL - TO -L OCAL D ISCREPANCY S CHEME
182
183   We have formulated the discrepancy between a given image x and the corresponding caption prompt
184   p in a joint CLIP latent space. The discrepancy with the caption is mainly focused on the information
185   of the whole image. This discrepancy, which we term a global discrepancy distance, could serve as
186   a clue for discrimination. However, it ignores some more detailed fine-grained clues that can also
187
      contribute to detection. As shown in Fig. 1 (a), the forgery artifacts in fake images usually exist in
      local areas. The performance could be further boosted if we could explore more local fine-grained
188
      semantic clues. To this end, we further introduce a global-to-local discrepancy scheme to explore
189
      more fine-grained local semantic forgery clues, as illustrated in Fig. 2 and described as follows.
190
191   First, for a given image x and its corresponding caption p, we denote the corresponding CLIP
192   representation as (I0 , T0 ), and we define the discrepancy between the whole image and the full
193
      caption as global distance Dg , formulated as:
194                                                              Dg = |I0                      T0 |,                                                           (3)
195
196
      where (I0 , T0 ) = CLIP(x, p). To further explore the fine-grained local semantic details, we focus
      on each semantic object described in the original full caption. We denote each semantic object
197
      with corresponding texts as {p1 , p2 , · · · , pn }, and we employ a pre-trained object detection model
198
      to detect each corresponding semantic object in the original image as {x1 , x2 , · · · , xn } with their
199   grounding boxes, as shown in Fig. 2. Then, we compute the discrepancy distance of each local
200   semantic object formulated as:
201
202                                                    Dil = |Ii            Ti |,          i = 1, · · · , n                                                    (4)
203   where (Ii , Ti ) = CLIP(xi , pi ) and the Dil is the local distance of ith semantic object. Then, we
204   simply average all the local semantic objects as the final local distance:
205

231   Dataset. Following recent works (Wang et al., 2020; Ojha et al., 2023), we first use the images
232   generated by following models to evaluate our method, including seven different GANs: (1) Pro-
233
      GAN (Karras et al., 2018), (2) CycleGAN (Zhu et al., 2017), (3) BigGAN (Brock et al., 2018), (4)
      StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019), (5) StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020), (6) GauGAN (Park et al., 2019),
234
      and (7) StarGAN (Choi et al., 2018). We also follow four different diffusion models with various
235
      settings: (8) ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), (9) LDM (Rombach et al., 2022), (10) Glide (Nichol
236   et al., 2021), (11) DALLE (Ramesh et al., 2021), and one high-quality (12) deepfakes method2 . To
237   validate the performance on more recent and challenging generative models, we evaluate on recent
238   DiffusionForensics (Wang et al., 2023) and GenImage (Zhu et al., 2024) dataset. As there exists an
239   overlap between the two datasets, we choose ADM, Glide, and (13) VQDM (Gu et al., 2022) from
240   GanImage, and (14) Stable-Diffusion-v1 (Rombach et al., 2022), (15) Stable-Diffusion-v2 (Rombach
241   et al., 2022), LDM, (16) DALLE-2, (17) Midjourney, (18) ProjGAN (Sauer et al., 2021), StyleGAN,
242   (19) Diff-ProjGAN (Wang et al., 2022), and 20) Diff-StyleGAN (Song et al., 2024) from Diffusion-
243   Forensics dataset. Following prior works, we train our model and other baselines on the images
244
      generated by ProGAN from (Wang et al., 2020). To demonstrate our method does not highly rely on
      large-scale training data, we only use a subset that contains 4,0000 fake and real images, respectively.
245
246   Evaluation metric. Following prior state-of-the-art detectors (Wang et al., 2020; 2023; Ojha et al.,
247   2023), we report accuracy (ACC) with a fixed 0.5 threshold and an average precision (AP) to evaluate
248   our method and other baseline detectors.
249   Baselines. We compare our method with the following state-of-the-art baseline detectors: 1) ResNet-
250   50 (He et al., 2016) with binary cross-entropy loss is a widely used backbone for image classification
251   task. 2) Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) has a hierarchical transformer with shifted windows
252   for downstream vision tasks. We use Swin-B/224⇥224 as our baseline. 3) Patchforensics (Chai
253   et al., 2020) proposes a patch-wise classifier for detection at patch-level. 4) F3Net (Qian et al., 2020)
254   proposes a two-stream network to mine two complementary frequency-aware clues. 5) DIRE (Wang
255   et al., 2023) introduces a reconstruction error representation between the original and diffusion-
256
      reconstructed image to train the classifier. 6) CNNDet (Wang et al., 2020) carefully designs pre- and
      post-preprocessing and data augmentation to detect CNN-generated images. We use Blur+JPEG (0.1)
257
      setting as our baseline. 7) UniFD (Ojha et al., 2023) uses CLIP to extract only the image embeddings
258
      with the nearest neighbor as classification head. 8) NPR (Tan et al., 2024) explores the artifacts
259   left by up-sampling layers in GAN and diffusion models to serve as discriminative clues. For a fair
260   comparison, we use the same CLIP:ViT-L/14 for UniFD and our method. We train the aforementioned
261   baselines from scratch with their released code using the same training set as ours. We categorize them
262   into traditional image-classification backbones (ResNet-50 and Swin-T), deepfake detectors (Patchfor
263   and F3Net), diffusion-generated image detectors (DIRE), and universal detectors (CNNDet, uniFD
264   and NPR). Note that all the above baselines are visual-only detectors.
265   Implementation details. We use the pre-trained CLIP:ViT-L/14 to map the images and text prompts
266   into 768 dimensions embeddings. The input images are center-cropped into 224 ⇥ 224, before being
267   fed into CLIP. A simple fully-connected MLP is employed as our classification head, with an input
268   dimension of 768 and an output dimension of 2, mapping the visual-language CLIP representation

308   unknown generative models, which is a critical challenge in this field. We train all the detectors

503   5   C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this paper, we find that fake images exhibit significant discrepancies between images and corre-
506   sponding captions compared to real images in joint visual-language space. Upon this observation, we
507   reframe the fake image detection from a multimodal image-text perspective and propose TIDY to
508   achieve universal fake image detection. Specifically, we first measure the distance between images
509   and corresponding captions in a joint visual-language space of pre-trained CLIP and then tune an
510   MLP head for detection. Considering the semantic divergence of local and global patches, and the
511   artifacts in local patches of fake images, we further introduce a global-to-local discrepancy scheme to
512
      mine more fine-grained local semantic clues. Specifically, we propose to explore the discrepancy on
      the whole image and each semantic object described in the caption. Extensive experiments demon-
513
      strate our method’s superiority against other state-of-the-art competitors in detecting various fake
514
      images with impressive generalization and robustness. We hope our method could provide insight on
515   how to formulate the AI-generated image detection task from a multi-modal perspective and also
516   foundations on how to leverage large pre-trained models to detect AI-generated content (AIGC) for
517   future research. In the future, we aim to extend our idea and method to other AIGC detection tasks
518   and facilitate the development of AIGC safety.
