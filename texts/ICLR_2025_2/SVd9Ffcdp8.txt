009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Revenue-optimal auction design is a challenging problem with significant the-
012            oretical and practical implications. Sequential auction mechanisms, known for
013            their simplicity and strong strategyproofness guarantees, are often limited by the-
014            oretical results that are largely existential, except for certain restrictive settings.
015            Although traditional reinforcement learning methods such as Proximal Policy Op-
016            timization (PPO) and Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) are applicable in this domain, they
017            struggle with computational demands and convergence issues when dealing with
018
               large and continuous action spaces. In light of this and recognizing that we can
               model transitions differentiable for our settings, we propose using a new rein-
019
               forcement learning framework tailored for sequential combinatorial auctions that
020
               leverages first-order gradients. Our extensive evaluations show that our approach
021            achieves significant improvement in revenue over both analytical baselines and
022            standard reinforcement learning algorithms. Furthermore, we scale our approach
023            to scenarios involving up to 50 agents and 50 items, demonstrating its applica-
024            bility in complex, real-world auction settings. As such, this work advances the
025            computational tools available for auction design and contributes to bridging the
026            gap between theoretical results and practical implementations in sequential auc-
027            tion design.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The effective allocation of scarce resources is a pervasive challenge across diverse domains, span-
033
      ning spectrum licensing (FCC), transportation infrastructure (Rassenti et al., 1982), online advertis-
      ing (Varian & Harris, 2014), and resource management (Tan et al., 2020). Combinatorial auctions
034
      (CAs) are a pivotal tool in addressing this challenge, offering a specialized auction format where
035
      bidders express valuations for combinations of items (or bundles). This allows for the incorporation
036   of interdependencies among items, ultimately leading to more efficient allocations. For example,
037   in spectrum auctions, bidders articulate preferences for combinations of licenses, capturing syner-
038   gies and complementarities. However, despite their potential, CAs are known for their significant
039   complexity, including computationally intensive winner determination problems and susceptibility
040   to strategic bidding behavior (de Vries & Vohra, 2003).
041
      Sequential Combinatorial Auctions (SCAs) make use of a sequential interaction with bidders тАФ par-
042   ticipants enter the auction in a predetermined order, strategically placing bids on available bundles
043   that align with their interests. The sequential nature of SCAs yields several advantages. Primar-
044   ily, it alleviates complexity by breaking down the problem into smaller, more manageable sub-
045   problems. Additionally, SCAs can be implemented as straightforward mechanisms with obvious
046   strategyproofness guarantees (Li, 2017). The predetermined order of bidder arrivals plays a crucial
047   role in ensuring no incentive for misreporting preferences, particularly when each stageтАЩs auction
048   mechanism is designed to be strategyproof. Maintaining strategyproofness at each stage involves
049   presenting bidders with a menu of options, allowing them to select their utility-maximizing choice.
050
      Such menu-based mechanisms enhance transparency and interpretability, simplifying the decision
      complexity for bidders who may not be experts in mechanism design.
051
052   Beyond the theoretical and practical advantages mentioned earlier, SCAs exhibit a surprising robust-
053   ness in terms of generality. Cai & Zhao (2017) has demonstrated that there exists a straightforward
      SCA capable of achieving a constant approximation to optimal auctions when biddersтАЩ valuations

054
      are XOS and O(log m)-approximation in sub-additive setting. Despite this promising result, finding
055   an optimal SCA mechanism remains an intricate task, primarily due to the vastness of its search
056   space. Existing results, such as those by Cai et al. (2022), primarily focus on constructing a sim-
057   ple mechanism that achieves constant-approximation in a restricted XOS setting. In contrast, our
058   research presents a general method for approximately identifying near-optimal mechanisms within
059   a broader subadditive valuation settings.
060
      Brero et al. (2020) introduced the use of reinforcement learning in auctions for the sequential setting,
061   framing the design problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)тАФfor example with the history of
062   decisions so far constituting the state, and posted prices and which agent to visit next constituting the
063   action. The reward is the payment collected from the bidder at each step. The overarching objective
064   is to learn an optimal policy function that maps states to actions (the тАЬmechanism,тАЭ) maximizing
065   the expected reward. To learn this policy function, Brero et al. (2020) use the Proximal Policy
066   Optimization (PPO) algorithm. However, their focus is on a class of simple mechanisms known as
067   Sequential Pricing Mechanisms (SPMs) with a straightforward menu structure where each item is
068   associated with a posted price and the cost of a bundle is simply the sum of prices of its constituent
069
      items. In the realm of optimal multi-item auctions, bundling (beyond item-wise posted price) is a
      well-established necessity, even for simpler additive valuations. This calls for approaches that center
070
      on learning a more expressive menu structure that is capable of accommodating complex valuations.
071
      Consequently, in the MDP formulation, the action space needs to expand, posing challenges for
072   directly applying standard RL methodologies to our setting.
073

075
      Main Challenges. Most conventional reinforcement learning techniques are ill-suited for the large
      and continuous action spaces in this combinatorial auction setting. For instance, Q-learning based
076
      approaches are tailored for discrete action spaces. While one workaround involves discretizing the
077
      action space, this strategy proves impractical as it often involves evaluating all possible actions,
078   which doesnтАЩt scale efficiently.
079
080   Although Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), Soft Actor-Critic
081
      (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) (Lillicrap et al.,
      2016) can handle continuous action spaces, they often struggle with sample efficiency and conver-
082
      gence issues due to the curse of dimensionality that arise in extremely large action spaces. Policy
083
      gradient methods such as PPO suffer from high variance in gradient estimates, leading to conver-
084   gence issues whereas Actor-Critic methods such as SAC, which involve learning a Q-function over
085   both state and action spaces, require a prohibitively large number of samples to adequately cover
086   their domain and generalize well. For these approaches to work, we would need extensive paral-
087   lel environments (for samples) or extended algorithm runtime (for convergence), both of which are
088   constrained by either hardware limitations or time constraints.
089
      However, unlike traditional RL problems, the transition dynamics in sequential auctions can be
090   accurately modeled, enabling the use of analytical gradients for more precise parameter updates.
091   Such approaches involving analytical policy gradients (APG) have demonstrated high efficiency
092   in domains such as differentiable physics and robotics (de Avila Belbute-Peres et al., 2018; Innes
093   et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2019; Qiao et al., 2020; Wiedemann et al., 2023). However, existing APG
094   methods usually require differentiable environments and rely on end-to-end training techniques such
095   as backpropagation through time (BPTT), with gradients given by the simulator itself. This is not
096   feasible in our framework, as the gradients are not readily available. Moreover, BPTT is prone
097   to optimization challenges over long trajectories, such as exploding or vanishing gradients, further
098
      complicating its applicability.
099   Given these limitations, a new approach is needed to handle the challenges of continuous action
100   spaces efficiently in combinatorial auctions. Our goal is to design an iterative method that leverages
101   analytical gradients to overcome sample inefficiency, high variance and the computational complex-
102   ities of existing methods.
103
104   Our Contributions. We introduce an new approach that uses fitted policy iteration (Bertsekas &
105   Tsitsiklis, 1995; Bertsekas, 2011) and analytical gradients for learning revenue-maximizing sequen-
106   tial combinatorial auctions. This method uses neural networks to approximate the value function and
107   policy function and iteratively update them in a twofold manner: initially refining the value func-
      tion to align with the policy function and subsequently adjusting the policy function to maximize

      rewards. While continuous action spaces pose challenges for the policy improvement step, we show,
109   through Proposition 2, how we can simplify this to learning revenue-optimal single-buyer auctions
110   via gradient descent.

112
      We implement the policy improvement step by extending the neural network architectures for static
      revenue optimal auction design, such as RochetNet (Du╠Иtting et al., 2023) or MenuNet (Shen et al.,
113
      2019), to the sequential setting by modifying the menu structure to include an additional term called
114
      the тАЭoffsetтАЭ. The offset captures the value of potential future states (тАЭcontinuation valueтАЭ). By incor-
115   porating the offset into the optimization objective, our approach aims to maximize both the current
116   revenue and the anticipated revenue from future states. This adapted network, trained through first-
117   order gradient methods, offers a more effective and stable approach to policy iteration in continuous
118   action spaces. Instead of parameterizing and learning menu options for each state and bidder, we
119   learn the weights of a neural network that takes in as input a state and outputs the corresponding
120   menu options. This letтАЩs us handle combinatorial auctions with up to 20 agents and a menu size of
121   up to 1024.
122   Furthermore, we demonstrate the scalability of our approach to accommodate a large number of
123   buyers and items, extending to as many as 50 buyers and 50 items for the additive-valuation setting,
124   significantly surpassing the capabilities of existing methods based on differentiable economics for
125   auction design. This scalability is achieved by learning the menus corresponding to the entry-fee
126   mechanisms. While this is less expressive than a combinatorial menu structure, it is more computa-
127   tionally efficient, making it a viable and efficient solution for scenarios involving a larger number of
128   buyers and items.

      Related Works. Our work is closely related with the literature of sequential combinatorial auc-
131
      tions design (Cai & Zhao, 2017; Cai et al., 2022), in which the previous papers focus on the the-
132

137   to achieve constant approximation in the item-independent XOS valuation setting. Compared to

139   mat (e.g., we allow bundle pricing rather than item pricing) through the use of deep learning based
140   approaches. In addition, there is a rich literature designing approximation-results for online combi-
141
      natorial auctions using simple posted-price mechanism (through Prophet Inequality), in which the
      items arrive in a sequential manner (Feldman et al., 2015; Du╠Иtting et al., 2020; Assadi & Singla,
142
      2019; Deng et al., 2021). Another loosely related research direction is dynamic mechanism de-
143
      sign (Ashlagi et al., 2016; Papadimitriou et al., 2022; Mirrokni et al., 2020), where the previous
144   papers focus on mechanism design problem dealing with forward-looking agents that the agents
145   may deviate their truthful reporting at current rounds to get more utility in the long-run.

515   5 C ONCLUSION
516   We have introduced a new methodological approach to the problem of learning simple and strate-
517   gyproof mechanisms for sequential combinatorial auctions. We formulate this as a reinforcement
518   learning problem and show how we can use first order gradients to learn menu options that maximize
519
      expected revenue. Through extensive experimental results, weтАЩve shown the superior performance
      of this approach compared to other RL methods and well-known analytical solutions.
520
521   We also point out some limitations and potential directions for future work. The use of neural net-
522   works in approximate methods introduces uncertainties, lacking theoretical guarantees for conver-
523   gence. Additionally, the optimality of solutions obtained by RochetNet in the policy improvement
524   step remains unknown. Despite these uncertainties, we empirically observe convergence in all our
525
      experiments, and it has been shown that RochetNet consistently retrieves optimal solutions when
      analytical solutions are available (Du╠Иtting et al., 2023). Another limitation lies in our dependence on
526
      the assumption of having ample samples from valuation distributions. While this is a common prac-
527
      tice in empirical approaches to mechanism design, it would be insightful to explore the effectiveness
528   of our approach when the number of available samples is limited.
529
530   Future work could also explore more intricate menu structures beyond entry-fee mechanisms, con-
531
      tinuing to seek computational efficiency improvements over bundle price enumeration. Addition-
      ally, there is a compelling question of designing mechanisms where we allow agents to select the
532
      best bundle efficiently, potentially in poly(m) time, as suggested by previous research (Schapira &
533
      Singer, 2008). Our current research focused on a fixed order of agent visits, which prompts the ex-
534   ploration of methods to dynamically learn the optimal order in which agents should be visited (Brero
535   et al., 2021). Extending our framework to accommodate non-deterministic allocation poses an in-
536   triguing challenge, and understanding how it can be implemented in the sequential setting needs
537   further attention. Lastly, it is interesting to assess the ability of our approaches to approximate the
538   non-sequential version of the auction problem. Innovations in this space could involve leveraging
539   AMA-based approaches instead of RochetNet, which would engage with several agents in each step
      of a sequential auction instead of just one agent.

732
733   We use the stable-baselines3 (Raffin et al., 2021) package to implement our baselines.
734
735   Actor Critic Networks. For all our neural networks, we use a simple fully connected neural net-
736   work with Tanh activation functions except for the last layer. We use R = 3 hidden layers with
737   k = 256 hidden units each.
738   For the actor network in PPO, we use sigmoid activation functions to squash the output to [0, 1]
739   range. The action distribution is a normal distribution with these sigmoid outputs as means. It is
740   then scaled appropriately. For example, consider setting A with n agents and m items. The action
741   space, which comprises of bundle prices, is of size 2m . The maximum possible valuation for a
742   subset T would simply be |T |. Consequently, we scale the output corresponding to the price of T
743   by |T |. We found this this approach performed better than using the SquashedDiagonalGaussian
744
      distribution where the noise is added before squashing the outputs using a tanh function. For the
      actor network in our approach, we only use a softplus activation to ensure the outputs are positive
745
      and leave them unscaled.
746
747   We make similar modifications for our large-scale settings involving entry-fee menu structure. For
748   these settings, we use sigmoid functions for the posted price and a softplus function for the entry-fee
749   for the actor networks in PPO as well as our approach.
750   We also found the using an offset before using sigmoid or softplus functions helped with training.
751   This ensures that the prices start low but increase gradually. We offset sigmoid function by 0.5 and
