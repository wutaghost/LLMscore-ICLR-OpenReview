011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid advancement of scientific progress requires innovative tools that can ac-
014            celerate discovery. While recent AI methods, particularly large language models
015            (LLMs), have shown promise in tasks such as hypothesis generation and experi-
016
               mental design, they fall short in replicating the collaborative nature of real-world
               scientific practices, where diverse teams of experts work together to tackle com-
017
               plex problems. To address the limitation, we propose an LLM-based multi-agent
018
               system, i.e., Virtual Scientists (V IR S CI), designed to mimic the teamwork inherent
019            in scientific research. V IR S CI organizes a team of agents to collaboratively gen-
020            erate, evaluate, and refine research ideas. Through comprehensive experiments,
021            we demonstrate that this multi-agent approach outperforms the state-of-the-art
022            method in producing novel and impactful scientific ideas, showing potential in
023            aligning with key insights in the Science of Science field. Our findings suggest
024            that integrating collaborative agents can lead to more innovative scientific outputs,
025            offering a robust system for autonomous scientific discovery.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The rapid acceleration of scientific progress necessitates more efficient and innovative tools for
031   exploring new concepts and tackling complex challenges (Park et al., 2023b). The concept of auto-
032   matic scientific discovery has emerged as a promising solution to expedite innovation, representing a
033
      long-standing ultimate goal within the scientific community (Langley, 1987). With the development
      of artificial intelligence (AI), automatic scientific discovery has witnessed the potential to revolu-
034
      tionize how research is conducted by automating key steps in the scientific process, ranging from
035
      hypothesis generation to experimental design (Raghu & Schmidt, 2020; Spangler et al., 2014).
036
037   More recently, foundational models, especially the large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023;
038   Dubey et al., 2024), have shown significant progress in general capabilities, facilitating their ap-
039
      plications in various stages of scientific discovery (Abramson et al., 2024; Chang & Ye, 2024),
      including literature reviews (Hsu et al., 2024), experimental designs (Huang et al., 2024), etc. A
040
      notable development is the AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024), which introduces a scalable system for
041
      end-to-end scientific paper generation, highlighting the potential of LLMs to drive autonomous sci-
042   entific discovery. Despite its capabilities, AI Scientist operates with ONE agent, which falls short of
043   replicating real-world scientific practices, where research is often conducted through collaborative
044   efforts involving diverse teams of experts (Kayacik et al., 2019).
045
      To address the limitations of a single executive system, such as the AI Scientist, and to better repli-
046
      cate the collaborative nature of real-world scientific discovery (Gauch, 2003), we focus on the idea
047
      generation phase in the research process, which demonstrates more collaborative aspects (Linsey
048   et al., 2005). From this end, we propose an LLM-based multi-agent system, Virtual Scientists
049   (V IR S CI), designed to harness the potential of LLM agents in assisting autonomous scientific idea
050   generation. Leveraging the inherent human-like reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Xie et al., 2024),
051   V IR S CI simulates the collaborative process of research idea generation, which is divided into five
052   steps (see Fig. 1): (1) Collaborator Selection, (2) Topic Selection, (3) Idea Generation, (4) Idea
053   Novelty Assessment, and (5) Abstract Generation. To be more specific, we construct a knowledge
      bank of the background of scientists of interest and develop digital twin (Tao et al., 2018) agents

      2.1   AI FOR S CIENTIFIC D ISCOVERY
111
112   In recent years, AI has fundamentally reshaped the landscape of scientific discovery by providing
113   powerful tools that enhance various research processes (Xu et al., 2021). AI techniques, especially
114   generative AI, can facilitate basic scientific discoveries, such as identifying complex molecular (Vi-
115   gnac et al., 2022) and protein structures (Abramson et al., 2024), drastically reducing the time re-
116   quired for experimental iterations. These advancements have found wide application across diverse
117
      fields such as chemistry (Liu et al., 2023a), meteorology (Bi et al., 2023), and medicine (Rajpurkar
      et al., 2022), etc. Besides, with the advent of LLMs, AI methodologies can step further and col-
118
      laborate in streamlining critical stages of the scientific pipeline, including hypothesis generation,
119
      experimental design, data acquisition, and analysis(Zheng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Miret &
120   Krishnan, 2024; Wysocki et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). Nevertheless, these approaches lack the
121   collaborative nature of the scientists intrinsic to real-world research. V IR S CI is the first to har-
122   ness the power of an LLM-based multi-agent system to facilitate the generation of research ideas in
123   autonomous scientific discovery.
124
125   2.2   M ULTI - AGENT S YSTEMS IN T EAM C OLLABORATION
126
127   A multi-agent system for team collaboration leverages autonomous agents to coordinate, commu-
128   nicate, and solve tasks within a shared environment, mimicking human teamwork dynamics (Dorri
129   et al., 2018). Traditional multi-agent systems typically involve semi-autonomous agents coordinat-
130
      ing through explicit protocols and structured messages to achieve common goals (Dunin-Keplicz
      & Verbrugge, 2011; Bakliwal et al., 2018). The advent of LLMs has revolutionized this landscape
131
      by enabling agents to utilize natural language for communication and collaboration in a believ-
132
      able proxy of human behavior (Park et al., 2023a), thereby fostering a more intuitive and flexible
133   interaction model. Recent studies have further verified the superior performance of LLM-based
134   multi-agent systems in various domains, such as programming, game playing, and complex rea-
135   soning tasks when compared to single-agent execution (Liu et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024; Light
136   et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024). In this work, we strategically implement the power of LLM-based
137   multi-agent systems to function as collaborative scientists, promoting de novo scientific ideas.

      3     T HE V IRTUAL S CIENTISTS
140
141
      In this paper, we aim to build a multi-agent system using a real-world academic dataset to simulate
142   how a scientist assembles a research team and collaboratively generates an abstract that details a
143   novel scientific idea. Our V IR S CI system consists of two components: a scientific research ecosys-
144   tem and a multi-agent system for scientific idea generation.
145
146   3.1   T HE S CIENTIFIC R ESEARCH E COSYSTEM
147
148   The scientific research ecosystem comprises two main components: paper information and corre-
149   sponding author information ranging from year ystart to yend . First, we select a year ybound as a
150   time point and split the papers into two subsets: past papers Bpast and contemporary papers Bcon .
151   We further extract authors from Bpast to form the complete set of scientists S, with each scientist’s
152
      background information stored in the author knowledge bank, and the adjacency matrix A, which
      represents the collaboration counts between scientists.
153
154   Past Paper Database To construct the past paper database Bpast using the Faiss, we selected papers
155   published before the ybound . Each paper includes essential information such as its title, citation
156   count, and abstract.
157   Contemporary Paper Database The contemporary paper database Bcon , also constructed with
158   Faiss, consists of papers published after ybound . Similarly, each paper’s basic information is struc-
159   tured in the same way as the past papers. Although using papers from this time range may raise
160   concerns about data leakage, given that LLMs are trained on data within this period, we will explain
161   in detail why this does not pose a threat to the overall validity of our experiments and conclusions
      in the Appx. C.

181   Author Knowledge Bank For each scientist in S, we extract their basic profile from the computer
182   science dataset, which includes their name, affiliations, citation count, research interests, and collab-
183   oration history. Using the KnowledgeBank module from AgentScope(Gao et al., 2024), we embed
184   these scientist profiles into the author knowledge bank. This allows agents to quickly access and
185   familiarize themselves with other initialized agents’ information. Notably, real author names are
186   masked to prevent data leakage and privacy problems during agent initialization (See Appx. B).
187   Adjacency Matrix Given the scientist set S, let A represent the adjacency matrix, where Ai,j de-
188   notes the number of times scientist i has collaborated with scientist j. To prevent agents from always
189   choosing previously collaborated scientists, overlooking the benefit of fresh collaborations that often
190   lead to more original and impactful research (Zeng et al., 2021), we increment all values in A by
191   1. This adjustment ensures that scientists with no prior collaborations still have a chance of being
192   selected, encouraging agents to explore new partnerships.

194   3.2   T HE M ULTI - AGENT S YSTEM FOR S CIENTIFIC C OLLABORATION

196   We first randomly sample a scientist s0 from S as the team leader. The team leader then follows these
197   steps to produce an abstract: (1) Collaborator Selection, (2) Topic Discussion, (3) Idea Generation,

199
      with the backgrounds of other team members without overloading the initialization prompt, we
      employ retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), used throughout all five steps.
200
      All necessary prompts and example scenarios are shown in Appx. F and G.

203   team of scientists, T = {s0 , . . . , si , . . . , sn }, where n denotes the team size. When s0 is selecting
204   collaborators, we convert the adjacency matrix, A, into a probability distribution using the following

214   gage in discussions based on a specific task description prompt. This process is applied not only to
215   topic discussion but also to subsequent collaboration steps. While allowing agents to decide when

      Dataset We build our scientific research ecosystem using real scientists’ information from the
295
      AMiner Computer Science Dataset 1 , which was constructed by extracting scientists’ profiles from
296
      online web databases (Tang et al., 2008). This dataset includes 1,712,433 authors and 2,092,356
297   papers, covering the period from 1948 to 2014, with disambiguated author names. To manage the
298   large volume of data, we set ystart , ybound , and yend to 2000, 2010, and 2014, respectively. For
299   quality assurance, we filtered out past papers lacking abstracts or with fewer than 10 citations, con-
300   temporary papers with fewer than 5 citations or missing abstracts, and authors with fewer than 50
301   papers or 50 co-authors. As a result, we extracted detailed information from 156 authors and 85,217
302   papers to construct the ecosystem and initialize the corresponding agents for the simulation. All
303   paper and author data are embedded using the “mxbai-embed-large” model (Lee et al., 2024).
304   Implementation We implement our system on top of the Agentscope framework (Gao et al., 2024),
305   which serves for LLM-empowered multi-agent applications. We evaluate our system using different
306   publicly available LLMs: GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2023) and Llama-3.1 (8b and 70b) (Dubey et al., 2024).
307   GPT-4o is accessible exclusively via a public API, while the Llama-3.1 models are open-weight and
308   invoked using the Ollama (Ollama, 2024) in our experiments. Each experimental run on Llama-3.1
309   (8b) takes approximately 10 minutes on 1 NVIDIA A100 40G GPU within a team discussion setting
310   of 4 members and 5 turns (K = 5). All experimental results are averaged on 20 runs.
311   Evaluation Metrics Since no single evaluation metric perfectly captures the novelty of scientific
312   outputs, we employ three common metrics that align with our intuition: (1) Historical Dissimilarity
313   (HD): The average Euclidean distance between the generated abstract embedding and embeddings of
314   the 5 most similar abstracts in Bpast (Shao et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2024). A larger distance indicates
315   that the generated abstract is more dissimilar from existing papers, suggesting a higher likelihood
316   of novelty. (2) Contemporary Dissimilarity (CD): The average Euclidean distance between the
317
      generated abstract embedding and embeddings of the top 5 most similar abstracts in Bcon . A smaller
      distance indicates greater similarity to newer papers, also suggesting a higher likelihood of novelty.
318
      (3) Contemporary Impact (CI): The average citation count of the top 5 most similar abstracts
319
      in Bcon (Yang et al., 2022). A higher citation count suggests that the generated abstract is more
320   likely to have a higher impact. To ensure comparability, we normalize each calculated metric using
321   the mean value derived from the entire corresponding database, with normalization defined as the
322   metric divided by the mean value. Since novelty is difficult to measure directly, we introduce a
323

351   Adjusting Settings for Fair Comparison with AI Scientist Given that the problem formulation of
352   the AI Scientist differs from ours, we must make several justifications to ensure relative fairness in
353   our comparisons: (1) Since the AI Scientist is limited to generating ideas from its pre-defined topics
354   (2D Diffusion, NanoGPT, and Grokking), we include NanoGPT in the topic selection prompt for
355   V IR S CI as the initial discussion topic, ensuring that the final abstracts align with the same research
356   direction. (2) Since our teamwork approach differs from the AI Scientist’s solitary investigation,
357   we need to ensure that the comparisons are made under the same inference cost. The AI Scientist
358   performs 50 turns of self-reflection during its idea generation, which does not apply to its paper
359
      generation. To align the inference costs, we set the number of team members to 4 and the number of
      discussion turns to 5, ensuring the experiments are conducted under approximately the same com-
360
      putational cost. (3) Since the AI Scientist lacks a scientific research ecosystem, it retrieves papers
361
      across all time ranges through the Semantic Scholar API (Fricke, 2018). To maintain consistency
362   with the AI Scientist, we replace our databases with the Semantic Scholar API for both paper re-
363   trieval and metric calculation. Specifically, after generating ideas and corresponding abstracts from
364   both the AI Scientist and our system, we use the generated ideas as queries to retrieve related papers,
365   extracting corresponding abstracts and citation counts for evaluation. (4) We evaluate the generated
366   abstracts using both our metrics (CD and CI) and the AI Scientist’s metric (LLM review score) (Lu
367   et al., 2024). The LLM review score is calculated by GPT-4o which conducts abstract reviews based
368   on a truncated version of the Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS) conference review
369   guidelines2 , shown in Fig. 17.

401   Effects of Team Size on Novelty The results shown in Fig. 3 indicate that increasing the number of
402   team members can enhance the novelty score of the generated abstracts. By adding new team mem-
403   bers, a broader range of ideas and perspectives can be facilitated, leading to more creative solutions
404
      and innovative outputs. However, this relationship is not strictly linear; our findings suggest that the
      peak novelty occurs with a team size of 8 members. While a moderate increase in team size may
405
      boost novelty, excessively large teams can introduce coordination challenges and communication
406
      barriers. These issues may dilute individual contributions and foster groupthink, where the focus
407   shifts from original ideas to achieving consensus. Thus, there appears to be an optimal team size
408   that maximizes creativity without overwhelming the collaborative process. This conclusion aligns
409   with existing literature, which suggests that while smaller teams tend to disrupt science and tech-
410   nology with new ideas and opportunities, larger teams often concentrate on refining and developing
411   existing concepts (Wuchty et al., 2007; Fortunato et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019).
412
      Effects of Discussion Turns on Novelty The number of discussion turns plays a crucial role in
413   enhancing the novelty score (Mezirow, 2003; Li et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024).
414   Our analysis (Fig. 3) indicates that an appropriate number of turns enables team members to explore
415   topics thoroughly, iterate on ideas, and refine their abstracts. This iterative process is essential for
416   deepening understanding and producing more sophisticated research outputs. While initial turns are
417   beneficial for generating ideas and facilitating discussions, an excessive number of turns can lead to
418   fatigue and reduced engagement. This may stifle creativity, as members may conform to dominant
419   ideas rather than proposing innovative thoughts. Therefore, balancing the number of discussion
420   turns is critical for maintaining high levels of novelty. Our findings suggest that peak novelty is
421
      achieved with a discussion turn count of 5.
422   Interaction Between Team Size and Discussion Turns Our findings further illustrate the inter-
423   action between team size and discussion turns. Larger teams with fewer turns can still produce
424   relatively higher novelty scores, while smaller teams with excessive discussion turns may strug-
425   gle to achieve the same level of innovation. This interplay emphasizes the importance of strategic
426   planning in collaborative settings. Optimizing both team size and the number of discussion turns
427
      can significantly enhance the likelihood of generating novel outputs, indicating that the design of
      collaborative processes should take these factors into account.

534   replicate the collaborative dynamics of scientific discovery. By addressing the limitations of tradi-

      A     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
757
758
      While our multi-agent system demonstrates notable improvements over the single-agent approach
759   in generating novel and impactful scientific ideas, it has several limitations. First, we have only vali-
760   dated our system on a single computer science dataset, which restricts the diversity of research ideas
761   and limits its capacity for simulating interdisciplinary collaborations. This focus on a single do-
762   main also reduces the generalizability of our results to other scientific fields. Additionally, while our
763   system effectively models collaboration, the simulated interactions may oversimplify the complexi-
764   ties of real-world teamwork, where multiple teams can collaborate dependently or independently on
765   related research, and agents often participate in different teams simultaneously.
766   To address these limitations, several future directions can be pursued. Expanding the system to
767   incorporate datasets from various scientific disciplines is a crucial next step. This would increase
768   the diversity of generated ideas and enable simulations of interdisciplinary collaborations, providing
769   a more realistic and holistic representation of real-world research environments. Another important
770   direction is to enhance the simulation of teamwork by allowing multiple teams to work concurrently
771   and enabling agents to contribute to multiple teams or projects simultaneously. Such improvements
772   would better reflect the collaborative dynamics of modern scientific research and provide a more
773
      powerful tool for the Science of Science community. This would allow researchers to probe deeper
      into the underlying processes of scientific collaboration, engaging with the dynamic and interactive
774
      nature of teamwork to gain insights into how collaboration fosters innovation.

777   B     E THICS S TATEMENT
778
779   This research uses publicly available data from the AMiner dataset, ensuring compliance with data
780   privacy policies. Author names are masked to prevent data leakage during simulations. Our system
781
      is intended to augment, not replace, human researchers, emphasizing the need for human oversight
      to ensure the quality and integrity of generated outputs. To promote transparency, we commit to
782
      sharing all relevant codes for reproducibility within the research community.

785   C     E FFECT OF THE P OTENTIAL DATA L EAKAGE
786
787   We acknowledge that the use of papers published between 2011 and 2014 may raise concerns about
788   data leakage, given that the LLMs employed in our experiments are trained on data within this
789
      time period. However, this potential issue does not pose a significant threat to the validity of our
      experiments for the following reasons. First, both the comparisons between our multi-agent system
790
      and AI Scientist, as well as the comparisons between different team settings, utilize the same LLMs.
791
      Since all models encounter the same exposure to training data from this period, any potential data
792   leakage would affect all experiments equally. Thus, the relative performance differences we observe
793   are not skewed by uneven data leakage. This ensures that the evaluation process remains fair and
794   that the corresponding conclusions drawn are valid. Moreover, our goal is not to demonstrate an
795   absolute measure of novelty but rather to explore how different collaboration strategies and team
796   settings influence the novelty of generated research outputs. As all team settings face the same
797   potential exposure to historical data, the novelty metrics still provide an accurate comparison of the
798   agents’ ability to generate distinct and original ideas under varying conditions. In summary, while
799   data leakage is a valid concern, it affects all models and settings uniformly in our experiments.
800
      Therefore, it does not undermine the relative comparisons we make or the conclusions we draw
      regarding collaboration strategies and team performance.

      A self-review mechanism is considered after Rabstract is finalized to pre-check its novelty. In this
807
      self-review, the optimized abstract Rabstract is provided to the team leader to assess novelty by
808
      comparing it to similar papers in Bpast . The prompt is:
809
                                Qreview = ⟨Qcheck , Rabstract , Bpast (Rabstract )⟩                        (7)

      If this is the first time undergoing the self-review and the team leader determines that the similarity
811   to existing papers is too high, the abstract will undergo further revision. The evaluation Rreview will
812   then be added to Eq. (6) for the next revision round:
813
814                 Q1,0 = ⟨Qabstract , Qjudgement , Rreview , Bpast (Rabstract ), Rabstract ⟩            (8)
815
816   If the abstract undergoes a second self-review and still does not meet the novelty requirement, it will
817   be discarded, and the team will generate a new idea. Once the self-review yields satisfactory results,
818   the final abstract will be produced, and the system will terminate. However, this self-review mech-
819   anism introduces uncertainty in total inference cost, making it difficult to ensure fair experimental
820   comparisons. We discuss the effectiveness of this module only in the ablation study (see Sec. 4.4).
