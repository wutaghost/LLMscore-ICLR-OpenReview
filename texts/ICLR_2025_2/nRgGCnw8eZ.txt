009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Excessive abstraction is a critical challenge in solving games with ordered sig-
012            nals—a subset of imperfect information games—that impairs AI performance.
013            This issue is caused by extreme implementations of imperfect recall, which dis-
014            card historical information. This paper presents KrwEmd, the first practical algo-
015            rithm to address this issue. We first introduce the k-recall winrate feature, which
016
               not only qualitatively distinguishes signal infosets by leveraging future and, more
               importantly, historical game information, but also quantitatively reflects their sim-
017
               ilarity. We then develop the KrwEmd algorithm, which clusters signal infosets
018
               using Earth Mover’s Distance to assess discrepancies between their features. Ex-
019            perimental results show that KrwEmd significantly enhances AI gameplay perfor-
020            mance compared to existing algorithms.

      in no-limit Texas Hold’em poker, a popular testbed for imperfect information games, even under

077   2     BACKGROUND AND N OTATION
078
079   Generally, Texas Hold’em-style poker games are modeled as imperfect-information games. How-
080   ever, for the task of hand abstraction, games with ordered signals (Gilpin & Sandholm, 2007b; Fu
081   et al., 2024) offer a better theoretical tool. The game with ordered signals is a subclass of imperfect-
082   information games, where the nodes (also referred to as histories, states, or trajectories) are further
083
      subdivided into two mutually independent parts: signals and public nodes. This allows for each
      aspect to be studied in isolation. Under this framework, the hand abstraction task in Texas Hold’em-
084
      style games is modeled as signal abstraction. Heads-up limit Texas Hold’em (HULHE) and heads-up
085
      no-limit Texas Hold’em (HUNL) are important AI testbeds. The rules for HULHE are provided in
086   the Appendix A, and the dealing rules for HUNL are the same as those in HULHE.

181   In general, two signal abstractions cannot be directly compared in terms of performance. However,

183   two abstractions αi and βi . If, for every ψ̂ ∈ Ψβi , there exists one or more abstracted signal infosets

      The concepts of perfect and imperfect recall are originally associated with imperfect-information
187
      games, indicating whether players are required to remember all the information encountered
188   throughout the game. Since games with ordered signals are a subset of imperfect-information games,
189   we extend the notion of signal perfect/imperfect recall to this framework. In a game G̃, a player
190   i ∈ Ñ+ is said to have signal perfect recall if, for any two signals θ1′ , θ2′ ∈ ψ ′ , every predecessor
191   θ1 of θ1′ corresponds to a predecessor θ2 of θ2′ such that θ2 ∈ ϑi (θ1 ) and θ1 ∈ ϑi (θ2 ). When all
192   players in the game G̃ have signal perfect recall, the game itself is said to have signal perfect recall.
193   In a game with signal perfect recall, denoted by G̃, let αi represent the signal abstraction for player
194   i ∈ N+ . The abstraction profile (αi , ϑ−i ) refers to a scenario in which player i employs the signal
195   abstraction αi , while the other players do not use any signal abstraction. If the game G̃ (αi ,ϑ−i ) re-
196   tains signal perfect recall, then αi is considered a signal abstraction with perfect recall; otherwise, it
197   is considered a signal abstraction with imperfect recall.

      3     R ELATED W ORK
201
      Our research focuses on hand abstraction techniques in AI systems for Texas Hold’em-style games
202
      (i.e. the signal abstraction in games with ordered signals), building on the foundational works of
203   Shi & Littman (2001) and Billings et al. (2003). These seminal studies introduced game abstraction
204   to simplify games while preserving key characteristics, initially relying on manual hand abstraction
205   by experts. The first automated hand abstraction was developed by Gilpin & Sandholm (2006), fol-
206   lowed by a more formal model for games with ordered signals in Texas Hold’em by Gilpin & Sand-
207   holm (2007b). They introduced the concept of lossless isomorphism (LI) through signal rotation.
208   Despite LI’s theoretical elegance, its low compression rates limited its use in large-scale games. In
209   contrast, lossy abstractions, which balance accuracy and scalability, showed greater potential. Meth-
210   ods such as the expectation-based clustering (Ehs) and the histogram-based potential-aware method
211
      were later introduced by Gilpin & Sandholm (2007a) and Gilpin et al. (2007). Studies by Gilpin &
      Sandholm (2008) and Johanson et al. (2013) later showed that the potential-aware method outper-
212
      formed Ehs in larger-scale games. In addition, Johanson et al. (2013) introduced the use of earth
213
      mover’s distance (EMD) in the potential-aware method, while Ganzfried & Sandholm (2014) pro-
214   posed a more efficient approximation algorithm, PaEmd, to further optimize this approach. This
215

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      methodology was further extended by Brown et al. (2015) to distributed environments, making
217   PaEmd the state-of-the-art solution for large-scale imperfect-information games.
218
219   Recently, Fu et al. (2024) proposed several novel tools, including abstraction resolution and com-
220
      mon refinement. They introduced two signal abstractions: potential outcome isomorphism (POI),
      which maximizes the number of abstracted signal infosets based on future information, and k-recall
221
      outcome isomorphism (KROI), which does so by incorporating historical information. They argued
222
      that current algorithms, which focus future considered only, tend to excessive abstraction, yet their
223   work did not provide a practical signal abstraction algorithm, leaving an open challenge.

      We begin by illustrating why historical information cannot be ignored in games with ordered signals.
229   Consider a player in HULHE with two distinct signal infosets: [9♢Q♡; Q♣9♢9♡; K♣; 4♢] and
230   [9♢Q♡; K♣4♢9♡; Q♣; 9♢], where [9♢Q♡] is the hole cards. Despite these two signal infosets
231   having the same hand strength, they differ in timing: in the first, the player forms a Full House by the
232   Flop, encouraging an optimistic early-game strategy. In the second, the player completes the hand
233   only by the River, leading to more cautious early play. A player’s strategy is observed by opponents,
234   influencing their decisions, and in turn, the opponent’s decisions are observed by the player, further
235   shaping the player’s strategy. This dynamic results in a completely different game scenario and
236   highlights that, despite the two infosets having equal strength by the River, their strategic differences
237
      prevent them from being grouped together. A player’s confidence in their current signal infoset
      guides their strategy, and winrate is a critical factor influencing this confidence.
238
239   We introduce isomorphism frameworks for winrate-based features, including potential winrate iso-
240   morphism (PWI) and k-recall winrate isomorphism (KRWI). These frameworks serve as signal ab-
241   stractions where the term isomorphism refers to the equivalence relations (reflexive, symmetric, and
242   transitive) satisfied by the abstracted signal infosets. In particular, this transitivity property helps de-
243
      termine whether two infosets belong to the same abstracted signal infoset. To avoid ambiguity, we
      refer to the abstracted signal infosets defined in these frameworks (POI, KROI, PWI, KRWI) as
244
      signal infoset equivalence classes. Two infosets are classified in the same equivalence class if they
245
      share an identical defined feature.
246
247   As the name suggests, a winrate-based feature is a set of data that reflects the strength of a signal
248   infoset by rolling it out to its subsequent terminal signals and comparing the players’ ranks pairwise.
249
      Winrate-based features distinguish signal infosets and require far less data compared to outcome-
      based features, which use histograms representing specific outcomes as the infoset progresses to the
250
      next phase. For example, in future considered only settings in heads-up Texas Hold’em, a Preflop
251
      winrate-based feature can be represented using only three data points (win, draw, loss), whereas an
252   outcome-based feature might require C(50, 3) data points (i.e., the number of combinations of three
253   community cards dealt from a 52-card deck after the player’s two hole cards). Clearly, fewer data
254   points reduce both time and space complexity. Nonetheless, this raises concerns about a potential
255   loss in resolution. In this section, we argue for the use of winrate-based features and demonstrate
256   that they do not significantly compromise resolution.

520   8   C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
521
522   This research introduces the first imperfect recall signal abstraction algorithm that considers histor-
523   ical information. This algorithm has the ability to adjust the scale of the abstracted signal infosets.
524   Based on this, we fully verified that the imperfect recall signal abstraction algorithms considering
525   historical information is superior to that only considering future information. Imperfect recall ab-
526   straction should be reexamined to introduce historical information and avoid excessive abstraction.
527   Krwemd can help existing AIs achieve better performance.
528   KrwEmd is more competitive than previous algorithms; however, it inevitably introduces significant
529   computational overhead. This is because KrwEmd uses the KMeans algorithm, whose time com-
530   plexity scales proportionally with the size of the input data (in our case, the size of the KRWI signal
531   infoset equivalence classes). In contrast, future considered only algorithms perform KMeans cluster-
532   ing using PWI as input, which is much smaller in scale. In Appendix D, we present an acceleration
533   method that reduces the computational cost of calculating the Earth Mover’s Distance in KrwEmd
534   to a scale comparable to that of future considered only algorithms. However, the complexity of the
535   clustering algorithm remains dependent on the size of the KRWI.
536   There are two potential directions for future improvements. The first is to adopt distributed com-
537   puting and approximation algorithms to reduce computational complexity. The second is to explore
538   non-KMeans algorithms and leverage machine learning techniques to incorporate historical infor-
539   mation more effectively. Regardless of the approach, incorporating historical information in hand
      abstraction will help build more powerful poker game AI systems.

896   KrwEmd is based on the KMeans++ clustering algorithm, where in each iteration, the distance
897   (equation 5) between every centroid and each k-recall winrate feature must be calculated. The cen-
898   troids are predefined, but the scale of k-recall winrate features varies depending on the game. For
899   instance , as shown in Table 2, in the River phase of HULHE, this number reaches an astounding
900   577366243. Computing the distance involves performing k Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) calcula-
901
      tions for every centroid-feature pair, which is highly computationally expensive.
902   It’s important to note that k-recall winrate features are actually combinations of multiple potential
903   winrate features. To optimize the process, we first calculate the EMD between centroids and po-
904   tential winrate features in the corresponding phase. We then express the distance between centroids
905   and k-recall winrate features as a linear combination of these precomputed EMDs.
906   Algorithm A4 is responsible for computing the distance between a given centroid rc =
907   (pc(r) , . . . , pc(r−k) ) and all k-recall winrate features, where rc[t] = pc(t) represents the poten-
908   tial winrate feature in phase t. Lines 2-5 enumerate all potential winrate features in phase t for
909   the centroid and compute the corresponding EMD distance. Lines 7-12 indicate that, for a k-recall
910   winrate feature, it is sufficient to retrieve its corresponding k+1 potential winrate features and, us-
911   ing precomputed distances, apply the weights w0 , . . . , wk to obtain the centroid’s distance to that
912   k-recall winrate feature. This approach reduces the computational burden of EMD to the scale of
913   potential winrate features. For example, in the River phase of HULHE and HUNL, this optimization
