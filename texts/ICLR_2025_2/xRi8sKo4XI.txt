009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) have achieved impressive success in text-formatted
012                learning problems, and most popular LLMs have been deployed in a black-box
013                fashion. Meanwhile, fine-tuning is usually necessary for a specific downstream
014                task to obtain better performance, and this functionality is provided by the owners
015                of the black-box LLMs. To fine-tune a black-box LLM, labeled data are always
016
                   required to adjust the model parameters. However, in many real-world applications,
                   LLMs can label textual datasets with even better quality than skilled human an-
017
                   notators, motivating us to explore the possibility of fine-tuning black-box LLMs
018
                   with unlabeled data. In this paper, we propose unsupervised prompt learning for
019                classification with black-box LLMs, where the learning parameters are the prompt
020                itself and the pseudo labels of unlabeled data. Specifically, the prompt is modeled
021                as a sequence of discrete tokens, and every token has its own to-be-learned categor-
022                ical distribution. On the other hand, for learning the pseudo labels, we are the first
023                to consider the in-context learning (ICL) capabilities of LLMs: we first identify
024                reliable pseudo-labeled data using the LLM, and then assign pseudo labels to other
025                unlabeled data based on the prompt, allowing the pseudo-labeled data to serve as
026                in-context demonstrations alongside the prompt. Those in-context demonstrations
027
                   matter: previously, they are involved when the prompt is used for prediction while
                   they are not involved when the prompt is trained; thus, taking them into account
028
                   during training makes the prompt-learning and prompt-using stages more consis-
029
                   tent. Experiments on benchmark datasets show the effectiveness of our proposed
030                algorithm. After unsupervised prompt learning, we can use the pseudo-labeled
031                dataset for further fine-tuning by the owners of the black-box LLMs.

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance in various text-formatted
037
      few-shot classification tasks, where the model takes the input text and outputs a corresponding
      label (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). In recent years, most popular LLMs have continued
038
      to grow in size and are increasingly deployed as black-box models, accessible through commercial
039
      application programming interfaces, such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020). These black-box models
040   provide promising results for data labeling and require minimal initial investment, as users can deploy
041   them directly for downstream tasks. Due to their strong performance and low initial investment, the
042   use of black-box LLMs for classification is gaining popularity in real-world applications.
043
      The performance of LLMs in downstream tasks can be further enhanced by supervised fine-tuning
044
      algorithms (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021). These methods always rely on labeled data for
045
      downstream tasks, allowing owners of black-box LLMs to fine-tune the model with these labeled
046   data and deliver a customized version of the black-box LLM after fine-tuning1 . In scenarios where
047   fine-tuning the black-box model is not possible, the learner can employ prompt learning algorithms
048   designed for black-box LLMs (Sun et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2022) to learn a prompt to label the
049   specific downstream task, as both the model parameters and the prompt can influence the final
050   performance. Nevertheless, these algorithms still require labeled data to learn the prompt.
051
      With the continuing advances and successes in LLM research, most popular LLMs can now label
052   textual datasets in many real-world tasks with quality equal to or better than skilled human annota-

      and then leveraging commercial fine-tuning services with these selected data. This process can be
077
      repeated iteratively, with each iteration adding newly identified reliable pseudo-labeled data, followed
078
      by further fine-tuning of the model by using the commercial fine-tuning services. However, this
079   iterative process is quite complex, and relying solely on a limited number of reliable pseudo-labeled
080   data at the beginning of the learning process carries a significant risk of overfitting.
081
082
      In this paper, we propose unsupervised prompt learning for classification with black-box LLMs, where
      we simultaneously learn the prompt itself and the pseudo labels of unlabeled data. The learned pseudo-
083
      labeled dataset can then be used to further fine-tune the black-box LLM using commercial fine-tuning
084
      services. Inspired by recent discrete prompt learning algorithms for black-box LLMs (Deng et al.,
085   2022; Diao et al., 2022), we assume the prompt is a sequence of discrete tokens, where each token is
086   sampled from a categorical distribution over the entire vocabulary. We optimize these categorical
087   distributions throughout the learning process. During inference, we sample each token according to
088   its categorical distribution to form the prompt. On the other hand, for learning the pseudo labels, we
089   propose to exploit the in-context learning (ICL) capability of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al.,
090   2023). We use the pseudo-labeled data as in-context demonstrations to facilitate the simultaneous
091   learning of pseudo labels and the prompt in the prompt-learning phase. As shown in Figure 1, we first
092   identify reliable pseudo-labeled data using the LLM, and then learn pseudo labels on other unlabeled
093
      data together with the prompt by fine-tuning on these reliable pseudo-labeled data. Different from
      previous prompt learning methods that learn prompts based on labeled data and then use labeled data
094
      as demonstrations only in the prediction stage, we learn prompts and the pseudo labels simultaneously
095
      in the training process. The demonstrations are selected by a specific algorithm and assigned pseudo
096   labels predicted by the LLM based on the prompt and their corresponding demonstrations.
097
098   These in-context demonstrations are important: they are previously only involved when the prompt
099
      is used for prediction, but they are not involved when the prompt is trained; thus, taking them
      into account during training makes the prompt-learning and prompt-using phases more consistent.
100
      Moreover, the ICL capability of LLMs allows them to make predictions based on a few labeled data
101
      as demonstrations. Theoretical studies have linked ICL behaviors to gradient descent algorithms,
102   suggesting that ICL can be viewed as implicitly constructing a model that performs empirical
103   risk minimization based on demonstrations, and then uses this model to predict unlabeled query
104   data (Akyürek et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023). By simultaneously learning both the pseudo
105   labels and the prompt during the prompt-learning phase, we ensure consistent prediction using
106   the learned prompt and pseudo-labeled demonstrations during the prompt-using phase, ultimately
107   generating reliable pseudo labels for the downstream task. We tested the proposed algorithm in
      benchmark applications and the results showed the effectiveness of the proposed method.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Supervised Fine-tuning with Open-sourced Models. Many parameter-efficient fine-tuning tech-
111   niques were proposed to fine-tune an open-sourced transformer-based LLM with labeled data in the
112   downstream task. Adapter-based fine-tuning methods introduce an adapter module into transformer,
113   allowing for fine-tuning without changing the pre-trained parameters (Houlsby et al., 2019; He et al.,
114   2021; Lei et al., 2023). Soft prompt fine-tuning algorithms append soft prompts or prefix vectors
115   to the input embeddings or hidden states during fine-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2023;
116   Wang et al., 2022). Re-parameterized fine-tuning methods use low-rank transformation to reduce
117   the number of trainable parameters while allowing operation with pre-trained parameters (Hu et al.,
118   2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). More recently, Chen et al. (2024) proposed a zero-order
119
      optimizer for large-scale deep neural networks. Malladi et al. (2023) proposed a memory-efficient
      zero-order optimizer to alleviate the heavy back-propagation in fine-tuning LLMs. However, all of
120
      these methods require that the open-source LLMs are first duplicated and then fine-tuned with labeled
121
      data in the downstream task, which is not applicable in our setting.
122
123   Supervised Prompt Learning with Black-box Models. Another line of work investigated prompt
124   learning for the black-box LLMs released as commercial application programming interfaces. Sun
125
      et al. (2022) optimized the input prompt for adaptation using derivative-free optimization techniques
      such as the evolutionary algorithms. Diao et al. (2022) employed the policy gradient algorithms
126
      to optimize the input prompt to fine-tune the model. While these methods consider the black-box
127
      learning scenario of LLMs, they still require the labeled data to learn the prompt.
128
129   Unsupervised Fine-tuning with Open-sourced Models. Several recent studies on large vision-
130   language models explored model fine-tuning using only leveraging unlabeled data in downstream
131
      tasks. These methods explore the parameters in the vision-language model to obtain class embeddings,
      which are then updated to align with the data distribution of the downstream task. For example,
132
      Huang et al. (2022) used the text encoder to generate reliable pseudo labels and then fine-tuned the
133
      model. Shu et al. (2022) augmented the unlabeled image and used the image encoder to generate
134   reliable pseudo labels and then fine-tuned the model. Tanwisuth et al. (2023) and Ma et al. (2023)
135   explored the class embeddings inside the LLMs to adapt the downstream tasks. Although these
136   algorithms only require unlabeled data, they assume an open-source setting where model parameters
137   are accessible during adaptation.

319   prompt z, and combine it with the unlabeled query and demonstrations. Then we present [xl , z, D]
320   to the black-box LLM f (·, ·, ·) and obtain the prediction. We then compute the loss according to
321   Equation (3) and update the categorical distributions by Equation (4). After learning the prompt and
322   pseudo labels for the downstream task, we can directly use the pseudo-labeled data for further fine-
323   tuning of the model by using the commercial fine-tuning service. We can use the learned categorical
      distributions to formulate the prompt to predict new unlabeled data.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      We construct the vocabulary V following previous work (Shin et al., 2020; Diao et al., 2022). We
325   adopt point-wise mutual information (PMI) to construct the vocabulary of candidate prompt tokens
326   in an unsupervised manner. For each sentence in the downstream task, we compute the PMI by
327   PMI(x1 , x2 ) = log(Pr(x1 , x2 )/(Pr(x1 ) Pr(x2 ))), where x1 and x2 are two adjacent words in the
328   sentence, and Pr(x) is the probability of an n-gram x. Therefore, the sentence is segmented based
329   on the PMI scores. We obtain a list of n-grams V by extracting those consecutive words after
330   segmentation and with a frequency of at least δ. As for N , we choose N between 50 and 200.

475
476
477
      In this paper, we investigate unsupervised prompt learning for classification with black-box LLMs,
      where we learn the prompt itself and the pseudo labels of unlabeled data simultaneously. After
478
      first identifying initial reliable pseudo-labeled data using the LLM, we propose a novel learning
479
      objective where we assign pseudo labels to other unlabeled data based on the prompt, allowing the
480   pseudo-labeled data to serve as in-context demonstrations alongside the prompt. These in-context
481   demonstrations are important: they are previously involved when the prompt is used for prediction,
482   while they are not involved when the prompt is trained; thus, taking them into account during training
483   makes the prompt-learning and prompt-using phases more consistent. Experiments on benchmark
484   datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed algorithm. After unsupervised prompt learning
485   and obtaining learned pseudo labels for the downstream task, we can then use the pseudo-labeled
      dataset for further fine-tuning by the owners of the black-box LLMs.
