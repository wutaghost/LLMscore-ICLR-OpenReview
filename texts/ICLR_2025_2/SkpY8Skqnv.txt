009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Learned optimizers (LOs) can significantly reduce the wall-clock training time of
012            neural networks, substantially reducing training costs. However, they can struggle
013            to optimize unseen tasks (meta-generalize), especially when training networks
014            much larger than those seen during meta-training. To address this, we derive
015            the Maximal Update Parametrization (µP) for two popular learned optimizer ar-
016            chitectures and propose a simple meta-training recipe for µ-parameterized LOs
017            (µLOs). Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that LOs meta-trained with our
018
               recipe substantially improve meta-generalization to wider unseen tasks when com-
               pared to LOs trained under standard parametrization (e.g., as they are trained in
019
               existing work). When applying our µLOs, each trained for less than 250 GPU-
020
               hours, to large-width models we are often able to match or exceed the performance
021            of pre-trained VeLO, the most performant publicly available learned optimizer,
022            meta-trained with 4000 TPU-months of compute. We also empirically observe
023            that learned optimizers trained with our µLO recipe also exhibit substantially im-
024            proved meta-generalization to deeper networks (5× meta-training) and remarkable
025            generalization to much longer training horizons (25× meta-training).

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep learning (Goodfellow et al., 2016) has enabled a great number of breakthroughs (Brown et al.,
029   2020; Brooks et al., 2024; Radford et al., 2021; Alayrac et al., 2022; Kirillov et al., 2023; Rombach
030   et al., 2022; Oquab et al., 2023). Its success can, in part, be attributed to its ability to learn effective
031   representations for downstream tasks. Notably, this resulted in the abandonment of a number of
032   heuristics (e.g., hand-designed features in computer vision (Dalal and Triggs, 2005; Lowe, 2004))
033
      in favor of end-to-end learned features. However, one aspect of the modern deep-learning pipeline
      remains hand-designed: gradient-based optimizers. While popular optimizers such as Adam or
034
      SGD provably converge to a local minimum in non-convex settings (Kingma and Ba, 2017; Li et al.,
035
      2023; Robbins, 1951), there is no reason to expect these hand-designed optimizers reach the global
036   optimum at the optimal rate for a given problem. Given the lack of guaranteed optimality and the
037   clear strength of data-driven methods, it is natural to turn towards data-driven solutions for improving
038   the optimization of neural networks.
039
      To improve hand-designed optimizers, Andrychowicz et al. (2016); Wichrowska et al. (2017); Metz
040
      et al. (2019; 2022a) replaced them with small neural networks called learned optimizers (LOs).
041
      Metz et al. (2022b) showed that scaling up the training of such optimizers can significantly improve
042   wall-clock training speeds and supersede existing hand-designed optimizers. However, LOs have
043   limitations in meta-generalization – optimizing new problems. For example, despite training for 4000
044   TPU months, VeLO (Metz et al., 2022b) is known to (1) generalize poorly to longer optimization
045   problems (e.g., more steps) than those seen during meta-training and (2) have difficulty optimizing
046   models much larger than those seen during meta-training. Given the high cost of meta-training LOs
047   (e.g., when meta-training, a single training example is analogous to training a neural network for
048   many steps), it is essential to be able to train learned optimizers on small tasks and generalize to
049   larger ones. Harrison et al. (2022) explore preconditioning methods to improve the generalization
050
      from shorter to longer optimization problems (e.g., ones with more steps). However, no works have
      tackled the meta-generalization of LOs to wider models in a principled way.
051
052   To address the meta-generalization problem of LOs, we recognize that this problem can be refor-
053   mulated as zero-shot hyperparameter transfer (Yang et al., 2022). The latter involves selecting
      optimal hyperparameters of hand-designed optimizers for training very large networks (that one

143   Generalization in LOs. One of the critical issues in LOs is generalization in the three main
144   aspects (Chen et al., 2022; Amos, 2022): (1) optimize novel tasks (often referred to as meta-
145   generalization); (2) optimize for more iterations than the maximum unroll length used in meta-
146
      training; (3) avoid overfitting on the training set. Among these, (3) has been extensively addressed
      using different approaches, such as meta-training on the validation set objective (Metz et al., 2019),
147
      adding extra-regularization terms (Harrison et al., 2022), parameterizing LOs as hyperparameter
148
      controllers (Almeida et al., 2021) and introducing flatness-aware regularizations (Yang et al., 2023).
149   The regularization terms (Harrison et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023) often alleviate issue (2) as a
150   byproduct. However, meta-generalization (1) has remained a more difficult problem. One approach
151   to tackle this problem is to meta-train LOs on thousands of tasks (Metz et al., 2022b). However,
152   this approach is extremely expensive and does not address the issue in a principled way leading
153   to poor meta-generalization in some cases, e.g. when the optimization task includes much larger
154   networks. Alternatively, Premont-Schwarz et al. (2022) introduced Loss-Guarded L2O (LGL2O)
155   that switches to Adam/SGD if the LO starts to diverge improving meta-generalization. However,
156   this approach needs tuning Adam/SGD and requires additional computation (e.g. for loss check)
157
      limiting (or completely diminishing in some cases) the benefits of the LO. In this work, we study
      aspects (1) and (2) of LO generalization, demonstrating how existing SP LOs generalize poorly across
158
      these dimensions and showing how one can apply µP to learned optimizers to substantially improve
159
      generalization in both these aspects.

      Parametrization is the unique stable abc-Parametrization where every layer learns features. The

      parametrization was derived for adaptive optimizers by Yang and Littwin (2023) and was applied
163   by Yang et al. (2022) to enable zero-shot hyperparameter transfer, constituting the first practical

172

173
      For these reasons, we do not study Depth-µP herein. Building on the latest works studying width
174   µP (Yang and Littwin, 2023; Yang et al., 2022), in this work, we show that µP can be extended to the
175   case of learned optimizers and empirically evaluate its benefits in this setting.

191   community is not only learning to solve optimization problems over T , but also to apply the learned
192   optimizer, fϕ , more generally to unobserved datasets and architectures. This transfer to new tasks

195   a hand-designed method such as SGD or Adam and ϕ represents optimization hyper-parameters such

197   Gradient descent is a standard approach to solving equation 1. However, estimating the meta-gradients
198   via backpropagation for very long unrolls is known to be noisy (Metz et al., 2019). Instead, gradients
199   are estimated using evolution strategies (Buckman et al., 2018; Nesterov and Spokoiny, 2017; Parmas
200   et al., 2018). Evolution strategies work by sampling perturbations to the LO’s weights (similar
201   to SPSA (Spall, 2000)), unrolling an optimization trajectory for each perturbation, and estimating
202   gradients with respect to evaluations of the meta-objective (usually the loss of the network being
203
      optimized, see eq. 1). In contrast to ES, which estimates one gradient per full unroll, PES (Vicol
      et al., 2021) allows estimating unbiased gradients at many points (called truncations) during the full
204
      unroll. This allows updating the optimizer’s parameters more often during meta-training. We use
205
      PES to estimate meta-gradients in our experiments.

530   6          C ONCLUSION
531
      We have demonstrated that applying or µLO meta-training recipe produces optimizers with substan-
532
      tially improved meta-generalization properties when compared to strong baselines from previous
533
      work. Remarkably, our µLOs even surpass VeLO-4000 (meta-trained for 4000 TPU months) on wider
534   versions of in-distribution tasks. Moreover, our experiments also show that µLOs meta-trained with
535   our recipe generalize better to wider and deeper out-of-distribution tasks than their SP counterparts.
536   Moreover, when evaluated on much longer training tasks, we observe that µLOs have a stabilizing
537   effect, enabling meta-generalization to much longer unrolls (25× maximum meta-training unroll
538   length). All of the aforementioned benefits of µLOs come at zero extra computational cost compared
539   to SP LOs. Our results outline a promising path forward for low-cost meta-training of learned
      optimizers that can generalize to large unseen tasks.

758
      Proposition 1. Assume that the LO fϕ is continuous around 0. Then, if fϕ (0) ̸= 0, the update, initial-
759
      ization, and pre-activation multiplier above is necessary to obtain a Maximal Update Parametrization.
760
761
762   Proof. The update produced by fϕ is denoted ∆W and we write ∇W the corresponding gradient, so
763
      that ∆W = fϕ (∇W ). For the sake of simplicity, n will be the output size and d the feature input
      size of our neural network. Our goal is to satisfy the desiderata of (Yang et al., 2022, Appendix
764
      J.2). We assume our initialization follows Initialization-µ in Sec 3. Overall, our goal is to study
765
      strategy so that if xi = Θ(1), then one needs to renormalize/initialize so that (W x)i = Θ(1) while
766   ((W + ∆W )x)i = Θ(1) so that the update is as large as possible. Note that given the assumptions
767   on f , if x = Θ( n1 ), then f (x) = Θ(1).
768
769   Output weights. Here, if input x has some Θ(1) coordinates, we initialize W = (wi )i≤n with
770   weights of variance 1 (which is necessary) and rescale the preactivations with n1 . For the update,
771
      we thus have that ∇W scales (coordinate wise) as Θ( n1 ) and we do not rescale the LR, given that
772   fϕ (∇W ) will also have coordinates in Θ(1).
773
774
      Hidden weights. Now, for the update, we observe that the gradient ∇W has some coordinates
775   which scale as Θ( n1 ), due to the output renormalization choice. Thus, the LO fϕ (∇W ) satisfies that
776   f (∇W ) = Θ(1), given that fϕ is continuous in 0 and satisfies fϕ (0) ̸= 0. Thus for the update, we
777   need to use ∆W = n1 fϕ (∇W ) so that ∆W x is coordinate wise bounded.
778
779
      Input weights. In this case, the gradient has coordinates which already scale in Θ( n1 ) (due to the
780
      output renormalization) and there is no need to rescale the LR.

       In this section, we extend our meta-generalization results for longer unrolls. Specifically, we verify
1137   whether µVeLO can generalize beyond 25× the meta-training unroll length for a width 1024 ViT
1138   to ImageNet task as its training curve in figure 6 (a) seems to slightly increase toward the end of
1139   training. It is important to note that the VeLO architecture takes as input the number of training
1140   steps remaining, thus, requiring the user to specify the total number of training steps (total_steps
1141   ) a-priori (e.g. as is done for many LR schedules in practice). Therefore, at each step, VeLO’s LSTM
1142   is conditioned on an embedding that provides the number of training steps remaining, allowing it to
1143   learn a schedule. Previous work analyzing VeLO-4000’s behavior has noted that changing the value of
1144   the total_steps hyperparameter leads to variable performance Rezk et al. (2023). Specifically, they
1145
       found that increasing the value of total_steps does not always lead to better performance Rezk
       et al. (2023). Figure 10 demonstrates that µVeLOM can successfully optimize a width 1024 ViT to
1146
       classify ImageNet images (same task as Figure 6 (a)) for 40, 000 training steps. However, we note
1147
       that it underperforms µVeLOM using total_steps = 25, 000. This is similar to what was found in
1148   previous work for VeLO-4000 Rezk et al. (2023).
