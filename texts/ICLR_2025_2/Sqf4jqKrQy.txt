009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models excel at generating diverse, high-quality images, but they also
012            risk producing unfair and harmful content. Existing methods that update text em-
013            beddings or model weights either fail to address biases within diffusion models
014            or are computationally expensive. We tackle responsible (fair and safe) text-to-
015            image (T2I) generation in diffusion models as an interpretable concept discovery
016            problem, introducing Concept Denoising Score Matching (CoDSMa) – a novel
017            objective that learns responsible concept representations in the bottleneck feature
018
               activation (h-space). Our approach builds on the observation that, at any timestep,
               aligning the neutral prompt with the target prompt directs the predicted score of
019
               denoised latent towards the target concept. We empirically demonstrate that our
020
               method enables responsible T2I generation by addressing two key challenges:
021            mitigating gender and racial biases (fairness) and eliminating harmful content
022            (safety). Our approach reduces biased and harmful generation by nearly 50% com-
023            pared to state-of-the-art methods. Remarkably, it outperforms other techniques in
024            debiasing gender and racial attributes without requiring profession-specific data.
025            Furthermore, it successfully filters inappropriate content, such as depictions of
026            illegal activities or harassment, without training on such data. Additionally, our
027            method effectively handles intersectional biases without any further training.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The rise of text-to-image diffusion models (T2I), such as Stable Diffusion, has significantly im-
033
      pacted content creation and visual communication by enabling high-quality visuals from simple text
      prompts (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2024). However, these models risk reinforcing stereo-
034
      types or generating harmful content, leading to societal consequences (Luccioni et al., 2023; Perera
035
      & Patel, 2023; Rando et al., 2022; Schramowski et al., 2023). Ensuring a responsible workflow that
036   prioritizes fair and safe generation is critical to reducing these risks.
037
038   In this work, we address responsible T2I generation through interpretable representation learning
039
      within the feature activations of the bottleneck layer in diffusion models, specifically the h-space, as
      introduced in Li et al. (2024). We define ‘responsible concepts’ as attributes related to both fairness
040
      and safety. Unlike Li et al. (2024), which identifies concepts in the h-space using generated images
041
      – a computationally expensive process – we propose an alternative approach that leverages denoised
042   latent representations. Inspired by visualizations from Katzir et al. (2024) on the denoising score
043   components in diffusion models, we explore the following: given the denoising latent for a neutral
044   prompt at timestep t (the neutral denoising latent), how does modifying the neutral prompt to a
045   target prompt affect the denoising score? Our findings show that at any timestep, the target prompt
046   directs the predicted denoising score (target score) to steer neutral denoised latents toward the target
047   concept. We use these target scores to learn concept representations in diffusion models. Further
048   details on our setup, observations, and visualizations are in section 4.2.
049   Building on our empirical observations regarding the role of the target score, we introduce Concept
050   Denoising Score Matching (CoDSMa), a novel score-matching objective designed to learn respon-
051   sible concept representations in the h-space. Previous work Kwon et al. (2023) has demonstrated
052   that semantic latent manipulation of images can be achieved through linear transformations in the
053   h-space, making it a strong candidate for the concept representation learning in diffusion models.
      Given a neutral prompt and a responsible concept (target concept), our goal is to learn a vector,

      referred to as the c-vector, which can be linearly added to the h-space to introduce interpretable
055   variations in the generated images, corresponding to a responsible concept. We achieve this by
056   introducing an objective that aligns the denoising score with the target score. Additionally, we
057   demonstrate that updating the c-vector in the direction of the gradient of CoDSMa steers the image

059
      We empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach for responsible T2I image generation,
060
      focusing on fairness and safe generation. Our method successfully mitigates gender and racial biases
061   in profession-related images, without requiring training on profession-specific data, outperforming
062   existing methods. We provide both quantitative and qualitative analyses showing that our objective
063   effectively reduces the generation of inappropriate content. Additionally, we present evidence that
064   our approach can address multiple biases simultaneously without the need for further finetuning.
065
      Our work makes the following key contributions. (1) Study of the intermediate denoising score re-
066
      veals that modifying the neutral prompt to the target prompt at any timestep guides the predicted de-
067   noising score to direct the neutral denoised latent towards the target concept. (2) Leveraging insights
068   from our empirical observations, we propose CoDSMa, a novel concept score distillation technique
069   for uncovering responsible concepts within the h-space of diffusion models. (3) Through extensive
070   quantitative and qualitative analysis, we demonstrate that CoDSMa enhances the fairness and safety
071   of T2I diffusion models, reducing unfair and inappropriate image generation by approximately 50%
072   compared to existing counterparts.

074   2   BACKGROUND
075
076   Responsible Generation using Diffusion Models: Recent work has seen a surge in methods to
077   mitigate biased and inappropriate content generation in Stable Diffusion models. Some approaches
078   modify input prompts by removing problematic words (Schramowski et al., 2023; Ni et al., 2023),
079   while others use prompt-tuning techniques (Kim et al., 2023) or learn projection embeddings on
080   prompt representations (Chuang et al., 2023) to filter out undesirable content. However, these meth-
081   ods primarily focus on text-based features and overlook the latent features that propagate through
082   the diffusion process. The authors of Gandikota et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2024) address this by fine-
083   tuning model weights to suppress harmful content generation, but these approaches can be compu-
084
      tationally expensive. Alternative methods like those in Schramowski et al. (2023) use classifier-free
      guidance to steer image generation away from undesirable content without additional training. Ap-
085
      proaches proposed in Gandikota et al. (2024); Chuang et al. (2023) offer efficient, closed-form so-
086
      lutions for embedding matrices to ensure responsible generation, though they lack adaptability and
087   fine generation control. Recent works (Parihar et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) modify the bottleneck
088   activations of diffusion models to ensure appropriate content generation. Similarly, our method uti-
089   lizes bottleneck activations but introduces a novel objective based on intermediate denoised latents,
090   enabling the discovery of responsible directions in the latent space of diffusion models.
091
092
      Concept Discovery in h-space: Kwon et al. (2023) were the first to identify the bottleneck layer
      of U-Net (h-space) as the semantic latent space, providing evidence that manipulations within the
093
      h-space result in semantically meaningful and interpretable changes in the generated images. Their
094
      method leverages CLIP classifiers to learn disentangled representations in the h-space, but this
095   comes at a high computational cost. In contrast, approaches like Haas et al. (2024) apply PCA
096   decomposition in the h-space, while Park et al. (2023) derive a local latent basis within the space
097   by utilizing the pullback metric associated with features to discover interpretable directions in an
098   unsupervised manner. Li et al. (2024) identifies interpretable directions for a given target concept
099   by using Stable Diffusion-generated images that align with the target concept. Our approach dif-
100   fers from Li et al. (2024) by identifying concepts through the intermediate denoised latent space
101   representations in diffusion models, enabling a more efficient and precise manipulation of underly-
102   ing features, rather than relying on the generated images themselves, which can be computationally
103
      expensive and may obscure concept representations.

156   This section presents a novel approach to enhancing fairness and safety in T2I diffusion models. We
157   reframe the problem as identifying responsible concept representations within the diffusion models
158   which enables unbiased and safe generation. We begin with a neutral prompt y (e.g., “a person”)
159   and a target prompt yp (e.g., “a woman”) representing a responsible concept. Our objective is to
160   identify a direction, termed as c-vector within the h-space of a pre-trained T2I diffusion models.
161   The c-vector, when applied as a linear transformation to the representations of the neutral prompt,
      induces semantically meaningful changes in the generation, aligning to the target concept.

235   Our objective is to discover interpretable representations in the h-space corresponding to the target
236   concept yp . Since the h-space (Li et al., 2024) of U-Net is designed to represent compressed and
237   abstracted semantic features of the data (e.g, object shapes, structure, textures), we aim to learn
238   a concept vector c ∈ RD , where D is the dimension of the h-space. The c-vector is randomly
239   initialized at the beginning of the training.

301   4.4   R ESPONSIBLE G ENERATION
302
303   In this section, we explore how the identified directions enable responsible image generation, using
304
      the c-vector learned through our approach for fair and safe generation, also illustrated in fig. 8.
305   Fair generation: Stable Diffusion has been shown to exhibit gender and racial bias when gener-
306   ating images for various professions, a challenge we aim to address. To do this, we first learn c-
307   vector that correspond to different societal groups. Specifically, we focus on binary gender classes:
308   {man, woman}, and three racial classes: {White, Black, Asian}, following the methodology of Li
309   et al. (2024). Utilizing the base prompt “a person”, we employ target prompts such as “a man”, “a
310   woman”, “a White person”, “a Black person”, and “an Asian person” to learn the concept vectors.
311   Once the training is complete, our objective is to generate images with uniformly distributed at-
312   tributes in response to prompts that typically produce biased representations of societal groups. For
313   instance, when employing the prompt “a photo of a doctor,” we aim to achieve balanced gender
314   representation during inference by uniformly sampling from the learned c-vectors for “man” and
315   “woman” in each image generation. These vectors are subsequently linearly combined with the h-
316   vectors extracted from the model’s middle block, conditioned on the prompt “a photo of a doctor”.
317   This approach facilitates fair generation in relation to professions during inference.
318   Safe generation: We aim to mitigate inappropriate content in generated images from unsafe text
319   prompts by employing a framework similar to Li et al. (2024). Two safety c-vectors are learned:
320   one for “anti-sexual” and another for “anti-violence” content, using negative prompting with target
321   prompts to obtain the target denoising score. For example, the “anti-violence” c-vector is trained
322   using a neutral prompt like “a scene” and the negative prompt “violence”. Similarly, the “anti-
323   sexual” c-vector is learned. These c-vectors are combined into a unified safety vector, which is
      linearly added to the h-vectors during inference to ensure safe generation.

      This section investigates the effectiveness of learned responsible concepts in ensuring fair and safe
327   generation. We explore properties such as mitigating multiple biases by composing directions and
328   interpolating attributes. All experiments utilize Stable Diffusion v.1.4 to evaluate the efficacy of our
329   approach.

331   5.1     FAIR GENERATION
332
333   Evaluation setting: We evaluate our method on the Winobias benchmark (Zhao et al., 2018), fol-
334   lowing the approaches in (Orgad et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Gandikota et al., 2024), which includes
335   36 professions with known gender biases. We learn c-vectors as outlined in section 4.4, updating
336   them over 1000 iterations with a batch size of 8. Unlike Gandikota et al. (2024), we do not learn
337   separate directions for each profession. Instead, we use the prompt “a person” to learn generalized
338
      directions applicable across professions, as detailed in section 4.4. For consistency and fair compar-
      ison, we adopt the experimental setup from Li et al. (2024) to evaluate gender and racial fairness.
339
      Five prompts per profession are used, including templates like “A photo of a ⟨profession⟩”. We also
340
      extend our evaluation to the Gender+ and Race+ Winobias datasets (Li et al., 2024), which introduce
341   terms like “successful” to trigger stereotypical biases (Gandikota et al., 2024). Additional dataset
342   details are in appendix D.1.
343
344   Metrics: We perform quantitative and qualitative analysis to evaluate the performance of our pro-
345   posed approach. We employ the modified deviation ratio, as defined in Li et al. (2024), to quantify
346
      the fairness of the generated images. Additionally, we assess image fidelity using the FID score
      Heusel et al. (2017) on the COCO-30K validation set, while image-text alignment is measured with
347
      the CLIP score Radford et al. (2021a) using COCO-30K prompts under fair concept directions.
348
      Further details on the evaluation metrics are provided in appendix D.3.

521   guide the generations toward the target concept while maintaining the neutral attributes intact.

      6      C ONCLUSION
524
525   Our work presents a significant step toward responsible text-to-image (T2I) generation by intro-
526   ducing Concept Denoising Score Matching (CoDSMa). We propose a novel method for ensuring
527   fairness and safety in image generation by learning responsible concept representations, utilizing the
528   interpretable h-space representations within diffusion models. We demonstrate that aligning a neu-
529   tral prompt with a target prompt effectively directs the denoising score to guide latent representations
530   toward the target concept at any timestep. Building on this insight, we introduce an objective that
531   learns responsible concept vectors in the h-space by matching the denoising score to the target con-
532   cept score. Extensive quantitative and qualitative evaluations demonstrate that CoDSMa enhances
533
      the fairness and safety of T2I diffusion models, significantly reducing biased and inappropriate con-
      tent generation. Furthermore, our approach effectively addresses multiple biases simultaneously
534
      without requiring additional fine-tuning, underscoring its scalability and practical application across
535
      diverse scenarios.

543   critical concerns around fairness, safety, and responsible AI use. Specifically, our method aims to

930   E.1   E VALUATION METRICS
931
      To assess inappropriateness in the generation, we utilize a combination of predictions from the Q16
932
      classifier and the NudeNet classifier on the generated images, in line with the approaches presented
933
      in Gandikota et al. (2023); Schramowski et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024). The Q16 classifier deter-
934   mines whether an image is inappropriate, while the NudeNet classifier identifies the presence of
935   nudity. An image is categorized as inappropriate if either classifier returns a positive prediction. We
936   evaluate the accuracy of the generated images using Q16/Nudenet predictions, which quantify the
937   level of inappropriateness. We generate five images for each prompt in the I2P benchmark during
938   the Q16/NudeNet accuracy evaluation.
939
      We evaluate image fidelity using the FID score Heusel et al. (2017) on the COCO-30k validation set,
940   while image-text alignment is measured with the CLIP score Radford et al. (2021a) using COCO-
941   30k prompts under the safe concept direction.
