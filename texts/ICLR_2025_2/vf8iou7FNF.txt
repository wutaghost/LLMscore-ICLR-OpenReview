                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) is considered a standard
012
               approach to fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) but faces challenges such
013
               as unsound reward models, sparse rewards, and difficulties in collecting human
014            preference data, limiting its effectiveness for complex, domain-specific tasks.
015
               We propose Reinforcement Learning via Symbolic Feedback (RLSF), a novel
016            fine-tuning paradigm where reasoning tools (e.g., solvers, provers, algebra sys-
017            tems) serve as the RL environment, providing fine-grained feedback via poly-sized
018            certificates (e.g., proofs) that characterize errors in the LLM-generated object with
019            respect to specific correctness criteria. RLSF aims to improve the domain-specific
020            understanding of LLMs more effectively than traditional reward signals. By en-
021            abling token-level corrections without requiring differentiable reasoning systems,
022            RLSF addresses key limitations of traditional reward models.
023            Via extensive evaluations, we show that our RLSF-based fine-tuning of LLMs
024            outperforms traditional approaches on five different applications (that have some
025            associated logical or domain constraints), namely, program synthesis from natural
026            language pseudo-code to programming language (+31.43% in functional correct-
027
               ness for Google’s CodeGemma-2b compared to supervised fine-tuning, +17.01%
               in functional correctness compared to GPT-3.5 – 100× larger), three chemistry
028
               tasks (+5.5% exact match for molecule generation, +19.4% exact match for for-
029
               ward synthesis, +33.7% exact match for retrosynthesis, using Meta’s Galactica-
030            1.3b, compared to GPT-4 – 1000× larger), and solving the Game of 24 (+25%
031            success rate using Meta’s Llama2-7b compared to traditional methods, and +7%
032            success rate compared to GPT-3.5 – 25× larger). A takeaway is that fine-tuning
033            via RLSF enables relatively smaller LLMs to significantly outperform closed-
034            source models that are orders of magnitude larger (e.g., GPT-4).

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) have had a dramatic impact on many sub-fields of
040   AI (Bommasani et al., 2021). Tasks that seemed impossible just a few years ago, are now routinely
041   solved by LLMs. Examples include language translation (Qin et al., 2024; Min et al., 2023), text-
042   to-image generation (Zhang et al., 2023), coding assistants (Liang et al., 2024), and open-ended text
043   generation (Achiam et al., 2023).
044   Despite their impressive performance, these models often struggle with tasks that require reasoning
045   or formal domain knowledge (Mao et al., 2024; Stechly et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022). This lim-
046   itation has sparked a growing interest in exploring methods that can better align LLM outputs with
047   logical or domain constraints, particularly through the incorporation of corrective feedback loops
048   between learning and reasoning processes (Ganesh et al., 2022; Kambhampati et al., 2024). For
049   example, Kambhampati et al. (2024) state that “LLMs cannot plan themselves but can play a variety
050
      of constructive roles in solving planning tasks–especially as approximate knowledge sources and
      candidate plan generators in the so-called LLM-Modulo Frameworks in conjunction with external
051
      sound model-based verifiers.”
052

      ticular, is rooted in the idea of combining the strengths of these two sub-fields of AI (Hitzler et al.,

079   integrating these two approaches, a hybrid system can be created that can leverage the strengths of
080   both paradigms, potentially leading to more robust and capable AI systems.
081
      One popular approach to fine-tuning LLMs is Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
082
      (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020), which relies on manually collecting correc-
083
      t/incorrect cases and creating an approximate (possibly unsound) black-box reward model. Such an
084   approach can be expensive, error-prone, and may not accurately capture the nuances of a reasoning
085   task. Moreover, the reward signal thus generated can be sparse and scalar in nature. Such sparse
086   reward signals can fall short of fully capturing those aspects of an LLM-generated object that may
087   be incorrect with respect to a well-defined specification (or inconsistent with domain knowledge).
088
      To address these challenges, we propose a new fine-tuning paradigm we refer to as Reinforcement
089
      Learning via Symbolic Feedback (RLSF) that is designed to improve the performance of LLMs
090   compared to traditional methods in complex reasoning tasks. Figure 1 highlights the differences
091   between RLHF and RLSF. In the RLSF setting, the LLM is considered as the RL agent to be fine-
092   tuned, while the environment is allowed access to reasoning tools, that can generate poly-sized
093   certificates of their analyses.
094
      In the forward direction (LLM to environment), formal objects generated (such as programs, proofs,
095
      molecules, or theories) by the LLM are fed to the RLSF environment, which leverages reasoning
096   tools to perform analysis or verification of such objects against specifications or domain knowledge.
097   In the reverse direction (environment to LLM), any certificate (that identifies what is wrong with the
098   LLM-generated object) produced by symbolic tools is used as part of a reward function to provide
099   corrective feedback to the LLM. Leveraging symbolic tools to generate poly-sized certificates 2 ,
100   which provide sound, fine-grained (token-level) feedback to LLMs, eliminates the need for manual

137   2   R ELATED W ORK
138
139   The idea of integrating symbolic feedback into reinforcement learning for RLSF has philosophical
140   origins in the Learning Rate Based (LRB) branching heuristic for SAT solvers (Liang et al., 2016).
141
      In their work, they model the variable selection problem as an online multi-armed bandit, a special
      case of reinforcement learning, to learn branching variables such that the learning rate of the solver
142
      is maximized. LRB uses a structured feedback signal based on the solver’s performance and conflict
143
      analysis, guiding the optimization of variable selection. This is one of the key inspirations behind
144   the RLSF approach, where we provide fine-grained feedback to LLMs via poly-sized certificates
145   generated by symbolic reasoning tools.
146
147
      Neurosymbolic Reinforcement Learning (NRL). We refer the reader to the Compendium of Neu-
      rosymbolic AI for a rich and recent literature survey on combinations of learning and reasoning
148
      systems (Hitzler et al., 2023). There has been considerable work in NRL (Acharya et al., 2023).
149
      However, all the NRL work that we are aware of focuses on combining symbolic reasoning tools
150   with the RL agent, often requiring reasoning systems to be differentiable and also limiting the agent’s
151   ability to handle large symbolic representations. In contrast, RLSF integrates symbolic tools with
152   the environment and eliminates the need for differentiability. Further, in the RLSF paradigm, a key
153   innovation is that we leverage the poly-sized certificates generated by the environment’s symbolic
154   tools to provide a fine-grained reward signal to the RL agent. This allows for a modular framework
155   with more expressive and interpretable feedback during training.
156   Combinations of LLMs with Symbolic Tools. Previous research has explored the integration of
157   program analysis tools during inference (Agrawal et al., 2024). While existing efforts have often re-
158   lied on binary or scalar signals for utilizing symbolic feedback during training or fine-tuning (Chen
159   et al., 2020; Jana et al., 2023; Jha et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2024), the RLSF paradigm leverages
160   richer token-level (dense) symbolic feedback, thus enabling significantly improved fine-tuning per-
161   formance as it enables more detailed and fine-grained correction, pinpointing specific areas in the
      output that need improvement. This allows the model to learn more effectively, as it receives clearer,

      3   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING VIA S YMBOLIC F EEDBACK (RLSF)
190
191
      In this section, we introduce the Reinforcement Learning via Symbolic Feedback (RLSF) algorithm.
192   The algorithm incorporates fine-grained, token-level feedback generated by reasoning or domain
193   knowledge tools, thereby addressing the limitations of traditional reward-based methods.
194
195
      The RLSF algorithm, outlined in Algorithm 1, fine-tunes a pre-trained language model Model using
      reinforcement learning (RL) with the help of the fine-grained certificate provided by a symbolic
196
      reasoning tool SymbolicReasoner . The framework leverages a reward function (RewardFunc) to
197
      compute vector (token-level) feedback (vector i ) based on the certificate generated by the symbolic
198   reasoning tool. This feedback aligns with the shape of the response generated by the language model,
199   facilitating fine-grained adjustments during fine-tuning. The algorithm operates over a specified
200   number of epochs Nepochs , iterating through a dataset D.
201
      Inputs and Outputs. The algorithm takes as input the pre-trained model (RL agent to be fine-tuned)
202
      Model , the symbolic reasoning tool SymbolicReasoner and the reward function RewardFunc (to
203
      be used as the RL environment), and the dataset D that consists of input prompts for Model . The
204   output is a fine-tuned model Model ′ . The algorithm performs the following steps:
205
206   Epoch Iteration. For each epoch from 1 to Nepochs , the algorithm iterates through D.
207   Batch Processing. For each batch batch i in the dataset D, the algorithm performs the following:
208    • Response generation (response i ): The Model generates a response response i given the input
209      prompt batch i .
210    • Certificate computation (cert i ): The symbolic reasoning tool SymbolicReasoner computes a
211      certificate cert i corresponding to the response response i . The certificate includes fine-grained
212      error/non-error messages extracted from the symbolic analysis of the prompt-response pair.
213    • Token-level (dense) feedback computation (vector i ): The reward function (RewardFunc) calcu-
214      lates a vector feedback (vector i ) based on the certificate cert i . The reward function processes
215      this certificate to generate token-level vector feedback. This feedback provides detailed guidance
         to the language model during fine-tuning, facilitating precise adjustments. The vector feedback

      4     R EASONING TASK A: NATURAL L ANGUAGE P SEUDO - CODE TO C++ C ODE
248
      Automated code synthesis from natural language (NL) descriptions is a crucial task in software en-
249
      gineering that has garnered considerable attention. Recent efforts, such as Ugare et al. (2024), have
250
      explored using LLMs for this. We evaluate how our RLSF paradigm improves LLM-based transla-
251   tion of an NL pseudo-code into a C++ code. To be correct, the code must be both (a) syntactically
252   correct per the g++ compiler and (b) functionally correct (or equivalent 3 ) w.r.t. a test suite.
253
254   4.1    B ENCHMARK AND RLSF SETUP
255
256   For training and evaluating the LLM, we utilize the SPoC dataset (Kulal et al., 2019), which includes
257   16,326 accepted C++ solutions for 677 problems from a competitive programming website (Code-
258   forces, 2023). On average, each program has 14.7 lines, ranging in [1, 457]. They employed 59
259   crowd-workers from Amazon Mechanical Turk to write NL pseudo-code for each line of C++ pro-
260   gram, ensuring a line-level granularity of textual description. Each problem is accompanied by a test
261
      suite of multiple test cases, curated by the problem-setter. We evaluate on the T EST P partition of
      SPoC, consisting of 158 problems (∼23.34%) and 1,778 pairs of pseudo-code and C++ code. The
262
      remaining 14,548 pairs from 519 problems (∼76.66%) are used for training the LLMs. Please refer
263
      to Section A.1 in the Appendix for additional implementation details.
264
265   Refer to Figure 2 for an overview of the RLSF setup for fine-tuning an LLM to translate pseudo-code
266   into code. In the supervised learning setup, we assume a training dataset D = {(pci , ci , TS i )}, con-

271   receives pci as input (with prompt in Figure 2) and generates code cbi , which may contain syntactic
272   errors. This code is passed through the compiler (here, g++) to identify lines with errors (E). If cbi
273   compiles, it is tested on TS i to compute the pass rate (r ∈ [0, 1]). If it does not compile, r = 0. Our
274   token-level fine-grained feedback assigns a high reward p = 1 + r to tokens in syntactically correct
275   lines (positive case) and a low reward n = 0 to tokens in erroneous lines (negative case).
276
      Our setup features a gradual progression of rewards and fine-grained feedback. Tokens in erroneous
277   lines receive a reward of 0, while those in syntactically correct lines of non-compiling code get a
278   reward of 1 (since p = 1 + r and r = 0). Tokens in a compiling code receive rewards p ∈ [1, 2],
279   based on the value of r. The special tokens <BOS> and <EOS> get a reward of p if cbi compiles, and
280   n otherwise. This token-level feedback fine-tunes the LLM through RL using PPO (Algorithm 1).

282   4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
283
284   We use three metrics to evaluate pseudo-code to C++ code translation with LLMs. CompAcc and
285   FCorrAcc provide more nuanced insights, while Pass@1 is a stricter pass/fail metric. In Section 4.4,
286   we present performance using all three metrics, focusing on the first two for comparison of methods.
287
      Compilation Accuracy (CompAcc). The percentage of generated C++ codes that are syntactically
288   correct, indicating that it compiles without errors using a g++ compiler.
289
      Functional Correctness Accuracy (FCorrAcc). The percentage of test cases demonstrating the
290   expected input-output behavior for each generated C++ code, averaged across all the generated
291   codes. If a generated code contains syntactical errors, FCorrAcc is zero for that code.
292   Pass@1. The percentage of generated C++ codes generated on the first attempt by the LLM that is
293   syntactically correct and passes all test cases in the problem’s test suite, with each code evaluated as
294   true if it passes all tests and false otherwise.

346
347   Chemistry tasks, such as molecule generation, forward synthesis, and retrosynthesis, are critical
348   benchmarks for assessing the capabilities of LLMs in real-world scientific applications (Schwaller
349   et al., 2019; Zhong et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b). These tasks require models to
350
      navigate the intricate rules of chemical structures and reactions, making them excellent challenges
      to evaluate domain-specific understanding. Leveraging LLMs in chemistry has the potential to sig-
351
      nificantly accelerate fields like drug discovery and materials science, enabling faster innovation and
352
      discovery (Guan & Wang, 2024; Yu et al., 2024b; Bhattacharya et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024).
353
354
      5.1    B ENCHMARK AND RLSF SETUP
355
356   We focus on three tasks, namely, Molecular Generation (MG), Forward Synthesis (FS), and
357   Retrosynthesis (RS). MG involves generating molecules given a description in natural language.
358   FS involves predicting the product of a chemical reaction given specific reactants and reagents.
359   RS focuses on identifying the reactants necessary to produce a specific target product. All out-
360   put molecules are generated in the SMILES format (Weininger, 1988), a widely used method for
361   encoding molecular structures as a sequence of symbols, making it a popular choice for LLM-
362   based chemistry models (Yu et al., 2024a). Section B.1 gives more details about the different tasks.
363
      USPTO-full dataset (Lowe, 2017) is a commonly used for FS and RS tasks, while ChEBI-20 and
      Mol-Instructions datasets (Edwards et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023) are used for the MG task. We
364
      use a high-quality version of these datasets from LlaSMol (Yu et al., 2024a), ensuring the removal
365
      of chemically invalid SMILES and inaccurate information. We focus on a subset of the dataset
366   where multiple outputs are possible for a given input, as this poses a significant challenge for LLMs.
367   In such cases, the model must better capture the syntax and semantics of a given task, rather than
368   relying on simple input-output mapping through supervised fine-tuning (SFT). This setup pushes
369   the model beyond memorization, testing its ability to generalize, making it a key evaluation of its
370   capabilities. We ensure that same input samples are not shared across dataset splits for the same and
371   also across the three tasks. More details can be found in Section B.2.
372   For the RLSF feedback, we handle both syntax and semantic errors. In our chemical modeling tasks,
373   we use RDKit (RDKit, 2023), a widely adopted cheminformatics toolkit, as the symbolic feedback
374   generator. RDKit analyzes model outputs, such as SMILES strings, and generates error logs that we
375   transform into token-level rewards whenever applicable. For instance, consider the model-generated
376   SMILES string C(C)(C)N(C)(C)(C). RDKit identifies an issue with the nitrogen atom, flagging
377   it for violating valence rules. Specifically, RDKit’s parser outputs the error: “Explicit valence for
      atom # 3 N, 4, is greater than permitted.” This indicates that the nitrogen atom (N, indexed as

395   In addition to valence issues, RDKit helps detect a wide range of errors, including hallucinated
396   (non-existent) atoms, syntax errors (e.g., invalid characters, unbalanced parentheses, or unclosed
397   rings), and semantic violations, such as the introduction of elements that violate conservation laws.
398
      RDKit’s consistent error format allows for straightforward parsing and categorization of issues,
      enabling targeted feedback. For example, in FS, we ensure adherence to the first law of chemistry by
399
      penalizing outputs that introduce atoms not present in the reactants. If the reactants contain hydrogen
400
      and carbon but the product introduces nitrogen, this discrepancy triggers a penalty. Similarly, for
401   molecule generation, we verify the presence of specified functional groups and penalize outputs that
402   omit them (Figure 3). The token-level feedback mechanism ensures that the model learns precise
403   corrections by addressing both syntax and semantic errors effectively. By integrating detailed checks
404   and aligning feedback with domain-specific requirements, our approach significantly enhances the
405   model’s ability to generate accurate, chemically valid outputs.

      5.2      E VALUATION METRICS
408
409   Three main metrics are used to assess performance on the FS, RS and MG tasks: Exact Match
410   (EM), Fingerprint Tanimoto Similarity (FTS), and Validity (Valid). EM measures the propor-
411   tion of predicted results that exactly match the gold standards. FTS quantifies structural similarities
412   between molecules using Tanimoto similarities of their Morgan fingerprints (Morgan, 1965). Va-
413   lidity assesses the ratio of valid predictions following SMILES grammar and the chemical valence
414   rules (Yu et al., 2024a). These metrics are commonly used in the field of cheminformatics and
415   molecular prediction (Schwaller et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2024a).
416
417   5.3      C OMPARATIVE M ODELS U SED
418
419   We conduct RLSF fine-tuning on two recent open-source code LLMs that we obtain
420   from HuggingFace (2024): mistral-7b-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023) from Mistral AI and
421   galactica-1.3b (Taylor et al., 2022) from Meta AI. We also compare results with the closed-
422   source gpt-4-turbo model i.e., GPT-4 (ChatGPT), that we access via the API of OpenAI
423   (2023). The two open-source models have 7 billion and 1.3 billion parameters, respectively. In
424   comparison, GPT-4 is rumored to have around 1.76 trillion parameters – about 1000 times more.

450   6     R EASONING TASK C: G AME OF 24 USING T REE OF T HOUGHTS (T OT)
451
452   The Game of 24 is a well-known benchmark that involves basic arithmetic operations such as ad-
453   dition, subtraction, multiplication, and division aimed at testing the mathematical capabilities of
454   LLMs. Briefly, the idea behind the Game of 24 is as follows: given 4 numbers and basic arithmetic
455   operations, obtain the target number 24. We refer the reader to the paper on Tree of Thoughts (ToT)
456   by Yao et al. (2024) and Section C for more details.
457
458   6.1   B ENCHMARK AND RLSF SETUP
459
460
      Similar to Yao et al. (2024), we collect data from 4nums.com, a website hosting mathematical
      games, selecting 1,362 games sorted by human solving time from easy to hard. For testing, we
461
      use the same games as (Yao et al., 2024), indexed 901-1,000. Success in each task is defined as
462
      producing a valid equation that equals 24 while using each input number exactly once. For the RLSF
463   fine-tuning phase, we use games indexed 800-900. Fine-tuning occurs during the “propose prompt”
464   steps (using prompt styles from Yao et al. (2024)), as shown in Figure 4. Pairs of “propose prompts”
465   and LLM responses are periodically evaluated using SymPy (Meurer et al., 2017) to identify syntax
466   and semantic errors. This feedback is used to fine-tune the LLM with Proximal Policy Optimization
467   (PPO) following Algorithm 1. For more details on different error categories and converting symbolic
468   feedback into fine-grained signals for fine-tuning, refer to Section C.2.
469
470   6.2   C OMPARATIVE M ETHODS AND M ODELS U SED
471
472   We incorporate the benchmarks previously used by Yao et al. (2024), i.e., standard Input-Output
473   (IO) prompting, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting, and ToT prompting. IO prompting uses five
474
      in-context examples, while CoT prompting includes three intermediate equations for these in-context
      examples. Both IO and CoT prompting are sampled 100 times per game for average performance
475
      assessment. To showcase the improvement due to fine-grained token-level feedback, we perform an
476
      ablation study where we compare the binary (scalar) and token-level versions of feedback for the
477   RL fine-tuning. After RL fine-tuning, we evaluate the performance on the test set using ToT with
478   the updated LLM. We perform RLSF fine-tuning on two popular smaller open-source LLM models
479   (gemma-2b-it (Gemma-Team et al., 2024) and llama2-7b-chat (Touvron et al., 2023)) that
480   we obtain from Huggingface (HuggingFace, 2024) and also compare against two closed-source
481   models GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023).

521   7   C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523
524   In this paper, we introduced RLSF, a fine-tuning paradigm that incorporates RL-based symbolic
525
      feedback into the fine-tuning process of LLMs. While we do not claim to improve general reason-
      ing capabilities, RLSF leverages symbolic reasoning tools to improve downstream domain-specific
526
      tasks where syntax and semantics play a critical role. Our results show a significant improvement
527
      in all five tasks, over different traditional prompting and fine-tuning methods. Notably, the RLSF-
528   tuned galactica-1.3b achieves superior results compared to GPT-4 (1000× larger) on the three
529   chemistry tasks, RLSF-tuned code-gemma-2b outperforms GPT-3.5 (100× larger) on the pro-
530   gram synthesis task. Similarly, RLSF-tuned llama2-7b-chat also outperforms GPT-3.5 (25×
531   larger) on Game of 24. Additionally, unlike traditional neuro-symbolic RL approaches, RLSF does
532   not require differentiable reasoning systems, making it more versatile and practical.
533
      Limitations and future work. This study demonstrates the initial potential of integrating symbolic
534   feedback into RL frameworks, with empirical improvements in specific domains such as program
535   synthesis, chemistry and mathematical tasks. While we do not aim to enhance the overall reasoning
536   capabilities of LLMs, our focus has been on developing a new fine-tuning paradigm that outperforms
537   traditional methods within specific domains. Future research may extend this to explore theoretical
538   guarantees, its impact across other reasoning tasks, and broader LLM reasoning capabilities. Lastly,
539   our focus has been solely on fine-tuning, but we believe that combining RLSF with multi-step sym-
      bolic feedback during inference could further boost performance.

821   sor directives along with a using namespace std; line to each LLM-generated code snippet
822   before calculating CompAcc, FCorrAcc, and the token-level feedback. This ensures that the LLM-
823   generated code compiles correctly with the g++ compiler.

825   A.2     A DDITIONAL R ESULTS
826
827   Most state-of-the-art code generation LLMs (Wang et al., 2022; Le et al., 2022; Shojaee et al.,
828
      2023; Dou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a) employing RL-based fine-tuning rely on either Boolean
      compiler feedback (e.g., +1 if the code compiles, −1 if it does not), Boolean success feedback
829
      (e.g., +x if all test cases pass, −y if at least one test case fails), or a combination of both. In our
830
      natural language pseudo-code to code translation experiments, presented in Table 1, our aim was
831   to compare these conventional binary scalar reward-based methods with the proposed fine-grained
832   vector reward in RLSF.
833
834
      For this comparison, we evaluated two types of scalar rewards: success feedback and compiler
      feedback, consistent with the binary reward schemes commonly used in state-of-the-art methods.
835
      Notably, success feedback provides an even sparser signal than compiler feedback. This is because
836
      the likelihood of LLM-generated code either (1) passing all test cases vs. failing at least one test case
837   or (2) successfully compiling vs. failing to compile, results in a more balanced distribution in the
838   second scenario. In other words, the disparity between success and failure cases is higher for success
839   feedback compared to compiler feedback. As shown in Table 4, LLMs fine-tuned with Boolean
840   success feedback underperform compared to those fine-tuned with Boolean compiler feedback.
841
      The proposed vectorized feedback in RLSF enhances the binary scalar feedback schemes used in
842
      state-of-the-art methods in two key ways: (a) it refines the success reward by incorporating the
843   number of test cases passed, moving beyond a binary outcome and, (b) it introduces a vectorized
844   symbolic feedback mechanism that assigns lower rewards to tokens in code lines flagged by the
845   compiler and higher rewards to tokens in unflagged lines. These innovations provide a more nuanced
846   and effective fine-tuning paradigm for code generation LLMs, as demonstrated by our results.

      B.2    DATASET SPLIT
899
900
901   We focus on three key tasks: Molecule Generation, Forward Synthesis, and Retrosynthesis, each of
902   which presents the challenge of having multiple valid outputs for a given input. We take datapoints
903
      from existing datasets (Lowe, 2017; Edwards et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024a) where
      multiple outputs are possible for a given input, as this scenario presents a significant challenge for
904
      LLMs. In such cases, the model must not only generate syntactically valid outputs but also grasp
905
      domain-specific rules and context to accurately capture task details. Simple supervised fine-tuning
906   (SFT), which directly maps inputs to outputs, often struggles with this complexity, relying more on
907   memorization. By handling tasks with multiple potential outputs for a single input, the model is
908   required to go beyond basic input-output mapping, forcing it to learn complex relationships within
909   the data. This setup is crucial for testing the model’s ability to generalize.
910
      To ensure proper evaluation, we identify sample input-output pairs across tasks that correspond
911
      to the same molecules or reactions, and group these samples consistently in either the training,
912   evaluation, or test set. In addition, samples with the same input but different outputs are handled
913   with care. For instance, in the RS task, a single product can be synthesized from multiple sets
914   of reactants, so we ensure that all samples with identical inputs remain in the same split to avoid
915   data leakage. For the Molecule Generation task, we used 3367 training samples, 354 validation
916   samples, and 933 test samples. The Forward Synthesis task consists of 10207 training samples, 1452
917   validation samples, and 2908 test samples. The Retrosynthesis task has 77561 training samples,
      2051 validation samples, and 4382 test samples.

939   C.1   G AME OF 24 USING T OT
940
941   To solve the Game of 24 using ToT, the process involves decomposing the problem into three steps,
942
      each representing an intermediate equation. Starting with the given input numbers, the LLM is
      prompted to propose possible next steps (or “thoughts”) using a “propose prompt”. Similar to Yao
943
      et al. (2024), we employ a breadth-first search (BFS) approach in ToT, maintaining the top 5 can-
944
      didates at each step. Now, we prompt the LLM using the value prompt to evaluate the “thoughts.”
945   The score given by the LLM using the value prompt helps prune the “thoughts” generated by the
946   “propose prompt.” These two prompts are repeated, starting with the three remaining numbers (from
947   all thoughts accepted after using the value prompt) to build the ToTs. This process is repeated until
948   you arrive at the final equation that results in the number 24.
949
950   C.2   C ONVERTING THE SYMBOLIC FEEDBACK TO FINE - GRAINED SIGNAL
951
952   In Game of 24, we use a combination of SymPy and regex-based checks to validate model outputs
953   and identify errors. SymPy performs syntax and semantic validation, ensuring the logical and math-
954   ematical correctness of the expressions, while regex is employed to verify numerical compliance
955   with the game’s rules.
956   Errors are classified into several categories for precise feedback. First, we detect instances where
957   invalid numbers are used in the expression. For example, if the input numbers are (3, 8, 3, 3) and
958   the model generates 3+8-3+10=18, the use of 10 is flagged as an error because it is not part of the
959   game input or an intermediate step.
960   Next, we verify the correctness of the equation using SymPy to ensure that the left-hand side equals
961   the right-hand side of the equation. Discrepancies such as 3×(8+3)=27 are flagged as errors.
962   Regex-based checks are also used to identify missing or extra numbers in the final expression. For
963   example, if the input is [3, 8, 3, 3] and the model outputs (3×8)+3=27, one of the 3s is missing.
964   In contrast, if the model produces (3×8)+3+2, the presence of extraneous 2 is flagged. Addi-
965   tional syntax issues, such as unbalanced parentheses, missing operators, or division by zero, are also
966   detected and flagged.
967   To provide fine-grained feedback, we perform token-level pinpointing whenever possible, assigning
968   penalties to erroneous tokens. For example, in the expression (3×8)+3 3, SymPy raises a syntax
969   error due to the missing operator between the two 3s, and regex flags both 3s as repeated without
970   justification. This allows errors to be traced back to specific tokens, enabling targeted corrections.
971   This detailed feedback ensures that the model learns from precise and actionable signals, improving
      its ability to generate accurate solutions in subsequent attempts.

974
       The RLSF algorithm is implemented by modifying the Transformer Reinforcement Learning (TRL)
975    library (von Werra et al., 2020), a popular and comprehensive framework integrated with Hug-
976    gingface transformers (Wolf et al., 2020) designed for training transformer language models with
977    reinforcement learning (RL). We use the Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) algorithm, commonly
978    used in Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Yang et al., 2024; Ouyang et al.,
979    2022; Stiennon et al., 2020), for fine-tuning the language model. However, TRL only allows for
980    scalar reward signals during the RL fine-tuning process. We modify the library to support reward
981    vector (dense) signals, allowing fine-grained feedback at the token level. This enhancement en-
982    ables RLSF to leverage symbolic feedback effectively during the RL process. We use LoRA (Hu
983
       et al., 2021) applied to all linear layers in the self-attention and FFN modules with lora r and
       lora alpha set to 16 during our fine-tuning. All our experiments were conducted on a high-
984
       performance CentOS V7 cluster equipped with Intel E5-2683 v4 Broadwell processors running at
985
       2.10 GHz and 64 GiB of memory. We used 4 NVIDIA V100 GPUs for the tasks in Sections 4 and
986    5, and 1 NVIDIA V100 GPU for the task in Section 6.
