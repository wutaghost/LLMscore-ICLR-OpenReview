009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, deep neural networks (DNNs) have excelled at learning from
012            high-throughput genome-profiling experiments to predict transcription factor (TF)
013            binding. TF binding is driven by sequence motifs, and explaining how and why
014            DNNs make accurate predictions could help identify these motifs, as well as their
015            logical syntax. However, the black-box nature of DNNs makes interpretation dif-
016
               ficult. Most post-hoc methods evaluate the importance of each base pair in isola-
               tion, often resulting in noise since they overlook the fact that motifs are contiguous
017
               regions. Additionally, these methods fail to capture the complex interactions be-
018
               tween different motifs. To address these challenges, we propose Motif Explainer
019            Models (MEMs), a novel explanation method that uses sufficiency and necessity
020            to identify important motifs and their syntax. MEMs excel at identifying multiple
021            disjoint motifs across DNA sequences, overcoming limitations of existing meth-
022            ods. Moreover, by accurately pinpointing sufficient and necessary motifs, MEMs
023            can reveal the logical syntax that governs genomic regulation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      The regulatory function of DNA sequences is determined by short DNA segments called “motifs”,
028
      where certain proteins called “transcription factors” (TFs) bind to regulate gene activity (Klug,
029
      1995; Alberts et al., 2002; Siggers & Gordân, 2014; Lambert et al., 2018). TF binding depends
030   on the arrangement and logical combination of these motifs. High-throughput experiments provide
031   a genome-wide view of regulatory activity in various cell types (Consortium et al., 2012). Tech-
032   niques like DNase-seq and ATAC-seq identify regions of open chromatin where TFs can bind, while
033   more targeted methods like ChIP-seq offer insights into specific protein–DNA interactions.
034
      Given the complexity and volume of data from these experiments, accurately predicting genome
035
      activity from DNA sequences requires models that can handle large datasets and capture the intricate
036   interactions and arrangement of motifs. Deep neural networks (DNNs), with their proven success in
037   various biological sequence prediction tasks, are well-suited for this challenge. They have achieved
038   state-of-the-art performance in predicting TF binding from DNA sequences (Alipanahi et al., 2015;
039   Avsec et al., 2021; Eraslan et al., 2019). Genomic DNNs take DNA sequences as inputs and learn
040   to predict a label from a regulatory profiling experiment, such as whether a TF binds to a given
041   sequence. The goal is to use these accurate models to identify the motifs and syntax governing
042   genomic regulation (Novakovsky et al., 2023).
043   Despite their success, the black-box nature of these models makes it difficult to understand how and
044   why they make specific predictions (Zednik, 2021; Tomsett et al., 2018). In regulatory genomics,
045   this has led to the development of post-hoc methods to explain genomic DNNs at the local (sample)
046   level. Given a model f and an input N -length DNA sequence x = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ) ∈ RN , these
047   methods aim to identify important motifs for the prediction f (x) by assigning an importance score
048   to each base pair xi , and then segmenting out high-importance regions as putative motifs. However,
049   many of these methods fall short because they evaluate the importance of individual base pairs in
050
      isolation, and do not leverage the fact that motifs are short, contiguous regions. Furthermore, these
      methods also fail to capture the complex interactions between motifs.
051
052   To address these challenges, Linder et al. (2022) introduced scramblers, a model-based explanation
053   method optimized for the discrete nature of biological sequences. Scramblers outperform other
      post-hoc methods by identifying important motifs using learned stochastic masks. However, when

      the input is a complex DNA sequence with multiple motifs, scramblers struggle to identify multiple
055   short contiguous regions as they do not leverage the key properties of motifs we know of. Due to

057   regulatory behavior.
058
      In this work, we propose a novel model-based explanation method called Motif Explainer Models
059
      (MEMs), which leverages the concepts of sufficiency and necessity to produce meaningful explana-
060
      tions. For complex DNA sequences composed of disjoint and contiguous motifs, we show that our
061   method accurately identifies sufficient and necessary motifs, and outperforms the current state-of-
062   the-art method of scramblers. Furthermore, by employing both sufficient and necessary explanations
063   together, MEMs reveal the logical syntax between motifs that governs genomic regulation.

065   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
066
067   Several methods have been proposed for discovering motifs from genomic DNNs.
068
069   Visualizing Convolutional Filters. Nearly all genomic DNNs are convolutional in their first lay-
070   ers (Alipanahi et al., 2015; Zhou & Troyanskaya, 2015; Kelley et al., 2016; Avsec et al., 2021). To
071   identify important motifs, many works have focused on visualizing CNN filters (Alipanahi et al.,
072
      2015; Kelley et al., 2016) because early convolutional layers often capture basic patterns, while
      deeper layers capture more complex features (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Yosinski et al., 2015; Si-
073
      monyan et al., 2014). However, this approach has shown limited success, as it assumes each filter
074
      learns one motif, and each motif is learned by one filter. Recent research shows that motifs are
075   distributed across multiple filters and layers (Tseng et al., 2024).
076
077   Measuring Influence via Post-hoc Explanation Methods. Popular post-hoc explanation meth-
078   ods like CAM (Zhou et al., 2016), LIME (Ribeiro et al., 2016), gradient-based approaches (Selvaraju
079   et al., 2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2021), Shapley value-based methods (Chen et al.,
080   2018; Teneggi et al., 2022), and perturbation-based methods (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Fong et al.,
081   2019) have been adapted to identify motifs. These methods assign importance scores to each base
082   pair (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Shrikumar et al., 2017), but they perform poorly for two reasons.
083   First, by evaluating base pairs individually, they miss key motifs because subsequences inherently
084   interact in complex ways to regulate function. Second, these methods are computationally expen-
085
      sive. For instance, integrated gradients and DeepLIFT require integrating over the entire DNN,
      while Shapley-based methods require exponential computations (Strumbelj & Kononenko, 2010;
086
      Lundberg & Lee, 2017). Additionally, the importance scores are often noisy, fragile, and fail to
087
      reveal the model’s true decision-making process (Ghorbani et al., 2019; Tseng et al., 2020), making
088   it difficult to rely on downstream tools like MoDISco to cluster them into motifs (Shrikumar et al.,
089   2018).
090
091   Scramblers. To overcome these limitations, Linder et al. (2022) proposed scramblers, a model-
092   based explanation method that learns stochastic masks to highlight the base pairs crucial for pre-
093   dictions. Scramblers predict position-specific scoring matrices (PSSMs), where unimportant base
094   pairs are “scrambled” by increasing their entropy. Scramblers have a distinct advantage over many
095   traditional post-hoc explanation methods due to their model-based approach: a scrambler only needs
096   to be trained once for any model predictive f , after which importance scores for any query DNA
097
      sequence can be obtained in a single evaluation. While scramblers outperform other post-hoc meth-
      ods, they still struggle with sequences composed of multiple disjoint and contiguous motifs. This
098
      is due to a regularization penalty that focuses on controlling entropy, rather than incorporating the
099
      core characteristics of motifs (small, contiguous, and disjoint). As a result, scramblers are limited in
100   complex settings and fail to uncover the logical syntax of motif interactions for genomic regulation.
101

103
104   We address the challenges of interpreting genomic DNNs by proposing a novel model-based ex-
105   planation method focused on identifying important motifs and the logical syntax governing gene
106   regulation. Specifically, our method can identify both sufficient or necessary motifs for predictions,
107   providing more accurate and interpretable explanations for genomic DNNs on complex DNA se-
      quences. Our contributions include:

149   2.1   S UFFICIENCY AND N ECESSITY
150
      Our method takes as input a pretrained predictor f : X 7→ Y and a fixed DNA sequence x, and
151   outputs a subset S ⊆ [d] that is considered “important” for the prediction f (x). We define the
152   importance of S using slightly modified notions of sufficiency and necessity originally proposed by
153   Bharti et al. (2024). We present our modified definitions, below for clarity:
154   Definition 1 (Sufficiency & Necessity (Bharti et al., 2024)). Let ϵ and ∆ > 0. Denote ρ : R×R 7→ R
155
      to be a similarity measure. For a predictor f and sample x, denote Ŷ (x) = 1[f (x) ≥ 0.5] to be the
156   predicted class of x by f .
157
158   A subset S ⊆ [d] is ϵ-sufficient with respect to a distribution V for f at x if
159                                             ρ(Ŷ (x), fS (x)) ≤ ϵ                                        (2)
160
      A subset S ⊆ [d] is ∆-necessary with respect to a distribution V for f at x if
161
                                               ρ(Ŷ (x), fS̄ (x)) ≥ ∆.                                       (3)

      In other words, this definition of sufficiency states that, for a reference distribution V, a subset of
163   features S is ϵ-sufficient if, with xS fixed, the average restricted prediction fS (x) is ϵ close to the
164
      predicted class Ŷ (x). Conversely, the definition of necessity states that, for V, a subset S is ∆-
165   necessary if, when the features in S are marginalized out, the resulting average restricted prediction
166
      fS̄ (x) is ∆ away from the predicted class Ŷ (x). Later in this work, we will demonstrate why
167   accurate sufficient and necessary explanations are essential for deducing motif syntax. Furthermore,
168   we highlight that while scramblers attempt to generate these explanations with some success, our
169   proposed method achieves far greater accuracy, enabling more reliable deduction of the underlying
170   motifs and their logic.

      2.3   S HORTCOMINGS OF S CRAMBLERS
217
218   While scramblers outperform common post-hoc methods in providing explanations, they still face
219   some key limitations that affect their overall effectiveness.
220
      Lack of Key Prior Knowledge. DNA motifs are generally recognized as small, contiguous, and
221
      disjoint subsequences within a larger sequence (Klug, 1995; Alberts et al., 2002; Siggers & Gordân,
222   2014; Lambert et al., 2018; Stormo, 2013; Maston et al., 2006). Thus, incorporating this key in-
223   formation as an inductive bias into explanation methods could greatly improve the quality of the
224   identified motifs. However, scramblers do not explicitly consider this prior knowledge; instead, they
225   learn a distribution Pg (X) over sequences, which optimizes jointly for the prediction reconstruction
226   error and for entropy. While this formulation is valid to produce necessary or sufficient explanations,
227   it fails to capture prior knowledge of motif biology, particularly that motifs occur as one or more
228   small, contiguous, and disjoint subsequences.
229   Limitations of the Conservation Penalty. While inclusion and occlusion scramblers aim to max-
230   imize and minimize the entropy of Pg (x) via their conservation penalty, C ON(Pg (X), B̃, X), their
231   effectiveness heavily depends on the choice of the tbits parameter. This parameter controls the en-
232   tropy and serves as the target value for the expected entropy of Pg (X). For example, a larger tbits
233   allows more entropy for Pg (x) with respect to either the background B̃ or the sample X, depending
234   on whether an inclusion or occlusion scrambler is being learned. The challenge with this formulation
235   is that we often do not know how many motifs exist or how distinct they are from the background
236   signal, making it difficult to determine an appropriate target entropy for Pg (x). Additionally, consid-
237   ering our prior knowledge of motif biology, there is no theoretically justifiable reason that enforcing
238   entropy will lead to the identification of small, contiguous motifs, which we will demonstrate in our
239
      experimental section.

243   To address the limitations of scramblers and provide more accurate explanations that highlight con-
244   tiguous and disjoint motifs, we propose Motif Explainer Models (MEMs). This model-based based
245   explanation approach is designed to incorporate the key properties of motifs, better capturing the
246   structure and arrangement of motifs within sequences, and offering a more precise and biologically
247   meaningful interpretation. A MEM is a model m : X 7→ [0, 1]N that outputs importance scores in

468
469    Lastly, we consider a data-generating process based on a repressive syntax. This rule assigns a
470    positive label if M1 is present and M2 is absent, and a negative label in all other cases. The logic in
471
       this rule is more involved as M2 represses M1 from generating a positive labeling. In this setting,
       for sequences with Y = 1, the smallest sufficient and necessary set is {M1 } since its sole presence
472
       results in a positive classification and removal in a negative classification. On the other hand, for the
473
       subset of negatively labeled sequences, Y = 0, the logic is more involved. When the label = 0 due
474    to both M1 and M2 being present, the sufficient and necessary set is M2 because its presence yields
475    the correct negative prediction and its removal results in the sequence having only M1 present which
476    yields a positive label. For the subset of negatively labeled sequences that contains M2 only, the set
477    {M2 } is both sufficient and necessary.

522    5                        C ONCLUSION & F UTURE D IRECTIONS
523
524
525
       In this work, we introduced Motif Explainer Models (MEMs), a novel explanation method for ge-
       nomic DNNs that identifies both sufficient and necessary motifs in complex DNA sequences. In
526
       contrast to current methods like scramblers, MEMs leverage prior domain knowledge as an induc-
527
       tive bias to cleanly identify individual motifs as disjoint and contiguous subsequences. Furthermore,
528    by discovering sufficient and necessary motifs separately, MEMs address the limitations of existing
529    post-hoc methods that often fail to capture the intricate logical relationships between motifs. Our
530    approach not only improves the interpretability of genomic DNNs, but also uncovers the logical
531    syntax governing gene regulation, distinguishing between as cooperative, repressive, and redundant
532    interactions.
533
       Through extensive experiments, we demonstrated that MEMs outperform current methods in de-
534    tecting important motifs and deciphering their underlying syntax. By providing more accurate and
535    comprehensive explanations, MEMs offer new insights into the functional roles of motifs in gene
536    regulation, paving the way for better understanding of transcription-factor binding and genomic
537    activity. In summary, MEMs represent a significant step forward in interpreting complex genomic
538    models, offering a robust framework for elucidating the logic behind motif interactions. Future work
539    may explore extending this framework to more diverse regulatory contexts, ultimately enhancing our
       ability to interpret the functional landscape of the genome.
