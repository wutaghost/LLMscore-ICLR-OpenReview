009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Realism constraints (or constraints on perceptual quality) have received consid-
012            erable recent attention within the context of lossy compression, particularly of
013            images. Theoretical studies of lossy compression indicate that high-rate common
014            randomness between the compressor and the decompressor is a valuable resource
015            for achieving realism. On the other hand, the utility of significant amounts of com-
016
               mon randomness at test time has not been noted in practice. We offer an explanation
               for this discrepancy by considering a realism constraint that requires satisfying
017
               a universal critic that inspects realizations of individual compressed images, or
018
               batches thereof. We characterize the optimal rate-distortion-perception trade-off
019            under such a realism constraint, and show that it is asymptotically achievable
020            without any common randomness, unless the batch size is impractically large.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Realism, or perceptual quality, of reconstructed signals is a long-standing open challenge in lossy
026   compression, particularly for image/video compression (Eckert & Bradley, 1998; Wu et al., 2012). It
027   has received renewed interest in the recent years due to the remarkable progress in image generation
028
      models and neural compression techniques. The idea is that reconstructed images should be indistin-
      guishable to humans from naturally occurring ones in addition to having a high pixel-level fidelity
029
      to the original source. This ensures that reconstructed images are free of obvious artifacts such as
030
      blocking, blurriness, etc.
031
032   The idea that the output of the decoder should resemble the source in a statistical sense is not
033   new. Advanced Audio Coding (AAC), for instance, includes a provision to add high-frequency
034
      noise to the output so that its power spectrum resembles that of the source (Sayood, 2012). But
      the idea has received renewed attention with the emergence of adversarial loss functions in learned
035
      compression (Santurkar et al., 2018; Tschannen et al., 2018; Agustsson et al., 2019; Blau & Michaeli,
036
      2019). In practice, this has proven to be a powerful method for ensuring that reconstructed images
037   have high perceptual quality (Agustsson et al., 2019; Mentzer et al., 2020; He et al., 2022a; Iwai et al.,
038   2024). Adversarial loss functions can in many cases be viewed as variational forms of statistical
039   divergences. Thus one can think of constraining the distribution of reconstructions to be close to that
040   of the source according to some divergence, in addition to requiring that each reconstructed image be
041   close to its respective source according to conventional notions of distortion.
042
      Rate-distortion theory characterizes the optimal trade-off between rate and distortion in lossy com-
043   pression (Pearlman & Said, 2011; Sayood, 2012). The fundamental object in the theory is the
044   rate-distortion function, for a given source distribution pX :

057   amounts of high-quality common randomness are needed to meet the R(1) (·, 0) bound (Saldi et al.,
058   2015; Wagner, 2022; Chen et al., 2022) (see also Xu et al. (2023)). The exception is the case in
059   which the realism constraint is imposed in a very weak form, namely that the histograms of the
060   source and reconstruction images should be close on a per-realization basis (Chen et al., 2022). Note
061   that common pseudorandomness, say generated from a shared seed, does not qualify as common
062   randomness for the purposes of the above results.
063   On the other hand, the theoretical prediction that lossy compression schemes would benefit from
064   substantial amounts of high-quality common randomness between the encoder and decoder has not
065   been observed in practice. To the best of our knowledge, there exist compression schemes (Agustsson
066   et al., 2023; He et al., 2022a; Hoogeboom et al., 2023; Ghouse et al., 2023; Mentzer et al., 2020;
067   Yang & Mandt, 2023), considered as state-of-the-art, that do not involve any common randomness.
068   While it is possible that future designs will find common randomness to be a valuable resource, it
069   seems more likely that the discrepancy between the theoretical prediction and practical experience
070
      lies with a flaw with the theoretical models.
071   Consider a communication system for which a strong realism constraint is imposed: the distribution
072   of the reconstructions must be close to the distribution of natural images, say, in Wasserstein or
073   total variation distance (TVD). If the source distribution is continuous, then the code cannot be
074   deterministic, for otherwise the reconstruction distribution would be supported on a countable set
075   (corresponding to the set of received bit strings). Thus some amount of randomization is required
076
      to meet the constraint. The decoder can randomize its output in a way that “spreads” the point
      masses out to form a continuous distribution, but adding independent noise at the decoder inevitably
077
      degrades the distortion. Common randomness is useful because it allows the discrete reconstruction
078
      points to be dispersed to form a continuous distribution without less overall distortion. This is the
079   basis for the finding that common randomness is a useful resource for compression under realism
080   constraints (Theis & Agustsson, 2021).1
081
082
      The above reasoning is evidently sensitive to the nature of the realism constraint. If we simply require
      that each reconstructed image appear realistic in its own right, without reference to the reconstruction
083
      ensemble, then the spreading process mentioned above is unnecessary. It follows that there would be
084
      no need for randomization. This is relevant because human observers, who are the ultimate arbiters of
085   realism in practice, are adept at identifying unrealistic features of individual images. Yet it is difficult
086   for human observers to distinguish between a continuous ensemble and one that is discrete with a
087   very large support set, since doing so would require viewing (and remembering) many images. In
088   short, human critics are very good at spotting unrealistic aspects of individual images but are expected
089   to be poor at detecting subtle ensemble-level differences.
090
      This suggests posing the realism constraint in a way that better captures the relative strengths and
091   weaknesses of human critics. The aforementioned strong realism constraint has also been challenged
092   in the context of other problems, such as generative modeling (Theis, 2024). We consider a novel
093   formulation of the lossy compression problem in which the goal is to satisfy a critic that is incredibly
094   discriminating when viewing individual images. In fact, a reconstructed image is declared unrealistic
095   if there exists some computable test, no matter how complex, that can distinguish it from the set of
096   typical source images (see Definition 3.5 to follow). At the same time, we assume that the critic
097   can glean information about the ensemble only by inspecting batches of individual samples. Under
098   this formulation, we show that the rate-distortion-perception function R(1) (·, 0) in (2) is achievable
099   without common randomness unless the batch size is unreasonably high—on par with the number
100
      of possible outputs of the decoder (Theorems 4.1 and 4.2). If common randomness is not needed
      to fool this critic, it should not be needed to fool any weaker (and more practical) critic, since the
101
      stronger critic subsumes the weaker one. This is akin to how in cryptography one might prove security
102
      guarantees assuming a very strong adversary, stronger than can be implemented in practice. The fact
103   that the adversary cannot be practically implemented is a strength of our approach. It is notable that
104   there exist compressors that can satisfy such discriminating critics at all. It is all the more notable that

149   2.2   L OSSY COMPRESSION ALGORITHMS WITHOUT COMMON RANDOMNESS
150
151   The performance of practical lossy compression schemes in terms of realism (or perceptual quality)
152   is generally measured with well established metrics such as FID (Heusel et al., 2017), LPIPS (Zhang
153
      et al., 2018), PieAPP (Prashnani et al., 2018), and DISTS (Ding et al., 2022). Distortion is often
      measured with PSNR. According to these metrics, the following lossy compression algorithms are
154
      state-of-the-art. In particular, these schemes achieve visually pleasing reconstructions at very low
155
      compression rates. None of these algorithms make use of common randomness. The schemes in
156   Mentzer et al. (2020), He et al. (2022a), and Agustsson et al. (2023) were obtained by training with
157   an adversarial loss, a method inspired from generative adversarial networks (GANs). The former
158   combines a conditional GAN with the scale hyperprior method of Ballé et al. (2018). The latter is
159   an extension of the ELIC scheme (He et al., 2022b), which is state-of-the-art in terms of rate and
160   distortion. The loss function of the latter was augmented, in particular, with an adversarial term
161   and an LPIPS term. The method proposed in Agustsson et al. (2023) is inspired from He et al.
      (2022b) and Mentzer et al. (2020). The schemes in Yang & Mandt (2023), Ghouse et al. (2023), and


                                                           3

162
      Hoogeboom et al. (2023) rely on diffusion models. The first uses a diffusion model conditioned on
163   quantized latents. The two other schemes first train an autoencoder for rate and distortion, then train
164   a diffusion model which improves the visual quality of the latter’s output. The fact that none of these
165   state-of-the-art algorithms make use of common randomness supports the theoretical results derived

435   focus should be shifted to the size of the batch inspected by the critic. A continuation of our work
436   could be to investigate realism metrics, where particular attention would be given to the choice of
437   the batch size. This could lead to highlighting specific strengths and weaknesses of existing realism
438   metrics. It may also inspire a critical assessment of the relative performance of existing compression

441
