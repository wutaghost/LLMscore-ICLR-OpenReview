011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Missing values present significant challenges in machine learning, often degrading
014            predictive performance. Traditional and deep learning imputation methods often
015            overlook the relationships between features and their connections to downstream
016            tasks. To address these gaps, we propose PIG (multi-task learning of Prediction
017            and Imputation via feature-relationship Graph learning), a model that integrates
018
               imputation and prediction by leveraging feature interdependencies. PIG utilizes
               a graph-based approach to capture intricate feature relationships, thereby enhanc-
019
               ing the accuracy of both imputation and downstream tasks. Our strategic train-
020
               ing process begins with pre-training for both tasks, ensuring the model learns
021            effective representations. This is followed by fine-tuning the entire model to fur-
022            ther optimize imputation and downstream tasks simultaneously. We evaluated our
023            method using nine benchmark datasets, three for regression and six for classifica-
024            tion. Our method showed superior imputation and prediction performance across
025            nine datasets, achieving an average rank of 1.33 for both imputation and regres-
026            sion tasks and 1.83 for imputation and 1.17 for classification tasks. Additionally,
027            in sensitivity analysis with respect to missing rates, our method demonstrated its
028            robustness, especially in predictive performance, compared to other methods that
029
               showed significant degradation.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Missing values arise from various factors across diverse fields (Kim et al., 2018; Yoon et al., 2016;
035   Li et al., 2021; Koren et al., 2009), underscoring the importance of effective imputation. Tradi-
036   tionally, basic statistical methods, such as mean, median, or mode, have been used for imputation;
037   however, these approaches often yield inaccurate results. Matrix completion methods, commonly
038   employed in recommendation systems, have demonstrated significant improvements in missing data
039
      imputation (MDI) (Koren et al., 2009; Hu et al., 2008; Rendle et al., 2014; Mazumder et al., 2010),
      yet they have limitations in handling categorical features. Recently, deep learning methods have
040
      been applied to MDI using AutoEncoders (AEs) and their variants (Gondara & Wang, 2018; Vin-
041
      cent et al., 2008), enabling flexible processing of mixed-type data that includes both numeric and
042   categorical features. Furthermore, some studies have utilized generative models, such as Variational
043   AutoEncoders (VAEs) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), to capture the underlying data
044   distribution and thereby generate plausible values for missing data (Nazabal et al., 2020; Yoon et al.,
045   2018).
046
      Despite the advancements offered by the aforementioned methods, they exhibit notable limitations.
047
      First, all these approaches focus solely on the imputation task, neglecting the downstream tasks,
048   which are the ultimate goal of the analysis. This oversight can result in sub-optimal performance in
049   downstream tasks. Second, while deep learning methods have demonstrated competitive accuracy
050   in imputation, they are often sensitive to irrelevant features that are commonly present in real-world
051   datasets (Grinsztajn et al., 2022). This sensitivity can lead to overfitting to noisy patterns of irrelevant
052   features, hindering the generalization of imputation performance. Consequently, these limitations
053   highlight a critical gap in both imputation and downstream tasks, necessitating the development of
      more integrated approaches.

054
      More recent methods, such as GRAPE (You et al., 2020), have demonstrated the potential of Multi-
055   Task Learning (MTL) to address both imputation and downstream tasks. Additionally, by transform-
056   ing tabular data into a bipartite graph structure and using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), GRAPE
057   implicitly captures feature relationships through edge-level prediction tasks for imputation, while
058   downstream tasks are framed as node-level predictions. However, despite its strong performance,
059   GRAPE still has limitations, such as an indirect representation of feature relationships through par-
060   tially observed data and challenges related to stability during MTL (Liu et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2020;
061   Chen et al., 2018).
062   To this end, we propose PIG, multi-task learning of Prediction and Imputation via feature-
063   relationship Graph learning. PIG incorporates a Graph Learning Layer (GLL) that explicitly learns
064   feature relationships, which are then fully utilized in a Graph Residual Imputation Layer (GRIL)
065   to consider only relevant features in imputation and thereby enhance its performance. Addition-
066   ally, PIG enhances the stability of MTL through a carefully designed training strategy. The training
067   process begins with the pre-training of the GLL and a Prediction Layer (PL), which is designed to
068   perform downstream tasks. This is followed by fine-tuning the entire model to simultaneously opti-
069
      mize both imputation and downstream tasks. This approach, as demonstrated to be effective in recent
      research (Qu et al., 2024), mitigates the complexities associated with multiple objectives, resulting
070
      in stable performance improvements, particularly in downstream tasks. The main contributions of
071
      this work are as follows.
072
073          • We present PIG, an MTL model that integrates imputation and prediction (classification or
074            regression) for mixed-type tabular data, leveraging their interdependencies to enhance both
075            tasks.
076
             • We propose a GLL which identifies feature relationships, and a GRIL, which utilizes these
077
               relationships to optimize both imputation and downstream tasks.
078
079          • We propose a training strategy to optimize PIG for both imputation and prediction tasks,
080
               which begins with the pre-training of a GLL and a PL, followed by fine-tuning the entire
               model.
081
082
      Through extensive experiments across diverse datasets that encompass both regression and classifi-
083
      cation downstream tasks, we rigorously evaluated the effectiveness of the proposed method. Several
084   ablation studies further explains the significant impact of individual components within our model,
085   thereby showing comprehensive and deliberate design of the proposed method.

088   2   R ELATED WORK
089
090   MDI methods have evolved significantly over time, transitioning from simple statistical methods to
091   more advanced machine learning and deep learning methods.
092   Statistical Methods. Early MDI methods included simple statistical methods such as mean, median,
093   and mode imputation. While these methods are easy to implement, they often overlook relationships
094   between features, leading to poor performance.
095
      Machine Learning Methods. More sophisticated methods have emerged, such as multiple impu-
096   tation by chained equations (MICE) (Van Buuren & Groothuis-Oudshoorn, 2011), k-nearest neigh-
097   bors (KNN) (Troyanskaya et al., 2001), and matrix completion methods. MICE iteratively predicts
098   missing values using estimators like Bayesian ridge or random forests, while KNN imputes missing
099   values based on similar data points. Matrix completion methods, commonly employed in recom-
100   mendation systems (Koren et al., 2009), have demonstrated strong performance in MDI by identi-
101   fying and leveraging latent structures among samples and features. However, both MICE and KNN
102   can face scalability issues and may struggle with large, complex datasets, and matrix completion
103   methods are generally limited to numeric data, posing challenges with categorical variables.
104   Deep Learning Methods. Recently, deep learning methods have emerged as powerful alterna-
105   tives for MDI. AEs (Gondara & Wang, 2018) and VAEs (Nazabal et al., 2020) project incomplete
106   data into a latent space to reconstruct missing values. GAN-based methods, such as generative
107   adversarial imputation networks (GAIN) (Yoon et al., 2018), utilize adversarial learning to gen-
      erate plausible imputed data, reporting strong performance across various settings. Building on

      3.2     T HE DETAILED MODEL ARCHITECTURE
180
181
      PIG has three main layers (Figure 1): GLL, GRIL, and PL. The GLL learns the feature relationships,
182
      represented as a matrix A ∈ Rd×d , that help both imputation and prediction. The GRIL takes X̃
183
      and A to correct the imputation residuals, then returns X̂ by filling in missing values of X. Then,
184
      PL takes X̂ and predicts targets Y in downstream tasks. Each main layer will be described in the
185
      following subsections.
186
187
      3.2.1    GLL: G RAPH L EARNING L AYER
188
189   Motivated by graph construction in various studies (Shi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2020;
190   Yan et al., 2018), we devised GLL to adaptively infer feature relationships, which are typically un-
191   known in real-world datasets. GLL utilizes independent representations: the destination-embedding
192   matrix E1 ∈ Rd×dE and the source-embedding matrix E2 ∈ Rd×dE where dE is the dimensionality
193   of feature embedding.
194   First, each embedding matrix is projected using P1 ∈ RdE ×dE and P2 ∈ RdE ×dE and activated
195   with a scaled hyperbolic tangent function as Z1 = α tanh (E1 P1 ) and Z2 = α tanh (E2 P2 ) where
196   α is a hyperparameter that controls sensitivity. Then, the feature relationship matrix A is computed
197   by applying the sigmoid function to the inner product of the projected embeddings:
198
                                            A = sigmoid Z1 Z2T ,

200   where each Aij indicates the informativeness of feature j for imputing feature i. This relationship
201   matrix A enables PIG to exploit feature dependencies for accurate imputation and prediction.

      3.2.4   O BJECTIVE FUNCTION
243
244   The core idea of PIG is to use appropriate feature relationships to improve the solution for both
245   imputation and prediction. The objective function is constructed over the three main layers (Figure 8
246   in Appendix E).
247
      First, GLL computes the regularization loss Lreg to ensure that the inferred relationship matrix A
248
      align with prior knowledge Aprior about feature relationships. Specifically, it uses binary cross-
249
      entropy to assess how much A deviates from Aprior . This loss encourages the model to deviate
250   from Aprior if deviations significantly enhance both imputation and prediction.
251
252   Second, GRIL computes the two types of losses: reconstruction loss Lrec and imputation loss Limp .
253
      The reconstruction loss ensures that the model accurately restores the actual values of the observed
      ones, whereas the imputation loss ensures that the model accurately guess true values of the missing
254
      ones. The imputation loss cannot be calculated directly because true values for unobserved ones
255
      are unknown. To solve this problem, observed values are randomly masked with probability pmask
256   to simulate missing values. Then, the imputation loss is computed using these artificially masked
257   values and the reconstruction loss is computed using only observed and unmasked values (Figure 2).
258
259
      Specifically, GRIL distinguishes between numeric and categorical features by applying proper
      losses: squared differences for numeric features and cross-entropy loss for categorical features.
260
      This approach enables PIG to flexibly and accurately process mixed-type datasets.
261
262   Lastly, PL computes the downstream task loss Ldt . Depending on whether the task is classification
263   or regression, the last loss is calculated differently. It uses cross-entropy loss between the predicted
264   labels Ŷ and the true labels Y for classification task, whereas it utilizes mean squared error (MSE)
265   between the predicted values Ŷ and the actual values Y for regression task.
266
      Finally, the total loss L of PIG is
267
268                                     L = λLreg + Lrec + Limp + Ldt ,                                   (4)
269
      where λ is a term that adjusts the regularization penalty.

305   3.4   T RAINING STRATEGY
306
307
      If parameter values were inappropriately initialized, then gradients of tightly related losses would
      have different directions. We carefully designed the two-step optimization strategy for PIG (Fig-
308
      ure 3) to avoid this ‘conflicting gradients’ problem via pre-training. In step 1, the strategy pre-trains
309
      GLL and PL separately, and finds prior feature relationships Aprior . In this process, only complete
310   data instances are used, so the pre-trained parameters may be sub-optimal. In step 2, GLL and PL are
311   initialized with the pre-trained parameters and then all the parameters in PIG are jointly optimized
312   using the total loss L. This strategy succesfully guides PIG to an improved solution (see Table 3).

      5      C ONCLUSION
523
524
      This paper has presented PIG, a framework for MTL of imputation and prediction as a downstream
525   task. We also proposed a heuristic method to easily find feature relationships that may be helpful
526   to imputation and downstream tasks. The framework can exploit these relationships in graph con-
527   volution to correct the initial imputation, then updates the relationships if necessary to improve the
528   accuracy of both tasks. To achieve the mentioned purposes, the objective function of PIG has several
529   loss terms, which are difficult to optimize simultaneously; thus, we suggested first pre-training GLL
530   and PL and then jointly optimizing the whole PIG. This training strategy successfully balances sev-
531   eral tasks. Empirically, PIG achieved the best performance in imputation and prediction tasks across
532   nine datasets, leading to the average rank of 1.78 for imputation task and 1.22 for downstream tasks.
533
      Consequently, PIG can be the robust and versatile approach in handling tabular data with missing
      values with specific downstream tasks.

      A    T YPES OF MISSINGNESS
649
650
      Missing data are of three types: Missing At Random (MAR), Missing Not At Random (MNAR),
651   and Missing Completely At Random (MCAR). MAR refers to the case that missingness depends
652   on the observed features, MNAR refers to the case that missingness depends on both observed and
653   unobserved data. In MCAR case, missingness is independent of both observed and unobserved data.
654   For example, consider two observed input features x1 and x2 , and an unobserved feature z. If the
655   missingness of x1 depends only on x2 or vice versa, the data is MAR. If the missingness of x1 or
656   x2 additionally depends on z, the data is MNAR. Finally, if missingness is independent of other
657   features, it is MCAR.
658   Among the three types, MCAR represents the simplest type of missing data and thus enables the
659   evaluation of imputation models without the introduction of biases unlike MAR and MNAR. Due
660   to this simplicity, most existing studies on MDI are based on the MCAR assumption as a stepping
661   stone towards more complex scenarios of MAR and MNAR. Therefore, this study also focuses on
662   the MCAR case to make our results be directly comparable with those studies.

686           impute missing values.
687         • Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE): A deep generative method that has a similar model struc-

      H      A BLATION STUDY: T HE EFFECT OF THE INITIAL IMPUTATION METHOD
865
866
      We tested the performance of PIG using different initial imputation methods (Table 6). We compared
867   four simple imputation strategies: SI, MEAN, MEDIAN, and CONSTANT. MEAN and MEDIAN
868   use each feature’s mean or median value to fill in the missing values, and CONSTANT replaces miss-
869   ing values with zeros. We first measured the imputation accuracy of these four imputation methods
870   and tested downstream task accuracy using the data imputed by these methods (the prediction model

873   PIG (imputation method), such as PIG (MEAN).

878   regardless of initial imputation methods, compared to two-phase training methods that use four sim-
879   ple imputation strategies.

881   accuracy much higher than the baseline models regardless of the initial imputation method. This
