009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Broad textual understanding and in-context learning require language models that
012            utilize full document contexts. Due to the implementation challenges associated
013            with directly training long-context models, many methods have been proposed for
014            extending models to handle long contexts. However, owing to differences in data
015            and model classes, it has been challenging to compare these approaches, leading
016            to uncertainty about how to evaluate long-context performance and whether it
017            differs from standard evaluation. We implement a controlled protocol for extension
018
               methods with a standardized evaluation, utilizing consistent base models and
               extension data. Our study yields several insights into long-context behavior. First,
019
               our findings suggest that perplexity can serve as a helpful way to measure how well
020
               models perform on tasks involving longer contexts. Second, current approximate
021            attention methods systematically underperform across long-context tasks. Finally,
022            we confirm that exact fine-tuning based methods are generally effective within their
023            extension range, whereas extrapolation remains challenging. All codebases, models,
024            and checkpoints will be open-sourced, promoting transparency and facilitating
025            further research in this critical area of AI development.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The pretraining data scale of large language models (LLMs) has expanded greatly in recent years
031   with open models trained up to 15T tokens (AI@Meta, 2024). Implementation challenges make it
032
      difficult to fully train models with longer context windows during pretraining (Liu et al., 2023a). Still,
      long-context windows are considered central, as they enable LLMs to perform tasks that require more
033
      extensive textual understanding, such as utilizing information from textbooks (Tanzer et al., 2024),
034
      summarizing novels (Kryściński et al., 2022), and engaging in many-shot learning (Bertsch et al.,
035   2024; Li et al., 2023a).
036
037   As a trade-off, researchers have proposed context extension, where an LLM initially pretrained on
038
      standard sequences is adapted for significantly longer context lengths (Chen et al., 2023a; Peng et al.,
      2023; Han et al., 2023; bloc97, 2023). These methods differ in the type of attention used and in
039
      post-training adaptation techniques. They vary in complexity, training requirements, and qualitatively
040
      exhibit significantly different performance profiles.
041
042   Unfortunately, there is a relatively poor understanding of the quantitative rankings of these different
043   methodologies. Owing to the perceived challenges of evaluation, several new metrics, such as long
044
      context perplexity (Chen et al., 2023a;b; Han et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2024), and retrieval accuracy
      (Mohtashami and Jaggi, 2023; gkamradt, 2023) have been introduced (Bai et al., 2023; An et al.,
045
      2023). However, the differences in long-context extension procedures make it hard to calibrate these
046
      metrics while controlling for other factors.
047
048   In this work, we implement a controlled protocol for context extension. The aim is to compare context
049   extension while removing spurious factors that impact LLM ability.
050   Modeling: We standardize on the same base models for comparison. Different base models behave
051   significantly differently, making it challenging to draw general conclusions. For instance, past work
052   evaluates LM-Infinite (Han et al., 2023) on LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) using different base models
053   (Xiao et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). We use five distinct open-weight base models with varying sizes
      and model families to validate the transferability of our findings from smaller to larger models.

054
      Extensions: We implement various context extension methods within the same framework. We use
055   a standardized recipe to eliminate potential gains from tailored hyperparameters. We also fix each
056   method’s post-training data, utilizing an identical and open-sourced training corpus (Fu et al., 2024;
057   Chen et al., 2023b).
058
      Metrics: We look at both intrinsic metrics, such as perplexity, and extrinsic properties, such as
059
      downstream task performance (Hsieh et al., 2024; gkamradt, 2023; Bai et al., 2023). We consider
060
      metrics within the extension length as well as an extrapolation to longer contexts.
061
062   Our study identifies several takeaways for future research. First, while there have been suggestions
063   that we need new ways to measure performance, our findings show that perplexity does align with
064
      how well models perform to some extent on various tasks in our controlled studies. Though some
      newer attention methods don’t show this pattern as clearly, we generally found that when models got
065
      better at predicting text, they also got better at most other tasks we tested them on.
066
067   Second, we find relatively poor results for approximate attention methods. While they can handle
068   longer length contexts, there generally is a trade-off in terms of accuracy for most of our bench-
069   marks. Exact frozen methods also tend to degrade model performance, showing high sensitivity to
070
      hyperparameters and often failing with a general training recipe.
071   Finally, continual fine-tuning with exact attention generally works well, particularly within the
072   extended context length. Specifically, Dynamic NTK (emozilla, 2023) works best among these
073   methods. Extrapolation to longer lengths remains a challenging problem. The aggregated results
074   across various base models further support the broader applicability of our findings.
075
076   2   R ELATED W ORK
077   Long Context Methods We divide extension methods into three broad classes: exact attention,
078   approximate attention, and context compression. Exact attention methods augment the parameter-
079   ization of attention. Position interpolation (PI) (Chen et al., 2023a), NTK-aware (bloc97, 2023),
080   Dynamic NTK (emozilla, 2023), YaRN (Peng et al., 2023), and CLEX (Chen et al., 2024), all based
081   on RoPE (Su et al., 2021), design position embeddings for length extension. These methods may be
082   applied with fine-tuning or to frozen models. Other exact attention methods focus on training-time
083
      improvements, such as contrastive training (Tworkowski et al., 2023). Approximate attention methods
      uses structured attention approximations to minimize the computational cost of length growth. Chen
084
      et al. (2023b) uses LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) and a specialized local attention mechanism to reduce
085
      further the computational overhead of further fine-tuning with long context. Other approaches break
086   the text into chunks and utilize a well-designed "chunk representation" to retrieve relevant chunks
087   for attention (Mohtashami and Jaggi, 2023; Xiao et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). LM-Infinite and
088   StreamLLM (Han et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023) retain only a few tokens from the beginning of
089   the text and a local window to keep the attention window within the pretrained length. Xu et al.
090   (2024) focuses on using retrievers to retrieve relevant blocks from long documents. Finally, context
091   compression methods, which we do not explore in this work, reduce length extension to length
092   compression via a summarization step (Jiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b).
093   Long Context Evaluation Benchmarks The Long Range Arena (LRA) (Tay et al., 2020) is
094   an early efforts evaluating the proficiency of processing long contexts. Since then, a growing
095   number of benchmarks have emerged, including LongBench (Bai et al., 2023), LEval (An et al.,
096   2023), and LooGLE (Li et al., 2023c). These benchmarks are a mixture of diverse downstream
097   tasks explicitly tailored to assess the capabilities of LLMs in understanding and generating lengthy
098   contexts. Among these benchmarks, LongBench stands out for its inclusion of diverse sequences
099
      with varying lengths, distributions, patterns, languages, and domains, enabling a comprehensive,
      nuanced evaluation. In addition to evaluating LLMs’ performance on downstream NLP tasks, there is
100
      another line of benchmarks that specifically focuses on assessing particular aspects of long context
101
      processing ability Liu et al. (2023b); Hsieh et al. (2024). For instance, Mohtashami and Jaggi
102   (2023) propose the passkey retrieval task to challenge a language model to accurately locate and
103   retrieve a simple passkey (a five-digit random number) in a long context sequence. Similarly, the
104   Needle in a Haystack (gkamradt, 2023) test requires the model to accurately recite the information
105   from a specified sentence(the "needle"). However, most existing works mainly focus on evaluating
106   mainstream commercial models (e.g. GPT-4 and Claude), open-source base models, or just perform
107   individual evaluations of a few long context methods. There is a lack of comprehensive, yet controlled
      evaluation on long-context extension techniques themselves.

194   An alternative approach is to modify the attention function itself. Approaches to handling longer con-
195
      texts fall into two main categories: approximate attention and attention modification. In approximate

196

197

198   sliding window attention, and retrieval attention.

      Base Model All models start from an identical base checkpoint. We choose to use five different base
246   models LLaMA2-7B, LLaMA2-13B, LLaMA2-70B (Touvron et al., 2023), Phi-2(Javaheripi et al.,
247   2023), LLaMA3-8B(Dubey et al., 2024)for context extension experiments, to verify whether the trends
248   and analyses we observed are consistent across different base models, thereby avoiding potential
249   over-generalization. Note that in our main findings, we only report results with LLaMA2-7B
250   base model to maintain conciseness and avoid redundancy as we find most of general findings from
251   LLaMA2-7B can be transferred to all other models. Results from other models are provided in
252   Appendix 7.7.
253   Fine-Tuning We sample 1B tokens from a long-context data mixture following Fu et al. (2024).
254   The data details are reported in Appendix 7.9. We focus on extending the context window from 4k
255   to 32k since most benchmarks require contexts under 32k. We maintain a fixed training recipe to
256   ensure consistency across all models (Chen et al., 2023b). We follow existing practices by keeping an
257   exponential moving average (EMA) of model weights with a constant decay, a linear learning rate
258   warm-up and zero weight decay. Most training hyperparameters are based on (Fu et al., 2024), with
259   the learning rate set to 2 × 10−5 . Our experiments are done on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
260   For LongLora, we fine-tune the weights of the LoRA adapter with trainable embedding and nor-
261   malization, then merge these trainable weights with the LLaMA2 base model for evaluation. For
262   Landmark Attention, the training context length is set to 512, with a block size of 64. For CLEX, we
263   set the max scale factor to 32 and use the SiLU activation function.
264   We reuse the original scale factor to maintain consistency for NTK, YaRN, and Position Interpolation
265   methods. However, this base factor significantly degrades continual fine-tuned models, particularly
266   causing performance deterioration in shorter sequences. Therefore, we conduct a grid search to
267   determine a better scale factor for different input lengths for NTK-RoPE method. Based on our
268   findings, we follow and improve upon Fu et al. (2024) to set the scale factor for NTK-RoPE method.
269   The scale factor and its relationship with perplexity are reported in the Appendix 7.11. Please refer to
      the Appendix 7.8 for detailed hyperparameter setups.

      Metrics We consider two sets of intrinsic metrics. The first is based on perplexity. We use the
286
      book corpus PG19 (Rae et al., 2019) and the Proof-pile dataset (Azerbayev et al., 2023) to evaluate
287   the long sequence language modeling performances. Following Press et al. (2022), all perplexity
288   evaluations are calculated using a sliding window with a window size of 256.
289
290
      The second is based on retrieval. We focus on the needle in the haystack task (gkamradt, 2023)(NIAH).
      NIAH involves identifying a specific, relevant piece of information (the "needle") within a large
291
      set of irrelevant data (the "haystack"). This task is commonly used to test the precision and recall
292
      capabilities of LLMs in scenarios where the relevant data is sparse and surrounded by a significant
293   amount of noise. Additionally, we evaluate with RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024). RULER enhances the
294   standard NIAH test by incorporating variations with different types and quantities of needles with
295   new task categories, such as multi-hop tracing and aggregation.
296
      For extrinsic metrics, we consider a collection of tasks. LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) is a family of
297
      bilingual, multitask evaluations for long-context understanding widely used in measuring the long-
298
      context abilities of LLMs (Jin et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). LongBench includes
299   single-document question answering, multi-document QA, summarization, few-shot learning, and
300   code completion. We follow Bai et al. (2023) to evaluate the models on 32k context window sizes by
301   truncating the prompt from the middle when the task length exceeds a designated context window
302   size. We also consider the ManyShots tasks, where the long-context model will be given several
303   examples as prompts. We use the Trec News (Li and Roth, 2002) dataset for this task.

307   5.1     R ESULT OVERVIEW
308
      Table 1 overviews the results across both types of evaluation. The main result demonstrate that
309
      fine-tuned exact attention methods for long contexts, such as NTK-32K and YARN, consistently
310   outperform approximate attention methods by a significant margin. This suggests that trading accuracy
311   for speed in approximate attention methods can result in a loss of important reasoning capabilities,
312   particularly for retrieval-based tasks. The performance disparity highlights the importance of exact
313   attention in maintaining high accuracy over extended contexts, emphasizing the need for careful
314   consideration of attention type in model design for long-context tasks. We now consider each type of
315   result in more detail.

526   7              L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
527
528   Our study has several limitations. The experiments are confined to context extensions up to 32k
529   tokens, and behavior patterns may vary at longer extensions. Additionally, our standardized training
530
      protocol with fixed hyperparameters might disproportionately affect certain models’ performance.
      Furthermore, our perplexity findings may be specific to our experimental settings and may not
531
      generalize to models beyond our test scope.
532
533   In this paper, we use a standardized approach to assess the performance of various long-context
534   methods in LLMs. Our study underscores the role of perplexity as a crucial, performance indicator
535   at length and highlights the trade-offs inherent in different attention mechanisms. We analyze the
536
      strengths and weaknesses of various approaches, providing valuable insights for future research. All
      our resources, including codebases, models, and checkpoints, will be open-sourced upon acceptance,
537
      fostering future advancements in this pivotal area of AI research.

794   7.2   K ENDALL CORRELATION OF DOWNSTREAM TASK PERFORMANCE AND PERPLEXITY
795
796   We use a non-parametric method, the ken-tau correlation to evaluate the correlation between down-
797   stream task performance and perplexity.
798
799   Consistency Across Tasks The results show a strong and statistically significant negative correla-
800   tion between perplexity and downstream performance for most tasks. This supports the claim that
801   lower perplexity values are generally associated with better downstream task performance.
802
803   Task-Specific Observations The strongest correlations are observed for Needle and RULER, where
804   Kendall’s tau indicates a robust alignment between perplexity and task performance rankings. For
805   Mshots, the correlation is moderate and statistically weaker, suggesting that perplexity’s predictive
806   ability may vary slightly depending on the task.
807
808   Impact of Perplexity Range Even when perplexity values are close (e.g., below 6), perplexity
809   rankings remain a reliable indicator of downstream performance. However, the narrower range may
      amplify the observed performance differences, highlighting the need for nuanced interpretation.
