009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal relations can materialize in many different ways. In their simplest form
012            –typically assumed in classical causal models and discovery approaches–, sim-
013            ilar variations of a cause lead to similar variations of an effect. However, this
014            ‘smoothness’ requires an observation of cause and effect at just the right scales.
015            Unfortunately, this conflicts with records often encountered in the real world, which
016            mix continuous measurements with once-in-a-while observations of sparse events.
017            Compactly modeling the causal effects between (discrete) events and continuous
018
               states is difficult to achieve with classical causal models. To ease this situation, we
               leverage transformations that derive different scales of observables, respectively, to
019
               decompose relations and allow for compact causal representations, called Deriva-
020
               tive Causal Models (DCM). We instantiate them using integral and derivative
021            transforms and demonstrate that the resulting Differential Causal Models (∂CM)
022            can be discovered automatically from data.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   There is a growing interest in modeling causal representations in everyday scenarios, such as those
028   commonly encountered in machine learning and applied settings (Schölkopf et al., 2021; Schölkopf,
029   2022; Lippe et al., 2022; Berrevoets et al., 2023; Runge et al., 2023). While new algorithms are
030   developed to handle an ever-increasing range of scenarios, the underlying causal representations
031   have changed little over the years. As a result, today’s causal models often lag behind when it
032   comes to concisely representing complex situations, and it can be quite cumbersome to model certain
033
      everyday applications. Classical modeling of even the simplest causal relations, expressed by the
      simple proposition, “Watering the flower pot will develop a flower”, requires knowledge of several
034
      (hidden) causal variables, as shown in Fig. 1 (‘classical’). Time-dependent relations, such as the
035
      lasting humidity state of the soil after the pot is watered, must be explicitly modeled by a human
036   practitioner. While this could be done with lagged causal mechanisms between the event of watering
037   and each time step of the flower size, the resulting representations are rather convoluted and more
038   complex to discover than the connections we want to convey. The resulting representations are rather
039   convoluted and more complex than the simple connection we want to convey. It would be more useful
040   to automatically introduce data transformations between scales of observation, such as sparse events
041   and continuous states, to compactly model such relations. Our approach focuses on relating these
042   different scales of observation, as current methods are ignorant of them.
043   The given example shows possible different qualities of variables: In general, information integration
044   allows us to correlate events that occur at a particular point in time with states that persist even after
045   the initial event has passed. Our approach is particularly useful for modeling systems that relate
046   different scales, such as sparsely occurring information, to persistent states. We anticipate a wide
047   range of applications ranging from modeling ‘everyday’ causality (e.g. Halpern (2016); Gerstenberg
048   (2022); Zhou et al. (2023)), process analysis (Van Der Aalst, 2012), and climate systems (Runge
049   et al., 2019a; Camps-Valls et al., 2023). We present the advantages of DCM motivated by an everyday
050
      introductory example. Furthermore, we show discovery on synthetic time series (Sec. 4.1) and an
      example of extracting rules of game dynamics (Sec. 4.2).

132   Related Work on Dynamical Systems and Time Series. Extensive research has been devoted to the
133   causal study of dynamical systems (Friston et al., 2003; Mooij et al., 2013; Blom et al., 2020; Peters
134   et al., 2022; Löwe et al., 2022) and the general modeling of these (Hyttinen et al., 2012; Mooij et al.,

136
      Most often, these works are concerned with solving (e.g. finding unique solutions or converged
      states of) differential equations (Iwasaki and Simon, 1994; Dash, 2005; Blom et al., 2020; Blom and
137
      Mooij, 2023). Such dynamics are common in causal modeling of climate systems (Zscheischler et al.,
138
      2020; Camps-Valls et al., 2023; Runge et al., 2023) and in general with time-lagged relationships
139   (Peters et al., 2013; Saggioro et al., 2020; Runge et al., 2019b; Gerhardus et al., 2023; Runge et al.,
140   2023). While our models can be applied to dynamical systems, our goal is to relate variables between
141   different scales rather than to find time-lagged relations. Thus, we do not aim to model feedback
142   systems, but to use differential relations as constraints between related quantities. In general, we
143   focus on identifying instantaneous causal relations within such systems.

      Decomposing Equations. In theory, arbitrarily complex functional relationships can exist between
163   any two causally related variables. However, such complex mechanisms are generally difficult
164   to understand and difficult to discover. In the simplest case, we can think of causal models that
165   contain only linear mechanisms. We propose to simplify causal models by considering multiple
166   transformed versions of a variable simultaneously, with the goal of reducing the complexity of their
167   structural equations. The transformations that translate between different scales of observation are now
168   considered part of the variables rather than the causal mechanism, and allow expert knowledge to be
169   used to select appropriate transformations that can potentially uncover low-complexity relations. For
170   two variables X, Y connected by a structural equation Y := f (X), there may exist transforms
171   φY , φX with φY ◦ Y = fˆ(φX ◦ X) such that fˆ : X → Y is linear for the right scales of
172   observation. In other words, this would allow the decomposition of a causal mechanism: Y :=

329   of a car, its derivative ‘speed’ and integral ‘distance traveled’ values may be read from instruments
330   and provided within the data. While previous work considers the time aspect of the data as a means
331   to infer delayed causal relationships, we use the time dimension exclusively to obtain integral and/or
332   derivative values.

361   4     D ISCOVERING D IFFERENTIAL C AUSAL M ODELS FROM DATA
362
363
      The previous sections were primarily concerned with the formalization of derivative and differential
364
      causal models for modeling systems and processes. We recognize that –in the field of causality– there
365
      is a great interest in the (automated) discovery of causal structures from data. In the following, we
366   develop the Greedy Derivative Model Search (GDS) algorithm for discovering differential causal
367   models using a score-based approach.
368
369
      Navigating the class of Markov-equivalent models, as in the GES algorithm (Chickering, 2002),
      is challenging for DCM because our constraints impose additional restrictions on the set of edges
370
      that can be added at each step. In particular, we are often prevented from adding or inverting
371
      certain edges as would be done in classical GES. We take inspiration from the GLOBE algorithm
372   of Mian et al. (2021), which exploits the minimal representation length (Janzing and Schölkopf,
373   2010) to still perform a graph search over the class of SCM. We also modify the standard Bayesian
374   Information Criterion (BIC; Schwarz (1978)) evaluation to significantly improve performance in
375   our setting. As a major benefit, we can verify that our new DCM constraints can be applied during
376   the edge selection phase. Pseudocode for the algorithm can be found in Appendix D. A Python
377   implementation of our algorithm and the evaluation scripts used can be found in the code repository:
      https://anonymous.4open.science/r/derivativeCausalModels-E62B/.

      4.1   G REEDY S EARCH FOR D ISCOVERING D IFFERENTIAL C AUSAL M ODELS
400
401
402
      Like GLOBE, our GDS algorithm consists of a forward and a backward phase that are repeated many
      times until convergence. In a forward phase, the algorithm iteratively selects new edges to add until
403
      no more edges can be added without increasing the score. The score of each edge addition is the
404
      difference between the unaltered graph and the graph with the candidate edge added. Since this is
405   a greedy heuristic, it runs the risk of getting stuck in local optima. For example, a node initially
406   explained by a single edge with a high score might be better explained by two separate weaker edges
407   added later. To eliminate such edges, an additional backward phase is performed, removing individual
408   edges from the parents and testing whether the score still improves (Mian et al., 2021). We repeat
409   this process up to 5 times or until convergence.
410
      In a first step we augment the data with transformed aspects of a variable. As discussed earlier,
411                                 (l)        (l±1)
      we recursively compute Xt from Xt              and require that the starting values of the previous time

418   To make the algorithm follow the DCM constraints, we impose several constraints on the selection of
419   possible candidate edges. Specifically, we impose the following constraints (see Fig. 3 (left)):

421   Since the transformation constraints between different aspects are not reflected in the data, we obtain

435   Bayesian Information Criterion. We use the BIC to estimate the cost of adding a new edge to a
436   model. Specifically, we compute the common formulation BIC = n ∗ ln(σ̂ 2 ) + k ∗ ln(n) (Priestley,
437   1981), where σ̂ 2 are the prediction residuals after linear regression (and polynomial fitting; see below),
438   k is the number of model parameters, and n is the number of samples. As a first step, we fit the
439
      parental weights using linear regression. In addition, when fitting the parents, we apply the LASSO
      regularization (Tibshirani, 1996) to zero out the weights of non-parent candidates in cases where
440
      ordinary least squares would still assign some average weight. LASSO introduces an α parameter that
441
      balances the goodness of fit with the sparsity of the weight vector via L1 regularization. Whenever a
442   previously included parent weight regresses to zero, it is removed from the set of parent candidates.
443
444   Polynomial Fitting. Because the data have aspects at different scales, the residual of a variable
445
      (especially integral aspects) is most likely to exhibit non-Gaussian behavior. Computing the variance
      of an integral aspect –that is, over the residuals after linearly fitting the parents– as is done in the
446
      standard BIC calculation, provides a strong incentive for the algorithm to predict integral aspects
447
      first in order to reduce excessive variance. To account for the potentially nonlinear behavior of
448   the residuals, we fit multiple polynomials to a given degree and only then compute the remaining
449   variance (see Alg. 4). The parameters needed to fit the polynomial are counted in the number of
450   model parameters k. The polynomial fit with the lowest BIC is then selected for further comparison
451   with other edges. Our experiments confirm our modeling choice, as we observe a severe drop in
452   performance when no polynomial fit is performed on the linearly fitted residuals (see Table 3).
453
      Evaluation. We evaluate our algorithm on random graphs with 6 nodes and a default edge proba-
454   bility of 50%. (See Appendix D for extensive evaluations varying the number of nodes and edge
455   percentages.) We generate one derivative and one integral aspect per variable and sample 10 initial
456   configurations. Each configuration is run for 50 time steps. Exact parameter details are given in
457   Appendix D.1. We measure edge recall and precision (higher is better ↑), as well as structural
458   intervention distance (Peters and Bühlmann, 2015, SID) (and structural Hamming distance (Acid
459   and de Campos, 2003; Tsamardinos et al., 2006, SHD) (lower is better ↓) in Appendix D) on the
460   coarsened graph. (We are not aware of any variant that is able to measure SID in the presence of
461   transformed variables). Full experimental details are given in Appendix D.1.
462   To the best of our knowledge, no causal discovery algorithm has been proposed to discover instanta-
463   neous effects under transformation constraints. Similar baselines, such as GES or GLOBE, do not
464   exploit constraints and do not guarantee that they lead to valid DCM. Nevertheless, GES should
465   be able to identify the causal relationships when given the individual aspects. We therefore report
466   the performance for GES as a baseline algorithm, unaware of the constraints that may be present in
467   the system. (Exact evaluation details are given in Appendix D.1). We evaluate each configuration
468
      over 20 seeds and report the results in Figure 3. We vary the α of the LASSO regression, compare
      it to ordinary least squares regression, and evaluate our algorithm without polynomial fitting of the
469
      residuals. We find that the small LASSO regularization with α = 0.01 outperforms ordinary least
470
      squares regression in terms of precision, while linear regression is slightly better in terms of recall and
471   SID score. As expected, fitting without polynomial fitting of the residuals significantly reduces the
472   performance. Overall, we observe a high standard deviation, which we attribute to the aforementioned
473   problem of a difficult to navigate search space. Selecting the wrong edges (or edge directions) at the
474   beginning of the algorithm makes recovery difficult.

519   5     C ONCLUSION
520
521   The observation of causal relations between single events and their continuous effects is a common
522   occurrence, particularly within everyday situations. The ability to concisely represent such causal
523   connections is a challenging, yet key requirement for the correct application of causal models. To
524   the best of our knowledge, we are first to conceptualize the notion of scales in DCM and ∂CM.
525
      The approach enables practitioners to model real-world scenarios more concisely, thereby allowing
      them to reason more effectively on the causal relations across different scales. In particular, the
526
      seamless embedding of integral aspects is if great importance, as they accumulate information about
527
      the past states into current observations. This property may be employed to reason backward in
528   time (e.g. performing Taylor approximations with the help of derivative aspects) to reconstruct
529   process sequences or trace back root causes using observations alone. To discover DCM and ∂CM
530   automatically from data, we propose the Greedy Derivative Model Search algorithm, which is able to
531   recover DCM from data under the presence of aspect transformations.
532
      Limitations and Broader Impact. The examples provided considered the instantaneous effects
533
      between variables. In other words, our experiments did not consider time-lagged relations between
534   variables, apart from the aggregational dynamics of integral quantities. We expect future work to
535   also incorporate time dependencies, including the incorporation of lagged connections. Although
536   our definition of DCM has several useful properties, it also imposes certain restrictions on the graph
537   structures that can be modelled. We have relaxed these constraints in a discussion in Appendix C
538   and hope to develop more general notions of DCM in the future. In general, we expect our newly
539   proposed DCM and ∂CM to enable practitioners to model real-world problems more concisely and
      identify qualitative relations more easily.
