009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Post-training quantization of Large Language Models (LLMs) has proven effec-
012            tive in reducing the computational requirements for running inference on these
013            models. In this study, we focus on a straightforward question: When aiming for
014            a specific accuracy or perplexity target for low-precision quantization, how many
015            high-precision numbers or calculations are required to preserve as we scale LLMs
016            to larger sizes? We first introduce a critical metric named the quantization ratio,
017            which compares the number of parameters quantized to low-precision arithmetic
018            against the total parameter count. Through extensive and carefully controlled ex-
019            periments across different model families, arithmetic types, and quantization gran-
020
               ularities (e.g. layer-wise, matmul-wise), we identify two central phenomenons. 1)
               The larger the models, the better they can preserve performance with an increased
021
               quantization ratio, as measured by perplexity in pre-training tasks or accuracy in
022
               downstream tasks. 2) The finer the granularity of mixed-precision quantization
023            (e.g., matmul-wise), the more the model can increase the quantization ratio. We
024            believe these observed phenomena offer valuable insights for future AI hardware
025            design and the development of advanced Efficient AI algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across a range of nat-
031
      ural language processing (NLP) tasks (Brown et al., 2020), and state-of-the-art models have ranged
032
      from 1.6B parameters (Radford et al. (2019)) to 1T parameters (Fedus et al. (2022)) in recent years.
033   Recent work has driven the development of even larger models given findings that LLMs exhibit
034   emergent capabilities at increased parameter counts (Wei et al., 2022a). As such, researchers have
035   endeavoured to understand the scaling laws of LLMs by characterising how the required number of
036   training tokens scales with parameter count to train compute-optimal models under a fixed compute
037   budget (Kaplan et al. (2020), Hoffmann et al. (2022)). These works provide insight on how to best
038   allocate resources in training increasingly large LLMs.
039   Despite recent scaling trends, the substantial size of LLMs and their accompanying computational
040   demands require significant energy and hardware resources. For instance, inference deployment of
041   the 405B parameter LLaMA-3.1 model (Dubey et al., 2024) requires 8 NVIDIA H100 GPUs to
042   store its 810GB of model weights, and consumes over 4500 Watts of power (based on the average
043   power consumption of 600W per H100 GPU). As such, quantization is emerging as a promising
044   solution to increase the accessibility of LLMs by reducing their memory requirement and inference
045   cost. Prior work has shown that weights and activations in pretrained transformer blocks often yields
046   magnitude outliers, which has been addressed by casting outliers to high precision, while quantizing
047
      the rest of the network to low precision (Dettmers et al., 2022). Such mixed-precision partitioning
      has been shown to preserve model performance with significant savings in memory footprint and
048
      model inference serving throughput.
049
050   With the increased usage of quantization to address the challenges of LLM deployment, and mo-
051   tivated by the importance of understanding systematic scaling laws in guiding further research in
052   mixed-precision quantization, we seek to answer an under-explored question: in an optimal mixed-
053
      precision mapping, how does the required ratio of low precision components change as model size
      increases? Alternatively, what are the scaling laws for mixed quantization in LLMs?

091   2.1   Q UANTIZATION O UTLIERS AND M IXED Q UANTIZATION
092
093
      Weight and Activation Outliers in LLMs A weight or activation value is considered an outlier
      when there is a significant deviation from its mean distribution. These values make traditional uni-
094
      form quantization less effective due to the large incurred dynamic range. In fact, activation outliers
095
      have been observed more frequently in large transformer-based models (Wei et al., 2022b; Zhang
096   et al., 2023a) as deeply cascaded layers accumulate quantization errors. To address this problem, two
097   strategies are widely adopted; in weight-only quantization, weights are casted to low precision while
098   activations are left in a higher-precision format such as FP16 or BF16 (Frantar et al., 2022; Chee
099   et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024). Meanwhile, weight-activation quantization relies on methods such
100   magnitude transfer from activations to weights using invertible scale matrices (Xiao et al., 2023) to
101   alleviate the effect of activation outliers before quantizing both weights and activations (Wei et al.,
102   2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023).
103   Recent works have proposed novel numerical formats with shared scaling/exponent components,
104   which better accommodate the dynamic range of outliers (Zhang et al., 2023a; Rouhani et al.,
105   2023a; Zou et al., 2024). For example, MXINT (Darvish Rouhani et al., 2020) is new standard
106   for hardware-efficient numerical formats sharing an exponent across a block of mantissas (Rouhani
107   et al., 2023b). The hardware efficiency of these methods often outperforms standard low-precision
      floating-point computation, although custom hardware support is required to be used in practice.

      Mixed-precision quantization Mixed precision approaches involve partitioning a model’s pa-
109   rameters into both high-precision and low-precision components, which have been shown to better
110   preserve model performance relative to uniform quantization. This is primarily seen in models that
111   exhibit different sensitivities to quantization at various layers. Some mixed-precision LLM quanti-
112   zation work adopts the concept of weight salience to guide the search for fine-grained bit allocation.
113   The first order (Li et al., 2023a) or second order weight gradient (Huang et al., 2024) have been used
114   to form such salience metrics, such that salient layers are left in higher precision while the rest are
115   casted to low precision. There are also works performing the search in an end-to-end style with the
116   quantized model performance as the objective, such as the accuracy on a downstream task (Zhang
117
      et al., 2023a). In both cases, mixed-precision can be seen as a promising approach to provide loss-
      less reduction in LLM memory requirements, reducing average bitwidths below levels achievable
118
      through uniform quantization (as in Badri & Shaji (2023); Lin et al. (2024); Chee et al. (2024)).
119
120
      Mixed-precision inference Mixed-precision inference methods targeting GPUs usually adopt reg-
121
      ular mixed-precision strategies and computation patterns; The authors of GPTQ3.int8() (Dettmers
122
      et al., 2022) decompose the matrix multiplication in every linear layer into two submatrix-
123   multiplications based on the activation magnitudes, achieving 2-3× inference speedup by casting
124   the low-magnitude submatrix to low precision. SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2023) represents a weight
125   matrix with grouped 3-bit integers and less than 1% sensitive weight elements with FP16 values,
126   achieving 2× speed up compared to a quantized and sparse PyTorch baseline. These approaches en-
127   able reducing model size, but additional careful treatment is needed to improve inference throughput.
128   For example, in Li et al. (2023a), mixed precision LLM quantization at 2-bit and 3-bit showed no
129   speedup compared to 4-bit, due to less efficient utilization of memory bandwidth. On the other hand,
130   Any-Precision LLM (Park et al., 2024) achieve throughput scaling at various precisions by provid-
131
      ing CUDA kernels with a novel weight packing approach following a bitplane layout, achieving
      1.3-1.8× speedup on mobile and edge devices. Additionaly, works such as FlightLLM achieve high
132
      throughput by leveraging custom hardware designs (Zeng et al., 2024).
133
134
      Mixed-precision training Mixed-precision quantization has also been adopted in training to re-
135
      duce the large memory footprint of gradient descent, which requires storage of optimizer states and
136
      gradients in addition to forward activations. It’s been shown the training process can tolerate ag-
137   gressive quantization and correct quantization noise in some components. Micikevicius et al. (2017)
138   is the pioneering work proposing storage of all weights, activations and gradients in FP16, while
139   updating a copy of weights in FP32. This work also proposed scaling up the forward pass loss
140   and unscaling the gradient before weight update to avoid underutilization of the FP16 representable
141   range, leading to half the memory requirement and speed-ups of 2-6× relative to FP32 training.
142
      Recently, more aggressive quantization has been studied for mixed-precision training. Mellempudi
143   et al. (2019) trains models with E5M2 FP8, maintaining a master copy of the weights in FP16,
144   and dynamically adjusting the scaling factor every few iterations. Hybrid-FP8 (Sun et al., 2019)
145   improves FP8 training by using E4M3 for forward propagation and E5M2 for backward propagation,
146   leading to matching performance to models trained with FP32. Popular implementation of FP8
147   mixed-precision training like TransformerEngine1 has achieved a training acceleration around 3-4×
148   compared to FP16 mixed-precision training.
149
150   2.2      S CALING L AWS OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL T RAINING
151
152   Enhanced language modelling performance at larger model sizes has led to interests in characterising
153   scaling laws of LLM performance with respect to parameter count, training compute budget and
154
      number of training tokens. Kaplan et al. (2020) showed, though empirical analysis, that Transformer
      performance follows a power law trend with each of these factors. The authors proposed that for any
155
      9× increase in parameter count, dataset size should be increased by a factor of approximately 5×
156
      to avoid a performance penalty, suggesting that higher performance gains are observed from scaling
157   parameter count than dataset size under a fixed compute budget. Contrastingly, Hoffmann et al.
158   (2022) argue that existing open weight LLMs are undertrained relative to their size, and parameter
159   count should be increased in line with the number of training tokens. The findings from Pearce &
160   Song (2024) later explained the discrepancy between Kaplan and Hoffman, reaffirming the validity
161

      of the Chincilla scaling laws and highlighting the need for high quality datasets for Language Model
163   training.
164
165   Despite the reconciliation of Kaplan’s scaling laws, we see the continued trend of scaling LLM
166
      sizes, partly validated by the findings from Wei et al. (2022a) regarding the unpredictable emer-
      gence of abilities in larger LLMs. The authors characterise how LLM performance in few-shot tasks
167
      including arithmetic, question answering and multi-task language understanding emerges beyond
168
      certain size thresholds, despite not being observable in small models - for example, performance on
169   arithmetic tasks from BIG-Bench is approximately random for GPT-3 models up to 13B parameters
170   and LaMDA models up to 68B parameters, sharply rising thereafter. The authors note there are few
171   compelling explanations for these emergence phenomena, although the required model depth for
172   reasoning tasks may be correlated with the number of reasoning steps. At any rate, these findings
173   raise the question of what emergent phenomena may observed for even larger models, and highlight
174   the importance of understanding scaling laws across a wide range of model sizes, without extrapo-
175   lating observations from small models.

219   Many studies have empirically demonstrated that larger models are more amenable to quantization
220
      (Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023), and in this work, we offer a systematic perspective on
      this finding by formulating the aforementioned Scaling Laws. Crucially, we illustrate that model
221
      size (Law 1) exhibit exponential scaling relative to the “ease of quantization” while quantization
222
      granularity (Law 2) exhibit power function scaling relative to the “ease of quantization” in a
223   mixed-precision setting. These observation suggest that the hidden law demonstrated under mixed
224   quantization settings are non-trivial. Here, we refer to the “ease of quantization” as the proportion
225   of high-precision components necessary to maintain model performance.

      4.1       E XPERIMENT S ETUP

231   Models and benchmarks We evaluate a range of model families, including LLama-2 (Touvron
232   et al., 2023), Gemma-2 (Team et al., 2024) and QWen-1.5 (Bai et al., 2023), at sizes ranging from
233   0.5B to 70B. We consider both pre-trained and instruction-tuned models. The primary results are
234   reported for QWen-1.5 as a wide range of pretrained checkpoints is available, enabling detailed
235   analysis of the proposed scaling laws. Further results are included in Appendix A.
236   We evaluate LLMs on WikiText2 (Merity et al., 2016), SlimPajama (Soboleva et al., 2023), Al-
237   paca (Taori et al., 2023), and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021b;a). For WikiText2, SlimPa-
238   jama, and Alpaca, we sub-sample the dataset used during search. For MMLU, we use
239   lm-evaluation-harness (Gao et al., 2023) to report the average accuracy over all subsets.
240
241   Quantization methods We use MXINT-4 as the low-precision format and BF16 as the high pre-
242   cision format. We also consider FP4 for low precision, which has garnered increased attention in
243   recent works (Liu et al., 2023b; Xia et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2023b; Xia et al., 2024a). For FP4,
244
      we use 2-bit exponent and 1-bit mantissa, which offers the highest performance without any post-
      quantization fine-tuning, as per Dotzel et al. (2024). We quantize both weights and activations for
245
      MXINT-4, however activations are kept in BF16 for the FP4 experiments as activation quantization
246
      was found to cause a detrimental effect in model performance at this precision (Liu et al., 2023a).
247
248
      Mixed-precision strategy For our primary experiments, we perform quantization at two granular-
249   ities: layer-wise and matmul-wise. In the former, the quantization ratio is determined by the number
250   of transformer layers casted to low precision. In the latter, we consider the precision for each indi-
251   vidual matrix multiplication; for example, QKV projections, multiplication of the attention scores
252   and MLP layers can each be quantized separately, even within the same layer. We find a solution for
253   Equation (1) by running random search with a trial number of 50 at 1024 subsamples per iteration.
254   We justify these choices in Appendix D and Appendix E. The inner loop of the search conducts
255   post-training quantization (PTQ), and the entire search process involves no training.
256
257   Platform and GPU hours We perform experiments on a 20-node cluster with eight A6000 48GB
258
      GPUs, a 256-core AMD EPYC processor and 1024GB RAM in each node. Experiments of models
      larger than 30B are performed on a cluster of DGX A100 eight-GPU pods. The effective run time
259
      of the experiments in total is approximately 15k A6000 GPU hours and 5k A100 GPU hours. We
260
      also spend around 1k GPU hours tuning search hyper-parameters, such as the number of trials.

420   As stated in Section 2.1, recent LLM quantization methods adopt fine-grained quantization, meaning
421   tensors are split into small blocks, quantized and then scaled individually. In this subsection, we em-
422   pirically verify our Scaling Law 2 by performing mixed-precision quantization search at block sizes
423   of 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, and 512. Additionally, we perform per-vector (per-row, per-column) scaled
424   quantization (Dai et al., 2021) and per-tensor scaled quantization. In per-vector scaled quantization,
425   each row of activations (corresponding to a column of weights) shares the same scaling factor. In
426   per-tensor scaled quantization, all elements in a tensor share the same scaling factor. To fit per-vector
427   and per-tensor quantization to the same plot, we consider the averaged number of elements in each
428
      vector or tensor as the block size.
429   Smaller block sizes enable lower quantization error and better model performance, since a block’s
430   scaling factor depends on the maximum element magnitude within the block. When a large scaling
431   factor is assigned to accommodate the activation outliers (as discussed in Section 2.1), the round-off
      error of remaining elements in the block can cause performance degradation. However, decreasing

470   5                                    D ISCUSSION
471
      Implications on AI Inference Hardware and System Designs A critical finding of this paper is
472
      the observed correlation between model size and quantization ratio, indicating that larger models
473   can accommodate exponentially more low-precision components without performance degradation.
474   This validates the recent trend of increasing support for low-precision arithmetic computation in
475   Deep Learning accelerators such as GPUs and TPUs. For example, the tensor cores of the H100
476   SXM achieve 3958 TFLOPs when operating at FP8 precision, while the compute capacity is ap-
477   proximately halved to 1979 TFLOPs at FP16 precision (Choquette, 2022). The insight from the
478   first LLM-MPQ scaling law, showing that larger LLMs require more low-precision computational
479   resources compared to their smaller counterparts, highlights the need for increased low-precision
480   resources in future hardware devices for efficient serving of large models.
481   Additionally, we’ve shown through the second LLM-MPQ scaling law that finer granularity in
482   mixed-precision approaches enables a higher quantization ratio when the model size is fixed. This
483   insight has direct implications in the design of parallelization strategies for multi-device or multi-
484   node environments (Zheng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b). Coarse-grained mixed-precision strategies
485   such as layer-wise mappings can generally be handled as a device allocation problem. Meanwhile,
      finer-grained mixed-precision approaches such as at the vector/column level necessitate more careful


                                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      handling, potentially demanding compiler-level partitioning strategies or even dedicated hardware
487   designs to realize the theoretical performance improvements.
488
489   Extension to Further Architectures and Arithmetic Formats It is natural to consider whether
490
      the observed findings in this work extend to larger LLMs, such as the recently released Llama-3.1-
      405b (Dubey et al., 2024), although the range of available pre-trained checkpoints is limited, due
491
      to the significant cost of training larger models. Additionaly, the same trends could be explored
492
      in different architectures including Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models such as Mixtral (Jiang et al.,
493   2024) and multimodal models such as Pixtral (Mistral AI). Finally, further arithmetic formats such
494   as ternary (Chen et al., 2024) and additional configurations from the MXINT (Rouhani et al., 2023a)
495   standard offer opportunities for further exploration. One specific challenge is the quantization ap-
496   proach used in this paper is emulated quantization following Zhang et al., where it incurs more
497   computation than natively supported FP16 inference, hence impedes the evaluation on larger mod-
498   els (eg. 400B). A possible future direction would be to test these scaling laws on large models using
499   actual MXINT4 and FP4 quantization upon the availability of compatible hardware.
500   LLM Evaluation: Navigating Layers of Complexity A number of challenges were faced during
501   experimentation regarding the reproducibility of accuracy and perplexity metrics for pre-trained
502   models. For example, the evaluation methodology for Llama-2 was extrapolated from the official
503   repository 3 since the official evaluation code was not released, leading to a gap between the reported
504   performance and our own evaluation. These discrepancies highlight the importance of open and
505   reliable benchmarks for pretrained language models 4 .
506   An additional observation was that despite the breadth of downstream tasks used to evaluate LLMs
507   in the literature, not all are effective in capturing the scaling trend of LLM performance at various
508   quantization methodologies. Some widely reported metrics, such as Wikitext2 and LAMBADA (Pa-
509   perno et al., 2016), showed negligible sensitivity to the quantization ratio in performance degrada-
510   tion across the models we evaluated, showing that the bias introduced by various downstream tasks
511   needs to be carefully considered when searching for the optimal quantization strategy for deploy-
512   ment. The core results in this work were reported using a subset of the SlimPajama dataset, as
513
      this led to a higher sensitivity to quantization ratio compared to instruction-tuning datasets such as
      Alpaca, as shown in Appendix C. Another important reason for this decision was to ensure that
514
      quantization search was performed under a similar data distribution to common LLM pretraining
515
      datasets.
516
517   Hypotheses on other Efficient AI Methods While we focused primarily on mixed-precision quan-
518   tization in this work, a clear direction for future research involves examining scaling trends for other
519
      AI efficiency methods, such as sparsity and low-rank approximations. We hypothesize that the scal-
      ing laws for such methods will closely resemble the scaling laws for quantization introduced in this
520
      work. More broadly, we hypothesize the existence of a broader scaling law governing how the
521
      ratio of approximate compute to exact compute scales with model sizes, and the granularity at
522   which approximate compute is applied.
523
524
525   6       C ONCLUSION
526   In this paper, we present two scaling laws of the mixed precision quantization of LLMs verified by
527   extensive experiments, i.e., LLM-MPQ scaling law 1) the quantization ratio for a fixed loss target
528   exponentially scales with the model size. 2) The max quantization ratio achievable for a given
529   loss target increases with finer quantization granularity. These two laws offer a guidance to further
530   studies on LLM quantization, and indicate a potential scaling trend for designing low-precision LLM
531   inference accelerators.
