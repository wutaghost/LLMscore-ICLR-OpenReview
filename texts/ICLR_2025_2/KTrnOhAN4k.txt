009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we study the generalization properties of diffusion models in a few-
012            shot setup, introduce a novel tuning-free paradigm to synthesize the target out-of-
013            domain (OOD) data, showcase multiple applications of those generalization prop-
014            erties, and demonstrate the advantages compared to existing tuning-based meth-
015            ods in data-sparse scientific scenarios with large domain gaps. Our work resides
016
               on the observation and premise that the theoretical formulation of denoising dif-
               fusion implicit models (DDIMs), a non-Markovian inference technique, exhibits
017
               latent Gaussian priors independent from the parameters of trained denoising diffu-
018
               sion probabilistic models (DDPMs). This brings two practical benefits: the latent
019            Gaussian priors generalize to OOD data domains that have never been used in the
020            training stage; existing DDIMs offer the flexibility to traverse the denoising chain
021            bidirectionally for a pre-trained DDPM. We then demonstrate through theoretical
022            and empirical studies that such established OOD Gaussian priors are practically
023            separable from the originally trained ones after inversion. The above analytical
024            findings allow us to introduce our novel tuning-free paradigm to synthesize new
025            images of the target unseen domain by discovering qualified OOD latent encod-
026            ings within the inverted noisy latent spaces, which is fundamentally different from
027
               most existing paradigms that seek to modify the denoising trajectory to achieve
               the same goal by tuning the model parameters. Extensive cross-model and do-
028
               main experiments show that our proposed method can expand the latent space
029
               and synthesize images in new domains via frozen DDPMs without impairing the
030            generation quality of their original domains.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Generalization ability, which enables the model to synthesize data from various domains, has long
036   been a challenge in deep generative models. The current research trend focuses on leveraging larger
037   models with more training data to facilitate improved generalization. The popularity of recent large-
038   scale models such as DALLE-2 (Ramesh et al., 2022), Imagen (Ho et al., 2022a) and StableDiffu-
039
      sion (Rombach et al., 2022) have demonstrated the impressive and promising representation abilities
      of the state-of-the-art (SOTA) diffusion generative models when trained on an enormous amount of
040
      images such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022). However, brute-force scaling up is not a
041
      panacea and does not fundamentally solve the generalization challenge. In other words, for data
042   domains that remain sparse in those already giant natural image datasets, such as the astrophysical
043   observation and simulation data, even the SOTA models fail to synthesize data suitable for rigorous
044   scientific research purposes, as those data usually follow physical distributions that are dramatically
045   distinguishable from natural images in computer vision, illustrated in the Fig. 1. In addition, scaling
046   up requires exhaustive resources, severely limiting the number of research groups that are able to
047   participate and contribute to the work, and consequently hindering research progress. Given the
048   concerns above, our work focuses on studying the generalization ability in a few-shot setup, where a
049   pre-trained diffusion generative model and a small set of raw data different from its training domain
050
      are provided, with the ultimate objective of generating new data samples for the target OOD domain.
051   The fundamental challenge of domain generalization in deep generative models lies in learning a
052   mapping function that accurately captures the structure of a high-dimensional, irregular data space
053   with an unknown distribution. This is even statistically difficult with large datasets when using non-
      parametric machine learning methods, a phenomenon known as the “curse of dimensionality,” and

180   2     D OMAIN D ISCOVERY AND E XPANSION OF DDPM S
181
182   This section presents our problem formulation under the few-shot scenario and our analytical studies
183   on the generalization properties of pre-trained DDPMs in the context of deterministic trajectories
184   from theoretical and empirical perspectives. The high-level takeaway from our in-depth study is that
185   the inverted OOD samples establish Gaussian separable from the trained ID prior. The key technical
186   challenge is to find qualified OOD latent free from the “mode interference”, which is distinguishable
187
      from the common understanding in tuning-based designs.
188
189   2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION AND N OTATIONS
190
      The general goal of DDPMs is similar to most previous generative models, which is to approximate
191
      an implicit data distribution q(x0 ) with a learned model distribution pθ (x0 ), as well as providing
192
      an easy-to-sample proxy (e.g., standard Gaussian). For the conventional unconditional generation
193   process to synthesize x′ ∼ q(x0 ) , we first draw x′T ∼ N (0, Id ) and obtain x′ = pθ (x′0:T ).
194
195   In this work, given a DDPM pθ trained on images x0 from a domain Did , we aim to study the
196
      behavior of q(xod,1:T |xod ) on other unseen domain Dod using N data samples xod ∈ Dod , with
      N to be a relatively smaller number compared to the usual requirements to train a diffusion model
197
      from scratch. The ultimate goal is to obtain new data samples x′od ∈ Dod , with the assumption to
198
      discover x′T,od ∼ q(xod,T |xod ), such that x′od = pθ (x′od,0:T ).
199
200   As for notations, we use ps and pi to represent the stochastic (Ho et al., 2020) and determinis-
201   tic (Song et al., 2021) generation processes, respectively. We omit θ as we use frozen pre-trained
202   models. In addition, we use the hyper-parameter η (Song et al., 2021) to characterize the degree
203
      of stochasticity in the generative process, with η = 1 for ps and η = 0 for pi . At intermediate
      stochastic levels, we adopt the notation pη=k with k equals a constant between 0 and 1. Similar
204
      to existing literature, T denotes the total diffusion steps. We use Xt to represent the latent (noisy)
205
      spaces formed by xt along denoising.

      2.4   DATA D EPENDENT P ROPERTIES : M ODE I NTERFERENCE AND S EPARABILITY
271
272   In the literature of GANs-based generative models (Goodfellow et al., 2014), “mode collapse” is
273   a common issue that describes the training failure when generated images tend to be very similar
274   given randomly sampled starting encodings from the Gaussian prior. Within the context of diffusion
275   models in our work, we explicitly reveal a phenomenon analog to the “mode collapse” in GANs,
276   which we refer to as “mode interference”, as qualitatively illustrated in Fig. 3 (a).
277   Intuitively, “mode interference” describes the case when the denoised images fall into the model’s
278   original training domain Did instead of the target unseen domain Dod due to the prior interference
279   in the latent spaces. Specifically, when we sample directly from the standard Gaussian to obtain a
280   latent encoding xT ∼ N (0, Id ), then the denoised image will surely fall into the original training
281   domain x0 ∈ Did with x0 ∼ pθ (x0 ), which is the vanilla generation process of a trained DDPM.
282   However, it contradicts our task objective to synthesize images x′0 ∈ Dod . As illustrated in Fig. 2,
283   since we are denoising the latent encoding via deterministic trajectories pi , the remaining critical
284
      technical challenge to generate x′0 is to find additional qualified latent encoding x′T free from the
      interference of the ID Gaussian mode in the sampling stage.
285
286   Notably, a key precondition to achieving the effective OOD latent sampling is that the established
287   OOD prior mode should be separable from the ID Gaussian prior mode (i.e., a standard Gaussian).
288   Otherwise, the denoised image would fall into the training domain as in Fig. 3 (c). The separability
289   is further supported and validated by our empirical verification below in Sec. 2.5.

      We show empirical verification from multiple perspectives to support our model parameter indepen-
      dent and data dependent properties described in Sec. 2.3 and Sec. 2.4.
294
295   Geometrical Properties of Gaussians. We leverage the geometrical measurements established of
296   the high-dimensional studies in mathematics (Blum et al., 2020), as additional empirical support
297   for the Gaussian priors in Sec. 2.3. Specifically, we compute several geometric metrics, including
298   the pair-wise angles (angles formed by three arbitrary samples), sample-to-origin angles (angles
299
      formed by two arbitrary samples and the origin), pair-wise distance (euclidean distance between
      two arbitrary samples) and distance between OOD and ID Gaussian centers, with results in Tab. 1.
300
301   The characteristics mentioned above are typical geometric properties of isotropic high-dimensional
302   Gaussians (Blum et al., 2020). Notably, three randomly sampled points from a high-dimensional
303   Gaussian distribution almost surely form an approximately equilateral triangle, with pairwise angles
304   close to 60◦ , and are nearly orthogonal to each other, as reflected by the 90◦ sample-to-origin angles
305
      shown in the first and second rows of Tab. 1, respectively. However, it is important to note that while
      these geometric properties are common in high-dimensional Gaussians, they are sufficient but not
306
      necessary conditions for identifying such distributions. In other words, these geometric properties
307
      alone are not enough to infer the underlying distribution without additional prior information. More
308   details about the geometric properties are in Appendix C.
309
310   Mode Separability. As revealed by our analysis in Sec. 2.4, the separability between ID and OOD
311
      Gaussian modes is critical for synthesizing target unseen domain images without modifying the
      model parameters and for avoiding the “mode interference”. We further provide validation from the
312
      statistical and learning-based classifier perspectives to support the separability claim.

      Following our extensive analysis, we note that the methodological challenges in this work can be
358   disentangled into two key points: sample qualified latent encodings from the OOD prior, and avoid
359   the mode interference. Sampling from the inverted high-dimensional OOD priors, however, is an
360   open and non-trivial challenging task given the theory-practice gap,3 and points to multiple possible
361   directions of solutions. In fact, high-dimensional Gaussian estimation itself remains a challenging
362   research topic, especially in a multi-variant case (Zhou et al., 2011; Bai & Shi, 2011). While we
363   present our proposed latent sampling method below, we have experimented with many other meth-
364   ods that may not yield the best performance, such as vanilla Gaussian sampling and MCMC, with
365   detailed discussions in Appendix D.
366   Few-Shot Latent References. Having obtained xod, T from the N raw images xod , it might seem
                                                                                        2
367   intuitive to sample directly from the estimated Gaussian distribution N (µest , σest ) based on these
368   inverted OOD latent encodings. However, this approach is empirically insufficient to avoid mode
369   interference because, in practice, the inversion does not yield a perfect Gaussian distribution, even
370   for well-trained in-domain cases (Zhu et al., 2023a). To establish a more precise starting point in the
371   OOD latent space, we propose using the inverted samples xod,T as the initial point for navigating the
372
      subsequent sampling process. Furthermore, this concept of latent references can be flexibly adapted
      to other downstream applications, where the initial latent encoding is determined by the task setup,
373
      such as in the style translation between GRB and sketch images, as illustrated in Fig. 1(b).

      deterministic diffusion, we adopt the standard DDIMs skipping step technique to accelerate both
464
      processes using 60 steps in total. Each direction takes an average of 3 - 6 seconds. The geometric
465   optimization tolerance ω is set to be 0.3 for distance for 0.1 for angles, which leads to a rejection rate

      6   F URTHER D ISCUSSIONS
528   Conclusion. To sum up, we study the domain generalization of DDPMs in the few-shot scenario.
529   From the analytical point of view, we explore the generalization properties of DDPMs on unseen
530   OOD domains. From the methodological perspective, our analytical results allow us to propose a
531   novel paradigm for synthesizing images from new domains without tuning the generative trajecto-
532   ries. We also showcase the superiority of our method in data-sparse cases with large domain gaps.
533
      Limitations and Broader Impact. The current limitations and challenge mainly come from the
534   OOD sampling. As previously discussed, the sampling from inverted OOD prior in high dimension-
535   ality is a challenging task and open research question, which also directly impact the synthesized
536   image quality. Improved sampling methods are worth investigating as future research directions.
537   This work falls into the category of generative models and their applications, we thus acknowl-
538   edge that it may pose the same risks of malicious use of synthetic data as other general generative
539   works. However, the primary objective of our work is not performance-driven but to provide a better
      understanding of the generalization properties of diffusion generative models.

854   A.1    G ENERALIZATION IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
855
      Domain Generalization (Wang et al., 2022a) that aims to generalize models’ ability to extended data
856
      distributions has been an important research topic in broad machine learning area (Ganin et al., 2016;
857
      Zhao et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Muandet et al., 2013; Li et al., 2017), with various computer
858   vision applications such as recognition (Peng et al., 2019; Rebuffi et al., 2017), detection (Zhang
859   et al., 2021) and segmentation (Hoffman et al., 2018; Gong et al., 2019). In the vision generative
860   field, it becomes an even more challenging task with the extra demand to sample from the gener-
861   alized distributions. One popular recent trend in the computer vision community is scaling up the
862   model and dataset sizes as the most intuitive and obvious solutions (Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al.,
863   2022a; Rombach et al., 2022). Another scenario to study the domain generalization of generative
      models is within the few-shot scenario, where we only have a limited amount of data compared to


                                                       16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      the training set. In this case, fine-tuning the given model on the limited images Kim et al. (2022) is
865   the most straightforward way to go.
866
867   Our work falls into the second category: provided with a pre-trained model and a small set of unseen
868
      images different from the model’s training domain, we seek to better understand the generalization
      abilities of DDPMs.
869

      A.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS AND D ETERMINISTIC VARIANTS
872
873
      Diffusion Models (DMs) Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015); Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019) are
874
      the state-of-the-art generative models for data synthesis in images (Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach
875   et al., 2022; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Gu et al., 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Hu et al., 2021b),
876   videos (Ho et al., 2022b), and audio (Kong et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2023b; Mittal et al., 2021).
877   There are currently two mainstream fundamental formulations of diffusion models, i.e., the denois-
878   ing diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) Ho et al. (2020) and score-based models Song et al.
879   (2020). One common perspective to understand both formulations is to consider the data generation
880   as solving stochastic differential equations (SDEs), which characterize a stochastic process. Based
881   on vanilla models, both branch develops their own deterministic variants, i.e., denoising diffusion
882   implicit models (DDIMs) Song et al. (2021) and consistency models Song et al. (2023), with their
883
      core idea to follow the marginal distributions in denoising. Compared to initial DDPMs and Score-
      based DMs with ancestral sampling, the deterministic variants are solving ODEs instead of SDEs
884
      and largely accelerate the generation speed with fewer steps.
885
886   We leverage the deterministic variant (DDIMs Song et al. (2021)) as the tool to achieve bidirectional
887   transition between latent noisy space and data space in this work.
888
889

      A.3   L ATENT S PACE OF D EEP G ENERATIVE M ODELS
891
892   Comprehensive studies of latent space of generative models (Karras et al., 2017; Abdal et al., 2019;
893
      Gal et al., 2022) help to better understand the model and also benefit downstream tasks such as data
      editing and manipulation (Zhu et al., 2016; Shen et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2020).
894
      A large portion of work has been exploring this problem within the context of GAN inversion (Xia
895
      et al., 2022), where the typical methods can be mainly divided into either learning-based (Zhu et al.,
896   2016; Richardson et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Alaluf et al., 2021) or optimization-based cate-
897   gories (Abdal et al., 2019; 2020; Huh et al., 2020; Creswell & Bharath, 2018). More recently, with
898   the growing popularity of diffusion models, researchers have also focused on the latent space under-
899   standing of DMs for better synthesis qualities or semantic control (Rombach et al., 2022; Zhu et al.,
900   2023a; Yang et al., 2024).
901
      Our work also contributes to a better understanding of latent spaces, and aims to introduce a new
902   synthesis paradigm to explore the intrinsic potential of DMs.
903

905   A.4   D IFFUSION M ODELS IN S CIENCE
906
907   While DMs have been extensively applied in data generation and editing within the multimodal
908   context (Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022b; Zhu et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024; Zhu et al.,
909   2023a), recent works have extended their application domains to scientific explorations, such as
910   astrophysics (Xu et al., 2023b;a), medical imaging (Kazerouni et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024a), and
911   biology (Wu et al., 2024b). Compared to conventional computer vision applications, scientific tasks
912   usually exhibit several distinct features. For instance, data acquisition and annotation are generally
913   more expensive due to their scientific nature, resulting in a relatively smaller amount of available
914
      data for experiments. Additionally, the evaluation of these works adheres to established conventions
      within their respective contexts, which are usually different from image synthesis evaluation based
915
      on perceptual quality.
916
917   Our work also experiments with several astrophysical datasets to showcase the potential of applying
      our proposed paradigm and method to such specific domains with limited data.

1236   Galaxy Data. The images from the GalaxyZoo dataset (Willett et al., 2013) are observation data
1237   of galaxies that belong to one of six categories - elliptical, clockwise spiral, anticlockwise spiral,
1238
       edge-on, star/don’t know, or merger. The original data format of those galaxy images are also
       RGB images, thus “somewhat” similar to natural images, but they contain important morphological
1239
       information to study the galaxies in astronomy.

       could reliably classify the generated images into one of the known categories.

       Radiation Data. For the radiation data from (Xu et al., 2023a), the original format is physical
1243   quantity instead of RGB images, which correspond to the dust emission.
1244
1245   Dust is a significant component of the interstellar medium in our galaxy, composed of elements such
1246
       as oxygen, carbon, iron, silicon, and magnesium. Most interstellar dust particles range in size from
       a few molecules to 0.1 mm (100 m), similar to micrometeoroids. The interaction of dust particles
1247
       with electromagnetic radiation depends on factors like their cross-section, the wavelength of the
1248
       radiation, and the nature of the grain, including its refractive index and size. The radiation process
1249   for an individual grain is defined by its emissivity, which is influenced by the grain’s efficiency factor
1250   and includes processes such as extinction, scattering, absorption, and polarization.
1251
1252
       In RGB images of dust emission, different colors represent emissions at three wavelengths: blue for
       4.5 µm, green for 24 µm, and red for 250 µm. The blue color typically indicates short-wavelength
1253
       dust emission from point sources, such as young stars or young stellar objects. The green color
1254
       represents mid-wavelength dust emission from warm and hot dust. The red color signifies long-
1255   wavelength dust emission from cold dust.
1256
1257   Warm/hot dust emission (green) is usually found around stars, which appear as blue-colored dots.
1258
       Since warm dust often mixes with cold dust on the outer edges of bubble structures, the resulting
       color is often yellowish. Cold dust extends farther from the stars, giving the background or areas
1259
       outside star clusters a red appearance. In the case of massive star clusters, stellar feedback, such
1260
       as radiation and stellar winds, can blow away the surrounding gas and dust, creating black or blank
1261   areas. Typically, RGB images show more extensive red emission with some orange/yellow emis-
1262   sion, displaying filamentary and bubble structures, along with blue and/or white dotted point source
1263   emissions.
1264
       The above background is considered as part of the underlying evaluation criteria when performing
1265
       subjective evaluation on the quality of generated radiation data.

1272   E.2.1    D ISCUSSION ON THE L ATENT S TEP t, S TOCHASTICITY AND M ODE I NTERFERENCE
1273
1274   In our main paper, we briefly discuss the impact of the latent diffusion step t where we perform
1275
       the inversion and OOD latent sampling. While we empirically find that t ≈ 800 is a reasonable
       range for the choice of t, we note there exists an entangled mechanism for the trade-off between the
1276
       sampling difficulty and the mode interference issue.
1277
1278   For the diffusion step t, recent studies (Zhu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024) suggest that t character-
1279   izes the formation of image information at different stages of the denoising process. Intuitively, the
1280   early stage of the denoising process (e.g., t > 800) represents a rather chaotic process, the mixing
1281
       step tm (Zhu et al., 2023a) signifies a critical stage where the image semantic information starts to
       form, and the later stage where t is close to 0 demonstrates a stage during which more fine-grained
1282
       pixel-level information are introduced to the final generated data. From the distribution point of
1283
       view, the influence of t can be interpreted as the convergence of distributions, where t = T is a stan-
1284   dard Gaussian by definition, thus the ID and OOD modes are more difficult to separate. However, as
1285   the denoising process gets closer to the real image space at t = 0, the sampling difficulty increases
1286   as the implicit distribution moves away from the standard Gaussian.
1287
       Meanwhile, the diffusion step t is not the only factor that impacts the trade-off between sampling dif-
1288
       ficulty and mode interference. While scarcely discussed in the main paper, we note the stochasticity
1289
       of the denoising trajectory also plays a similar role as the diffusion step in this work. The stochas-
1290   ticity of the denoising trajectory in DMs has been proven to be generally beneficial in improving the
1291   synthesis quality (Karras et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023a). In this
1292   work, while we choose the η = 0 for the main paper, a tolerance for a certain range of stochasticity
1293   allows us to follow a “relatively deterministic” denoising process pη=k , with k ̸= 0, instead of the
1294   completely deterministic pi . We hereby refer to it as “bandwidth of the unseen trajectories,” denoted
1295   as Bη,t , which can be used to quantify the “mode interference”. Another interpretation is to analog
       the trajectory bandwidth Bη,t to the actual subspace volume occupied by the OOD latent samples.

1364
