011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Molecular representation learning is pivotal for various molecular property pre-
014            diction tasks related to drug discovery. Robust and accurate benchmarks are
015
               essential for refining and validating current methods. Existing molecular property
               benchmarks derived from wet experiments, however, face limitations such as data
016
               volume constraints, unbalanced label distribution, and noisy labels. To address
017
               these issues, we construct a large-scale and precise molecular representation dataset
018            of approximately 140,000 small molecules, meticulously designed to capture an
019            extensive array of chemical, physical, and biological properties, derived through a
020            robust computational ligand-target binding analysis pipeline. We conduct exten-
021            sive experiments on various deep learning models, demonstrating that our dataset
022            offers significant physicochemical interpretability to guide model development
023            and design. Notably, the dataset’s properties are linked to binding affinity metrics,
024            providing additional insights into model performance in drug-target interaction
025            tasks. We believe this dataset will serve as a more accurate and reliable benchmark
026
               for molecular representation learning, thereby expediting progress in the field of
               artificial intelligence-driven drug discovery.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Molecular Representation Learning (MRL) is crucial in leveraging artificial intelligence for drug
032   discovery applications. These applications span a range of areas, including molecular property
033   prediction Hu et al. (2019); Hou et al. (2022); Xia et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2023); Feng et al.
034   (2023c), molecular dynamics simulation Zaidi et al. (2022); Feng et al. (2023b), chemical reaction
035   prediction Wang et al. (2021); Tang et al. (2024), drug-target interactions Feng et al. (2023a), and high-
036   throughput drug virtual screening Gao et al. (2024). The MRL approach uses deep learning models
037   to encode molecules into meaningful latent representations, effectively capturing and preserving their
038   molecular properties. The success of models heavily depends on the quality of evaluation datasets,
039
      which reveal limitations and guide improvements in model design. From the aspect of application, a
      more appropriate model can promote specific application scenarios.
040
041   There are now several datasets available for evaluating molecular representation learning models Wu
042   et al. (2018); Ramakrishnan et al. (2014); Chmiela et al. (2017); Davis et al. (2011); Tang et al. (2014).
043   Among them, MoleculeNet Wu et al. (2018) stands out as the most frequently utilized benchmark
044   for evaluating molecular representation models, especially within the domain of molecular property
045
      prediction. It consists of a comprehensive range of properties sourced from various public datasets,
      making it an acknowledged standard for assessing the efficacy of diverse molecular machine learning
046
      methods. However, despite its popularity, MoleculeNet presents several challenges, including: 1)
047
      Data Volume Constraints. A considerable number of the properties documented in MoleculeNet are
048   derived from costly and intricate wet experiments, resulting in a limited dataset size. As illustrated
049   in Figure 1a, more than half of the tasks in the MoleculeNet dataset comprise fewer than 10,000
050   data points. In such scenarios, models frequently encounter overfitting issues, which may worsen
051   under stricter split settings, such as scaffold split. 2) Unbalanced Label Distribution. Many of
052   MoleculeNet’s datasets exhibit severe label imbalances that can distort performance metrics. Figure 1b
053   illustrates the proportions of samples whose label equals 1 in all multi-label binary classification tasks
      within MoleculeNet. It is evident that the majority of proportions tend to be closer to 0 or 1, indicating


                                                          1
                   Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
                   the prevalence of the unbalanced issue of MoleculeNet. 3) Label Noise. The dependency on wet
055                experiments introduces a degree of uncertainty in the dataset labels, some of which may be imprecise
056                due to the inherent limitations of experimental methods. This factor compromises the reliability of
057                the data. As pointed out by Walters (2023), numerous flaws exist within the MoleculeNet, including
058                invalid structures, undefined stereocenters, and conflicting labels caused by data curation errors.4)
059                Inconsistency. MoleculeNet compiles its data from several public databases, assigning different
060                molecular sets to each property task. This aggregation process not only leads to inconsistencies but
061                also allows batch effects to manifest. In summary, the various issues outlined make the results of
062                MoleculeNet unstable and susceptible to influence from factors such as varying hyperparameters and
063
                   random seeds. Consequently, many existing methods Zhou et al. (2023); Feng et al. (2023c); Yu et al.
                   (2023) resort to hyperparameter search techniques in pursuit of improved performance. However, the
064
                   dominance of hyperparameters over the method itself compromises the reliability of the benchmark
065
                   and poses challenges in accurately profiling methods.

                   To address these issues, we propose MoleculeCLA: a large-scale benchmark for molecular property
084                prediction via computational ligand-target binding analysis(Figure 2). Ligand-target binding, which
085                is an important task in drug discovery, is a complicated process influenced by various molecular
086                properties Decherchi & Cavalli (2020). Consequently, docking tools are designed to incorporate
087                many different meaningful components to fit the final docking score. From these components, we
088                meticulously select diverse items relevant to physical, chemical, and biological molecular properties,
089                outlined in detail in Table 1. Notably, MolecueCLA is derived from a computational approach that
090                does not rely on wet experiments. This method not only enhances data accessibility but also scales
091                conveniently to accommodate large amounts of data.
092                Building on the above content, in this work, we construct a large-scale and precise dataset involving
093                10 representative protein targets spanning a wide range of drug functionalities. We use the docking
094                software Glide Friesner et al. (2004); Halgren et al. (2004) to obtain 9 properties for a total of
095                140,697 molecules, covering chemical, physical, and biological properties. We evaluate various MRL
096                methods, including traditional descriptor-based and deep-learning approaches. The performance
097                results are more stable and provide better explanations about the methods themselves, indicating that
098
                   our dataset is more robust and reliable. Additionally, these properties have a strong correlation with
                   binding affinity, enabling us to further use this dataset to select appropriate models for drug-target
099
                   interaction tasks.

194
195   Generalization is a crucial aspect of model evaluation, so we use scaffold splitting to minimize
196   data leakage and ensure robust testing. Scaffold splitting ensures that structurally diverse molecules
197   populate the training, validation, and test sets, exposing the model to a wide range of molecular
198
      scaffolds and enhancing its generalization to new, unseen molecules, thereby mimicking real-world
      situations. Specifically, our dataset is divided using scaffold splitting, resulting in a training set of
199
      112,557 molecules, a validation set of 14,070 molecules, and a test set of 14,070 molecules. This
200
      division is used in all subsequent experiments.

248   Our dataset comprises chemical, physical, and biological properties, and to comprehensively evaluate
249   baseline models, we assess them based on their latent representation and model transfer ability. For
250   latent representation, we employ the linear probe method. This method is widely utilized in deep
251   learning model evaluation and provides a sole assessment of the quality of the representation and
252   insights into what abstract information the model has captured Akhondzadeh et al. (2023); Radford
253
      et al. (2021); Liang et al. (2022). In terms of transfer ability, we commonly load the parameter values
      from the pre-trained model to process the input, then update all parameters including the molecular
254
      encoder to predict the task label as a standard fine-tuning paradigm. Additionally, we fine-tune
255
      models to assess their performance in the drug-target interaction task. This evaluation can reflect
256   whether our dataset can be used to choose the appropriate model for the drug-target interaction task.

298   3.2.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
299
300   In linear probe experiments, we evaluate the latent representations of various baseline models and
301
      molecular descriptors. Molecular descriptors are mathematical representations of a molecule’s
      properties. Unlike the abstract latent representation learned by the model, the dimensions in the
302
      descriptor vector correspond directly to different physical and chemical information of the molecules,
303
      e.g., topological polar surface area and the number of all atoms. We chose the widely used molecular
304   descriptor calculation software Mordred Moriwaki et al. (2018) to obtain the 2D and 3D descriptors.
305   After generating the descriptors, we drop columns containing empty values. Following this process,
306   the 2D descriptor dimension is 904 and the 3D descriptor dimension is 51. Then, molecular descriptors
307   and latent representations are used as input to train a linear regression model implemented with the
308   Scikit-learn package Pedregosa et al. (2011).
309
310   3.2.2     R ESULTS & A NALYSIS
311
312
      The average Pearson correlation coefficients for 10 protein targets using the linear probe method are
      presented in Table 4. These results provide a deeper understanding of the effectiveness of various
313
      molecular representation learning models. Below, we explore specific findings that reveal how these
314
      models encode molecular information, offering valuable insights into model development and design.
315   We also provide the RMSE, MAE, R2 results in Appendix 5.6.
316
317
      Descriptors Provide More Direct Molecular Information Descriptors3D have only 51 features,
318   but its overall performance is still better than that of the AttrMasking and GraphMAE. Descriptors2D
319   and Descriptors2D&3D have similar results across all tasks and outperform the limited-feature
320   Descriptors3D . Therefore, in the following discussion, "descriptors" will refer to Descriptors2D . As
321   observed in Table 4, descriptors consistently achieve the highest Pearson correlation coefficients
322   across all tasks. This result may be due to the inclusion of certain features in the descriptors, such as
323   acidic group count, number of hydrogen atoms, and hybridization ratio, which are directly correlated
      with properties like lipophilicity, hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interaction, and others. However,


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      when we use baseline model latent representations and molecular descriptors as input to train a
325   multi-layer perceptron model for each task, as detailed in the Appendix 5.5, we find that some model
326   latent representations outperform the descriptors. This indicates that while descriptors provide more
327   direct molecular information, model latent representations may contain more abstract semantics,
328   leading to better results when they are used with more expressive downstream models.
329
330   Appropriate Mask Strategy Potentially Enhances Graph-based Models The analysis of results
331   from three GNN models (AttrMasking, GraphMAE, and Mole-BERT) that utilize graph data as input
332   but employ different masking strategies suggests that Mole-BERT potentially performs better than
333   the others. AttrMasking randomly masks some atoms and then predicts the masked atom types.
334   GraphMAE further employs a re-mask decoding strategy to reconstruct atom features. In contrast,
335   Mole-BERT identifies a challenge with masking atom types due to the limited and unbalanced
336
      nature of atom sets. Instead, it employs a context-aware tokenizer, encoding atoms into chemically
      relevant discrete values for masking, thereby achieving better results. This observation implies that an
337
      appropriate pre-training mask strategy could enhance a model’s ability to capture molecular features.
338
339
      3D Coordinate Information Improves Molecular Latent Representation When comparing
340
      models utilizing 2D graph-only information (AttrMasking, GraphMAE, and Mole-BERT) with
341
      those integrating 3D coordinate information (3D Denoising, Frad, SliDe, Uni-Mol, Uni-Mol+), a
342   consistent trend emerges: the 3D-oriented models consistently outperform 2D graph-only models.
343   This consistent superiority suggests that 3D information significantly enriches the representation
344   of small molecular features, leading to more robust and informative latent representations. This
345   observation underscores the potential value of incorporating 3D structural data into molecular
346   representation learning models, offering promising avenues for further advancements in the field.
347
348   Fitting Force Fields Reveals Deeper Molecular Properties Further comparison of models that
349   utilize 3D coordinate information reveals that physically informed pre-training models (3D Denoising,
350   Frad, and SliDe) perform better than SE(3)-invariant Transformer-based models (Uni-Mol and Uni-
351   Mol+) in capturing hydrogen bonds (hbond), electrostatic interactions (ecoul), and rotatable bond
352   torsions (erotb). The 3D denoising, Frad, and SliDe models theoretically fit the force fields of small
353
      molecules. As reported in SliDe, the correlation coefficients of the estimated force fields are ranked
      as SliDe > Frad > 3D denoising, and our experimental results reflect the same trend. Additionally, in
354
      the einternal task, which is related to internal torsional energy, the SliDe method stands out among
355
      the other models. These results indicate that training strategies closely aligned with the intrinsic
356   properties of small molecules, such as force fields, indeed enhance the model’s ability to capture
357   meaningful molecular representations.
358
359   The Superiority of UniMAP We observe that UniMAP achieves the best performance among all
360   deep-learning-based methods in Table 4. This outstanding excellence can be attributed to several key
361   factors of UniMAP. Firstly, UniMAP’s fragment-level masking and alignment strategies emphasize
362   the functionality of molecular fragments, which are recognized as chemical semantic units and play a
363   crucial role in determining the bioactivity of molecules. Furthermore, the integration of fingerprint
364   regression and functional group prediction supervision enriches UniMAP’s representation, allowing
365   it to capture intrinsic molecular structural information, similar to descriptors, making it easier to
366   fit chemical and physical tasks. Finally, the shared Transformer that fuses SMILES and molecular
367
      graphs in a single-stream approach may enhance the expressive power of molecular embeddings.

      3.3.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
372   For the fine-tuning setting, we select the best four models(UniMAP, Uni-Mol, Frad, SliDe) from the
373   previous baselines and fine-tune them on five representative tasks covering all property categories.
374   This approach is chosen due to the high cost of training all parameters on numerous regression tasks,

376   outlined in the original papers. We train a separate model for each protein target and simultaneously
377   predict the five tasks. Additionally, each model is trained three times using different random seeds to
      ensure robust and consistent performance.

460
461   In this work, we present MoleculeCLA: a large-scale dataset consisting of approximately 140,000
462   small molecules derived from computational ligand-target binding analysis, providing 9 properties
463   that cover chemical, physical, and biological aspects. Our experiments demonstrate the importance
464   of constructing a large-scale, scalable dataset, enabling more reliable and noise-free evaluation of
465   molecular representation models. We introduce a novel methodology that does not directly rely
466   on wet experimental data. Instead, our approach uses ligand-target binding analysis to establish
467   a connection between real-world properties and computational metrics. This method avoids the
468
      inherent noise of wet experiments, eliminates batch effects, and ensures consistent properties for the
      same molecules. We believe our work not only provides a reliable benchmark that contributes to
469
      advances in the development of molecular representation learning but also serves as a successful case
470
      study in computational approaches, potentially inspiring benchmark construction in other domains
471   within AI for Science.

661   5.2     R ELATED WORK
662
663   5.2.1    M OLECULAR R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
664
665   Various molecular pre-training methods have been proposed to leverage large amounts of unlabeled
666   data to learn meaningful molecular representations, benefiting different downstream tasks. There are
667   three common pre-training strategies: Masking, which involves masking atoms or substructures in
668   the 1D SMILES Weininger (1988) or 2D graphs of molecules and training the model to recover the
669
      masked content Hu et al. (2019); Hou et al. (2022); Xia et al. (2022); Feng et al. (2023c) ; Contrastive
      Learning, which aligns representations of different molecular modalities for modality fusion Liu et al.
670
      (2021); Feng et al. (2023c); Stärk et al. (2022) ; and 3D Denoising, a physically informed pre-training
671
      strategy that adds noise to the coordinates of 3D molecular conformers and trains the model to predict
672   the noise, aiming to approximate learning the molecular force field Zaidi et al. (2022); Feng et al.
673   (2023b); Ni et al. (2023), has demonstrated its superior performance on a variety of physical quantum
674   tasks.
675
676
      5.2.2    M OLECULAR B ENCHMARKS
677
678   Existing methods typically evaluate their performance on molecular property prediction tasks. Molecu-
679   leNet Wu et al. (2018) is a commonly used benchmark that includes multiple subtasks covering
680   molecular physicochemical and biological properties, with labels organized into classification and
681   regression tasks. QM9 Ruddigkeit et al. (2012); Ramakrishnan et al. (2014) and MD17 Chmiela
682   et al. (2017) are quantum property-related tasks that include energetic and electronic properties
683   and molecular force field predictions as regression tasks. Since quantum properties are highly
684   related to 3D conformers, these tasks are often used as benchmarks for 3D-based methods. More-
685   over, drug target interaction datasets such as KIBA Tang et al. (2014), Davis Davis et al. (2011),
686
      and LBA Townshend et al. (2020) are evaluated for binding affinity prediction tasks, integrating
      molecular pre-trained encoders to extract molecular ligand representations. Additionally, several
687
      benchmarks Zhang et al. (2023); van Tilborg et al. (2022) concentrate on bioactivity cliffs, wherein
688
      structurally similar molecular pairs exhibit significant disparities in potency.
689
690
691   5.2.3    C OMPARISON WITH OTHER C OMPUTATIONAL DATASETS
692
693
      There are also computational datasets in other domains. The Harvard Clean Energy Project Hachmann
      et al. (2011) features an automated, high-throughput framework for evaluating millions of molecular
694
      motifs via quantum chemistry. Lyu et al. (2019) explores structure-based docking of a library
695
      comprising 170 million compounds from 130 reactions, aiming to create an extensive library for
696   identifying novel chemotypes. DOCKSTRING García-Ortegón et al. (2022) constructs a dataset that
697   includes docking scores and poses and develops a series of pharmaceutically relevant benchmark tasks,
698   such as virtual screening and de novo design of selective kinase inhibitors. Different from previous
699   computational approaches, MoleculeCLA is specifically designed as a benchmark for molecular
700   representation learning methods. Beyond evaluating final docking scores, MoleculeCLA incorporates
701   intermediate, clearly defined components relevant to various molecular properties as regression tasks
      for assessing different molecular representation learning methods.

      5.4   BASELINE M ODEL D ESCRIPTION
736
737   AttrMasking Hu et al. (2019) In this model, input node and edge attributes (such as atom types in a
738   molecular graph) are randomly masked. The Graph Neural Network (GNN) is then trained to predict
739   the masked attributes.
740
      GraphMAE Hou et al. (2022) GraphMAE pre-trains a GNN by masking input node features with a
741
      [MASK] token and encoding the corrupted graph. During decoding, it re-masks selected node codes
742
      with a [DMASK] token and uses a GNN decoder to reconstruct the masked node features, optimizing
743   with scaled cosine error.
744
745   Mole-BERT Xia et al. (2022) Mole-BERT paper proposes a context-aware tokenizer, encoding
746
      atom attributes into meaningful discrete codes. With the expanded atom "vocabulary", the authors
      introduce a novel node-level pre-training task called masked atoms modeling and triplet masked
747
      contrastive learning for graph-level pre-training.
748
749   Uni-Mol Zhou et al. (2023) Uni-Mol is a universal 3D molecular representation learning framework
750   designed to enhance the representation ability and application scope of MRL methods by incorporating
751   3D information. It features two pretrained models with SE(3)-Transformer architecture: one trained
752
      on 209M molecular conformations and another on 3M protein pocket data. Uni-Mol also includes
      fine-tuning strategies for various downstream tasks.
753
754   Uni-Mol+ Lu et al. (2023) Uni-Mol+ addresses the challenge of accurately predicting quantum
755   chemical (QC) properties by leveraging 3D equilibrium conformations. Unlike previous methods
      using 1D SMILES sequences or 2D molecular graphs, Uni-Mol+ starts with a raw 3D molecule


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      conformation generated by inexpensive methods like RDKit. This conformation is iteratively updated
757   to its target DFT equilibrium conformation using neural networks.
758
759   3D Denoising Zaidi et al. (2022) This paper presents a pre-training technique based on denoising for
760
      molecular property prediction from 3D structures, particularly addressing the challenge of limited
      data. By using large datasets of 3D molecular structures at equilibrium, the method learns meaningful
761
      representations for downstream tasks. The denoising objective, linked to score-matching, corresponds
762
      to learning a molecular force field from equilibrium structures.
763
764   Frad Feng et al. (2023b) Frad introduces a hybrid noise strategy, applying noise to both dihedral
765   angles and coordinates, and a fractional denoising approach that decouples these noises, focusing
766
      on coordinate denoising. This approach maintains force field equivalence and enhances sampling of
      low-energy structures.
767
768   SliDe Ni et al. (2023) This paper introduces a new method for molecular pre-training called sliced
769   denoising (SliDe), which is grounded in classical mechanical intramolecular potential theory. SliDe
770   employs a novel noise strategy that perturbs bond lengths, angles, and torsion angles to improve
771   the sampling of molecular conformations. It also uses a random slicing technique to avoid the
772
      computational burden of calculating the Jacobian matrix, which is crucial for estimating the force
      field.
773
774   UniMAP Feng et al. (2023c) UniMAP is a universal SMILES-graph representation learning model
775   designed to capture fine-grained semantics between SMILES and graph representations of molecules.
776   It starts with an embedding layer to obtain token and node/edge representations, followed by a
777   multi-layer Transformer for deep cross-modality fusion. UniMAP introduces four pre-training tasks:
778
      Multi-Level Cross-Modality Masking , SMILES-Graph Matching, Fragment-Level Alignment, and
      Domain Knowledge Learning.
