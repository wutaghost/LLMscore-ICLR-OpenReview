009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks (DNNs) achieve state-of-the-art performance on many
012            tasks, but this often requires increasingly larger model sizes, which in turn leads
013            to more complex internal representations. Explainability techniques (XAI) have
014            made remarkable progress in the interpretability of ML models. However, the
015            non-relational nature of Graph neural networks (GNNs) make it difficult to reuse
016            already existing XAI methods. While other works have focused on instance-based
017            explanation methods for GNNs, very few have investigated model-based methods
018
               and, to our knowledge, none have tried to probe the embedding of the GNNs for
               well-known structural graph properties. In this paper we present a model agnostic
019
               explainability pipeline for GNNs employing diagnostic classifiers. This pipeline
020
               aims to probe and interpret the learned representations in GNNs across various
021            architectures and datasets, refining our understanding and trust in these models.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are pivotal in harnessing non-Euclidean graph-structured data (Kipf
027   & Welling, 2017) for tasks ranging from social network analysis to bioinformatics. Despite their
028   success, the black-box nature of GNNs poses significant challenges as classical XAI methods can’t
029   be directly applied on GNNs due to the lack of a regular structure (e.g. vertices can have different
030   degrees). In this case, explaining a prediction means identifying important parts of the relational
031   structure, or input features of nodes. An issue is that finding the explanation is itself a combinatorial
032   problem, making XAI methods for GNN intractable (Longa et al., 2023a; Ying et al., 2019).
033   Previous surveys (Agarwal et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2022) highlighted the lack of comprehensive,
034   robust and model-agnostic explainability methods. We also identified (sec C) few model-level ex-
035   plainability methods. They focus on explaining the decision-making process at a high level, often
036   by generating graph patterns or motifs that influence the model’s predictions but none allow for the
037   interpretability of intermediate layers and none highlight the role of graph properties. One paper
038   identified the room for probing classifier (Akhondzadeh et al., 2023), as developed for Natural Lan-
039
      guage Processing (Giulianelli et al., 2018), (Belinkov, 2021). Their research question was ”will
      the hidden representation contain information about the number of hydrogen atoms or the presence
040
      of aromatic rings?”. We aim to address a more fundamental and general question : Does the hid-
041
      den representation encode information about the graph-theoretic properties ? We propose a simple,
042   model-agnostic, probing classifier pipeline to interpret GNN embeddings by probing for encoded
043   graph properties (see fig. 1) across various architectures and datasets. We investigate both local
044   properties like betweennes centrality, as well as global properties like average path length. To our
045   knowledge, this is the first work to explore this direction.
046
      We demonstrate the ability of diagnostic classifiers to effectively highlight graph-theoretic properties
047
      in GNN learned latent representations (fig. 7). We explore how different regularization techniques
048   (none, L2 weight decay, dropout) affect the representation of graph properties (fig. 14). We com-
049   pare how various GNN architectures (GCN, R-GCN, GIN, GAT) differ in their ability to represent
050   graph properties, analyzing whether these differences align with their mathematical frameworks (ta-
051   ble 6). The approach is validated through applications to toxicity and fMRI datasets, confirming
052   the alignment of probed properties with domain knowledge (table 8), before exploring the pipeline’s
053   inferential power, uncovering structural properties that might not yet have been extensively studied
      (table 23).

      2.1     G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
081
082   Nowadays we have some theoretical understanding of the representational restrictions and capabil-
083   ities of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with regard to the Weisfeiler-Lehman test (Akhondzadeh
084   et al., 2023). We know that these cannot capture certain graph properties, such as connectivity or
085   triangle-freeness (Franks et al., 2024; Kiefer, 2020; Kriege et al., 2018), due to their reliance on
086   local structure. This constraint is also present in (message passing) GNNs.
087   Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf & Welling,              2017) are GNNs where  for a single

088
      layer, the node representation is computed as: X ′ = σ D̃ −1/2 · Ã · D̃ −1/2 · X · W . We know
089
090
      that GNNs which rely solely on local information, like the GCN and its relational variant (R-
      GCN) (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018), cannot compute important graph properties, such as girth and
091
      diameter or eigenvector centrality (Garg et al., 2020). We are therefore also investigating more glob-
092
      ally aware networks like GAT (Graph Attention Network) (Veličković et al., 2018) and GIN (Graph
093   Isomorphism Network) (Xu et al., 2019).
094
095   GAT makes use of self-attention and is thereby more expressive than the GCN. However, its reliance
096
      on feature-dependent weights and structure-free normalisation limits its ability to capture specific
      structural properties that do not directly depend on edges. This is particularly true for tasks where
097
      node features alone are not enough, and global graph structures are crucial (e.g., tasks requiring
098
      knowledge of subgraphs or non-local patterns). GIN aggregates node features in a way that mimics
099   the Weisfeiler-Lehman test. By using the MLP equivalent to an Injective Update Function, GIN
100   avoids oversimplifying the aggregation step, making it as expressive as the WL test. Thus, it is
101   likely to excel at encoding complex graph properties and solving classification tasks.

103   2.2     G RAPH PROPERTIES
104
105   Graph theory is a branch of mathematics that studies the properties and relationships of graphs.
106   Graphs can be undirected or directed and analysed through both local and global properties. Lo-
107   cal properties (like node degree or clustering coefficient) are based on the neighbors of a node. In
      contrast, global properties (such as diameter and characteristic path length) assess the overall graph


                                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      structure. Global graph properties can be associated with higher level complex systems’ charac-
109   teristics like the presence of repeated motifs in the graphs or information-flow properties. See the
110   appendix B for a list of local and global properties used in our experiments.
111
112
      We can distinguish different global properties, basic ones like the number of nodes a graph has,
      clustering and centrality ones, graph motifs and substructures, spectral and small-world properties.
113
      As an higher-order analysis, the recurrence of specific motifs within network substructures—such as
114
      triangles, cliques, or feed-forward loops can be seen as the fundamental building blocks that dictate
115   the system’s functionality and resilience. Small-worldness, as characterised by Barabási (Albert
116   & Barabási, 2002), reveal how networks can maintain short path lengths despite their expansive
117   size and sparse connectivity. This kind of higher order properties are very interesting in order to
118   understand how the macroscopic behaviour of complex systems emerges from the intricate interplay
119   of their microscopic components (Barabási et al., 2002). For example how diseases spread in social
120   networks, how neurons interact in the brain, or how information propagates through the Internet.
121   GNNs synthesise local topological features into global structures, abstract these representations into
122   higher-order graph attributes. Each layer progressively expands the receptive field, mirroring how
123   hierarchical feature learning works in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for images. Probing
124   their learnt representations should act as a scalable proxy to investigate how global arrangement
125   and connectivity patterns influence a system’s function. In other terms, by dissecting these learned
126   embeddings, we can possibly delve into the intricate relationships between a network’s macroscopic
127   arrangement and its emergent behaviours. Based on the message passing paradigm in GNNs, as
128   layers progress, one would expect an increased abstraction in the selection of graph properties. Ini-
129
      tially, local features like node degree dominate, but deeper layers progressively capture more global
      properties, such as connectivity patterns and centrality. Through hierarchical pooling or readout
130
      mechanisms, GNNs can aggregate node embeddings into a single, global graph-level embedding.
131
      Graphs that share structural similarities or patterns of interaction among nodes are organised closely
132   in the embedding space, allowing the model to differentiate between classes of graphs, such as those
133   with and without long paths (fig. 1). This is why, AI engineers are likely to focus on the vali-
134   dation framework, emphasizing the alignment between probing results and the predictions derived
135   from the message-passing paradigm—particularly insights revealed in the initial layers of the model.
136   In contrast, from the standpoint of domain researchers—such as chemists or neuroscientists— the
137   most compelling aspect is found in the later layers of the model, where the abstract representations
138   become increasingly capable of rendering the problem linearly separable, thus facilitating clear in-
139   terpretability of classification decisions and offering domain-specific interpretations.

142   2.3   P ROBING CLASSIFIERS
143
144   In prior work (Hupkes et al., 2018) probing classifiers have been used for linguistic properties. Here,
145   we adapt them for graph features. Unlike unsupervised techniques such as PCA or T-SNE, which
146   are useful to visualize input data with regard to the embedding latent space, we adopt a supervised
147
      framework to quantitatively assess how specific properties are encoded within the embedding space
      of GNNs. Let g : fl (x) 7→ ẑ represent a probing classifier, used to map the learned intermediate
148
      representations from the original model f to a specific property ẑ. The choice of a linear classifier for
149
      g is motivated primarily by its simplicity. If a linear probe performs well, it suggests the existence of
150   a hyperplane in the representation space that separates the inputs based on their properties, indicating
151   linear separability.
152
153
      Another advantage of a simple linear probe is avoiding the risk that a more complex classifier
      might infer features that are not actually used by the network itself (Hupkes et al., 2018). While
154
      other non-linear probes have been explored in the literature (Belinkov, 2021), even studies show-
155
      ing improved performance with complex probes maintain the same logic: Perf(g, f1 , DO , DP ) >
156   Perf(g, f2 , DO , DP ) holds across representations f1 (x) and f2 (x) when evaluated by a consistent
157   probe g. This consistency ensures valid comparison, underscoring that if a property can be predicted
158   well by a simple probe, it is likely relevant to the primary classification task. From an information-
159   theoretic perspective, training the probing classifier g can be viewed as estimating the mutual infor-
160   mation between the learned representations fl (x) and the property z. This mutual information is
161   denoted as I(z; h), where z refers to the property and h represents the intermediate representations
      (Belinkov, 2021).


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      This supervised approach allows us to define hyperplanes or higher-dimensional decision boundaries
163   that partition the embedding space according to the chosen graph property. The R2 score serves
164   as this information-theoretic measure indicating how well the hyperplane divides the inputs in the
165   embedding space. The R2 score (, see appendix A for a formal definition) measures the proportion
166   of variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s). A R2 near
167   1 indicates that the embeddings are highly informative about ẑ, suggesting that the neural model has
168   internalized this property in a linearly accessible manner.
169   By defining specific properties that could divide the embedding space and assessing how well the
170   corresponding hyperplanes make the embedding space linearly separable, we gain quantitative in-
171   sights into the abstract features aggregated within the embeddings. This method moves beyond mere
172   hypothesis generation based on clustering patterns observed through techniques like PCA, provid-
173   ing a rigorous framework for understanding how well the embedding space represents complex
174   graph properties. It can also be thought as complementary from the T-SNE and PCA visualisation
175   techniques, as it provides a quantitative measure of the separability of the embeddings based on
176   hypothesised properties of interest. We illustrate the evolution of the separability of graphs in the
177
      embeddings in figs. 4, 7 and 16 using a T-SNE visualisation and the corresponding separability with
      the properties thanks to the probing.

182   All three datasets have the same setup: given a set of graphs G 1 , G 2 , . . . , G N , predict the corre-
183   sponding binary labels y 1 , y 2 , . . . , y N .
184
185   The Grid-House dataset inspired by (Agarwal et al., 2023) is designed to evaluate the compo-
186   sitionality of GNNs. It features two concepts: a 3x3 grid and a house-shaped graph made of five
187   nodes. The dataset consists of Barabási-Albert (BA) graphs (Barabási, 2009) with a normal distri-
188
      bution of the number of nodes. The negative class includes a BA graph connected to either a grid
      or a house, while the positive class contains a BA graph connected to both a grid and a house (see
189
      fig. 2). In order to ensure that the average number of nodes is the same between classes, the number
190
      of nodes is a uniformly distributed between 6 and 21 for the grid graphs, between 7 and 22 for the
191   house graphs, and between 1 and 16 when both are present. During generation, we ensure no test
192   set leakage by removing isomorphisms. On 2,000 graphs, we perform an 80/20 train/test split.
193
194
      For accurate classification, models need to identify and combine simple patterns. Recognizing iso-
      lated patterns or single node features is not sufficient. The dataset helps investigate how GNNs
195
      combine multiple concepts and addresses the “laziness” phenomenon, where networks learn pat-
196
      terns characterising only one class and predict the other by default (Longa et al., 2023b).
197
198   The dataset has been structured such that an optimal, linearly separable solution requires the com-
199   bination of local properties, such as eigenvector centrality and betweenness centrality, or the identi-
200
      fication of global structural motifs, like counting the number of squares (i.e., four-node cycles). A
      random Barabási-Albert graph can’t contain any four-node cycles, while a grid subgraph will con-
201
      sistently exhibit four such cycles. A house subgraph contains exactly one four-nodes cycle and one
202
      three-nodes cycle. Therefore, a graph that contains both a grid and a house will have a total of five
203   four-node cycles. The presence of a three-node cycle could help the diagnostic of one type of graph
204   in the negative class but is not necessary nor sufficient for solving the classification problem. On the
205   contrary, counting the number of four-node cycle is necessary and sufficient. Thus, distinguishing
206   between the classes does not really necessitates leveraging centrality-based measures but only rec-
207   ognizing the presence of a specific number of four-node cycles, enabling the model to effectively
208   differentiate between the positive and negative classes. Thus the interesting results of fig. 4.
209   The Grid-House dataset serves a critical purpose in our study: it offers a controlled and well-defined
210   environment for rigorously testing our hypotheses. The simplicity of these controlled constraints
211   allow us to verify whether the GNN operates as intended in a setting where extraneous factors are
212   minimized. If a model struggles with this dataset, it would likely underperform on real-world, more
213   complex graphs, underscoring its diagnostic value.

243   (GCN, GIN, or GAT) layers, followed by a pooling operation (mean- (Kipf & Welling, 2017), sum-

245
      optimized the hyperparameters to obtain the best models possible. For the Grid-House dataset the

246
      the one with the best accuracy. As expected, there is a correlation of 0.992 between task accuracy
247   and the maximum probing accuracy as seen in fig. 6.
248
249   We compared different regularization methods. The explicit L2 regularization encourages the net-
250   work to keep the weights small, and we expect that this would make the embeddings less sensitive
251
      to fluctuations in the input data which would translate in later layers being more selective to graph
      properties. Dropout randomly disables a fraction of the neurons during each training iteration which
252
      forces the network to learn redundant representations, as any neuron could be dropped out. These

      Probing pre-pooling layers to predict global graph properties presents challenges due to the varying
      numbers of nodes across graphs and the individual states for each node. To handle this, one ap-
292
      proach would involve concatenating and flattening the embeddings into a matrix with dimensions
293
      (number of nodes, number of features), padding with zeros if a graph has fewer nodes than the max-
294   imum in the dataset. However, flattening introduces issues because nodes do not have a canonical
295   ordering; instead, they follow an arbitrary order based on their appearance in the dataset. This in-
296   consistency can undermine permutation invariance, especially since a simple linear classifier applied
297   to the flattened embeddings is not inherently permutation invariant.
298
      To address this, we compare two methods. One where we mean-pool the node embeddings before
299
      probing them like in fig. 12. But we might miss relevant basic properties. At the same time, we
300   make sure that our probe is not extrapolating more structural information that what is encoded in
301   juxtaposed node embeddings. A probing classifier, by operating on the set of embeddings simul-
302   taneously, could learn to infer relationships and patterns between nodes, leveraging the contextual
303   relationships implicit in their embeddings. The other one where we first sort the embeddings in
304   descending order based on their norms before concatenating fig. 4. This ordering depends only on
305   the inherent properties of the embeddings themselves, not on their original ordering in the graph. As
306   such, it inherently respects permutation invariance because reordering the nodes does not affect their
307   norms or the resulting sorted order. Conveniently, sorting in this way ensures that any padding zeros
308
      align at the end of the sequence, enabling learnable representations for graphs with varying node
      counts. While sorting for permutation invariance is not widely discussed in the literature, it pro-
309
      vides a practical solution by using the embeddings’ properties to enforce consistent ordering across
310
      graphs.

446
447   6.1   E XPECTATIONS
448
      With Grid-House we hypothesized that the GNNs would benefit from leveraging the of square to
449
      render the problem linearly separable. Based on their mathematical restrictions, we hypothesized
450
      that the GIN would perform better than the GAT and the GCN. Regularization methods should
451   either refine the representations, as seen with L2 regularization, or distribute them more broadly, as
452   achieved with dropout.
453
454
      Based on the message-passing paradigm, we anticipated a clear absence of the of square in the
      first layer. Additionally, we expected that the mean-pooling and norm-sorting methods would not
455
      significantly alter how representations are probed, except for basic properties like the number of
456
      nodes, which are easily interpretable from the tensor of node vectors but not from an aggregated
457   representation.
458
459   For the ClinTox Molecular dataset, based on the literature (Kengkanna & Ohue, 2024; Chen et al.,
460
      2021; Jiang et al., 2021) some few properties have been found to be link with toxicity such as the
      node degree (i.e. the valency), subgraph patterns (functional groups, chemical fragments), and the
461
      overall graph connectivity.
462
463   Based on existing literature on functional connectivity (FC) network properties in ASD and MDD
464   appendix G, we hypothesized that specific properties would be critical in classifying brain networks
465   for the fMRI FC connectomes dataset. For ASD, we expect betweenness centrality to play a sig-
466
      nificant role at the node level, reflecting local overconnectivity. At the graph level, we anticipate
      that clustering coefficient, characteristic path length, and small-worldness will be essential in cap-
467
      turing the local and global network disruptions seen in ASD, particularly the imbalance between
468
      local overconnectivity and long-range underconnectivity. For MDD, we hypothesise that increased
469   clustering coefficients, modularity, number of triangles and number of squares will be key features
470   for classification, as they could indicate of heightened local interconnectedness and disrupted global
471   integration.

      6.2   F INDINGS
474
475   We first demonstrate the feasibility of our probing method through the Grid-House dataset. In
476   line with our expectations, The number of squares metric dominated across all layers and models,
477   with GIN showing enhanced expressivity. Secondary properties like avg path lenght figs. 7 and 8
478   showed early significance but gradually diminished through the layers, demonstrating how GNNs
479   act as low-pass filters on graph signals, the receptive field select the most discriminative properties.
480   These findings align with the labels, as the density or the average path length is increase when both
481   a grid and a house are added to a graph. Mean-pooling and norm-sorting methods exhibit different
482
      behaviors that can be explained by the type of information the classifier access : aggregated graph
      data or detailed node embeddings. The probe might also infer relationships and patterns that are
483
      implicitly encoded in individual node embeddings but become explicit when considered collectively.
484
485   The analogy with computer vision lead to the question of supervision signal as we would expect un-
      supervised model to capture structural information more effectively than supervised ones (Kingma


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      & Welling, 2022). Unsupervised learning algorithms can discover hidden patterns and structures in
487   data without explicit instructions, potentially uncovering relationships that may not be immediately
488   obvious to humans. Following this logic, this ability to find latent structures could be advantageous
489   in capturing complex structural information in graph data and lead to similar breakthought than dis-
490   covering new particle physics phenomena using clustering and dimensionality reduction on collider
491   data or identifying subtypes of cancer with clustering and PCA in genomic studies.
492
      Using the ClinTox Molecular dataset to assess molecular toxicity, we explored how key graph
493   properties, such as the average degree and spectral radius, are utilized by our GIN architecture. The
494   average degree, closely linked to atomic valency, reflects a molecule’s potential for interactions. The
495   spectral radius offers a complementary hypothesis, suggesting that the overall structural stability of
496   a molecule, independent of specific atomic features, may also be a key factor in toxicity prediction.
497
      With the fMRI FC connectomes dataset, the results provided new insights that extended beyond
498
      our initial hypothesis. The prominence of the number of triangles highlighted the importance of
499   local structural motifs. This makes sense in the context of functional connectivity, where local over-
500   connectivity in specific brain regions, such as sensory and association cortices, has been observed
501   in individuals with ASD. The strong role of triangle motifs may reflect the tight, redundant local
502   connections that characterize these regions, supporting the hypothesis that local overconnectivity is
503   a key factor in ASD. The spectral radius and density and graph energy being particularly significant
504   is also logical, as these properties are closely related to the overall connectivity strength and the
505   compactness of connections within subnetworks. The outcomes for both the ASD and the MDD
506   datasets showed promising results that should be discussed more deeply with neuroscientists. The
507
      results mainly suggest the importance of graph substructures or spectral and small-world properties
      over more basic graph properties to explain how graph neural networks predict these neurological
508
      disorders in the FC matrices of patients. This is a result which, to the best of our knowledge, has
509
      never been investigated before.

511
512   6.3   F UTURE WORK
513
514   Our methodology has several limitations. While we addressed dataset issues such as leakage and
515   isomorphic graphs, a key challenge remains the lack of guarantees that GNNs find globally optimal
516   solutions, despite their theoretical capacity as universal function approximators. This is particularly
517   evident in fMRI data, where multiple layers of complexity—from MRI limitations and BOLD signal
518   characteristics to Pearson correlation for functional connectivity—introduce noise and inaccuracies.
519   Investigating additional graph properties like girth or complex motifs could be beneficial. Prelim-
520
      inary work on alternative architectures (e.g., GATv2, GraphSAGE, ChebNet, Set2Set, HO-Conv,
      DiffPool) has begun but is not yet complete. An extensive exploration of 1-WL, 2-WL and 3-WL
521
      GNN equivalent could bolster the paper’s contributions by showing clear restrictions and capabil-
522
      ities of these models. As a future encouraging work, the question of supervision signal would be
523   very insightful in understanding what type of properties are learn by unsupervised models.

      7     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   We demonstrate the relevance of using probing classifier as a model-agnostic explainability method
530   for graph neural networks. We manifest both the expressivity of different GNN architectures and
531
      their ability to solve a graph classification problem through optimal feature extraction. They render
      it linearly separable in the space of their embeddings through the computation of graph properties.
532
      We validate domain knownledge with the Clintox Molecular dataset and investigate the possibility
533
      of formulating hypotheses on the emergent dependence of complex systems attributes to basic and
534   more higher level structural properties with the fMRI FC connectomes dataset. We explore how the
535   macroscopic behavior of complex systems emerges from the intricate interplay of their microscopic
536   components. There is a manifest emergence of molecular qualities like toxicity with regard to their
537   structural properties like node degree (atom valency) and spectral radius (the molecule’s stability).
538   To explain a macro attribute, there are instances where structural properties may offer more insight
539   than the mere aggregation of element properties. Both can be interconnected, yet the challenge
      remains in disentangling these relationships.

      We are using R2 as the main metrics. The R2 score (coefficient of determination) measures the
867   proportion of variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s).
868   For a probing classifier, it would indicate how well the probe’s predictions match the actual proper-
869   ties being probed. More formally, R2 is defined as:
870

875   Where: z (i) is the ground truth value of the property ẑ for the i-th data point in the probing dataset
876   DP . ẑ (i) is the predicted value of ẑ produced by the probing classifier g. z̄ is the mean of the ground
877   truth values z (i) over the dataset. The numerator represents the residual sum of squares (how far
878   off the predictions are), and the denominator represents the total sum of squares (the variance in the
879   ground truth values).
880   An R2 value ranges from 0 to 1, where: R2 = 1 means the probing classifier perfectly explains the
881   variance in the target property (i.e., the learned representations fully capture the property). R2 = 0
882   means the probing classifier does no better than predicting the mean z̄, implying the representations
883   do not capture any useful information about the property. Good R2 score should indicate how the
884   model achieves its behavior on the original task Hupkes et al. (2018).
885   A good R2 score gives a sense of how well the features at each layer can be separated linearly
886   to predict the target labels. The second reason is that a more complex probe “bears the risk that
887   the classifier infers features that are not actually used by the network” (Hupkes et al., 2018).
888   Of course, other non linear probes have been explored in the literature (Belinkov, 2021). If a
889   few studies observed better performance with more complex probes, the logic remained the same
890   Perf (g, f1 , DO , mathcalDP ) > Perf (g, f2 , DO , DP ), of two representations f1 (x) and f2 (x),
891   holds across different probes g. The important criteria is to compare the results obtained by the
892   same measurement system. In general, if we can predict one property on one embedding for a given
893
      classification problem, then it means this properly is useful for the problem resolution.
894   From an information-theoretic perspective, training the probing classifier g can be viewed as esti-
895   mating the mutual information between the learned representations fl (x) and the property z. This
896   mutual information is denoted as I(z; h), where z refers to the property and h represents the inter-
897   mediate representations (Belinkov, 2021).

       C    L ITERATURE REVIEW ON RELATED W ORK
973
974
       Existing post-hoc GNN explanations methods can be classified into two main categories: instance-
975    level and model-level methods (Barredo Arrieta et al., 2020). See (Agarwal et al., 2023; Dai et al.,
976    2022) for nice reviews on the subject. In the realm of instance based methods, gradient-based meth-
977    ods use the gradients of the output with respect to the input or intermediate features to measure
978    the importance of each component of the graph. Decomposition-based methods try to decompose
979    the input graph into smaller subgraphs or paths that can account for the output. Surrogate-based
980    methods use a simpler, more interpretable model to approximate the behavior of the original GNN
981    and provide explanations based on the surrogate model. And finally Perturbation-based methods
982    which perturb the input graph by removing or adding nodes, edges, or features, and observe the
983
       changes in the output to identify the influential components. The most mainstream technique, GN-
       NExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) achieves explanation by removing redundant edges from an input
984
       graph instance, maximizing the mutual information between the distribution of subgraphs and the
985
       GNN’s prediction. It is able to provide an explanation both in terms of a subgraph of the input
986    instance to explain, and a feature mask indicating the subset of input node features which is most
987    responsible for the GNN’s prediction.
988
989
       For model-based techniques, few methods come to mind (Saha et al., 2022; Azzolin et al., 2023; Vu
       & Thai, 2020; Wang et al., 2023; Xuanyuan et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021). The
990
       most mainstream method seems to be XGNN (Yuan et al., 2020). The authors of XGNN investi-
991
       gate the possible input characteristics used by a GNN for graph classification. But they formulate
992    the problem as a reinforcement learning problem and generate graph patterns iteratively. Such an
993    iterative approach is often intractable for large graphs. Moreover, it does not allow for both node
994    classification and graph classification explanations, nor does it allow for an investigation of the
995    learning process through the different layers of the GNN. In general, none of the techniques allow
996    for an interpretation of the hidden representations states with graph properties.

2541   Our brain is a network, more precisely a complex network of functionally interconnected regions
2542
       specialised in specific cognitive tasks, sharing information with each other. In the last three decades,
       the field of biological neuroscience and computational cognitive neuroscience have provided and
2543
       incredible amount of knowledge on the role, function and biological structure of such regions of
2544
       interests, aiming at better understanding both the biological organisation of the brain (which we can
2545   refer to as the ’hardware implementation’), the representation embedded in this hardware and the
2546   computational strategy employed to treat this kind of representation (Marr, 1984). In other terms, we
2547   got better at understanding how each region independently organises itself and processes and forms
2548   information (cite Connecting network science and information theory). The main problem for mod-
2549   ern computational neuroscience consists of understanding the brain’s plasticity (how regions change
2550   over time), the inter-individual differences (how regions specialise differently between people) and
2551   how the brain integrates the information (how regions communicate with regard to each other).
2552   For example we understand very well more basic brain structures like the cerebellum due to its high
2553   inter-individual similarity but we have a lot more difficulties modelling the prefrontal cortex which
2554   is so different from an individual to the other (Kanai & Rees, 2011; Gu & Kanai, 2014; Mills et al.,
2555   2021). In other terms, we do understand well the brain operating in segregation but not so much in
2556   integration (Aine, 1995). Functional segregation refers to the distinct specialisation of anatomical
2557   brain regions and functional integration refers to the possible temporal dependencies between the
2558   activity of anatomically separated regions of the brain.
2559   Because the representation of a system composed by agents and interactions among them by a
2560   complex network is an effective way to extract information on the nature and topology of such
2561   interactions, it makes a lot of sense to study the integration of the brain network through its temporal
2562   dependencies. Understanding the mathematical properties of such a network with regard to some
2563   functional state of the brain network therefore helps understanding how the integration system of
2564   the brain and its architecture are linked to ways of processing information. Using Marr’s paradigm
2565
       to reformulate : understanding the functional communicative structure of the brain network helps
       understanding its algorithmic footprint. In terms of information theory, we could say that it helps
2566
       understanding the relationship between topology and dynamics.

2569   One way of accessing the brain activity is to use fMRI imaging. With fMRI measurements at ultra-
2570   high-field (3 Tesla, 7 Tesla or even 11 Tesla), hydrogen nuclei present in water and fat molecules
2571
       align with the scanner’s powerful magnetic field. When radio waves briefly disturb this alignment,
       the nuclei return to their initial alignment with the magnetic field, this is known as the resonance
2572
       and causes local changes in the magnetic field. These changes are detected by receiver coils. The
2573
       collected data from these interactions enable the precise determination of the 3D locations of these
2574   events, in the so-called voxels, which can then be visualised. This process underlies the BOLD
2575   (Blood Oxygen Level Dependent) response, which is crucial for functional Magnetic Resonance
2576   Imaging (fMRI) as it reflects changes in blood flow and oxygenation associated with neuronal ac-
2577   tivity. We use the magnetic response of blood flow as a proxy for brain activity.
2578
       Then, relying on fMRI, we have several ways to study the functional connectivity of the brain. Func-
2579
       tional connectivity is defined as the temporal dependence of neuronal activity patterns of anatom-
2580   ically separated brain regions (Aertsen et al., 1989; Friston et al., 1993) and studies have shown
2581   that we could study functional connectivity between brain regions as the level of coactivation of
2582   functional MRI time-series (Lowe et al., 1998; 2000). As a result, conceptualising the brain as an
2583   integrative network of functionally interacting brain regions offers a powerful framework for under-
2584   standing large-scale neuronal communication. It provides a method for investigating how functional
2585   connectivity and information integration relates to human behaviour and how this organisation may
2586   be altered in neurodegenerative diseases (Bullmore & Sporns, 2009; Greicius et al., 2009).
2587   To understand how a specific brain region interacts with others, researchers most often analyse its
2588   resting-state activity and use simple pearson correlation of time-series data of a region with the
2589   time-series data of all other brain regions, they create a functional connectivity map (fcMap), which
2590   visually represents the strength of these connections (Biswal et al., 1997; Cordes et al., 2000). This
2591   is basically a matrix with value and we can understand it as a non relational data structure, in other
       terms, a graph.
