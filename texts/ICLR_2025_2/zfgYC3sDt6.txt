                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Confidence calibration is critical for the safe deployment of machine learning
               models in the real world. However, such issue in vision-language models like
013
               CLIP, particularly after fine-tuning, has not been fully addressed. In this work,
014
               we demonstrate that existing prompt tuning methods usually lead to a trade-off of
015            calibration between base and new classes: the cross-entropy loss in CoOp causes
016            overconfidence in new classes by increasing textual label divergence, whereas the
017            regularization of KgCoOp maintains the confidence level but results in underconfi-
018            dence in base classes due to the improved accuracy. Inspired by the observations,
019            we introduce Dynamic Outlier Regularization (DOR) to ensure the confidence
020            calibration on both base and new classes after fine-tuning. In particular, we propose
021            to minimize the feature deviation of novel textual labels (instead of base classes)
022            sampled from a large vocabulary. In effect, DOR prevents the increase in textual
023
               divergence for new labels while easing restrictions on base classes. Extensive
               experiments demonstrate that DOR can enhance the calibration performance of
024
               current fine-tuning methods on base and new classes.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs) like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) have become
031   the de facto standard in today’s zero-shot tasks including image recognition (Wortsman et al., 2022),
032   open-vocabulary segmentation (Liang et al., 2023) and knowledge-augmented retrieval (Ming &
033
      Li, 2024). To transfer pre-trained CLIP knowledge to domain-specific downstream tasks efficiently,
      various parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques including prompt tuning (Zhou et al.,
034
      2022b) and adapter (Gao et al., 2024) have been proposed. Despite the promising improvement in
035
      accuracy, the reliability issue such as confidence calibration in fine-tuned VLMs has been largely
036   overlooked. Without fully understanding the miscalibration in fine-tuned VLMs, it can exacerbate
037   safety concerns in high-stakes applications like medical diagnosis and autonomous driving.
038
039
      In confidence calibration, we generally expect the model’s confidence level to be consistent with its
      empirical accuracy. In the literature, zero-shot CLIP is often recognized for its excellent performance
040
      in confidence calibration (Minderer et al., 2021). Prior work (Wang et al., 2024) finds that CLIP
041
      fined-tuned on base classes generally suffers from miscalibration on novel classes within the same
042   task, where the model is expected to generalize (Zhou et al., 2022b; Yao et al., 2023). However, they
043   concentrate on novel classes that are not present in the fine-tuning, without adequately explaining the
044   miscalibration issue on base classes. The community still lacks a comprehensive understanding of
045   the fundamental cause and mitigation strategies of the miscalibration issue during fine-tuning.
046
      In this work, we first investigate how current prompt tuning methods (e.g., CoOp (Zhou et al.,
047
      2022b), KgCoOp (Yao et al., 2023)) interfere with the calibration of CLIP. We empirically find
048   that existing prompt tuning methods fail to maintain the calibration performance on both base and
049   new classes simultaneously, compromising one of them: CoOp exhibits overconfidence on new
050   classes and KgCoOp provides underconfident predictions on base classes. We provide a thorough
051   explanation from the perspective of textual label divergence. In particular, CoOp increases the
052   divergence of textual label distribution through cross-entropy loss, resulting in excessively high
053   confidence misaligned with actual accuracy on new classes. Instead, KgCoOp hinders the increase of
      textual distribution divergence, maintaining the confidence level on base and new classes. However,


                                                        1

054
      this leads to the underconfidence issue due to the improved accuracy on base classes. There arises a
055   question: Is it possible to ensure the calibration on both base and new classes after fine-tuning?
056
057   To tackle the above challenges, we introduce Dynamic Outlier Regularization (DOR). The high-level
058
      idea behind DOR is to control the divergence of unseen textual distribution under the supervision of
      zero-shot CLIP without affecting the vanilla fine-tuning objectives. First, we construct a set of textual
059
      outliers from a large lexical database – WordNet (Miller, 1995), and ensure that the selected classes
060
      are relevant but non-overlapped with the base classes in the fine-tuning task. In fine-tuning, we reduce
061   the feature discrepancy of novel textual labels between the fine-tuned model and the zero-shot CLIP.
062   In each epoch, the novel textual labels are dynamically sampled from the constructed set of textual
063   outliers. Leveraging dynamic textual outliers, DOR prevents the increase in textual divergence for
064   new labels while easing restrictions on base classes.
065
      We verify the effectiveness of DOR over 11 image classification datasets and four types of ImageNets
066
      with covariant shifts, under the evaluation protocol of base-to-new generalization and domain gen-
067   eralization, respectively. Empirical results show that DOR can enhance the overall calibration of
068   existing prompt-tuning methods (see Table 1), on both base and new classes. Moreover, our method
069   can maintain and even improve the generalization performance of those tuning methods (See Table 1
070   & 2 ). DOR also achieves significant improvements in the presence of covariate shifts. In addition to
071   prompt-based tuning methods, we demonstrate that such a regularization criterion can be extended to
072   visual fine-tuning methods with image outliers.
073   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
074
075         1. We provide an in-depth analysis of textual distribution divergence to understand the miscali-
076            bration in fine-tuned CLIP. We also show that current prompt-tuning methods typically lead
077            to a trade-off between base and new classes, compromising one of them.
078         2. We propose DOR, a simple yet effective regularization that ensures the calibration perfor-
079            mance on both base and new classes. Our method is compatible with existing prompt-tuning
080            methods and can be extended to visual fine-tuning methods with image outliers.
081         3. We conduct extensive experiments and show that DOR achieves superior performance on a
082            wide range of real-world tasks. For instance, DOR achieves an average of 8.09% reduction
083            in Expected Calibration Error (ECE) for CoOp over the 11 downstream datasets. The superi-
084            ority of DOR is also verified on medical image datasets like PathMNIST, demonstrating its
085            potential for practical applications.

204   CoOp leads to overconfidence on new classes by increasing the textual divergence. In the analysis
205   of Subsection 3.1 and Figure 2b, we show that CLIP tuned by CoOp exhibits overconfidence on
206   new classes, but keeps excellent calibration on base classes. This is caused by the CE loss, which
207
      maximizes the posterior p(y | x) for the ground-truth label y and minimizes the probability for other
      labels. In other words, CE loss tends to enlarge the distance between the image feature and all textual
208
      features except the ground truth. As the image feature remains unchanged during fine-tuning, it can
209
      be translated to large distances among all textual labels, including base and new classes. This is
210   supported by the results presented in Figure 2a, which shows that CoOp significantly increases the
211   FD score of textual features compared to the zero-shot CLIP. Consequently, as is shown in Figure 3,
212   the gap between the maximum logit and the others widens for both base and new classes. Therefore,
213   the tuned CLIP with CE loss will make softmax predictions with high confidence on both base and
214   new classes. It aligns with the improved accuracy on base classes, but is not consistent with the
215   nearly unchanged accuracy on new classes. This explains why CLIP tuned by CoOp tends to be
      overconfident on new classes.

234   4    M ETHOD : DYNAMIC O UTLIER R EGULARIZATION

236   In the previous analysis, we show that the textual divergence is the key to the calibration performance
237   of fine-tuned CLIP. To preserve the calibration of zero-shot CLIP, our key idea is to regularize the
238   textual divergence of new classes without restricting those of base classes. To this end, we propose to
239   utilize textual outliers to improve the reliability of fine-tuned CLIP. Such a regularization can mitigate
240   the overconfidence in the new classes, which are not explicitly inclusive in the fine-tuning.
241
242   Selecting textual outliers With this in mind, we construct a set of text outliers using nouns from
243   WordNet (Miller, 1995), a large English lexical database containing over 150,000 words. We select
244   nouns from WordNet that do not overlap but share higher-level concept relations with the base classes
245   used in the fine-tuning, and then incorporate them into our regularization term for prompt tuning. We
246   demonstrate the effectiveness of using relevant text outliers in Table 4.

294

497
      task, selecting random data or far-OOD data completely unrelated to the original task may not be
498
      optimal for confidence calibration under base-to-new evaluation. Interestingly, the random selection
499   can serve as a strong baseline, demonstrating the robustness of our proposed regularization item.
500   Moreover, while the oracle can achieve impressive performance on the calibration of new classes, the
501   fixed number of outliers may cause the model to overfit them, resulting in a decline in generalization.
502   Additionally, we do not know which categories will be used at test time during the fine-tuning phase.
503   Therefore, we dynamically use near-OOD as regularization data, which reduces calibration errors on
504   new classes while preserving performance on base classes.
505
506   Can the criterion of DOR be extended to visual tuning? In this paper, we primarily focus on
507   prompt tuning and analyze how textual divergence impacts confidence calibration. Such analysis may
508
      limit the potential scope of CLIP fine-tuning methods. To address this, we further consider a similar
      regularization approach based on image outliers for fine-tuning on visual representation. Specifically,
509
      we use ImageNet-1k (Deng et al., 2009) as an outlier repository and conduct experiments on four
510
      downstream datasets. To avoid potential semantic overlap between the outlier and fine-tuning data,
511   we use class names to filter out images from semantically similar classes. We construct the outlier set
512   by retaining images from 50% of the classes of ImageNet-1k, which ensures these classes differ as
513   much as possible from those used during fine-tuning. For visual representation fine-tuning methods,
514   we utilize CLIP-adapter (Gao et al., 2024) and visual prompt tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022).
515
      As is shown in Table 5, we observe that visual-based DOR can successfully reduce the calibration error
516
      on new classes. For example, DOR outperforms VPT and CLIP-adapter baseline by reducing ECE
517   by 4.64% and 1.82% on the DTD dataset, respectively. In general, visual-based DOR achieves better
518   average calibration across various downstream datasets and leaves space for further improvement.
519   Given that expected image outliers are not always accessible easily as text, a potential method is to
520   generate them by diffusion (Du et al., 2024) for high-quality image outliers.

522
      7   C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
523
524
      In this paper, we introduce Dynamic Outlier Regularization (DOR), a simple yet effective technique
525
      that enhances the confidence calibration on both base and new classes. We show that current prompt
526   tuning methods typically lead to a tradeoff between the base and new classes. Through the textual
527   divergence, we provide a thorough explanation for the limitations of those tuning methods. By
528   utilizing relevant but non-overlapped outlier outliers, DOR regularizes the textual distribution to
529   preserve calibration capacity in zero-shot CLIP. Our method is compatible with existing prompt-
530   tuning methods and can be extended to improve visual fine-tuning methods, like adapters. We hope
531   future research can extend the insight in this work to other VLMs.
532
533   Limitations Similar to previous regularization methods of CLIP, our method involves a hyperpa-
534   rameter λ to control the weight of regularization, which will require extra computational costs for
535   tuning. Moreover, our analysis is limited in the scope of CLIP, leaving other kinds of VLMs to be
536
      explored in the future.

      A     R ELATED WORK
757
758
      Vision-language models. Large pre-trained large vision-language models (Jia et al., 2021; Radford
759
      et al., 2021) have been verified that effectively comprehend visual concepts using language supervision
760   and apply them in downstream tasks (e.g. image classification (Radford et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
761   2022b; Lu et al., 2022; Naeem et al., 2023), knowledge-augmented retrieval(Ming & Li, 2024) and
762   visual question answering (Parelli et al., 2023)) in a zero-shot manner. Despite VLM’s effectiveness in
763   generalizing new visual concepts, the performance of zero-shot CLIP still lags behind the fine-tuned
764   performance on specific downstream tasks (Zhang et al., 2024b). To further boost the downstream
765   adaptation of pre-trained VLMs, many parameter-efficient tuning methods like vanilla prompt tuning
766   (Zhou et al., 2022b;a; Khattak et al., 2023a) and adapter tuning (Gao et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
767   2022) have been proposed for high efficiency. Moreover, many regularization-based tuning methods
768
      have been proposed to preserve the generalization performance on unseen classes Yao et al. (2023;
      2024); Zhu et al. (2023); Roy & Etemad (2023); Khattak et al. (2023b). Despite the great success of
769
      CLIP fine-tuning, their effectiveness on safety-related evaluation like confidence has largely been
770
      overlooked, which is essential for real-world deployment.
771
772
773
      Confidence calibration. Confidence calibration has been widely studied to ensure that the con-
      fidence levels output by models accurately reflect their empirical accuracy. To achieve this, the
774
      state-of-the-art calibration methods can be categorized into regularization methods and post-hoc
775
      methods. For regularization methods, they either explicitly or implicitly regularize modern neural
776   networks to have better calibration. Although regularization methods may not designed for calibration,
777   they generally have better calibration performance including L2 regularization (Guo et al., 2017),
778   Entropy regularization (Pereyra et al., 2017), focal loss (Mukhoti et al., 2020), etc. On the other hand,
779   post-hoc methods fix the output probability after the training phase. For post-hoc methods, the most
780   representative and simple method is temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017), which learns a single
781   scalar for rescaling the softmax logit. ATS (Joy et al., 2023) modifies the predicted confidence by
782   per-data-point adaptive temperature. Another type of post-hoc calibration is binning-based calibra-
783   tion (Zadrozny & Elkan, 2001; 2002). For instance, Mix-n-Match (Zhang et al., 2020) leverages
784
      ensemble and composition techniques to achieve data efficiency and maintain accuracy in confidence
      estimates. Recently, given that existing post-hoc calibration on base classes can not transfer to new
785
      classes, DAC (Wang et al., 2024) fixes the logit scale of prediction via textual deviation-informed
786
      score in a post-hoc manner. Different from them, we address the calibration issue during the fine-
787   tuning phase. In this work, we introduce a regularization based on dynamic outliers. We demonstrate
788   that DOR boosts the calibration performance of many existing state-of-the-art prompt tuning methods
789   of CLIP without affecting the vanilla fine-tuning objective.
790
791   Outlier regularization in trustworthy machine learning. The outlier plays an important role in
792   trustworthy machine learning research including out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, noisy label
793   learning, adversarial attack, long-tailed datasets re-balancing, etc. In OOD detection, outliers are
794   typically used to simulate the distribution of OOD data, thereby increasing the distinction between
795   ID data and OOD data (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Ming et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024).
796   Recently, Dream-OOD generate the expected OOD data by diffusion (Du et al., 2024). In noisy label
797   learning, ODNL (Wei et al., 2021) leverages outliers as dynamical noisy labels to improve model
798   robustness against noisy labels. To address the extreme class imbalance, Open-sampling (Wei et al.,
799
      2022) re-balance class priors via open-set noisy labels. OAT (Lee et al., 2021) leverages outlier data
      to improve model generalization in adversarial robustness, which regularizes the softmax probabilities
800
      to be a uniform distribution for outliers. In this paper, we utilize text outliers to control the divergence
801
      of unseen textual distribution, and further improve the calibration of fine-tuned CLIP.

903    textual divergence is a significant factor for confidence estimation in this part. As the image feature
904    remains unchanged in the fine-tuning, the textual divergence can be translated to the variance of
905    logits, which is computed by the similarity between the image feature and different text features. In
906    the following, we formally show the relationship between the logit variance and the confidence.

       In DOR, we randomly sample a batch of textual outliers from the selected textual outlier set, in each
1171
       iteration. Therefore, the textual outliers used in each iteration can be different, which establishes
1172   a dynamic regularization. To evaluate the impact of outlier update frequency on performance, we
1173   conduct an ablation study by varying the frequency at which outliers are sampled for the batch with
1174   the update intervals in the range [1, 10, 100, 1000]. We present this ablation with average ECE on the
1175   base-to-new datasets with two prompt tuning methods including CoOp and CoPrompt.
1176
       As shown in Table 11, low update frequencies (or large update intervals) are observed to negatively
1177
       impact the calibration performance of DOR. The phenomenon suggests that infrequent updates may
1178   allow the model to overfit to noise and reduce its calibration performance. In short, The experimental
1179   results highlight the benefits of employing a dynamic update strategy in DOR, since it helps mitigate
1180   overfitting to noise (Wei et al., 2021) and achieves superior calibration performance.

1271   I       C OMPARISON WITH EXISTING CALIBRATION METHODS
1272
1273   To further validate the effectiveness of our proposed DOR, we compare it with two recent calibration
1274   approaches for CLIP. We consider two main calibration strategies: post-hoc scaling and regularization-
1275   based training. For post-hoc scaling, we compare DOR with Zero-Shot-Enabled Temperature Scaling
1276   (ZS-TS) (LeVine et al., 2023). Specifically, we perform post-hoc calibration on the fine-tuned
1277   model using ImageNet-1k and evaluate the learnable temperature τ on both the base and new
1278   classes. For regularization-based calibration, we incorporate DOR into calibrated robust fine-tuning
1279
       method (CaRot) (Oh et al., 2024) like Equation 10. We report the average ECE performance on the
       base-to-new datasets.
1280
1281
1282   DOR outperforms post-hoc scaling under base-to-new evaluation. We present the results in
1283   Table 15. We observe that the temperature τ optimized on ImageNet-1k significantly mitigates the
1284   overconfidence and improves the calibration of the fine-tuned CLIP on new classes. For instance,
1285   it reduces the Expected Calibration Error (ECE) of the state-of-the-art method PromptSRC from
1286   4.15% to 3.48%. However, such calibration can not used on the base classes. ZS-TS exacerbates the
1287   model’s underconfidence and increases the ECE from 3.75% to 6.74% on PromptSRC. In contrast,
1288
       our approach effectively mitigates the miscalibration issue on new classes while maintaining the
       calibration performance on the base classes.
1289
1290
1291   DOR boosts existing regularization-based methods for better calibration. As is shown in
1292   Table 16, we observe that CaRot achieves decent calibration performance on both base and new
1293   classes. Furthermore, our DOR method can be effectively integrated into CaRot and improve
1294   calibration on both base and new classes. For instance, DOR reduces the ECE by 1.14% and 1.21%
1295   on base and new classes of the OxfordPets dataset, respectively. This demonstrates that DOR is a
       flexible regularization strategy compatible with various fine-tuning methods.
