009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
                Large language models often encounter challenges with static knowledge and hal-
012             lucinations, which undermine their reliability. Retrieval-augmented generation
013             (RAG) mitigates these issues by incorporating external information. However,
014             user queries frequently contain noise and intent deviations, necessitating query
015             rewriting to improve the relevance of retrieved documents. In this paper, we in-
016             troduce DMQR-RAG, a Diverse Multi-Query Rewriting framework designed to
017             improve the performance of both document retrieval and final responses in RAG.
018             Specifically, we investigate how queries with varying information quantities can
019             retrieve a diverse array of documents, presenting four rewriting strategies that op-
020
                erate at different levels of information to enhance the performance of baseline
                approaches. Additionally, we propose an adaptive strategy selection method that
021
                minimizes the number of rewrites while optimizing overall performance. Our
022
                methods have been rigorously validated through extensive experiments conducted
023             in both academic and industry settings.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) possess powerful comprehension and generation abilities (Touvron
030   et al., 2023a;b), demonstrating remarkable performance across various downstream tasks (Jiang
031   et al., 2023; Su et al., 2024). However, the parametric knowledge within LLMs is inherently static,
032
      making it challenging for them to provide up-to-data information in real-time scenarios (Yao et al.,
      2023). Additionally, LLMs are prone to hallucinations when addressing factual questions (Guan
033
      et al., 2024; Hoshi et al., 2023), undermining the reliability of generated answers.
034
035   To address these issues, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Gao et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024)
036   has emerged as a method to enhance LLMs by retrieving and incorporating external knowledge.
037   However, due to noise and intent bias in the original queries, direct retrieval often fails to yield
038
      sufficiently relevant documents (Ma et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2024). Therefore, query rewriting is
      critical for retrieving the pertinent documents as shown in Figure 1(b).
039
040   Substantial research has explored improved methods for query rewriting (Chan et al., 2024; Wang
041   et al., 2024a; Mao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023), which can be
042   broadly categorized into two families: training-based and prompt-based approaches. Training-based
043   methods (Wang et al., 2024a; Mao et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023) involve supervised fine-tuning of
044
      models using annotated data or reinforcement learning, leveraging downstream retrieval metrics as
      rewards. In contrast, prompt-based methods (Zheng et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
045
      2023a) utilize prompt engineering to guide LLMs in specific rewriting strategies. However, most
046
      methods generate a single rewritten query, which often leads to a lack of diversity in the retrieved
047   documents. This results in a low recall of genuinely relevant documents, as demonstrated by our
048   experiments. Furthermore, some methods focus on specific query types, such as complex multi-hop
049   or multi-intent queries, limiting their applicability for general-purpose queries 1 .
050
051       1

052   while “Which paper has more citations, the Transformer paper or the ResNet paper?” is a multi-intent query.
053   Both require multiple retrievals to answer. In contrast, “What is the citation count for the Transformer paper?”
      is a general-purpose query that can be answered with a single retrieval.

084   In this paper, we propose DMQR-RAG, a general-purpose multi-query rewriting method aimed
085   at retrieving diverse documents with a high recall of relevant documents in RAG. Our motivation
086
      is shown in Figure 1. The most relevant approach to our work is RAG-Fusion (Rackauckas, 2024),
      which utilizes multiple rewriting queries to retrieve additional documents and applies the Reciprocal
087
      Rank Fusion algorithm (Cormack et al., 2009) for reranking. Unlike RAG-Fusion, our method
088
      is inspired by information diversity to enhance the transmission of information from queries to
089   documents (Maron & Kuhns, 1960; Baeza-Yates et al., 1999; Weikum & Vossen, 2001), leading to
090   the development of four rewriting strategies based on different levels of information. Each rewritten
091   query can retrieve different documents, and we subsequently use a cross-attention embedding model
092   to rerank these documents. However, increasing the number of rewritten queries is not always
093   beneficial, as it can introduce noise. To address this, we employ a rewriting strategy selection
094   method that adaptively identifies a limited number of suitable strategies for rewriting and retrieval
095   based on the specific query. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
096
097          • We propose a general-purpose multi-query rewriting framework that employs various
098            strategies based on the amount of information. Our findings indicate that multi-query
099            rewriting generally outperforms single-query rewriting, with our information-based multi-
100
               query approach often surpassing vanilla RAG-Fusion.
101
             • We introduce a rewriting strategy selection method that identifies the most suitable ap-
102            proach for each query, achieving better performance in both document retrieval and final
103            responses in RAG with fewer rewrites.
104
105          • To facilitate fair comparisons of the rewriting module in RAG, we establish a standard-
106            ized setup for rewriting based on well-established practices, mitigating the influence of
107            extraneous variables within the complex RAG pipeline. Extensive evaluations under both
               academic and industry settings validate the effectiveness of our methods.

      We first introduce the traditional RAG workflow and propose a standardized setup to explore how the
111   rewriting strategy impacts performance. Building on this foundation, we present our DMQR-RAG
112   framework and the adaptive rewriting selection method.

      2.1     F ORMAL S ETUP FOR M ULTI -Q UERY R EWRITING
115
116   Given a user’s query q, the traditional RAG process begins by rewriting the query to obtain q ′ . Next,
117   a retriever searches for relevant documents, represented by the set D. These documents are then
118   reranked to produce D′ . Subsequently, the top K documents are concatenated with the original
119   query q and input into an LLM to generate the final response A. The entire process is as follows:
120
          q ′ = Rewriter(q),    D = Retriever(q ′ ),     D′ = Reranker(D),          A = LLM(q, TopK(D′ )). (1)
121
122
      However, the elongated pipeline introduces multiple processing steps, each of which can be executed
123
      in various ways, impacting the final outcome (Wang et al., 2024b). These factors include, but are
124   not limited to: the source and quality of documents, the chunking strategy, the embedding model
125   for document chunks, the vector database for storing embeddings, the algorithms used for retrieval
126   (keyword, semantic, or hybrid), and the reranker and LLM models. In this context of high variability
127   and complexity, it is challenging to independently evaluate the effectiveness of the rewriting module.
128   Therefore, a formal setup tailored for rewriting is essential.
129
      Without loss of generality, we propose to standardize the Retriever and Reranker to current main-
130   stream methods, and evaluate the (multi-query) Rewriter using different LLMs to demonstrate that
131   the rewriting approach is generalizable across various LLMs in a recognized and fair context. Specif-
132   ically, we treat the Retriever as a black box, using results from the Bing search engine to ensure data
133   timeliness and to avoid the complexities of hyperparameter tuning required by other retrieval meth-
134   ods. Additionally, we employ the widely adapted BAAI-BGE-reranker (Xiao et al., 2023) as the
135   Reranker. In summary, our formal setup for multi-query rewriting can be defined as follows:
136
                                        q ′ = {q, RS1 (q), RS2 (q), ..., RSn (q)},                                  (2)
137
138             D = RetrieverBing (q ′ ),      D′ = RerankerBGE (D),           A = LLM(q, TopK(D′ )).               (3)
139                                                                   ′
      where RSi denotes different rewriting strategies, and q contains the original query q along with
140   all its rewritten versions 2 . These queries are used for retrieval, and all the retrieved documents are
141   collated and submitted to the downstream modules.

143   2.2     T HE D IVERSE M ULTI -Q UERY R EWRITING F RAMEWORK
144
145   Due to their advanced natural language understanding capabilities (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), LLMs
146   are often used as the foundational tool for query rewriting. In this section, we will first explore vari-
147   ous LLM-based rewriting strategies from an informational perspective, followed by an introduction
148   to the rewriting strategy selection method.
149
150   2.2.1    R EWRITING S TRATEGIES
151
      Current rewriting methods often rely on a straightforward, single rewrite of the original query, which
152
      frequently fails to retrieve relevant documents. Additionally, multi-query rewriting approaches, such
153   as RAG-Fusion (Rackauckas, 2024), tend to be simplistic, producing rewrites that are nearly identi-
154   cal and lacking in diversity. This limitation hampers their ability to enhance overall performance.
155
156
      An effective multi-query rewriting strategy should meet the following informational criteria: each
      rewritten query must be diverse, providing unique information not present in the others. By en-
157
      hancing the diversity of information in the rewritten queries, we increase the likelihood of retrieving
158
      a broader range of documents, ultimately improving our chances of obtaining genuinely relevant
159   documents (Maron & Kuhns, 1960; Baeza-Yates et al., 1999; Weikum & Vossen, 2001).
160
          2
161         Note that incorporating the original query q is essential because some users can indeed express their inten-
      tions accurately, providing valuable context that improves the relevance of the retrieved documents.


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Specifically, we propose four rewriting strategies that focus on adjusting or preserving the infor-
163   mation in the original query. These strategies aim to refine the query while also providing unique
164   insights to facilitate the retrieval of a diverse set of documents.
165
166   Information Equality. User-generated queries often contain irrelevant noise and unclear intent
167   (Gao et al., 2023b), leading to deviations from the intended retrieval objective. Therefore, a general
168   rewriting approach is necessary to denoise and refine the original query. We refer to this method
169   as General Query Rewriting (GQR), which refines the original query q while retaining all relevant
170   information and eliminating noise, thereby enhancing retrieval precision. This strategy is in line
171   with the approach proposed by Ma et al. (2023).
172   Furthermore, to ensure alignment with search engine preferences while maintaining the same
173   amount of information, we introduce Keyword Rewriting (KWR). This strategy aims to extract
174   all keywords from the query q, particularly nouns and subjects. By doing so, KWR enables search
175   engines to directly address user needs and quickly locate relevant documents (Gupta & Vidyapeeth,
176   2017), while also reducing the parsing burden on the search engine.
177
178   Information Expansion. By incorporating prior information into the original query, we can assist
179   the retriever in recalling a more diverse range of documents for responses (Gao et al., 2023a; Wang
180   et al., 2023a). We propose leveraging the prior knowledge of LLMs to generate a pseudo-answer
181   for retrieval, a method we term Pseudo-Answer Rewriting (PAR). The rationale behind this method
182   is twofold: first, inherent semantic differences between queries and answers can introduce retrieval
183
      biases; second, the pseudo-answer enriches the original query with additional information. Although
      this pseudo-answer may not be factually accurate, it is semantically aligned with the real answer and
184
      can capture relevant response patterns, aiding in the retrieval of more pertinent documents, especially
185
      when LLMs encounter hallucinations (Guan et al., 2024; Hoshi et al., 2023).
186
187
      Information Reduction. When a query contains excessive detail, the retriever often struggles to
188
      identify the most essential and useful information (Zheng et al., 2024), resulting in discrepancies
189   between the retrieved documents and the user’s primary needs. Additionally, this situation places a
190   significant burden on downstream modules, such as the reranker and generator (Wang et al., 2023b).
191   Therefore, it becomes crucial to discard superfluous details and extract key information. We define
192   this strategy as Core Content Extraction (CCE).
193
      To maintain universality, we combine all strategies to create a scalable strategy pool, denoted by
194
195                                   RS = {RSGQR , RSKWR , RSPAR , RSCCE , . . . },                              (4)
196
197   where RSGQR , RSKWR , RSPAR , RSCCE represent the four rewriting strategies mentioned above 3 . This
198   strategy pool can dynamically incorporate new rewriting strategies based on practical needs, en-
199   suring that our method remains flexible and universally applicable. For example, we can include
200
      sub-query rewriting for multi-hop queries and even integrate training-based methods to enhance
      effectiveness further (Wang et al., 2024a; Mao et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023).
201

      2.2.2      A DAPTIVE R EWRITING S TRATEGY S ELECTION
204   In practical industrial scenarios, user queries are diverse. While multi-query rewriting can enhance
205   retrieval diversity, applying a fixed set of strategies to every query is not optimal. Therefore, it is
206   crucial to dynamically select rewriting strategies tailored to each specific query, generating multiple
207   rewritten queries that best suit the original intent.
208
      We implement this selection method using lightweight prompting and few-shot learning. Specifi-
209
      cally, we incorporate descriptions of the rewriting strategies in the strategy pool RS into the prompts
210   of the LLMs as contextual information. These descriptions outline the applicable query types and
211   the roles of each strategy, enabling the LLMs to gain a comprehensive understanding of all available
212   rewriting strategies. To enhance strategy selection in challenging cases, we also adopt a few-shot ap-
213   proach by providing the LLMs with multiple demonstrations, which assist them in selecting suitable
214   rewriting strategies for difficult queries. The detailed prompt is shown in Table 1.

255

257   AmbigNQ (Min et al., 2020), designed to address the inherent ambiguity in Natural Questions

260
      various question types and necessitates up-to-date world knowledge for accurate responses. We also
261
      conduct experiments on industry datasets, which will be described later.

291   3.1.3    BASELINES
292
      We adopt prompting and fine-tuning methods as our baseline approaches. For prompt-based meth-
293
      ods: (1) LLM Rewrite (abbreviated as Rewrite) (Ma et al., 2023) utilizes prompts to harness the
294   inherent capabilities of large language models for general retrieval rewriting. (2) Hyde (Gao et al.,
295   2023a) employs zero-shot prompting to guide large language models in creating a pseudo-document
296   that captures the semantics of the target document, which is then used for retrieval. For fine-tuning
297   methods: (1) Rewrite-Retrieve-Read (RRR) (Ma et al., 2023) uses the accuracy of model responses
298   obtained through rewriting and retrieval as a reward signal to fine-tune the T5 model via reinforce-
299   ment learning. (2) RQ-RAG (Chan et al., 2024) constructs a dataset of search queries across multiple
300   scenarios and trains a model to perform rewriting, decomposition, and clarification of the original
301   queries. Additionally, we compare the effectiveness of using the original query directly, without
302
      rewriting, referred to as Original Query Retrieval (OQR).
303
304   3.1.4    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
305
      The experiments are implemented using PyTorch and employ several LLMs for our rewriting tasks,
306
      demonstrating that our DMQR-RAG framework is applicable to various models, including Llama3-
307
      8B (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2-7B Yang et al. (2024), and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). After
308   generating multiple rewrites, document retrieval and response generation are conducted according
309   to the setup outlined in Section 2.1. Specifically, each proposed rewriting method independently
310   retrieves 10 documents, recalling a total of 50 documents, which are then reranked by the reranker.
311   By default, we use the Bing search engine as the retriever and BGE (Chen et al., 2009) as the
312   reranker. Both the baselines and our method utilize GPT-4 as the response model, leveraging the
313   reranked Top-5 documents as additional context.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
649
650
      Existing rewriting methods can be categorized into two categories: training-based and prompt-based.
651
      Training-based methods (Wang et al., 2024a; Mao et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023) use labeled data for
652
      supervised fine-tuning on the model or employ reward scores for reinforcement learning to achieve
653   better rewriting results. RQ-RAG (Chan et al., 2024) constructs an innovative dataset that contains
654   search queries and rewritten results across multiple scenarios, which is used to train an end-to-end
655   model that refines search queries. RRR (Ma et al., 2023) proposes a novel training strategy for
656   rewriting, which leverages the performance of response model as a reward and optimizes retrieval
657   queries through reinforcement learning. However, these methods require substantial costs for dataset
658   construction and training.
659   Prompt-based methods (Zheng et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a) leverage various
660   prompting strategies to directly instruct large models to perform multiple rewriting tasks. Hyde (Gao
661   et al., 2023a) utilizes LLMs to generate a pseudo-answer for the original query in advance. This
662   pseudo-answer is semantically closer to the correct answer, making it easier to retrieve the correct
663   results. Step-back Prompting (Zheng et al., 2024) tackles queries with extensive details by rewriting
664   them at a higher conceptual level to retrieve more comprehensive answers. Least-to-most prompting
665   (Zhou et al., 2023) decomposes a complex query into several easier-to-address subqueries, which are
666   individually retrieved to gather all documents necessary to answer the original query. While avoiding
667
      additional training costs, these methods focus only on specific query types, lack generalizability, and
      produce retrieval results with insufficient diversity. Therefore, our approach proposes multi-strategy
668
      rewriting, using prompt-based methods to guide the model in preforming multiple rewrites according
669
      to different strategies. This effectively addresses various types of queries and enhances the diversity
670   of retrieval results.

673   B    T HE EVALUATION CRITERIA IN INDUSTRIAL SCENARIO
674
675   We use two anonymous internal models of 13B and 7B sizes as the rewriter and generator, respec-
676   tively. To measure the performance of end-to-end responses, we incorporated several relevant met-
677   rics: Correctness, Faithfulness, Relevance, Thoroughness, Harmlessness, Readability, Logicality,
678   and Creativity, each rated from 0 to 5.
679
680   Correctness The correctness criterion measures the accuracy of the response in relation to the
681   initial query. It assesses whether the provided answer is factually accurate and directly addresses
682   the question posed. Correctness is fundamental as it builds the trustworthiness of the system in an
683   industrial scenario, where precision is paramount.
684
685   Faithfulness Faithfulness refers to the accuracy and factual consistency of the generated response
686   with respect to the source information from which it is derived. An answer is considered faithful if
687   the claims made in the answer can be inferred from the context.
688
689   Relevance The concept of answer relevance entails that the response should address the asked
690   question without introducing other useless information. An answer is deemed relevant if it properly
691   tackles the question. This suggests that the metric does not consider the factuality of the response,
692   but it does incur penalties if the answer is either incomplete or contains superfluous information.
693
694   Thoroughness The thoroughness criteria focus on how adequately the response solve the problem.
695   An answer is considered detailed when it not only answers the question but also elaborates on the
696   how and why, encompasses related dimensions, and anticipates follow-up questions or concerns that
697   might arise from the initial inquiry
698
699   Harmlessness The criterion of harmlessness emphasizes the importance of generating responses
700   that do not perpetuate or incite harm or bias. This includes avoiding language that could be con-
701   sidered offensive, discriminatory, or incendiary. It also encompasses ensuring that responses do not
      propagate misinformation or dubious claims that could lead to real-world consequences.


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Readablity The readability criterion assesses how easily the text of the response can be read and
703   understood by users. A highly readable response is one that uses clear language, avoids unnecessary
704   jargon, and structures information in a way that is consistent and easy to follow
705
706   Logicality In scenarios involving reasoning tasks, the logicality criterion is particularly crucial. It
707   ensures that responses are not only accurate but also logically structured, providing clear and rational
708   explanations for how conclusions are derived from the presented facts. This is vital for maintaining
709   the system’s reliability and user trust.
710
711   Creativity This criterion evaluates the novelty and uniqueness of the responses provided to a
712   query. It ensures that the solutions are not only effective but also innovative, potentially offering
713   new perspectives or methods that may improve upon existing processes.
