009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Scientific posters are an effective and expressive medium for conveying the
012            core ideas of academic papers, facilitating the communication of research tech-
013            niques. However, creating high-quality scientific posters is a complex and time-
014            consuming task that requires advanced skills to summarize key concepts and ar-
015            range them logically and visually appealingly. Previous studies have primarily
016            focused on either content extraction or the layout and composition of posters, of-
017            ten relying on small-scale datasets. The scarcity of publicly available datasets
018            has further limited advancements in this field. In this paper, we introduce a new
019
               task called layout-aware scientific poster generation (LayoutSciPG), which aims
               to generate flexible posters from scientific papers through integrated automatic
020
               content extraction and layout design. To achieve this, we first build a new dataset,
021
               namely SciPG, containing over 10,000 pairs of scientific papers and their corre-
022            sponding posters. We then propose a multimodal extractor-generator framework,
023            which employs a multimodal extractor to retrieve key text and image elements
024            from the papers and designs an interactive generator with an adaptive memory
025            mechanism to seamlessly paraphrase the extracted content and generate a struc-
026            tured layout. This approach effectively tackles challenges related to GPU mem-
027            ory consumption and long-term dependencies when handling the lengthy inputs
028            (scientific papers) and outputs (posters). Finally, both qualitative and quantita-
029            tive evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach while highlighting
030
               remaining challenges.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recent years have witnessed a significant increase in the number of scientific papers published in
036   various academic conferences and journals. For example, AAAI, a prominent international confer-
037   ence on artificial intelligence, received over 2,300 papers in 2024 alone. It is time-consuming for
038
      researchers to digest all these papers. Scientific posters offer an effective and expressive way to
      present the core ideas and findings from original papers, enabling researchers to quickly grasp the
039
      overall content. However, creating a high-quality scientific poster from scratch that is both informa-
040
      tive and aesthetically pleasing is a challenging task. Poster design is a complex and time-consuming
041   task that requires both a deep understanding of the paper’s content and experience in design. Conse-
042   quently, the need for automatic generation of readable, informative, and visually appealing posters
043   has become increasingly important.
044
      Scientific poster generation involves multimodal understanding and reasoning, as both the paper
045
      document and the poster contain tightly integrated text and image elements 1 . Previous approaches
046
      for automatic poster generation have primarily focused on either the layout and composition of
047   posters (Paramita & Khodra, 2016; Qiang et al., 2016; 2019) or content extraction (Xu & Wan,
048   2021; 2022). These methods have not performed well because those emphasizing layout and com-
049   position often neglect the importance of robust content extraction, relying on simple summarization
050   models such as TextRank (Mihalcea & Tarau, 2004). Conversely, approaches that focus on content
051   extraction typically use LaTeX templates for poster generation, which lack diversity and flexibility

097   2.1       T EXT S UMMARIZATION
098
099   Text summarization generally falls into two categories: extractive summarization (Cheng & Lapata,
100   2016; Yao et al., 2018; Nallapati et al., 2017) and abstractive summarization (Nallapati et al., 2016;
101
      Yao et al., 2020; See et al., 2017). Extractive summarization focuses on identifying the most salient
      parts of the input document and using them directly as the output summary. For example, Cheng
102
      & Lapata (2016) employed a neural attention model to select sentences or words from the input
103
      document as the summary. Similarly, Nallapati et al. (2017) developed a recurrent neural network
104   (RNN) for extractive summarization. While abstractive summarization involves paraphrasing or
105   rewriting the important parts of the input document into a concise summary. For instance, Lead3
106   (Nallapati et al., 2016) used an attentional encoder-decoder RNN for abstractive text summarization.
107

      See et al. (2017) designed a hybrid pointer-generator network that can copy words from the source
109   text via pointing, enabling more robust summary generation. However, these approaches are tailored
110   for pure text summarization and do not account for the graphical elements in the original document.
111
112
      Our LayoutSciPG combines both abstractive and extractive summarization, as it requires extracting
      key elements from a document and rewriting it into a concise form. A closely related task is scientific
113
      document summarization (Jaidka et al., 2016; Parveen et al., 2016), but existing work in that area
114
      has primarily focused on producing pure text summaries. In contrast, our focus is on generating
115   multimodal scientific posters.

118   2.2      M ULTIMODAL S UMMARIZATION
119
      Our LayoutSciPG closely aligns with multimodal summarization, which focuses on extracting the
120
      most important information from various modalities to create summaries. For example, Zhu et al.
121
      (2018) introduced the first multimodal summarization (MSMO) model and compiled a dataset that
122   includes both text and image modalities. Li et al. (2020b) extended this approach to video-based
123   news articles, employing conditional self-attention for text and video fusion. More recently, He et al.
124   (2023) proposed a unified transformer-based model that effectively aligns and attends to multimodal
125   inputs. While LayoutSciPG similarly involves summarizing multimodal documents, it also requires
126   structuring the multimodal summary within a specific layout, incorporating layout prediction as an
127   essential component.

129   2.3      AUTOMATIC P OSTER G ENERATION
130
131   Existing methods for poster generation have primarily concentrated on either the scientific poster
132   composition (Paramita & Khodra, 2016; Qiang et al., 2016; 2019) or content extraction (Xu &
133   Wan, 2021; 2022). Approaches that emphasize layout composition often overlook the importance of
134   robust content extraction, relying on basic summarization models like TextRank (Mihalcea & Tarau,
135   2004). In addition, they have typically employed simple probabilistic graphical models to infer
136
      panel attributes, but these approaches require human annotation of poster panels. On the other hand,
      content-focused methods typically use predefined LaTeX templates for poster generation, which
137
      limits both the flexibility and aesthetic appeal of the resulting scientific posters. Additionally, current
138
      datasets in this domain are relatively small, with fewer than 300 paper-poster pairs each. To address
139   this, we have constructed a new dataset comprising 11,302 pairs of high-quality scientific documents
140   and posters.

185   4.1   OVERVIEW
186   LayoutSciPG aims to generate a poster from a multimodal document containing both text and im-
187   ages. We decompose this task into several subtasks: first, summarizing the document by extracting
188   important sentences and images; next, paraphrasing the extracted sentences into a concise format
189   suitable for poster presentation; and finally, placing the paraphrased sentences and images in ap-
190   propriate locations on the poster. To achieve this, we propose a multimodal extractor-generator
191   framework, which performs multimodal content extraction, paraphrasing, and layout generation.
192   Figure 1 provides an overview of our approach, which includes the following modules:
193
             • A Multimodal Document Extractor (MDE) encodes both sentences and images from the
194            paper document and selects which of them should be extracted;
195
196
             • An Interactive Generator (IG) fulfills both paraphrasing the extracted sentences and lay-
               out generation of text and image elements.
197
198   Given a scientific paper document D = {T , V }, where T = {t1 , t2 , ..., tn } is a sequence of n
199   sentences and V = {v1 , v2 , ..., vm } is a collection of m images. MDE first extracts the important
200   multimodal elements X = {Xt , Xv } from D, and IG paraphrases the selected sentences Xt into Yt
201   and predicts the layout information L = {Lt , Lv } of {Yt , Xv }, where Xt and Yt denote the directly
202   extracted and paraphrased sentences, respectively. Xv is a subset of images extracted from D. Lt
203
      = {xt0 , y0t , w0t , ht0 } and Lv = {xv0 , y0v , w0v , hv0 } are the bounding box (bbox) coordinates of the Yt
      and Xv , where the first two values indicate the top left corner location, and the last two indicate the
204
      width and height.

      4.3   I NTERACTIVE G ENERATOR
250
251   We propose an interactive generator that unifies multimodal generation (i.e., text, images, and lay-
252   out) using BART (Lewis et al., 2019) in an interactive fashion. Given an input list from the extractor,
253   at each step, we automatically feed one element of the input into the generator, which then returns
254
      its predicted result. This process is repeated iteratively until the entire input list has been processed.
      Instead of relying on a dedicated module for image representation, we adopt a similar approach
255
      as in the extractor by utilizing an index token, such as “[img i]”, to indicate that the i-th image is
256
      the target. This allows all target outputs to be transformed into a textual format, while the visual
257   input are represented as the image feature, which are concatenated with textual token embeddings
258   as input for the generator. Additionally, we encode the bounding boxes for each target sentence and
259   image in the format “x0 , y0 , w, h”, appended at the end of each sequence as the final target output,
260   where (x0 , y0 ) represents the top-left corner coordinates and (w, h) denotes the width and height.
261   An example of the input-to-output format is provided on the right side of Figure 1. Note that we
262   use four “[mask]” tokens to indicate the layout coordinates in the input, ensuring alignment with
263   the pretraining task. To address the challenge of long target generation, we draw inspiration from
264   the memory mechanism in the Recurrent Memory Transformer (RMT) (Bulatov et al., 2022) and
265
      introduce an adaptive memory module. This memory module enhances the model’s ability to han-
      dle long-term dependencies in extended generation tasks, while the interactive generation approach
266
      mitigates GPU memory limitations.

366
      LayoutSciPG is a multimodal extraction and generation task that involves producing textual, pic-
367
      torial, and layout outputs. To assess the quality of each output type, we define distinct metrics for

371
      which calculates the overlap lexical units between generated and ground-truth sentences. This metric
      includes ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2 and ROUGE-L.
372
373   ImgP and ImgR: For the extracted images, we use ImgP) and ImgR) to evaluate the images se-
374   lected by our method. ImgR represents the recall of extracted image elements, calculated as
           The number of correct images
375   The total number of ground truth images . ImgP represents the precision of extracted image elements, calcu-
376   lated as TheThe  number of correct images
                   total number of extracted images .

      To quantitatively evaluate layout performance, we introduce the following metrics:

379   ments. Generally, these elements do not overlap significantly, so the overlap score tends to be low.

388   For the multimodal content extraction, we employ there baselines: NeuralExt (Xu & Wan, 2021)
389
      , MSMO (Zhu et al., 2018) and AdaD2P (Fu et al., 2022). NeuralExt is a neural extractive model

390
      that jointly generates text and selects the most relevant image from multimodal input. We adapt
391
      MSMO for the content extraction task. AdaD2P is originally designed for document-to-slide gener-

393   generation, there are no established baselines to compare. We still adapt the content paraphrasing
394   and layout prediction modules of AdaD2P for our task.

658   A1.DATASET P REPROCESSING
659
660   DATA P ROCESSING
661
662
      The collected scientific papers and poster pairs are in the PDF and PNG format, respectively. Data
      processing aims to extract the text and image elements and match them between documents and
663
      posters. However, it is not easy to exactly extract these multimodal elements, especially for images
664
      like tables and figures, from documents and posters because they contain rich structure and complex
665   layouts. Except utilizing OCR tools to extract text information and its corresponding bounding box
666   coordinates, it is also necessary to leverage the document layout analysis algorithms to obtain the
667   layout information of figures.
668
      Paper document extraction. In order to extract the image elements in the paper document, we
669
      employ a state-of-the-art (SOTA) document layout analysis model, i.e., VGT (Da et al., 2023), which
670
      are trained on the open sourced datasets such as Docbank (Li et al., 2020a) and Doclaynet (Pfitzmann
671   et al.). Thus, we can obtain all the image elements in the document. For the text elements, we use
672   an open-sourced OCR tool, i.e., PaddleOCR 5 , to extract them.
673
674
      Poster extraction. Compared to the paper document, a poster contain more complex and richer
      layout structure. Existing SOTA document layout analysis models like VGT perform not good on
675
      the poster. In addition, we also compare some commercial API service for document layout analysis.
676
      Finally, we select the best of them, i.e., Azure document layout analysis service 6 , to extract text and
677   image elements and their corresponding bounding boxes in the poster.
678
679   E LEMENT A LIGNMENT
680
681   The elements, i.e., text sentences and images, extracted from posters and documents need to be
682   aligned each other. Thus, the aligned labels can be served as the supervised signals to guide which
683   elements should be extracted from document and what to be generated according to the extracted
684
      elements. To this end, we employ a sentence matching model and an image matching model to
      achieve this:
685
686   Sentence Matching. We match sentences from the posters to their corresponding paper documents
687   by using RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) to extract sentence embeddings from both. Matching sentences
688   are identified by calculating cosine similarity between the embeddings.
689   Image Matching. For image elements, we match images from the posters to those in the corre-
690   sponding document. We use a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) vision encoder to extract
691   visual embeddings of all images in both the poster and the document, then match them based on
692   the highest cosine similarity. Note that some images in posters may not appear in the corresponding
693   document, resulting in no match. To simplify the process, we ignore any images whose highest
694   visual embedding similarity is below a threshold of δ = 0.8.
