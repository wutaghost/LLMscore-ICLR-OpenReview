                                                 A BSTRACT
022
023            We propose KG4D, a novel method for generating time-aware 4D representations
024
               from a single static image or video. Previous methods largely rely on weak su-
               pervision signals, failing to introduce fine-grained supervision necessary for cap-
025
               turing detailed spatio-temporal dynamics. In contrast, our approach employs Har-
026
               monic Spatio-temporal Encoding (HSE) to achieve efficient spatio-temporal sep-
027            aration during training, allowing the model to represent dynamic scene changes
028            more accurately. Furthermore, Keypoint Feature Calibration (KFC) ensures pre-
029            cise pose consistency, and Wasserstein Gradient Flow (WGF) enhances motion
030            coherence, effectively reducing artifacts. Comprehensive evaluation and ablations
031            demonstrate that our proposed KG4D outperforms existing state-of-the-art meth-
032            ods on various benchmarks in dynamic 4D generation and novel viewpoint syn-
033            thesis, validating its effectiveness and superior generation capability.
034

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Recent advancements in neural rendering and dynamic modeling (Tewari et al., 2022) with Gaus-
039
      sian splatting (Doe & Smith, 2023) have emerged as a promising approach for generating dynamic
      scenes by synthesizing 3D content from 2D inputs (Cao et al., 2019), such as images and video
040
      sequences. This method leverages probabilistic splatting to create a continuous and temporally co-
041
      herent representation of dynamic environments (Cao et al., 2019). However, it still faces challenges:
042   (1) Difficulty in maintaining temporal consistency across frames (Chen & Lin, 2023), especially
043   with complex motions and viewpoint changes; (2) Low-dimensional supervision signals struggling
044   to guide the fitting of high-dimensional data distributions and the lack of physically grounded con-
045   ditioning, leading to unstable and inconsistent outputs. These issues cause instability in captur-
046   ing fine-grained spatio-temporal local patterns, such as keypoint misalignments and incorrect local
047   topologies in dynamic scenes (Cheng et al., 2020).
048   To address these challenges, various baseline methods have been proposed. DreamGaussian4D
049   (Zhao & Wang, 2023) introduced 4D Gaussian splatting (Huang et al., 2023a) to improve temporal
050   consistency but struggles with complex motions and viewpoint changes, leading to artifacts and in-
051   stability due to the inefficiencies in representing dynamic scenes. Other methods, like Consistent4D
052   (Li et al., 2022), Animate124 (Kim et al., 2021), and GaussianFlow (Huang et al., 2023b), focus
053   on pixel-level consistency and geometric constraints but fail to capture the full spatio-temporal dy-
      namics, resulting in artifacts. Similarly, 4Diffusion (Chen & Lin, 2023) enhances spatio-temporal


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      coherence by multi-view video diffusion (Chen & Lin, 2023) but suffers from frame flickering.
055   Despite progress, these methods remain limited by sparse supervision and the absence of robust
056   physical conditioning.
057
058
      In this work, we propose KG4D (Keypoint-Guided 4D Gaussian Splatting) to resolve the limitations
      of current methods effectively. (i) To ensure temporal consistency and reduce artifacts caused by
059
      complex motions and varying viewpoints, we introduce Harmonic Spatio-temporal Encoding (HSE),
060
      which decouples spatial and temporal dimensions, allowing the model to capture fine-grained sub-
061   structures along different dimensions and achieve smoother transitions across frames. (ii) Building
062   on this, to tackle the problem of insufficient supervision and the inability to capture fine-grained spa-
063   tial patterns, we propose Keypoint Feature Calibration (KFC). By utilizing keypoint guidance, KFC
064   ensures precise alignment of local topology, achieving pose reconstruction and correcting keypoint
065   misalignments. (3) To further refine the motion dynamics, we leverage Wasserstein Gradient Flow
066   (WGF) for stable, consistent probability flow along temporal subspace, significantly reducing visual
067   artifacts and enhancing the overall quality of motion generation, even in highly dynamic scenes.
068   We conduct comprehensive evaluations of KG4D across multiple benchmarks, demonstrating an
069   overall 40% improvement among different metrics, particularly in temporal coherence (Mildenhall
070   et al., 2020) and visual quality. However, these improvements come with a 50% reduction in training
071   speed due to the increased computational complexity. Despite this trade-off, KG4D establishes a
072   new benchmark for dynamic 4D scene generation (Chen & Lin, 2023).
073   Our contributions are as follows:
074
075          • We propose a novel Harmonic Spatio-temporal Encoding (HSE) for 4D representation that
076            more effectively captures dynamic scene changes by decoupling spatial and temporal in-
077            formation.
078
             • We introduce Keypoint Feature Calibration (KFC), which provides additional supervision
079            of local patterns in spatial dimensions to ensure accurate keypoint alignment in 3D Gaus-
080            sian Splatting.
081
             • We implement Wasserstein Gradient Flow (WGF) in the temporal dimension to enhance
082
               motion consistency and stability, effectively reducing artifacts and improving visual coher-
083
               ence.
084
085          • We establish a new benchmark for 4D Gaussian splatting-based methods through com-
086
               prehensive evaluations, demonstrating KG4D’s state-of-the-art performance in generating
               realistic, temporally consistent dynamic scenes.

092
093   4D Gaussian Splatting extends the traditional 3D Gaussian Splatting method by incorporating dy-
094   namic changes along the temporal dimension, thereby enabling the modeling of dynamic scenes.
095
      Each Gaussian entity possesses attributes such as spatial position, shape, and color, and undergoes
      corresponding transformations over time. This approach allows for the dynamic capture of motion
096
      and changes within the scene while maintaining spatial detail, making it suitable for applications in
097
      video content generation and animation production.

      2.2   C ATEGORY-AGNOSTIC P OSE E STIMATION
101
      Keypoint detection plays a central role in pose estimation, motion capture, and dynamic scene gen-
102   eration. It enables the sharing of pose representations across different object categories without the
103   need for independent training for each category. By introducing category-agnostic keypoint repre-
104   sentations and a unified feature extraction mechanism, accurate keypoint detection not only captures
105   the pose information of individual entities but also provides essential motion guidance for subse-
106   quent three-dimensional dynamic modeling. This method significantly enhances the generalization
107   capability of cross-category pose estimation, offering robust support for pose detection of various
      objects within dynamic scenes.

      2.3   G RADIENT F LOWS IN WASSERSTEIN M ETRIC
109
110   The Wasserstein gradient flow (Santambrogio, 2017; Mokrov et al., 2021) refers to the evolution
111   of probability distributions driven by the steepest descent of an energy function with respect to the
112   Wasserstein distance. This flow describes how a probability measure evolves over time by follow-
113   ing the gradient of a function in the metric space of probability distributions endowed with the
114   Wasserstein-2 distance. This concept was formalized by (Jordan et al., 1998) (JKO) in 1998 , where
115   they showed that the Fokker-Planck equation can be expressed as a gradient flow in Wasserstein
116
      space.
117   Recent advancements in Gradient flows have since gained significant traction in machine learn-
118   ing and variational inference, applied to problems like density estimation, sampling, and genera-
119   tive modeling, where optimization occurs over distribution spaces (Ansari et al., 2020; Fan et al.,
120   2021). A key commonality between these applications and 4D reconstruction lies in optimizing
121   high-dimensional distributions, where the flow efficiently adjusts distributions in both temporal and
122
      spatial dimensions. The connection to optimal transport is central to the Wasserstein gradient flow’s
      desirable properties (Nguyen et al., 2023; Arbel et al., 2019), as the Wasserstein distance originates
123
      from minimizing the cost of transporting mass between distributions. This makes the Wasserstein
124
      gradient flow particularly suitable for tasks involving dynamic distributions. In our work, we de-
125   coupled the 4D Gaussian Splatting process into temporal and spatial dimensions and demonstrated
126   that both satisfy the Fokker-Planck equation, allowing us to leverage the desirable properties (i.e.
127   stability, exponential convergence) of Wasserstein gradient flow for efficient gradient-based learning
128   in high-dimensional settings.

193   4.1   OVERVIEW
194
195   Overall framework. Learning 4D representations without explicit ground truth is inherently chal-
196   lenging due to the complexity of the high-dimensional data. To tackle this, we introduce KG4D, a
197   two-stage spatio-temporal modeling framework derived from DreamGaussian4D. In the first stage,
198
      we optimize a static 3D Gaussian model by leveraging multi-view 2D diffusion priors (Zhang et al.,
      2023a) to refine pixel-wise geometry and texture, while keypoint (Contributors, 2020) reconstruction
199
      and local structural refinement are guided by KFC in 3D space.
200
201   In the second stage, a temporal deformation network, denoted as D, is employed to capture dynamic
202   changes via Harmonic Spatio-temporal Encoding (HSE). This network initializes with the 3D Gaus-
203   sians and keypoint priors derived from the first stage, facilitating further keypoint reconstruction
204
      across the temporal domain. Specifically, the 3D Gaussians are embedded using HSE, and D is
      responsible for decoding spatial shifts ∆Xt , rotations ∆rt , and scaling ∆st at a given time t. The
205
      HSE embedding is formulated as follows:
206

      mation network D then predict the transformations from these encodings:

      5.1       E XPERIMENT OVERVIEW
346
347
      We comprehensively evaluated the performance of the proposed KG4D model in dynamic 4D scene
348   generation using two approaches: from static images (randomly selecting 8 images from the DG4D
349   and Animate124 datasets) and from videos (utilizing the Consistent4D dataset and challenging
350   videos collected online). We used 14 key points as 3D Gaussian distribution parameters (set to
351   100, with the 4D model inheriting the 3D parameter settings) and incorporated Keypoint Feature
352   Calibration Loss (KFC Loss) and Wasserstein Gradient Flow Loss (WGF Loss). All experiments
353   were conducted on a 24GB 4090 GPU.
354
355   5.2       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
356
357   This study employs a phased camera pose training and Gaussian distribution strategy. In the first
358   phase, the focus is on pose optimization (using Keypoint Feature Calibration loss (KFC = 100) as
359   the key weight) with 500 iterations; in the second phase, feature fine-tuning is conducted (50 itera-
360   tions) without training geometry. The camera radius is set to 2, and the field of view is 49.1 degrees.
361
      Gaussian distribution sampling involves 5,000 points with an initial density of 10%, which is grad-
      ually increased over 3,000 iterations, dynamically optimizing position and opacity. The learning
362
      rate for the deformation field is maintained constant at 0.00064 and is slowly initiated using a delay
363
      multiplier. The learning rate for the grid is set to 0.0064, and Harmonic Spatio-temporal Encoding
364   (ωk = 1) is used for encoding.
365
366   In this experiment, we utilize the DG4D and Animate124 datasets for the task of generating 4D
367
      scenes from static images, randomly selecting five static images for testing. Additionally, our
      method also supports the generation of 4D scenes from videos. For video inputs, we perform quan-
368
      titative evaluations using the Consistent4D dataset. To comprehensively assess the quality of the
369
      generated results, we employ a variety of evaluation metrics, including LPIPS, FVD, PSNR, SSIM,
370   FID, and FV4D. Specifically, LPIPS measures the perceptual differences between generated images
371   and real images, FVD evaluates the quality of generated videos, PSNR is used to measure the peak
372   signal-to-noise ratio of images, SSIM assesses the structural similarity of images, FID measures the
373   distribution differences between generated and real images, and FV4D is dedicated to evaluating the
374   quality of 4D scene generation. These metrics provide a comprehensive evaluation of the generated
375   results from multiple dimensions, including perceptual quality, structural similarity, signal-to-noise
376   ratio, and distribution consistency.

      B    O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
889
890
      Given two probability distributions, Optimal transport (OT) seeks to minimize the total transporta-
      tion cost between them, where the cost is defined by a specified metric, often referred to as the
891
      ground cost. In modern machine learning, OT has found wide applications in domains such as com-
892
      puter vision, natural language processing, and generative modeling due to its ability to measure the
893   distance between probability measures, aligning samples or features from different distributions.
894
895   Formally, Let µ and ν be two probability measures on measurable spaces X and Y, respectively,
896
      and let c(x, y) represent the cost of transporting a unit mass from point x ∈ X to point y ∈ Y. The
      goal of OT is to find a joint probability distribution γ ∈ Π(µ, ν), where Π(µ, ν) denotes the set of
897
      all couplings of µ and ν, that minimizes the total transportation cost:
898                                                       Z
899
                                   OT(µ, ν) = inf                c(x, y) dγ(x, y)
900                                             γ∈Π(µ,ν)   X×Y
901
      In practice, solving this optimization problem directly is computationally expensive, especially for
902
      high-dimensional data, as it often leads to intractable computations. To address this, an entropy-
903   regularized version of the OT problem, known as the Sinkhorn distance or Wasserstein distance, is
904   often employed.
905
906
      The Sinkhorn algorithm introduces an entropic regularization term to the OT formulation, making
      the optimization more tractable. The regularized OT problem is defined as follows:
907
                                                  Z
908
909
                         OTϵ (µ, ν) = inf               c(x, y) dγ(x, y) + ϵH(γ)
                                           γ∈Π(µ,ν)   X ×Y
910                   R
911   where H(γ) = X ×Y γ(x, y) log γ(x, y) dx dy is the entropy of the coupling γ, and ϵ > 0 is the
912   regularization parameter. The regularization smooths the optimization landscape, allowing efficient
913   computation via iterative scaling algorithms.
914   The Sinkhorn algorithm alternates between updating the row and column marginals of the coupling
915   matrix using iterative scaling. It converges to a near-optimal solution while maintaining computa-
916   tional efficiency, making it suitable for large-scale applications.
