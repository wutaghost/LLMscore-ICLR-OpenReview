                                                A BSTRACT
040
041           Recent advances in text-guided video editing have showcased promising results
042           in appearance editing (e.g., stylization). However, video motion editing in the
043           temporal dimension (e.g., from eating to waving), which distinguishes video edit-
044           ing from image editing, is underexplored. In this work, we present UniEdit, a
045
              tuning-free framework that supports both video motion and appearance editing by
              harnessing the power of a pre-trained text-to-video generator within an inversion-
046
              then-generation framework. To realize motion editing while preserving source
047
              video content, based on the insights that temporal and spatial self-attention layers
048           encode inter-frame and intra-frame dependency respectively, we introduce auxiliary
049           motion-reference and reconstruction branches to produce text-guided motion and
050           source features respectively. The obtained features are then injected into the main
051           editing path via temporal and spatial self-attention layers. Extensive experiments
052           demonstrate that UniEdit covers video motion editing and various appearance
053           editing scenarios, and surpasses the state-of-the-art methods. Our code will be
              publicly available.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
055
056   The advent of pre-trained diffusion-based [28, 60] text-to-image generators [56, 57, 55] has revo-
057   lutionized the fields of design and filmmaking, opening new vistas for creative expression. These
058   advancements, underpinned by seminal works in text-to-image synthesis, have paved the way for inno-
059   vative text-guided editing techniques for both images [47, 26, 4, 5] and videos [73, 6, 44, 78, 19, 53].
060   Such techniques not only enhance creative workflows but also promise to redefine content creation
061
      within these industries.
062   Video editing, in contrast to image editing, introduces the intricate challenge of ensuring frame-wise
063   consistency. Efforts to address this challenge have led to the development of methods that leverage
064   shared features and structures with the source video [6, 44, 40, 78, 53, 7, 36, 70, 20] through an
065   inversion-then-generation pipeline [47, 60], exemplified by Pix2Video’s approach [6] to consistent
066   appearance editing across frames. To transfer the edited appearance from the anchor frame to the
067
      remaining frames consistently, it employs a pre-trained image generator and extends the self-attention
      layers to cross-frame attention to generate each remaining frame. Despite these advancements in
068
      performing video appearance editing (e.g., stylization, object appearance replacement, etc.), these
069
      methodologies fall short in editing video motion (e.g., replacing the movement of playing guitar with
070   waving), hampered by a lack of motion priors and limited control over inter-frame dependencies,
071   underscoring a critical gap in video editing capabilities.
072
073
      Previous attempts [73, 49] at video motion editing through fine-tuning a pre-trained generator on
      the given source video and then editing motion through text guidance. Although effective, they
074
      necessitate a delicate balance between the generative prowess of the model and the preservation of
075
      the source video’s content. This compromise often leads to restricted motion diversity and unwanted
076   content variations. In response, our work aims to explore a tuning-free framework that adeptly
077   navigates the complexities of editing both the motion and appearance of videos. To achieve this, we
078   identify three technical challenges: 1) it is non-trivial to incorporate the text-guided motion into the
079   source content, as directly applying video appearance editing [53, 20] or image editing [5] schemes
080   leads to undesirable results (as shown in Fig. 5); 2) preserving the non-edited content of the source
081   video; 3) inheriting the spatial structure of the source video during appearance editing.
082   Our solution, UniEdit, harnesses the power of a pre-trained text-to-video generator (e.g., LaVie [71])
083   within an inversion-then-generation framework [47], tailored to overcome the identified challenges.
084   Particularly, we introduce three key innovations: 1) To inject text-guided motion into the source
085   content, we highlight the insight that the temporal self-attention layers of the generator encode
086   the inter-frame dependency. Acting in this way, we introduce an auxiliary motion-reference branch
087   to generate text-guided motion features, which are then injected into the main editing path via
088   temporal self-attention layers. 2) To preserve the non-edited content of the source video, motivated
089   by the image editing technique [5], we follow the insight that the spatial self-attention layers of the
090
      generator encode the intra-frame dependency. Therefore, we introduce an auxiliary reconstruction
      branch, and inject the features obtained from the spatial self-attention layers of the reconstruction
091
      branch into the main editing path. 3) To retain the spatial structure during the appearance editing, we
092
      replace the spatial attention maps of the main editing path with those in the reconstruction branch.
093
094   To our knowledge, UniEdit is the first to explore the task of text-guided, tuning-free video motion
095   editing. In addition, its unified architecture not only facilitates a wide array of video appearance
096
      editing tasks, as shown in Fig. 1, but also empowers image-to-video generators for zero-shot text-
      image-to-video generation. Through comprehensive experimentation, we demonstrate UniEdit’s
097
      superior performance relative to existing state-of-the-art methods.

      2.1   V IDEO G ENERATION
102
103   Researchers have achieved video generation with generative adversarial networks [65, 58, 69],
104   language models [77, 80], or diffusion models [30, 59, 27, 25, 3, 68, 81, 21, 71, 8, 54, 31, 79]. To
105   make the generation more controllable, endeavors have also incorporated additional structure guidance
106   (e.g., depth map) [18, 10, 83, 11, 22, 72], or conducted customized generation [73, 75, 37, 84, 66, 46].
107   These models have generally learned real-world video distribution from large-scale data, and achieved
      promising results on text-to-video or image-to-video generation. Based on their success, we leverage

108
      the learned prior in the pre-trained model to achieve tuning-free video motion and appearance editing.
109
110   2.2   V IDEO E DITING
111
112   Tuning-Free Appearance Editing Video appearance editing [14, 38, 12, 67], like turning a video
113
      into the style of Van Gogh, aims to produce a new video aligned with the appearance in editing
      instructions while maintaining the structure of the source video. Inspired by approaches in image
114
      editing [26, 5], a line of studies [53, 6, 53, 40, 36, 70] perform tuning-free video appearance editing
115
      by leveraging the T2I models with self-attention manipulation and inter-frame propagation to ensure
116   consistency. Follow-up studies leverage the edit-then-propagate framework with neatest-neighbor
117   field [20], estimated optical flow [78], or temporal deformation field [51]. AnyV2V [42] innovatively
118   decomposes the video editing task into two sub-tasks: image editing and video-referenced I2V
119   generation, therefore supporting various editing tasks by replacing the image editing tool. The
120   primary difference with UniEdit is that UniEdit employs an end-to-end pipeline. Flatten [13] extracts
121   optical flow from the source video and designs flow-guided attention to improve visual consistency.
122   Though effectively enhance consistency in appearance editing, it’s not suitable for motion editing,
123   where the optical flow of the edited video should not be consistent with the source video.
124   Training-Based Appearance Editing Meanwhile, previous work [19, 44] also explored fine-tuning
125   a pre-trained generation model tailored for the video editing task. Video-P2P [44] achieved local
126
      editing via video-specific fine-tuning. I2VEdit [52] leverages image editing approaches to improve
      video editing performance and elaborately designs motion alignment training to enhance temporal
127
      consistency, which is inherently incompatible with motion editing. Moreover, approaches trained on
128
      single input video could lead to inferior performance due to the overfitting.
129
      Motion Editing Recent studies have also explored video motion editing with text guidance [73,
130
      49], user-provided motion [35, 61, 17], or specific motion representation [50, 62, 39, 24]. For
131   example, Dreamix [49] proposed fine-tuning a pre-trained text-to-video model with mixed video-
132   image reconstruction objectives for each source video. Then the editing is realized by conditioning
133   the fine-tuned model on the given target prompt. MoCA [76] decoupled the video into the first-
134   frame appearance and the optical flow, and trained a diffusion model to generate video conditioned
135   on the first frame and the text. However, it struggled to preserve the non-edited motion (e.g.,
136   background dynamics) as it generates the entire motion from the text. ReVideo [50] successfully
137   decouples content and motion and achieves precise trajectory-based motion control. Different from
138   the aforementioned approaches that require fine-tuning or user-provided motion input, we are the first
139
      to achieve tuning-free motion and appearance editing with text guidance only.

      3     P RELIMINARIES : V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS
142   Overall Architecture Modern text-to-video (T2V) diffusion models typically extend a pre-trained
143   text-to-image (T2I) model [56] to the video domain with the following adaptations. 1) Introducing
144   additional temporal layers by inflating 2d convolutional layers to 3d form, or adding temporal
145   self-attention layers [64] to model the correlation between video frames. 2) Due to the extensive
146   computational resources for modeling spatial-temporal joint distribution, these works typically
147   first train video generation models on low spatial and temporal resolutions, and then upsampling
148
      the generated results with cascaded models. 3) Other improvements like efficiency [1], training
      strategy [21], or additional control signals [18], etc. During inference, given standard Gaussian
149
      distribution zT ∼ N (0, 1), the denoising UNet is used to perform T denoising steps to obtain the
150
      outputs [28, 60]. If the model is trained in latent space [56], a decoder is employed to reconstruct
151   videos from the latent domain.
152
153   Attention Mechanisms In particular, for each block of the denoising UNet, there are four basic
154   modules: a convolutional module, a spatial self-attention module (SA-S), a spatial cross-attention
155
      module (CA-S), and a temporal self-attention module (SA-T). Formally, the attention operation [64]
      can be formulated as:
156

159   where Q (query), K (key), V (value) are derived from inputs, and d is the dimension of hidden states.
160   Intuitively, CA-S is in charge of fusing semantics from the text condition, SA-S models the intra-
161   frame dependency, SA-T models the inter-frame dependency and ensures the generated results are
      temporally consistent. We leverage these intuitions in our designs as elaborated below.

185   Method Overview. As shown in Fig. 2, our main editing path is based on an inversion-then-
186   generation pipeline: we use the latent after DDIM inversion [60] as the initial noise zT 1 , then perform
187
      denoising process starting from zT with the pre-trained UNet conditioned on the target prompt Pt . For
      motion editing, to achieve source content preservation and motion control, we propose to incorporate
188
      an auxiliary reconstruction branch and an auxiliary motion-reference branch to provide desired source
189
      and motion features, which are injected into the main editing path to achieve content preservation and
190   motion editing (as shown in Fig. 3). We propose the pipeline of motion editing and appearance editing
191   in Sec. 4.1 & Sec. 4.2 respectively. To further alleviate the background inconsistency, we introduce
192   a mask-guided coordination scheme in Sec. 4.3. We also extend UniEdit to text-image-to-video
193   generation (TI2V) in Sec. 4.4.

279
      To further improve the editing performance, we suggest leveraging the foreground/background
280
      segmentation mask M to guide the denoising process [16, 15]. There are two possible ways to obtain
281   the mask M : the attention maps of CA-S modules with a threshold [26]; or employing an off-the-shelf
282   segmentation model [41] on the source and generated videos. The obtained segmentation masks can
283   be leveraged to 1), alleviate the indistinction in foreground and background; 2), improve content
284   consistency between edited and source videos. To this end, we leverage mask-guided self-attention in
285   the main editing path to coordinate the editing process. Formally, we define:
286
                                                            QK T
287                          m-attn(Q, K, V ; M ) = softmax( √ + M )V.                                    (5)
288                                                           d
289   Then the mask-guided self-attention:
290
             SAmask := m-attn(Q, K, V ; M f ) ⊙ Mm + m-attn(Q, K, V ; M b ) ⊙ (1 − Mm ),                  (6)
291
292   where M f , M b ∈ {−∞, 0} indicate the foreground and background masks in the editing path
293   respectively, Mm ∈ {0, 1} denotes the foreground mask from the motion-reference branch, and ⊙ is
294   Hadamard product. In addition, we leverage the mask during the content preservation and motion
295
      injection for the features obtained from the reconstruction branch and the motion-reference branch
      (e.g., we replace Qm with Mm ⊙ Qm + (1 − Mm ) ⊙ Q).

      4.4   T2V M ODELS ARE Z ERO -S HOT TI2V G ENERATORS
298
299   To make our framework more flexible, we further derive a method to incorporate images as input
300   and synthesize high-quality video conditioned on both image and text-prompt. Different from some
301   image animation techniques [2], our method allows the user to guide the animation process with text
302   prompts. Concretely, we first achieve image-to-video (I2V) generation by: 1) transforming input
303   images with simulated camera movement to form a pseudo-video clip [49] or 2) leveraging existing
304   image animation approaches (e.g., SVD [2], AnimateDiff [23]) to synthesis a video with random
305
      motion (which may not consistent with the text prompt). Then, we perform text-guided editing with
      UniEdit on the vanilla video to obtain the final output video.

531   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
532
      In this paper, we design a novel tuning-free framework UniEdit for both video motion and appearance
533   editing. By leveraging a motion-reference branch and a reconstruction branch and injecting features
534   into the main editing path, it is capable of performing motion editing and various appearance
535   editing. There are nevertheless some limitations. Firstly, we observe performance degradation when
536   performing both types of editing simultaneously. Secondly, since our work is based on T2V models,
537   the proposed method also inherits some of the shortcomings of the existing models, such as inferior
538   performance in understanding complex prompts. We exhibit the failure cases in Appendix B.5.

886   A    D ETAILED E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
887
888   Base T2V Model. We instantiate the proposed method on LaVie [71], which is a pre-trained
889   text-to-video generation model that produces consistent and high-quality videos. To achieve a fair
890   comparison, we only leverage the base T2V model in LaVie and load the open-source pre-trained
891   weights for video editing tasks in the experiments. Note that the edited video clip could further be
892   seamlessly fed into the temporal interpolation model and the video super-resolution model to obtain
893
      video with a longer duration and higher resolution.
894
895   Video Preprocessing. For each input video, we resize it to the resolution of 320 × 512, followed by
896
      normalization, which is consistent with the training configuration of LaVie. Then, the pre-processed
      video is fed into the base model of Lavie to perform video editing. To maximize the generation power
897
      of LaVie, we set all input videos to 16 frames. For a source video, it takes 1-2 minutes to edit on an
898
      NVIDIA A100 GPU.
899
900
      Configurations. For real source videos, we inverse them with 50 DDIM inversion steps and perform
901
      DDIM deterministic sampling with 50 steps for generation. For the generated videos, we use the
902   same start latent of synthesizing the source video as the initial noise zT for the main editing path and
903   two auxiliary branches. We use the commonly used classifier-free guidance technique [29] with a
904   scale of 7.5.
905
906   Details of User Study. As a text-guided editing task, in addition to CLIP scores, it is crucial to
907   evaluate results through human subjective assessment. To achieve this, we utilized MOS (Mean
908   Opinion Score) as our metric and collected feedback from 10 experienced volunteers. We randomly
909   selected 20 editing samples and permuted results from different models. Volunteers were then tasked
910   to evaluate the results based on two perspectives: frame consistency and textual alignment. They
911   provided ratings for these aspects on a scale of 1-5. Specifically, frame consistency measures the
912   smoothness of the video, aiming to avoid dramatic jittering and ensure coherence between the content
913   of each frame. Textual alignment assesses whether the editing results adhere to the text guidance and
914
      maintain the content of the source video. In the end, we computed the average user ratings for each
      method as our final results.
915
916   As illustrated in Tab. 1, UniEdit shows the best performance on frame consistency. Regarding textual
917   alignment, UniEdit significantly outperforms all other baselines, demonstrating its capacity to support
      diverse editing scenarios.

      To investigate the impact of mask-guided coordination, we begin by visualizing masks obtained
949
      from 1) the attention map in CA-S modules; 2) the off-the-shelf segmentation model SAM [41],
950
      followed by presenting both qualitative and quantitative results of implementing UniEdit with or
951   without mask-guided coordination.
952
953   As verified by previous work [26], the attention maps in CA-S modules contain correspondence
954
      information between text and visual features. The underlying intuition is that the attention maps
      between each word and the spatial features at point (i, j) indicate ‘how similar this token is to
955
      the spatial feature at this location’. We visualize the text-image cross attention map alongside
956
      the synthesized frame in Fig. 10. We observe spatial correspondences that align with the video
957   output from the attention map. For instance, areas with higher values of the token ‘man’ and ‘NYC’
958   correspond to the foreground and background, respectively. We further employ a fixed threshold
959   (0.4 in practice) to derive binary segmentation maps from the attention maps. For comparison,
960   we also display the segmentation mask obtained by point prompt on SAM. It’s observed that the
961   cross-attention mask is generally accurate and could serve as a reliable proxy in practice when an
962   external segmentor is not available.
963   We examine the impact of mask-guided coordination through both qualitative and quantitative results
964   across 4 settings: {w/o UniEdit, UniEdit w/o mask, UniEdit with mask from CA-S, UniEdit with
965   mask from SAM}. Qualitatively, shown in Fig. 11, the implementation of UniEdit significantly
966   enhances the consistency between the edited videos and the original video. The application of the
967   mask-guided coordination technique further improves the consistency of unedited areas (e.g., color
968   and texture). The quantitative results in Tab. 5 align coherently with this analysis.

       B.3    M ORE O BSERVATION AND A NALYSIS ON THE P ROPOSED C OMPONENTS

1028   Difference Between QK and V Features in SA-S Modules To comprehend why we can have
1029   inhomogeneous QK and V and their differences, we visualized the results of swapping different
1030   features (QK or V) in SA-S modules during style transfer tasks on the source video in Fig. 12a. As
1031   can be seen, compared to editing with no feature replacement (2nd row), replacing QK in the 3rd row
1032   results in the edited video adopting the same spatial structure as the source video. Simultaneously,
1033   replacing V eradicates the style information in the 4th row, meaning the texture details from the
1034
       source video are utilized to replace the style depicted by the target prompt. To summarize, the query
       and key features (in SA-S modules) dictate the spatial structure of the generated video, while the
1035
       value features tend to influence the texture, including details such as color tones.
1036
1037   Influence of Spatial Structure Control in Motion Editing We explored the role of spatial control
1038   in motion editing. The proposed method synthesizes videos with larger modifications when removing
1039   the spatial control mechanism on both the motion-reference branch and the main editing branch. We
1040
       visualized the results in Fig. 12b. It can be observed that although the motion-reference branch can
       still generate the target motion without the control of spatial structure, the layout deviates significantly,
1041
       for example, the raccoon assumes a different pose and location. We regard this as a suboptimal
1042
       solution because, compared to the results presented in the 3rd row, the results w/o spatial structure
1043   control modifies the object position of the source video, leading to a decrease in consistency between
1044   the edited result and the source video.

       B.4     A NALYSIS AND C OMPARISON ON I NFERENCE T IME

1082   We conduct a theoretical analysis of the additional cost of UniEdit and an empirical comparison with
1083   baseline methods in terms of inference speed.
1084
1085
       Theoretically, our method primarily involves feature replacement operations in attention modules,
       achieved through forward hook registration and introducing minimal additional computation. There-
1086
       fore, the main difference between synthesizing a video from random noise and editing a video
1087
       with UniEdit lies in the batch size of the denoising process (i.e., vanilla generation: batchsize=1,
1088   appearance editing: batchsize=2, motion editing: batchsize=3), and this process could be further
1089   accelerated through multi-GPU parallel processing techniques. Additionally, we utilize LaVie [71] as
1090   the base T2V model in the paper, which takes approximately 45 seconds to synthesize a 16-frame
1091   video. Our method can be even faster when adapted to more efficient base models.
