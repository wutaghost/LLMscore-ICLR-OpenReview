                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Neurosymbolic AI is a growing field of research aiming to combine neural net-
               work learning capabilities with the reasoning abilities of symbolic systems. In
011
               this paper, we tackle informed classification tasks, i.e., multi-label classification
012
               tasks informed by prior knowledge that specifies which combinations of labels
013            are semantically valid. Several neurosymbolic formalisms and techniques have
014            been introduced in the literature, each relying on a particular language to rep-
015            resent prior knowledge. We take a bird’s eye view on informed classification
016            and introduce a unified formalism that encapsulates all knowledge representation
017            languages. Then, we build upon this formalism to identify several concepts in
018            probabilistic reasoning that are at the core of many techniques across represen-
019            tation languages. We also define a new technique called semantic conditioning
020            at inference, which only constrains the system during inference while leaving the
021
               training unaffected, an interesting property in the era of off-the-shelves and foun-
               dation models. We discuss its theoritical and practical advantages over two other
022
               probabilistic neurosymbolic techniques: semantic conditioning and semantic reg-
023
               ularization. We then evaluate experimentally and compare the benefits of all three
024            techniques on several large-scale datasets. Our results show that, despite only
025            working at inference, our technique can efficiently leverage prior knowledge to
026            build more accurate neural-based systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Neurosymbolic AI is a growing field of research aiming to combine neural network learning capa-
032   bilities with the reasoning abilities of symbolic systems. This hybridization can take many shapes
033   depending on how the neural and symbolic components interact (Kautz, 2022; Wang et al., 2023).
034   An important sub-field of neurosymbolic AI is Informed Machine Learning (von Rueden et al.,
035   2023), which studies how to leverage prior knowledge to improve neural-based systems. There
036   again, proposed techniques in the literature can be of very different nature depending on the type of
037   task (e.g. regression, classification, detection, generation, etc.), the formalism used to represent the
038   prior knowledge (e.g. mathematical equations, knowledge graphs, logical theories, etc.), the stage
039
      at which knowledge is embedded (e.g. data processing, neural architecture design, learning proce-
      dure, inference procedure, etc.) and benefits expected from the hybridization (e.g. explainability,
040
      performance, frugality, etc.).
041
042   In particular, informed classification studies multi-label classification tasks where prior knowledge
043   specifies which combinations of labels are semantically valid. In our work, the architecture of the
044   neural model (e.g. fully connected, convolutional, transformer-based, etc.) mainly depends on the
045
      modality of the input space (e.g. images, texts, etc.). Therefore, we consider model-agnostic neu-
      rosymbolic techniques that integrate prior knowledge during learning, inference or both, but leave
046
      the design of the architecture outside the reach of the technique. To lighten our formalism, we re-
047
      strict ourselves to the supervised setting throughout the paper, even though some of the techniques
048   we mention can be used in a semi-supervised setting. Several neurosymbolic formalisms and tech-
049   niques have been introduced in the literature, each using a particular language to represent prior
050   knowledge: HEX-graphs in Deng et al. (2014), propositional formulas in Xu et al. (2018), tractable
051   circuits in Ahmed et al. (2022a), linear programs in Niepert et al. (2021), Prolog in Manhaeve et al.
052   (2021), ASP in Yang et al., First Order Logic in Badreddine et al. (2022), etc. Because of this di-
053   versity, it is difficult to identify the shared concepts underlying most techniques introduced in the
      literature. It is also challenging to establish meaningful comparisons between techniques. In this


                                                         1

      paper, we take a bird’s eye view on informed classification and propose a unified formalism that
055   encapsulates all representation languages for propositional knowledge. We build upon this formal-
056   ism to study a family of neurosymbolic techniques that leverage probabilistic reasoning to integrate
057   prior knowledge, a trend that has gained significant traction in the recent literature (Xu et al., 2018;

061   stage: semantic conditioning at inference. This is a particularly useful property in the era of off-
062   the-shelves and foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021), which are pre-trained on massive
063
      amounts of general data to then be applied in a multitude of heterogeneous downstream tasks.

169   3.1   N EURAL MULTI - LABEL CLASSIFICATION
170
171   In supervised machine learning, the objective is to learn a relationship between an input domain X
172   and an output domain Y from a labeled dataset D :“ pxi , yi q1ďiďd P pX ˆ Yqd .
173   Deep learning systems usually adopts a functional framework to tackle supervised learning tasks. A
174   functional relation f : X ÞÑ Y is assumed and a neural network (i.e., a parametric and differentiable
175   computational graph) M is designed to model this relation based on assumed properties of f . To
176   learn the parameters of the neural network a differentiable cost function L measuring the distance
177   between predictions and labels is chosen, backpropagation computes the gradient of the loss with
178   respect to each parameter and gradient descent is used to minimize the empirical error. Inference is
179   done by processing the inputs through the neural network.
180   However, when the output domain Y is (at least partly) discrete, a differentiable distance cannot be
181   defined directly on Y and such a framework cannot be applied strictly. This is especially relevant
182   for classification tasks. Classification tasks are usually categorical: the output domain consists of
183   a finite set of variables. In multi-label classification tasks however, elements in the output domain
184   Y are subsets of a finite set of classes Y. Usually called labelsets, we call them states (see Section
185   2.1) and note Y “ BY . Interestingly, categorical classification can be seen as a specific instance of
186   multi-label classification informed with prior knowledge (see Example 4).
187   Hence, we adopt a slightly modified framework, called pseudo-functional, where a third module I
188   (besides M and L), called the inference module, has to be defined to bridge the gap between the
189   continuous nature of the neural network (needed for gradient descent) and the discrete nature of the
190   output space. This third module, although essential, is not often explicitly described. An illustration
191   can be found on Figure 1.

      4.2   P ROPERTIES
348
      As shown above, Definition 5 encapsulates very diverse neurosymbolic techniques. However, be-
349   yond this unified view, we can analyze specific properties of neurosymbolic techniques that are
350   critical to their deployment. Formal definitions and proofs can be found in Appendix B.
351
352   Syntactic invariance A neurosymbolic technique is invariant to syntax when equivalent formu-
353   las produce identical loss and inference modules when fed to L and I. Interestingly, since LpY, κq
354   and IpY, κq essentially depend on the boolean function represented by κ and not on the syntax κ, it
355   allows to generalize the technique defined for a particular propositional language to other languages.
356   Because probabilistic reasoning essentially depends on the boolean function represented by the the-
357   ory κ and not its syntax, techniques that rely on probabilistic reasoning (e.g. see Definitions 6, 7 and
358   8) are naturally invariant to syntax. The case of semantic conditioning, which was introduced several
359   times in the neurosymbolic literature using various knowledge representation languages, illustrates
360
      the importance of this property and the utility of this unified view of informed classification. On the
      contrary, because fuzzy regularization is based on the syntax of propositional formulas, it cannot be
361
      easily generalized to other representation languages. Besides, this sensitivity to syntax implies that
362
      two equivalent propositional formulas (i.e., representing the same prior knowledge) may produce
363   different regularization terms, which could lead to performance disparities that are challenging to
364   elucidate to end users. Finally, even when the mathematical definition of a technique can be easily
365   generalized, this does not mean that its implementation and computational complexity is equivalent
366   regardless of the propositional language.
367
368   Consistency A neurosymbolic technique is consistent (defined in (Ahmed et al., 2022a)) when
369   the inference module can only produce outputs that satisfy the prior knowledge. By definition,
370   techniques that only impact the loss module (e.g. techniques based on regularization terms) cannot
371   verify this property, whereas the inference module of semantic conditioning guarantees consistency.
372   Besides retaining syntactic invariance and consistency from semantic conditioning, semantic condi-
373   tioning at inference has other useful properties that make it a suitable choice compared to semantic
374   conditioning and regularization. First, we show in Section 4.3 that it is more tractable computa-
375   tionally. Second, integrating prior knowledge only at inference time offers more flexibility than
376   integration during training. For instance, it can be used if prior knowledge is unavailable at training
377   time (for instance Giunchiglia et al. (2023) provides prior knowledge to an existing task of object
      detection) or susceptible to evolve. This is a particularly useful property in the era of off-the-shelves

      4.3   A LOOK ON COMPLEXITY
383
384   As mentioned in Section 2.2, all three neurosymbolic techniques defined in Section 4 internally rely
385   on solving MPE and PQE problems. Unfortunately, MPE and PQE are NP-hard and #P-hard respec-
386   tively for most propositional languages commonly used to represent knowledge (e.g. propositional
387   logic, boolean circuits, linear programs, ASP, etc.). This implies that scaling probabilistic neu-
388   rosymbolic techniques to large classification tasks (i.e., tasks with a large number of variables) on
389   arbitrary prior knowledge requires an exponential amount of computing resources (unless P “ NP)
390
      and is therefore not realistic. Hence, it is critical for any technique to identify (as much as possible)
      its domain of tractability, which is rarely done in the neurosymbolic literature.
391
392   Hopefully, there are fragments of propositional languages for which MPE and PQE are tractable.
393   Boolean circuits in Decomposable Negational Normal Form (DNNF) can solve MPE problems in
394   linear time (in the size of the circuit) and deterministic-DNNF (dDNNF) can additionally solve PQE
395   problems in linear time. An approach that has become predominant in the literature is knowledge
396
      compilation (Darwiche & Marquis), which consists in translating a theory from an initial proposi-
      tional language (e.g. CNF) into a target propositional language that can solve reasoning problems
397
      efficiently (i.e., in a time polynomial in the size of the compiled formula).
398
399   Finally, counting problems are known to be much harder in general than optimization problems
400   (Toda, 1991). For instance, MPE can be solved in polynomial time for formulas representing match-
401   ing constraints (by reduction to finding a maximum weight-sum matching (Edmonds)), while PQE
402
      is still #P-hard (Amarilli & Monet). As semantic conditioning at inference only relies on solv-
      ing MPE for its inference module, compared to semantic conditioning and semantic regularization
403
      which both rely on solving PQE to compute their loss module, this implies that semantic condition-
404
      ing at inference remains tractable for a larger class of tasks than semantic conditioning and semantic
405   regularization.

409
410   In this section, we evaluate empirically the impact of neurosymbolic techniques on four informed

      classification tasks: a categorical task, two hierarchical tasks and a simple path prediction task.

413   5.1   A NEW MULTI - SCALE EVALUATION
414
      Most papers in the field evaluate the benefits of their neurosymbolic technique on a single neural
415
      network architecture. Although informative, such a methodology paints a very limited picture of the
416
      benefits of the technique and leaves many questions unanswered. In particular, it does not allow to
417   estimate how these benefits evolve when resources given to the system (e.g. network scale, dataset
418   size, training time, etc.) increase.
419
420
      To overcome those limitations, we selected for each task a single architectural design that can be
      scaled to various sizes (e.g. DenseNets (Huang et al., 2017)) and compared the performance of the
421
      three neurosymbolic techniques against an uninformed baseline (independent multi-label classifica-
422
      tion) across network scales. We report the exact accuracy (Ahmed et al., 2022a) (called coherent
423   accuracy in Deng et al. (2014)), i.e., the share of instances which are well classified on all labels,
424   as our evaluation metric. More details on the methodology and the experimental setup (number of
425   epochs, hyperparameters, etc.) are given in Appendix C.

      6   R ELATED WORK
487
488   In this paper, we restricted our formalism to supervised classification tasks informed with propo-
489   sitional prior knowledge. However, many techniques in the literature work with prior knowledge
490   expressed in a higher order language or solve classification tasks where full supervision is lacking.
491
492   Predicate languages Propositional languages use propositional variables to represent atomic
493   facts, which constitute the smallest unit of discourse to represent the world. Predicate languages
494   decompose atomic facts into a more fine grained representation, then leverage this compositional
495   representation through quantification to provide a more expressive language. Predicate languages
496   are often used in the neurosymbolic literature (e.g. First Order Logic in (Badreddine et al., 2022),
497
      Prolog in (Manhaeve et al., 2021), ASP in (Yang et al.)) to represent compositional knowledge about
      the input space or impose a structural bias on the neural architecture. However, probabilistic neu-
498
      rosymbolic techniques systematically rely on grounding to perform probabilistic reasoning, which
499
      limits the impact of predicate languages on the computational side.
500
501   Supervision settings In real world applications, labeling large amounts of data is difficult, expen-
502   sive and slow, especially for multi-label classification tasks featuring many classes (Deng et al.).
503   Therefore, much work has been done to formalize and exploit cheaper supervision settings where
504   input samples are not fully labeled. In the semi-supervised setting (Seeger, 2000; Grandvalet &
505   Bengio, 2004), only a fraction of input samples are fully labeled while the rest is unlabeled. Closely
506   related is the partial-labels setting (Durand et al.), where only a subset of the classes are labeled
507   for each input sample. Partial labels can typically be found when prior knowledge represents a
508   functional dependency between a set of latent variables and a set of observed variables, like in the
509
      MNIST-Add task (Manhaeve et al., 2021; Badreddine et al., 2022; Maene & De Raedt; van Krieken
      et al.), which aim is to learn a latent representation of hand-written digits from observing only their
510
      sum. Some neurosymbolic techniques have been specifically designed for these supervision settings
511
      (Xu et al., 2018; Ahmed et al., 2022b).
512
513
514   7   C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we introduce a formalism for supervised classification informed by prior knowl-
517
      edge, define a new neurosymbolic technique called semantic conditioning at inference which in-
      tegrates this prior knowledge during inference. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first neu-
518
      rosymbolic technique based on probabilistic reasoning which only impacts inference. We evaluate
519
      our technique alongside two existing probabilistic techniques on several large datasets and across
520   neural network scales. We show experimentally that semantic conditioning at inference can im-
521   prove the performances of a neural classification system on large datasets and on networks of
522   all sizes. Besides, we demonstrate that semantic conditioning at inference preserves key proper-
523   ties (i.e., syntactic invariance and consistency) and remains competitive with semantic conditioning
524   while only working at inference, making it more flexible and tractable.
525
      Future directions for our work may include, amongst others, reproducing our experiments on more
526   datasets, investigating the semi-supervised and partial-labels settings. Finally, we assume through-
527   out the paper that the knowledge is known a priori, which is often not the case in practice. Discov-
528   ering the structure of the task at hand and training the model simultaneously is an important field of
529   research.

839
840
      Although they are not usually thought of as propositional languages, graphs can allow to express
841
      knowledge about variables by relating them to elements of a graph. As opposed to most proposi-
842   tional languages, graph-based languages are not universal (i.e., they cannot represent any boolean
843   function), but specialized for a specific type of knowledge. They are often used to represent frag-
844   ments of universal languages in a concise and more intuitive way. Besides, unlike most propositional

846   itly assumed from the syntax), graph-based languages share very similar syntaxes but vary greatly
847   in their semantics.

849   vertices) of a graph G “ pV, Eq: a theory is a couple T :“ pG, ςq where G “ pV, Eq is a graph and
850   ς : E Ñ Y (resp. ς : V Ñ Y) is bijective.
851
      Example 7. The simple path language (resp. matching language) is an edge-based language where
852
      a state y P BY satisfies T iff the set of selected edges forms a simple path (resp. perfect matching)
853   in G.

       C.1.1    C ATEGORICAL CLASSIFICATION
1081
1082   We mentioned earlier (see Section 3) how categorical classification tasks could be framed as a multi-
1083   label classification with prior knowledge. MNIST is one of the oldest and most popular dataset
1084   in computer vision and consists of small images of hand-written digits (e.g.        or ). Since its
1085   introduction in LeCun et al. (1998), it has been used as a toy dataset in many different settings.
1086   Likewise in neurosymbolic literature, many researchers used MNIST as a basis to build structured
1087
       dataset compositionally (e.g. the PAIRS dataset in Marra et al. (2020), the MNIST-Add dataset in
       Manhaeve et al. (2021); Badreddine et al. (2022); Maene & De Raedt; van Krieken et al. or the
1088
       Sudoku dataset in Augustine et al. (2022)).

1090
       C.1.2    H IERARCHICAL CLASSIFICATION
1091
1092   The Cifar-100 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2009) is composed of 60,000 images classified into 20 mutually
1093   exclusive super-classes (e.g. reptile), each divided into 5 mutually exclusive fine-grained classes
1094   (e.g. crocodile, dinosaur, lizard, turtle, and snake). While most papers only consider the categorical
1095   classification task arising form the 100 fine-grained classes, we keep all 120 classes to produce a
1096   multi-label classification task where prior knowledge captures mutual exclusion and the hierarchy
1097   between super and fine-grained classes.
1098   The ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) (Russakovsky et al., 2015) is an
1099   image classification challenge which has become a standard benchmark in computer vision to com-
1100   pare performances of deep learning models. As of August 2014, ImageNet contained 14,197,122
1101   annotated images organized into 21,841 synsets of the WordNet hierarchy (Miller, 1995), however
1102   standard image classification tasks often use a subset of those, usually 1,000 or 100 synsets. The
1103   WordNet hierarchy defines subsumption (or inclusion) between classes, and can be used in many
1104   ways to create a task of binary multi-label classification with prior knowledge.
1105   For our experiments, we sample 100 classes from 1k ImageNet and add all their parent classes.
1106   We then prune classes that have only one parent class and one child class to avoid classes having
1107   identical sample sets. We thus obtain a dataset of ImageNet samples labeled on a hierarchy of 271
1108   classes. Prior knowledge for this task includes the hierarchical knowledge coming from WordNet, as
1109   well as exclusion knowledge that we obtain by assuming two classes having no common descendants
1110   are mutually exclusive.
1111
1112   C.1.3    S IMPLE PATH PREDICTION
1113
       The Warcraft shortest path task (Pogančić et al., 2019; Yang et al.; Niepert et al., 2021; Ahmed et al.,
1114
       2022a) uses randomly generated images of terrain maps from the Warcraft II tileset. Maps are build
1115
       on a 12 ˆ 12 directed grid (each vertex is connected to all its neighbors) and to each vertex of the
1116   grid corresponds a tile of the tileset. Each tile is a RGB image that depicts a specific terrain, which
1117   has a fixed traveling cost. For each map, the label encodes the shortest s-t path (i.e., a path from
1118   the upper-left to the lower-right corners), where the weight of the path is the sum of the traveling
1119   costs of all terrains (i.e., grid vertices) on the path. The terrain costs are used to produce the dataset
1120   but are not provided during training nor inference. In the original dataset (Pogančić et al., 2019),
1121   output variables correspond to vertices in the grid and a state satisfies the simple path constraint if
1122   the vertices set to 1 constitute a simple s-t path.
1123   This representation comes with several issues. First, as noted in Ahmed et al. (2022a), the set of
1124   vertices ambiguously encode more than one path (because of cycles in the grid, there are several
1125   possible simple paths that go through the same vertices). Besides, computing MPE and PQE for
1126   simple path constraints on general directed graphs are respectively NP-hard (Karp) and #P-hard
1127   (Valiant). To make this task tractable, (Ahmed et al., 2022a) transforms the output space in the
1128   following way: edges of the grid are chosen as output variables instead of vertices and only simple
1129   paths with a maximal length of 29 (the maximal length found in the training set) are kept. This
1130
       implies that the test set might not be consistent with the constraints since it might contain a path
       longer than 29 edges. Besides, such method would not scale to larger grids.
1131
1132   In our experiments, we adopt a different approach. We keep the set of edges as our output vari-
1133   ables, but we turn the grid into an acyclic graph by only connecting vertices to their right and lower
       neighbors. Acyclicity is a sufficient condition to compile simple path constraints to an Ordered Bi-


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       nary Decision Diagram (see code/circuits/AcyclicSimplePath.py in the supplemen-
1135   tary material), which makes MPE and PQE tractable. This transformation allows us to scale to larger
1136   grids without an explosion of the computational cost. We recompute the labels for the new output
1137   space using the terrain costs.
1138
1139
1140
1141

       C.3     M ETRICS
1272
1273   There are plenty of metrics that can be used to evaluate a classification system.
1274
1275   Simple accuracy averages how many classes were correctly labeled on each sample, however, since
1276
       multi-label classification datasets with background knowledge are often highly unbalanced (far more
       negative classes that positive ones) it is often unfit to the task. Precision, recall and f1-score metrics
1277
       can help tackle with this issue, but they lose track of the semantics of the task.
1278
1279   Semantic consistency counts how many outputs are consistent with the background knowledge.
1280   Since sci is provably consistent, this metric is of little interest for us.
1281   Metrics that are not based on the binary outputs but need to access probability scores associated with
1282   each classe, like threshold metrics (e.g. map@50, map@75, auc) or top-k metrics, are not accessible
1283   to our classification system as is.
1284
       Eventually, we decide to use exact accuracy, which counts how many samples are perfectly labeled:
1285
       this is the most demanding metric since a single mistake disqualifies the whole output. This metric
1286   is also used in Ahmed et al. (2022a) and in Xu et al. (2018) (where it is called coherent accuracy).
