011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate control of quantum states is crucial for quantum computing and other
014            quantum technologies. In the basic scenario, the task is to steer a quantum system
015            towards a target state through a sequence of control operations. Determining the
016            appropriate operations, however, generally requires information about the initial
017            state of the system. Gathering this information becomes increasingly challeng-
018            ing when the initial state is not a priori known and the system’s size grows large.
019            To address this problem, we develop a machine-learning algorithm that uses a
020
               small amount of measurement data to construct its internal representation of the
               system’s state. The algorithm compares this data-driven representation with a
021
               representation of the target state, and uses reinforcement learning to output the
022
               appropriate control operations. We illustrate the effectiveness of the algorithm
023            showing that it achieves accurate control of unknown many-body quantum states
024            and non-Gaussian continuous-variable states using data from a limited set of quan-
025            tum measurements.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Controlling the state of noisy intermediate-scale quantum systems Preskill (2018) is a major chal-
032   lenge for quantum computing and other quantum technologies. In recent years, reinforcement learn-
033   ing (RL) François-Lavet et al. (2018) has emerged as a powerful strategy for designing quantum
034   control policies Chen et al. (2013a); Bukov et al. (2018); Bukov (2018); Zhang et al. (2019); Yao
035   et al. (2021); Borah et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2021); Sivak et al. (2022); Porotti et al. (2022); Metz
036   & Bukov (2023); Reuer et al. (2023). One of the benefits of this approach is that, unlike conven-
037   tional model-based quantum control, RL can be used to adaptively learn quantum control policies
038   without any knowledge of the underlying quantum dynamics Sivak et al. (2022). Beyond quantum
039
      control, RL has found applications in quantum information science, including quantum error cor-
      rection Fösel et al. (2018); Nautrup et al. (2019); Zeng et al. (2023), quantum simulation Bolens
040
      & Heyl (2021), quantum compilation Zhang et al. (2020); Fösel et al. (2021); Moro et al. (2021),
041
      quantum sensing Xiao et al. (2022) and quantum communications Wallnöfer et al. (2020).
042
043   Many of the existing control methods focus on the preparation of a known target state from a known
044   initial state Chen et al. (2013a); Bukov et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2019); Porotti et al. (2022); Sivak
045
      et al. (2022); Metz & Bukov (2023). In this task, RL algorithms are often used to learn control
      policies from exact state descriptions. In practice, however, limited device calibration and imper-
046
      fections in the setup can lead to uncertainty on the initial state of the system. It is then important to
047
      supplement RL algorithms with a state characterization step, in which useful information is gathered
048   from quantum measurements. In many of the existing protocols, the relevant piece of information is
049   the fidelity between the system’s state and the target state Chen et al. (2013a); Bukov et al. (2018);
050   Bukov (2018); Zhang et al. (2019); Porotti et al. (2022); Metz & Bukov (2023). Estimating the
051   fidelity through measurements, however, becomes challenging as the system size grows Flammia
052   & Liu (2011); da Silva et al. (2011). To circumvent this problem, a few works explored the possibil-
053   ity of directly using measurement outcomes for reward calculation Reuer et al. (2023); Borah et al.
      (2021); Sivak et al. (2022), but scalability still remains a challenge.

      In this paper, we introduce a Representation-Guided Reinforcement Learning (RGRL) algorithm,
      which combines a neural network for learning unknown quantum states with an RL algorithm to
082
      steer an uncharacterized quantum state towards a target state using only measurement statistics. The
083
      core of our algorithm is a representation network Zhu et al. (2022) that produces its internal repre-
084   sentation of quantum states and uses it to estimate a measure of the similarity between the state under
085   control and the target state Wu et al. (2023a). The similarity estimate is then used in the RL stage to
086   compute rewards and to determine the control operations needed to steer the quantum system to the
087   desired state. Overall, our algorithm provides an illustration of how a machine without any built-in
088   knowledge of quantum physics can learn to control a piece of quantum hardware. The effective-
089   ness of this approach is illustrated through numerical simulations, showing strong performance for
090   various scenarios of quantum control. The contributions are:
091   (1) We develop a novel algorithm, RGRL, that combines state representations with reinforcement
092   learning for quantum control. By leveraging neural representations constructed from limited mea-
093   surement data, the RGRL algorithm can evaluate the similarity between the current and target states
094   and determine the optimal control actions. This approach facilitates adaptive learning of control
095   policies without requiring pre-existing knowledge of the quantum system’s dynamics.
096   (2) We provide a scalable solution for quantum state preparation with limited initial state informa-
097   tion. One of the major challenges in quantum state control is dealing with the uncertainty of the
098   initial state, especially as the system size increases. Our RGRL algorithm addresses this issue by
099   using measurement statistics to construct an internal representation of the quantum state. This ap-
100   proach significantly reduces the reliance on precise initial state information, making the algorithm
101   scalable and applicable to larger quantum systems.
102   (3) We demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness in controlling many-body ground states and
103   continuous-variable states. Specifically, we demonstrate its application to controlling many-body
104   ground states, which are crucial for understanding complex quantum systems and phase transitions.
105   Additionally, we apply the algorithm to continuous-variable states, which are essential for quan-
106   tum error correction and other quantum information processes. These numerical results highlight
107   the algorithm’s robustness and adaptability across different types of quantum states and systems,
      validating its broad utility in practical quantum technologies.

165   3.1   OVERVIEW
166
167   Our algorithm applies to the scenario where a classical learning agent gathers information from and
168   performs actions on a quantum system. Following RL terminology, we will refer to the quantum
169
      system as the agent’s environment. The interactions between the agent and the environment are
      mediated by an experimenter, who performs quantum measurements and other quantum operations
170
      based on the agent’s instructions. Our algorithm consists of two stages: quantum state characteriza-
171
      tion/learning and quantum control, as depicted in Fig. 1. In the state learning stage, the agent has
172   access to many copies of an unknown quantum state. The set of quantum measurements that can
173   be performed in the state is denoted by M. At the beginning of the protocol, the experimenter ran-
174   domly chooses a subset S ⊂ M of quantum measurements. In each control step, the experimenter
175   performs each measurement M ∈ S in multiple copies of the state ρ, obtaining the frequency dis-
176   tribution of the outcome dM := Tr(ρM). The set of measurement statistics D := {dM }M∈S is
177   fed into a (pre-trained) representation network to produce the data-driven state representation rρ .
178   The distance between rρ and rρt in the representation space is taken as an indicator of the distance
179   between ρ and ρt , where rρt , i.e., the representation of the target state ρt , can be obtained from the
180
      simulated measurement data of ρt corresponding to the measurement set S.
181   In the kth control step, the agent associated with the RGRL algorithm receives the measurement
182   data Dk from the environment as its partial observation and selects an action ak ∈ A ( from a finite
183   set of predefined actions based on the policy π and the measurement observations Dk . Each action
184   corresponds to a tuning of the physical parameters. Upon receiving the action determined by the
185   RGRL algorithm, the experimenter implements it on the system, driving the current quantum state
186
      to a new state in the next control step. This process is repeated in each environment-agent interaction
      cycle.
187
188   Here, the control policy is modeled by a conditional probability πθ (a|D) of action a depending on
189   partial observations D, parameterized by a neural network with parameters θ. At the kth step, a
190   reward rk ∝ −||rρk − rρt || is assigned, where ρk denotes the quantum state at the kth step, and
191   || · || denotes the Euclidean distance in the representation space. Note that the partial observation
192
      D can be mapped to a dense representation vector r with a pretrained neural network. From the
                                                                                        T
193   initial state to a terminal state, one full trajectory of tuples {(Dk , ak , rk )}k=1 is called an episode,
194
      where T denotes the maximum length of an episode. For each episode, a cumulative reward R :=
         PT
195   − k=1 γ k−1 rk is assigned, where γ denotes the discount rate. The goal of the RL algorithm is to
196   learn a policy πθ : D → A that maximizes the averaged cumulative reward R̄ over multiple episodes
197
      by optimizing the parameters θ through gradient ascent, i.e., ∆θ ∼ ∇θ R̄ Sutton et al. (1999); Silver
      et al. (2014). More details about this neural network structure can be found in the supplementary
198
      material. This model-free RL algorithm can discover the optimal policy without knowledge of the
199
      explicit model of the quantum dynamics of the environment.

202   3.2   S TRUCTURE AND PRETRAINING OF R EPRESENTATION N ETWORK
203
204   Here, we introduce the implementation of the representation network utilized in our framework.
205   As illustrated in Figure 2a, the representation network fξ takes as input the parameterization mi
206   of the measurement Mi ∈ S and its outcome statistics di for the specific state ρ. For each pair
207   Di = (mi , di ), the representation network produces a vector ri = fξ (mi , di ). These vectors,
208   corresponding to different pairs, are then combined into a single vector r by an average function.
209
      We utilize two distinct methods to train the representation network. When only measurement data
210   of the quantum states is available, we employ the GQNQ architecture proposed by Zhu et al. in Zhu
211   et al. (2022). Specifically, we conduct self-supervised learning based on these measurement data.
212   The process involves inputting the measurement data into the representation network to generate
213   representations of the quantum states. Subsequently, we reconstruct the measurement statistics from
214   these representations using a generation network shown in Figure 2b. During training, we minimize
215   the reconstruction loss between the predicted measurement statistics pi and the ground truth di for
      each measurement Mi ∈ S .

      Environment. The environment for the RGRL algorithm is modeled as a quantum oracle, which
241   applies the control policy to the quantum system and retrieves measurement data from the controlled
242   quantum state. Based on the control policy generated by the RGRL algorithm, the oracle adjusts the
243   physical parameters accordingly. The measurement data consist of statistical outcomes obtained
244   from quantum states measured on various bases.
245
      Algorithm. The environment of controlling the ground state can be modeled as a Markov decision
246
      process (MDP), therefore, it is highly suitable for RL Sutton & Barto (2018). We make use of
247   model-free RL as the intelligent agent to optimize the control policies. The input of the RL policy
248   network denoted as r, is the neural representation of the current ground quantum state. The action
249   a controls the tuning of the physical parameters. The reward is proportional to the negative distance
250   between the current quantum state and the target quantum state in the representation space. More
251   precisely, the kth control step of the reward is given by

      To demonstrate that the quality of state representations affects the performance of quantum control,
325   we investigate the same control scenarios using two different types of state representations. The
326   first type is obtained by predicting the outcome statistics of measurements that have not yet been
327   performed, as described in Ref.Zhu et al. (2022). The second type is obtained for predicting the
328   order-two Rényi mutual information between two subsystems of a quantum state, as outlined in
329   Ref. Wu et al. (2023b). Our results in Figure. 3a indicate that using state representations designed for
330   predicting Rényi mutual information leads to higher quantum fidelity in our control scenarios. This
331   improvement is attributed to the fact that these state representations capture the nonlinear properties
332   of quantum states, thereby more clearly distinguishing different topological phases of matter.
333   Figure 3b shows an example of the trajectory of the ground state evolution under control, transi-
334   tioning from a trivial SPT phase to a topological SPT phase. Figure 3c presents an example of a
335   controlled ground state moving from a trivial SPT phase towards the phase boundary. Our RGRL
336   algorithm finds the optimal path in phase space for controlling a ground state from the trivial SPT
337   phase to the topological SPT phase, as illustrated in Figure 3b. For controlling a ground state in
338   the trivial SPT phase towards a state across the phase boundary, the controlled state successfully
339
      reaches the phase boundary but fails to arrive at the exact critical point, as shown by Figure 3c. This
      is because the state representations obtained by our representation network are quite similar near the
340
      phase boundary, preventing the RL algorithm from accurately distinguishing different states across
341
      the boundary.

359   4.2   G ENERATING THE OUTPUT OF A QUANTUM PROCESS
360
361   As an additional application, we employ our RGRL algorithm to produce a target output from an
362   unknown quantum process. We consider the scenario where a known target output state can be
363   obtained by applying the unknown quantum process to a certain input state. The goal is to find an
364   input that causes the quantum process to approximately generate the target output.
365
      To achieve this, we apply the unknown quantum process to multiple copies of a randomly chosen
366   input state, resulting in multiple copies of the corresponding output state. By performing random-
367   ized measurements on the output, we estimate the similarity between the actual output state and
368   the target output state. Based on the estimated similarity, we then modify the input preparation.
369   This learning-and-control cycle is repeated until we identify an appropriate input that produces an
370   accurate approximation of the target output.

428   5    C ONCLUSIONS
429
430   We developed a reinforcement-learning algorithm for steering a quantum system, initially in an

684
685   Numerous studies have explored the use of neural networks to learn quantum systems Carleo &
686   Troyer (2017); Torlai et al. (2018); Carrasquilla et al. (2019); Zhu et al. (2022); Schmale et al.
687
      (2022) and predict their properties Zhang et al. (2021); Xiao et al. (2022); Du et al. (2023); Wu et al.
      (2023a); Qian et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2018); Gray et al. (2018); Koutnỳ et al. (2023); Torlai et al.
688
      (2018; 2019; 2018; 2019); Carrasquilla et al. (2019); Smith et al. (2021); Schmale et al. (2022);
689
      Tang et al. (a;b). Along this line, an interesting approach involves learning concise state represen-
690   tations Zhu et al. (2022) from the outcome data of randomized measurements Huang et al. (2020);
691   Elben et al. (2023). These data-driven representations preserve essential information about quantum
692   states and enable the assessment of their similarities Wu et al. (2023a). This work advances the
693   field by integrating reinforcement learning (RL) to develop a representation-guided RL algorithm
694   for efficient quantum control.
695
      While many existing RL-based quantum control methods Chen et al. (2013a); Bukov et al. (2018);
696   Zhang et al. (2019); Porotti et al. (2022); Sivak et al. (2022); Metz & Bukov (2023) focus on prepar-
697   ing a known target state from a specified initial state, our algorithm runs in a state-agnostic manner.
698   The only input to the neural network is the outcome data from randomized measurements, without
699   any description of the state being controlled, including the initial state. This is a key distinction
700   from previous approaches. Many quantum control strategies have used quantum fidelity between
701   states for reward calculations Chen et al. (2013a); Bukov et al. (2018); Bukov (2018); Zhang et al.
      (2019); Porotti et al. (2022); Metz & Bukov (2023). However, in our scenario, quantum fidelity is

      not accessible. Instead, we use the distance between state representations as a proxy for quantum
703   fidelity when calculating rewards.

       E    LIMITATIONS
1027
1028
       In this study, we focus on controlling the ground states of a Hamiltonian or the output state of
1029   an unknown quantum process. While these tasks are critical for quantum information processing,
1030   the types of quantum states currently under consideration remain limited to specific categories. In
1031   the future, it will be possible to generate a wider variety of quantum states using tailored quantum
1032   circuits, which can then be utilized to learn neural representations. Ultimately, our proposed RGRL
1033   algorithm can be applied to efficiently control these quantum states. To achieve this, we can train
1034   on a diverse set of quantum states generated by highly parameterized circuits, offering significant
1035   flexibility. A representation network, such as a large language model, could then be used to learn
1036   the structure of these quantum states. Looking ahead, large language models could play a crucial
1037
       role in characterizing quantum states with sufficiently broad variability, enabling RL to efficiently
       manage both the dynamic evolution and computational behavior of these states.
