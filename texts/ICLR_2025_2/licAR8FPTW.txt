009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Scalable oversight aims to train systems to perform tasks that are hard for humans
012            to specify, demonstrate and validate. As ground truth is not available for such
013            tasks, evaluating scalable oversight techniques is challenging. Existing methods
014            measure the success of an oversight method based on whether it allows an arti-
015            ficially weak overseer to successfully supervise an AI to perform a task. In this
016            work, we additionally measure the robustness of scalable oversight techniques by
017            testing their vulnerability to reward hacking by an adversarial supervisee. In ex-
018            periments on a synthetic domain, we show that adding an explicit reward hacking
019
               incentive to the model being trained leads it to exploit flaws in a weak overseer,
               and that scalable oversight methods designed to mitigate these flaws can make the
020
               optimization more robust to reward hacking. We hope these experiments lay a
021
               foundation for future work to validate scalable oversight methods’ ability to miti-
022            gate reward hacking in realistic settings.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Scalable oversight is central to the current efforts to align strong future models that may elude
028
      human supervision (Bowman et al., 2022). Therefore, weaknesses in scalable oversight are of great
029
      significance for AI safety.
030
031   A standard method for studying scalable oversight techniques is sandwiching (Cotra, 2021), where
032   a weak overseer is used to train a more capable model to perform a task evaluated by an expert
033
      overseer. While successful sandwiching experiments may give an example of a scalable oversight
      technique succeeding, this example may not convince us that this technique will succeed robustly.
034
      If reward hacking was narrowly avoided, a slight change in the choice of parameters, training for a
035
      slightly different task, or training a more powerful model might induce reward hacking. One way
036   to combat this, originally described by Shlegeris & Greenblatt (2023), is to add a margin of safety
037   by deliberately incentivizing the model to reward hack. A simple way to implement this is to add a
038   term in the reward function inversely proportional to ground truth performance, as assigned by an
039   expert. In this setting, the model is incentivized to produce outputs that are still rewarded highly
040   by the actual overseer while being bad in practice - aka failing silently. If this approach produces a
041   model that performs poorly on the ground truth while the overseer believes it is performing well, we
042   may conclude the combination of oversight technique, overseer and task is not safe.
043   Overseers can be flawed in many different ways. We investigate how scalable oversight techniques
044   can mitigate these flaws and where they might fall short in this task. We identify several classifica-
045   tions, and invite future work to propose more:
046
047          • Overseers can be bad at noticing problems, vs. weighing problems. As an example, finding
048            a vulnerability vs. deciding whether the vulnerability is applicable to the current context.
049            If they are bad at noticing problems but good at weighing them, a method such as Critiques
050
               might completely cover for their weakness, while if they are bad at weighing issues the
               addition of a critiquer could actively hurt performance. For instance, the critiquer could
051
               convincingly flag good examples as bad, leading to false positives.
052
053          • Overseer mistakes can be easy vs. hard to learn. The harder they are to learn, the stronger
               the model in training might need to be before the overseer starts being exploited.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
             • Overseer mistakes can cause the overseer to underestimate the reward, or overestimate
055            it. An overestimate may result in reward hacking, while an underestimate may reduce
056            performance.
057
058   We test three aspects of scalable oversight techniques:
059
060          • Robustness to reward hacking: How difficult is it for an adversarial model to learn to hack
061
               the (possibly flawed) overseer at the expense of the true reward?
062          • Robustness to overseer flaws: How well do the methods mitigate systematic flaws on the
063            part of the overseer?
064          • Efficiency: How well can we learn to optimize the true reward given only a small amount
065            of ground-truth supervision signal?
066
067   Our hope is that this work will provide insight into the study of scalable oversight and what is
068   required to achieve robust results in the face of uncertain generalization to the deployment settings.
069
070   2   R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Scalable oversight. Scalable oversight is the ability to provide reliable supervision—in the form
073   of labels, reward signals, or critiques—to models in a way that will remain effective past the point
074   that models start to achieve broadly human-level performance (Bowman et al., 2022). A scalable
075   oversight training technique aspires to train an artificial intelligence (AI) system to achieve some
076   goal, despite a limited or flawed capacity to measure the goal. Different techniques (Consultancy
077   (Kenton et al., 2024), Critiques (Khan et al., 2024; Saunders et al., 2022), Debate (Irving et al.,
078
      2018)) may involve varying amounts of models, querying procedures and parameters, and they all
      have some set of instructions the overseer(s) should follow. For instance, Reinforcement Learning
079
      from Human Feedback (RLHF) uses an additional reward model, and the Critiques protocol uses an
080
      additional critique model.
081
082   As a basic design requirement, if the overseer follows the instructions perfectly without making any
083   mistakes, the model will get higher reward for taking actions that the overseer would prefer, should
084
      it be aware of the information the model knew about the situation. On top of this basic requirement,
      techniques attempt to (and are differentiated by their ability to):
085
086
             • Have a low computational complexity for the overseer. The introduction of the Debate
087            technique had this as an explicit goal—theoretically allowing a polynomial time judge to
088            supervise a PSPACE complexity task (Irving et al., 2018), although there have been updates
089            to this (Brown-Cohen et al., 2023). As an example, reading a book is more complex than
090            reading a summary and a critique of the summary.
091          • Not require the overseer to do things they might be prone to doing poorly. For instance,
092            humans might be worse at giving calibrated numbers than choosing which of two outputs
093            they prefer. They also might be worse at spotting code vulnerabilities than recognizing
094            them when brought to their attention.
095          • Require the overseer to only supervise a small number of examples. This is achieved for
096            instance by the reward model in RLHF giving much more oversight than was used to train
097            it.
098
             • Have the training either succeed or, at least, fail loudly in case the overseer implements the
099            instructions imperfectly. Failing loudly means that the model did not learn the goal and the
100            overseer is aware of the shortcoming, while failing quietly means the overseer thinks the
101            model has learned the goal correctly. Failing quietly can also be seen as reward hacking.
102
103   Sandwiching. The sandwiching paradigm (Cotra, 2021) is a framework designed to test scalable
104   oversight techniques by creating a scenario where a model is more capable than non-experts but
105   less capable than experts. Non-experts attempt to train or align the model without relying on expert
106   help or preexisting expert knowledge. At the end of the experiment, experts evaluate how well the
107   non-experts succeeded. This setup mimics the future challenge of overseeing more advanced AI
      systems on tasks we cannot specify well or validate confidently. While the sandwiching paradigm is

124   promising, a successful experiment may not guarantee success in the future deployment case due to
125   various reasons. In this paper we focus on two reasons:
126
127
             • The success of the experiment may depend on specifics of the implementation, such as the
               training hyperparameters, the size and architecture of the model, or the amount of data.
128
               There is no way to tell whether the technique robustly teaches the model the goal correctly,
129
               or whether reward hacking was narrowly avoided by chance (Shlegeris & Greenblatt, 2023).
130            To mitigate this risk, we propose adding an incentive to perform reward hacking, described
131            in the next section.
132
             • The success of a scalable oversight technique might depend on which specific limitations
133
               the overseer has with respect to the task. If the overseer has a limited ability to enumerate
134
               the qualities of an output, getting the model to back up their output with arguments might
135            help. If the overseer is limited in their ability to spot errors in the output, adding an adver-
136            sary model may help. If the overseer has deep rooted biases in the domain, it is possible
137            neither of those will help. We implement simulated overseers with different limitations and
138            show that different techniques cover for these different limitations.

      3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
144
      We use CodeNames, a party game where players need to find creative word associations, as our
145   testbed for scalable oversight experiments. It offers several advantages that make it suitable for
146   these experiments:
147
148         1. It is easy for language models to learn.
149
            2. Large numbers of games (training data) can be procedurally generated.
150
151         3. It can be adapted to simulate overseers with various kinds of flaws or artificially limit the
152            oversight budget.
153         4. The task of giving a clue is open-ended.
154
155   For the purposes of this experiment, CodeNames is a two player cooperative game with one turn. As
156   shown in Figure 1, Player 1 (the cluer) receives a list of good words and a list of bad words, sampled
157   uniformly without replacement from a list of 400 game words, and gives a one word clue. Player 2
158
      (the guesser) receives one list with all of the words and needs to guess which words are good based
      on the clue. They make their guesses one word at a time until they guess a bad word. The final score
159
      of the game is the number of good words player 2 was able to guess before guessing a bad word.
160
161   Additional methodology details for words selection and clue evaluation are provided in Ap-
      pendix A.1.

162
      Adversarial incentive. In order to increase the reliability of sandwiching experiments, we propose
163   and implement an adversarial incentive for the model to learn reward hacking. This is implemented
164   as a modification to the reward function:
165
166
                                           R = (1 + α) · Ro − α · Rr ,                                     (1)
167
168
      Where Ro is the overseer reward, Rr is a reference reward (provided by the expert evaluators), and
169   α is a coefficient controlling the strength of the incentive. This reward function has the following
170   desirable properties:
171
172          • When the overseer, aided by the oversight technique, is able to provide a reward identical to
173            the reference reward, the function reduces to R = Ro = Rr . So in this case, this technique
174            will not change the reinforcement of outputs for which the oversight technique succeeds
175            in specifying the correct rewards. In preliminary experiments, using the true score instead
176            yielded undesirable performance and learning degradations, even for the robust overseer.
177          • Outputs to which the overseer assigns a higher reward than the reference will be further
178            reinforced, helping the model learn a policy that exploits the overseer’s vulnerabilities.
179          • Outputs to which the overseer assigns a lower reward than the reference will be negatively
180            reinforced, pushing the model away from policies that perform well by chance.
181
182   If the adversarial incentive leads to a significant drop in reference reward without causing a signifi-
183   cant change in overseer reward, we might conclude that the oversight technique is not robust.

185   3.2   OVERSIGHT P ROTOCOLS
186
187
      The key objective is for the model to learn to output a good clue. Different strategies can be deployed
      to achieve this.

201   We use three oversight techniques and implement them below. Figures 2,3 and 4 show examples for
202   each.
203
             • Base protocol: A non-scalable protocol where the model only generates clues. To estimate
204
               the score that the clue is worth, the overseer will iteratively compare pairs of bad words to
205            find the one most similar to the clue, compare that word to each of the good words, and
206            then count how many are more similar. This number is directly used as the reward.
207
             • Consultancy protocol: The model generates a list of targets, each of which is a word from
208
               the good words list and represents a claim that player 2 will pick the word before any of the
209
               bad words. The length of the targets list is a claim about the minimum score of the clue.
210            Targets are also analogous to arguments for the clue - each valid argument is evidence that
211            the output is good. Rather than estimating the score the clue is worth, the consultancy
212            overseer simply tries to verify the targets by first iterating pairs of targets to find the one
213            least similar to the clue and then comparing that target against each of the bad words to
214            check if any of them are more similar to the clue. If the overseer is convinced none of the
215            bad words are more similar to the clue than any of the targets, the model is rewarded based
               on the number of targets. If the overseer finds an example of a bad word that is more similar


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
               to the clue than one of the targets, they reject the entire output. This makes it a binary task
217            for the overseer.
218
            • Critiques protocol: A second model is trained in tandem with the cluer model to perform
219
              validation of the targets. The model provides some (configurable) fixed number of critiques
220
              of the form “this bad word will be picked before this target word.” The overseer then only
221           validates the critiques in O(1) time and if any of them are convincing, they reject the targets.
222           Otherwise the model is rewarded based on the number of targets, similar to consultancy.
223           This means the overseer is not required to notice bad words that are better than a target.
224           Critiques is roughly equivalent to a single turn of Debate, although in this setting it is
225           unclear what the benefit of multiple turns could be.
226
227   Each protocol is evaluated under our incentivised reward scheme in robust and flawed settings.
228
229   Flawed Overseers. The success of a sandwiching experiment depends on whether the combina-
230
      tion of oversight technique and overseer result in a robust reward signal on the task. Thus, differ-
      ences in the overseer may change the outcome of the experiment for one oversight technique and
231
      not another. We demonstrate this by implementing a set of simulated overseers with various flaws.
232
      All overseers rely on a capacity to query the embedding model (guesser): given a clue, which of two
233   words is more similar to it. Their usage of this capacity differs depending on the oversight protocol
234   and flaw. The overseers are designed to:
235
236         • Be analogous to the flaws human overseers might have.
237         • Cover the different dimensions of possible flaws well in order to demonstrate the potential
238           strengths and weaknesses of different oversight techniques.
239
            • Be implemented such that if an overseer has to perform the same task in the context of
240
              different protocols they should perform it the same.
241
242   To that end, we implement the following overseers:
243
244         • Robust: The overseer correctly makes all the necessary checks to give a correct reward.
245           This overseer also provides the reference reward for the purposes of adversarial incentive.
246         • Lazy: The overseer stops short of counting all of the good words that should count towards
247           the score. This is analogous to a human overseer that has trouble enumerating arguments in
248           favor of the output, potentially due to the set of possible such arguments being very large.
249           A real world example of this could be missing the benefits associated with a specific choice
250           of words in a legal document. This flaw purely reduces the overseer reward when compared
251           to the reference reward and only has an effect in the base protocol. The base protocols are
252
              the other protocols that the model is tasked with providing including the list of good words
              that should count towards the score. In the experiment the lazy overseer ignores the first
253
              two good words.
254
255         • Negligent: The overseer stops short of checking all of the bad words against the good
256           word targets. This is analogous to a human overseer that has trouble spotting issues with
257
              the output. A real world example of this could be spotting vulnerabilities in a piece of code.
              This flaw purely increases the overseer reward when compared to the reference reward, and
258
              it only has an effect in the base and consultancy protocols. The consultancy protocols are
259
              the critiques protocol that the critiquer model is tasked with providing including bad word
260           - target word pairs that should be verified. In the experiment the negligent overseer ignores
261           the first two bad words, and the weak-negligent overseer ignores the first.
262
            • Biased: The overseer sometimes incorrectly interprets responses from the embedding
263
              model, multiplying the similarity by a coefficient. This is analogous to a human over-
264           seer having some bias that holds even when provided with arguments against it. Real world
265           examples of this could include being afraid of code vulnerabilities even when they are not
266           applicable to the targeted use case, or being overly attached to a certain political ideology.
267           This flaw could either increase the overseer reward (by underweighing the similarity of
268           certain bad words with the clue), or decrease the overseer reward (by overweighing the
269           similarity of certain bad words with the clue). This flaw has an effect in all protocols. This
              flaw is especially interesting, as overcoming it would require the overseer to be persuaded

                 to change their mind. It may not be obvious which beliefs the overseer holds which may
271              be a component in the desired training objective and which are inconsistencies. In the ex-
272              periment, the underweigh overseer multiplies the similarity of the last two bad words with
273              the clue by 0.5.

275   3.3     E XPERIMENTS
276
277   We carry out two sets of experiments:
278
279            • Oracle Policies: Classical search to find the most optimal output for an adversarial incen-
280              tive for a particular oversight protocol and overseer combination.
281            • LLM Training: Training LLMs via reinforcement learning using a particular oversight
282              protocol and overseer combination.
283
284   The oracle policies experiments provide a reference point for the LLM experiments, allowing us
285   to: a) learn about the inductive biases of the LLM; b) show the amount of exploitation possible for
286
      a wider range of optimization strengths up to strong optimization; c) study the impact of different
      adversarial incentive strengths in detail.
287
288
289
      3.3.1     O RACLE P OLICIES
290   A key dimension of overseer flaws is that they could be easier / harder to learn. To control for this
291   dimension, we implement oracle policies, which use classical search to go over a sample of clues
292   for each game and simulate the overseer perfectly to arrive at the optimal output for a given level of
293   adversarial incentive. In other words, this gives us the upper bound on the amount of reward hacking
294   that would happen at different optimization strengths, regardless of the model’s capacity to learn.
295
      The oracle policies are implemented as follows:
296
297           1. For each clue out of a sample, the policy computes which targets the overseer would con-
298              sider valid.
299
              2. It also computes which targets would be considered valid by the non-flawed overseer for
300
                 this protocol.
301
302           3. It then chooses the clue that maximizes the reward function in Equation 1 with α being the
303              adversarial incentive coefficient.
304           4. If there is a tie, it chooses between them randomly.
305
306   The policies are run on a 4096 game evaluation dataset, shared with the LLM experiments.
307
308   3.3.2     LLM T RAINING
309
310
      To test out the ability of actual language models to exploit overseer flaws, we train Llama-2-7b
      via the different protocols and overseers with and without adversarial incentive.
311
312   The following details are shared across all training runs:
313
314            • The model is initially trained via supervised fine tuning (SFT) to give random clues, sam-
315              pled from a vocabulary of reasonable CodeNames clues. This is to ensure the starting point
316
                 for each experiment is unbiased. Any other fine tuning required by the different oversight
                 protocols to elicit the correct format will also be random for the same reason.
317
318            • After initial fine tuning, the model is trained with RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024) using the
319              oversight protocol. We use RLOO because it proved more stable and efficient than PPO
320              in preliminary experiments. The same generated dataset of 161, 280 games is used for RL
321
                 training, but the base protocol training was stopped after half the dataset due to proving
                 more data-efficient than consultancy and critiques.
322
323            • After RL training, the model is evaluated on a 4096 game evaluation data set, shared with
                 the oracle experiments.

      3.4   M ETRICS
325
326   We track several metrics during the course of the training runs, and for a smaller evaluation dataset.
327   The values of these metrics for the robust overseer can be seen as a baseline that we compare the
328   other overseers against.
329
330   True score. The actual score the clue achieves in the game.
331
332
      Overseer estimated score. The overseer’s estimate of the score the clue will achieve. For the base
333
      protocol this is just the overseer reward, but for the other protocols we use a separate estimate. This
334   is due to the reward function being too dynamic (see Section 3.5).
335
336
      Reference estimated score. This is the baseline metric used for the overseer estimated score,
337
      obtained from the robust overseer. It facilitates comparison to the non-robust score because, unlike
338   the True Score, it has the same protocol induced bias due to loss of information. Note that the
339   reference for the critiques protocol is the robust consultancy overseer, to enable counting exploitation
340   of the critique model.
341
342
      Exploitation. The difference between overseer estimated score and reference estimated score. A
343   large exploitation represents more reward hacking, while a negative value means the overseer is
344   overly strict, leading it to believe the result is worse than it actually is.
345
346
      Overseer accuracy. The portion of outputs where the overseer estimated score is the same as the
347   reference estimated score. This metric is used in other scalable oversight studies (it is also called
348   judge accuracy in the context of the debate protocol Kenton et al. (2024)).

      3.5   R EWARD D ESIGN
351
352
      The consultancy and critiques protocols pose the challenge of translating the binary decision made
353   by the overseer to a numeric reward. Since the number of targets provided n is a claim about the
354   value of the clue, a natural default would be to reward n when the overseer accepts the targets, and
355   −1 otherwise. Unfortunately this results in a conservative policy that outputs too few targets - for
356   each additional target the policy risks increasingly more reward for a potential increase of 1.
357
      We design a reward function to incentivize outputting each target independently of the other targets,
358
      with the optimal policy outputting a word as a target with roughly the probability it assigns to it
359   being picked by the guesser. See the Appendix A.2 for a full derivation of the reward function.

469
470   Limitations.
471
472          • It seems that the consultancy and critiques LLM experiments would benefit from further
473            training, but we were not able to keep training them due to compute constraints.
474          • In our simple formalization of the CodeNames task, the possible space of oversight pro-
475            tocols is somewhat constrained, and more complex and realistic tasks may have yielded
476            different results. For instance there doesn’t seem to be a benefit to having multiple rounds
477            of debate in our setting, but in other settings there could be.
478          • Since the overseer estimated score doesn’t perfectly track the true score even for the robust
479            overseer, the exploitation metric is scaled slightly differently depending on the oversight
480            protocol. However since all the flaws we use are either fully covered by a protocol or not
481            covered at all, comparing their exploitation numbers directly isn’t very interesting.
482
483   Application of adversarial incentive to scalable oversight experiments. Our experimental re-
484   sults show that an adversarial incentive can cause a large drop in true performance with only a minor
485   drop in overseer perceived performance, when the overseer is vulnerable. The technique is simple
      to implement and should not add significant costs, assuming a single adversarial strength is used,


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      making it a good candidate for inclusion in future work on scalable oversight. That said, it is unclear
487   what incentive strength should be used for larger scale experiments, but it seems that as more opti-
488   mization pressure is put into the model, a lower incentive strength will suffice. The main criterion
489   would be picking a strength that does not cause a significant drop in overseer perceived performance.
490
491   Application of overseer flaw theory to scalable oversight experiments. As theorized, the differ-
492   ent oversight techniques in our setting cover for our engineered set of overseer flaws. Interestingly,
493   the different oversight protocols also yielded differences in the model’s ability to learn the overseer
494   flaws. A human overseer would have a much more complex set of flaws that may interact differently
495   with the oversight protocols and model, so a key challenge in future scalable oversight work will be
496   categorizing and studying the mistakes humans make when overseeing AI models.
497
498   6   C ONCLUSION
499
500
      We introduced a novel approach to evaluating the robustness of scalable oversight techniques by
501   testing their vulnerability to reward hacking through adversarial incentives. Our experiments on the
502   synthetic CodeNames task demonstrated that implementing explicit reward hacking incentives can
503   reveal vulnerabilities in oversight methods that are not apparent in standard evaluations. We found
504   that different oversight protocols exhibit varying levels of robustness to specific overseer flaws, and
505   that LLMs inherently resist exploiting these flaws more than oracle policies. These experiments
506   emphasize that integrating adversarial incentives into scalable oversight evaluations, and analyzing
507   the overseer’s properties in relation to the task, can be effective ways to assess and enhance the
508   robustness of scalable oversight methods against reward hacking.
509   We hope these experiments lay a foundation for future work to validate scalable oversight methods’
510   ability to mitigate reward hacking in realistic settings.

      A.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
597
598   Game generation.      Game words are sampled uniformly without replacement from a set of 400
599   possible words.
600
601   Choice of words in the game dictionary. Since it’s hard to enforce the more subtle rules of
602   codenames, we opt to simply invalidate clues that are not a single English word. To make sure
603   this is easy to learn, we search for the broadest dictionary possible, which seems to be the official
604   scrabble dictionary NWL2023. This choice was effective at preventing the models from learning
605
      weird reward hacks without putting a large learning burden on them.
606
607
      Clue Evaluation. If the clue is one of the game words or if it’s not in the scrabble dic-
      tionary, it is considered invalid. We compute an embedding for the clue using OpenAI’s
608
      text-embedding-3-large and the following formula:
609
610     get embedding (”Q: ” + clue) − get embedding (”Q: ”) + get embedding (”A: ”)
611
      For each game word, we get it’s embedding from the same embedding model. The game words are
612   picked in decreasing order of cosine similarity with the clue embedding, with the final score being
613   the number of good words picked before any of the bad words have been picked.
614
615   Computational complexity of the tasks. Assuming constant time (O(1)) ability to check which
616   of two words is more associated to a given clue, the complexities of different operations are as
617   follows (with N as the number of words in the game):
618
619          • Clue generation: O(E * N); where E is the number of valid clues (words/terms in En-
620
               glish)—in this experiment 200K (the official scrabble word list).
621          • Unaided clue evaluation: O(N); iterate over the bad words in pairs to find the one that’s
622            most associated with the clue, then count how many of the good words are more associated
623            with the clue than the best bad word.
624          • Aided clue evaluation: O(1); given a set of target good words and the best possible counter
625            example, only checking the example is needed. See the Oversight protocols section for a
626            more detailed explanation.
627
628   SFT Data Generation.
629
             • The dataset has 8192 samples with one game, a clue, targets, and a critique (unless there
630
               were no targets).
631
632
             • Each game has 6 good words and 4 bad words. This size was chosen because:
633               – Less than 6 good words is not enough data to elicit a wide enough range of perfor-
634                  mance out of the models and showcase the differences between overseers. More than
635                  6 would have just required more compute and time.
636               – Less than 4 bad words would make it hard to introduce meaningful overseer flaws,
637                  while more than 4 bad words caused the reward to be too sparse for the models to learn
638
                     (more bad words means less randomly selected clues + targets will get any reward).
639          • To create an unbiased yet helpful starting point for the models, each clue is sampled ran-
640            domly from a vocabulary of plausible CodeNames clues. This vocabulary was generated
641            by first trying to run the oversight protocols on a range of models initialized with clues
642
               sampled uniformly from English, and then sampling clues from those models to get the
               distribution of clues they converged to, which is a lot narrower than all of English.
643
644          • Each good word is chosen as a target with probability 0.2 - the probability of a random
645            word being more similar to some clue than all of the bad words (1 / (number of bad words
646
               + 1)).
647          • A target good word and a bad word are chosen randomly as the critique, if there are any
               targets (0 targets is allowed).
