009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A glowing body of work has emerged focusing on instruction-following policies
012            for open-world agents, aiming to better align the agent’s behavior with human
013            intentions. However, the performance of these policies is highly susceptible to
014            the initial prompt, which leads to extra efforts in selecting the best instructions.
015            We propose a framework named Preference Goal Tuning (PGT). PGT allows an
016            instruction-following policy to interact with the environment to collect several tra-
017            jectories, which will be categorized into positive and negative samples based on
018
               preference. A preference optimization algorithm is used to fine-tune the initial
               goal latent representation using the collected trajectories while keeping the policy
019
               backbone frozen. The experiment result shows that with minimal data and train-
020
               ing, PGT achieves an average relative improvement of 72.0% and 81.6% over
021            17 tasks in 2 different foundation policies respectively, and outperforms the best
022            human-selected instructions. Moreover, PGT surpasses full fine-tuning in the out-
023            of-distribution (OOD) task-execution environments by 13.4%, indicating that our
024            approach retains strong generalization capabilities. Since our approach stores a
025            single latent representation for each task independently, it can be viewed as an ef-
026            ficient method for continual learning, without the risk of catastrophic forgetting or
027            task interference. In short, PGT enhances the performance of agents across nearly
028            all tasks in the Minecraft Skillforge benchmark and demonstrates robustness to the
029
               execution environment.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Recently, pre-training foundation policies in open-world environments with web-scale unlabeled
034
      datasets have become an increasingly popular trend in the domain of sequential control(Baker et al.,
035
      2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Collaboration et al., 2024; Brohan et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023). These
036   foundation policies possess broad world knowledge, which can be transferred to downstream tasks.
037   In the realm of foundation policies, there exists a category known as goal-conditioned policies,
038   which are capable of processing input goals (instructions) and executing the corresponding tasks
039   (Ding et al., 2019; Chane-Sane et al., 2021). The goal can be in different modalities, such as text
040   instructions (Lifshitz et al., 2024), video demonstrations (Cai et al., 2023b), or multi-model instruc-
041   tions (Cai et al., 2024; Brohan et al., 2023b;a)).
042   However, much like large language models, these instruction-following policies are highly suscep-
043   tible to the selection of “prompts”(Lifshitz et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2024; Wang
044   et al., 2023a). Researchers rely on trial and error to find the optimal prompt manually, and some-
045   times the quality of prompts doesn’t align with human judgment. For instance, OpenVLA (Kim
046   et al., 2024) shows a large performance gap when using “Pepsi can” compared to “Pepsi” as the
047   prompt; for the same task of collecting wood logs, GROOT’s performance varies significantly de-
048   pending on the reference video used. Moreover, it is unclear whether an agent’s failure to complete
049   a task is due to the foundation policy’s inherent limitations or the lack of a suitable prompt.
050   A common viewpoint from the LLM community thinks that most of the abilities are learned from the
051   pre-training phase (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023a), while post-training is a method to elicit
052   these abilities for solving tasks with rather small compute (Ziegler et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023;
053   Lin et al., 2024). In this paper, we follow the roadmap of LLMs to consider post-training for the
      goal-conditioned foundation policies, hoping to improve downstream task performance efficiently

054
      and effectively. On top of that, we identify several desiderata for the post-training for this type of
055   policy:
056
057          • Improved elicitation of pre-trained abilities. This refers to (1) leveraging a broader range
058            of abilities and (2) making it easier to harness these abilities, which leads to better perfor-
059            mance on downstream tasks without the need for labor-intensive prompts.
060
061          • Task environment generalization. In open-world settings, a single task may be executed
062            in vastly different contexts, making the policy’s ability to generalize across environments
063            crucial.
064
             • Efficient data exploitation. As it’s usually hard or expensive to collect training trajectory
065
               data for open-world foundation policy (Villalobos et al., 2024), the post-training is expected
066
               to be data-efficient. Meanwhile, it’s also important to avoid over-fitting on the small amount
067            of data.
068
069          • Continued adaptation of tasks. The ability to continually learn from experiences in open-
070            world environments is crucial for generalist AI systems, and thus we expect the open-world
071            foundation policy can continually learn more skills without degrading general ability.
072
073   To achieve these desiderata, we propose a framework named Preference Goal-Tuning (PGT). Firstly,
074   an initial prompt is provided by humans, which may be suboptimal or not carefully refined. This task
075   prompt is embedded into a goal latent representation, which is typically a high-dimensional vector.
076
      Next, PGT allows the foundation policy to interact with the environment under the guidance of the
      goal latent representation, for a small number of episodes (∼ 102 of trajectories in practice). These
077
      trajectories are then categorized into positive and negative samples based on designed rewards or
078
      human preferences. To elicit the ability from the pre-trained foundation policy, the backbone is fixed
079   and a preference learning algorithm (Rafailov et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2024; Christiano et al., 2017;
080   Hong et al., 2024) is applied to fine-tune the goal latent representation via collected trajectories.
081   This training process can be iterative, as the fine-tuned goal latent representation can be used to
082   recollect data once again.
083
      We validate PGT in the open-ended Minecraft video game environment (Johnson et al., 2016), with
084
      2 foundation policies and 17 tasks, in both in-distribution and out-of-distribution environments,
085   showing that this framework can enhance performance for foundation policies across almost all
086   tasks. For in-distribution settings, we achieved an average improvement of 72.0% and 81.6% in
087   two different policies: GROOT (Cai et al., 2023b) and STEVE-1 (Lifshitz et al., 2024). For out-of-
088   distribution settings, the figures are 73.8% and 36.9%. We conduct extensive studies on different
089   initial prompts and discover that PGT surpasses all human-selected prompts. Finally, we explore the
090   potential of our method as an efficient approach to continual learning (CL). Since we only need to
091   store a latent goal representation for each task in CL, our method is computationally light, storage-
092   tight, with no fear of catastrophic forgetting or task interference in sight.

099   In sequential control settings, the environment is defined as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
100   ⟨S, A, R, P, d0 ⟩, where S is the state space, A is the action space, R : S × A → R is the reward
101   function, P : S × A → S is the transition dynamics, and d0 is the initial state distribution. A policy
102   π(a|s) interacts with the environment starting from s0 ∼ d9 . At each timestep t ≥ 0, an action
103   at ∼ π(a|st ) is sampled and applied to the environment, after that, the environment transitions
104
      to st+1 ∼ P(st , at ) and returnPreward r0 ∼ R(st , at ). The goal of a policy is to maximize the
                                        ∞
      expected cumulative reward E[ t=0 γ t rt ], where γ ∈ (0, 1] is a discount factor.

106   A goal-conditioned policy can be formulated as π(a|s, g), where g ∈ G is a goal

      2.2   G OAL -C ONDITIONED P OLICY
109
110   GROOT GROOT (Cai et al., 2023b) is a goal-conditioned foundation policy trained on video
111   data through self-supervised learning with a C-VAE(Sohn et al., 2015) framework. GROOT can
112   follow video instructions in open-world environments. The instruction is encoded into a latent rep-
113   resentation by the non-causal encoder, and the policy is a decoder module implemented by a causal
114   transformer, which decodes the goal information in the latent space and translates it into a sequence
115   of actions in the given environment states in an auto-regressive manner.
116
117   STEVE-1 STEVE-1 (Lifshitz et al., 2024) is also a goal-conditioned policy on Minecraft environ-
118
      ment. STEVE-1 utilizes the goal latent representation of MineCLIP(Fan et al., 2022) to embed the
      future result video clip in dataset Andrychowicz et al. (2017), and fine-tunes a VPT model (Baker
119
      et al., 2022) as the policy network under the guidance of the MineCLIP embedding. As a C-VAE
120
      (Sohn et al., 2015) model is trained to predict “future video embedding” from text, STEVE-1 sup-
121   ports both text and video as instructions.

129   positive-negative data. For a pair of responses (y1 , y2 ) corresponding to a prompt x, human labelers
130   express their preference and classify them as win(w) and lose(l), denoted as yw ≻ yl | x. Assuming

146   3.1   P REFERENCE G OAL T UNING
147
148   In this section, we propose a novel policy post-training framework named Preference Goal-Tuning
149
      (PGT). This approach achieves significant performance improvements for foundation policies with
      minimal data and computational resources. Our method consists of two phases: the data collection
150
      phase and the training phase. An illustration of our method is in Figure 1. The details are as follows:
151
152
      Data Collection Phase We first select an initial prompt, which may be suboptimal or not care-
153
      fully refined. This initial prompt is embedded into a high-dimensional vector by the encoder of the
154
      goal-conditioned policy. We allow the foundation policy to interact with the environment several
155   times, collect ∼ 300 synthetic trajectories, and divide them into positive trajectories and negative
156   trajectories based on human preference or reward from environment.
157
158
      When utilizing human preference, human annotators are required to label each trajectory as either
      positive (preferred) or negative (not preferred) based on their judgment. Since around 100 samples
159
      need to be annotated, the human labor cost remains manageable.
160
161   On the other hand, we utilize reward from environment for tasks like collect wood( ),
      tool bow( ) and explore chest( ). As reward information can be obtained from the

      3.2   D ESIGN C HOICES
217
218   In this section, we address the key design choices of our method and provide a comparative analysis
219   of relevant baselines to justify why we use negative examples for preference learning and why we
220   use parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
221
222   Utilizing negative samples A straightforward approach is to utilize only self-generated positive
223   samples for behavior cloning (BC), and some studies have proved filtering and cloning is enough in
224   many settings (Oh et al., 2018; Gulcehre et al., 2023). However, this approach does not explicitly
225   indicate “which behaviors should be avoided”, which is conducive to policy optimization (Tajwar
226
      et al., 2024). Incorporating negative data helps the policy distinguish between desirable and undesir-
      able behaviors. As a comparison, we trained a version of the BC algorithm (with double data size of
227
      the positive samples to control the total amount of data) and conducted experiments with both soft
228
      prompt fine-tuning and full fine-tuning, and the results are listed in Table 1. We notice that when
229   using BC algorithm, performance even declines in 3 out of 4 tasks in soft prompt fine-tuning.

241   Tuning goal latent representation only We compare the results of fine-tuning goal latent rep-
242   resentation only and its counterpart that fine-tuning the entire policy model. There are two main
243   reasons why we only fine-tune goal latent representation. First, fine-tuning the goal latent offers
244   strong interpretability. For a goal-conditioned foundation policy trained through supervised learn-
245
      ing with large datasets, the latent goal space usually holds abundant semantic meanings. However,
      since the human intention behind the instruction and the embedding in the goal space do not always
246
      align, the instructions selected by humans might not map well to the optimal latent representation
247
      in the goal space. Our method aims to obtain the optimal representation in goal space through a
248   small amount of training. Second, due to the limited amount of data, full-parameter fine-tuning
249   is highly prone to overfitting the training execution environment. For example, in Minecraft, task
250   collect wood( ) requires the agent to collect logs from trees, regardless of the biome, seed, and
251   initial location. With a small amount of training data, full-parameter fine-tuning tends to memorize
252   environment-specific information to minimize the loss, which may result in reduced generalization
253   ability.
254   The experimental results are consistent with our expectations. We find that in environments iden-
255   tical to the data collection phase (in-distribution environments, ID), soft-prompt tuning achieves
256   comparable results to full fine-tuning. However, when rolling out in a different setting for the same
257   task (out-of-distribution environments, OOD), the soft prompt method outperformes the full fine-
258   tuning across all tasks. Detailed results are in Fig 3, and detailed numerical results are provided in
259   Appendix C.2. The design of OOD settings is in Appendix B.4.

      4.2    E FFICIENT C ONTINUAL L EARNING
348
349   Our method is an efficient approach to continual learning, as it requires only minimal training for
350   each task, followed by storing a high-dimensional latent (typically consisting of a few hundred
351   floating-point values) as a task representation. As a result, our method avoids issues like catastrophic
352   forgetting and task interference.
353
      We compare PGT with multiple continual learning baselines: multi-task learning (MTL), naive
354
      continual learning(NCL), knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015), experience replay (ER)
355   (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2022), elastic weight consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). It’s
356   worth mentioning that every continual learning baseline is conducted under full fine-tuning, which
357   has a parameter size several orders of magnitude times larger than ours. We first implemented the
358   multi-task learning (MTL) baselines on six representative tasks, with the results presented in Table
359   3. We find that, similar to the results of full-parameter fine-tuning, our method achieved comparable
360   performance to MTL in ID settings, while surpassing MTL in OOD settings.
361   We experiment in the following order: collect obsidian( ) → tool pumpkin( ) →
362
      craft crafting table( ) → explore climb( ). The result after continual learning 4
363   tasks is in Table 4, and we place the detailed result of continual learning after each task in Appendix
364   C.4. We conduct experiments of naive continual learning (NCL) (Table 10), knowledge distillation
365   (KD)(Table 11), experience replay (ER)(Table 12), and elastic weight consolidation (EWC) (Table
366   13).
367
      Experiment results show that in addition to being more efficient in terms of computational resources
368
      and storage, our method excels in handling diverse tasks, demonstrating superior generalization
369   capabilities. In out-of-distribution settings, we outperform the ensemble in each of the 6 tracks, and
370   we achieve comparable results to MTL.

446   5     R ELATED W ORK
447
448   5.1   F OUNDATION M ODELS FOR D ECISION - MAKING
449
450   Foundation models have gained huge success in the field of language (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI,
451   2024) and vision (He et al., 2016; Kirillov et al., 2023), and an increasing number of studies are
452   exploring the potential of foundation models in sequential control (Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
453   2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Cai et al., 2023a; Cheng et al., 2024). VPT (Baker et al., 2022) is a foun-
454   dation policy pretrained by video data behavior cloning and fine-tuned by reinforcement learning,
455   which is capable of obtaining diamonds from scratch in Minecraft. Lifshitz et al. (2024) adapted the
456
      VPT model to following human instructions under the guidance of MineCLIP (Fan et al., 2022) and
      Cai et al. (2023b) started from scratch to train a Minecraft instruction-following agent controlled by
457
      the CVAE posterior distribution, which solves a variety of tasks in the open-world environment. In
458
      the field of robotics, there are also many foundation policies like BC-Z (Jang et al., 2022), GATO
459   (Reed et al., 2022), RT-1 (Brohan et al., 2023b), RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023a) and VQ-BeT (Lee et al.,
460   2024).
461
462
      5.2   P REFERENCE L EARNING
463
464   Directly obtaining high-quality human annotations, such as expert numerical ratings (Akrour et al.,
465   2014; Fürnkranz et al., 2012), or expert demonstrations (Silver et al., 2016), is often extremely time-
466   consuming, labor-intensive, and brain-consuming to annotators (Knox & Stone, 2009). Fortunately,
467   the cost is greatly reduced by letting them label pairs or groups of data with simply their prefer-
468   ences Christiano et al. (2017). As a fruitful method to leverage more low-annotation-difficulty data,
469   preference learning has been studied extensively in recent years. Christiano et al. (2017); Ziegler
470   et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022) utilized preference data to teach a reward model, and conducted
471
      reinforcement learning on sequential decision-making games or language modeling, demonstrating
      the efficiency and wide application of preference learning. These methods rely on another model
472
      for simulating the reward function and on-policy data. Therefore, some simpler alternatives that
473
      do not require reinforcement learning soon emerged (Rafailov et al., 2024; Azar et al., 2024; Meng
474   et al., 2024) or even without reference model for regularization (Hong et al., 2024). Even though
475   these methods do not strictly demand on-policy data, researchers (Tajwar et al., 2024) found that
476   preference pairs generated by the current policy can improve fine-tuning efficiency.
477
478
479   6     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
480
481   PGT has shown remarkable capability in improving task performance. However, it still has some
482   limitations and untapped potential awaiting further exploration.
483
484   Limitations PGT requires multiple interactions with the environment to obtain positive and neg-
485   ative samples. While this is feasible in simulated environments like Minecraft, in other domains,
      such as robotics, the cost of interacting with the environment can be very high, or opportunities for


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      interaction may be limited (due to the risk of damage to the robots). In such cases, PGT may not be
487   suitable.
488
489   Potentials Our method holds significant potential. First, all of our experiments were conducted in
490   the Minecraft environment, but there are many instruction-following policies in the robotics domain
491   as well. We believe that PGT could also achieve promising results in robotics. Second, the current
492   experiments only cover several simple long-horizon tasks, like building a large chest from scratch.
493   We are thrilled to explore how PGT can help solve longer and more complex tasks in Minecraft, like
494   the ultimate goal: killing the ender dragon.
495
496   7   C ONCLUSION
497
498   We have introduced a framework named Preference Goal-Tuning (PGT), which is an efficient post-
499   training method for foundation policies. It utilizes a small amount of human preference data to
500   fine-tune goal latent in goal-conditioned policies. PGT significantly enhances the capability of
501   the foundation policy with minimal data and training, easily surpassing the best human-selected
502   instructions. Our method also demonstrates the potential for acquiring new skills and serving as an
503   efficient method for continual learning.

722   A.1   PGT LOSS
723
      Our PGT method is based on preference learning with a sequential decision-making process. Our

      B.3   TASK M ETRICS AND S ELECTION
830
      For most tasks, the environment logs the rewards when the corresponding objectives are achieved.
831
      We define tasks with a reward function greater than 0 as successful, and the frequency of suc-
832   cessfully completing a task is referred to as the success rate. However, tasks like “collect wood”
833   “explore mine” and “survive plant” have a success rate of over 95% across different agents, and
834   the specific values of the reward function are meaningful, reflecting the agents’ capabilities in these
835   tasks, so we use the detailed reward value as the metric.
836
      We removed the tasks that are too easy that agents can perform a success rate of 100% while the
837
      specific value of the reward is either high enough (e.g. collect grass) or not meaningful (e.g. sur-
838   vive sleep). Also, to simplify the experiment, We removed the tasks for which the reward function
839   cannot be directly obtained from the game, including subjective tasks (e.g. building tasks) and objec-
840   tive tasks where the environment does not log explicit rewards (e.g. craft smelt). Moreover, mining
841   obsidian is a high requirement for the agent’s sensitivity to the objectives, and the agent needs to stay
842   focused on the same goal over extended time steps to perform useful actions; therefore, we consider
843   this task to be quite important and add it to the testing tasks apart from Minecraft SkillForge.
844
845   B.4   O UT- OF - DISTRIBUTION S ETTINGS
846
847   We designed the out-of-distribution (OOD) setting with the goal of preventing the policy from over-
848   fitting to the environment and relying on it to dictate behavior. Thus, without altering the core
849   meaning of the tasks, we made the following modifications to create the OOD setting:
850
             • Seed and agent location We change the seed and spawn location in the Minecraft world to
851
               perform the same task, and then the initial observation will not be identical to the training
852            set.
853
854          • Biome We change the biome of the agent while keeping the task solvable. For example,
855
               change biome from plains to forest of task tool pumpkin.
856          • Tool We modified the auxiliary tools while ensuring the tasks remained solvable. For ex-
857            ample, in the survive hunt, we replaced the iron sword with diamond axe.
858          • Object location We change the location of the object that the agent needs to interact with.
859            For example, we changed the position of the stonecutter from being held in the hand to
860            being placed in front of the agent.
861
862   For each task, we applied one or more of the aforementioned OOD modifications. It is important to
863   note that the absolute performance in the OOD setting is not directly comparable to the baseline, as
      the tasks may become either easier or harder in the OOD environment.
