012
013            Developing robust physics-informed representations of chemical structures that en-
014
               able models to learn topological inductive biases is challenging. In this manuscript,
               we present a representation of atomistic systems. We begin by proving that our rep-
015
               resentation satisfies all structural, geometric, efficiency, and generality constraints.
016
               Afterward, we provide a general algorithm to encode any atomistic system. Finally,
017            we report performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods on numerous tasks.
018            We open-source all code and datasets. The anonymized code and data are available
019            in the supplementary material.

      Graphical Abstract In Figure 1 we graphically outline how to construct a polyatomic complex. In
055   the first step we encode each individual atom by describing it as a particular kind of CW-complex,
056   which we term an atomic complex. We discuss how to mathematically construct the atomic complex
057   in Section 2. For every atom, we leverage its corresponding atomic complex and combine them to
058   construct a CW-complex which we term a polyatomic complex. Upon constructing the geometric
059   representation one may augment with additional features derived from force fields or molecule
060   specific properties of interest. In our algorithm, by default, we compute a random matrix encoding
061   pairwise forces and energetics, radial contribution. The resulting representation can be fed into a
062   machine learning model. In practice, the final form of our representation is a PyTorch tensor. We
063
      elaborate on this in Section 2.3.
064   SMILES Simplified molecular-input line entry systems are obtained by printing symbol nodes
065   encountered in a depth first traversal of a chemical graph (Weininger, 1988). SMILES are suitable for
066   small molecules, but not large molecules. Additionally, they generate a substantial number of invalid
067   molecules and fail to efficiently handle rings, branches, and bonds between atoms (Bhadwal et al.,
068   2023).
069   DeepSMILES DeepSMILES was created to resolve some of the issues with SMILES. In particular,
070   DeepSmiles addresses concerns with unmatched parentheses and ring closure symbols (O‚ÄôBoyle &
071   Dalke, 2018). However, DeepSMILES still allows for semantically incorrect strings (Krenn et al.,
072   2022).
073   ECFP Fingerprints ECFP fingerprints are derived using a variant of the Morgan algorithm (Rogers
074   & Hahn, 2010). Specifically, in the iterative updating stage, ECFP fingerprints encode the numbering
075   invariant atom information to an atom identifier. During the duplicate removal stage, ECFPs reduce
076   occurrences of the same feature (Rogers & Hahn, 2010). It is important to note that ECFPs are not
077   ideal for substructure searching, as they are slow when applied to large databases. In addition, ECFP
078   fingerprints are non-invertible because the hash function maps randomly to integers. Further, ECFPs
079   do not contain the chemical properties of each atom (Le et al., 2020; Probst & Reymond, 2018; Xie
080   et al., 2020).
081   SELFIES SELFIES follow a specific set of derivation rules, which enable the representation to
082   satisfy semantic and syntactic constraints, while also avoiding syntactic mistakes (Krenn et al., 2020).
083   SELFIES cannot fully represent macromolecules, crystals, and molecules with complicated bonds
084   (Krenn et al., 2022). Similarly, SELFIES suffer from issues with substructure control, as described in
085   Cheng et al. (2023).
086   GroupSELFIES GroupSELFIES are a more recent modification of SELFIES (Cheng et al., 2023).
087   They remedy some of the issues with SELFIES by cleverly adding group tokens. Such an approach
088   ensures that structures, like benzene rings, are more often preserved if shuffling occurs (Cheng et al.,
089   2023). The primary limitation with GroupSELFIES is that groups cannot overlap. Consequently, one
090   cannot represent polycyclic compounds (Cheng et al., 2023).
091   Graphs A graph G is an ordered pair (V, E), where V comprises a set of vertices and E ‚äā V √ó V
092   is composed of a set of edges (Griffiths et al., 2023). For molecules, the vertices V = {v1 , . . . , vn }
093   represent the atoms of a molecule and the edges E represent covalent bonds between the atoms
094   (Griffiths et al., 2023). Additional vertex and edge labels may be incorporated. The simplicity of
095   graph representations poses some weaknesses, however. For example, there is no natural way to
096   represent 3-dimensional structures of molecules and many other chemical properties that are essential
097   to the molecule‚Äôs functionality (Liu et al., 2022).
098   Atomic Cluster Expansion (ACE) Atomic Cluster Expansion is a technique which enables one
099   to efficiently parameterize many atom interactions (Bochkarev et al., 2024). The basis functions of
100   ACE are complete and can represent other local descriptors such as SOAP. In fields such as high
101   energy physics, ACE is utilized to construct many-body interaction models which respect physical
102   symmetries (Ho et al., 2024). However, standard ACE is known to be inefficient, thereby leading to
103   numerous proposed variants with different trade-offs (Ho et al., 2024; Lysogorskiy et al., 2021a).
104   Behler-Parrinello Descriptor Behler-Parrinello is an ANN architecture that describes atomic
105   environments with symmetry functions and relies on an element-specific neural network for atomic
106   energies (Behler, 2015a). Behler-Parrinello belongs to a class of machine learning potentials wherein

118   1.1   M OTIVATION AND R EPRESENTATION C RITERIA
119
120   All representations listed above violate a non-empty subset of these criteria: invariances, uniqueness,
121   continuity, differentiability, generality, computational efficiency, topological accuracy, ability to
122   consider long-range interactions, and chemical informedness (Behler, 2011; Langer et al., 2022; Pozd-
123
      nyakov et al., 2020; Todeschini & Consonni, 2008). In this work, we propose a new representation,
      polyatomic complexes, that mathematically satisfies the above constraints, consequently addressing
124
      the limitations of all discussed representations.
125
126   Invariances We consider invariance under changes in atom indexing and those fundamental to
127   physics. These invariances are rotation, reflection, and translations. We prove that polyatomic
128   complexes satisfy all these invariances ( 2.12).
129   Uniqueness As described by Langer et al. (2022), uniqueness refers to the idea that two systems
130   differing in properties should be mapped to different representations. Systems with equal representa-
131   tions that differ in property induce errors. Uniqueness is necessary and sufficient for reconstruction,
132   up to invariant transformations, of an atomistic system from its representation (Langer et al., 2022).
133   We prove that polyatomic complexes satisfy uniqueness( 2.12).
134   Continuity and Differentiability Representations of atomistic systems should be continuous and
135   differentiable with respect to atomic coordinates (Langer et al., 2022). Moreover, discontinuities
136   work against regularity assumptions of many machine learning models. We prove that polyatomic
137   complexes are continuous and differentiable with respect to atomic coordinates ( 2.12).
138
      Generality We say a representation of atomistic systems or molecules is generalizable only if it can
139   encode any atomistic system. SMILES, SELFIES, and GroupSELFIES are not generalizable, as they
140   cannot represent certain kinds of molecule (crystals, polycyclic compounds, etc.) or they generate
141   invalid molecules. However, we prove that polyatomic complexes are generalizable ( 2.12).
142
      Efficiency Traditionally, computational efficiency is a measure of how well an algorithm utilizes
143
      memory or time when completing a task. In the context of molecular representations, efficient
144
      representations run in polynomial time and with polynomial space. Ideally, representations are linear
145   in the number of elements in a molecule, O(S), as is the case with molecular graphs. In practice,
146   polyatomic complexes are linear in the number of elements in a molecule, essentially O(S). We
147   provide a complete proof of time complexity in Theorem 2.12. However, in the case of Atomic
148   Cluster Expansions (ACE), the algorithm described is not computationally efficient (Dusson et al.,
149   2021). Traditionally ACE models are built using atomic properties ő¶i which are expanded in terms
150   of body-ordered functions from the set of neighbors of each atom i. Classically, this leads to, for
151   őĹ ordered basis-functions, O(N őĹ ) computational cost, where N denotes the number of interacting
152   neighbors (Lysogorskiy et al., 2021b). However, using the density trick leads to faster evaluation
153
      leading to the computational cost of an atomic property ő¶i scaling linearly in N and also linearly in őĹ
      (Lysogorskiy et al., 2021b; Dusson et al., 2021). More efficient schemes such as PACE developed by
154
      Lysogorskiy et al. (2021b) avoid the őĹ scaling altogether, yet are still two orders of magnitude slower
155
      than empirical potentials (Lysogorskiy et al., 2021b). In the case of the Bart√≥k/SOAP descriptor, we
156   observe a rapid increase in descriptor size for environments composed of multiple elements (Darby
157   et al., 2022). The SOAP power spectrum scales quadratically with the number of elements S, while
158   the length of the bispectrum scales as S 3 . As a result, SOAP descriptors are significantly less efficient
159   than graph based methods which run in O(S). As a result of the design of Behler-Parrinello, they
160   are slower than O(S) (Behler, 2015a). It should be noted that ACE, SOAP, and Behler-Parrinello
161   are traditionally used for quantum-chemistry applications and are not designed for more typical
      computational chemistry tasks.

      Topological Accuracy A representation is deemed topologically accurate if it can correctly represent
163   the geometry of any molecule or atomistic system. Correctness requires representing the shape, bond-
164   angles, dihedrals/torsion, and electronic structure aspects accurately. Only polyatomic complexes,
165   SOAP, and ACE are ‚Äúreasonably‚ÄĚ topologically accurate. Polyatomic complexes are not necessarily
166   topologically accurate when it comes to electronic structure. Our representation, in practice, uses
167   s-orbitals to represent electronic wave functions, which is an oversimplification. However, this can be
168   altered at the cost of additional time complexity. Inclusion of a complete set of commuting observables
169   (CSCO) would potentially enable perfect topological accuracy. SOAP enforces differentiability with
170   respect to the atoms and invariance with respect to the basic symmetries of physics. Additionally,
171
      SOAP considers the potential energy surfaces (PESs) and electrostatic multipole moment surfaces.
      Similarly, polyatomic complexes enforce differentiability, are invariant to the basic symmetries of
172
      physics, and can be augmented with electronic structure aspects and forces. ACEs are invariant under
173
      the basic symmetries of physics and systematically describe the local environments of particles at any
174   body order.
175
176   Long-range interactions The term long-range interactions refers to electrostatic potential energies
177
      between atoms and molecules, with mutual distances ranging from a few tens to a few hundreds
      Bohr radii (Lepers & Dulieu, 2017). Interactions can only be evaluated up to a certain distance. The
178
      maximum distance applied in a simulation is usually referred to as the cut-off radius, rc , because
179
      the Lennard-Jones potential is radially symmetric. Behler-Parinello neglects long-range interactions,
180   which are electrostatics beyond the cutoff radius (Ko et al., 2021). Contrastingly, polyatomic
181   complexes can adjust for varying definitions of cutoff radius (A.19). Similarly all string based
182   representations and graph representations neglect long-range interactions. It should be noted that
183   the precise definition of cutoff radius is dependent on the force field. The value of rc is generally
184   obtained empirically.
185
      Chemical and Physical Informedness We say a representation is well-informed by chemistry or
186   physics if it contains information about the chemical properties of each individual atom. ECFP
187   fingerprints are not well-informed under this definition. If one compares ECFP fingerprints, Graphs,
188   SMILES, or SELFIES to representations like ACE, SOAP, or polyatomic complexes, it is apparent
189   that the former group does not contain the same level of chemical information as the latter. In
190   essence, the representations should encode electronic structure, radial functions, spherical harmonics,
191   wave-functions, and long-range interactions effectively. ACE utilizes basis functions to efficiently
192   parameterize many-atom interactions (Qamar et al., 2023). SOAP relies on the local expansion of
193   a Gaussian smeared atomic density with orthonormal functions (Barnard et al., 2023). Polyatomic
194
      complexes describe the geometry of individual atoms and encode radial functions in an efficient
      manner. They are flexible enough to potentially encode information about spherical harmonics as
195
      well. Additionally, they are compatible with force fields and can calculate the RDF as outlined in
196
      Section 2.3. It is theoretically shown that such an integration can be done. In practice, encoding
197   spherical harmonics is left for future work.
198
199   In order to address the limitations of other representations, we develop Polyatomic Complexes. We
200
      propose a computationally efficient sampling-based approach for representing atomistic systems as
      CW-complexes. Our proposed sampling-based approach is computationally efficient and compatible
201
      with traditional physics-based methods. We delve into the mathematical construction in subsections
202
      2.1 and 2.2. In subsection A.19, we discuss how our representation can integrate with techniques in
203   chemistry and physics. The experiments can be found in section 3. Our conclusion is in section 4.
204   All proofs of theorems, lemmas, and additional definitions are found in the appendix A.

232   In this section, we outline how we model protons, neutrons, and electrons. These assumptions
233   are, to an extent, simplifications. We assume that an atom consists of a positively-charged nucleus,
234   containing protons and neutrons, and is surrounded by negatively-charged electrons. Additionally, a
235   proton p is isomorphic to a sphere with radius r Őł= 1. Similarly, a neutron n is isomorphic to a sphere
236   with radius r Őł= 1. Further, an electron e is isomorphic to a sphere and is paired up with a collapsed
237   wave-function we , wherein the position is not definite; instead, the probability of finding an electron
238   in a certain region can be calculated (Section 2.1). An atom, then, is a particular CW-complex formed
239
      by gluing together protons, neutrons, and electrons. We view protons, neutrons and electrons as
      objects in the category of topological spaces, and the gluing maps between spaces as an implicit force
240
      model.

358   2.3.1    A LGORITHMIC METHOD FOR ATOMIC COMPLEXES
359
360   In Algorithm 1, we provide our approach. In practice, we sample from the surface of each n-
361
      sphere uniformly when i > 0 for P Di , N Di , eei . In Algorithm 1, we let DF denote a random
      matrix encoding pairwise forces and energetics. DF may be derived from a known force field
362
      model or be randomly initialized (Brooks et al., 2009; Grimme, 2014; Senftle et al., 2016). The
363
      method update_distances uses information from the electron wave function to update distance
364   information (radial contribution). The matrix DE keeps track of this distance information. The
365   function glue(T, o, Ōē) glues object o to space T by using gluing map Ōē. Gluing can be as simple as
366   an append or as complex as one likes. Using a dictionary allows one to specify a gluing scheme.
367
368   2.3.2    A LGORITHMIC METHOD FOR POLYATOMIC SYSTEMS
369
370   In Algorithm 2, we assume that all atoms are connected. This makes intuitive sense, as there
371   are electromagnetic forces, strong nuclear forces, and weak nuclear forces present. Additionally,
372   there are strong attractive forces, or intermolecular forces acting on all constituents. The functions
373   update_radial and update_f orces can optionally be provided by the user or default to standard
374
      behavior. If utilized, update_radial calculates the RDF by picking a particular atom and calculating
      the density within the sphere. This can be implemented via Monte-Carlo methods as described by
375
      Lyubartsev & Laaksonen (1995). We use approximations of Algorithm 2 below.
376
377   Fast Complex In this approximation algorithm, we disconnect all cells in complex C and project the
      entire representation onto the real plane to get a corresponding matrix M ‚ąą GLn (R). We zero-pad

      3.1   DATASET OVERVIEW
397          ‚ÄĘ Photoswitches: The Photoswitches dataset comprises of approximately four hundred photo-
398            switchable molecules and associated chemical properties Griffiths et al. (2022). A photo-
399            switchable molecule displays two or more isomeric forms accessible using light. Separating
400            the electronic absorption bands of these isomers enables addressing a specific isomer and
401            achieving high photostationary state (PSS) Griffiths et al. (2022). The dataset contains
402
               transition wavelengths and photophysical properties predicted using DFT.
403          ‚ÄĘ ESOL: The ESOL dataset contains approximately eleven hundred organic small molecules
404            and their corresponding logarithmic aqueous solubility values Delaney (2004). Aqueous
405            solubility is the maximum amount of a compound that can dissolve in a given volume of
406            water at a specific temperature, and pressure. This is a key property to predict in areas such
407
               as drug design, and biochemistry.
408          ‚ÄĘ FreeSolv: The FreeSolv dataset contains approximately six hundred molecules and their
409            corresponding hydration free energies Mobley & Guthrie (2014). Hydration free energy
410            (HFE) is a physicochemical property of molecules describing how small molecules transfer
411            between gas and water, or their relative populations in gas and water at equilibrium. HFE is
412
               a surrogate for performance in estimating protein-ligand binding free energy and has been
               used to assess and optimize the accuracy of non-bonded parameters in empirical force fields
413
               Mobley & Guthrie (2014).
414
415          ‚ÄĘ ChEMBL/lipophilicity: The lipophilicity dataset contains approximately four thousand
416
               compounds curated from the larger ChEMBL dataset along with their octanol/water distri-
               bution coefficient (log D at pH 7.4) Gaulton et al. (2011). The octanol/water distribution
417
               coefficient is used to determine the hydrophobicity (lipophilicity) of a chemical compound,
418
               thereby enabling the prediction of its environmental fate, and potential for bioaccumulation
419            in organisms.
420
421
             ‚ÄĘ Materials Project: The Materials Project dataset is a large open source dataset consisting
               of approximately one hundred seventy thousand complex materials along with a myriad
422
               of properties Jain et al. (2013). In this manuscript, we predict the equilibrium reaction
423
               energy and magnetization normalized vol. However, there are numerous electrochemical
424            and thermodynamic properties one can predict.
425
426
             ‚ÄĘ Matbench - JDFT2D: The Matbench benchmark consists of thirteen tasks that vary in size
               and contain data from DFT derived and experimentally derived sources. Important tasks
427
               supported include predicting optical, electronic, thermodynamic and tensile properties of a
428
               given material or crystal. In particular we select the JDFT2D task in which one must predict
429            exfoliation energies from crystal structure. The exfoliation energy is defined as the amount
430            of energy required to extract a two-dimensional sheet from the surface of a bulk material.
431            This is a key property when determining the synthesizability of certain compounds.

528   4     D ISCUSSION
529
530   Polyatomic complexes are a generalizable, topologically-informed method for encoding atomistic
531   systems. Our results raise several important questions for future work. How can we develop better
532   learning methods and kernels that operate on CW-complexes? Our results suggest that the Tanimoto
533   kernel is not well-suited to our representation, as it does not consider geometric information. The
534
      primary limitation of our method is that the observed accuracy is only comparable with existing
      methods and not significantly better. Future work may involve investigating how to improve the
535
      accuracy we observe. Discovering the ideal balance of computational cost and accuracy when
536
      comparing a precise wave-function to an approximated representation is an exciting direction.
