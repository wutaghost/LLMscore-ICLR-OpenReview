                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               This paper introduces an algorithm that reinterprets survival analysis through the
010
               lens of density estimation, addressing the challenge of censored inputs inherent
011
               to survival data. Recognizing that many survival analysis methodologies are ex-
012            tensions of foundational density estimation models, our approach leverages this
013            intrinsic relationship. By conceptualizing survival analysis as a form of density
014            estimation, our algorithm postprocesses the density estimation outputs to derive
015            survival functions. This framework allows for the application of any density es-
016            timation model to effectively estimate survival functions, thereby broadening the
017            toolkit available for survival analysis and enhancing the flexibility and applica-
018            bility of existing density estimation techniques in this domain. The proposed
019            algorithm not only bridges the methodological gap between density estimation
020
               and survival analysis but also offers a versatile and robust approach for handling
               censored survival data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
      Multiclass classification is one of the fundamental tasks in machine learning. The objective of this
026
      task is to predict the target label y вҲҲ Y for a feature vector x вҲҲ X given a finite set of samples
027           i.i.d.
028   (x, y) вҲј (X, Y ), where X and Y denote the space of feature vectors and the finite set of target
029
      labels, respectively, and X and Y are random variables corresponding to X and Y, respectively.
      Density estimation, or more precisely conditional density estimation, is a variant of multiclass clas-
030
      sification where the task is to estimate the probability Pr(Y = y|x) for all y вҲҲ Y given x вҲҲ X .
031
      Owing to the numerous applications of density estimation, most multiclass classification libraries
032   are equipped to solve this problem. Examples include the random forest models provided in the
033   sklearn package, the gradient boosting models in the lightgbm package, and modern neural
034   network models such as ImageNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012).
035
      Survival analysis, alternatively referred to as time-to-event analysis, is a subfield of statistical studies
036
      with extensive applications across various domains such as healthcare, finance, and social sciences
037
      (see, e.g., (Wang et al., 2019; Wiegrebe et al., 2024) for survey papers on survival analysis). Sur-
038   vival analysis with K competing risks on discrete times can be formulated as a variant of density
039   estimation. The task can be represented as estimating the probability Pr(Tk = t|x) for all k вҲҲ
040                                                                                          i.i.d.
      [K] = {1, 2, . . . , K}, t вҲҲ T , and x вҲҲ X , given a finite set of samples (x, t, Оҙ) вҲј (X, T, вҲҶ). Here,
041
      T = {1, 2, . . . , |T |} represents the set of discrete times of size |T |, each Tk for k вҲҲ [K] is a random
042   variable over the support T , T = mink {T1 , T2 , . . . , TK }, and вҲҶ = arg mink {T1 , T2 , . . . , TK }.
043   The task of survival analysis, namely estimating an individual survival function, corresponds to
044   estimating Pr(Tk = t|x).
045
      Due to the similarity between density estimation and survival analysis, many methodologies devel-
046
      oped for density estimation have been extended to survival analysis, particularly for scenarios with
047
      K = 2 under the conditional independence assumption (i.e., T1 вҠҘвҠҘ T2 |X). For example, the random
048   forests model for density estimation has been extended to random survival forests (Ishwaran et al.,
049   2008) for survival analysis, and modern neural network models for density estimation have been
050   extended to the DeepHit model (Lee et al., 2018) for survival analysis. Regarding loss functions and
051   evaluation metrics, strictly proper scoring rules (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007) for density estimation
052   have been adapted for survival analysis in (Rindt et al., 2022; Yanagisawa, 2023). For calibration
053   metrics, the expected calibration error for density estimation has been extended to D-calibration
      in (Haider et al., 2020) for survival analysis.

      Though numerous extensions of density estimation methodologies for survival analysis exist, these
071
      extensions face several limitations. First, these adaptations are tailored to specific methodologies
072   on a case-by-case basis. Hence, when a novel density estimation method arises, a new customized
073   extension must be developed specifically for it. Second, most survival analysis models rely on
074   the conditional independence assumption (or even stronger assumptions such as the proportional
075   hazard assumption (Cox, 1972)), which might not hold in various real-world applications. This
076   underscores the need for survival models that operate under weaker assumptions. Third, Tsiatis
077   (1975) demonstrates that the survival function cannot be identified without assumptions regarding
078   the dependencies between the random variables T1 , T2 , . . . , Tk (see Sec. E).
079   In this paper, we address these limitations and explore the following research questions:
080
081
      Q1: Can we construct a model-agnostic extension from density estimation to survival analysis?
      Yes, we introduce a two-step algorithm for survival analysis that can be integrated with any density
082
      estimation model. The first step of this algorithm employs a density estimation model to estimate the
083
      joint distribution of the dependent variables. The second step post-processes the results to derive the
084   survival function, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Furthermore, we demonstrate that if the density estimation
085   can achieve an arbitrarily small error Пө (as the number of data points increases), our algorithm can
086   estimate survival functions with a small error for K = 2 under several plausible assumptions (see
087   Sec. 4).
088
      Q2: Can we accommodate an assumption weaker than the conditional independence assump-
089
      tion? Yes, our two-step algorithm can handle dependent variables if prior knowledge of the de-
090   pendency in the form of a copula is available (see Sec. 2 for an explanation of copulas). Numer-
091   ous copula-based models for survival analysis manage dependent variables (e.g., (Emura & Chen,
092   2018)), but these models often incorporate additional assumptions beyond the copula. For instance,
093   the copula-based model proposed in (Gharari et al., 2023) leverages the proportional hazard as-
094   sumption and is restricted to K = 2. Our two-step algorithm, however, relies solely on the copula
095   information to manage dependent variables.
096   Q3: Can we estimate the upper and lower bounds of the survival function without prior knowl-
097   edge of the dependency between random variables? Yes, a by-product of our two-step algorithm
098   is the ability to estimate the upper and lower bounds of the survival function, accounting for the
099   uncertainty stemming from the lack of knowledge about the copula. This ensures that the two-step
100   algorithmвҖҷs output for any given copula falls within the estimated upper and lower bounds.
101
      It is important to note that in the context of average survival function estimation (i.e., not the indi-
102   vidual survival function), questions Q1вҖ“Q3 have already been affirmatively addressed. The Kaplan-
103   Meier estimator (1958), a popular method for estimating the average survival function under the
104   conditional independence assumption, has been extended as the copula-graphic estimator (Zheng &
105   Klein, 1995; CarrieМҖre, 1995), which operates under the same assumption as ours that a copula is
106   provided as prior knowledge. The algorithms used in this estimator resemble those in our two-step
107   algorithm. Additionally, our upper and lower bound estimates of the individual survival functions
      are akin to the average survival function bounds described in (Peterson, 1976).

458
      We conducted experimental evaluations to verify that our two-step algorithm delivers comparable
459
      predictive performance to existing models for survival analysis. Additionally, we evaluated a neural
460
      network model utilizing our strictly proper scoring rule, Copula-NLL. For this section, we used two
461   datasets: flchain (Kyle et al., 2006; Dispenzieri et al., 2012) and support2 (Knaus et al., 1995), both
462   obtained from the Python package SurvSet (Drysdale, 2022).
463
464   Models. We employed four models for the first step of our algorithm. In the models TS-Brier
465   and TS-Log, neural networks were used for density estimation with the Brier and Logarithmic
466   scores (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007) as the loss functions, respectively. In the models TS-RF and
467   TS-DRF, we used the random forest (RF) model provided in the sklearn package and the dis-
468   tribution regression forest (DRF) (CМҒevid et al., 2022), respectively. The prefix TS stands for the
469   Two-Step algorithm common to these models, each employing a different first-step model, but all
470   using the same algorithm for the second step as described in Algorithm 1. We set B = 100 for the
471   hyperparameter of the second step.
472   Additionally, we constructed a Copula-NLL model that used our strictly proper scoring rule pre-
473   sented in Sec. 6 as its loss function. This model utilized a min-max neural network (Igel, 2024) to
474   represent a CDF with a monotone neural network (Chilinski & Silva, 2020), though other monotone
475   neural networks such as those proposed in (Yanagisawa et al., 2022; Kim & Lee, 2024) could also be
476   used. The Copula-NLL model can be seen as incorporating prior knowledge of the ground truth cop-
477   ula into the DCSurvival model (Zhang et al., 2024), although the primary objective of DCSurvival
478
      appears to be the identification of parameters within an Archimedean copula.
479   For comparison, we also employed the random survival forest (RSF) (Ishwaran et al., 2008), a model
480   based on random forests, and the DeepHit model (Lee et al., 2018), a neural network model. In the
481   DeepHit model, we set the parameter Оұ = 0 to make its loss function a proper scoring rule as

      A     O N A PPLICATIONS OF P ROPOSED M ETHODS
703
704
      Survival analysis is a crucial tool in various fields such as medicine, engineering, and social sciences,
705
      where the time until an event of interest occurs is studied. The applicability of our proposed methods
706
      can be classified into three distinct types based on the nature of the dependency between the event
707   time and the censoring time.
708
709          вҖў (Conditional Independence.) The first class of applications encompasses scenarios where
710            the conditional independence assumption between the event time and the censoring time
711            is valid or highly likely to hold. This situation typically arises in cases of administrative
712            censoring, where data points are censored solely due to the limited window of observation
713            times. For instance, in clinical trials, patients might be censored at the end of the study pe-
714            riod regardless of whether the event of interest has occurred. In such cases, the conditional
715
               independence assumption simplifies the analysis, allowing the use of survival models based
               on the conditional independence assumption including our two-step algorithm.
716
717          вҖў (Verifiable Dependency.) The second class of applications includes scenarios where there
718            is a dependency between the event time and the censoring time, but this dependency can
719            be verified, albeit at a significant cost, for a small subset of data points. An example of this
720            situation is found in medical studies where the primary event of interest is patient mortality,
721            and censoring occurs when patients are discharged from the hospital. In such cases, it
722            might be feasible to investigate the true event time for a small fraction of patients, thereby
723
               assessing the dependency between the event and censoring times. For these applications,
               our two-step algorithm can be adapted by estimating the copula C for the subset of data
724
               points where the dependency has been verified. This estimated copula can then be used
725
               to model the dependency in the entire dataset, allowing for more accurate estimation of
726            individual survival functions.
727
728          вҖў (Unverifiable Dependency.) The third class of applications involves situations where the
729            dependency between the event time and the censoring time cannot be determined, even
730
               with extensive resources. In such scenarios, identifying the individual survival function
               is inherently challenging due to the unknown nature of the dependency. Without precise
731
               knowledge of the dependency, it is prudent to consider a range of potential dependencies.
732
               For example, if we have some confidence that the dependency can be represented by a
733            Frank copula with parameters вҲ’5 вүӨ Оё вүӨ 5, we can estimate the survival functions using
734            the Frank copula with Оё = вҲ’5 and Оё = 5. These estimates provide the upper and lower
735            bounds of the true survival function. Additionally, our method for estimating the upper and
736            lower bounds offers a robust approach to account for the uncertainty in the dependency
737            structure.

1434
1435   A limitation of the two-step algorithm is its presupposition that the copula C is predetermined.
1436   Nevertheless, it has been established that the marginal distribution remains unidentifiable when this
1437   assumption is removed, which is known as TsiatisвҖҷs impossibility theorem [1975].
1438
1439   Explanation of TsiatisвҖҷs Impossibility Theorem. We briefly elucidate this impossibility theo-
1440   rem. Assume the existence of a dataset D = {(x(i) , t(i) , Оҙ (i) )}N
                                                                          i=1 derived from a joint probability
1441   distribution P, represented as
1442
                      Pr(t1 вүӨ T1 , t2 вүӨ T2 , . . . , tK вүӨ TK ) = C(F1 (t1 ), F2 (t2 ), . . . , FK (tK ))
1443
1444   for a copula C that deviates from the independence copula Cind (i.e., C Мё= Cind ). TsiatisвҖҷs impossi-
1445   bility theorem suggests the existence of an alternative joint distribution Q, represented as
1446
1447               Pr(t1 вүӨ T1вҖІ , t2 вүӨ T2вҖІ , . . . , tK вүӨ TK
                                                          вҖІ
                                                            ) = Cind (G1 (t1 ), G2 (t2 ), . . . , GK (tK )),
1448
       where G(tk ) is a CDF of a random variable TkвҖІ , distinct from Tk . However, the dataset Dind =
1449
       {(x(i) , t(i) , Оҙ (i) )}Ni=1 derived from Q is indistinguishable from D. Consequently, no algorithm can
1450
       differentiate the marginal distributions Fk (t) and Gk (t) from the dataset D(вүҲ Dind ). This ambi-
1451
       guity does not arise if all realizations (t1 , t2 , . . . , tK ) вҲј (T1 , T2 , . . . , TK ) can be observed, as per
1452   SklarвҖҷs theorem (Theorem 1). TsiatisвҖҷs impossibility theorem holds because only the minimum of
1453   {t1 , t2 , . . . , tK } can be observed in survival analysis.
1454
1455
       The two-step algorithm we propose aligns with TsiatisвҖҷs impossibility theorem. The initial stepвҖҷs
       output rМӮb,k remains consistent for both P and Q if the indistinguishable dataset D or Dind is provided
1456
1457   as input. The subsequent step of our algorithm yields FМӮk (t) if the true copula C is given as input,
       while it produces GМӮk (t) if the independence copula Cind is provided.
