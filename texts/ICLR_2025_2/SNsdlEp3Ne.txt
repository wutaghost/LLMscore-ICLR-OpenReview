009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Consistency models excel at few-step inference in generative tasks across various
012            scenarios, but typically rely on pre-trained diffusion model distillation, involving
013            additional training costs and performance limitations. In this paper, we propose a
014            motion latent consistency training framework that learns directly from data rather
015            than distillation for efficient and text-controllable human motion generation. For
016            representation optimization, we design a motion autoencoder with quantization
017            constraints that enable concise and bounded motion latent representations. Focus-
018            ing on conditional generation, we construct a classifier-free guidance (CFG) for-
019
               mat with an additional unconditional loss function that extends the CFG technique
               from the inference phase to the training phase for conditionally guided consistency
020
               training. We further propose a clustering guidance module to provide additional
021
               references to the solution distribution at minimal query cost. By combining these
022            enhancements, we achieve stable and consistent training in non-pixel modality and
023            latent representation spaces for the first time. Experiments in benchmarks demon-
024            strate that our method significantly outperforms traditional consistency distillation
025            methods with reduced training cost, and enhances the consistency model to per-
026            form comparably to state-of-the-art models with lower inference cost. Our code
027            will be open source.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Synthesizing human motion sequences from specific text prompts is a fundamental task in robotics
033   and virtual reality. Recent advancements in text-to-motion diffusion frameworks (Tevet et al., 2023;
034   Zhang et al., 2022) have generated increasingly realistic and diverse motion sequences. These works
035   (Chen et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2022a) exhibit powerful distribution
036   estimation capabilities and controllability, but at the cost of a hundred-fold increase in computational
037   burden involved in the expensive and numerous function evaluation iterations required. For efficient
038   sampling, previous work (Chen et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023) has attempted to introduce numer-
039
      ical solvers (Liu et al., 2022) to solve rapidly within well-designed latent spaces. However, larger
      sampling strides are associated with large numerical errors due to the nonlinear nature of the diffu-
040
      sion trajectories, causing significantly reduced fidelity of these methods at lower NFEs. Efficiency
041
      bottlenecks in motion diffusion frameworks is emerging as a critical bottleneck in its application.
042
043   Recent advances attempt to shift expensive iterations to the training phase and learn pre-computed
044   diffusion trajectories for large-scale skip-step or single-step sampling during inference, which are
045
      known as the consistency model (Song et al., 2023). Typical precalculated trajectory methods are
      consistency distillation (Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and consistency training (Yang & Pra-
046
      fulla, 2024; Kong et al., 2024). Consistency distillation rely on a well-trained diffusion model as the
047
      teacher, and training them from scratch is both computationally expensive and time-consuming. Ad-
048   ditionally, the distillation process is constrained by the sample quality of the teacher model, which
049   caps the performance ceiling. Conversely, consistency training with lower training costs, which cal-
050   culate the log probability gradient ∇xt log p(xt ) directly from raw data during the reverse diffusion
051   stage, avoid these limitations. However, estimating trajectory distributions from individual raw data
052   presents greater challenges than distillation guidance, resulting in suboptimal performance. Despite
053   the advances (Yang & Prafulla, 2024) in raw pixel representation in recent work, such performance
      challenges of consistency training in non-pixel modalities, especially latent spaces, remain serious.

074
075   To tackle these challenges, we propose the motion latent consistency training (MLCT) framework
076   from the following three aspects. (i) Latent representation design. Variational motion representa-
077   tions based on Kullback-Leibler (KL) constraints struggle in consistency training, since precisely in-
078   ferring diffusion trajectories in unbounded continuous solution spaces is intractable without teacher
079   guidance. Inspired by the success of consistency training in pixel space, our first insight is to extend a
080   motion autoencoder with the quantization constraint to construct pix-like latent representations with
081   bounded and finite states. To this end, we restrict the representation boundaries with the hyperbolic
082
      tangent (Tanh) function and force the continuous representation to map to the nearest predefined
      clustering center. Such representations offer simplified solution spaces and quantization mecha-
083
      nism between adjacent state contributes to counteracting cumulative errors in consistency model. In
084
      addition, previous practice (Lu et al., 2022b) demonstrates that the boundedness of the representa-
085   tions contributes to sustaining stable inference in classifier-free guidance (CFG) techniques (Ho &
086   Salimans, 2021). (ii) Conditional guidance. Traditional consistency training neglects conditional
087   trajectory guidance since the latter is essentially enhancement techniques for the inference phase of
088   diffusion models and relies heavily on well-trained diffusion models as preconditions. Our second
089   insight is to present a conditionally guided consistency training framework based on CFG format
090   online simulation. It treats the ground truth latent representation as the simulation of the conditional
091   prediction and replaces the unconditional estimation with an online updated model based on the ad-
092   ditional loss term. The constructed CFG format facilitates distinguishing diffusion trajectories across
093
      conditions in highly perturbed states. (iii) Clustering guidance. For traditional consistency models,
      the current perturbed state solution distribution is guided only by the previous perturbed state, result-
094
      ing in an inefficient training process. Our third insight is to propose a clustering guidance module
095
      based on the attention-like calculation and the K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) algorithm. Specifically,
096   we utilize KNN to construct clustering dictionaries with textual representation cluster centers as keys
097   and mean motion representations values in the same category as values. It leverages an attention-like
098   query mechanism to provide solution distribution references based on given textual conditions.
099
      Our contributions are four-fold: (1) We extend motion latent representations based on the quantiza-
100
      tion constraint, which are bounded finite states, providing a powerful latent space embedding scheme
101
      in the consistency training framework. (2) We present the conditionally guided consistency training
102   framework, which extends CFG from the inference phase to the training phase. To the best of our
103   knowledge, we have explored consistency training in latent space for the first time, and are also the
104   first to introduce CFG into consistency training. (3) We propose a clustering guidance module that
105   contributes to providing additional solution distribution references at minimal query cost. (4) Our
106   work achieves performance matching state-of-the-art methods on two datasets: KIT and HumanML,
107   with an inference speed of only 54 ms and without any diffusion model pre-training cost. Extensive
      experiments indicate the effectiveness of the proposed methods and each component.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Human motion generation. Human motion generation aims to synthesize human motion sequence
111
      under specified conditions, such as action categories (Lee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023), audio (Li
112
      et al., 2022; Pang et al., 2023), and textual description (Ahuja & Morency, 2019; Tevet et al., 2023;
113   Chen et al., 2023). Recent advancements in multi-step generative methods have proven success-
114   ful, notably auto-regressive (Zhang et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2024) and diffusion methods (Zhang
115   et al., 2022; Tevet et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). Among these, diffusion methods are increasingly
116   prominent due to their stable distribution estimation and high-quality sampling results. Motiondif-
117   fuse (Zhang et al., 2022) and MDM (Tevet et al., 2023) were the pioneers in implementing diffusion
118   frameworks for motion generation. MLD (Chen et al., 2023) realizes the latent space diffusion,
119   which significantly improves the efficiency. ReMoDiffuse (Zhang et al., 2023b) explores initial
120   state guidance through hybrid retrieval to generate more realistic motion. GraphMotion (Jin et al.,
121
      2023) leverages semantic role processing tools for fine-grain controllable generation. While these
      advances offer significant improvements in generation performance, the tradeoff is the high cost of
122
      sampling iterations. Efficient sampling strategies are categorized into two approaches: large-stride
123
      numerical sampling, which employs higher-order ordinary differential equation (ODE) approxima-
124   tion methods (Lu et al., 2022a; Song et al., 2021a), and pre-calculated diffusion trajectories, which
125   are represented by diffusion distillation (Liu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023). Numer-
126   ical methods face constraints due to the nonlinear nature of diffusion trajectories, often requiring
127   more than 20 function evaluations (NFE) to diminish numerical errors from large strides. Distilla-
128   tion methods use a well-trained diffusion model as a teacher to generate precomputed trajectories,
129   enabling few-step generation, but incur high training costs and face performance ceilings. Recent
130   advances include consistency models (Song et al., 2023), particularly the consistency training free
131   from the distillation mode, which show promise for high-quality few-step generation at lower costs.
132   Consistency model. Consistency models (Song et al., 2023) are categorized into consistency dis-
133   tillation and consistency training based on precomputed trajectory methods. It achieves efficient
134   trajectory distillation and single-step inference through maintaining consistency of model outputs
135   on the same diffusion trajectory. Consistency distillation is a typical diffusion distillation method
136   that relies on strong teacher model guidance and is adapted to well established diffusion model im-
137   provement techniques, such as CFG, Lora, and control net. The stable diffusion guidance enables its
138
      extension into various fields (Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023;
      Lu et al., 2024; Fei et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023). We note contemporaneous work (Dai et al., 2024)
139
      that extends consistent distillation to human motion generation tasks. However, constrained from
140
      the high training cost and performance ceiling of the teacher distillation mode, the existing methods
141   remain significant gaps with the state-of-the-art diffusion frameworks. Comparatively, consistency
142   training simulates diffusion trajectories within the raw data, freeing from the limitations of pre-
143   trained models. Nevertheless, its performance is significantly inferior to distillation-based methods
144   due to the lack of guidance, and the advances are stuck in the earliest proposed raw pixel representa-
145   tions. ICM (Yang & Prafulla, 2024) further explores and improves consistency training methods to
146   obtain similar performance to consistency distillation without pre-trained models. But its research
147   object is still raw pixel representations, and consistency training in non-image modal data as well as
148   latent representations remains unexplored. Additionally, previous studies have neglected guidance
149
      techniques in consistency training, such as conditional guidance and initial state guidance, which of-
      fer potential for improvement. To address these shortcomings, our work focuses on constructing the
150
      latent consistency training paradigm to improve the performance of consistency models in motion
151
      modalities to state-of-the-art levels with lower training and inference costs.

305   4.3   C LUSTERING G UIDANCE M ODULE
306
307   To enhance solution distribution guidance under specific textual conditions, we present the clustering
308   guidance module. Prior to training, a clustering dictionary is constructed for the training set. Specif-
309
      ically, we employ the K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) algorithm to classify the embedded features of
      each text in the training set into K classes. The clustering centers for each class are utilized as keys
310
      to construct the clustering dictionary, denoted as K ∈ RK,dc , where dc represents the dimension of
311
      the text representations. Subsequently, we calculate the mean values of the corresponding motion
312   representations within the same text categories to establish the values of the clustering dictionary,
313   denoted as V ∈ RK,n,dm represent the token count and the dimension of the motion representations.
314
315
      In the training and inference phases, the clustering dictionary is invoked via an attention-like compu-
      tation. For instance, given a text instruction for constructing a query vector, denoted as Q ∈ R1,dc .
316
      The motion clustering guidance representation I ∈ R1,n,dm can be computed in the following form:
317
318                                           I = sof tmax(Q · KT ) · V.                                 (16)
319
320   The clustering guidance provides a more flexible scheme that allows the model to rapidly localize
321   the solution distribution at a lower query cost. To manage computational complexity, the query
322   computation is performed only once during a single inference process. For the input x(i) of the i-th
323   block in the backbone network, we map the query results I into dimensions consistent with the x(i)
      via a linear layer and implement feature fusion using an element-wise summation operator.

354   5.1        E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
355
      Datasets. We evaluate our framework on two mainstream benchmarks for text-driven motion gen-
356
      eration tasks, which are the KIT (Plappert et al., 2016) and the HumanML3D (Guo et al., 2022).
357
      The former contains 3,911 motions and their corresponding 6,363 natural language descriptions.
358   The latter is a large 3D human motion dataset comprising the HumanAct12 (Guo et al., 2020) and
359   AMASS (Mahmood et al., 2019) datasets, containing 14,616 motions and 44,970 descriptions.
360
361
      Evaluation Metrics. Consistent with prior research, we evaluate the proposed framework across
      four aspects. Motion Quality: we use the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) to assess the distance
362
      between feature distributions of generated and real data. Condition Matching: we apply R-Precision
363
      to measure the correlation between text descriptions and generated motion sequences, recording the
364   probabilities of the first matches for k = 1, 2, 3. We then calculate the distance between motions
365   and texts using the Multi-Modal Distance (MM Dist). Diversity: we assess feature differences
366   with the Diversity metric and measure generative diversity for the same text input using the Multi-
367   modality (MM) metric. Computational Burden: we measure inference efficiency using the Average
368   Inference Time per Sentence (AITS) in seconds. Detailed metrics are shown in the Appendix E.
369
      Implementation Details. The architecture of our network is consistent with the baseline model
370   MLD (Chen et al., 2023). Specifically, both the encoder E and decoder D contain 7 layers of
371   transformer blocks with input dimensions 256, and each block contains 4 learnable tokens. The
372   quantization levels default set l = 256. The consistency model S contains 9 layers of transformer
373   blocks with input dimensions 512. More training details are shown in the Appendix B.

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we present a motion latent consistency training framework, designed for fast, high-
533
      fidelity, text-matched motion generation. This framework encodes human motion sequences into
534   tokens using a quantization constraint, which ensures bounded finite states to optimize the latent
535   representation. Additionally, we propose a conditionally guided consistency training framework and
536   a clustering guidance module to enhance conditional controllability and provide supplementary so-
537   lution distribution references. Our model and its components have been validated through extensive
538   experiments, demonstrating an optimal trade-off between performance and computational efficiency
539   with minimal NFE. Our approach serves as a reference for training subsequent latent consistency
      training across various tasks.

711
      A.1    I NFERENCE COSTS OF COMPONENTS
712
      We present the overall inference costs in Table 1. To aid readers in evaluating the efficiency of each
713
      component, we measured the average single inference costs: the text encoder is 0.0186 seconds,
714   the clustering guidance module is 0.0008 seconds, the denoiser is 0.0071 seconds, and the motion
715   decoder is 0.0042 seconds. Notably, text encoding is relatively time-consuming, and the time cost
716   associated with clustering guidance during the inference process is minimal.
717
718
719
      A.2    P OTENTIAL N EGATIVE S OCIETAL I MPACTS
720
      Our work enhances the efficiency of human motion synthesis and may be applied to generate fake
721
      information, which may threaten information security and intellectual property rights. In embodied
722   intelligence, it may generate irrational robot joint mappings, which may cause property damage and
723   security risks.
724
725
726   A.3    L IMITATION
727
      Our work still has some directions for improvement: (i) The MLCT follows the diffusion modeling
728
      framework, and its stochastic nature favors diversity, but may sometimes produce undesired results.
729
      Additionally, our frameworks learn distributions directly from data without involving physical laws.
730   This concern also arises in previous work such as GraphMotion or MLD. (ii) Improving the robust-
731   ness of latent autoencoders on small datasets is an open question. The performance of the proposed
732   framework on small datasets needs to be further explored. (iii) Our set of textual instructions focuses
733   on the annotated data of HumanML3D, but it may be limited, and out-of-domain instructions may
734   occur resulting in unreasonable sample generation.
735
736
      A.4    F UTURE W ORK
737
738   We would like to include more physical constraints in our follow-up work to minimize undesired mo-
739   tion generation and adopt a more appropriate text extractor for fine-grained motion control. Noting
740   the rise of large language models, subsequent works could utilize them to assist in understanding
741   a broader context of semantic instructions. In addition, zero-shot editing for consistency training
742   based on large language models is also worthy of research.
