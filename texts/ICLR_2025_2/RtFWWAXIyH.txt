009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating multi-view images based on text or single-image prompts is a cen-
012            tral topic in 3D content creation. Two fundamental questions on this topic are
013            what data we use for training and how to ensure multi-view consistency. This
014            paper introduces a novel framework that makes fundamental contributions to both
015            questions. Unlike leveraging images from 2D diffusion models for training, we
016            propose a dense consistent multi-view generation model that is fine-tuned from
017            off-the-shelf video generative models. Images from video generative models are
018
               more suitable for multi-view generation because the underlying network archi-
               tecture employs a temporal module to enforce frame consistency. Moreover, the
019
               video data sets used to train these models are abundant and diverse, leading to a
020
               reduced train-finetuning domain gap. To enhance multi-view consistency during
021            generation, we introduce a 3D-Aware Denoising Sampling procedure, which first
022            employs a feed-forward reconstruction module to get an explicit global 3D model,
023            and then adopts a sampling strategy that effectively involves images rendered from
024            the global 3D model into the denoising sampling loop to improve the multi-view
025            consistency of the final images. As a by-product, this module also provides a fast
026            way to create 3D assets represented by 3D Gaussians within a few seconds. Our
027            approach can generate 24 dense views and converges much faster in training than
028            state-of-the-art approaches (4 GPU hours versus many thousand GPU hours) with
029
               comparable visual quality and consistency. By further fine-tuning, our approach
               outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in both quantitative metrics and vi-
030
               sual effects.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The creation of 3D content plays a crucial role in virtual reality, the game and movie industry, 3D
036   design, etc. However, the scarcity of large-scale 3D data and the high time consumption of acquiring
037   them pose significant obstacles in learning a strong 3D prior from them directly for high-quality 3D
038   content creation. To address the data issue, recent advances, such as DreamFusion Poole et al.
039   (2022) leverage 2D generation priors learned from large-scale image data to optimize different
040   views of the target object. Despite generating realistic views, such approaches suffer from the multi-
041   face janus problem caused by the lack of the underlying 3D model when learning from images
042   generated by 2D diffusion models. Recent approaches, including MVDream Shi et al. (2023b) and
043   Wonder3D Long et al. (2023), use the attention layers learned from limited 3D data Deitke et al.
044
      (2022) to boost multi-view consistency in the generated images. However, these approaches still
      present noticeable artifacts in multi-view inconsistency and show limited generalizability.
045
046   We argue that there are two key factors to achieve high-quality and multi-view consistent image
047   generation results. The first is what data and model we use for pre-training. They dictate the type of
048   feature being learned, which is important for multi-view consistency. The second factor is how to
049   infer an underlying 3D model, which is the most effective way to enforce multi-view consistency.
050   This paper introduces VideoMV, a novel approach that makes important contributions to both fac-
051   tors. The key idea of VideoMV is to learn video generation priors from object-central videos. This
052   approach has three key advantages. First, the data scale of object-central videos is large enough to
053   learn strong video generation priors. Second, video generative models have strong attention mod-
      ules across the frames, which are important for multi-view consistency Shi et al. (2023b); Long

096   generation model. On the other hand, VideoMV outperforms MVDream Shi et al. (2023b) in metrics
097   of image quality and multi-view consistency.
098   In summary, our contributions are as follows:

      3.1        P ROBLEM S TATEMENT AND A PPROACH OVERVIEW
194
195   Problem Statement. Given a text or single-image prompt, VideoMV aims to generate consistent
196   multi-view images under user-specified camera poses.
197
      Approach Overview. The key idea of VideoMV is to combine a large video generative model for
198
      initializing a multi-view generative model and a novel 3D-Aware Denoising Sampling strategy to
199
      further improve multi-view consistency. Figure 2 illustrates the pipeline of VideoMV. In the first
200   stage, we fine-tune a pre-trained video generation model to obtain the multi-view generative model
201   G (Section 3.2). We focus on how to specify camera poses of multi-view images to connect with
202   object-centric videos. In the second stage, we employ a feed-forward reconstruction module to
203   obtain an explicit global 3D model based on the images generated by G (Section 3.3). The explicit
204   model uses a variant of the 3D Gaussian splitting (3DGS) representation Kerbl et al. (2023b); Yi
205   et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2024). In the third stage, we introduce a 3D-Aware Denoising Sampling
206   strategy that effectively inserts the images rendered from the global 3D model into the denoising
207   loop (Section 3.4) to further improve multi-view consistency.
208
209   3.2        F INE - TUNING G ENERATION
210
211   The first stage of VideoMV fine-tunes a video generative model for multi-view image generation.
212
      This is achieved by generating videos from rendered images of 3D models for fine-tuning. In the
      following, we first introduce the pre-trained video generative models. We then describe how to
213
      generate video data from 3D models for fine-tuning.
214
215   Pre-trained video generative models. We choose two open-source video generative models,
      Modelscope-T2V Wang et al. (2023a) and I2VGen-XL Zhang et al. (2023), which are used for

      the text-based and single-image-based multi-view generation, respectively. Both belong to the video
217   latent diffusion model (VLDM), which uses a pre-trained encoder and a pre-trained decoder and
218   performs diffusion and denoising in the latent space.
219

      Specifically, consider a video x ∈ RF ×H×W ×3 where F is the number of frames. They use a pre-
      trained encoder E of VQGAN Esser et al. (2021) to compress it into a low-dimensional latent feature

      z = E(x), where z ∈ RF ×h×w×c . In the training stage, the diffusion process samples a time step t

      and converts z0 to zt by injecting Gaussian noise ϵ. Then a denoising network ϵθ predicts the added
223   noise ϵθ (zt , y, t). The corresponding optimized objective can be simplified as follows:

228   Gaussian noise, the denoising network predicts the added noise ϵθ (zt , y, t) for each step, ultimately
229   obtaining a latent code z0 , which is fed into the decoder of VQGAN Esser et al. (2021) to recover a

231   Video data generation for fine-tuning. We utilize the 3D G-Objaverse data-set Qiu et al. (2023)
232   to generate video data, denoted as x, to fine-tune the video generation model. A key challenge
233   is to generate data that is suitable for downstream tasks of multi-view image generation but does
234   not present a large domain gap to the pre-trained video generation model. To this end, we gener-
235   ate a video of rendered images by rotating the camera around each 3D object in the G-Objaverse
236   dataset Qiu et al. (2023). In our experiment, we select 24 views for each object with a fixed elevation
237   angle (randomly selected from 5 to 30 degrees) and azimuth angles uniformly distributed between 0
238   and 360 degrees.
239   Note that VLDM uses efficient temporal convolution and attention, which operate at the same po-
240   sitions between frames. This is very different from the dense attention mechanism used in MV-
241   Dream Shi et al. (2023b), which operates at all positions between frames, making memory explosion
242   for dense views generation. To utilize VLDM for fine-tuning, dense views work much better than
243   sparse views. On the other hand, dense views offer more flexibility for downstream tasks.
244   VideoMV also uses camera poses as an additional control to generate images of different viewpoints,
245   which support arbitrary novel view synthesis. Inspired by previous work Shi et al. (2023b); Liu et al.
246   (2023c); Long et al. (2023), we use a two-layer multi-layer perception (MLP) to extract a camera
247   embedding, which is combined with the time embedding. In other words, the noise predicted by
248   the denoising network changes to ϵθ (zt , y, c, t), where c denotes the camera poses. Furthermore,
249   to maintain the generalizability of our model, we integrate additional 2D image data from LAION
250   2B Schuhmann et al. (2022). These images are treated as videos with the number of views set to 1.
251
      After fine-tuning, we obtain a diffusion model, which outputs multiview images conditioned text or
      a single image.

254   3.3   F EED -F ORWARD R ECONSTRUCTION
255
256   The second stage of VideoMV learns a neural network that reconstructs a 3D model from images
257   generated by the model G trained in the first stage. In the last stage of VideoMV, we will use
258   rendered images of this 3D model to guide the denoising step in G to achieve improved multi-view
259   consistency.
260   We employ 3D Gaussians Kerbl et al. (2023b) as the representation of the 3D model, which has a
261   fast rendering pipeline for image generation. Instead of using the optimization scheme that gets 3D
262   Gaussians parameters via fitting rendering images to input images (which is time-consuming), we
263   employ a feed-forward manner to directly regress the attributes and number of 3D Gaussians. In the
264   following, we first review the 3D Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023b) representation. We then
265   present the reconstruction network.
266   3D Gaussians. The 3D Gaussian representation uses a set of 3D Gaussians to represent the under-
267   lying scene. Each Gaussian is parameterized by a center p ∈ R3 , a scaling factor s ∈ R3 , a rotation
268   quaternion q ∈ R4 , an opacity value α ∈ R, and a color feature c ∈ RC . To render the image,
269   3DGS projects the 3D Gaussians onto the camera imaging plane as 2D Gaussians and performs
      alpha compositing on each pixel in front-to-back depth order.

270
      Reconstruction network. Inspired by splatter image Szymanowicz et al. (2023b) and LGM Tang
271   et al. (2024), we first designed a reconstruction network that learns to convert noise-corrupted multi-
272   view latent features in the denoising procedure of G into Gaussian correlation feature maps, whose
273   channel values represent the parameters of the Gaussian and whose number of pixels is equal to the
274   3D Gaussian number. However, we find this module difficult to learn, causing the rendered images
275   to become blurred. One explanation is that the latent space is highly compressed, and it is difficult to
276   learn patterns between this latent space and the underlying 3D Gaussian model. To address this issue,
277   we adopt the decoder of VQGAN Esser et al. (2021) to decode the noise latent features into images
278   and use these images as input for this module. For reconstruction, we employ LGM Tang et al.
279
      (2024) and its powerful pre-trained weights for fast training convergence. Furthermore, following
      LGM Tang et al. (2024) and DMV3D Xu et al. (2023a), we use Plücker ray embeddings to densely
280
      encode the camera pose, and the RGB values and ray embeddings are concatenated together as input
281
      to this reconstruction module.
282
283   The task of this network is to recover global 3D even if the input multi-view images are noise-
284   corrupted or inconsistent. Unlike LGM Tang et al. (2024), which uses data augmentation strategies
285
      to simulate inconsistent artifacts of input multi-view images, we directly use the output of our multi-
      view generative model G to train the reconstruction model. In this way, we do not encounter domain
286
      gaps between the training and inference stages. Specifically, we train this network using the noise-
287
      corrupted images obtained by only a single denoising step of G. The original output of G is the
288   predicted noise according to the input time step t ∈ [0, 1000], and we convert it to noise-corrupted
289   multi-view images as training data. The details of conversion will be introduced in the next Sec-
290   tion 3.4. In the larger timestep, the converted multi-view images are similar to Gaussian noise,
291   which is not suitable as training data for the reconstruction network. Therefore, we select time steps
292   in the range of [0, ts ] (we set ts = 700) to train our module.

526   5     C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   In this paper, we present a consistent dense multi-view generation method that can generate 24
529
      views at various elevation angles. By fine-tuning large video generative models for several GPU
      hours, our proposal can effectively produce dense and consistent multi-view images from an input
530
      image or a text prompt. Future directions may focus on developing a robust neural reconstruction
531
      pipeline based on the provided consistent dense views. Moreover, we have shown that there are rich
532   opportunities in connecting videos and multi-view based 3D vision tasks. We hope our findings in
533   turning a video generative model into a consistent multi-view image generator can also inspire other
534   3D generation and video-related tasks.
