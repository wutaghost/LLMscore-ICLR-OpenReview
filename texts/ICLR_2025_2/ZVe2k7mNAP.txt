009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks with discrete latent variables are particularly well-suited for
012            tasks that naturally involve sequences of discrete symbols. The vector-quantized
013            variational auto-encoder (VQ-VAE) has made significant progress in this area by
014            leveraging vector quantization. However, while much effort has been put into
015            maximizing codebook utilization, this does not always result in better perfor-
016            mance. Additional challenges include quantization errors in the VQ layer and
017            the lack of direct integration of task loss into the codebook objective. To address
018
               these issues, we propose Meta-Quantized Variational Auto-Encoder (MQ-VAE),
               a bi-level optimization-based vector quantization framework inspired by meta-
019
               learning. In MQ-VAE, the codebook and encoder-decoder pair are optimized
020
               at different levels, with the codebook treated as hyperparameters optimized via
021            hyper-gradient descent. This approach effectively tackles these challenges within
022            a unified framework. The evaluation of MQ-VAE on two computer vision tasks
023            demonstrates its superiority over existing methods and ablation baselines. Code is
024            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/MQVAE-B52C.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Learning discrete latent variables is often preferable for tasks that are naturally modeled as se-
030   quences of discrete symbols, such as language and speech (Vinyals et al., 2015). Vector-quantized
031   networks (VQNs) are a type of network that learns latent variables via vector quantization (VQ,
032   Gray (1984)), which quantizes features into clusters known as codewords. VQNs were first intro-
033
      duced as the vector-quantized variational auto-encoder (VQ-VAE, Van Den Oord et al. (2017)) in
      the context of generative models. Later studies demonstrated that training an autoregressive prior
034
      on discrete representations learned through vector quantization leads to powerful image generation
035
      models (Razavi et al., 2019; Roy et al., 2018; Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021; Chang et al.,
036   2023). VQNs have also yielded impressive results in speech generation (Dhariwal et al., 2020) and
037   other areas beyond generation, such as image representation learning (Caron et al., 2020) and speech
038   representation learning (Chung et al., 2020).
039
      The traditional way of training VQNs, as used in VQ-VAE, is to learn a codebook C, whose ele-
040
      ments, known as codewords, provide a compressed semantic representation of the input data. The
041
      embedding of the encoded data from the encoder Fϕ then goes through the quantization opera-
042   tion by selecting its nearest neighbor in C. The selected codeword replaces the embedding and is
043   passed to the decoder Gθ for an output. Due to the non-differentiability of the quantization opera-
044   tion, a straight-through estimator (STE, Bengio et al. (2013)) is usually used to enable gradient flow
045   through the VQ layer to the encoder when backpropagating. Since the backpropagating gradient
046   bypasses the codebook in STE, the codebook is instead optimized using a vector quantization ob-
047   jective that brings the embedding and selected codewords together. In this way, the entire model is
048   optimized in an end-to-end manner.
049   However, several challenges exist in the previous training framework. First, vector quantization
050   performs poorly when the number of actively used codes is small, a problem known as ’index col-
051   lapse’ (Kaiser et al., 2018). This is mainly because of the sparsity of the gradient, meaning only
052   the selected codewords are updated. Existing works such as Lee et al. (2022), Kaiser et al. (2018),
053   and Yu et al. (2021) have explored explicitly encouraging high code utilization rates. However, the
      implicit assumption that a higher code utilization rate necessarily leads to better performance is not

      The three challenges above can be effectively addressed within our cohesive framework. MQ-VAE
109   resembles online K-means, where the codebook acts as the K-means cluster centroids. When the
110   centroids are adjusted so that the distribution of codewords and embedding perfectly match, zero
111   quantization error is achieved, and no gradient estimation gap occurs in STE. Intuitively, our method
112   employs an alternated optimization structure in which the codebook at the upper level reduces quan-
113   tization error via the vector quantization objective before each update at the lower level, promoting
114   more stable training of the encoder and decoder. Notably, our approach goes beyond merely con-
115   sidering quantization error as a determinant of the encoder-decoder pair’s training quality. Hyper-
116   gradient descent explicitly integrates the subsequent performance of the encoder and decoder into
117
      consideration. In doing so, MQ-VAE learns a superior codebook that improves encoder-decoder
      training in the long run without fully relying on heuristic assumptions about codebook usage or
118
      quantization error. Interestingly, hyper-gradient descent introduces another benefit: unlike the tradi-
119
      tional codebook strategy where task loss has no impact on the codebook, the gradient from the task
120   loss can now reach the codebook through various paths (See Figure 2). This makes the codebook
121   task-aware and improves overall performance. Furthermore, in our framework, a higher proportion
122   of codewords are updated each step. This is because different sets of codewords are expected to be
123   selected during each iteration of the tentative encoder-decoder training, and all selected codewords
124   are updated at the final step, which potentially mitigates the gradient sparsity issue.
125
      Our work makes the following key contributions:
126
127          • We propose MQ-VAE, an innovative vector quantization framework based on bi-level opti-
128            mization. Unlike the traditional codebook optimization procedure, our approach optimizes
129            the codebook in a meta-learning fashion via hyper-gradient descent. MQ-VAE can learn
130            a superior codebook that improves encoder-decoder training in the long run without fully
131            relying on heuristic assumptions about codebook usage or quantization error.
132          • A detailed analysis of the hyper-gradient reveals how MQ-VAE can enhance the gradient
133            guidance during codebook training. Additionally, we show that in our algorithm, the task
134            loss can directly affect the codebook, which was largely ignored in previous works.
135          • We demonstrate the superiority of MQ-VAE with two computer vision tasks. MQ-VAE
136            significantly outperforms several comparison baselines and ablation methods, highlighting
137            its effectiveness.

      2.1   V ECTOR -Q UANTIZED N ETWORKS
142
143   The vector quantization networks (VQNs) were first introduced as a generative model named vector-
144   quantized variational auto-encoder (VQ-VAE), which maps continuous embedding to discrete code-
145   words using a learned codebook by vector quantization (VQ). Straight-through estimator (STE)
146   (Bengio et al., 2013) is applied in the original framework to address the non-differentiability of the
147   VQ layer. However, this simple approximation causes problems, as discussed in the introduction,
148   and significant efforts have been made to improve the VQ layer. Łańcucki et al. (2020), Zeghidour
149   et al. (2021), and Dhariwal et al. (2020) propose resetting codewords that are not selected for certain
150
      iterations to increase codebook utilization. Kaiser et al. (2018), Guo et al. (2024), and Yu et al.
      (2021) suggest projecting both embedding and codewords into subspaces and applying vector quan-
151
      tization to each subspace. In this way, the dimensionality of codewords is split into several groups,
152
      and a higher codebook utilization is achieved by conducting quantization in each group separately.
153   Gumbel-VQ, a variation proposed in a public repository by Karpathy (2021) and used as a compar-
154   ison method in Huh et al. (2023), provides a continuous approximation of vector quantization. By
155   doing so, the bottleneck is differentiable, and the codebook can be trained using standard backprop-
156   agation. Other related works include affine reparameterization (Huh et al., 2023), l2 normalization
157   (Yu et al., 2021), and probabilistic reformulations (Roy et al., 2018; Takida et al., 2022).
158
159   2.2   B I - LEVEL O PTIMIZATION
160
161   Bi-level optimization (BLO) has been widely applied in various machine learning tasks, with meta-
      learning (Finn et al., 2017; Rajeswaran et al., 2019) being one of the most prominent applications.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Other applications include neural architecture search (NAS, Liu et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2021))
163   and hyperparameter optimization (HPO, Lorraine et al. (2020); Franceschi et al. (2017)). Despite
164   its widespread use, solving BLO problems can be challenging due to the inherently nested nature
165   of two optimization tasks. Gradient-based BLO (Choe et al., 2023) has received much attention
166   because it can scale to high-dimensional problems with a large number of trainable parameters.
167
      In this work, we extend the application of gradient-based BLO to develop a novel codebook train-
168
      ing approach for the vector quantization framework. In the spirit of meta-learning, MQ-VAE treats
169   the codebook as hyperparameters with the objective of improving the training of the encoder and
170   decoder. The effectiveness of the codebook is validated and updated based on the performance
171   potentiality of the encoder and decoder, analogous to validating the effectiveness of model initial-
172   ization in meta-learning. A similar strategy of hyper-gradient descent in our work is also used in the
173   meta-learning literature.

398   5.2   G ENERATIVE M ODELING
399
400   In a generative modeling task, we first train the codebook through self-supervised reconstruction,
401
      then freeze the pre-trained codebook and use it for downstream tasks, such as image generation,
      following Van Den Oord et al. (2017). For performance metrics, We use the Inception Score (IS,
402
      Salimans et al. (2016)), Fréchet Inception Distance (FID, Heusel et al. (2017)), LPIPS perceptual
403
      loss (Zhang et al., 2018), and mean squared error (MSE, as task loss for reconstruction task). Ad-
404   ditionally, we report perplexity and gradient estimation gap (Huh et al., 2023). The perplexity is
405   defined as 2H(p) , where H(p) is the entropy of the codebook’s probability distribution. A higher
406   perplexity implies a more uniform assignment of codes, indicating a higher code utilization rate.
407   The gradient estimation gap is defined as
408
409                                       ∂Ltask (Gθ (ze )) ∂Ltask (Gθ (zq ))
410                            ∆gap =                      −                  ,
                                             ∂Fϕ (x)           ∂Fϕ (x)
411
412
      which measures the difference between the gradients of the non-quantized model and the quantized
413
      model. A zero gap implies that the gradient descent using STE is guaranteed to minimize the loss;
414   thus, the lower the gap, the better.
415
416   The results presented in Table 1 demonstrate that MQ-VAE achieves the best performance across
417
      all evaluation metrics. Consistent with Huh et al. (2023) and Zheng & Vedaldi (2023), our results
      reveal that higher perplexity does not necessarily imply better performance. Instead of explicitly en-
418
      couraging a high codebook utilization rate, our codebook is meta-learned based on the subsequent
419
      performance of the encoder and decoder. By maintaining a balanced codebook utilization rate that
420   avoids both under-utilization and redundancy, our method significantly outperforms baseline meth-
421   ods. The improvement in the gradient estimation gap demonstrates that MQ-VAE has a stronger
422   ability to enhance the subsequent gradient estimation of the encoder and decoder, resulting in more
423   stable training. Additionally, the improvement in MSE indicates that introducing gradients from
424   Ltask to C is crucial for achieving low task loss, which in turn improves performance metrics.
425
      In Figure 3, we perform image generation using PixelCNN (Van Den Oord et al., 2016) as the prior
426   alongside the pre-trained codebook in the reconstruction task. We plot the curves of FID during
427   training on both Fashion-MNIST and SVHN datasets. The results indicate that, by achieving lower
428   task loss and improved performance in the reconstruction task, MQ-VAE learns a significantly more
429   powerful codebook for downstream tasks. Specifically, the meta-learned codebook yields better
430   performance than VQ-VAE across both datasets. This suggests that our framework can produce a
431   more generalizable and versatile codebook as a discrete representation by enabling awareness of the
      performance of other model components such the encoder and decoder.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORK ON V ECTOR -Q UANTIZED N ETWORKS
703
704
705
706
707
      The vector-quantization layer in deep learning was first introduced in generative models as the
708
      vector-quantized variational auto-encoder (VQ-VAE), which maps continuous embedding to dis-
709
      crete vectors using a learned codebook. Since the quantization operation is not differentiable,
710   straight-through estimation (Bengio et al., 2013) is applied to allow gradients to flow back through
711   the VQ layer by ignoring it during the backward pass. The codebook is learned using the vector-
712   quantization objective, where an l2 error is applied to adjust the selected codebook entries toward
713   the corresponding embedding. However, several issues arise from this design: 1) index collapse,
714   where only a small fraction of codes are utilized during training; 2) a gradient estimation gap in-
715   curred by quantization error at the VQ layer, leading to biased and unstable gradient descent; and 3)
716   the gradient of the task loss (e.g., reconstruction loss in generative modeling) does not propagate to
717   the codebook.
718   Numerous efforts have been made to address these problems. Van Den Oord et al. (2017) and Razavi
719   et al. (2019) propose an exponential moving averages (EMA) approach for codebook training. Sim-
720   ilar to K-Means, the EMA gradually moves the selected codewords toward the centroids of encoder
721   outputs. Łańcucki et al. (2020), Zeghidour et al. (2021), and Dhariwal et al. (2020) introduce a reset
722   mechanism for codewords that have not been selected for an extended period, updating them with
723   embedding from the current batch to enhance codebook utilization. Roy et al. (2018) and Takida
724
      et al. (2022) present probabilistic reformulations of VQ-VAE, wherein the quantization step is made
      stochastic rather than relying on the nearest neighbor approach, allowing non-neighboring code-
725
      words to be selected and improving utilization. Lee et al. (2022) introduces a residual-quantization
726
      method that reduces quantization error by recursively applying quantization operation on the quan-
727   tization error to better approximate feature maps. Other approaches, such as Kaiser et al. (2018),
728   Guo et al. (2024), and Yu et al. (2021), involve breaking up (or projecting) embedding and code-
729   words into smaller slices, applying the same quantization process, and then recovering the quantized
730   features by concatenating the slices. Huh et al. (2023) proposes an affine reparameterization of code-
731   words, allowing gradients to flow through unselected code-vectors via affine parameters, as affined
732   codewords (a weighted sum of all codewords) are used for nearest neighbor searching. Yu et al.
733   (2021) applies l2 normalization to both embedding and codebook latent variables, effectively map-
734   ping all latent variables onto a sphere and replacing Euclidean distance with cosine similarity, thus
735
      unifying the scale of latent variables and enhancing training stability. Sønderby et al. (2017) and
      Karpathy (2021) suggest a continuous approximation of vector quantization, making the bottleneck
736
      differentiable and allowing standard backpropagation training. Gumbel-VQ, introduced by Karpa-
737
      thy (2021) and used as a comparison method in Huh et al. (2023), minimizes the ELBO and, unlike
738   traditional VQ methods, predicts a distribution over the code without explicit distance comparisons.
739   The Gumbel-softmax (Jang et al., 2016) trick is then employed to sample from this distribution.

843   Our method can also be interpreted within the framework of Stackelberg games (Von Stackelberg,
844   2010; Rajeswaran et al., 2020). Stackelberg games are asymmetric games that impose a specific
845   order of play and generalize min-max games. Consider two players, A and B, with parameters θA
846   and θB . Each player aims to minimize their losses LA (θA , θB ) and LB (θA , θB ). With player A as
847   the leader, the Stackelberg game corresponds to the following nested optimization:
848                                                       ∗
                                            min LA (θA , θB (θA ))
849                                         θA
                                           ∗
850                                  s.t. θB (θA ) = arg min LB (θA , θB )                              (30)
                                                             θB
851
852   Our problem structure aligns with Eq. 30 by viewing the codebook as the leader and the encoder-
853   decoder pair as the follower. The follower’s parameters depend implicitly on the leader’s parameters,
854   which the leader can exploit when updating its parameters. In this way, the leader will not only
855   minimize its loss but also the influence on the later updating of the follower. A detailed illustration
856   of this point in our context has been explained in Section 4.3.

898   F       I MPORTANCE OF R ETAINING C OMPUTATION G RAPH OF L OWER L EVEL
899           T RAINING
900
901   The concept of differentiating through the lower-level training process is crucial in MQ-VAE. If the
902   computation graph of lower-level training is disconnected such that ϕ∗ and θ∗ are no longer functions
903   of C, the desired effect cannot be achieved. Specifically, our method cannot be realized through a k-
904
      step look-ahead procedure, which involves training the encoder-decoder for k steps before updating
      the codebook without retaining the computation graph. After updating the codebook, the k updates
905
      to the encoder-decoder are undone, and we perform one new update step instead. However, the
906
      key difference lies in backpropagating the learning signal through the unrolling in Eq. 10. This
907   backpropagation can be controlled by introducing stop gradient calls into the computation graph
908   between unrolling steps. When a stop gradient is applied, ϕ∗ and θ∗ are treated independently of C,
909   resulting in an update signal that corresponds only to the first term in Eq. 14, which is insufficient
910   for optimizing the codebook effectively.
