                                                 Abstract
010
011
                Video prediction is the process of learning necessary information from
012
                historical frames to predict future video frames. How to focus and eﬀi-
013             ciently learn features from historical frames is a critical step in this pro-
014             cess. For any sequence of video frames, the background changes little or
015             remains almost constant, while the foreground changes significantly and is
016             the main focus of our video prediction learning. However, current known
017             video prediction learning methods do not consider how to utilize the dif-
018             ferent characteristics of the foreground and background to further improve
019             prediction accuracy. To fully leverage the different characteristics of the
020             foreground and background and enhance prediction accuracy, we propose
021
                a Foreground-Background Separation Video Prediction (FBSVP) model
                in this paper. Through the foreground and background separation mod-
022
                ule, historical video frames are separated into foreground and background
023
                frames. In the video prediction module, the foreground and background
024             frames are predicted and learned separately. First, the features of historical
025             frames are fused into the current frame through a historical attention fusion
026             module using an attention mechanism. Then, the complementary tempo-
027             ral and spatial features are fused through a spatio-temporal fusion module.
028             Finally, the learned foreground and background features are fused in the
029             foreground and background fusion module to predict the final video frame.
030             Experimental results show that our proposed FBSVP model achieves the
031             best performance on popular video prediction datasets, demonstrating its
032
                significant competitiveness in this field.

      1     Introduction
035
036
      Video can be seen as a special type of temporal data that is well-suited for modeling using
037   Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The work by Ranzato et al. (2014) first utilized RNNs
038   to model the spatiotemporal dynamics of videos in an unsupervised manner, which inspired
039   a series of subsequent studies Finn et al. (2016); Srivastava et al. (2015); Oliu et al. (2018);
040   Zhang et al. (2019). However, RNN-based approaches primarily focus on capturing tem-
041   poral features of videos while overlooking spatial information. To address this limitation,
042   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) were introduced Shi et al. (2015) to complement
043   the RNNs, resulting in the widely adopted hybrid architecture of convolutional and recur-
044   rent layers in most video prediction models Shi et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2017; 2018b;
045
      2019); Guen & Thome (2020); Ballas et al. (2015). This hybrid architecture allows models
      to leverage the ability of convolutional units to model spatial relationships and the potential
046
      of recurrent units to capture temporal dependencies. Although popular in the literature,
047
      these classical video prediction architectures still have two main limitations. Firstly, in dense
048   prediction tasks like video prediction, models need to have a suﬀiciently large receptive field
049   to capture rich contextual information. Previous works attempted to enlarge the receptive
050   field of prediction units through 3D convolutions Wang et al. (2018a); Yu et al. (2020), but
051   the receptive field is primarily determined by the kernel size of the integrated convolutional
052   operators. Increasing the receptive field would significantly increase the model’s memory
053   consumption and computational cost. Secondly, existing video prediction models struggle
      to effectively fuse captured spatial and temporal features to enhance prediction accuracy.


                                                      1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Many current approaches simplify the training process by independently modeling these two
055   features Villegas et al. (2017); Denton et al. (2017), only performing simple fusion when gen-
056   erating predicted frames. In reality, spatial and temporal features are complementary, and
057   fully integrating both features during training is crucial to better understand the patterns
058   of video variations and improve the model’s perception ability.
059
      To address the above issues, we propose a video prediction model based on Foreground-
060
      Background Separation (FBSVP). Due to the differences in the characteristics of the video
061   frame foreground and background, we separate the foreground and background of the video
062   frames and then predict them separately. This allows for more effective video prediction
063   tailored to their respective characteristics and enables more focused and eﬀicient learning of
064   video frame motion patterns. It avoids the interference caused by different feature changes,
065   which can lead to a decrease in prediction performance. Since separate prediction for the
066   foreground and background reduces the complexity of the prediction, it helps to lower the
067   diﬀiculty of prediction, naturally improving the accuracy. Finally, the more accurately
068   predicted foreground and background features are effectively fused to produce the final
069
      predicted video frame. Experimental results show that the proposed FBSVP outperforms
      other state-of-the-art methods in major video prediction tasks.

072   2     Related Work
073
074   2.1   Video Prediction
075
076   The latest research progress in video prediction provides some useful insights into how to
077   predict future visual frames based on historical observations. In this section, we will discuss
078   recent advancements in video prediction methods. Ranzato et al. (2014) utilized recurrent
079
      neural networks (RNN) to model videos based on a language model. Srivastava et al. (2015)
      proposed FC-LSTM, an improved variant of RNN with long short-term memory (LSTM)
080
      that enhances the model’s ability to capture temporal dependencies in videos. Shi et al.
081
      (2015) introduced ConvLSTM, which replaces the fully connected layers in FC-LSTM with
082   convolutional layers to improve perception of visual data and save parameters. Similarly,
083   Ballas et al. (2015) employed convolutional layers with gated recurrent units (GRU) for video
084   prediction. However, Wang et al. (2017) argued that both temporal and spatial information
085   should be equally considered and proposed ConvLSTMs (ST-LSTM) with spatial modules
086   to model the spatial representation of each frame. They further introduced Casual LSTM
087   Wang et al. (2018a) to increase the temporal depth of the model and Gradient Highway Unit
088   to alleviate gradient propagation issues in deep prediction models. Guen & Thome (2020)
089   introduced PhyCell, which separates physical dynamics from unknown factors to predict
090
      more reliable motion. Additionally,Wu et al. (2021) proposed Motion-GRU to independently
      model transient changes and motion trends for more satisfactory predictions.
091
092   Despite the significant achievements of the aforementioned methods, the models still have
093   relatively narrow receptive fields, making it challenging to capture rich contextual informa-
094   tion and improve the perception ability of video features.
095
096   2.2   Foreground-Background Separation
097
098   Foreground-background separation methods have been designed and proposed in many com-
099
      puter vision tasks (Cristani et al., 2010; Garcia-Garcia et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023; Ding
      et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023). Shao et al. (2022) proposed a foreground-
100
      background separation (FBS) X-ray contraband detection framework, using an attention
101
      module to make the detection framework more focused on the foreground. The proposed
102   framework can separate contraband items as the foreground from other irrelevant items us-
103   ing only available bounding box labels and accurately identify contraband items in severely
104   occluded and overlapped X-ray images. This demonstrates that separating the foreground
105   and background and focusing more on the foreground can effectively improve model perfor-
106   mance. Zhang et al. (2022) proposed a foreground-background separation mutual generative
107   adversarial network (FSM-GAN) framework for video anomaly event detection, which can
      identify the spatio-temporal features of the foreground under background conditions and


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      achieve satisfactory results even on large-scale datasets. Yang et al. (2021) believe that
109   the foreground and background should be treated equally and proposed a collaborative
110   video object segmentation method through a multi-scale foreground-background integra-
111   tion (CFBI+) approach, improving the results of video object segmentation. This indicates
112   that the relationship between the foreground and background is inseparable and comple-
113   mentary.Besides the aforementioned papers, there are also other related excellent papers(An
114   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Moayeri et al., 2022).
115   Inspired by the excellent performance of foreground-background separation methods in
116   various applications, this paper proposes a video prediction model based on foreground-
117   background separation (FBSVP) to enhance video prediction performance.

      3.1   Foreground-background video frame extraction
156
157   Currently, the methods for extracting the foreground and background mainly come from the
158   open-source toolkit provided by OpenCV, which includes seven different algorithms: KNN
159   (Zivkovic & Van Der Heijden, 2006) (K-nearest neighbors) based on the K-nearest neighbors
160   algorithm, MOG (KaewTraKulPong & Bowden, 2002)/MOG2 (Zivkovic, 2004)(Mixture of
161   Gaussians) based on the mixture of Gaussians algorithm, CNT (Counting) based on pixel
      counting algorithm, GMG (Godbehere et al., 2012) based on pixel color feature algorithm,


                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      LSBP (Guo et al., 2016)(Local SVD Binary Pattern) based on local SVD binary pattern
163   algorithm, and GSOC (Google Summer of Code) algorithm similar to LSBP. Among these,
164   KNN and MOG2 have the best practical application results. This paper chooses MOG2
165   because this algorithm can more flexibly adjust parameters according to the scene to adapt
166   to different situations.

      3.3.3   Foreground-background Spatiotemporal Fusion Module
252   In the first half of the process, to reduce the mutual interference of features learned by the
253   model, the foreground and background features are trained separately, which helps to focus
254   more on learning the motion patterns of video frames and enhances the model’s prediction
255   capabilities. To predict the actual video frames, it is necessary to fuse the learned foreground
256   and background features. The foreground and background features are two important and
257   inseparable characteristics of a video frame, influencing and complementing each other. The
258   foreground features can indirectly reflect the characteristics of the background features, and
259   similarly, the background features can indirectly reflect the characteristics of the foreground
260
      features.
261   Therefore, for models that adopt separate training for the foreground and background, it
262   is crucial to thoroughly fuse the learned foreground and background features. Since we
263   have designed a model that learns three features simultaneously, it is important to consider
264   learning the fusion of these features while learning the foreground and background features.
265   This approach enables better prediction of the actual video frames.

330   3.4     Decoder
331
332   As shown on the right side of Figure 1, the decoder architecture corresponds to a mirrored
333   version of the convolutional encoder. It encodes the predicted original video frames, fore-
334   ground video frames, and background video frames separately. The features in the residual
335
      connections are fused with the decoded feature maps through channel concatenation. Due
      to the extensive feature fusion, the most recent spatial feature maps already incorporate
336
      temporal feature maps. To maintain consistency with the encoder, the decoder ignores
337
      predicted temporal feature maps that are absent in the encoder’s input. Ultimately, the de-
338   coder generates the next predicted video frame for the original, foreground, and background
339   video frames separately. These three predicted frames serve as the basis for preparing the
340   prediction of the next frame.

574   6     Conclusion
575
576   In this paper, we propose a video prediction model based on foreground-background separa-
577   tion (FBSVP). By training the foreground and background features separately, FBSVP can
578   effectively avoid the mutual interference that occurs during the joint learning of different
579
      features, which often leads to a decrease in prediction performance. This approach also al-
      lows the model to focus more on the relatively important foreground features, enabling it to
580
      better learn the motion characteristics of video frames. To fully learn and fuse the features
581
      of video frames, we designed three different fusion modules: the historical attention fusion
582   module, the spatio-temporal fusion module, and the foreground-background spatio-temporal
583   fusion module. The latter module re-fuses the previously separately trained foreground and
584   background features to predict the actual video frames. The proposed model was evaluated
585   on major video prediction tasks, and the experimental results demonstrate that our FBSVP
586   model achieves the best performance on popular video prediction datasets, showcasing its
587   significant competitiveness in the field.

801
802
      B.1   Moving MNIST
803
      The Moving MNIST dataset is a standard dataset for video prediction. Each sequence in the
804   dataset consists of 20 consecutive frames with a resolution of 64×64. Each sequence shows
805   how two random digits move at a constant speed and bounce within the 64x64 frames.
806   The handwritten digits are randomly sampled from the MNIST dataset (LeCun, 1998).
807   By assigning different initial positions and velocities to each digit, an infinite number of
808   sequences can be generated, allowing us to accurately evaluate the performance of each
809   model without worrying about data scarcity. In the default setting, the models are trained
      to predict the future 10 frames after observing the first 10 frames in the sequence. Although


                                                   15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      the movement in Moving MNIST may seem simple at first glance, generating consistent
811   future frames in long-term prediction tasks can be quite challenging, as the digits may
812   frequently bounce or occlude each other. We use a Moving MNIST generation script to
813   generate Moving MNIST sequences from the standard MNIST training set. The models are
814   tested on the oﬀicial Moving MNIST test set.
815
816   B.2   TraﬀicBJ
817
818   Traﬀic flow prediction is of great significance for traﬀic management and public safety, while
819   being highly challenging due to various complex factors. We consider traﬀic flow prediction
820   as a fundamental problem in spatio-temporal forecasting. Previous methods for traﬀic flow
821
      prediction have suffered from low prediction quality due to the complex dependencies on
      road networks and nonlinear dynamics.
822
823   Traﬀic flow data is collected from the chaotic real-world environment. They do not change
824   uniformly over time, and there is a strong temporal dependency between the traﬀic condi-
825   tions at adjacent timestamps. We use the TraﬀicBJ dataset (Zhang et al., 2017) to evaluate
826   the traﬀic prediction capability of our proposed model. TraﬀicBJ contains trajectory data
827
      of Beijing collected from taxi GPS, where each frame is a 32×32×2 image grid with two
      channels, namely inflow and outflow as defined in Zhang et al. (2017). Following previous
828
      works Wang et al. (2019); Guen & Thome (2020), we normalize the data to [0,1] using
829
      min-max normalization. The training model predicts the subsequent 4 frames by observing
830   the previous 4 frames.
831
832   B.3   KTH
833
834   The KTH Action Dataset (Schuldt et al., 2004) consists of six types of human actions
835   (walking, jogging, running, boxing, waving, and clapping), performed multiple times by 25
836   subjects in four different scenarios: outdoors, outdoors with scale variation, outdoors with
837   different clothing, and indoors. All video clips were recorded with a static camera at a frame
838   rate of 25fps on a homogeneous background, with an average duration of four seconds. To
839
      ensure comparability, we followed the experimental settings in Wang et al. (2017; 2018b);
      Villegas et al. (2017) by resizing the video frames to 128 × 128 pixels. The dataset was
840
      divided into a training set (persons 1-16) and a test set (persons 17-25), with all models
841
      trained on the training set using all six action categories. The models were trained to predict
842   the next 20 or 40 frames based on observations from the previous 10 frames. The challenge
843   of this human motion prediction task lies not only in its flexible prediction length but also
844   in the complex dynamics involving the randomness of human intention.
845
846   B.4   KITTI and Caltech Pedestrian
847
848   Generalization ability is one of the fundamental challenges in artificial intelligence technol-
849   ogy, particularly in unsupervised environments, which is a core research focus in machine
850   learning. To evaluate the generalization ability of the proposed FBSVP model, we assess
851
      its prediction results across different datasets through spatiotemporal forecasting learning.
      KITTI (Geiger et al., 2013) is one of the most popular datasets for mobile robotics and au-
852
      tonomous driving. It consists of several hours of traﬀic scenes recorded using high-resolution
853
      RGB images. Caltech Pedestrian (Dollár et al., 2009) is a driving dataset focused on pedes-
854   trian detection, containing approximately 10 hours of 640 × 480 30 FPS videos captured
855   from vehicles driving in urban environments. Following the experimental setup in Yu et al.
856   (2020); Lotter et al. (2016), the proposed model is trained on the KITTI dataset and tested
857   on the Caltech Pedestrian dataset. The frame rate of the Caltech Pedestrian dataset is
858   adjusted to match KITTI (10 FPS). All frames in both datasets are center-cropped and
859   resized to 128 × 160. Furthermore, the proposed model is trained to predict the next frame
860   based on the previous 10 frames as input. During testing, the prediction time horizon is
861   extended to 10 frames.
