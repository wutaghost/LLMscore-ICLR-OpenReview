                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011
012
                   Large language models (LLMs) acquire knowledge during pre-training, but over
                   time, this knowledge may become incorrect or outdated, necessitating updates af-
013
                   ter training. Knowledge editing techniques address this issue without the need
014
                   for costly re-training. However, most existing methods are designed for single
015                edits, and as the number of edits increases, they often cause a decline in the
016                model’s overall performance, posing significant challenges for sequential edit-
017                ing. To overcome this, we propose Orthogonal Subspace Editing, O-Edit. This
018                algorithm orthogonalizes the direction of each knowledge update, minimizing in-
019                terference between successive updates and reducing the impact of new updates on
020                unrelated knowledge. Our approach does not require replaying previously edited
021                data and processes each edit knowledge on time. It can perform thousands of edits
022                on mainstream LLMs, achieving an average performance improvement that is 4.2
023
                   times better than existing methods while effectively preserving the model’s per-
                   formance on downstream tasks, all with minimal additional parameter overhead.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) are trained on vast amounts of textual data, enabling them to store
030   extensive knowledge about various aspects of the human world, sparking the potential for general
031   artificial intelligence. However, LLMs face significant challenges, including the propagation of
032   inaccurate or outdated knowledge, as well as the generation of bias or harmful content (Cai et al.,
033   2024b; Chen et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024). Given the substantial computational costs of re-
034
      training LLMs to address these issues, there has been growing interest in model editing techniques
      (Yao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), which aim to update specific content within the model while
035
      minimizing computational costs. Existing model editing methods can be categorized into two main
036
      types: parameter-modifying methods that directly alter a small subset of model parameters (Dai
037   et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2023a;b; Hu et al., 2024a;b; Gupta et al., 2024a), and parameter-preserving
038   methods that without changing the model parameters (Wang et al., 2024b; Cai et al., 2024a; Zheng
039   et al., 2023). In this paper, we focus on parameter-modifying editing methods.
040
      Most existing research focuses on editing models a single time (Han et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
041
      2024b;a; Mazzia et al., 2024). However, as real-world knowledge continuously evolves, models will
042
      need to be updated repeatedly to remain accurate. This shift has led to the concept of sequential
043   model editing (Ma et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2023), which involves performing
044   multiple knowledge edits to progressively update the model as new knowledge needs to be incor-
045   porated. Currently, sequential editing is often achieved through multiple iterations of single edits.
046   Recent studies have shown that as the number of edits increases, the success rate of edits significantly
047   declines and impairs the model’s general capabilities, such as reasoning and contextual understand-
048   ing, thereby limiting the scalability of model editing (Gu et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024a;b). This
049   challenge is akin to adding new floors to an existing building—each addition risks compromising
050   the overall stability. While some research has analyzed the bottlenecks of sequential editing from a
051
      theoretical perspective (Ma et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024a), there is still no effective solution has yet
      been developed to address this issue through direct modifications of the model weights.1 .

      To address the scalability issue of sequential editing, this paper introduces Orthogonal Subspace
055   Editing (O-Edit), a simple yet effective method for sequentially editing language models. Our key
056   insight is based on the observation that existing editing methods primarily perform updates within
057   specific low-rank subspaces. Based on this premise, we assume that both the update directions from
058   previous editing tasks and the directions of updates to the model’s implicit knowledge can be cap-
059   tured. Therefore, for the current editing knowledge, the direction of parameter updates should be
060   chosen to minimize the impact on these prior update directions. O-Edit accomplishes this by pro-
061   jecting the update direction of the current knowledge into an orthogonal subspace, ensuring that the
062   neural network’s output for previous knowledge remains unchanged while the projected direction
063
      remains effective for the current edit. To enhance O-Edit, we introduce O-Edit+, a post-processing
      method designed to ensure complete orthogonality between subspaces. We validate the effective-
064
      ness of our methods by utilizing two knowledge editing datasets and four downstream task datasets.
065
      Furthermore, our analysis, conducted from both experimental and theoretical perspectives, clearly
066   demonstrates that strong orthogonality between each update matrix is crucial for enabling sequential
067   editing. Figure 1 illustrates how our methods adjust the update direction for each piece of knowl-
068   edge.

      3     O-EDIT: SEQUENTIAL EDITING WITH GRADIENT PROJECTION
136         MEMORY
137
138   In this section, we will introduce O-Edit, as illustrated in Figure 2. We discuss two key-value
139   memory-based knowledge editing methods, ROME and MEMIT in Appendix B.3 and B.4, followed
140
      by our optimization method in section 3.1, which incrementally edits new knowledge in orthogonal
      subspaces, while preserving previously edited knowledge.
141
142   3.1     T OWARDS AN ORTHOGONAL EDITING METHOD
143
144   Previous methods share a common feature: all new knowledge is updated within a shared space,
145   which directly affects the weights of the model. If an update for new knowledge is applied with-
146
      out considering prior knowledge, the direction of this update can affect both the previously edited
      knowledge and the implicit knowledge within the model, potentially leading to catastrophic forget-
147
      ting (Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023c). Therefore, to effectively support sequential editing, the
148
      process of updating new knowledge should adhere to the following criteria:
149
150   Criterion 3.1: The update direction for each piece of knowledge should be orthogonal to the direc-
151   tions of previously edited knowledge, ensuring minimal interference with previously edited knowl-
152
      edge.
153   Criterion 3.2: The update direction for each piece of knowledge should be orthogonal to the implicit
154   knowledge directions within the original model, ensuring minimal interference with the model’s
155   existing implicit knowledge.
156   In the following sections 3.1.1 and 3.1.2, we will detail how we optimized ROME and MEMIT to
157   fulfill the two criteria mentioned above within the context of sequential editing.
158
159   3.1.1    T HE KNOWLEDGE TO BE EDITED SHOULD BE MUTUALLY ORTHOGONAL
160
161   Editing the First Piece of Knowledge: To comply with criterion 3.1, we implement the follow-
      ing steps in a sequential editing process. We commence by editing the first piece of knowledge

      using the pair (x1 , y1 ). Upon completion of this initial edit, we obtain an updated set of parameters
163   ∆W[total] = ∆W[1] . To preserve this edited knowledge, we constrain the gradient update direc-
164   tions for subsequent edits. It is important to note that during the editing process with methods such
165   as ROME and MEMIT, parameter adjustments are made without gradient computation, as the cal-
166   culation of v∗ necessitates training, while the adjustment of Wproj occurs in a single step. Since
167   ROME and MEMIT do not involve computing the gradient direction of the required update matrix,
168   we draw on the insights from (Wang et al., 2023b) and utilize ∆W[total] to approximate the direction
169   of model parameter updates. They argue that the gradient space from prior training tasks can be
170   effectively captured by the update matrix. Next, we perform Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)
171
      on ∆W[total] = U ΣV T and extract the sub-matrix ∆Wr corresponding to the top r singular values,
      defined as the Core Gradient Space (CGS) by (Saha et al., 2021). Updates along the CGS direc-
172
      tion induce maximum changes in knowledge (Farajtabar et al., 2019), whereas updates in directions
173
      orthogonal to the CGS minimize interference with previously edited knowledge2 .

242
243   In Section 3, we introduced O-Edit, an algorithm for approximate orthogonal sequential knowledge
244   editing. To further enhance the orthogonality between different pieces of knowledge, we propose O-
245
      Edit+, a post-processing method that eliminates the need for cosine similarity calculations. Specif-
      ically, for the second piece of knowledge, we compute v∗ using Eqn.17 and apply Eqn.18 to obtain
246
      the update parameter ∆W[2] . Subsequently, ∆W[2] undergoes post-orthogonal processing, achieved
247
      as follows:
248
249                         ∆W[2] = ∆W[2] − ∆Wr (∆WrT ∆Wr )−1 ∆WrT ∆W[2] ,
250                          ∇Gq = ∇Gq − ∆Wr (∆WrT ∆Wr )−1 ∆WrT ∇Gq ,                                 (10)
251
                            ∆W[2] = ∆W[2] − ∇Gq (∇GTq ∇Gq )−1 ∇GTq ∆W[2] .
252
253   The processed ∆W[2] from Eqn.10 is then used as the update direction for the second piece of
254   knowledge. Similar to O-Edit, we subsequently update the hyperparameters r, q, and ∆W[total] for
255   the next knowledge edit. We detail the computation process of Eqn.10 and the pseudo-code for
256   O-Edit and O-Edit+ in Appendix B.5. Readers can refer to Appendices B.8 and B.9 for details on

451   5.3   F URTHER A NALYSIS
452
453   How do edits disturb model outputs? We aim to study how each added piece of editing information
454   affects the subsequent outputs of the model. Theoretically, if an editing method is effective, the
455   output distribution of unrelated knowledge in the model should remain as consistent as possible with
456   the pre-edit state when using this method. If the editing information is integrated into the subject’s
457   editing layer through newly created (k∗ , v∗ ) pairs, the information from v∗ will influence the hidden
458   states of subsequent Relation Tokens (“The SpaceX is located in”) via the attention module and
459
      gradually propagate through decoding to impact the final model output. To investigate how this
      newly added information affects the hidden states of relation tokens, we conducted the following
460
      two sets of experiments:
461
462   We preserved the update matrix ∆Wi for each i-th edit from 1500 edits. Subsequently, we first
463   measured the impact of adding a single ∆Wi on the final-layer hidden states of relationPtokens for
                                                                                                   n
464   each edited piece of knowledge i. Then, we measured the impact of adding ∆W[total] = i=1 ∆Wi
465
      on the final-layer hidden states of relation tokens. The results were dimensionally reduced using
      t-SNE, as shown in Figure 7 (a). It can be observed that the distribution difference between single
466
      and multiple edits in MEMIT is significant, indicating that multiple edits affect the model’s final
467
      outputs. In contrast, the distributions for O-Edit+ show almost no difference, suggesting that the
468   results of multiple edits do not affect the model’s output distribution for each edited knowledge.
469
470   We also examined the distribution of relation tokens in the original model compared to the dis-
471
      tribution after adding ∆Wunrelated = ∆W[total] − ∆Wj . Theoretically, ∆Wunrelated should carry no
      meaningful information for the edited knowledge j, and we expect the distribution after adding
472
      ∆Wunrelated to remain consistent with Woriginal . The experimental results are shown in Figure 7 (b).
473
      It can be seen that using O-Edit+ with ∆Wunrelated has almost no effect on the edited knowledge j,
474   while MEMIT causes a shift in the distribution.
475
476   How do edits disturb each other? To investigate the extent of interdependencies among knowl-
477
      edge updates during the sequential editing process, we preserved the update matrix ∆Wi for each
      i-th edit. Upon completion
                              Pn of the sequential editing, the model’s cumulative update matrix is com-
478
      puted as ∆W[total] = i=1 ∆Wi . For the j-th edit, we compute ∆Wunrelated = ∆W[total] − ∆Wj ,
479
      which excludes the update matrix ∆Wj corresponding to kj . According to Hu et al. (2024a), under
480   ideal sequential editing, the knowledge vector kj used during the j-th edit should not activate any
481   unrelated ∆W̸=j (i.e., any update matrix other than ∆Wj ), meaning ∥∆Wunrelated · kj ∥2 = 0. We

484   reaching approximately 2.5 and others exceeding 10. This indicates that in the original method, any
485   unrelated ∆W̸=j (i.e., any update matrix other than ∆Wj ) could significantly activate kj , leading
      to a substantial deviation from the ideal state v∗ and resulting in the failure of MEMIT in sequential

      6       L IMITATIONS
521   While O-Edit and O-Edit+ demonstrate robust sequential editing performance, several limitations
522   persist. Due to computational constraints, we restricted our experiments to Mistral-7B and Llama3-
523   8B, leaving the scalability of our methods on larger models untested. Additionally, constructing or-
524   thogonality between edits adds computational overhead, which may prolong editing times. However,
525   O-Edit and O-Edit+ require maintaining only two additional matrices, making them both model-
526   agnostic and compatible with other sequential editing techniques. Furthermore, we did not evaluate
527   O-Edit and O-Edit+ against other editing methods, such as fine-tuning (FT), as these approaches
528
      tend to falter after only a few sequential edits, whereas ROME and MEMIT can support more exten-
      sive editing sequences. Despite these challenges, we believe our methods hold promising potential,
529
      particularly in the early stages of research on sequential model editing.

532   7       C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we present two innovative methods—O-Edit and O-Edit+ that leverage orthogonal sub-
534   space editing for sequential knowledge editing in language models. These methods effectively mit-
535   igate catastrophic forgetting of both edited and existing knowledge by incrementally applying edits
536   in orthogonal subspaces. Our methods distinguish themselves through their attention to data privacy,
537   efficient parameter utilization, and strong generalization capabilities for downstream tasks. Com-
538   prehensive empirical evaluations indicate that O-Edit and O-Edit+ significantly outperform existing
539   methods, establishing them as promising avenues for future advancements in sequential knowledge
      editing.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
703
704   A.1    K NOWLEDGE E DITING
705
706   From the perspective of whether model parameters are modified, (Yao et al., 2023) categorized
707   knowledge editing methods into two major classes: preserving the model’s parameters and modify-
708   ing the model’s parameters. This paper primarily focuses on the latter. On one hand, meta-learning
709   has been used to predict parameter updates for networks, typically employing a hypernetwork to
710   edit language models. (Cao et al., 2021) used a bidirectional LSTM to predict weight updates for
711
      editing, while (Mitchell et al., 2022a) utilized low-rank decomposition of gradients to fine-tune lan-
      guage models, known as MEND, and (Tan et al., 2024) extended single-step edits to batch edits
712
      using a least squares method based on MEND. On the other hand, (Meng et al., 2023a; Dai et al.,
713
      2022) employed a causal probe to localize knowledge within the intermediate layers of the model,
714   demonstrating that editing in the MLP of the middle layers yields the best results. (Dai et al., 2022)
715   performed knowledge editing by modifying the activation values of specific neurons. (Meng et al.,
716   2023a) used a constrained least squares method to precisely solve for the parameter updates required
717   for editing and extended this approach to batch editing (Meng et al., 2023b).
718
719   A.2   S EQUENTIAL E DITING
720
721   Some studies have extended knowledge editing methods to sequential editing. From the perspective
722   of modifying model parameters, (Ma et al., 2024) theoretically analyzed that the bottleneck limiting
723
      sequential editing in models lies in the condition number of matrices, and they attempted to support
      sequential editing by controlling the growth of the matrix condition number. (Hu et al., 2024b) at-
724
      tributed the decline in performance during sequential editing to pattern mismatch, where different
725
      layers detect different patterns, making a single layer incapable of accommodating all the edited
726   knowledge. Thus, they selected the optimal layer from multiple layers for editing. Additionally,
727   (Hu et al., 2024a) explored the root causes of failures in sequential editing, deriving a closed-form
728   solution from linear associative memory. They posited that lossless sequential editing can only be
729   achieved when the edited knowledge is completely orthogonal. From the perspective of adding ad-
730   ditional parameters while freezing model parameters, SERAC (Mitchell et al., 2022b) stores edits
731   in memory. When an input is received, a classifier checks whether it corresponds to any cached
732   edits. If a match is found, a counterfactual model uses the input and relevant edits to predict outputs.
733   GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2023) uses semantic similarity in the model’s latent space by adding an
734
      offline key-value adapter at the selected layers, applying edits only to inputs that are similar to the
      keys cached in the encoding. WISE (Wang et al., 2024b) uses a dual-parameter storage scheme,
735
      where the main memory is used for pre-trained knowledge and the side memory is designated for
736
      edited knowledge. By incorporating a knowledge sharding mechanism, it allows for editing knowl-
737   edge in different parameter subspaces and merges them into the shared side memory without causing
738   conflicts. In this paper, we consider the scenario of directly updating model parameters.
739
740   A.3   C ONTINUAL LEARNING
741
742   The orthogonal concept presented in this paper is inspired by continual learning. Existing continual
743   learning methods typically update all tasks within a shared vector space (Ke & Liu, 2023), which
744   directly affects the model’s hidden layer outputs (Wang et al., 2024a). Some studies (Farajtabar
745   et al., 2019; Saha et al., 2021) have proposed a promising approach to address this issue by perform-
746   ing gradient descent optimization in directions orthogonal to the gradient subspaces of past tasks,
747
      effectively mitigating catastrophic forgetting. GPM (Saha et al., 2021) divides the gradient space
      into two key areas: the “Core Gradient Space” (CGS) and the “Residual Gradient Space” (RGS). By
748
      learning in the orthogonal directions of the CGS related to previous task inputs, it ensures minimal
749
      interference with past tasks. Based on GPM, TRGP (Lin et al., 2022) introduces a “trust region”
750   concept to select old tasks relevant to new ones, reusing their frozen weights through scaled weight
751   projections. By optimizing the scaling matrix and updating the model along orthogonal directions
752   to the old tasks’ subspace, TRGP effectively facilitates knowledge transfer without forgetting. O-
753   LoRA (Wang et al., 2023b) suggests that parameter information updated through low rank can be
754   approximately equivalent to gradient information, which expands the application scenarios of con-
755   tinual learning and enables effective learning even in scenarios where gradient information cannot
      be obtained.

779   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) decomposes any matrix W ∈ Rm×n into three matrices:
780   W = U ΣV T , where U ∈ Rm×m and V ∈ Rn×n are orthogonal matrices, and Σ is a diagonal ma-
781   trix containing the singular values σi of W , ordered in descending magnitude. SVD is instrumental
782   in solving the rank-r approximation problem, where the goal is to find a matrix W̃ that minimizes
783   ∥W̃ − W ∥2 subject to rank(W̃ ) ≤ r. According to the Eckart–Young–Mirsky theorem (Eckart &
                                                                                Pr
784   Young, 1936), the optimal rank-r approximation W̃ is given by W̃ = i=1 σi ui viT , obtained by
785   truncating the SVD of W to retain the top r singular values and their corresponding singular vectors,
786   where r ≤ min{m, n}.

1053   B.7    BASELINE E DITING M ETHODS
1054
       We selected five popular model editing methods as baselines:
1055
1056          • Fine-Tuning (FT), we employ the reimplementation guidelines from Yao et al. (2023).
1057            This involves utilizing the Adam optimizer and implementing early stopping to minimize
1058            − log PLM [∗|p], while only adjusting Wproj .
1059          • Elastic Weight Consolidation (FT-EWC) has been shown to effectively mitigate catas-
1060            trophic forgetting by updating weights based on a Fisher information matrix, which is de-
1061            rived from past edits and scaled by a factor λ. In line with Wang et al. (2024b), we have
1062            chosen to omit the constraints of the L∞ norm in this implementation.
1063          • MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a) adeptly manipulates the gradient of fine-tuned language
1064            models by capitalizing on a low-rank decomposition of the gradients, thereby enhancing
1065            the accuracy of the editing process. We use the default settings from Yao et al. (2023).
1066          • ROME (Meng et al., 2023a) has been previously discussed. In this experiment, we edit the
1067            8th layer, which is regarded as a crucial location for knowledge storage. We utilize second
1068            moment statistics C ∝ E[kk T ] computed from more than 100,000 samples of hidden states
1069            k derived from tokens sampled across all Wikipedia text in context.
1070          • MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023b)—the detailed computation process can be found in Appendix
1071            B.4. We set λ = 15, 000 to balance the knowledge in the model with the knowledge
1072            required for editing. Other settings are consistent with those in ROME.
1073          • R-Edit (Gupta et al., 2024a) attributes the suboptimal performance of ROME and MEMIT
1074            to the inadequacy of the calculated k∗ in representing the subject of the queried knowledge.
1075            R-Edit enhances the calculation of k∗ in Eqs. 15 and 18 to address this issue.
1076          • WilKE (Hu et al., 2024b) argues that different types of knowledge should be distributed
1077            across various layers. For each piece of knowledge edited, WilKE first determines the
1078            optimal layer for editing and then applies either ROME or MEMIT to perform the edit.
1079            Due to the time and computational cost of finding the optimal layer, we restrict the editable
                layers in this paper to l = {5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10}.

1155
1156   First, we wanted to see if both ∆W[total] and ∇G contributed effectively. We set up three baselines:
1157   ➊ using only ∆W[total] ; ➋ using only ∇G; and ➌ using both ∆W[total] and ∇G without orthogonal
1158   processing for ∇G according to Eq.7(26). The results are shown in Table 3. We observed that while
1159   using either ∆W[total] or ∇G alone yielded better results than the original method, their performance
1160   was still inferior to using both together. The lack of orthogonalization for ∇G led to knowledge
1161   conflicts within the model, resulting in inferior performance compared to O-Edit+.
1162   How does the degree of orthogonality between knowledge affect the effectiveness of sequential
1163   editing?
1164
       We compared the effects of different hyperparameter selections on editing performance between
1165
       O-Edit and O-Edit+, as shown in Tables 5 and 6. In O-Edit+, two noteworthy phenomena were
1166
       observed. First, MEMIT’s λ3 is smaller than that of ROME due to ROME’s stronger constraints,
1167   which can degrade the performance of unrelated knowledge (Loc.) during sequential editing. Con-
1168   sequently, we opted for a larger λ3 = 2.5 to mitigate ROME’s influence. Second, while a smaller
1169   λ3 improves performance with MEMIT, it still negatively impacts unrelated knowledge, and a larger
1170   λ3 affects the editing effect (Rel., Gen.). Therefore, selecting an appropriately sized λ3 is crucial
1171   for optimal overall editing performance.
1172
       In the O-Edit setting, we compared the editing performance under four different settings. The results
1173   showed that stronger constraints led to better outcomes, as λ1 and λ2 effectively controlled the
1174   correlation between different edits. Larger λ1 values resulted in smaller correlations between edits,
1175   while larger λ2 values reduced the correlation between edited and implicit knowledge within the
1176   model.

1195            for testing modification edits, the authors (Zhang et al., 2024a) collect triplets about popular
1196            entities, where the subject corresponds to one of the top-viewed pages in Wikipedia. They
1197            also collect a dataset by randomly sampling entities from Wikidata, which we use as the

1203            the implications of an edit for real-world applications.

1207            evaluate the post-edited model’s performance on other descriptions of the subject.
1208          • Compositionality and Reasoning (Res.): This requires the post-edit model to conduct
1209            reasoning with the changed facts. For example, when we change the current president of
1210            the U.S. from Donald Trump to Joe Biden, the answer to the question “Who is the First
1211            Lady of the United States?” should also be changed.

1215            modified fact and expected to change by the edit; they were indeed modified. For exam-

1332
1333
1334   Can any method of reducing ∆W[total] improve the ability of sequential editing?
1335
       Hu et al. (2024b) posits that ∥∆W[total] ∥2 is a key determinant of sequential editing, referred to as
1336
       “toxicity”. A higher ∥∆W[total] ∥2 imposes greater constraints on sequential editing performance.
1337
       O-Edit+ effectively reduces ∥∆W[total] ∥2 by diminishing projections in specific subspaces. Conse-
1338   quently, a plausible hypothesis is that any method capable of reducing ∥∆W[total] ∥2 could potentially
1339   enhance sequential editing performance. To evaluate this hypothesis, we compare O-Edit+ with four
1340   methods on COUNTERFACT: ➊ reducing the number of training steps to decrease ∥v∗ − W k∗ ∥2 ,
1341   thereby reducing ∥∆W[total] ∥2 with each edit; ➋ randomly deleting some values in the update pa-
1342   rameters, setting them to zero; ➌ randomly selecting a set of orthogonal subspaces and removing
1343   the projection of ∆Wi onto them; ➍ multiplying the ∆W obtained by the original method by a co-
1344   efficient η that is less than 1, updating the matrix as ∆W = η · ∆W . We adjust the hyperparameters
1345   to ensure that the ∥∆W[total] ∥2 generated by these methods approximates that of O-Edit+. As shown
1346
       in Table 8, although these five methods yield a similar ∥∆W[total] ∥2 , the first four fail to achieve
       effective sequential editing. This indicates that while reducing ∥∆W[total] ∥2 is a necessary but not
1347
       sufficient condition for successful sequential editing, choosing the correct projection space to ensure
1348
       minimal impact between knowledge is the key to the success of ours.
