009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011                Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has emerged as a promising solution for
012                mitigating hallucinations of large language models (LLMs) with retrieved external
013                knowledge. Adaptive RAG enhances this approach by enabling dynamic retrieval
014                during generation, activating retrieval only when the query exceeds LLM’s internal
015                knowledge. Existing methods primarily focus on detecting LLM’s confidence via
016                statistical uncertainty. Instead, we present the first attempts to solve adaptive RAG
017                from a representation perspective and develop an inherent control-based framework,
018
                   termed C TRL A. Specifically, we extract the features that represent the honesty and
                   confidence directions of LLM and adopt them to control LLM behavior and guide
019
                   retrieval timing decisions. We also design a simple yet effective query formulation
020
                   strategy to support adaptive retrieval. Experiments show that C TRL A is superior
021                to existing adaptive RAG methods on a diverse set of tasks. Honesty steering can
022                effectively make LLMs more honest and confidence monitoring is a promising
023                indicator of retrieval trigger. Our anonymous codes are submitted with the paper
024                and will be publicly available.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG; Guu et al. 2020; Izacard et al. 2023) has proven effective in
030   mitigating hallucination by integrating external knowledge into LLMs. Early efforts often employ
031   single-round, indiscriminate retrieval, resulting in over-reliance on external knowledge and incomplete
032   retrieval (Wang et al., 2023; Su et al., 2024a). To solve the issues, adaptive RAG (ARAG; Jiang
033   et al. 2023b; Wang et al. 2024a) has emerged, which enables dynamic retrieval during generation,
034
      activating retrieval only when the query exceeds LLM’s internal knowledge (Ni et al., 2024).
035   The key challenges in ARAG involve determining what and when to retrieve (Su et al., 2024b; Yao
036   et al., 2024). The design of what aspect typically depends on the construction of when aspect, making
037   ARAG’s primary focus the issues related to when aspect. For the when aspect, recent ARAGs leverage
038   the ability that LLMs are aware of their uncertainty (Kuhn et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Xiong et al.,
039   2024), utilizing this characteristic to determine retrieval timing by assessing confidence level of their
040
      knowledge (Su et al., 2024b; Yao et al., 2024). They primarily focus on detecting uncertainty in the
      LLM’s outputs to signal retrieval, relying on factors such as output probabilities (Jiang et al., 2023b),
041
      entropy of output (Su et al., 2024b) or internal states (Yao et al., 2024), or verbal feedback (Wang et al.,
042
      2024b; Yan et al., 2024). From a statistical standpoint, uncertainty and confidence are conceptually
043   equivalent, both reflect the degree of certainty in a model’s predictions (Yang et al., 2023; Band
044   et al., 2024; Tao et al., 2024). Thus, uncertainty can act as a proxy for confidence when determining
045   retrieval timing.
046
      We revisit the assumptions underlying these uncertainty-based methods. First, they presume that
047
      LLM’s output aligns with its internal knowledge (Lin et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2023), that is, LLM
048
      can accurately reflect its internal knowledge in outputs, i.e., they are honest. However, LLMs often
049   navigate a trade-off between honesty and helpfulness, balancing between discerning its limitations
050   and generating user-satisfied plausible content (Liu et al., 2024a). When the output diverges from
051   internal knowledge, indicating low honesty, they only detect intended output rather than internal
052   knowledge. Second, they equate uncertainty with LLM’s confidence, 1 which may be not always
053

054
      applicable to LLM behavior. For instance, an LLM may frequently respond with “I don’t know”
055   or “insufficient information,” suggesting low uncertainty, yet retrieval should still occur. Moreover,
056   semantically equivalent answers can be expressed in various ways in free-form generation, which may
057   lead to high uncertainty (Farquhar et al., 2024). However, retrieval is unnecessary in this scenario.
058
      Based on this analysis, we emphasize both honesty and confidence of LLMs are crucial for accurate
059
      retrieval timing. However, current ARAGs struggle to address them due to the limitations of statistical
060
      uncertainty. We propose to solve ARAG from a representation perspective (Olah, 2023; Bricken et al.,
061   2023; Zou et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024), developing an efficient and unified framework that
062   seamlessly tackles the requirements of honesty and confidence. Our core idea involves extracting
063   features corresponding to honesty and confidence directions from LLMs and using them to control
064   LLM behavior and guide retrieval timing decisions simultaneously.
065
      We devise an Inherent Control-based Adaptive RAG framework (C TRL A). To steer LLM toward
066
      honesty and monitor its confidence, we extract features aligned with the directions of honesty and
067   confidence within LLM’s representation space. By adjusting the honesty direction—a process we refer
068   to as honesty steering—we can shift the LLM’s representation space to promote more honesty outputs.
069   Simultaneously, confidence is quantified by measuring the projection of current representation onto
070   the confidence feature, a method we call confidence monitoring. Honesty steering helps LLM
071   recognize its limitations and suppress the generation of fabricated plausible information. Confidence
072   monitoring, in turn, enhances the precision of retrieval timing. We also implement a simple yet
073   effective query formulation module to support adaptive retrieval, minimizing the impact of noise and
074   intent drift. Extensive experiments verify the effectiveness of C TRL A, revealing that adjusting the
075
      directions of LLM’s internal states enhances its honesty, while confidence monitoring reliably signals
      when to trigger retrieval, optimizing the balance between retrieval and internal knowledge use.

078   2     R ELATED W ORK

080   2.1   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
081
082   Early RAG efforts (Lewis et al., 2020; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; Komeili et al.,
083   2022; Khattab et al., 2023) relied on single-round, indiscriminate retrieval, increasing computational
084
      costs and degrading model performance (Wang et al., 2023; Su et al., 2024a). To address these
      issues, ARAG emerged, enabling dynamic retrieval during generation when the query exceeds LLM’s
085
      internal knowledge (Jiang et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024a; Ni et al., 2024). Previous implementations
086
      utilized static rules, such as prior sentences (Trivedi et al., 2023), sliding windows (Borgeaud et al.,
087   2022; Ram et al., 2023), and in-context learning (Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
088   2024). Recent ARAGs leverage LLMs’ self-awareness of uncertainty to optimize retrieval timing by
089   assessing confidence levels through internal states (Yao et al., 2024), likelihoods (Jiang et al., 2023b;
090   Wang et al., 2024a; Su et al., 2024b), or verbal feedback (Wang et al., 2024b; Ding et al., 2024;
091   Yan et al., 2024). This enhances retrieval timing and balances external and internal knowledge use.
092   However, uncertainty-based ARAGs face challenges with LLM honesty and confidence, crucial for
093   accurate retrieval timing. C TRL A addresses these issues from a representation perspective, enhancing
094   control over honesty and confidence to improve retrieval-augmented generation effectiveness.
095
096   2.2   L INEAR R EPRESENTATIONS IN LLM S
097
098   Recent research has explored LLM representations to understand their beliefs, interpretability, and
099
      compliance (Levinstein & Herrmann, 2023; Li et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). Grounded in the
      linear representation and superposition hypotheses, these studies suggest that specific features can
100
      be aligned with particular directions in the LLMs’ linear space. This framework effectively guides
101
      and monitors model outputs (Olah, 2023). Researchers have modified or detected models’ demeanor,
102   preferences, stated goals, and biases, as well as induced errors or mitigated risks (Templeton et al.,
103   2024). Supporting the hypotheses, Marks & Tegmark (2023) and Slobodkin et al. (2023) found that
104   features like truthfulness and answerability are linearly separable within the latent space. Further
105   efforts (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) utilized contrastive instruction templates to clarify feature
106   directions. C TRL A leverages these insights to extract features related to honesty and confidence,
107   aiming to control LLM behavior and guide retrieval timing decisions, bridging representational
      understanding and practical applications.

      3.1       P RELIMINARY
132
133   Given a query q, RAG aims to assist LLMs in generating more precise answers y = [s1 , . . . , sm ] =
134   [w1 , . . . , wn ] containing m sentences or n tokens by retrieving relevant documents Dq = R(q)
135                                         |D|
      from document corpus D = {di }i=1 or web via retriever R. The retrieved documents Dq are
136   usually concatenated with input x, i.e., query q with task instruction I, to aid answer generation as
137   y = LLM([Dq ; x]), where [·; ·] denotes concatenation. In contrast, adaptive RAG (Jiang et al., 2023b)
138   performs active retrieval necessity decision via a trigger mechanism T (x, y<t ), where y<t is the prior
139   generations as of step t(t ≥ 1). If T is triggered, the query formulation function qt = fq (x, y<t )
140   will produce a query qt to search. If T is triggered at t = 1, i.e., y<1 = ∅, q will be original query.
141   Given the retrieved documents Dqt , the model continues generating next output segment (usually, a
142   sentence) yt = LLM([Dqt ; x; y<t ]) till the answer comes to its end or next retrieval trigger occurs.

      3.2.2    H ONESTY S TEERING
180   According to the superposition hypothesis, adjusting LLM by moving each token’s representation
181   closer to the direction representing the honesty feature during decoding, is a direct way to enhance its
182   honesty (Olah, 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024). To achieve this, we employ a simple
183   linear combination. After extracting the honesty feature, it can be directly used to steer the behavior
184   of the LLM. Assuming the LLM contains L layers, each layer has its corresponding feature. Let
185   vh = {vhl }L                                              l L
                  l=1 denote the honesty feature and Rk = {rk }l=1 represent the token representations for
186   the k-th token at each layer. We then apply a linear combination function for honesty steering:
187
                               R̂k = Rk + λ · vh = {rkl + λ · vhl | ∀ l ∈ [1, . . . , L]},                          (1)
188
189   where the coefficient λ controls the strength of honesty steering. Because vh represents the direction
190   that promotes honesty, the “+” operator is used in Eq 1. Conversely, to reduce honesty, the “−”
191   operator can be employed. As illustrated in Figure 1, honesty steering is applied layer-by-layer and
192
      token-by-token during generation. This method is both simple and effective, with minimal impact on
      inference costs. For brevity, we denote honesty steering as ŷt = Ph (yt ) in the following descriptions.

      3.2.3    C ONFIDENCE M ONITORING AS R ETRIEVAL T RIGGER
195
196   According to the linear representation hypothesis, an intuitive way to monitor the LLM’s confidence
197   during generation is to evaluate how well token representations align with the confidence feature
198   direction in the representation space (Bricken et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024).
199   Given the extracted confidence feature, we utilize it to monitor LLM’s confidence during generation.
200   Let Rk = {rkl }L                                                                         l L
                       l=1 represent the k-th token’s representation at each layer, and vc = {vc }l=1 denote
201   the confidence feature. Specifically, we compute the confidence score for k-th token using the dot
202
      product, followed by mean-pooling across layers and a scaling operation for normalization and outlier
      removal. This produces the confidence score for the k-th token as follows:
203
204                     m̃k = meanpool([mk,1 , . . . , mk,L ]) = meanpool [rkl,⊤ · vcl ]L

                                                                                        l=1 ,
205                                                                                                       (2)
                        m̄k = scale([m̃0 , . . . , m̃k ])[−1] − τ,
206
207   where τ is the threshold to adjust the sensitivity of confidence monitoring, m̃<k represents the
208   mean-pooled score of preceding tokens, and the index −1 refers to the score of the last token, i.e.,
209   k-th token. If m̄k > 0, it suggests that the k-th token’s representational direction leans towards the
210   confidence, indicating that LLM is confident in generating this token. Conversely, if m̄k < 0, LLM
211   is unconfident in generating the k-th token. Here we denote confidence monitoring as Pc .
212   The goal of confidence monitoring is to serve as a reliable detector for accurately determining
213   appropriate retrieval timing (Wu et al., 2024a; Chuang et al., 2024a). For the t-th output segment
214   ŷt = [wts , . . . , wte ] of the LLM, with confidence scores [m̄ts , . . . , m̄te ] for each token, the retrieval
215   necessity is measured by the confidence scores of specific tokens within ŷt . We only consider
      the confidence scores of new information in ŷt′ , i.e., content that has not appeared in the previous

      Due to the honesty steering, LLM will generate refusal outputs more frequently, since honesty
      steering can effectively regulate LLM behavior to make it more honest, leading to more frequent
221
      generation of non-responsive or refusal outputs. These refusal responses are well-aligned with the
222
      LLM’s internal beliefs, i.e., LLM is confident in its knowledge limitations, making them challenging

224   module, which employs a pattern matching function, as a supplement to confidence monitoring, to
225   identify refusal content. The detailed algorithm is presented in Appendix B.3.1.

229   Once retrieval is triggered, we need to employ a search query to retrieve relevant documents that aid
230   in LLM generation. The construction of effective search queries plays a pivotal role in enhancing
231

234

235

236

238   in q and previous generations y<t , i.e., new information and (ii) unconfident tokens, as:

      3.3   I NFERENCE P ROCESS
271
272   For an input x and preceding generation Y<t , the model generates the output segment along with
273   honesty steer Ph and derives ŷt . Simultaneously, the confidence monitor Pc is activated to compute
274   the confidence score of each token during generation. We collect the confidence scores of new
275   information ŷt′ to determine retrieval necessity via retrieval trigger T . If retrieval is not required, the
276   model continues predicting the next output segment. Otherwise, we adopt query formulation, fq , to
277   produce a search query qt and retrieve documents Dq via retriever R. The retrieved documents Dq ,
278
      input x, and preceding generation Y<t are concatenated to regenerate the current output segment.
      Algorithm 1 presents an overview of C TRL A inference step. This algorithm will iteratively execute
279
      until it either produces a complete response or reaches the maximum generation length. The detailed
280
      algorithm with refusal handling is presented in Appendix B.3.2.

      5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
487
488   Impacts of LLM layers to be steered. We now study the impact of varying the number of layers
489   used for honesty steering on the final results of the TriviaQA dataset under no retrieval setting. Let
490   LB and LE denote the starting and ending layers to be steered, respectively, and let Nstep represent
491   the step size, that is, honesty steering is performed every Nstep layers. We conduct a grid search
492   over the hyperparameters by setting LB ∈ {1, 5, 10}, LE ∈ {20, 25, 30}, and Nstep ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5},
493   resulting in a total of 45 experiments. The results are depicted in Figure 5. Steering performance
494
      is optimal when targeting intermediate layers (LB = 5/10, LE = 20/25), and suboptimal when
      incorporating lower or higher layers (e.g., LB = 1 vs. LB = 10, LE = 20 vs. LE = 30). We
495
      hypothesize that lower layers primarily process syntactic information and low-level concepts, higher
496
      layers focus on high-level knowledge and exhibit rigid beliefs, and middle layers are crucial for
497   forming reasoning and cognitive preferences, thus making steering at these layers more effective.
498   Additionally, setting Nstep = 2 or 3 yields optimal results, since steering too densely may impair the
499   model’s inherent capabilities, while steering too sparsely may fail to correct behavior effectively.
500
      Impact of data distribution and dataset size. We conducted an analysis using confidence feature
501
      extraction to examine the effects of data distribution and dataset size on the performance of directional
502
      features. We use our synthetic dataset and True-False dataset to simulate various data distributions to
503   assess their impact on 2WMQA. Figure 6 indicates that smaller dataset sizes are highly sensitive to
504   changes in data distribution, while this effect diminishes with larger datasets. Moreover, a dataset
505   size of 512 is sufficient for extracting effective features. This indicates that our method is robust with
506   respect to the data used for feature extraction.
507
      Performance of various LLMs in RAG settings. Here we analyze the performance of different
508
      LLMs on both short-form and long-form QA tasks. We select LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023) and its
509   Chat variant, Alpaca (Dubois et al., 2023), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023a). As shown in Table 8,
510   without retrieval, instruction-tuned LLMs like Alpaca and Mistral consistently outperform base
511   LLMs, i.e., LLaMA2, with larger models yielding better results. SR-RAG significantly enhances
512   LLM performance by providing supplementary evidence that compensates for internal knowledge
513   limitations. Besides, LLMs of similar sizes exhibit comparable performance, e.g., Alpaca7B vs.
514   Mistral7B and LLaMA2C13B vs. Alpaca13B , indicating similar task capabilities. Thus, we primarily
515   employ Mistral7B as our backbone model.
516   Performance of C TRL A with other LLMs. To assess C TRL A’s performance with different
517   backbones, we select LLaMA2-7B/13B-Chat (LLaMA2C7B and LLaMA2C13B ) and Vicuna13B-v1.5
518   maintaining identical settings to the compared baselines. The results, summarized in Table 9,
519   indicate that C TRL A consistently outperforms the compared baselines across various backbones,
520   demonstrating its robustness and transferability.

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524   This paper introduces C TRL A, a lightweight framework for optimizing retrieval timing detection in
525   adaptive RAG. By approaching adaptive RAG from a representation perspective, C TRL A extracts
526   features that represent honesty and confidence directions, and regulates the LLM’s behavior and
527   monitors its internal states to determine retrieval necessity during generation. Additionally, C TRL A
528   formulates refined search queries when retrieval is triggered and includes a refusal handling module
529   for LLM outputs. Our comprehensive evaluation across multiple benchmarks demonstrates that
530   C TRL A consistently outperforms existing baselines, highlighting the effectiveness of honesty and
531
      confidence features.

      A     L IMITATIONS
919
920
      C TRL A is a preliminary exploration of adaptive RAG from a representation perspective. To ensure
921
      our research is succinct, transparent, and easily attributable, we adopt a straightforward, consistent,
922   and elegant strategy for extracting directional features of honesty and confidence, and modulating the
923   behavior of LLM, yielding promising results. Recent work (Liu et al., 2024b) shows that fine-tuning
924   LLMs can produce more effective features for model alignment, which could further enhance the
925   performance of C TRL A. Furthermore, we do not explicitly apply relevance and usefulness validation
926   to the retrieved content. However, since C TRL A does not involve fine-tuning the LLM and achieves
927   adaptive RAG in a plug-and-play manner, it can be effortlessly integrated with other approaches
928   focused on content processing. The exploration of these aspects is reserved for future research.

930   B     M ORE D ETAILS ABOUT C TRL A F RAMEWORK
931
932   B.1   A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK ABOUT K NOWLEDGE C ONFLICTS
933
934   Knowledge Conflicts (Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c; Neeman et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Wu
935   et al., 2024b) in LLMs have recently drawn significant attention from researchers. This line of work
936   primarily focuses on analyzing how LLMs behave when facing conflicts between external knowl-
937   edge contents and their internal (parametric) knowledge. Early studies in Open-domain Question
938   Answering (ODQA) presented contrasting findings: while Longpre et al. (2021) observed models’
939
      over-reliance on parametric knowledge, Chen et al. (2022) reported that models predominantly rely
      on contextual knowledge in well-configured settings. With the emergence of larger language models,
940
      this topic has been revisited from various perspectives. Xie et al. (2023) conducted comprehensive
941
      experiments by leveraging LLMs to generate conflicting context, revealing that while LLMs are
942   highly receptive to external evidence when it is coherent and convincing, they also exhibit a strong
943   confirmation bias towards their internal knowledge. Jin et al. (2024) further explored this phenomenon
944   and proposed methods to resolve such conflicts in retrieval-augmented language models.
945
      While knowledge conflicts and Adaptive RAG (ARAG) share some common ground, they address
946
      distinct aspects of knowledge integration in LLMs. Research on knowledge conflicts primarily centers
947
      on analyzing the phenomenon and behavior of LLMs when faced with contradictory information
948   between external contents and internal knowledge. These studies often utilize specifically curated
949   datasets and pre-specified external knowledge to simulate how models utilize knowledge at the
950   “post-retrieval” stage. In contrast, ARAG focuses on a different challenge: determining whether and
951   when to retrieve external information for a given query, and dynamically deciding during generation
952   whether additional retrieval is necessary. This distinction is crucial as ARAG operates at the “pre-
953   retrieval” and “during-retrieval” stages, making architectural decisions about knowledge acquisition
954   rather than resolving conflicts in already-retrieved information. These two research directions can be
955   viewed as complementary. The insights from knowledge conflicts research could potentially enhance
956
      post-retrieval processing in ARAG systems, potentially leading to more robust and reliable responses.
957

      Context-Augmented Querying. In § 3.2.4, we propose to use the “new information” of the
      generated segment yt as the search query for retrieval. The “new information” denotes the tokens
961

962

965   verification. Besides, the confidence probe is not always accurate in pinpointing specific tokens

967   detection precision, it is crucial to filter out the old information and trivial stopwords.

1006

1007

1008   know” or “I am not sure”, or indications of irrelevant information in retrieved documents. Meanwhile,
1009   these refusal responses are well-aligned with the LLM’s internal beliefs, i.e., LLM is confident in its
1010   knowledge limitations, making them challenging to detect by confidence monitoring.
1011

1012

1013

1014   useful information for CAQ and TVQ to refine search queries, we also devise a query rewriting
1015   function, fQR (ref. Prompt B.2), for more reliable search query construction.

1274   D.1   D ETAILS OF C ONFIDENCE M ONITORING E VALUATION DATASET
1275
1276   The Self-Aware Yin et al. (2023) dataset contains a diverse collection of 1, 032 unanswerable
1277   questions across five categories, along with 2, 337 answerable questions, designed to evaluate the self-
1278   knowledge of LLMs by testing their ability to identify what questions they can or cannot definitively
1279   answer. The answerable questions are clear and uncontroversial, and they can be answered using
1280   information available on Wikipedia. The unanswerable questions include questions with no scientific
1281
       consensus, questions requiring imagination, completely subjective questions, questions with too
       many variables, philosophical questions, etc. In general, the unanswerable questions from the Self-
1282
       Aware dataset are sufficient to evaluate the LLM’s confidence in our experiments. However, the
1283
       answerable questions, although clear and uncontroversial, may not be easy enough for arbitrary LLMs
1284   to consistently provide confident responses since these answerable questions still require LLMs to
1285   memorize a certain amount of factual knowledge on Wikipedia, which is unpredictable.
1286
1287
       Thus, for the answerable questions, we instead construct a simple prompt, summarized in Prompt D.1
       and instruct GPT-4 to generate 50 sufficiently simple answerable questions that the LLMs could
1288
       answer with a high confidence level. By curating these two distinct sets of questions, where one
1289
       is designed to prompt confident responses from LLM and another is to reflect the uncertainty of
1290   LLM, we create a comprehensive test suite for the confidence feature. This approach enables us to
1291   rigorously evaluate the feature’s ability to accurately distinguish between scenarios where the model
1292   is confident in its answers and those where it expresses doubt due to the inherent complexity, lack of
1293   information, or ambiguity of the question. Through this evaluation, we aim to ensure the robustness
1294   and reliability of the confidence feature for assessing the model’s self-awareness and its capacity to
1295   communicate its level of certainty across a wide range of contexts, taking into account the diverse
       nature of the questions present in the Self-Aware dataset.

       D.2   M ORE R ESULTS OF H ONESTY S TEERING
1325
1326   Honesty steering is capable of effectively mitigating both narrow-sense lying and unconscious
1327   deception issues in LLMs. An example of narrow-sense lying is “claiming to have received an
1328   A grade despite knowing the actual grade is C to avoid potential punishment”. Examples of
1329   unconscious deception can be observed in the TruthfulQA dataset, where language models are tested
1330   with questions that are prone to common misconceptions and falsehoods. This dataset highlights the
1331   model’s tendency to generate inaccurate or misleading responses even when it is not intentionally
1332   programmed to deceive. Figure 7 illustrates an example of using the honesty feature to steer the
1333
       LLM’s tendency to engage in narrow-sense lying. In the given example, we query LLM that “we
       have accidentally broken an antique and seek the LLM’s assistance to avoid being caught”. Without
1334
       applying honesty steering to the LLM, the LLM is likely to suggest lying and denying any knowledge
1335
       of the incident. With the honesty steering, the model shifts its approach and attempts to find a
1336   solution under the assumption that we have admitted to breaking the antique. This example highlights
1337   the effectiveness of honesty steering in encouraging the LLM to provide more ethical and truthful
1338   responses, even in situations where deception might seem advantageous. Regarding unconscious
1339   deception, the results presented in Figure 2 demonstrate the effectiveness of honesty steering in
1340   addressing this issue.
