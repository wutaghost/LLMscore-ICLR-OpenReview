009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding and accurately following instructions is critical for large language
012            models (LLMs) to be effective across diverse tasks. In this work, we conduct a
013            rigorous investigation into the factors that enable generalization to unseen instruc-
014            tions. Through controlled experiments, inspired by the Turing-complete Markov
015            algorithm, we demonstrate that such generalization only emerges when training
016
               data is diversified enough across semantic domains. Our findings also reveal that
               merely diversifying within limited domains fails to ensure robust generalization. In
017
               contrast, cross-domain data diversification, even under constrained data budgets,
018
               significantly enhances a model’s adaptability. We further extend our analysis to
019            real-world scenarios, including fine-tuning of specialist and generalist models.
020            Our research provides important insights for dataset collation, particularly when
021            optimizing model performance by expanding training data for both specialist and
022            generalist scenarios. We show that careful consideration of data diversification
023            is key: training specialist models with data extending beyond their core domain
024            leads to significant performance improvements, while generalist models benefit
025            from diverse data mixtures that enhance their overall instruction-following capabil-
026            ities across a wide range of applications. Our results highlight the critical role of
027
               strategic diversification and offer clear guidelines for improving data quality.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rapid advancements in large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized a wide range of tasks,
032   including language comprehension (Wang et al., 2020), generation (Brown et al., 2020), knowledge-
033   based question answering (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), and solving complex reasoning problems in fields
034   like mathematics (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and programming (Chen et al., 2021a;
035   Austin et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2022). These successes hinge on the foundational
036   capabilities of LLMs, such as knowledge retrieval, reasoning, planning, and notably, instruction
037   following—enabled through instruction-tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Taori et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
038
      2022; Sanh et al., 2022a). Instruction-tuning trains LLMs on a wide variety of instruction-output
      pairs, allowing them to handle diverse prompts and better generalize to new tasks.
039
040   While knowledge retrieval focuses on accessing stored information and reasoning involves multi-
041   step problem-solving, instruction following concerns the accurate interpretation and execution of
042   diverse natural language prompts (Zhou et al., 2023b). This capability is vital for user interaction,
043   as it involves understanding the intent behind instructions and performing tasks without relying on
044
      complex logic (Liu et al., 2024a). Despite its importance, the mechanisms underlying instruction
      following remain less explored compared to other capabilities like reasoning.
045
046   The current research landscape on instruction tuning has produced varied and sometimes contradictory
047   findings, ranging from the impact of dataset selection (Zhou et al., 2023a) to the effects of scaling
048   up data (Zeng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). These disparate findings suggest that instruction
049   following in LLMs is influenced by the scale and composition of fine-tuning data in complex
050
      ways Dong et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b). However, a systematic investigation into the core
      components of LLM performance—specifically isolating instruction following from reasoning and
051
      knowledge retrieval—has been limited.
052
053   Our work addresses this gap by focusing explicitly ONLY on the Instruction-Following capabilities
      of LLMs. We first introduce a systematic analysis of instruction diversity through a controlled

054
      symbolic task—string rewrites—inspired by the Turing-complete Markov algorithm Markov (1954).
055   By isolating the effects of instruction diversity, we focus on the model’s ability to follow instructions
056   without conflating this with reasoning capabilities. Through controlled experiments, we examine the
057   impact of instruction diversification across various semantic domains on the model’s ability to adapt
058   to unseen instruction semantics. We find that generalization to unseen task semantics emerges ONLY

060   limited domains does not guarantee robust generalization. In contrast, cross-domain diversification
061   significantly enhances the model’s adaptability to new instructions, highlighting the importance of a

092   2.1     S TRING R EPLACEMENT TASK
093
094   To start a systematic investigation of instruction-following, we model instruction-following tasks
095   as string-replacement operations, which are fundamental in theoretical computer science. String
096   replacement forms the basis of Markov Algorithms (Markov, 1954), a Turing-complete model where
097   sequences are iteratively transformed using ordered rewrite rules. These algorithms represent a
098
      structured, rule-driven process in which the first applicable rule is used to replace the leftmost
      matching substring, continuing until no further matches exist. This structured transformation mirrors
099
      the sequential, instruction-driven behavior we aim to explore. Appendix A provides illustrative
100
      examples of Markov algorithms in action.
101
102   In our study, we focus on a simplified form of this process. The replacement rule R—a pair like
103   aa → bac—is applied to an input string ξ (e.g., caaba), yielding an output string τ (e.g., cbacba).
104
      The rule is applied to the leftmost occurrence of a match, and if no match exists, the original input
      remains unchanged. For instance, applying ι : iss → art to ξ = mississippi produces
105
      τ = martissippi, while applying ι to ξ = canada leaves the string unaltered.
106
107   This task, though simple, serves as a powerful proxy for instruction tuning by teaching models
      to handle structured, rule-based transformations. Training on these string rewrites allows models

      to internalize core aspects of instruction-following tasks, equipping them with the capability to
109   generalize across a wide range of rule-based tasks without the overhead of more complex operations.

      2.2                      E XPERIMENT S ET-U P
113
      We designed two string replacement tasks to evaluate the model’s ability to follow simple transforma-
114   tion rules:
115   Basic Replacement: Apply the rule ι : x → y to any input string containing x. The model replaces
116   the first occurrence of x with y and returns the modified string; and
117   Conditional Replacement: Apply the rule ι : x → y if x is present in the input string; otherwise,
118   return the input unchanged.
119
      The model takes in ξ and ι, outputs either the transformed string or the original input if no match is
120   found.
121
122   Task 1 focuses on fundamental rewrite operations, while Task 2 introduces a conditional decision-
123
      making aspect. This experimental setup allows us to assess the model’s capacity for both direct rule
      application and handling cases where no action is required.
124
125   In later sections, we also generalize the task from regular to context-sensitive, where x and y represent
126   abstract structures e.g. x = a2 will represent all squared terms.
127   We train GPT-2 models (256 dimensions, 6 layers, 4 attention heads) on synthetic instruction/outcome
128   pairs. The dataset contains S × I sequences, with S input strings and I replacement rules. Models
129   are tested on 105 unseen examples to assess generalization across rules. Full training details are in
130   Appendix B.

      2.3.1                      I NSTRUCTION D IVERSITY I S D ECISIVE T O G ENERALIZATION
151
152   Figure 2a presents the generalization accuracy for models trained on a fixed budget of I × S = 106
153   examples, as a function of the number I of different instructions in the training set. The number
154   of examples per instruction (S) decreases as I increases. In these experiments, all input sequences
155   feature at least one instance of the replacement rule.
156   In Figure 2a,a sharp step-shaped transition is observed: models trained on fewer than Imin (where
157   Imin = 300) unique instructions consistently fail to generalize, regardless of example count per
158   instruction. Conversely, models exposed to over Imax (where Imax = 1, 000) distinct instructions
159   generalize effectively to unseen instructions, even with minimal examples per instruction. This
160   phase transition underscores our hypothesis that the sheer number of distinct instructions (I) is
161   a key driver for generalization, affirming the necessity of cross-domain diversification for robust
      instruction-following for unseen instructions.

      2.3.2   D IVERSIFICATION A LLOWS G ENERALIZATION I N C ASE -BASED R EASONING S ET-U P
163
164   In earlier experiments, the model replaced a sub-string always present in the input. Now, we introduce
165   a more complex task where some rules may not apply, requiring the model to decide whether a rule
166   is applicable and either perform the operation or leave the input unchanged. This two-step process
167   challenges the model to determine rule applicability and execute the correct action simultaneously.
168
      To explore this, we introduce a third parameter in the training set: the frequency of “No-Ops”
169
      (instructions that cannot be satisfied), which we vary between 10% and 50%. The size of the training
170   and test sets remains the same as in previous experiments,keeping the data size constant.
171
172   Figure 2b presents the generalization accuracies of trained models, as a function of the number of
173
      instructions and the frequency of No-Ops. Interestingly, despite No-Ops dominating the dataset1 ,
      the model generalizes well to unseen instructions after training on around 400 distinct cases. The
174
      proportion of No-Ops does not significantly affect generalization beyond that point, demonstrating
175
      that training on diverse instructions effectively teach the model to assess rule relevance and apply
176   them accurately.
177
178   Overall, our conclusions remain consistent with previous experiments, albeit with a slightly lower
179
      number of instructions needed for generalization (400 vs. 500). This demonstrates the effectiveness
      of diversification in more complex scenarios involving case-based reasoning and rule relevance
180
      assessment.

244   3              R EWRITING WITH A BSTRACTION
245
246   In real-world applications, following instructions often extends beyond simple "search-and-replace"
247   tasks, requiring a model to abstract high-level concepts and ground them in specific contexts.We
248   therefore extend the string-rewriting experiment to simulate this challenge by including the aspect
249   of abstraction and grounding into our set-up by distinguishing between abstract rules and their
250   groundings to the input. This is crucial because abstract rules act as entry points into different
251   semantic domains, each requiring unique reasoning strategies. This mirrors real-world tasks, such as
252
      translation and question answering, where the nature of the instructions varies widely depending on
      the task’s context and domain-specific requirements.
253
254   Concretely, here we introduce a mathematical deduction task. Specifically, the model is tasked with
255   simplifying algebraic expressions by applying specified deduction rules. Here, handling different
256   abstract rules in mathematics is analogous to following instructions across distinct semantic domains.
257   In this experiment, we present the model with a randomly generated mathematical expression I and
258   an abstract mathematical deduction rule ιabs : (X = Y ) where X and Y are math expressions (e.g.
259   an example abstract rule would be a2 − b2 = (a + b) × (a − b), to assess the model’s ability to
260   identify the relevant sub-expression in ξ (e.g. in (2x + 5)2 − (3y − 6)2 , here a = 2x + 5,b = y − 6 )
261   and correctly apply the transformation (get (2x + 5 + 3y − 6) × (2x + 5 − (y − 6))). We observe
262   how well the model generalizes when trained on datasets of varying rule diversity.

354   Building on our foundational insights from controlled string-rewriting tasks, we extend our analysis
355   to training a real-world specialist instruction-follower.
356   Here, we investigate how cross-domain instruction diversity impacts the model’s ability to handle
357   real-world tasks that require a nuanced balance between instruction-following and domain-specific
358   expertise by the example of code generation.
359
      Code generation is primarily an instruction-following task, where models translate explicit prompts
360
      into executable code. Pre-trained code LLMs, having encountered common coding patterns during
361
      pre-training on large code corpora, are fine-tuned to utilize these structures effectively. Unlike
362   reasoning tasks that demand creative problem-solving, code generation focuses on adhering to
363   established patterns and passing test cases, emphasizing precise procedural execution over complex
364   reasoning.
365
      In this experiment, we aim to demonstrate that a diverse set of instructions can significantly enhance
366
      a model’s adaptability to new coding instructions. This analysis provides crucial evidence for
367
      optimizing instruction-tuning datasets in real-world scenarios where both precision and generalization
368   are paramount.

468
469   In this study, we evaluate the impact of cross-domain instruction diversification on large language
470   models (LLMs) by comparing our approach with a baseline model trained exclusively on UltraInteract-
471   SFT dataset (Yuan et al., 2024). UltraInteract-SFT is a collection of complex, multi-step reasoning
472   problems emphasizing on math problem-solving, code generation, and logical reasoning, promoting
473   robust reasoning and planning capabilities in LLMs.
474   While UltraInteract-SFT primarily focuses on math and coding problems and contains a rich collection
475   of those problems, its scope is limited to these domains. OpenOrca (Lian et al., 2023) and Alpaca,
476   though sparse and varied, introduce broader instruction-following tasks.
477
      To assess the effectiveness of cross-domain instruction diversity, we constructed a training set that
478
      includes a mixture of UltraInteract-SFT, OpenOrca, and Alpaca datasets. While UltraInteract-SFT
479
      is rich in math, coding and complex QA problems, it remains limited to primarily these domains
480   despite its challenging and diverse nature within them. OpenOrca and Alpaca, on the other hand,
481   introduce instruction-following tasks across a broader range of domains, enriching the training
482   data with varied instruction types. We gauged the model’s overall capabilities using the same set
483   of benchmarks consisting of coding (Austin et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021a), math (Hendrycks
484   et al., 2021b; Cobbe et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023), knowledge (MMLU Hendrycks et al., 2021a),
485   instruction following (Zhou et al., 2023b) and chain-of-thought reasoning (Suzgun et al., 2022) as
      (Yuan et al., 2024) and computed average performance.

507   6     R ELATED WORKS
508
509
      Datasets for instruction-tuning. Many datasets for instruction-tuning have been proposed. The best
510   quality is achieved for sets collated by human annotators Khashabi et al. (2020); Ye et al. (2021);
511   Sanh et al. (2022b); Wang et al. (2022); Longpre et al. (2023); Conover et al. (2023); Köpf et al.
512   (2023), but their size is constrained by the cost of annotation. Alternative methods, which use large
513   language models to generate instruction sets, have been proposed Wang et al. (2023b); Honovich
514   et al. (2022); Taori et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2023); Chiang et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023a); Köksal
515   et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023). They provide larger instruction sets, at the cost of reduced annotation
516   quality.
517   Data curation for instruction-tuning. It is widely recognized that the quality of instruction-tuning
518   datasets has a massive impact on the performance of fine-tuned models. Previous works acknowledged
519   the contributions of several key factors. Most research on the subject insist on the importance of
520   the size and quality of the instruction sets (Chung et al., 2022; Iyer et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a).
521   Liang et al. (Liang et al., 2024) point out the importance of consistent formats. Several recent
522   works Zhou et al. (2023a); Cao et al. (2023) suggest that models fine-tuned on carefully selected
523   examples can achieve high performance with small datasets. Various strategies for data curation have
524
      been proposed, focusing on instruction diversity, and the quality of answers (Zhou et al., 2023a; Cao
      et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b). Several authors discuss the benefit of
525
      mixing tasks from different categories Longpre et al. (2023); Iyer et al. (2022); Bukharin & Zhao
526
      (2024). Closest to our work, Dong et al. (Dong et al., 2024) discuss the impact of mixing general and
527   domain-specific instructions, in order to achieve the best results with the smallest dataset.

      In this work, we systematically studied instruction-following of language models via a suite of
533
      carefully designed controlled experiments. By introducing a symbolic string rewrite framework, we
534   provide a focused model to isolate and study models’ instruction-following abilities, offering a new
535   perspective on this fundamental capability. Our experiments further demonstrate that instruction
536   diversity, even within a fixed data budget, plays a critical role in improving model generalization. This
537   finding underscores the value of diverse instruction semantics over large dataset size, in enhancing
538   performance across both specialized and generalized LLM applications. Finally, we offer practical
539   insights into dataset collation strategies, highlighting that proper diversification can significantly
      outperform dataset expansion.

      A     C OMPLEMENT ON M ARKOV ALGORITHMS
811
812
      Markov algorithms Markov (1954) are ordered sets of rewrite rules, operating on sequences of
813   symbols in a fixed alphabet U. A sequence S is processed by applying the first rewrite applicable
814   to S, at the leftmost position if several exist: i.e. the rewrite rule ss → tr transforms the sequence
815   S = mississipi into S ′ = mitrissipi. The algorithm is then applied to S ′ , and the process is
816   repeated until either no rules apply, and the algorithm is said to be blocked, or a special rule, called a
817   stop rule is invoked, and the algorithm terminates and returns the final rewritten sequence.
818
      Specifically, the algorithm uses and alphabet A, which includes the alphabet U used buy the sequences
819   to be processed (henceforth, small case latin letters), a set of additional symbols (henceforth, the
820   small case greek letters {α, β . . . }, and a special symbol · indicating a stop rule.
821
822
      For instance, we could define the following algorithm, with U = {a, b}, and A = {a, b, α, β, ·}, and
      the rules

827

830                                                     →     α                                              (5)

      where x and y stand for any letter a or b. This will transform any sequence of a and b into a

      concatenation of the sequence and its reverse. Applied on abb, the algorithm will perform the

      following rewrites:
