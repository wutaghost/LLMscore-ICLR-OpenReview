009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The success of autoregressive transformer models with discrete tokens has in-
012            spired quantization-based approaches for continuous modalities, though these of-
013            ten limit reconstruction quality. We therefore introduce SALAD, a per-token
014            latent diffusion model for zero-shot text-to-speech, that operates on continuous
015            representations. SALAD builds upon the recently proposed expressive diffusion
016
               head for image generation, and extends it to generate variable-length outputs. Our
               approach utilizes semantic tokens for providing contextual information and de-
017
               termining the stopping condition. We suggest three continuous variants for our
018
               method, extending popular discrete speech synthesis techniques. Additionally, we
019            implement discrete baselines for each variant and conduct a comparative analysis
020            of discrete versus continuous speech modeling techniques. Our results demon-
021            strate that both continuous and discrete approaches are highly competent, and that
022            SALAD achieves a superior intelligibility score while obtaining speech quality
023            and speaker similarity on par with the ground-truth audio.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Autoregressive (AR) modeling is often correlated with discrete representations, probably due to
028
      the remarkable success of Large Language Models (LLMs), which operate on a discrete modality.
029
      (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019). Inspired by the success of LLMs, continuous modalities,
030   such as audio and images, are quantized to be modeled discretely. In image generation, quantization
031   is often achieved by discrete autoencoders (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), which are later optimized
032   with adversarial losses to improve fidelity (Esser et al., 2021). Works that focus on audio generation
033   usually employ Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ) (Zeghidour et al., 2021), a process that itera-
034   tively refines the approximation by quantizing the residual. The resulting discrete codes are used for
035   discrete AR modeling (Esser et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Copet et al., 2024).
036   Discrete modeling over continuous domains requires quantization, which degrades the reconstruc-
037   tion quality and upper-bounds the fidelity. Using multiple RVQ quantizers enhances the fidelity, but
038   the fine RVQ codes might quantize noise, which can be detrimental for discrete modeling methods.
039   Discrete autoencoders may also suffer from low codebook utilization (Mentzer et al., 2023), and
040   multimodal models that work on discrete representation suffer from stability issues (Team, 2024).
041   We therefore suspect that quantizing inherently continuous modalities may be sub-optimal, and fo-
042   cus on continuous alternatives instead.
043   Predicting continuous distributions with regression losses such as L1 or L2, induce a unimodal
044   distribution, an unrealistic assumption for most generative tasks. We hypothesize that multimodal
045   distributions, which enable multiple local maxima, can represent more complex patterns and is cru-
046   cial for generative one-to-many tasks. Recent works in image generation have explored approaches
047   to modeling continuous distributions. GIVT (Tschannen et al., 2023) represents the continuous dis-
048   tribution using a Gaussian Mixture Model, while AR-Diffusion (Li et al., 2024) suggests a per-token
049   diffusion head to model the continuous frame distributions.
050   We suggest SALAD (Speech synthesis with Autoregressive LAtent Diffusion), a per-token latent
051   diffusion model for zero-shot speech synthesis over continuous representations, inspired by the per-
052   token diffusion head suggested by Li et al. (2024). We enable the generation of variable length
053   outputs, addressing a challenge that is absent in image generation methods, where the number of
      tokens to generate is fixed. We utilize semantic tokens (Kharitonov et al., 2023; Borsos et al., 2023a)

094
095   Zero-Shot TTS Inspired by the success of in-context learning, there has been great interest in
096   text-to-speech (TTS) systems that can generalize to unseen speakers during inference. This task is
097   commonly known as zero-shot TTS, and provides many benefits due to its flexibility and increased
098   quality (Wang et al., 2023). Zero-shot TTS systems, including SALAD, typically formulate the
099   problem as a language modeling task, containing text and audio tokens. Such methods make use of
100
      a speaker prompt – a short recording from the target speaker – and synthesize the text according to
      the prompt (Le et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023; Łajszczak et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024).
101
102
      Semantic Tokens Quantized embeddings of self-supervised audio models (Hsu et al., 2021;
103
      Baevski et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2021), commonly known as semantic tokens, have been shown
104
      to capture phonetic and prosodic information, and show benefits when modeling long-range depen-
105   dencies in various speech processing tasks. These tokens became popular as intermediate represen-

      tations for speech synthesis (Kharitonov et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Borsos et al., 2023a), un-
109   conditional audio generation (Borsos et al., 2023b), and for text-audio multimodal tasks (Rubenstein
110   et al., 2023). SALAD predicts semantic tokens as an auxilary task to obtain contextual information
111   and determine the stopping condition.
112
113   RVQ codes prediction Various audio generation methods have designed unique ways to predict
114   the RVQ codes matrix, each with their advantages and limitations. AudioLM (Borsos et al., 2023b)
115   flattens the codes matrix into a long sequence, which greatly increases the token count in transformer
116   models. Most followup works avoid flattening and embed all RVQ residual layers into a single token.
117   Vall-E (Wang et al., 2023) generates the initial coarse code vector with an AR model and then uses an
118
      non-autoregressive (NAR) model to predict the rest of the codebooks. AudioGen (Kreuk et al., 2022)
      uses a single AR model to predict all codes of each timestep in parallel. MusicGen (Copet et al.,
119
      2024) extends AudioGen by introducing a delay pattern, ensuring each code is predicted based on its
120
      coarser RVQ layers, leading to a better approximate factorization. SoundStorm (Borsos et al., 2023a)
121   employs the fast NAR decoding algorithm by MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022) to generate acoustic
122   tokens based on semantic tokens. NaturalSpeech3 (Ju et al., 2024) trains a factorized codec, which
123   disentangles speech characteristics into discrete factors and predicts each factor using a MaskGIT
124   procedure. As opposed to all above methods, SALAD directly predicts a continuous latent space,
125   thus avoiding the need to predict multiple residuals codes.
126
127   Continuous models When learning a continuous distribution, recent works typically use diffu-
128   sion models, which were developed to sample from complex continuous probability distributions,
129   inspired by non-equilibrium thermodynamics (Ho et al., 2020). Several works attempt to synthe-
130   size speech using a diffusion process, which has the challenge of generating variable length out-
131
      puts (Kong et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Popov et al., 2021). For that end, most diffusion-based
      works rely on a duration predictor that predicts the audio length in advance, which might be in-
132
      ferior to determining the length on-the-fly during synthesis (Shen et al., 2023; Le et al., 2024).
133
      MELLE (Meng et al., 2024) predicts Mel spectrograms autoregressively using a Gaussian sampling
134   module, and parameterizes the next frame using a Gaussian distribution, which restricts it to learn
135   only unimodal distributions. MELLE relies on an additional binary classifier that indicates when to
136   stop, which is a highly imbalanced classification problem. In contrast, SALAD operates on VAE la-
137   tent tokens, which allows sampling diverse inputs while training, and uses a diffusion head, capable
138   of modeling multimodal distributions. SALAD relies on semantic-tokens to determine the stopping
139   condition, a more balanced representation which also provides contextual information.

145   Definitions We denote the raw audio sequence as a = (a1 , ..., am ) where ai ∈ [−1, 1] with
146   sampling rate fS . The text is y = (y1 , .., yk ) where yi ∈ A, and A is the text vocabulary. We obtain
147   compressed audio representations using a variational autoencoder (VAE), trained with adversarial
148
      losses to obtain high-fidelity reconstructions. The VAE’s encoder E predicts a sequence of means
      and variances of normal distribution: (µ1 , ..., µn ), (σ12 , ..., σn2 ) = E(a) where σi , µi ∈ Rd and d is
149
      the VAE bottleneck dimension. The VAE downsamples the sequence with a stride r. We sample
150
      xi ∼ N (µi , σi2 ) and denote x = (x1 , .., xn ) as the continuous acoustic tokens. The VAE’s decoder
151   D is used for reconstruction â1 , ..., âm = D(x1 , .., xn ). We also extract semantic tokens and denote
152   them by w = (w1 , .., wm ), which have the same downsampling stride as the VAE. Our goal is to

154   latent features as s = s1 , ..., sp , our training objective can be formulated by: p(x|y, s).

      3.2     SALAD: S PEECH SYNTHESIS USING AUTOREGRESSIVE LATENT D IFFUSION
200
      SALAD performs zero-shot text to speech, which can synthesize speech based on a given text and a
201   speaker prompt. It does so by predicting the continuous VAE latents x with the per-token diffusion
202   head. Our approach utilizes semantic tokens w as an auxiliary representation that provides contex-
203   tual information and determines the stopping condition. We provide two variants for SALAD:
204
205           • Semantic to Acoustic (S2A) - predicts acoustic features based on semantic tokens, and
206             relies on an external text-to-semantic model to produce the semantic tokens (Figure 3).
207           • Text to Acoustic (T2A) - predicts acoustic features and semantic features directly from text,
208             relying on the stopping condition of the semantic tokens (Figure 4).
209

      3.2.2    T EXT- TO -ACOUSTIC (T2A)
271
272   Decoupling TTS into T2S and S2A requires training two models of similar size, and applying two
273   steps of inference, which greatly increases the compute requirements and latency. Therefore, we
274   suggest an end-to-end text-to-acoustic model (T2A) which predicts the acoustic features directly
275   from the text and the speaker prompt. The T2A model predicts the semantic and acoustic features
276   in parallel, where the semantic token prediction is an auxiliary task that allows conditioning on
277
      contextual information and provides a stopping condition. We add an additional prediction MLP
      to predict the discrete semantic tokens. We adopt the delay pattern suggested by (Copet et al.,
278
      2024) such that every acoustic token xi can be predicted based on the semantic token wi . Define
279
      ri = (wi , xi−1 ), we extract contextual features from our transformer backbone, based on the text
280   and speaker prompt zi = Θ(t, s, r1 , ...ri ), which is used to predict wi+1 using the cross-entropy
281   loss Ls and xi using the diffusion loss La . We weigh the two losses to L = αLa + (1 − α)Ls . We
282   halt the generation after the semantic prediction head samples an EOS token. We note that the audio
283   duration is predicted on the-fly based on the model’s predictions, unlike most diffusion-based TTS
284   models, where the audio duration is predetermined.

286   3.3     D ISCRETE BASELINES
287
288   All SALAD models use common discrete architectures and only replace the input projection and
289   prediction head, so we can implement a discrete variant for each method proposed in Section 3.2.
290   The discrete methods use an RVQ-GAN quantizer, which yields a sequence of discrete codes
291
      (q 1 , ..., q Q ) for each frame. These codes are predicted from the contextual embedding z by MLP
      prediction heads, one for each codebook. The S2A-AR discrete model predicts all codes in parallel,
292
      using the delay pattern proposed in MusicGen (Copet et al., 2024). The S2A-NAR discrete model
293
      implements SoundStorm (Borsos et al., 2023a), which applies Q MaskGIT procedures, one for each
294   codebook. Unlike the vanilla SoundStorm implementation, we replace the confidence-based un-
295   masking with random unmasking, as we have found it to be superior. Given K MaskGIT steps,
296   SoundStorm requires QK passes through the transformer, as it employs a MaskGIT procedure per
297   RVQ layer, unlike SALAD-NAR, which requires K transformer passes. The T2A discrete model
298   predicts semantic and acoustic tokens in parallel from text, using the delay pattern described above.
299   We use the MusicGen parallel prediction method, treating the semantic tokens as the coarsest codes.
300   In our listening test, we compare to the external XTTS (Casanova et al., 2024), a commercial SOTA
301   zero-shot TTS model.

306
307   Datasets We train all models on the English subset of multi-lingual LibriSpeech (MLS) (Pratap
308   et al., 2020), which contains 10M examples of 10-20 seconds, resulting in 45K hours. To avoid
309   over-exposure of a few speakers, we limit the maximal number of utterances per speaker to 10K,
310   resulting in 5.2M examples. We evaluate all models on LibriSpeech test-clean (Panayotov et al.,
311   2015), which consists of 2620 utterances by 40 speakers. All speakers in the test set are excluded
312
      from the training set. We filter the dataset to utterances with lengths of 8-25 seconds, and then limit
      to at most 15 samples per speaker, resulting in 564 utterances for evaluation.
313
314
315
      Tokenization To derive acoustic tokens, we train continuous β-VAE-GAN, with a varying bot-
      tleneck dimension d ∈ {8, 16, 24, 32}, and set the KL-divergence regularization to β = 5 · 10−5 ,
316
      as done in Tschannen et al. (2023). We also train discrete RVQ-GAN models with q ∈ {4, 8, 12}
317
      codebooks, each with 1024 entries. In addition, we apply quantizer dropout (Zeghidour et al., 2021)
318   with p = 0.5. All compression models are trained on MLS-English, DAPS, LibriTTS, LibriTTS-
319   R and LJ-Speech, which balance between high and mid quality recordings (Shechtman & Dekel,
320   2024). The all-training hyperparameters follow the original recipe proposed by Kumar et al. (2024).
321   We extract semantic tokens by quantizing the embeddings of the 11th layer of W2V-BERT (Barrault
322   et al., 2023) using minibatch K-means with 1024 centroids. We further compress the semantic to-
323   kens using a BPE tokenizer with a vocabulary of 16384 tokens. This is done to shorten the sequence
      and balance the tokens’ distribution (Dekel & Fernandez, 2024). We note that only the T2S model

328   Architecture We use a transformer backbone with d = 1024, df f = 4096, 24 layers, 16 heads,
329   sinusoidal positional embedding, GeLU activation, and a dropout rate of 0.1, resulting in models
330   with roughly 350M parameters. VAE embeddings are projected using a linear layer, while RVQ
331   tokens are embedded using Q lookup tables, which are summed into a single embedding. We use
332   Classifier-Free Guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) and randomly omit the speaker prompt with p =
333   0.1 during training. In the MaskGIT NAR experiments, we use the cosine masking schedule, and
334   apply a total of 64 inference steps, where the SoundStorm model with 4 codebooks performs 16
335
      inference steps per layer. RVQ codes are predicted using a Q MLP heads with four hidden layers.
      We sample from discrete distributions using Top k = 10 sampling, with a temperature of τ = 1, a
336
      repetition penalty of 1.05, and a CFG scale of α = 3.
337
338   We use a diffusion process with T = 1000 steps, where betas are logarithmicly spaced between
339   β0 = 2e − 4 and βT = 0.03. Our per-token diffusion head is an MLP network with 12 residual
340   layers, that predicts the noise ϵ given the transformer embedding vector z, the noisy input xt , and the
341
      diffusion step t. Each residual block consists of layer normalization, linear layer, SiLU activation,
      and dropout with p = 0.1. During inference, we apply 20 diffusion steps for sampling, with a default
342
      noise scale of 1. We use the AdamW optimizer, with lrmax = 3e − 4 and lrmin = 3e − 5, weight
343
      decay 0.1, and a clip gradient norm of 1, and train with FP16 mixed precision. We linearly warm
344   up the learning rate from lrmin across 32K iterations to lrmax and decay the learning rate back to
345   lrmin over 300K steps using a cosine schedule. Each global batch size has approximately 150K
346   acoustic tokens (200 samples). Each model was trained with 8 A100 80GB GPUs.
347
348   Metrics We measure Audio Quality using UTMOS (Saeki et al., 2022) which produces a qual-
349   ity score in the range of 1-5 (higher is better). Intelligibility is measured by the character error
350   rate (CER) in percentages (%) between the ground-truth text and the Whisper transcripts (Radford
351   et al., 2023) of the synthesized audio. Speaker Similarity is measured by the cosine similarity to the
352   prompt, comparing the embedding of WavLM-TDNN (Chen et al., 2022), a popular speaker veri-
353   fication model. This metric was also reported in Vall-E and subsequent studies (Wang et al., 2023;
354
      Chen et al., 2024). The similarity score predicted is in the range of [−1, 1], where a larger value
      indicates a higher similarity.
355
356   For the subjective Listening Tests, we selected one random utterance for every speaker in Lib-
357   riSpeech test-clean (20 female and 20 male speakers), resulting in 40 utterances for evaluation.
358   The selected utterances were confined to have at most 200 characters to enable the comparison
359   with XTTS xttsv2 (Casanova et al., 2024) (xttsv2 demo limitation). For each sample, we selected a
360
      three-second-long speaker prompt from another random utterance of the same speaker. Each system
      synthesizes the desired utterance based on the same text and speaker prompt. All experiments were
361
      conducted on the Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) crowd-sourcing platform with votes collected
362
      from 39-58 subjects qualified as masters (Sodré & Brasileiro, 2017).

      5   D ISCUSSION
514
515
516   Compressing complex signals such as audio and images often introduces a tradeoff between per-
517   ception and generation. For tasks involving perception or understanding, compression can lead to
518   information loss, resulting in degraded performance. However, for generation, compression has
519
      proven to be highly effective, as the generative model has to learn a lower-dimensional distribu-
      tion. Multimodal models typically aspire to work with symmetric representations, in which the
520
      input and output representations are identical, as commonly done in language models. Develop-
521
      ing generative methods that can operate upon less-compressed representations would alleviate the
522   perception-generation models, and improve multimodal models that operate on symmetric repre-
523   sentations. While RVQ is a powerful compression mechanism, capable of providing high-fidelity
524   representations, it may lead to noise quantization. Working with continuous representations can be
525   more robust to noise, as continuous models scale the noise according to its magnitude.
526
527
528   Limitations The diffusion head inference process is slower than the RVQ prediction heads, as it
529   requires an iterative process for the token sampling. Moreover, it does not allow to measure likeli-
530
      hood or confidence, which can be useful for decoding algorithms such as beam search or confidence
      based unmasking. Optimal balancing of the discrete and continuous losses in the continuous T2A
531
      model is not easy to obtain. During training, the gradients of the discrete semantic loss increase,
532
      while the gradients of the continuous diffusion loss decrease.
533
534
535   Future work Follow-up works can extend our work and develop multimodal models that operate
536   on symmetric representations, and are capable of perception and generation. They can also derive a
537   generation stopping condition that does not rely on any discrete representation. Additionally, future
538   works can implement diverse inference strategies that adapt the number of diffusion steps per token
539   (e.g. more diffusion steps in the first tokens), or develop a quality metric for a diffusion process, to
      allow decoding algorithms such as beam-search to be used during inference.
