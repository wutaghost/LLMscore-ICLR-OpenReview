               Communication is a prerequisite for collaboration. When scaling networks of AI-
               powered agents, communication must be versatile, efficient, and portable. These
013
               requisites, which we refer to as the Agent Communication Trilemma, are hard
014
               to achieve in large networks of agents. We introduce Agora, a meta protocol
015            that leverages existing communication standards to make LLM-powered agents
016            solve complex problems efficiently. In Agora, agents typically use standardised
017            routines for frequent communications, natural language for rare communications,
018            and LLM-written routines for everything in between. Agora sidesteps the Agent
019            Communication Trilemma and robustly handles changes in interfaces and mem-
020            bers, allowing unprecedented scalability with full decentralisation and minimal
021            involvement of human beings. On large Agora networks, we observe the emer-
022            gence of self-organising, fully automated protocols that achieve complex goals
023
               without human intervention.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Human language evolved primarily for communication purposes (Fedorenko et al., 2024). Despite
030   its inherent ambiguity, natural language provides great versatility and allows humans and machines
031   to collaborate and achieve complex goals that they otherwise could not (Russell & Norvig, 2016).
032   Decades of literature in computer science explored how to foster collaboration between agents mod-
033   elled as programs (Wooldridge & Jennings, 1995; Gilbert, 2019). Several research papers design
034   networks of agents to solve complex problems by leveraging each model’s specialisation, the so-
035   called rule-based agents paradigm (Wooldridge, 2009). Despite its influence, such a paradigm faces
036   two major limitations: agents hardly adapt to environmental changes and require structured data that
037   limits their versatility (Gilbert & Terna, 2000).
038   With the advent of Large Language Models (LLM) (Vaswani et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020), there
039   has been a resurgent interest in networks of collaborative agents. LLMs can solve a variety of
040   problems (Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024a) expressed in natural language as they excel at
041   following instructions (Schulman et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2024). LLMs also showed remarkable
042   improvements at handling structured data such as graphs and formatted languages (Kassner et al.,
043   2020; Collins et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024).
044   In terms of performance (e.g., accuracy on classification), the literature suggests that specialised
045   LLMs outperform general purpose models (Hu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024), as well as mitigating
046   the difficulties of handling gargantuan models and the drawbacks of data and model centralisation
047   (Song et al., 2023).
048
      Thus, we hypothesise that:
049
050       Hypothesis
051
052       A network of heterogeneous LLMs can automate various complex tasks with nearly no hu-
053       man supervision via specialised and efficient protocols.

054
      However, networks of LLM-powered agents face three key challenges that make communication
055   at scale significantly more difficult:
056
057          • LLMs are heterogeneous: different LLMs have different architectures, makers, capabilities
058            and usage policies.1
059          • LLMs are (mostly) general-purpose tools: enumerating and standardising each task they
060            can perform is infeasible.
061
             • LLMs are expensive: the computational footprint and inference time of “small” LLMs
062            dwarfs that of comparable, specialised APIs.
063
064   Scalable communication between heterogeneous LLMs must be versatile, i.e., capable of handling
065   a variety of use cases, efficient, i.e., requiring the least computational effort, and portable, i.e.,
066   supporting the protocol should require the least human effort possible. The above-mentioned issues
067   constitute the Agent Communication Trilemma, which we expand in Section 3.
068   In light of this, the aim of this paper is the following:
069
070       Key Contribution
071
          We design and implement a communication protocol between heterogeneous LLM-powered
072
          agents and assess its feasibility and scalability for solving high-order tasks.
073
074
075   We sidestep the Trilemma with Agora, a meta protocol that relies on the dual use of structured data
076   for frequent communications and natural language for infrequent ones. With Agora, we instantiate
077   large networks of LLM-powered agents that solve complex tasks autonomously by leveraging effi-
078   cient communications schemas. In such networks, we observe agents develop an emergent fully
079   automated protocol to solve a complex task starting from an instruction expressed in natural
080   language. We believe that this observation can serve as a basis to renew interest in emergent proto-
081   cols/languages in large networks of LLMs (Lazaridou et al., 2018; Chaabouni et al., 2019; Lazaridou
082
      & Baroni, 2020; Chaabouni et al., 2022).
083   The paper is structured as follows. We first outline the key challenges that constitute the Agent
084   Communication Trilemma (Section 3); we then detail how Agora addresses the Trilemma and serves
085   as a communication protocol for networks of LLMs (Section 4). Finally, in Section 5, we provide
086   two fully functional demos2 : the former, with two agents, to clarify Agora’s operating principles; the
087   latter, with 100, to prove Agora’s scalability and show the emergence of self-organising behaviours.

      3.1   V ERSATILE VS . P ORTABLE C OMMUNICATION
148
149
      In networks of agents, versatility and portability are at tension for two fundamental reasons (Olivé,
150   2007). A prerequisite for two agents who communicate is (1) a shared conceptual understanding of
151   the topic on which they communicate. For instance, two agents can communicate about the weather
152   if they both ‘know’ what it means to be sunny, rainy and overcast. For example, they should share
153   a similar notion of describing and measuring temperature (e.g., in degrees Celsius). In addition,
154   (2) agents must encode and decode messages in a way that is intelligible for both. Continuing
155   the weather example, if two agents exchange data using JSON objects, both the sender and the
156   receiver must know the syntax (e.g., the keys of a JSON object, such as temperature) and the
157   semantics (e.g. temperature is a 32-bit floating point value representing the temperature, in
158
      central London, as measured in degrees Celsius) of the exchanged messages.
159   In complex scenarios, defining routines whose syntax and semantics satisfy requisites (1) and (2)
160   may be difficult. For example, a programmer has to manually implement a method to decode (or
161   decode) messages to (or from) other agents. Additionally, the programmer must explicitly instruct
      the agent about how to manipulate and reason about the message content, often by interpreting API


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      documentation describing the semantics of the message. Therefore, there is a trade-off between the
163   breadth of messages (versatility) and the implementation cost (portability).
164
165   An example of high-portability, low-versatility is the Open Banking Platform (OBP), which uses a
166
      well-defined Open API schema for data transfer (OBL, 2024). OBP is highly portable because it
      uses a fixed range of well-known concepts which developers can implement; however, it is restricted
167
      to discussing a narrow domain of banking data and is thus not versatile. On the other end of the
168
      spectrum, rules-based Semantic Web agents (Berners-Lee et al., 2001) that exchange RDF (Beckett
169   et al., 2014) encoded documents are highly versatile since ontologies (Wooldridge, 2009, p. 180)
170   enable the description of structured relations between essentially any concept. Still, they require
171   developers to program agents to implement the specific ontologies used by the network (e.g., if a set
172   of RDF triples states that the temperature is 38°C, an agent must be able to interpret the concepts of
173   “temperature” and “Celsius”).
174
175
      3.2   E FFICIENT VS . V ERSATILE AND P ORTABLE C OMMUNICATION
176
177
      As previously mentioned, rule-based agents excel at the tasks they are designed to solve but hardly
178   adapt to new environments. Decades of research in reinforcement learning (Sutton, 2018) and then
179   in deep reinforcement learning (Arulkumaran et al., 2017; Henderson et al., 2018), introduced a
180   paradigm where agents learn to optimise their reward as proxy of the task we want them to solve.
181   Agentic-LLMs, i.e., multi-agent systems powered by language models, is a recent paradigm for
182   machine-to-machine communication that relies mostly on their proficiency at handling natural lan-
183   guage and following instructions (Li et al., 2023a).
184   Natural language is highly expressive, making it a suitable choice for versatile communication (Rus-
185   sell & Norvig, 2016). Additionally, LLMs trained on massive corpora seem to develop an implicit
186   understanding of various concepts that abstracts and makes communication independent from
187   their internal architecture. Moreover, LLMs can integrate external tools, write code and invoke
188   APIs with relatively little or no training (Schick et al., 2024), since the only requirement is a natural-
189   language description of the tool and its parameters.
190   Conversely, natural language as a communication medium has two major drawbacks. While en-
191   gineering and hardware improvements (Dubey et al., 2024b) mitigate costs over time, the compu-
192   tational requirements of invoking an LLM dwarf those of comparable APIs, representing a major
193   bottleneck for scaling networks of LLMs. On the other hand, using closed-source pay-per-usage
194   LLMs hosted by third parties is expensive and raises concerns in terms of replicability of the re-
195   sults (La Malfa et al., 2023). Additionally, natural language is inherently ambiguous: while LLMs
196   have a certain degree of “common sense” to fulfil requests, non-determinism and natural language
197   specifics leave space for errors that routines minimise (for instance, if someone asks for the temper-
198
      ature in Fahrenheit and the agent has a tool that returns the temperature in Celsius, the model must
      know that Celsius and Fahrenheit are both units of measure for temperature). These factors make
199
      LLMs and natural language more prone to errors than other alternatives like handwritten APIs.
200
201   In conclusion, RESTful APIs (efficient), RDF tuples (portable) and natural language (versatile)
202   are all trade-offs in the Trilemma. While some approaches are more useful in practice than others,
203   the fact that no communication format achieves all three properties simultaneously suggests that we
204
      need a hybrid communication protocol that leverages all of them. The next section outlines our
      solution.

251   4.1   C OMMUNICATION IN ( AN ) AGORA
252
253   Agora introduces a machine-readable way to transfer and refer to protocols, namely the protocol
254   documents (PDs). A PD is a plain-text description of a communication protocol.3 PDs are self-
255
      contained, implementation-agnostic, and contain everything an agent needs to support a protocol:
      this means that most descriptions of existing protocols, such as RFCs, are also suitable PDs. How-
256
      ever, instead of relying on a central body to assign identifiers, a PD is uniquely identified by its hash
257
      (for multiplexing).
258
259   In Agora, the most frequent communications have dedicated efficient routines, and the least frequent
260   ones use inefficient but flexible LLMs and natural language. In particular:
261
             • When possible, frequent communications are handled through traditional protocols, for
262            which there are standard, human-written implementations (e.g., OBP);
263
             • For communications that happen less frequently (or for which there are no standard pro-
264
               tocols), agents can use structured data as an exchange medium (which can be handled by
265
               LLM-written routines);
266
             • For communications that might be frequent for one side but not the other, the agents still
267
               use structured data, but one side can choose to use an LLM, while the other uses a routine;
268

287   It is entirely up to the agent to handle a query using a human-written routine, an LLM-written

291   Hierarchical communications support any form of communication (maximum versatility) , al-

293   LLMs can implement routines on their own (since PDs fully describe the syntax and semantics of a
294   protocol), human programmers only need to provide an overview of the tools the agent has access
295   to, which means that the implementation effort required on the human side is minimal (maximum
296   portability). In other words, Agora sidesteps the Communication Trilemma by employing rou-
297   tines for frequent requests and resorting to natural language when agents need to negotiate efficient
298
      ways to solve a problem or errors occur.

300   4.2   A N E XAMPLE OF C OMMUNICATION OVER AGORA
301
302   We now describe how two agents, Alice and Bob, can efficiently communicate over Agora using a
303   PD routine, as illustrated in Figure 3. Alice initially sends a query with the hash of its corresponding
304   PD. Bob uses the hash to determine if he has a corresponding routine. If so, he calls it and handles
305   the communication without invoking the LLM. Otherwise, Bob handles the response with the LLM
306   itself.
307   If Bob uses an LLM to reply several times to queries that follow a given protocol over time, to the
308   point where using an LLM every time becomes expensive, he can use the LLM to write a routine
309   that handles future communications.
310
      If the routine fails or the communication is a one-off instance that does not require a protocol, Alice
311
      and Bob use natural language, which is again handled by the LLM. Natural language is also available
312
      to bootstrap communication between nodes that have never interacted before, as well as to negotiate
313   new protocols. That said, the lower cost of routines and the lack of ambiguity are strong incentives
314   for agents to prefer structured data.
315
316
      Note that PDs can be shared with other nodes in the network, which means that two agents that have
      never interacted before can use protocols developed by other agents.

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
352
      The design of Agora for our working demos follows three key principles:
353
354
             • Minimality. Agora enforces the basic standards that allow for efficient negotiation and use
355            of protocols, leaving everything else to PDs or other higher-order standards;
356
357          • Decentralisation. Agora does not rely on central authorities, with any collection of nodes
358            being able to use Agora independently;
359          • Full backward compatibility. Agora supports existing communication protocols and
360            schemas such as OpenAPI and JSON-Schema.
361
362   From a practical point of view, Agora uses HTTPS as base communication layer and JSON as
363   format to exchange metadata. When sending a message in a given protocol, an agent sends a JSON
364   document with three keys: the protocol hash, the body of the request formatted according to the
365   protocol, and a non-empty list of sources from which the protocol can be downloaded. The receiver
366   downloads the PD from its preferred source and, upon checking that the hash matches, stores it
367   for future uses. This hash-based identification system ensures that any node can reference any PD
368
      without relying on a central authority to assign identifiers. Where PDs are stored is entirely up to the
      agents; aside from regular cloud storage, hash-based indexing makes decentralised storage options
369
      (such as IPFS Benet (2014)) viable. Additionally, since essentially all protocols can be stored as
370
      PDs, Agora has full backwards compatibility with existing protocols (although human programmers
371   are encouraged to provide existing, standardised implementations instead of having the LLM re-
372   implement them from scratch).
373
374
      To simplify negotiation, an agent can expose an endpoint with a list of supported protocols: a poten-
      tial sender can thus compare the list with its own to automatically determine if there is a common
375
      protocol. The sender can also use a potentially unsupported protocol, although the receiver can
376
      choose to reject it by returning a predefined error message.
377
      Refer to Appendix B for a more formal description of Agora.

      5.2     D EMO : R ETRIEVING W EATHER DATA
379
380   Consider two agents, Alice and Bob. Alice is a Llama-3-405B (Dubey et al., 2024b) powered agent
381   managing the bookings of a guided tour service in London.5 Bob is a GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023)
382   agent for weather service that provides weather forecasts for a given date and location. As part of
383   the user interaction loop, Alice notifies the user if heavy raining is expected on a booked date.
384
      To check the weather, she initially uses her LLM to send a natural language query to Bob (phase
385   A1):
386
387         Alice - Natural Language
388
389
            What is the weather forecast for London, UK on 2024-09-27?
390
391
      Bob uses his Toolformer LLM (Schick et al., 2024) to query his database (phase B1) and returns a
392   natural language reply (phase B2):
393
394         Bob - Natural Language
395
396         The weather forecast for London, UK, on 2024-09-27 is as follows:
397
            “Rainy, 11 degrees Celsius, with a precipitation of 12 mm.”
398
399
      Over time, the cost of invoking an LLM for phases A1 and B2 dominate all the other costs; Al-
400   ice and Bob thus decide to develop a protocol. Alice checks if Bob already supports a suitable
401   protocol but finds none. Therefore, she decides to negotiate a protocol with Bob. After a few
402   rounds of negotiation, Alice and Bob agree on the following protocol: Alice sends a JSON docu-
403   ment with two fields, location and date, and Bob replies with a JSON document containing
404   three fields, namely temperature (in degrees Celsius), precipitation (in millimetres), and
405   weatherCondition (one of “sunny”, “cloudy”, “rainy” and “snowy”). From there on, Alice
406   specifies the protocol hash when performing a query. An example of exchanged message (excluding
407   Agora’s metadata) is:
408         Alice - PD
409
410         {"location": "London, UK", "date": "2024-09-27"}
411
412
413   Both Alice and Bob independently decide to write a routine to handle their side of the communica-
414   tion. From now on, Alice and Bob do not need to use the LLM to transmit traffic data: a routine
415
      now automates phases A1, B1 and B2 and leverages the costs of invoking the respective LLMs.
416
417   A cost analysis. In our demo, negotiating the protocol and implementing the routines cost 0.043
418   USD in API calls, compared to an average cost of 0.020 USD for a natural-language exchange. This
419   means that, as long as Alice and Bob use the agreed-upon protocol more than twice, Agora reduces
420
      the overall cost. Please refer to Appendix C for a transcription of the negotiation process and the
      final protocol.
421
422   As a final note, we stress that the entire communication happened without human intervention.
423   Additionally, should Bob become unavailable, Alice can simply reuse the PD with a new node that
424   may use a different LLM/database/technology stack.

450   We design a network of 85 assistant agents interacting with 15 server agents, all powered by LLMs.
451   The server agents offer various services, such as booking hotel rooms, calling taxis, ordering food,
452   etc. An example of a sub-network for food delivery is sketched in Figure 4, left. Their specialisation
453   is handled via prompting, as in Deshpande et al. (2023); Joshi et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023a). As part
454   of their workflow, server agents must interact with several tools and databases; additionally, some
455   servers need to interact with other servers to complete assistants’ requests (e.g., taxi services use the
456
      traffic data agent to adjust estimated fares for a run). We bootstrap the network by leveraging the
      underlying communication layer (as described in Section 4 and Figure 2) and inform the nodes of
457

458
      agents (e.g. the Taxi Service server knows that the server on port 5007 is a traffic server, but it does
459   not know how to communicate with it and what information it requires);
460
461   To showcase the portability of Agora throughout the network, we use different database technologies
462
      (SQL and MongoDB) and different LLMs, both open- and closed-source (GPT-4o, Llama-3-405B,
      and Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024)). We then generate 1000 random queries, which range from
463
      simple ones, such as requesting today’s weather, to more complex ones, like booking rooms in ski
464
      resorts, buying tickets for movies, ordering one of each dish from a menu, and so on. For each query,
465   assistants receive a JSON document (which represents the task data) and are tasked with fulfilling
466   the request and returning a parsed response that follows a given schema. Queries are distributed
467   among assistants following a Pareto distribution, to simulate some assistants sending significantly
468   more requests than others. Each node can also read and share PDs to one of three protocol databases.
469   Overall, these design decisions result in a very heterogeneous network, testing the limits of Agora.
470   Refer to Appendix D for further implementation details.
471
472   Emergent protocols in large networks. Once the connections are established and the networks
473   can send and receive messages, we observe several noteworthy behaviours. As PDs are progressively
474   shared between agents (see Figure 5b), we observe the emergence of a decentralised consensus on
475
      the appropriate protocols for a given task. An example of this behaviour involves ordering food
      from restaurants: an agent queries another to request food to be delivered to a certain address. The
476
      restaurant agent requests a delivery driver from a food delivery service, who, in turn, checks with
477
      the traffic data agent to see if the traffic is smooth enough to fulfil the delivery. None of the agents
478   know each other’s roles and the protocols involved beyond their immediate communication. Still,
479   the interaction of the various agents creates an automated workflow that takes care of everything.
480   The emergence of such a protocol is illustrated in Figure 4 (right). In contrast with some recent
481   literature on the emergence of complex protocols (Chaabouni et al., 2019), we observe that with
482   the proper incentives (i.e., efficiency), agents in Agora escape the inefficient trap of committing to
483   longer messages in large scale communications.

      6              C ONCLUSIONS
513
514
      In this paper, we introduced Agora, a meta protocol that sidesteps the Agent Communication
515
      Trilemma by using a mix of natural language and structured protocols. We showed that Agora
516   agents can negotiate, implement and use protocols, creating self-organising networks that solve
517   complex tasks. Additionally, we demonstrated the scalability of Agora by testing a 100-agent demo
518   and achieving a five-fold reduction in costs compared to natural language-only communication. Our
519   results showcase the power of negotiation as a basis for efficient, scalable, and decentralised agent
520   networks. As LLMs continue to improve and as interactions between them increase, LLM-powered
521   agent networks have the potential to surpass the scale limitations of single LLMs. Developing
522   frameworks and protocols that enable decentralised, flexible and efficient communication, either
523   through Agora or other technologies, can lay the foundations for a future where complex activities
524
      are partially, if not fully, automated by LLMs.

       B     AGORA S PECIFICATION
1027
1028
       In this section, we provide a more formal description of Agora.
1029
1030
       B.1   T RANSACTIONS
1031
1032   An Agora transaction operates as follows. Suppose that an agent, Alice, is trying to communicate
1033   with another agent Bob:
1034
1035          • Alice sends to Bob over HTTPS a JSON document containing three fields:
1036              – protocolHash: The hash of the protocol document. If natural language is used,
1037                then the value of protocolHash is null;
1038              – protocolSources: A list of URIs where the protocol document can be found.
1039                Must be empty if protocolHash is null and non-empty otherwise;
1040              – body: A string containing the body of the request as specified by the given protocol.
1041
              • If Bob does not have the protocol document, he fetches it (either from the sources provided
1042            by Alice or from another repository);
1043
              • If Bob is unable to use the protocol, he returns a JSON document with one field, namely
1044
                status, which is equal to “rejected”;
1045
1046          • Otherwise, Bob computes the response using the LLM, routines, or a combination of both;
1047          • Bob sends as response a JSON document with the following fields:
1048              – status: a string indicating the status of the response (can be “success” or “failure”);
1049              – body: the response returned by the agent.
1050
              • Note that "status":"failure" must be used only for errors that are not covered by
1051
                the protocol document (e.g., the agent failing to instantiate the LLM); when the protocol
1052
                prescribes how to handle an error, the agent should return "status":"success" and
1053            the correct error message as body.
1054
1055
       B.2   P ROTOCOL D OCUMENTS
1056
1057   A protocol document is identified by its SHA1 hash. Protocol documents can refer to other protocol
1058   documents by adding a preamble containing the protocol hash and at least one source for the protocol
1059   document. Further discussions concerning the optimal way to reference other protocol documents
1060   is left to future work.
1061
1062   B.3   P REFERRED P ROTOCOLS
1063
1064
       In order to signal to other agents which protocols are supported by default, an agent can expose
       an endpoint /.wellknown. This endpoint returns a JSON object where each key is a supported
1065
       protocol hash and each value is a non-empty list of protocol sources.

1244   D.1   I MPLEMENTATION N OTES
1245
1246   We implemented our demo in Python, using Flask servers as basis for our agents. Each agent is
1247   either a user or a server:
1248
              • Users receive a random task, some randomly generated data and a description of the task
1249
                data (including its schema). Their objective is to execute the requested action and return a
1250
                reply according to a certain schema. This allows us to generate a large number of queries
1251            without needing to handcraft them. Note that all tasks are single-round, i.e. they can be
1252            fulfilled in one round of communication;
1253
              • Servers receive queries from other users and reply to them using a combination of three
1254            types of tools:
1255
                   – Database tools, which involve connecting to a personal SQL or MongoDB database
1256
                      (assigned at random). Depending on the server, some databases are initialised with
1257                  dummy data;
1258
                   – Mock tools, which are simplifications of actual tools (e.g., for taxi service agents, the
1259                  assignTaxi tool is a mock tool that, instead of actually sending a taxi to a location,
1260                  mimics the request flow);
1261               – External tools, which are tools that enable the agent to start a Agora communica-
1262                  tion with a predefined server, although no information about the respective agents’
1263                  schema is provided. In other words, the skiLodge agent can open a channel with
1264                  the weatherService agent
1265
       Moreover, we added three protocol databases, which are simple Flask servers that host protocol
1266
       documents. The first protocol database is a peer with the second one, the latter of which is also a
1267
       peer with the third protocol database (but the first protocol database is not a peer of the third one).
1268   Every 10 executed queries, one protocol databases shares its protocol documents with its peers. This
1269   simulates the propagation of protocol documents between different databases.
1270
1271   Picking a Protocol Users track the number of communications with a given server about a certain
1272   type of task until it hits one of two thresholds: one for using a protocol instead of natural language
1273   and one for negotiating a protocol ex novo.
1274
       When the first threshold is hit, the user invokes the LLM to check if either the server or the reference
1275   protocol database (which is randomly assigned to the user at the start of the demo) already have
1276   a suitable protocol. If there are none, the user continues using natural language until the second
1277   threshold is hit: in that case, the user begins a negotiation with the server and submits the final
1278   protocol to the reference protocol database.
1279
       Similarly, each server has a counter that tracks the number of natural language communications
1280
       with any user since the last negotiation. Once the counter hits a threshold, the server requests a
1281   negotiation with the user, regardless of how many of the tracked queries were sent by the current
1282   user. After negotiation, the counter is reset.
1283
1284
       In our demo, we set the thresholds for the user to respectively 3 and 5 communications, and the
       threshold for the server to 10.
1285

       APIs For GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro, we used respectively the OpenAI and Google API. For
1287
       Llama 3 405b, we used the SambaNova API. Prices per million tokens are reported in Table 1.

1289   Bootstrapping Quality-of-Life Extensions For the sake of bootstrapping the network, while im-
1290   plementing the demo we added two features to our nodes:
1291
1292          • Providing each node with a simple protocol for multi-round communication in natural lan-
1293            guage;
1294          • Allowing the protocol document to include machine-readable metadata, such as the name
1295            or a short description of the protocol. This helps an agent to determine quickly which
                protocols, among a list of potential protocols, can be suitable for a certain task.

1310   Preliminary Tests We first ran a series of qualitative tests to determine which among the consid-
1311   ered LLMs (OpenAI GPT 4o, Llama 3 405b, Gemini 1.5 Pro) were the most suitable for negotiation
1312   and programming. We found that while all three LLMs were capable of negotiating and implement-
1313   ing protocols, GPT 4o was the most robust, followed by the Llama 3 405b and finally Gemini 1.5
1314
       Pro. Surprisingly, the main factor behind the brittleness of Gemini 1.5 Pro was not the model’s
       inherent performance, but rather the lack of robustness of the API itself: even with tailored retry
1315
       systems, the API sometimes failed to respond in a nondeterministic manner (i.e. the same query
1316
       would at times succeed and at times fail). We believe that our experience was due to temporary
1317   server issues, rather than fundamental problems with the model.
