009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-Form Question Answering (LFQA) refers to generating in-depth,
012            paragraph-level responses to open-ended questions. Although lots of LFQA meth-
013            ods are developed, evaluating LFQA effectively and efficiently remains chal-
014            lenging due to its high complexity and cost. Therefore, there is no standard
015            benchmark for LFQA evaluation till now. To address this gap, we make the
016            first attempt by proposing a well-constructed, reference-based benchmark named
017            Chinese exAmination for LFQA Evaluation (CALF), aiming to rigorously assess
018            the performance of automatic evaluation metrics for LFQA. The CALF bench-
019
               mark is derived from Chinese examination questions that have been translated
               into English. It includes up to 1476 examples consisting of knowledge-intensive
020
               and nuanced responses. Our evaluation comprises three different settings to ana-
021
               lyze the behavior of automatic metrics comprehensively. We conducted extensive
022            experiments on 7 traditional evaluation metrics, 3 prompt-based metrics, and 3
023            trained evaluation metrics, and tested on agent systems for the LFQA evaluation.
024            The results reveal that none of the current automatic evaluation metrics shows
025            comparable performances with humans, indicating that they cannot capture dense
026            information contained in long-form responses well. In addition, we provide a
027            detailed analysis of the reasons why automatic evaluation metrics fail when eval-
028            uating LFQA, offering valuable insights to advance LFQA evaluation systems.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Long-form Question Answering (LFQA) (Fan et al., 2019) targets at generating in-depth, paragraph-
034   level responses to open-ended questions. It requires models to have comprehensive domain-specific
035   knowledge or use evidence from retrieved documents (Nakano et al., 2022; Akash et al., 2023)
036   to provide accurate and relevant answers. Despite increased efforts have been put to enhance the
037   reasonableness and completeness of long-form answers, developing automatic, reliable, and human-
038   aligned evaluation metrics for LFQA is still unexplored. This oversight highlights the necessity for
039
      more robust approaches that can accurately evaluate the quality of generated responses in a way that
      aligns with human judgment and ensures trustworthiness.
040
041   However, evaluating LFQA is particularly challenging, as paragraph-level answers can overwhelm
042   evaluators, requiring them to have a comprehensive understanding of the domain. Previous manual
043   evaluations tend to rely on crowd-sourced workers for annotation, but the limited domain expertise
044   inevitably causes low reliability. For automatic evaluation for LFQA, ROUGE (Lin, 2004) is always
045
      used to gauge the quality of the response relative to a reference. However, Krishna et al. (2021) have
      criticized ROUGE for its limited informativeness in long-form contexts. Recently, Xu et al. (2023)
046
      reveals these shortcomings and advocates for the use of experts to ensure higher annotation quality
047
      and test several automatic evaluation metrics according to expert annotation. Although they analyze
048   how well automatic metrics align with expert preferences, a modest sample size and reliance on
049   unstructured and reference-free ELI5 comments severely constrain their benchmark. Also, due to
050   the advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; 2024), numerous studies begin
051   to leverage LLMs to develop more nuanced evaluation metrics (Li et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; Fan
052   et al., 2024a;b). Therefore, the potential of LLMs in LFQA evaluation remains unexplored. Beyond
053   the above efforts, our objective is to gain a comprehensive understanding of automatic evaluation
      methods for LFQA, encompassing both traditional metrics and LLM-based metrics.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
126
127   Development of LFQA LFQA (Fan et al., 2019) requires models to generate paragraph-level
128   responses to open-ended questions which is more complex compared to datasets like SQuAD (Ra-
129   jpurkar et al., 2016), TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), and NarrativeQA (Kočiský et al., 2017), where
130
      answers are primarily words or phrases extracted directly from documents. In LFQA, models must
      generate nuanced responses based on their knowledge or existing evidence documents. Several stud-
131
      ies have analyzed the discourse structure of long-form answers (Xu et al., 2022) and have sought to
132
      enhance the performance on LFQA. (Chen et al., 2023a; Akash et al., 2023).
133
134   Evaluation of LFQA The automatic evaluation of LFQA remains challenging and underexplored.
135   Initially, ROUGE (Lin, 2004) was used as an automatic evaluation metric to calculate the similarity
136   between a candidate and a reference. Later, Krishna et al. (2021) pointed out that ROUGE is not
137
      an adequately informative metric for LFQA evaluation. For human annotation, H URDLES (Krishna
      et al., 2021) and W EB GPT (Nakano et al., 2022) employed A/B testing, where crowdsourced an-
138
      notators were instructed to choose the better of two candidate answers. Since annotation of LFQA
139
      requires high expertise, the results of crowdsourced workers may be unreliable. To address the
140   gap, Xu et al. (2023) employed experts for annotation, and tested several evaluation metrics, such
141   as ROUGE (Lin, 2004), BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020), and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021),
142   on an expert-annotated dataset. Their findings validated that no existing metrics fully align with
143   human judgment. However, the dataset they used lacks expert-written references, sourced from
144   Reddit/ELI5, and is limited in scale, comprising only about 120 samples. In response to these lim-
145   itations, we propose constructing a high-quality and larger benchmark for the evaluation of LFQA.
146

148   3     M ETHODOLOGY
149   To reasonably test the evaluation ability of different metrics for LFQA when having a reference
150   to look up, we construct CALF, a comprehensive benchmark composed of different topics and
151   questions. In this section, we will describe the construction process of CALF in detail.

153   3.1   D ESIGN P RINCIPLE
154

156   ability of high-quality reference answers. These references serve as benchmarks, allowing evaluators
157   to determine the precision and completeness of responses generated by the QA systems. Specifically,
158
      a reference answer provides a baseline to assess whether the response captures all necessary details
      and adheres closely to the factual correctness established in the reference. This dual check ensures
159
      that the generated answer is not only correct but also exhaustive in covering the question’s scope.
160
161   However, crafting such references is both challenging and resource-intensive. Producing a reliable
      reference requires domain experts who have a vast knowledge and understanding of the subject

188   Marking Platforms (OMP). These responses are typically more nuanced and knowledge-intensive
189   compared to those found in ELI5, reflecting the rigorous academic standards and detailed content
190   required in examination settings.

192   Diverse Benchmark To guarantee a diverse and representative benchmark for our LFQA sys-
193   tem, we meticulously collect questions in six distinct domains: history, politics, geography, law,
194
      medicine, and psychology. For each domain, we draw from a pool of over 20 examination papers,
      ensuring a broad spectrum of topics and difficulty levels.
195
196   Our data in history, politics, and geography are sourced from CEESQ. Here, we have access to
197   student responses via the OMP. From this platform, we select pairs of student responses that are
198   particularly clear and detailed, enhancing the quality of our dataset. In addition, we employ LLMs
199   to generate model responses, enabling us to rigorously test and compare the evaluation capabilities of
200
      various metrics between human-generated and model-generated responses. For the domains of law,
      medicine, and psychology, we do not use real student responses, but rely solely on model-generated
201
      answer pairs. This approach allows us to focus on assessing the performance of our LFQA system
202
      in generating high-quality, accurate responses in highly specialized and technical fields.

206   CALF consists of 1476 examples from Geography, Politics, History, Law, Medicine, and Psychol-

210   3.3   DATA P ROCESSING
211
212   The data processing pipeline can be divided into three phases: Data Collection, Data Transforma-
213   tion, and Model Response Generation.
214
215   Data Collection We gather examination papers primarily in image format and employ Optical
      Character Recognition (OCR) systems to meticulously extract various components such as contexts,


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      questions, student responses, and references. For the fields of Law, Medicine, and Psychology,
217   where student responses are inaccessible, we focus exclusively on extracting contexts, questions,
218   and references. The OCR system is conducted using the API provided by VolcEngine. After OCR
219   processing, we conduct a thorough manual review to correct any recognition errors.
220
221   Data Transformation References, when sourced from examination settings, are meticulously
222   structured into concise information points, a format that facilitates scoring for educators. However,
223   unlike these examination-derived references, those collected from evidence documents in practical
224   applications often take the form of natural language narratives, incorporating examples, summaries,
225   and other auxiliary information (Xu et al., 2022). To bridge this gap and enhance the utility of our
226   references, we employ GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) to enrich our concise references with detailed ex-
227   planations and expanded arguments. We list detailed explanations and human annotations of our
228   reference transformation process in Appendix B.2. In addition, we provide an example in Appendix
229
      B.3. The transformation prompt is shown in Appendix C.7.
230
231   Model Response Generation To rigorously assess the performance of LFQA systems, our bench-
232   mark employs three comparison modes for each question, i.e., human vs. human, human vs. model,
233
      and model vs. model in Geography, History, and Politics, and model vs. model exclusively in Law,
      Medicine, and Psychology. That is, the two responses to be compared are either human-written or
234
      model-generated. When generating model responses, we focus on evaluating whether LLMs can
235
      understand the semantic meaning of texts well and properly select the better response. Therefore,
236   we do not impose strict requirements on answer quality. Instead, we ensure the difficulty of CALF
237   by selecting models with similar ranking in the LMSYS Arena (Chiang et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
238   2023; 2024b). Specifically, we leverage Llama-3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) and GPT-3.5-turbo-1106-
239   preview (OpenAI, 2023) for response generation. For model-generated answers, we prompt using
240   ”Generate reasonable answers to the following questions. Use references or examples if needed”,
241   and the generation temperature is set to 1.0 to encourage diverse and creative responses.
242
243   3.4   H UMAN A NNOTATION
244
245   The Human Annotation Process can be separated into the following steps: Annotator Decision,
246   Annotation Setting, Annotation Process, and After-Annotation Validation.
247
248   Annotator Decision LFQA evaluation suffer from distinct challenges. Firstly, paragraph-level
249   responses can overwhelm annotators, leading to a loss of focus. Secondly, annotators must have
250   deep domain knowledge to accurately judge responses against references. Lastly, the syntactic and
251   semantic complexities of long-form responses often intertwine correct and incorrect information
252   within single sentences. To address these issues, we first hire five annotators from relevant aspects
253
      or who have taken relevant courses, including Computer Science, Law, and Medicine. Then we
      provide them with clear and detailed annotation recipes for better quality control.
254
255
256   Annotation Setting Guided by Xu et al. (2023), our evaluation criteria mainly focus on factual-
257
      ity, completeness, and clarity according to the reference. Unlike typical A/B testing, our method
      employs a triple-choice format to better capture the subtle differences between answers, as they of-
258
      ten show comparable levels of information overlap with the reference, with additional information
259
      useless or verbose according to the central topic.
260
261
      Annotation Process The annotators assess two responses against a given reference and select
262
      the more informative and complete answer or declare a ”tie” if both are comparable. The pro-
263
      cess includes Identify Key Information, Check for Key Information in Responses, Handling
264   Responses, and Compare Overlapping Information. During the process, we treat a piece of in-
265   formation as the basic unit. Firstly, annotators extract the key information needed to answer the
266   question and check whether the responses under evaluation contain similar statements. If a similar
267   one is present, they further assess whether this statement is fully correct, partially correct, or entirely
268   incorrect. Finally, they will select a better one based on the overlapped information. Our annota-
269   tion guidelines are provided in Appendix B.1. Detailed explanations and examples of fully correct,
      partially correct, and entirely incorrect answers can be found in Appendix B.1 and Appendix B.3.

      4.2      E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
392
393   The overall experimental results are shown in Table 3. We also report our results without tied QA
394   pairs in Appendix C.2.
395
396   CALF as a Challenging Benchmark The CALF benchmark poses significant challenges for all
397   existing automatic evaluation metrics for LFQA, with accuracy rates consistently falling below 65%,
398   suggesting performance levels not much better than random guessing. All traditional metrics are not
399   suitable for LFQA evaluation due to their static property, thus failing to handle flexible outputs. Even
400   advanced models like GPT-4o struggle to consistently make correct evaluations, though it remains
401
      the most promising among the available automatic metrics.
402
      To provide a more detailed analysis, we report the accuracy rates for three comparison
      modes—human vs. human, human vs. model, and model vs. model—within the domains of ge-
403
      ography, politics, and history, as shown in Table 4.
404
405   The results from these detailed experiments reveal that a majority of evaluation metrics perform best
406   in the human vs. human setting and fail in the Model vs. Model setting and Human vs. Model
407   setting. We hypothesize that human-written answers tend to be more precise and concise, making
408
      it knowledge-intensive and easier for the evaluation metrics to distinguish the better answer (Saito
      et al., 2023). Furthermore, there is a notable variation in performance across different domains,
409
      particularly with LLM-based metrics. For instance, GPT-4o achieves an accuracy of only 35% in
410
      Geography compared to 59% in Law at the model vs model setting.
411
412   CoT and G-Eval Provide Limited Improvement for LLMs in LFQA Evaluation To implement
413   CoT, we prompt the LLMs to ”think step by step.” This method generally yields better performance
414   compared to vanilla prompts. However, the observed performance gains are not substantial, sug-
415   gesting that CoT alone is insufficient for significantly enhancing LFQA evaluation.
416
      Additionally, we employ G-Eval by generating responses from LLMs five times and selecting the
417
      most frequent answer. The results show that G-Eval does not consistently improve performance,
418   further suggesting that LLMs may struggle to robustly determine the better answer (Zheng et al.,
419   2024a). This underlines the need for more advanced techniques to enhance the reliability and accu-
420   racy of LFQA evaluations.
421
      To further assess the effectiveness of G-Eval (Liu et al., 2023b), we analyze the consistency of the
422
      model’s responses across five iterations. The results, presented in Figure 2, suggest that by sam-
423
      pling multiple times, the model can make correct decisions, but the consistency of these decisions
424   across iterations is low. This indicates that while the model has the potential to be used for LFQA
425   evaluation, its ability to consistently choose the correct answer with higher reliability—or ”confi-
426   dence”—needs to be improved.
427

      6   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518
      Here, we discuss the automatic evaluation of LFQA and the feature work in this area.
519
520
      Evaluation of LFQA Remains Challenging Despite the growing use of LLMs for evaluation
      tasks, our findings demonstrate that relying solely on LLMs for LFQA evaluation is challenging
521
      and unreliable. Also, traditional metrics also fall short in this context. Therefore, the exploration
522
      of semantic-sensitive evaluation metrics is essential for accurate LFQA assessment. Our bench-
523   mark, built on expert-written questions and references, lays a solid foundation for future research in
524   developing more effective evaluation methods.
525
526   Inclusion of Multi-Disciplines In this work, we primarily focus on plain text records within the
527   realm of social sciences, as we believe that most LFQA tasks originate in this format and domain.
528   However, recognizing that some LFQA questions may pertain to natural sciences, we plan to expand
529   our dataset to include subjects like mathematics and physics. By incorporating question types such
530   as theorem explanations, we aim to enhance the diversity and comprehensiveness of our benchmark,
531   making it more authoritative and representative across multiple disciplines.
532
533   7   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We present CALF, a comprehensive benchmark consisting of up to 1,500 records from six subjects,
536   sourced from various Chinese examinations. This benchmark is designed to evaluate the effective-
537   ness of current LFQA evaluation metrics including traditional metrics, prompt-based metrics, and
538   trained metrics, and shed light on the evaluation of LFQA. Additionally, we conduct experiments
539   using agent-system evaluation metrics. Our future work will focus on developing semantic-sensitive
      evaluation metrics and expanding the dataset to include a broader range of disciplines.

829   B.1   A NNOTATION R ECIPE
830
831   Annotation Process: When provided with a question, a reference, and two candidate answers, the
832
      evaluation process should proceed as follows:
833
            1. Identify Key Information: We begin by listing all the key pieces of information required
834
               to answer the question based on the reference. Here, we treat a piece of information as our
835            basic unit and use them for further selection.
836
            2. Check for Key Information in Responses: For each piece of key information in the ref-
837
               erence, we determine whether it is included in each of the candidate answers. Since key
838
               information may consist of a main argument accompanied by detailed explanations, we
839            focus only on whether the main argument is present in the answers, disregarding the de-
840            tailed descriptions. If an answer includes the main argument but provides its own detailed
841            explanation, it should still be considered correct.
842
            3. Handling Responses: For similar statement pairs in reference and response, we categorize
843            them into three groups, i.e., Totally Correct, Partially Correct, and Totally Wrong.
844
845
                  • Totally Correct: If an answer contains information that is completely accurate and di-
                    rectly relevant to the question, it is considered totally correct. Such answers contribute
846
                    fully to the overlapping information score.
847
                  • Partially Correct: If an answer contains information that is only partially accurate
848
                    or relevant, it is considered partially correct. The impact of this information on the
849
                    overlapping score is reduced, depending on the extent of correctness and relevance.
850
                  • Totally Wrong: If an answer contains information that is entirely incorrect or irrel-
851
                    evant to the question, it is considered totally wrong. Such answers should have their
852                 overlapping information score set to 0.
853
            4. Compare Overlapping Information: The final comparison between the two answers will
854
               be based on the amount of overlapping key information. For example, if the reference
855
               contains three key pieces of information, and Answer A contains two of them while Answer
856            B contains one, Answer A will be considered the better response.
857

             Overall, an instigator in Chinese criminal law refers to an individual who incites others to commit a crime. Specifically, such individuals intentionally lead or
1255         provoke others to decide to carry out criminal activities.\n\nFirstly, the fundamental characteristic of an instigator is that they incite others to commit a crime
1256         without directly participating in the criminal behavior themselves. They play a role in inducing others to form criminal intentions. For instance, a person may
             encourage, persuade, or offer some benefit to successfully prompt another to decide to commit acts such as theft or fraud while not partaking in the actual
1257         criminal process.\n\nSecondly, certain conditions must be met to establish instigation. Subjectively, the instigator must have the intention to induce others to
             form the criminal intent and decide to commit the crime, which is known as the intent to instigate others to commit a crime. This intent must be explicit; the
1258         instigator must clearly know whom they are instigating and what crime they are instigating. Without explicit intent, instigation cannot be established;
1259         behavior that inadvertently causes others to form criminal intentions cannot constitute instigation. Objectively, the instigator must have carried out acts of
             instigation, commonly manifested as encouragement, deception, persuasion, requests, bribery, or coercion, prompting specific individuals to commit specific
1260         crimes. Whether the instigatee actualizes the criminal intent or determination and whether the instigatee actually carries out the criminal act do not affect the
             establishment of the instigator.\n\nRegarding the criminal liability of instigators, several key points are noteworthy. First, instigators are punished according
1261         to their role in the joint crime. If the instigator plays a major role in the crime process, they will be punished as the main perpetrator; if they only play a minor
1262         or auxiliary role, they will be punished according to the standard for accomplices. Although instigators typically play a major role and are generally punished
             as main perpetrators, it is possible for an instigator with a minor role to be punished as an accomplice. Second, if the instigatee does not commit the
1263         instigated crime, the instigator may still be deemed guilty but subject to lighter or reduced punishment. This scenario, usually referred to as \"attempted
             instigation,\" indicates that attempted instigation is also punishable, underscoring the independent and punishable nature of instigative behavior under
1264         Chinese criminal law. Third, instigating minors to commit crimes should result in harsher punishment, especially in cases where those under 18 are incited to
1265         commit criminal acts.\n\nThough instigators possess independent criminality or punishability, they are not an independent crime category. The instigator
             should be charged according to the specific crime instigated. For example, if the instigation pertains to robbery, the instigator should be prosecuted under
1266         robbery charges. In summary, through these three points, we can gain a deeper understanding of the provisions concerning instigators in Chinese criminal law

1267         detailed analysis based on individual cases. However, these basic principles provide a relatively comprehensive understanding of instigators. These principles
