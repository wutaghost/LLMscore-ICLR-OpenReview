009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Pruning is a widely used technique for compressing large neural networks that
012            eliminates weights that have minimal impact on the model’s performance. Current
013            pruning methods, exemplified by magnitude pruning, assign an importance score
014            to each weight based on its magnitude and remove weights with scores below
015            a certain threshold. Nonetheless, these methods often create a gap between the
016            original dense and the pruned sparse model, potentially impairing performance.
017            Especially when the sparsity ratio is high, the gap becomes more pronounced. To
018
               mitigate this issue, we introduce a method to bridge the gap left by pruning by
               utilizing a low-rank approximation of the difference between the dense and sparse
019
               matrices. Our method entails the iterative refinement of the sparse weight matrix
020
               augmented by a low-rank adjustment. This technique captures and retains the
021            essential information often lost during pruning, thereby improving the performance
022            of the pruned model. Furthermore, we offer a comprehensive theoretical analysis of
023            our approach, emphasizing its convergence properties and establishing a solid basis
024            for its efficacy. Experimental results on LLaMa models validate its effectiveness on
025            large language models across various pruning techniques and sparsity levels. Our
026            method shows significant improvements: at 50% sparsity, it reduces perplexity by
027            53.9% compared to conventional magnitude pruning on LLaMa-7B. Furthermore,
028            to achieve a specific performance target, our approach enables an 8.6% reduction
029
               in model parameters while maintaining a sparsity ratio of about 50%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Pruning is a crucial technique in the field of model compression, particularly for large language
034
      models (LLMs), which have become the cornerstone of natural language processing tasks (Devlin,
035
      2018; Brown, 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Pruning involves the removal of specific weights or
036   parameters from the neural network that are considered to have minimal impact on the overall
037   performance of the model (LeCun et al., 1989; Hassibi et al., 1993; Han et al., 2015b; Frankle &
038   Carbin, 2018; Frankle et al., 2020). This technique is especially valuable for LLMs, which often
039   contain billions of parameters and require substantial computational resources for both training and
040   inference (Han, 2017; Touvron et al., 2023; Minaee et al., 2024).
041
      One of the most representative pruning techniques is magnitude pruning, which removes weights that
042   have the smallest absolute values. This method is based on the assumption that smaller weights have
043   less effect on the network’s overall performance (Han et al., 2015a). Furthermore, as model sizes
044   continue to grow, the number of redundant parameters also increases. For LLMs with billions of
045   parameters, even half of the layers can be dropped without significantly affecting performance (Men
046   et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024). However, recent research shows that pruning can cause irreparable loss
047   of knowledge and performance drops, especially for difficult tasks, a phenomenon known as the Junk
048   DNA Hypothesis (Yin et al., 2024). This consistent degradation in performance is observed across a
049   spectrum of pruning methods, including magnitude pruning, SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023),
050
      and Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), and applies to both unstructured pruning and structured N:M pruning.
051   Conventional approaches to post-pruning recovery face a three-fold challenge: (1) Computational
052   burden: Re-training (Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Xia et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024a) and knowledge
053   distillation (Hinton, 2015; Wan et al., 2024a; Muralidharan et al., 2024) methods are computationally
      expensive and time-consuming. (2) Data and model dependency: These techniques typically require


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      either extensive datasets or access to a high-performing teacher model, which may not always be
055   feasible. (3) Sparsity inconsistency: Recent low-rank approximation methods (Li et al., 2023;
056   Mozaffari et al., 2024; Zhang & Papyan, 2024) often fail to maintain a consistent sparsity pattern,
057   making them unsuitable for structured pruning, which is crucial for hardware efficiency.
058
      In this study, we address the challenge of post-pruning recovery by approximating the dense matrix
059
      as the sum of an updated sparse matrix with a maintained sparsity pattern and a low-rank matrix. We
060
      propose an iterative refinement process that concurrently updates the sparse matrix and the low-rank
061   component in a data-free manner. This approach effectively recovers crucial information typically
062   lost during pruning. Our method features an adaptive low-rank approximation that dynamically
063   adjusts to complement the sparse matrix, enabling efficient information recovery. Unlike traditional
064   techniques that rely on large datasets or high-performing teacher models, our approach operates
065   directly on model weights, offering a computationally efficient and broadly applicable solution. This
066   approach aims to improve the performance of the pruned model without significantly increasing the
067   parameter count. Our method combines the advantages of both sparse and low-rank approximation,
068   ensuring the model maintains its efficiency while enhancing its accuracy. In addition to our empirical
069
      findings, we provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the iterative refinement process, which
      rigorously demonstrates the favorable convergence properties of our method.
070
071   Experimental results on the LLaMa models demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. When
072   applying 50% sparsity, our method achieves a 53.9% reduction in perplexity compared to conventional
073   magnitude pruning. The benefits of our approach become increasingly evident as sparsity increases:
074   at 60% sparsity, we observe a 92.0% decrease in perplexity, while at 70% sparsity, an impressive
075
      99.6% reduction is achieved. These findings highlight the effectiveness of our method, especially in
      scenarios of high sparsity where traditional approaches typically face considerable challenges.
076
077   To summarize, the main contributions of this paper are:
078
079          • In this work, we bridge the gap between the original dense and pruned sparse model by
080
               leveraging a low-rank component. This approach effectively fills the gap left by pruning,
               enhancing the model’s performance with minimal parameter increase.
081
082          • We develop an iterative algorithm that incrementally refines the sparse weight matrix
083            and incorporates the low-rank approximation. By prioritizing the preservation of weight
084
               components associated with larger singular values, our method allows for a more aggressive
               reduction of less important components, leading to a more precise approximation.
085
086          • We provide a thorough theoretical analysis of our proposed method, which offers a rigorous
087            foundation understanding of the effectiveness, convergence, and stability of our approach.
088          • We validate the effectiveness of our method on LLMs across various sparsity levels, achiev-
089            ing substantial perplexity reductions compared to baselines. This is particularly noteworthy
090            at high sparsity levels, maintaining robust improvements even as high as 70% sparsity.

096   Neural network pruning is a crucial technique for model compression, aiming to reduce parameters
097   while preserving performance. In this section, we first present the general framework for (local)
098   layer-wise pruning, outlining the key steps and components involved and it is illustrated in Fig. 1(a).
099
      Let W ∈ Rm×n represent a weight matrix, and M = M(W , D) ∈ Rm×n denote its corresponding
      importance score matrix. Here, M : Rm×n × D → Rm×n is a metric function that computes the
100
      importance scores based on the weight matrix W and an optional dataset D. The most straightforward
101
      approach is to use the magnitude of the weights as the importance score, i.e., Mij = |Wij |. More
102   sophisticated methods can be employed to capture the importance of each weight more accurately,
103   such as the SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and Wanda (Sun et al., 2023). Given a pruning
104   ratio p ∈ [0, 1], we set threshold h as the p-th percentile of M as the decision boundary for pruning.
105   Then the binary pruning mask P is obtained by Pij = I(Mij > h). The pruned sparse weight
106   matrix S is obtained by S = W ⊙ P , where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. This zeroes out
107   less important weights while retaining significant ones. An optional weight update procedure can be
      applied to refine the pruned weights further. For example, after the pruning process, a re-training

500   5   R ELATED W ORK
501
502   LLM Compression. LLMs have become essential in natural language processing tasks, but their
503   substantial size poses challenges in terms of computational resources and efficiency. Various tech-
504   niques have been proposed to compress these models while maintaining their performance, including
505
      pruning, low-rank compression, quantization, and knowledge distillation (Cheng et al., 2017; Choud-
      hary et al., 2020; Haroush et al., 2020). In this study, we focus on the pruning technique, and the
506
      low-rank approximations are utilized to recover the lost performance. Pruning LLMs with billions of
507
      parameters differs significantly from pruning smaller models (Gale et al., 2019; Frankle et al., 2020;
508   Kurtic & Alistarh, 2022), as current pruning techniques often necessitate extensive re-training after
509   the pruning process, which is prohibitively costly for LLMs (Komatsuzaki et al., 2022; Chung et al.,
510   2024; Snell et al., 2024). Pruning methods are often categorized into unstructured and structured
511   pruning (Liu et al., 2017; Fan et al., 2019; He & Xiao, 2023). Structured pruning techniques, including
512   layer pruning (Chen & Zhao, 2018; Kim et al., 2024b), channel pruning (He et al., 2017; Zhuang et al.,
513   2018), and N:M pruning (Sun et al., 2021), focus on eliminating entire neurons, layers, or N out of M
514   elements in a regular pattern. Unstructured pruning removes individual weights without considering
515   the underlying structure, which can often lead to better performance but is less hardware-friendly.
516
      Low-rank compression reduces the dimensions of the weight matrix by focusing on larger singular
      values in both the column and row spaces (Cheng et al., 2005; Idelbayev & Carreira-Perpinán, 2020).
517
      While quantization (Xiao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024) and knowledge distillation (Wan et al., 2024b)
518
      have been used to compress LLMs, they are orthogonal to our approach.
519
520   Post-Pruning Performance Recovery. To recover the performance after pruning step, several
521   post-pruning recovery techniques have been explored such as re-training the pruned model and using
522
      knowledge distillation (Muralidharan et al., 2024). However, these approaches can be computationally
      expensive and time-consuming. Recent research has focused on incorporating low-rank approxi-
523
      mations to recover the lost performance. By adding a low-rank component to the pruned model,
524
      it is possible to approximate the original dense model more closely with minimal computational
525   overhead (Li et al., 2023; Mozaffari et al., 2024; Zhang & Papyan, 2024).
526
527
528   6 C ONCLUSION
529   In this work, we present an iterative weight update algorithm for low-rank refinement of sparse-
530   pruned models. Our approach aims to bridge the performance gap between dense and pruned sparse
531   models in large language models. The proposed method offers a computationally efficient solution
532
      that does not rely on extensive datasets or high-performing teacher models, making it a practical
      choice for improving sparse model performance. A notable advantage of our method is its sparsity-
533
      preserving property, which allows for the concurrent update of the sparse matrix while maintaining its
534
      sparsity pattern and incorporating a low-rank component. This approach effectively recovers crucial
535   information lost during pruning, leading to performance recovery, especially at high sparsity ratios.
536
537   These experimental results highlight the potential of combining low-rank and sparsity in LLMs.
538
      Future work could explore methods for automatically determining the optimal rank for the low-rank
      component based on the specific characteristics of each layer or the overall model architecture.
