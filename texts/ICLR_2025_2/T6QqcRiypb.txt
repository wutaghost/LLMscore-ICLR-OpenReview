011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The persistent challenge of medical image synthesis posed by the scarcity of an-
014            notated data and the need to synthesize “missing modalities” for multi-modal
015            analysis, underscored the imperative development of effective synthesis methods.
016            Recently, the combination of Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) with latent diffusion
017            models (LDMs) has emerged as a viable approach for efficiently adapting pre-
018            trained large language models, in the medical field. However, the direct applica-
019            tion of LoRA assumes uniform ranking across all linear layers, overlooking the
020
               significance of different weight matrices, and leading to sub-optimal outcomes.
               Prior works on LoRA prioritize the reduction of trainable parameters, and there ex-
021
               ists an opportunity to further tailor this adaptation process to the intricate demands
022
               of medical image synthesis. In response, we present SeLoRA, a Self-Expanding
023            Low-Rank Adaptation module, that dynamically expands its ranking across layers
024            during training, strategically placing additional ranks on crucial layers, to allow
025            the model to elevate synthesis quality where it matters most. Our analysis shows
026            that SeLoRA strikes the best balance between synthesis quality and training effi-
027            ciency. The proposed method not only enables LDMs to fine-tune on medical data
028            efficiently but also empowers the model to achieve improved image quality with
029            minimal ranking. The code of our SeLoRA method is publicly available at this
030            link.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Foundation models Tu et al. (2024); Huang et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2023) are increasingly gaining
037   traction in medical imaging, offering a new paradigm for data processing and analysis. While most
038   foundational models are trained with large natural-image datasets Tu et al. (2024), such as ImageNet,
039
      the shortage of medical images is increasingly problematic (Deng et al., 2009). Medical image syn-
      thesis presents a valid approach to address this issue by generating synthetic images to expand and
040
      enhance scarce image datasets. Challenges in medical image synthesis have been widely explored,
041
      leading to various proposed methods. These models are typically trained from scratch for a single
042   modality like brain MRI Dalmaz et al. (2022), lung CTsMendes et al. (2023), cataract surgery sam-
043   ples Frisch et al. (2023), and others. However, training models from scratch requires long training
044   times and can lead to performance limitations due to dataset size.
045
      Recognizing the success of pre-trained foundational models on natural images, recent works have
046
      shifted towards adapting these models, such as stable diffusion Rombach et al. (2022), to enable
047
      more efficient training for medical image synthesis. For example, leveraging text-based radiology
048   reports as a condition to incorporate detailed medical information, and fine-tuning latent diffusion
049   models has achieved significant performance gains (Chambon et al., 2022a;b). Further exploration
050   involves the incorporation of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, which not only makes
051   fine-tuning more efficient but also demonstrates superior performance compared to full fine-tuning
052   (Dutt et al., 2023). In this context, we place a particular emphasis on applying the Low Rank
053   Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2022) method, a type of PEFT method, for fine-tuning stable diffusion
      in medical image synthesis.

      Originally designed for adapting large language models, LoRA hypothesizes that the weight matrix
068   updated during fine-tuning exhibits a low ‘intrinsic rank’. Therefore, it proposed to use the multipli-
069   cation of two trainable low-rank decomposition matrices to mimic the weight update for a specific
070   task, expressed as follows:
071
                                                W = W0 + AB,                                             (1)
072
073   The weight matrix, W din ×dout , is updated by adding the product of trainable low-rank decomposi-
074   tion matrices, A ∈ Rdin ×r and B ∈ Rr×dout to the frozen original weight matrix W0din ×dout . The
075   rank, r, is deliberately chosen to be significantly smaller than the dimension of the original weight.
076   Consequently, the trainable parameters under this configuration constitute a fraction of W0 ’s param-
077   eter count, achieving parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
078
      The design of LoRA introduced the challenge of selecting optimal rank. Small ranks yield sub-
079
      optimal performance, while large ranks escalate parameter count, and searching for the optimal rank
080   for each individual LoRA on different layers is computationally expensive. In most large language
081   models, where LoRA is primarily applied, the Transformer-like architecture typically have similar-
082   sized weights across layers. Therefore, LoRA proceeds with having uniform rank across all layers,
083   assuming that the updated weights also have similar ranks throughout. This simplifies the challenge
084   of selecting optimal by reducing it from searching an optimal rank for each layer to selecting a
085   single rank for the entire model. However, given our context of applying LoRA to Stable Diffusion,
086   the bottleneck structure of the Denoising U-Net within Stable Diffusion introduces a diverse range
087   of weight matrix shapes. Using a uniform rank becomes problematic, as the inherent rank of these
088
      weight matrices can vary significantly - larger matrices may require higher ranks, while smaller ones
      may need lower ranks. Consequently, selecting a uniform rank for LoRAthat is applied on LDMs
089
      may lead to suboptimal results, ultimately compromising the quality of synthesized images.
090
091   In addressing the challenge of rank selection and aiming to achieve superior synthesized image
092   quality with minimal trainable parameters, we draw inspiration from the concept of self-expanding
093   neural network Mitchell et al. (2024). Departing from approaches of setting a predefined uniform
094
      rank, our approach advocates for dynamically expanding the rank of LoRA to better align with the
      unique needs of each layer. As a result, in our work, we present Self-Expanding Low-Rank Adap-
095
      tation (SeLoRA), akin to LoRA’s structure but distinguished by the dynamic growth of ranks guided
096
      by Fisher information during training. This enables SeLoRA to flexibly adapt to the inherent char-
097   acteristics of each layer, guaranteeing enhanced medical image synthesis quality while minimizing
098   challenges related to rank adjustments.

101   2   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Low-rank adaptation has gained significant attention due to its memory-efficient nature, enabling a
104   broader community to fine-tune increasingly large models. Building upon LoRA, several research
105   directions have emerged, primarily focusing on reducing the number of trainable parameters through
106   techniques such as quantization, random weights, various product operations, and adaptive rank
107   selection. These methods are relevant to our work, as we aim not only to enable adaptive rank in
      LoRA but also to minimize the overall parameter count.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      QLoRA, for example, reduces the memory of LoRA weights by quantizing them to 4 bits using two
109   methods: 4-bit NormalFloat quantization and Double Quantization. This results in a fourfold re-
110   duction in memory usage for LoRA weights (Dettmers et al., 2023). VeRA replaced the trainable
111   low rank matrices with an frozen random matrices, and uses two trainable scaling vectors between
112   and after these frozen random matrices to adapt to the fine-tuning tasks, further reducing the param-
113   eter count (Kopiczko et al., 2024). Tied LoRA builds on VeRA by exploring various combinations
114   of shared and frozen weights for both scaling vectors and low-rank matrices (Renduchintala et al.,
115   2024). Other approaches replace the original low-rank matrix multiplication with alternative opera-
116   tions to reduce the parameter count even further. KronA, for instance, uses the Kronecker product to
117
      substitute the standard matrix multiplication in LoRA (Edalati et al., 2022), while LyCORIS incorpo-
      rates both the Kronecker and Hadamard products to create a its variant of LoRA (Yeh et al., 2024).
118
      All of these methods aim to reduce LoRA’s memory requirements through different techniques. Its
119
      important to note that these approaches are complementary to our proposed methods, suggesting
120   greater efficiency when our approaches combined with these existing techniques.
121
122
123   Adaptive Rank Selection A particularly relevant line of research to our method is adaptive rank
124
      selection, which aims to dynamically select the optimal rank for LoRA during training. This is
      especially pertinent as it addresses the critical issue of rank selection problem. Noah directly injects
125
      LoRA, adapter, and bitfit into an attention layer, using evolutionary search to identify the optimal
126
      rank and configuration (Zhang et al., 2022; Zaken et al., 2022; Houlsby et al., 2019). Similarly,
127   Generalized LoRA introduces a super-net that can mirror the structure of LoRA, scalar weights,
128   or bias weights, forming a generalized version of LoRA. It also pursues adaptive rank selection
129   through through evolutionary search during training (Chavan et al., 2023). While these approaches
130   can effectively find an optimal rank through evolutionary search, they come with trade-offs. The
131   repeated search process can significantly extend training time, and in some cases, the search explores
132   high-rank matrices, which inherently have a large number of parameters. This increased parameter
133   count can lead to higher memory usage, counteracting LoRA’s original goal of memory efficiency.
134
135   Rank Pruning Rank pruning first sets an upper bound to the rank of LoRA, and gradually prunes
136   the rank of each LoRA adapter during training to reduce the parameter count and search for the op-
137   timal rank. DyLoRA uses a nested dropout loss to sort and train the rows of the low rank matrices,
138   placing higher-information rows at lower row indices. After training, the low-rank matrices can be
139   pruned to a user-defined rank without losing significant information, since the most informative rows
140   at the lower row indices are preserved (Valipour et al., 2022). AdaLoRA assigns an importance score
141
      for each row of the weight matrix, evaluated based on the magnitude of singular values, thereby
      allows pruning of less important ranks during training (Zhang et al., 2023). SoRA introduces a spe-
142
      cially designed gating function that zeros out redundant ranks during training, effectively reducing
143
      the rank and improving efficiency.(Ding et al., 2023). While these pruning-based methods effec-
144   tively achieve adaptive rank across layers, the fundamental challenge of rank selection remains only
145   partially addressed. By introducing a fixed upper bound as the initial rank for the trainable low-rank
146   matrices, these methods may limit the exploration of more optimal configurations.
147
      To address the limitations of fixed upper bounds while selecting optimal rank across layers, we chose
148
      to explore the possibility of rank expansion during training. A parallel work, ALoRA, also introduces
149
      rank expansion but with different rules, expanding rank only when pruning is not triggered Liu et al.
150   (2024). Our approach, however, places greater emphasis on how to increase the rank strategically.
151   By using Fisher information as a guideline, we aim to expand the rank in each layer more effectively,
152   ensuring that the rank growth aligns with the specific needs of the model.
153

155   3   S E L O RA
156
157   In addressing the challenge of rank selection and maintaining superior performance with minimum
158   rank, we propose Self-Expanding Low-Rank Adaptation (SeLoRA). The general idea of SeLoRA is to
159   initialize the trainable low-rank decomposition matrices with rank r = 1 and dynamically expand its
160   rank individually during training to adapt to varying layer needs. Now, to facilitate adaptive growth,
161   we address two key research questions: (1) How can to expand the rank without perturbing the
      output? (2) At what juncture should the expansion occur?

      3.1   H OW TO EXPAND ?
163
164   When SeLoRA expands itself by adding a new rank, the model’s final prediction should remain
165   constant. Hence, to prevent perturbations in the model’s output when introducing a new rank, a
166   straightforward approach is to force the product of the expanded rank to be 0. However, simple
167   all-zero initialization poses a challenge as it hinders gradient flow through the expanded rank during
168   initial back-propagation. Consequently, utilizing methods e.g., Fisher information to assess the
169   expansion is not applicable. Therefore, we propose to initialize the expanded rank of matrix B to
170
      be 0, and the expanded sub-matrix, K, of matrix A to be randomly initialized. Hence, the expanded
      form of SeLoRA can be expressed as follows:

174                                                                   0

176   SeLoRA maintains the desired output while allowing the gradient to propagate through A.

      3.3   O N C ONVERGENCE OF R ANK E XPANSION A LGORITHM
236
237   While SeLoRA can dynamically expand its rank and determine the timing of expansion, a question
238   arises: Will SeLoRA continue to grow indefinitely without ever converging to an optimal rank?
239   To address this concern, we demonstrate the existence of a theoretical upper-bound for the rank
240   determined by the rank expansion algorithm. To analyze the behavior of the rank expansion, we let
241   c be the average fisher information across a SeLoRA module. Now, the FI-Ratio can be approximated
242   as:
243
244                                      (rank + 1) × (in size + out size) × cexpand
245                         FI-Ratio =                                               ,
                                            rank × (in size + out size) × coriginal
246
247   where rank is the current rank of SeLoRA at the layer being examined, and in size and out size are
248   the input and output dimensions of the layer being examined, respectively.
249   When the rank of a SeLoRA is small, it tends to favor expansion due to the influence of the terms
250   associated with rank. This behaviour is advantageous because, at lower ranks, the parameter count
251   remains minimal, and the potential performance gains far outweigh the minor cost of adding ad-
252   ditional parameters. As the rank grows, the FI-Ratio strikes a balance between (rank + 1) / rank
253   term and any notable Fisher information figures in the expanded SeLoRA that significantly increase
254   cexpand .
255   Once the rank reaches a sufficiently large value, the boost in FI-Ratio from the (rank + 1)/rank
256   ratio diminishes. As a result, further expansion will only occur if there is a substantial difference
257   between the mean of expanded and original FI-Score, cexpand and coriginal . This mechanism natu-
258   rally discourages unnecessary expansion at higher ranks, thereby preventing overfitting and ensuring
259   parameter efficient fine tuning.
260   Hence, the FI-Ratio is modulated by both the rank and the significance of the update, ensuring a
261   balance between expansion and avoiding overfitting. To further mitigate the risk of overfitting, a
262   larger expansion threshold can be chosen.

297
298   The experiments utilize the base Stable Diffusion model weights from ‘runwayml/stable-diffusion-
299   v1-5’ in Hugging Face (Rombach et al., 2022). The Stable Diffusion model consists of three compo-
300   nents: variational autoencoder (VAE), denoising U-Net, and the text encoder. When applying LoRA
301
      and its variants to Stable Diffusion, we injected them into every linear layer of the denoising U-Net
      and the text encoder. Injection into the VAE was omitted, as previous work has shown that fine-
302
      tuning the VAE component of the Stable Diffusion model does not yield significant performance
303
      improvements on medical images (Chambon et al., 2022b). Additionally, to accommodate the text
304   encoder of stable diffusion, any text finding longer than 76 tokens is truncated, as outlined in the
305   CLIP approach (Radford et al., 2021).
306
307
      In our experiments, we compared LoRA, DyLoRA, and AdaLoRA to our proposed methods. We fine-
      tuned the model using a mean squared error (MSE) loss, evaluating it on the validation set after each
308
      epoch, and kept the best-performing model for testing. The models were fine-tuned for 10 epochs on
309
      the IU X-RAY dataset and 100 epochs on the Montgomery County CXR dataset, due to its smaller
310   size. For our method, we selected a threshold of λ = 1.1 and t = 40 training steps. Although
311   these parameter choices are somewhat arbitrary, a relatively large t is preferred to allow SeLoRA
312   to fully converge before evaluating expansion. For LoRA and DyLoRA, we set the rank to r = 4,
313   while AdaLoRA was initialized with r = 6and reduced to the target rank of r = 4. The choice of
314   r = 4 ensures a comparable number of trainable parameters to our trained SeLoRA, enabling a fair
315   comparison.

347
348   Our results clearly highlight the superior performance of the proposed method, SeLoRA, in terms of
349   both fidelity and alignment with the prompt (text condition). In Tables 1 and 2, SeLoRA consistently
350   outperforms all other methods across evaluation metrics for these relatively small datasets. This
351   demonstrates SeLoRA’s potential and suggests it is a promising candidate for further exploration
352   and application in various medical image synthesis tasks, especially given the inherent scarcity of
353   medical datasets Papanastasiou et al. (2023).

358   still achieve lower scores. This efficiency highlights SeLoRA’s effectiveness in parameter efficient
359   training, particularly in memory-constrained environments.

361   representations obtained from intermediate layers of the Inception v3 model. The results, shown in
362   the rightmost two columns of Tables 1 and 2, align with previous findings, reaffirming that SeLoRA
363   consistently outperforms other methods. Additionally, we observed a noticeable improvement in
364   FID scores for the IU X-Ray dataset when evaluated with lower dimension. While the original
365   FID scores were nearly tied with LoRA for the IU X-Ray dataset, our method demonstrates clear
366
      superiority when assessed across varying dimensions of FID score. This further highlights SeLoRA’s
      robustness and its suitability for medical image synthesis, especially in scenarios where traditional

425   sections of the attention weights. attn1 and attn2 refer to the first and second attention layers, where
426   the first is self-attention and the second is cross-attention between text and image embedding.
427   In the text encoder part, large ranks for SeLoRA lie at the q and k parts of the attention weights. For

430   most dramatically at locations where the latent representations of text and image intersect (where
431   conditioning is more apparent). Hence, it validates our hypothesis that our proposed expansion
      method allows SeLoRA to focus and place more rank on crucial layers.

      This work introduces a novel parameter-efficient method named SeLoRA, designed to effectively
480   fine-tune stable diffusion models for generating X-ray images based on text (radiology) prompts.
481   Our method enables progressive expansion in the rank of LoRA, enabling more precise image syn-
482   thesis with minimal added rank. Through exploratory analysis, we demonstrate that the proposed
483   FI-Ratio is capable of effectively guiding SeLoRA to expand its rank and allocate more rank to cru-
484   cial layers. We believe that SeLoRA, when combined with stable diffusion, can be easily employed
485   to adapt to various medical datasets containing text-image pairs, and potentially being applicable for
      clinical text synthesis. Moreover, given the increasing work on segmentation and detection in 3D
