009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Autoregressive construction approaches generate solutions to vehicle routing
012            problems in a step-by-step fashion, leading to high-quality solutions that are near-
013            ing the performance achieved by handcrafted, operations research techniques. In
014            this work, we challenge the conventional paradigm of sequential solution con-
015            struction and introduce an iterative search framework where solutions are instead
016
               deconstructed by a neural policy. Throughout the search, the neural policy col-
               laborates with a simple greedy insertion algorithm to rebuild the deconstructed
017
               solutions. Our approach surpasses the performance of state-of-the-art operations
018
               research methods across three challenging vehicle routing problems of various
019            problem sizes.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Methods that can learn to solve complex optimization problems have the potential to transform
025   decision-making processes across virtually all domains. It is therefore unsurprising that learning-
026   based optimization approaches have garnered significant attention and yielded substantial advance-
027
      ments (Bello et al., 2016; Kool et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2020). Notably, reinforcement learning (RL)
      approaches are particularly promising because they do not rely on a pre-defined training set of rep-
028
      resentative solutions and can develop new strategies from scratch for novel optimization problems.
029
      These methods generally construct solutions incrementally through a sequential decision-making
030   process and have been successfully applied to various vehicle routing problems.
031
032   Despite recent progress, learning-based methods for combinatorial optimization (CO) problems usu-
033
      ally fall short of outperforming the state-of-the-art techniques from the operations research (OR)
      community. For instance, while some new construction approaches for the capacitated vehicle rout-
034
      ing problem (CVRP) have surpassed the LKH3 solver (Helsgaun, 2000), they still struggle to match
035
      the performance of the state-of-the-art HGS solver (Vidal et al., 2012), particularly for larger in-
036   stances with over 100 nodes. One reason for this is their inability to explore as many solutions as
037   traditional approaches within the same amount of time. Given the limitations of current construction
038   approaches, we propose challenging the traditional paradigm of sequential solution construction by
039   introducing a novel iterative search framework, neural deconstruction search (NDS), which instead
040   deconstructs solutions using a neural policy.
041
      NDS is an iterative search method designed to enhance a given solution through a two-phase
042   process involving deconstruction and reconstruction along the lines of large neighborhood search
043   (LNS) (Shaw, 1998) and ruin-and-recreate (Schrimpf et al., 2000) paradigms. The deconstruction
044   phase employs a deep neural network (DNN) to determine the customers to be removed from the
045   tours of the current solution. This is achieved through a sequential decision-making process, in
046   which nodes are removed one at a time based on the network’s guidance. The reconstruction phase
047   utilizes a straightforward greedy insertion algorithm, which inserts customers in the order given by
048   the neural network at the locally optimal positions. The core concept of NDS is shown in Figure 1.
049   Note that NDS is trained using reinforcement learning, which makes it adaptable to problems for
050
      which no reference solutions are available for training.
051   The overall concept of modifying a solution by first removing some solution components and then
052   conducting a rebuilding step has been successfully used in various vehicle routing problem methods.
053   Non-learning based methods that use this concept include the rip-up and reroute method from Dees
      & Smith (1981), LNS from Shaw (1998), and the ruin and recreate method from Schrimpf et al.

093   2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
094
095   Construction Methods The introduction of the pointer network architecture by Vinyals et al.
096   (2015) marked the first autoregressive, deep learning-based approach for solving routing problems.
097   In their initial work, the authors employ supervised learning to train the models, demonstrating its
098
      application to the traveling salesperson problem (TSP) with 50 nodes. Building on this, Bello et al.
      (2016) propose using reinforcement learning to train pointer networks, showcasing its effectiveness
099
      in addressing larger TSP instances.
100
101   For the more complex CVRP, the first learning-based construction methods were introduced by
102   Nazari et al. (2018) and Kool et al. (2019). Recognizing the symmetries inherent in many combi-
103   natorial optimization problems, Kwon et al. (2020) develop POMO, a method that leverages these
104
      symmetries to improve exploration of the solution space during both training and testing. Extending
      this concept, Kim et al. (2022) propose a general-purpose symmetric learning framework.
105
106   Various techniques have been proposed to enhance performance in neural combinatorial optimiza-
107   tion. For instance, Hottung et al. (2022) introduce efficient active search, which updates a subset of
      parameters during inference. Choo et al. (2022) propose SGBS, combining Monte Carlo tree search

108
      with beam search to guide the search process more effectively. Additionally, Drakulic et al. (2023)
109   and Luo et al. (2023) focus on improving out-of-distribution generalization by re-encoding the re-
110   maining subproblem after each construction step. To enhance solution diversity during sampling,
111   Grinsztajn et al. (2022) and Hottung et al. (2024) explore approaches that learn a set of policies,
112   rather than a single policy.
113
      Instead of constructing solutions autoregressively, some approaches predict heat maps that highlight
114
      promising solution components (e.g., arcs in a graph), which are then used in post-hoc searches to
115   construct solutions (Joshi et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2022b; Min et al., 2023). Other
116   approaches focus on more complex variants of routing problems, such as the VRPTW (Falkner &
117   Schmidt-Thieme, 2020; Kool et al., 2022a; Berto et al., 2024a;b).
118
119   Improvement Methods Improvement methods focus on iteratively refining a given starting so-
120   lution. In addition to the ruin-and-recreate approaches discussed in the introduction, several other
121   methods aim to enhance solution quality through iterative adjustments. For instance, Ma et al.
122   (2021) propose learning to iteratively improve solutions by performing local modifications. Simi-
123   larly, several works have guided the k-opt heuristic for vehicle routing problems (Wu et al., 2019;
124   da Costa et al., 2020), although they are constrained by a fixed, small k. More recently, Ma et al.
125   (2023) introduced a method capable of handling any k. Furthermore, Ye et al. (2024a) and Kim
126
      et al. (2024) integrate learning-based approaches with ant colony optimization to allow for a more
      extensive search phase. Additionally, several divide-and-conquer methods have been developed to
127
      address large-scale routing problems (Kim et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2024b).

132   3.1   D ECONSTRUCTION P OLICY
133
134   For solution deconstruction, a neural policy is employed to sequentially select customers for removal
135   from a given solution. We model this selection process as a Markov decision process. Let s be a fea-
136   sible solution to a vehicle routing problem (VRP) instance l, which involves customers c1 , . . . , cN .
137
      A policy network πθ , parameterized by θ, is used to select M customers for removal. At each step
      m ∈ {1, . . . , M }, an action am ∈ {1, . . . , N } is chosen according to the probability distribution
138
      πθ (am | l, s, v, a1:m−1 ), where am corresponds to selecting customer cam , l is the instance, s is
139
      the solution, v is a random seed, and a1:m−1 are the previous actions. We condition the policy on
140   a random seed v to encourage more diverse rollouts as explained in Hottung et al. (2024). Each
141   seed is a randomly generated binary vectors of dimension dv (we set dv = 10 in all experiments).
142   Finally, after all M customers are selected the reward can be computed as discussed in the following
143   sections.
144

146
147   The deconstruction policy in NDS is trained using reinforcement learning. During the training pro-
148   cess, solutions are repeatedly deconstructed and reconstructed, aiming to discover a deconstruction
149   policy that facilitates the reconstruction of high-quality solutions. Algorithm 1 outlines our train-
150   ing procedure. It is important to implement the algorithm in a way that allows processing batches
151   of instances in parallel to ensure efficient training. However, for clarity, the pseudocode presented
152
      describes the training process for a single instance at a time.

      Greedy Insertion The greedy insertion procedure reintegrates the customers removed by the pol-
204   icy, inserting them one by one into either existing or new tours. Specifically, if M customers have
205   been removed, the procedure performs M iterations, where in each iteration, a single customer cam
206   is inserted. At each iteration m, the cost of inserting customer cam at every feasible position in the
207   current tours is evaluated. Throughout this process, various constraints, such as vehicle capacity
208   limits, are taken into account. If at least one feasible insertion point is found within an existing tour,
209   the customer cam is placed at the position that incurs the least additional cost. If no feasible insertion
210   is available, a new tour is created for customer cam .
211   The order in which removed customers are reinserted significantly impacts the overall performance.
212   We reinsert customers either in the order determined by the neural network or at random. Allowing
213   the network to control the reinsertion order gives it control over the reconstruction process, enabling
214   it to find ordering strategies that lead to better reconstructed solutions. If customers are ordered at
215   random, a deconstructed solution should be reconstructed multiple times using different insertion
      orders. This can provide more stable learning signal during training.

      3.3     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
217
218   We design a transformer-based architecture that consists of an encoder and a decoder. The encoder
219   is used to generate embeddings for all nodes based on the instance l and the current solution s. The
220   decoder is used to decode a sequence of actions based on these embeddings in an iterative fashion.
221
222   3.3.1    E NCODER
223
      The encoder processes the input features xi for each of the N + 1 nodes, where x0 corresponds to
224
      the depot’s features, alongside the current solution s that needs to be encoded. Initially, each input
225
      vector xi is mapped to a 128-dimensional node embedding hi through a linear transformation. The
226   embeddings h0 , . . . , hN are sequentially processed through several layers. First, two attention layers
227   encode static instance information. Next, a message passing layer allows information exchange
228   between consecutive nodes in the solution. This is followed by a tour embedding layer, which
229   computes embeddings for each tour within the solution. Finally, two additional attention layers
230   refine the representations. The attention mechanisms employed are consistent with those used in
231   prior work (e.g., Kwon et al. (2020)), and detailed descriptions are omitted here for brevity.
232
233   Message Passing Layer The message passing layer updates the embedding of a customer ci by
234   incorporating information from its immediate neighbors (i.e., nodes that are visited before and after
235   ci in the solution s). Specifically, the embedding hi of customer ci is updated as follows:
236
                      h′i = Norm hi + FF ReLU W 3 hi ; W 1 hprev(i) + W 2 hnext(i)

239   In this equation, prev(i) and next(i) represent the indices of the nodes immediately preceding and
240   following ci in the solution s. The weight matrices W 1 and W 2 are used to transform the embed-
241   dings of these neighboring nodes, while W 3 is applied to the concatenated vector of hi and the
242
      aggregated embeddings from the neighbors. The ReLU activation function introduces non-linearity
      into the transformation. The output of this transformation is processed through a feed-forward net-
243

244

245   instance normalization.

248   incorporating information from the tour they are part of. To this end, a tour embedding is first

250   aggregated tour embedding is then used to update the individual customer embeddings. Specifically,

      3.3.2    D ECODER
259
260   Given the node embeddings generated by the encoder, the decoder is responsible for sequentially se-
261   lecting customers for removal. The overall architecture of our decoder is identical to that of Hottung
262   et al. (2024), which utilizes a multi-head attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) followed by a
263   pointer mechanism (Vinyals et al., 2015). This architecture has been widely used in many routing
264   problems methods (Kool et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2020).
265   Our approach differs from previous works in that we account for the already selected customers at
266   each decision step. This contrasts with construction-based methods, where each decision is inde-
267   pendent of prior selections. To address this, we integrate a gated recurrent unit (GRU) (Cho, 2014),
268   which is used to compute the query for the multi-head attention mechanism. At each decision step,
269   the GRU takes the embedding of the previously selected customer as input, updating its internal
      state to incorporate past decisions.

286   3.4   S EARCH
287
288   At test time, we leverage the learned policy within a search framework that supports batched rollouts,
289
      enabling fast execution. Importantly, this framework is problem-agnostic, meaning it contains no
      problem-specific components, allowing it to be applied to a broader range of problems than those
290
      evaluated in this paper.
291
292   Our search framework consists of two main components: the improvement step function (illustrated
293   in Figure 2) and the high-level augmented simulated annealing (ASA) algorithm (Algorithm 2).
294   The improvement step function aims to enhance a given solution by iteratively applying the policy
295
      model through a series of deconstruction and reconstruction steps. The ASA algorithm integrates
      this function and supports batched execution for improved performance on the GPU. It is important
296
      to note that we parallelize solely on the GPU, requiring only a single CPU core during test time.
297
298
299
      Improvement Step The improvement step, the core component of the overall search algorithm, is
      depicted in Figure 2. The process begins with an initial solution s0 that is passed to the policy DNN,
300
      which generates K rollouts, each consisting of M actions that specify the customers to be removed.
301
      Once the policy DNN completes its execution, these rollouts are sequentially applied to produce new
302   candidate solutions. Specifically, the solution s0 is first deconstructed based on the actions from the
303   first rollout (yielding s¯0 ) and then reconstructed into s′0 . After reconstruction, a simulated annealing
304   (SA) based acceptance criterion is used to determine whether s′0 or s0 should be retained, resulting in
305   s1 . This process is repeated in each subsequent iteration. After K iterations, the final solution sK is
306   returned, representing the outcome of K consecutive deconstruction and reconstruction operations.
307   By performing these iterations sequentially, the solution s0 is significantly modified, often leading
308   to notable cost improvements between the initial input s0 and the final output sK .
309
310   Augmented Simulated Annealing We introduced a novel simulated annealing (SA) algorithm to
311   conduct a high-level search specifically designed for GPU-based parallelization. While parallel SA
312   algorithms have been proposed in prior work, (Ferreiro et al., 2013; Jeong & Kim, 1990; Onbaşoğlu
313   & Özdamar, 2001), their main concern is on the information exchange between CPU or GPU cores.
314
      In contrast, our approach focuses on executing parallel rollouts of the policy network on the GPU.
315   At a high level, the ASA technique, shown in Algorithm 2, modifies solutions over multiple itera-
316   tions using a temperature-based acceptance criterion. This criterion allows worsening solutions to
317   be accepted with a certain probability, which depends on the current temperature. The temperature
318   λ is manually set at the start of the search (line 2) and is gradually reduced after each iteration (line
319   15), resulting in a decreasing probability of accepting worsening solutions during the improvement
320
      step (line 6). For a detailed discussion on SA, we refer the reader to Gendreau et al. (2010).

322   Kwon et al. (2020), which creates a set of augmentations l1′ , l2′ , . . . , lA

      4.1   P ROBLEMS
359
360   CVRP The CVRP is one of the most extensively studied variants of the VRP. The goal is to deter-
361   mine the shortest routes for a fleet of vehicles tasked with delivering goods to a set of N customers.
362   Each vehicle begins and ends its route at a depot and is constrained by a maximum carrying ca-
363   pacity. We use the instance generator from Kool et al. (2019) to create scenarios with uniformly
364   distributed customer locations, and the generator from Queiroga et al. (2022) for generating more
365
      realistic instances, with clustered customer locations to better simulate real-world conditions.
366
367
      VRPTW The VRPTW extends the traditional CVRP by adding time constraints for customer
      deliveries. Each customer has a time window, defining the earliest and latest allowable delivery
368
      times. Vehicles can arrive early but must wait until the window opens, adding scheduling complexity.
369
      All routes start at a central depot, with a fixed service duration for deliveries and travel times based
370   on the Euclidean distance. The objective is to minimize the total travel time while respecting both
371   vehicle capacity and time windows, making VRPTW more complex than the standard CVRP. To
372   generate customer locations and demands, we use the CVRP instance generator from Queiroga et al.
373   (2022), while time windows are generated following the methodology outlined by Solomon (1987).
374
375   PCVRP The PCVRP is a variant of the VRP in which not all customers need to be visited. Each
376   customer is associated with a prize, and the objective is to minimize the total travel cost minus the
377   sum of collected prizes. Similar to the CVRP, all vehicles start and end their routes at a central
      depot and are constrained by vehicle capacities. To generate PCVRP instances, we use the instance

378
      generator from Queiroga et al. (2022) to create customer locations and demands. Customer prize
379   values are generated at random, with higher prizes assigned to customers with greater demand,
380   reflecting the increased resources required to service them.

      4.2   S EARCH P ERFORMANCE
383
384   Baselines We compare NDS to several heuristic solvers, including HGS (Vidal, 2022), SISRs
385   (Christiaens & Vanden Berghe, 2020), and LKH3 (Helsgaun, 2017). Additionally, we include
386   PyVRP (Wouda et al., 2024) (version 0.9.0), which is an open-source extension of HGS for other
387   VRP variants. For the CVRP, we further compare NDS to the state-of-the-art learning-based meth-
388   ods, SGBS-EAS (Choo et al., 2022), BQ (Drakulic et al., 2023), LEHD (Luo et al., 2023), and
389
      GLOP (Ye et al., 2024b).
390
391   NDS Training For each problem and problem size, we perform a separate training run. Training
392
      consists of 2000 epochs for settings with 1000 or fewer customers. For the 2000 customer setting, we
      resume training from the 1000 customer model checkpoint at 1500 epochs and train for an additional
393
      500 epochs. In each epoch, we process 1500 instances, with each instance undergoing 100 iterations,
394
      128 rollouts, and 10 initial improvement steps. The learning rate is set to 10−4 and 15 customers are
395   selected per deconstruction step across all problem sizes. The training durations are approximately
396   5, 8, 15, and 8 days for the problem sizes 100, 500, 1000 and 2000, respectively. The training curves
397   are presented in Appendix A, while visualizations of policy rollouts are available in Appendix B.
398
399   Evaluation Setup At test time, we limit the runtime to 5, 60, 120, and 240 seconds of wall time
400   per instance for HGS, SISRs, and NDS to ensure a fair comparison, as these methods process test
401   instances sequentially. SGBS-EAS and LEHD, which process instances in batches, are given an
402   equivalent search budget per batch. All approaches are restricted to using a single CPU core. For the
403   CVRP, we use the test instances from Kool et al. (2019) for N =100 (10,000 instances), Drakulic et al.
404   (2023) for N =500 (128 instances), and Ye et al. (2024b) for N =1000 and N =2000 (100 instances
405
      each). For the VRPTW and PCVRP, we generate new test sets consisting of 10,000 instances for
      N =100 and 250 instances for settings with more than 100 customers.
406
407
      NDS Test Configuration For NDS, the starting temperature λstart is set to 0.1 and decays expo-
408
      nentially to 0.001 throughout the search. The threshold factor δ is fixed at 15. During the improve-
409
      ment step, 200 rollouts are performed per instance, and each deconstructed solution is reconstructed
410   5 times (1× based on the selected order of the DNN and 4× using a random customer order). The
411   number of augmentations is set to 8 for the CVRP and VRPTW, and 128 for the PCVRP.
412
413   Results Table 1 presents the performance of all compared methods on the test data. The gap is
414   reported relative to HGS for the CVRP, and to PyVRP-HGS for the VRPTW and PCVRP. Across the
415   12 test settings, NDS delivers the best performance in 11 cases, with HGS being the only approach
416   able to outperform it on the CVRP with 100 customers. Compared to other learning-based methods,
417   NDS shows significant performance improvements across all CVRP sizes. On the CVRP with 2000
418   customers, NDS achieves a 7 percentage point improvement over the best-performing learning-based
419   method, LEHD, and a 12 percentage point improvement over GLOP. Against the state-of-the-art
420
      HGS and its extension, PyVRP-HGS, NDS performs especially well on larger instances, achieving
      a gap of more than 2% across all problems for instances with 2000 customers. For the PCVRP,
421
      NDS also attains substantial gaps relative to PyVRP-HGS, exceeding 4% on instances with 500 or
422
      more nodes. When compared to SISRs, NDS maintains a small advantage on larger instances and
423   demonstrates significantly better performance on small instances.

      the DNN, while for the remaining four iterations we use a random order. We compare our standard
475
      setting to using only random orderings for all five insertion iterations to assess whether the ordering

      efficient policy that surpasses handcrafted methods in this use case.

497
498   One major advantage of learning-based solution approaches is their ability to adapt precisely to the
499   specific type of instances at hand. However, in real-world scenarios, concept drift in the instance
500   distributions cannot always be avoided. In this experiment, we evaluate whether the learned policies
501   of NDS can handle instances sampled from a slightly different distribution. For the CVRP with
502
      N =500, we train a policy on instances with medium-capacity vehicles and customer locations that
      follow a mix of uniform and clustered distributions. We then evaluated the learned policy on in-
503
      stances with low- and high-capacity vehicles, and customer locations following either uniform or
504
      clustered distributions. Additionally, we train distribution-specific models for each test setting for
505   comparison. As a baseline, we compare against HGS and SISRs, giving all approaches the same
506   runtime. The results are shown in Table 3, where NDS (OOD) represents the model’s performance
507   when the training and test distributions differ, and NDS (ID) represents the setting where the train-
508   ing and test distributions are identical. Overall, the performance difference between the two settings
509   is minimal, indicating that NDS generalizes well across different distributions. Interestingly, the
510   distribution of customer locations has a larger impact on performance than vehicle capacity.

      5     C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this work, we introduced a novel search method, NDS, which leverages a learned policy to de-
525   construct solutions for routing problems. NDS presents several key advantages. First, it delivers
526   superior performance, consistently outperforming state-of-the-art OR methods under equal runtime.
527   Second, NDS scales effectively to larger problem instances, handling up to N =2000 customers,
528   due to the fact that the number of customers selected by the policy is independent of the problem
529   size. Third, it demonstrates strong generalization across different data distributions. Finally, NDS
530   is easily adaptable to new vehicle routing problems, requiring only small adjustments to the greedy
531   insertion heuristic and the model input.
532   A notable limitation is the reliance on a GPU for executing the policy network. Future research
533   could explore model distillation techniques to lower the computational requirements or investigate
534   whether the underlying principles of the learned policies can be approximated using faster, more
535   efficient algorithms.
