009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Class imbalance is a prevalent issue in real world machine learning applications,
012            often leading to poor performance in rare and minority classes. With an abundance
013            of wild unlabeled data, active learning is perhaps the most effective technique in
014            solving the problem at its root – collecting a more balanced and informative set of
015            labeled examples during annotation. Label noise is another common issue in data
016
               annotation jobs, which is especially challenging for active learning methods. In
               this work, we conduct the first study of active learning under both class imbalance
017
               and label noise. We propose a novel algorithm that robustly identifies the class
018
               separation threshold and annotates the most uncertain examples that are closest
019            from it. Through a novel reduction to one-dimensional active learning, our al-
020            gorithm DIRECT is able to leverage classic active learning theory and methods
021            to address issues such as batch labeling and tolerance towards label noise. We
022            present extensive experiments on imbalanced datasets with and without label noise.
023            Our results demonstrate that DIRECT can save more than 60% of the annotation
024            budget compared to state-of-art active learning algorithms and more than 80% of
025            annotation budget compared to random sampling.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large-scale deep learning models are playing increasingly important roles across many industries.
030
      Human feedback and annotations have played a significant role in developing such systems. Pro-
031   gressively over time, we believe the role of humans in a machine learning pipeline will shift to
032   annotating rare yet important cases. However, under data imbalance, the typical strategy of randomly
033   choosing examples for annotation becomes especially inefficient. This is because the majority of the
034   labeling budget would be spent on common and well-learned classes, resulting in insufficient rare
035   class examples for training an effective model. To mitigate this issue, many recent active learning
036   algorithms have focused on labeling more class-balanced and informative examples (Aggarwal et al.,
037   2020; Kothawade et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; 2024b; Soltani et al., 2024). For many large-scale
038   annotation jobs, this challenge of data imbalance is further compounded by label noise – a critical
039
      and common issue that results from annotator decision fatigue and perception differences. A rich
      body of literature on agnostic active learning (Balcan et al., 2006; Dasgupta et al., 2007; Hanneke
040
      et al., 2014; Katz-Samuels et al., 2021) addresses this challenge on low-complexity model classes
041
      (e.g. linear models). However, for deep learning models, these algorithms often becomes ineffective
042   due to the large model class complexity. In this paper, we propose a novel active learning strategy for
043   both class imbalance and label noise. Our algorithm DIRECT sequentially and adaptively chooses
044   informative and more class-balanced examples for annotation while being robust to noisy annotations.
045   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first deep active learning study to address the challenging yet
046   prevalent scenario where both imbalance and label noise coexist.
047
      To bridge the gap between the imbalanced deep active learning and the agnostic active learning
048   literature, we propose a novel reduction of the imbalanced classification problem into a set of one-
049   dimensional agnostic active learning problems. For each class, our reduction sorts unlabeled examples
050   into an list ordered by one-vs-rest margin scores. The objective of DIRECT is to find the optimal
051   separation threshold which best separates the examples in the given class from the rest. By relating
052   our problem to that of finding the best threshold classifier, we are able to employ ideas from the
053   agnostic active learning literature to learn the separation threshold robustly under label noise. By
      annotating around the threshold, the annotated examples are more class-balanced and informative.

      To summarize our main contributions:
085   • We propose a novel reduction that bridges the advancement in the theoretical agnostic active
086     learning literature to imbalanced active classification for deep neural networks.
087   • Our algorithm DIRECT addresses the prevalent imbalance and label noise issues and annotates a
088     more class-balanced and informative set of examples.
089   • Compared to state-of-art algorithm GALAXY (Zhang et al., 2022), DIRECT allows parallel
090
        annotation by multiple annotators while still maintaining significant label-efficiency improvement.
      • We conduct experiments across eight dataset settings, four levels of label noise and for both ResNet-
091
        18 and large pretrained model (CLIP ViT-B32). DIRECT consistently outperforms existing baseline
092
        algorithms by saving more than 60% annotation cost compared to the best existing algorithm, and
093     more than 80% annotation cost compared to random sampling.

      Class-Balanced Deep Active Learning Active earning strategies sequentially and adaptively choose
      examples for annotation. Many uncertainty-based deep active learning methods extend the traditional
100
      active learning literature such as margin, least confidence and entropy sampling (Tong & Koller, 2001;
101
      Settles, 2009; Balcan et al., 2006; Kremer et al., 2014). These methods have been shown to perform
102   among the top when fine-tuning large pretrained models and combined with semi-supervised learning
103   algorithms (Zhang et al., 2024a). More sophisticated methods have been proposed to optimize chosen
104   examples’ uncertainty (Gal et al., 2017; Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018; Beluch et al., 2018), diversity
105   (Sener & Savarese, 2017; Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017; Citovsky et al., 2021), or a mix of both (Ash
106   et al., 2019; 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Elenter et al., 2022; Mohamadi et al., 2022). However, these
107   methods often perform poorly under prevalent and realistic scenarios such as label noises (Khosla
      et al., 2022) or class imbalance Kothawade et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2022; 2024a).

      Deep Active Learning under Imbalance Data imbalance and rare instances are prevalent in almost
109   all modern machine learning applications. Active learning techniques are effective in addressing the
110   problem in its root by collecting a more class-balanced dataset (Aggarwal et al., 2020; Kothawade
111   et al., 2021; Emam et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Coleman et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Cai, 2022;
112   Zhang et al., 2024b). To this end, Kothawade et al. (2021) propose a submodular-based method that
113   actively annotates examples similar to known examples of rare instances. GALAXY(Zhang et al.,
114   2022) constructs one-dimensional linear graphs and applies graph-based active learning techniques in
115   annotating a set of examples that are both class-balanced and uncertain. While GALAXY outperforms
116   existing algorithms, due to a bisection procedure involved, it does not allow parallel annotation. In
117
      addition, bisection procedures are generally not robust against label noises, a prevalent challenge
      in real world annotation tasks. Our algorithm DIRECT mitigates all of the above shortcomings
118
      of GALAXY while outperforming it even with synchronous labeling and no label noise, beating
119
      GALAXY in its own game. Lastly, we distinguish our work from Zhang et al. (2024b), where the
120   paper studies the algorithm selection problem. Unlike our goal of proposing a new deep active
121   learning algorithm, the paper proposes meta algorithms to choose the right active learning algorithm
122   among a large number of candidate algorithms.
123
      Agnostic Active Learning for Label Noise Label noise for active learning has been primarily
124
      studied under the extensive literature on agnostic learning. We refer the interested reader to the
125
      survey (Hanneke et al., 2014) for a thorough discussion. All of these works, beginning with the
126   seminal works by Balcan et al. (2006); Dasgupta et al. (2007), follow a familiar paradigm of
127   disagreement based learning. This involves maintaining a version space of promising hypotheses
128   at each time and constructing a disagreement region of unlabeled examples. For any unlabeled
129   example in the disagreement region, there exists two hypotheses in the version disagreeing on their
130   predictions. An example then chosen for annotation by sampling from a informative sampling
131   distribution computed over the disagreement region. Several approaches have been proposed for
132   computing such sampling distributions, e.g. Jain & Jamieson (2019); Katz-Samuels et al. (2020;
133   2021); Huang et al. (2015). As described in Section 4.2, our main subroutine VReduce is equivalent
134
      to fixed-budget one dimensional threshold disagreement learning based on the ACED algorithm of
      Katz-Samuels et al. (2021). We remark that these algorithms tend to be overly pessimistic in training
135
      deep neural nets, and this paper hopes to close this gap.
136
137   Deep Active Learning under Label Noise Label noisy settings has rarely been studied in the deep
138   active learning literature. Related but tangential to our work, several papers have studied to use active
139   learning for cleaning existing noisy labels (Lin et al., 2016; Younesian et al., 2021). In this line of
140
      work, they assume access to an oracle annotator that will provide clean labels when queried upon.
      This is fundamentally different from our work, where our annotator may provide noisy labels. Another
141
      line of more theoretical active learning research studies active learning with multiple annotators with
142
      different qualities (Zhang & Chaudhuri, 2015; Chen et al., 2022). The primary goal in these work is
143   to identify examples a weak annotator and a strong annotator may disagree, in order to only use the
144   strong annotator on such instances. In our work, we assume access to a single source of annotator
145   that is noisy, which is prevalent in annotation jobs today. Recently, Khosla et al. (2022) proposed a
146   novel deep active learning algorithm specialized for Heteroskedastic noise, where different “regions”
147   of examples are subject to different levels of noise. Unlike their work, our work is agnostic to the
148   noise distributions and conduct experiments on uniformly random corrupted labels.
149   To our knowledge, no deep active learning literature has studied the scenario where both imbalance
150   and label noise present. Yet, this setting is the most prevalent in real-world annotation applications.

176   A deep active learning algorithm iteratively chooses batches of examples for annotation. During the

182   iteration. The ultimate goal of deep active learning is to obtain high predictive accuracy for the
183   trained neural network while annotating as few examples as possible.

196   confidence (Settles, 2009), margin (Tong & Koller, 2001; Balcan et al., 2006) and entropy (Kremer
197   et al., 2014) sampling, simply sort examples based on their predictive sigmoid scores pb and annotate

201
202   Objective of DIRECT. To mitigate the above issue with the decision boundary, we propose to
203   identify the optimal separation threshold. The threshold best separates the minority and majority
204   classes and approximately equalizes the number of examples from both classes around its vicinity
205
      (see Section 4.1 for formal definition). We note the optimal separation threshold could be relatively
      distant from pb = .5, as shown in Figure 2a. Our overall objective is to label examples that are both
206
      uncertain and class-balanced, and can be decomposed into the following two-phased procedure:
207
208
       1. Identify the optimal separation threshold j ⋆ that best separates the minority class from the
           majority class, as shown in Figure 2a.
209
       2. Annotate equal number of examples next to j ⋆ from both sides.
210
211   Limitation of GALAXY(Zhang et al., 2022). As discussed above, the neural network decision
212   boundary pb = .5 does not necessarily best separate minority and majority class examples. GALAXY
213   draws inspiration from graph-based active learning. It relies on the fact that the best separation
214   threshold must be a cut, namely thresholds with a minority class example to the left and a majority
215   class example to the right (see Figure 2b). The algorithm aims to find all cuts in the sorted graph as
      shown in Figure 2b. However, GALAXY suffers from three weaknesses:

      1. During active learning, the neural network is still under training and cannot perfectly separate
217      the two classes of examples yet. Therefore, the sorted graph could have a significant number of
218      cuts. As an example in Figure 2b, when annotating around all of such cuts, the algorithm could

223   3. GALAXY finds all cuts through a modified bisection procedure, which only allows for sequential

302   4.2   A LGORITHM
303
      We are now ready to state our algorithm DIRECT as shown in Algorithm 1. Each round of DIRECT
304
      follows a two-phased procedure, where the first phase aims to identify the optimal separation threshold
305
      for each class. The second phase then annotates examples closest to the estimated optimal separation
306   thresholds for each class. We spend half each round’s budget for both phases.
307
308   During the first phase, to identify the optimal separation threshold for all classes, we loop over
309
      each class k and run a agnostic active learning procedure for the corresponding 1-D class-k v.s. rest
      reduction. Our agnostic active learning subroutine is formally outlined in Algorithm 2, VReduce.
310
      Our method corresponds to the fixed-budget ACED Katz-Samuels et al. (2021) algorithm, adopted to
311
      the threshold classifier scenario. To explain the intuition about ACED in this scenario, we first note
312   that in the separable case with L(hj ⋆ ) = 0, we could simply run a bisection procedure to learn jk⋆ , the
313   optimal separation threshold for the k-th class. However, as j ⋆ cannot perfectly separate the classes,
314   ACED circumvents this by maintaining a version space of possible thresholds, namely the interval
315   [I, J]. Statistically with high likelihood, the optimal separation threshold jk⋆ lies in this version space
316   of [I, J]. During each of the m rounds of VReduce, Bparallel samples are annotated and the version
317   space’s length is shrank by a factor of 1/c. The shrinkage rate c is determined by the budget and
318   batch size, so that after the final iteration, the version space has exactly one hypothesis left. The
319   second phase of DIRECT simply annotates examples closest to each optimal separation threshold,
320
      aiming to annotate a class-balanced and uncertain examples.

322   the neural network is retrained. In practice, this number is usually determined by the constraints
323   of computational training cost. On the other hand, we let Bparallel denote the number of examples

341   Theoretical Comparison with GALAXY. As mentioned in Section 3.2, GALAXY’s graph-based
342   approach aims to identify all cuts and sample examples around all of them equally. On the other
343   hand, DIRECT aims to identify only the separation threshold and sample around it, which is superior
344   as we have argued before and shown in our results. We now present a more theoretical comparison.
345   As we show in Appendix A, the graph-based approach in GALAXY will identify and annotate
346   around at least one more cut in addition to the optimal separation threshold, with probability at least
                         1
347
      1 − exp(−b log( 1−η   )/2). Here, b is the budget of a single round of annotation and η is the label
348   noise ratio (see Appendix A for more details). This implies, when the budget b is large, GALAXY
349
      will likely annotate around unnecessary cuts. This is in contrast with the agnostic active learning
      approach we take in DIRECT, where as shown by Katz-Samuels et al. (2021), the probability of
350
      misidentifying the optimal separation threshold decays exponentially w.r.t. budget b. In other words,
351
      with a large budget b, with high likelihood, DIRECT will focus its annotation around the optimal
352   separation threshold. Lastly, time complexity analysis in Appendix C shows DIRECT’s superior
353   speed compared to BADGE and GALAXY.

782   C    T IME C OMPLEXITY
783
784   The computation complexity for each batch of DIRECT is O(KN log(N )+Btrain N ) for data selection
785   plus the training and inference costs of the neural network. O(KN log(N )) comes from sorting
786   examples by their margin scores for each class and O(Btrain N ) is the cost for running Algorithm 2
787   for O(Btrain ) iterations. Each iteration of Algorithm 2 only costs O(N ) time as we can efficiently
788   solve the objective by cumulative sums. We note that the cost associated with neural network training
789   and inference is always the dominating factor.
790   For comparisons, BADGE has time complexity O(Btrain N (K + D)), significantly more expensive
791   than DIRECT, with D denotes the dimensionality of the penultimate layer features. In addition,
792   GALAXY has computational complexity of O(KN log(N )) + Btrain KN , also more expensive than
793   DIRECT. In all of our experiments, both BADGE and GALAXY indeed is slower than DIRECT. We
794   further note that the time complexity factor of K in DIRECT can be easily parallelized by conducting
795   the K sorting procedures on different CPU cores.
796   In total, our experiments are conducted on NVIDIA 3090 ti GPUs. Each trial of the ResNet-18
797   experiment takes less than two hours while each trial of the LabelBench experiments takes roughly
798   12 hours.

      ResNet-18 Experiments. ResNet-18 with passive training has been the standard evaluation in
803
      existing deep active literature (Ash et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022). Our experiment setup utilizes the
804   CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011) and PathMNIST (Yang
805   et al., 2021) image classification datasets. The original forms of these datasets are roughly balanced
806   across 9, 10 or 100 classes. We construct an extremely imbalanced dataset by grouping a large
807   number of classes into one majority class. For example, given a balanced dataset above with M
808   classes. We generate an imbalanced dataset with K classes (K < M ) by the first K − 1 classes from
809   the original dataset and combining the rest of the classes K, ..., M into a single majority class K.
      Imbalance ratios are shown in Table 1.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      For neural network training, we utilize the standard passive training on labeled examples with cross
811   entropy loss and Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014). The ResNet-18 model (He et al., 2016) is
812   pretrained on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) from the PyTorch library. To address data imbalance,
813   for all algorithms, we utilize a reweighted cross entropy loss by the inverse frequency of the number
814   of labeled examples in each class. For experiments with label noise, we further add a 10% label
815   smoothing during training (Müller et al., 2019) for all algorithms.
816
      LabelBench Experiments. Proposed by Zhang et al. (2024a), LabelBench evaluates active learning
817   performance in a more comprehensive framework. Here, we fine-tune the large pretrained model
818   from CLIP’s ViT-B32 model (Radford et al., 2021). The framework also utilizes semi-supervised
819   learning method FlexMatch (Zhang et al., 2021) to further leverage the unlabeled examples in
820   the pool for training. We conduct experiments on the two imbalanced datasets in LabelBench,
821   with FMoW (Christie et al., 2018) and iWildcam (Beery et al., 2021). Similar to the ResNet-18
822   experiments, for all algorithms, we use a 10% label smoothing in the loss function to improve training
823   under label noise. We did find FlexMatch to perform poorly under the combination of imbalance and
824   label noise, so we used the passive training method for label noise experiments.
