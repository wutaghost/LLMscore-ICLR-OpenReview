011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Accommodating human preferences is essential for creating AI agents that deliver
014            personalized and effective interactions. Recent work has shown the potential for
015            LLMs to infer preferences from user interactions, but they often produce broad
016
               and generic preferences, failing to capture the unique and individualized nature
               of human preferences. This paper introduces PREDICT, a method designed to
017
               enhance the precision and adaptability of inferring preferences. PREDICT incor-
018
               porates three key elements: (1) iterative refinement of inferred preferences, (2)
019            decomposition of preferences into constituent components, and (3) validation of
020            preferences across multiple trajectories. We evaluate PREDICT on two distinct
021            environments: a gridworld setting and a new text-domain environment (PLUME).
022            PREDICT more accurately infers nuanced human preferences improving over ex-
023            isting baselines by 66.2% (gridworld environment) and 41.0% (PLUME).

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   A fundamental component of effective interaction is understanding the preferences of those with
029   whom we engage. Successfully recognizing and accommodating these preferences leads to more
030   pleasant and efficient experiences (Felfernig et al., 2006). While such preferences can be verbalized
031   explicitly, they can also be inferred implicitly from past interactions. Ideally, an AI agent should be
032   able to both use explicit feedback and learn from implicit cues. As human directions are commonly
033
      expressed in natural language, creating a mapping from implicit cues to natural language could
      enable a natural-language conditioned agent to seamlessly integrate both implicitly and explicitly
034
      defined preferences. This work focuses on this gap by proposing a method to infer natural language
035
      preferences from a user’s actions.
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) possess strong priors about human behavior (Brown et al., 2020).
038   Previous work has demonstrated that these priors can provide the basis to infer user preferences in
039
      domains such as robotic manipulation (Peng et al., 2024) and collaborative authoring (Gao et al.,
      2024). However, current methods infer preferences without reflection nor refinement, resulting in
040
      generic outcomes that limit the models’ adaptability toward the uniqueness and nuance of an indi-
041
      vidual’s preferences. We propose PREDICT (Preference Reasoning by Evaluating Decomposed
042   preferences Inferred from Counterfactual Trajectories), which is comprised of three algorithmic
043   contributions to enhance the precision and efficiency of preference inference: (1) iteratively re-
044   fining inferred preferences until the induced trajectory closely aligns with the user’s example, (2)
045   breaking down (or decomposing) inferred preferences into constituent components, and (3) validat-
046   ing the inferred preferences across multiple user examples. The preference inferred by predict are
047   used to condition the behavior or generations of an AI assistant.
048   We systematically demonstrate the benefits of PREDICT’s contributions on two environments: a
049   gridworld environment, where an agent learns to pick up objects based on a user’s preferences
050   over colors and shapes, and PLUME, a text-based environment where an agent learns to write text
051   that aligns with a user’s preferences. PLUME is a new environment and is a contribution of this
052   paper. PREDICT demonstrates improvements of 66.2% over behavioral cloning in the gridworld
053   environment, and 41.0% over CIPHER (Gao et al., 2024) in PLUME. In PLUME, we augment
      PREDICT with in-context learning and achieve a further 17.9% improvement. The innovations

061   Natural Language Conditioned Agents Language is the most natural way for humans to commu-
062   nicate and express themselves. As a result, considerable research has focused on natural language-
063
      conditioned agents across a variety of domains. BabyAI (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2019) introduces
      an environment for natural language conditioned gridworld agents. Further advancements, such as
064
      gSCAN (Qiu et al., 2021), investigate how gridworld agents handle compositionality, while Zhong
065
      et al. (2020) explore the ability of agents to learn environment dynamics from text. Misra et al.
066   (2018) proposes LingUNet as way to fuse language and vision in a simulated 3D world. Blukis et al.
067   (2018; 2020) extend this for continuous drone control. In room-to-room navigation, works such as
068   CLIPNav (Du et al., 2023) and Embodied CLIP (Khandelwal et al., 2022) use CLIP embeddings
069   (Radford et al., 2021) to condition agents on visual-language aligned representations.
070
      In robotic arm manipulation, Lynch & Sermanet (2021) condition trajectories on both goal images
071
      and natural language, demonstrating successful task completion with limited language labelling.
072   Jang et al. (2021) builds upon this and use videos as goal contexts. For pick-and-place tasks, Shridhar
073   et al. (2022) uses a CLIP-based two-stream architecture, and Mees et al. (2022; 2023) demonstrate
074   long-horizon task completion via hierarchical approaches.
075
      In natural language generation, prompting (Radford et al., 2019) and in-context learning (Brown
076
      et al., 2020) have proven effective methods for controlling the generation of text, especially in a
077
      preference-driven context (Sun et al., 2023; 2024).
078
079   Personalization Some prior approaches of adapting models to user preferences involve RLHF (Sti-
080   ennon et al., 2020) and fine-tuning (Tan et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2024), which can be compute-
081
      intensive and inaccessible to some practitioners without the budget or scale of needed data. With
      the rise of LLMs with strong instruction-following capabilities, methods like prompting to adapt
082
      a user’s profile have become more popular (Shen et al., 2024; Salemi et al., 2024), however these
083
      approaches often rely on explicit feedback provided from the user (Lin et al., 2024). PREDICT
084   circumvents these issues by learning from implicit user signals, breaking down preferences into
085   sub-components to generate tailored user-preferences, all without the need of fine-tuning.
086
087
      Preference-Conditioned Agents Combined preference inference and conditioning has recently
      gained traction, with the following two works being most aligned with our approach.
088
089   Peng et al. (2024) explores preference learning in quadrupedal mobile manipulation using an object
090   detection module to map image observations to text. An LLM then infers preferences by comparing
091   pairs of trajectories. These preferences are in turn used to improve task alignment with user prefer-
092   ences. Gao et al. (2024) propose the PRELUDE environment, where an LLM learns writing style
093
      preferences in a collaborative authoring task. We discuss this work in detail in Section 4.3.
094   These methods rely on a single inference step, whereas our approach uses iterative refinement for
095   more precise preferences, and validation across several user examples for robustness.

106   iterative refinement steps is reached or no updates are made in sub-step (i). The inferred preferences
107   are then used to condition and align the behaviors or generations of an AI assistant. A visualization

      3.1         I TERATIVE R EFINEMENT
141
142   PREDICT conditions the AI assistant (e.g. an LLM) on the inferred user preferences to generate a
143   candidate solution (e.g. summary, email, or trajectory) for a given task (e.g. summarize an article).
144   Example candidate solutions (e.g. haikus on the top and trajectories on the bottom) are provided in
145   Fig. 1 on the far left. If no prior user demonstrations have been seen, the AI assistant is conditioned
146   on an empty preference set.
147   The candidate solution is then compared to the user’s demonstration. If the candidate trajectory
148   exactly matches the user’s demonstration then the current inferred preferences are considered suffi-
149   cient to explain the user’s behavior and no further learning is required. This means all subsequent
150   steps in the PREDICT algorithm are skipped.
151
      However, if the assistant’s solution differs, we prompt an LLM (prompt outline: Fig. 1 “Iterative
152
      Refinement” [update here and in figure to “Preference Update”]) to update the inferred preferences
153   so that they explain the difference between the candidate solution and the user’s demonstration.
154   Examples of inferred preferences can be seen in Fig. 1 “Updated Preferences” [need to add this
155   label to figure].
156
      PREDICT then breaks down the updated preferences by prompting an LLM to break the up-
157
      dated preferences into their components parts (prompt outline: Fig. 1 “Breakdown”). Examples of
158
      LLM-identified preference components are provided in Fig. 1 “Preference Components”. Breaking
159   down the preferences provides several advantages. The components provide greater coverage of
160   the preference space with less data, e.g., three components can be combined to cover nine distinct
161

      preference sets. Further, preference components make it easier to refine preference sets by adding,
163   removing, or modifying single components rather than modifying long-form preference descriptions
164   (see Fig. 1 “Updated Preferences” for examples). Lastly, preference components remove ambiguity
165   when validating preferences. If we validate a compound preference and only a single component is
166   incorrect, then the entire compound preference including any useful components may be discarded.
167
      PREDICT then generates a new candidate trajectory by conditioning the AI assistant on the updated
168
      preference components. We repeat the process of generating AI assistant solutions, comparing to the
169   user demonstrations, updating the inferred preferences, and breaking the updated preferences into
170   components until the candidate solutions exactly match the user’s demonstrations, or a maximum
171   number of iteration steps is reached. In all experiments, we use a maximum of three preference
172   update steps per user demonstration.
173
174   3.2       VALIDATING P REFERENCES
175
176   PREDICT validates each preference component in the inferred preference set against each of the
177   most relevant user demonstrations by prompting an LLM to determine whether the demonstration
178   strongly confirms, somewhat confirms, is neutral toward, somewhat contradicts, or strongly contra-
179   dicts the preference (prompt outline: Fig. 1 “Validation”). Each answer is mapped to a score from
180
      +2 (strongly confirms) to -2 (strongly contradicts). If the mean score across all demonstrations is
      below a manually specified threshold, the preference is removed. Examples of retained and dis-
181
      carded preference components are in Fig. 1 “Validated Preferences”. To avoid discarding correct,
182
      but rare, preferences, a preference component must be validated against a minimum of two user
183   demonstrations before it can be removed. In our experiments, we use a validation threshold of 0.25.
184
185   Preference Aggregation Following CIPHER Gao et al. (2024), before solving a new task, PRE-
186
      DICT retrieves up to five previous, relevant examples. The preferences inferred for each example
      are aggregated, and an LLM is prompted to remove redundancy and condense the combined set of
187
      preferences. The condensed preference set is then used to complete the task.

190   4       E XPERIMENTAL S ET U P
191
192   All of our experiments consist of three phases per task. First, the user completes the task using
193   their true preferences. Second, the agent attempts to complete the task using its currently inferred
194   preferences (if any). Finally, the agent compares its attempt at task completion with the user’s
195   example to infer new preferences to use going forward.
196   All agents are evaluated along two dimensions: preference quality that measures similarity between
197   the true and inferred preference sets, and action quality that evaluates an agent’s task completion
198   against the user’s true preferences. Note that the first task completion will always be conditioned on
199   an empty preference set and that we evaluate the preference set used to solve the task. Thus, the first
200   step is equivalent across all agents, and we omit its results.
201   The agent learns from 4 – 10 users (depending on the task) with five examples per user, and perfor-
202   mance is reported as the mean across all examples, users, and across five seeds (standard deviation
203   is reported over these seeds). The user preferences for the assistive writing tasks are in Appendix F
204   (Table 5), whereas the PICK UP task has a rule-based user preference construction procedure de-
205   scribed in Section 4.2. For all experiments, we use GPT-4o as the inferring agent except when we
206   compare LLMs of different sizes and quality (shown in Fig. 2) 2 . For the assistive writing tasks,
207   GPT-4o is used as a synthetic human. The synthetic human prompts can be found in Appendix H.2.

234   4.2   E NVIRONMENT 1: PICK UP
235
236   We develop the PICK UP (Policy Imitation by Comprehending Key User Preferences) task in a grid-
237   world environment populated with various objects of different shapes and colors. See Appendix A
238   Fig. 4 for an overview of PREDICT applied to the PICK UP task. Users in the environment navigate
239   to pick up objects with attributes (i.e., shape/color) they like, while avoiding objects with attributes
240   they dislike, before navigating to an end goal location. When an object is collected, a reward of
241   +1 is awarded for each liked attribute and a reward of -1 is awarded for each disliked attribute. For
242   example, an object whose shape and color are both liked would have a reward of +2, while an object
243
      whose shape is liked and color disliked has a reward of 0. Note the reward function is used only for
      evaluation purposes and does not play a role in preference learning.
244
245   For PICK UP, we automatically transform trajectories into a structured language description. Fig. 5
246   (Appendix D) shows a visual and natural language representation of the environment and its trajec-
247   tories. The objective of a preference inferring agent in this environment is to be able to collect the
248   same objects that the user’s would. To accomplish this, they must first identify the user’s likes and
249
      dislikes, and then navigate the world to collect the appropriate objects. We include the presence of
      neutral objects in the environment, which adds ambiguity to the system as neutral objects are only
250
      picked up if they are along the shortest path between desirable objects or the goal, which is not iden-
251
      tifiable from the text representation of the user’s example. Thus, from the perspective of an inferring
252   LLM, the environment is only partially observable. This design is intentional; motion is inherently
253   difficult to encode in language, so many tasks will be partially observable to an LLM. Due to the
254   partial observability, we require three validations to discard a preference in PICK UP.
255
      In this environment, each task instance is defined by a user identifier and an environment lay-
256
      out containing seven random objects placed at random locations in a 5x5 grid. The user identi-
257
      fier maps to a unique and private set of preferences. Each user’s preference set contains exactly
258   one liked shape, one liked color, one disliked shape, and one disliked color, however this infor-
259   mation is not provided to the inferring agent. These are all specified in the structured format:
260   <likes/dislikes><attribute>. Users are neutral toward all the remaining attributes.
261   The well-defined structure of the preferences in PICK UP allows us to map a preference set to a set
262   of positive reward objects and negative reward objects. We then use this mapping to condition an
263   A* agent that collects all the positive objects while avoiding negative objects.
264   The preference structure also enables direct comparison of preferences. To this end, we report
265   the Intersection over Union (IoU) between the inferred and true preference sets as the preference
266   quality metric. A downside of the rigid preference structure is that it requires us to decompose
267   preferences, which prevents us from addressing RQ2 in this environment. For the action quality
268   metric, we measure the cumulative reward, or return, of the agent’s trajectories. Each liked/disliked
269   attribute (shape or color) in the set of collected objects adds +1/-1 to the score respectively. For all
      experiments, we use 10 distinct users (N = 10).

      4.3   E NVIRONMENT 2: A SSISTIVE W RITING
271
272   PRELUDE: Gao et al. (2024) propose PRELUDE (PREference Learning from User’s Direct Edits)
273   as an environment to evaluate preference inferring algorithms. PRELUDE consists of two tasks:
274   summarizing articles and writing emails from notes. Each task has a set of users with each user
275   having a distinct set of preferences. Each user additionally writes their summaries/emails on differ-
276   ent topics, with each topic corresponding to a different source of articles/notes (e.g., chat forums,
277   paper abstracts, encyclopedia articles). The summarization and email writing tasks have five and
278   four users respectively.
279   For each task instance, the agent must write a summary or email using the article / notes and any
280   inferred preferences it has learned up to that point. The user is then asked if the agent’s output is
281   satisfactory based on their true preferences. If the agent’s output is satisfactory, the cost to the agent
282   is zero. If the agent’s output is not satisfactory, the user edits the agent’s output according to their
283   preferences, and a cost based on the extent of the edit is incurred.
284   PLUME: The objective of the PRELUDE environments is to evaluate how well a model infers a
285   user’s preferences and the cost of incorrectly inferred preferences. Therefore, it is vital that the
286   measure of inferred preference quality is highly correlated with the cost function. We analyze PRE-
287   LUDE (see below) and find that the chosen metrics, the editing process, and the sets of preferences
288   used are key limitations of the environment, which contribute to a weak correlation between the
289   quality of the preferences and the quality of the generated writing.
290   For these reasons, we develop a new environment based on same underlying tasks as PRELUDE,
291   which we call PLUME: Preference Learning from User Memos and Emails. As in Gao et al. (2024),
292   PLUME uses GPT-4o as a proxy-human to be our user. In the following sections, we provide a
293   detailed description of each limitation and how it is addressed by PLUME. An example of how
294   PREDICT is applied to PLUME’s summary task can be seen in Fig. 1.
295   Metric Correlation We begin by investigating the magnitude of the correlation between the pro-
296   posed preference quality metric — preference set accuracy3 — and action quality metric — Leven-
297   shtein distance (Levenshtein, 1966) — used in PRELUDE (Gao et al., 2024). To find this correlation,
298   for a given context, we generate the powerset of the preference set. We then create a population of
299   agents, each conditioned on one of the subsets from the powerset. These agents and a user com-
300   plete five instances of the task within their context, on which we measure the preference and action
301   quality. Intuitively, agents conditioned on larger subsets of the true preference set have a higher pref-
302   erence quality score and their generation quality should reflect this. We repeat across every context
303
      and both tasks, and calculate the Pearson correlation between every preference quality metric and
      every action quality metric. The results are shown in Appendix E.3 Table 4 (for metric correlation
304
      by task).
305
306   The results, reported in the first column of Appendix E.3, show a weak correlation (< 0.5) be-
307   tween the PRELUDE’s preference accuracy and Levenshtein distance. This can be explained by the
308   inherent limitations with the metrics. The accuracy metric relies on the “highest” BERTScore, and
309
      therefore cannot differentiate partially correct preferences from perfectly correct preferences. More-
      over, the Levenshtein distance can vary substantially between generations, leading to a wide range
310
      of possible costs even when the exact same preferences are used for the generation (an illustrative
311
      example of this is shown in Appendix G.1). Gao et al. (2024) allude to this fact as a motivation for
312   their two-stage editing process, and when we compare the results to a version of PRELUDE where
313   the user always generates summaries/emails directly from the article/notes instead of editing the
314   agent’s summary/email (PRELUDENoEdit ), we see a further drop in correlation. However, we pro-
315   pose addressing this issue using improved metrics, as the editing procedure itself imposes notable
316   limitations, which are discussed below.
317
      To this end, we investigate and compare several new preference and generation-quality metrics.
318   For the preference quality metric, we test using the BERTScore (Zhang* et al., 2020) directly. For
319   the action quality metric, we additionally test length-normalized Levenshtein distance (ln-L-dist),
320   BERTScore, and an LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023) metric inspired from the editing procedure
321   in PRELUDE. The LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation is a per preference-component match (PPCM) that
322

      The Editing Procedure Asking whether a generation matches the user’s preferences is inherently
335
      ambiguous in cases where they only partially meet the user’s preferences. Even if this ambiguity is
336
      resolved, generations that are not selected for editing incur no cost, which removes any incentive to
337   further improve the quality of the learned preferences. This limits the environments ability to differ-
338   entiate a wide range of methods. Lastly, the editing process unduly influences the user’s writing, as
339   demonstrated in Appendix G.2.
340
      In place of the editing, PLUME has the agent and user independently solve the task at every step.
341
      This removes any ambiguity on whether a generation should be edited and incur a cost, provides a
342
      smoother curve along which to evaluate different methods, prevents agents from influencing users,
343   and enables the agent to learn from every user example.
344
345   Preference Sets We observe the following limitations with PRELUDE’s preference sets: (1) certain
346
      preference components have little impact on the generated text, due to unclear definitions (e.g.,
      skillful foreshadowing) or similarity to default LLM behavior (e.g., clear); (2) Some
347
      preferences are repeated across several contexts (e.g., short, brief, concise appear in four of
348
      five summarization contexts); and (3) There is a large variance in preference set complexities (e.g.,
349   targeted to young children, storytelling, short sentences, playful
350   language, interactive, positive vs. question answering style).
351
352
      To address these, PLUME reworks the preference sets with the following criteria: (1) each prefer-
      ence set contains an equal number of components, (2) within each task, preference sets should have
353
      a shared structure, (3) as much as possible, preferences should be orthogonal to each other, avoid-
354
      ing overlapping preferences (e.g., write in the style of old-timey radio and use
355   archaic language) or contradictory preferences (e.g., use emojis and use a formal
356   tone). (4) Preferences should not follow an LLMs default biases — i.e., generating an output con-
357   ditioned on no preference should lead to a low score. A full list of the preferences used in PRELUDE
358   and PLUME is shown in Appendix F (Table 5). We encourage future researchers to use PLUME
359   with different preference sets to adjust difficulty or examine specific concepts.
360
      Knowledge of Contexts Instead of treating each article/notes topic as a distinct user, PRELUDE
361   introduces the additional challenge of context awareness where a single user has different prefer-
362   ences based on the topic of the article/notes. Therefore, prior to writing a summary or an email the
363   agent must first identify the correct context. However, this is orthogonal to the challenge of inferring
364   preferences from user examples. As this work focuses on how to infer preferences, the version of
365   PLUME used in all experiments assume a distinct known user per topic. We note that PLUME is
366   easily adaptable to use hidden contexts if desired.

      Discussion. Fig. 2 shows that the performance of PREDICT scales better with the quality of the
      underlying LLM (e.g., Llama3 70B-instruct vs. GPT-4o), compared to every other methods. Addi-
466
      tionally, as expected, performance increases as more user examples are seen (Fig. 6), with the largest
467
      performance gain from the first example.
468
469   While BERTScore is a more representative metric than the accuracy used in Gao et al. (2024),
470   it does not fully capture the impact of the inferred preferences: preferences can be written very
471
      differently, but lead to similar outcomes (e.g., use hashtags for emphasis versus write
      in the style of tweet). For this reason, we focus primarily on action quality in this paper,
472
      but encourage future work to investigate alternatives metrics that better capture preference intent.
473
474   While PREDICT outperforms all preference-conditioned and no-learning baselines, ICL and PRE-
475   DICT perform equally well on email writing with ICL outperforming PREDICT on summariza-
476   tion. All summarization tasks have a formatting/structure preference (e.g., write in the
477
      style of a tweet), which are difficult to capture using natural language preference descrip-
      tions. PREDICT often tries to capture these preferences using multiple relevant, but imper-
478
      fect preferences (e.g., use hashtags for emphasis, include emojis to create
479
      a playful tone, employ attention grabbing phrasing). We further investigate
480   the performance gap by comparing the performance across preference sets (Fig. 3) and find that
481   ICL excels on sets with the strongest structural preferences (e.g., write in the style of
482   a screenplay). In contrast, PREDICT outperforms ICL on the preference sets requiring
483   a more nuanced understanding of tone (e.g., be intensely emotional or be sharply
484   critical). While ICL generally performs well, preference conditioning has several advantages:
485   (1) preferences are easier to interact with than a dataset of in-context examples, (2) at inference
      time, it requires 10x fewer tokens, and (3) it can benefit a wider range of tasks, e.g., human-agent

508   5.1           L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
509
      While the methods proposed in this work provide a number of significant improvements, their limi-
510
      tations and challenges provide interesting avenues for future work.
511
512   First, in this paper we focus on learning with the fewest user examples possible. However another
513   aspect of efficiency is the total number of prompt and generated tokens used, and adding more
514
      refinement and preference validation steps increases the number of tokens used. In our experiments,
      PREDICTFull used (5.87x / 6.07x) more (prompt / generated) tokens on average than PREDICTBase .
515
      Given the monetary and environmental cost of LLMs, reducing the number of tokens while retaining
516
      performance is an important area for improvement.
517
518   Another limitation is the requirement to represent all trajectories in language. While this is possible
519   for the environments used here, it may not be possible in all domains (e.g., any environment requiring
520
      an understanding of subtle movement patterns). Future work is needed to investigate the use of
      multimodal foundations models, such as VLMs, to address this limitation.
521
522   Lastly, a full-scale human trial would provide a greater understanding of the benefits and limitations
523   of the proposed method. We look forward to investigating this more closely in future work.
524   Ethical Concerns The proposed method allows for greater personalization of assistive agents. How-
525   ever inferring a user’s preferences could be seen as an invasion of privacy. With this in mind, these
526   methods should be applied only with explicit consent from human users.

      6          C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this paper, we propose three novel contributions to guide an LLM to better infer preferences
531
      from user examples and introduce a new environment for evaluation. First, we iteratively refine
532   preferences by using a preference conditioned agent to test inferred preferences. Second, we break
533   preferences down into their constituent components. Third, we validate preferences against other
534   user examples. We demonstrate on both navigation and writing environments that the proposed
535   method improves performance by as much as 66.2% and 58.8%.

       G.1   L EVENSHTEIN D ISTANCE AND M ULTIPLE G ENERATIONS
1191
1192   We present here an inherent issue with Levenshtein distance. For all three generations, we prompt
1193   gpt-4o to summarize the encyclopedia article on hotdogs. The first two generations (A. and B.) use
1194   identical prompts, in which we ask it to use the preference: “use a haiku”. In the third generation, we
1195   ask it to use the preference “use a single short sentence”. We then calculate the Levenshtein distance
1196   between each pair, reported in Table 6. Notably, the Levenshtein distance and length normalized
1197
       Levenshtein distance, in which a lower score represents higher similarity, would both indicate that
       generation A and C are more similar to generation A and B, even though generation A and B share
1198
       the same exact same prompt while generation C uses a entirely different preference. BERTScore
1199
       does a better job of identifying the most similar texts, however the PPCM scores, which compares
1200   writing directly to the true preferences, clearly labels both Generation A. and Generation B. as
1201   “strongly exhibiting” the preference “use a haiku”, and Generation C. as “strongly contradicting”
1202   the preference.

       G.2   E DITING I NFLUENCES THE U SER
1243
1244   In this next example, we demonstrate how the editing procedure can influence the output of the user.
1245   We again ask an LLM to summarize the encyclopedia article on hot dog. Let’s assume, an inferring
1246   agent has incorrectly inferred the preference ”question answering style”. Using this preference, the
1247   preference-conditioned agent writes:
1248
                What is a hot dog?
1249
                A hot dog is a dish made of a sausage (typically a wiener or frankfurter) served in
1250
                a partially sliced bun. It can refer to the sausage itself, which is grilled, steamed,
1251            or boiled.
1252
                How is a hot dog typically prepared?
1253
                Hot dog preparation varies, but common condiments include mustard, ketchup,
1254
                relish, and onions. Some toppings can also include cheese sauce, sauerkraut, chili,
1255            bacon, or jalapeños.
1256            Where did hot dogs originate?
1257
                The sausages used in hot dogs were culturally imported from Germany, and the
1258            dish became popular in the U.S. as a working-class street food. Over time, hot
1259            dogs became closely associated with American culture, particularly baseball.
1260            Where are hot dogs most popular?
1261            While they are especially associated with New York City, hot dogs became
1262            widespread across the United States throughout the 20th century, becoming an
1263            integral part of American cuisine.
1264
1265   We ask the user if the above is satisfactory for it’s true preference of: “style targeted to young
1266
       children”. It does not, so we then ask the user to edit the above for it true preference “style targeted
       to young children”, and the user writes:
1267
1268            What is a hot dog?
1269            A hot dog is a yummy snack made of a sausage (like a wiener or frankfurter)
1270            inside a soft bun. Sometimes people call the sausage itself a hot dog, and it can be
1271            cooked by grilling, steaming, or boiling.
1272            How is a hot dog usually made?
1273            Hot dogs can be made in different ways, but they often have yummy toppings like
1274            mustard, ketchup, relish, and onions. Some people like to add even more fun stuff
1275            like cheese, sauerkraut, chili, bacon, or spicy jalapeños.
1276            Where did hot dogs come from?
1277            The sausages in hot dogs came from Germany, and a long time ago, they became
1278            super popular in the U.S. as a quick and tasty street food for busy people. Now,
1279            hot dogs are a big part of American culture, especially at baseball games!
1280            Where are hot dogs the most popular?
1281            Even though hot dogs are popular all over, they are especially loved in New York.
1282            Over the years, hot dogs have become a favorite food all across the United States!
1283
       While the above summary does correctly follow the true preference, it is still formatted in a question
1284
       answering style, which is not part of the user’s preferences. I.e., the preference-inferring agent’s
1285   error has directly impacted the user’s writing. This is another reason why PLUME removes the
1286   editing procedure.
1287
1288
       For reference, if the user is directly asked to summmarize the encyclopedia article on hot dogs using
       it’s true preference of “style targeted to young children”, it outputs:
1289
1290            A hot dog is a yummy food that’s made with a sausage, like a wiener or frankfurter,
1291            inside a soft bun. You can cook the sausage in different ways, like grilling or
1292            boiling. People love to put tasty toppings on their hot dogs, such as ketchup,
1293            mustard, cheese, and even onions!
1294            Hot dogs came from Germany, but they became super popular in the US, espe-
1295            cially at baseball games. Now, you can find hot dogs all over the country, and in
                some places, like Chicago, people have special ways of making them!
