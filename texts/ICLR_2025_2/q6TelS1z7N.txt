009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study correlation clustering where the pairwise similarities are not known in
012            advance. For this purpose, we employ active learning to query pairwise similarities
013            in a cost-efficient way. We propose a number of effective information-theoretic
014            acquisition functions based on entropy and information gain. We extensively
015            investigate the performance of our methods in different settings and demonstrate
016            their superior performance compared to the alternatives.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
020
      Clustering is an important unsupervised learning problem for which several methods have been
021
      proposed in different contexts. Correlation clustering (CC) (Bansal et al., 2004; Demaine et al.,
022
      2006) is a well-known clustering problem, especially beneficial when both similarity and dissimilarity
023   assessments exist for a given set of N objects. Consequently, CC studies the clustering of objects
024   where pairwise similarities can manifest as positive or negative numbers. It has found a wide range
025   of applications including image segmentation (Kim et al., 2011), bioinformatics (Bonchi et al., 2013),
026   spam filtering (Ramachandran et al., 2007; Bonchi et al., 2014), social network analysis (Bonchi et al.,
027   2012; Tang et al., 2016), duplicate detection (Hassanzadeh et al., 2009), co-reference identification
028   (McCallum & Wellner, 2004), entity resolution (Getoor & Machanavajjhala, 2012), color naming
029   across languages (Thiel et al., 2019) and clustering aggregation (Gionis et al., 2007; Chehreghani
030   & Chehreghani, 2020). CC was initially explored using binary pairwise similarities in {‚ąí1, +1}
031
      (Bansal et al., 2004), and was later extended to support arbitrary positive and negative pairwise
      similarities in R (Charikar et al., 2005; Demaine et al., 2006). Finding the optimal solution for CC is
032
      known to be NP-hard and APX-hard (Bansal et al., 2004; Demaine et al., 2006), presenting significant
033
      challenges. As a result, various approximate algorithms have been developed to address this problem
034   (Bansal et al., 2004; Charikar et al., 2005; Demaine et al., 2006; Ailon et al., 2008; Elsner & Schudy,
035   2009). Among these, methods based on local search are noted for their superior performance in terms
036   of clustering quality and computational efficiency (Thiel et al., 2019; Chehreghani, 2023).
037
      Existing methods generally assume that all N2 pairwise similarities are available beforehand.

038
039
      However, as discussed in (Bressan et al., 2019; Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020), generating pairwise
      similarities can be computationally intensive and may need to be obtained through resource-intensive
040
      queries, e.g., from a human expert. For instance, determining interactions between biological entities
041
      often requires the expertise of highly trained professionals, consuming both time and valuable
042   resources (Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020). In tasks like entity resolution, obtaining pairwise similarity
043   queries through crowd-sourcing could also involve monetary costs. Therefore, a central question
044   emerges: How can we design a machine learning paradigm that effectively delivers satisfactory CC
045   results with a limited number of queries for pairwise similarities between objects?
046
      In machine learning, active learning is generally employed to address such a question. Its objective
047
      is to acquire the most informative data within a constrained budget. Active learning has proven
048   effective in various tasks, including recommender systems (Rubens et al., 2015), sound event
049   detection (Shuyang et al., 2020), analysis of driving time series (Jarl et al., 2022), drug discovery
050   (Viet Johansson et al., 2022), and analysis of logged data (Yan et al., 2018). In the context of active
051   learning, the selection of which data to query is guided by an acquisition function. Active learning
052   is most commonly studied for classification and regression problems (Settles, 2009). However, it
053   has also been studied for clustering and is sometimes referred to as supervised clustering (Awasthi
      & Zadeh, 2010). The objective is to discover the ground-truth clustering with a minimal number of

054
      queries to an oracle (e.g., a human expert). In this scenario, queries are typically executed in one
055   of two ways: (i) By asking whether two clusters should merge or if one cluster should be divided
056   into multiple clusters (Balcan & Blum, 2008; Awasthi & Zadeh, 2010; Awasthi et al., 2017); (ii) By
057   querying the pairwise relations between objects (Basu et al., 2004; Mazumdar & Saha, 2017b;a; Saha
058   & Subramanian, 2019; Bressan et al., 2019; Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020; van Craenendonck et al.,
059   2018b; Silwal et al., 2023; Gullo et al., 2023; Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024; Kuroki et al., 2024).
060
      Among the aforementioned works on active learning for clustering, only (Mazumdar & Saha, 2017b;
061   Bressan et al., 2019; Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020; Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024; Kuroki et al., 2024)
062   consider the setting that we are interested in: (i) The clustering algorithm is based on CC; (ii) The
063   pairwise similarities are not assumed to be known     in advance; (iii) We assume access to a single
      noisy oracle, to which a fixed budget B ‚Č™ N2 of queries for pairwise similarities can be performed;

064
065   (iv) Access to feature vectors is not assumed by the algorithm, meaning that information about
066   the ground-truth clustering is solely obtained through querying the oracle for pairwise similarities.
067   Throughout the paper, this setting will be referred to as active correlation clustering.
068
      The work in (Mazumdar & Saha, 2017b) develops a number of pivot-based CC algorithms that satisfy
069   guarantees on the query complexity, assuming a noisy oracle. However, the algorithms are purely
070   theoretical and are not implemented and investigated in practice, and require setting a number of
071   non-trivial parameters (e.g., they assume the noise level is known in advance which is unrealistic).
072   The work in (Bressan et al., 2019; Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020) proposes adaptive and query-efficient
073   versions of the simple pivot-based CC algorithm KwikCluster (Ailon et al., 2008). However, as
074   demonstrated in (Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024), such pivot-based methods perform very poorly
075   for active CC with noise. The work in (Gullo et al., 2023; Kuroki et al., 2024) address query-efficient
076   CC by formulating it as a multi-armed bandit problem. However, this leads to a number of limiting
077
      assumptions in practice. We defer a detailed comparison to Appendix E.
078   The work in (Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024) proposes a generic active CC framework that over-
079   comes the limitations of previous work and offers several advantages: (i) The pairwise similarities
080   can be any positive or negative real number, even allowing for inconsistencies (i.e., violation of
081   transitivity). This allows the oracle to express uncertainty in their feedback; (ii) The process of
082   querying pairwise similarities is decoupled from the clustering algorithm, enhancing flexibility in
083
      constructing acquisition functions that can be employed in conjunction with any CC algorithm.
      (Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024) employs an efficient CC algorithm based on local search, whose
084
      effectiveness (and superiority over pivot-based methods) has also been demonstrated in the standard
085
      CC setting (Thiel et al., 2019; Chehreghani, 2023), and dynamically computes the number of clusters;
086   (iii) The framework is robust w.r.t. a noisy oracle and supports multiple queries for the same pairwise
087   similarity if needed (to deal with noise).
088
089
      Furthermore, (Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024) proposes two novel acquisition functions, namely
      maxmin and maxexp, to be used within their framework. They demonstrate that the algorithm QECC
090
      from (Garc√≠a-Soriano et al., 2020) performs poorly in the presence of even a very small amount of
091
      noise and is significantly outperformed by their methods. In this paper, we adopt the generic active
092   CC framework in (Aronsson & Chehreghani, 2024) with a focus on the development of more effective
093   acquisition functions. The contributions of this paper are the following:
094
095   ‚ÄĘ We investigate the use of information-theoretic acquisition functions based on entropy and infor-
096     mation gain for active CC. We propose four different acquisition functions inspired by this (see
097     Section 3). Although information-theoretic acquisition functions have been extensively studied in
098     the context of active learning (Roy & McCallum, 2001; Kirsch & Gal, 2022), prior research has
099     focused mainly on (active) supervised learning scenarios, where the goal is to query data labels
100     from an oracle rather than pairwise relations. To our knowledge, our work is the first attempt to
101     propose information-theoretic acquisition functions to active learning with pairwise relations, as
102
        well as to non-parametric models like CC. Computing the necessary quantities in this setting is
        significantly more complex. The methods proposed in this paper can be applied beyond active CC,
103
        including to the active learning of other pairwise (non-parametric) clustering models.
104
105   ‚ÄĘ We conduct extensive experimental studies on various datasets that demonstrate the superior
106     performance of our acquisition functions compared to maxmin and maxexp (and other baselines),
107     and investigate a number of interesting insights about the active CC framework from (Aronsson &
        Chehreghani, 2024) (see Section 4 and Appendix C).

256   The acquisition function aEntropy calculates the uncertainty of pairs based on the mean-field approxi-

535   In this paper, we proposed four effective information-theoretic acquisition functions to be used
536   for active CC: aEntropy , aEIG-O , aEIG-P and aJEIG . All of our methods significantly outperform the
