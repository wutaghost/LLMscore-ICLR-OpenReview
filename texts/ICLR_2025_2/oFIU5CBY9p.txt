                                                      A BSTRACT
008
009
                Tabular data is one of the most ubiquitous data modalities, yet the literature on
010             tabular generative foundation models is lagging behind its text and vision counter-
011             parts. Large Tabular Models (LTMs) could revolutionize the way tabular data is
012             used: not as any single dataset analyzed in a vacuum, but contextualized using their
013             metadata and with respect to related datasets. Creating an LTM is difficult, due to
014             the heterogeneous feature spaces of different tabular datasets, metadata, and prior
015             knowledge. In this work, we propose LaTable: a novel tabular diffusion model
016             that addresses these challenges. We show LaTable can be trained across tabular
017             datasets. Through extensive experiments, we find that LaTable displays early signs
018
                of scaling laws previously encountered in foundation model regimes. Moreover,
                LaTable outperform baselines in out-of-distribution few-shot data generation.

021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Motivation. Foundation models in the image and text domains epitomize the value of large scale
024   training with impressive results that push the boundaries of AI capabilities. In this work, we propose
025   LaTable, a tabular diffusion model that can be trained across vastly different datasets and demonstrates
026   early signs of scaling laws, in hopes that LaTable become the basis for a tabular foundation model.
027   Tabular foundation models, or Large Tabular Models (LTMs), lag far behind their text and vision coun-
028   terparts, and existing LTM research focuses on representation and supervised learning Van Breugel
029   & Van Der Schaar (2024). Despite this lag, tabular data is ubiquitous in the real world Borisov
030   et al. (2022a); Shwartz-Ziv & Armon (2022), ranging from electronic health records Fatima & Pasha
031   (2017) to census data Doxsey-Whitfield et al. (2015), from cybersecurity Buczak & Guven (2015)
032   to credit scoring Dastile et al. (2020), and from finance to natural sciences Huang et al. (2012).
033   A generative LTM could transform these fields through enabling few-shot generation of synthetic
034   data, and providing a base model that can be fine-tuned to other tasks like representation learning or
035
      prediction van Breugel & van der Schaar (2023). Few-shot capabilities are especially interesting for
      the tabular domain, where datasets are often smaller, and augmenting existing datasets via few-shot
036
      generation of additional columns is of significant interest to researchers and practitioners alike.
037
038   Aim. Developing a generative LTM is difficult. First, until recently there has been a lack of large
039   tabular metadatasets. Second, different datasets cover vastly different features, and the existing tabular
040   datasets may also use different formats or lack labels for any specific task. Third, tabular datasets
041
      may require domain knowledge, or some prior on tabular features. As a result of this, training models
      meaningfully on large amounts of heterogeneous data remains underexplored. In this paper, we take a
042
      step towards LTMs by developing a versatile tabular generative model with the following desiderata:
043
044        D1 Cross-dataset generation. We need an LTM to be able to generate different tables, which
045           requires it being able to generate different features and variable number of features.
046        D2 Categorical and numerical feature generation. Tables consist of combinations of both
047           continuous and discrete data, and we want to be able to model each explicitly. 1
048
           D3 Use textual context. The model will need to understand contextual metadata, including the
049           dataset description, feature names, and allowed categories for categorical features.
050
           D4 Equivariance w.r.t. column order. Table column order is usually arbitrary, which poses
051
              an equivariance desiderata. If we define generative model G : S L × M → OL , where
052

058   Contributions. Our contributions are as follows:
059
060         1. We introduce LaTable, a novel tabular diffusion model that satisfies these desiderata. Thus,

070   2   R ELATED WORK
071
072   Tabular single-dataset generative models. Tabular data is challenging due to its lack of structural
073   meaning (unlike images), mixed-type variables, and often limited size Van Breugel & Van Der Schaar
074   (2024). A growing body of work is developing generative models tailored to tabular data Choi et al.
075   (2017); Xu et al. (2019); Watson et al. (2023), including score-based diffusion models Dieleman et al.
076
      (2022); Kotelnikov et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023). Nonetheless, most methods ignore or naively
      one-hot-encode categorical data (e.g. feature names and categories), which loses context that could
077
      help overcome data availability issues (D2 D3). Naturally, methods developed for single datasets are
078
      also not designed for cross-dataset generation (D1), or equivariance with respect to columns (D4).
079
080   LLM-based approaches. Pretrained language models may provide a solution to cross-dataset
081   generation, as they contain general knowledge and can represent strings as numerical vectors in a
082
      space structured by language supervision. Uniformly, all the works in this direction convert the rows
      of tables into strings that can be processed by the LLM. The string generated by the LLM is then
083
      converted back to a tabular format. Borisov et al. (2022b) fine-tuned GPT-2 Radford et al. (2019)
084
      on tables, and they aim to achieve approximate equivariance (D4) through randomly generating
085   feature permutations, and sampling token distributions autoregressively rather than sampling only the
086   most likely sentences. Solatorio & Dupriez (2023) note that the approach in Borisov et al. (2022b)
087   retains GPT-2’s original vocabulary, yet most tokens may not appear in the tables of interest. They
088   instead use a fixed-set vocabulary Padhi et al. (2021), which reduces the chances of generating invalid
089   samples and thus improves efficiency. Zhao et al. (2023) show that an untrained, smaller LLM can
090   generate more accurate data and do so more cheaply than Borisov et al. (2022b). We note that in
091   contrast to us, previous works like Borisov et al. (2022b); Solatorio & Dupriez (2023); Zhao et al.
092   (2023) fine-tune their model on just a single tabular dataset, i.e. they do not attempt cross-dataset
093
      training and do not aim to create a generative model that generalizes beyond the training data (D1).
094   The advantage of LLM-based generation is its simplicity and the fact that it does not require any
095   preprocessing of data, since the raw table can be presented to the LLM directly via prompting and in-
096   context learning. However, adapting LLMs as LTMs comes with serious disadvantages Van Breugel
097   & Van Der Schaar (2024). First, LLMs are expensive during both fine-tuning and inference. A
098   1-minute training job for CTGAN takes a whopping 540× longer for Borisov et al. (2022b) (see their
099
      Appendix B.5, Table 6). One of the core sources of this inefficiency is the linearization of table
      rows as sentences, and generating these autoregressively. For tabular data, where many columns
100
      are typically numerical, this is problematic—it means that a single numerical variable is implicitly
101
      modelled as an autoregressive series of categorical variables (e.g. 1.23 is modelled as 1 → . → 23).
102   Hence, generating this number with an LLM requires 3 expensive forward calls to the model (plus
103   one for the field separation token). Fitting the whole table in context might also become difficult
104   with datasets containing hundreds of columns. Furthermore, the LLM training objective is not apt at
105   approximating continuous distributions. Hopkins et al. (2023) show that LLMs do not accurately
106   generate simple distributions (e.g. uniform). Van Breugel & Van Der Schaar (2024) visualize how
107   modelling a simple Gaussian with an LLM is non-trivial. By opting for an end-to-end numerical
      representation of the data and a diffusion model framework, LaTable circumvents these limitations.

      Summary. We propose LaTable, which satisfies the desiderata D1-D4. LaTable uses an encoder-
111   only transformer as backbone for mixed-type diffusion. The input of the model consists of noised
112   feature values (i.e. from the forward noising process), but also the dataset description, column
113   names, conditioning mask (in case of conditional generation), and boolean missingness mask. All
114   metadata strings (e.g. dataset description and feature names) and categorical features are first encoded
115   using a pretrained LLM into a fixed-length embedding. All inputs are subsequently mapped to a
116   common hidden space using separate element-wise MLPs, and added to provide the direct input of the
117   transformer. The output of the transformer is decoded on a feature-by-feature basis, where categorical
118   features are mapped to probabilities over the original features. See Figure 1 for an overview.

131   3.2     G ENERATIVE MODEL CHOICE
132
      We use a score-based diffusion model Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019) for modelling the
133
      data distribution. Diffusion models have increased in popularity thanks to their capability to produce
134
      samples of higher quality than GANs and VAEs Goodfellow et al. (2020); Kingma (2013). We use

142   3.3.1     T RANSFORMER BACKBONE (D1, D4)
143
      We use an encoder-only transformer at the core of LaTable. The transformer backbone is useful for
144   our setting, as it allows variable-length input and output, which allows us to train across datasets
145   (D1). We do not use a positional encoding for the input, such that the transformer is equivariant
146   with respect to the input (D4). In contrast, a recurrent architecture would not necessarily satisfy this
147   equivariance property. 3 We denote the transformer’s hidden dimension by dh .

149   3.3.2     U SING CONTEXT (D3)
150
151

      Categorical variables. To handle categorical variables, we draw inspiration from Continuous
      Diffusion for Categorical Data (CDCD) Dieleman et al. (2022). The original CDCD learns high-
188
      dimensional embeddings for each category separately, before applying L2 normalization to each
189
      embedding (after which noise is added for the denoising). During training, CDCD adds noise to
190   these categorical embeddings (identical to continuous diffusion), and trains a model that predicts
191   probabilities for the original categories. This model is trained using some classification loss, e.g.
192   cross-entropy. During sampling, the outputted probabilities are used to predict the score in the
193   continuous space. Specifically, they use score interpolation—which is effectively a weighted mean of
194   all category embeddings, where weights are given by the probabilities predicted by the score model.
195

201   embeddings using a shallow MLP followed by an L2 normalization layer, which we together denote

      4                                 E XPERIMENTS
294
295
296   In this section, we will explore the tabular data generation capabilities of LaTable. We show that
297
      LaTable displays early signs of scaling laws commonly found in foundation model regimes. Then, we
      benchmark the performance of LaTable against popular baseline tabular models, as well as ablation
298
      experiments that justify the design choice of our method.
299
300   Evaluation Pipeline We follow a four-step process to evaluate the performance of LaTable in the
301   few-shot out-of-distribution generation setting, partially inspired by the "Train on Synthetic, Test on
302   Real" methodology. (1) We obtain the final model checkpoint after training has converged (under
303
      different training settings). (2) We choose a test dataset. More details about how the test datasets are
      selected and what test datasets we use will be discussed shortly. (3) We perform minor fine-tuning
304
      on 100 data samples (which is very small for synthetic data generation models), and we generate
305
      synthetic data given the dataset metadata. (4) Finally, we use a well-known classifier model to fit the
306   generated synthetic data, and evaluate on real data using downstream performance metrics such as
307   accuracy, f1 score, and Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (rocauc). By default,
308   we will use the CatBoost classifier because it is has strong performance on tabular dataset tasks.
309
      Training data. In order to make meaningful advancement towards foundation models, we need to
310
      gather a training set for tabular data whose scale surpasses any existing collections of tabular datasets.
311
      We build upon the recent effort of Tablib Eggert et al. (2023), which scrapes over 600M tables from
312   the web. After filtering through the data via heuristics and standard data cleaning, we arrive at a
313   subset of 100K tabular datasets. Finally, we chop each dataset down to 1K rows ensure an even
314   spread of data during training. In the end, the training set totals 100M rows, spanning 30K columns.
315
      Test data, and combating data leakage. Because the datasets from Tablib are scraped from the web,
316
      there is no sensible way to rule out data contamination or data leakage in the training data. In order
317
      to best prevent data leakage and contamination, we gathered several tabular datasets that were not
318   commonly found on the web. These datasets are Cardio, URL, WiDS, Insurance, and Heloc Stoian
319   et al. (2024). In addition, for the tabular datasets that we ask LaTable to generate, we deliberately
320   pick the ones that LaTable cannot generate from zero-shot, hence verifying that LaTable did not
321   remember the training data. The core component of our evaluation is to show that training across a
322   large number of datasets indeed gives LaTable the ability to generate synthetic tabular datasets with
323   very few fine-tuning examples, and potentially pave a path for building larger and better LTMs for
      future research.

      4.1   S CALING LAWS : M ODEL AND DATA
325
326   In this section, we show that LaTable displays early signs of scaling laws commonly found in
327   foundation model regimes. We vary different training settings, and record the final downstream
328   performance from the synthetic data generation after the training has converged. We change the
329   model size by varying the number of transformer layers in the architecture, arriving at model sizes of
330   40M (2 layers), 80M (6 layers), and 118M (10 layers). We change the dataset sizes by varying the
331   number of tables we include in the training set, arriving at dataset sizes of 1M (1K tables), 10M (10K
332
      tables), and 100M (100K tables). When varying the model sizes, the dataset size is fixed at 100M
      (100K tables). When varying the dataset sizes, the model size is fixed at 118M (10 layers).
333
334   As we can see in Figure 2, the performance of LaTable follows a log-linear trend, mirroring that
335   of early scaling laws in foundation models regimes. That is, as we increase the model/dataset size
336   exponentially, we obtain a linear increase in the final performance. Interestingly, the model seems
337   to yield the best performance when trained with roughly the same dataset size as parameter count,
338
      which could suggest further directions for tabular scaling laws.

454   5     D ISCUSSION
455
456   Summary We have motivated this paper by describing the effect Large Tabular Models (LTMs)
457   could have on the way tabular data is used: not as any single dataset analyzed in a vacuum, but
458   contextualized using their metadata and with respect to related datasets. We acknowledge the difficulty
459   in creating an LTM, due to the heterogeneous feature spaces of different tabular datasets, different
460   specifications of tabular data tasks, and even messy/insufficient data. In order to make a meaningful
461   first step towards creating LTMs, we propose LaTable, which can be trained across tabular datasets
462   (D1), takes information from categorical, numerical, and textual features (D2, D3), is equivariant
463
      with respect to the input (D4), and displays early signs of scaling laws previously encountered
      in foundation model regimes as well as outperform baselines in out-of-distribution few-shot data
464
      generation. We would like to conclude this paper with discussions about limitations, impacts, and
465
      next steps.
466
467   Binary classification, Multi-class, and Regression It is insightful to discuss a bit more about the
468   way we select the test datasets. We have selected five test datasets—Cardio, URL, WiDS, Insurance,
469
      and Heloc—so that we can best ensure there will not be any data leakage issues. At the same
      time, all five datasets belong to the same task—binary classification. This is not a coincidence.
470
      Intuitively, binary classification is much easier than multi-class classification and regression, for
471
      downstream models and synthetic data generation alike. This is because the number of modes for
472   a binary classification data distribution is likely to be significantly lower than other tabular dataset
473   tasks. When running experiments for LaTable, we have attempted to execute the same evaluation
474   pipeline for multi-class classification and regression datasets (like Gesture), but the performance did
475   not come close to the downstream results obtained on binary classification tasks, or the ground truth
476   downstream performance. We postulate that multi-class classification datasets and regression datasets
477   are inherently more complex datasets, and therefore more difficult to generate. This marks the proper
478   understanding of different types of tabular datasets to be a valuable and worthy direction of future
479   research.
480   Going forward. We think it would be beneficial for the community to list out a few directions of
481   LTM research
482
483           • Scaling Laws. Unlike in text and in vision, we don’t have a good intuition of the type of
484             scaling laws that would appear in LTMs. For instance, we do not yet know whether there is
485             a scaling law for model size and dataset size, like the ones we have seen in large language
                model regimes. Nor do we know the optimal compute ratio when it comes to training and


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
               evaluating LTMs. The proper scale of an LTM model and its dataset is likewise uncertain.
487            All of these are valuable directions for future research.
488
             • Dataset Tasks. As we have noted in earlier sections, LaTable performs well across binary
489
               classification datasets, but the reason why its performance drops on multi-class and regres-
490
               sion datasets still elude us. It is possible that the latter datasets are inherently more difficult
491            to capture. Whether or not that is the case, much more research effort should be spent into
492            understanding the different types of tabular datasets, and how they are different in the nature
493            of their tasks.
494
             • High-quality data. Apart from Tablib, we have also tried training on WikiTables, another
495            large-scale tabular dataset in the wild. However, LaTable’s performance does not improve
496            with WikiTable training, possibly due to the fact that tables that cover a wide range of topics
497            with context from the Wikipedia article are not high-quality tabular datasets. These exper-
498            iment results emphasize the importance of good quality data in improving generalization
499            performance of LTMs. The issue of data quality may be resolved by better understanding of
500            large metadatasets like TabLib, and using heavy-handed curation and selection. To avoid
501            data leakage between the training and evaluation sets, this also requires robust tools for
502            identifying and removing duplicates, which is hard for tabular datasets since pre-processed
503
               and filtered copies are common. Another possible avenue for future work is to use more
               specialized databases for training, e.g. of high-throughput biological data.
504
505
      Limitations. LaTable can be extended and improved in multiple ways:
506
507          • Scale and data. As discussed in the previous section, LaTable is a relatively small model
508            trained on a relatively small dataset. Scaling up LaTable and discovering larger scaling laws
509            is one of the key challenges for future research. In addition, we must simultaneously search
510            for high-quality data that spans binary classification, multi-class, and regression problems.
511          • Extending variable and table types. We restrict ourselves to numerical and categorical
512            data, but future work could consider date time, full string descriptions, time-series, and
513            relational databases to increase applicability and impact. In addition, future work could also
514            explore in-depth the inter-feature relationships and interactions between features as source
515            of information.
516          • Bias. In this work, we did not explore possible bias in the data and pre-trained LLMs, or
517            how LaTable copies that bias. More research into bias is required before applying LaTable
518            to the real-world, see broader impact below.
519
520   Broader Impact. It is insightful to recognize why LTMs can be difficult to create. Even though
521   there is an abundance of tabular data in the real world, in-the-wild tabular data can also be very large
522
      and complex, often containing thousands of columns and millions of rows. This introduces obvious
      problems such as complicated data preprocessing pipelines, expensive computation, etc. Because
523
      the scale of tabular data is so large, even the features that are observed during training might not be
524
      fully captured by the model—there may be a significant shift in the feature distribution from train to
525   test, let alone how one feature interacts (e.g. correlates) with other features, and the interaction of
526   features will often require domain knowledge to effectively annotate, i.e. LLMs may not be able to
527   approximate. All the difficulties we have discussed in this paper should be taken into consideration
528   when quantifying the difficulty of creating foundation models for tabular data. Nevertheless, LTMs
529   possess the huge potential to transform how we process real-world data as we know it. We believe the
530   broader impact of LaTable, and continued work in this area, is primarily positive. LaTable can enable
531   better synthetic data using fewer samples, which could promote more responsible AI. For example, to
532   improve minority representation using data augmentation, ML model testing through data simulation,
533
      and data democratization through private synthetic data. The latter is especially interesting, since
      few-shot generation may require a smaller privacy budget than standard synthetic data (after all the
534
      model need not learn the full distribution from the private data and may be less likely to memorize
535
      this data). Nonetheless, we need to acknowledge the risk: LaTable may make errors and may reflect
536   or exacerbate societal biases that are present in the data, or in the pretrained LLM embeddings. More
537   research into possible biases is required. The quality and fairness of generated data should always be
538   evaluated before applying LaTable to real-world sensitive settings like healthcare and finance. We
539   look forward to future research on tabular foundation models.
