009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent approaches attempt to adapt powerful interactive segmentation models,
012            such as SAM, to interactive matting and fine-tune the models based on synthetic
013            matting datasets. However, models trained on synthetic data fail to generalize to
014            complex and occlusion scenes. We address this challenge by proposing a new mat-
015            ting dataset based on the COCO dataset, namely COCO-Matting. Specifically, the
016            construction of our COCO-Matting includes accessory fusion and mask-to-matte,
017            which selects real-world complex images from COCO and converts semantic seg-
018            mentation masks to matting labels. The built COCO-Matting comprises an exten-
019
               sive collection of 38,251 human instance-level alpha mattes in complex natural
               scenarios. Furthermore, existing SAM-based matting methods extract intermedi-
020
               ate features and masks from a frozen SAM and only train a lightweight matting
021
               decoder by end-to-end matting losses, which do not fully exploit the potential of
022            the pre-trained SAM. Thus, we propose SEMat which revamps the network ar-
023            chitecture and training objectives. For network architecture, the proposed feature-
024            aligned transformer learns to extract fine-grained edge and transparency features.
025            The proposed matte-aligned decoder aims to segment matting-specific objects and
026            convert coarse masks into high-precision mattes. For training objectives, the pro-
027            posed regularization and trimap loss aim to retain the prior from the pre-trained
028            model and push the matting logits extracted from the mask decoder to contain
029            trimap-based semantic information. Extensive experiments across seven diverse
030
               datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our method, proving its efficacy
               in interactive natural image matting. Code is available in the supplementary file.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Image matting (Ruzon & Tomasi, 2000; Levin et al., 2007) aims to predict the alpha mattes for
037   foreground subjects, enabling their seamless extraction from complex backgrounds. This capability
038   is indispensable for a multitude of applications, including film production, video editing, and graphic
039
      design (Li et al., 2023a). Mainstream matting methods (Yao et al., 2024a) take auxiliary trimaps as
      input, which divide the image into foreground, background, and unknown regions, and thus facilitate
040
      the image matting to the refinement of the unknown regions. Nonetheless, labeling trimaps is labour-
041
      consuming which limits their practical use. To address this, interactive matting (Ding et al., 2022)
042   has emerged as a solution, replacing trimaps with more accessible cues such as bounding boxes
043   (BBox), points, or scribbles. Among these, BBox are particularly advantageous because of their
044   ease of acquisition, superior accuracy relative to other prompts (Ye et al., 2024), and compatibility
045   with object detection networks for automatic matting on predefined classes (Li et al., 2024). Thus,
046   our work primarily focuses on interactive matting using BBox as the auxiliary input.
047
      Unfortunately, due to the hard-to-obtain alpha matte annotations, existing interactive matting meth-
048   ods (Li et al., 2024) combine an alpha matte with multiple background images (Ye et al., 2024) to
049   generate synthetic training data. These synthetic data suffer from a distribution discrepancy com-
050   pared to natural data, hindering the network’s generalization to natural scenes. To overcome this,
051   recent methods have sought the help from the robust pre-trained networks, like Segment Anything
052   Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) which is trained on one billion real-world masks. For instance,
053   approaches like MatAny (Yao et al., 2024b) and MAM (Li et al., 2024) utilize SAM’s intermediate
      features or segmentation masks to construct an additional matting network.

      However, the SAM-based approaches have not fully exploited the potential of pre-trained SAM, and
      are encumbered by several critical issues. (1) Inappropriate Data. Training on synthetic datasets
082
      like RefMatte (Li et al., 2023b) or simple natural datasets like P3M-10K (Li et al., 2021a) in Fig-
083
      ure 1a is insufficient for robust generalization. This limitation not only impedes the model’s adap-
084   tation to diverse natural scenes like HIM2K dataset (Sun et al., 2022a) but also risks undermining
085   the diverse pre-trained knowledge inherent in SAM. (2) Feature Mismatch. The intermediate fea-
086   tures and predicted masks from a frozen SAM are not optimally aligned with the nuanced demands
087   of matting which necessitates an enhanced sensitivity to edge refinement and transparency percep-
088   tion, as well as the mask prediction for transparent matting-specific objects that are not common in
089   segmentation datasets. (3) Loss Constraints. Traditional matting losses supervise the network to
090   learn the synthetic training data, neglecting the broader imperative of generalization across natural
091   images. This oversight limits the model’s applicability and efficacy in real-world scenarios.
092   Contribution. To solve these challenges, we propose the COCO-Matting dataset consisting of
093   complex natural images and Semantic Enhanced Matting (SEMat) framework to revamp training
094   datasets, network architecture, and training objectives, greatly improving the interactive matting
095   performance. Our main contributions are highlighted below.
096   Firstly, to solve the first challenge of inappropriate data, we construct the COCO-Matting dataset
097   based on the renowned COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014). Considering the complicated interactions
098   between human and their surrounding environment, we focus on humans as the primary subjects
099   and create 38,251 instance-level alpha mattes. Notably, as shown in Figure 1a, our dataset is unique
100   in featuring complex natural scenarios, setting it apart from others. Specifically, the construction
101   of our COCO-Matting is composed of Accessory Fusion and Mask-to-Matte. (1) Accessory Fusion
102   aims to solve the problem of missing accessories for human mask annotations through the overlap
103   rate between masks, i.e., merging the accessory masks, such as hats, backpacks, or items being held
104
      that are considered part of human in matting tasks. (2) For Mask-to-Matte, it is proposed to solve
      the problem of coarse mask annotations by converting binary masks into continuous high-precision
105
      alpha mattes with a trained trimap-based network (Hu et al., 2023) to match the matting annotations.
106
107   Secondly, to address the second and third challenges, we design a novel and effective SEMat frame-
      work which improves the network architecture and training objectives. (1) Our network in SEMat

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
130
131   Interactive Matting. Trimap-based methods (Yao et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2023) have demonstrated
132   impressive accuracy in image matting. However, trimaps are hard to obtain in real-world scenarios.
133   Consequently, several approaches have sought alternative auxiliary inputs, such as backgrounds (Lin
134   et al., 2021), coarse segmentation maps (Yu et al., 2021), and interactive prompts (e.g., points,
135
      scribbles, and BBox) (Yang et al., 2022; 2020).
136   To eliminate the ambiguity of multiple objects in one image, UIM (Yang et al., 2022) introduces
137   interactive prompts and decouples the matting into foreground segmentation and transparency pre-
138   diction. However, it is trained and tested only on the synthetic dataset (Xu et al., 2017). Recently,
139   MatAny (Yao et al., 2024b) and MAM (Li et al., 2024) leverage the robust generalization capabili-
140   ties of pre-trained SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) to transfer from interactive segmentation to interactive
141
      matting. MatAny employs a training-free two-stage pipeline: it creates trimaps by eroding the masks
      generated from SAM and then predicts alpha mattes with a trained trimap-based network (Yao et al.,
142
      2024a). In contrast, MAM trains a learnable mask-to-matte module that takes predicted mask and
143
      intermediate features as input. On the other hand, SmartMat (Ye et al., 2024) extracts candidate fea-
144   tures from the DINOV2 pre-trained ViT (Oquab et al., 2024) based on both saliency and interactive
145   information, achieving a unified approach for both automatic and interactive matting.
146
147
      Matting Datasets. Matting, as opposed to segmentation, aims to predict the alpha mattes of target
      objects which are continuous transparency between 0 and 1 rather than binary masks. The majority
148
      of matting datasets are limited in size due to the high annotation cost, often consisting of only a
149
      few thousand or even just a few hundred foreground objects (Liu et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021). For
150   instance, the Composition-1k dataset (Xu et al., 2017) comprises 481 foreground instances, encom-
151   passing a variety of matting scenarios such as hair, fur, and semi-transparent objects. Considering
152   the repetition of some foregrounds in the Composition-1k dataset (cropped patches from the same
153   image), the Distinction-646 dataset (Qiao et al., 2020) is proposed, featuring 646 distinct foreground
154   images. Focusing on specific categories, the AM-2K dataset (Li et al., 2022) includes 20 categories
155   of animals, with 100 real-world images per category, showcasing diverse appearances and forms. To
156   amass a sufficient amount of training data, each foreground in the above datasets is typically merged
157   with 20 to 100 different backgrounds to synthesize an expanded dataset (Li et al., 2023a).
158   Although there exists natural human matting dataset P3M-10K (Li et al., 2021a) which contains
159   approximately 10,000 annotations, it is designed for simple single-instance scenarios, where each
160   image features only a single person as the primary subject. Training with the synthetic or sim-
161   ple natural datasets may prevent the network from learning complicated semantic cues and lead to
      inaccuracies in distinguishing foreground from background in complex real-world scenarios.

181   3.1   I NVESTIGATION AND M OTIVATION
182
      Interactive matting (Li et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024) aims to predict the alpha matte of a specific
183
      object using a simple prompt, such as BBox. However, its training process typically requires labor-
184
      intensive and scarce alpha mattes. To mitigate this challenge, synthetic techniques are often em-
185   ployed to expand the training dataset by applying spatial transformations to foreground alpha mattes
186   and overlaying them onto diverse background images. Unfortunately, training on synthetic data
187   frequently fails to accurately distinguish between foreground and background in real-world and
188   complex scenes. For example, as illustrated in Figure 1b (III), SmartMat (Ye et al., 2024), a SoTA
189   interactive matting method, has insufficient semantic comprehensibility and struggles to correctly
190   segment foreground objects in scenes with complex occlusions, such as wrongly predicting inter-
191   section regions when two people overlap.
192
193   3.2   DATASET C REATION
194
195
      Inspired by the findings in Section 3.1, we aim to leverage a large-scale, real-world segmentation
      dataset to improve the network’s semantic understanding. The COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014),
196
      being one of the most representative and comprehensive benchmarks for segmentation, is a natural
197
      choice. However, due to the low-quality annotated masks in the original COCO dataset, we use the
198   images from COCO combined with the refined annotations from COCONut (Deng et al., 2024) to
199   construct our COCO-Matting dataset. Given the complex interactions between humans and their
200   surrounding environments, we focus on humans as the primary subjects, which also has many real-
201   world applications, such as background replacement in video conferences (Lin et al., 2021) and
202   movie special effects production (Fielding, 2013).
203
      Construction of the COCO-Matting dataset is fraught with two principal challenges. Firstly, the
204   “human” category within the COCO dataset excludes accessories, such as hats, backpacks, or items
205   being held, which are considered part human in matting tasks (Li et al., 2021a), thus creating a
206   difference in label distribution. Secondly, annotations in COCO dataset are rough binary masks
207   that lack precision and transparency, such as the delineation of hair strands. In response to these
208   challenges, we devise a comprehensive pipeline including 1) Accessory Fusion 2) Mask-to-Matte as
209   shown in Figure 2, and establish the COCO-Matting dataset.

212   dataset. Specifically, given the instance segmentation masks for an image, we transform each binary

293   To address the first challenge, we propose a Feature-Aligned Transformer, which fuses the prompt
294   with image patches during the input stage through an additional patch embedding layer and in-

296   prompt region while tuning itself to extract fine-grained and transparency features. For the second
297   challenge, we introduce the Matte-Aligned Decoder, which adds several matting tokens and the
298

299

302   Feature-Aligned Transformer. Since our goal is to perform matting for specific objects of interest,
303   we use their BBox prompts as additional guidance to help the ViT backbone focus on learning
304
      the features of these target objects. This extra guidance is essential because, without it, ViT would
      extract generic features for segmentation, making it difficult to distinguish the foreground object and
305
      its transparency. So in Figure 3, we convert the BBox prompt into a binary mask and concatenate
306
      it with the input image to forward a learnable embedding layer. This process directs ViT to identify
307   which objects are of interest, thereby aligning the extracted features with the matting task.
308
309   Additionally, we incorporate LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) into the ViT backbone for efficient fine-tuning,
310
      further aligning the feature extraction with the matting task. Moreover, by focusing on the features
      of the specific objects indicated by the BBox prompts, the ViT backbone can learn more fine-grained
311
      details, such as precise edges and transparency, which are crucial for accurate matting.

      During training, our learnable matting tokens and matting adapter are meticulously fine-tuned to
325   discern matting-specific objects, ensuring accurate segmentation and mask generation that align
326   with matting requirements. Unlike HQ-SAM (Ke et al., 2024), which derives binary masks from a
327   single HQ-token, we employ multiple matting tokens to generate a trimap that meticulously distin-
328   guishes between foreground, background, and unknown regions of an object. Additionally, p̂SAM is
329   preserved throughout the training process to maintain the pre-trained SAM’s inherent priors.
330
      However, due to memory constraints, SAM extracts features at a reduced resolution and generates
331   logits downsampled by a factor of 4, leading to a loss of fine details. To address this, we propose a
332   lightweight matting decoder that stacks residual blocks (He et al., 2016) in a UNet architecture (Ron-
333   neberger et al., 2015). The central concept is that the network takes the concatenation of the image
334   and upsampled matting logits Upsample(p̂Mat ) as input. The U-shaped structure, augmented with
335   skip connections, effectively retains high-resolution image details, allowing the decoder to recover
336   fine-grained details within the matting logits and produce high-precision alpha mattes. More details
337   of the matting decoder and trainable parameters in SEMat are presented in Appendix A.2 and A.3.
338

      Datasets and Metrics. We employ the Distinction-646 (Qiao et al., 2020), AM-2K (Li et al., 2022),

411   matting, and SIM (Sun et al., 2021) for synthetic image matting. We leverage five standard metrics
412   for evaluation: Sum of Absolute Difference (SAD), Mean Squared Error (MSE), Mean Absolute
413   Difference (MAD), Gradient (Grad), and Connectivity (Conn) (Rhemann et al., 2009), where a
414   lower value indicates superior performance. Additionally, we assess our approach on the HIM2K
415   human instance matting dataset (Sun et al., 2022a) with the Instance Matting Quality (IMQ), where
416   a higher value signifies a more favorable outcome. For fair comparison, all methods take images
417   resized proportionally to 1024 resolution as input in evaluation.
418   Training Details. We initialize our network with SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), HQ-SAM (Kirillov
419   et al., 2023), and SAM2 (Ravi et al., 2024), respectively. The learnable modules are trained with
420   60k iterations and a batch size of 2 on one Nvidia L40S GPU. AdamW is chosen as the optimizer
421   with a learning rate of 5e-5. The rank is set to 16 in LoRA. λR and λT are set to 0.2 and 0.05.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we propose the COCO-Matting dataset and SEMat framework to revamp training
535   datasets, network architecture, and training objectives. Solving the problem of inappropriate syn-
536   thetic training data, unaligned features and mattes from a frozen SAM, and end-to-end matting loss
537   lacking generalization, we greatly improve the interactive matting performance on diverse datasets.
538   Limitations: Our method heavily relies on the pre-trained SAM, and its limitations may also affect
539   our model’s performance. For instance, while SAM is trained on large-scale data and effectively
      segments common objects, it struggles with rare objects, possibly limiting our model’s capabilities.

      A.2   D ETAILS OF THE M ATTING D ECODER
731
732
      Our matting decoder with the UNet shape takes the concatenation of the image and upsampled
733   matting logits Upsample(p̂Mat ) as input and extracts matting features through four downsampling
734   residual blocks, progressively reducing the resolution to one-eighth of the original. Subsequently,
735   intermediate features {fi } extracted from the ViT backbone are fused with the matting features
736   through a residual block at the bottom of matting decoder. Lastly, four upsampling residual blocks
737   with skip connections restore the features to the original resolution, yielding the alpha mattes.
