009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Dense embeddings are essential for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG),
012            search, classification, and clustering systems. Recent methods improve dense
013            embeddings by enriching them with instructions that incorporate downstream
014            task information, enabling a single model to generate task-specific embedding
015            spaces. However, we empirically show that requiring all tasks to share the same
016            model parameters imposes significant representational limitations. To address
017            these challenges, we introduce Mixture of Task Experts (MoTE), a novel trans-
018            former block designed for embedding architectures. MoTE employs dedicated
019
               parameter sets tailored to the unique requirements of each task and is paired with
               a task-aware training framework to improve representation quality. Experiments
020
               on 56 datasets spanning 7 tasks demonstrate that MoTE outperforms instruction-
021
               conditioned models, achieving, on average, 1.62 higher NDCG@10 on retrieval
022            datasets, 1.54 higher MAP on re-ranking datasets, and a 0.65 improvement in
023            overall performance. Notably, these gains are achieved without altering inference-
024            time information, training data, inference speed, or number of active parameters.

088   average, 1.62 higher NDCG@10 on retrieval datasets, 1.54 higher Mean Average Precision (MAP)

      A core challenge during the development of embedding models is the careful calibration of a unique
      embedding space capable of powering multiple downstream applications without significant perfor-
124
      mance degradations in any of them (Muennighoff et al., 2022; Neelakantan et al., 2022). For exam-
125
      ple, a STS task requires the semantically similar questions “Who was Isaac Newton?” and “Who was
126   the father of Calculus?” to be close in the embedding space while a retrieval task requires them to be
127   far apart because the latter does not answer the former (Gao et al., 2021). To address this challenge,
128   Wang et al. (2024); Muennighoff et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023) augment the
129   input text with contextual information about the downstream task. This strategy leverages text in-
130   structions to condition the generation of different embedding representations using the same model
131   and text. Contemporary methods leveraging this strategy vary in the level of detail provided in the
132   instructions. From example, Wang et al. (2022) use text-type or task-specific information such as
133   “Query:” and “Document:” in retrieval use cases while Su et al. (2022) use dataset-specific instruc-
134
      tions with more detailed information such as “Represent the Amazon comment for classifying the
      sentence as positive or negative:”.

      2.3    M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS
139
140   The application of MoE (Yuksel et al., 2012) to generative models (Jiang et al., 2023; Touvron
141
      et al., 2023b) has recently increased the popularity of MoE transformer architectures (Jiang et al.,
      2024; Gao et al., 2022b). MoE architectures are composed of MoE blocks that replace traditional
142
      dense blocks throughout the architecture or at every other block. In dense transformer blocks all
143
      intermediate token representations are processed through a single Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP).
144   In contrast, MoE blocks leverage a dynamic routing mechanism g and multiple MLPs (or experts) to
145   process intermediate token representations. Specifically, for each intermediate token representation,
146   the routing mechanism g uses the token representation to identify the subset of experts that should
147   process the representation (Lepikhin et al., 2020). Replicas of the intermediate token representation
148   are then dispatched to the selected experts where they are processed independently. Lastly, a pool-
149   ing layer aggregates the experts’ output to generate a unique representation for each of the tokens.
150   During training, an auxiliary expert-balancing objective is leveraged to learn the routing mechanism
151   while ensure a consistent training of and a balanced workload across the experts modules (Jiang
152
      et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2022b).

      Nussbaum et al., 2024), they also tend to be larger (e.g. ∼7B parameters vs 100M - 1.5B parameters respec-

      3.1   M IXTURE OF TASK -E XPERTS
176
177
178
      We set ourselves to tackle the representational limitations of instruction-conditioned embedding
      models while retaining their ability to share relevant task-independent knowledge. Following with
179
      the example in Section 2.2, consider that we want to embed “Who was Isaac Newton?”, once for a
180
      retrieval-query use case and once for a classification use case. The embedding model should possess
181   the ability to learn and apply specialized transformations that can prioritize information that will
182   aid in matching relevant documents (e.g., the question asks about the entity Issac Newton and “who
183   was” is tailored to retrieve biographical details) or emphasizing global features of the sentence for
184   the classification tasks (e.g., question type classification) according to the intended downstream task.
185   Second, in contrast to fully independent specialized models, task-independent knowledge should be
186   shared and accessible across tasks. For example that “Newton” in this context refers to a person
187   instead of a unit of measure should be learned across all experts regardless of which task the training
188   data contained physics examples. This section describes how MoTE mitigates this limitations (Wang
189
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023).

      The MoTE architecture interleaves MoTE blocks at Every Other Block (EOB) of the transformer
217   architecture8 . The MoE blocks employ a novel context-based routing mechanism R : I → E to
218   establish a mapping between the tasks i ∈ I and the expert modules e ∈ E (Figure 3-b) that is used
219   to route the intermediate representation of [i|t] through it’s task-associated expert. Task-routing
220   offers several advantages over standard MoE token-level routing. First, tokens belonging to the
221   same sequence are deterministically routed through the same expert based on their associated task.
222   This approach facilitates the model’s capacity to learn task-specific experts. Second, token-level
223   MoE requires of an auxiliary objective to ensure that the tokens are routed across the experts in a
224   balanced fashion to ensure expert specialization while prevent expert overloading or collapse. In
225
      contrast, task-routing mitigates this complexity by optimizing intermediate token representations
      only through the primary loss function and relying on contextual task information to determine the
226
      sequence routing. Critically, by utilizing only one expert per MoE block, MoTE maintains the same
227
      number of active parameters, latency and computational efficiency as the original dense transformer
228   architecture while enabling task-specialized weight representations.
229
230   As illustrated in Figure 3-c, the expert blocks e contain a MLP block and two normalization layers.
231
      The MLP component facilitates specialized transformations for different text types while the nor-
      malization layers enables MoTE to learn different centroids and variances for each taskBa (2016).

      3.1.1    U P - CYCLING I NITIALIZATION
234
235   MoTE’s task-routing mechanism requires of task-augmented inputs to train the task experts. How-
236   ever, contemporary embedding models only leverage task-augmented inputs during contrastive train-
237   ing stages while relying on a task-agnostic generic pre-training stage. Consequently, this presents
238   a challenge because experts can neither be meaningfully trained through the generic pre-training
239   stage nor dense transformers checkpoints resulting from the generic pre-training stage can be di-
240   rectly leveraged to initialize the MoTE architecture prior the contrastive pre-training stage9 .
241   To overcome this limitation, we propose to up-cycle (Komatsuzaki et al., 2022; He et al., 2024)
242   the dense transformer checkpoint resulting from the generic pre-training stage to initialize MoTE’s
243   contrastive pre-training stage. Existing dense embedding models are typically trained using task-
244   agnostic self-supervised objectives such as masked language modeling or next token prediction.
245   Consequently, their checkpoints contain a single MLP that is incompatible with MoTE’s multi-
246   expert architecture. Our proposed initialization strategy involves consistently replicating the original
247
      MLP block from the dense checkpoint across |E| expert modules.
248   By up-cycling pre-trained dense transformer checkpoints, we achieve four critical objectives. First,
249   MoTE is capable of preserving the foundational linguistic knowledge acquired during generic pre-
250   training. Second, it creates a coherent initialization of the task-experts when composed with the
251   remaining weights of the architecture. Third, it enables us to leverage the rich pool of existing
252   pre-trained langauge models. Lastly, it constitutes a flexible initialization strategy that supports
253
      a varying range of task-specialized experts in MoTE. This approach bridges the gap between task-
      agnostic generic pre-training and task-routed embedding models while providing a novel application
254
      of the MoE up-cycling mechanism.

      3.2     TASK - AWARE TRAINING
258   Embedding models must generate semantically meaningful representations across a diverse range
259   of tasks and domains. We argue that downstream tasks exhibit distinct representation requirements,
260   which manifest in nuanced approaches to hyper-parameter selection and training strategies. Con-

262   tifying similar data points while distinguishing them from arbitrarily different samples across do-
263   mains. Consequently, high-quality hard negatives must exhibit a large domain diversity indepen-
264   dently of the anchor. In contrast, STS tasks require to discriminate between semantically similar
265   yet distinct textual representations. Consequently, high-quality hard negatives must exhibit high se-

271   where strategies optimal for one task can degrade the performance in another.

273   To overcome this limitation we propose to combine MoTE with task-aware contrastive training. This
274
      method constructs task-specific mini-batches and dynamically adjusts training hyper-parameters —
      such as batching strategies, contrastive temperature, and objective functions — based on each task’s
275
      requirements. It employs task-specific hyper-parameters and a hierarchical data structure that orga-
276
      nizes training samples by task and source dataset, ensuring that each expert learns from data relevant
277   to its task during contrastive learning. Task metadata is passed from the dataloaders to the training
278   module to facilitate this process. When combined with MoTE, this approach enables gradient spe-
279   cialization during training, allowing each expert to be optimize using task-specific hyper-parameters
280   and data effectively.
281
      A drawback of this approach is that it can increase the search space by a factor of |T |, where |T | is
282
      the number of tasks. To address this, we propose two mitigation strategies: first, selectively focus
283   hyperparameter tuning on a subset of parameters relevant to tasks where optimal training strategies
284   are unclear or where preliminary analysis indicates significant performance variability. This reduces
285   the multiplicative factor in the search space by applying it only to this subset of parameters, rather
286   than the full parameter set. Second, utilize the dense embedding model as a proxy to systemati-
287   cally analyze hyperparameter impacts across tasks (Appendix A). This strategy reduces the search
288   space to be equivalent to training the dense transformer model plus one additional optimization run
289   that dynamically combines task-specific adjustments. Together, these strategies effectively mitigate
290   the computational overhead of task-aware optimization, making it comparable to traditional hyper-
291
      parameter tuning methods.

293   3.3   E XPERT AVERAGING
294
295   Due to the nature of the MoTE architectures we can increase the expressive power of the embedding
296
      model without increasing the dimensionality of the embeddings or incurring latency overheads.
      However, MoTE can reduce the model’s throughput in selective cases where we do not have enough
297
      memory to process large batch sizes — like document classification or retrieval indexing — due to
298
      the increased GPU memory consumed by the specialized weights.
299
300   To address this we developed a post-training approach that decreases the memory footprint by com-
301   bining the independently-trained experts into a single expert. We refer to the resulting model as
302
      Expert Averaged (EA). Specifically, we obtain EA by averaging each of the layers across experts
      resulting in a single MLP block. After conducting this process we obtain a model that retains over
303
      98.2% of the MoTE performance across tasks while maintaining the same number of layers and
304
      memory footprint as the original dense transformer architecture (Section 5.5).

519   6     C ONCLUSION
520
521
      Traditionally, embedding models have relied on a single model to produce a single embedding space.
      The recent rise of instruction-conditioning strategies challenges this paradigm by enabling the gen-
522
      eration of distinct downstream vector representations for the same input text using contextual task
523
      instructions. However, as demonstrated in this paper, this approach limits the model’s expressive
524   power. To address this, we introduced MoTE, a framework that employs a dedicated set of parame-
525   ters for each task to enhance task specialization.
526
527
      Through task-aware training, MoTE effectively captures the nuances of individual tasks dur-
      ing multi-task learning, resulting in significant performance improvements. Specifically, MoTE
528
      achieves, on average, 1.62 points higher NDCG@10 on retrieval datasets, 1.54 points higher Mean
529
      Average Precision (MAP) on re-ranking datasets, and 0.65 higher dataset performance across tasks.
530   Critically, these improvements are achieved without altering inference-time information, training
531   data, latency, or the number of active parameters, demonstrating MoTE’s efficiency and effective-
532   ness compared to traditional instruction-conditioned embedding models.

      A.1   BATCHING S TRATEGY
690
691   The selection of in-batch negative samples is inherently task-dependent, with varying embedding
692   characteristics influencing optimal sampling approaches. Tasks emphasizing local semantic nu-
693   ances, such as Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) (Agirre et al., 2012), require negatives that capture
694   proximal semantic distinctions. Consider paraphrase identification, where sentences like “Vivendi
695   shares closed 1.9 percent at 15.80 euros in Paris after falling 3.6 percent on Monday” and “in new
696   york, vivendi shares were 1.4 percent down at $18.29” demand fine-grained contextual differenti-
697   ation. Conversely, tasks focused on global semantic representations, including classification and
698
      clustering, benefit from more diverse negative sampling strategies that capture broader semantic
      variations.
699
700   To empirically validate this hypothesis, we conducted a controlled experiment training an embedding
701   model under two distinct sampling regimes: homogeneous and heterogeneous. By maintaining all
      other experimental parameters constant, we isolated the impact of sampling strategy.
