009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural network-based classification models often suffer from catastrophic
012            forgetting during class-incremental learning (CIL). Previous studies reveal that it
013            results from the overlap between seen and future classes after being mapped by
014            model to its feature space through extracting the features. In this paper, we analyze
015
               that this overlap mainly results from the over-collapse of seen classes, where the
               model tends to map originally separated one seen class and its adjacent regions in
016
               input space to be mixed in the feature space, making them indistinguishable. To this
017
               end, we propose a two-step framework to Prevent the Over-Collapse (POC). During
018            training, POC first learns and applies a set of transformations to the training samples
019            of seen classes. Based on our theoretical analysis, the transformation results will
020            locate in the adjacent regions of the seen classes in the input space so that we can
021            let them represent the adjacent regions. Then, the model’s optimization objective is
022            modified to additionally classify between the seen classes and the adjacent regions,
023            separating them in model’s feature space so that preventing the over-collapse. To
024            retain the model’s generalization on the seen classes, a deterministic contrastive loss
025            that makes the separate features of seen classes and adjacent regions close is further
026
               introduced. Since POC uses the adjacent regions exclusively for classification, it
               can be easily adopted by existing CIL methods. Experiments on CIFAR-100 and
027
               ImageNet demonstrate that POC effectively increases the last/average incremental
028
               accuracy of six SOTA CIL methods by 3.5%/3.0% on average respectively.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Over the past few years, incremental learning (IL) has attracted extensive attention to facilitate a
034   model learning from a sequence of tasks. Within class-incremental learning (CIL), each task centers
035   on image classification and introduces new classes. The primary goal of CIL is to develop a unified
036
      classification model that maps an input into a feature space by extracting its features and then classifies
      it among all of the encountered classes with the features. However, if the model is solely fine-tuned
037
      for each new task, it will suffer from a severe problem known as catastrophic forgetting, where its
038
      knowledge of the old tasks fades and the performance degrades greatly (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989).
039
040   Various approaches have emerged to address catastrophic forgetting, broadly categorized into three
041   groups: replay-based (Bang et al., 2021; Iscen et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2023; Rolnick et al., 2019;
042
      Tiwari et al., 2022), regularization-based (Aljundi et al., 2018; 2019; Jung et al., 2020; Sun et al.,
      2023), and architecture-based (Aljundi et al., 2017; Douillard et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019; Pham et al.,
043
      2021; Yoon et al., 2018) methods. Despite their efficacy in CIL, the primary optimization objective of
044
      existing methods, typically a classification loss among the seen classes, has overlooked the necessity
045   of learning a representation compatible with future classes. Specifically, as depicted in Figure 1(a),
046   their optimization objective will lead to the over-collapse of seen classes, where the model will map
047   originally separated one seen class and its adjacent regions in the input space to be mixed in the
048   feature space in order to improve model’s generalization on the seen class. Although this phenomenon
049   is observed, analyzed and desired in other classification tasks (Li et al., 2018; Fawzi et al., 2018),
050   its effect is not considered within the CIL scenario yet. However, as shown in Figure 1(a) and our
051   experiments, the over-collapse will increase the risk of overlap between the areas covered by the seen
052   and future classes after being mapped into model’s feature space, making them indistinguishable. As
053
      stated by Masana et al. (2022), this overlap can result in knowledge forgetting of seen classes since
      the model will misidentify the samples of seen classes belonging to future classes after learning.

093   2     R ELATED W ORK
094
095   2.1   C LASS -I NCREMENTAL L EARNING
096
097   Class-incremental learning (CIL) strives to enable a unified model to learn sequentially from different
098
      image classification tasks while retaining knowledge from previous ones (Hung et al., 2019; Singh
      et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). Generally, the methods fall into
099
      three categories: (1) replay-based; (2) regularization-based; (3) architecture-based.
100
101   Replay-based methods (Bang et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019) offer an intuitive solution
102   by storing samples from previous tasks in a memory buffer. When learning new tasks, the stored
103   samples are amalgamated with new data to train the model. Initially, Riemer et al. (2018) used an
104
      equal proportion of new and stored samples to calculate the loss. Douillard et al. (2020) and Hou
      et al. (2018) further utilized stored samples to calculate a distillation loss for knowledge transfer.
105
106   To mitigate memory usage for storing samples, regularization-based methods (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato,
107   2017; Wang et al., 2021) augment the classification loss with a regularization term, preventing
      crucial parameters from changing too much. For instance, Aljundi et al. (2018) quantified parameter


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      importance for old tasks, then regularized alterations of significant parameters. Alternatively, Li &
109   Hoiem (2017) employed knowledge distillation to maintain logits of new samples on old classes.
110
111   Architecture-based methods offer an alternative approach by modifying the model or maximizing
112
      feature distance across different tasks, making different tasks mutually independent. In the work
      of Rajasegaran et al. (2019) and Serra et al. (2018), upon the arrival of a new task, a specifc part of the
113
      model is trained and then fixed. In the work of Chaudhry et al. (2020), Tang et al. (2021) and Xiang
114
      & Shlizerman (2023), the authors designed special losses to maximize the distance across tasks.
115
116   A shared problem of the aforementioned methods is their exclusive emphasis on enhancing discrimi-
117   nation among the seen classes during current task learning. However, this singular focus will lead to
118
      the over-collapse of seen classes, causing catastrophic forgetting. Instead, with seen classes, our POC
      produces samples in their adjacent regions and trains the model to classify between them and the
119
      seen classes. This strategy can prevent the over-collapse, making the model adaptable to future tasks.
120
121   While similar to FACT (Zhou et al., 2022) and IL2A (Zhu et al., 2021) in generating extra data
122   for training, our POC differs in key aspects. Their foundational principle is to train the model to
123   classify between seen and future classes in advance. It is based on assumption that positions of future
124
      classes can be predicted with the seen classes using mixup. However, future classes vary a lot and are
      unpredictable. Differently, POC aims to enhance the feature’s discrimination by producing samples
125
      in adjacent regions of seen classes and training the model to classify between these regions and seen
126
      classes. In this way, the over-collapse is prevented and the future classes will be naturally protected
127   from overlapping, regardless of their actual positions. Detailed experiments are in Section 4.2.2.
128
129   2.2   O UT- OF -D ISTRIBUTION D ETECTION
130
131
      Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Bai et al., 2021; Cai & Fan, 2022; Jeong & Kim, 2020) aims at
      predicting whether a sample aligns with the training data. In a specific approach (Cai & Fan, 2022;
132
      Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018; Mohseni et al., 2021; Tack et al., 2020), researchers pre-trained a model to
133
      predict the geometric transformation on input samples. It was shown that the transformed samples
134   are OOD-like, while similar to their original samples (Tack et al., 2020). It inspires us to learn a set
135   of transformations whose application results are adjacent to the seen classes. By classifying them
136   from the seen classes, their features will be separated so that the over-collapse will be prevented.

194   3.2     P REVENT THE OVER -C OLLAPSE
195
196
      With the above purpose, we propose POC that can be easily adopted by the CIL methods to help
      address the problem and improve their performance. As illustrated in Figure 2, POC works in two
197
      steps “Adjacent Region Labeling” and “Collapse Prevention” during training. In the first step, POC
198
      learns and applies a set of transformations to the training samples of seen classes to produce samples
199   in and representative of their adjacent regions in the input space. In the second step, the model is
200   expanded with another classifier and its optimization objective is modified to additionally classify
201   between the adjacent regions and the seen classes, separating them in model’s feature space so that
202   preventing the over-collapse. Furthermore, to protect the generalization of the model on the seen
203   classes, we introduce a deterministic contrastive loss that makes the separate features of the seen
204   classes and adjacent regions close. During testing, the expanded classifier is masked, allowing the
205   original model to classify inputs among seen classes without introducing additional inference costs.
206
207   3.2.1    A DJACENT R EGION L ABELING
208
      To prevent the over-collapse, an intuitive first step is to generate samples in the adjacent regions of the
209
      seen classes in the input space and let them represent the adjacent regions. Initially, we experimented
210   with applying mixup, rotation and other transformations to the training samples of seen classes to
211   generate desired samples. However, we found that the resulting samples were too distant from or close
212   to the original ones, unable to represent the adjacent regions and leading to suboptimal performance.
213   Inspired by out-of-distribution (OOD) detection works (Cai & Fan, 2022; Golan & El-Yaniv, 2018;
214   Mohseni et al., 2021; Tack et al., 2020), we propose to learn a new set of transformations to generate
215   suitable samples. To substantiate this approach, we begin by proving that affine transformations have
      the capability to generate OOD samples through the following proposition:

271
272   The proposed two steps separate the features of seen classes and adjacent regions without constraining
273   their distance, which will make the model map the seen classes and adjacent regions to be distant
274   from each other in the feature space for better classification. However, since the training samples
275   cannot completely represent their classes, an actual testing sample can locate in the adjacent regions
276
      produced with training samples. In this way, the model’s generalization on the seen classes will be

277
      the feature space, crossing the classification boundary and leading to wrong classification result. To
278

533   5   C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we analyze the over-collapse problem and propose a framework to Prevent the Over-
536   Collapse (POC) for CIL. POC learns and applies a set of transformations to the seen classes to
537   produce samples in adjacent regions. Then the model’s optimization objective is modified to classify
538   between the adjacent regions and the seen classes to prevent the over-collapse. We also introduce a
539   deterministic contrastive loss to preserve the model’s generalization on the seen classes. Extensive
      experiments show the POC’s effectiveness in improving the performance of existing CIL methods.

1325   C.6   E FFECT OF T RANSFORMATION N UMBER
1326
1327   This section assesses the impact of the number of transformations under task settings (1), (3), (4),
1328   and (6), utilizing LUCIR as the baseline method. In Section 4.2.1, the number of transformations
1329   is set to be 3 and here we compare the performance of POC on CIFAR-100 when the number
1330   is 1, 5, 7. The findings, listed in Table 16, highlight that POC achieves superior performance
1331   when the transformation number is 3. This suggests that an excessively small or large number of
1332   transformations does not yield optimal results.
1333   We further compare the Inter-Class Distance (ICD) and Intra-Class Generalization (ICG) among
1334   different transformation numbers and the results are illustrated in Figure 7. The findings indicate
1335   that while a larger number of transformations may enhance generalization on seen classes, it simul-
1336   taneously diminishes the separation between future and seen classes within the feature space. This
1337   indicates the significance of selecting an optimal transformation number.
1338   It is noteworthy that the ICD decreases as the number of transformations decreases. We analyze the
1339   reason is that when the transformation number decreases, less transformed results will push each
1340   other away due to DCL so that they will be less compact. In details, when the transformation number
1341   decreases, the transformed result of the seen class by one transformation will be affected by fewer
1342   other results through DCL, which pushes different results away from each other. Therefore, one
1343   transformed result can be less compact and can cover more adjacent regions. When the number is 1,
1344   DCL only constrains the transformed result to be close to the seen class. Therefore, the transformed
1345   result will surround the seen class to minimize the distance. In this way, training the model to classify
1346
       between the seen class and more adjacent regions will decrease ICD.
1347   To consolidate our analysis, we calculate the average cosine similarity between two samples from
1348   one transformation result of one seen class. We find that after learning 6 tasks, the average cosine
1349   similarity decreases from 0.89 to 0.8 when transformation number decreases from 7 to 1, indicating a
       less compact distribution of transformation results.
