                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Offline reinforcement learning (RL) enables policy learning from pre-collected
010
               offline datasets, relaxing the need to interact directly with the environment. How-
011            ever, limited by the quality of offline datasets, it generally fails to learn well-
012            qualified policies in suboptimal datasets. To address datasets with insufficient
013            optimal demonstrations, we introduce Goal-cOnditioned Data Augmentation
014            (GODA), a novel goal-conditioned diffusion-based method for augmenting sam-
015            ples with higher quality. Leveraging recent advancements in generative modeling,
016            GODA incorporates a return-oriented goal condition with various selection mech-
017            anisms. Specifically, we introduce a controllable scaling technique to provide
018            enhanced return-based guidance during data sampling. GODA learns a compre-
019
               hensive distribution representation of the original offline datasets while generating
               new data with selectively higher-return goals, thereby maximizing the utility of
020
               limited optimal demonstrations. Furthermore, we propose a novel adaptive gated
021
               conditioning method for processing noised inputs and conditions, enhancing the
022            capture of goal-oriented guidance. We conduct experiments on the D4RL bench-
023            mark and real-world challenges, specifically traffic signal control (TSC) tasks, to
024            demonstrate GODA’s effectiveness in enhancing data quality and superior per-
025            formance compared to state-of-the-art data augmentation methods across various
026            offline RL algorithms. Our code will be publicly accessible upon review.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reinforcement learning Sutton & Barto (2018) aims to learn a control policy from trial and error
032   through interacting with the environment. While demonstrating remarkable performance in various
033
      domains, this approach typically requires vast amounts of training data collected from these inter-
      actions. Such data-intensive requirements become impractical in applications where environmental
034
      interactions are costly, risky, or time-consuming, such as robotics, autonomous driving Li et al.
035
      (2024) and traffic signal control (TSC) Zhang & Deng (2023). Offline RL offers a feasible solution
036   to these challenges by enabling policy learning directly from pre-collected historical datasets, thus
037   significantly reducing the need to interact directly with the environment.
038
039
      Although offline RL makes policy learning less expensive, its performance is highly dependent
      on the quality of the pre-collected datasets and may suffer from lack of diversity, behavior policy
040
      bias, distributional shift, and suboptimal demonstrations Prudencio et al. (2023). The performance
041
      of offline RL tends to decline drastically when trained with suboptimal offline datasets. Previous
042   studies have attempted to address these issues by constraining the learned policy to align closely
043   with the behavior policy Lyu et al. (2022) or by limiting out-of-distribution action values Kostrikov
044   et al. (2021). Although these approaches have shown performance improvements, they retain the
045   inherent defects of offline datasets, remaining highly dependent on data quality.
046
      Several studies have addressed the limitations of offline RL using data augmentation methods to gen-
047
      erate more diverse samples. One approach involves learning world models to mimic environmental
048   dynamics and iteratively generate synthetic rollouts from a start state Zhang et al. (2023). While this
049   method significantly improves sample efficiency and data diversity, it suffers from compounding
050   errors and fails to control the quality of generated trajectories. Other research leverages generative
051   models to capture the distributions of collected datasets and randomly sample new transition data Lu
052   et al. (2024). Although these methods demonstrate some performance improvements, they remain
053   inefficient when dealing with datasets containing limited optimal demonstrations. This inefficiency
      stems from their inability to effectively control the quality of generated data.

067   We attempt to address this challenge by taking advantage of generative modeling to augment higher-
068   quality data with directional goals. Unlike previous studies that sample data unconditionally and
069   randomly Lu et al. (2024), we introduce GODA to incorporate representative goals, guiding the
070   samples toward higher returns. Given the exceptional performance of diffusion models Ho et al.
071   (2020); Karras et al. (2022) in the field of generative artificial intelligence, GODA utilizes a diffusion
072   model as its generative framework. GODA is trained to capture a comprehensive representation of
073   the data distribution from the original dataset while sampling new data conditioned on selective
074
      high-return goals. This approach maximizes the utility of the limited well-performed trajectories
      in the original datasets. Inspired by Decision Transformer Chen et al. (2021), we define the ’goal’
075
      as the return-to-go (RTG), which represents the cumulative rewards from the current step until the
076
      end, coupled with its specific timestep in trajectories. RTG explicitly indicates the expected future
077   rewards for a given behavior at the current timestep for a specific trajectory. We assume that at the
078   same timestep across different trajectories, a higher RTG signifies a higher goal. To generate samples
079   that exceed the quality of the original dataset, we introduce three goal selection mechanisms and a
080   scaling technique to control our expected goals during sampling.
081
      We present an illustrative example of how GODA operates in Figure 1. The task is to identify the
082
      shortest path from the starting point to the target. By setting higher RTG goals during sampling,
083   GODA can potentially discover a more efficient route that yields a higher return (the RTG at the first
084   timestep is equal to the return). To better incorporate goal conditions, we further propose a novel
085   adaptive gated conditioning approach. This method utilizes a condition-adaptive gated residual
086   connection and a gated long skip connection to capture multi-granularity information effectively.
087   GODA is an off-the-shelf solution that can seamlessly integrate with other offline RL optimization
088   approaches on various tasks to achieve superior results. We summarize our contributions:
089   1) We propose a goal-conditioned data augmentation method, namely GODA, for offline RL. It
090   achieved enhanced data diversity and quality for offline datasets with limited optimal demonstra-
091   tions. 2) We introduce novel directional goals with selection mechanisms and controllable scal-
092   ing to provide higher-return guidance for the data sampling process in our employed generative
093   models. Additionally, we propose a novel adaptive gated conditioning approach to better capture
094   goal guidance. 3) We show GODA’s competence through comprehensive experiments on the D4RL
095   benchmark compared with state-of-the-art data augmentation methods across multiple offline RL
096   algorithms. We further evaluate the effectiveness of GODA on a real-world application, i.e., traffic
097
      signal control with datasets obtained from widely used controllers in real-world deployments. These
      evaluations verify GODA’s effectiveness in addressing various challenges, significantly enhancing
098
      the applicability of RL-based methods for real-world scenarios.

103   Offline RL. Conventional offline RL methods aim to alleviate the distributional shift problem, i.e.,
104   a significant drop in performance due to deviations between learned policy and the behavior policy
105   used for generating the offline data Hu et al. (2023). To address this issue, various strategies have
106   been employed, including explicit correction Xu et al. (2022), such as constraining the policy to a
107   restricted action space Kumar et al. (2019), and making conservative estimates of the value function
      Yu et al. (2021); Kumar et al. (2020), with the aim of aligning the behavior policy with the learned

108
      policy. Some recent studies exploit the strong sequence modeling ability of Transformer models to
109   solve offline RL with trajectory optimization. For instance, Decision Transformer Chen et al. (2021)
110   and its variants Wu et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024) utilize a GPT model to autoregressive predict
111   actions given the recent subtrajectories composed of historical RTGs, states, and actions. Diffusion
112   models have also been adopted in offline RL given its exceptional capability of multi-modal distri-
113   bution modeling. Diffuser Janner et al. (2022) employs diffusion models for long-horizon planning,
114   effectively bypassing the compounding errors associated with classical model-based RL. Hierar-
115   chical Diffuser Chen et al. (2024) enhances this approach by introducing a hierarchical structure,
116   specifically a jumpy planning method, to improve planning effectiveness further.
117   Data augmentation in Offline RL. Data augmentation proactively generates more diverse data to
118   improve policy optimization. For instance, TATU Zhang et al. (2023) uses world models to produce
119   synthetic trajectories and truncates those with high accumulated uncertainty. However, model-based
120   RL often suffers from compounding errors in the learned world models. GuDA Corrado et al. (2024)
121   introduces human guidance into data augmentation functions (DAFs), i.e., translation, rotation, and
122   reflection, for generating expert-quality data, while human intervention is costly and lacks scalabil-
123
      ity. Diffusion models are also directly applied to data augmentation through the sampling process.
      SynthER Lu et al. (2024) is the first work that employs diffusion models to learn the distribution of
124
      initial offline datasets and unconditionally augment large amounts of new random data. However,
125
      it fails to control the sampling process to steer toward high-return directions actively. DiffStitch Li
126   et al. (2024) attempts to enhance the quality of generated data by actively connecting low-reward
127   trajectories to high-reward ones using a stitching technique.
128
129
      We propose enhancing the quality of generated data from a different perspective by introducing a
      controllable directional goal into our generative modeling. This approach selectively reuses optimal
130
      trajectories to guide the sampling process toward achieving higher returns.

      4.1     S ELECTIVE G OALS AND C ONTROLLABLE S CALING

201   4.1.1    R ETURN - ORIENTED GOAL
202
203   Prior diffusion-based work Lu et al. (2024) lacks the ability to guide the sampling process in the
204   desired direction. We attempt to introduce a return-oriented goal as a condition of our diffusion
205
      model. Inspired by Decision Transformer, we adopt RTG Chen et al. (2021), cumulative rewards
                                                                              PT
206   from the current step till the end, as the explicit goal condition ĝt = t′ =t rt′ . For each transition
207
      sample represented as a tuple (s, a, s′ , r) within a trajectory, RTG quantifies the expected future
      rewards for the current behavior, effectively serving as a goal. In other words, a higher RTG at a
208
      specific timestep typically signifies a higher goal for the policy to pursue. Since the same behavior
209
      at different timesteps often yields varying RTGs across different trajectories, we combine the RTG
210   with its corresponding timestep in the trajectory as the condition for each specific transition sample.
211   The timestep signal acts as a timestamp for each goal.
212

214
215   During dataset preprocessing, we first organize offline samples into trajectories, compute the RTG
      for each, and append timesteps to every sample. To fully leverage well-performing samples and

233   proach, we rank all trajectories based on their return values and select the top n trajectories. During
234

235

236
      RTG-prior goal condition. We group RTGs by their associated timesteps and then sort them to
237   select the top n RTGs along with their corresponding timesteps as goal conditions. This approach
238   selectively reuses high-RTG transitions for data augmentation, focusing on transitions that are most
239   likely to yield higher returns. (3) Random goal condition. We randomly select m RTG and timestep
240   pairs (ĝt , t) as sampling goal conditions for each batch of samples. This increases the diversity of the
241   augmented data while paying less attention to the optimal trajectories for improving performance.

244
245

246

247
      to represent a higher return expectation. This approach enables flexible adjustment of goal values to
248   drive the sampling process toward higher-quality data. As illustrated in Figure 1, a higher RTG goal
249   at each timestep directs the sampling process toward a trajectory with a greater overall return. Since
250   RTG values can be either positive or negative in certain tasks, we propose multiplying positive goals
251   by the scaling factor and dividing negative goals by it.

502   5.5   A BLATION S TUDY
503
      To validate the effectiveness of GODA’s components, we conduct experiments using different con-
504
      figurations. More detailed ablation and sensitivity studies can be found in Appendix C.
505
506   Condition Selection. We test three condition selection mechanisms as described in Section 4.1.2:
507   return-prior, RTG-prior, and random goal conditions. As shown in the left part of Figure 5, the
508   return-prior method demonstrates superior performance compared to the other two approaches.
509
      Moreover, GODA with the return- and RTG-prior conditions outperforms SynthER when tested
      on two offline RL algorithms. In contrast, the random-prior method shows results comparable to
510
      SynthER. This suggests that high-goal conditions identified by the return- and RTG-prior methods
511
      enable GODA to generate samples beyond the original data distribution. Randomly selected goal
512   conditions, however, fail to target high-reward regions, producing similar results to SynthER.
513
514   Goal scaling factor. We further examine the effect of different scaling factors, testing values ranging
515
      from 0.8 to 2.0. As seen in the middle part of Figure 5, the performance improves as the scaling
      factor increases but slightly degrades when the scaling factor exceeds 1.1. Scaling factors below 1.0
516
      shrink the selected goals, leading to suboptimal samples. Conversely, scaling factors above 1.1 push
517
      the selected goals too far beyond the training data distribution, resulting in diminished performance.
518
519   Adaptive gated conditioning. Finally, we evaluate the impact of the adaptive gated conditioning
520   method. We compare GODA with two variants: one using only adaLN conditioning Peebles &
521
      Xie (2023), and another using in-context conditioning, where condition embeddings are directly ap-
      pended to the input embeddings. From the right part of Figure 5, it is clear that GODA with adaptive
522
      gated conditioning achieves the best results and the adaLN and in-context conditioning show simi-
523
      lar performance. Additionally, all three methods outperform SynthER which lacks goal conditions.
524   This demonstrates that the inclusion of goal conditions is crucial for guiding the sampling process
525   toward high returns, and our adaptive gated conditioning method enhances the model’s ability to
526   fully utilize these conditions.

      6     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
530
531
      This paper proposes a novel goal-conditioned data augmentation method, namely GODA, which
      integrates goal guidance into the data augmentation process. We define the easily obtainable return-
532
      to-go signal, along with its corresponding timestep in a trajectory, as the goal condition. To achieve
533
      high-return augmentation, we introduce several goal selection mechanisms and a scaling method.
534   Additionally, we propose a novel adaptive gated conditioning structure to better incorporate goal
535   conditions into our diffusion model. We demonstrate that data augmented by GODA shows higher
536   quality than SynthER without goal conditions on different evaluation metrics. Extensive experi-
537   ments on MuJoCo locomotion tasks and a maze task confirm that GODA enhances the performance
538   of classic offline RL methods when trained on GODA-augmented datasets. Furthermore, we evalu-
539   ate GODA on real-world traffic signal control tasks. The results demonstrate that GODA is highly
      applicable to TSC problems, making RL-based methods more practical for real-world applications.

771   B.1   BASELINE AND E VALUATION RL M ETHODS
772
773   For data augmentation baseline methods, we adopt the implementation of TATU from
774   https://github.com/pipixiaqishi1/TATU, DiffStitch from https://github.com/guangheli12/DiffStitch,
775   and SynthER from https://github.com/conglu1997/SynthER to conduct experiments not covered in
776
      their respective papers
777   For the evaluation RL methods, we use IQL and TD3+BC from the Clean Offline Reinforcement
778   Learning (CORL) codebase Tarasov et al. (2024) for D4RL tasks. For TSC tasks, we employ the
779   implementation of BCQ, CQL, and DataLight from https://github.com/LiangZhang1996/DataLight.
780
781
782   B.2   D4RL S ETTINGS
783
784   For Gym-MuJoCo tasks from the D4RL benchmark, Random, Medium-Replay, and Medium
785
      datasets are adopted. To elaborate, Random datasets contain transition data generated by a ran-
      domly initialized policy. Medium datasets consist of a million data points gathered using a policy
786
      that achieves one-third of the performance of an expert approach. Medium-replay datasets contain
787
      the stored experience in a replay buffer during the training of a policy until it reaches the score in
788   Medium datasets.
789
790
791   B.3   TSC S ETTINGS
792
793   We further elaborate on the TSC tasks adopted as the real-world challenges in this section. We
794   formulate the TSC problem as a MDP and define the state, action, and reward function as follows:
795
      State. For behavior policies (AMP and ACL), the state representation includes the current phase,
796
      traffic movement efficiency pressure, and the number of effective running vehicles Zhang et al.
797   (2022). For evaluation algorithms, BCQ and CQL use the same state representation as AMP, while
798   DataLight adopts the number of vehicles, along with the total velocity saturation and unsaturation
799   degrees Zhang & Deng (2023).
800
      Action. The action is generally defined as the phase selection for the next time period.
801
802   Reward. AMP, BCQ, CQL and DataLight use pressure Zhang et al. (2022) as the reward while ACL
803   uses queue length. It is worth noting that we use the opposite of these metrics as the final reward
804   function.
805   Due to the absence of certain key signals in the original datasets from the DataLight codebase, we
806   generate a total of 24K samples for each dataset using the behavior policies for each task. Addi-
807   tionally, we augment 20K samples for each task using our GODA model. The task horizon for each
808   TSC scenario is set to 3600 seconds, with a control step of 15 seconds. For the sampling process,
809   we employ the return-prior goal condition selection method and set the goal scaling factor to 0.85,
      given the negative reward values in TSC tasks.
