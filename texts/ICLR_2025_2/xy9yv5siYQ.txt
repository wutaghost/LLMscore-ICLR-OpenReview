030                                                  A BSTRACT
031
032
                 Dynamic scene reconstruction aims to recover the time-varying geometry and
033              appearance of a dynamic scene. Existing methods, however, heavily rely on the
034              existence of multiple-view captures or the accurate camera poses estimated by
035              Structure from Motion (SfM) algorithms. To relax this constraint, we introduce
036              a method capable of reconstructing generic dynamic scenes, from casually cap-
037              tured monocular videos without known camera poses. Unlike recent works that
038              treat static and dynamic content separately, we propose a unified Hexplane-based
039              Gaussian field to capture the complex effects of scene deformation and camera mo-
040              tion. The Hexplane decomposition enables feasible disentanglement for effective
041
                 optimization. Combined with an efficient camera pose initialization strategy, our
                 approach significantly improves view synthesis quality and camera pose estimation
042
                 accuracy over previous methods, while enhancing computational efficiency.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
046
047
      Reconstructing the dynamic scene from a causal video plays a crucial role in understanding and
048   interacting with the real world. Recent studies have made significant strides in modeling complex
049   static 3D scenes (Fu et al., 2024; Kerbl et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2024b) and dynamic
050   3D scenes (Liu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Cao & Johnson, 2023; Gao et al., 2021; Lei et al.,
051   2024; Wang et al., 2024). Most existing methods rely on multiple simultaneous captures with the
052   known camera poses, typically estimated via SfM systems such as COLMAP, as input. However, the
053   multiple-view setting limits their use to causal monocular videos and the SfM systems are not always
      robust when dealing with dynamic video data due to camera motion blur and the presence of dynamic

054
      objects. Consequently, recovering persistent geometry, radiance, and motion from a monocular video
055   without known camera poses – the most common scenario for in-the-wild data – remains an open and
056   challenging problem.
057
058
      Recent monocular approaches have demonstrated the ability to operate on casual dynamic videos
      without known camera poses (Liu et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2024). However, these methods typically
059
      rely on disentangling static and dynamic regions using two separate representations. For instance,
060
      Liu et al. (2023) employs two distinct TensorRFs (Chen et al., 2022) to model the static and dynamic
061   regions independently where the dynamic TensorRF is trained from scratch using camera poses
062   estimated via the static TensorRF, with no information sharing between the two representations. Since
063   deformation and camera movement occur simultaneously during video captures, modeling them with
064   two separate representations could lead to suboptimal reconstruction results. Furthermore, these
065   methods often suffer from prolonged optimization times due to the random or heuristic initializations
066   for camera poses. For example, RoDynRF requires approximately 20 hours for optimization, while
067   DGMarbles (Stearns et al., 2024) necessitates 5 hours.
068   Motivated by the above observations, we introduce Mono-DynGS, an algorithm for efficient dynamic
069   scene reconstruction from casual monocular vides. Inspired by the recent success of 3D Gaussians
070   Splatting(3DGS), we represent the dynamic scene as a set of 3D Gaussians for its desired deformable
071   and compositional capability. Instead of using random initialization, we first introduce an efficient
072   camera initialization module by estimating the relative camera pose of every image pair. Concretely,
073   the relative camera pose can be represented by the SE(3) transformation of a set of 3D Gaussians
074   from the first camera view to the second one. Concurrently, we utilize a deformation field to model
075
      the motion of deformable objects from the canonical space to different timesteps. To model complex
      interactions between scene elements and camera motions, we propose a unified representation
076
      shared by both static and dynamic regions. This representation exhibits dual properties: it is unified
077
      during rendering for high-quality dynamic reconstruction, yet allows for feasible disentanglement
078   to facilitate effective optimization. In particular, we employ a Hexplane-based encoder for six
079   planes: {(x, y), (x, z), (y, z), (x, t), (y, t), (z, t)}. During optimization, the first three planes contain
080   information enabling the reconstruction of static backgrounds and camera motion. , the remaining
081   three planes model the underlying deformation and, together with the first three spatial planes, recover
082   the dynamic regions. During inference, all six planes are utilized collectively to obtain high-quality
083   rendering results at different timesteps from arbitrary viewpoints. Furthermore, we incorporate depth
084   and optical flow estimations to regularize the optimization of the proposed Mono-DynGS, thereby
085   enhancing geometric consistency.
086   We conducted comprehensive experiments across three diverse datasets: DyCheck (Gao et al.,
087   2022), NVIDIA DynamicNeRF (Gao et al., 2021), and MPI Sintel (Butler et al., 2012). Our
088   evaluation focused on two key tasks: dynamic novel-view synthesis and camera pose estimation.
089   We compared our results with previous work, including approaches both with and without known
090   camera poses. Our method consistently outperformed existing techniques in both tasks across all
091   three datasets, demonstrating its robustness and effectiveness in handling a wide range of dynamic
092
      scene reconstructions and camera motion estimations.

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Dynamic Novel-view Synthesis The modeling of dynamic view synthesis can be generally divided
097   into 2 categories based on how the temporal modeling is handled. The first category generally models
098   dynamic scenes as a 6D-input radiance field which views location, time, and view direction as inputs.
099   For instance, To utilize flow priors, NSFF (Li et al., 2021) adds flow prediction as an auxiliary task
100   and constrains the predicted volume density based on the flow provided. Also, some works are built
101   based on previous static novel synthesis works. For instance, K-Planes (Sara Fridovich-Keil and
102   Giacomo Meanti et al., 2023) and HexPlane (Cao & Johnson, 2023) extend TensoRF (Chen et al.,
103   2022) to dynamic scenes. DyNeRF(Li & Li, 2022) conditions the radiance field on a per-time-instant
104
      feature vector which is jointly optimized with the radiance field. However, this category will be
      incompatible with dealing with dynamic scenes with large deformation due to the lack of explicit
105
      representation of object motion.
106
107   The second set of models deploys a deformable field to connect the real world with canonical
      representation. D-NeRF (Pumarola et al., 2021) simply uses a pure MLP combined with sinusoidal


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      embeddings to represent deformation fields, while TiNeuVox (Fang et al., 2022) imposes multi-
109   resolution grids for interpolation to incorporate deformation in different scales. SWAGS (Shaw et al.,
110   2023) builds on 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) and uses a simple MLP for the deformation field, and
111   CoGS (Yu et al., 2024a) mainly focuses on controllable 3D Gaussian Splatting based on learning
112   dynamic scenes. 4DGS (Wu et al., 2024) incorporates Hexplane as its deformable field. Mosca (Lei
113   et al., 2024) uses DQB interpolation on some key points as its deformable field, while Shape-of-
114   motion (Wang et al., 2024) imposes Fourier transformation to fit the trajectory of dynamic Gaussians.
115   However, most of them fail on casual videos since they require camera poses.
116
117   Camera Pose Estimation under Monocular Video The second line of related work, mostly
118   consisting of SLAM and SfM systems, aims to reconstruct the scene directly from RGB images
119
      by jointly estimating camera parameters and 3D geometries. For example, MonoSLAM (Davison
      et al., 2007) and ORB-SLAM (Campos et al., 2021) reconstruct point clouds and camera poses with
120
      sole images by associating feature correspondences. For SfM systems, Bundler (Snavely, 2008) and
121
      COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016) provide a method to estimate camera parameters for large
122   image sets. Several methods like (Yen-Chen et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021), have
123   developed ideas on estimating camera poses using a NeRF model. (Fu et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024)
124   shows how to incorporate the rising 3D Gaussian Splatting model with camera pose estimation. For
125   camera pose estimation in dynamic scenes, Lei et al. (2024) uses a cluster-based deformable field
126   to deal with dynamic foregrounds while jointly optimizing camera poses. (Liu et al., 2023) uses a
127   simple MLP as its deformable field and introduces numerous regularizations to enforce geometry
128   consistency. However, most of them use separate representations for static backgrounds and dynamic
129   foregrounds. In contrast, We propose to jointly optimize camera poses and a concise representation
130
      of dynamic scenes in an end-to-end manner.

132   3     M ETHOD
133
134   Given a sequence of input images {Ii | 1 ≤ i ≤ N } representing a monocular dynamic video, along
135   with the camera intrinsics, our goal is to recover the corresponding camera poses {Pi | 1 ≤ i ≤ N }
136   for each frame and generate photo-realistic images for arbitrary novel views and timesteps. To
137   this end, we propose Dy-MonoGS which jointly optimizes a set of 3D Gaussian with continuous
138   deformable files and the corresponding camera poses for all input frames. As illustrated in Fig 2, we
139
      begin with the efficient relative pose estimation technique to recover the coarse camera trajectory
      which we find is a crucial step to facilitate the whole scene optimization (Sec. 3.2). Upon the pose
140
      initialization, we further optimize a set of Gaussians with a Hexplane-based encoder by disentangling
141
      the static(Sec. 3.3.1) and dynamic aspects(Sec. 3.3.2). Meanwhile, we refine the initial camera poses
142   and improve the geometric consistency of proposed Mono-DynGS by leveraging the dense depth and
143   optical flow predictions.

      3.3     H EXPLANE - BASED G AUSSIAN F IELD
217
218   Given the initial camera poses, we propose a Hexplane-based Gaussian field to model both the static
219   geometry and dynamic deformation through a unified representation. Recognizing that nearby 3D
220   Gaussians typically share similar spatial and temporal information, we introduce an efficient spatial-
221   temporal encoder that utilizes a 4D Hexplane to decompose the 4D neural voxel into various multi-
222   resolution planes. Specifically, the spatial-temporal structure encoder comprises six plane modules:
223   {Rxy , Rxz , Ryz , Rxt , Ryt , Rzt }. All 3D Gaussians can be represented by plane features derived
224
      from these modules. These six planes can be naturally decomposed into two distinct sets: i) three
      spatial planes {Rxy , Rxz , Ryz }; and ii) three temporal planes {Rxt , Ryt , Rzt }. This decomposition
225
      allows us to construct the static Gaussian field using the spatial planes and the deformable Gaussian
226
      field using the temporal planes.
227

305   ∆µ = ξx (fd ) and the Gaussian’s rotation ∆q = ξq (fd ). Then, the deformed Gaussian G ′ can be

309   the deformable Gaussian field, we introduce two regularization terms that focus on the smoothness of

364
      scene, most of them failed in the DyCheck dataset due to the large deviation of testing camera poses
      from training camera trajectories. Our model outperforms all existing works in DyCheck scenes as
365
      shown in the quantitative results in Tab 1 and the qualitative results in Fig 3 The improvement can be
366
      attributed to 2 factors: firstly, our model adopts a 2-stage optimization, which first optimizes in a local
367   Gaussian manner to produce relative poses between frames; and the relative poses are used for the
368   initialization of camera poses, which enables better optimization over the global camera trajectory and
369   facilitates the aggregation of observations over different frames; secondly, our model uses a Triplane
370   to replace the redundant static Gaussian set, which reduces the possibility of overfitting, and since the
371   optimization of Triplane field is much slower than native gaussian attributes, the optimization is done
372   over all frames, which increase the integrity over different timesteps.

      5    C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this work, we propose Mono-DyGS, a novel end-to-end framework that jointly optimizes camera
479
      poses and dynamic scene representation on monocular videos. We demonstrate that previous works
480   either deal with static backgrounds and dynamic foregrounds separately or require an extremely
481   long training duration. We impose the relative pose initialization which significantly reduces the
482   training time and improves the performance of camera pose estimation. Leveraging from the explicit
483   representation of 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), we propose a concise representation for both static
484   backgrounds and dynamic foregrounds based on Hexplane. We show the effectiveness and robustness
485   of our approach on challenging scenes like the DyCheck dataset. Thanks to the advantages of
      Gaussian splatting, our approach achieves rapid training and inference speeds.
