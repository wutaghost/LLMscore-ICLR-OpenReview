009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning time-series models are often used to make forecasts that inform
012            downstream decisions. Since these decisions can differ from those in the training
013            set, there is an implicit requirement that time-series models will generalize outside
014            of their training distribution. Despite this core requirement, time-series models are
015            typically trained and evaluated on in-distribution predictive tasks. We extend the
016            orthogonal statistical learning framework to train causal time-series models that
017            generalize better when forecasting the effect of actions outside of their training
018
               distribution. To evaluate these models, we leverage Regression Discontinuity De-
               signs popular in economics to construct a test set of causal treatment effects.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      Deep learning models have recently seen major improvements, including in time-series modelling
024   Liu et al. (2024); Nie et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Das et al. (2024); Zhang & Yan (2023).
025   Time-series models are often used to make forecasts that inform decisions, such forecasting demand
026   for goods or transportation to optimize pricing, or forecasting health markers to optimize treatment
027   decisions Makridakis et al. (2022); Nowroozilarki et al. (2021); Makridakis et al. (2023).
028
      The decision making procedures that leverage time-series forecasts typically optimize for a target
029
      outcome, which leads to actions that differ from those observed in the training data. This results
030   in a key implicit requirement on time-series models: that their predictions will generalize to action
031   values outside of the observational distribution. Despite this requirement, the only complex tasks on
032   which time-series forecasting models are trained and evaluated are in-distribution predictive tasks
033   Liu et al. (2024); Melnychuk et al. (2022); Lim (2018); Nie et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Das
034   et al. (2024); Zhang & Yan (2023); Nowroozilarki et al. (2021); Makridakis et al. (2022; 2023).
035
      Asking models to predict on out-of-distribution actions can drastically degrade performance. Figure
036   1 shows predictions on a passenger rail demand forecasting task, in which the model predicts the
037   daily number of seats sold at a given price for a tain. The price is the action that the forecast will
038   help optimize. We fit a well tuned LightGBM Ke et al. (2017) model, and an improved version of the
039   Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) Lim et al. (2020). Both models have comparable performance
040   for in-distribution prices (Fig. 1a, 1b, details in §4). We then predict demand under a 30% price
041   increase (green line). Of course, we would expect higher prices to cause a decrease in demand.
042   Instead, both models predict an increase in demand. Figure 1c confirms that this effect is wide-
043   spread, affecting 13% and 28% of the predictions for the LGBM and TFT models, respectively.
044   This incorrect behaviour appears because models heavily rely on the information contained in in-
045   distribution actions for their predictions. Indeed, in the collected data actions are set by an informed
046   decision maker (an operator of the train company who knows when demand will be high from
047   experience), which confounds observed actions and associated outcomes. In our data, we do observe
048   higher prices when demand is high (e.g., during holidays), and low prices when demand is low. This
049   signal breaks when predicting on out-of-distribution actions (they are not chosen by an informed
050
      decision maker), leading to inaccurate predictions.
051   In this work, we develop a training and evaluation procedure for causal forecasting time-series mod-
052   els. Causal models aim to learn the causal relationship between the action (e.g., price) and its effect
053   on a specific outcome (e.g., demand), conditioned on other observational features. That is, they aim
      to capture the change in outcome caused by a change in action, and not just predict the outcome from

      A few causal forecasting models have been proposed, but they use approaches that do not map
      to well defined causal effects Bica et al. (2020a;b); Melnychuk et al. (2022), do not correct for
075
      regularization bias Li et al. (2020a); Bica et al. (2020a;b); Melnychuk et al. (2022); Lim (2018), or
076
      take inspiration from causal frameworks for heuristics Brodersen et al. (2015); Schultz et al. (2024).
077   In this work, we leverage the orthogonal statistical learning framework of Foster & Syrgkanis (2023)
078   to learn a causal forecasting model. We extend this framework to a well-defined time-series causal
079   problem and to high-dimensional treatments, and instantiate it on top of state-of-the-art time-series
080   architectures to support complex use-cases.
081
      Evaluating causal forecasting models is even more challenging, as outcomes under alternative treat-
082
      ments are by definition unobserved. Existing work thereby rely on simple simulations with known
083   counterfactuals Bica et al. (2020a;b); Li et al. (2020a); Lim (2018), or in-distribution forecasting
084   performance Lim (2018); Melnychuk et al. (2022). The former is often too simplistic to really com-
085   pare models, while the later can be misleading and inflate the performance of non-causal models.
086   We show details of this effect in §2.1.
087
      To evaluate forecasting models on causal tasks, we take inspiration from Regression Discontinuity
088
      Designs (RDDs) popular in econometrics Hahn et al. (2001); Imbens & Lemieux (2008), to con-
089   struct a test set of causal effects estimated under different assumptions than the typical model-fitting
090   assumption. We can then compare the treatment effects estimated by forecasting models using tradi-
091   tional evaluation metrics. We train and evaluate our causal forecasting models on two relevant tasks:
092   demand forecasting using a proprietary dataset of passenger rail prices and demand over time; and
093   the public health dataset MIMIC-III Johnson et al. (2016). We show that, compared to traditional
094   time-series forecasting models and a state-of-the-art causal model, our causal forecasting models
095   predict causal effects that are 36% and 1% closer to those estimated with RDDs, respectively.

115   2.1   BACKGROUND : C AUSAL T REATMENT E FFECTS AND O RTHOGONAL L EARNING
116
117   To formalize the importance of causality in forecasting for optimizing decisions, we first need to
118
      introduce treatment effects. In the reminder of this paper, we use the potential outcomes’ model
      of causal inference Holland (1986); Imbens & Rubin (2015), and flexible data models Robinson
119
      (1988); Nie & Wager (2020); Chernozhukov et al. (2018).
120
121   Potential Outcomes represent the values of an outcome under different possible actions (also com-
122   monly called treatments or decisions in the literature). Consider a two actions setting: A = a or
123   A = b. For a time-series n and time step t, the potential outcomes Ytn (a) and Ytn (b) are the value of
124
      the demand Ytn we would observe under each action (price). Of course, we only ever observe one
      potential outcome, as Ytn = 1Ant =a Ytn (a) + 1Ant =b Ytn (b) where 1 is the indicator function.
125
126   Treatment Effects represent the causal effect of a change in action (price) on the outcome of interest
127   (demand). Formally, the treatment effect of going from action a to b, for time-series n, at time step
128   t, is Ytn (b) − Ytn (a). The fundamental challenge of causal inference is that we can only ever
129   observe one potential outcome, for the treatment we chose. As a result, we cannot train a supervised
130
      treatment effects model. However, we can estimate expected causal effects under unconfoundedness,
      the main assumption in the literature which we make when learning our models:
131
132   Assumption 1 (Unconfoundedness). Consider a set of possible treatments T . We say that the time-
133   series (Yt (At ), At , S, Xt ) are (conditionally) uncounfounded if, for every time-step t > τ :
134
                                  {Yt (At ), At ∈ T } ⊥
                                                      ⊥ At | S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ
135
136
      Intuitively, this assumption means that in sub-populations defined by the conditioning variables,
137
      the groups receiving each treatment are comparable (have the same potential outcomes distribu-
138   tion). Unconfoundedness is verified by construction in conditionally randomized trials, as treat-
139   ments are assigned independently of potential outcomes. It is also verified when context variables
140   S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ include all pre-treatment variables that are common causes of An and Y n Pearl
141   (2009). In our running example, this consists in all variables that influence both demand and pricing
142   decisions, such as the cities of arrival and departure, day of the trip, and more. Under Assumption 1,
143   a well known identifyability result holds (see e.g., Imbens & Rubin (2015); Pearl (2009)):
144   Proposition 1 (CATE Identifyablity). Under Assumption 1, we can identify the Conditional Average
145   Treatment Effect (CATE) as follows:
146
147        CATE ≜ E Yt (b) − Yt (a) | S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ

148               = E Yt | At = b, S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ − E Yt | Tt = a, S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ .
149
150   This important result shows that  we can estimate the Conditional Average         Treatment Effect (CATE)

151   from conditional expectations E Yt | At = a, S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ . This is a familiar quantity to
152   ML practitioners, as a flexible forecasting model f : A, S, X, A1:τ , Y1:τ → Y trained with a mean-
153   squared error
                    will converge to this conditional expectation
                                                                      in the limit of infinite data. Estimating the
154   CATE E Yt (b) − Yt (a) | S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ is then as simple as predicting on the two treatment
155   values f (Tt = b, . . .) − f (Tt = a, . . .). Unfortunately, practice is more challenging.
156   The need for causal forecasting arises when fitting complex forecasting models with finite sample
157   sizes. In observational data collected from regular interactions, such as observed demand for pas-
158   senger rail seats during regular operation, some treatments will be over-represented. For instance, in
159   our data high prices are over-represented when demand is high, and low prices when demand is low.
160   Of course, we expect higher prices to cause a decrease in demand, but the correlation in the data is
161   flipped due to the transportation company operators using their experience and domain knowledge
      about demand to set higher prices when underlying demand is high, thereby increasing revenue.

      2.2   O RTHOGONAL L EARNING FOR T IME S ERIES M ODELS
222
223   We adapt the orthogonal statistical learning framework described above by making two key ex-
224   tensions: defining daily treatment effects with an observation cut-off; and extending the R-loss to
225   categorical and linear effects with various encodings for θ’s predictions.
226
      Treatment effects in time series. The first step is to formalize the treatment effects to predict,
227   and the context to predict them with. In this paper, we focus on daily treatment effects defined
228   in Proposition 1. This modelling choice lets us focus on single-day treatments, though supporting
229   sequences of treatments is an interesting avenue for future work. Focusing on a time-step t, this
230   implies that Y = Yt in Equation 1 and A = At in Equation 2.
231
      Defining the relevant feature set (W in Eq. 1, 2) is more tricky. Recall from §2 that our time
232
      series consist in static (S) and temporal (X1:t ) features. These features are naturally included (W ⊃
233   (S, X1:t )). In time-series, results from past time steps, such as past prices and associated demands
234   (A1:t−1 , Y1:t−1 ), are typically very predictive. However, both the uncondoundedness Assumption
235   1 and the orthogonal leaning data model (Eq. 1, 2) implicitly assume that features W follow the
236   observational distribution at training and prediction time. This assumption is not verified when
237   models are used to influence past decisions, and A<t , Y<t cannot be included in X. This is an
238   important point: observational past treatment are very predictive of future demand, but confound
239   the daily treatment effect and are the cause of reversed elasticity predictions we observed (§2.1,
240   Figure 1). To avoid this confounding, we introduce a cut-off τ after which treatments can deviate
241
      from the distribution. That is, in time-steps 1 : τ the model is not used to affect treatments, while
      for t > τ , the treatment At can differ from the training distribution. We then set the features as
242
      W = (S, X1:t , A1:τ , Y1:τ ) ≜ Wt , which we use as input for our daily treatment effect of At on Yt .
243
244   High dimensional effects. The other extension we require is support for higher-dimensional treat-
245   ments. We consider both categorical and linear treatments and treatment effects. We start with
246   categorical treatments, for which we propose two different encodings. In our demand forecasting
247   dataset, possible prices are discrete (in our health application too), following a large but tractable
248   set of possible values (about thirty possible prices) of size d ≜ |A|. We extend the approach of
249
      Robinson (1988); Nie & Wager (2020); Foster & Syrgkanis (2023) (Equations 1, 2) to model each
      time-step t as follows:

271   an accurate θ around likely prices. The model is under-constrained further from typical prices, where

275

301
302   We now instantiate the theory described in §2.2 using deep-learning time-series architectures to fit
303   the three models we require (m, e, θ). We then show how to adapt the training procedure to fit
304   Algorithm 1. Finally, we adapt the prediction procedure to output causal forecasts.
305
      Time-Series Architectures for Orthogonal Learning. We need out time-series architecture to take
306   four types of inputs: static features S, temporal features X1:t , and actions and outcome values before
307   τ , A1:τ and Y1:τ . We fit three models, all using the same architecture. First e(·) which predicts
308   the treatment sequence A>τ . Second, m(·) which predicts expected outcomes (without knowing
309   the treatments). Third, θ(·) which predicts a vector of treatment effects (interpreted differently
310   depending on the encoding, see §2.2). Our inputs are non-traditional, and the reason why we use a
311   modified TFT Lim et al. (2020) (modification details in Appendix C.3). We also experiment with
312   the state-of-the-art iTransformer architecture Liu et al. (2024), passing static features’ embeddings
313   along temporal ones, and training with randomly sampled t values by truncating the time-series at
314
      t (the iTransformer processes entire time-series and otherwise “knows the future”). This results in
      very slow training and prediction, and we can only apply it to one of our datasets.
315

318   trained with the mean squared error (MSE) loss. The loss for e(·) depends on the encoding function.
319   We use the cross entropy for the one-hot encoding case, the binary cross-entropy for the cumulative

      encoding, and the MSE for the linear encoding. In all cases, we train θ(·) by minimizing the R-loss
      l(·, m, e, zt ) from Eq. 5 with data points zt ∈ S2 .

332   How can we measure their performance of our causal forecasting models? This is a core challenge
333   in causal inference, which fundamentally rely on untestable assumptions Pearl (2009), such as As-
334   sumption 1 or related data models (Eq. 1-4). In §1, we used domain knowledge on the sign of
335   the CATE to argue that non-causal models fail to learn valid causal relationships. However, these
336   qualitative observations do not enable quantitative comparisons between models. To the best of
337   our knowledge, their is currently no reliable method to quantitatively evaluate the quality of causal
338   models, without relying on the same assumptions used in fitting the models.
339   To address this challenge, we design another approach to estimate our causal effects of interest,
340   that we scale to evaluate ML models. Specifically, we leverage Regression Discontinuity Designs
341   (RDDs) (§3.1) and changes in treatments over time to estimate a subset of daily CATE values in
342   a time-series. This approach, which uses a different assumption than Assumption 1 (§3.2), lets us
343   identify a set of CATE values on our test set time-series, which we use to build as evaluation task
344   for causal effects (out-of-distribution actions) on which to compare ML models (§3.3).
345
346   3.1   BACKGROUND : R EGRESSION D ISCONTINUITY D ESIGNS
347
      Regression Discontinuity Designs (RDD) leverage a continuity assumption to estimate a treatment
348
      effect at a cut-off point that triggers a change in treatment Hahn et al. (2001); Imbens & Lemieux
349
      (2008); Lee & Lemieux (2010). Formally, RDDs require a variable X with an associated cut-off
350   value c that corresponds to a change in treatment. That is, A = a when X < c, and A = b when
351   X ≥ c. RDDs provide identifyability under the following assumption:
352
      Assumption 2 (Continuity). The potential outcomes’ conditional expectation, E[Y (b)|X = x, V ]
353   and E[Y (a)|X = x, V ] respectively, are both continuous in x.
354
355   Under assumption 2, we can estimate the CATE at the cutoff X = c:
356   Proposition 4 (CATE Identifyablity from RDD). Under Assumption 2, we have that:
357

             E Y (b) − Y (a) | X = c, V = lim+ E Y | X = x, V − lim− E Y | X = x, V
358                                            x→c                        x→c
359
      Practical estimators based on Proposition 4 typically fit linear or polynomial models g : X , V → Y,
360
      using an step variable for the cut-off 1{X = c} to measure the discontinuity induced by the change
361   of treatment. This discontinuity captures the CATE at X = c. Since we are interested in the limit
362   at X = c, one often uses a weight kernel K(·) that decreases the importance of datapoints further
363   from the cut-off X = c. Typical weight kernels include the rectangular ”window” kernel of width
364   h, or a triangular kernel with weights decaying linearly between X = c and (c − h, c + h).
365

      4     E VALUATION
439
      We evaluate our approach on two tasks, and compare models on in-distribution forecasts and our
440
      RDD-based causal task that evaluates a model’s ability to predict on out-of-distribution actions.
441
      Causal models perform worse in distribution as they do not “cheat” by using the confounded choice
442   of actions as signal. As a result, their predictions are better on out-of-distribution actions.

907   C.3   TFT
908
909   We developed a custom version of the Temporal Fusion Transformer Lim et al. (2020). We focused
910
      on this architecture as it supports static features, past and futures features as input, and temporally
      causal processing of the input, where most architectures focus on predicting future time step from
911
      the previous ones Liu et al. (2024),Zhang & Yan (2023),Wu et al. (2021),Nie et al. (2023). We apply
912
      several changes to the original TFT:
913
914          • We remove the encoder of the TFT and replace it by a series of encoder blocks. Figure
915            7a presents the architecture of our encoder block. Each encoder block is made of self-
916            attention and residual blocks. Each block divides the length of the input by 2. Hence, at
917            the beginning of the encoder, the model processes high frequency features, and the deeper
               the encoder is the lower frequency the model is considering.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
             • We use conditional mechanisms that show interesting performances on Image Generation.
919            Static features go through a dense model to create an embedding s gathering all the static
920            information. We then apply successive Cross Attention mechanisms between the token s
921            and the tokens of the temporal features Rombach et al. (2022) to mix temporal and static
922            features. Finally, we apply adaptive group normalization Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) where
923            s is the class embedding.
924
             • We replace the masked self-attention decoder by a new decoder block. Figure 7b shows
925
               the layers used in the decoder block. This block is almost the symmetric of the encoder
926
               one. We remove the cross-attention layer in the decoder as it increases the expressivity of
927            the model. We also add skip connections between the encoder the decoder blocks as done
928            in Ronneberger et al. (2015). Thus, the information of high frequency features are directly
929            transferred to the decoder without passing thought the entire encoder in order to help the
930            expressivity of the model.
931
932
             • We use causal convolution layers to prevent any possible leak of information from future
               features Xt+i to impact Yt prediction. We also apply masking in our self-attention layers
933
               as done in Li et al. (2020b).

1006   Our two background sections §2.1 and §3.1 already discuss the closest related work. In this section,
1007   we expand on this discussion and discuss other related papers.
1008
       Time-series forecasting has seen a flurry of recent activity. The main task studied in the litera-
1009
       ture is that of multivariate time series forecasting, with the goal of forecasting (Yin )i∈[t:t+h] from
1010
       (Yin )i∈[0:t] . Another common task is to forecast (Yin )i∈[1:t] from (Xin )i∈[1:t] . Various approaches
1011   have been proposed, though not clear winner has emerged yet. TSMixer Chen et al. (2023) relies
1012   only on fully connected linear layers to process entire time-series feature-wise, before mixing fea-
1013   tures at each time-step. The iTransformer Liu et al. (2024) architecture similarly leverages fully
1014   connected layers, but processes intermediate representations with transformer layers channel wise.
1015   PatchTST Nie et al. (2023) gathers patches time-steps and features that it treats as tokens in a self-
1016   attention mechanism, before reconstructing a time-series shaped output. In all these architectures
1017   and others Ilbert et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2021), there is no insurance against future information
1018   leaking into previous time-steps. While this can be fine in some cases, this is not compatible with
                                                                 n
1019
       our specific task. Indeed, our causal models predict Yt+l    based on past features (Yin )i∈[0:t] , static
                   n                          n
1020   features S and temporal features (Xi )i∈[0:t+l] . A key point in our task is to ensure causality in the
1021   forecasting, preventing any leak of information from (Xin )i∈[t+1:t+h] . Supporting this requirement
1022
       is not common in recent time series papers, and the reason why we build on the TFT Lim et al.
       (2020), which does provide an adequate structure.
1023
1024   Causal time-series models have seen less progress, though some time-series models aim to esti-
1025   mate the CATE. Bica et al. (2020b); Lim (2018) rely on the same meta-architecture, including a
       network computing the treatment propensity score and a network computing the outcome (though


                                                           19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Bica et al. (2020b) tries to learn unobserved confounders, a challenging task). Both models use
1027   LSTM or RNN architectures merged with new mechanisms: a propensity network learns to predict
1028   propensity scores, which are then used to train the outcome network with the inverse probability of
1029   treatment weighting. This loss, mainly used for policy learning Foster & Syrgkanis (2023), can be
1030   high variance and does not full corrects for regularization bias. This is why we decided to train our
1031   model with the Residualized Loss (R-Loss) Foster & Syrgkanis (2023); Nie & Wager (2020), which
1032   also gives us a more flexible approach for treatment encoding.
1033   Brodersen et al. (2015) take a different approach, and use structured time-series models to explicitly
1034   model counterfactuals under no-treatment as a synthetic control. This approach is well suited to
1035   binary treatments with several observations, to be able to model outcomes without treatment. It is
1036   less applicable to our case with high dimensional treatments and complex conditioning variables.
1037   However, it could interesting to explore this approach as a multi-time-steps alternative to our RDD
1038   causal test set.
1039   With the recent rise of the transformer architecture for sequential models, new techniques have
1040   been developed to merge transformers with causal inference Melnychuk et al. (2022). The authors
1041   leverage an adversarial loss Bica et al. (2020a) to encourage the model to learn balanced features
1042   for the treated and untreated populations and learn the causal link between the treatment and the
1043   outcome. Melnychuk et al. (2022) use a transformer backbone with two small dense layers on top
1044   trained with this adversarial loss. This identification approach differs from classical causal inference
1045   frameworks, and it is unclear that it converges to a well defined conditional causal effect.
1046   Regression Discontinuity Designs (RDDs) are studied theoretically, and frequently used empiri-
1047   cally, in economics Hahn et al. (2001); Imbens & Lemieux (2008); Lee & Lemieux (2010). Re-
1048   searchers typically use RDDs to estimate one specific ATE. In contrast, we estimate many ATEs
1049   (one for each time series), and combine them with time-series features Wt to create a CATE dataset.
1050   To the best of our knowledge, this is a novel appraoch to evaluate causal models. Researchers have
1051   used RDDs to estimate ATEs in time-series before though. Hausman & Rapson (2018) surveys typ-
1052   ical approaches and pitfalls, and proposes empirical checks to perform on the data to verify if RDDs
1053
       are applicable. In our setting, we typically do not have enough data around switching times to per-
       form such checks, though it would be interesting to take inspiration from common RDD checks to
1054
       improve our causal test sets. We leave this direction for future work.
