009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Radiology report generation (RRG) is a challenging task, as it requires a thorough
012            understanding of medical images, integration of multiple temporal inputs, and ac-
013            curate report generation. Effective interpretation of medical images, such as chest
014            X-rays (CXRs), demands sophisticated visual-language reasoning to map visual
015            findings to structured reports. Recent studies have shown that multimodal large
016            language models (MLLMs) can acquire multimodal capabilities by aligning with
017            pre-trained vision encoders. However, current approaches predominantly focus on
018
               single-image analysis or utilise rule-based symbolic processing to handle multi-
               ple images, thereby overlooking the essential temporal information derived from
019
               comparing current images with prior ones. To overcome this critical limitation,
020
               we introduce Libra, a temporal-aware MLLM tailored for CXR report generation
021            using temporal images. Libra integrates a radiology-specific image encoder with
022            a MLLM and utilises a novel Temporal Alignment Connector to capture and syn-
023            thesise temporal information of images across different time points with unprece-
024            dented precision. Extensive experiments show that Libra achieves new state-of-
025            the-art performance among the same parameter scale MLLMs for RRG tasks on
026            the MIMIC-CXR. Specifically, Libra improves the RadCliQ metric by 12.9% and
027            makes substantial gains across all lexical metrics compared to previous models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Radiology reports are a key component of biomedical radiology analysis, designed to enhance clarity
033
      and efficiency in medical communication. These structured reports are typically divided into distinct
      sections, such as Findings, Impression, Indication, Technique, Comparison, and History(Ganeshan
034
      et al., 2018). Serving as the primary medium for radiologists to convey their findings and conclusions
035
      from imaging studies like chest X-rays (CXRs), radiology reports play a crucial role in guiding
036   diagnostic and therapeutic decisions for various diseases(Najjar, 2023). However, generating these
037   reports manually is a complex and time-consuming task. Automating radiology report generation
038   (RRG) presents a valuable opportunity to boost radiologist productivity, improve communication,
039   and alleviate burnout (Zhang et al., 2020b). Despite its potential, RRG is a challenging task due to
040   the intricate nature of medical imaging and the need for precise and accurate documentation.
041
      Although the advancements in paradigm models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) and InstructBLIP (Dai
042   et al., 2023), they still exhibit a significant performance gap when applied to RRG tasks. In particu-
043   lar, their effectiveness diminishes in biomedical contexts due to the substantial differences between
044   biomedical and general image-text pairs (Tu et al., 2023; Saab et al., 2024). Consequently, these
045   models often perform at a surface level, akin to a layperson-level understanding, when dealing with
046   specialised medical imaging tasks. Recent advancements have explored continued pre-training of
047   general-purpose foundation models for medical tasks, but these models still fall short in addressing
048   the complexities of medical image analysis (Li et al., 2023a; Chaves et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024).
049   Harnessing the full potential of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) for understanding and
050   reasoning over multimodal data in the biomedical domain poses a significant challenge, primarily
051   due to the granularity and specificity required in medical image analysis (Wang et al., 2023a). Pre-
052   liminary attempts to apply MLLMs to RRG have largely concentrated on generating reports from a
053   single image, often neglecting the crucial temporal relationships between images taken at different
      time points (Zhang et al., 2024b). The MIMIC-CXR Database (Johnson et al., 2019a) reveals that

054
      67% of patients underwent at least two studies at different time intervals, underscoring the neces-
055   sity of incorporating temporal context in clinical practice. This highlights a critical gap in existing
056   models, which are not equipped to handle the dynamic progression of medical conditions reflected
057   in sequential imaging studies.
058
      In standard clinical practice, radiologists rely heavily on comparing current imaging results with pre-
059
      vious studies to assess temporal changes. However, many models inadvertently introduce spurious
060
      references to prior examinations that do not exist during inference, leading to inaccurate reports. Re-
061   cent advancements1 , such as MedVersa (Zhou et al., 2024) and MAIRA-2 (Bannur et al., 2024), can
062   process multiple images simultaneously. These models insert visual tokens from different images
063   at specific points within the textual input, hinging on the LLM to interpret reference information.
064   Additionally, LLaVA-Rad (Chaves et al., 2024) utilised GPT-4V (OpenAI et al., 2024) for radi-
065   ology report extraction and augmentation, eliminating hallucinations associated with prior image
066   references in the dataset. Despite these efforts, existing models still lack integrated mechanisms
067   for perceiving temporal information within their architectures, which restricts the model’s ability to
068   achieve the temporal awareness required for comprehensive reporting. Meanwhile, existing MLLMs
069
      typically utilise visual embeddings from the last or penultimate layer of the image encoder (Chen
      et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a), which can only capture the global characteristics of the image.
070
      However, RRG tasks require not only identifying findings but also capturing subtle details such as
071
      severity, extent, and the progression of findings (Sloan et al., 2024). Such fine-grained details are
072   difficult to represent using embeddings from any single layer of the image encoder. (Jiang et al.,
073   2024). Therefore, for the high-granularity demands of RRG tasks, relying solely on representation
074   from a single layer limits the quality of report generation. To tackle these limitations, we aim to
075   enhance the temporal awareness of MLLMs for RRG by tackling two main challenges:
076   ◦ It is non-trivial to design effective structures in MLLMs to handle prior study citations across
077      various time points in the RRG task.
078
      ◦ The scarcity of effective connectors in MLLMs capable of handling the high-granularity require-
079      ments of downstream tasks.
080
      To overcome these challenges, we propose Libra (Leveraging Temporal Images for Biomedical
081
      Radiology Analysis), a novel framework designed to incorporate temporal change information into
082
      the RRG task. Libra employs a pre-trained visual transformer, RAD-DINO (Pérez-Garcı́a et al.,
083   2024), as the image encoder to generate robust image features, which are then refined using a new
084   adapter specifically designed for the temporal awareness, before being fed into the medical large
085   language model (LLM), Meditron (Chen et al., 2023b). Through a two-stage training strategy, Libra
086   demonstrates the potential of MLLMs for specialised radiology applications. Our modular approach
087   integrates state-of-the-art open-source pre-trained models for image and text while using a temporal-
088   aware adapter to align these modalities with the text embedding space. Extensive experiments show
089   that Libra substantially outperforms existing MLLMs in RRG tasks, demonstrating superior cross-
090   modal understanding and reasoning capabilities. Specifically, our contributions include:
091   • We present Libra, the temporal-aware MLLM capable of capturing temporal changes and over-
092     coming the challenge of handling prior study citations, setting a new state-of-the-art performance
093     in RRG tasks on the MIMIC-CXR dataset among MLLMs of the same parameter scale.
094   • We designed the Temporal Alignment Connector (TAC) with two components: the Layerwise
095     Feature Extractor (LFE), which extracts high-granularity image feature embedding from the en-
096     coder, and the Temporal Fusion Module (TFM), which integrates temporal references from prior
097     studies. This enhances Libra’s ability to capture and utilise temporal information in the RRG task.

      2.2     T EMPORAL A LIGNMENT C ONNECTOR (TAC): N EW A DAPTER D ESIGN
144
145   To address the challenges of integrating temporal information and aligning high-granularity image
146   features in RRG tasks, we developed the TAC module. It bridges the gap between visual features
147   extracted from multiple temporal snapshots of a patient’s CXRs and the language model. The TAC
148   consists of two sub-modules: the LFE, which is to extract the layerwise representation of images,
149   and the TFM, which is to capture the temporal-aware representation between two images.
150

      2.3   P ROMPT D ESIGN
217
218   To enhance Libra’s ability to perceive temporal changes and integrate medical information in the
219   RRG task, we designed a comprehensive prompting strategy comprising a system prompt and de-
220   tailed clinical prompts, as shown in Figure 1. The system prompt is crafted to enable the LLM to
221   recognise temporal variations: “The assistant specialised in comparing Chest X-ray images, identi-
222   fying differences, and noting temporal changes.” Following this, additional sections of the report,
223   such as {Indication}, {History}, {Comparison}, and {Technique}, are incorporated into the clinical
224
      instructions (e.g., in Appx. B.4). The clinical prompt to Libra is: “Provide a detailed description of
      the findings in the radiology image. Following clinical context: {...}.” If no clinical instructions are
225
      available, the prompt defaults to: “Provide a detailed description of the findings in the radiology im-
226
      age.” After tokenizing and embedding the prompt and the answer, we insert the image patch tokens
227   at the specified location, typically between the system prompt and the clinical prompts.

      2.4   T EMPORAL - AWARE T RAINING
230
231   In this study, we focus on frontal-view images, either posterior-anterior (PA) or anterior-posterior
232   (AP), and the Findings sections of radiology reports, as they contain the most direct clinical observa-
233   tions. We adopt a two-stage training strategy inspired by recent advancements in MLLM fine-tuning
234   techniques (McKinzie et al., 2024). This proposed approach aims to progressively teach the model
235   essential skills, including visual feature alignment and temporal information extraction. Libra’s
236   training process involves two stages: temporal feature alignment and downstream task fine-tuning.
237   In the first stage, the visual encoder and LLM weights are frozen, and the TAC is trained with Find-
238   ings and Impression generation, along with CXR-related VQA tasks to extract high-quality image
239   representations and capture temporal changes. In the second stage, we apply Low-Rank Adaptation
240   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) to fine-tune the pre-trained LLM on the Findings section generation task,
241   keeping the visual encoder and connector weights frozen. Unlike traditional full fine-tuning, LoRA
242   achieves comparable performance with significantly lower training costs. The detailed training con-
243   figuration, including learning rate schedules and model parameters, is provided in Appx. B.1.

249   Task Description We focus on generating the Findings section of radiology reports for a set of
250   frontal CXRs to ensure fair comparison with previous work. The Findings section contains descrip-
251   tions by radiologists of both normal and abnormal findings. Typically, these CXRs are accompanied
252   by additional sections such as Indication and Technique, which provide the rationale for the study,
253   including clinical history or specific requests from the referring physician. While not directly related
254
      to diagnostic interpretation, these sections serve as routine records and assist the model in better un-
      derstanding temporal changes between images across different periods. Therefore, we incorporate
255
      clinical instructions about the current image as prompts to guide Libra to complete the RRG task.
256
257   The most common CXR is frontal views, either PA or AP. While other views, such as lateral images,
258   are occasionally used for diagnostic purposes, they mainly serve as supplementary tools to aid in
259   interpreting anatomical structures more comprehensively (Islam et al., 2023). Therefore, the frontal
260
      view remains the standard perspective for clinical interpretation of CXRs, and our study is consistent
      with prior work in RRG tasks, including studies by Chaves et al. (2024) and Hyland et al. (2024).
261
      For both the current and prior images, we exclusively use a single frontal view.
262

301   3.2      E VALUATION M ETRICS
302
303   We evaluate the generated reports using lexical and radiology-specific metrics, following established
304   protocols.. For lexical metrics, we use ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), BLEU-{1, 4} (Papineni et al., 2002),
305   METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) and BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). For radiology-specific metrics,
306
      we use RadGraph-F1 (Jain et al., 2021), RGER (Delbrouck et al., 2022a), F1-CheXpert (Irvin et al.,
      2019a), CheXbert vector similarity (Yu et al., 2022), as well as RadCliQ version 0 Yu et al. (2022).
307
308   Additionally, we introduce a novel temporal entity F1 score (called F 1temp ) to assess the model’s
309   ability to capture temporal information. Clinical metrics typically emphasise the accuracy of medical
310   findings, prioritising the detection of clinically relevant entities. In contrast, the temporal entity score
311   specifically measures the accuracy of entities related to progression over time described in the report.
312
      Detailed descriptions of all metrics and an illustrative F 1temp analysis are provided in Appx. B.3.
313   Temporal Entity F1 Following the work of Bannur et al. (2023), we set a reward list that includes
314   common radiology-related keywords associated with temporal changes. Temporal entities are then
315   extracted from both the ground truth (Egt ) and the generated reports (Egr ). Importantly, no stem-
316   ming or lemmatization is applied during token processing to preserve the precise description of
317   temporal changes. After extraction, we compute precision (Ptemp ) and recall (Rtemp ), which are
318
      subsequently used to calculate the temporal entity score, defined as the harmonic mean of precision
      and recall (Van Rijsbergen, 1974), also known as the F1 score.

326   Although MIMIC-CXR provides an “official” test split, strict comparisons with prior studies remain
327   challenging due to differences in test set inclusion criteria and pre-processing steps. For instance, Yu
328   et al. (2022) and Jeong et al. (2023) included only one image per study, resulting in a test set of 1,597
329   samples, while Tanida et al. (2023) followed the Chest ImaGenome split (Wu et al., 2021b). Such
330   variations in test set distributions can significantly impact the reported results (Park et al., 2024).
331   To ensure fairness, we use a widely adopted test set focused on frontal-view CXRs, aligned with
332
      previous studies such as MAIRA-1 (Hyland et al., 2024) and LLaVA-Rad (Chaves et al., 2024) 3 .
333   Additionally, the latest concurrent work M4CXR (Park et al., 2024) employs multi-turn chain-of-
334   thought (Wei et al., 2023) prompting to generate reports, which differs from our task setup. Further-
335   more, we do not compare with the recent work MAIRA-2, as it is a MLLM specifically designed
336   for grounded radiology report generation task, which incorporates lateral views and prior study re-
337   ports for each subject within the input prompt. Bannur et al. (2024) emphasises a positive transfer
338
      between this task and general report generation, which is also beyond the scope of our study 4 .
339   Taking these considerations into account, we compared our model with state-of-the-art models, in-
340   cluding LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2023a), CheXagent (Chen et al., 2024), GPT-4V (OpenAI et al.,
341   2024), Med-PaLM (Tu et al., 2023), LLaVA-Rad and MAIRA-1. The results are shown in Table 2.
342   Since many of these models are not publicly available, we present their evaluation results as reported
343   in the original sources.

      To evaluate the impact of TAC on Libra’s performance in the RRG task, we initialised a model with
426   the RAD-DINO (Pérez-Garcı́a et al., 2024) image encoder, TAC, and Meditron (Chen et al., 2023b)
427   as the LLM. A baseline experiment (Libra-1) was conducted by fine-tuning only the TAC for the
428   Findings generation task. As shown in Table 4, we performed ablation studies by progressively re-
429   moving different TAC components, including TFM, LFE, the Prior Image Prefix Bias (PIPB), and
430   the entire TAC. Removing TFM restricted the model to processing only the current image, using a
431   configuration similar to LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) but with a four-layer MLP to align the image rep-
      resentation with the LLM’s hidden dimensions. Without LFE, the model follows the LLaVA setup,

      utilising the penultimate layer of the image encoder. The results indicate that removing any TAC
433   submodule leads to a decline in all metrics compared to Libra-1. Removing TFM caused a notable
434   drop in the F1temp score (↓>2%), emphasising its role in capturing temporal information. Remov-
435   ing LFE especially decreases RadGraph-related scores, highlighting its importance in extracting
436   detailed image features. The removal of PIPB impacted clinical scores more than lexical scores.
437   Finally, removing the entire TAC results in substantial declines across all metrics, underscoring its
438   critical role in integrating image details and temporal information5 .

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this study, we presented Libra, a temporal-aware multimodal large language model specifically
530   designed for chest X-ray report generation tasks. Trained exclusively on the open-access MIMIC-
531
      CXR dataset (Johnson et al., 2019b), Libra utilises a two-stage training framework that leverages
      a radiology-specific pre-trained image encoder and language model, connected through a specially
532
      designed Temporal Alignment Connector to bridge visual and textual modalities. By identifying
533
      observations from current CXR and referencing prior scan, Libra generates radiology reports with
534   substantial improvements across all metrics compared to existing models of the same parameter
535   scale. Qualitative analysis in the case study highlights Libra’s ability to effectively utilise the tem-
536   poral relationships between current and prior images, addressing the challenge of hallucinations
537   related to prior study references in RRG tasks. Libra sets a new paradigm for MLLMs in multi-
538   modal medical AI research. In the future, we aim to further enhance Libra’s clinical applicability
539   and accuracy, paving the way for the development of general-purpose AI models in the medical
      domain. For a detailed discussion of Libra’s limitations and future work, please refer to Appx. A.3.

979    Radiology Report Generation Several efforts have been made to develop models tailored for ra-
980
       diology report generation (RRG), driven by the need to address the long-tail distribution of ob-
       servations in chest X-rays and provide fine-grained descriptions of findings. This task has gained
981
       prominence as an important objective for the designed system (Wang et al., 2018b).
982
983    Such systems inherently require a language generation component. Initially, recurrent neural net-
984    works were used (Liu et al., 2019), but these have recently been replaced by Transformer archi-
985    tectures (Miura et al., 2021b; Chen et al., 2022), including large language models (LLMs) such as
986
       PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022) and Vicuna-7B (Chiang et al., 2023).
987    To ensure clinical accuracy in generated text, some studies have moved beyond standard language
988    modelling losses, instead employing reinforcement learning (RL) to optimise for rewards that pri-
989    oritise “clinically relevant” features, such as the inclusion of specific findings (Liu et al., 2019; Irvin
990    et al., 2019b) or maintaining logical consistency (Miura et al., 2021a; Delbrouck et al., 2022a).
991    However, these methods often depend on external models like CheXbert (Smit et al., 2020) or Rad-
992
       Graph (Jain et al., 2021) to extract clinical entities, adding complexity to the optimisation process.
993
994    With the advancement of LLMs, an increasing number of studies have demonstrated that plain auto-
995    regressive language modelling can achieve substantial results. However, we acknowledge that the
996    benefits of more complex training objectives or RL-based approaches may be complementary. Con-
997    sequently, research on leveraging temporal information in RRG tasks can be broadly categorised
998
       into two types: LLM-based methods and non-LLM-based methods.
999    non-LLM-based Model These architectures are typically transformer encoder-decoder models and
1000   their variants. They are often composed of multiple modules that require separate training. Mean-
1001   while, they handle single and double image inputs by symbolically differentiating tasks and employ-
1002   ing two distinct neural network architectures: one designed for single-image inputs and another for
1003   double-image inputs.
1004   Serra et al. (2023) utilises symbolic alignment in its Longitudinal Projection Module and employs
1005   a separately trained BERT-based (Devlin et al., 2019) text generator. RECAP (Hou et al., 2023a)
1006   implements a transformer encoder-decoder with symbolic task differentiation and two-stage train-
1007   ing, beginning with classification tasks followed by report generation. TiBiX (Sanjeev et al., 2024)
1008   adopts a transformer model with causal attention layers and incorporates learnable padding tokens
1009   to handle cases without prior images. BioViL-T (Bannur et al., 2023) is a self-supervised vision-
1010
       language training framework that features a CNN–Transformer hybrid multi-image encoder trained
       jointly with a BERT-based text model.
1011
1012   On one hand, the difference in model parameter sizes, and on the other, as LLM-based models
1013   generally outperform other types of models in the RRG task, papers on non-LLM-based models
1014   or those using small language models typically do not compare their methods with LLM-based
1015   approaches. At the same time, we conducted comparisons and discussions to reaffirm this point, as
1016
       detailed in Appx. D.2.
1017   LLM-based Model Current LLM-based models have achieved notable success in the RRG task,
1018   largely benefiting from visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2023). Structurally, these models (Li
1019   et al., 2023a; Chaves et al., 2024; Hyland et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024) typically
1020   consist of an image encoder and an adapter that bridges the connection to the LLM. These adapters
1021   often rely on single-layer hidden representations (e.g., the last or penultimate layer) from pre-trained
1022
       image encoders, limiting their ability to simultaneously integrate information from multiple images.
1023
1024   In end-to-end training, LLM-based models handle multiple image inputs by concatenating them with
1025   textual prompts to form a composite input for the LLM. For example, the input format is “<Current
       Image Placeholder> + <Prior Imgae Placeholder> + <Prompt>”.

       Although our model adopts an end-to-end training paradigm, it does not rely on the vanilla approach

1029   setting it apart from the default configurations used in previous works. On the other hand, TAC

       A.2   R ESEARCH O BJECTIVES
1050
1051
1052
1053   Temporal Information Temporal change is a crucial factor in understanding disease progression,
1054   especially in radiology, where paired images and corresponding radiology reports capture changes
1055
       over time. This temporal context is often reflected in comparisons between current and prior scans,
       documenting symptom evolution or newly identified findings.
1056
1057   The relativity of position within the timeline is a key determinant of temporal information. This
1058   relative positioning does not alter the established description of well-established facts about symp-
1059   toms already present in the current image. For instance, if the prior image is recent, descriptions of
1060   changes will be minimal, whereas an older prior image may highlight more pronounced changes.
1061   Research Object Our study focuses on the chest X-ray (frontal view), rather than the individual
1062   patient. Specifically, we aim to generate a report for the current image while referencing the temporal
1063   information provided by a single prior image as auxiliary information.
1064
       Our model is primarily designed to handle scenarios with limited temporal information in the RRG
1065   task. For instance, in a case where a patient has two scans taken just milliseconds apart, the current
1066   and prior images would be nearly identical, as no pathological changes would manifest within such
1067   a short interval. This extreme scenario demonstrates how the model handles clinical practice under
1068   limited temporal information. In such cases, the correct diagnosis for this minimal interval would
1069   be that the patient’s condition is “stable.” In our design, the dummy prior image addresses scenarios
1070   where a true prior image is absent.
1071   However, patients often undergo multiple prior scans, which may include temporal information from
1072   different orientations. This more complex temporal information lies beyond the scope of our current
1073   study. We will provide a detailed discussion of this scenario in Appx. A.3.

       A.3   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1076
1077
1078
1079   While our work represents a step forward in leveraging temporal information for radiology report
       generation, it also has several limitations that warrant further exploration.

                                                         20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Handling Multiple Prior Scans Our current model is designed to process a single prior scan along-
1081   side the current scan. While this approach aligns with standard clinical workflows, which typically
1082   prioritise the most recent prior study for comparisons, it overlooks scenarios where multiple prior
1083   scans could offer a richer temporal perspective. For instance, analysing a sequence of images span-
1084   ning an extended period could provide deeper insights into gradual disease progression. Future
1085   efforts should focus on extending our framework to incorporate multiple prior scans efficiently, en-
1086   abling a more nuanced understanding of temporal patterns in clinical data.
1087   Temporal Information Beyond Image Comparisons At present, our model captures temporal
1088   information through paired image comparisons and their corresponding textual reports. However,
1089   clinical assessments often draw upon a broader context, including historical notes, laboratory results,
1090   and other longitudinal patient data. Expanding our approach to integrate these diverse temporal data
1091   sources could facilitate a more holistic understanding of disease trajectories and patient history,
1092   significantly enhancing clinical applicability.
1093   Challenges with Sparse Temporal Data In cases where prior scans are unavailable or only mini-
1094   mally informative (e.g., scans taken within a very short time interval), our use of a “dummy prior
1095   image” addresses the lack of temporal data. However, the model’s ability to interpret and generate
1096   meaningful outputs under these constraints may still be limited. Future work could explore methods
1097   for imputing or synthesising temporal context to address such cases more effectively.
1098
       Computational Complexity Our use of temporal alignment mechanisms and multi-layer feature
1099   integration introduces increased computational demands. This complexity poses challenges for real-
1100   time deployment, particularly in resource-constrained clinical environments.
1101
1102
       Generalisability Across Modalities and Datasets This study focuses exclusively on frontal-view
       chest X-rays and utilises the MIMIC-CXR dataset (Johnson et al., 2019b). However, our approach
1103
       has not been evaluated on other imaging modalities (e.g., CT or MRI) or datasets such as CheX-
1104
       pert (Irvin et al., 2019b) or PadChest (Bustos et al., 2020).
1105
1106   Based on the identified limitations, we propose several directions for future research and develop-
1107   ment:
1108
       • Develop frameworks for integrating multiple prior scans with dynamic temporal reasoning.
1109
1110   • Extend the model to handle multi-modal imaging and textual data for comprehensive diagnostics.
1111
1112   • Investigating the integration of temporal data from diverse clinical sources, including electronic
1113     health records (EHRs)
1114
       • Exploring lightweight model architectures for faster inference while maintaining high perfor-
1115     mance.
1116
1117
       These enhancements will not only address the current limitations but also extend the potential of
1118
       temporal-aware multimodal models in radiology and beyond.

       B.2   D ETAILED D ESCRIPTION OF DATASETS
1135
1136   MIMIC-CXR. (Johnson et al., 2019b) This is a large, publicly accessible dataset comprising
1137   377,110 DICOM images across 227,835 studies, each accompanied by a radiology report (John-
1138   son et al., 2019b). For images, we use the commonly available JPEG files from MIMIC-CXR-
1139   JPG (Johnson et al., 2019a), rather than the original DICOM files, and we preprocess the dataset to
1140   exclude non-AP/PA scans. For each report, we extract the Findings, Impression, Indication, History,
1141   Comparison, and Technique sections using rule-based heuristics supported by the official MIMIC
1142   code repository (Johnson et al., 2018).
1143   For the Findings section generation task, we discard studies where the Findings could not be ex-
1144   tracted, while allowing for missing content in other clinical sections. The same approach is applied
1145   to the Impression section generation task. In all our experiments, we adhere to the official MIMIC-
1146   CXR dataset split. Additionally, we retrieve prior images by following the chronological order of
1147   studies as indicated by the official labels, selecting the closest prior study as the reference image. It
1148   is important to note that, to prevent data leakage between the train, validation, and test sets, prior
1149   images are retrieved only from within the same split.
1150   Medical-Diff-VQA. (Hu et al., 2023) This dataset is a derivative of the MIMIC-CXR dataset, fo-
1151   cused on identifying differences between pairs of main and reference images. The data split adheres
1152   to the original labelling, ensuring no data leakage occurs. In total, this dataset comprises 700,703
1153   question-answer pairs derived from 164,324 main-reference image pairs. The questions are divided
1154   into seven categories: abnormality, location, type, view, presence, and difference. Each pair consists
1155   of a main (current) image and a reference (prior) image, both taken from different studies of the
1156
       same patient. The reference image is always selected from an earlier visit, with the main image
       representing the later visit. Of the seven question types, the first six types focus on the main image,
1157
       while the “difference” questions involve both images.
1158
1159   MIMIC-Ext-MIMIC-CXR-VQA. (Bae et al., 2023) This dataset is an extension of the MIMIC-
1160   CXR dataset, specifically designed for VQA tasks within the medical domain, specifically focused
1161   on CXRs. It includes questions generated from 48 unique templates covering seven content types:
1162
       presence, anatomy, attribute, abnormality, size, plane, and gender. Each template was developed
       with the guidance of board-certified medical experts to ensure clinical relevance, addressing both
1163
       standard medical VQA content and more complex logical scenarios. In total, the dataset consists of
1164
       377,391 unique entries. Since annotations are based on single images, the current image serves as a
1165   dummy prior image for all entries in our experiment.
1166
1167   For this study, we carefully selected datasets that provide complete reports and temporal informa-
1168
       tion (i.e., prior images) to ensure alignment with our research objectives for the RRG task. After
       thoroughly evaluating other datasets, we found them unsuitable for the following reasons:
1169
1170   CheXpert. (Irvin et al., 2019b) This dataset includes annotated scans with label-specific annotations
1171   rather than full medical reports. While useful for training image encoders or annotation models, it
1172   is not appropriate for the RRG task, which requires complete diagnostic reports.
1173   PadChest. (Bustos et al., 2020) Although it includes reports and corresponding prior images, its
1174   reports are in Spanish, placing cross-language training beyond the scope of our model.
1175
       IU-Xray. (Demner-Fushman et al., 2016) This dataset lacks patient-level metadata and prior study
1176
       information, which is critical for our focus on temporal information in chest X-rays.
1177
1178   Chest ImaGenome Dataset. (Wu et al., 2021a)(a derivative of MIMIC-CXR) This dataset does not
1179   follow the official MIMIC-CXR split, risking data leakage across train, validation, and test sets.

1183   Lexical Metrics We employed common natural language generation metrics to quantify the over-
1184   lap between generated and reference reports. Specifically, ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004) measures the
1185   length of the longest common subsequence between the generated and reference reports. BLEU-
1186   {1, 4} (Papineni et al., 2002) calculates n-gram precision and applies a brevity penalty to discourage
1187   overly short predictions. METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), computes the weighted harmonic
       mean of unigram precision and recall, with an additional penalty for fragmenting consecutive word

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       sequences. Finally, we report BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020a), which leverages pre-trained con-
1189   textual embeddings from BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) to match words in candidate and reference
1190   sentences based on cosine similarity. We used default parameters for all of these evaluation metrics.
1191
1192
       For radiology-specific metrics, we used as many of the same evaluation scores as possible from
       previous studies (Tu et al., 2023; Hyland et al., 2024; Bannur et al., 2024; Chaves et al., 2024),
1193
       including the following:
1194
1195   RadGraph-based metrics The RadGraph model (Jain et al., 2021) is designed to parse radiology
1196   reports into structured graphs. These graphs consist of clinical entities, which include references to
1197   anatomy and observations, as well as the relationships between these entities. This structured rep-
1198
       resentation enables a more detailed and systematic analysis of radiology reports, facilitating down-
       stream tasks such as information extraction, report generation, and clinical decision support.
1199
1200   These include RadGraph-F1 (Jain et al., 2021), which computes the overlap in entities and relations
1201   separately and then reports their average. And a variant of it, RGER (Delbrouck et al., 2022b), which
1202   matches entities based on their text, type, and whether they have at least one relation6 .
