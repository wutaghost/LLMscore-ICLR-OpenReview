023                                                 A BSTRACT
024
025             Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated their remarkable performance
026
                across various language understanding tasks. While emerging benchmarks have
                been proposed to evaluate LLMs in various domains such as mathematics and
027
                computer science, they merely measure the accuracy in terms of the final prediction
028
                on multi-choice questions. However, it remains insufficient to verify the essen-
029             tial understanding of LLMs given a chosen choice. To fill this gap, we present
030             CLR-Bench to comprehensively evaluate the LLMs in complex college-level
031             reasoning. Specifically, (i) we prioritize 16 challenging college disciplines in com-
032             puter science and artificial intelligence. The dataset contains 5 types of questions,
033             while each question is associated with detailed explanations from experts. (ii) To
034             quantify a fair evaluation of LLMs’ reasoning ability, we formalize the criteria with
035             two novel metrics. Q→A is utilized to measure the performance of direct answer
036             prediction, and Q→AR effectively considers the joint ability to answer the question
037
                and provide rationale simultaneously. Extensive experiments are conducted with
                40 LLMs over 1,018 discipline-specific questions. The results demonstrate the key
038
                insights that LLMs, even the best closed-source LLM, i.e., GPT-4 turbo, tend to
039
                ‘guess’ the college-level answers. It shows a dramatic decrease in accuracy from
040             63.31% Q→A to 39.00% Q→AR, indicating an unsatisfactory reasoning ability.

137    2      TASK S TATEMENT
138
139    In order to systematically evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, CLR-Bench incorporates a
140    diverse set of question types that reflect various challenges. Each question type is carefully designed
141    to test different aspects to verify LLM’s mastery of in-depth rationalizing. Moreover, given a question,
142    LLMs are required to provide rationales to prove their understanding beyond the direct prediction. In
143    this section, we formally define the question types and the rationale we expect for each type.
144
145    2.1      M ULTI - TYPE R EASONING TASKS
146
      Multi-choice (MC) questions present a single question with multiple possible answers including
147
      disturbing choices, from which LLMs must select the most appropriate one. This type of question
148   evaluates an LLM’s ability to discern and provide the correct answer from a set of plausible options;
149   Multi-select (MS) questions are similar to multi-choice questions but require LLMs to select more
150   than one correct answer. The more disturbing situations assess the LLM’s capability to handle more
151   complex queries where multiple factors must be considered. Moreover, LLMs also face the dilemma
152   of either choosing more for the full mark or selecting the most confident one to get half of the score;
153   Fill-in-blank (FB) questions require LLMs to complete a sentence or statement with the appropriate
154   term or phrase, measuring how well the LLM could generate contextually relevant content;
155   Open-ended (OE) questions allow for a wide range of responses, requiring LLMs to formulate
156
      detailed and comprehensive answers. It tests the LLM’s ability to generate well-rounded explanations;
      True-or-false (TF) questions require students to determine the veracity of a given statement. This
157
      type examines the LLM’s ability to assess factual accuracy based on their knowledge.
158
159    2.2      F ORMALIZATION OF R ATIONALE
160
161    In this subsection, we define the expected rationale for each question type in CLR-Bench. The
       rationale must demonstrate not just correctness but a clear understanding of the contexts.

179   knowledge reserve and an anti-disturbance ability, but also decision-making performance when facing
180   the dilemma of more marks but higher risks, or a safer selection but lower accuracy;
181   Mastery of concepts for TF: The rationale should concisely state the concepts behind the truth value,
182   citing relevant facts or rules that support the decision;
183   Conceptual understanding for FB: Explaining FB questions requires reasons why the chosen
184   word or phrase fits the sentence, showing an understanding of context and meaning, as well as the
185
      underlying knowledge or concepts in the statement;
      Open reasoning ability for OE: As the most difficult task, the rationale for OE, different from the
186
      corresponding solution, should provide a structured, detailed, and well-rounded explanation, covering
187
      all relevant aspects and reflecting comprehensive reasoning steps to infer the final answer.

195   3     C ONSTRUCTION D ETAILS OF CLR-B E N C H
196
197   To ensure the quality of our benchmark, We recruit eight domain experts to handle 16 college-level
198   disciplines. Their expertise is maximally leveraged through our novel three-stage strategy. In this
199   section, we introduce the core procedures of constructing a high-quality dataset leveraging both
200   expert knowledge and GPT-4o as a tool.
201
202   3.1   H IERARCHICAL T OPIC G RAPH
203
204
      A hierarchical topic graph (HTG) G is effectively designed to support multiple disciplines, ensuring
      that key topics across computer science and artificial intelligence are captured in a way that mirrors
205
      their complexity and interrelationships. To comprehensively model discipline-specific knowledge,
206
      we refer to the table-of-content in the textbooks where the multi-level structure ensures that the graph
207   reflects high-level overviews and detailed subtopics to guide the question collection process.
208
209   DEFINITION: Hierarchical Topic Graph. Let O be a well-structured ontology for constructing a
210   HTG G = {O, E, R}, where O = {T , R} defines the topics T and relations R̂ between domain-
211   specific topics E. The ontology O is derived from expert knowledge in 16 disciplines and structured
212
      around a multi-level hierarchy. Specifically, T consists of three levels of topics, where each level
      represents increasingly specific subtopics.
213
214   Each triple (h, r, t) in G comprises a head topic h, a relation r, and a tail subtopic t, where h, t ∈ T
215   and r ∈ R. We define the relations R as "has_subtopic" to represent the hierarchical structure,
      capturing the relationship between general topics and their more granular subtopics. For instance, in

263
264   One of the key contributions of CLR-Bench is the incorporation of expert-verified rationales, which
265   test whether LLMs truly understand the underlying concepts behind their answers. While manually
266   generating detailed rationales for thousands of questions would be both time-consuming and resource-
267   intensive, we streamline this process by integrating GPT-4o alongside expert knowledge. This
268   hybrid approach maximizes both efficiency and quality, reducing the manual burden on experts while
269   ensuring that the rationales produced are both comprehensive and accurate. The detailed prompt
      templates for expert-guided rationale generation are illustrated in Appendix C.

      The process operates in a feedback loop where experts provide carefully designed prompts to GPT-4o,
271   which generates initial rationale drafts. These drafts include all necessary explanations, logical
272   reasoning, and relevant information tied to the question. Experts then review the output, making
273   refinements or modifications as needed to meet high-quality standards. This collaborative approach
274   leverages the speed and coverage of GPT-4o while maintaining the nuanced accuracy that expert
275   verification ensures. By combining human expertise with the capabilities of GPT-4o, we can produce
276   high-quality rationales at a fraction of the cost and time of fully manual methods.
277   By using GPT-4o as an initial rationale generator, we significantly reduce the effort required for
278   manual rationale creation, making the process scalable across large datasets. Experts no longer
279   need to write rationales from scratch but can instead focus on refining and verifying the generated
280   explanations. This hybrid model ensures both speed and quality in rationale production, enabling us
281   to maintain high standards while accommodating the breadth of questions across multiple disciplines.
282   Rather than assessing LLMs purely on their ability to select correct answers, we evaluate their
283   capacity to generate well-structured, coherent explanations that demonstrate true comprehension.
284   This shift from answer prediction to reasoning evaluation is fundamental in advancing LLMs for

290
291
292   In this section, we formally define our novel evaluation protocol with two new metrics, especially
293
      introducing the criteria on reasoning performance in terms of Q→AR. Insightful analysis is provided
      through systematically evaluating 40 state-of-the-art LLMs spanning both open and closed-source
294
      over 1,018 questions in CLR-Bench.
295
296   In this part, we would like to highlight one of our contributions by introducing a novel evaluation
297
      protocol considering the performance of both final prediction (Q→A) and rationale (Q→AR). We
      provide a detailed demonstration of a fair measurement for each type of question.
298
299   Q→A. (i) Exact Matching is applied when the expected answer is discrete and well-defined, as in
300   MC, MS, TF, and FB questions. (ii) Semantic Similarity. The evaluation of OE questions remains a
301   hard problem for the community. We leverage RoberTa-large (Liu et al., 2019) to sketchily compute
302   the semantic similarity between the gold solution and the provided version by LLM. The threshold
303
      is set at 0.90, which means that the solution with higher scores will be treated as correct, while
      the remaining questions will be passed to the next step. (iii) GPT-4o-assisted Expert Evaluation.
304
      To improve the experts’ efficiency and maximize their efforts on OE evaluation, we have carefully
305
      designed a prompt template that asks GPT-4o to provide a draft including the essential reasoning
306   steps and relevant information. Experts will then verify the draft and fix the final gold rationale one
307   by one to ensure accuracy and completeness.
308
309
      Before evaluating Q→AR that measures the ability to answer and rationalize at the same time, we
      introduce another intermediate metric for rationale only, i.e., Q→R. (i) Semantic Similarity. We
310
      compute the similarity score between the LLM-generated rationale and a gold-standard rationale
311
      provided by experts, filtering out rationales that are semantically close to the label. (ii) GPT-4-
312   assisted Expert Evaluation. Similarly, we process the rationales that are marked lower than 0.9 with
313   the help of GPT-4o and experts. Differently, we have a set of particularly designed templates for each
314   question type, details of all the prompts used for Q→A and Q→R are introduced in Appendix D. All
315   the rationales will be marked {0, 0.5, 1} for the score.
316
      Q→AR. Based on the previously computed Q→A and Q→R score, we could automatically calculate
317
      the Q→AR score accordingly. We enforce a strict evaluation to punish the potential ‘guessing’
318   behavior, particularly in cases where the model provides a correct answer but is associated with an
319   incorrect or incomplete rationale. Specifically, (i) if both the answer and the rationale are correct,
320   the model is awarded a full score of 1.0. (ii) However, if the answer is correct but the rationale is
321   only partially correct, the score is reduced to 0.5. (iii) When the answer is correct but the rationale is
322   completely wrong, the score is significantly penalized, dropping to 0.0. (iv) On the other hand, if the
323   answer is incorrect but the rationale is correct, the model still receives some credit with a score of 0.5,
      and with a partially correct rationale, the score becomes 0.25. (v) If both the answer and rationale are

340   4.1   O NE - SHOT SETTING
341
      Unlike benchmarks such as MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) that evaluate LLMs in five-shot,
342
      we introduce a standardized one-shot setting in CLR-Bench to ensure uniformity across models,
343   particularly for smaller LLMs. Experts first maintain a fixed set with a single example per question
344   type and discipline. The example guides the LLMs in structuring their responses while preventing
345   them from leveraging extensive context. For instance, LLMs are required to output in the format of
346   {Rationale:{}, Answer:{}}. This constraint ensures a consistent output format across all
347   models, promoting fairness in evaluation, especially when comparing large and small LLMs. By
348   using one-shot examples, we also aim to highlight the fairness in CLR-Bench of evaluating the
349   reasoning capabilities without potential over-reliance on multiple context examples.

485   In recent years, the development of various benchmarks has significantly improved the evalua-
      tion of large language models (LLMs) from multiple task perspectives. General benchmarks like

      MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024a), GLUE (Wang et al., 2021), Su-
487   perGLUE (Sarlin et al., 2020), CommonSenseQA (Talmor et al., 2018), BIG-Bench (Srivastava et al.,
488   2022), and the AI2 Reasoning Challenge (ARC-Challenge) (Clark et al., 2018) have played a pivotal
489   role in advancing the understanding of language, reasoning, and question-answering tasks, leading to
490   more specialized and comprehensive assessments. More recent benchmarks, such as BoolQ (Clark
491   et al., 2019), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), and SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2018), further enhance
492   the evaluation of model comprehension and complex reasoning abilities. Task-specific benchmarks

496   To facilitate performance comparisons between LLMs, platforms like the OpenLLM Leader-
497   board (Aidar Myrzakhan, 2024) and OpenCompass (Contributors, 2023) have been established.
498   However, as LLMs evolve rapidly, leaderboard scores are becoming increasingly saturated at the
499   top. Models such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude-3 (Anthropic, 2023b), and open-source
500   models like the Qwen (Yang et al., 2024) series are achieving near-perfect results across various
501
      benchmarks. This trend underscores the need for more challenging benchmarks to push the limits of
      LLM capabilities. Moreover, existing benchmarks typically focus solely on reporting the evaluation
502
      results of LLMs, limiting assessments to whether an answer is correct while overlooking the reasoning
503
      process and rationale that reveal a model’s true understanding of a question. To address this gap, we
504   introduce a multi-type rationalized dataset featuring 1,018 questions across five types and 16 subjects
505   in computer science and AI, designed to challenge LLMs with college-level reasoning tasks. For
506   a more robust evaluation, we propose two new metrics: Q→A and Q→AR, which assess both the
507   correctness of answers and the quality of their rationales.
508
      Our findings show that LLMs tend to guess college-level answers without fully grasping the rationale
509
      behind them. Interestingly, larger models do not consistently outperform smaller ones in terms of
510   reasoning. Through extensive experiments involving 40 LLMs, we provide valuable insights into
511   performance variations across question types, topics, and model sizes.

      6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
515
516
      In this paper, we present CLR-Bench, a novel benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the
      reasoning capabilities of large language models in college-level tasks, focusing on computer science
517
      and artificial intelligence. We release a high-quality multi-type question-answering dataset comprising
518
      1,018 questions spanning 16 disciplines. A novel reasoning evaluation paradigm is proposed through
519   Q→A and Q→AR metrics. Unlike traditional benchmarks that solely assess the correctness of final
520   answers, our framework goes beyond by requiring models to provide coherent rationales for their
521   answers, ensuring a deeper evaluation of their reasoning capabilities.
522
      Through extensive experiments on 40 LLMs, we observed significant gaps between the accuracy
523
      of answers (Q→A) and the combined performance on answers and rationales (Q→AR). Our key
524
      insights include: (i) LLMs tend to ‘guess’ the answers since higher Q→A often fails to lead to
525   higher Q→AR. Even when models achieve high accuracy on answers alone, their Q→AR scores
526   were notably lower, indicating that models often fail to fully understand the rationale behind their
527   correct answers. This observation underscores the need for better reasoning mechanisms within
528   LLMs and suggests that current models may rely on shortcuts or superficial patterns rather than truly
529   grasping the underlying concepts. ii Model size does not consistently guarantee a better reasoning
530   ability. Smaller models may even surpass larger ones in terms of Q→AR, even though they fall
531   behind on Q→R. We believe this observation may inspire the community for further exploration
532   toward achieving a more robust understanding beyond the direct answer prediction.
533   In future work, our benchmark will continue expanding to more college-level disciplines including
534   law, physics, accounting, chemistry, biology, etc, and include more advanced techniques and LLMs
535   to deeply investigate the development of research on improving reasoning abilities.
