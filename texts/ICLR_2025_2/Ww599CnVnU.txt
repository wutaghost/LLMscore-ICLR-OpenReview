011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep learning-based automatic sleep staging demonstrates strong performance as
014            a promising solution for diagnosing sleep disorders. However, deep learning mod-
015            els often struggle to generalize on unseen subjects due to variability in physiologi-
016
               cal signals, resulting in degraded performance in out-of-distribution scenarios. To
               address this issue, domain generalization approaches have recently been studied
017
               actively to ensure generalized performance on unseen domains during the training.
018
               Among those techniques, contrastive learning has proven its validity in learning
019            domain-invariant features by aligning samples of the same class across different
020            domains. Despite its potential, many existing methods are insufficient for ex-
021            tracting truly domain-invariant representations, as they do not explicitly reduce
022            domain-relevant information embedded in the features. In this paper, we argue
023            that addressing superfluous information is a key to bridging the domain gap. Fur-
024            thermore, existing methods often neglect the multi-scale nature of sleep signals,
025            potentially missing important temporal and spectral characteristics. To address
026            these limitations, we propose a novel Multi-Scale Minimal Sufficient represen-
027
               tation learning (MSMS) framework, which effectively reduces domain-relevant
               information while preserving essential temporal and spectral features for sleep
028
               stage classification. We evaluate our method on publicly available sleep staging
029
               benchmark datasets, SleepEDF-20 and MASS. Experimental results demonstrate
030            that our approach consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Sleep staging, the process of identifying and tracking transitions between different sleep stages over
036   time, plays a pivotal role in analyzing sleep quality and treating sleep disorders (Scott et al., 2023).
037   Typically, experts categorize sleep states into five stages—Wake, N1, N2, N3, and rapid eye move-
038   ment (REM)— using polysomnography (PSG), which records various physiological signals. While
039   manual sleep staging remains the gold standard, it is both labor-intensive and time-consuming, of-
040   ten requiring trained specialists to carefully examine hours of physiological data. To alleviate these
041   challenges, deep learning (DL)-based techniques have emerged as a powerful alternative. Despite
042   such advanced, numerous DL-based techniques inevitably struggle when confronted with out-of-
043   distribution (OOD) data (i.e., unseen domain), leading to significant performance degradation caused
044
      by a discrepancy in data distribution (Zhou et al., 2022).
045   The challenge of OOD generalization in sleep staging is particularly prevalent due to the high vari-
046   ability in physiological signals between individuals. For instance, insomnia patients typically exhibit
047   increased high-frequency activity and reduced slow-wave sleep in electroencephalogram (EEG) sig-
048   nals, which measure brain activity (Buysse et al., 2008). Moreover, age-related changes add to this
049   complexity; research has shown that slow-wave sleep decreases with age—by as much as 2% per
050
      decade in adults—while the proportions of N2 and REM sleep undergo significant shifts across the
      lifespan (Ohayon et al., 2004). These patient-specific characteristics, or covariates, pose a significant
051
      challenge for DL models, often causing them to perform poorly on data from unseen subjects.
052
053   Domain generalization (DG) aims to enhance the robustness of DL models by improving their abil-
      ity to generalize across unseen data domains. Prior works in DG have focused on learning domain-

085   in sleep staging (Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024b).
086   Contrary to their superiority, those approaches often struggle to extract genuinely domain-invariant
087   representations. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), these methods primarily focus on increasing the shared
088   information between positive samples, thereby facilitating sufficient representation learning, where
089   the learned features retain all task-relevant information. However, this approach does not effectively
090   eliminate domain-relevant information, which often remains embedded within the features as su-
091   perfluous information-unshared information across different samples (Federici et al., 2020). For this
092   reason, minimal sufficient representation learning, which aims to minimize superfluous information,
093
      is a crucial approach for achieving robust domain-invariant representations in DG. However, this ap-
      proach risks overfitting the features of the final layer, potentially reducing the diversity of learned
094
      representations. This limitation is particularly significant in sleep staging tasks, where multi-level
095
      features are essential for capturing distinct frequency characteristics. More advanced methods are
096   required to address these limitations, incorporating both the elimination of superfluous informa-
097   tion and the utilization of multi-scale learning to preserve feature diversity and effectively capture
098   hierarchical representations.
099
      In this work, we propose a novel framework called Multi-Scale Minimal Sufficient representation
100
      learning (MSMS), designed to leverage multi-scale domain-invariant features to effectively bridge
101
      distribution gaps. The primary objective of our MSMS is to minimize domain discrepancies by
102   reducing superfluous information via minimal sufficient representation learning. We argue that min-
103   imizing this superfluous information is crucial for extracting more robust domain-invariant features,
104   as domain-relevant characteristics are still present in it, as illustrated in Figure 1(b). To mitigate
105   potential information reduction and to enhance the model’s capabilities for capturing diverse tem-
106   poral and spectral characteristics inherent in sleep signals, we apply the proposed objective function
107   across encoder features extracted from multiple layers. Consequently, the main contributions of our
      work are:

120   2.1   S LEEP S TAGING
121
122   Sleep staging refers to the classification of sleep states, which is crucial for assessing sleep qual-
123   ity and diagnosing sleep disorders (Melek et al., 2021). Many DL methods have been developed
124   to classify sleep stages using PSG. Conventional DL approaches focused on CNN-based encoder
125   architectures designed to effectively capture the temporal characteristics of EEG signals (Tsinalis
126   et al., 2016; Supratak et al., 2017). Recent studies have introduced techniques that enable models
127
      to learn representations across multiple scales of the encoder, effectively reflecting diverse temporal
      and spectral characteristics from different perspectives (Eldele et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b; Lee
128
      et al., 2024). For example, Eldele et al. (2021) developed a multi-resolution CNN leveraging varying
129
      filter widths to capture features across multiple scales effectively. Similarly, Lee et al. (2024) pro-
130   posed SleePyCo, which employed contrastive learning and a feature pyramid to capture multi-level
131   features, which were then utilized in a transformer-based classifier. Despite these advancements,
132   previous approaches often fail to generalize effectively to unseen subjects due to inadequately ad-
133   dressing variability in physiological signals across individuals. To overcome this limitation, our
134   MSMS method proposed extracting subject agnostic features via the minimal sufficient representa-
135   tion learning.
136
137   2.2   D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
138
139   Domain generalization techniques have been introduced to enhance model performance on unseen
140   domains (Li et al., 2018a; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2021). A common strategy in these
141   approaches is to learn domain-invariant representations by aligning samples from different source
142
      domains (Volpi et al., 2018; Ding et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). For example, Li et al. (2018b)
      introduced a model that learns invariant features by considering the changes across conditional dis-
143
      tributions over labels. Yao et al. (2022) utilized proxy-based contrastive learning to acquire domain-
144
      invariant representations by facilitating effective domain alignment. Dayal et al. (2024) introduced
145   margin-based adversarial learning that uses margin loss-based discrepancy to learn domain-invariant
146   features. Building on these advancements, several studies have investigated the application of do-
147   main generalization to sleep staging tasks, aiming to OOD challenges (Jia et al., 2021; Yang et al.,
148   2023; Wang et al., 2024a). For instance, Yang et al. (2023) proposed a novel framework that uses
149   mutual reconstruction and orthogonal projection techniques to extract domain-invariant features, ad-
150   dressing subject variability. Wang et al. (2024a) proposed a method for obtaining domain-invariant
151   features through both epoch-level feature alignment and sequence-level alignment. Despite this su-
152   periority, they often overlook the importance of capturing both temporal and spectral information
153
      concurrently. Unlike the existing methods in the literature, our MSMS effectively captures both
      temporal and spectral information while ensuring domain-invariant representations by reducing su-
154
      perfluous information across multiple feature levels simultaneously.

251   In contrastive learning-based DG, the feature space is typically encouraged to become more domain-
252   invariant by increasing the similarity of samples belonging to the same class across various domains.
253   However, while these methods may provide a sufficient presentation, they do not necessarily ensure
254   the learning of a minimal sufficient representation. As a result, domain-relevant information that is
255   not shared between different domains often remains within superfluous information, thereby making
256   it insufficient to achieve domain-invariant features. We posit that minimal sufficient learning is more
257   effective in obtaining domain-invariant features compared to contrastive learning-based approaches.

349
350   We evaluated the performance of our proposed method on two different sleep staging datasets:
351   SleepEDF-20 (Kemp et al., 2000) and Montreal Archive of Sleep Studies (MASS) (O’reilly et al.,
352   2014). The sleepEDF-20 dataset comprises PSG recordings from 20 subjects aged from 25 to 34.
353   MASS contains PSG recordings from 62 subjects aged from 25 to 69. For the SleepEDF-20 dataset,
354   we extracted a single-channel EEG (Fpz-Cz) sampled at 100Hz, and for the MASS dataset, we uti-
355   lized the F4-LER channel, downsampled to 100Hz. For both SleepEDF-20 datasets, we combined
356
      the N3 and N4 stages into a single N3 stage. The class distribution of two datasets is in Appendix
      B.5. This process is a commonly used data preprocessing method in sleep staging, and we adhered
357
      to the settings of numerous previous studies to ensure a fair comparison (Seo et al., 2020; Phyo et al.,
358
      2022; Lee et al., 2024). In this study, we treat each subject as a separate domain, aligning with the
359   common practice in sleep research. This approach accounts for the substantial inter-subject variabil-
360   ity in physiological signals, as highlighted in prior works (Phan et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023; Ko
361   et al., 2024).

      5.2   I MPLEMENTATIONS D ETAILS
364
365
      The model was pre-trained with a batch size of 1024, an initial learning rate of 5 × 10−4 , and
366   a weight decay of 1 × 10−4 for the Adam optimizer. The temperature hyperparameter τ for the
367   contrastive loss was set to 0.07, while the regularization parameter α was set to 0.001. We extracted
368   features from the last two layers (j = 4, 5) of the encoder to align the multi-scale feature. The sleep
369   staging process follows the same architecture as the transformer-based classifier utilizing multi-scale
370   features (j = 3,4,5), as proposed in SleePyCo. For sleep staging, the pre-trained encoder was frozen,
371   and only the classifier was trained, with the sequence length set to L = 10.
372   We employed the widely adopted k-fold cross-validation protocol to evaluate the performance of
373   domain generalization. For each fold, we designated specific unseen subjects as the test set and
374   repeated the experiment, ensuring that each subject was included in the test set exactly once. For the
375   SleepEDF-20 dataset (k = 20), we partitioned the data into training, validation, and test sets with a
376   ratio of 15:4:1, respectively. For the MASS dataset (k = 31), we used a ratio of 45:15:2 for training,
377   validation, and test sets. All experiments were conducted on a server equipped with an NVIDIA
      RTX A6000 D6 48GB GPU.

      5.3   R ESULTS
407
408   We conducted a comprehensive evaluation in comparison to state-of-the-art methods for sleep stag-
409   ing, as well as various domain generalization techniques, including ERM (empirical risk minimiza-
410   tion) (Vapnik, 1998), IRM (minimizing risk across different environments) (Arjovsky et al., 2019),
411   PCL (a proxy-based contrastive learning approach) (Yao et al., 2022), COMET (hierarchical con-
412   trastive learning in medical time series) (Wang et al., 2024b), and SleepDG (distribution matching
413   of both global and local sleep sequences) (Wang et al., 2024a). All DG approaches, except for
414
      SleepDG, were trained using the SleePyCo backbone. The comparison was carried out using multi-
      ple metrics, including accuracy (ACC), macro-averaged F1 score (F1), and Cohen’s Kappa (κ). As
415
      shown in Table 1 , our method demonstrated superior performance across both benchmark datasets,
416
      SleepEDF-20 and MASS.
417
418   Specifically, for the SleepEDF-20 dataset, our approach achieved an accuracy of 86.7%, an F1 score
419   of 81.1%, and a κ of 0.818. Similarly, for the MASS dataset, our method yielded competitive results
420
      with an accuracy of 88.3%, an F1 score of 83.6%, and a κ of 0.826. We conducted statistical t-testing
      between our method and the baseline, calculating a p-value (P < 0.001) on both SleepEDF-20 and
421
      MASS datasets. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method
422
      over the baseline model, SleePyCo (Base), which employed supervised contrastive learning (SCL).
423   Additionally, our approach outperforms other competitive contrastive learning-based domain gener-
424   alization methods, such as PCL and COMET. This illustrates that our method can more effectively
425   handle domain shifts, leading to better generalization on challenging sleep staging task.

      6        C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we proposed a novel framework, Multi-Scale Minimal Sufficient representation learn-
533
      ing (MSMS), which mitigates domain gaps by reducing superfluous information while simultane-
534   ously aligning multi-scale features to consider various temporal and spectral characteristics inher-
535   ent in physiological signals. Extensive experiments conducted on publicly available sleep staging
536   datasets demonstrate that our approach consistently outperforms SOTA techniques. These results
537   highlight that our method ensures generalization to unseen domains. While this framework demon-
538   strates promising results, future research will explore incorporating mechanisms to mitigate dis-
539   tribution shifts across datasets, thereby improving the framework’s robustness and applicability in
      real-world scenarios.
