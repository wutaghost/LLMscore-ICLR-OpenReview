011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The functional or structural spatial regions within tissues, referred to as spatial
014            niches, are elements for illustrating the spatial contexts of multicellular organ-
015            isms. A key challenge is querying shared niches across diverse tissues, which
016
               is crucial for achieving a comprehensive understanding of the organization and
               phenotypes of cell populations. However, current data analysis methods predomi-
017
               nantly focus on creating spatial-aware embeddings for cells, neglecting the devel-
018
               opment of niche-level representations for effective querying. To address this gap,
019            we introduce QueST, a novel niche representation learning model designed for
020            querying spatial niches across multiple samples. QueST utilizes a novel subgraph
021            contrastive learning approach to explicitly capture niche-level characteristics and
022            incorporates adversarial training to mitigate batch effects. We evaluate QueST
023            on established benchmarks using human and mouse datasets, demonstrating its
024            superiority over state-of-the-art graph representation learning methods in accurate
025            niche queries. Overall, QueST offers a specialized model for spatial niche queries,
026            paving the way for deeper insights into the patterns and mechanisms of cell spatial
027
               organization across tissues.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   A spatial niche in tissue sections is a cell and its surroundings with specific functionality or struc-
033
      ture. Cells within tissues operate in complex spatial niches, where their functions are significantly
      influenced by interactions with neighboring cells. These niches are characterized by the spatial
034
      arrangement of cells and the molecular signals exchanged among them, playing a crucial role in
035
      regulating higher-order tissue functions such as immune responses, tissue regeneration, and disease
036   progression (Wu et al., 2021; Meylan et al., 2022; Palla et al., 2022). Importantly, the behavior of
037   the same cell type can vary dramatically depending on its niche (Wagner et al., 2016), underscoring
038   the necessity of understanding the spatial context in cellular behavior.
039
      Recent advances in spatial transcriptomics technologies (Xia et al., 2019; Eng et al., 2019; Rao et al.,
040
      2020; Rodriques et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022) have enabled scientists to inspect this important
041
      biological component from a computational view, as these technologies manage to generate gene
042   expression data for each cell or sequencing spot while preserving its location information. Some
043   researchers have endeavored to develop context-aware representation learning methods on spatial
044   transcriptomics data (Hu et al., 2021; Dong & Zhang, 2022; Long et al., 2023). However, these
045   methods focus on cell or node embedding clustering on a single spatial transcriptomics sample,
046   limiting their application to studying spatial niches. Also, most methods are unable to remove the
047   batch effect, limiting their usability on multiple samples.
048   Extending the analysis of spatial niches across multiple samples amplifies our ability to discern
049   universal patterns and unique variations that single-sample studies might overlook. By integrat-
050   ing spatial transcriptomics data across different samples, including, different tissues, developmental
051   stages, or disease conditions, we may uncover how similar niches or microenvironmental features
052   manifest in diverse biological contexts, shedding light on the underlying mechanisms that drive nor-
053   mal physiology and pathological states. Recent researchers have made a step towards this multiple
      sample integration target (Zeira et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), which are the


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      so-called ‘alignment’ methods. However, these methods focus on cross-sample similarity on the
055   cell-cell level rather than considering it on a niche level. Although the cell-cell alignment results
056   may generally indicate niche similarity structure, they cannot directly serve as niche similarity mea-
057   surement. Furthermore, these methods are limited to aligning only two samples per training run,
058   making them impractical for analyzing large cohorts of spatial transcriptomics samples.
059
      Querying spatial niches across different samples presents several unique challenges. First, explicit
060
      representations are needed to compute the similarities among spatial niches and perform query tasks.
061   However, existing graph deep learning methods typically focus on node embedding rather than sub-
062   graph embedding. Some methods (Wu et al., 2022; Fischer et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) have
063   tried to represent a subgraph via posing a pooling layer or readout function on the node embedding,
064   but this simple approach cannot guarantee proper utilization of information within and between
065   subgraphs under the self-supervised setting. Second, the gene expression data between different
066   samples may contain considerable batch effect (Korsunsky et al., 2019), which must be removed
067   before comparing niches on a sample-wise level.
068   To address the above challenges, we present QueST, a novel niche representation learning model
069   that explicitly learns biologically meaningful representations for spatial niches and can perform spa-
070   tial niche queries across multiple samples. We construct benchmarks for the niche query problem
071   with the Human Dorsolateral Prefrontal Cortex (DLPFC) dataset (Maynard et al., 2021) and the
072   Mouse Olfactory Bulb Tissue (MOBT) dataset (Guo et al., 2023), and evaluate QueST against sev-
073   eral state-of-the-art graph representation learning and alignment methods on these benchmarks. Our
074   framework makes the following key contributions:
075
076
             • Novel Niche Representation Learning Strategy: We introduce a novel graph contrastive
               learning strategy to explicitly learn the representation of a niche, which includes obtaining
077
               subgraph embedding via a pooling layer and partially shuffling the graph while leaving
078
               certain parts fixed to generate positive and negative niche pairs.
079
080          • Adversarial Batch Effect Removal: We borrow the idea of Adversarial Auto-encoder
081            (Makhzani et al., 2015) and introduce an adversarial training strategy to remove batch in-
082
               formation from the model’s latent space during the training stage and empower the encoder
               with the ability to remove batch effect from unseen samples.

085   2   TASK F ORMULATION
086
087
      Consider a spatial transcriptomics dataset D that includes gene expression measurements for N cells,
088   G genes, and T samples. For each cell i, we have access to the gene expression vector xi ∈ RG , its
089   spatial location si ∈ R2 , and a one-hot batch annotation bi ∈ NT indicating the sample from which
090   cell i originates.
091
      We construct a spatial graph G = (V, E) based on the spatial proximity relationship among cells
092
      within each sample, where each node has node features that correspond to a cell’s gene expression
093
      vector. We define the k-hop subgraph centered at cell i as its corresponding spatial niche, denoted
094   as Niche(i). Consequently, the dataset D will contain up to N niches in total. We categorize these
095   niches into two sets:
096
097
      Query niche set Q: a small set of niches that are well studied, possessing known properties of
      interest, such as specific cell type patterns.
098
099   Reference niche set R: a larger set of niches where the properties of interest remain largely un-
100   known and are worth exploration.
101   Now we introduce the main objective of this paper:
102
103       Niche Query Problem: What is the most similarity niche in R to a query niche in Q?
104
105   Figure 1 provides a schematic illustration of this problem. Each query niche corresponds to a ”simi-
106   lar niche(s)” area on a reference sample, where a series of niches will all serve as the correct answers
107   to the Niche Query Problem. We aim for the model not only to identify a single correct niche within
      the ”similar niche(s)” area but also to reveal this entire area.

      In this section, we present QueST, the self-supervised graph auto-encoder framework, to query
156   niches on multi-sample spatial transcriptomics data based on graph neural networks. Besides the
157   main graph auto-encoder architecture, QueST has a contrastive learning module and an adversar-
158   ial training module, which are two critical components that distinguish QueST from previous ap-
159   proaches. The contrastive learning module enables the model to discriminate between niches with
160   different cell type compositions and topological structures, while the adversarial training module
161   removes the batch effect from the latent niche representation. We present a schematic illustration of
      QueST model architecture in Figure 2.

      4.1   B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
292
      As there are currently no available multi-sample datasets with niche-level annotations that can serve
293   as benchmarks, we synthesize our niche query benchmarks based on existing spatial transcriptomics
294   datasets with cell-type annotation.
295
296
      Specifically, we use two widely used datasets in spatial transcriptomics: the Human Dorsolateral
      Prefrontal Cortex (DLPFC) dataset and the Mouse Olfactory Bulb Tissue (MOBT) dataset. These
297
      datasets represent niche query problems with varying levels of challenges. On each dataset, we first
298
      separate samples into query and reference set, and then generate niches that need to be queried. The
299   DLPFC dataset consists of 12 samples from the human brain, with ground truth cell type annotation
300   illustrated in Figure A.1. We partition this dataset into a query set with 1 sample and a reference
301   set with 11 samples. This setting requires the model to perform batch effect removal and niche
302   query on a large cohort of spatial transcriptomics samples. The MOBT dataset contains 3 samples
303   from different sequencing technologies including 10X Visium, Stereo-seq (Chen et al., 2022) and
304   Slide-seq V2 (Stickels et al., 2021). The radius of data spots for each technology differs; they are
305   50µm, 35µm and 10µm, respectively. As the ground truth cell type annotation shown in Figure A.2,
306   the variation in spot resolution affects the spatial distribution of the sequencing spots or cells, which
307
      is an even harder task. It requires the niche query methods not only to remove the batch effect in
      gene expression but also to model the spatial context on heterogeneous graphs. We split the MOBT
308
      dataset into a query set with 1 sample from Stereo-seq and a reference set with 2 samples from 10X
309
      Visium and Slide-seq V2. The detailed description of these two datasets can be found in section
310   A.1.
311
312   Once the query and reference sample sets are established, any niche within the query sample can
313
      correspond to a niche query problem. In this paper, we generate several query niches by changing
      these two parameters:
314
315   1. Size: This refers to the number of cells included in the niche. We consider three scenarios with
316   the query niche containing 50, 100, and 200 cells.
317   2. Cell Type Pattern: This refers to the number of cell types contained in the niche. We also
318   examine three scenarios, where the query niche includes 1, 2, and 3 cell types.
319
320
      By combining these two aspects of variation, in total 9 query niches can be defined for each query
      sample, as illustrated in Figure A.3 and Figure A.4. See section A.2 for detailed generation of query
321
      niches.

      4.4   Q UE ST A BLATION E XPERIMENTS
456
457   In this section, we present the results of the ablation experiments, as shown in Table 5. From these
458   results, we can draw several important observations:
459
      First, removing the contrastive learning module significantly degrades the model’s performance.
460
      This finding is intuitive, as the primary purpose of incorporating contrastive learning is to help
461
      the model distinguish between potentially similar yet fundamentally different niches (for instance,
462   niches with the same cell type composition but different arrangements are treated as negative pairs)
463   and to recognize identical niches in varying external environments (positive pairs). Our model em-
464   ploys subgraph pooling to generate niche embeddings, which can obscure the topological structure
465   within the niche or subgraph. The introduction of contrastive learning effectively mitigates this
466   issue.
467

469   fact that, while the information aggregated in node embeddings considers the microenvironments

471   central cell. Given that a spatial niche comprises dozens of cells, it is challenging to identify a

521   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
522
523
524   In this study, we introduce the Niche Query Problem and develop QueST, a novel and specialized
525
      framework for querying spatial niches across diverse spatial transcriptomics samples. QueST em-
      ploys a subgraph contrastive learning approach to explicitly learn niche representation, incorporates
526
      an adversarial training framework to perform batch correction, and finally uses cosine similarity
527
      between latent representation vectors to perform spatial niche query. Experimental results demon-
528   strate the efficacy of these critical modules and validate QueST’s capability for accurately querying
529   spatial niches in various settings. To the best of our knowledge, QueST is the first graph deep learn-
530   ing model tailored for querying spatial niches in spatial transcriptomics data. We believe that with
531   the increased volume of spatial transcriptomic data, this niche query problem will be a foundation
532   for investigating pan-cancer or cross-species studies, and both the QueST method and the query
533   benchmark we developed will lay the groundwork for addressing this problem.
534   Certain limitations exist for QueST and further work is desired to improve the framework. At
535   present, QueST requires computing k-hop subgraphs with a fixed k to extract spatial niches on ref-
536   erence samples for comparison with the query niche. Handling niches with various sizes and shapes
537   simultaneously on reference samples is a potential future direction for improvement. Furthermore,
538   the graph auto-encoder architecture limits the scale of the model. Introducing novel architecture
539   such as transformers may help scale up the model for larger pre-training and querying tasks on
      datasets with millions of cells or even more.
