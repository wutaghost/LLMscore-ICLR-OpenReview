009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In continual learning with pretrained large language models (LLMs), where data
012            from instruction fine-tuning (IFT) tasks arrives in a sequence, fine-tuning on later
013            tasks will often lead to performance degradation on earlier tasks. This is espe-
014            cially pronounced when the IFT tasks come from diverse domains. In this set-
015            ting, how can we mitigate catastrophic forgetting of earlier tasks and retain what
016
               the LLM has learned? Inspired by a classical continual learning method—L2
               penalty to previous weights—we propose Sequential Fine-tuning with Averaging
017
               (SFA), a method that merges models with earlier checkpoints trained on previ-
018
               ous tasks during the course of training. SOTA approaches typically maintain a
019            data buffer of past tasks or impose a penalty at each gradient step. In contrast,
020            our method achieves comparable results without the need to store past data, or
021            multiple copies of parameters for each gradient step. Furthermore, our method
022            outperforms penalty methods like L2 and Elastic Weight Consolidation, as well
023            as other common merging techniques such as Task Arithmetic, and TIES Merg-
024            ing. Finally, we show that using our method, a single model can simultaneously
025            perform well on a range of fine-tuning tasks in diverse domains, including Math,
026            Law and Code.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) on new tasks often leads to catastrophic forgetting: the
031   rapid degradation of performance on previously learned tasks (Scialom et al., 2022; Lesort et al.,
032   2019; Delange et al., 2021; Belouadah et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023). This poses a major challenge
033   for continual learning scenarios, where data comes in a stream of sequences of tasks that may not
034   reappear. As such, we are in need of fine-tuning procedures that would allow LLMs to continually
035   adapt to new knowledge without sacrificing past abilities.
036
      Previous work has analyzed catastrophic forgetting of different types of information, as well as the
037   impact of scale. Scialom et al. (2022) explain that LLMs can perform worse on past fine-tuning
038   tasks as they learn new ones. Furthermore, Luo et al. (2023) show a model can also forget general
039   knowledge, not specific to a single past task. Finally, forgetting also grows in severity as model size
040   increases (Luo et al., 2023). Existing state-of-the-art approaches to mitigate forgetting primarily
041   focus on modifying the training data used in fine-tuning. These methods either maintain a data
042   buffer of past tasks (Robins, 1995; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2022; de Masson d’Autume et al.,
043   2019), or generate approximations of past task data for joint training with current tasks (Shin et al.,
044   2017; Mocanu et al., 2016). However, both strategies introduce additional costs. Data buffers
045
      increase memory overhead and require careful management, while generating data approximations
      necessitates extra training and computational resources. Likewise, more classical methods of
046
      continual learning that incorporate a penalty directly into training to constrain weights ((Kirkpatrick
047
      et al., 2017), L2 penalty) are memory-intensive as they require storing multiple copies of model
048   parameters to be used at each gradient step.
049
050   Despite ongoing research into combating forgetting, several key questions remain. What im-
051   pact does the domain of the fine-tuning tasks have? Specifically, does catastrophic forgetting get
052   even worse when there is a domain shift and if so, by how much? Finally, can we make model-based
053   interventions that can alleviate the cost of storing past data or model parameters, generating new
      data or doing additional expensive training?

      In this paper, we systematically investigate forgetting in pretrained LLMs as they fine-tune on tasks
055   from distinct domains in a continual learning setting. More specifically, we focus on settings where
056   the model sees a sequence of fine-tuning tasks from Law, Math, and Code. In this setting, we analyze
057   the ability of a model pretrained on general language generation to fine-tune to this sequence of new
058   tasks, and track the degradation of the models’ existing knowledge on old tasks. By doing so we
059   offer empirical evidence about the nature and rate of forgetting in LLMs during continual learning.
060
      To combat forgetting, we propose Sequential Fine-tuning Averaging (SFA), a novel method that
061   merges the model being trained on a new task with a checkpoint from a previous task during
062   training. This averaged model is then further trained on the new task. By reusing previous
063   checkpoints, SFA promotes knowledge retention across tasks and domains. Our experiments focus
064   on the continual learning settings where data from a sequence of tasks stream in, and only the
065   current task data is available. As such, our solution offers the advantage of not needing to store
066   past data, and simply relies on updating model parameters during fine-tuning with combinations
067   of past and current weights. Furthermore, our solution also does not require training an additional
068   past data generator, because it uses previous model checkpoints as proxies for such data. Our work
069
      offers a way forward to obtaining a single model that can perform well on a variety of fine-tuning
      domains, shedding light on the generalization ability of continual learning LLMs. Our work can be
070
      summarized by the following contributions:
071
072
073          • We introduce Sequential Fine-tuning Averaging (SFA), a method for mitigating forget-
074            ting by averaging model checkpoints from past tasks during fine-tuning on a new task.
075
               This enables the model to retain knowledge on past tasks/domains while learning a new
               task/domain.
076
             • We show how SFA can be understood as an approximation of a classical continual learning
077
               algorithm: applying an L2 penalty between the current model and checkpoints from past
078            tasks. This analysis bridges classical continual learning algorithms such as L2 penalty
079            with commonly used model merging techniques, thus providing intuition for why model
080            merging can be so effective at mitigating forgetting.
081          • We compare SFA to other techniques for mitigating forgetting across a range of tasks,
082            domains, and models. We show consistent results that across models and tasks/domains,
083            our method achieves comparable results to using a data buffer, while outperforming other
084            model merging techniques, as well as more classical continual learning methods.

088   Forgetting and Continual Learning A large and growing body of literature investigates differ-
089   ent aspects of catastrophic forgetting in continual and sequential learning. When the training data
090   consists of disjoint tasks, training classifiers can cause catastrophic forgetting (Rebuffi et al., 2017).
091   Furthermore, if forgetting occurs, it can be tracked during training and is dependent on when exam-
092   ples are seen by the model: models are less likely to remember earlier training examples (Jagielski
093   et al., 2022; Tirumala et al., 2022). Interestingly, forgetting can also occur for general knowledge
094   rather than for specific tasks, and is more severe for larger models (Luo et al., 2023). Lesort et al.
095   (2022) show that overlap between tasks and task repetition in continual learning settings can mitigate
096
      catastrophic forgetting of such examples resulting in solutions to forgetting that involve maintain-
      ing a data buffer with past data. Such solutions can also be extrapolated to LLMs where continual
097
      learning with data repetition can prevent catastrophic forgetting (Scialom et al., 2022). Mitigating
098
      forgetting in continual learning can also occur by introducing a penalty in the loss objective. L2
099   penalty in continual learning constrains the weights of a model as it is learning a new task by intro-
100   ducing a penalty based on the difference between the current and initial model’s weights. Similarly,
101   Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) also introduces a penalty to con-
102   strain the weights of a model and mitigate increased loss on learned tasks while incorporating the
103   importance of specific weights on learned tasks.
104
105   Model Merging There exist many techniques and applications for merging multiple models to
106   create a single model with improved generalization on a given set of tasks. Model souping (Worts-
107   man et al., 2022a) involves averaging the parameters of existing models to create a new model. This
      technique can be applied after training multiple variations of a model on data during a hyperpa-

108
      rameter sweep to combine the models and achieve higher performance than any individual model.
109   Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023) involves finding and adding task vectors to create a multi-task
110   model. Wise-FT (Wortsman et al., 2022b) merges the weights of an initial and a fine-tuned model.
111   Our method builds upon these 3 works, but with key differences as described in Section 3.
112   Additional influential model merging techniques include: Ramé et al. (2023) use a model soup-
113   ing approach to obtain a network with improved out-of-distribution performance by averaging the
114   weights of models fine-tuned on different tasks. TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) only merges influential
115   parameters whose signs are in the direction of greatest movement across the models. Fisher merging
116   (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Dhawan et al., 2023; Jhunjhunwala et al., 2023) requires keeping data from
117
      all previous tasks and computing gradients.
      Finally for merging different textual domains, Branch-Train-Merge (BTM) (Li et al., 2022) main-
118
      tains a set of distinct domain models that can be merged and then trained to create new experts.

121   3     M ETHODOLOGY: S EQUENTIAL F INE - TUNING AVERAGING (SFA)
122
123   Our method, Sequential Fine-tuning Averaging (SFA), leverages existing techniques in model merg-
124   ing (Ilharco et al., 2023; Wortsman et al., 2022a;b) to mitigate forgetting in the continual learning
125   setting. In this method, we consider a pretrained LLM that is trained on a sequence of instruction
126   fine-tuning tasks from different domains. While the model is being fine-tuned on the current task,
127
      we periodically average the parameters of current model with an earlier checkpoint that resulted
      from fine-tuning on a previous task. We then continue fine-tuning this new averaged model on the
128
      current task.
129
130   More precisely, let θo denote the parameters of the network optimized for the last task. Let θt be the
131   parameters of the current model at time t ≤ T during fine-tuning on a new task. Then, every pT
132   iterations, we reset the parameters to be a weighted combination of θo and θt , where the weighing is
133
      determined by a hyperparameter 0 ≤ β ≤ 1 (default: 0.5).
134
135
      Algorithm 1 Sequential Fine-tuning Averaging
136     Input: θo , p, β
137     Update model parameters θt at each time step t
138
        if t mod pT = 0 or t = T then
           θt = (β)θo + (1 − β)θt
139
140
141   By averaging with an optimized model of the last learned task, our method prevents the current
142   model parameters θt from moving significantly from the original model’s and thus losing optimal
143   performance on the past task (Section 6.3). In this way, our technique combines the intuition of
144
      continual learning with Rehearsal (Robins, 1995), Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023) and Wise-
      FT (Wortsman et al., 2022b). However, unlike Rehearsal-based methods that store data in a buffer,
145
      we use a model fine-tuned on a past task/domain. Furthermore, unlike Task Arithmetic, our method
146
      merges a past checkpoint of a given model with the current model, rather than the task vectors from
147   individual models. Finally, while our method focuses on merging during actual fine-tuning and
148   across tasks/domains, Wise-FT merges a pretrained and a fine-tuned model. In this way, our work
149   generalizes Wise-FT throughout continual learning. As the number of tasks increases, we continue
150   to average the most recent θo , which has high performance on all previous tasks, with the current
151   model parameters θt . For example, we merge a model already trained on 2 tasks with the current
152   model training on a third task. We then update θo to be the merged model of all 3 tasks. We find that
153   we are able to preserve performance on all past tasks through continuous averaging.

241   In order to measure and mitigate forgetting, we fine-tune our models on tasks in 3 distinct domains:
242   Law, Math and Code. For each domain, we fine-tune our model on a dataset featuring domain-
243   specific knowledge, as well as unique instruction tasks. For Law, we combine CaseHOLD (Zheng
244   et al., 2021), Terms of Service (ToS) (Lippi et al., 2019; tos, 2023), and Overruling (Zheng et al.,
245   2021) to create a more general Law dataset. For Math, we use MetaMathQA (Yu et al., 2023), and
246   for Code we use MagiCoder110k (Wei et al., 2023). We believe that required task knowledge across
247   these 3 domains is distinct with minimal overlap. As such, we purposefully aim to test our models’
248
      ability to generalize across a wide range of knowledge to measure the validity of our method under
      maximal domain shifts.
249
250   Evaluation Metrics for Data: In our work, we reference the forgetting of various tasks. We define
251   forgetting specific knowledge as a decrease in performance on a given task during evaluation for a
252   model already fine-tuned on the task. For example, if evaluation performance on Task A drops when
253   a model fine-tunes on Task B, given that the model has already fine-tuned on task A, we consider
254
      the model to forget Task A. To evaluate performance on our fine-tuning data, we use the metrics and

309
      tuning on Task B, every pT steps we average the weights with the final model after fine-tuning on

310
      during cross-domain sequential fine-tuning. Figs. 2 and 3 show that as p decreases, signifying more
311

361   6.3   L2-D ISTANCE AND ACCURACY
362
363   We previously show how SFA approximates applying an L2 penalty. In order to further explore this
364   intuition of SFA and its relation to constraining parameter weights, we also show how accuracy and
365   L2 distance are correlated. We use the setup described in Fig. 3 where our model first fine-tunes on
366   Math, then Law. As Fig. 5 shows, when proportion of fine-tuning before averaging p decreases on
367
      SFA (purple curve), the L2 distance to the initial Math model decreases, while the accuracy on Math
      increases. This is in direct contrast to sequential fine-tuning without intervention (black pentagon),
368
      because of its much higher L2 distance to the initial model. As such, p directly relates to L2 distance,
369
      as well as performance on previous tasks, because averaging frequency constrains how much model
370   parameters can change from their initial positions. The values for this figure can be found in Table 2.

516   7   C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this paper, we provide a comprehensive evaluation of domain forgetting in a continual learning
519   setting, and offer solutions to allow models to retain knowledge from all domains they fine-tune on.
520   After showing how quickly a given model can forget learned tasks as it sequentially fine-tunes on
521   new ones, we evaluate methods that aim to mitigate this forgetting. We introduce SFA and show
522   how, by treating a past model as representative of past data, we can use parameter averaging to
523
      retain knowledge as the model fine-tunes on new tasks/domains. We likewise compare SFA to L2
      penalty, and show how model merging methods can approximate imposing a penalty in continual
524
      learning. The final performance of SFA is comparable to continual learning with rehearsal, but has
525
      the advantage of not maintaining a data buffer. Furthermore, our solution surpasses other commonly
526   used model merging and penalty techniques by incorporating infrequent model merging into the
527   fine-tuning of a model.

      A.3   F ORGETTING UNDER S EQUENTIAL F INE - TUNING
865
866   We start by confirming that fine-tuning on a sequence of different tasks leads to performance degra-
867   dation on previously learned tasks. This forgetting phenomenon occurs across different task domains
868   and for different model sizes. In this work, we focus on catastrophic forgetting of capabilities ac-
869   quired during instruction fine-tuning instead of base pretrained model capabilities. This is because,
870   as we will show, forgetting of skills learned during instruction finetuning can be quite severe and
871   experiments at this scale are more feasible. We fine-tune our models on a sequence of instruction,
872   language generation datasets that test general knowledge to measure forgetting. Specifically, we
873
      use Scialom et al. (2022)’s: Text Simplification (Simpl), Inquisitive Question Generation (InqQG),
      Headline Generation with Constraint (HGen), COVID-fact, Covid QA (CQA), and Twitter Stylom-
874
      etry (TwSt). Many of these tasks incorporate existing datasets which we describe in Appendix A.5.
875
      In our first experiments, we fine-tune the T0 3B (3B) and T0pp (11B) models (see Appendix A.4
876   for model descriptions) on the sequence of tasks described in Section 5 while measuring forgetting
877   on the first task. The results are shown in Fig. 8. The model is first trained on Simpl which leads to a
878   decrease in validation loss shown in blue. Subsequently, the model is trained on a sequence of other
879   tasks; the decrease in validation loss on these tasks is shown in different colors. During this process,
880   we continue to monitor the validation loss on Simpl, displayed in pink. As models fine-tune on new
881   tasks, their performance on Simpl consistently declines as loss increases. This is true at both the
882   3B and 11B (Fig. 8) model scales, indicating that merely scaling up parameter size does not help
883   mitigate forgetting despite the increased capacity.
884   But how severe is this forgetting? We quantify this by comparing a model that was trained on and has
885   then forgotten Simpl to a model that has never seen Simpl. In Fig. 9, the pink line shows validation
886   loss on Simpl for a model trained on a sequence of fine-tuning tasks starting with Simpl. As the
887   model learns new tasks, its performance deteriorates. After 2000 steps, the sequentially fine-tuned
888   model’s loss on Simpl is the same order of magnitude as that of the multitask model trained on all
889   tasks except Simpl. Thus if a model that has learned Simpl is finetuned on other tasks for as little
890
      as 2000 steps, its performance degrades to that of a model that has never seen Simpl. This indicates
      significant forgetting, as the model loses the ability to respond to tasks it previously was able to.
