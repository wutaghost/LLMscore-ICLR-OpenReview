009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As Large Language Models (LLMs) grow increasingly powerful, multi-agent sys-
012            tems—where multiple LLMs collaborate to tackle complex tasks—are becoming
013            more prevalent in modern AI applications. Most safety research, however, has
014            focused on vulnerabilities in single-agent LLMs. These include prompt injection
015            attacks, where malicious prompts embedded in external content trick the LLM
016            into executing unintended or harmful actions, compromising the victim’s appli-
017            cation. In this paper, we reveal a more dangerous vector: LLM-to-LLM prompt
018
               injection within multi-agent systems. We introduce Prompt Infection, a novel
               attack where malicious prompts self-replicate across interconnected agents, be-
019
               having much like a computer virus. This attack poses severe threats, including
020
               data theft, scams, misinformation, and system-wide disruption, all while propa-
021            gating silently through the system. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that
022            multi-agent systems are highly susceptible, even when agents do not publicly
023            share all communications. To address this, we propose LLM Tagging, a defense
024            mechanism that, when combined with existing safeguards, significantly mitigates
025            infection spread. This work underscores the urgent need for advanced security
026            measures as multi-agent LLM systems become more widely adopted.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to evolve and become more adept at following in-
032   structions (Peng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b), they introduce not only new capabilities but also
033
      new security threats (Wei et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023). One such threat is prompt injection, an
      attack where malicious instruction from external documents overrides the victim’s original request,
034
      allowing the attacker to assume the authority of the model’s owner (Greshake et al., 2023; Perez
035
      & Ribeiro, 2022). However, research into prompt injection has primarily focused on single-agent
036   systems, leaving the potential risks in Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) poorly understood (Liu et al.,
037   2024c;a; Guo et al., 2024).
038
039
      Addressing this gap is growing crucial. Multi-agent systems play a key role in enhancing LLMs’
      power and flexibility, from social simulations (Park et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023)
040
      to collaborative applications for problem-solving (Lu et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024) and code gen-
041
      eration (Wu, 2024; Lee et al., 2024). Recently, frameworks like LangGraph (LangGraph), AutoGen
042   (Wu et al., 2023), and CrewAI (CrewAI, 2024) have accelerated the widespread adoption of multi-
043   agent systems by individuals and corporations, enabling agents with unique roles and tools to work
044   together seamlessly (Topsakal & Akinci, 2023). While these tools enhance MAS functionality by
045   connecting agents to internal systems, databases, and external resources (Kim & Diaz, 2024; Qu
046   et al., 2024), they also introduce significant security risks (Ye et al., 2024).
047
      However, most studies on MAS safety focus on inducing errors or noise in agent behavior, over-
048   looking the more severe risks posed by prompt injection attacks (Huang et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
049   2024a; Gu et al., 2024). This is concerning since prompt injection allows attackers to fully control
050   a compromised system—accessing sensitive data, spreading propaganda, disrupting operations, or
051   tricking users into clicking malicious URLs (Greshake et al., 2023). We attribute this research gap to
052   the complexity of MAS, where not all agents are exposed to external inputs. While compromising a
053   single agent through traditional prompt injection is straightforward, extending the breach to shielded
      agents within the system remains less clear.

054
      In this paper, we bridge the gap between prompt injection in single-agent systems and MAS. We in-
055   troduce Prompt Infection, a novel attack that enables LLM-to-LLM prompt injection. In this attack,
056   a compromised agent spreads the infection to other agents, coordinating them to exchange data and
057   issue instructions to agents equipped with specific tools. This coordination results in widespread
058   system compromise through self-replication, demonstrating how a single vulnerability can quickly
059   escalate into a systemic threat.
060
      Through extensive empirical studies, we show that multi-agent systems are highly susceptible to a
061   range of security threats. For instance, in sophisticated data theft attacks, agents can collaborate to
062   retrieve sensitive information and pass it to agents with code execution capabilities, which can then
063   send the data to a malicious external endpoint. We also demonstrate that prompt infections spread
064   in a logistic growth pattern in social simulations. Lastly, we find that more powerful models, such
065   as GPT-4o, are not inherently safer than weaker models like GPT-3.5 Turbo. In fact, more pow-
066   erful models, when compromised, are more effective at executing the attack due to their enhanced
067   capabilities.
068   To address this, we explore a simple defense mechanism called LLM Tagging. This technique ap-
069   pends a marker to agent responses, helping downstream agents differentiate between user inputs and
070   agent-generated outputs, reducing the risk of infection spreading. Our experiments show that nei-
071   ther LLM Tagging nor traditional defense mechanisms alone are sufficient to prevent LLM-to-LLM
072   prompt injection. However, when combined, they provide robust protection and effectively mitigate
073   the threat.
074   These findings challenge the assumption that MAS are inherently safer due to their distributed ar-
075   chitecture. The threat arises not only from external content but also within the system, as agents can
076   attack and compromise one another. We hope our work offers valuable insights for developing more
077   secure and responsible multi-agent systems.

082   2   R ELATED W ORKS
083
084
085   Prompt Injection. Instruction-tuned LLMs have demonstrated exceptional ability in understand-
086   ing and executing complex user instructions, enabling them to meet a wide range of dynamic and
087   diverse needs (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, this adaptability introduces
088   new vulnerabilities: Perez & Ribeiro (2022) revealed that models like GPT-3 are prone to prompt
089   injection attacks, where malicious prompts can subvert the model’s intended purpose or expose
090   confidential information. Subsequent work expanded prompt injection to real-world LLM applica-
091   tions (Liu et al., 2024b;c) and LLM-controlled robotics (Zhang et al., 2024c). Liu et al. (2024a)
092   introduced an automated gradient-based method for generating effective prompt injection. Indirect
093
      prompt injection, where attackers use external inputs like emails or documents, poses further risks
      such as data theft and denial-of-service (Greshake et al., 2023). Cohen et al. (2024) introduced an
094
      AI worm that compromises a user’s single-agent LLM and spreads malicious prompts to other users
095
      (e.g., via email). Recent advancements in multimodal models have also led to image-based prompt
096   injection attacks (Sharma et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024). Defenses include finetuning methods like
097   StruQ (Chen et al., 2024) and Signed Prompt (Suo, 2024), which are limited to open-source models.
098   Prompt-based approaches like Spotlighting (Hines et al., 2024) are applicable to black-box models.
099
      Safety in Multi-Agent Systems. As LLM-based MAS become more prominent, understanding their
100
      security is increasingly critical. Recent work, such as Evil Geniuses (Tian et al., 2024), introduces
101
      an automated framework to assess MAS robustness. Other studies explore how injecting false infor-
102   mation or errors can compromise MAS performance (Ju et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Attacks
103   designed to elicit malicious behaviors from agents are examined in PsySafe (Zhang et al., 2024d).
104   Our work is closely related to recent efforts investigating prompt injection attacks in MAS (Zhang
105   et al., 2024a; Gu et al., 2024). However, Zhang et al. (2024a) lacks the self-replication feature
106   needed for scalable attacks, focusing instead on availability attacks that cause repetitive or irrelevant
107   actions in two agents. Similarly, Gu et al. (2024) targets multimodal models with image-retrieving
      tools but is limited to adversarial image inputs and does not incorporate self-replication.

      In this section, we introduce Prompt Infection, a self-replicating attack that propagates across agents
111   in a multi-agent system once breached. A malicious actor injects a single infectious prompt into
112   external content, such as a PDF, email, or web page, and sends it to the target. When an agent
113   processes the infected content, the prompt replicates throughout the system, compromising other
114   agents.

155   reducing the system to a repetitive cycle dominated by the infection.

222   Application Structure. We simulate the compromise of a multi-agent application equipped with
223
      various tool capabilities, such as processing external documents (email, web, PDF), writing code,
      and accessing databases via CSV. The first agent is tool-specific (e.g., document reader), while sub-
224
      sequent agents—strategist, summarizer, editor, and writer—refine outputs. We explore two com-
225
      munication methods: global messaging, where agents share complete message histories, and local
226   messaging, where agents access only partial histories from predecessors. Local messaging reduces
227   computational overhead and minimizes information overload (Qian et al., 2024) and makes it harder
228   for Prompt Infection to propagate due to limited communication. The simulation is performed using
229   OpenAI’s GPT-4o and GPT-3.5 Turbo models.
230
      Dataset. We create a dataset of 120 user instructions across three tool types (email, PDF, web),
231
      paired with synthetic PDFs and emails embedded with malicious prompts. For web scenarios, agents
232   are allowed to visit relevant URLs, but the malicious prompt is injected into the retrieved web
233   documents within our simulation, not into the actual websites. This results in 360 unique pairs of
234   user instructions and attack phrases for scams, content manipulation, and malware threats. For data
235   theft, we generate synthetic user data (e.g., names, occupations, email addresses, phone numbers)
236   stored in a CSV file.
237
      Evaluation. For data theft, at least three agents with distinct roles (PDF/Email/Web Reader, CSV
238   Reader, Coder) must be compromised. A successful infection occurs when the first agent is com-
239   promised, the CSV reader retrieves sensitive data, and the coder writes a POST request to exfiltrate
240   the data. For scams, content manipulation, or malware, the system is compromised if the final agent
241   produces malicious output while concealing the infection prompt.
242
      Baseline. To evaluate the impact of self-replication in Prompt Infection, we establish a Non-
243
      Replicating Prompt Infection baseline. In this setup, the infection lacks self-replication: a malicious
244   prompt embedded in the external content instructs the agent to ”say ’perform A’.” This results in the
245   second agent receiving the instruction ”perform A,” allowing us to directly compare the effectiveness
246   of self-replication in spreading the infection across agents.

250   4.2   S OCIETY OF AGENTS
251
252   Society Structure. Recently, there has been a surge in using LLM agents for social simulations and
253   as non-player characters (NPCs) in games (Park et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Hua et al., 2024). To
254   assess the impact of Prompt Infection in a society of agents (Weiss, 1999), we simulate a simple
255   LLM town where agents engage in random pairwise dialogues. Population sizes of 10, 20, 30, 40,
256   and 50 agents are tested to evaluate how infections might propagate in differently sized communities.
257   Each turn consists of four dialogue exchanges between paired agents, mimicking interactions found
258   in social or game environments.
259   Infection Simulation. Since actors in social simulations or games are typically not designed to
260   carry out explicit user requests, we simulate direct prompt injection (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022) by
261   overriding the original system instructions governing the LLM agents. The simulation begins with
262   one compromised citizen, assuming infection by a player or external actor, after which the infection
263   spreads through dialogues between agents.
264   Memory Retrieval. For memory retrieval, we adopt the system from Park et al. (2023), where
265   top K = 3 memories are selected based on importance, relevancy, and recency scores. Recency is
266   determined using an exponential decay function over the number of turns since the memory’s last
267   retrieval. Importance is rated by the LLM on a scale of 1 to 10, and relevancy is calculated using
268   OpenAI’s embedding API and maximum inner product search. GPT-4o serves as the LLM for these
269   agents. Importantly, memory is not explicitly shared across agents, requiring infection prompts to
      spread iteratively from agent to agent.

360   RQ3. How Do Infection Prompts Propagate in Open, Non-Linear Agent Interactions?
361   Unlike the Section 5.1, where agent relationships are predetermined in a linear fashion, here we
362   explore a more dynamic environment where agent connections evolve unpredictably. This setup
363   allows us to study how an infection prompt spreads naturally through a decentralized network of
364   agents. At the outset, only one agent carries the infection, and the prompt propagates based on the
365   evolving interactions between agents.
366   As shown in Figure 6a, in smaller populations (10 and 20 agents), full infection is achieved by
367   turn 4.7 and turn 6.3, corresponding to approximately 47% and 31.5% of the total number of agents,
368   respectively. In larger populations—30, 40, and 50 agents—the infection spread takes proportionally
369   less time, with full infection occurring at around 23.3% (for 30 agents), 24.2% (for 40 agents), and
370   21.4% (for 50 agents) of the total turns. This suggests that, in larger populations, the infection spread
371   tends to become more efficient relative to the population size.
372   Initially, the spread follows an exponential-like trend, but as the infection reaches saturation, the
373   rate slows down, transitioning to a logistic growth pattern. This non-linear dynamic indicates
374   that larger populations experience a more gradual but extended infection phase, with a relatively
375   higher per-agent infection rate compared to smaller populations. Figure 6a supports this trend by
376   illustrating that as the number of agents increases, the infection not only spreads faster but

472   represents the average attack success rate for a specific defense type, with green bars showing rates
473   without LLM Tagging and purple bars showing rates with LLM Tagging.
474
475
476   However, none of the tested defense strategies, including LLM Tagging, prove particularly effective
477   when used in isolation. LLM Tagging alone reduces the attack success rate by only 5%, which is
478   understandable, as traditional prompt injections can still occur even when the LLM is informed of
479   the source of external inputs (e.g., ”The following is the latest email:”).
480   As shown in Figure 7, the Marking strategy is the most promising but still permits 76% of attacks.
481   Although its initial success rate was 0%, we devised a counterattack that neutralized the marking
482   symbol (ˆ) by interleaving each word of the infection prompt with underbars ( ). Other techniques,
483   such as delimiting data and sandwiching, allow nearly all attacks, indicating limited effectiveness in
484   preventing LLM-to-LLM infections. These findings suggest that pairing LLM Tagging with other
485   defense techniques, such as marking or instruction defense, is crucial for mitigating prompt
      infections.

      7   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488   Our experiments focused on the GPT family, leaving other LLMs like Claude, Llama, and Gem-
489   ini underexplored, though prior research suggests our findings may generalize (Zou et al., 2023).
490   Preliminary tests on Claude showed similar vulnerabilities, but full results were unavailable due to
491   computational costs. We primarily examined basic multi-agent architectures, but we believe Prompt
492   Infection likely applies to more complex systems, as self-replication allows the infection to spread
493   wherever communication between agents exists. For LLM Tagging, we used handcrafted attacks,
494   but recent studies (Liu et al., 2024a; Mehrotra et al., 2024) show that algorithmically generated
495   prompts can bypass such defenses, indicating a need for stronger countermeasures. In multi-agent
496
      systems, attack prompts are often exposed, offering detection opportunities but highlighting the need
      for stealthier methods to evade manual review.

499   8   C ONCLUSION
500
501   We presented Prompt Infection, a novel prompt injection attack that exploits self-replication to prop-
502   agate across LLM-based multi-agent systems, leading to data theft, malicious actions, and system
503   disruption. Our experiments demonstrated that self-replicating infections consistently outperformed
504
      non-replicating attacks across most scenarios. Additionally, more advanced models, such as GPT-
      4o, pose greater risks when compromised, executing malicious prompts more efficiently than GPT-
505
      3.5. We found that social simulations and games are also vulnerable to Prompt Infection, especially
506
      when memory retrieval systems are left unsecured. To mitigate this, we proposed LLM Tagging as a
507   defense, which, when combined with techniques like marking and instruction defense, significantly
508   reduced infection success rates. Ultimately, our findings reveal that threats can arise not only from
509   external sources but also internally, as agents within the system can exploit one another, emphasizing
510   the need for robust multi-agent defense strategies.

      E THICAL S TATEMENT
513
514
      While prompt injection attacks have been known for years (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022), our work
515   demonstrates that they remain a significant threat, particularly in the context of multi-agent systems.
516   By publicly disclosing the vulnerabilities and attacks explored in this paper, our goal is to encourage
517   immediate and rigorous defense research, while promoting transparency regarding the security risks
518   associated with LLM systems. To mitigate potential harm, we ensured that no prompts were in-
519   jected into publicly accessible systems, thereby preventing unintended use by others. Additionally,
520   we strongly emphasize that the disclosed attack techniques and prompts should never be used
521   maliciously or against real-world applications without proper authorization.
