               Accurately assessing internal human states is critical for understanding their pref-
012
               erences, providing personalized services, and identifying challenges in various
013            real-world applications. Originating from psychology, adaptive testing has be-
014            come the mainstream method for human measurement. It customizes assessments
015            by selecting the fewest necessary test questions (e.g., math problems) based on
016            the examinee’s performance (e.g., answer correctness), ensuring precise evalua-
017            tion. However, current adaptive testing methods still face several challenges. The
018            mechanized nature of most adaptive algorithms often leads to guessing behavior
019            and difficulties in addressing open-ended questions. Additionally, subjective as-
020            sessments suffer from noisy response data and coarse-grained test outputs, further
021
               limiting their effectiveness. To move closer to an ideal adaptive testing process, we
               propose TestAgent, a large language model (LLM)-empowered adaptive testing
022
               agent designed to enhance adaptive testing through interactive engagement. This
023
               marks the first application of LLMs in adaptive testing. To ensure effective assess-
024            ments, TestAgent supports personalized question selection, captures examinees’
025            response behavior and anomalies, and provides precise testing outcomes through
026            dynamic, conversational interactions. Extensive experiments on psychological,
027            educational, and lifestyle assessments demonstrate that our approach achieves
028            more accurate human assessments with approximately 20% fewer test questions
029            compared to state-of-the-art baselines. In actual tests, it received testers’ favor in
030            terms of speed, smoothness, and other two dimensions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056   Designing effective assessments to evaluate specific human states is essential in various contexts,
057   such as analyzing personality traits, diagnosing mental health issues, and measuring learning abil-
058   ities (Kaufman et al., 2022; Laher et al., 2022). Traditional assessments often rely on paper-and-
059   pencil formats, wherein questionnaires or selected questions are presented to participants. Based on
060   participants’ answer performance (e.g., answer correctness on math questions), experts can evaluate
061   their states (e.g., mathematic ability). While these straightforward testing methods are functional,
062   they are labor-intensive and require expert involvement. Moreover, the uniform testing environment
063   can hinder personalization, complicating the tailoring of assessments to individual needs. There-
064
      fore, recent efforts focus on another testing form called computer-based Adaptive Testing (Liu et al.,
      2024), which aims to customizes assessments for each examinee by dynamically adjusting questions
065
      based on their performance. Adaptive testing allows for accurate and personalized evaluations with
066
      fewer questions, leading to widespread use in standardized testing, such as Graduate Management
067   Admission Test (GMAT) and Graduate Record Examinations (GRE) (Mills & Steffen, 2000).
068
069   However, existing adaptive testing methods still face significant challenges, primarily due to three
070
      factors: (1) Mechanized Testing Process. Most adaptive algorithms are limited to fixed-answer
      questions, like multiple-choice formats. This rigidity can lead to guessing on unfamiliar questions
071
      (Brown, 2022), compromising assessment accuracy. Additionally, adaptive methods struggle with
072
      open-ended scenarios that involve varied answer formats, such as mathematical problem-solving.
073   (2) Noisy Answer Data. In subjective assessments, answers may not reflect true internal states,
074   introducing noise into the training of adaptive testing algorithms. For example, in personality tests,
075   respondents may provide socially desirable answers rather than genuine feelings, leading to unre-
076   liable results (Stein & Swan, 2019). Similarly, in mental health evaluations, social pressures may
077   cause individuals to conceal or misreport symptoms, skewing outcomes (McDonald, 2008). (3)
078   Coarse-Grained Test Output. The adaptive question selection method provides a diagnosis value
079   at the end of the test. It is difficult for the test-taker to make self-adjustments based on the diag-
080   nosis value. While traditional methods attempt to mitigate noise through expert-driven multi-step
081
      questions, the time and labor involved make large-scale implementation challenging (Josephson &
      Shapiro, 2013; Segal et al., 2019).
082
083   Recently, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in human-like
084   tasks, including reasoning, planning, and decision-making (Lee et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). This
085   observation suggests the potential of LLM-driven agents to simulate human social behaviors across
086   various contexts. Inspired by this potential, we propose the development of an LLM-based adaptive
087
      testing agent to overcome current testing limitations. Imagine that an intelligent agent, similar to
      a human expert, that can engage in interactive dialogues with examinees, analyze their responses,
088
      and dynamically generate personalized questions. Such an agent could transcend the mechanical
089
      constraints and noise-related issues inherent in traditional assessments.
090
091   Motivated by these considerations, we introduce TestAgent, an LLM-based agent designed to en-
092   hance adaptive testing through interactive engagement. This represents the first application of LLMs
093
      in adaptive testing. To ensure effective testing, TestAgent is designed to support personalized ques-
      tion selection, capture the examinee’s response behavior and anomalies, and deliver precise test-
094
      ing outcomes. Specifically, TestAgent inherits the dynamic question selection capabilities of tradi-
095
      tional adaptive testing, catering to personalized needs while improving testing efficiency. Addition-
096   ally, an autonomous feedback mechanism and anomaly management module have been introduced
097   to ensure a smoother and more intelligent testing process. TestAgent also generates detailed diag-
098   nosis reports to provide test-takers with a deeper understanding of their results, thereby making the
099   testing experience more personalized and transparent, while significantly reducing resource costs.
100   We conducted extensive experiments using datasets from three distinct domains, including personal-
101   ity measurement, educational math exam, and mental health test. The quantitative prediction results
102   and qualitative analysis indicate that TestAgent’s testing efficiency and methodology surpass tradi-
103   tional testing methods. Moreover, during actual tests, TestAgent was favored by testers for its speed,
104
      smoothness, and two other dimensions.

      2.1    P ROBLEM D EFINITION
130
      The goal of adaptive testing is to provide the test taker with tailored questions. It aims to do this
131   in the fewest number of test rounds. It consists of two key components, the Adaptive Question
132   Selection and Cognitive Diagnosis. After the test taker answers a question, Cognitive Diagnosis
133   update their ability estimate based on the feedback of the question, and then further questions are
134   selected based on the Adaptive Question Selection algorithm. The specific definition is as follows:
135
      Definition 1 (Definition of Adaptive Testing). During the t-th step of testing, the test taker’s re-
136
      sponse to question q is y. The previous sequence of test question-answer pairs is denoted as
137   S = {(q1 , y1 ), . . . , (qt , yt )}. At this point, the cognitive Diagnosis model updates the ability values
138   based on S using cross-entropy loss. The question selection algorithm π selects the best question
139   based on the current θt for the test-taker to answer, i.e., qt+1 ∼ π(θt ). This process continues
140   iteratively until it stops after T steps. The cognitive Diagnosis returns θT as the test result.
141
      There are several issues with traditional adaptive processes. First, label y may not align with the true
142
      ability. In many cases, such as in math ability tests, the test-taker might randomly guess the correct
143   answer which will significantly affect test accuracy. Second, test-takers may withhold information
144   known about question q due to various reasons, leading to inaccurate test results. Third, the cognitive
145   diagnosis model outputs θT as the test result. However, this may not be intuitive for the test-taker.
146   Test-takers tend to prefer receiving a diagnosis report that includes various analyses rather than a
147   simple estimate of their abilities. These three issues will be addressed in our framework.
148
149   2.2    OVERVIEW
150
151   Similar to the process of adaptive testing, our framework also follows an iterative approach. The
152   Figure 2 shows the pipeline of the entire working process of TestAgent. First, the Question Bank
153
      needs to be established. To do this, Domain Verification is required to determine the dimensions
      of the test and then followed by Data Integration. Cognitive Diagnosis Training will then complete
154
      the establishment of the Question Bank for use by TestAgent. Unlike traditional adaptive tests, our
155
      TestAgent transforms the entire testing process into a natural language conversation to break the
156   Mechanized Testing Process at each step. As shown in Figure 1, instead of having the test-taker
157   directly choose the answer y for the question q, the TestAgent presents the question q in the form
158   of a natural language query posed by a character C. This is exactly what the Question Generation
159   module does. Then the test-taker receives the transformed question b = C(q) and responds with
160   a conversation. Next, the TestAgent obtains y from the conversation after passing through the Au-
161   tonomous Feedback Mechanism and the Anomaly Management modules to address the issue of
      Noisy Answer Data. These two modules are aimed at obtaining more effective and stable labels.

162
      Specifically, Autonomous Feedback Mechanism judges whether the label y obtained by the agent
163   is consistent with the response of the test-taker. If they are not consistent, the system automatically
164   generates a similar question bnew for the test-taker to answer, continuing this process until they are
165   consistent. Anomaly Management is used to handle situations where the answer y to a question
166   exhibits anomalous behavior, such as when a tester tries to guess the answer or avoids responding to
167   the question. If an anomaly occurs, it will use natural language guidance to progressively ask ques-
168   tions, reducing the likelihood of receiving misleading answers. After obtaining the accurate label y,
169   the Cognitive Diagnosis module updates the test-taker’s ability. Then Adaptive Question Selection
170   module choose the most suitable question from the Question Bank. This process forms an iterative
171
      cycle.
172   To address the issue of Coarse-Grained Test Output, TestAgent utilizes Neural Architecture to pro-
173   vide initial analysis based on θT and the Response Sequence. This analysis is combined with Expert
174   Analysis to ultimately form the Diagnosis Report. This report includes test results and suggestions
175   for the test-taker. The implementation of these methods will be detailed in the following sections.

177   2.3   AUTONOMOUS F EEDBACK M ECHANISM
178
179   During the conversational test, TestAgent analyzes the label y based on the response from the test-
180   taker. For some questions testing, TestAgent only needs to analyze whether the test-taker answered
181   correctly like mathematical ability test. However, in more general tests like personality tests, TestA-
182   gent needs to analyze personality trait labels from a segment of daily dialogue of the test-takers. In
183
      such cases, it is highly likely that situations arise where the label cannot be analyzed. For example,
      if a test-taker responds with “I don’t know what to do”, it clearly deviates from providing an answer
184
      and cannot be analyzed for a label. Therefore, we propose the Autonomous Feedback Mechanism
185
      to address this issue.
186
187   When the test-taker provides a response, the Autonomous Feedback Mechanism assesses from three
188   perspectives: domain relevance, response alignment, and logical coherence to determine the out-
189
      come. From a domain relevance perspective, TestAgent leverage the intelligence of the Autonomous
      Feedback Mechanism associates questions with answers. If the response significantly deviates from
190
      the expected answer to the original question q, it is deemed unsuccessful. In terms of response
191
      alignment, responses are categorized into M types representing the degree of alignment with the
192   question. The value of M is dependent on the specific test. For example, in a mathematical abil-
193   ity test, responses are either right or wrong. At this point, M = 2. However, for more complex
194   tests like personality assessments, ranging from “complete disagreement” to “complete agreement”
195   across seven dimensions, M = 7 making response analysis challenging. When response alignment
196   is ambiguous, it is considered unsuccessful.
197
      Regarding logical coherence, the Autonomous Feedback Mechanism evaluates whether the test-
198   taker’s response demonstrates internal logical consistency. Even if the response is related to the
199   question, if it lacks coherence, contains contradictions, or is illogical, it is deemed unsuccessful.
200   Logical coherence ensures that responses are not only superficially related to the question but also
201   logically sound. If all three aspects are successful in their assessments, the label is returned; oth-
202   erwise, based on the intelligence of the Autonomous Feedback Mechanism, a similar question is
203   generated based on the properties of the question. This process continues until a label is determined.
204
205   2.4   A NOMALY M ANAGEMENT
206
207   During specific tests, test-takers may guess the correct answer by chance, intentionally provide in-
208   correct answers, or exhibit overconfidence in their responses, which can distort the assessment. Such
209   anomalies can result in incorrect label y. These situations commonly occur in practice. The three
210   most common types of anomalies in psychology are: Guessing Anomaly, Misleading Anomaly, and
211   Overconfidence Anomaly. We are exploring these three types of anomalies.
212   Guessing Anomaly Test-takers may answer based on luck or incomplete understanding of the ques-
213   tion, which does not accurately reflect their true abilities. In the case of Misleading Anomaly, test-
214   takers deliberately provide incorrect answers, possibly due to lack of interest in the test or psycho-
215   logical resistance to the question. Detecting this anomaly is challenging. Overconfidence Anomaly
      occurs when test-takers demonstrate excessive confidence in their answers, even when uncertain.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      While we cannot eliminate the subjective factors of test-takers entirely, we can strive to avoid these
217   three types of anomalies to increase the accuracy of the tests. Anomaly Management, in conjunction
218   with cognitive diagnosis, analyzes these anomalies. To assess the impact of anomalies on test re-
219   sults, Anomaly Management utilizes the cognitive diagnosis capability θ to assist in judgment. For
220   a question q where TestAgent receives feedback label y, cognitive diagnosis estimate the probability
221   P (q, y) of the test-taker answering question q with label y based on the current capability θ. If the
222   current capability value makes it difficult to answer with label y, Autonomous Feedback Mechanism
223   is employed for judgment.
224   Misleading Anomaly Anomaly Management conducts reasoning based on context. By tracking the
225   context of the test, inconsistencies or contradictions in a test-taker’s responses to multiple questions
226   on the same topic can be identified. For instance, if a test-taker provides a correct definition of a
227   concept in one question but contradicts it in subsequent questions, it may be intentionally mislead-
228   ing. Anomaly Management then dissects the question to engage in more detailed dialogue with the
229   test-taker to avoid such issues.
230   Overconfidence Anomaly Anomaly Management not only accepts the test-taker’s response but also
231   requests reasons or explanations for the answers. If a test-taker displays high confidence in their re-
232   sponse but lacks sufficient reasoning or logic when explaining their choice, the model can determine
233   their confidence is unfounded. Anomaly Management and Autonomous Feedback Mechanism com-
234   plement each other, working in conjunction. Successful anomaly detection often requires a new
235   round of questions to verify anomalies, increasing the precision of the tests.

240

241

242

      3.2   ACCURACY T EST

      Data Partition and Evaluation Methods To validate the efficiency of the adaptive testing method
285   in selecting questions, a common practice involves randomly dividing each student’s data into a
286   query set Du and a support set Dt (Ghosh & Lan, 2021). The support set Dt is used to simulate the
287   question selection process and estimate the final ability value θt , while the query set Du is used to
288   assess the accuracy of these estimates.
289
      We performed 5-fold cross-validation on all datasets. For each fold, we allocated 60% of the students
290
      for training, 20% for validation, and 20% for testing. In each fold, we employed an early stopping
291   strategy using the validation set to train the parameters for each method. To mitigate overfitting, we
292   randomly shuffled these partitions at the beginning of each training epoch. The performance metrics
293   for evaluation included Accuracy (Gao et al., 2021) and the Area Under the Receiver Operating
294   Characteristic Curve (AUC) (Bradley, 1997).
295
      Compared Approaches We employed three baselines for comparison: Random: This method
296
      randomly selects questions and serves as a reference for improvement compared to several baselines.
297   FSI: (Lord, 2012): It utilizes maximum Fisher information to select questions. KLI: (Chang &
298   Ying, 1996) It utilize Kullback-Leibler information to select questions. MAAT: (Bi et al., 2020) It
299   employs an active learning (Krishnakumar, 2007) approach to measure question informativeness to
300   select questions. The cognitive diagnosis model here adopts IRT (Ackerman et al., 2003) model.
301
      Result Our TestAgent algorithm represents the new generation of adaptive testing, aimed at surpass-
302
      ing the limitations of traditional methods. In Table 1, we conducted a comprehensive comparison
303   of the TestAgent with other model testing approaches. We not only provided accuracy (ACC) and
304   area under the curve (AUC) metrics for test lengths of 5, 10, 20, and 50, but also used them as
305   benchmarks to assess the performance of various models.
306
      Our TestAgent framework demonstrates outstanding overall performance on these three datasets.
307
      Particularly noteworthy is the exceptional performance of the SCL dataset when utilizing the TestA-
308
      gent model, showcasing the remarkable capabilities of TestAgent in handling complex datasets.
309   Compared to traditional algorithms, our framework shows improvements in the majority of test
310   steps, with the most significant enhancement seen at test step 5. On average, we achieved a rela-
311   tive improvement of 1.77% in AUC@5 and a notable increase of 0.91% in ACC@5. These results
312   clearly demonstrate the highly accurate capability estimation provided by our framework.

      random guessing.

492   3.8    F URTHER S TUDY
493
494   Challenges In comparison to traditional testing, TestAgent is a conversational test based on a large
495   language model. However, responses from large language models can exhibit fluctuations and even
496
      errors. Therefore, we conducted robustness testing to record instances of fluctuations and errors. We
      randomly sampled 50 interaction records in cases of failure, manually annotated and categorized
497
      their failure modes. The results are shown in Table 3: Primarily, there were some hallucination
498
      issues in the responses and summaries of answers (34%). Secondly, we also found instances of false
499   negatives when using large model-based indicators, i.e., correct predictions that were misjudged
500   as incorrect, but the proportion was relatively small (12%). In some cases, there were additional
501   redundant conversational sentences generated in the summaries and responses to questions(26%).
502   Additionally, at times, the model deviated from the role of the testing expert as specified in the
503   prompts, assuming other identities for conversation, which is not in line with test guidelines(28%).
504   These issues will be gradually addressed in future work.

514   4     C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this paper, we proposed an innovative conversational testing method that combined Large Lan-
517   guage Models (LLMs) with Adaptive Testing technology, enhancing the flexibility and accuracy of
518
      traditional testing approaches. By introducing an LLM as a testing expert, we are able to dynam-
      ically adjust test content through multiple rounds of dialogues, thereby improving user experience
519
      and the precision of test results. Experimental results demonstrate that this method excels in as-
520
      sessments of psychology, abilities, and personality traits, effectively shortening testing time and
521   enhancing the interpretability of diagnosis reports. In the future, we will introduce multimodal sys-
522   tems that utilize speech, images, and other modalities to assist large language models in testing
523   can enhance the dimensions of testing. The TestAgent system, through its generated dialogues and
524   personalized question selection, not only boosts testing efficiency but also offers fresh insights and
525   directions for the future of psychological testing.

      A     R ELATED WORK
757
758   Adaptive Testing typically includes two modules: the cognitive diagnosis model and the question
759   selection algorithm. Below is an introduction to these two components.
760
761
      Adaptive Testing (1) Cognitive Diagnosis Models. It is built on the foundation of psychomet-
      ric theory, gaining popularity in assessments to provide more personalized feedback on students’
762
      latent abilities. It assumes that a test taker’s ability remains constant throughout the testing pro-
763
      cess (Chang, 2015), allowing estimation of ability based on prior responses to questions using
764   gradient-based optimization. The most classic form is the Item Response Theory (IRT) model
765   (Ackerman et al., 2003). The simplest one-parameter logistic (1PL) model is represented as:
766   p(correct response to question j) = sigmoid(θ − bj ), where bj ∈ R represents the characteris-
767   tics of each question, and θ ∈ R is the student’s ability to be estimated. Other representative models
768   include Matrix Factorization (MF) (Koren et al., 2009; Toscher & Jahrer, 2010), Deterministic In-
769   puts, Noisy-And gate (DINA) (De La Torre, 2009; Von Davier, 2014), and recently proposed Neural
770   Cognitive Diagnosis Models (Cheng et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a; Shen et al.,
771   2024) that leverage neural networks to model interactions between students and questions. In the
772
      case of specific CDM and response data, Maximum Likelihood Estimation (binary cross-entropy
      loss) is typically used to estimate θ for subsequent selection algorithm use.
773
774   (2) Selection Algorithms. The selection algorithm is a core component in achieving adaptivity in
775   adaptive testing, aiming to estimate student abilities accurately with the fewest testing steps re-
776   quired. Traditional algorithms are based on uncertainty or information metrics such as the well-
777   known Fisher information (FSI) (Lord, 2012)and other methods (Chang & Ying, 1996; Rudner,
778
      2002; van der Linden, 1998; Veerkamp & Berger, 1997; Kang et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2023). In
      recent years, some data-driven methods have been proposed (Nurakhmetov, 2019; Zhuang et al.,
779
      2022; Ghosh & Lan, 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023)while some heuristic
780
      methods have also been proposed (Veldkamp & Verschoor, 2019; Gilavert & Freire, 2022; Feng
781   et al., 2023; Mujtaba & Mahapatra, 2021; Yu et al., 2024) . However, most of these approaches are
782   based on traditional paper tests, which lack the advantages of conducting assessments through a test
783   booklet and may not achieve comprehensive testing.
784
      Large Language Model and AI Agents In recent years, there have been many breakthroughs in
785
      various directions involving large language models. The emergence of agents based on large lan-
786
      guage models has garnered increasing attention from researchers as a burgeoning field. Numerous
787   applications have been developed in specific domains and tasks, showcasing the powerful and ver-
788   satile capabilities of these agents (Yao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024; Chan et al.,
789   2023). Through domain fine-tuning, external knowledge bases, and more, a personal agent capable
790   of assisting users in daily tasks can be created. With the enhancement of agent capabilities, human
791   involvement becomes increasingly important to effectively guide and oversee the agents’ actions,
792   ensuring they align with human needs and objectives. Human-agent interaction agents can serve as
793   guides for humans and have been applied in education (Kalvakurthi et al., 2023; Swan et al., 2023),
794   health (Ali et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024), and other fields (Gao et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2022),
795
      demonstrating the diverse capabilities of large language models. Large language models can also
      be used in a manner that establishes an equal partnership with humans, such as being empathetic
796
      communicators (Hasan et al., 2023; Liu-Thompkins et al., 2022) or functioning as human-level par-
797
      ticipants (Bakhtin et al., 2023; , FAIR).The measurement agent proposed in this paper is a universal
798   measurement agent. By utilizing the corresponding dataset, one can obtain the corresponding agent
799   using the method proposed in this paper, enhancing the effectiveness of human measurements across
800   various domains and offering a novel measurement approach based on natural language dialogue in
801   the testing field.

818   B.2   F INE - TUNE D ETAILS
819
820   In this study, the fine-tuning process is based on the pre-trained ChatGLM model, aiming to cus-
821   tomize the model for the specific personality diagnostic task to improve its performance in handling
822   MBTI personality analysis tasks. To achieve this, we perform fine-tuning using LoRA (Low-Rank
823   Adaptation) technology through the torchkeras framework.
824   Data Processing: The fine-tuning data is divided into three parts: instructions, character labels,
825   and expert reports. The instruction is a simple prompt, formatted as follows: ”Based on personality
826   test classification and relevant dialogues, analyze the character traits and provide the corresponding
827   diagnostic report.”
828   Character labels include the labels obtained through the ability classifier training mentioned earlier.
829
830   Expert reports are the personality diagnostic reports provided by the official MBTI website for the
831
      16 personality types.
832   Each piece of fine-tuning data consists of an input formed by combining the instruction and the
833   character label, and the output is the diagnostic report suggestion, which corresponds to the expert’s
834   diagnostic report. Thus, the construction of fine-tuning data is completed.
835   Parameter Settings: In this work, several hyperparameters are carefully chosen for fine-tuning the
836   model. The maximum sequence length is set to 1024 tokens, ensuring that input sequences longer
837   than this are truncated.
838
      For the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) method, three key parameters are used: the rank r is set to
839
      8, which controls the size of the low-rank matrices; the scaling factor α is set to 32, which adjusts
840
      the influence of the low-rank adaptation; and the dropout rate p is set to 0.1, which applies a 10%
841   dropout during training to help with regularization.
842
843   Training-related hyperparameters include a batch size of 8, a learning rate of 2 × 10−6 , and a total
844
      of 10 training epochs. Additionally, early stopping is applied with a patience of 2 epochs, meaning
      that training stops if the validation loss does not improve over 3 consecutive epochs.
845
846   Finally, mixed precision training is employed with a setting of ’fp16’ to improve computational
847   efficiency, and when saving the model, the maximum shard size is set to 1GB, ensuring that the
848   model is saved in manageable chunks for later use.
