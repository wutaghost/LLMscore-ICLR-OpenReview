009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We examine Transformer-based in-context learning for contextual data of the form
012            {(xi , yi )}i=1,N and query xN +1 , where xi ∈ Rd and yi ∼ p(Y |f (xi )), with
013            f (x) a latent function. This is analyzed from the perspective of functional gra-
014            dient descent for f (x). We initially perform this analysis from the perspective
015            of a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), from which an alternative kernel-
016            averaging perspective is manifested. This leads to a generalization, allowing an
017
               interpretation of softmax attention from the perspective of the Nadaraya-Watson
               kernel-weighted average. We show that a single attention layer may be designed
018
               to exactly implement a functional-gradient step in this setting (for RKHS latent
019
               functions), extending prior work for the special case of real-valued Y and Gaus-
020            sian p(Y |f (x)). This is also generalized for softmax attention and non-RKHS
021            underlying f (x). Though our results hold in a general setting, we focus on cate-
022            gorical Y with p(Y |f (x)) modeled as a generalized linear model (corresponding
023            specifically to softmax probability). Multi-layered extensions are developed for
024            this case, and through extensive experimentation we demonstrate that for categor-
025            ical Y a single-layer model is often highly effective for in-context learning. We
026            also demonstrate these ideas for real-world data, considering in-context classifica-
027            tion of ImageNet data, showing the broad applicability of our theory beyond the
028
               commonly-studied settings of synthetic regression data.

032
      There has been significant interest recently in understanding the few-shot-learning capabilities of
033   Transformers (von Oswald et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2023; Akyurek et al., 2022;
034   Garg et al., 2022; Mahankali et al., 2023; Schlag et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). This has been
035   in part motivated by the impressive few-shot-learning capabilities of large language models (LLMs)
036   (Brown et al., 2020). Much of that prior work has focused on the self-attention component in Trans-
037   formers and its application to modeling real-valued functional outputs conditioned on covariates
038   (Ahn et al., 2024; von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023; Schlag et al., 2021), rather than com-
039   plete Transformer layers (self-attention followed by a feedforward layer with skip connection). Fur-
040   ther, there has been little consideration of categorical observations, which are relevant for token
041
      prediction in language modeling.
042   Few-shot learning is here understood as contextual learning with data {C (l) }l=1,L , where C (l) =

057   The Transformer has been recognized as a few-shot learner of the latter type, i.e., a meta learner.

058   Specifically, the Transformer learns to infer a latent function fi (x) linked specifically to the real-

060   2023; Ahn et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). It is usually assumed within the Transformer design
061   that the latent function f (l) (x) resides in a particular functional class. For example, f (l) (x) has been
062   assumed to be a linear model (von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023; Mahankali et al., 2023), or
063   more generally, it may reside in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) (Cheng et al., 2024).
064   Existing work studying the Transformer as an in-context learner has focused on several comple-
065   mentary directions. For instance, Garg et al. (2022) examined the functional classes admitted by
066   Transformers, Muller et al. (2022) took a Bayesian analysis perspective, and others considered
067   Transformers as functional few-shot learners (von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023; Cheng
068   et al., 2024). However, these studies have simplified the assumptions about the functional class
069   and model architectures in favor of stronger theoretical foundations. Prominent examples include
070   reducing the Transformer to linear attention layers (Schlag et al., 2021; von Oswald et al., 2023;
071
      Ahn et al., 2023; 2024), thus limiting the functional class to linear models (Zhang et al., 2023; von
      Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023). More recently, these ideas have been leveraged to expand
072
      the functional class to smooth functions in an RKHS with associated kernel attention (Cheng et al.,
073
      2024). These studies have provided important insights, but they have limitations in the context of
074   some Transformer properties and applications. In this work we seek to build upon the above impor-
075   tant contributions.
076
077   • We introduce an analytical framework for in-context learning that is applicable to settings for
078     which the observations may be modeled as draws from an underlying model p(Y |f (l) (x)), where
079     Y may take many forms. We connect the case of real Y and Gaussian p(Y |f (l) (x)) to prior
080     work (Schlag et al., 2021; von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023; 2024), while focusing on
081     categorical Y and softmax p(Y |f (l) (x)).
082   • We show that the Transformer’s forward pass can be interpreted as taking steps of kernel averaged
083     functional gradient descent (Lemma 1 and Section 3). This crucially allows us to analyze the
084     softmax attention mechanism (Proposition 1).
085   • We show that one attention layer may be designed to exactly perform a step along the steepest-
086     descent direction connected to the functional gradient for categorical Y and softmax model
087     p(Y |f (x)) (Sections 3 and 4). This extends prior work that considered real Y and Gaussian
088     p(Y |f (x)) (Mahankali et al., 2023).
089   • We analyze function-space gradient descent dynamics when the gradient-operator is nonlinear,
090     as it is for categorical and count Y (and likely other cases). This highlights a potential role for a
091     feedforward (FF) element with skip connections (Section 4).
092
      • We examine our theoretical results in a series of experiments on both synthetic and real-world
093     data (Section 5). Notably, we present a Transformer-based in-context classifier for the ImageNet
094     dataset to demonstrate its efficacy and scalability.

      The above analysis demonstrates the effectiveness of Transformers for performing in-context learn-
515
      ing for data Y drawn from a model of the form p(Y |f (x)) for context-dependent and generally
516
      nonlinear f (x). The analysis was motivated by gaining understanding of Transformer mechanisms,
517   but we have also found that the simplified representations uncovered may serve as good initializa-
518   tions of parameters for less constrained Transformer representations. There are several issues that
519   deserve further study. For inference with categorical Y , we observed that a single-layer model is
520   often sufficient. For multiple layers, to model the nonlinear E(wc )|fi , we considered attention layers
521   alone (linear approximation to expectation), and Transformer blocks composed of an attention layer
522   followed by feedforward elements; the latter affording significant modeling flexibility. Nevertheless,
523   we found a single-layer model sufficient, providing possible explanations.
524   We also discussed the difficulty of learning the Trained TF Transformer parameters from a random
525   initialization, consistent with the analysis in Liu et al. (2023). Given the difficulty of training Trans-
526   formers, one may wonder if the lack of observed improvement in performance with more layers may
527   be caused by training difficulties. While this must be explored further, we note that the GD-based
528   model, with far fewer parameters to learn than Trained TF, also did not manifest significant improve-
529   ment with more layers (even when we invoked the modeling sophistication of the FF element).
530   The fact that the FF element did not yield significant improvements here should not be taken to imply
531   that it is unimportant in other applications. An aspect of the in-context learning considered here is
532   the presence of covariates xi (like done previously for real Y (von Oswald et al., 2023; Cheng et al.,
533   2024; Ahn et al., 2023)). While we encoded our input-data categories with embedding vectors (like
534   in language models), in language models there are no covariates xi (Vaswani et al., 2017; Brown
535   et al., 2020). One conjecture is that language models may predict the next token using inference
536   like that considered here (with that inference performed near the output layers of a large language
537   model). However, if this conjecture is true, it is possible that many of the other (numerous) layers of
538
      the Transformer nearer to the input layer may be performing context-dependent feature generation
      (analogous to inferring features that play a role like xi ). Much more work is needed to explore this
539
      and other issues uncovered in this research.

      Assume that F corresponds to a reproducing kernel space (RKHS) (Scholkopf & Smola, 2002;
655
      Cheng et al., 2024), which includes linear models (Ahn et al., 2024; von Oswald et al., 2023; Ahn
656
      et al., 2023; Mahankali et al., 2023) as a special case. For input x ∈ Rd , we first consider function
657   f (x) ∈ F with a scalar output, represented as f (x) = ⟨κ(x, :), f ⟩H , where H is a Hilbert space, f ∈
658   H, and there is an associated kernel κ(xi , xj ) = ⟨κ(xi , :), κ(:, xj )⟩H . We briefly review functional
659   gradients for scalar-output f (x) (Scholkopf & Smola, 2002; Bagnell, 2012; Cheng et al., 2024), to
660   set the stage for the vector outputs of interest here. Defining the evaluation functional Ex (f ) =
661   ⟨f, κ(:, x)⟩H = f (x), the corresponding functional gradient is specified in terms of a scalar ϵ and
662   g ∈ H. Specifically, we have Ex (f + ϵg) = Ex (f ) + ϵEx (g) = Ex (f ) + ϵ ⟨κ(x, :), g⟩H =
663   Ex (f ) + ϵ ⟨∇Ex , g⟩H + O(ϵ2 ); the last equality effectively defines a functional gradient in terms of
664   the linear term in a Taylor expansion of Ex (f +ϵg) about f (and here the O(ϵ2 ) term is exactly zero).
665   For Ex , the corresponding functional gradient in H is ∇Ex = κ(x, :), and Ex is termed linear, as
666   the second-order and higher terms are zero. A common nonlinear example used in kernel machines
667
      is Ex (f ) = 21 (y − f (x))2 for which via chain rule ∇Ex (f ) = (f (x) − y)κ(x, :) (Scholkopf &
      Smola, 2002). For general E(f ), omitting the dependency on x for notational simplicity, ∇E is
668
      termed the corresponding functional gradient.

      A.7   D ETAILS ON T RAINED TF D ESIGNS
944
945   For the Trained TF variants of the above GD-motivated setups, the model is free to learn parameters,
946   without restrictions. Specifically, the attention matrices WQ , WK , WV and P are learned without
947   restrictions. Additionally, there is the matrix Z at the output, the results of which then feed into
948   the softmax model at the output; Z is also learned. When they are not set as one-hot vectors, the
949   category-dependent vectors wc are also learned.
950
      The Trained TF version of the linearized multi-layered model above simple corresponds to multiple
951
      layers of attention, with all parameters learned. While with GD we restrict the form of P (ℓ) as in
952   (27), for Trained TF, the attention matrices have complete freedom wrt the parameters learned.
953
954   The Trained TF version of the nonlinear representation corresponds to the original design of the
955
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), in which the attention layer is followed by FF elements, with
      skip connections employed with both. The only element missing from the original Transformer is
956
      layer normalization, which we avoid because it is not evident in the GD-based formulation.
957
958   The Trained TF, with attention layers alone (corresponding to the aforementioned linearized multi-
959   layered GD) or with blocks composed of an attention layer and FF element (corresponding to the
960   aforementioned multi-layered GD with FF elements) offer significant modeling power. However,
961
      the training of these models via Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) was found to be challenging from a
      random parameter initialization. As discussed in the main paper, parameter initialization was found
962
      to be very important for Trained TF, consistent with prior work (Liu et al., 2023). In particular, to get
963
      good results with Trained TF, we found initialization with the appropriate GD variant is important.
