                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Spiking Graph Networks (SGNs), as bio-inspired neural models that address en-
               ergy consumption challenges for graph classification, have attracted considerable
011
               attention from researchers and the industry. However, SGNs are typically applied in
012
               static scenarios with real-valued inputs and cannot be directly utilized for dynamic
013            prediction because of their limited capacity to handle dynamic real-valued features,
014            denoted as architectural inapplicability. Moreover, they suffer from accuracy loss
015            due to the inherently discrete nature of spike-based representations. Inspired by
016            recent graph ordinary differential equation (ODE) methods, we propose the frame-
017            work named Continuous Spiking Graph ODE Networks (CSGO), which leverages
018            the advantages of graph ODE to address the architectural inapplicability, and em-
019            ploys high-order structures to solve the problem of information loss. Specifically,
020            CSGO replaces the high energy-consuming static SGNs with an efficient Graph
021
               ODE process by incorporating SGNs with graph ODE into a unified framework,
               thereby achieving energy efficiency. Then, we derive a high-order spike representa-
022
               tion capable of preserving more information. By integrating this with a high-order
023
               graph ODE, we propose the second-order CSGO to address the information loss
024            challenge. Furthermore, we prove that the second-order CSGO maintains stability
025            during the dynamic graph learning. Extensive experiments validate the superiority
026            of the proposed CSGO in performance while maintaining low power consumption.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Spiking Graph Networks (SGNs) (Zhu et al.,
032   2022; Xu et al., 2021) are a type of artificial neu-
033   ral network specifically designed to process graph
034   information in a manner similar to the human
035   brain. SGNs typically transform static and real-
036   valued graph features into discrete spikes, and
037   then emulate the neuron’s charging and discharg-
038   ing processes to achieve spikes representation for
039
      graph classification. The distinctive features of
      SGNs is their ability to capture semantic spik-
040
      ing representations while maintaining low energy
041
      consumption, making them well-suited for event-
042   based processing tasks (Yao et al., 2021), such            Figure 1: Illustration of information loss and ar-

045   Bauer et al., 2019), and graph classification (Li          architecture inapplicability refers to the unsuit-
046   et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021).          ability of dynamic real-valued SGN methods.
047
      SGNs are commonly applied in scenarios that handle static real-valued or continuous binary in-
048   puts (Guo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2023). For real-valued features, SGNs initially
049   sample binary features on each node using a Bernoulli Distribution (Zenke & Ganguli, 2018), where
050   the probability corresponds to the feature’s real values. These features are then propagated by simu-
051   lating the neuron’s charging and discharging processes. However, limited research has explored the
052   application of SGNs to dynamic real-valued inputs. Typically, simply sampling binary features and
053   calculating the spiking representation at each dynamic step results in significant energy consumption,
      rendering it unsuitable for low-power devices. Additionally, the inherent characteristic of SGNs that


                                                             1

054
      transforms continuous features into binary representations leads to information loss and performance
055   degradation (Yan et al., 2021). As shown in Figure 1, the architectural inapplicability and information
056   loss problems limit the application of SGNs for dynamic graph prediction in real-world scenarios.
057
058
      Inspired by recent developments in graph ordinary differential equations (Graph ODEs) (Battaglia
      et al., 2016; Kipf et al., 2018), which typically use GNNs to obtain node representations through
059
      ordinary differential equations, and recognizing that higher-order neural networks can preserve more
060
      information (Luo et al., 2023), we propose utilizing high-order SGNs and Graph ODEs to address
061   these challenges. However, incorporating Graph ODE with SGNs for dynamic prediction tasks is
062   difficult due to the following challenges: (1) How to efficiently incorporate the SGNs and Graph
063   ODE into a unified framework? SGNs and Graph ODEs process information along two distinct
064   dimensions: the latency dimension in SGNs and the dynamic evolution in graph-based models. A
065   key challenge is how to merge these dual processes into a cohesive framework that maintains the
066   energy-efficient properties of SGNs while leveraging the dynamic learning capabilities of Graph
067   ODEs. (2) How to alleviate the problem of information loss of SGNs? SGNs achieve low power
068   consumption by discretizing continuous features, but this binarization often leads to a significant
069
      loss of fine-grained information, reducing performance. Addressing the information loss in SGNs is
      therefore another critical challenge. (3) How to guarantee the stability of the proposed framework?
070
      Traditional graph-based techniques encounter the challenge of exploding and vanishing gradient
071
      problems when modeling the dynamic evolution of GNNs. Therefore, devising a stable model for
072   graph learning with theoretical guarantee constitutes the third prominent challenge.
073
074   To tackle these challenges, we propose a framework named Continuous Spiking Graph ODE Net-
075
      works (CSGO) for dynamic graph learning tasks. Specifically, to address the first challenge, we
      approach it by considering SGNs as a type of ODE and integrating it with Graph ODE into a frame-
076
      work, denoted as CSGO-1st. The CSGO-1st structure models the initial representation using SGNs
077
      by considering the inner time latency at each dynamic step and evaluates the dynamics evolution
078   with Graph ODE iteratively. Furthermore, to address the information loss problem caused by SGNs,
079   we derive a high-order spike representation with second-order SGNs structure and incorporate with
080   high-order Graph ODE, which referred to as CSGO-2nd. Moreover, we provide the theoretical
081   guarantee that CSGO successfully mitigates issues related to exploding and vanishing gradients. We
082   perform comprehensive evaluations of CSGO against state-of-the-art methods on various graph-based
083   datasets, showcasing the efficacy and versatility of the proposed approach.
084   In summary, the contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) Problem Setup. We present a
085   novel problem in SGNs, emphasizing the challenge of achieving high performance while maintaining
086   low power consumption for dynamic graph classification tasks. (2) Novel Architecture. We propose
087   the CSGO, which efficiently incorporates SGNs and Graph ODE into a unified framework, retaining
088   the energy-efficient properties of SGNs while preserving the ability to capture dynamic changes
089   in Graph ODE. Furthermore, we first derive the second-order spike representation and study the
090   backpropagation of second-order SGNs to mitigate the information loss problem. (3) Theoretical
091   Analysis. We provide a theoretical proof demonstrating that CSGO effectively mitigates the issue
092
      of exploding and vanishing gradients, ensuring the stability of our proposed method. (4) Extensive
      Experiments. We evaluate the proposed CSGO on extensive graph-based learning datasets, which
093
      evaluate that our proposed CSGO outperforms the variety of state-of-the-art methods.

099
      Graph Ordinary Differential Equations. Recently, numerous methods based on dynamic GNNs
      have emerged for modeling dynamic interaction systems (Battaglia et al., 2016; Kipf et al., 2018; Chen
100
      et al., 2018; Ju et al., 2024). These methods commonly employ GNNs to initialize node representations
101
      at discrete timestamps, which are then utilized for predicting node behaviors. Nevertheless, these
102   discrete methodologies often necessitate the presence of all nodes at each timestamp (Huang et al.,
103   2020; 2021; Yin et al., 2023a; 2022), which is challenging to achieve in real-world scenarios. In
104   contrast, ODE has proven to be effective in modeling system dynamics when dealing with missing
105   data (Chen et al., 2018). Recent works (Poli et al., 2019; Gupta et al., 2022) involve initializing state
106   representations with GNNs, followed by the establishment of a neural ODE model for both nodes
107   and edges, guiding the evolution of the dynamical system. Additionally, high-order correlations (Luo
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) have been shown to efficiently model the dynamic evolution of graphs.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      We integrate energy-efficient SNNs into Graph ODE, thereby retaining the low energy characteristics
109   of SNNs while harnessing the dynamic learning capabilities of Graph ODE.
110
111   Spiking Graph Networks. SGNs (Zhu et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021) have emerged as a promising
112
      solution for addressing energy consumption challenges on graph classification tasks. Recently,
      various SGNs (Xu et al., 2021; Wang & Jiang, 2022; Zhu et al., 2022) have demonstrated low energy
113
      consumption and high bio-fidelity. These models employ similarly reactive spiking neurons (Gerstner
114
      & Kistler, 2002) to process propagated graph data, achieving both low energy consumption and
115   maintained bio-fidelity. However, these methods are generally applied in the scenarios of static
116   real-valued or continuous binary inputs (Guo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2023). There
117   is still limited research focusing on dynamic spiking graphs with real-valued inputs. Although some
118   works have attempted to apply static SNNs to dynamic graphs (Li et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024) by
119   calculating the static spiking representation at each dynamic step and then predicting the evolution
120   with a new spiking layer, these methods generally demand substantial energy consumption, rendering
121   them impractical for low-power devices. Our approach ingeniously combines SGNs with Graph ODE
122   to effectively capture the dynamic changes while maintaining low power consumption.

193   4     M ETHODOLOGY
194
195   In this part, we present the proposed CSGO for dynamic spiking graph learning. CSGO incorporates
196   Graph ODE with SGNs into a unified framework, which preserves the advantage of Graph ODE for
197   low energy consumption dynamic evolution. To mitigate the problem of information loss attributed
198   to SGNs, we involve the derivation of second-order spike representation and differentiation for
199   second-order SGNs, and then coordinate with high-order Graph ODE, referred to as CSGO-2nd.
200   Finally, we present a theoretical proof to ensure that CSGO effectively mitigates the challenges
201   associated with gradient exploding and vanishing. The details of CSGO are illustrated in Figure 2.

483   6     C ONCLUSION
484
485   In this paper, we address the practical problem of continuous spiking graph learning and propose an
      effective method named CSGO. CSGO integrates SNNs and Graph ODE into a unified framework


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      from two distinct dimensions, thus retaining the benefits of low-power consumption and fine-grained
487   feature extraction. Considering that the high-order structure would help to relieve the problem of
488   information loss, we derive the second-order spike representation and investigate the backpropagation
489   of second-order SNNs, by incorporating with high-order Graph ODE, we introduce the second-order
490   CSGO. Furthermore, to ensure the stability of CSGO, we prove that CSGO mitigates the gradient
491   exploding and vanishing problem. Extensive experiments on diverse datasets validate the efficacy
492   of proposed CSGO compared with various competing methods. In future work, we will explore the
493   higher-order structure for more efficient continuous graph learning.

      F    S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS
908
909
      In this part, we examine the sensitivity of the proposed CSGO to its hyperparameters, specifically
910
      the time latency parameter (T ) in SNNs, which plays a crucial role in the model’s performance. T
911
      controls the number of SNNs propagation steps and is directly related to the training complexity.
912   Figure 4 shows the results of T across different datasets. We initially vary the parameter T within
913   the range of {5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11} while keeping other parameters fixed. From the results, we find
914   that, the performance exhibits a increasing trend initially, followed by stabilization as the value of T
915   increases. Typically, in SNNs, spiking signals are integrated with historical information at each time
916   latency. Smaller values of T result in less information available for graph representation, degrading
917   the performance. However, large values of N increase model complexity during training. Striking a
      balance between model performance and complexity, we set T to 8 as default.
