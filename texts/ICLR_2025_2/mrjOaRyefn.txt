010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Although safety-enhanced Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remark-
013            able success in addressing various complex tasks in a zero-shot manner, they remain
014            vulnerable to jailbreak attacks, particularly unknown jailbreak attacks. Adversarial
015            training has demonstrated significant potential across multiple domains for enhanc-
016            ing robustness against such attacks. However, when applied to LLMs, existing
017            adversarial training techniques are constrained by the substantial computational
018
               resources required to execute discrete adversarial prompts during each training
               iteration. We argue that continuous adversarial training is essential for enhancing
019
               generalized defense capabilities in LLMs, as opposed to conventional one-stage
020
               adversarial training. To address these issues, we propose a two-stage adversarial
021            tuning framework. In the first stage, we introduce hierarchical meta-universal
022            adversarial prompt learning to efficiently generate token-level adversarial prompts
023            by leveraging a task-based universal adversarial prompt, thereby accelerating the
024            generation process. In the second stage, we propose automatic adversarial prompt
025            learning to iteratively construct out-of-distribution adversarial prompts, further
026            enhancing the defense capabilities of LLMs. We conducted comprehensive experi-
027            ments on three widely used jailbreak datasets, comparing our framework with six
028            defense baselines under five representative attack scenarios across three LLM fami-
029
               lies. Specifically, our methods exhibit superior defense performance against both
               known and unknown jailbreak attacks in a zero-shot setting. Additionally, regard-
030
               ing the computational efficiency of generating token-level adversarial prompts, we
031
               demonstrate both empirically and theoretically that our method achieves approxi-
032            mately a 15× speedup. Furthermore, we show that a trade-off between model utility
033            and adversarial robustness still exists, similar to previous adversarial training ap-
034            proaches, and propose a hybrid training strategy to improve both model utility and
035            robustness. Importantly, our adversarial tuning framework demonstrates broad gen-
036            eralizability across various attack strategies and target LLMs (including large 110B
037            models), highlighting its potential as a transferable defense mechanism. Our code
038            is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LLMAT-5CFB.
039            Warning: This paper contains red-teaming data and model-generated content that
040            can be offensive!

043   1    I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Despite LLMs having shown superiority in tackling a wide range of complex tasks in a zero-shot
046   way, recent studies revealed that LLMs are susceptible to jailbreak attacks Yu et al. (2023); Zou et al.
047   (2023); Zheng et al. (2024a); Feng et al. (2024). The jailbreak attack can manipulate the prompt to
048   bypass the model’s alignment and produce harmful responses. Such attacks can be executed through
049   token-level jailbreak Zou et al. (2023) and prompt-level jailbreak attacks Yu et al. (2023); Russinovich
050
      et al. (2024), both of which have shown a high success rate in eliciting potentially harmful behavior.
      As model capacity improves, such security risk raises the possibility of significant real-world harm,
051
      highlighting the need for the development of safe LLMs.
052
053   Since the discovery of jailbreak attacks, various defense mechanisms have been proposed, encom-
      passing both system-level Robey et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023; 2024) and model-level Madry et al.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (2018); Zheng et al. (2024b) strategies. Specifically, system-level defenses introduce external safety
055   measures to mitigate harmful prompts. For instance, smoothLLM Robey et al. (2023) generates
056   multiple outputs from modified jailbreak prompts and employs majority voting to select the most
057   secure response. Conversely, model-level defense strategies involve direct modifications to the LLM
058   to mitigate malicious risks and enhance resilience against adversarial prompts. These approaches
059   range from safety training methodologies Touvron et al. (2023); Siththaranjan et al. (2023) to refusal
060   mechanisms and adversarial training techniques Madry et al. (2018). For example, safety training
061   integrates safety datasets during tuning phases. Additionally, a few studies explore adversarial
062   training algorithms that enhance robustness against various LLM attacks, although these require
063
      extensive computational resources. Despite significant efforts to develop defe es for LLMs, current
      methodologies still struggle to effectively defend against unknown jailbreak attacks, such as opti-
064
      mized adversarial prompts Liu et al. (2024b); Andriushchenko et al. (2024) and in-the-wild malicious
065
      prompts Shen et al. (2024); Du et al. (2023). This limitation naturally raises the question: Can we
066   enhance the generalized defensive ability of LLMs to defend against unknown jailbreak attacks?
067
068   We answer this question by introducing adversarial tuning, which involves generating adversarial
069
      prompts to explore worst-case scenarios by optimizing datasets for defense against jailbreak attacks.
      However, integrating adversarial tuning directly into the fine-tuning process is a non-trivial task
070
      and presents more significant challenges compared to traditional adversarial training. (1) High
071
      cost of generating token-level adversarial prompts. Generating token-level adversarial prompts
072   requires extensive computational resources, making it difficult to integrate into LLM fine-tuning
073   loops. The primary computational intensity arises from the numerous iterations required to optimize
074   adversarial suffixes via gradient computations for individual adversarial examples. For instance,
075   generating a single prompt with GCG can take 20 minutes on Llama-7B using an A100 GPU Zou
076   et al. (2023) with an average of 330 iterations. Introducing adversarial training directly into LLMs is
077   impractical, as the computation of numerous adversarial samples leads to an exponential increase
078   in computational requirements (e.g., calculating just 1,000 samples would take approximately 330
079   hours.) (2) Automating model-dependent Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Adversarial Prompts.
080
      Existing methods for generating out-of-distribution adversarial prompts primarily rely on manual
      curation, which is both time-consuming and costly. For instance, Chu et al. Chu et al. (2024)
081
      manually extract jailbreak prompts from webpages and open-source communities such as Reddit and
082
      Discord. A straightforward idea is to employ the Automatic Adversarial Prompt Generation (AAPG)
083   methods Chao et al. (2023); Mehrotra et al. (2023) to construct OOD adversarial prompts. However,
084   current AAPG methodologies are primarily designed to generate adversarial prompts in a black-box
085   manner and do not focus sufficiently on exploring OOD adversarial prompts. For example, the
086   PAIR Chao et al. (2023) leverages LLMs to automatically construct adversarial prompts in parallel,
087   resulting in the generation of semantically similar harmful behaviors compared to the initial harmful
088   query. Automating the exploration of model-dependent out-of-distribution adversarial prompts to
089   uncover worst-case scenarios remains a significant challenge.
090   We argue that enhancing generalized defense capabilities requires continuous adversarial training
091   of LLMs rather than using basic one-stage adversarial training. To address the aforementioned
092   challenges, we propose a two-stage adversarial tuning framework. In the first stage, we introduce
093   hierarchical meta-universal adversarial prompt learning to efficiently generate token-level adversarial
094   prompts. Specifically, within the outer universal adversarial optimization, we establish clear judge-
095   based update rules using a limited number of samples to learn task-specific universal adversarial
096   suffixes. In the inner adversarial prompt optimization, we start with a task-specific universal adversar-
097
      ial example and iteratively refine individual adversarial examples instead of generating them from
      scratch. This approach accelerates the generation of adversarial prompts while incurring minimal
098
      additional computational costs on universal adversarial suffixes. In the second stage, we present
099
      automatic adversarial prompt learning, which comprises automatic adversarial prompt refinement
100   and continued adversarial fine-tuning. To explore OOD adversarial prompts, we design a strategy
101   provider and memory reflection mechanism aimed at assisting the adversary in crafting more diverse
102   adversarial prompts. This involves the attack agent iteratively refining the adversarial prompt by
103   utilizing suggestions from the strategy provider and historical memory data, enabling the exploration
104   of the worst-case scenarios for LLMs. Finally, the target LLM is continuously fine-tuned on OOD
105   adversarial prompts, thereby enhancing its generalized defense capabilities.
106   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We introduce a continuous adversarial tuning
107   framework consisting of a two-stage refinement process designed to enhance defense capabilities

108
      against models without needing additional pre- or post-filtering. We conduct comprehensive ex-
109   periments to evaluate effectiveness of our approach using three recognized jailbreak datasets, six
110   defensive baselines, and six attack methods. The experimental results demonstrate that our defense
111   strategies effectively counter adversarial attacks, outperforming SOTA defense methods. (2) Further-
112   more, we show that a trade-off between model utility and adversarial robustness still exists, similar
113   to previous adversarial training approaches, and propose a hybrid training strategy to improve both
114   model utility and robustness. (3) We further investigate transferability of the proposed adversarial tun-
115   ing framework, finding that our adversarial examples generalize across various LLM families under
116   different attack strategies. These results highlight its potential as a universal defense mechanism.

      Generating token-level adversarial prompts incurs significant computational overhead, making it
190
      challenging to integrate into the adversarial fine-tuning loop. A naive idea is to use the universal
191   adversarial suffix as the initial starting point. However, directly using the traditional universal

196   for efficient adversarial prompt generation.

289   Although the initial stage of adversarial tuning enhances the model’s adversarial robustness (e.g.,

505     5             R ELATED W ORK
506    Jailbreak Attack on LLMs. Although LLMs have demonstrated remarkable proficiency in handling
507    complex and functional tasks, they remain susceptible to jailbreak attacks Mangaokar et al. (2024);
508    Mazeika et al. (2024); Paulus et al. (2024); Chao et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a); Yuan et al. (2023).
509    Recent studies indicate that jailbreak attacks can manipulate LLMs to circumvent the model’s safety
510    mechanisms and generate harmful outputs. These attacks can be broadly classified into two categories:
511    token-level jailbreak attacks Geisler et al. (2024) and prompt-level jailbreak attacks Kang et al. (2024);
512    Deng et al. (2023); Shayegani et al. (2023), both of which have exhibited a high success rate in
513    inducing potentially detrimental behavior in commercial LLMs. In token-level attacks, the objective
514
       is to optimize the set of tokens provided as input to the target LLM. For instance, techniques like
       GCG Zou et al. (2023) employ discrete optimization methods to optimize tokens greedily. On the
515
       other hand, prompt-level attacks rely on semantic manipulation and automated prompt-generation
516
       techniques Chao et al. (2023) to create adversarial prompts.
517
518    LLM Defense. To prevent the jailbreak attack, recently various defense mechanisms have been
519    proposed Wallace et al. (2024); Chao et al. (2024); Chu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b; 2023);
520
       Liu et al. (2024c); Ren et al. (2024), encompassing both system-level and model-level strategies.
       System-level defenses Zeng et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2024); Ji et al. (2024); Zou et al. (2024); Zheng
521
       et al. (2024b); Li et al. (2023) involve implementing external safety measures for either input or
522
       output. For instance, SmoothLLM Robey et al. (2023) generates multiple outputs from modified
523    jailbreaking prompts and uses majority voting to select the most secure response. As another example,
524    Self-Reminder Xie et al. (2023) employs system prompts and reminders to bolster the LLM’s focus
525    on secure responses. Model-level defense approaches Wang et al. (2024a); Zheng et al. (2024c);
526    Hasan et al. (2024) involve direct modifications to the LLM, aiming to mitigate the malicious risk and
527    enhance resilience against jailbreak attacks. One straightforward tactic involves incorporating safety
528    datasets into the tuning phases to inoculate the LLM against harmful instructions. However, current
529    methods often struggle to effectively defend against unknown jailbreak attacks (such as in-the-wild
530    attacks Chu et al. (2024), automatic prompt attacks Chao et al. (2023), and adaptive adversarial
531
       prompt Liu et al. (2024b)), which hampers improving LLMs’ generalized defense capabilities.
532
533
       6 C ONCLUSIONS
       In this paper, we propose an adversarial tuning framework to defend against jailbreak attacks. Our
534    framework efficiently generates adversarial prompts to explore worst-case scenarios for LLMs,
535    addressing both token-level and prompt-level vulnerabilities. By iteratively refining these prompts,
536    we enhance the model’s resilience to unknown jailbreak attacks without additional pre/post-filtering.
537    Our experiments demonstrate the efficacy of our approach across various attack strategies and LLM
538    types, outperforming existing defenses. Notably, our framework shows transferability, enhancing
539    defense across different model sizes without re-generating adversarial examples. These results
       underscore adversarial tuning’s potential as a robust, scalable defense for ensuring LLM safety.

883   B.2     E VIDENT J UDGE
884
885   In assessing whether an LLM has been ’jailbroken’ meaning it has been manipulated to produce
886   harmful outputs—we require a method that is both accurate and scalable. Traditional evaluation
887
      methods fall into two categories: classifier-based and heuristic-based. Classifier-based methods use
      pre-trained models to detect toxicity or explicit content, but they may miss other forms of harm,
888
      such as malware or harmful role-play scenarios. Heuristic methods rely on keyword detection but
889
      can generate false positives or negatives. Our goal is to introduce an approach that incorporates
890   uncertainty and contextual understanding to better evaluate harmful content.
891
892   Evident Judge. We propose the Evident Judge, an agent designed to determine if an LLM is
893
      generating harmful content. The Evident Judge operates in two main steps:
894   (1) Incorporating Policy Knowledge. Rather than relying solely on a pre-trained classifier, our
895   approach uses policy knowledge to inform the decision-making process. To incorporate expert
896   knowledge on jailbreak techniques, we integrate OpenAI’s policy guidelines1 into the system prompt.
897   (2) Expert Aggregation. We gather opinions from multiple experts who understand the uncertainties
898   involved and combine these to form a consensus.
899
900
      Specifically, let’s consider a set of N expert judges, each with their own parameters ϕi . These judges
      analyze the responses from the target LLM using specially crafted prompts. The expert first examines
901
      the LLM’s response, and then the expert assigns a score to the response, indicating the likelihood of
902
      jailbreak. These two stages result in a tuple (ai , si ) for each expert, where ai is the analysis and si is
903   the score for the response. The reasoning analysis and score execution are denoted as
904
905                                            (ai , si ) = πϕi (x̂1:m , r)                                  (19)
906   where ai is the expert’s analysis and si is the score for jailbroken response r.
907
      Aggregation Framework. To combine the individual decisions of the judges, we use an aggregation
908
      framework Ω, which outlines all possible outcomes:
909
910            • {“jailbreak”}: The model is jailbroken.
911            • {“non-jailbreak”}: The model is not jailbroken.
912
               • {“jailbreak&non-jailbreak”}: The model may or may not be jailbroken.
913
914            • {∅}: No conclusion can be drawn.
