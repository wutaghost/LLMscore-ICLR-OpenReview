011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Rapid advancements in genomic foundation models (GFMs) have delivered a se-
014            ries of breakthroughs across a diverse set of tasks for RNA, however RNA Sec-
015
               ondary Structure Prediction (SSP) remains a pivotal task in computational biol-
               ogy. Despite achieving breakthroughs in pseudoknot-free SSP, where state-of-
016
               the-art models can achieve above 80% macro-F1, performance on the pseudoknot-
017
               inclusive problem remains stagnate, with previous methods achieving below 50%
018            macro-F1 on all three of our test-sets. This is due to a variety of challenges: a
019            ginormous search space that limits heuristic performance, the major class imbal-
020            ance problem that limits the usual classification methods, and the inherent lack of
021            data that limits deep learning methods. Further data acquisition is implausible due
022            to requiring extensive biological resources and being associated with a high cost.
023            In this work, we propose a novel approach to enhance RNA secondary structure
024            prediction by implementing a novel data augmentation technique, specifically de-
025            signed for the pseudoknot-inclusive SSP problem. Our method leverages masked
026
               language modelling (MLM) with a surrogate model to produce accurate and use-
               ful data augmentations, and we further utilise uncertainty quantification strategies
027
               to identify areas within the dataset where augmentation is most effective - thereby
028
               helping to mitigate the class imbalance problem, and further improving on the
029            generalisability of the models. We further extend three GFMs, and fine-tune them
030            using the augmented datasets to demonstrate the efficacy and high performance of
031            the models. Notably, the newly extended and augmented models achieve state-of-
032            the-art performance, achieving over 89% F1 on RNAStrAlign, and over 66% F1
033            on bpRNA test sets respectively. We therefore highlight the effectiveness of data
034            augmentation for genomic data, and release our code and datasets to assist future
035            researchers.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
      Ribonucleic Acid (RNA) is a crucial biological molecule that is essential for the function and regula-
040
      tion of living organisms (Morris & Mattick, 2014). Recently, it has garnered significant interest due
041
      to its potential applications in therapeutics and vaccine development (Wang & Farhana, 2024; Zhu
042   et al., 2022). RNA sequences are composed of the nucleotides Adenine (A), Cytosine (C), Guanine
043   (G), and Uracil (U), which form bonds, and fold into secondary structures. The arrangement of nu-
044   cleotides is critical for the formation of these structures, as it heavily influences the folding process,
045   which enables RNA to interact with various cellular components (Ganser et al., 2019). This fold-
046   ing process relies on base-pair interactions, with both the length of the nucleotide sequence and the
047   specific types of interactions being important for the stability and functionality of the folded RNA
048   (Sanchez de Groot et al., 2019). The secondary structure serves as a foundation for the creation of
049   local structures and motifs within the RNA (Tinoco & Bustamante, 1999).
050   The sequence of nucleotides that RNA is made up of can be seen as it’s own unique language,
051   with an inherent structure. This is the essence of Genomic Foundation Models (GFMs), where each
052   nucleotide is fed into a large scale model, which can understand and model the underlying principles
053   of biology (Nguyen et al., 2024a). This central idea behind GFMs has sparked an interest in applying
      large-scale models to genomic problems, specifically in RNA, thus, there are many previous works,

      The rapid development of Natural Language Processing (NLP) in recent years is underpinned by
112   the demand for high-quality data (Feng et al., 2021). Obtaining high-quality data remains a signif-
113   icant challenge, particularly for genomic data, where data acquisition requires substantial time and
114   resources to ensure biological validity (Vicens & Kieft, 2022). This lack of data is a fundamental
115   problem across various areas of genomics, such as transcriptomics (Lacan et al., 2023), DNA se-
116   quences (Lee et al., 2023), and protein genotypes (Minot & Reddy, 2022). To address this problem,
117   various methods have been employed, such as sequence-based augmentation (Lee et al., 2023), and
118   the usage of generative models (Anand & Huang, 2018). However, there remains little usage of data
119   augmentation for RNA-based tasks. The highly structured nature of genomic sequences prevent tra-
120
      ditional data augmentation methods from being effective (Ching et al., 2018), as a simple change
      in the sequence could have a dramatic effect on the resulting structure, thus only context-aware
121
      data augmentation strategies are viable for this problem (Yang et al., 2018). Moreover, for GFMs,
122
      this necessitates the usage of Single Nucleotide Tokenisation (SNT), as to prevent misalignment be-
123   tween tokenised inputs and labels within text-based models (Yang & Li, 2024). As commonly used
124   NLP data augmentation strategies are designed for unstructured text (Wei & Zou, 2019), they are
125   unsuitable for application to highly-structured genomic data.
126
      One method of mitigating the structured nature of genomic data is the usage of masked-language
127
      modeling (MLM), which has been recently utilised by researchers within the GFM space (Karollus
128
      et al., 2024; Hwang et al., 2024). However, this introduces its own set of challenges, such as the
129   reliability of model predictions, which must be considered. One method of mitigating this challenge
130   that has recently been explored is the usage of uncertainty quantification (UQ) within pre-trained
131   language models (Wang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). Uncertainty quantification has emerged
132   as a vital technique for assessing and improving the reliability of model predictions, with various
133   works (Kong et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022) demonstrating an over-fitting problem in fine-tuned
134   pre-trained models. This is due to the extremely large parameter size of the models, and the small
135   amount of data being provided for fine-tuning model predictions (Kong et al., 2020). Apart from
136   simply adding more data in the fine-tuning process, we further explore the effect that uncertainty
137
      quantification methods have on improving model performance.

141   3   M ETHODOLOGY
142
143
144   GFMs have demonstrated effectiveness in genomic context-based learning, accurately predicting
145   masked nucleotide bases within sequences (Yang & Li, 2024). Given the highly context-dependent
146   nature of RNA secondary structures, we propose to directly mask a portion of the original dataset
147
      and employ MLM to generate augmentations of experimentally observed sequences, thereby in-
      creasing the sample size per instance. Although MLM for data augmentation has been previously
148
      explored (Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), we introduce a novel variation by incorporating uncer-
149
      tainty quantification into the methodology, designed to identify genomic regions with high or low
150   uncertainty. Furthermore, we tailor this method specifically for the pseudoknot-inclusive secondary
151   structure prediction (SSP) problem by leveraging known pseudoknots and prior data to enhance the
152   effectiveness of the masking approach. We term this method Sequence Location Masking (SLM),
153   which is designed to be seamlessly integrated into any GFM training pipeline by simply substituting
154   the original training set with the augmented one.
155
      The augmentation pipeline consists of several key steps. First, a GFM capable of MLM is selected
156   for data augmentation. Second, the original dataset is loaded, ensuring it has been appropriately
157   pre-processed. Third, an uncertainty quantification method is chosen; in accordance with current
158   literature, we offer several approaches, including Softmax entropy and temperature scaling (Guo
159   et al., 2017). Next, the proportion of nucleotide bases to be masked within each sequence is deter-
160   mined. This proportion must be carefully balanced, as insufficient masking may result in sequences
161   that are too similar, potentially leading to overfitting during model training. Finally, the number of
      augmentations per genome instance is selected.

      3.1     S EQUENCE L OCATION M ASKING
163
164   To implement Sequence Location Masking (SLM), it is essential to first apply uncertainty quan-
165   tification to identify regions within the sequence characterised by high uncertainty. This process
166   involves several steps:
167
      • Secondary Structure Prediction: The augmentation begins by inputting the sequence into a
168     model to predict its secondary structure. This model provides a set of predictions that serve as the
169     basis for subsequent uncertainty quantification.
170   • Mask Location:
171     After predicting the secondary structure, uncertainty quantification is performed on the predictions
172     to identify locations with high or low levels of uncertainty. Based on these uncertainty levels,
173     regions with the highest uncertainty are selected for masking.
174   • Augmentation Process:
175     The augmentation process involves calculating an uncertainty score for each possible nucleotide
176
        at every position in the sequence. For each position, the nucleotide associated with the lowest
        uncertainty score is selected as the predicted base, unless the uncertainty score exceeds a pre-
177
        specified cut-off value; in that case, the true label is used instead.
178
      • Incorporation of Uncertainty Cut-off:
179     A hyper-parameter y is introduced, serving as an uncertainty cut-off value. This cut-off determines
180     the threshold at which the true label (i.e., the actual nucleotide) is used instead of the predicted
181     nucleotide. Specifically, if the uncertainty score at a given position exceeds the cut-off value y,
182     the true nucleotide is retained. This process is repeated across all positions within the sequence.
183
      We further describe this approach from an algorithmic standpoint, from the perspective of a single
184
      sequence for simplicity. We break this down into two key algorithms:
185
186
      3.1.1    Algorithm 1: Using uncertainty to select the mask
187
188   We provide a template algorithm utilising uncertainty quantification for the masking strategy in
189   algorithm 1, given a sequence x from the training set, model G as our model to predict nucleotides
190   at each position, u(xi ) as our uncertainty quantification method being applied to a nucleotide at
191   position i in the sequence, and m as our masking percentage.
192   Within this algorithm, we use model G to generate predictions of each class at each nucleotide.
193   We then iterate through each nucleotide, calculate the uncertainty score using u(xi ), and record
194   the lowest uncertainty score. After this, we sort through all positions in x, and select the top m
195   nucleotides from the sorted array. Lastly, we apply masking at these positions to get the final masked
196   sequence.
197
198   Algorithm 1 Masking Sequence based on Uncertainty
199    1: Input: Sequence x with L positions, masking percentage m, genomic foundation model G
200    2: for each position i in sequence x do
201    3:     Use model G to predict nucleotide at position xi
202    4:     Calculate uncertainty score u(xi ) using softmax entropy or temperature scaling
203    5: end for
204    6: Sort all positions i by uncertainty score u(xi ) in descending order
205    7: Select the top m positions with the highest uncertainty scores

       8: Output: Masked sequence xm

      3.2    U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION M ETHODS
243
244   In this section we outline the uncertainty quantification methods used within this work, and provide
245   insight into how this may affect the data augmentation results.
246
      • Softmax Entropy The softmax function transforms the raw outputs of the model into a proba-
247
        bility distribution over the classes. Softmax alone is known to be prone to overestimation and is
248     unsuitable for uncertainty quantification (Holm et al., 2023), therefore we use the probabilities
249     obtained through softmax to calculate the entropy value for each possible class.
250   • Temperature Scaling Temp Scaling learns a parameter (T or temp) for all classes. The idea is to
251     "soften" the softmax with T > 1 (i.e., raise the entropy), and to "harden" the softmax with T < 0
252     (i.e., lower the entropy) (Guo et al., 2017). The exact parameter (T) is optimised with respect to
253     Negative Log Likelihood (NLL) on the validation set.

308   4.2   G ENOMIC F OUNDATION M ODELS
309
      In this section, we outline the algorithms employed in this study. We begin with IPKnot++ (Sato
310
      et al., 2021) and pKiss (Janssen & Giegerich, 2014). The latter of which is recognised as the leading
311
      thermodynamic method for pseudoknot prediction, while the former uses integer programming to
312   navigate the search space. Both algorithms do not require training data, and thus are incompatible
313   with our augmentation method. Whilst other deep learning approaches exist in current literature
314   (e.g., Ufold (Fu et al., 2021)), they utilise a differing data-format known as base-pair sequence
315   (BPSEQ). As our augmentation method requires the secondary structures to be in dot-bracket nota-
316   tion form, they were excluded from this analysis.
317
      Next, we describe the three foundation models used to demonstrate the efficacy of our method.
318   The first, OmniGenome-52M (Yang & Li, 2024), is an RNA-based architecture pre-trained on the
319   OneKP dataset (Initiative, 2019), utilising a transformer encoder-only architecture with 52 million
320   parameters. The second, HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2024b), is a DNA-based model pre-trained on
321   the Human Reference Genome dataset (Consortium, 2013), featuring a transformer decoder-only,
322   sequence-to-sequence architecture with 47 million parameters. Lastly, SpliceBERT (Chen et al.,
323   2024) is an RNA-based model pre-trained on RNA sequences from 72 vertebrates using MLM,
      employing a transformer encoder-only architecture with 19.7 million parameters.

      These models were specifically selected due to their utilisation of Single Nucleotide Tokenisation
325   (SNT). Whilst other foundation models for RNA and DNA are present in current literature, their
326   applicability to this problem is hindered by the usage of K-mers or Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) to-

332   4.3   M ETRICS
333
334   In this section we outline the metrics utilised to assess the performance of the secondary structure
335   prediction algorithms.
336
337          • F1-Score - F1-Score represents the harmonic mean of the precision and recall. Precision
338
               measures the proportion of correct positive predictions out of all positive predictions made,
               and recall measures the proportion of correctly predicted positive instances out of all actual
339
               positive instances. F1 measures the number of correct tokens for each target structure.
340
341          • Matthew’s Correlation Coefficient - Matthew’s Correlation Coefficient (MCC) averages
342            the model scores across all possible classes, thereby showing model performance even
343            when there is a large class imbalance. This, combined with F1-Score, is crucial for demon-
344
               strating model performance on the pseudoknot-inclusive problem, due to the significant
               class imbalance in the structural contents (Chicco & Jurman, 2020).
345
346          • Accuracy - Accuracy measures the percentage of correct predictions by the algorithm, in
347            this case, the number of correctly predicted nucleotides within the structure. Accuracy can
348            be misleading due to over-emphasising the class imbalanced data, thus F1 and MCC are
349            included for comparison.
350
351   4.4   U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION
352
353   We aim to investigate the point of uncertainty at which algorithm performance improves. Our hy-
354   pothesis posits that identifying the position of nucleotides that exhibits high uncertainty, and subse-
355
      quently masking those nucleotides during data augmentation, the algorithm can improve its effec-
      tiveness in previously weak areas, thereby improving overall performance. To test this hypothesis,
356
      we re-apply the augmentation methodology on both bpRNA and RNAStrAlign datasets and re-train
357
      the three algorithms on each augmented dataset.
358
359   This ablation study consists of two primary aspects. First, we aim to gain insight into the relation-
360   ship between augmented and non-augmented sequence similarity, and how this affects algorithm
361
      performance. Intuitively, we anticipate that high similarity may not improve model performance, as
      repeated data could lead to overfitting. Conversely, a high mutation rate may cause hallucinations.
362
      We hypothesise that the optimal value lies between 10% to 50%. To evaluate our hypothesis, we
363
      implement the augmentation strategy with a 10% variation at each interval, ranging from 10% to
364   50%.
365
366   Secondly, we aim to examine the relationship between the number of augmentations and model per-
367
      formance. It has previously been proven that increasing the proportion of augmented data does not
      necessarily correlate with improved performance, it may instead cause the model to over-fit on the
368
      oversampled minority classes (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019). However, the model must receive
369
      sufficient augmented data to effectively capture the class imbalance, and discern nucleotides rela-
370   tionships within the RNA sequence, and their relationship with the secondary structure. Therefore,
371   we conduct an ablation study on the ratio of augmentations to non-augmented data, to gain insight
372   into the efficacy of our method across both small and large amounts of augmented data.

475   5.3.1             S EQUENCE S IMILARITY
476
477
      The hyper-parameter introduced in this study, the percentage of augmented sequences, demonstrates
      a strong dependence on the dataset utilised. Our ablation study reveals that the percentage of nu-
478
      cleotides masked directly influences model performance, although this relationship is not direc-
479
      tional. An increase in model masking may cause a positive or negative effect, due to the relationship
480   being dependent on the original dataset utilised. Our initial hypothesis posited that an increased
481   percentage of nucleotides may result in an increased performance for similar sequences through
482   introducing diversity amongst the results. Conversely, a low percentage of nucleotides may result
483   a high similarity between sequences, and could cause overfitting. This phenomena is directly ob-
484   served when using random masking, however we mitigate this with the introduction of uncertainty
485   quantification. This stems from the uncertainty quantification method providing additional insight
      into areas with low proportions within the dataset, thereby enabling prediction re-calibration.

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519   We propose Sequence Location Masking (SLM) in this work to address the challenges of
520   pseudoknot-inclusive secondary structure prediction (SSP). Our approach demonstrates state-of-the-
521   art performance on both bpRNA and RNAStrAlign test datasets, outlining the effectiveness of our
522
      data augmentation technique across a diverse range of sequence lengths and pseudoknot types. Ad-
      ditionally, we conducted an ablation study to investigate the hyper-parameters introduced, analysed
523
      their impact on model performance, and identified the critical parameters that improve model per-
524
      formance. Future work should aim to expand the applicability of SLM by exploring its integration
525   with other types of deep learning models outside of GFMs. SLM should further be investigated in
526   the context of larger and more diverse genomic tasks to evaluate its robustness and adaptability.
