009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The presence of noisy labels poses a significant challenge for training accurate
012            deep neural networks. Previous works have proposed various robust loss functions
013            designed to address this issue, which, however, often suffer from several drawbacks,
014            such as underfitting or insufficient noise-tolerance. Furthermore, there is currently
015            no reliable metric to guide the design of more effective robust loss functions. In
016            this paper, we introduce the Variation Ratio as a novel metric to measure the
017            robustness of loss functions. Leveraging this metric, we propose a new family of
018
               robust loss functions, termed Variation-Bounded Losses (VBL), characterized by a
               bounded variation ratio. We investigate theoretical properties of variation-bounded
019
               losses and prove that a smaller variation ratio would lead to better robustness.
020
               Additionally, we show that the variation ratio provides a more relaxed condition
021            than the commonly used symmetric condition for achieving noise-tolerant learning,
022            making it a valuable tool for designing effective robust loss functions. We modify
023            several commonly used loss functions to the variation-bounded form. These
024            variation-bounded losses are characterized by their simplicity, effectiveness, and
025            theoretical guarantees. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of our
026            method in mitigating various types of label noise.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In recent years, deep neural networks (DNNs) have made significant advancements across various
032   machine learning tasks (LeCun et al., 2015; Han et al., 2020). However, the quest for superior perfor-
033
      mance often requires a significant investment in large, high quality annotated datasets. Unfortunately,
      obtaining accurate annotations is often prohibitively expensive and challenging. Noisy labels, which
034
      stem from human errors, imperfect labeling processes, or cognitive biases, are prevalent in real-world
035
      datasets and can significantly degrade model performance in supervised learning (Wei et al., 2021).
036   Moreover, the ability to generalize from noisy supervision plays a pivotal role in the alignment of
037   large language models (Burns et al., 2023). To address this issue, the development of noise-tolerant
038   learning has become crucial in the realm of weakly supervised learning, attracting increasing attention
039   and research focus. Among various approaches, the design of robust loss functions stands out due to
040   its simplicity and universality (Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Ma et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
041   2023; Ye et al., 2023).
042   Previous works (Van Rooyen et al., 2015; Ghosh et al., 2017) theoretically proved that symmetric
043   loss functions are inherently tolerant to symmetric and asymmetric label noise under some moderate
044   assumptions. However, the fitting ability of symmetric loss functions is constrained by the overly
045   strict symmetric condition (Zhou et al., 2021). Symmetric losses such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
046   (Ghosh et al., 2017) and Normalized Cross Entropy (NCE) (Ma et al., 2020) have proven challenging
047   to optimize (Ghosh et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2020). Some methods, such as Active Passive Loss
048   (APL) (Ma et al., 2020) and Active Negative Loss (ANL) (Ye et al., 2023), use two symmetric losses
049   simultaneously to improve the fitting ability, but some studies point out that they still converge slowly
050
      compared to common loss functions (Englesson & Azizpour, 2021). Thus, it is worth considering
      whether symmetric conditions should also be leveraged to design more effective robust loss functions.
051
052   Since symmetric loss functions are challenging to optimize, a common approach is to interpolate
053   between symmetric MAE and fast-converging CE. Examples of such hybrid loss functions include
      Generalized Cross Entropy (GCE) (Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018), Symmetric Cross Entropy (SCE) (Wang


                                                         1

054
      et al., 2019), Taylor Cross Entropy (Taylor-CE) (Feng et al., 2021), and Jensen-Shannon Divergence
055   Loss (JS) (Englesson & Azizpour, 2021). However, this approach to enhancing fitting ability comes
056   at the expense of robustness due to the CE component. Consequently, they fail to achieve complete
057   noise-tolerance and are likely to overfit a part of noisy labels.
058
      Recently, Zhou et al. (2021; 2023) have introduced another class of robust loss functions, known as
059
      Asymmetric Loss Functions (ALFs). They theoretically demonstrate that asymmetric loss functions
060
      exhibit noise-tolerance to clean-label-dominant noise. However, the proposed asymmetric losses, such
061   as the Asymmetric Unhinged Loss (AUL), are overly complicated with numerous hyperparameters
062   and often suffer from underfitting . Although they proposed the asymmetric condition, they did not
063   provide a straightforward method for its implementation. To date, no design guidelines have been
064   established to help create simpler and more efficient asymmetric loss functions.
065
      In this paper, we introduce a new metric for loss functions, called the Variation Ratio, which measures
066
      the robustness of loss functions. Then, we propose a new family of robust loss functions, namely
067   Variation-Bounded Losses (VBL), whose variation ratio is bounded. We provide comprehensive
068   theoretical analyses of variation-bounded losses and demonstrate that the variation ratio is critical
069   for both symmetric (Ghosh et al., 2017) and asymmetric (Zhou et al., 2021) conditions. From the
070   symmetric condition perspective, we prove that a smaller variation ratio can lead to a tighter excess
071   risk bound for various types of label noise. From the asymmetric condition perspective, we prove
072   that if the variation ratio is less than a certain constant with respect to the label distribution, the
073   variation-bounded loss becomes asymmetric and, consequently, noise-tolerant. Our analyses reveal
074   that restricting the variation ratio is a more relaxed condition than the symmetric condition. This
075
      suggests that the variation ratio can serve as a valuable tool for designing more effective robust loss
      functions. Furthermore, we modify several commonly used loss functions to the variation-bounded
076
      form for practical applications.

340   3.5   C OMBINATION OF NCE AND VBL.
341
342   Recently, the most advanced robust loss functions often combine their proposed methods with
343   Normalized Cross Entropy (NCE) (Ma et al., 2020). Notable examples include Active Passive
344   Loss (APL) (Ma et al., 2020), Asymmetric Loss Functions (ALFs) (Zhou et al., 2021), and Active
345   Negative Loss (ANL) (Ye et al., 2023). The combination of two different robust loss functions, can
346   mutually enhance the optimization processes of each other, thus improving the overall fitting ability
347
      of the model. These works (Ma et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2023) have experimentally
      demonstrated that combining NCE often results in improved performance.
348
349   Inspired by them, our proposed VBL can also be combined with NCE to obtain better performance.
350   We formulate the combination of NCE and VBL as follows
351                                     LNCE+VBL = ќ± ¬Ј LNCE + ќ≤ ¬Ј LVBL                                  (3.10)
352
353   We can easily prove that NCE+VBL is still noise-tolerant. Previous work (Zhou et al., 2021) proved
354   that symmetric loss functions are completely asymmetric, and the combination of asymmetric loss
355   functions remains asymmetric. Because NCE is symmetric (i.e., also asymmetric), and we have
356   already proved that VBL is asymmetric. So NCE+VBL is still asymmetric and therefore noise-
357   tolerant.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduce the Variation Ratio to measure the robustness of loss functions. Beside on
535   this, we propose a new family of robust loss functions, namely Variation-Bounded Losses (VBL). We
536   prove that a smaller variation ratio represents a better robustness and show that variation-bounded
537   losses have a more relaxed condition than symmetric losses. Our concise robust loss functions has
538   shown positive results for mitigating label noise in a variety of label noise types. We believe that they
539   can be widely applied in scenarios where obtaining accurate annotations is challenging. Additionally,
      the variation ratio will serve as a valuable tool for designing more effective robust loss functions.
