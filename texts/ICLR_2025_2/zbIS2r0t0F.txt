011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce a novel model for updating perceptual beliefs about the environment
014            by extending the concept of Allostasis to the control of internal representations.
015
               Allostasis is a fundamental regulatory mechanism observed in animal physiology
               that orchestrates responses to maintain a dynamic equilibrium in bodily needs and
016
               internal states. In this paper, we focus on an application in numerical cognition,
017
               where a bump of activity in an attractor network is used as a spatial-numerical
018            representation. While existing neural networks can maintain persistent states, to
019            date, there is no unified framework for dynamically controlling spatial changes in
020            neuronal activity in response to environmental changes. To address this, we cou-
021            ple a well-known allostatic microcircuit, the Hammel model, with a ring attractor,
022            resulting in a Spiking Neural Network architecture that can modulate the location
023            of the bump as a function of some reference input. This localized activity in turn is
024            used as a perceptual belief in a simulated subitization task – a quick enumeration
025            process without counting. We provide a general procedure to fine-tune the model
026
               and demonstrate the successful control of the bump location. We also study the
               response time in the model with respect to changes in parameters and compare
027
               it with biological data. Finally, we analyze the dynamics of the network to un-
028
               derstand the selectivity and specificity of different neurons to distinct categories
029            present in the input. The results of this paper, particularly the mechanism for mov-
030            ing persistent states, are not limited to numerical cognition but can be applied to a
031            wide range of tasks involving similar representations

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Neural networks in the brain are structured networks with a space-like structure, topologically anal-
037   ogous to a metric space (i.e. Euclidean) (Gerstner et al. (2014)). The use of these organized structure
038   are found not only in concrete representations, such as head direction (Pisokas et al. (2020)), ori-
039
      entation and compass for foraging (Stentiford et al. (2024)), and place and grid cells (Whelan et al.
      (2022)), but also in more abstract topographic maps that represent emotions, relationships, and, as
040
      we show in this paper, numbers (Leslie et al. (2008)). Moreover, these structures extend beyond
041
      biological systems; they are increasingly being employed in artificial embodied agents, demon-
042   strated through tasks such as numerical cognition through embodied learning mechanisms and on
043   development of grounding transfer from concrete concept of sensorimotor experiences to abstract
044   conceptual knowledge (Alessandro & Angelo (2021)).
045
      A key property of these networks is their capacity to hold persistent states, or bumps of activity
046
      (Gerstner et al. (2014)), which are associated with the representation of information in all of the
047
      mentioned systems of concrete and abstract representations (Hopfield (2015)). In this paper, we ex-
048   plore one such key network, the ring attractor model, which uses a bump of activity that is localized
049   to dynamically control spatial changes in neuronal activity (i.e., activities influenced by changes
050   in surrounding contextual features) (Pisokas et al. (2020)). Although these networks can represent
051   to track changes in the environment, this raises the question of how we can exert arbitrary control
052   over the position of bump activity in a structured spiking neural network. We infer that a structured
053   network (i.e., ring attractor models, place cell and grid cells, Kohonen maps) would more closely
      resemble the brain’s functionality than continuous neural field models, for which results in these

054
      continuous neural field models already exists (Gerstner et al. (2014)). Moreover, we pose a question
055   on whether these bumps can represent abstract information that does not have explicit dynamics.
056
057   To approach the first question, we propose the use of self-regulation as a mechanism for controlling
058
      the bump activity towards a desired state. The self-regulation mechanism here considers a neu-
      ronal model of set-point thermo-regulation, i.e., Hammel Model (Boulant (2006)), where this acts
059
      as a negative feedback control system for the bump activity. Considering that self-regulation can
060
      achieve arbitrary control of manifold physiological variables, these variables have similar properties
061   to the bump activity (i.e., there is always a “place” where the bumps are supposed to be). An im-
062   portant aspect of self-regulation is allostasis (Sterling (2012)), which extends the simpler concept
063   of homeostasis (i.e. the maintenance of physiological set-points) to cover adaptive, predictive and
064   competitive aspects of self-regulation. Thus, we further extend the usage of allostasis to tackle per-
065   ception and computation in structured neural networks by demonstrating the predictive behaviors
066   towards its desired state based on numerical cognition tasks.
067   Thus, we introduce subitization to approach the second question as a predictive task over numer-
068   ical capability. We use subitizing as a model system to explore abstract properties with neither
069   intrinsic nor explicit dynamics, yet with a putative metric representation: the number line (Leslie
070   et al. (2008)). Subitization is a special mechanism capable of recognizing quantity strictly through
071   perception without the need for counting (Dehaene & Cohen (1994)) and it can be characterized
072   as a form of categorization (Benoit & Henri Lehalle (2004)). This requires the distinction of ele-
073   ments and their mapping to specific internal representations; in this case, bump activity is mapped
074   to numerical representations. Since subitization tasks are associated with the prediction of discrete
075
      number representations, this form of representations can be modeled as an accumulator model with
      bi-directional mappings (Gallistel & Gelman (2000)). Thus, we can consider numerosity tasks, such
076
      as subitization, to exemplify the spatial-temporal control of these mappings. While there are other
077
      models that encode numerosity tasks into a single neuron network (Rapp & Martin Paul Nawrot
078   (2020)), we use our allostatic network approach to achieve results that can match behavioral and
079   neuronal findings.

133
      that function as afferent neurons, providing input for the downstream tasks to be done by the external

137   controlled by the Hammel model. Each neuron in the model is an Integrate and fire (IAF) neuron,

159   To control the position, we use a homeostatic circuit inspired by the Hammel model of autonomic
160   nervous system for temperature regulation in mammals (Boulant (2006)). This model is composed
161   of four neurons (figure 1B). In the original model, an insensitive neuron (I) receives input for the

      of these two neurons is processed through two downstream neurons. These neurons control opposite
163   effectors that aim to either generate or lose heat. This depends on a reference signal relayed to some
164   sensitive neurons by heat receptors in the skin or internal tissues. We adapt this concept to align
165   internal representations, i.e. the localized bump activity representing the system’s predicted belief,

168   sensitive neuron that are typically a set-regulatory point can be dynamically adjusted to responses

      loop.

173   It is known that under a symmetric kernel with local excitation and surrounding inhibition, the
174   ring attractor network can hold persistent activities in a localized region (Gerstner et al. (2014)).
175   These activities can be directed in a particular direction within the ring attractor by breaking the
176   symmetry of activities in the network. Typically, these are achieved through offsets implemented by
177
      an asymmetric connection (Khona & Fiete (2022); Zhang (1996)). Thus, we hypothesize that this
      control on shifting the position of the bump can be maintained by breaking the symmetry through
178
      gain modulation, which will be explained in the next section. This mechanism is in charge of an
179
      outer layer of two population of neurons connected to the ring attractor as shown in figure (1C).
180   The homeostatic effector neurons of the Hammel model are connected to the Left Gain Modulation
181   (LGM) and (RGM) neurons to simulate the process of generating or losing heat, respectively.
182
183
      Finally, a readout network is defined to feed back into the homeostasis model as a reference signal,
      where these inputs are based upon the position of the bump in the ring attractor (figure 1D). This
184
      circuit is inspired from models of familiarity discrimination that occur in the Perirhinal Cortex for
185
      processing information as a downstream task (Bogacz R (2001)). This model is used for creating
186   a reference signal for representation perception neurons (figure 1B) from a cumulation of ring at-
187   tractor neurons’ responses. This usually involves three layer of neurons: representation neurons,
188   implemented by the neurons in the ring attractor, familiarity discrimination neurons, referred to as
189   the number discrimination neurons in our model; and decision neurons, implemented by the repre-
190   sentation perception neuron in the Hammel Model (sensitive neurons) (figure 1D). These decision
191   neurons project downstream specific rate encodings based on representations from the ring attractor.
192   The external output that drives the changing threshold in the Hammel model as a function of the
193   input works as an accumulation of responses from the environment (figure 1D). A use case for these
194
      mechanisms in our model will be presented later.

      3   S UBITIZING AS AN ALLOSTATIC PROCESS : B EHAVIORAL RESPONSES

256   As an example, we introduce the AlloNet as a model of subitizing. Subitizing can be seen as a
257   “number sense” that we define, in this paper, as an allostatic match between external inputs and
258   internal representations (figure 3A).
259
      To set up our model to subitize, we define the ring attractor as a putative number line representation
260
      (Hamdan & Gunderson (2017); Revkin (2008)). The input to the network is represented as four
261
      Poisson spike train generators which feeds onto the insensitive neurons (here renamed as external
262   perception neurons) and tuned to generate firing rates in the range [0 − 200]Hz depending on the
263   number of co-activated inputs. Note that this guarantees permutation invariance of the inputs (i.e.,
264   activation sequences {1, 0, 1, 0} generate the same input as {0, 1, 0, 1} and so on).
265
      We define the number representations by the bijection:
266
267           {[0Hz, 50Hz], [51Hz, 100Hz], [101Hz, 150Hz], [151Hz, 200Hz]} 7→ {1, 2, 3, 4}.                (5)
268   As seen in figure 2C, once the input is given, the model adjusts its internal representation to match to
269   the corresponding numerosity. Similarly, we set up four readout neurons to match the corresponding
      frequencies as those of the input.

302   3.1   T HE A LLO N ET MODEL COMPARISON TO BEHAVIORALLY PLAUSIBLE REACTIONS TIMES
303         AND ERROR RATES
304
      To investigate the relevance of our model as a model of subitizing, we simulated two common
305
      subitizing experiments in humans. In the first experiment, we tested whether the network reproduces
306   relevant reaction times as those observed in humans. To do so, we define reaction time as the time
307   of the first spike from the readout neuron corresponding to the input numerosity. The results are
308   shown in figure 3B. We parameterize the experiment further by changing the excitatory synaptic
309   time constant of the gain modulation network.
310
      The model reproduces behavioral responses in two significant ways and fails in one crucial aspect.
311
      For longer synaptic time constants (1000ms), the reaction time increases as a function of numerosity
312   and also becomes more variable. However, with shorter synaptic time constants (900ms), the model
313   presents paradoxical behavior: smaller numerosities (numbers less than 3) are noticeably slower
314   than higher numerosities, while this behavior reverses for numerosities higher than 3. In the case of
315   human data, it takes less than half a second to perceive the presence of one, two, or three objects,
316   but the speed and accuracy fall dramatically beyond this limit (Dehaene (2011)). This human data
317   showcases two aspects of the model, where longer synaptic time constants (1000ms) aligned to
318   human responses for numbers less than 3, while short synaptic time constants (900ms) aligned with
319   the sudden increase in reaction time for humans on numbers greater than 3. However, the model
320
      fails on the aspects of the dynamics being much slower than human reaction times.
321   An additional experiment evaluated the error rate of the model (figure 3c). To do so, we fixed a
322   specific time during the run iteration in which the model should report the perceived numerosity and
323   tested the error rate for four different ranges of response timesteps. Here, we define error rate as
      (number of misfired timestep ÷ total timestep). For early response times, error rate increases with

340   4   S UBITIZING AS AN ALLOSTATIC PROCESS : N EURAL DYNAMICS
341
342   To understand the responses for different excitatory synaptic time constants, we investigate through
343   a heat-map that visualizes the activity of neurons across different time windows (figure 4). The
344   excitatory synaptic time constant may affect the reaction time of the network due to two possible
345
      outcomes: the slow angular speed of the shifting cues in the ring attractor network or instability of
      persistent states in the network. Although the first outcome can be directly inferred as time taken
346
      to be inversely proportional to the angular speed (Seen in τ = 900 in comparison to τ = 1000
347
      (figure 4)), the second outcome deters that high angular speed can also affect the network to have
348   slow response time. In the case that the network has excessive angular speed (greater than 0.018
349   rad/ms), this may diminish the ring attractor’s capability to track towards the desired persistent state
350   and oscillate between different signals (Chen (2024)) (Seen in τ = 1250 in comparison to τ = 1000
351   (figure 4)).
352
      As a further investigation of the network, we start by studying the responses of a single neuron to
353
      different numerosities over different trials. As an example, we show neuron number 63 in the ring
354   attractor which is tuned to respond to number 3 (figure 3A).
355
356   The response of a given neuron is composed of transients for higher numerosities (as the bump
357
      transits its neighborhood) and persistent activity for the numerosity it is tuned to. Note however
      that for later times the bump becomes unstable and starts a new wander due to the noise of in the
358
      network. This explains the inconsistent error rates for higher response times in figure 3C.
359
360   We also studied the gain field in the vicinity of neuron 63, composed of only transient responses for
361   numerosities greater than or equal to the tuned numerosity (figure 5A). These two profiles of activity
362   resemble the responses of the Entorhinal Cortex, the Parahipocampal Cortex and the Hippocampus
363
      in humans (Kutter et al. (2023)).
364   To understand a bit better the dynamics of the bump, we studied the position coordinate (Hopfield
365   (2015)) of the bump and its speed. The position coordinate consistently represents the numerosity
366   across trials (figure 5B) and the velocity shows evidence of a constant acceleration towards the
367   corresponding numerosity. Furthermore, by investigating the scaling of the speed with the synaptic
368   time constant we discover the reason for the difference in reaction times for slower time constants:
369
      the bump movement profile becomes shallower for lower numerosities due to lack of excitation from
      the input (figure 5C, dotted line).

372   5   C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
373
374   We have proposed a model of allostatic control of persistent states in spiking neural networks, named
375   the AlloNet. Additionally, we propose it as a computation model of subitizing in animals, defining
376   the number sense as a homeostatic alignment of internal representations and external inputs. Our
377   model reproduces qualitatively important behavioral aspects of subitizing in humans as well as ob-
      served neural dynamics of the regions known to be involved in number perception.

456   window for each numerosity. Dotted line is the corresponding step for lower synaptic time constant
457   (τex = 900ms).

459
460
      The Hammel model is involved in self-regulation. It works by minimizing the error between a given
461
      input and a fixed set value. By setting variability and abstraction to the threshold (as numerosity is
462   not a one-dimensional signal), we allowed the model to handle more general representations. We
463   think that this idea can be extended further to solve more general computational problems (Hopfield
464   (2015)).
465
      The self-regulatory aspect of the model implies that there is an autonomic response (internal or
466
      covert change) and a behavioral response (external or cover). This is analogous to the duality be-
467
      tween subitizing or approximating, counting (Dehaene & Cohen (1994)), both involve different
468   systems and one of them is behavioral (Pecyna et al. (2022)) while the latter is purely autonomic or
469   spontaneous (Castaldi et al. (2021)) and is modality independent (Togoli & Arrighi (2021)).
470
471
      The self-regulatory aspect of subitizing is thus the main contribution of this work. This change
      of perspective has the potential to unify different systems that share the same properties of the
472
      current model under a common view that could be considered as a neuromorphic version of active
473
      inference (Parr et al. (2022)). Indeed, similar models have been described in insects for controlling
474   head direction (Pisokas et al. (2020)) which have inspired computational (Stentiford et al. (2024))
475   and robotic models (Robinson et al. (2022)). However, in contrast with many existing models, our
476   current model does not include any sort of learning but adaptation, a property that is key not only
477   for subitizing (Togoli & Arrighi (2021)) but for general perception and control as well. We think
478   learning will add degrees of freedom to the model like the possibility of building a full Approximate
479   Number System.
480   There are still many challenges to overcome, and they are the focus of current computational and
481   theoretical work. One of them is the fact that the model has to be slow to be stable (i.e. the difference
482   between the fast time scales of spiking and the slow dynamics of the bump) needs to be significant.
483   Furthermore, the qualitative descriptions here need to be validated against actual data that not only
484   approaches the difference in age when subitizing but, perhaps more importantly, the dysfunction
485   of such systems. We think our overall approach is productive in investigating numbers and similar
      abstract senses in animals as well as in robots.
