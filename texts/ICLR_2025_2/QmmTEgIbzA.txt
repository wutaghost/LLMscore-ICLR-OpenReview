009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative models are capable of producing human-expert level content across a
012            variety of topics and domains. As the impact of generative models grows, it is
013            necessary to develop statistical methods to understand the population of available
014            models. These methods are particularly important in settings where the user may
015            not have access to information related to a model’s pre-training data, weights,
016
               or other relevant model-level covariates. In this paper we extend recent results on
               representations of black-box generative models to model-level statistical inference
017
               tasks. We demonstrate – both theoretically and empirically – that the use of these
018
               representations are effective for multiple model-level inference tasks.
019
020
021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Generative models have recently met or surpassed human-level standards on benchmarks across
024   a range of tasks (Nori et al., 2023; Katz et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). While these claims
025   warrant skepticism and further robustness evaluation (Ness et al., 2024), the impressive capabilities
026   have created a competitive environment for training state-of-the-art models and have inspired the
027
      development of complementary methods to adapt models to particular use cases. For example,
      quantizing the weights of a neural model enables a trade-off of model precision with vRAM and
028
      disk space (Gholami et al., 2022) while methods such as Low Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
029
      2021) and prompt-tuning (Lester et al., 2021) enable compute- and data-efficient model adaptation.
030   Other methods such as retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020), model merging (Matena
031   & Raffel, 2022), constrained decoding (Hokamp & Liu, 2017), etc. (Dettmers et al., 2024; Edge
032   et al., 2024), have similarly contributed to the rapid development of a large population of diverse
033   and accessible models.
034
      Each model in the population has an accompanying set of covariates – scores on benchmarks, train-
035
      ing mixture proportions, model safety scores, etc. – that are a function of the model, the training
036   set, the architecture, the retrieval database, or derivatives thereof. For a given model, the covariate
037   of interest may not be available to the user or it may be too expensive to calculate. For example, it
038   may not be known if a proprietary model has been trained on copyrighted data. Or, it may be too
039   resource intensive to directly evaluate how toxic every model is. Methods for predicting model-level
040   covariates are necessary in these settings, and others, to fully understand the behavior and properties
041   of a model.
042   In this paper we extend recent theoretical results for vector representations of generative models
043   (Acharyya et al., 2024) to model-level statistical inference settings. Our results show that the em-
044   beddings of the responses from a collection of generative models can be used for consistent inference
045   for a wide class of inference problems. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the representations for
046   three downstream inference tasks, including predicting the presence of sensitive information in a
047   model’s training mixture and predicting a model’s safety. We include empirical investigations of
048   performance sensitivity to hyperparameters required to generate the model-level vector representa-
049   tions.
050   Contribution. Our contribution is the theoretical and empirical validation for using vector repre-
051   sentations of black-box generative models for model-level inference tasks. The representations that
052   we study herein are based on the embeddings of their responses. The theoretical results apply to any
053   generic, well-behaved embedding function. Given the representations, any standard vector-based
      method can be used for inference on the models.

      1.1   BACKGROUND & RELATED WORK
055
056   Our work is an extension of recent theoretical and empirical results on embedding-based represen-
057   tations of generative models (Acharyya et al., 2024), which itself is a continuation of a long-line
058   of embedding-based investigations of the inputs and outputs of generative models (Mikolov, 2013;
059   Reimers, 2019; Neelakantan et al., 2022; Patil et al., 2023). Of particular relevance is recent empiri-
060   cal work defining the data kernel (Duderstadt et al., 2023) and investigations into its ability to track
061   the dynamics of interacting models (Helm et al., 2024), in which the experiments demonstrate the
062   ability to parlay the embeddings of a collection of inputs or outputs into useful vector representations
063
      of the generative models themselves in both white-box and black-box settings. The results herein
      further theoretically and empirically validate these findings in the context of statistical inference.
064
065   Our work is also related to using embedding-based techniques for inference on complicated objects
066   such as entire mouse connectomes (Wang et al., 2020), physiological data (Chen et al., 2022), and
067   classification distributions (Helm et al., 2021). In each of these settings, the authors define a pairwise
068   distance matrix on the objects and apply multi-dimensionsal scaling to obtain vector representations
069
      of each of the objects. Once there is one vector representation per object, standard inference methods
      for the specific task can be used. The method we study herein follows this general formula, with the
070
      additional complication that the objects are random mappings.
071
072   Lastly, our work is a part of the relatively new literature on inference on generative models. For
073   example, FlashHELM (Perlitz et al., 2023) uses the score on a subset of a benchmark such as HELM
074   (Liang et al., 2022) to quickly identify where a model fits on a leaderboard. More recent work
075
      proposes scaling laws to predict the performance of base models on a suite of benchmarks as a
      function of training FLOPs (Ruan et al., 2024). The method proposed herein does not assume access
076
      to a scoring function nor does it assume access to a function of the model’s weights at inference time.
077
      Perhaps most importantly, our setting and method can be applied to general model-level prediction.

      2.3     A N ILLUSTRATIVE EXAMPLE – “WAS RA F ISHER GREAT ?”
202
203
      The first model-level inference task we study is a toy example where the task is to predict the
204   probability that a model will respond “yes” to the question “Was RA Fisher great?”. The question is
205   chosen due to its subjectiveness – there is no correct answer to the question of a person’s greatness
206   – and its duality – Ronald A. Fisher is considered one of the most influential statisticians in history
207   and is considered an advocate of the 20th century Eugenics movement (Bodmer et al., 2021).
208
      We use a 4-bit version of Meta’s LLaMA-2-7B-Chat (Touvron et al., 2023) as a base model and
209
      consider a collection of models parameterized by fixed context augmentations. Each augmentation
210   ai contains information related to RA Fisher’s statistical achievements (i.e., ai = “RA Fisher pio-
211   neered the principles of the design of experiments”) or to his involvement in the eugenics movement
212   (i.e., ai = “RA Fisher’s view on eugenics were primarily based on anecdotes and prejudice.”) and is
213   prepended to every query. The covariate corresponding to a given model is calculated by prompt-
214   ing the base model with the appropriately formatted prompt “Give a precise answer to the question
215   based on the context. Don’t be verbose. The answer should be either a yes or a no. ai . Was RA
      Fisher great?” until there are 100 valid responses. We let yi be the average number of “yes”es.

      3.1   H AS A MODEL SEEN SENSITIVE INFORMATION ?
292
293
      Modern language models are trained with trillions of tokens of text (Touvron et al., 2023). For
294
      proprietary models such as OpenAI’s GPT series or Anthropic’s Claude series, the exact sources
295   of the training mixtures are unknown and – given the models’ propensities to produce content that
296   is strikingly similar to copyrighted content (Henderson et al., 2023) – its curation and use is eth-
297   ically questionable (Lemley & Casey, 2020). Further, the training mixture of some models may
298   include sensitive informative such as personal information, trade secrets, or government-classified
299   information that should never be presented to the end user. Developing classifiers to identify models
300   that are either trained on sensitive or copyrighted information or are likely to produce sensitive or
301   copyrighted information is thus paramount to uphold the rights of the stakeholders of the original
302   content. There has been recent work on this topic in settings with access to model weights or token
303
      likelihoods (see, e.g., (Duderstadt et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023)).
304   To investigate the utility of DKPS for this purpose, we again use a 4-bit version of

309
      erence”, “Computers & Internet”, and “Sports” to be “not-sensitive”, and data from the remaining
      topics to be “orthogonal”. We trained each of the 50 adapters with 500 question-answer pairs for
310
      3 epochs with a learning rate of 5 × 10−5 and a batch size of 8. Each adapter is rank 8, has a
311

368   3.2   H OW SAFE IS A MODEL ?
369
370   Model safety is one of the biggest concerns when deploying a language model in production. An
371   unsafe model is prone to propagating harmful stereotypes (Ferrara, 2024), using toxic language
372   (Wen et al., 2023), and misunderstanding the user’s intent (Ji et al., 2023) – all of which can adversely
373   affect the user and their experience. Hence, developing techniques to understand how unsafe a model
374
      is an important aspect of the model-production pipeline. As with predicting if a model has had access
      to sensitive information above, we investigate using DKPS to predict model safety through the lens
375

453   4    D ISCUSSION
454
455   We have demonstrated – both theoretically and empirically – that embedding-based representations
456   of generative models can be used for various model-level inference tasks. While the results we
457
      presented show the potential of our approach, there are choices throughout the collection-of-models-
      to-covariate-prediction pipeline that can affect performance and implementation practicality.
458
459   As mentioned in Section 2.2, one major decision is the query distribution Pquery (or, more practi-
460   cally, the set {q1 , . . . , qm }). In particular, the representations of the models that the query set induces
461   may or may not be relevant to a particular inference task. We demonstrate this phenomenon in Sec-
462   tion 3.1 where the queries from the “sensitive-only” query distribution can induce representations of
463
      similar discriminative ability as queries from the “orthogonal-only” query distribution with 1/5 of
      the queries. We expect a similar but less dramatic effect within the distributions of “sensitive-only”
464
      queries and anticipate that curating an “optimal” set of queries for a given g and for a fixed m will
465
      soon be a highly active research area.
466
467   Another decision is the distance function used to define D. The MDS of the distance matrix studied
468   herein (Eq. (1)) produces representations of the models that are consistent for objects that cap-
469
      ture the true mean discrepancy geometry of the model responses. For model-level covariates that
      cannot naturally be described as a function of mean discrepancy geometry, we do not expect that
470
471
      information-theoretic optimal performance when using ψb is possible for any n, m, and r. Instead,
      for proxies of the models to minimize information loss, it is necessary to replace the Frobenius
472
      norm of the differences of the average embedded response with a more expressive distance such as
473
      an extension of a distance defined directly on the cumulative distributions of responses or with a
474   task-specific distance (Helm et al., 2020). Related theoretical work (Tang et al., 2013) suggests that
475   our results can be extended to more expressive distances. We do not expect the naı̈ve replacement of
476   the Frobenius norm with a more expressive distance function to be universally better for model-level
477   inference tasks in practice as there are likely computational and query set quality trade-offs to con-
478   sider. As with the active curation of an optimal query set, we expect these trade-offs to be important
479   future research topics.
480   In the experiments above we have access to n models and n′ < n corresponding model-level covari-
481   ates. We induce a DKPS with the entire collection of models and use the n′ labeled models to predict
482   a label for the remaining n − n′ models. In practice an unlabeled model may not be available when
483   inducing the DKPS or, if n is large, it may be too expensive to induce a DKPS whenever a prediction
484   for a new unlabeled model is required. Out-of-sample techniques (Bengio et al., 2003; Trosset &
485   Priebe, 2008) can be used to meet these imposed constraints at the cost of a slight degradation in
      representation quality and, hence, inference performance.


                                                            9
486
      Implementation of inference in DKPS in practice will require an upfront, one-time cost of generating
487   responses from a subset of models in the collection and scoring their outputs. Since this is required
488   to compare the models with respect to the covariates anyway we followed the timing paradigm
489   presented in (Perlitz et al., 2023) and did not include this cost when comparing the methods in
490   Figure 5. Once the models in the initial subset are scored we expect the relative time efficiency (and
491   implied relative computational efficiency) of inference in DKPS to be worthwhile to practitioners.
492
      We fixed r = 1 and let m grow throughout our experiments. For Theorems 1 and 2 to hold, both
493   m and r must grow. In practice, the trade-off between getting responses for more queries or getting
494   more responses for the same queries depends on the distributions Fij . For example, if Fij is a point
495   mass then r > 1 for qj is unnecessary. Conversely, if Fij is a complicated distribution on Rp then
496   more responses are necessary to properly estimate it and, hence, to properly capture the difference
497   between average model responses.
