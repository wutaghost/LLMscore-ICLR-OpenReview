009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) present significant risks when used to generate non-factual
012            content and spread disinformation at scale. Detecting such LLM-generated content is
013            crucial, yet current detectors often struggle to generalize in open-world contexts. We
014            introduce Learning2Rewrite, a novel framework for detecting AI-generated text with
015            exceptional generalization to unseen domains. Our method leverages the insight that
016            LLMs inherently modify AI-generated content less than human-written text when tasked
017
               with rewriting. By training LLMs to minimize alterations on AI-generated inputs, we
               amplify this disparity, yielding a more distinguishable and generalizable edit distance
018
               across diverse text distributions. Extensive experiments on data from 21 independent
019
               domains and four major LLMs (GPT-3.5, GPT-4, Gemini, and Llama-3) demonstrate that
020            our detector outperforms state-of-the-art detection methods by up to 23.04% in AUROC
021            for in-distribution tests, 37.26% for out-of-distribution tests, and 48.66% under adversarial
022            attacks. Our unique training objective ensures better generalizability compared to directly
023            training for classification, when leveraging the same amount of learned parameters. Our
024            findings suggest that reinforcing LLMs’ inherent rewriting tendencies offers a robust and
025            scalable solution for detecting AI-generated text.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate exceptional capabilities across various tasks (Radford et al.,
030   2019; Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2020).
031   However, they can be misused for illegal or unethical activities, such as spreading misinformation (Chen &
032   Shu, 2023), scaling spear phishing campaigns (Hazell, 2023), facilitating social engineering and manipulation
033   of social media (Zhang et al., 2024), and generating propaganda (Pan et al., 2023). LLMs also facilitate
034   academic dishonesty (Zellers et al., 2019; Mvondo et al., 2023), and training foundation models with generated
035   content can lead to irreversible defects in resulting models (Shumailov et al., 2023). These issues highlight
036   the urgent need for reliable algorithms to detect LLM-generated text.
037   Various methods for detecting generated text have been proposed (Solaiman et al., 2019; Fagni et al., 2021;
038   Mitrović et al., 2023; Mitchell et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Bao et al., 2024; Mao et al.,
039   2024). Most of these detectors employ pre-trained models, extracting hand-crafted features and heuristics,
040   such as loss curvature (Bao et al., 2024) and rewriting distance (Mao et al., 2024), and apply thresholds to
041   distinguish LLM from human data. However, these thresholds are highly domain-dependent, obfuscating the
042   establishment of a universal detection standard.
043   In this paper, we present L2R (Learning to Rewrite), which trains an LLM to perform more edits when being
044   asked to rewrite human-generated data and fewer edits when rewriting on LLM-generated data across a
045   diverse set of domains. Unlike traditional detectors, which work well in-distribution (ID) but often struggle to
046   generalize among out-of-distribution (OOD) domains (including adversarial attacks), our algorithm leverages

      3.1       R EWRITING FOR LLM D ETECTION
138
139   Rewriting input with LLM and then measuring the change proves to be a successful way to detect LLM-
140   generated content. Given an held-out input text set Xtrain with LLM and human generated text, and its

                                                                                           3

141
      corresponding label set Ytrain , an LLM F (·) is prompted to rewrite the input x ∈ Xtrain using a prompt p.
142
      The rewriting output is F (p, x). Particularly, the prompt p can be set to: Refine this for me please.
143
144   The edit distance between the input text and the rewritten output, D(x, F (p, x)), is then computed for all
145   x ∈ Xtrain . Mao et al. (2024) adopts the Levenshtein distance (Levenshtein et al., 1966), which is defined as
146
      the minimum number of insertions, deletions, or substitutions required to transform one text into the other.
      With the Levenshtein distance, a similarity score we use for classification is calculated based on:
147
148                                                           Levenshtein(F (p, x), x)

                                                             max(len(F (p, x)), len(x))
150
151   Mao et al. (2024) trains a classifier, such as logistic regression or decision tree, to threshold the similarity
152   scores and predict if it is written by an LLM. However, as shown in Figure 1, the threshold of rewriting with a
153   vanilla LLM often varies from one domain to another, causing RAIDAR to fail to generalize to new domains.

155   3.2   F INE -T UNING THE R EWRITE M ODEL
156
157   L2R works on the premise that human-written and AI generated text would cause a different amount of
158   rewrites and a boundary can be drawn to separate both distributions. Thus we can finetune such a rewrite
159   model F ′ (·), that gives as much rewrite as possible for human texts, while leaving the AI texts unmodified,
160
      demonstrated in Figure 2. Given some human text xh ∈ Xtrain and AI text xai ∈ Xtrain , our objective
      becomes:
161
                                     max{D(xh , F ′ (p, xh )) − D(xai , F ′ (p, xai ))}                         (2)
162
163   Since the edit distance is not differentiable, we use the cross-entropy loss L(·) assigned to the input x by
164   F ′ (·) as a proxy to the edit distance. As a result, for each of input x with label y = 1 (AI) or 0 (human), our
165   loss function becomes:
166                                                                         1    (AI)
                                      min{L(Xtrain ) · ytrain }, ytrain =                                           (3)
167                                                                         −1 (human)

169   In this way, we flip the sign of the loss of the human texts. Since the overall loss would be minimized, this
170   effectively encourages the rewrites to be different from human input and identical to the AI input.
171
172   3.3   C ALIBRATION L OSS DURING F INE -T UNING
173
174   When fine-tuning the rewrite model on Equation 3, the rewrite model aims to maximize the edits on human-
175   generated text and minimize the edits on LLM-generated texts. However, without posting regularization and
176   constraint on the unbounded loss, the rewrite model takes the risk of being corrupted (e.g., verbose output for
177   all rewrite and over-fitting with more edits on human-generated text rewrite) which we evaluated in §A.5.
178   Therefore, we propose a calibration loss, which prevents the over-fitting problem by imposing a threshold
179   value t on the absolute value of the loss on each given input. For human text xh , we apply gradient
180   backpropagation only if the absolute loss L(xh ) < t. For AI text xai , we apply backpropagation only if
181   L(xai ) > t. Otherwise, the gradient is set to 0. We show a pseudocode for the algorithm in 1.
182
      Therefore, rather than minimizing the loss proxy, our objective becomes separating the distribution of human
183   and AI rewrites to two ends of the threshold t. Concretely, this enables the model to only optimize against
184   the hard examples, and leave those already correctly classified unchanged, so that we prevent overfitting.
185   This is similar to DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), where we fine-tune the rewrite model using only preference
186   data, namely the rewrites that are not yet separated by the existing boundary. This process is depicted by the
187   graphical illustrations in Figure 1.

      4     DATASET
202
203
      Existing detectors are often evaluated on datasets such as SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2016), XSum (Narayan
204   et al., 2018), and Writing Prompts (Fan et al., 2018). However, these datasets typically represent a narrow
205   subset of available data, both in terms of timeliness and domain coverage. This limitation raises concerns
206   about over-fitting and uncertainty regarding how these detectors would perform when deployed in real-world
207   scenarios, highlighting the necessity in creating a dataset of diversely-distributed texts for training.
208
209   4.1    DATA C OLLECTION
210
211   To ensure the robustness and generalizability of our detection model, we construct a dataset consisting of
212   human-written text from 21 distinct domains, including finance, entertainment, cuisine, etc. For each domain,
213   we collect the texts either by crawling online platforms like Wikipedia or by sampling from publicly available
214
      datasets. From these collections, we randomly select 200 complete paragraphs as text snippets which yields
      an average length of 120 words among the samples. For each of these 200 human-written samples per
215
      domain, we generate four AI-written counterparts using four state-of-the-art LLMs: GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024),
216
      GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI, 2020), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024), and Llama-3-70B-Instruct (Meta, 2024).
217   This results in a total of 21,000 text samples across all domains. Detailed descriptions of the domains and
218   their sources are provided in §A.1, and examples of the dataset are shown in Figure 5.
219
220   4.2    P ROMPT D IVERSITY
221
222   Conventionally, AI-generated text is created by prompting LLMs to either rewrite a given text or continue
223   writing from a given prefix, often using a single, static prompt for the entire process (Mitchell et al., 2023;
224   Bao et al., 2024; Verma et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024). However, real-world text generation involves a wide
225   variety of prompts, which can significantly alter the distribution of the generated text. Previous work (Mao
226
      et al., 2024) has shown that one straightforward way to bypass the RAIDAR detector is by using the prompt
      "Help me rephrase it, so that another GPT rewriting will cause a lot of modifications,"
227
      which suggests that data generated by different prompts are different in distribution, indicating the importance
228
      of prompt diversity. To address this, we curate a dataset of 200 rewrite prompts, each containing slight
229   variations in phrasing and instructions. For each generated text, a prompt is randomly sampled from this
230   dataset. Examples of the prompts we use are provided below:
231
232       • Refine this for me please:
233       • Please rewrite this content in your own words:
234       • Make this text more formal and professional:

      For Llama rewrite, AUROC increases from 0.7888 to 0.7970. This shows that diverse prompts effectively

247   4.3    DATA C LEANING
248
249   In collecting human-written text, we ensure that no data is generated after November 30, 2022, the release
250   date of ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2020), avoiding contamination of human dataset with AI-generated content.
251   Instead of removing all entries that are either too short (less than 10 words) or too long (over 300 words), we
252   retain them while maintaining an overall average length of 120 words across different domains, with standard
253
      deviation in length being 108 words. For AI-generated text, we carefully remove any extraneous suffixes,
      such as “Sure, here is a...,” to avoid them be detected in this way.

      5.1    E XPERIMENT S ETUP
266
267   We perform all experiments on one NVIDIA A100 GPU with 40GB VRAM. We use ’meta-Llama/Meta-
268   Llama-3-8B-Instruct’ (AI@Meta, 2024) as the open-sourced rewrite model in all experiments. To fine-tune
269   the Llama model with 8B parameters, we employ 4-bit QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2024), with parameter r
270   set to 16, lora_alpha set to 32, and lora_dropout set to 0.05, unless otherwise noted. We use an initial
271   learning rate of 5e-6, a weight decay of 0.01, and a batch size of 32 to train until convergence. We use 70% of
272   the dataset for training and the rest for testing in all experiments.

287   5.3   C OMPARE L2R WITH OTHER D ETECTORS
288
289   We compare the performance of L2R with Fast-DetectGPT, Ghostbusters, and RAIDAR (Llama Rewrite and
290   Gemini Rewrite), by measuring the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) scores.
291   The resulting scores for each domain along with their average and standard deviation can be found in Table 1.
292   L2R constantly outperforms both configurations of RAIDAR in all domains; outperforms Fast-DetectGPT in
293   20 of 21 domains by an average of 23.04% in AUROC; and outperforms Ghostbusters in 20 of 21 domains
294   by an average of 19.56% in AUROC. L2R has a 5.62% lower AUROC score than Fast-DetectGPT on legal
295
      document domain, and a 1.62% lower AUROC score than Ghostbusters on literature creative writing domain,
      which might be due to the unique distributions of these domains: legal documents require a more rigorous
296
      writing style, while creative writing has a more casual style, thus leaving fewer room for rewrite even for
297
      human writers.

299   In general, the fluctuating AUROC scores indicate the challenging nature of our dataset and the diversity and
300   independence of the distributions across domains. These results also show that L2R has better knowledge of
301
      the intricate differences between human and AI texts in various domains compared with the baselines, and is
      more capable in the real-world setting.

304   5.4   OOD DATASET E VALUATION
305
306   We showed that L2R outperforms the state-of-the-art detectors ID in terms of AUROC scores, but it is equally
307   important to assess its robustness under OOD conditions, as training-based detectors are prone to overfitting to
308   familiar domains and generator models. We first evaluate this by showing its performance on OOD datasets.
309
      To assess L2R’s performance on OOD data, we adopt the M4 dataset (Wang et al., 2024), an OOD dataset
310
      that is different from our training data in multiple dimensions, including data generation models, text length,
311
      decoding strategy, and domains. We show a comparison in 2.
312
313   The results of the OOD evaluation are presented in Table 3. We include both ID and OOD results to highlight
314   the degree of overfitting for each detector. While the Llama Logits method achieves the highest ID AUROC,
315
      its OOD result is the lowest, indicating significant overfitting to the training data. Similarly, Ghostbuster
      shows overfitting with its OOD AUROC being roughly half of its ID performance. The naive rewrite-based
316
      approach shows superior robustness compared with these other methods, but L2R trained with reduced
317
      parameters, i.e. rank r set to 4 and lora_alpha set to 8 (reduced params), outperforms Llama Rewrite by
318   3.45% ID and 4.67% OOD. This demonstrates that our fine-tuning does not simply overfits the rewrite model
319   to the training data, but enhances its classification performance across diverse distributions.
320
321
      We notice that reducing the number of training parameters make the model more generalizable, and further
      investigate the impact of fine-tuning parameters on L2R’s performance ID and OOD. By adjusting the
322
      LoRA parameters r and lora_alpha, we define four fine-tuning configurations with the number of trainable
323
      parameters ranging from 851,968 to 6,815,744, with details listed in §A.4. Figure 3 illustrates the results,
324   where we observe a consistent increase in ID AUROC, accompanied by a decline in OOD AUROC as the
325   number of parameters grows. This suggests that the model becomes increasingly overfitted to the training
326   distribution. L2R either outperforms Llama Logits OOD or both ID and OOD, and all four configurations
327   outperform Ghostbusters and Fast-DetectGPT both ID and OOD. Also, the first two configurations surpass
328   RAIDAR in terms of AUROC across both settings.

412
413     Mao et al. (2024) introduces the rewrite attack where a GPT-3.5-Turbo model is prompted to refine an input
414     paragraph, generated by AI, in such a way that a subsequent rewrite by another GPT model would result
415
        in significant changes. Mao et al. (2024) showed that this type of attack is particularly effective against
        rewrite-based detectors, as it disrupts the rewrite we use for classification. As shown in Table 4, L2R again
416
        achieves the highest AUROC on these attack samples, further demonstrating its robustness through fine-tuning.
417
        This is because its fine-tuning objective creates separable gap between human and AI rewrite ratios that is
418     large enough so that the attack samples remain in the AI distribution despite the perturbations. Concretely, the
419     average edit ratio of human texts is 0.6981, and of AI texts is 0.8606. After attack, the ratio for AI decreases
420     to 0.8386, which suggests that the rewrite attack is effective in shifting the AI distribution towards human,
421     but there still exists a clear gap between both distributions, so that L2R’s classification performance only
422     degrades marginally.

434   5.6   C OMPARE L2R WITH D IRECT F INE -T UNING
435
436   A valid concern regarding L2R’s superior performance is whether it is due to our fine-tuning objective, which
437   enhances model’s rewriting ability, or is solely from the fact that we exploit the vast parameters of an LLM.
438   To answer this question, we compare L2R with the ’Llama Logits’ baseline in Table 3 and 4. The Llama
439   logits detector involves fine-tuning a Llama-3-8B model not for rewrite, but directly for binary classification.
440   Previously, we show that despite the Llama classifier has the highest ID AUROC score among all detectors,
441   surpassing L2R by 7.65%, it has the lowest AUROC when evaluated OOD, up to 51.35% lower than L2R,
442   which suggests that its performance ID is due to overfitting. This highlights the importance of our fine-tuning
443   objective function in ensuring domain-agnostic detection accuracy. Also, the Llama classifier is inferior under
444   adversarial attacks, with 14.65% and 23.64% lower AUROC for decoherence and rewrite attacks, respectively.
445   This again shows L2R’s robustness in capturing the true underlying distributions of human and AI data.

      6     C ONCLUSION
464
465   We present L2R, a method designed to enhance the detection of LLM-generated text by learning to rewrite
466   more on LLM-generated inputs and less on human generated inputs. L2R excels in identifying LLM-generated
467   content collected across various models and 21 unique domains, both ID and OOD, and under adversarial
468   attacks. Our work demonstrates that LLMs can be trained to detect content generated by other LLMs,
469   surpassing previous detection methods in accuracy and generalizability.

740   A.5   E FFECTIVENESS OF THE C ALIBRATION L OSS
741
742   An important contribution of ours that improves the fine-tuning performance is the calibration loss, as
743   proposed in §3.4. Without this loss, the model tends to overfit during fine-tuning as shown in Figure 4, where
744
      the model loss drastically decrease after 1500 steps, resulting in verbose rewrite even for LLM-generated text.
      We conduct an ablation study on five domains where the AUROC score is only 0.62 after the model overfits.
745
      We hypothesized that this technique could benefit model learning because the threshold effectively prevents
746
      further modification to model weights once an input, labeled either AI or human, falls in its respectively
747   distribution already. Since our purpose is simply to draw a boundary rather than separate the distributions
748   as much as possible, this halt in further weight adjustments facilitates the model to only perform parameter
749   update on those inputs which are not yet correctly classified, so that it could converge more efficiently and
750   effectively. Concretely, applying the calibration loss improves detection performance by 4.54% in AUROC
751   among the 21 domains, even comparing with a model tuned with the loss before over-fitting.

791   A.7   E FFICIENCY OF L2R
792
      We compare the efficiency of L2R against Llama Logits measured by FLOPS. Llama Logits only requires a
793
      forward pass of the language model, while L2R requires decoding to generate the rewrites. The FLOPS of a
794   forward pass is 2N per token, where N is the number of parameters (Kaplan et al., 2020). Decoding is more
795   expensive due to attention computations, but the use of KV cache has reduced this to linear complexity with
796   respect to sequence length, thus the FLOPS per token remains approximately 2N (Pope et al., 2022). Overall,
797   we believe we shall not sacrifice robustness just to make the algorithm fast, and our generative method is
798   important to publish due to the improved robustness.
