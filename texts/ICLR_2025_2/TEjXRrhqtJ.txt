011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Explaining video data predictions is challenging due to the complex spatio-
014            temporal information in videos. In particular, the existing perturbation-based
015            methods for video interpretation often fail to consider different temporal contexts,
016
               making them ineffective for dynamic videos where the important regions change
               rapidly or appear ephemerally across frames. To address this, we propose a novel
017
               video interpretation method, time importance score-aware extremal perturbation
018
               masks (TIEM), that enhances explainability by focusing on temporal dynamics
019            in videos. TIEM exploits a dual perturbation process: first, it evaluates temporal
020            importance across frames via temporal perturbation and then generates spatio-
021            temporal extremal perturbation masks using the temporal importance explicitly.
022            Our experimental results demonstrate that TIEM resolves the key challenges of
023            the existing methods, providing more precise explanations across the time domain
024            in synthetic white-box models and black-box models for real-world videos.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Artificial intelligence (AI)-based predictive models have been widely used across various domains
030   such as healthcare, finance, autonomous driving, and video analysis, especially due to the powerful
031   predictive performance of deep neural network (DNN) models (LeCun et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2020;
032   Ashfaq et al., 2022). However, despite their widespread use, these DNN models are often referred
033
      to as “black-box models,” meaning that their internal workings are not transparent to humans. This
      lack of transparency makes it difficult to trust their predictions and therefore difficult to use them
034
      for decision-making as well (Guidotti et al., 2018; Buhrmester et al., 2021). To address this issue,
035
      a range of explainable AI (XAI) techniques are being developed that interpret and explain how
036   the predictive models work internally (Adadi & Berrada, 2018; Samek & Müller, 2019). A visual
037   explanation method, one of the representative XAI techniques, is used to explain black-box models
038   for images and videos by visualizing the input regions that influence the model’s output (Selvaraju
039   et al., 2017; Chattopadhay et al., 2018; Alicioglu & Sun, 2022).
040
      So far, research on visual explanation has focused primarily on interpreting the model’s predictions
041
      for a single image, which leads to visually clear explanations of the predictions(Adebayo et al.,
042   2018). However, visual explanation methods for a single image are difficult to directly apply to
043   prediction models that use video data as its input, since the video data is not a simple collection
044   of multiple images. The images that make up video data have complex recurrent spatio-temporal
045   dependencies, such as the order and connections between the images and the interactions between
046   objects within the images (Zhou et al., 2018). Therefore, to interpret the predictions for a video,
047   visual explanation methods need to consider not only the spatial information in the images, but also
048   the temporal information across the images.
049   To illustrate how temporal information in a video provides insight to humans, Fig. 1 shows the
050   frames composing a “tennis swing” video. The images presented at the top of the figure visualize
051   a video composed of the four frames arranged in the correct order of the original video, while the
052   images at the bottom visualize a video composed of the same frames, but their order is shuffled. In
053   the figure, the original video contains both spatial and temporal information across the four frames,
      allowing us to recognize it as a video of a “tennis swing.” On the other hand, the shuffled video

070   1.1   M OTIVATION AND C ONTRIBUTION
071
072
      In the recent development of XAI methods for video predictions, there have been significant efforts
      to consider the temporal information inherent in video data. To this end, the existing perturbation-
073
      based works (Li et al., 2021; Uchiyama et al., 2023) mainly attempt to address the temporal infor-
074
      mation in videos. They extend the visual explanation method for a single image by expanding the di-
075   mensions for the time domain, but not considering it explicitly. As shown in the example in Fig. 1, the
076   interactions of the spatial information between the consecutive frames are significant for the effective
077   estimation of the temporal information. To consider this observation, the existing works estimate the
078   temporal information by blending the spatial information of the adjacent multiple frames in the time
079   domain. As a result, this blending-based approach can generate a natural and smooth visual expla-
080   nation. In particular, it is effective in interpreting the predictions for gentle videos with low temporal
081   dynamics, in which the important regions across frames change gradually in the spatial domain, e.g.,
082   a video in which important objects in the video shift gently across the frames (Li et al., 2021).
083   However, such a blending-based approach makes the estimation of the temporal information highly
084   dependent on the spatial information across the adjacent frames. This may lead to an over-integration
085   of the spatial and temporal information and a failure to consider temporal contexts of different
086   lengths. As a result, the blending-based approach may be ineffective in interpreting the prediction
087   for dynamic videos with high temporal dynamics, in which the important regions change rapidly or
088   appear ephemerally across frames.

      Here, we classify the issues that may arise due to the limitation of the blending-based approach
102   into temporal concentration and temporal spillover. To clearly describe the issues, we provide an
103   example of a visual explanation for a synthetic white-box model by using STEP in Fig. 2. In the
104   visual explanation, the important regions are highlighted in green. The white-box model generates
105   random numbers for each pixel and computes predictions using only the numbers of the white re-
106   gions. Therefore, its ground truth for visual explanation is the white region in each frame. In the
107   example, the white-box model is designed so that the important regions change rapidly and appear
      ephemerally in frames 7 and 8, while the important regions gently shift across frames 4-6 and 9-13.

108
      Temporal concentration indicates a phenomenon in which the estimated important regions are
109   overly concentrated on specific frames that have an excessive influence on the prediction. This also
110   leads to the frames near the excessively influential frames being overlooked in the interpretation. In
111   the example, this issue is clearly shown as most of the estimated important regions are concentrated
112   in frames 7 and 8, leading to the neglect of the white regions in frames 4-6 and 9-13. In addition,
113   temporal spillover refers to a phenomenon in which the estimated important regions in one frame
114   spill over into its adjacent frames. This issue is observed in frames 6 and 9 of the example. In those
115   frames, the edges are estimated as important regions as in frames 7 and 8, even though they do
116   not lie within the ground truth region. In real-world applications, these issues can occur in videos,
117
      where a particular action is present in the video for only a short time, such as sports and accident
      safety. The results and discussion of the issues in real-world datasets are presented in Section 4.2.
118
119   In this paper, we propose a novel video interpretation method based on perturbation, called time
120   importance score-aware extremal perturbation masks (TIEM). To overcome the above issues, it con-
121   siders the temporal dimension separately, through dual perturbation. More specifically, TIEM first
122   analyzes how much each frame is significant for the prediction by using temporal perturbation, in
123
      terms of various window lengths. Then, it evaluates the time importance score (TIS) of each frame
      by combining the analyses of the frame for the window lengths. The TIS enables TIEM to estimate
124
      the temporal importance of each frame considering its temporal relations to the adjacent frames in
125
      different temporal contexts. Finally, TIEM finds a spatio-temporal visual explanation by conducting
126   the TIS-aware spatial analysis for each frame based on extremal perturbation. This novel dual pertur-
127   bation of TIEM not only avoids the over-integration of spatial and temporal information across the
128   adjacent frames but also estimates the temporal dynamics more precisely. Through the experiments
129   with the synthetic white-box model and the black-box model for real-world datasets, it is shown that
130   the proposed method resolves both temporal concentration and spillover. In addition, it outperforms
131   the state-of-the-art video interpretation method, especially in terms of temporal interpretation.

      3.1    OVERVIEW
177
178   We consider model-agnostic interpretation of video prediction models based on perturbation. In a
179   video prediction model, a video with T frames, each of width W and height H, is considered as
180   an input. The video is denoted by X = (xt )Tt=1 , where xt ∈ RH×W ×3 . The prediction model is
181   denoted by Φ, which can be either a classification model or a regression model. In the case of a
182   regression model, it is expressed as Φ(X) ∈ R, whereas in the case of a classification model, the
183   probability of class c, when the ground truth class of X is c out of the total C classes, is expressed
184   as Φc (X) ∈ R after passing through the softmax function. The objective of perturbation-based
185
      interpretation is to learn an importance map M = (mt )Tt=1 , where mt ∈ [0, 1]H×W , that visualizes
      the significant regions of X during prediction using Φ. Each element of mt , mi,j,t , corresponds to
186
      each pixel of frame t, xi,j,t , where i, j denote the spatial coordinates in each frame. The perturbation
187
      operation by the mask is expressed as M ⊗ X = M ◦ X + (1 − M) ◦ (k ∗ X), where ◦ denotes the
188   Hadamard product, ∗ represents the convolution operator, and k denotes a Gaussian blur kernel.

      dynamics-focused dual perturbation approach that utilizes both temporal and extremal perturba-
217   tions. It enhances the existing video interpretation methods by separating the learning structure for
218   the importance map into two distinct dimensions: temporal and spatial. In a TIS calculation phase,
219   TIEM appropriately calculates the temporal importance across frames, called the time importance
220   score (TIS), using temporal perturbation. Then, in an importance map generation phase, based on the

223   come the challenges of video interpretation by avoiding over-integration of the spatial and temporal

      4.3   D ISCUSSIONS
508
509   The proposed method, TIEM, can effectively interpret video predictions via its temporal dynamics-
510   focused dual perturbation. The experimental results demonstrate this strength, but TIEM can still be
511   enhanced in a variety of aspects. In particular, we observe a few limitations of TIEM in the results.
512
      The process for calculating the TIS can be improved and more sophisticated. In TIEM, the average
513
      importance of the windows to which a frame belongs is considered as the TIS of the frame as
514
      described in equation 4. This is a simple and effective way to represent the temporal importance of
515   each frame, but it makes limited use of the given windows. For example, an insignificant frame may
516   be overestimated if the frame is included in a long window containing significant frames as in frame
517   1 of the breaststroke video in Fig. 6. (The detailed analysis of the TIS is provided in the appendix.)
518   To avoid this, a more powerful temporal explanation of frames can be synthesized by considering
519   more complex connections between frames through the interaction of multiple windows.
520
      The TIS-aware importance map can be generated more precisely. TIEM fits the importance map
521   using the TIS of each frame. This effectively considers the temporal information across frames, but
522   it does not address spatial information appearing across frames continuously as shown in Fig. 6. As
523   a result, TIEM focuses well on the signature frames, but often extracts spatial information that is
524   discontinuous across the frames. Therefore, if we extend its importance map generation process to
525   exploit the spatial information of adjacent frames together, such as STEP, a more complete spatio-
526   temporal perturbation mask can be obtained.

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this paper, we have studied model-agnostic visual explanations for videos via perturbation. We
      have first examined the key challenges of the existing methods–specifically, temporal concentra-
532
      tion and temporal spillover–which are especially prevalent in dynamic videos. To address these is-
533
      sues, we proposed a novel method called TIEM, which utilizes a dual perturbation strategy focused
534   on temporal dynamics. This method incorporates temporal perturbation to evaluate the TIS across
535   frames in a video and extremal perturbation to generate a TIS-aware importance map for the video.
536   The dual perturbation strategy enables TIEM to effectively capture the temporal dynamics within a
537   video by explicitly exploiting the TIS of a video when fitting the importance map for the video. Our
538   experiments with synthetic and real-world videos demonstrated that TIEM outperforms the existing
539   methods. In particular, it is clearly shown that the concept of dual perturbation in TIEM mitigates
      the key challenges of the existing methods.
