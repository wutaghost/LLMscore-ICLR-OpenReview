009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Image denoising techniques based on deep learning often struggle with poor gen-
012            eralization performance to out-of-distribution real-world noise. To tackle this
013            challenge, we propose a novel noise translation framework that performs denois-
014            ing on an image with translated noise rather than directly denoising an original
015            noisy image. Specifically, our approach translates complex, unknown real-world
016            noise into Gaussian noise, which is spatially uncorrelated and independent of im-
017            age content, through a noise translation network. The translated noisy images
018
               are then processed by an image denoising network pretrained to effectively re-
               move Gaussian noise, enabling robust and consistent denoising performance. We
019
               also design well-motivated loss functions and architectures for the noise transla-
020
               tion network by leveraging the mathematical properties of Gaussian noise. Ex-
021            perimental results demonstrate that the proposed method substantially improves
022            robustness and generalizability, outperforming state-of-the-art methods across di-
023            verse benchmarks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Image denoising aims to restore the pure signal from noisy images and serves as a critical prepro-
029   cessing step to improve the visual quality of input images, extending the applicability of various
030   downstream tasks. Recent advances in deep learning have significantly improved the performance
031   of image denoising models (Zhang et al., 2017; 2018; Guo et al., 2019; Zamir et al., 2020; 2022b;a;
032   Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). A common assumption in early approaches was that camera
033
      noise could be modeled as Gaussian noise (Mao et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2017; 2018), which sim-
      plified the process of generating noisy-clean image pairs by adding synthetic Gaussian noise. This
034
      allowed for the creation of large datasets that could be used to train denoising models in a supervised
035
      manner, playing a crucial role in advancing the development of denoising models.
036
037   Although these models trained on synthetic dataset perform well under controlled environments,
038   they often struggle to generalize to real-world scenarios due to the fundamental differences between
039
      synthetic and real noise distributions (Guo et al., 2019). In response, researchers have collected
      clean-noisy image pairs from real images (Abdelhamed et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2019)
040
      to address realistic noises, but models trained on this data still tend to overfit to the specific noise-
041
      signal correlations present in the training data. Capturing the full spectrum of noise distributions in
042   real world images is impractical and even unrealistic.
043
044   To address this challenge, we propose a novel noise translation framework for image denoising to
045
      better generalize to diverse real-world noise using a limited training dataset. The intuition behind
      our framework is as follows. While existing denoising algorithms trained on images with Gaussian
046
      noise exhibit limited performance when applied to real noisy images, we observed that adding Gaus-
047
      sian noise to these noisy images significantly improves their effectiveness in denoising, as shown
048   in Figure 1. This observation motivated us to explore the idea that, instead of directly denoising
049   unseen real noise, first translating it into known Gaussian noise and then applying denoising could
050   improve the model’s ability to generalize across unseen and OOD noise. To this end, we introduce
051   the lightweight Noise Translation Network, which, prior to the denoising process, utilizes Gaussian
052   injection blocks to transform arbitrary complex noise into Gaussian noise that is spatially uncorre-
053   lated and independent of an input image. The translated images are then processed by the pretrained
      denoising networks specialized for Gaussian noise, resulting in the clean denoised images. Our ex-

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
087
088
      Image denoising In recent years, deep learning has led to significant progress in image de-
089   noising, achieving impressive results by leveraging paired noisy and clean images for training.
090   DnCNN (Zhang et al., 2017) pioneered the use of CNNs for image denoising, which paved the
091   way for further advancements involving residual learning (Gu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Zhang
092   et al., 2019), attention mechanisms (Liu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019), and transformer mod-
093   els (Zamir et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2024). Despite its success, acquiring the noisy-clean pairs
094   required for supervised training remains a significant challenge. To address this, self-supervised ap-
095   proaches (Lehtinen et al., 2018; Krull et al., 2019; Batson & Royer, 2019; Pang et al., 2021; Li et al.,
096   2023) have emerged to train networks using only noisy images, but these models typically perform
097
      considerably worse than their supervised counterparts. Additionally, zero-shot approaches (Quan
      et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021; Mansour & Heckel, 2023) have been proposed for image denoising
098
      even without training dataset, but they require substantial computational cost at inference, making
099
      them impractical for real-time applications. In contrast to these methods that aim to reduce depen-
100   dency on supervised data, out approach leverages supervised data but focuses on achieving good
101   generalization performance with a limited amount of data.
102
103   Generalization for denoising Generalization is a critical challenge in image denoising, as the
104   performance of denoising models often degrades when encountering noise characteristics that were
105   not seen during training. To handle unseen noise type or levels, DnCNN (Zhang et al., 2017) em-
106   ployed blind Gaussian training to adapt to various noise levels, while Mohan et al. (2020) designed
107   a bias-free network to prevent overfitting to noise levels in the training set. More recent works em-
      ployed masking-based learning (Chen et al., 2023) or leverage the pre-trained CLIP encoder (Cheng


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2024) to prevent overfitting by encouraging the model to understand global context rather than
109   relying on local patterns. While these approaches enhance robustness to unseen noise, they often
110   struggle to produce high-quality image restoration, particularly in complex real-world scenarios.
111
112
      To address real-world noise, researchers have focused on constructing training datasets that closely
      resemble real noise distribution. This includes collecting real clean-noise image pairs (Abdelhamed
113
      et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2018; Yue et al., 2019) and learning to generate realistic noise through data
114
      augmentation (Jang et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2021) or adversarial attacks (Yan et al., 2022; Ryou
115   et al., 2024). However, these approaches are limited to the noise distributions represented in the
116   training dataset and fail to generalize effectively to unseen OOD noise. Our approach overcomes
117   this limitation by incorporating a noise translation process that transforms the complex distribution
118   of real noise into a known Gaussian distribution, improving performance on OOD data significantly.

      3     M ETHOD

      In this section, we present a robust image denoising framework featuring a novel noise translation
123   process, designed to effectively handle diverse unseen noise. Our framework consists of: (1) a noise
124   translation network to transform the arbitrary noise in an input image into ideal Gaussian noise, and
125   (2) a denoising network to remove the translated noise to produce a clean output.
126
127   3.1     I MAGE D ENOISING N ETWORK
128
129   First of all, we train a denoising network as follows. Our image denoising network aims to recover
130   a clean image from a noisy input, which can be mathematically formulated as
131
                                                  Iˆ = D(I; θ),                                          (1)
132
133   where D(·; θ) denotes an image denoising network parameterized by θ, and I, Iˆ ∈ RH×W ×C
134   represents a noisy input image and its corresponding denoised output, respectively.
135                                                                                                   ˆ
      The goal of supervised training for our denoising network is to ensure that the denoised output I,
136   to closely match the ground-truth clean image IGT . To achieve this, the model parameters θ are
137   optimized by minimizing the following loss function:
138
                                             L = ∥D(I; θ) − IGT ∥.                                       (2)
139
140
      Our method is model agnostic, allowing us to use existing denoising models and focus only on how
141   to effectively remove Gaussian noise. To train our image denoising network to eliminate Gaussian
142   noise, we utilize a training dataset consisting of clean images paired with their corrupted versions
143   with synthetic additive Gaussian noise. We additionally use Gaussian-augmented real noisy-clean
144   image pairs, where the noisy images are further corrupted with Gaussian noise. In our framework,
145   our image denoising network is optimized with the loss function in (2) to make it specialized in
146   removing Gaussian noise.

248   3.2.3    G AUSSIAN I NJECTION B LOCK
249
      As illustrated in Figure 2, our noise translation network is built upon a lightweight U-Net architec-
250   ture, where each layer is composed of Gaussian Injection Blocks (GIBlock). GIBlock incorporates
251   a Nonlinear Activation-Free (NAF) block from NAFNet (Chen et al., 2022) along with our key idea
252   to align the discrepancy between training and inference stage: Gaussian noise injection.
253
      In the training stage of noise translation network, random levels of Gaussian noise is augmented to an
254
      input image I to address diverse noise conditions, enhancing the robustness of the noise translation
255
      network. In contrast, in inference stage, noisy input images are given directly to the noise translation
256   network without adding extra Gaussian noise, because the direct noise augmentation to the input
257   images degrades output quality. To establish a consistent Gaussian noise prior in both the training
258   and inference stage, we inject Gaussian noise into every intermediate block of the noise translation
259   network. Since the noise translation network is designed based on U-Net with residual connections
260   between the input I and the output IT , the distortion of the signal caused by injected Gaussian
261   noise is alleviated, while allowing the noise translation network to utilize the Gaussian prior for
262   transforming unseen real noise. Our ablation studies further demonstrate that the proposed Gaussian
263   noise injection is crucial for the noise translation network to effectively translate unseen noise into
264
      Gaussian noise during the inference stage.

299   Training details Since our approach is model-agnostic, we employ existing architectures such as
300   NAFNet (Chen et al., 2022), Restormer (Zamir et al., 2022a), and KBNet (Zhang et al., 2023) as our
301   image denoising network, which is pretrained on BSD400 (Martin et al., 2001), WED (Ma et al.,
302   2016), and SIDD medium (Abdelhamed et al., 2018) datasets. BSD400 and WED datasets consist of
303   clean images only, while SIDD dataset is composed of real noisy-clean image pairs. During training
304   a denoising network, noisy images are generated by adding Gaussian noise with a standard deviation
305   of 15 to clean images of BSD400 and WED datasets, and noisy images of SIDD datasets. Each
306   training batch consists of images drawn equally from two sources: half from the combined BSD400
307
      and WED datasets, and the other half from the SIDD dataset. The denoising models are trained for
      200K iterations with a batch size of 32, except for Restormer, where the batch size is reduced to 4
308
      due to the limitation of computational resources. After training a denoising network, we train the
309
      noise translation network with the SIDD dataset only, where noisy images are augmented by adding
310   stochastic level of Gaussian noise with a range of 0 to 15. The noise translation network is trained
311   for 5K iterations with a batch size of 4. Both the image denoising network and noise translation
312   network adopt the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with an initial learning rate of
313   10−3 , which is reduced using a cosine annealing schedule, down to 10−7 and 10−5 , respectively.
314   Each image is randomly cropped to 256 × 256 for training. All trainings were conducted using two
315   NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs.

      5        C ONCLUSION
517
518
      We presented a novel noise translation framework for robust image denoising. Our framework
      allows us to effectively remove various unseen real noise, even with limited amount of training
519
      data. By employing the noise translation network, we transform arbitrary out-of-distribution (OOD)
520
      noise into Gaussian noise for which our image denoising network has learned during training. The
521   noise translation network is designed with well-motivated loss functions and architecture, enabling
522   effective noise translation while preserving image contents. Our experiments demonstrate that the
523   proposed approach significantly outperforms state-of-the-art denoising models in diverse OOD real-
524   noise benchmarks. Finally, we highlight that the generalization issue remains a critical challenge in
525   image denoising, and our approach offers a promising solution to address this problem.
