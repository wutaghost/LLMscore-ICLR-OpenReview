009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision Language Models (VLMs) have revolutionized the creation of general-
012            ist web agents, empowering them to autonomously complete diverse tasks on
013            real-world websites, thereby boosting human efficiency and productivity. How-
014            ever, despite their remarkable capabilities, the safety and security of these agents
015            against malicious attacks remain critically underexplored, raising significant con-
016
               cerns about their safe deployment. To uncover and exploit such vulnerabilities in
               web agents, we provide AdvWeb, a novel black-box attack framework designed
017
               against web agents. AdvWeb trains an adversarial prompter model that gener-
018
               ates and injects adversarial prompts into web pages, misleading web agents into
019            executing targeted adversarial actions such as inappropriate stock purchases or er-
020            roneous bank transactions—actions that could lead to severe consequences. With
021            only black-box access to the web agent, we train and optimize the adversarial
022            prompter model using Direct Policy Optimization (DPO), leveraging both suc-
023            cessful and failed attack strings against the target agent. Unlike prior approaches,
024            our adversarial string injection maintains stealth and control: (1) the appearance
025            of the website remains unchanged before and after the attack, making it nearly
026            impossible for users to detect tampering, and (2) attackers can modify specific
027
               substrings within the generated adversarial string to seamlessly change the attack
               objective (e.g., purchasing stocks from a different company), greatly enhancing
028
               attack flexibility and efficiency. We conduct extensive evaluations, demonstrating
029
               that AdvWeb achieves high success rates in attacking state-of-the-art GPT-4V-
030            based VLM agents across various web tasks in black-box settings. Our findings
031            expose critical vulnerabilities in current LLM/VLM-based agents, emphasizing
032            the urgent need for developing more reliable web agents and implementing effec-
033            tive defenses against such adversarial threats.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The rapid evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) has
039
      enabled the development of generalist web agents, which are capable of autonomously interacting
      with real-world websites and executing tasks, such as making stock purchases, performing health-
040
      care operations, or handling financial transactions (Nakano et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b; Yao et al.,
041
      2022). These agents, by leveraging tools, APIs, and complex website interactions, hold tremen-
042   dous potential for enhancing human productivity across various domains like finance, healthcare,
043   and e-commerce (Zhou et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). Despite their success,
044   these agents also bring unprecedented security challenges, especially in terms of their robustness to
045   malicious adversarial attacks, which remains underexplored in existing literature.
046
      Recent works have proposed adversarial attacks against generalist web agents to uncover vulnerabil-
047
      ities before real-world deployment (Yang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a). However,
048   existing approaches are either limited by high attack costs, requiring human effort in manually de-
049   signing the attack prompts (Wu et al., 2024b; Liao et al., 2024), or focused primarily on individual
050   attack scenarios (Mo et al., 2024), leaving gaps in developing more efficient and adaptable attack
051   frameworks for web agents. Many adversarial attacks against LLMs and VLMs have also been
052   proposed to automatically optimize the attack prompts (Guo et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). How-
053   ever, they can not be flexibly adapted to attack VLM-based agents (Zou et al., 2023) and struggle to
      achieve transferability to our black-box attack setting (Liu et al., 2024c).

      To address these challenges, we propose AdvWeb, a novel black-box controllable attacking frame-
074
      work specifically designed to exploit vulnerabilities in generalist web agents. Our approach gener-
075   ates and injects invisible adversarial strings into web pages, misleading the agents into performing
076   targeted adversarial actions, such as executing erroneous financial transactions or purchasing in-
077   correct stocks, which can lead to significant consequences. By using Direct Policy Optimization
078   (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), AdvWeb optimizes adversarial string generation based on both suc-
079   cessful and unsuccessful attacks against the black-box web agent, allowing for efficient and flexible
080   attacks. Notably, AdvWeb enables attackers to easily control and modify adversarial strings without
081   re-optimizing them, making it possible to achieve different attack goals, such as targeting different
082   companies or actions, with minimal additional effort.
083   To evaluate the effectiveness of AdvWeb, we test our approach extensively against SeeAct (Zheng
084   et al., 2024), a state-of-the-art (SOTA) VLM-based web agent framework, on various web tasks in a
085   black-box setting. Our results demonstrate that AdvWeb is highly effective, achieving a 97.5% at-
086   tack success rate against GPT-4V-based SeeAct across different website domains and various tasks,
087   significantly outperforming baseline methods. Our attacks also exhibit strong controllability, with
088   a 98.5% attack success rate after changing the attack targets without further optimizations. Exper-
089
      iments also show that our stealthy attack strings can be flexibly hidden in different HTML fields
      while maintaining high attack success rates. These findings highlight the vulnerability of current
090
      LLM/VLM-based agents and underscore the need for developing more robust defenses to safeguard
091
      their deployment in the real world.
092
093   Our key contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We propose AdvWeb, the first black-box
094   targeted attacking framework against VLM-based web agents, which trains a generative model to
095
      automatically generate adversarial prompts injected into the HTML contents. (2) We propose a two-
      stage training paradigm that incorporates reinforcement learning (RL) from the black-box feedback
096
      of the victim agents to optimize the adversarial string. (3) We perform real-world attacks against
097
      SOTA web agent on 440 tasks across 4 different domains. We show that our attack is effective,
098   achieving an attack success rate of 97.5%. Our adversarial strings are also highly controllable,
099   with a 98.5% transfer-based attack success rate to different attack targets. (4) We conduct a series of
100   ablation studies and show that the proposed training framework is crucial and effective for black-box
101   attacks. Our generated adversarial strings can also be robustly adapted to different attack settings,
102   achieving near 97.0% attack success rate when we vary different HTML fields.

104   2    R ELATED WORK
105   Adversarial Attack on LLM. Many approaches have been proposed to jailbreak aligned LLMs,
106   encouraging them to generate harmful content or answer malicious questions. Due to the discrete
107   nature of tokens, optimizing these attacks is more challenging than in image-based attacks (Carlini
      et al., 2024). Early works (Ebrahimi et al., 2018; Wallace et al., 2019; Shin et al., 2020) optimize

108
      input-agnostic token sequences to elicit specific predictions or generate harmful outputs, leveraging
109   greedy search or gradient information to modify influential tokens. Later, ARCA (Jones et al., 2023)
110   refines these techniques by simultaneously assessing the impact of multiple token swaps. The GCG
111   Attack (Zou et al., 2023) then successfully optimized suffixes to elicit affirmative responses, making
112   attacks more effective. However, the adversarial strings generated by all previous works are unread-
113   able and are easily detected by perplexity-based detectors. AutoDan (Liu et al., 2024c) improves
114   the stealthy of the generated adversarial prompts by leveraging a carefully designed hierarchical
115   genetic algorithm that maintains semantic meaningfulness. Additionally, AmpleGCG (Liao & Sun,
116   2024) and AdvPrompter (Paulus et al., 2024) directly employ generated models to generate adver-
117
      sarial suffixes without relying on gradient-based optimization. However, these attacks are primarily
      enforced towards simple objectives (e.g., eliciting confirmative responses to harmful queries) and
118
      are no longer effective against more complex attack objectives on VLM-powered web agents. To
119
      address this limitation, we present the first attack framework capable of handling diverse and com-
120   plex objectives (e.g., manipulating a stock purchase decision) while maintaining stealthiness and
121   controllability.
122
123
      Web Agents. As LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) and
      VLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023) rapidly evolve, their capabilities
124
      have significantly expanded, particularly in leveraging visual perception, complex reasoning, and
125
      planning to assist with daily tasks. Some works (Nakano et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2024b) build gen-
126   eralist web agents by leveraging the LLMs augmented with retrieval capabilities over the websites,
127   which is useful for information seeking. Recent works (Yao et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Deng
128   et al., 2024) have developed web agents that take raw HTML content as input and can directly
129   perform tasks in simulated or realistic web environments based on human instructions. However,
130   HTML content can be noisier compared to the rendered visuals used in human web browsing and
131   provides lower information density, which leads to low task success rates and limited deployment in
132   practice. To fully leverage the model capabilities, SeeAct (Zheng et al., 2024) proposes a generalist
133   web agent framework that consists of a two-stage pipeline and incorporates rendered screenshots as
134
      input, yielding stronger reasoning and achieving SOTA task completion performances. Therefore,
      in this work, we focus on attacking SeeAct as our target agent. However, it is important to note that
135
      our proposed attack strategies are readily applicable to all web agents that use webpage screenshots
136
      and/or HTML content as input.
137
138   Existing Attacks against Web Agents. To the best of our knowledge, there exists only a limited
139   body of research examining potential attacks against web agents. Yang et al. (2024) and Wang et al.
140
      (2024) investigate the insertion of backdoor triggers into web agents through fine-tuning backbone
      models with white-box access, aiming to mislead agents into making incorrect purchase decisions.
141
      Wu et al. (2024b) and Liao et al. (2024) manipulate the web agents by injecting malicious instruc-
142
      tions into the web contents, misleading the agent to execute the indirect prompts, leading to wrong
143   results or privacy leakage. However, the malicious instructions are manually designed and written
144   with heuristics, leading to limited scalability and flexibility. Wu et al. (2024a) shares a similar spirit
145   with us by focusing on automatically optimizing adversarial input to mislead the web agents. How-
146   ever, they either require white-box access to the target agent to perform gradient-based optimization
147   or have limited attack success rates by transferring successful attacks on multiple CLIP models to
148   proprietary VLM-based agents. In contrast, our work attacks the web agents in a black-box set-
149   ting. By leveraging reinforcement learning to learn from both positive and negative feedback of the
150   black-box model, we train a generative model to generate the adversarial strings that can efficiently
151
      and flexibly attack the web agents to perform targeted actions.

      3.2   T HREAT M ODEL
174   Attack Objective. We consider targeted attacks against the web agents that change the agent’s ac-
175   tion to a targeted adversarial action aadv = (o, radv , e) that contains a targeted adversarial argument
176   while keeping the operation the same. This attack can lead to severe consequences since the agent
177   will proceed with the normal operation but with a wrong malicious argument. For example, a re-
178   quest to buy Microsoft (r) stocks can be attacked to buy NVIDIA (radv ) stocks instead, leading to
179   huge financial losses to the user.
180   Environment Access and Attack Scenarios. Since most web agents are powered by proprietary
181   VLMs, we consider the black-box setting where the attacker does not have access to the agent
182   framework, the backend model weights, or the backend model logits. The attacker only has access
183   to the HTML content h in the website, and the only capability is limited to altering h to hadv . This
184   setting is realistic among various attack scenarios. For example, a malicious website developer can
185   make profits from intentionally modifying the contents in the website during routine maintenance
186   or website updates, compromising the user safety. Moreover, such attacks can also happen when
187   a benign website developer unconsciously uses contaminated libraries to build the webpages, as
188
      demonstrated in a recent report from CISA (Synopsys, 2024), where the resulting websites may
      contain hidden but exploitable vulnerabilities.
189
190   Attack Constraints. In order to improve the attack efficiency, we additionally require the adversar-
191   ial attack to be both stealthy and controllable. For the stealthiness requirement, since the rendered
192   screenshot i = I(h) is influenced by the HTML contents h, it is crucial for the attack to remain
193   undetectable by users. Therefore, we impose a constraint on the attack that the rendered image must
194
      remain unchanged even after the attack on the HTML contents. Formally, this constraint is expressed
      as I(h) = I(hadv ), ensuring that any modification to h does not affect the visual information per-
195
      ceived by the user. Regarding the controllability constraint, for an effective attack strategy, it is
196
      crucial that the attacker can swiftly adapt to a new adversarial target by simply modifying the adver-
197   sarial prompt, without needing further interaction and optimization with the agent. Formally, if the
198   target action triplet needs to be altered from aadv = (o, radv , e) to a′adv = (o, radv

236   4     A D V W E B : C ONTROLLABLE B LACK - BOX ATTACKS ON W EB AGENTS
237
238   AdvWeb is an advanced attacking framework leveraging reinforcement learning from AI feedback
239   (RLAIF), specifically designed and optimized for adversarial attacks against black-box web agents.
240   AdvWeb first effectively reduces the search space of adversarial HTML contents hadv that satisfy
241   the attack constraints. Since we only have black-box access, we then employ RL algorithms to train
242   a generative model to generate the adversarial strings added to the static HTML contents, optimizing
243   the targeted attack objective described in Section 3.2. Unlike existing attacking algorithms against
244   LLMs (Deng et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Paulus et al., 2024), our proposed framework uniquely
245   incorporates both positive and negative feedback signals from target black-box models. This
246
      dual-signal approach improves the learning process, which enables the model to capture and exploit
      nuance attack patterns that are characteristic and effective against the sophisticated black-box web
247
      agent, achieving high attack success rates in the targeted attack. In Section 4.1, we detail the process
248
      of our automated data generation pipeline, which facilitates the efficient collection of training data.
249   Furthermore, in Section 4.2, we introduce a novel RLAIF-based training paradigm, which is critical
250   to help the model learn from the nuance attack patterns. The model trained with this innovative
251   training methodology is highly effective in attacking web agents, which generate adversarial strings
252   that can mislead the web agent to perform target actions.

254   4.1    AUTOMATIC ATTACK AND FEEDBACK COLLECTION
255
256   Adversarial HTML Content Design. The search space of adversarial HTML contents hadv is
257   high-dimensional and discrete, with complex constraints including stealthiness and controllability
258   given by the screenshot rendering process I and the substitution function D, respectively. To im-
259   prove the optimization efficiency, eliminate the stealthiness constraint, and make the optimization
260   of controllability tractable, we reduce the search space of hadv with a specific design. Concretely,
261
      we choose to inject a segment of prompt q into benign HTML contents h to create the adversarial
      version hadv . To ensure that the injected prompt q remains invisible in the rendered website image,
262
      we hide q within certain HTML fields or attributes (e.g., “aria-label” = q), such that the injected
263
      prompt will not be rendered on the website. Additionally, to ensure the prompt pattern is control-
264   lable and transferable to different target actions via direct substitution operations (i.e., the D(·, ·, ·)
265   function), we embed placeholders (e.g., “{target argument}”) for the target argument in the injected
266   prompt q and train the model to first generate a prompt template with placeholders and then fill in
267   the desired attack targets. We also fix the injection position at the ground truth HTML element e
268   to further reduce the search space. By leveraging HTML hiding techniques with specific fields and
269   placeholders, we effectively reduce the search space, satisfy the stealthiness constraint, and simplify
      the optimization process for achieving controllability.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
333
334   Victim Web Agent. To demonstrate the effectiveness of AdvWeb, we employ SeeAct (Zheng et al.,
335   2024), a state-of-the-art web agent powered by different proprietary VLMs (Achiam et al., 2023;
336   Team et al., 2023). SeeAct operates by first generating an action description based on the current
337   task and the webpage screenshot. It then maps this description to the corresponding HTML contents
338   to interact with the web environment.
339   Dataset and Metrics. Our experiments utilize the Mind2Web dataset (Deng et al., 2024), which
340   comprises real-world website data for evaluating LLM/VLM-based agents. This dataset includes
341   2, 350 tasks from 137 websites across 31 domains. We select those tasks that involve critical events
342   that lead to severe consequences. Specifically, we focus on a subset of 440 tasks across 4 different
343   domains. We further divide the subset into 240 training tasks and 200 testing tasks. We use attack
344   success rate (ASR) as our evaluation metric to evaluate the effectiveness of the attack. An attack is
345   successful if and only if the action given by the agent matches exactly our targeted adversarial action
346   triplet aadv = (o, radv , e), where the agent must select the correct HTML element and perform the
347
      correct operation.
348   Implementation Details. For the LLM-based attack prompter, we leverage GPT-4 as the backend
349   and generate 10 adversarial prompts per task with a temperature of 1 to ensure diversity. We initialize
350   our generative adversarial prompter model from Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2. During SFT
351   in the first training stage, we set a learning rate of 1e − 4 and a batch size of 32. For DPO in the
352   second training stage, the learning rate is maintained at 1e − 4, but the batch size is reduced to 16.
353   Baselines. Since there is no existing black-box attack against web agents that work in our set-
354   ting, we adapt the following four SOTA attacks against LLMs/VLMs to our setting. (1) GCG (Zou
355   et al., 2023) is a white-box adversarial attack that optimizes an adversarial suffix string leveraging
356   the token-level gradient from the target model. In our black-box setting, we follow common prac-
357   tice (Wu et al., 2024a) to optimize the adversarial string against strong open-source VLM, LLaVA-
358   NeXT (Liu et al., 2024a), and transfer the attack to our agent. (2) AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024c) is a
359
      white-box attack that leverages the logits of the target model to optimize the adversarial suffix using
      genetic algorithms. We follow similar setting to optimize the adversarial prompts against LLaVA-
360
      NeXT and transfer the attack to our model. (3) COLD-Attack (Guo et al., 2024) is an algorithm
361
      that adapts energy-based constrained decoding with Langevin dynamics, which also requires white-
362   box access to model gradients. The algorithm generates fluent and stealthy adversarial prompts by
363   introducing corresponding energy functions. (4) Catastrophic Jailbreak (Huang et al., 2024) is a
364   black-box attacking algorithm that focuses on manipulating variations in decoding methods to dis-
365   rupt model alignment. By removing the system prompt, varying decoding hyper-parameters, and
366   sampling methods, it enables attacks on the model with minimal computational overhead. In our
367   setting, the attacker does not have access to the agent prompt, we therefore adopt the decoding
368   hyper-parameter variation as our baseline.

      To uncover the vulnerabilities of VLM-powered web agents in the real world, we propose the first
532
      controllable black-box targeted attack against web agents under various real-world tasks across dif-
533
      ferent domains. We show that the proposed attack AdvWeb achieves much higher attack success
534   rates than baselines against web agents powered by different proprietary VLMs as backends. Be-
535   sides, the proposed attack AdvWeb is controllable that does not require optimizing a new adversarial
536   string each time we change the attack target – only replacing the corresponding sub-string of the at-
537   tack target (e.g., replace NVIDIA by Apple in the stock purchasing task). Despite some limitations
538   as we discuss in Appendix C, we believe our proposed attacking framework can underscore the im-
539   portance of web agent safety, highlighting the need to explore effective defense approaches against
      such powerful adversarial attacks.
