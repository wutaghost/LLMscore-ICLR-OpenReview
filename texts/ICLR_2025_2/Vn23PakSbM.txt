009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advances show that large language models (LLMs) generalize strong per-
012            formance across different natural language benchmarks. However, the large
013            size of LLMs makes training and inference expensive and impractical to run in
014            resource-limited settings. This paper introduces a new approach called fine-tuning
015            stacks of language models (FSLM), which involves stacking small language mod-
016            els (SLM) as an alternative to LLMs. By fine-tuning each SLM to perform a
017            specific task, this approach breaks down high level reasoning into multiple lower-
018            level steps that specific SLMs are responsible for. As a result, FSLM allows for
019            lower training and inference costs, and also improves model interpretability as
020
               each SLM communicates with the subsequent one through natural language. By
               evaluating FSLM on common natural language benchmarks, this paper highlights
021
               promising early results toward generalizable performance using FSLM as a cost-
022
               effective alternative to LLMs.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Since the publication of the transformer paper Vaswani et al. (2017), a considerable amount of
029   research devoted to large language models (LLMs) has shown that LLMs are capable of generalizing
030   well on natural language benchmarks and that new emergent properties appear as LLMs increase in
031   scale. Devlin et al. (2019); Wei et al. (2022). LLMs seem to follow some empirical scaling laws,
032
      where larger datasets, compute and model size contribute to improvements in model performance.
      Kaplan et al. (2020)
033
034   As language models and datasets increase in size, a growing need emerges to identify methods to run
035   language models in resource-limited settings where large amounts of compute are inaccessible. In
036   fact, multiple methods have been documented and researched in recent years to make LLM training
037   or inference more computationally efficient. One such method is fine-tuning: given a pre-trained
038
      model, fine-tuning that model for specific tasks can cause that model to score better on benchmarked
      tasks downstream. Brown et al. (2020) Furthermore, more efficient methods of fine-tuning such as
039
      LoRA and QLoRA also show that adding a trainable adapter to LLMs whose weights are frozen
040
      also allows for faster fine-tuning while showing strong signs of solid model performance. Hu et al.
041   (2021); Dettmers et al. (2023)
042
043   Additionally, recent work indicates that small language models (SLM), such as Microsoft’s Phi-3,
044
      can still achieve decent performance on natural language benchmarks. This finding is important, as
      it suggest that small language models, which are a few orders of magnitude smaller than state-of-
045
      the-art LLMs, can still achieve solid performance on various benchmarks. Abdin et al. (2024)
046
047   This paper aims to build on both the fine-tuning and small language model directions, in order to
048   identify methods that allow for cost-effective training and inference in resource-limited settings. As
049   a result, this paper proposes a new model framework called Fine-tuning Stacks of Language Mod-
050
      els (FSLM) - or ”stacking” - which involves chaining multiple specialized small language models
      together such that the framework’s input and output resemble those of performant language models.
051
052   FSLM takes loose inspiration from the human brain, where different components specialize in dif-
053   ferent tasks. For small language models, because each SLM has limited capabilities due to its small
      size, FLSM aims to fine-tune each SLM to specialize in a specific task. As a result, the motivat-

      ing question becomes: how small can the SLMs be, such that the fine-tuned stack of SLMs is still
055   capable of generalizing on various natural language benchmarks?
056
057   Our work challenges the lower-bound for SLM size by evaluating an FSLM stack of four Pythia
058
      models of 160 million parameters each. Biderman et al. (2023) By fine-tuning this FSLM stack
      on the Alpaca dataset, and benchmarking FSLM and models of similar size, this paper shows that
059
      FSLM stacks show promise as lightweight alternatives to heavier LLMs.
060
061   Thus, this paper’s contributions can be summarized as:
062
063          • Proposing the FSLM stack as a lightweight framework to evaluate small language models
064            in resource-limited settings.
065          • Introducing model distillation to fine-tune SLMs in order to minimize the need for human
066            supervision or labeling.
067
             • Identifying early signs of FSLM generalizability by comparing FSLM of Pythia-160M
068            models with Pythia and Flan models of comparable sizes
069
070          • Documenting model explainability by looking at the intermediary outputs between SLMs
071
               in the FSLM stack.

073   2     R ELATED W ORK
074
075   2.1   M ODEL F INE -T UNING
076
077   In recent years, researchers have shown that pre-training a language model in a self-supervised
078   fashion, followed by fine-tuning that same model to a variety of tasks, improves model perfor-
079   mance downstream on natural language benchmarks. OpenAI’s GPT is a notable example of fine-
080   tuning a pre-trained model. Brown et al. (2020) Because fine-tuning entire models is expensive,
081   researchers have developed different methods to minimize computational cost while still achieving
082
      similar model performance.
083   Hu et al. (2021) introduced Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) as a fine-tuning approach. LoRA
084   freezes the weights of the original pre-trained model, and adds an ”adapter” component, located
085   between the original model output and the actual text output. Instead of the adapter being a fully
086   connected layer, the adapter uses matrix factorization to generate low-rank matrix multiplications
087   that approximate the fully connected equivalent. Low-rank matrix multiplication, however, is less
088
      computationally expensive than running inference on a fully connected layer. Hu et al. (2021) then
      show that LoRA can maintain or even improve model performance. Dettmers et al. (2023) devel-
089
      oped QLoRA, which performs quantization to further improve LoRA. Both QLoRA and LoRA are
090
      considered to be Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, a group of methods that aim
091   to increase the efficiency of fine-tuning models. Xu et al. (2023)
092
093
      2.2   M ODEL C OMPRESSION
094
095   Model compression techniques aim to either shrink a given model’s size, or to train a smaller model
096   to learn from a larger one.
097
098
      For instance, quantization reduces the precision of the model weights, thus decreasing the overall
      size of the model. Even though the model loses precision, if quantization is implemented correctly,
099
      the model should maintain a similar level of performance while experiencing a speedup for training
100
      and inference. Jacob et al. (2017)
101
102   Model pruning removes weights whose values are close to zero, thus eliminating weights that may
103   not be contributing to the model’s main inference. Cheng et al. (2024)
104   Model distillation is another method of interest: using a teacher-student architecture, a smaller
105   ”student” model learns from a larger ”teacher” model that should be already well-trained. As a
106   result, the teacher model distills its internal knowledge to the student model, by providing the student
107   model inputs and outputs to learn from during this training process. Hinton et al. (2015); Sanh et al.
      (2020)

142   3.1   FSLM M ETHOD OVERVIEW
143
144
      The FSLM framework consists of four small language models (SLM) that each specialize in a spe-
      cific task, as shown in Fig. 1. A human user would supply a prompt to the FSLM framework, and
145
      the FSLM framework responds with a textual output. Internally, the SLMs look for specific textual
146
      elements from either the user’s input or another SLM’s output. As a result, each individual SLM
147   is compensating for its limited capabilities by instead specializing in a specific task. As a result,
148   the overall framework follows an information flow where textual information is slowly processed
149   towards the intended model output.
150
151
      3.2   C HOICE OF M ODELS
152
153   We use the Pythia 160M GPT-NeoX architecture from the Pythia suite, as Pythia allows for ease of
154   future scalability as we can evaluate on different model sizes. Biderman et al. (2023) Pythia also
155   integrates well with LM-eval, which we use to evaluate FSLM on natural language benchmarks.
156   Gao et al. (2024)
157
158   3.3   C HOICE OF DATASET
159
160   We use the Alpaca dataset to train FSLM in an instruction-tuning manner. Taori et al. (2023) Alpaca
161   contains 52,000 self-instruct generated instructions covering a wide array of applications. As of this
      writing, we selected a subsample of 5,000 instructions to fine-tune FSLM.

201   4.2   Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS FOR M ODEL I NTERPRETABILITY
202
      Our FSLM implementation with four Pythia-160M is capable of simple question and answering in
203
      a coherent manner, as shown in Table 2. Since our FSLM framework (approximately 640M param-
204   eters, or around 1.4 GB) is sufficiently small to run on most mobile phones or personal computers,
205   the coherence and natural-sounding response further show promise that FSLM can run in resource-
206   limited settings and exhibit human-like responses.
207
      The intermediary outputs of SLMs within FSLM is of particular interest, because these responses
208
      allow us to directly evaluate model interpretability. Accordingly, we observe in Table 2 that the
209
      intermediary SLM outputs match very strongly with each pre-defined task, at least from the per-
210   spective of a human observer. While this shows that LoRA fine-tuning for FSLM is cost-effective,
211   these intermediate SLM responses also serve as a checkpoint to flag potential mistakes or halluci-
212   nations. Because each SLM is specialized for a specific task, we expect the scope of the responses
213   for each SLM to be somewhat bounded. As a result, if we detect that one of the responses seems
214   wrong, it may be sufficient to only re-tune that single SLM, instead of the whole FSLM stack. In
215   addition to promoting model explainability, this design would also minimize compute costs needed
      to fix overall model performance throughout model deployment.

239   5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
240
241   The objective of this paper was to evaluate whether FSLM, a stack of task-specific SLMs, can per-
242   form well on natural language benchmarks and also exhibit natural-sounding text responses. By
243   running natural language benchmarks, we determined that there were promising signs showing that
244   FSLM’s Pythia models perform on par with vanilla Pythia models of comparable sizes, suggesting
245
      that stacking fine-tuned specialized models can lead to accurate models at small scales. Addition-
      ally, by observing the full response of a sample model output, we determined that the final output
246
      was coherent and natural-sounding, and that the intermediary outputs were also highly aligned to
247
      each SLM’s intended task. Additionally, FSLM’s modular design could allow for easy model de-
248   bugging and replacement of faulty SLMs. These results demonstrate encouraging signs that stacks
249   of highly specialized small language models can perform as well as equivalent models of the same
250   size, making FSLM architectures a potential area of interest for resource-limited compute settings.
251
      One main limitation concerns the limited scope for natural language benchmark evaluations. Be-
252
      cause FSLM is a new implementation, we needed to write additional code to integrate it with existing
253
      lm-eval tasks, which initially limited the scope of tasks we could run as of this writing. Conse-
254   quently, future work should increase the number of natural language benchmarks, and also evaluate
255   model perplexity for token generation, and rouge scores for model summarization. Furthermore,
256   surveys with human observers interacting with FSLM would be beneficial, as we would be able to
257   quantitatively assess the quality and helpfulness of human-to-model interactions.
258
      Another limiting factor is the fine-tuning scope. Future work should try different fine-tuning datasets
259
      and determine to what extent dataset quality influences model performance downstream. On a sim-
260   ilar topic, model pre-training should also be documented, as shown by the flan-T5 models’ superior
261   performances. Future work should investigate fine-tuning SLMs across different architectures that
262   underwent different pre-training processes.
