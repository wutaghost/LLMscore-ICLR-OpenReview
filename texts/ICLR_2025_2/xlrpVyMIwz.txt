010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Positional Encoder Graph Neural Networks (PE-GNNs) are a leading approach
013            for modeling continuous spatial data. However, they often fail to produce cali-
014            brated predictive distributions, limiting their effectiveness for uncertainty quan-
015            tification. We introduce the Positional Encoder Graph Quantile Neural Network
016
               (PE-GQNN), a novel method that integrates PE-GNNs, Quantile Neural Net-
               works, and recalibration techniques in a fully nonparametric framework, requiring
017
               minimal assumptions about the predictive distributions. We propose a new net-
018
               work architecture that, when combined with a quantile-based loss function, yields
019            accurate and reliable probabilistic models without increasing computational com-
020            plexity. Our approach provides a flexible, robust framework for conditional den-
021            sity estimation, applicable beyond spatial data contexts. We further introduce a
022            structured method for incorporating a KNN predictor into the model while avoid-
023            ing data leakage through the GNN layer operation. Experiments on benchmark
024            datasets demonstrate that PE-GQNN significantly outperforms existing state-of-
025            the-art methods in both predictive accuracy and uncertainty quantification.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large spatial datasets are collected in a wide range of applications in economics (Anselin, 2022),
031   meteorology (Bi et al., 2023), urban transportation (Lv et al., 2014; Derrow-Pinion et al., 2021;
032   Kashyap et al., 2022), social networks (Xu et al., 2020), e-commerce (Sreenivasa & Nirmala, 2019)
033
      and other fields. Gaussian Processes (GPs) (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006; Cressie & Wikle, 2011)
      are a fundamental tool for modelling spatial data on continuous domains. They are flexible and
034
      interpretable models for unknown functions, both in spatial and more general regression settings.
035
      However, with time complexity O(n3 ) and storage complexity O(n2 ), naive GP methods quickly be-
036   come intractable for large datasets. This has led to a large range of approximate inference methods,
037   such as those based on sparse approximations to covariance or precision matrices (Reinhard Furrer
038   & Nychka, 2006; Lindgren et al., 2011), low rank approximations (Cressie et al., 2022) or nearest
039   neighbour approximations (Vecchia, 1998; Datta et al., 2016; Katzfuss & Guinness, 2021).
040
      Given the difficulty of GP computations, it’s of interest to explore scalable methods for large spatial
041
      datasets using neural networks (NNs) and to enhance their ability to quantify uncertainty. A state-of-
042   the-art method for making spatial predictions using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) is the Positional
043   Encoder Graph Neural Network (PE-GNN) of Klemmer et al. (2023). Our contribution is to make
044   three key modifications to the PE-GNN architecture to enhance its ability to make accurate spatial
045   predictions and to quantify uncertainty. These modifications will be explained further below.
046
      NNs are popular in data modeling and prediction tasks like computer vision and natural language
047
      processing (NLP). However, traditional NNs struggle to handle spatial dynamics or graph-based
048   data effectively. GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Veličković et al., 2018; Hamilton et al., 2017) offer
049   a powerful and scalable method for applying NNs to graph-structured data. The idea is to share
050   information through the edges of a graph, allowing nodes to exchange information during learning.
051   GNNs are versatile and can uncover nonlinear relationships among inputs, hidden layers, and each
052   node’s neighborhood information. The success of GNNs in spatial applications largely depends on
053   the spatial graph construction, including choice of distance metric and the number of neighboring
      nodes, and traditional GNNs often struggle to model complex spatial relationships. To address

054
      this, Klemmer et al. (2023) introduced the PE-GNN, which enhances predictive performance in
055   spatial interpolation and regression. However, PE-GNN is not designed to provide a full probabilistic
056   description of the target’s distribution, and assuming a Gaussian distribution for predictions can lead
057   to poorly calibrated intervals, such as 80% intervals that fail to contain the true outcome 80% of
058   the time. Recently, Bao et al. (2024) proposed a new framework called Spatial Multi-Attention
059   Conditional Neural Processes (SMACNPs) for spatial small sample prediction tasks. SMACNPs
060   use GPs parameterized by NNs to predict the target variable distribution, which enables precise
061   predictions while quantifying the uncertainty of these predictions.
062   Methods based on quantile regression are an alternative approach to probabilistic forecasting mak-
063   ing rapid progress in recent years. Si et al. (2022) introduced a novel architecture for estimating
064   generic quantiles of a conditional distribution, proposing a set of objective functions that lead to en-
065   hancements in density estimation tasks. In one dimension, this method produces quantile function
066   regression and cumulative distribution function regression. Kuleshov & Deshpande (2022) argue
067   that the method of Si et al. (2022) is inefficient with high-dimensional predictors. To address this,
068   they modify the original formulation to incorporate a post hoc recalibration procedure whereby an
069
      auxiliary model recalibrates the predictions of a trained model. The first model outputs features,
      usually summary statistics like quantiles, representing a low-dimensional view of the conditional
070
      distribution. The auxiliary model, the recalibrator, uses these features as input to produce calibrated
071
      predictions using Si et al.’s quantile function regression framework. The main drawback is that it
072   requires training two separate models, each needing its own training set.
073
074   Our work makes three contributions. (1) We propose a new architecture that merges the two-step
075
      procedure of Kuleshov & Deshpande (2022) into a single model by postponing the concatenation of
      the τ value proposed by Si et al. (2022). In this way, we enhance the network’s ability to model un-
076
      certainty and introduce a regularization mechanism. The model becomes robust to high-dimensional
077
      predictor spaces, even though few assumptions are made about the form of the target’s conditional
078   distribution. This change allows a single model to fully describe the predictive conditional distribu-
079   tion and to generate quantile predictions and prediction intervals as byproducts. It can be applied
080   to any context, not just spatial regression or GNNs. We show how to integrate this strategy into the
081   PE-GNN framework to create an intrinsically calibrated model with no extra computational cost.
082   (2) We introduce a structural change to PE-GNN. Instead of applying the GNN operator to the con-
083   catenation of the nodes’ features and the spatial embedding, we apply it only to the features. (3) In
084   PE-GNN, the GNN operator uses neighbours’ features to create new node representations but does
085   not include the target value of neighboring nodes. Our third contribution introduces the mean target
086
      value of a node’s neighbours as a feature after the GNN layers, closer to the output. This allows the
      model to use neighboring observations of the target variable when making predictions.
087
088   The structure of this work is as follows: Section 2 offers a brief background overview, Section 3
089   outlines the proposed method for geographic data prediction, Section 4 shows experimental results
090   on three real-world datasets, and Section 5 concludes.

332   framework. This severely mitigates the problem of quantile crossings by constraining the flexibility
333   of independent quantile regression neural network models. If τ is introduced at the prediction layer,

      5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we have proposed the Positional Encoder Graph Quantile Neural Network (PE-GQNN)
      as an innovative framework to enhance predictive modeling for geographic data. Through a series
532
      of rigorous experiments on real-world datasets, we have demonstrated the significant advantages
533
      of PE-GQNN over competitive methods. The empirical results underscored the capability of PE-
534   GQNN to achieve lower MSE, MAE, and MPE across all datasets and GNN backbones compared
535   to traditional GNN and PE-GNN. Notably, PE-GQNN demonstrated substantial improvements in
536   predictive accuracy and uncertainty quantification, as evidenced by its consistent performance in
537   quantile calibration metrics such as MPE and MADECP. The PE-GQNN framework’s ability to pro-
538   vide a full description of the predictive conditional distribution, including quantile predictions and
539   prediction intervals, provides a notable improvement in geospatial machine learning. PE-GQNN
      provides a solid foundation for future advancements in the field of geospatial machine learning.
