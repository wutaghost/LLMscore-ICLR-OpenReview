009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT-4, are adept at generating coherent
012            and fluent responses within conversational contexts. However, there has been a
013            paucity of comprehensive research exploring LLMs to dynamically update their
014            knowledge in response to corrections of misinformation provided by users during
015            dialogue sessions. In this paper, we present a novel framework termed Knowledge
016
               Editing In Conversation (KEIC), along with an accompanying dataset, devised to
               assess the efficacy of LLMs in aligning the user update in an in-context setting,
017
               given the previous chat history containing a false statement that conflicts with the
018
               subsequent user update. Through in-depth investigations, we observe that the con-
019            temporary LLMs exhibit a modicum of proficiency in this task. To enhance their
020            in-context knowledge editing abilities, we propose a structured strategy to handle
021            the information update for LLMs in a multi-turn conversation. We demonstrate
022            that our approach is effective and suggest insights for research communities in
023            this emerging and essential issue.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Fluidity and inconsistency are characteristics of natural conversations. It is not rare to encounter
028
      scenarios where an individual’s initial statement is based on false or obsolete information. As the
029
      conversation progresses, the speaker may rectify their statements upon recognizing an error or when
030   presented with fresh information. Intriguingly, the other speaker adapts seamlessly to these changes
031   and continues carrying on the conversation. From the cognitive psychology perspective, this adap-
032   tive process involves entailing the information update or alteration of stored knowledge that has
033   already been in one’s memory (Schrauf & Rubin, 2000; Wagner et al., 2000).
034
      Over the past few years, the advancements in large language models (LLMs) have fostered an envi-
035
      ronment where people find it commonplace to engage in extended conversations with chatbots (Ope-
036   nAI, 2022; 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023b; Team et al., 2023; 2024; Dubey et al.,
037   2024, inter alia). These dialogues often encompass the sharing of daily experiences and emotional
038   exchanges. A critical attribute for LLMs—especially in long-term interaction—is the capacity to
039   have such adaptability similar to humans, meaning the LLM should be adept at updating any misin-
040   formation or outdated knowledge shared by the human interlocutor earlier in conversation (Huang
041   et al., 2024). This adaptability feature, which we termed in-context knowledge editing (KE) or
042   Knowledge Editing In Conversation (KEIC), is crucial for LLMs to serve as intelligent, long-term
043   conversational companions.
044   Henceforth, a natural question arises: Do state-of-the-art LLMs have an innate capacity for KEIC?
045   Before answering this, we summarize the advantages that LLMs shall be equipped with once they
046   are proficient at KEIC, envision several real-world scenarios that favor models with KEIC capacity,
047   and provide reasons why current approaches may not be suitable under these circumstances. Related
048   work is in Appendix A.
049   These include: (1) Not all false statements require (and should not do so) parameter editing, as some
050   of them are non-factual (see Figure 1). (2) To achieve KEIC, the LLM shall excel in temporal and
051   contextualized information in an entire dialogue, as it can deal with real-time user intent, discern
052   which information is obsolete, and resolve any discrepancies thereafter. (3) End users do not need
053   to prepare examples for LLMs (Zheng et al., 2023a), nor to re-initiate the dialogue sessions, espe-
      cially when conversations grow longer. In practice, the model can seamlessly update its knowledge

103        • We introduce a new KEIC task for LLMs to be intelligent companions. We devise the
104           KEIC framework to decompose a multi-turn dialogue and cope with the misinformation in
105           the earlier conversation. The concept also applies to hallucination, the notorious problem
106           of LLMs, and could further improve their reliability in a zero-shot and in-context setting.

116   2       TASK D EFINITION
117
118   The KEIC task aims to test if an LLM can dynamically update its knowledge when the user corrects
119   the previous false fact. We first outline the CoQA task (Reddy et al., 2019) since we construct our
120   KEIC dataset from it. Next, we define how to elicit knowledge stored in LLMs and formalize its
121   form in a conversation. Finally, we present the KEIC framework and show it can fit any chat data.
122
123   2.1     C O QA F RAMEWORK
124
125   The CoQA task aims to test whether a dialogue system can answer the question Qi when a passage P
126
      and previous chat history {Q1 , A1 , ..., Qi−1 , Ai−1 } are given. Each question-answer pair (Qi , Ai )
      is associated with a consecutive text span of support sentence Ri ∈ P that serves as a rationale for
127
      answering Qi . The conversation flow is denoted as [P, Q1 , A1 , ..., Qi , Ai ]. The term passage is used
128
      interchangeably with story.
129

      2.3   KEIC F RAMEWORK
163
164   In our KEIC dataset, we extend each instance from CoQA by labeling misinformation in the passage
165   and adding a correction. We denote a k-turn conversation as [T1 , ..., Tk ], where Tj is the j-th turn
166   ∀j ∈ [1, k], and each turn Tj = (uj , bj ) is a pair of user and chatbot utterances, respectively. The
167   conversation flow is [u1 , b1 , u2 , ..., bk ] after unrolling.
168   In the scenario of the KEIC task, there exist (1) a false fact, (2) a new fact, and (3) other contexts in
169   a conversation. Consequently, we design the KEIC framework to segment the dialogue into smaller
170   components, which also adheres to the multi-turn evaluation framework (Zheng et al., 2023b). We
171   define four disjoint phases, and each turn of the conversation belongs to one of them. The concept
172   is as simple as classifying each turn into four categories:
173           • False phase (Tf ) contains false information, and the user will point it out later. In Figure 1,
174             the false fact is in T2 (specifically, u2 ), and the user corrects it in T4 (specifically, u4 ).
175          • Update phase (Tu ) involves in updating misinformation or in-context KE process. Tu is
176            a general notation for KEIC (see Section 3). In Figure 1, the update phase has T4 .
177
             • Test phase (Ti ) assesses if the update phase rectifies an LLM’s knowledge successfully. In
178
               Figure 1, we can ask the LLM “Is Pipi (Betty’s pet) a cat?” in u7 to evaluate its answer.
179
180          • Other phase (To ) consists of the previous, on-going chat. One may think any turn here is
181
               more or less unrelated to KEIC. In Figure 1, T1 , T3 , T5 , and T6 are in the other phase.
182
183   2.4   M APPING A RBITRARY D IALOGUE INTO KEIC F RAMEWORK
184
      To standardize our KEIC methods and dataset construction in this task, we elaborate on the Decom-
185
      position in Figure 2, using CoQA data as an example. We mathematically define the above mapping
186   process as f : {T1 , ..., Tk } → {Tf , Tu , Ti , To }. For each turn Tj , the mapping f works as follows:
187
             • If either uj or bj (hallucination) contains false information, then Tj ∈ Tf . In CoQA data,
188
                T1 is always in the false phase because we render a piece of text in the passage P obsolete
189             for the user to correct afterward (and P ∈ u1 ).
190
191
             • If uj updates misinformation in the false phase (uj is effective) or involves in KEIC pro-
               cess, then Tj ∈ Tu . CoQA does not have this phase. We devise four methods in Section 3.
192
193          • If uj consists of the question with which we want to test the LLM, then Tj ∈ Ti . In CoQA,
194            it is a question and is usually the last turn.
195          • Any Tj that does not belong to the false, update,
                                                                and test phases falls into the other phase. In
196            CoQA, if the i-th question is selected among (Q1 , A1 ), ..., (Qn , An ) for the test phase,
                                         Si−1
197            then its previous QA pairs m=1 (Qm , Am ) fall into the other phase. If i = 1, then To = ∅.

      3     K NOWLEDGE E DITING I N C ONVERSATION (KEIC) M ETHODS
201
202
      We propose four methods in the update phase: One-turn correction, Verification, Reiterate, and
      Deletion. In this paper, Tu = {Tc , Tv , Tr , Td }, where each approach can be combined with the
203
      others.
204
205
      One-Turn Correction (OTC) One-turn correction is a correction phase (Tc ) that contains a
206
      single sentence (see u4 in Figure 1). Once an LLM exhibits innate KEIC similar to humans, a
207   simple OTC shall suffice. We apply the mining approach (Jiang et al., 2020) to extract the correction
208   utterances from the DailyDialog (Li et al., 2017). Specifically, we select 15 sentences using 15
209   keywords that may be associated with corrections. For example, “Wrong. It’s not [old fact], but
210   [new fact].” and “Actually, [new fact].” are two types of our templates (whether the templates
211   contain the old fact; see Appendix B for all). In this paper, we are explicitly referring to the simplest
212   KEIC method when OTC is mentioned.
213
214   Verification (Tv ) After the test phase, we launch the Verification to confirm if an LLM is sure
215   of its response via re-questioning (see u9 in Figure 3b). It mimics a real-world scenario when one
      shows disbelief or skepticism, which may stimulate it to reflect on the user update.

      4.1   DATASET C OLLECTION
273
274   We first discard the CoQA data that does not have any YN questions. After setting the random seed
275   to 0, we randomly select one YN question for the test phase. Once the test question is selected, the
276   corresponding support sentence and previous QA pairs are determined. Hence, the KEIC framework
277   is aligned with CoQA (see Section 2.4). The remaining task is to modify the (old) support sentence.
278   To ensure the new support sentences are “effective, fluent, and ethically sound,” we collect them
279   through Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk), an online crowdsourcing platform. Our task is only
280   visible to workers from English-speaking countries with HIT approval rate ≥ 95% and |HITs|
281   ≥ 1,000 (Karpinska et al., 2021). Each data is distributed to three workers, and we perform a
282   meticulous examination of their results (see Appendix E for details): They must fill in the blank
283   only—without altering or pasting the context near the blank—so we can replace the old fact with
284   the new one while maintaining contextualized (if not global) fluency in the story. We pay each
285   worker $0.1 or $0.15 in each assignment. Finally, our KEIC dataset consists of 1,317 data in training
286
      set (Dtrain ) and 464 in validation (Dval ). Each data has three non-trivial and effective corrections
      to the original CoQA (more examples are in Appendix E). The average number of turns in the other
287
      phase is 8.27 and 8.48, respectively. We denote DKEIC = Dtrain ∪ Dval (|DKEIC | = 1,781).

419    Transferability of correction phase across KEIC approaches We first elaborate on our findings
420    that different types of correction utterances significantly impact the performance (see Section 3).
421    For instance, in GPT-3.5 (0613), we find that six templates, with only new knowledge to fill in,
422    usually outperform the other nine in Verication, yet they significantly underperform in OTC and
423
       Reiterate. We speculate that the other nine templates contain the negation of old knowledge, so they
       may boost GPT-3.5’s KEIC ability to update the answer in the OTC and Reiterate methods. In other
424
       words, these six templates perform poorly in OTC, suggesting GPT-3.5 does not pay attention to the
425
       correction phase if it only contains new knowledge. Consequently, after we re-question the model
426    in Verification and tell it to reflect the update, GPT-3.5 may pay more attention to it and replies
427    the updated answer. As for the other nine templates, we hypothesize that after re-questioning, the
428    model is confused about which knowledge is correct, which means even if the GPT-3.5 response was
429    indeed based on new information in the test phase, it may return to the old answer in the Verification
430    phase, implying GPT-3.5 is not confident of its earlier answer. This observation also explains why
431    there is a drastic drop in update between the performance of K = 5 and 15, as the other templates
       are poor at capturing the information update in different KEIC approaches (see Figure 5a). As for

508   6   C ONCLUSION
509
510   As discrepancies arise in dialogue, either from users to correct themselves or from LLMs to start
511   hallucinating, the capability of LLMs to accurately and efficiently update information on the fly is an
512   essential yet underexplored issue. Inspired by this, we formalize it as the KEIC task and present the
513   multi-turn framework to decompose the chat history. Then, we propose a structured methodology
514   to systematically gauge the LLMs’ KEIC ability in a zero-shot setting. Distinguished from existing
515
      datasets, we release a challenging dataset for LLMs to recognize the misinformation mid-paragraph
      in long-term conversations. Extensive studies have shown, in the main, that the correction phase
516
      containing the negation of the false fact performs better, the update phase is indispensable, its loca-
517
      tion also affects the result in each approach, Reiterate is an economical approach, KEIC algorithm
518   can update nearly 75% of fact within a paragraph in extended conversations, and the KEIC task does
519   not disappear with time and the scale of LLMs. Our framework and dataset form the foundation
520   for constructing chatbots that are not only coherent but adaptive for long-term companionship. The
521   limitation of this work, including the key takeaways of our framework, is in the Appendix. The code
522   and dataset will be made publicly available; we also include them in the Supplementary Material.
523
524   Ethics Statement Any LLM shall not be treated as an authoritative source of facts, even though
525   we test LLMs’ adaptability and use their outputs as a knowledge base. It is important to note that
526   our work could be potentially exploited by malicious users to produce harmful responses; hence, it
527   should not be used in any harmful way. Our KEIC dataset is constructed based on the CoQA (and
528
      should follow its license), and the correction templates are excerpted from the DailyDialog dataset.
      On the other hand, the new support sentences are generated by MTurk workers and validated by
529
      us. We provide them with ethics statements (see Figure 10 in Appendix E) and manually filter out
530
      unsafe or unethical responses while preserving effectiveness. Nevertheless, as our primary goal is to
531   modify existing knowledge, some results might still be offensive or inappropriate for some people.
532   Our framework can be used for training. To avoid data contamination, however, the update sentences
533   generated by workers should be used solely for inference unless a publicly available technical report
534   or manuscript explicitly mentions they are used for training to ensure fairness in LLM evaluations.
535
536   Reproducibility Statement Appendix A is the related work, Appendix B lists 15 correction tem-
537   plates, Appendix C visualizes the Deletion approach, Appendix D contains the proof of our KEIC
538   algorithm, Appendix E details how we validate MTurk responses and how hard our non-trivial in-
539   formation update is, Appendix F provides the exact prompt to implement two modules in our KEIC
      algorithm, Appendix G gives more time/cost estimations, and Appendix H has more experiments.

      L IMITATIONS
865
866
      Practicality and Key Takeaways In this paper, we present the ultimate goal for intelligent LLMs
867   in the KEIC task: A single update sentence (i.e., OTC) should effectively edit the LLM’s in-context
868   knowledge, mimicking human behavior. Considering real-time response requirements and the cost
869   of token usage, incorporating an additional phase for LLMs to reiterate the updated fact through Re-
870   iterate is beneficial. Ideally, there should be no significant difference in how or when users correct
871   themselves. Nevertheless, our findings reveal that clearly negating the false facts is far more effec-
872   tive than simply stating the updated information. Additionally, our results highlight a noticeable
873   gap between CAM and CBA settings. Given that these contemporary LLMs have not fully excelled
874   in the KEIC task, it would be advantageous to dispatch each component of our framework to spe-
875
      cialized or more robust LLM-based system(s) for now. In this work, we leverage the invaluable,
      human-annotated CoQA dataset to assess whether LLMs can capture user updates within long utter-
876
      ances and extended conversations. Real-world data, however, lacks proper labels. While our KEIC
877
      algorithm can still be applied by repetitively scanning the entire chat to overwrite contradictions, it
878   risks deleting other important information. Hence, before LLMs are trained with KEIC, it may be
879   beneficial to maintain a classifier detecting whether a user is updating knowledge, along with one or
880   more systems capable of handling the “Decomposition” and “Arrangement and Injection” processes
881   in the background.
882
883   KEIC Dataset Our dataset is limited to YN questions and does not cover various open-domain
884   questions. However, as we take a step forward to construct our dataset in this task—which can be
885   viewed as the zero-shot KE task in chat format—we speculated it would be much easier to edit the
886   misinformation within a short utterance.8 Thus, our goal is to find an existing dataset where a false
887   fact lies within a long context. Hence, we select CoQA. After that, we resort to simple YN questions
888
      and try to keep our evaluation method noise-free so as not to increase the interference. Another
      direction for future work is to expand our work and test other open-domain questions in the CoQA.
889
890
      KEIC Framework Our framework is designed for multi-turn chat format, so it may require “fill-
891
      ing” or “padding” in some datasets during the mapping process, in the sense that they are not so
892
      “natural.” For example, the bot utterances in the false and update phase are not in the original CoQA
893   data (e.g., b1 and b7 in Figure 3a), nor they are all inherently learned or generated by LLMs. We
894   pre-fined these texts in this paper as they can be used for evaluating the current KEIC capabilities of
895   LLMs uniformly—though, admittedly, all human-generated prompts are not optimal in this sense—
896   and save the API calls. To assess whether they play an important role in this task, we additionally
897   conduct the ablation analysis by removing these texts in the OTC (see Table 5 in Appendix H). An-
898   other direction for future work is to propose new approaches to extend the update phase (Tu ) and
899   explore various combinations of existing in-context KE methods.
900
901   Experiments This paper is an in-depth study of the KEIC task, yet the experiments do not cover
902
      other open-domain LLMs. Consequently, constantly testing whether they are on par with GPT-3.5
      is also a promising avenue of research. Regarding correction template generation, while we em-
903
      ploy the mining approach, we have not conducted an exhaustive evaluation of possible text com-
904
      binations within these templates (they are included in Appendix B.3). When evaluating our KEIC
905   methodologies, we presume that specific processes are error-free without confirming whether all
906   these processes fulfill our intended requirements. As a result, it is also worthwhile to conduct in-
907   depth analyses of Reiterate (e.g., how successful LLMs are in reiterating the story) and Deletion
908   (e.g., the two modules and extraction templates used in our KEIC algorithm). Similar to the oracle
909   of Reiterate, it is also worth experimenting with the oracle of Verification. In the Deletion method,
910   there are opportunities to investigate several approaches for condensing excessively long text that
911   exceeds the conversation limit. Various operations of D ELETE, including masking the old infor-
912   mation, have not been implemented. Owing to the cost, we have not tested whether the Deletion
913
      method can substantially boost the performance of other “poor” templates with only one slot for
      new knowledge. Other limitations (such as modifying multiple facts simultaneously or evaluating
914
      open-ended questions) are beyond the scope of this research, and we leave them for future work.
915
916       8
            LLMs may fail at either locating the false utterance within a long story or overwriting it with the updated
917   fact. Incidentally, our ablation analysis (without FP in Table 5) tests this scenario by removing the context after
      the support sentence. We find that the percentage of update increases when the passage is abridged.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
919
920
      On top of adaptability, consistency has long been considered an ongoing and formidable challenge
921   in the domain of chatbot development (Vinyals & Le, 2015; Li et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2018), and
922   a plethora of training methods has been put forward in an attempt to bolster the coherence of chatbot
923   responses (Yi et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al.,
924   2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024, inter alia). To gauge the aptitude of a chatbot in maintaining con-
925   sistency, existing benchmarks that focus on contradiction detection have been employed (Welleck
926   et al., 2019; Nie et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2022). These dialogue benchmarks, on the whole, catego-
927   rize contradictory responses by chatbots as erroneous, and a common thread amongst most of them
928   is the objective to deter chatbots from generating responses that conflict with their previous state-
929
      ments. Nevertheless, an often overlooked aspect of these benchmarks is the dynamism of natural
      conversations—they do not consider the information in earlier chat may have been rendered obsolete
930
      by the user. In such cases, to align with the user’s updated knowledge, we highlight that the chat-
931
      bot sometimes even needs to contradict its previous in-context response to ensure the conversation
932   remains accurate and coherent (see Figure 1). We hypothesize that these conversational datasets,
933   although aiming to improve an LLM’s consistency and reduce self-contradiction is of paramount
934   importance, may hamper its adaptability—an emerging issue of contemporary LLMs. In light of
935   this, balancing between the two seemingly paradoxical yet highly correlated tasks during training
936   would be one of the key challenges and opportunities for future work.
937
      In previous work, knowledge editing (KE) typically involved proposing an efficient methodology to
938   modify the parameters of an LLM (De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022a; Meng et al., 2023).
939   Efficient as they may be, these approaches are vulnerable to overfitting, where the edited LLMs do
940   not generalize well on other inputs or tasks (Cohen et al., 2024). Concurrently, there has been a
941   surge in exploiting additional system(s) and keeping the LLM unchanged (Mitchell et al., 2022b;
942   Murty et al., 2022). To this end, their frameworks generally can be broken down into three compo-
943   nents: a memory storage system that acts as a new knowledge base, a scope classifier that determines
944   whether the input sequence is relevant to the external memory, and a counterfactual model trained
945   on new knowledge. In parallel, there exist approaches that utilize external sources or specialized
946
      LLMs to aid or calibrate model predictions (Pan et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024;
      Gou et al., 2024, inter alia). In sum, these methods require either parameter modification or addi-
947
      tional systems; they often struggle with the rapid change of information or are incompatible with
948
      online conversations (Kamoi et al., 2024; Miao et al., 2024). Each fact in the previous KE datasets
949   is usually a short sentence (De Cao et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022), focusing on
950   querying a specific real-world knowledge. On the other hand, the D IAL FACT dataset aims to im-
951   prove fact-checking performance in chat format (Gupta et al., 2022), yet the dataset is not suitable
952   for assessing an LLM’s long-term adaptability. Regarding the QA datasets for benchmarking an
953   LLM’s self-correction capability, there are HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), CommonsenseQA (Tal-
954   mor et al., 2019) and S TRATEGY QA (Geva et al., 2021), to name a few. However, these datasets
955   do not simulate human interactions in long-term dialogue either. To address this gap, we design the
956   KEIC framework and create our dataset based on the CoQA (Reddy et al., 2019) in this standard,
957
      which applies to both conversational (long and short) and non-conversational (e.g., math and cod-
      ing) datasets.9 Our framework serves as a stepping stone for standardizing dataset construction in
958
      this task and could facilitate the evaluation of future LLMs across different domains, particularly in
959
      aligning user updates or addressing hallucination, the long-standing problem.

       H.5     E XPERIMENTS ON THE T EXT G RAD F RAMEWORK
1696
1697   T EXT G RAD is the pioneering work with a released software for universal, automatic “differentia-
1698   tion” via text for LLM-based systems, similar to the PyTorch backprop function. The core idea is that
1699   they treat a black-box LLM or more sophisticated systems as a “single neuron,” so the input/output
1700   of that “neuron” can be both in text form. Thus, the “gradient” with respect to this “neuron” is,
1701   naturally, the text. Prior to OpenAI o1, the most recent “think-before-speak” application15 , they de-
1702   sign an automatic way to prompt the GPT-4o (partly GPT-3.5) to stick to the text objective function,
1703   provide textual (“gradient”) feedback, improve the answer by utilizing various “HTML tags,” which
1704   is effectively a more complicated CoT framework. Notwithstanding their remarkable success across
1705
       various tasks, one of the most concerning issues in their current applications is the cost, as either
       (1) the internal processes are not publicly available or (2) the token consumption cannot be easily
1706
       calculated in advance.
1707
1708   In this paper, we additionally conduct their framework by feeding our best LLM outputs (that is, the
1709   0125 version of GPT-3.5) in the OTC baseline on the validation set into their T EXT G RAD, hoping
1710   to identify the error and update the answer. However, our preliminary results show that, when using
1711
       GPT-4o (0513) in the first run (costs around $250), the best performances of (update, no update)
       with respect to CAM and CBA are (29.1%, 70.3%) and (27.2%, 72.4%). Moreover, after we set the
1712
       backend LLM to GPT-3.5 (0125), the best performance of (update, no update) with respect to CAM
1713
       and CBA are (30.3%, 68.9%) and (24.6%, 74.9%) in 3 runs (worse than without applying their
1714   framework, as shown in Figure 7). It would be worth experimenting with using their framework
1715   directly or tweaking the prompts (see below).
1716
1717
       The prompts are the following (with a slight modification to the example from their website16 ):
       (1) role description of a variable: “yes/no question to the LLM” (2) role description of an answer:
1718
       “concise and accurate answer to the yes/no question (the answer should begin with yes or no)” (3)
1719
       evaluation instruction: “Here’s a yes/no question: {question}. Evaluate any given answer to this
1720   yes/no question, be smart, logical, and very critical. Just provide concise feedback.”
