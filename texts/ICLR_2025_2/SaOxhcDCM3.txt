011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013             Large Language Models (LLM) are already widely used to generate content for
014             a variety of online platforms. As we are not able to safely distinguish LLM-
015             generated content from human-produced content, LLM-generated content is used
016
                to train the next generation of LLMs, giving rise to a self-consuming training
                loop. From the image generation domain we know that such a self-consuming
017
                training loop reduces both quality and diversity of images finally ending in a
018
                model collapse. However, it is unclear whether this alarming effect can also be
019             observed for LLMs. Therefore, we present the first study investigating the self-
020             consuming training loop for LLMs. Further, we propose a novel method based
021             on logic expressions that allows us to unambiguously verify the correctness of
022             LLM-generated content, which is difficult for natural language text. We find that
023             the self-consuming training loop produces correct outputs, however, the output
024             declines in its diversity depending on the proportion of the used generated data.
025             Fresh data can slow down this decline, but not stop it. Further, we observe simi-
026             lar results on a real natural language dataset. Given these concerning results, we
027
                encourage researchers to study methods to negate this process.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Transformer-based language models have received much attention in the machine learning commu-
032
      nity in recent years. Especially large language models (LLM) trained on massive amounts of data
033   from the internet became state of the art in many benchmarks (Brown et al., 2020) and specialized
034   conversational LLM applications like ChatGPT1 have a massive influence on society already. LLMs
035   can be used for multiple tasks ranging from code generation (Chen et al., 2021; Sobania et al., 2022;
036   Fan et al., 2023) and automated program repair (Sobania et al., 2023) to text summarization (Yang
037   et al., 2023) and teaching assistance (Baidoo-Anu & Ansah, 2023).
038
      Due to their convincing generated outputs, LLMs can be used to generate a large amount of content
039   that is posted online to coding platforms like GitHub and Stackoverflow2 , social media platforms
040   like Reddit3 and other platforms on the internet. Even academic writing is already being influenced
041   by LLM outputs (Geng & Trotta, 2024; Liang et al., 2024). Such LLM-generated text is often hard to
042   distinguish from human-generated content (Sadasivan et al., 2023) and in turn might unwillingly be
043   used to train the next generation of LLMs. Even paying for human-generated content might not be
044   an option in the future, as workers at paid services like Amazon’s Mechanical Turk also use LLMs
045   to produce content (Veselovsky et al., 2023). Consequently, a self-consuming training loop emerges
046   in which future models are trained repeatedly on LLM-generated data from previous generations.
047
      This process was first observed for generative models in the image domain (Martı́nez et al., 2023;
      Alemohammad et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2024; Bertrand et al., 2023). These studies found that
048
      this self-consuming training loop leads to a decline in quality and diversity of generated images,
049
      ultimately resulting in a so called model collapse. This was also observed for repeatably fine-tuning
050   LLMs leading to a decrease in diversity (Guo et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2024). However, it

      is unclear what happens with LLMs that are trained in such a self-consuming training loop from

056
057   Therefore, we present the first study analyzing the behavior of LLMs trained over many generations
058
      in a self-consuming training loop. We conduct experiments on a GPT-style model in different set-
      tings and measure both quality and diversity of samples from the trained model at each generation in
059
      the self-consuming training loop. The settings differ in the way a dataset is created for each gener-
060
      ation (so called data cycles) as well as the proportion of original real and LLM-generated synthetic
061   data samples. To better analyze the behavior of the trained models we conduct our experiments on
062   a dataset consisting of logic expressions. In contrast to natural language, this logic expressions can
063   be evaluated unambiguously. This allows us to analytically and accurately measure correctness and
064   diversity of the generated samples. Furthermore, to confirm the generalizability of our results we
065   conduct additional experiments on a natural language dataset.
066
      We find that repeatedly training new models with synthetic data from previous models initially
067   improves quality. However, diversity degenerates and the learned distribution inevitably collapses
068   to a single point. In extreme cases this happens already after less than 10 generations. Additionally,
069   we find that the speed at which diversity degenerates depends on the data cycle as well as on the
070   proportion of real and synthetic data. Fresh real data added during the data cycle slows down but
071   can not negate the effects of a self-consuming training loop.
072
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
073
074          • The first comprehensive empirical study of the self-consuming training loop for LLMs
075            trained from scratch,
076
             • A novel method to unambiguously evaluate quality/correctness and diversity of LLM-
077
               generated outputs,
078
079          • An in-depth analysis and discussion of the effects and implications of this self-consuming
080            training loop for LLMs.

118
      Related Work Current research has analyzed the self-consuming training loop mostly in the context
      of image generating models. Martı́nez et al. (2023) first studied the self-consuming training loop
119
      for diffusion models and find that the self-consuming training loop leads to a collapse in diversity
120
      of the generated images. Other work have given theoretical frameworks for this phenomenon and
121   investigate more complex data cycles in the context of image generation (Alemohammad et al.,
122   2023; Shumailov et al., 2024; Bertrand et al., 2023). Both Alemohammad et al. (2023) and Bertrand
123   et al. (2023) find that fresh real data can lead to stability within the self-consuming training loop.
124
      Shumailov et al. (2024) observe a degeneration of diversity for Variational Autoencoders and Gaus-
125
      sian Mixture Models if some of the output is used again as input. Additionally, the authors perform
126
      experiments for iteratively fine-tuning LLMs in a self-consuming way and observe a degradation in
127   quality. They find that degradation is less strong than in the imagine generation context and that
128   keeping some original data helps mitigating this phenomenon. Other contemporary work also an-
129   alyzed repeated fine-tuning of LLMs in a self-consuming way and observed a decrease in lexical
130   diversity (Guo et al., 2023).
131
      However, new generations of LLMs are usually trained from scratch with web scraped datasets while
132
      fine-tuning is mainly done with curated datasets. So the analysis of the self-consuming training loop
133   when training from scratch is of pressing concern. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first
134   to study the behavior of LLMs trained in a self-consuming loop from scratch. Furthermore, self-
135   consuming loops in LLMs have not yet been analyzed with a method that allows to unambiguously
136   evaluate the quality and diversity of generated model output.

146   3.1   V ERIFICATION WITH A L OGIC E XPRESSION DATASET
147
      LLMs usually generate natural language texts and their performance is typically measured by using
148
      similarity metrics like the BLEU score (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE score (Lin, 2004) and BERT
149
      score (Zhang et al., 2019) as well as perplexity (Jurafsky & Martin, 2009). However, those metrics
150   rely on measuring similarity of outputs with expected reference data, which can only serve as a proxy
151   for quality of a language model (Callison-Burch et al., 2006; Gehrmann et al., 2023). Consequently,
152   we propose using a dataset consisting of logic expressions. Those expressions can be represented as
153   a sequence making them a good fit for language modeling. In contrast to natural language text, we
154   can easily and systematically evaluate the quality of logic expressions by verifying their correctness.
155   An expression is defined to be syntactically correct, if it can be parsed without an error. The semantic
156   correctness can be evaluated if an expression is either True or False. If the original dataset only
157   consists of True expressions, then a high-quality trained model should also only generate True
158
      expressions.
159   We build a logic expression as a tree in a recursive way specified in the function
160   GenerateLogicExpression(d), where d is the desired depth of a logic expression tree. If the
161   desired tree depth is reached when calling the function, a random Boolean is returned (either True
      or False). Otherwise, a logic operator (not,and,or) is selected and the function is called again

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      recursively with d − 1. To build the entire original dataset we use this recursive function to sample
163   new random logic expression trees with a random depth between dmin and dmax until we have a
164   dataset with m unique expressions that evaluate in a True Boolean expression. The complexity
165   of the dataset can be easily controlled by adjusting dmax . For our experiments we chose a initial
166   dataset size of m = 10, 000 with expressions of minimum depth dmin = 1 and maximum depth
167   dmax = 5. Algorithm 2 in Appendix B describes the generation of our logic expression dataset in
168   pseudo-code.
169   The resulting logic expression trees can then be saved as strings, e.g., not ( True and
170   False ). This allows us to evaluate them in Python using the eval() function to test whether
171   they are correct or trigger an error and whether they evaluate to a True or False Boolean. Ad-
172   ditionally, we can encode those strings to a sequence of tokens (True, False, not, and,
173   or, (, ), <eos>, where <eos> is a stop token and indicates the end of an expression) and
174   use these sequences to train a language model. Examples of logical expressions can be found in
175   Appendix E.1.

188   3.3   M EASURING THE D IVERSITY OF THE M ODEL’ S O UTPUT
189
      A generative model does not only need to generate correct outputs but also a diverse set of outputs.
190
      To measure the diversity of a sample from a LLM in the logic expression experiments we use the
191
      Levenshtein diversity (Beijering et al., 2008; Wittenberg et al., 2023) in our experiments. This metric
192   calculates the pairwise Levenshtein distance between each expression in the sample normalized by
193   the number of tokens of the longer expression of each pairwise comparison and averaged over the
194   number of pairwise comparisons. If the average pairwise normalized Levenshtein distance is close
195   to zero the diversity within a sample is low. A value closer to one suggests a stronger diversity within
196   a sample. The Levenshtein distance itself is a metric for measuring the distance between two strings
197   and is defined as the number of edits (deletion, addition, substitution) required to change one string
198   to another (Levenshtein et al., 1966). Normally, this edit distance is calculated on a character basis,
199   however, we calculate it on the tokens from our encoding of the logic expressions to accommodate
200
      for the different length each token has in character representation.
201   To measure the diversity of a sample in the natural language experiments we employ the n-gram
202   diversity score (Meister et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Padmakumar & He, 2024) which measures
203   the ratio between unique and total n-grams in a text. A smaller value indicates less diversity. We
204   also investigate the compression rate (Shaib et al., 2024) and the vocabulary size of the generated
205   samples.
206   We provide further details for all diversity metrics in Appendix C.

      3.4   DATA C YCLES
209
210   We define a data cycle as the way a dataset Dt is constructed in generation t from the original data
211   D0 , potential fresh data Ft from the original distribution, and the generated data from the current
212   and previous generations S1...t . This way of constructing a new dataset may vary and different
213   data cycles are possible. In the image generation domain, previous work suggests that the self-
214   consuming training loop is influenced by the data cycle being used (Alemohammad et al., 2023).
215   Therefore, inspired by their work we conduct our experiments with the following four different data
      cycles (also depicted in Fig. 2):

239
      a full synthetic data cycle. Normally, new datasets would still contain the original data when they
240
      are collected in practice. However, we can use this data cycle to study the most extreme changes in
241   behavior of models from generation to generation.
242
243   Balanced Data Cycle: We refer to the second data cycle as the balanced data cycle. In this data
244
      cycle, we construct the new dataset Dt from equal parts of all the previous samples S1...t and the
                                                                             1
      original dataset D0 so that every previous generation contributes m ∗ t+1 logic expressions to the
245
      new dataset Dt of size m.
246
247   Incremental Data Cycle: The third data cycle we study is called the incremental data cycle. In this
248   data cycle, the new dataset is created by a portion (1 − λ) of the last dataset Dt−1 and a portion λ of
249   new sampled data St so that Dt = (1 − λ) ∗ Dt−1 + λ ∗ St while holding the size m of the dataset
250   constant each generation. A λ = 1 would result in a full synthetic data cycle. We chose λ = 0.1 for
251
      our initial experiments and later used different values of λ to study the influence of this parameter
      on diversity.
252
253   Expanding Data Cycle: The first three data cycles create a dataset of equal size in each generation.
254   In practice, however, the dataset would grow each generation by adding new generated data to the
255   already existing dataset. Additionally, fresh data samples Ft from the original distribution (e.g.
256   human generated) would also be added at each generation. Consequently, the last data cycle we
257
      study is an expanding data cycle. At each generation the new dataset is created so that Dt =
      Dt−1 + (1 − λ) ∗ Ft + λ ∗ St , where λ is the portion of generated data we add each generation
258
      and (1 − λ) is the portion of fresh real data added at each generation. For this data cycle, we chose
259
      λ = 0.9 for our initial experiments and later used different values of λ to study the influence of fresh
260   data in an expanding data cycle on diversity.

263   3.5      M ODEL A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING
264
265   We employ a GPT-style LLM using the open-source implementation nanoGPT 4 (MIT license) in
266
      our experiments. The model accepts a context of up to 256 tokens and consists of 6 attention layers
      with 6 attention heads each and an embedding dimensionality of 384, resulting in roughly 10.6
267
      million parameters.
268

      During training we use a batch size of 64 and a dropout rate of 0.2, training for 5000 iterations
271   minimizing cross entropy loss, starting with a learning rate of 10−3 decaying to 10−4 , to achieve a
272   sufficient fitting of the training data. We split the dataset in 90% training and 10% validation data.
273   During training we calculate the validation error every 250 iterations and use the model parameters
274   with the lowest validation error as our final model Mt for each generation t in the self-consuming
275   training loop.
276
      We use the trained model at each generation to sample 10, 000 logic expressions. We also performed
277   experiments for larger amounts of logical expressions (20, 000, 30, 000, & 40, 000) and observed
278   no impact on the results for varying dataset sizes (see Appendix D.4). During sampling, we auto-
279   regressively generate new tokens with temperature 0.8 and feed them back into the model until a stop
280   token <eos> is sampled up to a maximum of 200 tokens per expression. We run the self-consuming
281   training loop for T = 50 generations in each experiment.
282
      We use the same model and setup to run the self-consuming training loop for our natural language
283   experiments but tokenize the text on a character level resulting in a vocabulary size of 65. During
284   sampling, we auto-regressively generate text of 1000 tokens in length until our sample St is of
285   equal size as the original dataset D0 (approximately 1.1 million tokens per generation). For the
286   expanding data cycle in the natural language experiments we only use a subset of D0 in order to
287   have enough fresh original data for 50 generations. Consequently, we generate less tokens each
288   generation (approximately 186, 000 tokens per generation).
289   All experiments are performed on a workstation using consumer NVIDIA graphics cards.

297   4.1   C ORRECTNESS OF G ENERATED C ONTENT
298
299   We first study the correctness of the expressions within a generated sample St from a model Mt at
300   each generation t. As described in Sect. 3.1, we consider an expression to be syntactically correct if
301   it can be parsed without error. Since the original dataset D0 only consists of True expressions, we
302   consider a generated expression to be semantically correct if it also evaluates to True. A semanti-
303
      cally correct expression is also syntactically correct.
304   Figure 3 displays the composition of samples St generated during a self-consuming training loop.
305   Each subplot presents the results for a different data cycle: a) full synthetic, b) balanced, c) incre-
306   mental, and d) expanding. Every bar in a subplot displays the composition of St generated from
307   model Mt at generation t, except for the first bar in each subplot which displays D0 . The green
308   portion of a bar indicates the number of syntactically correct expressions that evaluate to True in
309
      that sample. The yellow part of a bar represents the number of syntactically correct expressions
      that evaluate to False, and the red part of a bar displays the number of syntactically incorrect
310
      expressions that result in an error when being parsed.
311
312   Overall, we see that only very few expressions are syntactically incorrect. This indicates that the
313   models are sufficiently trained and can correctly learn the syntactic rules of a logic expression. We
314   see a drop of around 20% in the number of semantically correct expressions from the original data
315
      D0 to the first sample S1 in every data cycle. Interestingly, the number of semantically correct
      expressions increases afterwards over the course of the self-consuming training loop. The speed
316
      at which this number increases depends on the data cycle. We can see the fastest increase for the
317
      full synthetic data cycle in which the whole sample consists of True expressions by generation
318   t = 6. The incremental data cycle takes consistently longer but also nearly reaches this point by
319   generation t = 36. While the balanced data cycle has an initially steeper increase in semantically
320   correct expressions, it does not reach the point of a completely semantically correct sample by the
321   end of 50 generations but comes very close to it. Lastly, the expanding data cycle shows this trend
322   as well. Overall, we see that the self-consuming training loop seems to help with generating more
323   semantically correct expressions in our experiments and the rate at which this happens differs by
      data cycle.

362   4.2   D IVERSITY OF G ENERATED C ONTENT
363
364
      While we can see an increase in correctness over generations in our experiments, contemporary
365
      work in the image generation domain suggests, that this comes with a loss of diversity (Martı́nez
366   et al., 2023; Alemohammad et al., 2023; Bertrand et al., 2023). Therefore, in this section we study
367   the diversity within a sample St of model Mt for each generation t.
368
369
      Figure 4 displays the average pairwise normalized Levenshtein distance over generations of a self-
      consuming training loop for different data cycles. For each data cycle, we observe a decrease in
370
      diversity over the course of generations, with the degree of decrease varying depending on the data
371
      cycle. For the full synthetic data cycle we see a steep decline in diversity with a collapse into a
372   single point by generation t = 39. The incremental data cycle is initially stable, but decreases
373   in diversity from the 10th generation onwards, with a decrease of 68% in diversity by the end of
374   the 50 generations. The balanced and expanding data cycle also decrease in diversity, however,
375   this decrease is way slower, with a 30% decrease in diversity for the balanced data cycle and a
376   22% decrease for the expanding data cycle. While not all data cycles fully collapse in diversity
377   by generation 50, ultimately, we expect all of them to eventually reach zero diversity if the self-
      consuming training loop is run for enough generations.

424   by approximately 36%. If the portion of fresh data is 25% we only observe a decrease in diversity
425   of approximately 11%. This indicates that fresh data cannot stop the self-consuming training loop

      5                                            D ISCUSSION
474
475   In our experiments we found that iteratively re-training LLMs from scratch with self generated data
476   might help with correctness of model output. This is sometimes already done in practice where
477
      the output of LLMs is used to improve their performance (Huang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022;
      Gulcehre et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) or generate new training data (Yu et al.,
478
      2023). In this self-training setting the origin of data is known and researchers can carefully filter
479
      and adjust the data. However, the self-consuming training loop is an emerging trend where the data
480   source is not known and LLM-generated content from the internet is unwillingly used to train new
481   models. This trend is concerning as we also found that this self-consuming training loop eventually
482   leads to a drastic loss of diversity of a model’s output. Our results confirm the findings on the
483   self-consuming training loop in the field of image generation (Martı́nez et al., 2023; Alemohammad
484   et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2024; Bertrand et al., 2023), further highlighting the importance of
485   this issue for generative models and LLMs in particular. These LLMs have an even greater impact
      on society than image generation models, leading to more LLM-generated data being produced and


                                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      potentially having a greater impact on society as a whole, as many people already rely on good
487   model performance.
488
489   Consequently, as we expect that a self-consuming training loop will harm model performance in the
490
      future, researchers and practitioners should carefully choose their data when training their models
      and test those models sufficiently for diversity. Additionally, the machine learning community at
491
      large needs to find ways to deal with this problem as generated text data is already present in new
492
      internet scraped datasets. While our results indicate that fresh real data can slow down the self-
493   consuming training loop, other studies suggest that this is not an option as we will run out of real
494   data eventually and have to rely on generated data for future models (Villalobos et al., 2022). Addi-
495   tionally, LLM-generated data has already found its way onto the internet, even infecting academic
496   publications (Geng & Trotta, 2024; Liang et al., 2024), and LLM-generated data may soon overtake
497   real data on the internet.
498
      Furthermore, this LLM-generated data is hard to differentiate from real data (Kreps et al., 2022;
499   Sadasivan et al., 2023; Huschens et al., 2023), making it difficult to curate datasets scraped from the
500   web. This makes the self-consuming training loop inevitable in the future. Therefore, we advise to
501   further study ways to maintain diversity of generative model outputs like quality diversity methods
502   (Pugh et al., 2015; Fontaine & Nikolaidis, 2021).
503
504
505   6   L IMITATIONS
506
507   One cause for limitations of our work are restricted computational resources. First, we ran our
508   experiments with a smaller GPT-style model than for example GPT-4. Even though we expect that
509   larger models with billions of parameters still suffer from the same loss of diversity if trained in a
510
      self-consuming training loop, we note that the behavior may vary for those larger models. However,
      a study for models of those size is not feasible with current compute options nor is it responsible in
511
      terms of the expected CO2 emissions (Strubell et al., 2019). Nevertheless, we performed additional
512
      experiments for the full synthetic data cycle with varying model sizes in Appendix D.3 and still
513   observe the loss in diversity across smaller and larger models. Therefore, we expect that our results
514   can be generalized to models of different size.
515
516
      Second, the main results of this work consist of only one run per experimental configuration. With
      more runs per experiment the results would be more robust, but conducting more runs for our exper-
517
      iments was simply not feasible with our available computing resources. As all our experiments point
518
      towards the same direction, however, we do not believe that this limitation is crucial. Nevertheless,
519   to further mitigate this limitation we performed additional experiments for the full synthetic data
520   cycle with fewer generations over multiple runs and with different initializations in Appendix D.2.
521   These results show that the trend of a decline in diversity still holds true over multiple runs.
522
      Lastly, datasets used to train LLMs cover a wide range of different data sources with different
523
      characteristics. Therefore, our results might be slightly different when performed with a real web
524
      scraped corpora. However, the experiments with textual data show that our results generalize past
525   logic expressions.
526
527
528   7   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper we studied the behavior of LLMs trained in a self-consuming training loop and found
531   that iteratively training LLMs from scratch with self generated data can initially help with correct-
532   ness of model outputs. However, this comes at a price, as the diversity of generated data eventually
533   degenerates and collapses into a single point. The rate of this decline in diversity depends on the
534   data cycle creating the training data at each generation. Furthermore, we found that fresh data can
535   slow down this process and preserve diversity of a model output for longer. This should encourage
536
      researches to carefully monitor their datasets and models to mitigate such a harmful self-consuming
      training loop in the future.
537
538   In future work, we will study how fine-tuning can influence the self-consuming training loop for
539   LLMs. Additionally, we plan to further investigate how the effects of a self-consuming training loop
      can be slowed down or completely negated.

788   To better understand the decline of diversity, we inspect the number of unique expressions sampled

797   t = 21 the number of unique individuals is below 10 and in generation t = 39 only a single
798   individual is sampled at each generation. Consequently, the diversity is collapsed to zero.

1055   We manually inspect the text samples for the first few generations and notice a few hallucinated
1056   words that are not present in the original corpus, indicating a drop in correctness. However, overall
1057   the generated text is subjectively mostly correct and Shakespeare like with a diverse set of samples.
1058   By generation 10 the diversity is already declining for all data cycles. This is most severe for the full
1059   synthetic data cycle which produces nearly the same output every time. The incremental data cycle
1060   is also very repetitive text with repeating words in half of the time. In the other half (not shown
1061   in the example), the output is just a repetition of a role without text (e.g. GLOUCESTER:\n\n

1063   show a strong repetition of words.
1064   By generation 25 the full synthetic data cycle is fully converged producing the same outputs for the
1065   rest of the self-consuming training loop. The incremental data cycle also shows heavy repetition
1066   or just samples a role repeatedly and by generation 50 only very few roles are sampled repeatedly
1067   without text besides a very few exceptions. The other two data cycles continue the trend of producing
1068   repetitive words and n-grams and by generation 50 only small diverse text samples are generated
1069   before the same word or n-gram is produced repeatedly.
1070   Overall, while one could argue that the text is more correct (fewer hallucinated words) at the end
1071   of a self-consuming training loop, the stark decrease in diversity and the generation of meaningless
1072   text makes the language model unusable.
