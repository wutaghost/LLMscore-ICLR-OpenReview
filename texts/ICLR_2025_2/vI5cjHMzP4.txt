009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            A unifying theme in Artificial Intelligence is learning an effective policy to control
012            an agent in an unknown environment in order to optimize a certain performance
013            measure. Off-policy methods can significantly improve the sample efficiency dur-
014            ing training since they allow an agent to learn from observed trajectories generated
015            by different behavior policies, without directly deploying the target policies in
016            the underlying environment. This paper studies off-policy evaluation from biased
017            offline data where (1) unobserved confounding bias cannot be ruled out a priori; or
018
               (2) the observed trajectories do not overlap with intended behaviors of the learner,
               i.e., the target and behavior policies do not share a common support. Specifically,
019
               we first extend the Bellman’s equation to derive effective closed-form bounds over
020
               value functions from the observational distribution contaminated with unobserved
021            confounding and no-overlap. Second, we propose two novel algorithms that use
022            eligibility traces to estimate these bounds from finite observational data. Compared
023            to other partial identification methods for off-policy evaluation in sequential envi-
024            ronments, these methods are model-free and do not rely on additional parametric
025            knowledge about the system dynamics in the underlying environment.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   A typical reinforcement learning agent learns from past data, i.e., from observed trajectories of
031   states, actions, and reward signals generated by the agent intervening in the underlying environment.
032   This data reflects the influence of the decision-making policy used to allocate actions based on the
033
      observed state, which is called the behavior policy. This policy might be selected by the agent in the
      past or by a different demonstrator operating in the same environment. Policy evaluation studies the
034
      problem of evaluating the effectiveness of a candidate target policy from the combination of past data
035
      and theoretical assumptions about the environment. When the behavior and target policies coincide,
036   the evaluation is called on-policy learning, in which the expected return of candidate policies given
037   the agent’s starting state (i.e., the value function) could be directly estimated with empirical means
038   (Sutton & Barto, 1998). In practice, however, the learner might have to learn about policies different
039   from the currently deployed one that generated the data, leading to the off-policy learning problem.
040
      Off-policy learning is a popular area of research, as it allows for more efficient learning by using data
041
      from different policies. Several algorithms have been proposed for off-policy evaluation from finite
042   observations, including Q-learning (Watkins, 1989; Watkins & Dayan, 1992), importance sampling
043   (Swaminathan & Joachims, 2015; Jiang & Li, 2016), and temporal difference (Precup et al., 2000;
044   Munos et al., 2016). These algorithms rely on two critical assumptions about the behavior policy.
045   First, no unobserved confounder affects the behavior policy’s selected action and the subsequent state
046   and reward. Second, the behavior policy is stochastic, covering all intended actions the target policy
047   selects given all observed states. When either of these assumptions does not hold, the effect of the
048   target policy is generally not identifiable, i.e., the model assumptions are insufficient to uniquely
049   determine the value function from the offline data (Pearl, 2000; Zhang & Bareinboim, 2019).
050   In recent times, researchers have been using partial identification methods to obtain reliable off-policy
051   evaluation in situations where there are unobserved confounders, and the behavior and target policies
052   have no common support (Kallus & Zhou, 2018; Zhang & Bareinboim, 2019; Kallus & Zhou, 2020;
053   Namkoong et al., 2020; Khan et al., 2023; Bruns-Smith & Zhou, 2023; Kausik et al., 2024). Partial
      identification is a well-studied problem in causal inference (Balke & Pearl, 1997; Zhang et al., 2022),

073   include (1) the marginal sensitivity model presuming access to a bound over the odds ratio between
074   the nominal and actual behavioral policies (Kallus & Zhou, 2018; 2020; Namkoong et al., 2020;
075   Khan et al., 2023; Bruns-Smith & Zhou, 2023); (2) additional parametric assumptions about the
076   system dynamics (i.e., reward function and transition distribution) are invoked under which bounds
077   are derived (Khan et al., 2023; Kausik et al., 2024); (3) a model-based algorithm is applied, which
078   requires estimation of the underlying system dynamics (Zhang & Bareinboim, 2019); (4) the decision
079   horizon is finite, i.e., the agent only determines a finite number of actions (Kallus & Zhou, 2018;
080   Zhang & Bareinboim, 2019; Namkoong et al., 2020; Khan et al., 2023; Kausik et al., 2024). See
081
      Appendix A for a more detailed survey on partial identification and robust reinforcement learning.
082   This paper studies model-free algorithms for robust off-policy evaluation from confounded offline
083   data generated by behavior policy with no-overlap support. We propose novel partial identification
084   algorithms using eligibility traces to obtain informative bounds over the expected return of candidate
085   policies from offline data generated from an unknown Markov decision process with an infinite
086   horizon. More specifically, our contributions are summarized as follows. (1) We extend the Bellman
087
      equation that permits one to derive optimal bounds over target value functions from the observational
      distribution generated by an unknown behavior policy. (2) We propose a causal off-policy temporal
088
      difference algorithm (C-TD(λ)) using eligibility traces to estimate bounds over the state value
089
      function from finite observations contaminated with unobserved confounding and no-overlap. (3) We
090   introduce an alternative eligibility traces algorithm following tree backup (C-TB(λ)) that obtains
091   bounds over the state-action value function from confounded observations. Finally, we evaluate our
092   proposed algorithms using extensive simulations in synthetic environments. Due to space constraints,
093   all proofs are provided in Appendix B; details on the experiment setup are provided in Appendix C.

160   Off-Policy Evaluation Despite the effectiveness of planning algorithms, they require detailed
161   parametrization of the transition distribution T and the reward function R, which are not accessible
      in many real-world applications. This means that a learning process must take place. A common

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      approach is off-policy learning, where the agent has access to observed trajectories generated by a
163   behavioral policy fX , different from the target policy π, operating in the same environment. More
164   specifically, for every time step t, the behavioral policy selects an action Xt ← fX (st , ut ) based on
165   the current state St = st and latent noise Ut = ut . Fig. 1a shows the graphical representation of
166   the data-generating process of the behavior policy; the added bi-directed arrows, e.g., Xt ←→ Yt ,
167   indicate the presence of an unobserved confounder U ∈ Ut affecting both the action Xt and outcome
168   Yt . We summarize observed trajectories of the behavior policy using the observational distribution P .
169   Off-policy evaluation attempts to estimate the effects of a candidate policy π(x|s) from the observa-
170   tional data generated by the behavior policy fX . Standard off-policy methods focus on the identifiable
171   setting where the target transition distribution T and reward function R remain consistent in both the
172   interventional Pπ and observational distribution P . Formally,

      When Def. 2 holds, the learner could recover the parametrization of the transition distribution T
      and reward function R from the observational data, following the identification formula in Eq. (6).
180
      Several off-policy algorithms have been proposed to estimate the effect of candidate policies from
181
      finite observations under causal consistency (Watkins, 1989; Watkins & Dayan, 1992; Swaminathan
182   & Joachims, 2015; Jiang & Li, 2016; Precup et al., 2000; Munos et al., 2016).
183
184   There exist graphical criteria in the literature (Pearl & Robins, 1995; Shpitser et al., 2010; Perković
185
      et al., 2015) to evaluate whether causal consistency (Def. 2) holds from causal knowledge of the
      environment, including the celebrated backdoor criterion (Pearl, 2000, Def. 3.3.1). However, in
186
      many practical applications, causal consistency could be fragile and does not necessarily hold due to
187
      some violations in the generative process. These include: (1) there exists an unobserved confounder
188   affecting the action Xt and subsequent outcomes Yt , St+1 simultaneously (blue, dashed arrows in
189   Fig. 1a); (2) there is no overlap in the support between the target and behavior policies, i.e., the
190   propensity score P (xt | st ) = 0 for some state-action pair st , xt . When either of these violations
191   occurs, applying standard off-policy methods may fail to recover the expected return of the target
192   policy, leading to estimation bias. The following example illustrates such challenges.
193   Example 1 (Windy Gridworld). Consider a Windy Gridworld described in Fig. 1b, where the red dot
194   represents the agent and the green square represents the goal state. The agent can take five actions
195   Xt - up, down, right, left, and stay-put. However, the agent’s movement is affected by the
196   wind; the direction of the wind Ut includes - north, south, west, east, and no-wind. For
197   every time step, the agent receives a constant reward Yt ← −1. The next state of the agent is shifted
198   by both its action and the wind direction through the mechanism St+1 ← St + Xt + Ut .
199   Our goal is to evaluate the expected return of a target policy π ∗ described in Fig. 2a, which consistently
200   moves towards the goal state regardless of the wind direction. As an input, we have access to observed
201   trajectories generated by a behavior policy Xt ← fX (St , Ut ), which could sense the wind and select
202   an action accordingly. For example, when the agent is located in the top-left corner (St = (0, 0))
203   and the wind is blowing south (Ut = (0, 1)), the behavior policy will decide to move right
204   (Xt = (1, 0)) so that the agent could get close to the center (St+1 = (1, 1)).
205   Figs. 2b to 2d shows the value function estimation obtained by standard off-policy methods, including
206   Q-Learning, one-step Temporal Difference (TD), and Eligibility Traces (TD(λ)). We also include
207   in Fig. 2e the ground truth value function computed from the underlying model parameters. The
208   simulation reveals that standard off-policy evaluation deviates from the ground truth return. In this
209   observational data, the wind direction Ut is thus an unobserved confounder affecting both the action
210   Xt and next state St+1 , violating causal consistency. See Appendix C for additional discussions.

483   5     C ONCLUSION
484
485   This paper investigates off-policy evaluation in Markov Decision Processes from offline data collected
      by a different behavior policy, where unobserved confounding bias and no-overlap cannot be ruled


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      out a priori. This leads to violations of causal consistency (Def. 2), which could pose significant
487   challenges to standard off-policy algorithms. We first extend the celebrated Bellman’s equation
488   to derive informative bounds over values functions from the observational data, which are robust
489   against bias due to the presence of unobserved confounding and no-overlap. Based on these extended
490   equations, we propose two novel model-free off-policy algorithms using eligibility traces – one based
491   on the standard temporal difference (C-TD(λ)), and the other based on the tree-backup (C-TB(λ)).
492   These algorithms permit us to bound value functions from finite observations consistently.
493
494   E THICS S TATEMENT
495
496   This paper investigates the theoretical framework of robust off-policy evaluation from biased offline
497   data generated by a different behavior. Since unobserved confounding or no-overlap cannot be
498   ruled out a priori, the agent’s system dynamics in the environment cannot be fully identified from
499   the offline data. To address this challenge, we proposed novel off-policy algorithms that allow the
500   agent to derive informative bounds over value functions induced by a target policy from biased
501   offline data. A positive impact of this work is that we address the potential risk of policy learning
502   from offline data with the presence of unobserved confounding. Our framework is inherently robust
503
      against confounding bias and may apply to various consequential domains involving complex human
      interactions, including healthcare, marketing, finance, and autonomous driving. More broadly,
504
      automated decision systems using causal inference methods prioritize safety and robustness during
505
      their learning processes. Such requirements are increasingly essential since black-box AI systems are
506   prevalent, and our understanding of their potential implications is still limited.

759   Our work builds upon the literature on the partial identification of causal effects, sensitivity analysis,
760   and robust reinforcement learning from offline data.
761
762
763
      Partial Identification and Sensitivity Analysis Seminal work of Manski (1990) developed the
      first bounds on causal effects in non-identifiable settings using observational data in the single-stage
764
      treatment model with contextual information (i.e., a contextual bandit model). These bounds were then
765
      expanded to the instrumental variable setting (Balke & Pearl, 1997; Imbens & Angrist, 1994) partially
766   identify counterfactual probabilities of causation (Tian & Pearl, 2000). More recently, (Zhang &
767   Bareinboim, 2021) improved the bounds for applicability to continuous outcomes. (Zhang et al., 2022)
768   established a general framework for estimating bounds on interventional and counterfactual effects.
769   While Zhang et al. (2022) develop informative bounds using both observational and experimental data,
770   they focus on general counterfactual queries by discretizing the exogenous latent space, formulating
771   bounds as polynomial programs over this discretization and a Bayesian framework to approximately
772   estimate bounds using MCMC.
773   Sensitivity analysis attempts to provide intervals on causal effects by assuming the level of confound-
774   ing, for example, via models such as Marginal Sensitivity analysis, which considers deviations in the
775   propensity score in relation to the estimated propensity (Rosenbaum, 2005; Richardson et al., 2014;
776   Todem et al., 2010; Vansteelandt et al., 2006; Kallus & Zhou, 2018; Kallus et al., 2019; Namkoong
777   et al., 2020; Jesson et al., 2022; Bruns-Smith & Zhou, 2023; Kausik et al., 2024). Other approaches
778   explore additional parametric assumptions about the structural functions, including linearity (Cinelli
779   et al., 2019) and Lipschitz continuity (Khan et al., 2023). Our work does not rely on additional func-
780
      tional constraints on the underlying system dynamics. Instead, we focus on the settings of standard
      discrete Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) with an infinite horizon. We develop robust off-policy
781
      evaluation algorithms to estimate closed-form bounds over the discounted cumulative rewards of
782
      candidate policies from offline observational data contaminated with unobserved confounding bias.
783
784
785   Robust Reinforcement Learning Unlike planning in a standard MDP, robust reinforcement
786   learning does not assume the parametrization of the transition probability function in the underlying
787   model to be precisely determined. Instead, it is contained in a set of model parameters which is
788   called the uncertainty set (Iyengar, 2005; Nilim & El Ghaoui, 2005; Xu & Mannor, 2010; Wiesemann
789   et al., 2013; Yu & Xu, 2015; Mannor et al., 2016; Petrik & Russel, 2019). The goal of the agent is
790   to learn a robust policy that performs the best under the worst possible case in the uncertainty set.
791   Similar problems have been studied under the rubrics of safe policy learning (Thomas et al., 2015;
792
      Ghavamzadeh et al., 2016) or pessimistic reinforcement learning (Shi et al., 2022).2
793   Robust RL algorithms with provable guarantees have been proposed in tabular settings or under the
794   assumptions of linear functions (Lim et al., 2013; Tamar et al., 2014; Roy et al., 2017; Badrinath &
795   Kalathil, 2021; Wang & Zou, 2021). Combined with the computational framework of deep learning,
796   robust RL algorithms have been extended to complex, high-dimensional domains (Pinto et al., 2017;
797   Zhang et al., 2020). More recently, (Panaganti et al., 2022) proposed Robust Fitted Q-Iteration
798
      (RFQI) to learn the best possible robust policy from offline data with theoretical guarantees on the
      performance of the learned policy. Our work differs from robust RL methods since it does not
799
      require a pre-specified uncertainty set of model parameters. Instead, we construct the ignorance
800
      region over the underlying system dynamics from the confounded observational data using partial
801   causal identification. Based on the learned uncertainty set, we then derived closed-form bounds
802   over the value functions of the target policy. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that
803   develops off-policy algorithms using eligibility traces to obtain evaluations of candidate policies
804   from biased offline data, possibly contaminated with unmeasured confounding or no-overlap, with
805   provable guarantees on the convergence of learned evaluations.
806

           Indeed, the idea of planning over a convex set of model parameters have been explored in online reinforce-
809   ment learning. (Strehl & Littman, 2008) utilized an extended dynamic programming to learn an optimistic policy
      over a confidence set of models to balance the trade-off between exploration and exploitation.

1179   Confounded Behavior Policy Consider now an off-policy learning task in the windy gridworld,
1180   where the agent’s goal is to evaluate the expected return of a target policy π ∗ described in Fig. 2a.
1181   Following such a policy π ∗ , the agent will consistently move towards the goal state s = (1, 2) from
1182   its current location, regardless of the wind direction.
1183   The detailed parametrization of the agent’s system dynamics in the windy gridworld remains unknown.
1184   Instead, its has access to observed trajectories generated by a behavior policy x ← fX (s, u) which
1185   could sense the wind and select an action accordingly; Fig. 9 provides a detailed description for this
1186   behavior policy. For example, when the agent is located in the top-left corner (s = (0, 0)) and the
1187   wind is blowing south (s = (0, 1)), the behavior policy x ← fX (s, u) will decide to move right
       (x = (1, 0)) so that the agent could get to the center (s′ = (1, 1)).
