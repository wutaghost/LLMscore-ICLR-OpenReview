009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Quantifying spatio-temporal (ST) measures of dynamical systems is a crucial
012            problem with wide ranging applications in climate modeling, epidemiology, phys-
013            ical processes to name a few. We are interested in the same but motivated by a
014            rather practical scenario where sparse information is collected non-uniformly. To
015            reconstruct the underlying dynamical system under such constraints, we propose
016            a novel algorithm for learning the Koopman operator via a Reproducing Kernel
017            Hilbert Space (RKHS) based on the Laplacian Kernel Extended Dynamic Mode
018            Decomposition (Lap-KeDMD). We further show that our kernel space resolves a
019
               fundamental issue that is required for a faithful reconstruction of the Koopman
               operator of the underlying ST data by proving its closability. We demonstrate our
020
               method on standard benchmark cases – Burger’s Equation, fluid flow across cylin-
021
               der and Duffing Oscillator. We then reconstruct the Koopman operator for a real
022            ST Seattle traffic flow data that is collected non-uniformly. Necessary compar-
023            isons are made between the current state of the art kernel methods corresponding
024            to Gaussian Radial Basis Function (GRBF) Kernel. Such empirical comparisons
025            leads us to conclude that Lap-KeDMD remarkably outperforms as compared to
026            that of aforementioned counter-part thereby, making the Laplacian Kernel a ro-
027            bust choice for such ST quantification.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Spatio-temporal (ST) data have become ubiquitous in the current age where surveillance systems
033
      and sensors are recording large volumes of video data in airports, roads, weather monitoring etc.
      One important aspect of dealing with ST data is the task of prediction. Numerous applications in
034
      traffic prediction, video action recognition, anomaly detection have led to advancement in deep
035
      learning techniques like spatio-temporal convolution, spatio-temporal transformers, graph based
036   spatio-temporal transformers to name a few (see, e.g. He et al. (2019)Zhou et al. (2016)Grigsby
037   et al. (2021)Yu et al. (2020)Yan et al. (2021)Li et al. (2022).
038
039
      On the other hand, there is an increased focus on understanding ST data from a dynamical systems
      perspective (see Budišić et al. (2012)) where the task is not ST prediction but rather spatio-temporal
040
      reconstruction. Theoretical investigations of dynamical systems, first initiated by Poincare, has re-
041
      cently made huge advancements due to many applications arising in system design of engineered
042   systems, optimization and control and understandings of complex physical phenomena like turbu-
043   lence. Currently, data-driven methods provide a promising route in ST reconstruction by learning
044   spatio-temporal modes of the dynamical system. One of the key and useful data-driven algorithm to
045   provide ST modes is Dynamic Mode Decomposition developed by Schmid (2010). DMD in princi-
046   ple is an unsupervised machine learning (ML) algorithm Fujii & Kawahara (2019), based on Krylov
047   subspaces Saad (2003) and Arnoldi algorithm Arnoldi (1951).
048   As far as ST reconstruction in concerned, ML architectures for instance Rubanova et al. (2019)
049   (ODE-RNNs) or Ye et al. (2020); Murata et al. (2020); Hasegawa et al. (2020), (Brunton & Kutz,
050   2022, Chapter 6 Section 8, Page 236) based on Convolutional Neural Networks and Autoencoders
051   offer a competitive platform to simulate, model and forecast complex, chaotic non-linear dynami-
052   cal systems but face serious issues in analyzing the underlying dynamics as they lack tendency to
053   operate or evolve with respect to time (cf. Mezić (2021); Sarker (2021); Haggerty et al. (2023)) Inter-
      estingly, such pitfall is overcome up by the data-driven techniques powered by the spectral analysis

054
      of the infinite-dimensional linear Koopman operators (cf. Koopman (1931); Koopman & Neumann
055   (1932); Mezić (2005)) over the underlying Hilbert space Williams et al. (2015a); Li et al. (2017) and
056   such framework of DMD is referred as Extended DMD (eDMD). However, since such data driven
057   methods are leveraged by the finite rank representations of the Koopman operators, which fails to
058   be compact Singh & Kumar (1979), hence making the usual L2 Hilbert space incomprehensible.
059
      In such cases, choosing (adequate) RKHSs for the action of Koopman operators to perform DMD
060
      naturally becomes a more justifiable options, which is called as Kernel Extended DMD (KeDMD)
061   Baddoo et al. (2022); Klus et al. (2018; 2020); Rosenfeld et al. (2022) and is central to this paper.
062   Recent investigation in KeDMD by Colbrook in (Colbrook, 2023, Page 44) recommends that one
063   should choose the kernel function space so that the Koopman operator on the underlying RKHS is
064   not just densely defined but also ‘closable’. However, coming up with such kernel spaces is non-
065   trivial Ikeda et al. (2022b;a). Among the popular choices of the kernel functions, is GRBF Kernel
066   (Steinwart et al., 2006, Page 4639) which not only just finds application in data-driven modeling but
067   also in various ML platforms such as support vector machines and etc.
068   There is a growing interest of data predictions based on collecting ST-reconstructions via Koopman
069   operators over the underlying RKHS. However, the problem of ST-reconstruction for data predic-
070   tions while collecting snapshots irregularly is still under-investigated. Ideally, full dataset is needed
071   to initiate DMD and is synthesis by stacking the data snapshots collected in a regular time-sampled
072   manner. Experimental analysis in Bevanda et al. (2024); Tu et al. (2014); Klus et al. (2016); Rosen-
073   feld et al. (2022); Brunton & Kutz (2022); Williams et al. (2015a); Schmid (2010; 2022); Baddoo
074   et al. (2022) (and references there in) seems to enjoy such hypothetical-ideal situation, which seems
075
      to be quite far from the actual reality. Nevertheless, describing and forecasting the time evolution
      of dynamical systems still remains a challenging problem in the setting of limited irregularly placed
076
      data snapshots. Lack of investigation in this direction motivates this very paper, which provide the
077
      solution to this problem by leveraging Kernelized eDMD and novelty of Laplacian Kernel.
078
079   Recovering ST modes is essentially a key to the data-driven methods and fueled by this, this paper
080   aims to provide the solution to the challenge of recovering data dynamics when we have irregular
081
      and sparse data in conjunction with limited dataset.
082   Our main contribution in the proposed work is summarized below:
083
084         1. We develop an RKHS by the Laplacian Kernel embedded as L2 Lebesgue measure in
085            Subsubsection 3.1 and provide the Koopman operator theoretic quantification (cf. Theo-
086            rem A.12 and Theorem A.19) of Koopman operators over this RKHS.
087         2. We show that the Koopman operators over this RKHS space is closable(Theorem 3.2) while
088            showing that the current GRBF Kernel leads to a failure of closability. (Theorem 3.3).
089
090
            3. We develop an algorithm to identify the Koopman operator over this RKHS based on irreg-
               ular and sparsely sampled data and tabulate our results in Subsubsection 3.2. In particular,
091
               we showcase our results on real ST data collected sparsely from speed sensors in Seattle.

223   For KeDMD methods, as already first noted by Colbrook (2023) and then also by, Giannakis & Valva
224   (2024) a challenge associated with RKHS techniques is that RKHS fails to exhibit invariance under
225   the action of the Koopman operator. Further, Koopman operator invariance problem is actually tied
226   with ST reconstruction of data matrix Bevanda et al. (2024) as well. The solution to this problem lies
227   in choosing a suitable RKHSs where Koopman operators need to be closable Ikeda et al. (2022b). If
228   in case closability criteria is not satisfied, this immediately will implicates that ST reconstruction is
229
      not possible. In the light of this, this section contains important theoretical results which leads up to
      the justification of closability of Koopman operators over the RKHS of Laplacian Kernel.

510   4   C ONCLUSION
511
512   We presented Lap-KeDMD to discover ST modes from a given limited spatio-temporal dataset and
513   compared the results in contrast to that of GRBF Kernel using spectral observables of Koopman op-
514   erators. We quantify bounded closable and compact Koopman operators on RKHS H ,1,Cn . Also,
515   main reason why we fail to achieve the closability of the Koopman operators over the GRBF Ker-
516   nel is because of the inner-product for the function space corresponding to the GRBF Kernel. In
517   particular, the measure present in the norm for the function space in equation 12 is unable to make
518
      the L2 integration finite. Since the closability of Koopman operator is directly linked with the
      ST-mode reconstruction, we see that reconstruction for the experiments via Laplacian Kernel are of
519
      higher quality in terms of information richness and data measure predictions as opposed to that of
520
      GRBF Kernel. We already anticipate such results due to the closability of the Koopman operators
521   over the the RKHS of Laplacian Kernel as opposed to that of the GRBF Kernel. For future direc-
522   tions, it will be interesting to investigate the workflow of the Lap-KeDMD algorithm to recover the
523   ST-modes of data if we have partial information of the dynamical system.
