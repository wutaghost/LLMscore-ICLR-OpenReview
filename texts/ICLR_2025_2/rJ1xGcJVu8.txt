                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               In various scientific and engineering fields, the primary research areas have re-
010
               volved around physics-based dynamical systems modeling and data-driven time
011
               series analysis. According to the embedding theory, dynamical systems and time
012            series can be mutually transformed using observation functions and physical re-
013            construction techniques. Based on this, we propose Embedding Duality Theory,
014            where the parameterized embedding layer essentially provides a linear estimation
015            of the non-linear time series dynamics. This theory enables us to bypass the param-
016            eterized embedding layer and directly employ physical reconstruction techniques
017            to acquire a data embedding representation. Utilizing physical priors results in a
018            10× reduction in parameters, a 3× increase in speed, and maximum performance
019            enhancements of 18% in expert, 22% in zero-shot, and 53% in few-shot tasks with-
020
               out any hyper-parameter tuning. All methods are encapsulated as a plug-and-play
               module at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PSR-001/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The explosion of real-time sensing data from the physical world opens up new opportunities for data-
026   driven time series analysis, achieving widespread recognition in energy, transportation, education,
027   meteorology, and other domains by leveraging the strong fitting capabilities of neural networks (Jin
028   et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024b; Mao et al., 2024). However, deep time series models
029   struggle to comprehend the underlying physical laws of data, leading to a propensity for overfitting
030   and lacking generalizability to unseen data (Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024a).
031   In numerous scientific and engineering disciplines, another central focus lies in dynamical systems
032   that evolve over space and time, exampled in fluid mechanics, thermodynamics, and neuroscience
033   (Brunton et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021). According to the Takens (Takens, 1980)
034   and Whitney theorems (Whitney, 1936), time series can be viewed as observations stemming from
035   underlying dynamical systems, leading to a principal way to model the essence of time series data.

129   Parameterized Embedding in Deep Time Series Models. The embedding technique, serving as a
130                           R        R
      space transformation n 7→ m , facilitates the mapping of discrete sparse features into continuous
131   dense vector representations, laying a solid foundation for success across various machine learning
132   domains. In time series analysis benchmark, mainstream methods utilize patch operation (Figure 3a)
133   to conduct local linear projection (Nie et al., 2022), with certain models using convolutions to address
134   inter-block information isolation (Hu et al., 2024c; Zhang et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2024). Additionally,
135   for audio modeling tasks, windowed spectral transformation techniques (Figure 3b), such as short-
136   time Fourier transform, are commonly employed to extract time-frequency representations, thereby
137
      addressing concerns related to information density (Erol et al., 2024). Grid embeddings (Figure 3c)
      are commonly employed to handle the spatial-temporal relationships within the data (Gupta et al.,
138
      2021; Wu et al., 2023). Furthermore, several studies leveraged self-supervised learning to obtain
139
      enhanced embedding representations (Lee et al., 2023; Fraikin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a).
140
141   Embedding Theory for Dynamics System Reconstruction. Since significant results proposed
142   by (Whitney, 1936; Takens, 1980) and formalized in (Sauer et al., 1991), the Embedding Theory
      has pervaded through almost all aspects of nonlinear dynamical systems (Definition 1). The time
143
                     R
      series x(t) ∈ n can be broadly interpreted as successive, though not always regular, observations
144

                                       R                                          R
      of a dynamical system F ∈ m via a measurement function h : m 7→ n (n < m). The main  R
      goal is to reconstruct the underlying system and explore its properties, which paved the way for
146   developing numerous techniques like derivatives (Packard et al., 1980), integrals (Gilmore, 1998),
147   time delay (Takens, 1980; Abarbanel et al., 1994), and principal component embedding (Broomhead
148   & King, 1986). The system dynamics is subsequently learned using preferred modeling tools such as
149   recurrent neural networks (Sangiorgio & Dercole, 2020), state space models (Alonso et al., 2024; Hu
150   et al., 2024a), and reservoir computing (Haluszczynski & Räth, 2019; Yan et al., 2024), etc. Attraos
151   (Hu et al., 2024a) pioneered using the time delay embedding technique in time series forecasting
152   tasks, while our paper extensively explores various embedding techniques (Section 4.1), and provides
153   comprehensive theoretical (Section 4.2) and experimental (Section 5) analysis.

261   Dynamical System Characteristics According to the Embedding Theory, insufficient phase space
262   dimensions cause dynamical structures to stack, obscuring their true shapes. Conversely, excessive
263   dimensions expand the structure excessively, amplifying noise effects. The threshold typically equals
264
      twice the latent dynamics, and exceeding this threshold results in spurious structures. Based on this,
      we propose the following conjectures, which are empirically validated in Section 5.1.
265
266   Conjecture 4.5 (Dim Scaling Law). For parameterized embedding, as the hidden dimensions
267
      increase, the model loss will generally decrease initially and then increase, as shown in Figure 6.
268   Conjecture 4.6 (Spurious Dynamics). Bidirectional modeling, whether through a transformer or
269
      SSM backbone, helps eliminate spurious dynamical structures that are sensitive to the positional
      inductive bias, consequently enhancing performance as empirically demonstrated in Table 2.

525   Discussion About Scalability. The remarkable improvements achieved by physics-guided embed-
526   dings in few-shot and zero-shot scenarios suggest their potential application in large-scale time series
527   foundation models (LTSFM) (Jin et al., 2023). A crucial aspect of advancing towards physics-guided
528   LTSFM is the necessity of the scaling laws. However, while physics-guided embeddings are available
529   in model depth (layers) expansion, they are constrained by physical priors in model width (hidden
530   dimensions), leading to significant constraints on memory capacity as the dataset size increases. One
531
      potential solution is to integrate physics-guided embeddings with a Mixture of Expert techniques
      (Cai et al., 2024). Diverse dynamical dimensions are established to enhance model representation
532
      and memory storage during the training stage, with an adaptive selection during the inference stage.
533
534   6 C ONCLUSION & F UTURE W ORK
535   Inspired by embedding theory, this paper demonstrates that the embedding layer in a deep time
536   series model is an estimation of the underlying dynamics of the data. Based on this, we explore
537   replacing parameterized embedding with numerical reconstruction techniques. Experiments show that
538   physics-guided embeddings significantly improve performance across various tasks and backbones.
539   In the future, we aim to advance physics-guided embeddings as a standard embedding technique for
      expert models and develop physics-guided time series foundation models (Liang et al., 2024).

      A     T ECHNICAL BACKGROUND
757

      A.1   H OW TO DETERMINE THE PHYSICAL HYPER - PARAMETER
760
      As mentioned earlier, the theoretical assurances of Takens’ theorem falter under finite precision and
761
      noise, prompting the exploration of "optimal" embedding parameters. The notion of an "optimal"
762   set suggests that embeddings vary in quality. Yet, assessing this quality necessitates a metric for
763   comparison. Apart from the empirical selection methods mentioned in the paper, there are also
764   other mainstream approaches available. Generally, these methods can be summarized in two broad
765   categories or arguments: prediction-based and topological arguments.
766
767
      • Prediction-based methods (Casdagli et al., 1991; Potapov, 1997) notions of embedding quality
        can be seen to be inspired by the application of embeddings in the context of time-series prediction.
768
        Fundamentally, good embeddings should enable better predictions. These methods generally try to
769
        maximize the amount of new information incorporated in each delay dimension with the aim that it
770     will provide more information about the true system state and aid in time series prediction.
771
      • Topological methods (Buzug & Pfister, 1992; Nichkawde, 2013) often concentrate on analyzing
772
        the attractor structure and the distribution of the manifold within its ambient space. Essentially, a
773
        well-structured embedding in terms of topology and geometry should aim to be adequately spread
774     out and unfolded within its ambient setting. This concept of quality aligns with Casdagli’s noise
775     amplification arguments. Geometrically-based methods may encompass metrics like the fill factor
776     and displacement from the diagonal. In essence, the considerations for determining the optimal
777     lag and embedding dimension for time delay embedding can be encapsulated by the notions of
778     irrelevance and redundancy.
779
780
      A.2   DYNAMICAL E NCODER
781
782   In the field of machine learning, particularly in the realm of dynamical systems modeling, articles
783   on chaotic dynamical systems primarily focus on employing recurrent neural networks (RNNs)
784   (Mikhaeil et al., 2022; Hess et al., 2023) and state-space models (Hu et al., 2024a; Alonso et al.,
785   2024) for modeling, relying on the autoregressive nature of models to capture underlying dynamics.
786   Additionally, some studies are dedicated to reservoir computing (Yan et al., 2024), simulating the
787   problems sensitive to initial values of partial differential equations by maintaining a random vector
788   reservoir. Furthermore, Neural ODEs (Li et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2021) and some Physics-Informed
789   Neural Networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019) attempt to uncover underlying patterns in data through
790
      a combination of data-driven and physics-constrained approaches. Increasingly, research indicates
      that deep learning models, i,e., Transformers (Hang et al., 2024) can also achieve impressive results.

845   C.1    E NCODER BACKBONE
846
847   In this paper, we have selected state-of-the-art models based on the CNN, Transformer, and SSM

849
      • Modern-TCN (Luo & Wang, 2024) is a pure convolutional architecture that incorporates both
850
        upsampling, downsampling techniques, and patching methods to stack models that separately
851     capture temporal and channel correlations.
852
      • PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022) is the first transformer architecture to introduce chunking operations,
853     employing a channel-independent strategy to apply the same backbone model to each time variable.
854     It continues to maintain state-of-the-art performance in many tasks to this day.
855   • TimeSSM (Hu et al., 2024b) is a recent model architecture that applies the SSM kernel, typically
856     utilizing patching operation and channel-independent modeling strategies, particularly excelling in
857     prediction tasks with outstanding performance.
