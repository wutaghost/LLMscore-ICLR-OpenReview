011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the accumulation of high-quality data and advancements in visual pretrain-
014            ing paradigms, recent Video Foundation Models (VFMs) have made significant
015            progress, demonstrating remarkable performance on popular video understanding
016
               benchmarks. However, conventional benchmarks (e.g. Kinetics) and evaluation
               protocols are limited by their relatively poor diversity, high evaluation costs, and
017
               saturated performance metrics. In this work, we introduce a comprehensive bench-
018
               mark suite to address these issues, namely VideoEval. We establish the Video Task
019            Adaption Benchmark (VidTAB) and the Video Embedding Benchmark (VidEB)
020            from two perspectives: evaluating the task adaptability of VFMs under few-shot
021            conditions and assessing their feature embedding’s direct applicability to down-
022            stream tasks. With VideoEval, we conduct a large-scale study of 20 popular
023            open-source vision foundation models. Our study reveals some insightful findings,
024            1) overall, current VFMs exhibit weak generalization across diverse tasks, 2) in-
025            creasing video data, whether labeled or in video-text pairs, does not necessarily
026            improve task performance, 3) the effectiveness of some pre-training paradigms
027
               may not be fully validated in previous benchmarks, and 4) combining different pre-
               training paradigms can help develop models with better generalization capabilities.
028
               We believe this study serves as a important complement to the current evaluation
029
               methods for VFMs and offers valuable insights for future research directions.

034
      The field of deep learning is experiencing a significant paradigm shift due to the emergence of
035
      foundation models (FMs). These models, exemplified by BERT Devlin et al. (2018), GPT Brown
036   et al. (2020); OpenAI (2023a;b), CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and Stable Diffusion Rombach et al.
037   (2021), are trained on massive and diverse data at scale and demonstrate remarkable adaptability to a
038   broad spectrum of downstream tasks.
039
      In the realm of video understanding, early researchers train backbone networks Feichtenhofer et al.
040
      (2019); Bertasius et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022); Fan et al. (2021) using visual classification tasks
041
      on large-scale labeled datasets like ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) and Kinetics Kay et al. (2017b).
042   However, the high cost associated with labeled data promotes the development of self-supervised
043   learning methods that capitalize on unlabeled data for visual pre-training Pan et al. (2021); Wei et al.
044   (2022); Feichtenhofer et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022a). Furthermore, researchers
045   delve into multimodal pre-training utilizing large-scale visual-text pairs Xu et al. (2021a); Yan
046   et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024a); Li et al. (2023), thereby enhancing their models’ capabilities and
047   demonstrating impressive zero-shot performance. Overall, fueled by the accumulation of high-quality
048   image and video data and advancements in visual pre-training paradigms, Video Foundation Models
049   (VFMs) witness remarkable progress in recent years. A new generation of VFMs Feichtenhofer
050
      et al. (2022); Tong et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023b); Bardes et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024);
      Wang et al. (2022b; 2024b) emerges, demonstrating outstanding performance on conventional video
051
      understanding benchmarks.

097   To tackle these problems, we build a comprehensive benchmark suite for evaluation of VFMs, namely
098   VideoEval. As shown in Figure 1, our method has the following key features: Comprehensive:
099
      First, we created the Video Task Adaptation Benchmark (VidTAB) to evaluate the adaptability of
      VFMs to unseen tasks with limited samples. We collected public datasets from various video task
100
      domains, including action recognition in special scenarios, AI for science, video content moderation,
101
      video quality/aesthetic assess, and emotion analysis. From these domains, we constructed eight
102   adaptation tasks and developed evaluation protocols and adaptation methods suitable for current
103   VFMs. Additionally, to assess the capability of VFMs’ feature embedding for downstream applica-
104   tions, we created the Video Embedding Benchmark (VidEB), which includes four tasks that evaluate
105   embedding at different granularities. Challenging & Indicative: Due to the diversity of test data
106   and the effectiveness of our evaluation protocols, our VideoEval can effectively distinguish between
107   various VFMs that perform similarly on traditional benchmarks, providing deeper insights into their
      true capabilities. Low-cost: Thanks to our training-light few-shot evaluation and training-free feature

      2     R ELATED WORK
137
138
      Video foundation models With the continuous growth of image Sharma et al. (2018); Changpinyo
139
      et al. (2021); Schuhmann et al. (2022) and video data Bain et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2024a); Chen
140
      et al. (2023; 2024a;b) and advancements in pre-training paradigms, research on Video Foundation
141   Models (VFMs) has progressed rapidly. Current VFMs are primarily built around two pre-training
142   paradigms: masked video modeling based on unimodal video data Feichtenhofer et al. (2022);
143   Tong et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023b; 2022a; 2023c); Girdhar et al. (2023); Ryali et al. (2023)
144   and video-text contrastive learning based on multimodal visual-text pairs Xu et al. (2021a); Wang
145   et al. (2023a); Yan et al. (2022); Cheng et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024a). Some works Wang
146   et al. (2022b); Li et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024) combine these paradigms, enabling VFMs to
147   extend further into multimodal understanding. Additionally, some studies introduce modalities like
148   audio and speech on top of video and text Chen et al. (2023; 2024a); Wang et al. (2024b), further
149
      expanding the capabilities of VFMs. Recently, InternVideo2 Wang et al. (2024b) leverages mature
      pre-training paradigms and large-scale high-quality data to scale VFMs to 6 billion parameters,
150
      achieving remarkable performance improvements.
151
152
153
      Evaluation of VFMs Previous works primarily utilize action recognition benchmarks focused on
      appearance and motion Kay et al. (2017b); Goyal et al. (2017a); Gu et al. (2017) to evaluate VFMs.
154
      To enhance evaluation diversity, some studies explore richer domains and tasks Thoker et al. (2022a);
155
      Deng et al. (2023); Schiappa et al. (2023), but they remain limited to action recognition tasks. The
156   InternVideo series Wang et al. (2022b; 2024b) and VideoGLUE Yuan et al. (2023) attempt to provide
157   a more comprehensive evaluation of VFMs by expanding the number of benchmarks and evaluation
158   protocols. However, these efforts are still based on existing benchmarks and incurred high validation
159   costs. In contrast, our work considers the characteristics and application scenarios of VFMs, offering
160   a comprehensive and low-cost evaluation solution through task definition and evaluation protocols,
161   aimed at rapidly verifying the generalization capabilities of VFMs—a crucial aspect currently lacking
      in the community’s development of these models.

      3         B UILDING V IDEO E VAL
183
      We argue that a powerful video foundation model should possess two key capabilities: (1) strong task
184
      adaptation ability, i.e., the ability to adapt to diverse, unseen tasks with limited training samples, and
185
      (2) the capacity to extract feature embedding that retain and distill key information from videos, di-
186   rectly supporting various downstream tasks. From these perspectives, we construct VideoEval, which
187   includes the Video Task Adaptation Benchmark (VidTAB) and the Video Embedding Benchmark
188   (VidEB). By creating diverse task scenarios and employing efficient evaluation methods, VideoEval
189   can quickly and comprehensively assess the generalization ability of VFMs in video understanding. In
190   this section, we present our VideoEval in detail. The construction pipeline for VideoEval is illustrated
191   in Figure 2, and the evaluation tasks we ultimately constructed are presented in Table 2.
192
193   3.1          V IDEO TASK A DAPTION B ENCHMARK
194
195   Collecting diverse dataset from public source. Previous benchmarks primarily focus on evaluating
196   video models based on human actions, overlooking many other tasks requiring video understanding.
197   Therefore, we consider five different application scenarios:
198
                     • Action Recognition in Special Scenarios (Action): While previous benchmarks have exten-
199
                       sively examined action recognition tasks, our focus here is to assess VFMs’ capabilities in
200
                       recognizing actions within special scenarios.
201
202                  • AI for Science (Science): Referencing previous work Zhao et al. (2024), we classify tasks
203                    related to medicine and natural sciences as a category.
204                  • Video Content Moderation (Safety): We group tasks related to identifying harmful or
205                    misleading information in video content.
206
                     • Video Quality Assessment (Quality): We categorize more subjective tasks into this group.
207                    The goal is to assess VFMs’ ability to learn low-level information and human aesthetic
208                    preferences.
209
                     • Emotion Analysis (Emotion): We group tasks related to human emotion analysis into this
210
                       category to evaluate VFMs’ ability to understand and analyze human emotions.

232             1. Remove Low-Quality Video Datasets: We manually exclude datasets with videos that have
233
                   low resolution (below 240p), low frame rate (below 15fps), insufficient quantity (fewer than
                   150 videos per category), or low annotation accuracy (below 90%).
234
235             2. Select Discriminative Tasks: For task difficulty screening, we first evaluate zero-shot
236                classification performance using CLIP-L Radford et al. (2021), EVA-g Sun et al. (2023),
237                ViCLIP-L Wang et al. (2024a), and Internvideo2-1B Wang et al. (2024b). We then classify
238
                   samples as follows: Easy: Samples that are correctly classified by three or more models.
                   Spatial: Samples that are correctly classified by both CLIP and EVA. Temporal: Samples
239
                   that are correctly classified by at least one of ViCLIP or Internvideo2-1B, but not by CLIP
240
                   and EVA. Hard: Samples that are incorrectly classified by all models. We use the zero-shot
241                classification accuracy of the models and the aforementioned proportions as references for
242                task selection. Based on this, we choose tasks with lower zero-shot classification accuracy,
243                higher proportions of Hard and Temporal samples, and lower proportions of Easy samples.
244                The proportions of each type of sample in the tasks we ultimately selected can be found in
245                Table 3.
246             3. Control the Number of Categories: For datasets that originally include category labels,
247                such as ARID Xu et al. (2021b) and Animal Kingdom Ng et al. (2022), we select categories
248                with sufficient samples to ensure evaluation accuracy and stability. We also control the final
249                number of categories to avoid making the adaptation task overly difficult. We observed that
250                both zero-shot testing and few-shot experiments based on current VFMs show that when
251                the number of categories is too high, models often perform no better than random guessing.
252                Although this issue may be mitigated as VFMs improve, we currently need to control the
253
                   number of categories to effectively showcase differences between models. We select the
                   main categories for each task and limit the number of categories to around 10 (based on
254
                   few-shot experiments).
255
256             4. Handling Multi-label and Regression Tasks: For datasets that are not originally classifica-
257                tion tasks, we transform the tasks into classification tasks. For example, for DOVER Wu et al.
258
                   (2023), which is used for video aesthetics and technical quality assessment (a regression
                   task), we assume that videos with quality scores in the top 40% are "high-quality videos" and
259
                   those with scores in the bottom 40% are "low-quality videos", thus converting the original
260
                   task into a binary classification task.
261
262   In total, we construct eight classification tasks to evaluate the adaptation capabilities of video
263   foundation models.
264

266   (2023) typically train video models using entire samples of training set, and most popular benchmarks
267   have large training sample sizes. We argue that this evaluation method overlooks the examination
268   of the adaptation capability of VFMs. As illustrated in Figure 3, under the scenario of using full
269   training samples, the differences between VFMs are difficult to discern. However, under a low-sample
      protocol, different foundation models exhibit varying degrees of task adaptation capabilities. We

354   3.2   V IDEO E MBEDDING B ENCHMARK
355
356   The main application domains of video embeddings we considering include: Label-Level: Classifica-
357   tion and Action Retrieval. Instance-Level: Retrieval, Copy Detection and Ranking. For label-level
358   tasks, VidTAB has already provided a flexible way to evaluate models. Therefore, VidEB aims to
359   assess existing models at a finer semantic level, focusing on instance-level tasks. Although ranking
360   tasks are common in recommendation system scenarios, they are influenced by user information
361   and interactions, in addition to video data. Based on prior research Ni et al. (2023), using frozen
362   embeddings for video features does not consistently improve recommendation tasks (resulting in
363
      minimal or even negative effects). Thus, we have narrowed the final dataset scope to instance-level
      retrieval and copy detection. Apart from the traditional classification tasks, the evaluation of repre-
364
      sentations typically involves standard benchmarks such as video action retrieval Han et al. (2020a);
365
      Xu et al. (2019); Han et al. (2020b), which primarily rely on class labels. However, this approach
366   often overlooks the overall scene context and exhibits an overlap with recognition tasks. In contrast,
367   inspired by previous works Plummer et al. (2015); Pizzi et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2007); Jiang et al.
368   (2014); Douze et al. (2021), we establish more rigorous criteria for embedding evaluation in Table 2.
369   Specifically, we require the model to determine the priority and retrieve individual samples based on
370   the overall similarity, rather than solely relying on class labels. This evaluation protocol provides a
371   more comprehensive assessment of the model’s capability to encapsulate subtle visual information.
372   Evaluation protocol. To facilitate fine-grained embedding evaluation, we incorporate two tasks for
373   assessment: (1) Hierarchical Video Retrieval aims to retrieve videos from a database that closely
374   matches the query video in terms of scene, viewpoint, and temporal context. According to previous
375   work Kordopatis-Zilos et al. (2019), videos related to the query are categorized into three levels based
376   on their similarity to the query: Duplicate Scene Videos (DSVs), Complementary Scene Videos
377   (CSVs), and Incident Scene Videos (ISVs), as shown in Table 2: Consequently, the retrieval tasks are
      structured into three hierarchical levels:

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
             • Duplicate Scene Video Retrieval: only DSVs are positive instances.
379
380
             • Complementary Scene Video Retrieval: both DSVs and CSVs are positive instances.
381          • Incident Scene Video Retrieval: DSVs, CSVs, and ISVs are all positive instances.
382
383
      For the evaluation metric, we follow Kordopatis-Zilos et al. (2019) to utilize the mean Average
      Precision (mAP) to assess the quality of video ranking. (2) Video Copy Detection aims to detect
384
      edited copies of the query video. Instead of the ranking/retrieval task where all video pairs need to
385
      be sorted according to video embedding similarity, it is required to identify a set of video pairs that
386   contain edited versions of the given query. Following Pizzi et al. (2024), we consider the micro-AP
387   (µAP) as our evaluation metric that operates on all queries jointly and takes the confidence scores
388   into account.

      4.1   TARGETS AND DETAILS OF EVALUATION
393
394   Evaluation targets We evaluate twenty open-source vision foundation models. Including: (1)
395   twelve video foundation models, covering different pre-training paradigms, model scales, and
396   training data scales, to analyze the impact of these factors on the generalization capability of
397   foundation models. (2) five image foundation models to observe how much generalization capability
398   trained on image data can exhibit in video understanding. (3) three image-to-video methods based
399   on image foundation models to assess the effectiveness of current efficient transfer methods.
400
401   Implementation details All models take 8 frames (16 frames if the model has temporal downsam-
402
      pling), with each frame being 224x224 in size as input. For VidTAB, to ensure fair comparison and
      efficient assessment, we train all models for the same number of epochs and made minor adjustments
403
      to the hyperparameters to ensure convergence. For VidEB, all models take 16 frames, with each frame
404
      being 224x224 in size as input. In hierarchical video retrieval, the similarity of video-level embedding
405   determines the ranking of retrieval results. In video copy detection, each sample is segmented into 5
406   clips. The detection confidence score for the entire video is derived from the maximum frame-wise
407   similarity computed for each query-reference pair. See the Appendix for more details.

507
508   The main results of VidEB are presented in Table 6. We evaluate the embedding performance using
509   different pre-training paradigms for IFMs and VFMs as frozen feature extractors. Surprisingly, IFMs
510   performs better than most VFMs, likely due to the existing gap in spatial modeling capabilities
511   between VFMs and IFMs.
512   For the pre-training paradigms of the model, (1) The contrastive learning (CL) based approach
513   consistently excels in embedding evaluation. Due to CL’s emphasis on the relationships between
514   samples during training, DINOv2, which focuses solely on vision, outperforms vision-language
515   contrastive methods like CLIP across multiple tasks. (2) The effectiveness of masked video modeling
516   is closely tied to the targets it reconstructs or aligns with. With higher semantic richness, it shows
517   progressive improvements in embedding quality for VideoMAE-L, V-JEPA-L, and UMT-Lstage1 . (3)
518   Vision-centric pretraining outperforms Multi-modal pretraining in vision-centric scenarios. Com-
519
      paring UMT-Lstage1 and InternVideo2-1Bstage1 with their multi-modal counterparts UMT-Lstage2
      and InternVideo2-1Bstage2 , the introduction of visual-text pair data in multi-stage training does
520
      not enhance performance in vision-centric scenarios. This is also consistent with the performance
521
      differences observed between DINO and CLIP-style pre-training methods.
522
523   Additionally, we assess the impact of fine-tuning on the embedding evaluation of these pre-
524   trained models. (4) Labels bring new semantic information or disrupt existing finer-grained semantic
525
      information. The performance variations after fine-tuning differ based on the pre-training strategy.
      For UMT-Lstage1 and InternVideo2-1Bstage1 , fine-tuning leads to a significant drop in performance
526
      (-12.1 for UMT and -16.5 for InternVideo) due to the introduction of more singular label information,
527
      which causes catastrophic forgetting. In contrast, VideoMAE and VideoMAEv2 show substantial
528   performance gains (+14.5 and +25.8, respectively) because the low-level semantics learned during
529   pre-training are less abstract and benefit more from the addition of high-level label information.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   We present VideoEval, a comprehensive benchmark suite for efficiently evaluating the VFMs. To this
536   end, we establish VidTAB, which explores suitable evaluation tasks and protocols for VFMs from
537   the perspective of assessing their adaptability to unknown tasks with limited samples. Additionally,
538   we create VidEB to evaluate the capability of VFMs’ feature embedding in directly supporting
539   downstream tasks. Utilizing VideoEval, we conduct a large-scale study involving 20 popular open-
      source vision foundation models, providing valuable insights for future research directions.

944
      Limitations Firstly, due to the limitations of diversity and accuracy in our video sources and
945   annotations, which were gathered from public resources, we plan to further enrich the task in the
946   future by incorporating manual annotations and self-collected data. Secondly, considering the
947   evaluation cost and simplicity, we currently only evaluate tasks like classification and retrieval, which
948   primarily rely on VFMs’ global information extraction capabilities. We have not yet considered tasks
949   like spatio-temporal action detection and temporal grounding, which assess other aspects of VFMs’
950   capabilities. We will expand the scope of evaluation in the future.
951
952   Potential negative societal impacts While our evaluation includes tasks like synthetic video
953   recognition and harmful information recognition, these serve only as indicators of the model’s overall
954   performance in this area and cannot be used to accurately evaluate the actual performance of a specific
955
      task. If researchers or engineers in society attempt to use VFMs to perform these specific tasks,
      our benchmark can serve as a reference for their choice of VFMs, but it cannot be used as the final
956
      standard for evaluating that task. Otherwise, it may have negative impacts on the corresponding
957
      real-world applications.
