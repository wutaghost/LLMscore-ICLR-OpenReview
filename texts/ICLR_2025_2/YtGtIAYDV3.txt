                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               In many applications, inferring graph topology, i.e., learning the graph structure
012            from a given set of nodal observations, is a significant task. Existing approaches
013            are mostly limited to learning a single graph assuming that the observed data are
014            homogeneous. In many applications, data sets are heterogeneous and involve mul-
015            tiple related graphs, i.e., multiview graphs. Recent work on learning multiview
016            graphs ensures the similarity of learned view graphs through edge-based similar-
017            ity between the graphs. In this paper, we take a node-based approach instead of
018            assuming that similarities and differences between networks are driven by individ-
019            ual edges, providing a more intuitive interpretation of network differences. More-
020
               over, unlike existing methods that employ Gaussian Graphical Models (GGM),
               which learn precision matrices rather than the actual graph structures, we charac-
021
               terize the graph using a Laplacian matrix. Thus, the approach is expected to work
022
               broadly beyond Gaussian graphical learning. We develop an optimization frame-
023            work to learn the individual graphical structures, assuming that the differences are
024            due to individual nodes that are perturbed across views. The proposed optimiza-
025            tion framework is presented for the special case of two views. Furthermore, we
026            derive the upper bound on the estimation error of the proposed graph estimator
027            and characterize the impact of the sample size, number of nodes, and the spec-
028            trum of the graph Laplacians on estimation errors. The approach is evaluated on
029            synthetic graph data for robustness against noise, graph density, and sample size.
030            Finally, the proposed framework is applied to two-view real-world graph data for
031
               graph learning and clustering.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Many real-world data are represented through the relations between data samples, i.e., a graph struc-
036   ture (Newman (2018)). Although many datasets, including social networks and traffic networks,
037   come with a known graph structure, there are a lot of applications where a graph is not readily avail-
038   able. For example, in many biological systems, e.g., gene regulatory networks (Li & Gui, 2006), the
039   underlying graph structure is not directly observable. In such cases, inferring the topology of the
040   graph is essential to analyze the data and model the relations.
041
      Existing graph inference approaches are mostly limited to homogeneous datasets, where observed
042   graph signals are assumed to be identically distributed and defined on a single graph. In many
043   applications, the data may be heterogeneous or mixed and come from multiple related graphs, i.e.,
044   multiview graphs. In these situations, learning the topology of the views jointly by incorporating
045   the relationships between views can improve performance (Tsai et al., 2022; Danaher et al., 2014;
046   Navarro et al., 2022).
047
      Traditional joint graphical structure inference methods are primarily based on Gaussian Graphical
048   Models. These methods extend graphical lasso (Friedman et al. (2008)) to a joint learning setup,
049   where they learn the precision matrices of multiple related Gaussian graphical models. They em-
050   ploy various penalties in the likelihood framework to exploit the common characteristics shared by
051   different views (Guo et al. (2011); Danaher et al. (2014); Lee & Liu (2015); Mohan et al. (2014); Ma
052   & Michailidis (2016); Huang & Chen (2015)). One prominent example of this approach is the joint
053   graphical lasso (Danaher et al. (2014)), where fused or group lasso penalties are used to encour-
      age topological similarity between views. However, these methods are limited by the assumption

054
      that the observed graph signals are Gaussian, which is usually not true for real-world applications.
055   Furthermore, they learn the precision matrices without imposing graph structure constraints on the
056   learned views. These joint learning approaches have recently been extended to learn multiple graph
057   Laplacian matrices instead of precision matrices (Yuan et al. (2023a); Zhang & Wang (2024a);
058   Karaaslanli & Aviyente (2024)). However, all of these methods quantify the pairwise similarity
059   between the views based on edge similarity. In many settings, such as gene regulatory networks
060   (Mohan et al. (2014)), the differences between views may be better explained through the changes
061   in the connectivity of a few nodes. This way of modeling the differences imposes a structure and
062   provides an intuitive interpretation of the network differences.
063   In this paper, we introduce a joint graph Laplacian learning framework where the differences across
064   the views are assumed to be driven by the perturbation to the individual nodes’ connectivity across
065   views. Based on this assumption, we introduce a Laplacian learning framework using the smooth-
066   ness criterion, i.e., the graph signals are smooth or low frequency with respect to the underlying
067   graph structure, with a regularization term that captures the node-based similarity across views. We
068   focus on learning graphs for the case of two views where each view is assumed to be a perturbed
069
      version of the other by changing the connectivity of r nodes with r << n. The corresponding opti-
      mization problem is solved using the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Finally,
070
      theoretical results are provided to quantify the upper bounds on the error between the estimated and
071
      true graph Laplacians as a function of the number of signals and nodes.
072
073   The main contributions of the proposed framework are:
074
             • Extending structured multiview graph learning from GGM to smooth graph signals, where
075
               a valid graph topology instead of precision matrices is learned. Using smoothness, our
076
               framework is not restricted to GGMs and can handle different types of smooth graph sig-
077            nals.
078
079
             • Introducing structure-based multiview graph learning, particularly a node perturbation
               model, in the context of smooth graph learning.
080
081          • Providing theoretical analysis and upper bounds on the estimation error of two-view Lapla-
082            cian learning in terms of the graph size, sample size, and the radius around the true Lapla-
083            cians, as the problem is non-convex. This estimation bound also suggests that the estimated
084
               values will not converge to the true values merely by increasing the sample size; rather,
               convergence also depends on the topology of the true graph structure.

087   2     BACKGROUND
088
089   2.1   R ELATED W ORK
090
091   Prior work in multiview graph learning has been mostly based on statistical models. These meth-
092   ods extend graphical lasso (Friedman et al. (2008)) to the joint learning case, where the precision
093
      matrices of multiple related GGMs are learned using various penalties in the likelihood framework
      to exploit the common characteristics shared by different views (Guo et al. (2011); Danaher et al.
094
      (2014); Lee & Liu (2015); Mohan et al. (2014); Ma & Michailidis (2016); Huang & Chen (2015)).
095
      The most notable among these is the joint graphical lasso (Danaher et al. (2014)), where fused or
096   group lasso penalties are used to encourage topological similarity between views. However, these
097   methods are limited by the assumption that the observed graph signals are Gaussian, suffer from
098   increased computational complexity in the case of pairwise penalties and learn conditional depen-
099   dencies instead of inferring the graph structure, which may not be suitable for subsequent learning
100   tasks. Recently, these joint learning approaches have been extended to multiple graph Laplacian ma-
101   trices (Yuan et al. (2023a)). However, this approach is still limited to Gaussian data and edge-based
102   similarities across views.
103   The graph signal processing (GSP) community has recently addressed the problem of learning mul-
104   tiview graphs from heterogeneous data. This work can be divided into two categories, depending on
105   whether one knows the association of the observed signals with the views a priori. In the first setup,
106   multiple datasets are given and each dataset is defined in a view (Navarro et al. (2022); Navarro &
107   Segarra (2022)). On the other hand, the second setup deals with the mixture of graph signals, where
      a single data set is given and the association of graph signals to the views is not known (Maretic


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      & Frossard (2020); Araghi et al. (2019); Karaaslanli & Aviyente (2022)). The focus of this paper
109   is the first category. This problem setting has been studied most extensively to infer the topology
110   of time-varying networks (Kalofolias et al. (2017); Yamada et al. (2019); Baingana & Giannakis
111   (2016); Sardellitti et al. (2019)), where the aim is to learn graphs at multiple time points and to
112   track changes in the structure of the graph over time. This problem can be posed as multiview graph
113   learning with a regularization term that promotes pre-specified changes between consecutive graphs.
114   More recently, the problem of multiview graph learning has been formulated with the assumption
115   of graph stationarity (Navarro et al. (2022)). In this formulation, the signals are assumed to be sta-
116   tionary, and pairwise similarity between all graphs is used to regularize the optimization. In (Zhang
117
      & Wang (2024a)), the authors propose a multiview graph learning method based on the smoothness
      assumption. However, all of this prior work in GSP quantifies the similarity across views through
118
      edge-based similarity without considering the structure of the differences between views explicitly.

331   Given the two view graphs, each Xk ∈ Rn×dk is generated from Gk using the smooth graph filter
332   h(Li ) (Kalofolias, 2016). In particular, each column of Xk is generated as Xk·j = h(Lk )x0 ; where
333   x0 ∼ N (0, I). In this paper, we considered three different types of graph filters: 1) Gaussian filter
334   (h(L) = L† ); 2) Heat filter (h(L) = exp(−αL), α = 5 in this paper); and 3) Tikhonov filter
335
      (h(L) = (I + αL)−1 , α = 20 in this paper). In the case of the Gaussian filter, the resulting signals
      are Gaussian distributed and the graph Laplacian and the precision matrix are equivalent to each
336
      other. We finally add η% noise (in ℓ2 norm sense) to each generated Xi . For each simulation, the
337
      average performance over 10 realizations is reported.

348   6.1.2    B ENCHMARK M ODELS
349
350
      We compare the proposed method with respect to the following methods:
351
              • SV: Single view graph learning approach in (Dong et al., 2016) which learns the graph
352             topology corresponding to each view independently by assuming that the signals are
353             smooth with respect to each view’s graph.
354
              • MVGL: Multiview graph learning approach in (Karaaslanli & Aviyente, 2024) which
355
                jointly learns multiple graph Laplacians assuming the signals are smooth with respect to
356             each view graph and the similarity between the views is enforced by minimizing the ℓ1 -
357             norm error between the view and the learned consensus graph.
358
              • PNJGL: Perturbed node joint graph learning in (Mohan et al., 2014) that jointly learns
359
                precision matrices assuming a GGM where the similarity between the views is enforced by
360             minimizing the row column overlap norm of the difference.
361
362   The performance of the methods is quantified by computing the average F1 score with respect to the
363   ground truth graphs across runs.

      6.2.2    S ENSITIVITY TO H YPERPARAMETERS
462
463   In the proposed objective function 4.1, there are three parameters that are tuned, (γ1 , γ2 , γ3 ), where
464   γ1 controls the sparsity of the learned Laplacian matrices, γ2 regulates the degree distribution (or
465   connectivity) of the learned graphs, and γ3 controls the norm of the difference between the views,
466   i.e., the perturbation. In our implementation, we optimize the values of (γ1 , γ2 , γ3 ) using a grid
467   search approach.
468   To assess the sensitivity of the PN-TVL model to these parameters, γ1 , γ2 , and γ3 were varied from
469   5 to 50 in increments of 5, and the effect of adjusting two parameters at a time was analyzed while
470   keeping the third fixed. As shown in Figure 3, the performance of the learning algorithm is most
471   sensitive to the value of γ1 which controls the sparsity of the learned graphs as when γ1 is fixed, the
472   performance does not change much for γ2 and γ3 . This is expected since the sparsity of the learned
473   graphs affect the accuracy of estimating V in addition to each view. Moreover, the optimal range
474   for these parameters is found to be between 25 and 35.

      7   C ONCLUSIONS
511
512
      In this paper, we introduced a graph signal processing-based approach for learning graph Laplacians
      of two closely related graphs. The proposed approach is based on the assumptions that the observed
513
      graph signals are smooth with respect to the underlying graph structures, and that the differences
514
      between the two views are driven by the perturbation of a few nodes’ connectivity. A cost function
515   that enforces these two objectives is proposed and the corresponding optimization algorithm is pre-
516   sented. Theoretical results are presented on the upper bound of the estimation error for the inferred
517   Laplacian as well as the view difference matrices. The proposed method is applied to both simulated
518   and real graph-based data and compared to the state-of-the-art graph inference methods. The results
519   show that PN-TVL is more robust regarding the increase in the number of perturbed nodes and added
520   noise. Moreover, PN-TVL works well for various signal models, i.e., Gaussian and non-Gaussian,
521   while graphical lasso-based methods like PNJGL’s performance drops for non-Gaussian signals.
522   Future work will consider extending the proposed framework to multiple views. To keep computa-
523   tional complexity linear with respect to the number of views, we will develop consensus graph-based
524   similarity constraints. Future work will also consider extending the proposed work to other node-
525   based structural similarities, such as the co-hub model, where the views are assumed to have the
526   same hub nodes.

      Proof. C ONVEXITY OF THE S ET L
919
920   A set S is convex if for any two matrices L1 , L2 ∈ S and any scalar λ ∈ [0, 1], the convex com-
921   bination λL1 + (1 − λ)L2 is also in S. That is, for any L1 , L2 ∈ L, we need to check whether
922   λL1 + (1 − λ)L2 ∈ L.
923
      At first, to prove that the set of positive semi-definite matrices is convex that is if L1 ⪰ 0 and
924   L2 ⪰ 0, then for any λ ∈ [0, 1],
925                                          λL1 + (1 − λ)L2 ⪰ 0.
926
      Therefore, the positive semi-definiteness constraint is preserved under convex combinations. For the
927                                                                      (1)          (2)
      constraint Lij = Lji ≤ 0 ∀ i ̸= j is convex. This is because if Lij ≤ 0 and Lij ≤ 0 for all i ̸= j,
928
      then for any λ ∈ [0, 1],
929                                                (1)             (2)
930                                     Lij = λLij + (1 − λ)Lij ≤ 0.
931   Hence, this constraint is preserved under convex combinations. The constraint L · 1 = 0 (i.e., each
932   row sums to zero) is linear, and the set of matrices whose rows sum to zero is an affine subspace,
933   which is convex. That is, if L1 · 1 = 0 and L2 · 1 = 0, then for any λ ∈ [0, 1],
934                       (λL1 + (1 − λ)L2 ) · 1 = λ(L1 · 1) + (1 − λ)(L2 · 1) = 0.
935
936   Thus, the zero row sum constraint is also preserved under convex combinations. Since each of the
937

       In simpler terms, as the norm of x increases without bound, the value of f (x) also tends to infinity.

1104   that has the shortest distance to x′ . Formally, the projection solves the following minimization
1105   problem:
1106                                           x∗ = arg min f (x),
                                                              x∈S
1107
       where f (x) = ∥x − x′ ∥2 represents the squared Euclidean distance between x and x′ .

                                                              x∈S
1111   is well-defined when S is closed and f is coercive.

1114
1115
1116
                                                     x∗ = arg min f (x),
                                                              x∈S
1117
       where f : Rn → R is a coercive function and S is a closed set. Because f is coercive, for any
1118
       sequence {xn } where ∥xn ∥ → ∞, we have f (xn ) → ∞. This implies that the function grows
1119

1120   bounded region.

1122   Furthermore, since S is closed, any convergent sequence {xn } ⊆ S will have its limit point inside
1123
       S. This guarantees that there is at least one point within S that minimizes f . Hence, the minimizer
       x∗ exists in S, ensuring that the projection is well-defined.
