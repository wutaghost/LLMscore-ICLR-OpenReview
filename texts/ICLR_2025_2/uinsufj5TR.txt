009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent work shows that Sparse Autoencoders (SAE) applied to large language
012            model (LLM) layers have neurons corresponding to interpretable concepts. These
013            SAE neurons can be modified to align generated outputs, but only towards pre-
014            identified topics and with some parameter tuning. Our approach leverages the
015            observational and modification properties of SAEs to enable alignment for any
016            topic. This method 1) scores each SAE neuron by its semantic similarity to an
017            alignment text and uses them to 2) modify SAE-layer-level outputs by emphasiz-
018            ing topic-aligned neurons. We assess the alignment capabilities of this approach
019
               on diverse public topic datasets including Amazon reviews, Medicine, and Syco-
               phancy, across the currently available open-source LLMs and SAE pairs (GPT2
020
               and Gemma) with multiple SAEs configurations. Experiments aligning to medi-
021
               cal prompts reveal several benefits over fine-tuning, including increased average
022            language acceptability (0.25 vs. 0.5), reduced training time across multiple align-
023            ment topics (333.6s vs. 62s), and acceptable inference time for many applica-
024            tions (+0.00092s/token). Our anonymized open-source code is available in the
025            attached zip file.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   A typical application of general-purpose LLMs is producing topic-specific generated text, also
031   known as topic alignment. Existing approaches for topic alignment tend to use one of the fol-
032   lowing approaches: manipulating model input (e.g., few-shot learning, steering vectors, or prompt-
033   tuning (Liu et al., 2023)), the model as a whole (e.g., fine-tuning, retraining (Bereska & Gavves,
034   2024)), or model output (e.g., output validation (Jarvis, 2023), regeneration, or filtering). As the
035   costs (Weng, 2024; Kaplan et al., 2020) and interpretability challenges (Thirunavukarasu et al.,
036   2023) associated with these existing approaches continue to scale (Villalobos et al.), they become im-
037   practical for applications that have multiple or changing alignment topics (Wu et al., 2024), that need
038
      some human control over the generation process, or that face precise, layer-level attacks (Mishra
      et al., 2024).
039
040   Recently, Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have been used as observational tools for LLM computa-
041   tions, and come from a set of tools in Mechanistic Interpretability (MI). When attached to an LLM
042   layer, these SAEs decompose the layer output into SAE neurons corresponding to individual top-
043   ics (e.g., the Golden Gate Bridge (Templeton, 2024)). Using SAEs as an MI approach for topic
044
      alignment can be promising because they provide:
045   Computational Efficiency Once Trained Precise alignment approaches make fewer changes to the
046   model, so they are likely to be more efficient. As most LLM neurons encode multiple, unrelated
047   concepts, known as polysemanticity (Bereska & Gavves, 2024), directly manipulating them could
048   lead to vastly unexpected outputs, especially when multiple neurons are altered at once (Bills et al.,
049   2023; Bereska & Gavves, 2024). Modifications at the next tier up in the layer-level outpu may be
050
      feasible and more efficient than the existing heavy-model editing alignment approaches.
051   Increased Control Recent research shows that SAE neurons can separate LLM layer outputs, which
052   contain many concepts, into their corresponding human-interpretable concepts. Modifying these
053   layer-level SAE neurons can align the eventual LLM outputs with more control than opaque ap-
      proaches that may unexpectedly produce unaligned outputs.

067   Although recent works have hypothesized that SAEs can be used for general topic alignment. (Gao
068   et al., 2024; Templeton, 2024), and in doing so would be the first of the MI approaches used for
069   downstream tasks, there are methodological gaps. First, to the authors’ knowledge, there is no
070
      method to automatically identify alignment neurons relevant to a topic from the large potential set
      of SAE neurons (M1). Second, no current methods manipulate SAE neurons for topic alignment
071
      in a context-sensitive way (M2). Additionally, for SAE topic alignment to be a viable alternative
072
      to fine tuning, it must address current limitations. First, methods must be able to work with any
073   SAE and LLM (C1) because SAEs have vastly different representational power (as we show in
074   Fig. 3). Methods should also provide an uncertainty metric that quantifies the alignment modification
075   across different tokens (C2). Finally, methods should produce quality output without compute-
076   intensive parameter tuning (C3)1 . Accordingly, the main contributions of this paper enable SAE
077   topic alignment by:
078
079                1. Introducing the first methods using SAEs for topic alignment by identifying & modifying
080
                      SAE activations for any set of alignment topics without parameter tuning.
081                2. Quantifying uncertainty using a new metric that measures how aligned token output is.
082                3. Performance evaluation over multiple experiments across different SAEs configurations,
083                   three public topics datasets, including Amazon reviews, Medicine, and Sycophancy, and
084                   across the only open-source LLM-SAE pairs to the author’s knowledge, GPT2 and Gemma.
085                   We observe promising results for topic alignment using correctness metrics, like increased
086                   language acceptability, and efficiency metrics, like reduced training time.

088   2        R ELATED W ORKS
089
090   While many related works surface alignment topics within generative models (including recent
091   works like Chen et al.; Stoica et al. (2023)), few come from an interpretability subfield called mech-
092   anistic interpretability (MI). These MI methods focus on reasoning for neuron-level calculations and
093   include:
094
095   Logit-lenses and layer-level observational mechanisms: Directly applying these observational
096   MI approaches for topic alignment is difficult. Many samples could be needed before identifying
097   possible alignment pathways, which would be impractical for general topic alignment tasks. SAEs
098   have an advantage here because they uniquely act as both an observation and modification mecha-
099   nism (Bereska & Gavves, 2024).
100
101   Probing and modified vectors: These layer-level approaches modify outputs towards a specific
102
      concept (e.g., ‘weddings’) (Zou et al., 2023; Han et al.; Turner et al., 2023). Approach limitations
      include the possible concepts represented, the uncertainty associated with the outputs, and the cost
103
      of training/verifying these probes across many concepts (Bereska & Gavves, 2024; Belinkov, 2021).
104
      Recently, Conmy & Nanda (2024) explored using SAEs to filter unaligned concepts from the steer-
105   ing vectors (by muting unrelated SAE neurons). This filtering approach could address some of the
106

      trust limitations for steering vectors, but generalizability and uncertainty limitations remain. Never-
109   theless, Conmy & Nanda (2024) uncover a valuable insight – SAE modification using set values is
110   very similar to steering vectors (Turner et al., 2023) because they both produce additive vectors for
111   layer-level output.
112
      SAEs are well-suited to address the limitations of other MI approaches and thus enable precise
113
      topic alignment. First, using SAEs with any model and any model layer is practical. There is
114
      already considerable investment in using SAEs across LLMs to observe the computational ‘thought
115   process’ during token generation (Huang et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024), and it would be efficient
116   to reuse these SAEs for alignment as well. Second, SAEs learn different topics/concepts jointly
117   (e.g., concrete nouns, syntax, more abstract concepts) instead of one at a time like modified vectors.
118   Third, because the SAEs can be used at calculation within a layer, placing it at the multi-layer
119   perceptron matches the intuition that alignment should occur (McDougall, 2023) where the model
120   refines its output process for next token generation (Bloom, 2024). Fourth, the success of this
121   method depends on the SAE representativeness, not the underlying LLM2 . Given these benefits, this
122   research aims to enable SAE for topic alignment by addressing the aforementioned methodological
123
      gaps and constraints.

147   SAE Mechanics As shown in Fig. 2a, SAEs process tokens. These tokens come from prompts
148   p ∈ Pset in a set of prompts. As tokens from a prompt, pt , pass through the model, they have a
149   dense latent representation, x(pt ). At layer l, the SAE takes the dense representation input x(pt )
150   and encodes it into a higher dimension using encoding matrix E with dimensions [dlatent , dhidden ].
151   This encoding process disentangles the dense polysemantic representation with multiple concepts
152
      into one where each SAE neuron i in the SAE’s hidden layer, hi , should correspond to a single con-
      cept (Bereska & Gavves, 2024) 3 with a preactivation value: γ(pt )i = (x(pt ) · E)i . Then, an activa-
153
      tion function, σ, only selects some of these neurons to contribute to the final output: σ(γ(pt )) (e.g.
154
      σ=Top-32k, where only the top 32 values from γ(pt ) are nonzero). The non-zero post-activation
155   neurons are decoded using D with dimensions [dhidden , dlatent ] and the resulting dense token
156   output, x′ (pt ) = σ(γ) · D is processed through the model.

167   ple neurons that encode the same concepts with slight contextual nuances (e.g., Anthropic’s neuron
168   cluster for sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2023)) or neurons that need to co-activate for a desired out-
169   put. Second, SAEs may have neurons that are still polysemantic and introduce some contamination

171
      site concepts (e.g., truth and lie are separate neurons), so modifying SAE neurons can upregulate
      the presence of a topic, but clamping the neuron to 0 does not necessarily negate that topic. Thus,
172
      an automated method that scores SAE neurons by their semantic relevance could penalize both con-
173
      textually irrelevant and polysemantic neurons without requiring manual SAE neuron identification
174   for each alignment topic and token context. These scores can then be used to align tokens in a
175   contextually-sensitive way so that alignment occurs on tokens that are easy to align vs. those that
176   are not (e.g., syntax tokens).

194
195   With the premise that SAE neurons activate highly on only concepts related to Palign are alignment
196   candidates. We could observe the SAE neurons that activate on tokens from Palign is a straightfor-
197
      ward approach to identifying alignment neurons; we call this the Strawman Approach. However,
      given how few tokens Palign typically contains compared to the thousands of SAE neurons, there is
198
      no way to determine how polysemantic an activated SAE neuron is or identify similar neurons that
199
      would also be useful for alignment but did not activate on tokens.
200
201   Our proposed approach addresses these challenges by using a large reference set, Pref with many
202   concepts or topics. By independently processing prompts in Pref , each SAE neuron 1) likely has
203
      at least some tokens it activates, and prompts in Pref that are similar to Palign can be used identify
      2) other relevant alignment neurons. This baseline can then be used to identify and penalize SAE
204
      neurons activated with tokens as follows:
205
206          • Aligned neurons have high activations only when prompts are close to Palign and should
207            be used for alignment.
208
             • Polysemantic neurons have prompts close and far activate high.
209
210
             • Unaligned neurons have prompts that are far and activate high and should be avoided.
211
      All SAE neurons fall in one of these three categories, and polysemantic should be considered pro-
212
      portional where they fall between aligned and unaligned. Thus, for a score to quantify if an SAE
213   neuron only activates highly on concepts related to Palign , we want to penalize neurons that fire

243   3.2      M ETHOD 2: M ODIFYING SAE S WITH A LIGNMENT S CORES
244
245   The alignment scores calculated in Sec.3.1 for each SAE neuron can now be used to modify incom-
246   ing tokens to different alignment topics in a contextually sensitive way.
247   Clamping Approach (Baseline) Based on the insights from Conmy & Nanda (2024), forcing a
248   specific feature to be clamped high, as. Templeton (2024) have previously done, is akin to creating
249   a steering vector. We use this idea as a baseline, where the 5 SAE neurons with the highest scores
250   are clamped to 10x their value (inspired by Templeton (2024)).
251   However, determining this clamping value is nontrivial and if it is too high or low, it produces
252   garbled output. Instead of parameter tuning per application, our approach modifies γ(pt ) so that the
253   SAE neurons selected post-activation are more aligned and still match the token’s context.
254
      Swap Approach (Proposed) As shown in Fig. 2b, the SAE decomposes the layer-level token out-
255
      put into preactivation values. Weighting these values by alignment scores can change the SAE
256
      neurons selected after the activation function. However, large modifications to preactivation values
257   can lead to garbled output. Instead, our approach uses the indices of the modified SAE neurons
258   post-activation with the original preactivation values, as shown below, before the decoding step.
259
260                                      σ ′ (γ(pt )) = I[σ(γ(pt )i ) ̸= 0)] · γ(pt )                      (3)

262   In contrast to vector-based steering and clamping, the additive vector in this proposed approach
263   changes based on the token context. We can quantify how related the generated output is to Palign
264   by multiplying final activations by ‘unalignment scores’, (1-scores), which we call contamination
265   = γ(pt ) ∗ (1 − score(hi )). Through these methods, we address existing gaps in identifying SAE

516   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
517
518   This work enables topic alignment using SAEs by proposing new methods to address gaps. These
519
      methods involve calculating alignment scores for each SAE neuron and modifying the SAE outputs
      in a contextually-sensitive way with no parameters. With a competitive correctness performance and
520
      computationally efficient inference-time modification that takes less than 0.001s/token on average,
521
      the proposed approach is promising due to the interpretability properties of the SAE, quanifiable
522   uncertainty, and lack of parameters. By unlocking topic alignment using SAEs, this work enables
523   using SAE alignment as a tool to study other interpretability questions and use in applications where
524   alignment topics change often. These results inspire research directions closely tied to exciting
525   interpretability challenges like:
526
527          • Designing Pref : To reduce one-time compute costs, is there a better way to design Pref
528
               using contrastive approaches? How can we design it so that potential Palign topics will be
               very close to some Pref prompts and very far away from others?
529
530          • Engineering SAE modification: We show SAE representational power varies by config-
531
               uration. Which layers should use SAE steering for alignment and what does that tell us
               about LLM self-repair (McGrath et al., 2023).
532
533          • HAI Perspective: This approach uses a large volume of data modifications, and users may
534
               benefit from an approach like Cho et al. (2024) to see how our methods change the outputs.
535   Further, research into improving the representational power of the underlying SAEs, testing on rare
536   alignment topics, and studying patterns of coactivation can further the potential of using SAEs for
537   topic alignment as an alternative to fine tuning. Finally, it is worth noting that this approach can
538   also address other limitations with fine-tuning (e.g., where privacy is essential, as Palign tokens are
539   never directly used for scoring, or when there are too few examples for successful fine tuning) and
      warrants exploration on these dimensions as well.

710   A.1   A DDITIONAL J USTIFICATION
711
712   As we focus on text generation, we do not compare with approaches related to other fine-tuning and
713
      alignment goals, such as high performance on a certain type of task (e.g. classification) or correcting
      how knowledgable, ‘truthful’, or factual models are, which have a rich body of supporting literature
714
      (Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Hadi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Additionally, while there
715
      is continued exploration in identifying circuits of LLM neurons, that line of work is nascent and not
716   ready for alignment applications without additional circuit discovery Tigges et al. (2024).
717
718   Specifically for alignment, we believe that existing approaches for alignment are limited, as shown
719
      in Fig. 7, and that the issues of polysemantic neurons and SAE representativeness are here to stay.
      First, there are far more concepts than the tens of thousands of hidden layer neurons in small SAEs,
720
      of which some ‘dead neurons’ do not activate on any large corpi of tokens Gao et al. (2024). Thus,
721
      many SAE neurons likely encode multiple concepts, especially in very rare cases. Increasing the
722   width of SAEs is a popular research direction, but there are computational limits on training using
723   current approaches. Additionally, if the SAE is too wide, it may learn concepts that are not present
724   in the LLM, making resulting alignment mechanisms difficult.
