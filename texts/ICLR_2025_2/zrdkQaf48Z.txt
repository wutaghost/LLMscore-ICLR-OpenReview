011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have led to their in-
014            creasing integration into human life. Understanding their inherent characteristics,
015            such as personalities, temperaments, and emotions, is essential for responsible
016            AI development. However, current psychometric evaluations of LLMs, often de-
017            rived from human psychological assessments, encounter significant limitations in
018            terms of reliability and validity. Test results reveal that models frequently refuse to
019
               provide anthropomorphic responses and exhibit inconsistent scores across various
               scenarios. Moreover, human-derived theories may not accurately predict model
020
               behavior in practical real-world applications. To address these limitations, we
021
               propose Core Sentiment Inventory (CSI), a novel evaluation instrument inspired
022            by the Implicit Association Test (IAT). CSI is built from the ground up with a sig-
023            nificantly broader range of stimuli words than traditional assessments. CSI covers
024            both English and Chinese to implicitly evaluate models’ sentiment tendencies,
025            which allows for a much more comprehensive assessment. Through extensive
026            experiments, we demonstrate that CSI effectively quantifies models’ sentiments,
027            revealing nuanced emotional patterns that vary significantly across languages and
028            contexts. CSI significantly improves reliability, yielding more consistent results
029            and a reduced reluctance rate, and enhances predictive power by effectively cap-
030
               turing models’ emotional tendencies. These findings validate CSI as a robust and
               insightful tool for evaluating the psychological traits of LLMs, offering a more
031
               reliable alternative to traditional methods.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated their remarkable capa-
038   bilities, extending their applications beyond conventional software tools to more human-like assis-
039   tants (Brown et al., 2020; Bubeck et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; 2024). These models are increasingly
040   integrated into various domains such as clinical medicine (Gilson et al., 2023), mental health (Stade
041   et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Lawrence et al., 2024; Obradovich et al., 2024), education (Dai et al.,
042   2023) and search engine (Bing Blogs, 2024), addressing diverse user requests. This evolution has led
043   to growing interest not only in task-specific performance but also in exploring the manifestation of
044   personalities, temperaments, and emotions when these models act as human-like assistants. Conse-
045
      quently, researchers are delving into psychometric analysis to better understand these aspects (Wang
      et al., 2023). Psychometric analysis provides a systematic approach to evaluate models’ behavior,
046
      offering both quantitative and qualitative insights into their behavioral tendencies. Such analysis
047
      is instrumental in constructing psychological profiles of LLMs, providing a foundation for under-
048   standing whether these models exhibit desired emotional and behavioral characteristics. Through
049   this approach, researchers uncover biases (Bai et al., 2024a; Naous et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024;
050   Taubenfeld et al., 2024), behavioral patterns (Coda-Forno et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023), and ethical
051   concerns (Biedma et al., 2024), helping identify harmful behaviors or unintended outcomes that may
052   emerge during deployment. This is critical for ensuring that AI systems are developed responsibly
053   and aligned with ethical standards, promoting their seamless integration into society (Yao et al.,
      2023; Wang et al., 2023).

      Current psychometric approaches to evaluating LLMs typically involve administering existing hu-
068
      man psychological scales, prompting the model to select answers, and ultimately deriving a self-
069
      reported score (Jiang et al., 2023; Safdari et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). However, these methods
070   face significant limitations in terms of reliability and validity. Reliability issues manifest in two
071   primary ways: (a) Model Reluctance. As illustrated in Figure 1a, model providers often implement
072   policies to prevent the anthropomorphization of their models. While these policies are important for
073   ethical reasons, our experiments have observed that models frequently refuse to answer questions,
074   responding with statements like: “As an AI language model developed by OpenAI, I do not pos-
075   sess consciousness or feelings.” (b) Poor Consistency. Figure 1b demonstrates the inconsistency in
076   results obtained through this method. Our experiments with the BFI revealed that slight changes,
077   altering the number of questions asked in each iteration in prompt settings, led to significantly dif-
078
      ferent outcomes. These deficiencies substantially undermine the reliability of existing methods.
      Beyond reliability concerns, current methods also face validity issues. The psychometric question-
079
      naires employed are fundamentally based on human research, and the underlying theories may not
080
      be applicable to deep learning models (Wang et al., 2023). Consequently, existing methods lack
081   predictive and explanatory power when assessing LLMs. The scores derived from these methods of-
082   ten fail to predict how models will perform in real-world scenarios, severely limiting their practical
083   applications.
084
      To address these limitations, we propose a novel evaluation instrument called Core Sentiment Inven-
085
      tory (CSI), inspired by the Implicit Association Test (IAT) (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995; Greenwald
086
      et al., 2003), a widely used tool in social psychology for examining automatic associations between
087   concepts and evaluative attributes.1 CSI aims to evaluate the sentiment tendencies of LLMs in an im-
088   plicit, bottom-up manner. Our approach involves using a curated set of the most representative and
089   common 5,000 neutral words in both English and Chinese as stimuli to assess the model’s positive
090   or negative tendencies toward each item. This far surpass the size of traditional psychological scales,
091   which typically use fewer than 100 items. These words are selected to avoid strong emotional con-
092   notations, ensuring that any sentiment detected stems from the model’s internal associations rather
093   than inherent word sentiment.2
094   Our bilingual approach provides a quantified CSI score across three dimensions and also serves
095   as a tool for qualitatively analyzing the model’s emotional tendencies, enabling us to explore per-
096   sonality differences in models across different scenarios. Through rigorous experimental testing
097   of mainstream LLMs using CSI, we have successfully uncovered their emotional tendencies. Our
098   experiments demonstrate that, while most models tend to exhibit positive emotions, there is a signif-
099   icant presence of negative emotions, covering a wide range of common usage scenarios. Moreover,
100   models display noticeable emotional differences between English and Chinese contexts. Compared

      bility, with significantly enhanced consistency in results and a reduced reluctancy rate—showing up

103

129   assessment to cover a wider range of indicators beyond just the BFI. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024)

      3      M ETHODOLOGY
188
189
      3.1       P RELIMINARIES
190
191   Our method is founded on the Implicit Association Test (IAT) (Greenwald & Banaji, 1995; Green-
192   wald et al., 2003), which measures the strength of automatic associations between mental represen-
193   tations of concepts. Traditionally, the IAT assesses how participants categorize stimuli by assigning
194   them to dual-meaning categories, revealing implicit biases or associations between specific concepts
195   (e.g., race) and positive or negative attributes. In our work, we adapt the IAT to evaluate the models’
196   implicit sentiment tendencies. We posit that if a model is more inclined to associate a given stimulus
197   word with positive words, it indicates a positive sentiment toward that stimulus, which may manifest
198
      when the model addresses topics related to that word. Conversely, if the model tends to associate
      the stimulus word with negative words, it suggests a negative sentiment, potentially influencing its
199
      responses involving that stimulus.
200
201
202   3.2       OVERVIEW OF THE M ETHOD
203
      As shown in Figure 2, we design a testing template based on the IAT. In each iteration, we sample
204
      a set of words from curated CSI to serve as stimuli, prompting the model to express its sentiment
205   inclination toward each word. Based on the model’s responses, we calculate the proportion of words
206   associated with positive, negative, and neutral sentiments to compute a comprehensive CSI Score.
207   CSI score quantifies the overall sentiment tendencies of the model across three dimensions: opti-
208   mism, pessimism, and neutrality. In addition to these quantitative metrics, our approach also sup-
209   ports qualitative analysis. By examining specific instances in which the model displays particular
210   sentiment tendencies, we gain deeper insights into how the model behaves in various scenarios, re-
211   vealing more nuanced emotional patterns. The following sections provide a detailed explanation of
212   CSI construction process and the testing methodology.

      Principle 1: Avoiding Words with Strong Emotional Connotations To ensure that any detected
239
      sentiment arises from the model’s internal associations rather than the inherent sentiment of the
240   words, we deliberately selected words that do not carry strong emotional connotations. According
241   to Baccianella et al. (2010), the expression of opinions and sentiment tendencies is predominantly
242   conveyed by modifiers (such as adjectives and adverbs), whereas heads (nouns and verbs) tend to be
243   more neutral. Thus, we chose nouns and verbs as the stimuli units for constructing CSI. These non-
244   modifier words enable us to reveal implicit biases and sentiment tendencies without being influenced
245   by explicit emotional content.
246
247   Principle 2: Ensuring Representativeness Of CSI Ideally, we would test the model’s senti-
248   ment bias towards every possible head word. However, this approach is computationally infeasible.
249   Therefore, we opted to focus on the most common words and we utilized real-world corpora that
250   are used for training large models, as well as datasets reflecting authentic interactions between users
251   and models. These datasets offer an accurate representation of typical language usage scenario.
252   We applied open-source part-of-speech (POS) tagging tools to these corpora and calculated word
253   frequencies for nouns and verbs. Based on this objective, data-driven method, we expand the word
254   set to 5,000 items. As shown in Table 2, we significantly increased linguistic coverage compared
255   to traditional psychometric scales, which typically contain fewer than 100 items (see Table 1). This
256   extensive coverage captures a more comprehensive representation of language, and better reflecting
257   real-world usage scenarios and providing deeper understanding of model behavior. Moreover, this
258   objective process minimized cultural and contextual biases from subjective word selection. It is
259   important to note that separate analyses were performed for both Chinese and English datasets, so
260
      the CSI for each language may differ due to linguistic nuances.

347
      Firstly, the scoring patterns reveal that most models exhibit a dominant optimism, bold score in
348   figure 3, likely resulting from value alignment processes during training. The only exception is

350   cant negative biases in many real-world contexts. The P score (Pessimism) range from 0.1288 to

352   nario, which constitutes a substantial proportion. This may hinder the development of responsible
353   AI systems that are expected to treat every scenario fairly.
354   Secondly, we observe differences in emotional expressions across languages. Notably, GPT-4o
355   shows minimal differences between English and Chinese. In contrast, Llama3.1-70B exhibits a
356   substantial bias, with pessimism being dominant in Chinese (P score of 0.4794) compared to
357   English (P score of 0.3056). This suggests that the model’s performance varies across different
358   language scenarios, a phenomenon that warrants further exploration. These differences may stem
359   from the pre-training corpora or may result from overemphasis on a particular language during the
360   value alignment process in the post-training stages.

371
372   Reliability is a fundamental aspect of psychometric evaluations, reflecting the consistency and stabil-
373   ity of a measurement instrument (Cronbach, 1951). We compared the reliability of our CSI method
374   with the traditional BFI method using two quantitative metrics: consistency rate and reluctancy rate.
375   The consistency rate measures the proportion of items where the model’s responses remained con-
376   sistent across repeated trials. A higher consistency rate indicates greater reliability. The reluctancy
377   rate quantifies the frequency of neutral or non-committal responses, such as “unrelated” or “neutral”
      in CSI and “neither agree nor disagree” in BFI. Higher reluctance indicates lower reliability.

514
      Our results address three key research questions and demonstrate the effectiveness of CSI method:
515
      (1) Quantification and Analysis of Sentiment Bias: CSI Score effectively quantifies and differenti-
516   ates sentiment biases in language models. Our method reveals varying emotional preferences when
517   models switch between languages. It serves as both a quantitative measure and a qualitative tool for
518   identifying emotional biases in specific scenarios, contributing to the development of responsible AI
519   systems. (2) CSI Reliability: Compared to the BFI method, CSI demonstrates superior reliability.
520   Models evaluated with CSI exhibit higher consistency and lower reluctance in their responses, indi-
521   cating a more stable and dependable measure of sentiment tendencies. (3) CSI Predictive Validity:
522   CSI accurately predicts sentiment in practical tasks such as story generation. The sentiment scores
523   of generated stories through CSI align well with the proportion of positive and negative words in
524
      the input, validating its effectiveness in assessing emotional biases of language models. In conclu-
      sion, CSI provides valuable quantitative and qualitative insights into language models’ sentimental
525
      tendencies, informing the future development of more responsible AI systems.

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This work introduces Core Sentiment Inventory (CSI), a novel implicit evaluation method that sur-
531   passes traditional psychometric assessments in analyzing the emotional tendencies of Large Lan-
532   guage Models. CSI effectively quantifies models’ sentiment across optimism, pessimism, and neu-
533
      trality, revealing nuanced emotional patterns that vary significantly across languages and contexts.
      Our experiments show that CSI improves reliability by up to 45% and reduces reluctance rates to
534
      near-zero compared to conventional methods. Moreover, it demonstrates a high predictive power in
535
      sentiment-driven tasks, with a correlation exceeding 0.85 between CSI scores and real-world text
536   generation outputs. These findings highlight CSI’s robustness and precision, establishing it as a su-
537   perior tool for understanding and optimizing the emotional alignment of LLMs, thereby promoting
538   more reliable and human-compatible AI systems.
