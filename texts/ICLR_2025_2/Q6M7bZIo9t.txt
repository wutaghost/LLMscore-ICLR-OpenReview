009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has gained significant popularity in mod-
012            ern Large Language Models (LLMs) due to its effectiveness in introducing new
013            knowledge and reducing hallucinations. However, the deep understanding of RAG
014            remains limited, how does RAG help the reasoning process and can RAG help
015            improve the reasoning capability remains question. While external documents are
016            typically considered as a method to incorporate domain-specific information, they
017            also contain intermediate reasoning results related to the query, this suggests that
018
               documents could enhance the reasoning capability of LLMs, which has not been
               previously explored. In this paper, we investigate this issue in depth and find that
019
               while RAG can assist with reasoning, the help is limited. If we conceptualize the
020
               reasoning process as a tree with fixed depth, then RAG struggles to assist LLMs in
021            performing deeper reasoning. Additionally, the information in the documents re-
022            quires preprocessing to filter out noise. We demonstrate that this preprocessing is
023            difficult to achieve simply fine-tuning of the LLM, it often necessitates numerous
024            additional transformer layers to solve the problem. To simplify the problem, we
025            propose DPrompt tuning, which effectively resolves the issue within just limited
026            transformer layers, leading to improved performance.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown, 2020) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across
032   a variety of tasks, including text generation and question answering (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
033
      2022), code generation (Gu, 2023), and information retrieval (Dai et al., 2024). However, current
      LLMs often suffer from serious hallucinations (Huang et al., 2023) due to a lack of factual infor-
034
      mation. Moreover, the knowledge embedded within LLMs is encoded in their parameters (Yang
035
      et al., 2024), meaning that incorporating new knowledge requires further fine-tuning, which is both
036   time-consuming and resource-intensive. Consequently, augmenting LLMs with external retrievers
037   has led to significant performance improvements (Lewis et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024; Izacard et al.,
038   2023).
039
      Despite the widespread adoption of RAG in modern LLMs, a comprehensive understanding of how
040
      RAG aids inference remains an open question. Most researchers currently view RAG primarily as a
041
      method to provide domain-specific knowledge (Zhang et al., 2024a), often seeking to adapt LLMs to
042   particular domains through RAG (Soudani et al., 2024). However, the impact of RAG on enhancing
043   reasoning capacity has yet to be thoroughly investigated.
044
045
      First, we aim to understand how RAG operates and whether it can improve the reasoning capabil-
      ities of LLMs. We can consider the LLM as computing p(y|q), where q represents the query and
046
      y is the corresponding answer. In this context, retrieval-augmented generation can be expressed as
047
      p(y|q, d1 , d2 , . . . , dk ), where di is the i-th document retrieved based on the query q. Additionally,
048   the well-known prompting technique, Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), which signifi-
049   cantly enhances the reasoning ability of LLMs, can be represented as p(y|q, x1 , x2 , . . . , xk ), where
050   xi denotes the step-by-step reasoning results. Both CoT and RAG aim to incorporate additional
051   information into the input to achieve better performance. It has been theoretically demonstrated and
052   experimentally verified that CoT effectively improves the reasoning ability of LLMs (Feng et al.,
053   2024; Wei et al., 2022; Chu et al., 2023). Thus, the question arises: Can RAG also enhance the
      reasoning capabilities of LLMs?

054
      Fixed-depth transformer models with log precision can be simulated by constant-depth circuits (Mer-
055   rill & Sabharwal, 2023), indicating that LLMs are limited to fixed-depth reasoning. When concep-
056   tualizing the reasoning path as a tree, the maximum depth remains constant (Merrill & Sabharwal,
057   2023). Chain of Thought (CoT) generates step-by-step reasoning or explanations rather than pro-
058   viding direct answers, formalized as x1 = f (x), x2 = f (x, x1 ), . . . , y = f (x, x1 , . . . , xk ). This
059   process allows CoT to effectively expand reasoning depth by execute f multiple times, potentially
060   reaching infinite depth with sufficient CoT steps (Feng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). In contrast,
061   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) does not facilitate multiple inferences; instead, it retrieves
062   existing relevant information to generate answers (Shi et al., 2023b). While RAG cannot stack LLM
063
      layers to enhance reasoning capability, the retrieved documents may include intermediate reasoning
      results, thereby reducing the number of layers needed for inference and enabling the LLM to tackle
064
      more complex problems, thus aiding its reasoning ability.
065
066   In this paper, we first demonstrate that RAG can enhance the reasoning ability of LLMs, enabling
067   them to tackle more complex problems. If the model can initially solve problems with a reasoning
068   depth of l, then with the addition of relevant document information, it could address problems with a
069
      reasoning depth of l +c, where c is a constant determined by the quality of the retrieved information.
      This shows that RAG can help to improve the reasoning capability of LLM.
070
071   However, in real-world RAG scenarios, the information retrieved from documents is not always
072   directly usable and often requires further processing because documents may contain noise infor-
073   mation (Jiang et al., 2023b;a), and some documents may even be completely distracting, containing
074   incorrect answers (Shi et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024). Such noise and distracting documents can
075
      negatively impact performance. While some works attempt to fine-tune the model to filter out noise
      (Xu et al., 2024) and distracting documents (Yan et al., 2024), these filtering processes may require
076
      additional reasoning depth and could ultimately harm overall performance. Also some works train
077
      another filter model (Yan et al., 2024; Asai et al., 2023), but this approach results in additional
078   reasoning cost and makes it difficult to eliminate the inherent noise in the documents.
079
080   Therefore, a critical question arises: How difficult is the filtering problem, and can we effectively
081
      solve it within a limited number of layers? If the efforts to filter out noise is even more than the
      help RAG brings, RAG fail to improve the reasoning capability. First, we show that fine-tuning
082
      methods like LoRA also struggle to filter out noise without compromising reasoning capability,
083
      since filtering irrelevant tokens while maintaining the original attention patterns for relevant tokens
084   proves challenging. This indicates that the filtering process cannot be effectively incorporated into
085   the original reasoning steps of LLMs, necessitating additional reasoning depth for filtering, which
086   can ultimately degrade the reasoning capability of the model.
087
      Then we show that judging the relevance of a token can hardly be implemented by limited num-
088
      ber of transformer layers. Considering the query ‘Alice is exhausted but Bob is still excited; how
089
      does Bob feel?’ Here, ‘exhausted’ is noise and should be excluded from the inference. However,
090   assessing relevance necessitates considering the token ‘Bob’ in the query alongside ‘Alice’ the sub-
091   ject of ‘exhausted’. Thus, evaluating relevance requires the information from three or more tokens.
092   Yet, the attention mechanism typically computes only pair-wise relationships, making it challeng-
093   ing to resolve this issue within a limited number of transformer layers (Sanford et al., 2024). With
094   the number of transformer layers required for preprocessing increases, utilizing the document in-
095   formation for inference would help less, and even hurt the reasoning capability. To address this,
096   we propose DPrompt tuning which transforms the triple-wise problem into a pair-wise one, and the
097   pair-wise problem can be effectively tackled by limited layer of transformers, ultimately enhancing
098
      the reasoning ability of Retrieval-Augmented Language Models (RALM).
099   The main contributions of this paper are: (1) We demonstrate that RAG can enhance the reasoning
100   ability of LLMs; however, this improvement is limited, allowing Retrieval-Augmented Language
101   Models (RALMs) to solve problems that require slightly more reasoning depth than before; (2) We
102   assert that fine-tuning the model to filter out noise while preserving its original functionality is chal-
103   lenging. This indicates that noise would lead to worse performance even after finetuning; (3) We
104
      further reveal that the inherent triple-wise nature of the filtering process may necessitate numerous
      transformer layers for effective document preprocessing, which could significantly degrade reason-
105
      ing ability. To address this, we propose a method to transform the problem into a pair-wise format,
106
      making it easier to solve.

117   In this section, we explore the extent to which RAG can enhance the reasoning ability of LLMs, aim-
118   ing to deepen our understanding of its role in reasoning. As demonstrated by Merrill & Sabharwal
119   (2023), transformer-based models are constrained to a fixed depth of reasoning due to the limited
120   number of layers. Chain of Thought (CoT) facilitates step-by-step reasoning, effectively stacking
121   multiple LLMs to address a single problem. Thus, the question is: How can RAG assist during the
122   reasoning process, and how much additional reasoning capacity can RAG provide to LLMs?
123
124   2.1   T HE FISSION OF RAG
125
      If we consider the reasoning steps of an LLM as a tree, the maximum reasoning depth is fixed at
126
      L due to the constraints of the depth of the model. Additionally, the maximum width of this tree
127
      is also limited, as the number of attention heads per layer is finite. For a reasoning tree T with L
128   layers, let the number of nodes in layer i be denoted as ni , and refer to the j-th node in the i-th layer
129   as ui,j . The retrieved document d contains relevant information that can be leveraged to replace
130   certain reasoning nodes with extracted document content. For instance, consider the query Who is
131   the actor playing Jason on General Hospital?’ (Yoran et al., 2023). In this scenario, there may be a
132   node ui,j representing the information about What is General Hospital?’. If we provide a document
133   containing detailed information about General Hospital, the computation for ui,j can effectively be
134   replaced by extracting relevant information from that document.
135   Definition 2.1 (Reasoning Tree). For a reasoning tree TL of L layers, and there are nl nodes in
136   each layer l, nL−1 = 1, all nodes in layer l are connected to at least to one node in layer l + 1. with
137   probability 1 − ql , node ul,i are connected to ul+1,j , and the isolated nodes are randomly connected
138   to a node in case of useless nodes.
139   In the reasoning tree, each node represents a calculation, with the output being some intermediate
140   reasoning results. The connection between nodes ui,j and ui−1,k indicates that the calculation of
141   ui,j relies on the output of ui−1,k . The parameter ql reflects the sparsity of these connections. Con-
142   sequently, for a node ui,j , a document may contain relevant information, allowing the calculation
143   for ui,j to be effectively replaced by information extracted from the document.
144   Definition 2.2 (Retrieval). For a reasoning tree TL , with probability pl , the nodes in layer l can be
145   replaced by retrieved documents.

147
      It’s important to recognize that processing documents also necessitates a certain depth of reasoning.
      However, extracting information from a document is fundamentally an information extraction pro-
148
      cess, whereas directly addressing a query is a purely inferential process. Therefore, we can assume
149
      that the cost of information extraction is lower than that of inference. This enables LLMs to leverage
150   external information, thereby simplifying complex reasoning tasks.
151
      Assumption 2.3. If the document d contains information about node u at the l-th layer, then only
152   λ · l layers are needed to extract the information from d, λ < 1.
153
154   This assumption suggests that while processing documents requires some reasoning depth, it is
155   generally shallower than the inference needed for directly addressing a query. Therefore, utilizing
156   information from documents should be a more efficient solution. In this way, RAG can eliminate
157   certain nodes and potentially enhance the expressive power of LLMs. However, this also indicates
158
      that RAG cannot completely minimize reasoning depth, as some layers are necessary for document
      processing. Consequently, for a reasoning tree TL , at least λ · L layers will be required to process
159
      the documents, even if we retrieve the documents directly lead to the answer.

264   In real-world applications of RAG, Due to the limited capacity of the retriever, distractions fre-

266   relevant documents, the presence of noise information is common (Jiang et al., 2023b;a; Xu et al.,

      The proof is detailed in Appendix B.3. From the above theorem, it is evident that achieving better
358
      performance requires the input to incorporate information that effectively distinguishes irrelevant
359   tokens (a large I(ŵ; v)) and the necessary information for inference (a large I(s; v)). Additionally,
360   there should be minimal noise incorporated into the input (a large δ). As previously noted, the self-
361   attention module struggles to filter out irrelevant information while retaining relevant tokens, which
362   makes it difficult to increase δ. Furthermore, it is difficult to integrate additional information
                                                                                                P        about
363   ŵ (i.e., to increase I(ŵ; v)) because the input to the MLP layer is given by v = j ai,j xj Wv ,
364   resulting in v being an embedding of dimension m and precision p. This limited dimensionality
365   makes it challenging to encapsulate all the necessary information for filtering out irrelevant docu-
366   ments for all involved tokens. Consequently, the Feed Forward Layer is unlikely to achieve optimal
367   performance when dealing with noisy input.

369   4   E XTRA LYAERS FOR F ILTERING
370
371
      Clearly, noise in the documents adversely affects the performance of large language models (LLMs),
372   and filtering out this information without compromising the performance ability of the retrieval-
373   augmented language model (RALM) is challenging. Additional layers are necessary for effectively
374   filtering out irrelevant information. If we cannot resolve the filtering issue within a limited number
375   of layers, the overall reasoning capability of the RALM may fall below that of standard reason-
376   ing models, resulting in retrieved documents failing to enhance the reasoning abilities of the LLM.
377   Therefore, the crucial question is: how many layers are required to effectively address the filter-
      ing problem?

      4.1   T HE T RIPLE -W ISE P ROBLEM
379
380   For an input sequence X = [xT0 , xT1 , . . . , xTn−1 ], the vector r indicates the relevance of each token.
381   Specifically, for each token xi , a relevance score of ri = 0 signifies that the token is irrelevant to
382   the query. It’s crucial to note that calculating ri does not solely depend on the token embedding
383   xi and the query; rather, it may require the involvement of three or more tokens. For instance,
384   in the query “Alice is exhausted, but Bob is still very excited, showing no signs of fatigue. How
385   does Bob feel?”, the word “exhausted” acts as noise and should be excluded during inference.
386
      However, determining relevance necessitates considering “Bob” in the query alongside “Alice”, the
      subject of “exhausted”. Therefore, identifying the relevance of a token demands information from
387
      multiple tokens, yet self-attention computes relationships only between pairs, making it challenging
388
      to address this issue within a single transformer layer.
389
390   Consider a simple scenario in which three tokens are sufficient to determine the relevance of a given
391   token. The task is to ascertain whether there exists information indicating that token xi is noise
392
      relative to the query. Let g(xi , xa , xb ) be the function designed to identify whether tokens xa and
      xb suggest that token xi is a relevant token, if g(xi , xa , xb ) = 0, then xi is a noise token, otherwise
393
      it is relevant. Thus, the problem can be framed as follows:

                                    ri =
397                                            1   else.

      And we need a transformer based model M to calculate r = M(X), and we can show that one
      layer of transformer can hardly complete the task.

402   then there is no one layer transformer M with embedding size m, precision p and H heads to judge
403
      the relevance of tokens.
404
      Consequently, a single layer of multi-head attention struggles to assess the relevance of a token. As
405   noted in Conjecture 19 of Sanford et al. (2024), even multiple layers of multi-head attention may
406   not effectively resolve this issue. The challenge arises from the triple-wise nature of the problem
407   contrasted with the pair-wise nature of attention; the model can only evaluate a token’s relevance
408   when its embedding contains substantial information. For example, to assess the token “fatigue” in
409   the sentence “Alice is exhausted, but Bob is still very excited, showing no signs of fatigue. How does

411   textual details from phrases like “no signs of” and “showing”. Therefore, a significant amount of
412   information must be incorporated into the embedding beforeP     any judgement can be made. However,
413
      a single layer of self-attention can only consider the input j ai,j xj , which contains information
414
      up to mp, where m represents the embedding dimension and p indicates precision. The term mp
      signifies the maximal entropy of the embedding, which means the maximal information the embed-
415
      ding can carry. This suggests that multiple attention layers may be necessary to encompass all
416
      relevant information effectively.
417
418   Furthermore, this method renders Assumption 2.3 unrealistic, as assessing relevance requires a deep

      formation extraction. If we assume that t layers are needed to evaluate relevance, then extracting
      information about node ul,j would require λ · l + t layers. This could potentially exceed l, and

      extracting information from documents may necessitate a greater reasoning depth than standard rea-
422                                                       t
      soning. Consequently, nodes located below layer 1−λ    cannot access document information because
423
      their reasoning would be finished faster than information extraction of documents. Then the reason-
424   ing depth cannot be easily reduced, as it primarily occurs in the lower layers. Therefore, leveraging
425   document information may not enhance reasoning ability; in fact, it could potentially degrade perfor-
426   mance if the model becomes overly reliant on document information rather than employing vanilla
427   reasoning.

431   Therefore, reducing the number of layers required for filtering is essential to enhancing the reasoning
      ability of retrieval-augmented language models (RALMs). In the retrieval-augmented generation

433   document and representing this summarized information as one or a few additional tokens (virtual
434   prompt tokens), we can evaluate the relevance of a token using only the information from the token
435   itself, the query, and the summarized document.

446
      be done because we fix the position of one information, then the triple-wise problem becomes two
447   pair-wise problem. Otherwise, we need to incorporate plenty of information before final judgement,
448   because we do not know which one could be useful when judging the relevance. With the virtual

452
      that a token cannot aggregate information from any tokens that appear later in the sequence; thus,

456   limiting the model’s ability to utilize document information to judge the relevance.

458
      for document tokens. This arrangement enables the information of query to be effectively transferred

      5   DP ROMPT TUNING
494
      As stated in the previous section, effectively addressing this problem requires the use of virtual
495
      tokens at the beginning of the prompt to represent the document’s information. To accomplish this,
496
      we propose training an additional model to extract information from the document and utilize the
497   embeddings to encapsulate this information. We then append these virtual tokens to the front of the
498   prompt before conducting inference.
499
500
      It is important to note that the model used for information extraction does not impose any additional
      inference costs on the LLM, because the calculations are solely based on the document, This allows
501
      us to precalculate the embeddings and directly retrieve the information while retrieving documents,
502
      ensuring that there is no extra cost during inference.
503
504   Specifically, we fine-tune a BERT-base-uncased model with an additional multi-layer perceptron
505   (MLP) to project the embeddings to the appropriate dimensions. Given an input prompt, we identify
506
      the relevant documents, encode them as virtual tokens, and then inject these tokens at the front of
      the prompt.
507
508   Additionally, we perform inference with the query both before and after the documents to optimize
509   performance. And we put the virtual tokens represents the documents before the query for all tokens
510   to effectively take advantage of the virtual token. We also employ LoRA fine-tuning methods to
511   enhance the model’s ability to leverage the information in the virtual document tokens.

535
536   In this paper, we highlight that while RAG can enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, it
537   often only expands a limited number of reasoning depth. However, noise within the documents can
538   negatively affect performance, and processing these documents demands lots of reasoning depth
539   due to its intrinsic triple-wise nature. To address this, we propose a method called DPrompt tuning
      which allows for effective resolution within few layers of the transformer.

       E     R ELATED W ORK
1275
       RAG in LLM. Many recent works have explored better RAG strategies. Shi et al. (2023b) treat the
1276
       model as a black box and design a retriever to enhance performance. However, Shi et al. (2023a)
1277
       found that distracting documents in the retrieved set significantly weaken performance, leading some
1278   to address this by fine-tuning the LLM (Yoran et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). Additionally,
1279   researchers have identified that noise in the prompt can negatively impact performance, prompting
1280   efforts to eliminate this noise and compress prompts (Jiang et al., 2023b;a; Pan et al., 2024).
1281
       Filtering the noise. Yoran et al. (2023) tries to finetune the LLM to better filter out distracting doc-
1282
       uments and achieve better performance, while RAFT (Zhang et al., 2024a) uses different proportion
1283
       of distracting documents and finetune the model to better understand the question. Self-RAG(Asai
1284   et al., 2023), use special tokens to represent that should the LLM use document information and
1285   achieve great performance. Xu et al. (2024) first highlighted the duality of RAG, encompassing
1286   both benefits and detriments. Understanding how these factors influence the reasoning capabilities
1287   of LLMs remains an open question. Wang et al. (2023) also try to filter out irrelevant documents
1288   by information-theoretic approaches while Islam et al. (2024) decompose the LLM to a MoE model
1289   and use one expert to conduct filtering. Also some methods (Glass et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023b;
1290   Dong et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) try to recognize the relevance of documents and place the
1291   more relevance one in the first one or the last due to the Lost in the Middle phenomenon (Liu et al.,
1292
       2024).
1293   Expressive Power of LLM. Merrill & Sabharwal (2023) proves that the expressive power of LLM
1294   is constrained to T C 0 , but Feng et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024) shows that by Chain of Thought
1295   prompting, we can infinitely stack transformer layers to solve any DP problems, Zeng & Lee (2023)
       further prove the rank needed to adapt the model for other takss by LoRA, and Petrov et al. (2024)


                                                         24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       shows that prompt tuning can be a universal approximator with enough prompt length, while prompt
1297   tuning fail to change the attention pattern and can only bias the output (Petrov et al., 2023). RAG is
1298   also one kind of prompting technique, and it also try to involve more information when conducting
1299   inference like CoT. But how does RAG helps to improve the expressive power of LLM has not been
1300   studied before.
