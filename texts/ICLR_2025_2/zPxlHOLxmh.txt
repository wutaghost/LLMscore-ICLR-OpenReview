011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The increasing number of patients with mental health illness has heightened the
014            cognitive load on therapists, making it challenging for them to provide person-
015            alized care that each patient requires. Summarizing counseling sessions can aid
016            mental health practitioners in recalling key details. However, most existing re-
017            search on summarization focuses primarily on text-based summaries which often
018            require significant cognitive effort to read and interpret. Visual-based summary
019
               such as mind maps is proven to help enhance cognitive understanding by giving
               a quick overview of topics and content. Nevertheless, due to the complex nature
020
               of counseling which involves substantial qualitative data, generating visual-based
021
               summaries using traditional AI models can be challenging. With the recent ad-
022            vancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), these models have demonstrated
023            the capability to perform tasks based on instructions and generate outputs in vari-
024            ous formats. In this study, we develop a web-based summarization tool that serves
025            as a pipeline in performing summarization of counseling transcripts into visual-
026            based mind map summaries using LLMs. We conducted a human evaluation to
027            validate the effectiveness of the generated visual-based summary based on criteria
028            of accuracy, completeness, conciseness and coherence. Our findings show that
029            our web-based summarization tool can effectively extract key points from coun-
030
               seling transcripts and present them in visual-based mind maps, demonstrating its
               potential in enhancing insights for therapists, ultimately simplifying the process
031
               of documenting counseling sessions.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The World Health Organization (WHO) emphasizes that there can be “no health without mental
038   health” (WHO, 2005). This statement holds true as mental illness affects one in four individuals
039   during their lifetime (Ginn & Horder, 2012). While common mental health conditions such as stress
040   or mild depression can often be self-managed, a fraction of the population encounters more serious
041   disorders, including schizophrenia and severe depression. These serious mental disorders typically
042   require professional counseling treatment such as receiving psychotherapy (Althoff et al., 2016; Kuo
043   et al., 2022). Generally, mental health counseling follows a well-structured approach, which may
044   contain discussions about the patient’s personal experiences and the emotional challenges that are
045
      affecting their mental state (Srivastava et al., 2022). To build a strong therapeutic relationship with
      the patient, therapists are required to establish trust and create a comfortable environment for open
046
      communication. One effective way to achieve this is by empathizing with the patient’s perspective
047
      and gaining a deep understanding of what the patient is trying to convey (Ivey et al., 2018). With
048   the concerning surge in the number of patients with mental health illness in recent years (Panchal
049   et al., 2020), it has certainly become challenging for therapists to manage their caseloads effectively
050   hindering the delivery of personalized care each patient needs (Malhotra et al., 2022; Werbart et al.,
051   2019). The heightened cognitive load resulting from these overwhelming circumstances could be
052   reduced by implementing automated summarization of counseling session transcripts. While most
053   therapists are trained to take notes during therapy sessions, having an automated summarization of
      the session’s context would enhance their ability to recall key details and gain a deeper understand-

      ing of their patients. Additionally, the summarization tool could help verify therapists’ memories,
055   ensuring accuracy and consistency in their understanding of each session.

057   With the advancements in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP), dialogue summarization
058
      is no longer a new concept. Several previous studies (Malhotra et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2019; Zhang
      et al., 2021) have explored methods for summarizing transcripts from dialogues, with some focusing
059
      specifically on extracting and summarizing dialogue acts. This can provide therapists with a clearer
060
      understanding of key interaction points, enhancing their ability to analyze client’s responses. The
061   outputs of these studies were primarily presented in text-based formats. Although text-based out-
062   puts are effective, incorporating visual elements such as mind maps can further enhance clarity and
063   facilitate a quicker understanding of the counseling session. This is because text-based summaries
064   generally require more cognitive effort to read and interpret which can be time consuming particu-
065   larly when dealing with dense information. Figure 1 and Figure 2 shows the comparison between
066   both text-based and visual-based summaries of a counseling session. As depicted in Figure 2, visu-
067   alization techniques can present summarized information by categorizing key points and organizing
068   them hierarchically. This approach reduces information complexity and makes the content more
069
      engaging and memorable (Hochheiser & Verma, 2021). However, developing visual-based sum-
      maries is highly subjective to the context of the counseling conversation, which primarily involves
070
      qualitative data. This makes it challenging for therapists to create such summaries in real-time dia-
071
      logue environment. Nevertheless, our work demonstrates that generating these visual summaries is
072   feasible with the capabilities of LLMs.

      shot prompting to facilitate the model in extracting key points from the counseling transcripts and

139   2.1      D IALOGUE S UMMARIZATION
140
141   Dialogue summarization is the process of condensing a conversation into a shorter version that
142
      covers the important context from the original dialogue (Feng et al., 2021). These summaries are
      typically presented in text form and can vary in length depending on the dialogue’s context density.
143
      Generally, summarization methods are categorized into two broad types: extractive and abstractive
144
      (Srivastava et al., 2022). Extractive methods select key information from the dialogue and present it
145   in sentences without significantly altering their original semantics. In contrast, abstractive methods
146   generate summaries that may include words or sentences not present in the dialogue. For exam-
147   ple, abstractive summaries involve paraphrasing the content of the conversation, typically aiming to
148   improve the overall flow and coherence of the text (Sharma & Sharma, 2022).
149
      Automatic summarization has been a subject of study for a long period of time (Paice, 1990). From
150
      early methods like keyword frequency analysis to modern AI-based models, the field of automatic
151   summarization has certainly evolved over time. Several studies have emerged in the recent years,
152   proposing AI-based strategies for producing abstractive summaries for medical and mental health
153   conversations. For example, Zhang et al. (2021) explored the summarization of doctor-patient di-
154   alogues by fine-tuning various pretrained transformer models, with a focus on the patient’s history
155   of present illness (HPI). Similar methods can be found in other research, such as Srivastava et al.
156   (2022), which focuses on summarizing counseling components. Their study involves an utterance
157   classification model which acts as a guide for their summarization model to generate abstractive
158   summaries based on each criterion.
159   The growing popularity of LLMs has further motivated research in this area. For example, a study by
160   So et al. (2024) demonstrated the use of LLMs to summarize stressors and symptoms from interview

165   that LLMs can successfully perform these tasks, highlighting their effectiveness in analysing com-

      up new possibilities. In this study, we leverage LLMs to generate summaries in PlantUML mind

      2.2      I NFORMATION V ISUALIZATION
178
      Information visualization involves the use of visual representations to depict abstract non-physical
179   data in order to enhance cognitive understanding (Card, 1999; Chan et al., 2024). This approach is
180   widely applied across various domains including education, healthcare, finance and mental health.
181   For instance, bar charts, line charts and pie charts can effectively display information about users’
182   health. Studies indicate that presenting clear and meaningful data helps users quickly comprehend
183   their health conditions and can significantly improve their motivation towards a healthier lifestyle
184   (Chan et al., 2024).
185   In the mental health domain, various research efforts have led to solutions aimed at improving mental
186   well-being. Mobile apps have been developed to monitor users’ mental health status. For example,
187   Emotion Guru, developed by van Cuylenburg & Ginige (2021) is an emotion-tracking application
188   that detects emotions through social media posts on Facebook. The app accesses the user’s Facebook
189   posts and calculates the sentiment polarity for each post. A bar chart is used to depict the overall
190   positive and negative posts from the past 24 hours. Additionally, there is a mood tracking feature
191   where users can manually record their mood each day. This data is then presented in a line chart,
192   showing mood trends over the past 7 days. A similar study by Yamashita et al. (2019) who developed
193
      a mobile app called PNViz (Positive-and-Negative Polarity Visualizer). This app captures the level
      of positivity through short voice messages recorded by users. The results are then displayed in a line
194
      graph allowing users to visualize trends of their mental health positivity levels.
195
196   Thus far, in the mental health field, visualization techniques have primarily been designed to help
197   patients better understand their own mental well-being. However, little attention has been given to
198   developing strategies that assist practitioners. Chan et al. (2024) conducted a review and found that
199
      only one of seven studies focused on visualization techniques designed specifically for practitioners.
      These studies mainly visualize quantitative data such as mood trends, which can be easily plotted
200
      on charts. As mental health practitioners often conduct counseling sessions, the data they work
201
      with is primarily qualitative rather than quantitative, making it more complex to represent through
202   traditional visualization methods (Hochheiser & Verma, 2021). The subjective nature of conver-
203   sations, emotions and experiences captured in these sessions requires more nuanced approaches to
204   visualization to provide meaningful insights for both practitioners and patients.
205
      In this study we utilize PlantUML2 , a diagramming tool that provides a simple markdown syntax for
206
      representing mind maps. This tool can facilitate LLMs to generate outputs in such formats, making
207
      it feasible to organize key points of the counseling session in a hierarchical structure.

260   In this study, we employ the Mental Health Summarization (MEMO) dataset as our main dataset

262
      contains 12.9K utterances from 212 counseling sessions between the therapist and patient (Malho-

263
      available sources such as YouTube, all of which involve dyadic conversations. The transcripts have
264
      been further preprocessed to include speaker labels (P - patient, T - therapist) for each utterance
265   and to correct transcription errors. Additionally, the transcripts are fully anonymized with actual
266   names replaced by synthetic ones. As shown in Table 1, the dataset is split into training, testing and
267   validation sets following a 70:20:10 ratio with 152 samples for training, 39 for testing and 21 for
268   validation.

      3.2   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
280
281
      In this study, we focus on utilizing LLMs, specifically GPT-4o Mini to summarize counseling tran-
      scripts into visual-based summaries. We first developed a web interface that serves as a platform
282
      for experimenting prompts using OpenAI’s API endpoint. We chose GPT-4o Mini as our primary
283
      LLM due to its lightweight nature, cost-effectiveness and strong performance compared to GPT-3.5
284   Turbo. We designed a prompt that instructs the LLM to summarize the counseling transcript specifi-
285   cally focusing on the patient’s responses towards the therapist’s questions. Additionally, the prompt
286   includes instructions to format the output according to the PlantUML mind map format. Since the
287   LLM may not be explicitly trained to generate outputs in such a format, we applied a one-shot learn-
288   ing technique by providing a short example of the desired output to guide the model’s response. In
289   cases where the output did not meet the desired format, we iteratively refined the prompt by adjust-
290   ing the instructions and the one-shot example. This method of iteratively experimenting with the
291   prompt has proven to be effective in improving the results as demonstrated by (Zamfirescu-Pereira
292
      et al., 2023). After a few iterations of prompt refining, the final devised prompt is used to run the
      experiment on all our selected samples. Refer to Appendix A.1 for a sample of the prompt. Since
293
      the generated output is in PlantUML mind map format which is text-based, we develop a script to
294
      convert it into a visual-based interactive mind map using a JavaScript package called jsmind4 .
295
296   For this experiment, we randomly sub-sampled 20 samples from the testing set of the MEMO
297   dataset. To validate the feasibility of using LLM for effectively summarizing counseling transcripts
298
      and generating outputs in the PlantUML format, we began with a subset of 20 samples. This ap-
      proach allows us to demonstrate the initial effectiveness of our methodology before scaling to larger
299
      datasets.

302   3.3   H UMAN E VALUATION
303
304   We created an online evaluation form as part of a human evaluation method to assess the generated
305   visual-based summaries. The evaluation focused on the following 4 criteria:
306
307   • Accuracy: How well does the visual-based summary reflect the key information from the text-
308     based summary?
309
310   • Completeness: Does the visual-based summary capture all the important points as presented in the
311     text-based summary?
312
      • Conciseness: Is the visual-based summary presented without unnecessary details or redundancy?
313
314
      • Coherence: Is the visual-based summary organized in a logical and understandable way?
315
316
      These criteria were selected based on evaluation methodologies used in previous studies (So et al.,
317
      2024; Van Der Lee et al., 2019; Srivastava et al., 2022; Hochheiser & Verma, 2021; Jin et al., 2024).
318   We invited researchers from the School of Information Technology at Monash University Malaysia
319   to conduct quality evaluation on the 20 generated visual-based summaries. They were asked to
320   assess each generated summary by comparing the visual-based summary with the corresponding
321   text-based summary provided from the dataset using the 4 criteria listed above. The evaluation
322   criteria were presented on a 5-point Likert scale and the survey was designed to provide quantitative
323   feedback only. Additionally, they had the option to view the original transcript as supplementary
      material.

      5.1      C OMPARISON WITH L EXIMANCER
385
      In this study, we further experiment with the use of Leximancer5 to analyze counseling session
386   transcripts. Leximancer is a widely used, semi-automated content analysis software designed for
387   qualitative text analysis. It utilizes word occurrence and co-occurrence patterns to map relationships
388   between words, thereby identifying high-level themes from an input text. Leximancer generates
389   concepts and themes by detecting word patterns that frequently appear together within the text (En-
390   gstrom et al., 2022). Previous studies in the mental health field such as Fanaian et al. (2013), have
391   employed Leximancer to analyze textual data such as opinions regarding a topic of interest. These
392   generated themes and concepts are then visually presented as an interactive concept map.
393   We randomly selected one sample from the sub-sample set used in our study and processed it us-
394   ing Leximancer’s default settings. By observation and comparison of our generated visual-based
395   summary in Figure 2 with the concept map generated by Leximancer in Figure 5, we found that the
396   themes and concepts presented were somewhat shallow and did not capture the deeper context and
397   underlying meaning of the conversations. This limitation presents a challenge for therapists as they
398   may need to invest additional time in reviewing the results or require specific expertise to effectively
399   use the software for transcript analysis. Our web-based summarization tool addresses this issue by
400
      providing a concise summary of the counseling session, offering a more efficient and accessible
      alternative for therapists.
