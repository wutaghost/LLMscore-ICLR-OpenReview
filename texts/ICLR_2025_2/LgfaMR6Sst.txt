                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Accurately solving partial differential equations (PDEs) is critical for understand-
011            ing complex scientific and engineering phenomena, yet traditional numerical
012
               solvers are computationally expensive. Surrogate models offer a more efficient
               alternative, but their development is hindered by the cost of generating sufficient
013
               training data from numerical solvers. In this paper, we present a novel framework
014
               for active learning (AL) in PDE surrogate modeling that reduces this cost. Unlike
015            the existing AL methods for PDEs that always acquire entire PDE trajectories, our
016            approach strategically generates only the most important time steps with the nu-
017            merical solver, while employing the surrogate model to approximate the remain-
018            ing steps. This dramatically reduces the cost incurred by each trajectory and thus
019            allows the active learning algorithm to try out a more diverse set of trajectories
020            given the same budget. To accommodate this novel framework, we develop an ac-
021            quisition function that estimates the utility of a set of time steps by approximating
022            its resulting variance reduction. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method
023
               on several benchmark PDEs, including the Heat equation, Korteweg–De Vries
               equation, Kuramoto–Sivashinsky equation, and the incompressible Navier-Stokes
024
               equation. Extensive experiments validate that our approach outperforms existing
025
               methods, offering a cost-efficient solution to surrogate modeling for PDEs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In many scientific and engineering applications, accurately solving partial differential equations
032   (PDEs) in the form of trajectories of states evolving over time is essential for understanding complex
033   phenomena (Holton & Hakim, 2013; Atkins et al., 2023; Murray, 2007; Wilmott et al., 1995). The
034   traditional approach involves running numerical solvers, which provide accurate solutions but are
035   computationally costly, taking several hours, days or even weeks to run depending on the complexity
036   of the problem (Cleaver et al., 2016; Cowan et al., 2001). As a result, there is significant interest
037   in developing surrogate models (Greydanus et al., 2019; Bar-Sinai et al., 2019; Sanchez-Gonzalez
038   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Brandstetter et al., 2022b; Lippe et al., 2024) that can approximate
039
      the solutions more efficiently. Surrogate models are obtained by solving regression tasks on some
      “ground truth” data. The ground truth data for PDEs are generated by numerical solvers, which
040
      are costly compared to those of standard regression problems. As a result, the expense of data
041
      acquisition presents a major bottleneck in the development of surrogate models for PDEs.
042
043   Active Learning (AL, Chernoff, 1959; MacKay, 1992; Settles, 2009) can address this challenge by
044   adaptively acquiring the most informative inputs, effectively reducing the amount of ground-truth
045
      data required to obtain a high-quality surrogate model. However, there is a general lack of research
      in AL for regression tasks (Wu, 2018; Holzmüller et al., 2023), let alone PDEs. Existing studies
046
      on AL for PDEs have predominantly dealt with univariate outputs such as energy (Pestourie et al.,
047
      2020; Pickering et al., 2022), or predictions at a single, fixed time point (Bajracharya et al., 2024; Wu
048   et al., 2023b). To our surprise, the only work directly addressing AL for prediction of trajectories is
049   that of Musekamp et al. (2024). In this work, the surrogate model is set as an autoregressive model
050   that predicts the evolved state of a PDE at time t + ∆t given a state at an arbitrary time point t,
051   and is trained on data acquired by existing regression-based AL methods (Holzmüller et al., 2023).
052   Specifically, at each round of acquisition, the AL method chooses initial conditions from which
053   entire trajectories are acquired. However, we argue that querying all the states in a trajectory is not
      sample-efficient, especially for autoregressive surrogate models.

069   Acquiring entire trajectories is inefficient mainly for two reasons. First, states within a trajectory are
070   often strongly correlated, undermining their diversity or the joint information gain (Houlsby et al.,
071   2011; Kirsch et al., 2019). We validate this assertion in Appendix C.1 through principal component
072   analysis. Secondly, even if they are not strongly correlated, it can be the case that only certain time
073   steps of a trajectory are the most informative due to the dynamics of the PDE. In both cases, noting
074   that the main cost is in running the numerical solver, it would be ideal to selectively acquire only the
075   most important time steps with the numerical solver, for a fraction of the cost of acquiring the entire
076
      trajectory. However, this is usually impossible without querying all the time steps that come earlier.
077   In this paper, we propose a novel, flexible framework for data acquisition that circumvents the
078   constraint of having to query all time steps in a trajectory, along with an AL strategy that leverages
079   this flexibility. Our method combines both a numerical solver and a surrogate model to acquire data
080   along a trajectory with reduced cost. Specifically, it selects which time steps along a trajectory to
081   query from the solver, while using the surrogate model to approximate the remaining steps. We also
082
      develop a novel acquisition function that guides our AL strategy in choosing which time steps to
      query to the numerical solver in each trajectory.
083
084   Overall, our framework, equipped with the novel AL strategy, significantly improves surrogate
085   model performance over previous methods. We validate our approach through extensive experiments
086   on benchmarks, including the Heat equation, Korteweg–De Vries equation, Kuramoto–Sivashinsky
087   equation, and the Navier-Stokes equation. Additionally, we analyze the behavior of our AL method,
088
      providing insights into the factors that contribute to its effectiveness.

151   3.1   F RAMEWORK OF DATA ACQUISITION
152
      We present our method, F LEX AL, which operates under a framework of data acquisition that is
153
      much more sample efficient than previous works. Algorithm 1 provides an overview of our frame-
154
      work. We start with a surrogate model Ĝ, or a committee of surrogate models {Ĝm }M        m=1 , trained
155
      with the initial dataset D. At every round of AL, we choose an initial condition u0 from the pool
156   P, similar to the existing AL methods for PDE trajectories. However, while existing methods ac-
157   quire the entire trajectory starting from the chosen initial condition u0 (Musekamp et al., 2024), our
158   method acquires a partial trajectory. Specifically, we select a subset of time steps to simulate from
159   u0 , rather than acquiring the full trajectory. The rationale behind this approach is that, given a fixed
160   budget, acquiring as many trajectories as possible—albeit partially—from different initial conditions
161   is often more beneficial than fully acquiring fewer trajectories. This strategy enables more efficient
      exploration of the data space and improves the overall sample efficiency of the framework.

      3.3   BATCH ACQUISITION A LGORITHM
247
248   With the acquisition function defined above, we present a batch acquisition algorithm given a pool
249   P of initial conditions . We define the cost of a batch {(u0j , Sj )}N
                                                                           j=1 as the total number of queries
250                   PN                                                    PN
      to the solver, j=1 ∥Sj ∥. A standard objective is to maximize j=1 a(u0j , Sj ) under a budget
251              P
      constraint j ∥Sj ∥ ≤ B, to which there is a known approximate solution (Salkin & De Kluyver,
252
      1975) that greedily maximizes the cost-weighted acquisition function a∗ (u0 , S) = a(u0 , S)/∥S∥
253
      until the total cost exceeds the budget B. However, this method faces two problems. First, it’s
254                                    PN
      questionable whether the sum j=1 a(u0j , Sj ) of individual acquisition values is actually a good
255
      representative for the utility of a batch. In fact, numerous works report that picking instances that
256
      maximize individual acquisition values can severely underperform compared to methods that take
257
      into account the interactions between those instances (Kirsch et al., 2019; Ash et al., 2019). The
258   problem is chiefly attributed to the lack of diversity and representativeness (Wu, 2018) caused by
259   oversampling of small, high value regions (Smith et al., 2023). Secondly, we are actually searching
260   over the product pool of the sampling pattern S and the pool P of initial conditions, whose size is
261   on the order of O(2L |P|). Both terms impose significant computational burden on optimizing the
262   cost-weighted objective a∗ (u0 , S).
263
      We therefore propose F LEX AL as an add-on to existing AL methods that acquire full trajectories.
264   Specifically, a full-trajectory AL method A, which we call a base method, first selects an initial
265   condition u0 . Musekamp et al. (2024) introduces several possibilities for such a method, including
266   QbC (Seung et al., 1992), Largest Cluster Maximum Distance (LCMD, Holzmüller et al., 2023),
267   Core-Set (Sener & Savarese, 2017), and stochastic batch active learning (SBAL, Kirsch et al.,
268   2023). We then optimize the cost-weighted acquisition function a(u0 , S) over the sampling pattern
269   S while holding u0 fixed, and add the pair (u0 , S) to the current batch. We iterate this two-stage
      process until the cost of the batch reaches our budget limit. Additionally, if the cost ever exceeds


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      the budget after adding a pair, we truncate the sampling pattern so that the cost is exactly equal
271   to the budget. By using F LEX AL as an add-on, the diversity and representativeness promoted by
272   base AL methods (Holzmüller et al., 2023; Kirsch et al., 2023; Musekamp et al., 2024) are upheld,
273   and the size of the optimization space for F LEX AL is reduced to O(2L ). The problem remains,
274   however, that O(2L ) is a prohibitively large space for optimization. We therefore use a simple
275   greedy algorithm for searching S. In the greedy algorithm, we start by initializing S with all entries
276   set to true. At each step of the greedy algorithm, we propose a neighboring pattern S ′ by applying
277   a bit-flip mutation, where each bit of S is flipped with a probability of ϵ. The proposal is accepted
278   only if the acquisition value a∗ (u0 , S ′ ) is higher than the current value a∗ (u0 , S). This process of
279
      proposal and acceptance/rejection is repeated T times. We use T = 100 and ϵ = 0.1 throughout our
      experiments. A more concise summary of the batch acquisition algorithm is given in Appendix D.2.
280
      The algorithmic complexity of batch acquisition is discussed in Section 5.7.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
285
286   AL for PDEs. The works by Pestourie et al. (2020); Pickering et al. (2022); Gajjar et al. (2022)
287   apply active learning to problems involving PDEs, but their tasks are limited to predicting QoI,
288   such as the maximum value of an evolved state. Li et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2023b) apply their AL
289   methods to single-state prediction. Bajracharya et al. (2024) explores the use of active learning in
290
      tasks of predicting steady states of PDEs, which can be seen as predicting single states at t → ∞.
      Finally, Musekamp et al. (2024) experiments with active learning in predicting PDE trajectories with
291
      autoregressive models.
292
293   Active selection of time points. While our work is the first to propose time step selection in active
294   learning (AL) for PDEs, the concept of selecting time points has been explored in other contexts.
295   For example, in physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), active selection of collocation points for
296
      training has been widely studied (Arthurs & King, 2021; Gao & Wang, 2023; Mao & Meng, 2023;
      Wu et al., 2023a; Turinici, 2024). “Labels” for PINNs, or the residual loss, can be calculated directly
297
      at any time point using closed form equations. There are also methods in Bayesian experimental
298
      design (BED) that choose observation times that maximize information gain about parameters of
299   interest (Singh et al., 2005; Cook et al., 2008). In those works, a trajectory is already “there”, but the
300   cost is attributed to the act of observing a time point. In contrast, in our setting, we cannot directly
301   acquire a time point, because there is a cost in the simulation of the trajectory.
302
      Multi-fidelity AL. Closely related to our work is multi-fidelity active learning Li et al. (2022b);
303
      Wu et al. (2023b); Hernandez-Garcia et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), where outputs are acquired at
304
      varying fidelity levels for each input, with associated costs inherent to each fidelity. In our context,
305   the task of actively selecting a sampling pattern for a given initial condition can be seen as a fidelity
306   selection problem, where acquiring all time steps corresponds to the highest fidelity but also incurs
307   the highest cost.

      5.1   BASELINE AL M ETHODS
314
315
      To compare with our method, we experiment with AL for full trajectory sampling introduced in
      Musekamp et al. (2024). Random sampling from the pool set is the simplest method. QbC (Seung
316
      et al., 1992) is a simple active learning algorithm that selects points according to maximum dis-
317
      agreement among members of a committee. LCMD (Holzmüller et al., 2023) is an AL algorithm
318   that uses a feature map. We concatenate the last hidden layer activations of committee members at
319   all time steps of a trajectory, and sketch the concatenated features to a dimension of 512 using a ran-
320   dom projection. Kirsch et al. (2023) proposes SBAL, which randomly samples data points x with
321   a probability distribution proportional to its temperature-scaled acquisition value p(x) ∝ a(x)m .
322   We use the acquisition function of QbC with temperature m = 1. We leave out Core-Set (Sener &
323   Savarese, 2017) because it generally underperforms compared to the above methods, according to
      both Holzmüller et al. (2023) and Musekamp et al. (2024).

354   5.2   TARGET PDE S
355
      We evaluate our method on a range of PDEs. The first is the Heat equation in one spatial dimension.
356   Next, we test the nonlinear Korteweg–De Vries (KdV) equation, which is known for exhibiting soli-
357   tary wave pulses with weak interactions (Zabusky & Kruskal, 1965). We then apply our method to
358   the Kuramoto–Sivashinsky (KS) equation, another nonlinear PDE in one dimension, notable for its
359   chaotic dynamics. Lastly, we consider the vorticity form of the incompressible Navier-Stokes equa-
360   tion (NS) in two spatial dimensions. All equations are solved with periodic boundary conditions.
361   Additional details are in Appendix A.1.

363   5.3   S URROGATE M ODELS
364
365   We use a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO, Li et al., 2020) to model the evolution operator G. In
366
      particular, we train it to predict the differences between states in adjacent time steps, following
      Musekamp et al. (2024). All models have four hidden layers. We use 16, 256, 128, and 32 modes
367
      for Heat, KdV, KS, and NS equations, respectively. We also normalize the data according to the
368
      initial dataset’s mean and standard deviation over all temporal and spatial dimensions. We use
369   teacher-forcing to train the FNOs, meaning that it’s simply trained on ground truth input-output
370   pairs from the solver G without backpropagating through two or more time steps. All models were
371   trained with Adam (Kingma, 2014) for 100 epochs, using a learning rate of 10−3 , a batch size of 32,
372   and a cosine annealing scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016).

512   6    C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this paper, we presented a novel framework for active learning in surrogate modeling of par-
515   tial differential equation (PDE) trajectories, significantly reducing the cost of data acquisition while
516   maintaining or improving model accuracy. By selectively querying only a subset of time steps in
517   a PDE trajectory, our method F LEX AL enables the acquisition of informative data at a fraction of
518   the cost of acquiring entire trajectories. We introduced a new acquisition function that estimates
519   the utility of a set of time steps based on variance reduction, effectively guiding the selection pro-
520
      cess in an adaptive manner. Through extensive experiments on benchmark PDEs, including the
      Heat equation, Korteweg–De Vries equation, Kuramoto–Sivashinsky equation, and incompressible
521
      Navier-Stokes equation, we demonstrated that our approach consistently outperforms existing AL
522
      methods, providing a more cost-efficient and accurate solution for PDE surrogate modeling.
523
524   Our results show that F LEX AL can significantly enhance surrogate modeling in PDEs, particularly
525   in scenarios where the numerical solver is computationally expensive. We further showed that the
526
      success of F LEX AL is driven by its ability to prioritize both diverse and informative time steps.
      Moving forward, this framework could be extended to more complex systems and integrated with
527
      other machine learning techniques, providing broader applicability in scientific and engineering
528
      simulations. Future work may also explore alternative acquisition functions and applications to
529   simulations outside the domain of PDEs.

      Heat Equation The one-dimensional (1D) Heat equation is given by:
787
788                                               ∂t u = ∂xx u,                                          (7)
789   where u = u(x, t) represents the temperature distribution as a function of space x and time t. This
790   equation describes the process of heat conduction and diffusion in a medium. The simplicity of
791   the Heat equation makes it a fundamental model for understanding diffusion-like processes across
792   various fields in science and engineering, such as thermal conduction, population dynamics, and
793
      chemical diffusion. For our experiments, we solve this equation using the pseudospectral method
      with Dormand–Prince solver as in Brandstetter et al. (2022a).

795
      Korteweg–De Vries (KdV) Equation The second equation we study is the Korteweg–De Vries
796
      (KdV) equation, given by:
797
798
                                           ∂t u + u∂x u + ∂xxx u = 0,                                    (8)
799   where u = u(x, t) represents a wave profile evolving over space and time. This nonlinear PDE
800   describes the evolution of shallow water waves, and its most famous characteristic is the presence of
801   solitons—solitary, stable wave packets that maintain their shape over long distances and weak inter-
802   actions with other waves (Zabusky & Kruskal, 1965). Solitons have important applications in fluid
803
      dynamics, plasma physics, and optical fiber communications. The KdV equation’s nonlinearity and

880   A.3     S IMULATION I NSTABILITY
881
      It was observed that using F LEX AL on the KdV equation, the simulation crashes on a small subset
882   of synthetic inputs. Analysis reveals that these synthetic inputs have unusually large norms and
883   particularly appear in later parts of trajectories due to accumulated error. We do not attempt to fix
884   this problem explicitly due to the risk of over-complicating our method, and simply refrain from
885   adding these time steps to the training dataset. This means that F LEX AL actually acquires a smaller
886   number of time steps than the budget B per round of acquisition, which could be problematic when
887   a large subset of inputs do crash. However, we find that this is not the case, and the number of such
888   inputs is small enough that F LEX AL can outperform other baselines. We report the comparison of
889   datasize across rounds in Table 6, for a single experiment. We can see that 13 time steps were left
890
      out in the first round due to instability, and no instability occurred in the rounds after.
891   Since queries that crash incur a cost, they should be avoided as much as possible. Previous works
892   in Bayesian optimization (Gelbart et al., 2014; Hernández-Lobato et al., 2015) propose methods
893   to learn these unknown constraints. Alternatively, one could simply test out large, random inputs.
894   In fact, we find that the maximum absolute value of an input being above 10 is a robust criterion
895   for predicting that the solver will crash. Either way, we could simply filter out time steps that fall
896
      outside of these constraints during runtime of the solver, and use the freed up budget on acquiring
      other trajectories. Another possible approach is to impose physical constraints on the surrogate
897
      model (Goswami et al., 2022) that reduces the risk of outputting abnormal synthetic inputs. For
898
      instance, the KdV equation is energy-conserving, and when this prior knowledge is encoded into the
899   surrogate model, the synthetic inputs would never be abnormally large like we experienced with our
900   naive surrogate models.

998
       is useful for analyzing the behavior of AL strategies. AL methods tend to improve performance on
999
       points with extreme errors, thus improving performance significantly in the top quantiles, while not
1000   so much in the middle quantiles. This is why AL methods perform differently depending on the
1001   nature of problem. For instance, problems with more irregularities tend to benefit signficantly more
1002   from AL methods, since the top quantile errors contribute significantly to the average error in those
1003   problems.
1004
       As expected, the baseline methods improve performance over random sampling in the 99% quantile,
1005
       but not so much in the 95% and 50% quantiles. Suprisingly, F LEX AL robustly outperforms the
1006   baselines in all error quantiles, which is rarely the case for existing AL methods. We can therefore
1007   infer that F LEX AL isn’t simply sacrificing the surrogate model’s performance in some trajectories
1008   to improve its performance in others. F LEX AL both sees a more diverse set of trajectories, and
1009   samples the most informative time steps in each trajectory, effectively accounting for how it can
1010   improve performance in both the high and middle quantiles of error.

1050
1051   Brandstetter et al. (2022b) identifies a potential problem with training an autoregressive surrogate
1052   model with teacher-forcing. The model experiences a distribution shift during inference, because
1053   errors accumulate during rollout unlike during training. They propose a simple fix, called the push-
1054   forward trick, which supervises the model Ĝ not with pairs of ui−1 and ui , but with pairs of Ĝ[ui−2 ]
1055   and ui , where Ĝ is constantly changing throughout training. An even simpler fix that they experi-
1056   ment with is augmenting the inputs with a Gaussian noise.
1057
       One might hypothesize that the advantage of F LEX AL comes from its regularizing effect, since the
1058
1059
       synthetic inputs in the training set are outputs from the surrogate model Ĝ. We therefore apply the
       pushforward trick and Gaussian noise augmentation on the best performing baseline method, SBAL.
1060
       The results in Table 11 shows that the effect of such regularization methods is minimal compared
1061
       to the effect of F LEX AL. This shows that the advantage of F LEX AL lies not just in its regularizing
1062   effect.

1113   D.1   M OTIVATION BEHIND THE ACQUISITION F UNCTION
1114
1115   Here we detail the motivation behind our acquisition function defined in Section 3.2. First, one can
1116
       imagine several alternative acquisition functions.
1117   The most straightforward alternative is to simply use the sum of the variances at time points for
1118   which bi = true. The variances are larger for the later time steps since they accumulate, and in our
1119   preliminary experiments, we found that this is catastrophic as undersampling the earlier time steps
1120   leads to the sampled trajectory being very out-of-distribution, and hence the trained surrogate model
1121   underperforming on the test distribution.
1122   It quickly became clear to us that we need some kind of measure of "how much total uncertainty
1123   will be reduced by sampling these time steps", instead of "how uncertain is our model on these time
1124   steps?" This would help select sampling patterns that reduce the out-of-distribution-ness introduced
1125   by Ĝ. One way to approximate this is to use mutual information, as used by Li et al. (2022b). In
1126   other words, we would rollout M trajectories with M surrogate models, and compute the mutual
1127   information between time steps for which bi = true and all time steps. However, in preliminary
1128   experiments, we found that this method underperforms, which we hypothesize is because relying
1129   simply on the covariance matrix of the committee between time steps is not a good enough method
1130
       for computing the posterior uncertainty.
1131   We identified two "pathways" through which sampling a time step reduces uncertainty in the remain-
1132   ing time steps. First, there is the "indirect" pathway: sampling a time step will reduce the model’s
1133   uncertainty on similar inputs, hence reducing uncertainty on the remaining time steps. This is what
       is approximated by mutual information. Then, there is the "direct" pathway: sampling a time step

       D.2   BATCH ACQUISITION A LGORITHM
1274
1275   Algorithm 2 summarizes the batch selection algorithm of F LEX AL. Starting with an empty batch
1276   B, the algorithm repeatedly selects initial conditions and their sampling patterns until reaching the
1277   budget limit. It first uses the base active learning method A to choose an initial condition u0 .
1278   Then, it optimizes which time steps to sample through a greedy procedure: starting with a pattern
1279   S that samples all time steps (all true values), it performs T iterations of random mutations. In
1280   each iteration, it generates a candidate pattern S ′ by randomly flipping entries in S with probability
1281   ϵ (using a binary mask C where each entry is drawn from a Bernoulli distribution and the XOR
1282
       operation ⊕). If this new pattern achieves a better value according to the cost-weighted acquisition
       function a∗ , it becomes the current pattern. To ensure the budget isn’t exceeded, if adding the
1283
       current pattern would go over budget, the algorithm truncates it by keeping only enough true values
1284
       to exactly meet the budget. The pair of initial condition and its optimized sampling pattern (u0 , S)
1285   is then added to the batch B.
