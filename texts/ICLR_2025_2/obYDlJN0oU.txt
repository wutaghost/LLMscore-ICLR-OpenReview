009                                              A BSTRACT
010
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have been trained on vast corpora of data, al-
011
               lowing them to learn internal representations of how humans would respond in
012            different scenarios. This makes them well-suited to simulate the actions of market
013            participants, to model their collective impact on financial markets and perform fi-
014            nancial forecasting. However, there also exist various sources of errors that could
015            affect the effectiveness of LLM agent-based simulations of the market. Firstly, in-
016            dividual market participants do not always make rational decisions, which might
017            not be captured by the logical reasoning process of LLMs. Secondly, the numer-
018            ical and financial literacy of LLMs are also not highly reliable, due to possible
019            knowledge gaps in their numerical understanding and possible hallucinations in
020
               their outputs. To tackle these issues, we propose our Massively Multi-Agents
               Role Playing (MMARP) method, which aims to produce highly accurate market
021
               simulations through theory-driven prompt designs. To reduce the impact of noisy
022
               actions caused by individual irrational investors, we leverage the LLM-generated
023            next-token weights to simulate repetitive prompting, and obtain the aggregated
024            market response. To minimize the effects of possible gaps in its numerical knowl-
025            edge or potential hallucinated outputs, we prompt the LLM using a range of price
026            inputs for each trading day. Finally, to produce simulated forecasts of market
027            prices, we perform the above prompting strategies across two types of LLM-agent
028            roles, buyers and sellers, and obtain the intersection price between their response
029            curves. Through experimental results, we show that MMARP can outperform
030            other deep-learning methods and various financial LLMs in forecasting metrics.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Financial markets are complex ecosystems that are driven by millions of market participants, each
036   making individual decisions about the value of an asset based on available information (Fama, 1970).
037   While traditional deep-learning models have been developed to predict the market in the past (Ding
038   et al., 2015; Hu et al., 2018; Xu & Cohen, 2018), they typically do so by identifying the historical
039
      patterns in market data, but do not fundamentally capture the individual decision-making processes
      that drive these patterns. On the other hand, Large Language Models (LLMs), which have been
040
      trained on vast corpora of human-produced data, have demonstrated the ability to learn internal
041
      representations (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023; Chen et al., 2024) of how humans might respond to different
042   prompts, enabling them to simulate human decision-making. This raises the possibility of simulating
043   the actions of market participants using LLM agents, to model their collective impact on the market.
044
045
      Generally, works that utilize LLM agents in Finance (Zhang et al., 2024b; Yu et al., 2024a;b) have
      focused on using them in advisor roles to enhance investor decision-making. For these works, the
046
      goal is typically to maximize profits, and these models are only evaluated over their profitability
047
      metrics (e.g., cumulative returns, Sharpe ratio). In contrast, our work seeks to use LLM agents to
048   simulate investor actions to study actual market dynamics, which could offer a novel framework for
049   financial researchers to understand and test hypotheses about market behavior. The accuracy of our
050   simulation would also be evaluated over forecasting metrics. This has been previously explored in
051   LLM agent-based simulation works, such as modeling pandemic spread across a population (Chopra
052   et al., 2024), or the U.S. election results (Zhang et al., 2024c). In the Finance domain, this has not
053   been studied in detail. Some recent works have began to explore the use of LLM agents to model
      investor actions (Zhang et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2024), but these were evaluated qualitatively based

054
      on how reasonable their behavior is, but not their actual predictive performances. In this work, we
055   aim to produce effective simulations of actual market behavior to generate accurate price predictions.
056
057   However, accurately simulating participants in the market is a difficult task. We can identify two
058
      challenges: Firstly, individual market participants do not always make rational decisions (Daniel &
      Titman, 1999). LLMs, while excelling at performing logical reasoning, might not be able to capture
059
      these irrational behavior when simulating investor actions (Alsagheer et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024),
060
      which reduces their effectiveness in this use case. Secondly, the numerical and financial literacy of
061   LLMs are also not highly reliable. In the past, LLMs have been shown to make simple but crucial
062   mistakes when handling numerical values1 , or produce hallucinations when performing reasoning
063   on financial texts (Koa et al., 2024). Because of these limitations, it might not be fully reasonable to
064   assume LLM agents can accurately replicate actual investor behavior (see Figure 1), which would
065   reduce the effectiveness of utilizing LLM agents to model participant behaviors in financial markets.

082   To deal with the above-mentioned challenges, we propose our Massively Multi-Agents Role Playing
083   (MMARP) framework, which utilizes a series of theory-informed prompt designs to produce highly
084   accurate market simulations. Firstly, to reduce the impact of noisy actions caused by individual ir-
085   rational investors, we leverage LLM-generated next-token weights to simulate repetitive prompting
086
      in order to obtain the aggregated market response, which is known to be less noisy and observable.
      Secondly, to minimize the effects of possible gaps in its numerical knowledge or potential halluci-
087
      nated outputs, we prompt the LLM across a range of price inputs for each trading day to obtain the
088
      aggregated response function. Finally, to produce simulated prediction of market prices, we perform
089   the above prompting strategies across two types of LLM-agent roles, buyers and sellers, and obtain
090   the intersection price between their response functions. Crucially, this is also the same mechanism
091   in which market equilibrium prices are determined in economic markets (Mankiw & Taylor, 2020).
092
      To demonstrate the effectiveness of MMARP, we perform experiments over some financial datasets
093
      and show that our method can outperform other deep-learning methods and various financial LLMs
094
      in forecasting metrics. In addition, we do a rigorous model study to show that the simulated behavior
095   is valid, by comparing our generated response curves to theory-based demand curves in economics.
096
097   The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as:
098
      • We investigate the validity of using LLM agents to model market participants in financial markets,
099
        which has not been extensively studied before this work. We observe various sources of stochastic
100     errors that could reduce the overall simulation accuracy, which include irrational investor behav-
101     iors, the lack of numerical understanding in LLMs, and possible hallucination in LLM outputs.
102
      • We propose a method that aims to produce highly accurate market simulations through theory-
103
        driven prompt designs. This is done through simulating the aggregate market response using the
104
        LLM-generated next-token weights, prompting across a range of prices for each trading day, and
105     using the intersection point between the LLM response curves to obtain accurate price forecasts.

113   2   BACKGROUND
114
115   LLM Agents in Financial Simulations. Since the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs),
116   works have also started to explore the use of LLM agents in Finance. Early works first explored
117
      augmented single-LLM agent models with tool-use (Zhang et al., 2024b) or memory (Yu et al.,
      2024a) to enhance their capabilities in making investing decisions. Later works would explore
118
      the use of multiple LLM agents, such as a group of seven analyst agents working with a manager
119
      agent (Yu et al., 2024b) to provide investing recommendations. Across these works, the main goal is
120   generally to maximize profits, and the performances are only evaluated on their profitability metrics.
121
122   More recently, works have began exploring the use of LLM agents to simulate market participants,
123
      which are more closely related to our work. Some of these include Agent-based Simulated Financial
      Market (Gao et al., 2024), which simulates the actions of four different types of investors, and
124
      StockAgent (Zhang et al., 2024a), which modeled the actions of up to 200 LLM agents to study
125
      their aggregate behaviors on price trends. In these works, each LLM agent is used to represent a
126   single investor, which could limit the effectiveness of the simulation, given that the market consists
127   of transactions from millions of investors each day. Currently, these agent-based simulations are
128   only studied empirically to observe if the simulated actions and overall price trends are reasonable.
129
      Another closely related set of works are those on macro-level LLM-agent simulations. These works
130
      seek to simulate the behavior of entire systems such as the economy (Li et al., 2024), which usually
131
      consists of millions of humans and cannot be individually modeled by a single LLM agent. To
132   do so, these works usually use a single LLM agent to model entire groups of the same archetypes
133   (Chopra et al., 2024). For example, to predict the U.S. election results (Zhang et al., 2024c), it might
134   be sufficient to simulate by the unique voter demographics, instead of each voter individually. Our
135   work follows this idea by seeking to model the buyers and sellers in financial markets as a whole.
136
      Numerical and Financial Literacy of LLMs. The numerical understanding of LLMs is not well-
137
      studied in literature. Different LLMs have different methods of tokenizing numbers (Jun, 2024),
138   which could affect their numeracy level. Empirically, LLMs have been shown to fail at simple
139   numerical tasks1 . The root of this problem likely stems from the continuous nature of numerical
140   values (Golkar et al., 2023). Unlike individual words, which are finite in nature, it is impossible for
141   LLMs to encounter all possible numerical values during training, resulting in possible gaps in its
142   numerical knowledge. It has been shown that the ability of LLMs to handle numbers correlates with
143   how frequently those numbers occur in the train data (Razeghi et al., 2022), and they are usually
144   unable to extrapolate outside the range of numbers they have been trained on (Wallace et al., 2019).
145   On the other hand, LLMs have been extensively shown to be able to process text-based financial
146   data, through multiple tasks such as sentiment analysis and financial forecasting (Xie et al., 2023;
147   Wu et al., 2023). However, it has also been observed that they can produce hallucinations (Koa et al.,
148   2024) when performing reasoning on financial texts, which could reduce their overall reliability.
149

150   man, 1949) for an asset can be modeled as a function d(X), where X consists of the input factors
151   such as the price or the non-price determinants such as its future price expectations (Mankiw &
152   Taylor, 2020). For each individual, the demand function can further be split into two components:

157   be affected by the idiosyncrasies of each individual (McFadden, 1972) and is difficult to predict.

180   3     M ASSIVELY M ULTI -AGENTS ROLE -P LAYING
181
182   The Massively Multi-Agents Role-Playing (MMARP) framework consists of three components. To
183   reduce noise from individual irrational investors, we use LLM-generated next-token weights to sim-
184
      ulate repetitive prompting, yielding an aggregated market response. To address gaps in LLMs’
      numerical knowledge or potential hallucinations, we prompt across a range of price inputs for each
185
      trading day to derive an aggregated LLM response function. Finally, we repeat these across buyer
186
      and seller roles to obtain the intersection of their response functions as the simulated market prices.
187
188
      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
189
190   To investigate the validity of using Large Language Model (LLM) agents to model market partici-
191   pants, we first look at the process in which the agents are utilized in financial multi-agent systems.
192
      Given some input information X of an asset, such as its price or news information, an LLM agent
193
      is typically prompted to produce an actionable response α. This response may take the form of a
194   binary buy or sell decision (Gao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024b) or some quantitative values indicating
195   the price and quantity of the asset to transact (Zhang et al., 2024a). For an LLM, the response is
196   sampled from its generated next-token probability based on the provided inputs. For example, given
197   the price of an asset, we can prompt an LLM whether to buy an asset. This can be formulated as:
198                                                 α ∼ ld (X),                                           (5)
199
      where ld (X) represents the LLM generative function that outputs the token probability for whether
200
      to buy an asset. For an LLM agent to realistically simulate a market buyer, this function should ac-
201   curately emulate the demand function of the individual, i.e., ld (X) = d(X), matching their actions.
202
203   3.2   I RRATIONAL PARTICIPANTS IN F INANCIAL M ARKETS
204
205   Following Equation 1, the individual demand function d(X) is composed of a rational component
206   drational (X) and a stochastic error term ϵ(X), which accounts for irrational or unpredictable indi-
207   vidual behaviors. Given that LLMs predominantly generate outputs that are grounded in reasoning
208   and logical patterns derived from training data (Kojima et al., 2022), they might struggle to simulate
209   the irrational components of investor behavior accurately (Alsagheer et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024).
210   To deal with this problem, a possible solution is then to generate a large number of outputs from the
211   LLM. By drawing a large number of response samples α, we would then be simulating the aggregate
                                                                                       PN
212   behavior across a large number of market participants, which would give us i=1 αi = D(X).
213   Then, by following the approximation found in Equation 4, we can obtain the equivalent simulation:

                                          i=1          i=1

      Given that LLMs excel at rational reasoning, this becomes a more realistic task, as the stochastic
217   error term ϵ(X) is now minimized. This shows that while it is difficult to use LLM agents to direct
218   model individual participants, it is possible to utilize them to simulate the aggregate market demand.
219
220
      Following the assumption that each rational participant would make the same reasonable response
      given the same inputs, the samples can all be drawn from the same LLM prompt ld (X). In this case,
221
      by providing the LLM with a set of defined options (e.g., Expensive, Cheap), we can also directly
222
      extract the ratio of their generated token probabilities to represent the ratio of the expected responses
223   from the market. This “trick” allows us to simulate the behavioral patterns of large-scale populations
224   using LLMs without repetitive prompting, which would minimize unnecessary computation costs.
225
226   3.3   H ALLUCINATIONS AND K NOWLEDGE G APS IN LLM S
227
228   Next, for an LLM to accurately simulate market reasoning, it would also need to understand and
229   respond to the input information X in the same way as participants do. However, various sources
230   of errors exist. Firstly, LLMs are known to produce hallucinations in their outputs (Zhang et al.,
231   2023), which would result in responses that differ from actual participants. While this is a problem
232
      common to all LLM works, it has also been observed in those dealing with financial texts (Koa et al.,
      2024). Secondly, the ability of LLMs to understand the numerical price values is not well-studied in
233
      literature. Empirically, LLMs have been shown to be weak at handling numerical-based tasks (Dziri
234
      et al., 2024; Frieder et al., 2024), without the use of external tools like interpreters (Gao et al., 2023).
235
236   From the observations above, we can further break down the LLM generative function into two:
237                                         ld (X) = l¯d (X) + ϵ̄(X),                                  (7)
238           ¯
      where ld (X) is the general internal knowledge representation of the LLM, while ϵ̄(X) represents
239   the stochastic error term that can arise from both its imprecise numerical understanding and the
240   hallucinated outputs produced by the LLM. This reduces the effectiveness of using LLM agents to
241   model market participants, due to the possible unpredictable random errors. In addition, by using
242   an LLM agent to simulate investor decisions across multiple time-steps, these error terms can also
243   accumulate, which could further limit the performance of the overall simulation. This has been
244   previously observed in financial agent-based works (Zhang et al., 2024a), where the simulation
245   results were shown to diverge greatly across multiple runs, over a trading period of only 10 days.
246   To deal with this limitation, we take inspiration from Monte-Carlo simulations, which are commonly
247   used in finance (Metropolis & Ulam, 1949). We prompt the LLM over a range of prices in the input,
248   i.e., X1 , X2 , · · · , XM . For each price, we get a response ld (Xj ), which can contain stochastic noise
249   due to knowledge gaps and hallucinations. Across the price range, we get the aggregate function:
250                                                            M

253   where the numerical understanding l¯d (X) is assumed to be fixed across runs given a frozen LLM.

      from the LLM’s knowledge gaps and hallucinations would tend towards a fixed mean c, which gives
257
      us the general internal knowledge of the LLM, plus a constant error offset. Formally, we then have:

267   3.4   S IMULATION -BASED F INANCIAL F ORECASTING
268
269   While the previous steps show that it is possible to simulate the market demand response across
      multiple price points, they are still not sufficient to obtain the actual price forecasts of the asset.

270
      In economics, the price value of an entity is typically uncovered through the interaction between its
271   demand and supply (Mankiw & Taylor, 2020). To simulate this, we repeat the above steps using
272   a different prompt to simulate the sellers for the asset in the market ls (X). Similarly, aggregating
273   across a range of prices, we can also obtain the agent-simulated market supply response LS (X).
274
      Following economic theory, to obtain the price forecast for that day, we can then find the equilibrium
275
      point yeq in which the two curves intersect, i.e., LD (X) = LS (X). In addition, note that each
276
      demand and supply LLM responses also contain a constant error offset c (shown in Equation 10),
277   which could possibly lead to imprecisions in the price forecasts. Following this, we additionally
278   introduce a learnt linear function f to remove this. Our price forecast ŷ can then be obtained through:
279
280                                                ŷ = f (yeq ).                                        (11)
281   Through MMARP, this step then allows us to obtain price forecasts using LLM multi-agent simula-
282   tions, which differs from both traditional deep-learning methods (which only capture historical pat-
283   terns) or single LLM-based reasoning methods (which could be affected by stochastic noise terms).

      4       E XPERIMENTS
286
287
      We perform two categories of experiments on MMARP. In the first, we probe the LLM agents across
288
      different price values, contextual information and agent roles to study how closely their responses
289   match real-life demand and supply curves. Given that it is difficult to collect the real-life behavioral
290   curves of an actual market (which would requires all transactional data points), these experiments
291   are evaluated qualitatively, which is similar to many agent-based simulation works in Finance (Gao
292   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). In the second, we use these curves to produce market price
293   forecasts, which can then be evaluated quantitatively. This was not commonly done in previous
294   agent-related works (Zhang et al., 2024b; Yu et al., 2024a;b), which focused on profitability metrics.
295   Through these experiments, our work aims to answer two research questions:
296
297   1. Does the simulated response behavior of MMARP accurately replicate those in real-life markets?
298   2. How does MMARP perform against deep-learning and LLM methods in financial forecasting?
299
300   Datasets We evaluate MMARP over three datasets related to financial markets. The first dataset is
301   stock price data for 5 large-capital stocks from the U.S. stock market, namely AAPL, MSFT, TSLA,
302   WMT and XOM. The second dataset is exchange rate data for USD against 3 popular currencies,
303   namely EUR, JPY and GBP. The third dataset is commodity price data for 3 items across different
304   markets, namely Gasoline (Energy), Wheat (Agriculture) and Gold (Precious Metals). For the con-
305
      textual information, we provide economic and financial news collected from Reuters2 . In addition,
      tweets that mentioned each stock (Koa et al., 2024) were also provided for the stock dataset. The
306
      experiments were conducted over the period of year 2020-2022. We provide all new information
307
      between the previous and current market close times, to forecast the percentage change between the
308   close prices based on this context. More information on the dataset can be found in Appendix A.
309
310   Baselines For the forecasting experiments, we evaluate the performance of MMARP against base-
311
      lines from traditional deep-learning models and both general and financial-based LLMs. For deep-
      learning models, we explore the use of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks (Li et al.,
312
      2019), Attentive Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) (Sawhney et al., 2020) and Transformer-based mod-
313
      els (Yang et al., 2022). For LLMs, we explore the use of GPT-4o and Mistral-v0.3 for the general
314   models, and InvestLM (Yang et al., 2023b) and FinGPT (Yang et al., 2023a) for the models that are
315   fine-tuned for financial tasks. Descriptions of their implementation can be found in Appendix B.
316

366   Next, we observe the LLM response behaviors given different contextual information in the prompts.
367   In economics (Mankiw & Taylor, 2020), the positioning and slope of the demand curve are affected
368   by the non-price determinants and price elasticity of demand respectively. A decrease in demand

      6   C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we explored the use of LLMs to model real-life market participants to produce sim-
528   ulated price market forecasts. For this task, we highlighted two challenges: the stochastic actions
529   caused by non-rational individuals in the market, and the stochastic errors caused by the numerical
530   knowledge gaps and possible hallucinated outputs of LLMs. To handle these challenges, we propose
531
      a prompt design framework, MMARP, which simulate repetitive prompting using LLM-generated
      next-token weights and probe the LLM across a range of price inputs to obtain an aggregated re-
532
      sponse function that can represent actual market behavior. To verify the effectiveness of MMARP,
533
      we conducted extensive experiments across three market-based datasets on stock prices, exchange
534   rates and commodity prices. Empirically, we can observe that the LLM’s responses to individual
535   price points are not precise, which would limit their effectiveness if they are used directly in agent-
536   based financial models. However, we also find that its aggregate response function contains the
537   traits of actual market behavior, which could be exploited to produce realistic market simulations.
538   To evaluate this quantitatively, we then produce price forecasts using the intersection point of the re-
539   sponse functions of LLM agents in buyer and seller roles. We find that our simulated price forecasts
      show strong competitive results, and can also outperform other baseline models using the IC metric.

729   We evaluate the performance of MMARP against baselines from traditional deep-learning models
