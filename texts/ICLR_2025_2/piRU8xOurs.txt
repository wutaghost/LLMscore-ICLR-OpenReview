009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            While Large Language Models (LLMs) have dominated a wide diversity of natu-
012            ral language tasks, improving their capabilities on structured inputs such as graphs
013            remains an open challenge. We introduce T REE T OP, a fine-tuning framework for
014            LLMs that significantly improves their ability to reason over structural relation-
015            ships in multi-party discussion trees, e.g. on social media platforms. T REE T OP is
016            a novel set of 17 tasks designed to test the ability of LLMs to selectively focus on
017            the structure and/or content of conversation tree graphs. We find that LLMs fine-
018            tuned on T REE T OP significantly outperform all baseline models (including state-
019
               of-the-art GNNs) in multiple settings: generalizing to unseen T REE T OP tasks, and
               performance on downstream social media inference tasks (e.g. controversy detec-
020
               tion), including their challenging “early-detection” variants. T REE T OP charts new
021
               ground toward LLMs with generalized understanding of structured inputs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved state-of-the-art over an extremely vast landscape
      of tasks that can be cast as token sequence-to-sequence problems (Zhao et al., 2023b; Srivastava
028
      et al., 2022), partially through the combined effect of instruction fine-tuning (Wei et al., 2021) and
029
      scaling (Chung et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a). A current open challenge for LLM capabilities is
030   the handling of structured inputs (such as tables, e.g. Sui et al. (2024)), where the output depends
031   strictly on tokens distributed throughout the input according to a certain pattern.
032
033
      Recently, graph-structured inputs have emerged as one of the new frontiers in structured inputs for
      LLMs (Pan et al., 2024; Tsitsulin et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2024). Graphs are
034
      flexible data structures containing objects (nodes) and relationships (edges) for which a wide variety
035
      of models have been crafted to complex systems from social to biological networks (Wu et al., 2020;
036   Zhang et al., 2020). Enabling LLMs to perform tasks on graph-structured inputs has the potential
037   to considerably expand their application scope. Recent works have focused on improving LLM
038   performance on classical graph problems such as edge existence and counting (Fatemi et al., 2023;
039   Perozzi et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). These LLMs, through appropriate training
040   or prompting, have already been shown to outperform Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Chami
041   et al., 2022) on graph learning tasks like node classification and link prediction (Ye et al., 2024).
042   In this paper, we continue this line of research by developing LLM capabilities for conversation
043   graph inputs: graphs that encode online forum discussions by mapping replies to their parent com-
044   ments, stemming from the root post. Often called conversation “trees”, these graphs are ubiquitous
045   due to the proliferation of online social platforms, and they are the input to many important learning
046   tasks, such as misinformation detection, controversy detection, and trend prediction (Olteanu et al.,
047   2019; Zeng & Tang, 2021; Ji et al., 2021; Hessel & Lee, 2019). Furthermore, conversation trees are
048   directed, acyclic, and temporal, creating unique topological learning challenges, and distinguishing
049   them from the usually undirected, cyclic, and static graphs found in standard GNN benchmarks
050
      (Hu et al., 2020a). These unique properties have established conversation trees as a distinct sub-
      field, drawing approaches from GNNs (Xu et al., 2023) to LSTMs (Mukiri & Burra, 2023) and their
051
      hybrids (Patel et al., 2022).
052

      a multi-agent discussion. Each node is attributed with a natural-language utterance, an utterance

      Conversation Trees. The ubiquity of social media has created a global shift in information con-
121
      sumption and human discussion (Akram & Kumar, 2017). Information on social media is frequently
122   presented as a central post and its subsequent comments, creating a dynamic exchange between the
123   original poster and other users. We call the graph structures projected by these posts with their
124   corresponding comments as “conversation trees”, a sub-class of text-attributed graphs (Yan et al.,
125   2023). The study of conversation trees has been motivated by several tasks which are central themes
126   in social media data. Some canonical problems in this domain are information flow (Bakshy et al.,
127   2012), controversial post detection (Benslimane et al., 2021; Garimella et al., 2018), and fake news
128   detection (Lillie & Middelboe, 2019; Han et al., 2020), among others. Similarly, bias detection
129   (Chen et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022), fraud detection (Liu et al., 2023b; Zeng & Tang, 2021), event
130
      detection (Gao et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2021) and malicious behaviour detection (Wu et al., 2022; Dou,
      2022) are other active research areas. Early detection (Zhou et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020) in such
131
      cases is also critical, since it enables proactive interventions by social media platforms. Here, we
132
      propose methodology for enabling LLM competency across this entire space of problems.

135   3     M ETHODOLOGY
136
137   We now describe our core contribution: T REE T OP, an instruction-tuning framework for LLM gen-
138
      eralization on conversation tree learning problems. Figure 1 illustrates the high-level pipeline (see
      Appendix Figure 6 for a flow diagram). T REE T OP consists of a novel set of structural tasks on
139
      conversation trees to fine-tune an LLM. The purpose of these tasks is to improve the LLM’s ability
140
      to understand structure of conversation trees, allowing the model to follow both the content and the
141   flow of the discussion between the constituent users.

      3.2   S TRUCTURAL TASKS
177
178   The core idea of T REE T OP is to enhance LLM understanding of tree topology, and use this enhanced
179   understanding alongside their inherent language understanding for downstream applications. Thus,
180   to achieve this goal, we train LLMs on primitive topological tasks on conversation trees. Our ap-
181   proach is analogous to the use of graph motifs (Paranjape et al., 2017) for complex graph-based
182   computations – we believe that LLMs will be able to compose multiple topological primitives to-
183   gether to solve general tree inference problems.
184   The primary workhorse of our framework is thus a collection of 17 reasoning tasks over conversation
185   trees, illustrated in Figure 3. 12 of these tasks are used for fine-tuning, and the remaining used for
186   evaluation. We designed these tasks to enable/evaluate four different “proficiency” categories on
187   trees: (i) comment × comment tasks, that focus on the relationships between comments; (ii) user ×
188   user tasks, that focus on the relationships between users - which T REE T OP-tuned LLMs infer from
189   the conversation trees (refer Figure 2b); (iii) node characteristics tasks, that focus on the topological
190   properties of nodes; and (iv) tree characteristics tasks, that focus on the topological properties of the
191   entire conversation tree.

      3.2.1    S TRUCTURAL TASK C ORPUS C REATION
217
218   We created our structural task dataset using a small subset of the Pushshift Reddit data released
219   by the authors of the Pushshift platform (Baumgartner et al., 2020), available for download here.
220   This dataset contains all the posts and comments of Reddit that were posted in the month of April
221   2019. Our fine-tuning corpi are built from a random sample S of 100K conversation trees from this
222   dataset. For each task, we sample an equal number of positive and negative examples by mining
223
      the trees from S, described further in the next section. Using this approach, we are able to generate
      any number of labeled questions, for any task. As described further in Section 4, we fine-tune both
224
      closed-source and open-source LLMs using the T REE T OP framework. We create a corpus with 10k
225
      questions per task for these experiments; we provide multiple data ablation studies on this corpus in
226   Appendix I.

284

      We now describe experiments testing our primary hypothesis: that fine-tuning an LLM on primitive
286
      structural tasks over conversation trees enables generalization and profitable further fine-tuning on
287
      downstream tree problems. For brevity, we refer to LLMs fine-tuned with T REE T OP as LLM-TTs,
288   and others as “native” LLMs. We show that (i) LLM-TTs can decisively solve seen structural tasks
289   and generalize to unseen structural tasks, whereas native LLMs cannot; (ii) LLM-TTs further fine-
290   tuned on downstream, real-world tasks outperform both GNN baselines and native LLMs fine-tuned
291   on those same tasks; and (iii) representations learned by LLM-TTs are robust and explainable.
292
      Fine-tuning: Throughout this section, we analyze the impact of T REE T OP fine-tuning with
293
      G EMMA-2B, PaLM-Bison, and G EMINI -P RO models. Each LLM-TT model was fine-tuned on
294   10k randomly-sampled tasks from each of the 12 “fine-tuning tasks” shown in 3, holding out the
295   other 5 “unseen” tasks for out-of-distribution evaluation. We adopt the unified encoding presented
296   in 3.1, with hardware and hyperparameter details listed in Appendix F.

490   5   D ISCUSSION
491
492   In this paper, we introduced T REE T OP, a framework and suite of tasks for fine-tuning LLMs to per-
493   form well on topological reasoning tasks over conversation tree inputs. As we showed through our
494
      experiments, LLMs fine-tuned with T REE T OP generalize significantly better to out-of-distribution
      reasoning tasks, and have achieved state-of-the-art on a variety of downstream tasks in various social
495
      media domain applications. We now address limitations and future directions.
496
497   A limitation of our study is that the vast majority of our datasets, including the entirety of our
498   primitive structural tasks, are derived from Reddit data. Only one of our downstream task datasets
499   comes from a different platform (namely X). While this limits our paper’s empirical insights, we
500
      believe that our strong generalization results shows the promise of the T REE T OP framework to
      allow profitable fine-tuning of LLMs across other social platforms and even other platform types
501
      such as Q&A forums and collaborative editing (Daxenberger & Gurevych, 2012). We also note
502
      that models such as those fine-tuned on T REE T OP are often used for content-moderation (Roberts,
503   2017; Gillespie, 2020). A serious risk for such models centers around their potential misuse for
504   content over-moderation, thereby promoting echo-chambers and insularity of thought (Kumar et al.,
505   2024). Practitioners and platform designers should ensure that auto-moderation models trained with
506   T REE T OP (or any similar framework) are deployed responsibly, such as preferring “soft” moderation
507   tags (shown in Martel & Rand (2023) to be effective) over bans/takedowns, providing definitional
508   clarity to moderation policies, and offering transparency into moderation decisions, such as open-
509   sourcing logs (Macdonald & Vaughan, 2024; Singhal et al., 2023). Additionally, our experiments
510   show that T REE T OP models are performant at early-detection (68.9% accuracy at 4 hours), which
511
      can enable effective human-in-the-loop moderation systems to be designed (Lai et al., 2022).
512   As LLMs become better-able to handle multimodality (Zhang et al., 2024), an important next step in
513   this line of research is to allow large models to learn signals from the joint distribution of topology
514   and non-text modes such as images and videos. Future efforts in this direction should be inspired
515   and motivated by existing non-LLM work in multimodal cascade prediction (Xie et al., 2020; Zhang
516   et al., 2018), and by the importance of multimedia content in such applications (Nakamura et al.,
517
      2019). Aside from multi-modality, another interesting future direction would be designing primitive
      tasks defined over multiple trees, involving resolving same-user behavior and cross-dependencies,
518
      as a way to fine-tune models for more complex longitudinal effects. More generally, we hope that
519
      the concepts underlying T REE T OP– fine-tuning on structural tasks after pre-training on language
520   tasks – may be a re-usable recipe for teaching LLMs to become performant on structured data.

       B    S UPPLEMENTARY: R ELATED W ORK

974
       One of the most seminal advances that has spurred the current wave of LLM research has been
975    the attention mechanism, and the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). This led to de-
976    velopment of newer language encodings, notably BERT (Devlin et al., 2018). Radford et al. (2018)
977    popularized generative pre-training followed by discriminative fine-tuning for downstream tasks - an
978    approach that is highly leveraged in the application of LLMs. Raffel et al. (2020) introduced the T5
979    paradigm, a unified framework to convert all text-based language problems into a text-to-text format.
980    This work also inspired modeling graph problems into text prompts. Wei et al. (2021) provided an
981    instruction-tuning framework to fine-tune LLMs for improved for zero-shot performance, whereas
982    Brown et al. (2020) showed an improvement in LLM performance under few-shot training. Given
983
       this success of LLMs on language understanding, they have been subsequently been applied to other
       modalities as well, notably images (Alayrac et al., 2022), and now to the creation of multimodal
984
       models (Team et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024).
985
986    Similarly, graph neural networks (Scarselli et al., 2008) were introduced for machine learning on
987    graphs, and their expressive power has been extensively studied theoretically (Xu et al., 2018) and
988    across multiple benchmarks (Chien et al., 2021). Extensive surveys (Wu et al., 2020; Zhou et al.,
989
       2020; Chami et al., 2022) on GNNs demonstrate that they have been a prominent approach to reason
       over graphs. Graphs have been successfully applied to a variety of problem domains, for instance,
990
       cryptography (Priyadarsini, 2015), transportation networks (Bisen, 2017), quantum physics (Gilmer
991
       et al., 2020), chemistry (Gilmer et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2020b), and in multiple domains of everyday
992    use and technology (Kaundal, 2017). For example, knowledge graphs have seen widespread use and
993    adoption in retrieval and query-based systems (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018; Yasunaga et al., 2022a).
994
995
       Multiple recent studies have leveraged cross-pollination of ideas between GNNs and LLMs. Zhang
       et al. (2023b) look at solving spatio-temporal problems on dynamic graphs using large language
996
       models. Zhang (2023) notes that using tools can help an LLM reason over graph structures. On
997
       the other hand, Yasunaga et al. (2022b) view a text corpus as a graph of documents, and propose
998    LinkBERT, an LM pre-training method that leverages links between documents. Similarly, Zhu et al.
999    (2021) and Qin et al. (2023) propose methods that use GNNs to improve the performance of a text
1000   encoder. Alternatively, Duan et al. (2023) use LLM embeddings to initialize the GNN node features,
1001   and along with Perozzi et al. (2024), contribute to the area of work which integrates LLMs and
1002   GNNs at the level of tokens and embeddings. In a parallel line of work, language models have been
1003   used to improve GNN performance. Xu et al. (2023) use a BERT model to encode textual features
1004   on nodes whereas He et al. (2023) leverage explanations from an LLM to improve performance of
1005   downstream GNNs.
1006   Similarly, in the domain of social media analysis, multiple studies have been performed that discuss
1007   the impacts of consuming social media information on the individual and the population (Amedie,
1008   2015; Bail et al., 2018; Akram & Kumar, 2017; Olan et al., 2024). Conversation trees are central to
1009   how information is consumed in social media – there is a central post that an original poster makes,
1010   and there are comments in response to this post. Information flow on social media has motivated
1011   several lines of work (Lerman & Ghosh, 2010; Bakshy et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2023a; Pröllochs
1012
       & Feuerriegel, 2023; Hardy et al., 2023). Controversial post detection is of central importance to
       ensure that social media users are not exposed to potentially harmful content (Benslimane et al.,
1013
       2021; Madhu et al., 2023; Garimella et al., 2018; Qiu et al., 2019). Similarly, a growing body of
1014
       research is dedicated to addressing the issue of fake news, with studies exploring various detection
1015   methods and their effectiveness (Han et al., 2020; Shu et al., 2017; Nakamura et al., 2019; Lillie &
1016   Middelboe, 2019; Ma & Gao, 2020; Dou et al., 2021). Multilingual generalization (Li & Li, 2022;
1017   Wen, 2023; Li et al., 2022), bias detection (Olteanu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022),
1018   fraud detection (Liu et al., 2023b; Zeng & Tang, 2021; Chen et al., 2024a), event detection (Abagissa
1019   et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2021) and malicious behaviour detection (Wu et al., 2022;
1020   Toshevska et al., 2023; Dou, 2022) are other important problems in this area.

       J     E MBEDDINGS A NALYSIS
2107
2108
       We also analyzed the embeddings of PaLM-TT, with an intent to test whether the model actually
2109   understood the topology - we designed our experiment to evaluate whether the topological charac-
2110   teristics are directly captured in the learnt embeddings output by the model. We used PaLM-TT for
2111   this analysis, given it was not possible for us to obtain embeddings from G EMINI-based models.
2112
       Our experimental results show a clear presence of clustering in the embedding space for inputs that
2113
       conform to similar topologies. In fact, a classifier trained purely on topological embeddings obtains
2114
       a 77% accuracy on the multi-class topology classification task using a simple logistical regression
2115   classifier. We now describe the experimental setup that leads to these conclusions.
2116

2119   In this section, we describe our experimental setup. The goal of these experiments is to show that
2120   embeddings learnt by the PaLM-TT model show clustering — i.e. topologies that are similar,
2121   by some independent analytical metric, cluster together. Towards this goal, we chose 5 different
2122   topologies, which are shown in Figure 11. These five topologies are chosen so that they vary in their
2123   depth and breadth. The hypothesis at the outset is to show that embeddings cluster by topology.
2124   We first begin by presenting the statistics of our topologies in Table 31. While the topologies are
2125   illustrated in Figure 11, we list them out in our table with an equivalent depth-first crawl of the tree:

       We now describe the method by which we obtain the embeddings. The embedding is obtained by
2154   doing a forward pass of the PCT encoding in PaLM-TT for each conversation tree in our dataset.

2157   embedding as E(PCT). Similarly, we forward pass the Post Comments encoding (refer Ap-
2158   pendix C.2) from the same conversation tree, and obtain E(PC). The embedding dimension for
2159   each token in PaLM-TT (as in PaLM-Bison) is 4096. Now we are left with the task of obtaining
