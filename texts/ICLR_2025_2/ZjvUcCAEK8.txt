009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Prevalent vectorized map construction pipelines predominantly follow an end-to-
012            end DETR-based paradigm. While these methods have achieved significant ad-
013            vancements, they are limited by their reliance on data from a single ego vehicle,
014            which restricts their effectiveness and can lead to perceptual uncertainty in han-
015            dling complex environmental scenarios. To address this limitation, we introduce
016            a novel framework: Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle Vectorized Map Construc-
017            tion (UMVMap). This framework effectively mitigates uncertainties by lever-
018            aging relevant non-ego information. UMVMap comprises two essential compo-
019
               nents: the Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle Vectorized Map Construction Net-
               work (UMVMap-Net), which optimally integrates data from multiple vehicles,
020
               and the Uncertainty-aware Non-ego Vehicle Selection (UNVS) strategy, which
021
               identifies and incorporates the most informative non-ego data to minimize un-
022            certainty. Comprehensive evaluations on the nuScenes dataset demonstrate that
023            UMVMap significantly outperforms the single-vehicle MapTRv2 baseline by a
024            margin of 9.1% and 9.9% respectively on the full and partial validation sets, with
025            each of its components proving to be both effective and robust.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Vectorized maps are crucial for autonomous driving Antonello et al. (2017); Gao et al. (2020),
031   providing abundant road details and rich semantic information that support tasks such as self-
032   localization Levinson et al. (2007) and path planning Da & Zhang (2022). Traditionally, vectorized
033   maps are generated using simultaneous localization and mapping (SLAM)-based techniques Shan
034   & Englot (2018); Shan et al. (2020), which require subsequent refinement by human operators and
035   incur high maintenance costs. In recent years, numerous approaches Liao et al. (2022); Ding et al.
036   (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b) have adopted end-to-end learning-based methods for vectorized map
037   construction, often utilizing a DETR-based architecture Carion et al. (2020).
038   Despite their advancements, these methods are constrained by relying solely on the perspective of a
039   single ego vehicle, which limits their ability to handle complex environmental conditions. As illus-
040   trated in Fig. 1 (a), in scenarios involving occlusion, single-vehicle approaches, such as MapTRv2
041   Liao et al. (2023), tend to produce ambiguous perceptions in the occluded areas, as these regions are
042   beyond the visibility of the ego vehicle. Additionally, the map element perception of single-vehicle
043   models becomes increasingly uncertain as the distance from the ego vehicle grows, often leading
044   to the omission of distant map elements in the constructed map. To illustrate this, we conducted a
045
      quantitative analysis by evaluating the performance of the single-vehicle method across a range of
      perception distances. As shown in Fig. 2 (a), the single-vehicle method exhibits a noticeable decline
046
      in performance as the perception range increases. In contrast, the multi-vehicle paradigm demon-
047
      strates a much smoother performance curve, suggesting that integrating auxiliary information from
048   non-ego perspectives can effectively mitigate the uncertainties inherent in the ego vehicle’s perspec-
049   tive. Also, it is intuitive that by observing from varied non-ego perspectives, the uncertainty of the
050   occluded areas can be effectively reduced. Furthermore, Fig. 2 (b) presents histograms depicting the
051   number of ego vehicles with varied non-ego vehicle numbers at various distance ranges. It is evident
052   that across all distance intervals, the majority of ego vehicles are able to obtain support from more
053   than 10 non-ego vehicles, which underscores the feasibility of leveraging information from non-ego
      vehicles as a supplementary aid.

082   To this end, we propose a novel Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle vectorized Map Construction
083   (UMVMap) framework, which is capable of addressing uncertain details with the aid of proper
084   non-ego information. The UMVMap consists of two key components: the Uncertainty-aware Multi-
085   Vehicle Vectorized Map Construction Network (UMVMap-Net), which optimally utilizes multi-
086
      vehicle data, and the Uncertainty-aware Non-ego Vehicle Selection (UNVS) strategy, which identi-
      fies the most beneficial non-ego data to reduce uncertainty. Specifically, UMVMap-Net is composed
087
      of an Uncertainty-guided Multi-Vehicle Information Integrator (UMVII) and a Segmentation Prior
088
      Guided Map Decoder (SPMDec). UMVII first integrates multi-vehicle data, while SPMDec de-
089   codes this integrated information to produce the vectorized map. Additionally, the UNVS strategy
090   is designed to select non-ego vehicles that provide complementary information, particularly in areas
091   where the ego vehicle’s perception is highly uncertain.
092

093

094
      TRv2 baseline by a margin of 9.1% and 9.9% respectively on the full and partial validation sets.
095   In addition, a comprehensive ablation analysis is carried out and the promising results prove the
096   effectiveness and robustness of our proposed key components within UMVMap.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
138
139
      Vectorized Map Construction. Vectorized maps are conventionally generated using simultane-
      ous localization and mapping (SLAM)-based techniques Shan & Englot (2018); Shan et al. (2020),
140
      and then refined through human intervention, which suffers from high maintenance expenses. In
141
      recent years, there has been a surge of interest in utilizing onboard sensors for vectorized map con-
142   struction. HDMapNet Li et al. (2022) generates Bird’s-Eye-View (BEV) semantic segmentations
143   and then performs a heuristic, time-consuming post-processing step to produce vectorized map in-
144   stances. Beyond this, VectorMapNet Liu et al. (2023) introduces the first end-to-end vectorized map
145   construction framework, employing auto-regression to sequentially predict points. Subsequently, an
146   increasing number of recent approaches Liao et al. (2022); Ding et al. (2023); Liao et al. (2023);
147   Zhang et al. (2024b) have adopted end-to-end methods for vectorized map construction, with many
148   leveraging a DETR-based architecture Carion et al. (2020). However, these methods are constrained
149   by their reliance on a single input and struggle to manage challenging environmental conditions,
150
      such as occlusions or adverse weather. StreamMapNet Yuan et al. (2024) takes a significant stride
      forward by incorporating temporal information through a streaming strategy, aiming to enhance the
151
      temporal consistency and quality of vectorized maps. Nevertheless, in some hard cases, construct-
152
      ing a high-quality vectorized map from the perspective of a single vehicle remains a formidable
153   task. To this end, in this paper, we propose to mitigate uncertainties by leveraging relevant non-ego
154   information, and collaboratively constructing superior vectorized maps.
155
156   Collaborative Perception for Autonomous Driving. Collaborative perception Han et al. (2023)
157   in intelligent transportation systems, which integrates information from multiple vehicles to enhance
158   environmental understanding, has been researched for over a decade despite being limited by the
159   lack of effective techniques and large-scale datasets Rauch et al. (2012); Kim et al. (2014). Existing
160   collaborative perception methods, based on the message-sharing strategy, can be broadly classified
161   into early (data-level) Chen et al. (2019), intermediate (feature-level) Xu et al. (2022); Yang et al.
      (2023) and late (object-level) Xu et al. (2023) ones. Among these, intermediate collaboration is


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      preferred for its optimal balance between performance and transmission bandwidth. In this case, the
163   concept of collaborative perception has been adopted in various tasks related to autonomous driving.
164   CoCa3D Hu et al. (2023) enhances camera detection capabilities through multi-agent collaboration,
165   leading to more comprehensive 3D detection. CoHFF Song et al. (2024) enhances camera-based
166   semantic occupancy prediction through collaborative perception, achieving greater accuracy and
167   comprehensiveness. This improvement is driven by the hybrid fusion of semantic and occupancy
168   features, along with the shared compressed orthogonal attention features between vehicles. By con-
169   structing collaborative neural rendering field representations to recover failed perceptual messages
170   sent by multiple agents, RCDN Wang et al. (2024) achieves a robust camera-insensitivity collabora-
171
      tive perception for 3D Neural modeling. Aside from prior studies, we are pioneering the exploration
      of vehicle-to-vehicle collaborative perception in vectorized map construction.

      3.2   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
187
188   The overall UMVMap framework includes an Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle vectorized Map
189   Construction Network (UMVMap-Net) and an Uncertainty-aware Non-ego Vehicle Selection Strat-
190   egy. UMVMap-Net is depicted in Fig. 3, which is primarily composed of an Uncertainty-guided
191   Multi-Vehicle Information Integrator (UMVII) and a Segmentation Prior Guided Map Decoder (SP-
192   MDec). First, UMVII is employed to properly integrate information from multi-vehicle data. Subse-
193   quently, SPMDec decodes the integrated information from UMVII to construct the vectorized map.
194   Furthermore, aside from properly integrating multi-vehicle information, selecting non-ego vehicles
195   that provide complementary information in regions where the ego vehicle’s perception is highly un-
196
      certain can also significantly enhance the quality of the vectorized map construction. To achieve this,
      we further design the Uncertainty-aware Non-ego Vehicle Selection Strategy (illustrated in Fig. 4).

332   To optimize the proposed UMVMap, we follow the optimization setting of MapTRv2 Liao et al.
333   (2023), which utilizes a basic one-to-one set prediction loss and two auxiliary losses including a
334   one-to-many set prediction loss and a dense prediction loss. To be specific, the basic one-to-one set
335   prediction loss is composed of three parts, including a classification loss, a point-to-point loss and
336   an edge direction loss:

338
339   where λc , λp and λd are the weights for balancing different loss terms. The classification loss is
340   implemented as a Focal Loss. The point-to-point loss is defined as the Manhattan distance computed
341   between each assigned point pair after hierarchical bipartite matching. The edge direction loss is
342
      designed as the cosine similarity of each pair of predicted edge and ground-truth edge. The one-to-
      many set prediction loss Lone2many is also adopted. Besides, dense prediction loss Ldense is utilized
343
      to further leverage semantic and geometric information, which consists of a depth prediction loss, a

      BEV segmentation loss and a PV segmentation loss. Note that Ldense is applied on B̂e , {Bjf }N     n

354   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
355
356   Datasets. We evaluate our proposed method using the popular and large-scale nuScenes Caesar
357   et al. (2020) dataset. The nuScenes dataset includes 2D city-level global vectorized maps and 1,000
358
      scenes, each approximately 20 seconds long. Key samples are annotated at 2Hz, with each sample
      providing RGB images from six cameras, covering a 360° horizontal field of view around the ego-
359
      vehicle. The dataset is split into 28K frames for training and 6K frames for validation. For all of
360
      the experiments on nuScenes, we utilize the whole validation set as Full Validation Set (with 6019
361   samples), while utilizing the validation samples with available non-ego vehicles as Partial Validation
362   Sets (with 2667 samples).
363
364
      Metrics. We conduct evaluation with Chamfer distance based Average Precision (AP) following
365
      previous mainstream works Li et al. (2022); Liao et al. (2023) for fair comparisons. The AP is
366   calculated under the average of three Chamfer distance thresholds of 0.5, 1.0, and 1.5 meters. The
367   perception ranges are [-15.0m, 15.0m] for the X-axis and [-30.0m, 30.0m] for the Y-axis. Three
368   types of map instances are selected for HD map construction, including pedestrian crossing, lane

      5       C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this study, we present UMVMap, an Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle Vectorized Map Con-
524
      struction framework designed to effectively mitigate uncertainties in vectorized map construction
525
      by leveraging relevant non-ego vehicle information. UMVMap is composed of two key compo-
526   nents: the Uncertainty-aware Multi-Vehicle Vectorized Map Construction Network (UMVMap-
527   Net), which optimally integrates data from multiple vehicles, and the Uncertainty-aware Non-ego
528   Vehicle Selection (UNVS) strategy, which identifies and incorporates the most informative non-
529   ego data to minimize uncertainty. Comprehensive evaluations conducted on the nuScenes dataset
530   demonstrate that UMVMap significantly outperforms the single-vehicle MapTRv2 baseline by a
531   margin of 9.1% and 9.9% respectively on the full and partial validation sets, with its components
532   showing notable effectiveness and robustness. Looking ahead, vehicle-to-vehicle collaborative per-
533   ception for vectorized map construction remains a promising direction for future exploration, with
534
      substantial potential for further advancements in autonomous driving.
