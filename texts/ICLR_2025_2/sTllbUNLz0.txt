011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Interpretable reinforcement learning (RL) seeks to create agents that are efficient,
014            transparent, and understandable to the populations that they impact. A signifi-
015
               cant gap in current approaches is the underutilization of human feedback, which
               is typically employed only for post-hoc evaluation. We propose to center the
016
               needs of end users by incorporating the feedback that would be obtained in a
017
               user study directly into the training of interpretable RL algorithms. Our approach
018            involves preference learning, where we learn preferences over high-level features
019            that are not directly optimizable during the RL training process. We introduce
020            an evolutionary algorithm that leverages user feedback to guide training toward
021            interpretable decision-tree policies that are better aligned with human preferences.
022            We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method through experiments using syn-
023            thetic preference data. Our results show an improvement in preference alignment
024            compared to baselines, yielding policies that are more aligned with underlying
025            user preferences but does so with sample efficiency in the number of user queries,
026
               thereby decreasing the burden on the user in providing such data.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Advancements in reinforcement learning (RL) have resulted in autonomous agents capable of per-
031   forming tasks with remarkable ability in many applications, such as robotics (Mahmood et al., 2018),
032   games (Vinyals et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2023), vehicular control (Yan et al., 2022), and more.
033   However, these achievements often come at the cost of interpretability: the neural networks governing
034   the agents’ decision-making processes are opaque, hindering understanding of their workings (Räuker
035   et al., 2023). This opacity not only hinders people’s ability to decide to trust and collaborate with
036   these agents but also poses significant challenges in diagnosing and rectifying their faults.
037   Interpretable RL seeks to address this challenge by generating understandable representations of
038   these decision-making processes (Glanois et al., 2021). However, existing techniques often do not
039   account for user needs or preferences. From the perspective of human-computer interaction, adopting
040   a user-centered approach is fundamental for ensuring that user needs are met (Lieberman et al., 2006).
041   Recognizing this, researchers in the interpretable machine learning field have increasingly advocated
042   for integrating human-centered methodologies into both the design (Schoonderwoerd et al., 2021)
043   and evaluation (Boyd-Graber et al., 2022; Colin et al., 2022) of interpretable models.
044   In interpretable RL, however, the goal of satisfying end user desires is, in practice, often not reached.
045   Evaluations of explanation quality and utility are frequently performed only after training (Narayanan
046   et al., 2022), so insights garnered from these evaluations are not readily integrated back into the model
047   itself. We envision utilizing human feedback during training to find RL policies that are better aligned
048   with the preferences and desired tasks of users. In this work, we focus on constructing interpretable
049   decision tree (DT) policies (McCallum, 1996; Pyeatt et al., 2001; Gupta et al., 2015) instead of
050
      opaque neural network policies. DTs (Quinlan, 1996) are a popular method for interpretable machine
      learning due to the consensus that they are human-understandable (Lipton, 2018; Rudin, 2019).
051
052   We propose to center user preferences through a novel learning approach that incorporates human
053   feedback into the training process of interpretable RL algorithms. The algorithm iteratively refines its
      estimate of the underlying user preferences over DT policies and constructs new candidate policies to

078   Contributions In this work, we make the following contributions. We propose “Preference Aligned
079   Selection of Trees via Evolutionary Learning” (PASTEL), the first algorithm to leverage preference
080   feedback for interpretable RL training. This algorithm consists of three main components. First, we
081   introduce a simple but effective evolutionary algorithm to guide training toward DT policies that
082
      are better tailored toward preferences of end users. Second, we maintain a population of preference
      estimates to help guide the selection of DT policies used for eliciting user feedback. Third, we improve
083
      efficiency by pruning the set of candidate DT policies for querying, maintaining an approximation of
084
      the Pareto frontier. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method through experiments on two RL
085   environments using synthetic but empirically justified preference data. We show that our approach
086   not only yields policies that are more aligned with user preferences but is also more sample efficient
087   in the number of user queries, decreasing the burden on human users. By bridging the gap between
088   training RL agents and evaluating their explanations, we believe our work opens new avenues for
089   developing more interpretable, user-centered RL systems. In summary, we contribute the following:
090
091          • A framework formalizing the idea of preference-based interpretable reinforcement learning,
092          • PASTEL, the first algorithm to leverage preference feedback to train interpretable RL
093            policies, and
094          • An evaluation of PASTEL showing that it can produce better preference-aligned DT policies
095            compared with standard techniques for generating interpretable DT policies.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
      Interpretable Machine Learning. Interpretable machine learning (ML) has become increasingly
100
      important as ML systems are deployed for more complex and sensitive use cases (Prashanth et al.,
101
      2016; Chebotar et al., 2021). The need for interpretability arises from the desire to understand,
102   appropriately trust, and effectively manage these systems. The most relevant line of work to ours is
103   in interpretable RL. Although many structures have been proposed to use in lieu of neural networks
104   for RL policies, including domain-specific programming languages (Verma et al., 2018) and human-
105   friendly prototypes (Kenny et al., 2022), we focus on DT policies due to the general consensus
106   that they are human-understandable (Lipton, 2018; Rudin, 2019). One challenge with DTs is their
107   discrete structure is not immediately amenable to gradient-based training. Although work has applied
      gradient-based training to DT policies by introducing non-linearities in the splits (Silva et al., 2020),


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the conversion of the soft tree (Irsoy et al., 2012) to a standard DT results in severe performance
109   loss, making this approach inapplicable in our setting. Moreover, prior work trains DT policies only
110   from the perspective of reward maximization (Bastani et al., 2018; Topin et al., 2021) under a fixed
111   interpretability objective (typically, depth minimization). Our approach not only focuses on obtaining
112   reward-maximizing DT policies but also aligning them with preferences. While our emphasis is on
113   interpretability, we note that our framework also readily lends itself to explanation generation.
114
      Learning from Human Feedback. There is growing interest in the area of learning from human
115   feedback (Griffith et al., 2013; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016; Stiennon et al., 2020) due to the difficulty
116   of specifying a concrete objective that captures the full extent of what people want ML algorithms
117   to do. Our work can be viewed as a general framework for incorporating human feedback into
118   interpretable machine learning. In contrast, previous work often treats the interpretability objective
119   as fixed (e.g., by specifying a maximum tree depth or known weight on the depth (Custode and
120   Iacca, 2023)) or only as a way to evaluate the resulting explanations. This prior knowledge can be
121   used to set an initial guess within our model, and the evaluation metrics can be used along with
122   our approach. Furthermore, we aim for a practical algorithm that demonstrates sample efficiency in
123
      gathering preferences, aligning with prior work that emphasizes the importance of minimizing user
      effort in interactive machine learning systems (Cakmak and Thomaz, 2012; Wilson et al., 2012).
124
125   Preference Elicitation. Preference elicitation and learning have been extensively studied across
126   various disciplines, addressing the need to understand preferences over a set of alternatives (Chajewska
127   et al., 2000; Conen and Sandholm, 2001; Loepp et al., 2014; Weernink et al., 2014; Li et al., 2023).
128   Approaches in this field often focus on ranking elicitation by employing methods such as pairwise
129
      comparisons (Eric et al., 2007; Lu and Boutilier, 2011a; Branke et al., 2017) or asking individuals
      to rank their top choices among all or a subset of alternatives (Lu and Boutilier, 2011b; Soufiani
130
      et al., 2013; Drummond and Boutilier, 2014; Zhao et al., 2018). These methods typically result in the
131
      elicitation of full rankings or partial orders for agents or groups. Our work, while building on these
132   works on preference elicitation, shifts the focus from eliciting rankings to learning the underlying
133   preference model parameters. Our approach shares similarities with Bayesian Experimental Design
134   (Chaloner and Verdinelli, 1995; Rainforth et al., 2024), where the objective is to learn information
135   about the preference model ✓, and aims to explore the integration of preference elicitation and
136   learning, with an emphasis on aligning models with the preferences of downstream users.

481   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
482
483   We proposed to incorporate user feedback directly into the explanation-generation process. This
484   approach provides a proof of concept that incorporating the preferences of users into the process
485   yields explanations that are better aligned with users goals and desires. By leveraging human
      feedback during training, our approach addresses a critical gap in existing interpretable RL methods,


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      which often fail to consider user needs in real-time. Through experiments in two environments,
487   we demonstrated that our method not only produces DT policies that are better aligned with the
488   underlying preferences but also does so efficiently, in 40 queries or fewer. Our work lays the
489   groundwork for future research on developing more user-centered, interpretable RL systems that
490   prioritize alignment with human expectations.
491
      Limitations and Future Work. One limitation of our work is the assumption of static user prefer-
492
      ences, which may not accurately reflect the dynamic nature of preferences in real-world applications.
493   To address this, future work could explore techniques for modeling and adapting to evolving user
494   preferences over time, allowing the system to update and refine policies as user goals shift. Another
495   limitation is the use of synthetic data for evaluation, which may not fully capture the diversity of
496   real-world user perspectives. In future studies, incorporating a wider range of user input and adopting
497   an application-grounded evaluation approach (Doshi-Velez and Kim, 2017) could provide more
498   meaningful insights, particularly by leveraging real-world data to model the latent factors that influ-
499   ence user preferences. This would enable more robust, user-centered policy generation in practical
500   settings.
