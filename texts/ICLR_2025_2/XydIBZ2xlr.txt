009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we present DM-Calib, a diffusion-based approach for estimating pin-
012
               hole camera intrinsic parameters from a single input image. Monocular camera
013
               calibration is essential for many 3D vision tasks. However, most existing methods
014            depend on handcrafted assumptions or are constrained by limited training data, re-
015            sulting in poor generalization across diverse real-world images. Recent advance-
016            ments in stable diffusion models, trained on massive data, have shown the ability
017            to generate high-quality images with varied characteristics. Emerging evidence in-
018            dicates that these models implicitly capture the relationship between camera focal
019            length and image content. Building on this insight, we explore how to leverage
020            the powerful priors of diffusion models for monocular pinhole camera calibration.
021
               Specifically, we introduce a new image-based representation, termed Camera Im-
               age, which losslessly encodes the numerical camera intrinsics and integrates seam-
022
               lessly with the diffusion framework. Using this representation, we reformulate the
023            problem of estimating camera intrinsics as the generation of a dense Camera Im-
024            age conditioned on an input image. By fine-tuning a stable diffusion model to gen-
025            erate a Camera Image from a single RGB input, we can extract camera intrinsics
026            via a RANSAC operation. We further demonstrate that our monocular calibration
027            method enhances performance across various 3D tasks, including zero-shot metric
028            depth estimation, 3D metrology, pose estimation and sparse-view reconstruction.
029            Extensive experiments on multiple public datasets show that our approach signifi-
030            cantly outperforms baselines and provides broad benefits to 3D vision tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Camera calibration is a foundational task in 3D computer vision, critical for numerous applications
036   such as camera pose estimation (Schönberger & Frahm, 2016), 3D reconstruction (Seitz et al., 2006),
037   and zero-shot metric depth estimation (Yin et al., 2023). Traditional methods primarily focus on
038   multi-view calibration, typically involving multiple images of fixed intrinsics (Pollefeys & Gool,
039   1997) or multiple images of checkerboard patterns (Zhang, 2000). However, these methods depend
040   heavily on dense multi-view images with sufficient overlap, making them cumbersome and often
041   impractical for sparse-view or even monocular setups. Consequently, monocular camera calibration
042   has garnered significant research interest.
043   Monocular camera calibration is inherently an ill-posed problem, requiring additional information
044   to address it. Traditional approaches have attempted to incorporate handcrafted knowledge, such
045   as the gravity direction (Veicht et al., 2024), Manhattan World constraints (Liu & Cui, 2023), and
046   human face priors (Hu et al., 2023). However, these handcrafted insights often fail to generalize
047   effectively across diverse real-world scenarios. To overcome these limitations, recent studies (Zhu
048
      et al., 2023) recast monocular camera calibration as a learning-based regression problem, leveraging
      a single image to directly infer its intrinsic parameters.
049
050   While learning-based methods benefit from data-driven knowledge, outperforming traditional ap-
051   proaches, they are constrained by the limited availability of public datasets. As a result, these meth-
052   ods tend to overfit on training data and exhibit poor generalization to unseen scenarios. This limi-
053   tation raises a critical question: What kind of knowledge is necessary to develop a robust camera
      calibration method that exhibits strong generalization capabilities?

      2.1    M ONOCULAR C AMERA C ALIBRATION
097
098   Calibrating camera intrinsics, also known as self-calibration, is one of the most fundamental problems
099   in geometric computer vision. Geometric methods typically assume multiple input images with
100   the same intrinsic matrix. These methods can be broadly classified as direct methods and stratified
101   methods. Direct methods (Zeller & Faugeras, 1996; Hartley, 1997; Luong & Faugeras, 1997) solve
102   the Kruppa’s equation (Gallego et al., 2018) to obtain camera intrinsic parameters, which are generally
103   more fragile to noises. In comparison, stratified methods (Hartley, 1993; Triggs, 1997; Pollefeys &
104
      Gool, 1997) estimate camera intrinsics from a projective reconstruction by gradually recovering the
      affine and Euclidean structures such as the plane at infinity and absolute quadrics.
105
106   Traditional monocular camera calibration methods typically assume specific geometric structures
107   to estimate intrinsics. For example, with Manhattan World assumption (Coughlan & Yuille, 1999),
      camera intrinsics can be inferred from vanishing points (Lee et al., 2013; Schindler et al., 2004;


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Wildenauer & Hanbury, 2012), or by jointly estimating the horizon line (Zhai et al., 2016; Simon
109   et al., 2018). Other approaches rely on calibration objects such as checkerboards (Zhang, 2000), line
110   segments (Zhang et al., 2016), spheres (Zhang et al., 2007), pyramid frustums (Jiang et al., 2009),
111   or even human faces (Hu et al., 2023; Liu & Cui, 2023). Despite producing satisfactory results,
112   these methods are constrained by their reliance on specific objects, which limits their applicability in
113   unconstrained, real-world scenarios. Recently, Zhu et al. (2023) introduced incident maps to regress
114   camera intrinsics, enabling the detection of geometric manipulations like cropping.
115   Apart from the above geometry-based methods, some works attempt to leverage the strong generative
116   models for camera calibration. To the best of our knowledge, the approach by He et al. (2024) is
117   the only existing method that formulates camera intrinsic estimation as a generative task, leveraging
118   incident maps with diffusion models. Although this method outperforms non-diffusion-based methods
119   such as Zhu et al. (2023) in unconstrained settings, we argue that the full potential of diffusion
120   models remains under-utilized and that its performance hinges on joint training with depth images. In
121   contrast, our approach introduces a camera representation that is more inherently compatible with
122
      diffusion models, thereby eliminating the need to generate non-textured incident maps or rely on the
      joint training of additional geometric information.

      2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS IN 3D TASKS
126   Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have emerged as powerful tools
127   across various domains, particularly in the field of computer vision, where Text-to-Image diffusion
128   models (Saharia et al., 2022b) and their extensions (Poole et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022;
129   Saharia et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023) have garnered significant attention. Compared to GAN-
130   based approaches (Bhattad et al., 2024), several studies have highlighted the advantages of diffusion
131   models, especially when used as prior geometric cues in 3D tasks. Notable examples include
132   view synthesis (Liu et al., 2023a; Long et al., 2024), camera calibration (He et al., 2024), normal
133
      estimation (Ye et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b), and depth estimation (Fu et al., 2024; Ke et al., 2024).
      In this work, we focus on leveraging diffusion models for camera intrinsic estimation, which serves as
134
      a foundation for enhancing a series of downstream tasks, such as monocular metric depth estimation,
135
      pose estimation, and sparse-view reconstruction.
136
137
      2.3   M ONOCULAR D EPTH E STIMATION
138
139   For depth estimation, several works (Ke et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024b) have shown that
140   diffusion models can be fine-tuned to predict affine-invariant depth, achieving not only finer detail
141   but also more accurate estimates than traditional methods (Ranftl et al., 2020; Yin et al., 2021; Yang
142   et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2022). Recent studies (Xu et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024; Garcia et al., 2024)
143   have highlighted that diffusion models can serve as pre-trained networks for deterministic, one-step
144   inference. However, none of the current approaches leverage pre-trained diffusion models for metric
145
      depth prediction. Existing zero-shot monocular metric depth estimation methods, such as (Bhat et al.,
      2023; Yin et al., 2023; Piccinelli et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024a), have demonstrated accurate results,
146
      yet they still face challenges in capturing geometric details and foreground-background relationships,
147
      particularly in outdoor environments. Moreover, these methods usually employ contrastive learning
148   pretrained encoders (i.e., DINO (Caron et al., 2021)) or classification pretrained encoders (Deng et al.,
149   2009), and randomly initialize the decoder, which are trained on fewer images compared to diffusion
150   models. Building upon this, we extend diffusion models to metric depth estimation. To the best of
151   our knowledge, our approach is the first to employ pre-trained diffusion models for metric depth
152   estimation, achieving finer geometric detail and competitive performance across diverse benchmarks.

177        3.1                                    P RELIMINARIES ON D IFFUSION M ODEL
178
179     Diffusion models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020) learn to model a data distribution pdata (x) by progressively
180
        denoising a noise variable that is initially sampled from a normal distribution. Recognizing the
        efficiency issue associated with generating high-resolution images, Rombach et al. (2022) introduced
181
        latent diffusion models (LDMs), which operate the diffusion process in the latent space of a pretrained
182     variational autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) with an encoder E and a decoder D.
183
184     For any given input image x, the corresponding latent code is generated by the VAE encoder: z =
185
        E(x). The forward diffusion process incrementally adds noise to these latents following zt := αt z +
        σt ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, I), and αt and σt are parameters defined by the noise schedule, with t ∼ pt
186
        representing the time step within the diffusion schedule. The denoising network, denoted as ϵθ , aims
187
        to reverse the diffusion process to recover the noise-free latent code ẑ from the final noisy latent code
188     zT . This is achieved by predicting the noise component ϵθ (zt , t) at each diffusion step. The original
189     image x is then reconstructed from this denoised latent code using the VAE decoder as x̂ = D(ẑ).
190     The whole diffusion model    is optimized by    minimizing the denoising score matching objective,
191     defined as follows: Ez,ϵ,t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , t)∥22 . This objective measures the squared Euclidean distance
192     between the actual noise ϵ and the predicted noise. By minimizing this objective, the denoising
193     network learns to accurately estimate the noise, thereby effectively reversing the diffusion process
194     and reconstructing the original data distribution.

196        3.2                                    C AMERA I MAGE R EPRESENTATION
197
         Monocular camera calibration aims to recover the camera intrinsics matrix K, which typically
198
         composed of four parameters: fx , fy , cx , and cy , corresponding to the focal lengths and the optical
199      center coordinates along the x-axis and y-axis, respectively. This numeric-based representation,
200      however, does not align well with image-based diffusion models, which are primarily designed for
201      generating spatial images. The challenge, therefore, becomes how to effectively leverage powerful
202      pretrained SD models to retrieve implicit camera information.
203
       To address this challenge, we propose a novel image-based representation, called “Camera Image”,
204
       which encodes the camera intrinsic parameters into a 3-channel color image (refer to Fig. 3 for visual
205    representation). This representation seamless integrates with existing diffusion models with minimal
206    architecture modifications. We reformulate the camera intrinsics into a 2-channel pseudo-spherical
207    representation defined by azimuth θ and elevation φ. This two-channel formulation enables us to
208    explore the choice of the third channel to prevent mode collapse. Given that VAE encoders typically
209    take a three-channel image as input, it is crucial to determine how to effectively fill the third channel.
210    Simply duplicating one of the existing channels θ, φ or adding a constant value channel leads to
211    suboptimal results, as detailed in Fig. 2. To enhance the camera representation, we propose a simple
212
       yet effective solution by incorporating the grayscale image g of the input x into the dense camera
       representation, reducing the domain gap between the input images and those generated by diffusion
213
       models. Consequently, our proposed camera image c is defined as follows,

      Monocular Metric Depth Estimation. To predict metric depth from a single image, the model
300
      must possess a deep understanding of the image perspective and estimate accurate intrinsic parameters
301   of the camera. By leveraging the proposed camera calibration method, we repurpose diffusion-based
302   image generators for accurate metric depth estimation. Previous works (Ke et al., 2024; Fu et al.,
303   2024) mainly investigate affine-invariant depth estimation. However, we find the VAE decoder D can
304   only predict values in limited range, thus limiting the performance of metric depth estimation. To fix
305   this issue, we formulate stochastic multi-step denoise SD model as one-step deterministic forward
306   process as shown in Fig. 5. Specifically, we first encode RGB image and our designed camera image
307   ĉ via VAE encoder into latent space, noting that no noise is added to both of the latent features. Then,
308
      the latent features are sent to the UNet to predict the latent depth features ẑd , and the final depth
309   predictions dˆ are obtained via the decoder of the VAE. Note that both U-Net U and the VAE decoder
310
      D are trained to allow predictions in any range. Given the depth labels d with its sparse mask M , the
      training loss is given by:

      Sparse-View 3D Reconstruction & Pose Estimation. Capturing sparse-view images with varying

503   4.5      A BLATION S TUDY
504
505   Ablation Study on Camera Calibration. We evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed camera
506   image representation and multi-resolution noise strategy through an ablation study on the GSV
507
      dataset (Anguelov et al., 2010), which includes 20 different camera intrinsic parameters. The study,
      conducted over 15k iterations, focuses on field-of-view prediction errors. As shown in Tab. 6, the
508
      naive approach using c = [θ, ϕ, θ] yields the poorest results due to the domain gap between the
509
      generated images and those produced by diffusion models. While multi-resolution noise(Kasiopy,
510   2023) improves performance slightly, it remains suboptimal. Incorporating our camera image
511   representation significantly reduces the error, and the combination of both strategies produces the
512   best results, demonstrating their complementary effectiveness.
513
      Ablation on Metric Depth Estimation. We investigate the impact of our strategy on metric depth
514
      estimation by training a subset of our dataset for 20k iterations, with results shown in Tab. 7. Training
515   solely on synthetic data has proven problematic, especially in indoor scenarios. Our second model,
516   using a traditional multi-step denoising pipeline, integrates both virtual dense and sparse depth data
517   but results in suboptimal performance due to the pipeline’s inability to effectively recognize sparse
518   areas in the ground truth. Additionally, prior methods that froze the VAE decoder during one-step
519   training have shown to be inadequate for metric depth estimation, as demonstrated in our experiments.
520   Omitting the camera image representation also slightly reduces accuracy. Interestingly, we found
521   that the network can still produce relatively satisfactory results without explicit camera intrinsic
522
      guidance, contradicting previous studies that highlight the necessity of such information for metric
      depth estimation (Yin et al., 2023). We attribute this to the powerful pretrained SD model’s capacity
523
      to capture subtle variations in camera intrinsic parameters.

533   5      C ONCLUSION
534   In conclusion, we introduced DM-Calib, a diffusion-based framework leveraging our proposed Cam-
535   era Image for monocular camera calibration. By utilizing the strong priors of stable diffusion models,
536   DM-Calibeffectively estimates camera intrinsic parameters. Extensive experiments demonstrate its
537   superior performance across a range of 3D vision tasks, consistently outperforming baseline methods
538   in real-world scenarios and varied imaging conditions. Future work could address ultra-wide-angle
539   images by incorporating more diverse training data and improve inference efficiency by developing a
      few-step diffusion (Luo et al., 2023) model to further enhance 3D vision tasks.

878   in (Zhu et al., 2023), we randomly scale images up to twice their original size and then crop them
879   back to the original resolution, with the camera intrinsics adjusted accordingly.

      A.2   M ETRIC DEPTH PREDICTION
890
      For metric depth prediction, we do not pad the images. Instead, we resize the maximum dimension of
891   the images to 768 while maintaining their aspect ratios. Additionally, we apply random horizontal
892   flipping and random cropping to enhance dataset diversity even in one dataset. Inspired by (Fu
893   et al., 2024), we incorporate a “scene distribution decoupler” into our model through text-guided
894   conditioned depth generation. Specifically, we utilize the CLIP tokenizer and encoder to encode the
895   terms “indoor geometry” and “outdoor geometry” for different environments. Based on this setting,
896   we treat the metric depth with different scale factor for indoor and outdoor: s = {sin , sout }, and the
897   depth label become ds = d/si with si ∈ s to fit the output of the training VAE decoder.
