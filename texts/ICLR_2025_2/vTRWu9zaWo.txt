011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The graduated optimization approach is a method for ﬁnding global optimal so-
014            lutions for nonconvex functions by using a function smoothing operation with
015
               stochastic noise. We show that stochastic noise in stochastic gradient descent
               (SGD) has the effect of smoothing the objective function, the degree of which is
016
               determined by the learning rate, batch size, and variance of the stochastic gradi-
017
               ent. Using this ﬁnding, we propose and analyze a new graduated optimization
018            algorithm that varies the degree of smoothing by varying the learning rate and
019            batch size, and provide experimental results on image classiﬁcation tasks with
020            ResNets that support our theoretical ﬁndings. We further show that there is an in-
021            teresting correlation between the degree of smoothing by SGD’s stochastic noise,
022            the well-studied “sharpness” indicator, and the generalization performance of the
023            model.

      1.1   BACKGROUND
029   The amazing success of deep neural networks (DNN) in recent years has been based on optimization
030   by stochastic gradient descent (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951) and its variants, such as Adam
031   (Kingma & Ba, 2015). These methods have been widely studied for their convergence (Moulines &
032   Bach, 2011; Needell et al., 2014) (Fehrman et al., 2020; Bottou et al., 2018; Scaman & Malherbe,
033   2020; Loizou et al., 2021; Zaheer et al., 2018; Zou et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020;
034   Chen et al., 2021; Iiduka, 2022) and stability (Hardt et al., 2016; Lin et al., 2016; Mou et al., 2018;
035   He et al., 2019) in nonconvex optimization.
036   SGD updates the parameters as xt+1 := xt − η∇fSt (xt ), where η is the learning rate and ∇fSt
037   is the stochastic gradient estimated from the full gradient ∇f using a mini-batch St . Therefore,
038   there is only an ωt := ∇fSt (xt ) − ∇f (xt ) difference between the search direction of SGD and the
039   true steepest descent direction. Some studies claim that it is crucial in nonconvex optimization. For
040   example, it has been proven that noise helps the algorithm to escape local minima (Ge et al., 2015; Jin
041   et al., 2017; Daneshmand et al., 2018; Vardhan & Stich, 2021), achieve better generalization (Hardt
042   et al., 2016; Mou et al., 2018), and ﬁnd a local minimum with a small loss value in polynomial time
043
      under some assumptions (Zhang et al., 2017). Several studies have also shown that performance can
      be improved by adding artiﬁcial noise to gradient descent (GD) (Ge et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2019;
044
      Jin et al., 2021; Orvieto et al., 2022).
045
046   (Kleinberg et al., 2018) also suggests that noise smoothes the objective function. Here, at time t, let

048              yt := xt − η∇f (xt ), xt+1 := xt − η∇fSt (xt ) = xt − η(∇f (xt ) + ωt ).

059   Graduated Optimization. Graduated optimization is one of the global optimization methods that
060
      search for the global optimal solution of difﬁcult multimodal optimization problems. The method
      generates a sequence of simpliﬁed optimization problems that gradually approach the original prob-
061
      lem through different levels of local smoothing operations. It solves the easiest simpliﬁed problem
062
      ﬁrst, as the easiest simpliﬁcation should have nice properties such as convexity or strong convexity;
063   after that, it uses that solution as the initial point for solving the second-simplest problem, then the
064   second solution as the initial point for solving the third-simplest problem and so on, as it attempts to
065   escape from local optimal solutions of the original problem and reach a global optimal solution.
066
      This idea ﬁrst appeared in the form of graduated non-convexity (GNC) by (Blake & Zisserman,
067
      1987) and has since been studied in the ﬁeld of computer vision for many years. Similar early
068
      approaches can be found in (Witkin et al., 1987) and (Yuille, 1989). Moreover, the same concept
069   has appeared in the ﬁelds of numerical analysis (Allgower & Georg, 1990) and optimization (Rose
070   et al., 1990; Wu, 1996). Over the past 25 years, graduated optimization has been successfully ap-
071   plied to many tasks in computer vision, such as early vision (Black & Rangarajan, 1996), image
072   denoising (Nikolova et al., 2010), optical ﬂow (Sun et al., 2010; Brox & Malik, 2011), dense corre-
073   spondence of images (Kim et al., 2013), and robust estimation (Yang et al., 2020; Antonante et al.,
074   2022; Peng et al., 2023). In addition, it has been applied to certain tasks in machine learning, such
075   as semi-supervised learning (Chapelle et al., 2006; Sindhwani et al., 2006; Chapelle et al., 2008),
076   unsupervised learning (Smith & Eisner, 2004), and ranking (Chapelle & Wu, 2010). Moreover,
077
      score-based generative models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Song et al., 2021b) and diffusion models
      (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021a; Rombach et al., 2022), which are
078
      currently state-of-the-art generative models, implicitly use the techniques of graduated optimiza-
079
      tion. A comprehensive survey on the graduated optimization approach can be found in (Mobahi &
080   Fisher III, 2015b).
081
082   Several previous studies have theoretically analyzed the graduated optimization algorithm. (Mobahi
083
      & Fisher III, 2015a) performed the ﬁrst theoretical analysis, but they did not provide a practical al-
      gorithm. (Hazan et al., 2016) deﬁned a family of nonconvex functions satisfying certain conditions,
084
      called σ-nice, and proposed a ﬁrst-order algorithm based on graduated optimization. In addition,
085
      they studied the convergence and convergence rate of their algorithm to a global optimal solution
086   for σ-nice functions. (Iwakiri et al., 2022) proposed a single-loop method that simultaneously up-
087   dates the variable that deﬁnes the noise level and the parameters of the problem and analyzed its
088   convergence. (Li et al., 2023) analyzed graduated optimization based on a special smoothing opera-
089   tion. Note that (Duchi et al., 2012) pioneered the theoretical analysis of optimizers using Gaussian
090   smoothing operations for nonsmooth convex optimization problems. Their method of optimizing
091   with decreasing noise level is truly a graduated optimization approach.
092
093   1.2   M OTIVATION
094
095   Equation (1) indicates that SGD smoothes the objective function (Kleinberg et al., 2018), but it is not
096   clear to what extent the function is smoothed or what factors are involved in the smoothing. There-
097   fore, we decided to clarify these aspects and identify what parameters contribute to the smoothing.
098
      Also, once it is known what parameters of SGD contribute to smoothing, an implicit graduated op-
      timization can be achieved by varying the parameters so that the noise level is reduced gradually.
099
      Our goal was thus to construct an implicit graduated optimization framework using the smoothing
100
      properties of SGD to achieve global optimization of deep neural networks.

      The graduated optimization algorithm is a method in which the degree of smoothing δ is gradually
217   decreased. Let us consider the case where the degree of smoothing δ is constant throughout the
218   training. Here, a larger degree of smoothing should be necessary to make many local optimal solu-
219   tions of the objective function f disappear and lead the optimizer to the global optimal solution. On
220   the other hand, Lemma 2.2 implies that the larger the degree of smoothing is, the further away the
221   smoothed function will be from the original function. Therefore, there should be an optimal value
222   for the degree of smoothing that balances the tradeoffs, because if the degree of smoothing is too
223   large, the original function is too damaged and thus cannot be optimized properly, and if it is too
224   small, the function is not smoothed enough and the optimizer falls into a local optimal solution. This
225
      knowledge is useful because the degree of smoothing due to stochastic noise in SGD is determined

258   Why the Use of Large Batch Sizes Leads to Solutions Falling into Sharp Local Minima. It is
259   known that training with large batch sizes leads to a persistent degradation of model generalization
260   performance. In particular, (Keskar et al., 2017) showed experimentally that learning with large
261   batch sizes leads to sharp local minima and worsens generalization performance. According to
262   equation (2), using a large learning rate and/or a small batch size will make the function smoother.
263   Thus, in using a small batch size, the sharp local minima will disappear through extensive smoothing,
264   and SGD can reach a ﬂat local minimum. Conversely, when using a large batch size, the smoothing
265   is weak and the function is close to the original multimodal function, so it is easy for the solution
266
      to fall into a sharp local minimum. Thus, we have theoretical support for what (Keskar et al., 2017)
      showed experimentally, and our experiments have yielded similar results (see Figure 3 (a) and (e)).

      6   C ONCLUSION
512   We proved that SGD with a mini-batch stochastic gradient has the effect of smoothing the function,
513   and the degree of smoothing is greater with larger learning rates and smaller batch sizes. This shows
514   theoretically that smoothing with large batch sizes is makes it easy to fall into sharp local minima and
515   that using a decaying learning rate and/or increasing batch size is implicitly graduated optimization,
516   which makes sense in the sense that it avoids local optimal solutions. Based on these ﬁndings, we
517   proposed a new graduated optimization algorithm that uses a decaying learning rate and increasing
518   batch size and analyzed it. We conducted experiments whose results showed the superiority of our
519   recommended framework for image classiﬁcation tasks on CIFAR100 and ImageNet. In addition,
520
      we observed that the degree of smoothing of the function due to stochastic noise in SGD can express
      the degree of smoothness of the function as well as sharpness does, and that the degree of smoothing
521
      is a good indicator of the generalization performance of the model.

1379      H              D ISCUSSION ON THE DEFINITION OF THE SMOOTHED FUNCTION
1380
1381      Recall the general deﬁnition of the smoothing of the function.
1382
          Deﬁnition H.1. Given a function f : Rd → R, deﬁne fˆδ : Rd → R to be the function obtained by
1383
          smoothing f as
1384
1385                                                            fˆδ (x) := Eu∼N (0; √1 I ) [f (x − δu)] ,
                                                                                                                           d
                                                                                                                     d
1386
1387      where δ > 0 represents the degree of smoothing and u is a random variable from a Gaussian
1388      distribution.
1389
          What probability distribution the random variable u ∈ Rd follows in the deﬁnition of smoothed
1390      function varies in the literature. (Wu, 1996; Mobahi & Fisher III, 2015b; Iwakiri et al., 2022)
1391      assumes a Gaussian distribution, while (Hazan et al., 2016) assumes a uniform distribution for the
1392      sake of theoretical analysis. So what probability distribution the random variable u should follow in
1393      order to smooth the function? This has never been discussed.
1394
          It is difﬁcult to conﬁrm from a strictly theoretical point of view whether the function fˆδ obtained by
1395
          using a random variable u that follows a certain probability distribution is smoother than the original
1396      function f (more precisely, it is possible with a Gaussian distribution). Therefore, we smoothed a
1397      very simple nonconvex function with random variables following several major probability distribu-
1398      tions and compared it with the original function. We deal with one-dimensional Rastrigin’s function
1399      (Törn & Zilinskas, 1989; Rudolph, 1990) and Drop-Wave function (Marcin Molga, 2005) deﬁned
1400      as follows:
