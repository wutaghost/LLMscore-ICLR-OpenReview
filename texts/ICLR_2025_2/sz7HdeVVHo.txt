009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Informative representations enhance model performance and generalisability in
012            downstream tasks. However, learning self-supervised representations for spatially
013            characterised time series, like traffic interactions, poses challenges as it requires
014            maintaining fine-grained similarity relations in the latent space. In this study, we
015            incorporate two structure-preserving regularisers for the contrastive learning of
016
               spatial time series: one regulariser preserves the topology of similarities between
               instances, and the other preserves the graph geometry of similarities across spatial
017
               and temporal dimensions. To balance contrastive learning and structure preserva-
018
               tion, we propose a dynamic mechanism that adaptively weighs the trade-off and
019            stabilises training. We conduct experiments on multivariate time series classifica-
020            tion, as well as macroscopic and microscopic traffic prediction. For all three tasks,
021            our approach preserves the structures of similarity relations more effectively and
022            improves state-of-the-art task performances. This approach can be applied to an
023            arbitrary encoder and is particularly beneficial for time series with spatial or geo-
024            graphical features. Our code is attached as supplementary material, which will be
025            made openly available with all resulting data after review.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Self-supervised representation learning (SSRL) theoretically can learn latent embeddings that fa-
030   cilitate downstream tasks (Saunshi et al., 2019; HaoChen et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2024). Also, it is
031   practically shown to improve model generalisability (Tendle & Hasan, 2021; Zhou et al., 2022). The
032   latter is particularly valuable for real-world applications, where both measurements and labels are
033   often uncertain and unreliable. In fact, SSRL has been widely applied across fields such as com-
034   puter vision, natural language processing, and recommendation systems (there are many literature
035   reviews, to name a few, Schiappa et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024).
036   Contrastive learning has become the mainstay technique in SSRL of time series. Lafabregue et al.
037   (2022) conducted an extensive experimental comparison over 300 combinations of network archi-
038   tectures and loss functions to evaluate the performance of time series representation learning for
039   clustering. One of their key findings is that the reconstruction loss used by traditional autoencoders
040   does not sufficiently fit temporal patterns. Instead, contrastive learning has emerged as a more effec-
041   tive approach, which embeds similar samples closer together while dissimilar samples farther apart
042   in the latent space (Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024).
043   Unique challenges arise when learning contrastive representations for spatial time series. First, data
044   with both temporal and spatial characteristics demand more fine-grained similarity comparisons,
045   which underpins contrastive learning. Financial time series may be considered similar even if some
046   variables show significant divergence, while movement traces with very different spatial features
047   can be anything but similar. Second, effective representation of spatial time series needs to capture
048   spatio-temporal patterns at the certain scale required by a practical task. For example, traffic interac-
049   tions involve two different spatial scales: at the macroscopic scale, traffic flow measures collective
050
      road usage evolving over the road network; at the microscopic scale, trajectories describe the motion
      dynamics of individual road users (e.g., car drivers, cyclists, pedestrians) in local road space.
051
052   To address the challenges, we incorporate two regularisers at different scales to preserve the orig-
053   inal similarity structure in the latent space for time series contrastive learning. One is a topology-
      preserving regulariser for the global scale, and the other is a graph-geometry-preserving regulariser

054
      for the local scale. This incorporation can be simplified as a weighted loss L = ηCLT · ℓCLT + ηSP ·
055   ℓSP + rη , where we propose a mechanism to dynamically balance the weights ηCLT and ηSP during
056   training. Within this mechanism, the adaptive trade-off between contrastive learning and structure
057   preservation is based on the uncertainties of their corresponding terms ℓCLT and ℓSP ; meanwhile, the
058   term rη adds regularisation against overfitting of the dynamic weights.
059
      The proposed approach is applicable to spatial time series in general, and we consider traffic interac-
060
      tion as a specific case. To validate the approach, we conduct experiments on tasks of 1) multivariate
061   time series classification, where we benchmark against the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) models,
062   i.e., TS2Vec Yue et al. (2022) and Lee et al. (2024); and 2) traffic prediction, where we use Li et al.
063   (2024a) for macroscopic benchmark and Li et al. (2024b) for microscopic. In addition, the effi-
064   ciency of this approach is evaluated with various model architectures. Below we summarise the key
065   contributions of this study:
066
067       • We introduce an approach that incorporates structure-preserving regularisation in contrastive
068         learning of multivariate time series, to maintain finer-grained similarity relations in the latent
069
            space of sample representations. We propose a dynamic weighing mechanism to adaptively
            balance contrastive learning and structure preservation during training.
070
071       • Preserving similarity structure can enhance SOTA performance on various downstream tasks.
072         The relative improvement on spatial datasets in the UEA archive is 2.96% in average classifica-
073         tion accuracy; on macroscopic traffic prediction task is 0.72% in flow speed RMSE and 0.27%
074
            in explained variance; on microscopic trajectory prediction task is 3.72% and 8.10% in missing
            rates under radii of 0.5m and 1m, respectively.
075
076       • This approach can be applied to an arbitrary encoder for self-supervised representation learning.
077         Preserving the structure of similarity relations is particularly beneficial for time series data with
078         spatial or geographical characteristics, such as in robotics, meteorology, remote sensing, urban
079
            planning, etc.

081   2      R ELATED WORK
082
083   2.1     T IME SERIES CONTRASTIVE LEARNING
084
085   Contrastive learning for time series data is a relatively young niche and is rapidly developing. The
086   development has been dominantly focused on defining positive and negative samples. Early ap-
087   proaches construct positive and negative samples with subseries within time series (e.g., Franceschi
088   et al., 2019) and temporal neighbourhoods (e.g., Tonekaboni et al., 2021); and later methods cre-
089
      ate augmentations by transforming original series (e.g., Eldele et al., 2021; 2023). More recently,
      Yue et al. (2022) generates random masks to enable both instance-wise and time-wise contextual
090
      representations at flexible hierarchical levels, which exceeds previous state-of-the-art performances
091
      (SOTAs). Given that not all negatives may be useful (Cai et al., 2020; Jeon et al., 2021), Liu & Chen
092   (2024) makes hard negatives to boost performance, while Lee et al. (2024) utilises soft contrastive
093   learning to weigh sample pairs of varying similarities, both of which reach new SOTAs.
094
095
      The preceding paragraph outlines a brief summary, and we refer the readers to Section 2 in Lee et al.
      (2024) and Section 5.3 in Trirat et al. (2024) for a detailed overview of the methods proposed in the
096
      past 6 years. These advances have led to increasingly sophisticated methods that mine the contextual
097
      information embedded in time series by contrasting similarities. However, the structural details of
098   similarity relations between samples remain to be explored.
099
100
      2.2     S TRUCTURE - PRESERVING SSRL
101
102   Preserving the original structure of data when mapping into a latent space has been widely and
103   actively researched in manifold learning (for a literature review, Meilă & Zhang, 2024) and graph
104   representation learning (Ju et al., 2024; Khoshraftar & An, 2024). In manifold learning, which is also
105   known as nonlinear dimension reduction, the focus is on revealing the geometric shape of data point
106   clouds for visualisation, denoising, and interpretation. In graph representation learning, the focus is
107   on maintaining the connectivity of nodes in the graph while compressing the data space required for
      large-scale graphs. Structure-preserving has not yet attracted much dedication to time series data.


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Ashraf et al. (2023) provides a literature review on time series data dimensionality reduction, where
109
      none of the methods are specifically tailored for time series.
110
111   Zooming in within structure-preserving SSRL, there are two major branches respectively focusing
112   on topology and geometry. Topology-preserving SSRL aims to maintain global properties such
113
      as clusters, loops, and voids in the latent space; representative models include Moor et al. (2020)
      and Trofimov et al. (2023) using autoencoders, as well as Madhu & Chepuri (2023) and Chen et al.
114
      (2024) with contrastive learning. The other branch is geometry-preserving and focuses more on local
115
      shapes such as relative distances, angles, and areas. Geometry-preserving autoencoders include
116   Nazari et al. (2023) and Lim et al. (2024), while Li et al. (2022) and Koishekenov et al. (2023)
117   use contrastive learning. The aforementioned topology and geometry preserving autoencoders are
118   all developed for dimensionality reduction; whereas the combination of contrastive learning and
119   structure-preserving has been explored majorly with graphs.
120
121   2.3   T RAFFIC INTERACTION SSRL
122
123   In line with the conclusions in previous subsections, existing exploration in the context of traffic
124   interaction data and tasks also predominantly relies on autoencoders and graphs. For instance, using
125   a transformer-based multivariate time series autoencoder (Zerveas et al., 2021), Lu et al. (2022)
126   cluster traffic scenarios with trajectories of pairwise vehicles. Then a series of studies investigate
127
      masking strategies with autoencoders for individual trajectories and road networks, including Cheng
      et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Lan et al. (2024).
128
129   Leveraging data augmentation, Mao et al. (2022) utilise graphs and contrastive learning to jointly
130   learn representations for vehicle trajectories and road networks. They design road segment positive
131   samples as neighbours in the graph, and trajectory positive samples by replacing a random part with
132   another path having the same origin and destination. Similarly, Zipfl et al. (2023) use a graph-based
133
      contrastive learning approach to learn traffic scene similarity. They randomly modify the position
      and velocity of individual traffic participants in a scene to construct positive samples, with negative
134
      samples drawn uniformly from the rest of a training batch. Also using augmentation, Zheng et al.
135
      (2024) focuses on capturing seasonal and holiday information for traffic prediction.

138   3     M ETHODS
139
140   This section begins by defining the problem of structure-preserving contrastive learning for spatial
141   time series. Following that, we explain the overall loss function to be optimised, where we propose
142   a dynamic weighing mechanism to balance contrastive learning and structure preservation during
143   training. Then we present the contrastive learning loss for time series, unifying both hard and soft
144
      versions in a consistent format. Lastly, we introduce two structure-preserving regularisers, which
      are respectively adapted to maintain the global and local structure of similarity relations.
145
146
147   3.1   P ROBLEM DEFINITION
148
      We define the problem for general spatial time series, with traffic interaction as a specific case.
149
      Learning the representations of a set of samples {x1 , x2 , · · · , xN } aims to obtain a nonlinear func-
150   tion fθ : x → z that encodes each x into z in a latent space. Let T denote the sequence length of a
151   time series and D the feature dimension at each timestamp t. The original space of x can have the
152   form RT ×D , where spatial features are among the D dimensions; or RT ×S×D , where S represents
153   spatially distributed objects (e.g., sensors or road users). The latent space of z can also be structured
154   in different forms, such as RP , RT ×P , or RT ×S×P , where P is the dimension of encoded features.
155
      By contrastive learning, (dis)similar samples in the original space should remain close (far) in the
156   latent space. Meanwhile, by structure preservation, the distance/similarity relations between samples
157   should maintain certain features after mapping into the latent space. We use d(xi , xj ) to denote the
158   distance between two samples i and j, and this also applies to their encoded representations zi and
159   zj . Various distance measures can be used to define d, such as cosine distance (COS), Euclidean
160   distance (EUC), and dynamic time warping (DTW). The smaller the distance between two samples,
161   the more similar they are. Considering the limitation of storage efficiency, similarity comparison is
      performed in each mini-batch, where B samples are randomly selected.

173   the value of LSP sometimes is below zero, the losses used as LCLT and LSP in this study all have

285   4.1     E XPERIMENT SETUP
286
287   We compare 6 losses for self-supervised representation learning (SSRL) of time series: TS2Vec,
288
      SoftCLT, Topo-TS2Vec, GGeo-TS2Vec, Topo-SoftCLT, and GGeo-SoftCLT. Among the losses,
      TS2Vec (Yue et al., 2022) and SoftCLT (Lee et al., 2024) are baselines, and the others extend these
289
      two with a topology-preserving or a graph-geometry-preserving regulariser. The comparison is then
290
      evaluated by downstream task performances using these differently encoded representations. Con-
291   sequently, the comparison and evaluation serve as an extensive ablation study focusing on the effects
292   of structure-preserving regularisers. Our experiments are conducted with an NVIDIA A100 GPU
293   with 80GB RAM and 5 Intel Xeon CPUs. For fair comparisons, we control the following condi-
294   tions during experiments: random seed, the space and strategy for hyperparameter search, maximum
295   training epochs, early stopping criteria, and samples used for evaluating local structure preservation.
296
297   4.1.1    BASELINES AND DATASETS
298
      The evaluation of performance improvement is on 3 downstream tasks: multivariate time series clas-
299
      sification, macroscopic traffic prediction, and microscopic traffic prediction. For every downstream
300   task, we split training/(validation)/test sets following the baseline study and make sure the same data
301   are used across models. Each experiment for a task has two stages, of which the first is SSRL and the
302   second uses the encoded representations to perform classification/prediction. Only the split training
303   set is used in the first stage, with 25% separated as an internal validation set to schedule the learning
304   rate for SSRL.
305
      The classification task is on 28 datasets3 retrieved from the UEA archive (Bagnall et al., 2018).
306   For each dataset, we set the representation dimension to 320 as used in the TS2Vec and SoftCLT
307   studies, train 6 encoders with the 6 losses, and then classify the encoded representations with an
308   RBF-kernel SVM. For traffic prediction, we use the dataset and model in (Li et al., 2024a) for the
309   macroscopic baseline, and those in (Li et al., 2024b) for the microscopic baseline. The macroscopic
310   traffic prediction uses 40 minutes (2-minute intervals) of historical data in 193 consecutive road
311   segments to predict for all segments in the next 30 minutes. The microscopic traffic prediction
312   forecasts the trajectory of an ego vehicle in 3 seconds, based on the history of up to 26 surrounding
313   road users in the past 1 second (0.1-second intervals). Both traffic prediction baselines use encoder-
314
      decoder structures. We first pretrain the encoder with the 6 different losses for SSRL, and then
      fine-tune the complete model for prediction. The baseline trained from scratch is also compared.
315
316   To facilitate clearer analyses when presenting results, we divide the datasets included in the UEA
317   archive into those with spatial features and those without. According to data descriptions in (Bagnall
318   et al., 2018), the UEA datasets are grouped into 6 categories: human activity recognition, motion
319   classification, ECG classification, EEG/MEG classification, audio spectra classification, and other
320
      problems. The human activity and motion categories, along with the PEMS-SF and LSST datasets

346   4.1.3      E VALUATION METRICS
347   Our performance evaluation uses both task-specific metrics and structure-preserving metrics. The
348   former serves to validate performance improvements, while the latter serves to verify the effective-
349   ness of preserving similarity structures. These metrics differ in whether a higher or lower value
350   signifies better performance. To consistently indicate the best method, in the tables presented in the
351   following subsections, the best values are both bold and underlined; the second-best values are bold.
352
      For classification, we use accuracy (Acc.) and the area under the precision-recall curve (AUPRC).
353   To evaluate macroscopic traffic prediction, we use mean absolute error (MAE), root mean squared
354   error (RMSE), the standard deviation of prediction errors (SDEP), and the explained variance by
355   prediction (EVar). Dealing with microscopic traffic, we predict vehicle trajectories and assess the
356   minimum final displacement error (min. FDE) as well as missing rates under radius thresholds of
357   0.5m, 1m, and 2m (MR0.5 , MR1 , MR2 ).
358
      As for metrics to evaluate structure preservation, we adopt a combination of those used in (Moor
359   et al., 2020) and (Lim et al., 2024). More specifically, we consider 1) kNN, the proportion of shared
360   k-nearest neighbours according to distance matrices in the latent space and in the original space; 2)
361   continuity (Cont.), one minus the proportion of neighbours in the original space that are no longer
362   neighbours in the latent space; 3) trustworthiness (Trust.), the counterpart of continuity, measuring
363   the proportion of neighbours in the latent space but not in the original space; 4) MRRE, the averaged
364   error in the relative ranks of sample distances between in the latent and original space; and 5) dis-
365   tance matrix RMSE (dRMSE), the root mean squared difference between sample distance matrices
366   in the latent and original space. We calculate these metrics at two scales to evaluate global and local
367
      structure preservation. For global evaluation, our calculation is based on EUC distances between
      samples; for local evaluation, the calculation is based on EUC distances between timestamps for at
368
      most 500 samples in a test set.

527   5                  C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper presents an approach to structure-preserving contrastive learning for spatial time series,
530   where a dynamic mechanism is proposed to adaptively balance contrastive learning and structure
531
      preservation. Our methods are experimentally demonstrated to improve the SOTA performance,
      including for multivariate time series classification in various contexts and for traffic prediction at
532
      both macroscopic and microscopic scales. In general, adding structure-preserving regularisation has
533
      a limited impact on representation learning efficiency. It can be computationally intensive when the
534   time sequence is long; however, evident performance improvement makes it an acceptable price to
535   pay for utilising the information embedded in time series data. Our experiments (albeit preliminary)
536   also suggest that preserving certain similarity structures may be crucial for downstream task perfor-
537   mance, highlighting that the structural information of similarities in spatio-temporal data remains
538   yet to be exploited. Given that many real-world practices involve spatial time series, this study and
539   future research based on it can be applied not only to traffic interactions, but also to any that can
      benefit from preserving specific structures in similarity relations.
