011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Domain shifts pose a significant challenge in deep learning applications. Exist-
014            ing methods typically address domain shifts by treating each domain in isolation,
015            overlooking the underlying factors driving the shifts, or focus on only one factor.
016            However, domain shifts in the real world often occur across multiple dimensions
017            simultaneously. For example, medical datasets from different hospitals can ex-
018            hibit variations in factors including demographics, equipment manufacturers, and
019
               imaging protocols, demonstrating a three-dimensional shifts. In this paper, we
               introduce a novel approach to address the complexity of multi-dimensional do-
020
               main shifts. Our method leverages an ensemble of mixtures of experts (EMoE),
021
               with each MoE specialized in different dimensions. Crucially, we innovate a do-
022            main estimator to address a particularly challenging issue frequently encountered
023            in practice: domain labels may be missing or unreliable. A significant advantage
024            of our method is its generalizability and adaptability to both centralized and fed-
025            erated learning settings, as well as its versatility across various tasks. Extensive
026            experiments on six datasets demonstrate the superiority of our method over state-
027            of-the-art domain generalization and personalized federated learning approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Deep learning has significantly advanced computer vision tasks in recent years. However, deploying
      these models in real-world scenarios often encounters severe performance degradation due to the
034
      problem of domain shifts (Quinonero-Candela et al., 2008). Domain Generalization (DG) (Wang
035
      et al., 2022) has been proposed to address these challenges, focusing on developing models robust
036   to changes in data distributions. A promising direction within these efforts is to characterize data
037   of data distributions as mixtures of domains, training models on one mixture and then aiming to
038   generalize across unknown mixtures, involving both seen and unseen domains.
039
      Traditional DG studies typically use domain labels indexed by a single factor. In contrast, real-world
040
      domain shifts often emerge from multiple factors. For instance, in medical image analysis, shifts
041
      can be attributed to differences in ethnic groups, equipment manufacturers, or imaging protocols
042   and workflows (Karani et al., 2018; Ciompi et al., 2017; Garrucho et al., 2022; Mårtensson et al.,
043   2020). Without utilizing the structure of multi-dimensional domains, existing methods fail to adapt
044   to the shifts across different domain dimensions simultaneously. Thus, it is crucial to consider
045   domain dimensions concurrently as they convey various aspects of data. For example, in medical
046   image analysis, domain shifts from different manufacturers or imaging protocols can affect model
047   robustness, while shifts among different ethnic groups of patients raise significant concerns about
048   fairness, as discussed in Parikh et al. (2019); Seyyed-Kalantari et al. (2020); Hinton (2018).
049   Due to the inherent heterogeneity of the data sources, domain shifts become even more challenging
050   in federated learning (FL), where models are trained across multiple decentralized clients without
051   exchanging local data samples. Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) (Sattler et al., 2020) and
052   Federated Domain Generalization (FDG) (Liu et al., 2021) were proposed to mitigate this problem
053   and improve generalization on clients involved in FL and unseen datasets outside the federated train-
      ing, respectively. Yet, these works presuppose that clients each have distinct and isolated domains.

066   This assumption does not hold in many real-world scenarios, such as in medical images, where the
067   data source at a single client may contain a variety of devices or diverse patient demographics.
068   In this paper, we address the complex issue of domain shifts in both centralized and federated learn-
069   ing scenarios, where data distributions are a mixture of multi-dimensionally indexed domains. Our
070   motivation stems from the need to effectively generalize across unseen mixtures of data domains, as
071   illustrated in Figure 1. A straightforward approach would be to group data with the same domain
072   indices across all dimensions and apply existing DG techniques. However, this method is funda-
073   mentally flawed, as certain groups may be underrepresented or entirely absent during training due
074   to the granular domain labels, making it difficult to ensure generalizability. Moreover, this approach
075   overlooks the inherent structure of domain labels, leading to suboptimal model performance.
076   To address these challenges, we introduce the Ensemble of Mixtures of Experts (EMoE) and its
077   federated learning counterpart, FedEMoE. For each domain dimension, we design a Mixture of
078   Experts (MoE) to address specific domain shifts, and we ensemble these MoEs to account for all
079   potential domain shifts factors. Our model is explicitly optimized to ensure equal effectiveness
080   across all domains within each dimension, enabling it to generalize robustly across a wide range of
081   test set mixtures. This design maximally leverages information from structured domain labels.
082   A significant challenge arises when domain labels are partially missing, or when predefined domain
083   labels cannot accurately characterize the data distribution. This is common in practical scenarios,
084   where domain labels—like patient demographics or equipment manufacturers—may be incomplete
085   or unable to fully capture the complexity of domain shifts. To address this, our approach integrates
086   a domain estimator that learns to assign experts based on the data itself, allowing the model not only
087   to bypass reliance on domain labels at inference time but also to surpass the performance of models
088
      using predefined domain labels. This solution addresses a key limitation highlighted in (Zhong et al.,
      2022), increasing the model’s flexibility and practicality in real-world applications.
089
090   Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We introduce a unified framework to address
091   the domain shifts challenge in both centralized and federated learning, based on mixtures of multi-
092   dimensionally indexed domains. (2) We propose a novel approach, EMoE, to effectively manage
093   multi-dimensional domain shifts, requiring only partial or no domain labels during training and test-
094
      ing. This method is naturally extended to federated learning, ensuring robust generalization across
      both training and unseen testing sites. (3) We integrate a domain estimator that assigns experts based
095
      on the data itself, enabling our model to overcome the limitations of missing or unreliable domain
096
      labels, further improving its adaptability. (4) We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across
097   five centralized learning tasks and one real-world federated learning scenario, outperforming exist-
098   ing DG, PFL, and FDG methods. Our approach comprehensively handles various domain shifts and
099   generalizes effectively to unseen data distributions without the need for domain labels.

103   2.1         D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
104
105   In domain generalization, training data from one or more source domains is used while test data
106   originates from different, unseen target domains. This approach, distinct from domain adaptation,
107   does not involve target domain data during training, as outlined by Quinonero-Candela et al. (2008).
      We explore scenarios where training data consists of diverse, multi-dimensional domains, acknowl-


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      edging that domain shifts are multifactorial and these domains are identified and known to some
109   extent. Koh et al. (2021) differentiates “domain generalization” from “subpopulation shift,” where
110   training and test domains overlap but vary in composition. Our study extends the concept of DG to
111   include both scenarios, in line with Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2020); Shi et al. (2021).
112
      Approaches in DG include: i) Domain-invariant representation learning, which seeks to extract
113
      features making domains indistinguishable for prediction, inspired by generalization error bounds
114
      (Ganin et al., 2016; Ben-David et al., 2010). Techniques include domain prediction penalization
115   (Ganin et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2019a; Huang et al., 2020), feature distribution matching across do-
116   mains (Sun & Saenko, 2016; Li et al., 2018a), domain gradient alignment (Koyama & Yamaguchi,
117   2020; Shi et al., 2021), data augmentation (Yue et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2022b),
118   and learning through pretext tasks (Carlucci et al., 2019). ii) Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) by
119   Arjovsky et al. (2020), aims for a universal representation where domain-specific optimal classifiers
120   are identical, addressing invariant causal relationships and domain-specific noise, with extensions
121   like IB-IRM (Ahuja et al., 2021). iii) Distributional Robustness, which minimizes worst-case losses
122   across data distributions from different training domains, addressing covariate shifts and subpop-
123
      ulation shifts, discussed in Rojas-Carulla et al. (2015); Quiñonero-Candela et al. (2008); Hu et al.
      (2018); Sagawa et al. (2019).
124
125
126   2.2   P ERSONALIZED F EDERATED L EARNING
127
      Personalized federated learning has been explored through various approaches, each offering unique
128
      strategies for tailoring models to individual clients. These approaches include: i) Clustered FL,
129
      which divides clients into clusters to develop an optimal model for each group, as discussed in Sattler
130   et al. (2020); Mansour et al. (2020); Ghosh et al. (2020). ii) Meta-learning techniques, which adapt
131   the model to new clients using prior knowledge, covered in works like Chen et al. (2018); Fallah
132   et al. (2020); Jiang et al. (2019); Khodak et al. (2019). iii) Local and global model interpolation,
133   an approach that combines local client models with a global model, as seen in Deng et al. (2020);
134   Corinzia et al. (2019); Mansour et al. (2020). iv) Multi-Task Learning (MTL), where separate but
135   related tasks are learned simultaneously, as explained in Vanhaesebrouck et al. (2017); Smith et al.
136   (2017); Zantedeschi et al. (2020) and further explored in Hanzely & Richtárik (2020); Hanzely et al.
137   (2020); T Dinh et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021a). v) Local fine-tuning methods,
138
      where individual models are adjusted post-training, as presented in Wang et al. (2019b); Yu et al.
      (2020). vi) Developing local representations or heads for individual clients, a strategy outlined in
139
      Arivazhagan et al. (2019); Liang et al. (2020); Collins et al. (2021a). vii) Creating personalized
140
      models using hypernetworks or supermodels, as proposed in Shamsian et al. (2021); Chen & Chao
141   (2021); Xu et al. (2022).
142
143
      2.3   F EDERATED D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
144
145   In contrast to PFL, Federated Domain Generalization (FDG) aims to enhance model performance
146   on unseen clients. Research in this area is limited; Liu et al. (2021) employ amplitude spectrum
147   analysis for data distribution as information to exchange, increasing costs and privacy risks. Jiang
148   et al. (2022) introduce a flatness-aware optimization, while Zhang et al. (2023) adjust aggregation
149   weights based on domain generalization gaps. Xu et al. (2022) combine PFL techniques by selecting
150   the most similar personalized local model for an unseen client.
151   Most existing PFL and FDG approaches treat each client as a distinct domain. However, this often
152   overlooks the significant interconnections in data distributions across different sites. Recent studies,
153   such as those by Marfoq et al. (2021) and Wu et al. (2023), propose that the data distribution at each
154   client’s end is more accurately represented as a mixture of several underlying unknown distributions.
155   Nevertheless, these studies do not fully exploit the available domain information.
156   Our work extends the data distribution assumptions from Zhong et al. (2022) in FL and Koh et al.
157   (2021) in centralized settings, treating data as mixtures of predefined domains. By considering the
158   multifactorial domain shifts, we concentrate on scenarios where the data distributions are character-
159   ized as mixtures of multi-dimensional indexed domains. Our model’s design is crafted to be readily
160   applicable to any new data distribution. This approach allows for a more nuanced understanding of
161   domain shifts, offering avenues for developing models that are both more robust and adaptable to
      diverse data landscapes.

185
186   In this section, we begin by formalizing our problem setting, followed by the introduction our pro-
187   posed solution, EMoE. We break down the framework of EMoE into four parts: (1) the learning
188
      objective, (2) the MoE ensemble design, (3) the domain estimator, and (4) the learning algorithms.
189
      3.1   P RELIMINARY
190
191   We address a setting where one predicts the label y ∈ Y from the input feature x ∈ X . We assume
192   the data are generated from multi-dimensional domains. Specifically, there are K dimensions of
193   domains with {M1 , M2 , · · · , MK } predefined domains in each dimension. For example, gender
194   can be one dimension with male and female as two domains, and race can be another dimension
195   with white, black, Asian as three domains. We denote the domain label by a domain vector z =
196   [z1 , · · · , zK ] ∈ Z = [M1 ] × · · · × [MK ] that indicates which domains this sample belongs to
197   (for each domain dimension). Our goal is to learn a predictor that works for any domains at any
198   domain dimension. Thus, we care not only about the average performance but the worst domain
199
      performance as well. Following Zhong et al. (2022); Koh et al. (2021), we regard each unique
      domain vector z to be associated with a data distribution Dz . Then  P we can formulate any data
200
      distribution as the mixture of multi-dimensional domains, i.e., D = z∈Z P (z)Dz , where {P (z)}
201
      denotes the mixture coefficients.
202

              P       Learning, domain generalization aims to train a model on a training distribution

205   bution Dts = z∈Z Pts (z)Dz , where the training and test domain mixture coefficients, {Ptr (z)}
206   and {Pts (z)}, are not identical. In Federated Learning, the aim is to developP    a model that learns

240   The design of EMoE is motivated by the need to effectively handle the complexity of multi-
241   dimensional domain shifts, which are common in real-world scenarios. Fig. 2 illustrates the
242   overview of our EMoE framework, consisting of a shared encoder (representation extractor) ϕ and
243   K distinct Mixtures of Experts (MoEs). Each MoE k is specialized to address domain dimension k
244   by incorporating a mixture of Mk experts, labeled as hk,m for each m ∈ [Mk ]. This modular design
245
      enables each MoE to focus on specific factors contributing to domain shifts, allowing the model to
      adapt flexibly to various domain combinations. By isolating and addressing shifts across multiple
246
      dimensions, EMoE enhances the model’s ability to generalize effectively to unseen domains. Ad-
247
      ditionally, this architecture is versatile and can be easily integrated with a wide range of existing
248   models, making it both effective and practical for addressing complex domain shifts.

278   3.4   D OMAIN E STIMATOR /E XPERT ROUTER
279
      In practical scenarios, it is common for specific domain labels for some data samples to be missing,
280
      unreliable, or unable to accurately characterize domain information. For example, a patient may
281   choose not to disclose their demographics, or the available domain labels might not fully capture the
282   complexities of the data. This issue often arises during both the training and testing phases. Missing
283   or inaccurate domain information may result from initial data collection or from data originating in
284   a new, previously unseen domain, where no corresponding domain expert exists. To address these
285   challenges, our framework incorporates a domain estimator to autonomously identify the domain of
286   the data, enabling the allocation of a suitable expert for prediction.
287   During training, the domain estimator calculates a set of domain assignment scores {ẑk } to assign
288   each sample to an expert via Gumbel-Softmax (Jang et al., 2016; Maddison et al., 2016):

316   marized in Algorithm 1. In the pretraining phase, the model is updated using two losses: Ldomain
317   for the domain estimator output lk and Ltask for the MoE output ok , aiming to establish a strong
318
      initialization. This phase uses possibly unreliable predefined domain labels. In the subsequent train-
      ing phase, the model focuses solely on Ltask applied to the final output o, discarding the predefined
319
      domain labels and training all components end-to-end based on data-driven domain estimation.
320

322   learning procedure. At each communication round, we first update the domain experts locally us-
323   ing the specific task loss Ltask , and then aggregate them at the server. Next, we update the shared
      representation and domain estimators by minimizing L = Ltask + λLdomain , where λ is a hyperpa-
      rameter. The learning procedure is summarized in Algorithm 2.

531   5      C ONCLUSION
532
      In this study, we introduced EMoE, a domain generalization method to address multi-dimensional
533
      domain shifts in both centralized and federated learning. Unlike prior methods focusing on sin-
534   gle dimensions or isolated domains, EMoE handles shifts across multiple dimensions, ensuring
535   strong generalization. Our approach uses an Ensemble of Mixtures of Experts (EMoE), with each
536   MoE specializing in a domain dimension. We also integrated a domain estimator to manage miss-
537   ing or unreliable labels, enhancing flexibility and practicality. Extensive experiments across six
538   datasets demonstrated EMoE’s superior performance, outperforming state-of-the-art DG, PFL, and
539   FDG methods. Particularly in federated learning, FedEMoE significantly improves fairness and
      robustness, setting a new benchmark for federated domain generalization research.

814   Colored MNIST (CMNIST) We use the digit and background colors as two domain dimensions.
815   The task involves classifying digits into 2 classes, with conditional shifts across domains. In the de-
816   fault setting, where digits are red and backgrounds are black, the classes are (0,1,2,3,4) vs (5,6,7,8,9).
817   When the digit color changes to green, the labels for digits 2 and 5 will flip. With a white back-
818   ground, the labels for digits 4 and 9 will flip. In the training set, the proportion of red to green
819   samples is set at 8:2, and the proportion of black to white backgrounds is also 8: 2. For the valida-
820
      tion set, the proportions are equal to 1:1 for all domain dimensions. In the test set, the proportion of
      red to green samples is 1:9, and the proportion of black to white backgrounds is also 1:9. The data
821
      sizes of train, validation, and test sets are 30000, 10000, and 20000, respectively.
822
823
      CelebA Adhering to the preprocessing steps outlined in Sagawa et al. (2019), we utilize CelebA’s
824
      celebrity face images. The objective is to categorize hair color as either ”blond” or ”not blond”.
825   Gender and age are employed as two domain dimensions. Notably, the dataset exhibits a significant
826   imbalance in group distribution. Within the training set, the most populous group is (not blond,
827   female, young) with 64,036 samples, whereas the smallest group is (blond, male, old), comprising
828   merely 463 samples.
829
830   FairFace (Kärkkäinen & Joo, 2019) FairFace is a publicly available face attribute dataset that
831   ensures a balanced representation of race, gender, and age for bias analysis. It features seven racial
832   groups, nine age groups, and two genders. In this dataset, gender and race serve as the domain
833   dimensions. The classification task involves determining whether an individual’s age is 40 or older.
834   The original dataset comprises 66,744 samples in the training set and 10,954 samples in the valida-
835   tion set. We evenly partition the initial validation set, using the first 50% for validation purposes and
836
      the remaining 50% for testing.
