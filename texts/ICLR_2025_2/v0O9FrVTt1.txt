009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Network propagation issues like the spread of misinformation, cyber threats, or in-
012            frastructure breakdowns are prevalent and have significant societal impacts. Iden-
013            tifying the source of such propagation by analyzing snapshots of affected networks
014            is crucial for managing crises like disease outbreaks and enhancing network se-
015            curity. Traditional methods rely on metrics derived from network topology and
016            are limited to specific propagation models, while deep learning models face the
017            challenge of data scarcity. We propose ASLDiff (Adaptive Source Localization
018
               Diffsion Model), a novel adaptive source localization diffusion model to achieve
               accurate and robust source localization across different network topologies and
019
               propagation modes by fusing the principles of information propagation and re-
020
               structuring the label propagation process within the conditioning module. Our
021            approach can not only capture the characteristics of propagation patterns effec-
022            tively but also adapt to real-world patterns quickly on synthetic propagation data
023            when domain data is limited. Evaluations of various datasets demonstrate ASLD-
024            iff’s superior effectiveness, accuracy, and adaptability in real-world applications,
025            showcasing its robust performance across different localization scenarios. The
026            code can be found at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ASLDiff-4FE0.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In today’s highly interconnected world, network propagation issues, such as misinformation spread,
032   cyber threats, and infrastructure failures, have far-reaching consequences for society. The ability to
033
      quickly identify the source of these disruptions is critical for mitigating their impact. By analyzing
      snapshots of affected networks, we can trace the origin of the spread, a process essential for man-
034
      aging crises like disease outbreaks (Ru et al., 2023), enhancing network security (Kephart & White,
035
      1993), and preventing further damage in scenarios such as power grid failures (Amin & Schewe,
036   2007).
037
038   Early methods (Lappas et al., 2010; Shah & Zaman, 2012; Prakash et al., 2012; Luo et al., 2013; Zhu
039
      & Ying, 2014a;b) for source localization in networks rely on metrics or heuristics derived from the
      network’s topology, applicable only to specific propagation models like the Susceptible-Infected (SI)
040
      or Independent Cascade (IC) models. Notably, Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2017) overcome this limita-
041
      tion by introducing a label propagation algorithm based on the intuition of source prominence (Shah
042   & Zaman, 2011), but still neglect the indeterminacy of information propagation that corresponds to
043   the uncertain nature of source localization. Besides, data-driven methods (Dong et al., 2019; Wang
044   et al., 2022; Hou et al., 2023) are also free from the propagation model limitation as they directly
045   learn a graph neural network (GNN) to capture the propagation process exhibited in empirical data.
046   Recently, deep generative models including variational autoencoders (Ling et al., 2022), normaliza-
047   tion flows (Xu et al., 2024) and diffusion models (Huang et al., 2023a; Yan et al., 2024) have been
048   adopted for solving the source localization problem, as they can quantify the indeterminacy in source
049   localization by learning the empirical data distribution and promote the state-of-the-art outcomes.
050   However, collecting real-world propagation data is difficult and costly, posing significant require-
051   ments on source localization models that can adapt to real-world environments with limited data.
052   This brings up two main following challenges. Firstly, real-world networks typically exhibit un-
053   known propagation patterns, which becomes far more challenging to characterize when data is
      limited. In this regard, existing learning-based methods (Dong et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022; Ling


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2024) rely purely on data to gain an understanding of the propagation pat-
055   terns, limiting their capability to generalize in unseen scenarios. Secondly, complex interrelations
056   between propagation patterns and network topology are difficult to capture with limited data.
057   Existing deep learning methods rely on a large amount of labeled data from the target network (i.e.,
058   identified source nodes from historical propagation) to account for the impact of structural hetero-
059   geneity on propagation patterns. However, these models struggle to generalize to new networks
060   when insufficient training data is available.
061   Therefore, in this paper, we propose a novel method, namely Adaptive Source Localization Diffsion
062   Model (ASLDiff), to achieve accurate and robust source localization across different network topol-
063   ogy and propagation patterns, especially under limited real-world data scenarios. Specifically, we
064   propose leveraging the diffusion model (DM) Ho et al. (2020) to tackle the complex source distribu-
065   tion conditioned on the network topology and the current observation of node states for the source
066   localization problem. To address the above two challenges, we enhance the purely data-driven ap-
067   proach by incorporating principles of information propagation—specifically, the prominence of the
068   source and the centrality of rumors—into the design of a conditional diffusion model. First, we pro-
069
      pose leveraging pre-calculated source estimations from a label propagation method and using them
      as informative priors to guide the diffusion and sampling process within the DM framework. This
070
      prior knowledge provides consistent guidance when specific information about the propagation pat-
071
      tern is limited. Second, to improve the predictive capability of the denoising network for the source
072   distribution, we enhance it with a conditional input that encodes propagation principles, i.e., the
073   prominence and centrality of nodes in relation to the infected nodes. To obtain this information, we
074   devise a label propagation process and parameterize it using a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN)
075   based architecture, allowing it to better fit empirical data in an inductive learning manner and capture
076   universal propagation patterns across diverse network topologies.
077
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
078   (1) We propose a diffusion model-based method ASLDiff for source localization, which effectively
079   learns from simulation and real-world data. ASLDiff effectively captures characteristics of propa-
080   gation patterns, demonstrating significant practical applicability across diverse scenarios.
081   (2) We design an innovative conditional diffusion model that incorporates principles of information
082   propagation for improved source distribution prediction. This includes a prior-guided diffusion pro-
083   cess and a propagation-enhanced conditional denoiser.
084   (3) We evaluate the performance of ASLDiff against state-of-the-art methods under various prop-
085   agation patterns and real network datasets. Additionally, we assess the model’s generalizability
086
      across different network topologies and propagation patterns, demonstrating its ability to overcome
      the identified challenges. ASLDiff shows a 7.5%-12.1% improvement in real-world propagation
087
      datasets, highlighting its accuracy and adaptability.

092   2.1   S OURCE L OCALIZATION
093
094   As the inverse problem of information propagation on networks, source localization refers to infer-
095   ring the initial propagation sources given the current diffused observation, such as the states of the
096   specified sensors or a snapshot of the whole network status (Shelke & Attar, 2019). It can be applied
097   to tasks like rumor source identification and finding the origin of rolling blackouts in intelligent
098   power grids (Shelke & Attar, 2019). Early approaches focus on single-source identification (Shah
099
      & Zaman, 2011; Zhu & Ying, 2014a;b). For example, Shah & Zaman (2011) develop a rumor-
      centrality-based maximum likelihood estimator under the Susceptible-Infected (SI) (Kermack &
100
      McKendrick, 1927) propagation pattern. Later, methods devised for multiple source localization
101
      have been proposed (Lappas et al., 2010; Luo et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2019;
102   Wang et al., 2022). However, most previous approaches fail to model the uncertainty of the location
103   of sources, as the forward propagation process is stochastic. To overcome this, generative models
104   have been adopted. SLVAE (Ling et al., 2022) utilizes the Variational Auto-Encoders (VAEs) back-
105   bone and optimizes the posterior for better prediction. However, it is difficult to converge when the
106   propagation pattern is complicated due to the nature of VAEs. DDMSL (Yan et al., 2024) models
107   the Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) (Kermack & McKendrick, 1927)infection process into
      the discrete Diffusion Model (DM) (Ho et al., 2020), and design a reversible residual block based


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016). However, it requires additional
109   data of the intermediate propagation states and cannot be generalized to other propagation patterns.
110   Our method demonstrates superior functionality and adaptability for real-world applications, requir-
111   ing fewer input data while addressing existing limitations, thus offering greater practical value. We
112   provide a comparison of typical multiple source localization methods in the Appendix A.

      3     P RELIMINARIES

116   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
117
118   Our research problem is formulated as follows. Given an undirected social network G = (V, E)
119   where V is the node set, E is the edge set, and Y = {Y1 , . . . , Y|V | } is an infection state of all nodes
120   in G, which describes that a subset of nodes in G have been infected. Each Yi ∈ {1, 0} denotes the
121   infection state of node vi ∈ V , where Yi = 1 indicates that vi is infected and otherwise Yi = 0
122   indicates it is uninfected. We aim to find the original propagation source X̂ from the propagated
123   observation Y , so that the loss with the ground Truth source set X ∗ ∈ {1, 0}|V |×1 is minimized,
124   i.e. X̂ = argminX ||X − X ∗ ||22 . To account for the uncertainty in source localization, we need to
125   construct a probabilistic model P (X|Y, G), which can be used to sample for the final prediction.
126
127   3.2   T YPICAL P ROPAGATION M ODELS
128
      Information propagation estimation involves approximating and reproducing the spread of informa-
129
      tion in a network and providing explanations based on propagation sources. This task has applica-
130
      tions in event prediction (Zhao, 2021), adverse event detection (Wang & Zhao, 2018), and disease
131   spread prediction (Tang et al., 2023). Models for this purpose fall into two main categories: in-
132   fection models and influence models. Infection models, such as the Susceptible-Infected (SI) and
133   Susceptible-Infected-Susceptible (SIS), manage transitions between susceptible and infected sta-
134   tuses in networks, offering different switching paths for these changes (Kermack & McKendrick,
135   1927; Keeling & Eames, 2005). Specifically, every infected node attempts to infect adjacent nodes
136   with probability β at each iteration. However, in the SIS model, infected nodes might revert to being
137   susceptible with a certain probability λ. A more complex case is the Susceptible-Infected-Recovered
138   (SIR) model, which additionally considers the recovered state.
139   Independent Cascade (IC) and Linear Threshold (LT) (Kempe et al., 2003) are two typical influence
140   models examining how influence spreads in social networks or infrastructure networks. The IC
141   model involves nodes that can either be active or inactive. The process begins with a set of initial
142   active nodes. At each step, any newly activated node can activate its inactive neighbors with a single
143   chance. The probability of activation is dependent on the weight of the edge between nodes. As
144   for the LT model, each inactive node becomes active only if it receives enough influence (over a
145
      threshold) from its neighbors.
146

152   more infected nodes, while the centrality of sources shows that nodes far from the source are less

154   world data by our analysis in the Appendix B. Based on these ideas, they propose to perform label
155   propagations on the observation state of the network. By setting Y [Y = 0] = −1 and Z t=0 ←     − Y,

273   previously described empirical knowledge of source nodes, we aim for this module to extract ef-

276   propagation (Wang et al., 2017), which firstly labels the infected or influenced nodes in a network as
277   the positive integer 1, while labeling the other nodes as -1. By propagating these labels throughout
278   the network, the features of proximity and centrality are captured. However, the rigid and ho-
279
      mogeneous nature of this propagation process lacks the requisite flexibility and adaptive learning

346   each cascade in both sets, we designate the infected nodes at the first 10% of the propagation time as
347   source nodes and take the network’s infection status at 30% of the propagation time as observation
348   input. In the context of real-world applications, we often can only collect sufficient data for analysis
349
      after some time has elapsed since the occurrence of the event. Therefore, attempting to predict what
      initially happened in the process when we have observed enough propagation patterns at a certain
350
      degree of infection time is very much in line with the needs of real-world operations. Please refer to

      the Appendix D for specific details of the datasets.

466   5.3.1 T RANSFER FROM S IMULATED P ROPAGATION PATTERN
467   To validate our method’s transferability and few-shot/zero-shot learning capabilities in real-world
468   scenarios, we simulate pre-training data using established propagation models (IC+SIS) within ac-
469   tual networks. ASLDiff undergoes pre-training and is fine-tuned on real propagation pattern data,
470   which effectively addresses the scarcity of real data by utilizing simulation data. Figures 4 present
471   the results on the Digg and Twitter datasets, respectively, compared with baseline GCNSI and LPSI,
472
      including no pre-training condition. These methods identify the sources without using the infor-
      mation of the underlying propagation model, which is appropriate for comparison. In the Digg
473
      dataset, ASLDiff, pre-trained on simulated data, requires only 3% of the real dataset for fine-tuning
474
      to achieve optimal performance, while models without pre-training need about 50%, demonstrating
475   our model’s few-shot capability. In the Twitter dataset, our pre-trained model can even reach opti-
476   mal performance without additional fine-tuning, demonstrating our model’s zero-shot capability. In
477   contrast, GCNSI cannot surpass pre-trained ASLDiff with any amount of fine-tuning data in both
478   datasets. This is attributed to the fact that GCNSI simply inputs designed features into the GNN
479   model, which is insufficient to enable it to capture the general distribution laws of different propaga-
480   tion patterns. LPSI, as a non-learning method, is incapable of learning superior features and patterns
481   from the simulation data and thus fails to surpass the pretrained ASLDiff.

937   We compare the performance of ASLDiff against three state-of-the-art baselines of source localiza-
938
      tion methods using propagation snapshot observations. To the best of our knowledge, these methods
      are the only ones that illustrate their superiority against other works on locating sources without
939
      knowing the underlying propagation pattern, which is the same as ours. The detailed information is
940
      presented as follows.
941
942         • NetSleuth (Prakash et al., 2012) utilizes a minimum description length approach to fil-
943           ter nodes from multiple sources, yet it is exclusively designed to operate within the
944           Susceptible-Infected (SI) model framework.
945
946         • LPSI (Wang et al., 2017) is a novel method for detecting multiple sources of information
947           diffusion in networks without a predefined propagation model, leveraging the concept of
948
              source prominence and label propagation to identify probable sources based on local peaks
              in the propagation landscape. In our experiment, the parameter α in LPSI is determined
949
              by testing it among the values {0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9} for each evaluation dataset and then
950
              selecting the best one.
951
952         • GCNSI (Dong et al., 2019) introduces a deep learning approach for identifying multiple
953           rumor sources in social networks without needing the underlying propagation model, us-
954           ing graph convolutional networks to enhance prediction precision through spectral domain
955           convolution and multi-order neighbor information. The setting of this model follows the
956           description in (Dong et al., 2019).
957
            • SLVAE (Ling et al., 2022) is a probabilistic framework designed to tackle the challenge of
958
              source localization in graph diffusion problems using a variational autoencoder approach to
959           quantify uncertainty and leverage prior knowledge. We follow the original implementation
960           in the paper, tune the learning rate from 0.001 to 0.05, and select the best one.
961
962         • TGASI (Hou et al., 2023) is a sequence-to-sequence framework for multiple rumor source
963           detection that considers heterogeneous user behavior in time-varying scenarios. It uses a
964           GNN-based encoder to generate multiple features and a GRU-based decoder with temporal
965
              attention to infer sources. TGASI is designed with transferability and uses a unique loss
              function.
966
967         • DDMSL (Yan et al., 2024) proposes a novel probabilistic model for source localization
968           and diffusion path reconstruction in complex networks. By formulating information prop-
969           agation as a discrete diffusion process, DDMSL employs a reversible residual network to
970           construct a denoising-diffusion model in discrete space. This approach allows for both
971           accurate source identification and comprehensive reconstruction of information diffusion
              pathways.

1001
1002   We perform additional experiments on how the maximum diffusion step affects the performance and
1003   time consumption of ASLDiff on jazz and network science datasets, which use the SIS propagation
1004   model for data synthesis. The results are shown in Figure 9 and Figure 10. It can be observed that
1005   when the diffusion step increases, the performance also improves. Specifically, the improvement
1006
       from the lowest to the highest f1-score in the jazz dataset is about 27%, which is higher than that
       in the network science dataset (17%). We also evaluate the performance when the diffusion step
1007
       becomes 1, which makes the model a VAE, and ASLDiff (F1) performance drops by nearly 90%.
1008
       The performance and diffusion steps show a positive correlation, demonstrating the beneficial effect
1009   of the diffusion framework. Sampling difficulty decreases as noise is more accurately added in the
1010   forward diffusion process. It is also reasonable that the sampling time increases as the diffusion step
1011   becomes higher. Hence, the tradeoff should be clearly considered when choosing the appropriate
1012   maximum diffusion timestep.

1015   H    L IMITATIONS
1016
1017
       Our proposed method also exhibits certain limitations. Our approach may, to some extent, depend
1018
       on the accuracy of the advice provided by soft labels, despite our application of various sophisticated
1019
       designs to enhance the model’s adaptability. As a result, when confronted with more complex sce-
1020   narios, our method might reveal limitations. On the other hand, the sampling speed of our multi-step
1021   diffusion model may be slower compared to some deep learning methods, which could become a bot-
1022   tleneck for applications requiring real-time localization. While computational constraints currently
1023   limit our model’s direct application to million-node networks, the core principles we developed can
1024   be integrated into hierarchical approaches. This hierarchical strategy would effectively reduce the
1025   network scale, allowing us to leverage our method’s proven strength in accurate source localization
       for moderately-sized networks. We will continue to conduct in-depth research in these areas.
