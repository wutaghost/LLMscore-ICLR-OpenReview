011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             Solving systems of linear equations is a fundamental problem, but it can be compu-
014
                tationally intensive for classical algorithms in high dimensions. Existing quantum
                algorithms can achieve exponential speedups for the quantum linear system problem
015
                (QLSP) in terms of the problem dimension, but even such a theoretical advantage
016
                is bottlenecked by the condition number of the coefficient matrix. In this work,
017             we propose a new quantum algorithm for QLSP inspired by the classical proximal
018             point algorithm (PPA). Our proposed method can be viewed as a meta-algorithm
019             that allows inverting a modified matrix via an existing QLSP solver, thereby
020             directly approximating the solution vector instead of approximating the inverse
021             of the coefficient matrix. By carefully choosing the step size η, the proposed
022             algorithm can effectively precondition the linear system to mitigate the dependence
023             on condition numbers that hindered the applicability of previous approaches. Im-
024             portantly, this is the first framework for QLSP where a tunable parameter η allows
025
                the user to control the trade-off between the runtime and the approximation error.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Background. Solving systems of linear equations is a fundamental problem with many applications
030   spanning science and engineering. Mathematically, for a given (Hermitian) matrix A ∈ CN ×N and
031   vector b ∈ CN , the goal is to find the N -dimensional vector x⋆ that satisfies Ax⋆ = b. While classical
032   algorithms, such as Gaussian elimination (Gauss, 1877; Higham, 2011), conjugate gradient method
033   (Hestenes & Stiefel, 1952), LU factorization (Schwarzenberg-Czerny, 1995; Shabat et al., 2018),
034   QR factorization (Francis, 1961; Kublanovskaya, 1962), and iterative Krylov subspace methods
035   (Krylov, 1931; Nocedal & Wright, 1999), can solve this problem, their complexity scales (at worst)
036   cubically with N , the dimension of A, motivating the development of quantum algorithms that could
037
      potentially achieve speedups.
038   Indeed, for the quantum linear system problem (QLSP) in Definition 1,1 Harrow, Hassidim, and
039   Lloyd (a.k.a. the HHL algorithm) (Harrow et al., 2009) showed that the dependence on the problem
040   dimension exponentially reduces to O (poly log(N )), with query complexity of O(κ2 /ε), under
041   some (quantum) access model for A and b (c.f., Definitions 2 and 3). Here, κ is the condition number
042   of A, defined as the ratio of the largest to the smallest singular value of A. Subsequent works, such as
043
      the work by Ambainis (Ambainis, 2012) and by Childs, Kothari, and Somma (Childs et al., 2017)
      (a.k.a. the CKS algorithm) improve the dependence on κ and ε; see Table 1. The best quantum
044
      algorithm for QLSP is based on the discrete adiabatic theorem (Costa et al., 2022), achieving the
045
      query complexity of O(κ · log(1/ε)), matching the lower bound (Orsucci & Dunjko, 2021).2
046
047   Solving QLSP is a fundamental subroutine in many quantum algorithms. For instance, it is used in
048   quantum recommendation systems (Kerenidis & Prakash, 2016), quantum SVM (Rebentrost et al.,
049
      2014), unsupervised learning (Wiebe et al., 2014), and solving differential equations (Liu et al.,
      2021), to name a few. Hence, improving the overall runtime of a generic QLSP solver is crucial in
050
      developing more sophisticated and efficient quantum algorithms.

      Challenges in existing methodologies. A common limitation of the existing quantum algorithms
055   is that the dependence on the condition number κ must be small to achieve the quantum advantage.
056   To put more context, for any quantum algorithm in Table 1 to achieve an exponential advantage
057   over classical algorithms, κ needs to be in the order of poly log(N ), where N is the dimension of A.
058   For instance, for N = 103 , κ needs to be around 4 to exhibit the exponential advantage. However,
059   condition numbers are often large in real-world problems (Papyan, 2020).
060
      Moreover, it was proven (Orsucci & Dunjko, 2021, Proposition 6) that for QLSP, even when A is
061   positive-definite, the dependence on condition number cannot be improved from O(κ). This is
062
                                                                                                     √ in
      contrast to the classical algorithms, such as the conjugate gradient method, which achieves O( κ)
063   reduction in complexity for the linear systems of equations with A ≻ 0. Such observation reinforces
064   the importance of alleviating the dependence on the condition number κ for quantum algorithms,
065   which is our aim.
066
      Our contributions. We present a novel meta-algorithm for solving the QLSP based on the proximal
067   point algorithm (PPA) (Rockafellar, 1976; Güler, 1991); see Algorithm 1. Notably, in contrast to some
068   existing methods that approximate A−1 (Harrow et al., 2009; Ambainis, 2012; Childs et al., 2017;
069   Gribling et al., 2021; Orsucci & Dunjko, 2021), our algorithm directly approximates x⋆ = A−1 b
070   through an iterative process based on PPA. Classically, PPA is known to improve the “conditioning”
071   of the problem at hand, compared to the gradient descent (Toulis et al., 2014; Toulis & Airoldi, 2017;
072   Ahn & Sra, 2022); it can also be accelerated (Güler, 1992; Kim et al., 2022).
073   An approximate proximal point algorithm for classical convex optimization has been proposed under
074   the name of “catalyst” (Lin et al., 2015) in the machine learning community. Our proposed method
075   operates similarly and can be viewed as a generic acceleration scheme for QLSP where one can plug

479   5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
480
481   In this work, we proposed a novel quantum algorithm for solving the quantum linear systems problem
482   (QLSP), based on the proximal point algorithm (PPA). Specifically, we showed that implementing a
483   single-step PPA is possible by utilizing existing QLSP solvers. We designed a meta-algorithm where
484   any QLSP solver can be utilized as a subroutine to improve the dependence on the condition number.
485   Even the current best quantum algorithm (Costa et al., 2022) can be significantly accelerated via
      Algorithm 1, especially when the problem is ill-conditioned.
