009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) are typically specialized for domain tasks through
012            supervised fine-tuning, which optimizes LLMs for likelihood-based objectives.
013            While supervised fine-tuning enables LLMs to generate text that conforms to the
014            language style of a specific domain, such as radiology, it often falls short in en-
015            hancing the model’s ability to perform detailed diagnostic reasoning that tailors
016
               to the need of individual patients. In this paper, we explore the use of reinforce-
               ment learning to better align LLMs with the intricate requirements of radiological
017
               practice. By framing the report generation process as sequential decision-making
018
               stages, we present Radiology-Guided Reinforcement Optimization (RGRO), a tai-
019            lored policy optimization framework designed specifically for medical language
020            tasks. RGRO moves beyond conventional likelihood-based training by directly
021            optimizing for radiology-specific objectives, including consistency with radiol-
022            ogy findings and adherence to established professional guidelines. Our empirical
023            evaluations demonstrate that RGRO significantly enhances the diagnostic preci-
024            sion and clinical utility of radiology reports generated by LLMs, outperforming
025            supervised fine-tuning methods and state-of-the-art models. Furthermore, RGRO
026            enables the seamless integration of expert radiologist feedback and external diag-
027
               nostic tools, all without the need for large-scale annotated datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The advent of large language models (LLMs) marks a major milestone in the field of natural lan-
032   guage processing, and has led to unprecedented advancements in various fields (Elyoseph et al.,
033   2024; Dagdelen et al., 2024; Wagner & Ertl-Wagner, 2023) such as text generation, text compre-
034   hension, and interactive dialogue. To adapt to target use cases, models are typically fine-tuned on
035   large corpora using supervised learning techniques that optimize likelihood-based objectives (Min
036   et al., 2023). This fine-tuning process enables them to excel at generating coherent and contextually
037   relevant text, aligning with the patterns observed in the training data. However, while fine-tuning
038   enhances language contextualization and stylistic adherence, it does not address the deeper require-
039
      ments of specialized domains, where reasoning and insights are paramount (Bhayana, 2024; Tang
      et al., 2023). Specifically, radiologists generate reports by synthesizing both positive and negative
040
      findings from medical images before forming a diagnostic impression (Omiye et al., 2024). This
041
      process demands not only language fluency but also meticulous diagnostic reasoning, adherence to
042   clinical standards, and relevance to individual patient cases. Given the high stakes involved in med-
043   ical diagnoses, precision and reliability in generated reports are crucial. These challenges call for a
044   paradigm shift in how LLMs are trained and applied in specialized fields.
045
      Conventional methods for adapting LLMs to radiology rely on prompt engineering and supervised
046
      fine-tuning (Yan et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2022). While these ap-
047
      proaches can infuse some domain-specific features into the model, it is heavily reliant on the avail-
048   ability of large-scale, high-quality annotated data—a scarce resource in the medical field (Bhayana,
049   2024; Liu et al., 2023a) due to confidentiality concerns, regulatory restrictions, and the considerable
050   effort required for expert annotations. Moreover, traditional likelihood-based training objectives,
051   which aim to maximize the probability of observed text sequences, may not align with the nuanced
052   demands of radiological practice (Lecler et al., 2023). This misalignment can result in outputs
053   that, although linguistically coherent, lack clinical efficacy or fail to adhere to professional guide-
      lines (Zhong et al., 2023). This gap underscores a critical challenge in deploying LLMs for decision


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      reasoning process and highlights the need for the alignment strategies tailored to domain-specific
055   requirements.
056
057   In this paper, we present radiology-guided reinforcement optimization (RGRO), a novel policy op-
058
      timization framework (Buffet et al., 2020) designed explicitly for radiology. By modeling the ra-
      diology report generation process as a continuous multi-stage decision phase, we first leverage in-
059
      struction fine-tuning to adapt LLMs to comprehend and synthesize detailed findings into coherent
060
      impressions (Nie et al., 2018). This initial phase conditions the model on the unique characteristics
061   of radiological language, training it to distill intricate imaging observations into meaningful sum-
062   maries. Subsequently, RGRO employs reinforcement learning guided by enhancement feedback
063   leveraging generative language models to iteratively align the model’s outputs with expert-driven
064   benchmarks based on explicit reward signals and directly optimizes for radiology-specific objectives.
065   This approach reduces reliance on the typically scarce and costly large-scale annotated datasets in
066   the medical domain. Distinctly differing from traditional likelihood-based training schemes, RGRO
067   focuses on enhancing fine-grained reasoning (Cosentino & Shekkizhar, 2024) performance within
068   a specific domain. Rather than relying solely on likelihood maximization, we directly optimize
069
      for domain-specific goals. This perspective allows the model to learn strategies that are not only
      generally plausible but are meticulously tailored to the radiological context.
070
071   Our contributions include:
072
073
             • We develop RGRO, a reinforcement learning-based policy optimization framework specif-
               ically tailored for medical language tasks, which transforms the report generation process
074
               into a sequential decision-making problem.
075
076          • By formulating custom reward functions, RGRO directly optimizes critical domain-specific
077
               objectives, such as clinical accuracy, diagnostic reasoning, and adherence to established
               reporting standards.
078
079          • Our approach enables the incorporation of pre-existing expert radiologist insights and exter-
080
               nal diagnostic information, which reduces the dependency on large-scale annotated datasets
               and addresses data scarcity challenges.
081
082   Through comprehensive experiments, we demonstrate that RGRO significantly improves the diag-
083   nostic precision and clinical utility of generated radiology reports, outperforming the state-of-the-art
084   models. While tailored for radiology, our framework lays the groundwork for applying reinforce-
085   ment learning to other specialized domains requiring precise and context-specific tasks.

089
      The general capabilities of large language models. Large language models have demonstrated
090   impressive capabilities in natural language understanding and generation. They can perform vari-
091   ous tasks such as text summarization, translation, question answering, sentiment analysis, and be-
092   yond (Zhou et al., 2023). The expressive transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017) enables LLMs
093   to capture complex relationships within data. Furthermore, fine-tuning techniques enable LLMs
094   to adapt to specific tasks or domains, which enhances their performance and applicability across
095   different fields.
096   Large language models for radiology report generation. In the medical domain, large language
097   models (Vaswani, 2017) have shown significant potential in generating radiology reports (Ma et al.,
098   2024; Bhayana, 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b). By understanding the context and
099   structure of radiology findings (Yuan et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020), these models can assist in
100   creating accurate and coherent reports. The ability of LLMs to synthesize information from various
101   sources allows for the generation of reports that adhere to clinical standards while ensuring clarity
102   and precision (Liu et al., 2019).
103   Reasoning capabilities of large language models. Large language models exhibit notable reason-
104   ing capabilities that enable them to process and analyze complex information (Kojima et al., 2022).
105   They can perform logical reasoning, make inferences, and understand nuanced prompts, which is
106   essential for tasks requiring deep comprehension. These reasoning abilities arise from training on
107   diverse datasets, which allows LLMs to understand context, relationships, and implications (Li et al.,
      2024). However, since LLMs are autoregressive models that generate text one token at a time based


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on previously generated tokens, they often face challenges in maintaining consistent and coherent
109   reasoning over long sequences. This autoregressive nature (Dalal et al., 2019) can lead to difficulties
110   in handling complex logical structures and maintaining accuracy across multiple steps of reasoning,
111   limiting their performance in tasks requiring deep, multi-step inference (Kalyanpur et al., 2024).
112
      Applying reinforcement learning to align with physicians. Reinforcement learning (RL) has
113
      become crucial for enhancing decision-making in large language models, refining model behavior
114
      based on user feedback (Kaufmann et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022). Previously, Proximal Policy
115   Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), a reinforcement learning algorithm, demonstrated how
116   policies could be efficiently updated using a surrogate objective, ensuring stability while allowing
117   multiple updates. More recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) has
118   been introduced to align models with human preferences while retaining their domain knowledge,
119   ensuring that fine-tuning does not compromise the expertise gained during pretraining. Applying
120   DPO to radiology impression generation represents a novel approach, offering a promising way to
121   adapt LLMs to meet the specific requirements of medical professionals.

248   In this study, we utilized LLM Parser (OpenAI, 2023) as a substitute for human evaluators to stream-
249

      on the model’s outputs, we leveraged LLM Parser to select the preferred output between two gener-
250
      ated responses. Specifically, for each pair of outputs from the model, LLM Parser was tasked with
251
      determining which output better aligned with the desired criteria, such as accuracy, relevance, and
252   clarity. This approach allowed for a more efficient and consistent evaluation process, reducing the
253   reliance on human involvement while maintaining a high standard of feedback quality.
254
255

256

257

258   accepted responses, while the samples unpreferred by LLM Parser serve as the rejected responses.
259
260   In this framework, we construct preference data as tuples (fi , impa , impr ), where fi represents the
261

262
      which to ”reject” (impr ), based on alignment with expert radiologist annotations. By comparing
263
      high-quality, expert-aligned impressions with those that are deemed suboptimal by the LLM Parser,
264   our approach leverages domain-specific expertise to guide the model’s learning process. By employ-

314
      To evaluate the performance of our model in generating clinically relevant and accurate radiology
315
      impressions, we employed two widely used metrics: ROUGE and BERTScore.
316
317   ROUGE (Lin, 2004) (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation): ROUGE is a set
318   of metrics for evaluating text generation by comparing the overlap between the generated text and
319   reference text. ROUGE-N measures n-gram overlap, such as Rouge-1 (R1) and Rouge-2 (R2), while
320
      ROUGE-L (RL) focuses on the longest common subsequence. Higher ROUGE scores indicate
      greater similarity to the reference, making it useful for assessing alignment with expert annotations.
321
322   BERTScore: BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2020) uses pre-trained BERT embeddings to evaluate the
323   semantic similarity between generated and reference texts. Unlike ROUGE, which focuses on token
      overlap, BERTScore captures deeper contextual meaning by computing cosine similarity between

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      token embeddings, making it ideal for assessing clinically relevant impressions. By combining
325   ROUGE and BERTScore, we evaluate both lexical similarity and semantic alignment with expert-
326   generated impressions.

476   6     D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
477
478   In this paper, we presented Radiology-Guided Reinforcement Optimization (RGRO), a fundamental
479   reinforcement learning-based framework designed to optimize large language models for radiology
480   reports. By framing the generation process as a sequential decision-making task, RGRO enables
481   the model to better align with clinical requirements, moving beyond traditional likelihood-based
482
      objectives. Our empirical results demonstrate that RGRO improves diagnostic accuracy and clinical
      utility compared to supervised fine-tuning and other baseline models.
483
484   While RGRO shows significant promise in enhancing the alignment of LLMs with radiology-
485   specific objectives, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the scope of our experiments
      was limited to a subset of radiology tasks, primarily focusing on diagnostic impressions derived


                                                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      from radiology reports. Future work could explore more diverse medical specialties and imaging
487   modalities to further validate the generalizability of our approach. Additionally, our reliance on
488   expert-labeled datasets, though partially mitigated by reinforcement learning, still presents chal-
489   lenges in terms of scalability, as large-scale expert annotations remain costly and time-consuming.
490
      Another limitation lies in the potential problem of overoptimization, a common issue in reinforce-
491
      ment learning when models become excessively tuned to specific feedback, diverging from the
492
      broader distribution of acceptable outputs. In this work, we did not explicitly address this con-
493   cern, leaving it as an important direction for future research. Expanding the framework to include
494   mechanisms for preventing overfitting to specific datasets or feedback loops is critical for ensuring
495   robustness across diverse clinical environments. Future iterations could incorporate more dynamic
496   and interactive feedback mechanisms, enabling models to adapt continuously to evolving clinical
497   guidelines and practices.
498
      RGRO is cost-effective compared to traditional approaches. Moreover, the use of reinforcement
499   learning in specialized domains provides a flexible mechanism to incorporate real-time feedback and
500   evolving clinical standards. While the RGRO framework shows great potential in generating better
501   radiology reports through reinforcement learning, further exploration into data diversity, feedback
502   mechanisms, and the prevention of overoptimization is necessary. We hope this work provides a
503   foundational step toward targeted optimization of LLMs for medical applications and improves the
504   practical utility of AI-driven radiology solutions.
