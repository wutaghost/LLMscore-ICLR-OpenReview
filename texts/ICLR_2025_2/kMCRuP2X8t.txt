                                                  A BSTRACT
013
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become increasingly popular in recom-
014
               mender systems due to their ability to model complex user-item relationships.
015
               However, current GNN-based approaches face several challenges: They primarily
016            rely on sparse user-item interaction data, which can lead to overfitting and limit
017            generalization performance. Moreover, they often overlook additional valuable
018            information sources, such as social trust and user reviews, which can provide
019            deeper insights into user preferences and enhance recommendation accuracy. To
020            address these limitations, we propose a multi-view GNN framework that inte-
021            grates diverse information sources using contrastive learning and language models.
022            Our method employs a lightweight Graph Convolutional Network (LightGCN)
023            on user-item interactions to generate initial user and item representations. We
024
               use an attention mechanism for the user view to integrate social trust information
               with user-generated textual reviews, which are transformed into high-dimensional
025
               vectors using a pre-trained language model. Similarly, we aggregate all reviews
026
               associated with each item and use language models to generate item representations
027            for the item view. We then construct an item graph by applying a meta-path to
028            the user-item interactions. GCNs are applied to both the social trust network and
029            the item graph, generating enriched embeddings for users and items. To align and
030            unify these heterogeneous data sources, we employ a contrastive learning mecha-
031            nism that ensures consistent and complementary representations across different
032            views. Experimental results on multiple real-world datasets such as Epinions, Yelp,
033            and Ciao demonstrate significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art
034            methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Recommender systems are powerful techniques widely adopted in various domains, including e-
      commerce platformsWang et al. (2020), online advertisingGharibshah and Zhu (2021), and video
040
      streaming services Liu et al. (2019). The main task of recommender systems is to predict whether
041
      a user will interact with a specific item. Collaborative filtering (CF) methods are among the most
042   successful approaches in recommender systems, effectively predicting how likely a user is to interact
043   with specific items Wang et al. (2019b). However, collaborative filtering (CF) methods face challenges
044   like data sparsity and the cold start problem. Data sparsity occurs when there are limited interactions
045   between users and items, making it difficult to generate accurate recommendations. GNNs have
046   significantly succeeded in recommender systems due to effectively capturing complex user-item
047   interactionsChen et al.He et al. (2020). Unlike traditional methods, GNNs can model direct and
048   indirect relationships by leveraging the graph structure of user-item interactions. This allows them
049   to learn rich, high-quality representations that account for collaborative patterns across the entire
050
      network. GNNs have successfully addressed challenges such as data sparsity and the cold start
      problem, leading to more accurate and personalized recommendations in domains like e-commerce,
051
      social media, and content streaming.
052
053   Neural Graph Collaborative Filtering (NGCF)Wang et al. (2019b) and LightGCNHe et al. (2020) are
      examples of successful GNN-based recommender models that have made significant contributions

054
      to improving recommendation accuracy. These models use graph convolution to capture local
055   information and aggregate data from neighboring nodes, incorporating collaborative signals into user
056   and item representations. GNNs in recommender systems rely heavily on user-item interactions,
057   which are often sparse, leading to several challenges, such as limiting the model’s ability to learn
058   meaningful patterns, propagating noisy information, which reduces the accuracy, over-smoothing,
059   and scalability issues. To address these issues, contrastive learning has recently been employed
060   to provide more robust and high-quality representations for users and items Yu et al. (2023). By
061   leveraging both positive interactions (e.g., items a user interacts with) and negative samples (e.g.,
062   items they don’t interact with), contrastive learning helps the model differentiate between similar and
063
      dissimilar user-item interactions, resulting in more discriminative embeddings and improving the
      overall recommendation accuracy, even in sparse data environments. However, additional sources of
064
      information, such as user social interactions and user reviews, are available in the form of unstructured
065
      text and can be utilized to improve the accuracy of GNN-based recommender systems. The question
066   is how to effectively integrate multi-source information, like user social data and reviews, into GNN-
067   based models. Specifically, since user reviews are unstructured, how can we leverage them to extract
068   meaningful semantic information and combine this data with GNNs to enhance recommendation
069   performance?
070
      Present work. The present work proposes a multi-view graph representation learning strategy
071
      designed explicitly for recommender systems, addressing the limitations of traditional methods that
072   often rely on sparse user-item matrices. By integrating multiple sources of information, such as user
073   trust relationships and user reviews, this approach creates a more robust and informative framework.
074   Incorporating user trust networks captures implicit connections between users, significantly enhancing
075   recommendation accuracy by factoring in social influences on user preferences. Additionally, the use
076   of a pre-trained language model like BERT allows the system to transform user-generated reviews
077   into meaningful embeddings, leveraging the nuanced sentiments expressed in these texts to enrich
078   user and item representations. A contrastive learning mechanism aligns these diverse representations,
079   ensuring consistency and complementarity among the various data sources, which fosters a cohesive
080
      understanding of user preferences and item characteristics. By embracing this multi-view perspective,
      the method effectively captures the complexities of user behavior and item attributes, offering a
081
      more comprehensive solution compared to existing graph neural network-based recommenders
082
      that typically focus on a singular view. The key properties of the proposed method are listed as follows:
083
084   • The proposed method incorporates trust relationships and user reviews alongside tradi-
085   tional user-item interactions, enriching the data landscape for representation learning.
086
087   • By utilizing pre-trained language models like BERT for review embeddings, the method
088   uses advanced natural language processing techniques to gain deeper insights into user sentiments
089   and preferences.
090
091
      • The contrastive learning approach used in the method helps integration of data taken from
      various sources.
092
093
      • The integration of user trust dynamics enables the model to adapt to the social context of
094   interactions, providing a more personalized recommendation experience.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      GNN-based recommender systems: GNNs have emerged as a powerful approach in graph-based
      recommender systems by effectively capturing complex relationships within user-item networksTang
100
      et al. (2008). These networks leverage the inherent graph structure of interactions to learn meaningful
101
      patterns. Typically, GNNs operate using the message-passing framework, where information is
102   propagated and aggregated across multiple layers. This process allows the model to incorporate
103   local and global information from neighboring nodes, enabling it to learn more expressive and rich
104   representations for users and items. GNNs model user-item interactions as a graph to generate user
105   and item embeddings by leveraging cross-layer information propagation. This process allows the
106   model to capture both local and global patterns in the graph, as information from neighboring nodes is
107   propagated through multiple layers. By aggregating this information, GNNs can produce more robust
      and informative embeddings for users and items, leading to improved recommendation accuracy

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Chen et al. Wang et al. (2019b)). Some studies, such as GraphRec Fan et al. (2019) and KCGN
109   Huang et al. (2021), have utilized user social interactions to enhance recommendation performance
110   by incorporating the influence of social connections into GNN-based models. Other models, such
111   as KGAT Wang et al. (2019a), extend GNNs to operate on knowledge graphs, which provide rich,
112   structured semantic information about entities and their relationships.
113   Contrastive Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs: Contrastive learning is a widely used self-
114   supervised technique that leverages both positive and negative samples to learn discriminative features
115   from data. By contrasting similar (positive) pairs with dissimilar (negative) ones, this approach helps
116   models differentiate between meaningful patterns, leading to more robust and effective representations.
117
      In the context of recommender systems, a positive pair typically consists of a user and an item with
      which they have interacted (e.g., a purchase, rating, or click), while a negative pair consists of a
118
      user and an item they have not interacted with. Recent studies show contrastive learning has gained
119
      attention for its ability to address challenges such as data sparsity and noisy interactions. Some
120   methods, such as HGCL Chen et al. (2023), propose a contrastive multi-view model that utilizes
121   meta-path to capture multi-views of heterogeneous graphs. In Zou and Wang (2023), a contrastive
122   learning model was proposed to learn representations by contrasting a heterogeneous graph with
123   a meta-path-based homogeneous graph extracted from the heterogeneous graph. The approach
124   leverages the structural differences between these two types of graphs. By applying contrastive loss,
125   the model aims to capture meaningful distinctions between the homogeneous and heterogeneous
126   graphs, thereby learning richer and more informative representations that reflect local and global
127   data patterns. SimGRACE Xia et al. (2022) is a self-supervised learning framework that introduces
128
      contrastive learning by generating contrastive views through perturbations applied directly to the
      GNN encoder. Unlike traditional methods that create contrastive views by augmenting the input
129
      data (such as node features or graph structures), SimGRACE perturbs the GNN model parameters
130
      themselves to create different perspectives of the same graph. Zou and Wang (2023) employs
131   contrastive learning to integrate the embeddings generated from user-item interactions with those
132   obtained from homogeneous graphs constructed using meta-paths.

361
362   To address Q2, several experiments were conducted under various scenarios to verify that incorpo-
363   rating multiple views of users and items is essential for learning high-quality representations. Here,
364   we primarily focus on the impact of source information in each user-view and item-view embedding
365   on recommendation performance. Our framework considers multiple perspectives of user and item
366   interactions (user-view, item-view, and the user-item interaction view). The user-view embedding
367   combines the user’s trust network with their interactions based on textual reviews. In contrast, the
368   item-view embedding integrates a meta-path-based graph of item interactions and reviews. In this
369
      section, we aim to analyze the impact of each information source and its influence on extracting
      high-quality embeddings for conducting contrastive learning with the user-item view embedding
370
      in the proposed method. We evaluated the impact of user and item information and defined five
371
      evaluation scenarios to demonstrate the effectiveness of each aspect of our approach. Details of
372   these scenarios are explained in Figure 2. The performance of our proposed method, along with
373   the compared scenarios, is presented in Figure 2 for both Hit Ratio and NDCG metrics. From these
374   results, we can conclude that the user’s trust network plays a crucial role in the user-view embedding.
375   Additionally, for the item-view embedding, the item’s reviews significantly influence the item’s
376   representation. Finally, in the fifth scenario (MvL-GNN), we observed that the performance of
377   our proposed method improves by incorporating both sources of information—user trust and item
      reviews—into the user-view and item-view embeddings.

      5.4   I MPACT OF THE HYPERPARAMETERS
400
401
402   The proposed method includes several adjustable parameters, such as the number of layers, final
403   embedding dimensions, and learning rate, which must be optimized to achieve the best performance.
404   To address the issue of over-smoothing common in GNNs, we experimented with different numbers
405
      of layers to evaluate their influence on the model’s performance. To address Q3, we analyzed the
      experimental performance on the Epinions and Yelp datasets using different numbers of layers, as
406
      shown in Figure 3. The results indicate that the best performance for both datasets is achieved when
407
      the model uses two layers. The reason for this is that adding more layers leads to over-smoothing,
408   a common issue in GNNs that becomes more pronounced as the number of layers increases. As
409   the model performs repeated aggregation and transformation steps, the node representations tend to
410   converge toward a uniform representation. This convergence reduces the model’s ability to capture
411   nuanced differences between nodes, ultimately hindering its performance.

468   6            C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we proposed MvL-GNN, a multi-view graph representation learning method specifically
471   designed to address the limitations of traditional recommender systems. This method addresses
472   the limitations of traditional approaches by incorporating multiple data sources, such as user trust
473   relationships and user reviews, to enhance recommendation accuracy. The approach leverages user
474   trust networks to capture implicit social influences. It integrates a pre-trained language model like
475   BERT to transform user-generated reviews into meaningful embeddings, which helps capture nu-
476   anced user sentiments. Furthermore, the method employs contrastive learning to align and integrate
477
      diverse data representations, ensuring consistency and complementarity between the different sources
      of information. Through extensive experimentation on three real-world datasets (Yelp, Ciao, and
478
      Epinions), we demonstrated the effectiveness of MvL-GNN in outperforming state-of-the-art models
479
      across multiple metrics, including Hit Ratio and NDCG. Including multiple perspectives—user trust
480   networks, item reviews, and meta-path-based item graphs—proved crucial in generating high-quality
481   user and item embeddings, enhancing accuracy and robustness in recommendation tasks. Conse-
482   quently, MvL-GNN has proven to be an effective solution for capturing the complex relationships
483   between users and items in a recommendation setting, leveraging multiple sources of information to
484   address common challenges such as data sparsity and cold start problems. Future work could explore
485   further enhancements to MvL-GNN by incorporating additional data sources or experimenting with
      more advanced contrastive learning techniques.
