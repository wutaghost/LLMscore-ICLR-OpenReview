                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
015            Generative models like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and Variational
016            Autoencoders (VAEs) often fail to capture the full diversity of their training data,
017            leading to mode collapse. While this issue is well-explored in image generation, it
018
               remains underinvestigated for time series data. We introduce a new definition of
               mode collapse specific to time series and propose a novel metric, DMD-GEN, to
019
               quantify its severity. Our metric utilizes Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a
020            data-driven technique for identifying coherent spatiotemporal patterns, and employs
021            Optimal Transport between DMD eigenvectors to assess discrepancies between the
022            underlying dynamics of the original and generated data. This approach not only
023            quantifies the preservation of essential dynamic characteristics but also provides
024            interpretability by pinpointing which modes have collapsed. We validate DMD-
025            GEN on both synthetic and real-world datasets using various generative models,
026            including TimeGAN, TimeVAE, and DiffusionTS. The results demonstrate that
027            DMD-GEN correlates well with traditional evaluation metrics for static data while
028
               offering the advantage of applicability to dynamic data. This work offers for the
               first time a definition of mode collapse for time series, improving understanding,
029
               and forming the basis of our tool for assessing and improving generative models in
030
               the time series domain.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Generative models gained significant attention in recent years, driven by recent advancements in
037   computational power, the availability of extensive datasets, and breakthrough developments in
038   machine learning (ML) algorithms. Notably, models like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
039   and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) excel at learning compact representations of data Goodfellow
040   et al. (2014); Pu et al. (2016); Oublal et al. (2024). These models are invaluable for generating realistic
041   samples for testing, training other models, or augmenting datasets to enhance the performance of ML
042   algorithms Zheng et al. (2023); Abdollahzadeh et al. (2023). However, recent studies have revealed
043   that generative models sometimes fail to produce diverse samples, leading to reduced effectiveness in
044   applications that necessitate a broad spectrum of variations Aboussalah et al. (2023); Berns (2022);
045
      New et al. (2023). An illustration of this challenge can be seen in GANs, which frequently experience
      mode collapse, a phenomenon where the generator focuses on a limited subset of the data distribution,
046
      leading to the production of repetitive or similar samples rather than capturing the full diversity
047
      of the training data Bang & Shim (2018); Pan et al. (2022); Eide et al. (2020). VAEs also face a
048   phenomenon called posterior collapse, where the model tends to generate outputs that are similar or
049   indistinguishable for different inputs. This limitation reduces the model’s ability to produce diverse
050   samples He et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2021). Diffusion models, while generally robust against mode
051   collapse compared to GANs and VAEs, are not entirely immune to difficulties in covering the full data
052   distribution. These challenges can become apparent when high classifier-free guidance is employed
053   during sampling or when training on small datasets Ho & Salimans (2022); Sadat et al. (2024); Qin
      et al. (2023).

      The issue of diversity in generative models has received significant attention in fields such as computer
055   vision and natural language processing Lee et al. (2023); Chung et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2020);
056   Ibarrola & Grace (2024), however, it remains relatively underexplored in the domain of time series
057   data. Due to the inherent time-varying nature of time series, the traditional definition of mode collapse
058   is inadequate, necessitating a new framework for this context. Unlike images, time series data often
059   exhibits patterns that change over time, such as trends, seasonality, or cyclic behavior. Moreover,
060   in the context of mode collapse for generative models, particularly GANs, modes refer to distinct
061   patterns or sub-groups within the data distribution that the model is attempting to learn and replicate.
062   However, defining modes for time series data is challenging due to several intrinsic characteristics of
063
      time series as they must be able to capture these evolving patterns rather than generating repetitive or
      static sequences.
064
065   Contributions. The contributions of our work are as follows:
066
067          • New Definition of Mode Collapse for Time Series: We introduce a new definition of mode
068            collapse specifically for time series data, leveraging DMD to capture and analyze coherent
069            dynamic patterns.
070          • Development of DMD-GEN Metric: We propose DMD-GEN, a new metric to detect mode
071            collapse, which consistently aligns with traditional generative model evaluation metrics
072            while offering unique insights into time series dynamics.
073          • Enhanced Interpretability: The DMD-GEN metric provides increased interpretability by
074            decomposing the underlying dynamics into distinct modes, allowing for a clearer under-
075            standing of the preservation of essential time series characteristics.
076          • Efficiency in Time Complexity: Our approach offers significant computational efficiency
077            as it requires no additional training, making it highly scalable for real-time applications.

082   2.1   M ODE C OLLAPSE FOR T IME S ERIES
083
      Before delving into the details of our new evaluation metric incorporating the aforementioned
084
      concepts, it is worth highlighting the challenges to be addressed in order to measure mode collapse
085   when dealing with time series. (i) Capturing Modes: For time series, we need to consider modes that
086   represent different unfolding patterns over time. Real-world time series data rarely exhibits a single
087   and clean pattern Lim & Zohren (2021); Kantz & Schreiber (2004). Instead, time series data often
088   exhibits multiple patterns simultaneously, e.g. superimposed on long-term trends and shorter-term
089   fluctuations representing unfolding modes. This makes it challenging to isolate and identify the
090   specific mode of interest. Moreover, unlike data with clear separations like images or text, time
091   series data is continuous. This makes it challenging to pinpoint the exact start and end points of a
092   specific unfolding pattern (mode). (ii) Similarity Measurement: Time series data often have different
093
      characteristics that make standard distance metrics such as Euclidean distance less effective as a
      similarity measure. For example, Euclidean distance is sensitive to different dimension scales in each
094
      dimension, and when the dimensionality is high, the distances may become dominated by differences
095
      in certain dimensions, leading to inaccuracies in similarity measurement. In addition, Euclidean
096   distance does not take into account the temporal dependencies present in time series data. It treats
097   each timestamp as independent, which may not be appropriate for time series data where the ordering
098   and temporal relationships between data points are crucial. More sophisticated measures, such as
099   Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) Müller (2007), are designed to handle these temporal dependencies
100   by aligning sequences in a way that minimizes distance, however, it cannot effectively capture the
101   underlying modes or coherent dynamic patterns in the data. This inability to recognize and preserve
102   the essential modes means DTW falls short in assessing mode collapse.
103   Unlike in images, the mode collapse issue in time series cannot be easily distinguished with human
104   eyes. Therefore, this area remains relatively under-explored. Few studies have formally addressed
105   this problem and proposed solutions within the domain of time series. The work of Lin et al. (2020)
106   developed a custom auto-normalization heuristic which normalizes each time series individually
107   rather than normalizing over the entire dataset. However, the custom auto-normalization heuristic
      only concerns mode collapse defined by the different offset values between each time series while it


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      does not take into account whether the model has generated the entire dataset’s trends and seasonality.
109   Additionally, DC-GAN Min et al. (2023) is the first time-series GAN that can generate all the
110   temporal features in a multimodal distributed time series. DC-GAN relies on the concept of directed
111   chain stochastic differential equations (DC-SDEs) Detering et al. (2020). While the authors did not
112   explicitly mention mode collapse, it would be valuable to investigate further whether the DC-GAN
113   successfully captures the full range of trends and seasonality present in the dataset.

115   2.2   DYNAMIC M ODE D ECOMPOSITION
116
117   Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) Schmid (2010); Peters (2019) is a data-driven and model-free
118   method used for analyzing the underlying dynamics of complex systems such as fluid dynamics. It
119
      is used to extract modal descriptions of a nonlinear dynamical system from data without any prior
      knowledge of the system required. DMD yields direct information concerning the data dynamics,
120
      allowing us to compare multiple time series. Given a dynamical system ẋ(t) = f (x(t), t; µ), where
121
      x(t) ∈ Rn is a vector of dimension n representing the state of the dynamical system at time t, µ ∈ Rp
122   contains parameters of the system, and f : Rn × R × Rp → Rn represents the dynamics. DMD
123   approximates a locally linear dynamical system ẋ ≈ Ax, where the operator A is the best-fit linear
124   approximation to f through regression. This linear approximation allows the representation of the
125   system’s behavior in a simplified framework and helps construct reduced-order models that capture the
126   essential dynamics of the systems. This is particularly useful for systems with large state spaces like
127   fluid dynamics Kutz (2017); Jiaqing & Weiwei (2018). Analogously, we approximate the dynamical
128   system linearly for discrete time series. Given a dynamical system x : t ∈ R 7→ x(t) ∈ Rn ,
129   we generate discrete-time snapshots of length m, arranged into two data matrices X, X′ ∈ Rn×m
130
      defined as follows,

323   Time and Complexity. The proposed DMD-GEN metric leverages DMD computation, Optimal
      Transport, and geodesic distance computation using principle angles, all of which contribute to


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      the overall computational complexity. The time complexity of each component can be estimated
325   separately: (i) DMD Complexity. The time complexity of DMD primarily depends on computing
326   the Singular Value Decomposition. Since we reduce the dimensionality to focus on dominant
327   modes, making this step more efficient in practice, the time complexity to compute each Mk (Xi ) and
328   Mk (X e j ) is O(n×k 2 ). (ii) Geodesic Distance Computation. Calculating the principal angles between
329   the subspaces involves QR decompositions and a SVD operation on the product of orthonormal
330   matrices. Therefore, the complexity of computing the geodesic distance is O(k 3 ). (iii) Optimal
331   Transport Complexity. To find the optimal mapping between the modes of original and generated
332   time series, the complexity of solving the optimal transport problem using Sinkhorn’s algorithm is
333   O(B 2 ) where B is the number of samples (Sinkhorn, 1967). Therefore, the overall complexity of
334   DMD-GEN is O(B × n2 × k 2 + B × k 3 + B 2 ). The significant advantage of DMD-GEN is that
335
      it does not require additional training, which makes the overall approach computationally feasible
      compared to metrics that involve model retraining.

      We evaluate the diversity of generative models across one synthetic dataset and three real-world

344
345   Sine waves. We generated a synthetic dataset consisting of two sets of sine waves to represent a

347                                       y(t) = A · sin(2πf t + ϕ),                                    (8)
348   where A is the amplitude, f is the frequency, t is the time variable and ϕ is the phase angle of the

351   each sequence be 24. A = 0.5 and f = 1 Hz for the first mode, and A = 5 and f = 0.5 Hz for the
352   second mode.
353   Stock price. To test our framework on a complex multimodal dataset, we used Google stocks data

355   daily open, high, low, close, adjusted close, and volume. The time series were then cut into sequences

503   5   C ONCLUSION
504
505   In this paper, we introduced a novel metric, DMD-GEN, specifically designed to evaluate generative
506   models and quantify mode collapse in time series. Using Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) and
507   Optimal Transport, DMD-GEN provides a robust framework to evaluate the similarity of dynamic
508   patterns between generated and original time series. Compared to existing metrics like Discriminative
509
      Score, and Predictive Score, DMD-GEN showed superior sensitivity in mode collapse detection.
      Furthermore, when comparing generative models, the results indicate that DMD-GEN consistently
510
      aligns with all other baseline metrics that require additional training, while DMD-GEN itself does
511
      not require any training, making it a more efficient alternative. DMD-GEN provides increased
512   interpretability by decomposing the underlying dynamics into distinct modes, allowing for a clearer
513   understanding of the preservation of essential time series characteristics. This highlights the potential
514   of DMD-GEN as a crucial tool for advancing generative modeling techniques in time series analysis,
515   promoting diversity in generated outputs. Future work can explore the integration of DMD-GEN into
516   the training process, potentially using it as a guiding metric to improve model training dynamically.

1014   On the Grassmann manifold, the geodesic distance between two subspaces is given by the length of
1015
       the shortest path connecting them. This distance is intrinsically linked to the principal angles between
       the subspaces. The projection distance quantifies the separation between subspaces in terms of these
1016
       principal angles.
1017
1018   By computing the squared norm of the derivative of the geodesic, we find that it equals the sum of
1019   the squares of the principal angles, which is the squared projection distance. Since the derivative’s
1020   norm is constant, the total length of the geodesic over the interval t ∈ [0, 1] is precisely the projection
1021
       distance.
1022   Therefore, the length of the geodesic γ(t) connecting Mk (X) and Mk (X)            e on the Grassmann
1023   manifold equals the projection distance between these two subspaces.
