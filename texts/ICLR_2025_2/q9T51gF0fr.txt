009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite extensive research on adversarial examples, the underlying mechanisms of
012            adversarially robust generalization, a critical yet challenging task for deep learn-
013            ing, remain largely unknown. In this work, we propose a novel perspective to deci-
014            pher adversarially robust generalization through the lens of the Weight-Curvature
015            Index (WCI). The proposed WCI quantifies the vulnerability of models to adver-
016
               sarial perturbations using the Frobenius norm of weight matrices and the trace of
               Hessian matrices. We prove generalization bounds based on PAC-Bayesian theory
017
               and second-order loss function approximations to elucidate the interplay between
018
               robust generalization gap, model parameters, and loss landscape curvature. Our
019            theory and experiments show that WCI effectively captures the robust generaliza-
020            tion performance of adversarially trained models. By offering a nuanced under-
021            standing of adversarial robustness based on the scale of model parameters and the
022            curvature of the loss landscape, our work provides crucial insights for designing
023            more resilient deep learning models, enhancing their reliability and security.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Building models to be resilient to adversarial perturbations remains an enduring challenge. An ac-
      tive line of research (Madry et al., 2018; Carmon et al., 2019; Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2020;
029
      Croce et al., 2020; Gowal et al., 2020) has emphasized that adversarial training, where models are
030
      optimized to withstand worst-case perturbations, is essential to bolster robustness. While standard
031   deep learning produces models that can generalize to unseen data well, adversarial training presents
032   a starkly different scenario. In particular, overfitting to the training set severely harms the robust
033   generalization of adversarial training, resulting in models that perform well on adversarial examples
034   in the training set but poorly on those in the test set. This phenomenon, known as robust overfitting
035   (Rice et al., 2020; Li & Li, 2023), underscores a significant gap in our understanding of deep learning
036   generalization under adversarial settings. To mitigate robust overfitting and improve the generaliza-
037   tion of adversarial training, various robustness-enhancing techniques have been proposed, such as
038   data augmentation (Zhang et al., 2018; Yun et al., 2019), ℓ2 weight regularization (Stutz et al., 2019),
039
      early stopping (Rice et al., 2020), adversarial weight perturbation (AWP) (Wu et al., 2020), incor-
      porating synthetically generated data (Gowal et al., 2021), sharpness-aware minimization (SAM)
040
      (Wei et al., 2023), to name a few. Nevertheless, there is still no clear understanding of why robust
041
      overfitting occurs or what factors are critical for achieving adversarially robust generalization.
042
043   A reliable indicator of adversarially robust generalization is useful for identifying the limitations of
044   state-of-the-art methods and gaining insights to guide the development of more robust models. To
045
      better understand the generalization of deep neural networks, a variety of metrics have been pro-
      posed, including margin-based measures (Pitas et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2018; 2019; Yang et al.,
046
      2020b), smoothness-based measures (Cisse et al., 2017), flatness-based measures (Petzka et al.,
047
      2019; Yu et al., 2021; Stutz et al., 2021; Petzka et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023;
048   Andriushchenko et al., 2023), and gradient-norm measures (Zhao et al., 2022; Ross & Doshi-Velez,
049   2018; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2019; Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2020; Dong et al., 2019).
050   Although these metrics have shown varying degrees of effectiveness in capturing the model’s gen-
051   eralization gap for both standard and adversarial training (Neyshabur et al., 2015; Bartlett et al.,
052   2017; Dziugaite et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Keskar et al., 2016; Foret et al., 2021; Zhuang et al.,
053   2022; Kwon et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2020), recent studies reveal that no single measure can perfectly
      estimate a model’s robust generalization capability (Kim et al., 2024). Therefore, it is important to

054
      develop new theoretical frameworks and more reliable indicators that can better capture a model’s
055   robust generalization capability.
056
057   Contributions. We introduce the Weight-Curvature Index (WCI), a novel metric that characterizes
058   the robust generalization performance of adversarially trained models by leveraging the Frobenius
059   norm of weight matrices and the trace of Hessian matrices (Definition 2.6). WCI has shown a strong
060   correlation with the robust generalization gap and can improve adversarial robustness. In partic-
061   ular, the definition of the WCI is motivated by our newly derived PAC-Bayesian bound on robust
062   generalization gap (Lemma 2.2 and Theorem 2.5), which establishes a rigorous link between model
063   parameters, loss landscape curvature, and robust generalization performance (Section 2). Empiri-
064
      cally, we demonstrate the strong correlation between the proposed WCI and the robust generalization
      performance for models during adversarial training, suggesting its potential for effective predictions
065
      of generalization gaps (Section 3). Moreover, we explore how the WCI dynamically interacts with
066
      the learning rate by introducing an algorithm that dynamically adjusts the learning rate based on
067   the WCI during training, which improves adversarial robustness by adapting to changes in model
068   behavior, providing insights into optimizing learning rate schedules to enhance model robustness
069   (Section 4). We compare WCI with other popular norm-based and flatness-based measures, high-
070   lighting its superior ability to understand the complex interactions for robust generalization between
071   weight scale and loss curvature in adversarial settings (Section 5). We conclude by summarizing our
072   findings and discussing future directions for advancing adversarial robustness research (Section 6).
073
074   Related Work. Identifying a reliable metric to characterize adversarially robust generalization has
075   been shown to be a challenging task in the existing literature. For instance, margin and smoothness
076
      measures often show strong negative correlations with the robust generalization gap (Yang et al.,
      2020a; Kim et al., 2024), implying that beyond a certain threshold, increasing margin and reducing
077
      smoothness may degrade robust generalization performance. In the context of norm-based measures,
078
      while studies have demonstrated that input gradient norm regularization could enhance adversarial
079   robustness (Ross & Doshi-Velez, 2018; Huang et al., 2023), recent findings by Kim et al. (2024) sug-
080   gest that a lower gradient norm does not invariably lead to improved robustness. Additionally, prior
081   research (Jiang et al., 2019; Dziugaite et al., 2020) has shown a strong correlation between cross-
082   entropy loss and robust generalization gap in standard training, leading to the use of early stopping
083   based on cross-entropy thresholds to prevent overfitting. However, extending this approach to ad-
084   versarial training is complicated by the varied loss functions utilized in methods like TRADES and
085   MART. In contrast, flatness-based measures have been demonstrated to poorly correlate with robust
086   generalization performance (Wen et al., 2024; Walter et al., 2024). Contrary to traditional assump-
087
      tions, sharper minima can sometimes correlate with lower robust generalization gaps, challenging
      the notion that flatter minima always leads to better generalization. Walter et al. (2024) argues that
088
      flatness alone cannot fully explain adversarial robustness. In contrast, Wen et al. (2024) suggests
089
      that sharpness minimization algorithms do not only focus on reducing sharpness to achieve bet-
090   ter generalization, calling for alternative explanations for the generalization of over-parameterized
091   neural networks. The aforementioned literature highlights the need for alternative explanations and
092   new theoretical frameworks to better understand robust generalization in adversarial contexts. The
093   role of the Hessian trace in generalization has been extensively studied, such as in Ju et al. (2022),
094   where it was shown that trace minimization correlates with improved generalization across tasks.
095   WCI extends this concept by integrating the trace and Frobenius norm of weights, creating a robust
096   proxy for adversarial training contexts. Regularization techniques targeting the Fisher Information
097   Matrix trace were discussed in Jastrzebski et al. (2021), highlighting the importance of early-phase
098
      curvature control. These insights complement our findings that robust overfitting is mitigated when
      WCI regularization is incorporated during training. PAC-Bayesian bounds that incorporate Hes-
099
      sians, as explored in Golatkar et al. (2019); Patracone et al. (2024), provide a theoretical framework
100
      supporting our derivations of WCI. Specifically, the inclusion of curvature measures aligns with the
101   PAC-Bayesian methodology, ensuring tight bounds on robust generalization errors.

142   Here, the prior distribution P represents our initial belief about the distribution of classifiers before
143   observing the data, which is typically chosen based on previous knowledge or uniform assumptions

149   Lemma 2.2 shows that the robust generalization gap can be upper bounded by three key components,
150   denoting as Perturbation Discrepancy, KL Divergence and Classifier Variability respectively, plus
151   some constant term. Equation 1 is based on the PAC-Bayesian framework, which is generic in terms
152   of the prior distribution P, posterior distribution Q and the loss function that the model aims to
153
      minimize. The first term Perturbation Discrepancy measures the difference between the expected
      loss of the classifier over the distribution of adversarial examples (perturbed inputs x + δ) and the
154
      expected loss under the posterior distribution Q over classifiers. Essentially, it quantifies how much
155
      more (or less) error is induced when using adversarially perturbed examples compared to the av-
156   erage error across different classifiers sampled from Q. The second term KL Divergence acts as a
157   regularizer in the derived generalization bound. It measures the divergence between the posterior
158   distribution Q of classifiers and the prior distribution P. A smaller KL divergence indicates that the
159   learned model (represented by Q) does not stray far from our initial assumptions or beliefs about the
160   model space (represented by P). This term ensures that the posterior distribution does not overfit the
161   adversarial perturbations seen in the training data. Finally, the last term Classifier Variability repre-
      sents the variance in the performance of different classifiers sampled from the posterior distribution

      Q on the adversarial examples. This term reflects how stable or consistent the classifiers are when
163   they are exposed to the same perturbed inputs. A high variability might indicate that some models
164   in the posterior distribution are significantly better or worse at handling adversarial perturbations,
165   suggesting a potential for improving robustness by focusing on these models.
166
      Since our objective is to establish a reliable index of robust generalization capacity, we only need
167
      to focus on terms relevant to model robustness; thus, we ignore the constant term in Equation 1.
168
      In addition, prior works have shown that reducing the Classifier Variability term also decreases the
169   Perturbation Discrepancy term (Doshi et al., 2024; Behboodi et al., 2022; Marion, 2024; Ge et al.,
170   2023). When the Perturbation Discrepancy is sufficiently small, the Classifier Variability can be
171   seen as being in sync with the Perturbation Discrepancy term. This synchronization occurs because
172   small perturbations typically do not cause significant deviations in the classifier’s output. Essen-
173   tially, if the perturbation introduced to the input data is minor, the classifier’s decision boundary is
174   less likely to be crossed, resulting in consistent and predictable outputs. Therefore, when the pertur-
175   bation discrepancy is minimized, it indicates that the perturbation is within the robustness range of
176   the classifier, thereby maintaining the stability of classification results across slightly varied inputs.
177
      Consequently, our analyses in the following sections predominantly focus on exploring the influ-
      ence of the KL Divergence and Classifier Variability terms on enhancing the model’s resistance to

      adversarial perturbations.

184

309
      itive correlation between WCI and generation gap is numerically proved using different seeds (see

      by substantial modifications in the loss surface, reflected by fluctuations in the Hessian matrix, af-

318   robust generalization gap reinforces the utility of WCI as a critical indicator of model performance

      amine the relationship between WCI and the robust generalization gap across various regularization

345   4   I MPLICATIONS OF WCI ON M ITIGATING ROBUST OVERFITTING
346
347   To ensure the model’s robust generalization performance, it is essential to maintain the value of
348   WCI to be sufficiently small. It is obvious from Equation 5 that the Frobenius norm can be easily
349   controlled to be small, but the size of the Hessian matrix of the loss function is not easy to control.
350   Liu et al. (2021) showed that one can tune the learning rate such that the KL divergence between the
351
      learned distribution by SGD and the posterior is minimized. Therefore, we explore the relationship
      between the Weight-Curvature Index and the learning rate of adversarial training algorithms in this

355   Learning Rate Adjustment using WCI. Selecting proper learning rates is crucial for training deep
356   neural networks as it sets the parameter update step size, affecting convergence speed and model
357   generalization (Smith & Topin, 2019). Liu et al. (2021) found that while tuning the learning rate can
358   minimize KL divergence, this only holds for smaller rates; larger rates do not require adjustments.
359   For a specific learning rate decay strategy, we need to consider when to decay, the initial learning
360   rate, and its decay rate. Therefore, we conducted a series of experiments under the same conditions
361   described in Rice et al. (2020), varying the learning rates to observe their effects during the training
362   process. Figure 2(a) shows learning curves with the same decay strategies but different decay timings
363
      at the 25th, 50th, 75th, and 100th epochs. Each decay reduces the learning rate to 10% of its original
      value for 50 epochs, followed by similar decays. These curves follow similar trends, indicating
364
      that the timing of learning rate decay does not significantly affect robust overfitting, suggesting no
365
      need for adjustments to the decay period from the initial experiment. Figure 2(b) explores different
366   initial learning rates and decay rates, applying a uniform decay at the 100th epoch. We tested initial
367   rates of {0.1, 0.01} and decay rates of {10%, 1%}. The best performance came from an initial rate
368   of 0.1 and a decay to 0.01, although its benefit decreased over time due to robust overfitting. Our
369   learning rate decay strategy maintains an initial rate of 0.1 for the first 100 epochs, then decays to
370   0.01, adjusting thereafter based on the value of the Weight-Curvature Index.
371

377   old. Therefore, we propose to preset a threshold: when WCI exceeds this threshold, we dynamically

      Connection of WCI with Robustness-enhancing Techniques. Understanding how existing
478
      robustness-enhancing techniques interact with the Weight-Curvature-Index can provide deeper in-
479
      sights into their efficacy in improving model robustness. For instance, ℓ2 weight regularization
480   (Stutz et al., 2019) reduces the Frobenius norm of weights (∥Wk ∥F ), which can lower WCI and
481   smooth the loss landscape, enhancing the model’s stability against adversarial inputs and boosting
482   robustness. Data augmentation techniques (Zhang et al., 2018; Yun et al., 2019) enhance the di-
483   versity and complexity of training data, indirectly affecting the model’s behavior and facilitating
484   exploration of flatter loss landscape regions, which might reflect in improved WCI-based robust-
485   ness assessments by reducing Tr(Hk ). Similarly, incorporating synthetically generated data (Gowal
      et al., 2021) broadens the model’s exposure to diverse training examples, helping achieve an optimal

      poor correlations with the robust generalization gap (Kim et al., 2024). The WCI provides a specific
510
      metric that combines weight magnitude and curvature, which may offer different insights, and we
511   have confirmed that WCI is strongly correlated with gap generation. Wen et al. (2024) suggested that
512   sharpness minimization algorithms do not only minimize sharpness to achieve better generalization,
513   which calls for the search for other explanations for the generalization of over-parameterized neural
514   networks. Through these analyses, WCI’s role as a crucial metric becomes apparent, especially in
515   gauging the underlying mechanisms of various robustness-enhancing techniques aimed at improving
516   the adversarial durability of machine learning models.

518   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
519
520   We introduced WCI, a novel metric that strongly connects with the robust generalization capabil-
521   ities of adversarially trained models. Our work opens avenues for future research, particularly in
522   applying WCI to mitigate robust overfitting and exploring its potential in further refining adversarial
523   defenses. Although we demonstrate the effectiveness of WCI-based learning rate adjustments in
524   mitigating robust overfitting, a limitation of integrating WCI in adversarial training is its high com-
525   putation demand for computing the trace of the Hessian matrices (Appendix D). Designing effective
526   optimization techniques to lower the costs of WCI computations and studying how to leverage WCI
527   in other algorithms for building robust models are interesting future directions. In addition, large
528
      fluctuations in WCI learning curves exist, which remain poorly understood and add an additional
      layer of unpredictability to utilizing WCI for training adjustments. Future research can investigate
529
      the root causes of such fluctuations, study how to stabilize the WCI measures during training, and
530
      develop more reliable learning rate adjustment strategies for further robustness improvement.

       We conduct experiments on the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and SVHN datasets, which are widely
993    used benchmarks for evaluating the robustness of deep learning models. CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100
994    consist of 60, 000 32 × 32 color images in 10 and 100 classes, respectively, with 50, 000 training
995    images and 10, 000 test images (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). SVHN contains 32 × 32 color images
996    of house numbers, with 73, 257 training images and 26, 032 test images. We use the standard data
997    splits for training and testing the models (Netzer et al., 2011). We use PreActResNet18 (He et al.,
998    2016) as the base architecture for all experiments, a widely used model for adversarial training. We
999    train the models using the standard cross-entropy loss and the SGD optimizer with a learning rate of
1000   0.1, a weight decay of 5 × 10−4 , and a momentum of 0.9. We also apply the piecewise learning rate
1001
       schedule that reduces the learning rate by a factor of 10 at epochs 100 and 150. We train the models
       for 200 epochs with a batch size of 128.
1002
1003
1004
       B.2   A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
1005
       We perform PGD-based adversarial training (Madry et al., 2017), which is a widely used approach
1006   for training robust models. In particular, we consider ℓ∞ -norm bounded perturbations with strength
1007   ϵ = 8/255. We generate adversarial examples by applying PGD with a step size of 2/255 (for
1008   SVHN 1/255) and 10 iterations. We use the same adversarial parameters for all experiments to
1009   ensure consistency. We train the models using adversarial examples generated during training to
1010   improve robustness. We also evaluate the models on adversarial examples generated during testing
1011   to assess their robustness against unseen attacks. We use the standard ℓ∞ norm for generating
1012   adversarial examples, which is a common choice for evaluating robustness against perturbations.

       loss. The robust accuracy is the percentage of correctly classified adversarial examples, while the
