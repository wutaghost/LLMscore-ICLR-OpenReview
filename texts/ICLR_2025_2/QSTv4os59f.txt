009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             We study streaming algorithms for Correlation Clustering. Given a complete
012             graph as an arbitrary-order stream of edges, with each edge labelled as positive
013             or negative, the goal is to partition the vertices into disjoint clusters, such that the
014             number of disagreements is minimized. In this paper, we give the first learning-
015             augmented streaming algorithms for the problem, achieving the first better-than-
016             3-approximation in dynamic streams. Our algorithms draw inspiration from re-
017             cent works of Cambus et al. (SODA’24), and Chakrabarty and Makarychev
018             (NeurIPS’23). Our algorithms use the predictions of pairwise dissimilarities be-
019
                tween vertices provided by a predictor and achieve an approximation ratio that
                is close to 2.06 under good prediction quality. Even if the prediction quality is
020
                poor, our algorithms cannot perform worse than the well known P IVOT algorithm,
021
                which achieves a 3-approximation. Our algorithms are much simpler than the re-
022             cent 1.847-approximation streaming algorithm by Cohen-Addad et al. (STOC’24)
023             which appears to be challenging to implement and is restricted to insertion-only
024             streams. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate
025             the superiority of our proposed algorithms over their non-learning counterparts.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Correlation Clustering is a fundamental problem in machine learning and data mining, and it has
031   a wide range of applications, such as image segmentation (Kim et al., 2014), community detection
032   (Shi et al., 2021), automated labeling (Chakrabarti et al., 2008), etc. Given a complete graph G =
033   (V, E = E + ∪ E − ), where each edge is labeled as positive (+) or negative (−), the goal is to find
034   a clustering C, i.e., a partition of V into disjoint clusters C1 , C2 , . . . , Ct , where t is arbitrary, that
035   minimizes the following cost:
036
          costG (C) := |{(u, v) ∈ E + : ∃i ̸= j : u ∈ Ci , v ∈ Cj }| + |{(u, v) ∈ E − : ∃i : u, v ∈ Ci }|.
037
038   That is, the number of negative edges in the same cluster plus the number of positive edges between
039   different clusters. (We often refer to as the number of disagreements.)
040
      This problem, introduced by Bansal et al. (2004), is known to be APX-hard (Charikar et al., 2005).
041
      Hence, significant efforts have been dedicated to designing approximation algorithms for this prob-
042   lem (Bansal et al., 2004; Charikar et al., 2005; Ailon et al., 2008; Chawla et al., 2015; Cohen-
043   Addad et al., 2022; 2023; Cao et al., 2024; Cohen-Addad et al., 2024b), culminating in a 1.437-
044   approximation via a linear program (LP) based rounding (Cao et al., 2024). There exists a purely
045   combinatorial algorithm that achieves a (2 − 2/13 + ε)-approximation (Cohen-Addad et al., 2024b).
046
      Partially due to storage limitations and the rapidly growing volume of data, graph streaming algo-
047
      rithms for Correlation Clustering have received increasing attention recently. In this setting, a graph
048   is represented as a sequence of edge insertions or deletions, known as a graph stream. The objective
049   is to scan the sequence in a few number of passes, ideally, 1 pass and find a high-quality clustering
050   of the vertex set with a low Correlation Clustering cost, while minimizing space usage. If the se-
051   quence contains only edge insertions, it is referred to as an insertion-only stream; if both insertions
052   and deletions are allowed, it is referred to as a dynamic stream. Since the output of the clustering
053   inherently requires Ω(n) bits of space (as each vertex needs a label to indicate its cluster member-
      ship), most previous research has primarily focused on the semi-streaming model, i.e., the algorithm

071
               Is it possible to obtain a practical, better-than-3-approximation algorithm for
072
               Correlation Clustering in both insertion-only and dynamic streams?
073
074   We affirmatively answer the above question by leveraging ideas from learning-augmented algo-
075   rithms (LAAs). An LAA uses predictions to enhance its performance. These algorithms stem from
076   practical scenarios where machine learning techniques exploit data structure to exceed the worst-
077   case guarantees of traditional algorithms. Our LAAs fit into the category of learning-augmented
078   streaming algorithms (Hsu et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022a; Aamand et al., 2023).
079
      It is worth mentioning that both our work and previous efforts on learning-augmented streaming
      algorithms mainly focus on using predictors to improve the corresponding space-accuracy tradeoffs.

      1.1 T ECHNICAL OVERVIEW
152
153
      Our LAAs rely on the influential P IVOT algorithm by Ailon et al. (2008) and the LP rounding
      algorithm by Chawla et al. (2015). The P IVOT algorithm begins by selecting a random permutation
154
      π over the vertices of the graph. It then iteratively forms clusters by choosing the vertex with the
155
      smallest rank according to π, along with its neighbors in the graph. Once a cluster is formed, it is
156   removed from the graph. This process continues until all vertices have been assigned to clusters. The
157   LP rounding algorithm first solves an LP corresponding to Correlation Clustering, and then applies a
158   P IVOT-based algorithm using the LP solution. Next, we describe our algorithms. The high-level idea
159   is to incorporate the above LP rounding approach with the “truncated” P IVOT algorithms (Cambus
160   et al., 2024; Chakrabarty & Makarychev, 2023), where our predictions correspond to a feasible LP
161   solution in some sense. Specifically, for dynamic streams, we maintain a certain number of ℓ0 -
      samplers during the stream and derive a truncated subgraph at the end of the stream. Then we run


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the P IVOT algorithm and the LP rounding style algorithm on the subgraph respectively and obtain
163   two clusterings. Finally, we output the clustering with the lower cost. For insertion-only streams, we
164   employ two different methods respectively to store at most k neighbors for each vertex during the
165   stream. The first method is similar to the P IVOT algorithm and the second method is similar to the
166   LP rounding style algorithm. Then we run the P IVOT algorithm on two stored subgraphs and output
167   the clustering with the lower cost. We note that the cost of a clustering cannot be exactly calculated
168   during the stream, since our algorithms cannot store the entire graph. Therefore, we apply the graph
169   sparsification techniques to approximate the clustering cost within a multiplicative factor of (1 ± ε).
170   The analysis is non-trivial, even in insertion-only streams. We categorize all clusters into pivot
171   clusters and singleton clusters, and analyze their costs respectively. Our key observation is that
172   the truncated version of the LP rounding algorithm is equivalent to the algorithm that first samples
173   a subgraph G′ according to the predictions and then runs the “truncated” P IVOT algorithms on
174   G′ . Our main technical contribution is to prove that 1) the cost of pivot clusters produced by the
175   truncated version of the LP rounding algorithm is at most 2.06β times the cost of optimal solution
176   (Lemma 3.6 and Lemma 4.2); 2) the optimal solution on G′ does not differ from the optimal solution
177
      on the original graph G by a lot (Lemma 4.3). In this way, our algorithms can keep the space small
      while achieving an approximation ratio better than 3 under good prediction quality.

179   2    P RELIMINARIES
180
      Notations. Throughout the paper, we let G = (V, E) be an undirected and unweighted complete
181
      graph with |V | = n, |E| = m, where each edge is labeled as positive or negative (i.e., E =
182   E + ∪ E − ). In some places of the paper, we identify the input graph only with the set of positive
183   edges, i.e., G+ = (V, E + ) and the negative edges are defined implicitly. For each vertex u ∈ V , let
184   N (u) be the set of all neighbors of u and N + (u) be the set of positive neighbors of u (i.e., vertices
185   that are connected by a positive edge). Correspondingly, let deg(u) := |N (u)| be the degree of u,
186   and similarly, deg+ (u) := |N + (u)|. We use costG (C) to denote the cost of the clustering C on
187   G. We say an algorithm achieves an α-approximation if it outputs a clustering C on G such that
188   OPT ≤ costG (C) ≤ α · OPT, where OPT denotes the cost of an optimal solution on G.
189
      Next, we give the formal definition of a β-level predictor.
190
      Definition 2.1 (β-level predictor). For any β ≥ 1, we call a predictor β-level, if it predicts the
191
      pairwise dissimilarities duv between any two
                                                 P vertices u and vPin V such that (1) (triangle inequality)
192   duv + dvw ≥ duw for all u, v, w ∈ V , (2) (u,v)∈E + duv + (u,v)∈E − (1 − duv ) ≤ β · OPT.
193
      Intuitively, a smaller β indicates a higher-quality predictor, and in this case duv can be used to
194
      determine how likely u and v are in the same cluster of the optimal solution. However, we point
195   out that the predictions can be completely independent of the input graph. In the worst case, the
196   predictions can be arbitrary, which is allowed for LAAs since robustness is a desired goal.
197
198
      We remark that both our definition of the β-level predictor and our algorithms are inspired by an
      approximation algorithm given by Chawla et al. (2015), who give an LP based algorithm for Corre-
199
      lation Clustering achieving 2.06-approximation and in some sense, the β-level predictor corresponds
200
      to a solution to the LP in Chawla et al. (2015).

243   3.2 I MPLEMENTATION IN DYNAMIC STREAMS
244
      In this subsection, we implement the offline algorithm in dynamic streams, as shown in Algorithm 1.
245   A key observation is that it suffices to store the positive edges incident to interesting vertices since we
246   apply pivot-based algorithms on the subgraph induced by interesting vertices and then try to assign
247   uninteresting vertices to pivot clusters. To this end, we maintain a certain number of ℓ0 -samplers for
248   each vertex, which can be achieved in the dynamic semi-streaming model (Jowhari et al., 2011). As
249   we will see in the analysis, the ℓ0 -samplers allow us to recover the edges incident to all the interesting
250   vertices with high probability. Thus we can simulate the clustering phase of the offline algorithm.
251   Specifically, we simulate Algorithms T RUNCATED P IVOT and T RUNCATED P IVOT W ITH P RED using
252   the stored information, and output the clustering with the lower cost.
253   Note that in the final step, the cost of a clustering cannot be exactly calculated, as our streaming
254   algorithm cannot store the entire graph. To overcome this challenge, we borrow the idea from
255   (Behnezhad et al., 2023) and utilize the graph sparsification technique (Ahn et al., 2012) to estimate
256   the cost. Specifically, during the streaming phase, we maintain a cut sparsifier H + for the subgraph
257   G+ . Let AGM-S PARSIFICATION be any algorithm for constructing a cut sparsifier that satisfies
258   the guarantee in (Ahn et al., 2012) (see Appendix B). For each item si = (ei = (u, v), ∆i ) in the
259
      dynamic stream, where ∆i ∈ {−1, 1} indicates the insertion or deletion of ei , we apply AGM-
      S PARSIFICATION(H + , si ) to determine whether (u, v) belongs to H + and, if so, its corresponding
260
      weight in H + . We also maintain the positive degree deg+ (u) of each vertex u. Then we can
261
      approximate the cost of a clustering up to a (1 ± ε)-multiplicative error with high probability.

      4    A N ALGORITHM IN INSERTION - ONLY STREAMS WITH SMALLER SPACE
370   Overview. We first briefly describe a single-pass (3 + ε)-approximation streaming algorithm by
371   Chakrabarty & Makarychev (2023). Initially, the algorithm adds a positive self-loop for each vertex
372   and picks a random ordering π : V → {1, . . . , n}. The rank of u is denoted as πu . Then it scans the
373   input stream. For each vertex, the algorithm stores its k positive neighbors with lowest ranks, where
374
      k is a constant. Subsequently, it runs the P IVOT algorithm (Ailon et al., 2008) on the stored graph
      and picks pivots in the order of π. Finally, it puts unclustered vertices in singleton clusters.
375
376   Our main idea is to incorporate the above algorithm with the algorithm from Chawla et al. (2015).
377   Our algorithm uses the predictions of pairwise dissimilarities between any two vertices. We employ
      two different methods to store at most k neighbors of each vertex. The first method is the same as


                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Chakrabarty & Makarychev (2023) and the second method is adapted from Chawla et al. (2015),
379   which adds neighbors with probabilities determined by predictions of pairwise dissimilarities. Fi-
380   nally, we obtain two clusterings (denoted as C1 and C2 ) and output the one with the lower cost.
381   Similar to Algorithm 1, here we also need to use the graph sparsification technique (Kelner & Levin,
382   2011) to approximate the cost of a clustering.

391   Let F (t) denote the set of fresh vertices and U (t) denote the set of unclustered vertices at the begin-

      We present the first LAAs for Correlation Clustering in the streaming setting by leveraging
534   β-level predictions. Specifically, we provide single-pass streaming algorithms that achieve a
535   (min{2.06β, 3} + ε)-approximation for Correlation Clustering in both insertion-only and dynamic
536   streams. In particular, our algorithm in the dynamic setting is the first better-than-3-approximation
537   algorithm for Correlation Clustering in this context. Additionally, our algorithm is quite simple and
538   easy for implementation. There are many interesting future research directions, such as achieving
539   better space-approximation trade-offs with predictions than the standard setting, and finding more
      applications of prediction-based graph sparsification or sampling.

      Correlation Clustering. In this paper, we focus on the minimization version of Correlation Clus-
759   tering (i.e., minimizing the number of disagreements), which is the most commonly studied version.

761   version, which is to maximize the number of agreements, and provided a 0.766-approximation al-
762   gorithm via SDP. This problem is further examined on general graphs (Charikar et al., 2005) and on
763   weighted graphs (Demaine et al., 2006).

      Srinivas, 2023; Brand et al., 2024; Henzinger et al., 2024; DePavia et al., 2024), sublinear-time algo-

      et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023; Cohen-Addad et al., 2024a; Ghoshal et al., 2024; Braverman et al.,

922   Space Complexity. We first analyze the space complexity of Algorithm 1. For each vertex u ∈ V ,
923   we mainly store its rank πu , positive degree deg+ (u), and 10c log n · σu independent ℓ0 -samplers.
924   We have the following lemma which states the space requirement of ℓ0 -samplers.
925   Lemma D.1 (Cambus et al. (2024)). The ℓ0 -samplers used in Algorithm 1 require O(ε−1 n log4 n)
926   words of space.
927
928   Furthermore, by Theorem B.6, the AGM-S PARSIFICATION algorithm uses O(n log6 n +
929   ε−2 n log5 n) words of space. Therefore, the space complexity of Algorithm 1 is O(n log6 n +
930   ε−2 n log5 n) words.

932   Approximation Guarantee. Next, we analyze the approximation ratio of Algorithm 1. We rely

1269   G.2     D ETAILED DESCRIPTIONS OF PREDICTORS
1270
1271   Noisy predictor. We use this predictor for datasets with available optimal clusterings. We form
1272
       this predictor by performing perturbations on optimal clusterings. Specifically, for any two vertices
       u, v ∈ V , if u and v are in different clusters in the optimal clustering, then we set the prediction duv
1273
       to be 1 − ε0 , otherwise ε0 , where ε0 ∈ (0, 0.5). For synthetic datasets with p = 0.95, we can assume
1274
       that the ground truths are also optimal solutions. For real-world datasets, we use the powerful LP
1275   solver Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2023) to get the optimal clusterings.
1276
1277   Spectral clustering. We use this predictor for E MAIL C ORE and L AST FM. It first maps all the
1278
       vertices to a d-dimensional Euclidean space using the graph Laplacian, then clusters all the vertices
       based on their embeddings. For any two vertices u, v ∈ V , we form the prediction duv to be
1279         ⟨xu ,xv ⟩
1280
       1 − ∥x  u ∥∥xv ∥
                        , where xu , xv ∈ Rd are spectral embeddings of u and v, and ⟨xu , xv ⟩ is the dot
1281   product of xu and xv . Note that a larger d indicates a higher-quality predictor.
1282   Binary classifier. We use this predictor for datasets where ground-truth communities are available.
1283   This predictor is constructed by training a binary classifier (based on an MLP model) to predict
1284   whether two vertices belong to the same cluster using node features. In this setting, the goal of Cor-
1285   relation Clustering aligns with that of community detection by treating edges between two vertices
1286   in the same (ground-truth) community as positive edges and edges between two vertices in different
1287   communities as negative edges. The predictions provided by the binary classifier (i.e., binary values
1288
       in {0, 1}) are then used as the pairwise dissimilarities duv in our algorithms.
