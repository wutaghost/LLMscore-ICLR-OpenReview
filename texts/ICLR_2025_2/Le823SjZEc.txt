009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In modern healthcare, whole-slide histological images (WSIs) provide information
012            on tissue structure and composition at the microscopic level. Integrating WSIs
013            and gene expression profiles enhances cancer diagnosis and treatment planning,
014            advancing clinical care and research. However, spatial transcriptomics is costly
015            and requires a long sampling time. The intrinsic correlation between histological
016            images and gene expressions offers the potential for predicting spatial transcrip-
017            tomics using Hematoxylin-Eosin (H&E) stained WSIs to reduce time and resource
018            costs. Although existing methods have achieved impressive results, they ignore the
019
               heterogeneity between modalities of image and gene expression. In this paper, we
               propose a Quantized Cross-modal Alignment (QCA) that exploits cross-modal in-
020
               teractions to address the issue of modal heterogeneity. Considering the interference
021
               of gene-unrelated image features, we develop a Gene-related Image Feature Quan-
022            tizer (GIFQ) to capture the gene-related image features. Meanwhile, we develop an
023            Asymmetric Cross-modal Alignment (ACA) approach, which facilitates the model
024            to generate discriminative predictions from similar visual presentations. In addition,
025            to fix the discriminability reduction, a Discriminability-Enhancing Regularization
026            (DER) is further devised to regularize both the virtual and real gene features. Ex-
027            perimental results on a breast cancer dataset sampled by solid-phase transcriptome
028            capture elucidate that our QCA model achieves state-of-the-art results for accurate
029            prognostication of gene expression profiles, increasing the performance by 13%
030
               at least. Our method utilizes deep learning technology to delineate the correlation
               between morphological features and gene expression, furnishing new perspectives
031
               and instruments for disclosing biomarkers in histological conditions. The code will
032
               be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   In modern healthcare, whole-slide histological images (WSIs) are the gold standard for cancer
039
      diagnosis, offering detailed views of tissue samples that enhance pathologists’ accuracy in identifying
      cancer Ghaznavi et al. (2013); Lu et al. (2021); Van der Laak et al. (2021). The combination of WSIs
040
      and gene expression profiles allows for visualizing tissue morphology alongside the corresponding
041
      gene expression profiles, thereby facilitating a more comprehensive understanding of cancer’s
042   molecular mechanisms. However, implementing spatial transcriptomics is a high cost and requires
043   extended sampling periods. This has prompted the exploration of alternative methodologies that can
044   mitigate these drawbacks while still harnessing the valuable information provided by WSIsRao et al.
045   (2021).
046
      The intrinsic correlation between the morphological features of Hematoxylin-Eosin (H&E) stained
047
      WSIs and the underlying gene expression patterns presents a promising avenue for developing
048   predictive modelsSchmauch et al. (2020). By leveraging the wealth of data present in H&E stained
049   WSIs, researchers aim to predict spatial transcriptomics data, significantly reducing the time and
050   resource expenditure associated with traditional spatial transcriptomics techniques. Previous studies
051   have demonstrated that sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics data contain thousands of genes,
052   many of which vary insignificantly Williams et al. (2022); Marx (2021). Predicting high-variable
053   genes could be beneficial for disease diagnosis and drug selection. Therefore, we expect to develop
      models to predict the expressions of these genes based on the H&E stained WSI.


                                                         1
054
      Despite the extensive and profound development of algorithms for deriving information from spatial
055   transcriptomics data Lu et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Shmatko et al. (2022), methodologies for
056   predicting spatial transcriptomics data with histological image information have not yet achieved
057   comparable advancements. Deep learning techniques frequently encounter the interference of gene-
058   unrelated image features and exhibit low performance He et al. (2020); Pang et al. (2021); Zeng et al.
059   (2022). Moreover, a methodology Xie et al. (2024) based on image retrieval tends to yield predictions
060   resembling the predominant samples within the top K selection and necessitates enhanced robustness
061   against outliers and sample imbalance. While specific probability-based models underscore the
062   relative associations of spatial gene expressions, their prediction is always around the mean values
063
      of each gene, lacking discriminability Srivastava et al. (2017); Thanh-Tung & Tran (2020). In
      addition, all the existing methods employ total normalization for gene expression profiles to predict
064
      the proportion of gene expressions at sampling points rather than predicting the actual value of gene
065
      expression.
066
067   Previous methods generally assumed that the conditional probability distribution of gene expression
068   profiles was continuous log-normal Limpert et al. (2001). Actually, one crucial pre-processing step
069
      in gene expression prediction is to perform log1p-transformation, which will significantly change
      the probability distribution of the transformed data. As shown in Figure 1, the log1p-transformation
070
      transfers the gene expression profiles from log-normal distribution to normal distribution, leading to
071
      the different value densities between high- and low-expressed ranges. This phenomenon reveals the
072   necessity to increase the variation of low-expressed genes, thereby predicting gene expressions with
073   high accuracy. Moreover, the sparse distribution of sampling spots in spatial transcriptomes causes
074   the spatial proximity of image patches to have a relatively small effect on their content similarity,
075   failing to improve prediction performance through spatial information effectively.
076
      To address the above challenges, this paper proposes a novel method termed the Quantized Cross-
077
      modal Alignment (QCA) model, which exploits cross-modal interactions between the image and
078   the gene information. Considering the interference of gene-unrelated image features, we develop a
079   Gene-related Image Feature Quantization (GIFQ) to selectively compress image features, highlighting
080   the gene-related features. Meanwhile, we propose an Asymmetric Cross-modal Alignment (ACA),
081   facilitating the model to generate discriminative predictions from similar visual presentations. In
082   addition, a Discriminability-Enhancing Regularization (DER) is devised to regularize both the virtual
083   and real gene features, consequently fixing the discriminability reduction and enabling the prediction
084   of the gene expression profiles. The main contributions of this study can be summarized as follows:
085
086
             • The proposed QCA method offers a solution for predicting spatial transcriptomics from WSI
               images through cross-modal interaction techniques. Based on the multimodal translation
087
               model, our method incorporates gene expression profiles to effectively align the image and
088
               gene models and optimize gene-related image features by utilizing the ACA and GIFQ. The
089            DER is also devised to fix the discriminability reduction.
090
091
             • The QCA model achieves state-of-the-art prediction results on a breast cancer dataset sam-
               pled by solid-phase transcriptome capture, verifying the model’s effectiveness in predicting
092
               spatial transcriptomics from WSI images. The model significantly surpasses existing meth-
093
               ods in terms of predictive correlation for marker genes (MGs), high-variable genes (HVGs),
094            and high-expressed genes (HEGs).
095
096
             • This study is the first to predict the actual gene expression profiles instead of the expression
               proportion using deep learning techniques. Our method can uncover genes related to
097
               biological histological features, establishing a statistical connection between morphological
098
               features and gene expressions.

143   2.2   F INITE S CALAR Q UANTIZATION
144
145   Vector quantization technology Gray (1984) has found extensive application in deep learning, particu-
146   larly in data compression Barnes et al. (1996). This technology achieves effective feature compression
147   by mapping continuous latent spaces to discrete coding spaces. In the VQ-VAE model Van Den Oord
148   et al. (2017), input data is first mapped to the latent space through an encoder network, subsequently
149   assigned to the nearest codebook vector via a quantization process, and finally reconstructed or
150   generated to resemble the input data through a decoder network. Constructing a discrete latent space
151   enhances the model stability and the convergence of the training process.
152   Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) technology Mentzer et al. (2023) is a technique used to optimize
153   vector quantization and is particularly applicable in domains such as image generation. The primary
154   advantage of FSQ lies in its ability to effectively mitigate the codebook collapse issue prevalent
155   in VQ-VAE. Moreover, FSQ is characterized by its simplicity of implementation, as it obtains an
156   implicit codebook vector by projecting VQ-VAE representations into a lower-dimensional space and
157   quantizing each dimension. This approach has demonstrated competitive performance in feature
158
      compression and possesses fewer parameters than VQ-VAE.
159   Inspired by FSQ, this paper proposes a GIFQ, which aims to compress image features and selectively
160   preserve image features associated with gene expressions through learning codebook vectors. This
161   approach seeks to enhance image features in deep learning models by reducing the interference of
      gene-unrelated features.

      3.1   A SYMMETRIC C ROSS - MODAL A LIGNMENT
203
204   In gene expression prediction tasks, neither probability-based models nor multimodal translation
205   models have failed to generate discriminative predictions from similar visual presentations. However,
206   samples with similar visual presentations may exhibit different gene expression features in reality.
207   Aligning gene expression features with image features can emphasize the correlation between the
208   modalities, effectively addressing the issue.
209
      One straightforward way is to constrain the features of the modalities to be identical symmetrically
210   Radford et al. (2021). However, it fails to highlight the similarity in the intra-modality. To address
211   this problem, we propose an Asymmetric Cross-modal Alignment (ACA) that calculates the similarity
212   matrices between image features and gene expression features separately and uses the cross-entropy
213   loss function to align these matrices, thereby guiding the image feature extractor to learn features
214   related to gene expression. Specifically, in scenarios where "image features are dissimilar, but gene
215   expressions are similar," the confusion has only a minimal impact. In contrast, scenarios where
      "gene expressions are dissimilar, but image features are similar" require attention. For similarity

292

293   and temporal extent of spatial transcriptomic sampling, high-expressed genes vary greatly compared

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
433
434
      The ablation experiments presented in Table 2 reveal that the model utilizes an asymmetric cross-
435   modal interaction framework to more accurately predict gene expression profiles using image infor-
436   mation, showcasing enhanced precision and stability compared to the computational approach of
437   directly aligning the features of the modalities. In addition, we can observe that the ACA solves the
438   problem of confusing predictions from similar visual presentations through an emphasis on scenarios
439   where "gene expressions are dissimilar, but image features are similar." This can be demonstrated
440   in high-variable genes (HVGs), which are significantly affected by similar predictions and obtain
441   0.055 improvements compared to the model, which constrains the features to be identical (0.152). In
442   addition, we can also find that identical feature alignment presents a significant improvement over the
443
      model without alignment in HVGs.
444   Regarding selecting the hyperparameter α, it is challenging to simultaneously achieve optimal results
445   for all evaluation metrics under specified values of α. Consequently, we employ the MSE to attain
446   the most balanced result. As shown in Table 3, the choice of α = 0.1 yielded the minimal MSE,
447   suggesting that imposing a relatively higher loss on the image feature extractor is appropriate. This
448   indicates that the model requires more substantial adjustments to image features than regulating gene
449
      expression feature extractors.
450   Besides, we utilized Shannon entropy Lin (1991) to evaluate the discriminability of each gene, with
451   higher entropy indicating greater discriminability in gene expression. Figure 4 (b) depicts the entropy
452   differences stratified by regularized weights. The figure shows that the model can adapt varying
453   amounts of information for different genes. This adjustment has enhanced the model’s ability to
454   distinguish gene expression features, particularly those with high entropy. Notably, although the
455
      model has achieved relatively stable results in predicting MGs, the average correlation coefficient
      did not surpass the outcome when β was set to 0. This discrepancy might be attributed to the lack of
456
      discriminability in MGs compared to HVGs and HEGs, necessitating further analysis of the biological
457
      significance of marker genes to enhance the stability of their prediction.

473
      In this paper, we propose a Quantized Cross-modal Alignment (QCA) model consisting of three
474   modules. The Asymmetric Cross-modal Alignment (ACA) improves the image features to address the
475   issue of predicting confusing gene expression from image features. The Gene-related Image Feature
476   Quantization (GIFQ) tackles the issue of mode collapse, which frequently occurs in gene prediction.
477   Furthermore, the Discriminability-Enhancing Regularization (DER) enhances the discriminability of
478   both virtual and real gene features to achieve an accurate prediction of gene expressions. The com-
479   parative and ablation experimental results indicate that the QCA model is significantly advantageous
480   in predicting gene expression profiles.
481   However, due to non-biological experimental factors, gene expression from different samples ex-
482   hibits significant variations, which may limit the absolute performance of the model in generating
483   gene predictions. Advancements in sampling techniques and matched spatial transcriptomic data
484   availability can enable a deeper exploration of the multimodal interaction latent space. At the same
485   time, bioinformatics analysis of gene ontology can help better determine and predict logically related
      genes.


                                                        9
486
      In summary, our QCA is a deep learning framework that utilizes cross-modal interaction technology
487   to address the adverse properties of gene expression profiles when interacting with other modalities.
488   Our study is the first to predict the actual gene expression profiles using deep learning techniques.
489   It provides new perspectives and tools for a deeper understanding of the complicated relationship
490   between gene expression and morphological structure.
