009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Outlier detection in high-dimensional tabular data is an important task in data min-
012            ing, essential for many downstream tasks and applications. Existing unsupervised
013            outlier detection algorithms face one or more problems, including inlier assump-
014            tion (IA), curse of dimensionality (CD), and multiple views (MV). To address
015            these problems, we introduce Generative Subspace Adversarial Active Learning
016            (GSAAL), a novel approach that uses a Generative Adversarial Network with mul-
017            tiple adversaries. These adversaries learn the marginal class probability functions
018
               over different data subspaces, while a single generator in the full space models
               the entire distribution of the inlier class. By design, GSAAL addresses MV while
019
               also handling IA and CD, and is the only method to address all three. We pro-
020
               vide a mathematical formulation of MV, theoretical guarantees for the training of
021            GSAAL, and its scalability analysis. Our extensive experiments demonstrate the
022            effectiveness and scalability of GSAAL and highlight its superior performance
023            compared to other popular OD methods, especially in MV scenarios.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Outlier detection (OD), a fundamental and widely recognized task in data mining, involves the
029   identification of anomalous or deviating data points within a dataset. Outliers are typically defined as
030   low-probability occurrences within a population Wang et al. (2019); Han et al. (2022). In the absence
031   of access to the true probability distribution of the data points, OD algorithms rely on constructing a
032   scoring function. Points with higher scores are more likely to be outliers. Existing unsupervised OD
033
      algorithms have one or more of the following problems, in high-dimensional tabular data scenarios.
034
             • The inlier assumption (IA): OD algorithms often make assumptions about what constitutes
035
               an inlier, which can be challenging to verify and validate Liu et al. (2020).
036
037          • The curse of dimensionality (CD): As the dimensionality of data increases, the challenge of
038            identifying outliers intensifies, decreasing the effectiveness of certain OD methods Bellman
039
               (1957)
040          • Multiple Views (MV): Outliers are often only visible in certain ”views” of the data and are
041            hidden in the full space of original features Müller et al. (2012)
042
043   We now explain these problems one by one.
044   The inlier assumption poses a challenge to algorithms that assume a standard profile of the inlier
045   data. For example, angle-based algorithms like ABOD Kriegel et al. (2008) assume that inliers have
046   other inliers at all angles. Similarly, neighbor-based algorithms like kNN Ramaswamy et al. (2000)
047   assume that inliers have other neighboring points nearby. These assumptions influence the scoring
048   as it measures the degree to which a sample deviates from this assumed norm. Consequently, the
049   performance of these algorithms may degrade if these assumptions do not hold Liu et al. (2020).
050
      This means that a general OD method should not make any inlier assumptions.
051   The curse of dimensionality Bellman (1957) refers to the decrease in the relative proximity of data
052   points as the number of dimensions increases. Simply put, with high dimensionality, the distance
053   between any pair of points becomes similar, regardless of whether none, one, or both of the points
      in a pair are outliers. This is particularly problematic for OD methods that rely on distances or on

070   (e.g., kNN Ramaswamy et al. (2000)), and cluster-based (e.g., SVDD (Aggarwal, 2017, Chapter 2)).

      Multiple Views refers to the phenomenon that certain complex correlations between features are
      only observable in some feature subspaces Müller et al. (2012). As detailed in Aggarwal (2017), this
073
      occurs when the dataset contains additional irrelevant features, making some outliers only detectable
074
      in certain subspaces. In scenarios where multiple subspaces contain different interesting structures,
075   this problem is exacerbated. It then becomes increasingly difficult to infer whether a point belongs
076   to a particular distribution based solely on its representation in a single subspace Keller et al. (2013).
077   This problem can occur regardless of the dimensionality of the dataset if the number of points is
078   insufficient to capture a complex correlation structure.
079
      The following example illustrates the three problems described above
080
081   Example 1 (Effect of MV, IA and CD). Consider the random variables x1 , x2 and x3 , where x1
082   and x2 are highly correlated and x3 is Gaussian noise. Figure 1 plots datasets with 20, 100 and
083   1000 realizations of (x1 , x2 , x3 ). It also contains the classification boundaries from both a locality-
084
      based method (green) and a cluster-based method (red) in the subspace. The cluster-based detector
      fitted in the full 3D space fails to detect the outlier shown in the figure (red cross) with n = 20 and
085
      100 realizations. However, the outlier is always detected in the 2D subspace, as we can see. Once
086
      we increase the number of samples over n = 1000, the cluster-based method detects the outlier in
087   the full space (MV). On the contrary, the locality-based method could not detect the outlier in any
088   tested scenario (MV + IA). If we increase the dimensionality by adding more features consisting of
089   noise, neither of the considered methods will detect the outlier in the full space (MV + IA + CD).
090
091   We are interested in tackling outlier detection whenever a population exhibits MV, like Müller et al.
092   (2012); Keller et al. (2013); Kriegel et al. (2009) and as showcased in Aggarwal (2017). Particularly,
093   the goal of this paper is to propose the first outlier detection method that explicitly addresses IA, CD,
094   and MV simultaneously.
095   As we will explain in the next section, we build on Generative Adversarial Active Learning
096   (GAAL) Zhu & Bento (2017), a widely used approach for outlier detection Liu et al. (2020); Guo
097   et al. (2021); Sinha et al. (2019). It involves training a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)
098   to mimic the distribution of outlier data, and it enhances the discriminator’s performance through
099   active learning Settles (2009), leveraging the GAN’s data generation capability. GAAL methods
100   avoid IA Liu et al. (2020) and use the multi-layered structure of the GAN to overcome the curse of
101   dimensionality Poggio et al. (2020). However, they often miss important subspaces, leading to MV.
102
103   Challenges. Training multiple GAN-based models in individual subspaces is not trivial. (1) The
104   joint training of generators and discriminators in GANs requires careful monitoring to determine
105   the optimal stopping point, a task that becomes daunting for large ensembles. (2) The generation of
106   difficult-to-detect points in a subspace remains hard Steinbuss & Böhm (2017). (3) While several
107   authors have proposed multi-adversarial architectures for GANs Durugkar et al. (2016); Choi &
      Han (2022), none of them address adversaries tailored to subspaces composed of feature subsets.

      This section is a brief overview of popular unsupervised outlier detection methods for tabular data
      related to our approach. We categorize them based on their ability to address the specific limitations
144
      outlined above. Table 1 is a comparative summary.
145
146
147
      Classical Methods Conventional outlier detection approaches, such as distance-based strategies
      like LOF and KNN, angle-based techniques like ABOD, and cluster-based methods like SVDD,
148
      rely on specific assumptions on the behavior of inlier data. They use a scoring function to measure
149
      deviations from this norm. These methods face the inlier assumption limitation by definition. For
150   example, local methods that assume isolated outliers fail when several outlying samples fall together.
151   In addition, many classical methods, which rely on measuring distances, are susceptible to the curse
152   of dimensionality. Both limitations impair the effectiveness of these methods Liu et al. (2020).
153
154   Subspace Methods Subspace-based methods Kriegel et al. (2009) operate in lower-dimensional
155   subspaces formed by subsets of features. They effectively counteract the curse of dimensionality
156   by focusing on identifying so-called “subspace outliers” Keller et al. (2012). These outliers, which
157   are prevalent in high-dimensional datasets with many correlated features, are often elusive to con-
158   ventional non-subspace methods Liu et al. (2008); Müller et al. (2012). However, existing subspace
159   methods inherently operate on specific assumptions on the nature of anomalies in each subspace
160   they explore, and thus face the inlier assumption limitation.

      Generative Methods A common strategy to mitigate the IA and CD limitations is to reframe the
163   task as a classification task using self-supervision. A prevalent self-supervised technique, particu-
164   larly for tabular data, is the generation of artificial outliers El-Yaniv & Nisenson (2006); Liu et al.
165   (2020). This method involves distinguishing between actual training data and artificially generated
166   data drawn from a predetermined “reference distribution”. Hempstalk et al. (2008) showed that by
167   approximating the class probability of being a real sample, one approximates the probability func-
168   tion of being an inlier. One then uses this approximation as a scoring function Liu et al. (2020).
169   However, it is not easy to find the right reference distribution, and a poor choice can affect OD by
170   much Hempstalk et al. (2008).
171   A first approach to this challenge proposed the use of naı̈ve reference distributions by uniformly
172   generating data in the space. This approach showed promising results in low-dimensional spaces
173   but failed in high dimensions due to the curse of dimensionality Hempstalk et al. (2008). Other
174   approaches, such as assuming parametric distributions for inlier data (Aggarwal, 2017, Chapter 2) or
175   directly generating in susbpaces Désir et al. (2013), can avoid CD when the parametric assumptions
176   are met. Methods that generate in the subspaces can model the subspace behavior, additionally
177
      tackling the MV limitation. However, these last two approaches do not address the IA limitation, as
      they make specific assumptions about the behavior of the inlier data.
178
179

181
182   Generative Adversarial Active Learning According to Hempstalk et al. (2008), the closer the
183   reference distribution is to the inlier distribution, the better the final approximation to the inlier
184   probability function will be. Hence, recent developments in generative methods have focused on
185   learning the reference distribution in conjunction with the classifier. A key approach is the use
186   of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), where the generator converges to the inlier distribu-
187   tion Goodfellow et al. (2014). The most common approaches for this are GAAL-based methods Liu
188
      et al. (2020); Guo et al. (2021); Sinha et al. (2019).
189   GAAL methods differ from other GANs for OD by training the detectors using active learning after
190   normal convergence of the GAN Schlegl et al. (2017); Donahue et al. (2017). This particular training
191   regime allows convergence guarantees of the detector Liu et al. (2020), in contrast to other GANs
192   for OD that rely on a reconstruction-based score Donahue et al. (2017); Schlegl et al. (2017); Akcay
193   et al. (2019). The convergence guarantees of the detector to the proper density is crucial for outlier
194
      detection in tabular data Liu et al. (2020); Hempstalk et al. (2008); Steinbuss & Böhm (2017).
195   The architecture of GAAL inherently addresses the curse of dimensionality, as GANs can incorpo-
196   rate layers designed to manage high-dimensional data Poggio et al. (2020). In practice, GAAL-based
197   methods outperformed all their competitors in their original work. However, they overlook the be-
198   havior of the data in subspaces and therefore may be susceptible to MV. Our method, GSAAL, incor-
199   porates several subspace-focused detectors into GAAL. These detectors approximate the marginal
200
      inlier probability functions of their subspaces. Thus, GSAAL effectively addresses MV while inher-
      iting GAAL’s ability to overcome IA and CD limitations.

205   Deep Outlier Detection Beyond Tabular Data Outlier detection is widely used for non-tabular
206   data types, especially for unstructured data Xu et al. (2023); Goodge et al. (2021); Schlegl et al.
207   (2017); Ruff et al. (2018); Perozzi et al. (2014). Deep methods dominate due to the complexity of
208   such data. Unlike tabular data, where deep methods focus on CD, the architecture for unstructured
209   data, such as images or natural language, is driven by the complexity of the data. For example,
210   image processing requires more complex layers, such as convolutional or residual layers, rather than
211   simple linear layers LeCun et al. (2015).
212   Most deep methods are rarely applied to tabular data in their original works. However, some of them
213   still appear as competitors in this domain Schlegl et al. (2017); Ruff et al. (2018). Since our focus is

      detection methods from other domains. Sections B.2 and B.3 present these additional experiments.

222   3.1   M ULTIPLE V IEWS
223
224   Several authors Aggarwal (2017); Müller et al. (2012); Keller et al. (2013); Kriegel et al. (2009);
225   Liu et al. (2008) have observed that at times the variability of the data can only be explained from
226   its behavior in some subspaces. Researchers variably call this problem “the subspace problem”
227   Aggarwal (2017); Kriegel et al. (2009) or “multiple views of the data” Keller et al. (2012); Müller
228
      et al. (2012). Previous research has largely focused on practical scenarios, leaving aside the need for
      a formal definition. In response, we propose a unifying definition of “multiple views” that provides
229
      a foundation for developing methods to address this challenge effectively.
230
231   The problem “multiple views” of data (MV) arises from two different effects. First, it requires the
232   ability to understand the behavior of a random vector x by examining lower-dimensional subsets of
233   its components (x1 , . . . , xd ). Second, it stems from the challenge of insufficient data to obtain an
234
      effective scoring function in the full space of x. As Example 1 shows, combining these two effects
      obscures the behavior of the data in the full space. Hence, methods not considering subspaces when
235
      building their scoring function may have issues detecting outliers under MV. The next definition
236
      formalizes the first effect.
237
      Definition 1 (myopic distribution). Consider a random vector x : Ω −→ Rd and Diagd×d ({0, 1}),
238
      the set of diagonal binary matrices without the identity. If there exists a random matrix u : Ω −→
239
      Diagd×d ({0, 1}), such that
240                                       px (x) = pux (ux) for almost all x,                              (1)
241
      we say that the distribution of x is myopic to the views of u. Here, x and ux are realizations of x
242
      and ux, and px and pux are the pdfs of x and ux.
243
244   It is clear that, under MV, using pux to build a scoring function instead of px mitigates the effects.
245   This comes as the subspaces selected by u are smaller in dimensionality. Hence it should take fewer
246   samples to approximate the pdf of ux. The difficulty is that it is not yet clear how to approximate
247   pux . The following proposition elaborates on a way to do so. It states that by averaging a collection
248   of marginal distributions of x in the subspaces given by realizations of u, one can approximate the
249   distribution of pux .

251   independent realizations of u: {ui }ki=1 . Then k1 i pui x (ui x) is an unbiased statistic for pux (ux).

      MV appears when there is a lack of data, and its distribution is myopic. To improve OD under MV,
253
      one can exploit the myopicity to model x in the subspaces, where less data is sufficient. Proposition 1
254
      gives us a way to do so, by approximating pux . In this way, under myopicity, this also approximates

502   5     L IMITATIONS & C ONCLUSIONS
503
504   5.1     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
505
      In section 4 we randomly selected subspaces for training the detectors in GSAAL, i.e. we took a
506
      uniform distribution of u. This was already sufficient to demonstrate the highly competitive per-
507
      formance of our method. In practice, this assumption seemed to perform well for our experiments.
508   However, GSAAL can work with any subspace search strategy to obtain the distribution of u, for
509   example, the methods exploiting multiple views Keller et al. (2013; 2012). We have not included
510   them in this paper due to the lack of an official implementation. In the future, we plan to benchmark
511   various subspace search methods in GSAAL.
512
      Next, GSAAL is limited to tabular data, since the “multiple views” problem has only been observed
513
      for this data type. The mathematical formulation of MV in section 3 does not exclude unstructured
514   data. The difficulty lies in identifying good search strategies for u for non-tabular data, which
515   remains an open question Gupta et al. (2017). However, depending on the type of unstructured data,
516   extending GSAAL to work with it is not immediate. Therefore, building a method that exploits the
517   theoretical derivations of GSAAL for structured data is future work.
518
519   5.2     C ONCLUSIONS
520
521   Unsupervised outlier detection (OD) methods rely on a scoring function to distinguish inliers from
522   outliers, since the true probability function that generated the dataset is usually unavailable in prac-
523
      tice. However, they face one or more of the following problems — Inlier Assumption (IA), Curse
      of Dimensionality (CD), or Multiple Views (MV). In this article, we have proposed the first mathe-
524
      matical formulation of MV, which allows for a better understanding of how to solve this occurrence.
525
      Using this formulation, we developed GSAAL, which is the first OD approach that solves MV, CD,
526   and IA. In short, GSAAL is a generative adversarial network with a generator and multiple detectors
527   fitted in the subspaces to find outliers not visible in the full space. In our experiments on 27 different
528   datasets, we demonstrated the usefulness of GSAAL, in particular, its ability to deal with MV and
529   its superior performance on OD tasks with real datasets. In addition, we have shown that GSAAL
530   can scale up to deal with high-dimensional data, which is not the case for our most competent com-
531   petitors. These results confirm GSAAL’s ability to deal with data exhibiting MV and its usability in
532   any practical scenario involving large datasets.

      A.1    P REVIOUS R EMARKS
763
764   Before starting to prove our main results, it is important to add a remark about our notation in
765   this article. Whenever we denote ux, we mean the operation resulting in the following vector:
766   u(ω)x(ω). Thus, ux is a random vector following its distribution pux . However, it is important to
767   remark that ux, and therefore, also ui x, does not state the usual matrix-vector multiplication. What
768   we mean by ux is the operation U ×M x, where U stands for the range-complete version of u and
769   ×M the usual matrix multiplication. This means that whenever we write ux we are considering
770
      the projection of x into the subspace of the features selected in u. This means that ui x is the
      random vector composed of the features selected by ui , and therefore, pui x (ui x) denotes subsequent
771
      marginal pdf of x. We do not state this in the main text as it functionally does not change anything
772
      of our derivations, and simply works as a notation. The only important remarks stemming from this
773   fact are the following:
774
775         1. px (ui x) = px (πui (x)), where πui denotes the projection of a point x into the subspace of
776            ui . Therefore, we can write px (ui x) = pui x (ui x).
777         2. The operator as stated before is not distributive. This is trivial, as given u a random matrix
778            as in definition 1, (1d − u)x is defined properly, as 1d − u ∈ Diag({0, 1}). However,
779            x − ux denotes the vector subtraction between two vectors with different dimensionality.
780
781   Additionally, ux should be understood and treated functionally as a different random vector, y. In
782   this sense, Definition 1 simply states that there has to exist a special random vector y that we can
783   prove that has the same distribution as x.
784   While not important to understand the following proofs and the derivations from the main text,
785   understanding this is crucial for anyone seeking to work with these definitions.
786
787   A.2    P ROOFS
788

1005

1006   et al. (2016). We considered the optimization to follow minibatch-stochastic gradient descent Good-

1008   necessary transformations to the gradients.

1010
       parameters for the method and the training. In this case, epochs refers to the total number of epochs
1011
       we will train in total, while stop epoch marks the epoch where we start step 2 of the GAAL training.
1012   Lines 1-3 initialize both the detectors in their subspaces and the generator with random weight
1013   matrices ΘDi and ΘG . Lines 4-13 correspond to the normal GAN training loop across multiple
1014   epochs, referred to as step 1 of a GAAL method, if epoch < stop epoch. Here we proceed with
1015   training each detector and the generator using their gradients. Lines 8-10 update each detector by
1016   ascending its stochastic gradient, while line 11 updates the generator by descending its stochastic
1017   gradient. After the normal GAN training, we start the active learning loop Liu et al. (2020) once
1018   epoch ≥ stop epoch. The only difference with the regular GAN training is that G remains fixed,
1019   i.e., we do not descend using its gradient. This allows us to additionally train the detectors and, in
1020
