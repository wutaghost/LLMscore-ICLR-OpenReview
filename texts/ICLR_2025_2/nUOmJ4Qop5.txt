009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid adoption of deep neural networks underscores an urgent need for mod-
012            els to be safe, trustworthy and well-calibrated. Despite recent advancements in
013            network calibration, the optimal combination of techniques remains relatively un-
014            explored. By framing the task as a multi-objective optimization problem, we
015            demonstrate that combining state-of-the-art methods can further boost calibra-
016            tion performance. We feature a total of seven state-of-the-art calibration algo-
017
               rithms and provide both theoretical and empirical motivation for their equal and
               weighted importance unification. We conduct experiments on both in-distribution
018
               and out-of-distribution computer vision and natural language benchmarks, investi-
019
               gating the speeds and contributions of different components. Our code is available
020            anonymously at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Peacock-1CE8 .

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Key requirements for the safe deployment of neural networks include multiple desirable qualities,
026   such as high accuracy, fast training speeds and trustworthy predictions. While the recent successes
027   in deep learning have increased the use of complex neural networks, a common observation is that
028   deep models tend to be miscalibrated, exhibiting either under- or over-confident predictions (Guo
029   et al., 2017).
030   Miscalibration can be particularly dangerous for high-stakes, safety-critical tasks such as medical
031   prognosis (Esteva et al., 2017; Bandi et al., 2019), object-detection (Munir et al., 2023a;b; Liu et al.,
032   2024), AI fairness and decision-making (Pleiss et al., 2017; Corvelo Benz & Rodriguez, 2023). Such
033   tasks demand reliable decision-making algorithms, necessitating accurate confidence estimates that
034   reflect a model’s uncertainty (Jiang et al., 2018; Kendall & Gal, 2017). Specifically, calibration
035   ensures that a model’s predicted confidences align with its actual correctness. For instance, if a
036
      model assigns 0.9 confidence to a set of 100 samples, we should expect the model to be correct for
      90 instances only.

077   ibration framework named Peacock. Our claim is that by jointly optimizing multiple calibration
078   objectives, performance boosts can be achieved for ID + OOD classification tasks. Theoretically,

080   errors of all its components. We also propose a novel weighted importance form of Peacock that

086   evaluate both ID and OOD performances of Peacock on popular synthetic and in-the-wild computer
087   vision and natural language tasks. Our contributions can be summarized into the following points.

532   6      C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   We present Peacock, a unified framework for neural network calibration. By formulating unification
535   as a multi-objective optimization problem, we demonstrate that combining calibration components
536   improves performance on both ID and OOD tasks. Our proposed weighted importance form of
537   Peacock is fast and effective in delivering good Pareto Optimal performance. Despite incorporating
538   multiple algorithms, Peacock’s complements post-hoc processing and remains fast in terms of com-
539   putational speed. Our method shows clear performance gains with RankMixup and MaxEnt loss
      offering the most improvements.

       A     C ALIBRATION A LGORITHMS AND M ETRICS
973
974
975    In this section, we further discuss in detail the various families of approaches commonly used to
976    improve and measure neural network calibration.
977
978
979
       A.1   E NTROPY- BASED M ETHODS
980
981
       Entropy-based methods have played an important role in calibrating deep neural networks, as max-
       imizing the entropy helps penalize overconfident predictions (Pereyra et al., 2017; Mukhoti et al.,
982
       2020; Neo et al., 2024). As mentioned in the main text, naively penalizing all predictions can cause
983
       underconfident predictions. While various works have proposed different approaches in controlling
984    the entropy term, the Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017; Mukhoti et al., 2020; Ghosh et al., 2022) and it’s
985    variants offer adaptive/automated mechanisms in obtaining suitable values of γ for each sample.
986
987
       While these automated mechanisms tend to help with ID calibration, many works fail to acknowl-
       edge the importance of OOD calibration since the parameters obtained during training/validation
988
       may not work during testing (Ovadia et al., 2019). As a work-around, we find that entropy-based
989
       methods can be extended to include OOD Maximum Entropy constraints (Jaynes, 1957; Neo et al.,
990    2024) or Dual logit manipulation (Tao et al., 2023), showcasing the versatility of entropy-based
991    methods. Since these methods all share the form of the Focal loss, we can easily pair all of them
992    together into a single step.
993
994
995    A.2   R EGULARIZERS
996
997    Mixup is an effective regularization technique that augments (Zhang et al., 2018) both input fea-
998    tures and labels. Mixup works particularly well on both wider and deeper networks (Zhang et al.,
999    2022) and can be particularly useful in improving network calibration (Thulasidasan et al., 2019;
1000   Chidambaram & Ge, 2024). As an extension to vanilla Mixup, RankMixup (Noh et al., 2023) can
1001
       be used to ensure that the augmented samples have lower confidences than the original samples.
1002
1003
       A.3   M ARGIN - BASED M ETHODS
1004
1005
       Margin-based methods tend to restrict model confidences by a constant margin/factor. For example,
1006
       label smoothing (LS) (Müller et al., 2019) softens the targets using a constant factor ϵ. Mathemat-
1007
       ically, the smoothed label si is acquired after uniformly adjusting the target si = (1 − ϵ)yk + Kϵ ,
1008   which is then used to train the network. Although vanilla LS can be used to improve miscalibration,
1009   imposing a constant smoothing factor for all training labels can lead to under-confident predictions.
1010   Furthermore, searching for a suitable ϵ is computationally expensive as it requires a grid-search
1011   across multiple models during the training phase.
1012
       Instead of implementing a fixed constant, several works have been proposed to adaptively or con-
1013
       ditionally approximate the label smoothing function during training. For example, MDCA (Heb-
1014   balaguppe et al., 2022) utilizes a regularization term, which enforces predicted confidences to be as
1015   close to the average accuracy as possible. This can lead to a parabolic smoothing function (Park
1016   et al., 2023), that is adaptively dependent on the predicted confidences. Which can be problematic,
1017   since both high and low confidence predictions are weakly penalized. Another approach would be
1018   to only conditionally smooth predictions based on a margin. For instance, MBLS (Liu et al., 2022a)
1019   and CALS-ALM (Liu et al., 2023a) propose to restrict output logits by a user defined margin, but
1020   can be sensitive to hyper parameter settings. CRL (Moon et al., 2020) ordinarily ranks predictions
1021   based on the number of times each sample is predicted correctly, however it requires a buffer to store
1022
       the correctness history. Which can be empty during the earlier stages of training and idle during later
       phases when the model’s accuracy is high.
1023
1024   By adopting a smoothing and indicator function, the Adaptive Conditional Label Smoothing (ACLS)
1025   (Park et al., 2023) method seeks to combine the benefits of both adaptive and conditional methods
       without the use of an additional correctness history.

       A.4   P OST- HOC P ROCESSING
1027
1028
       The fundamental idea behind post-hoc processing methods is to obtain a mapping func-
1029
       tion/temperature that modifies the model’s logits thus changing it’s predicted confidence. The most
1030   popular post-processing step is the vanilla temperature scaling (TS) (Platt & Karampatziakis, 2007),
1031   which manipulates the model’s confidences without changing the final class label predictions. For
1032   example, a value of T < 1 leads to a lower entropy or “peaky” distributions and a value of T > 1
1033   gives higher entropy or “flatter” predictions.
1034
       The typical approach in obtaining the temperature parameter, is to minimize the average calibration
1035
       error or NLL over a seperate valdation set. While vanilla TS has been found to be effective in
1036   reducing network over-confidence (Guo et al., 2017), it generally reduces the confidence of every
1037   sample - even when predictions are correct. Other forms of post-hoc processing include calibration
1038   using, model ensembles (Zhang et al., 2020), splines (Gupta et al., 2021) and distribution matching
1039   (Kuleshov & Deshpande, 2022; Tomani et al., 2023). For our post-processing step, we use AdaTS
1040   since it is the SOTA method for post-processing methods and adaptively chooses a samplewise
1041   temperature for scaling model predictions.
1042
1043
1044   A.5   C ALIBRATION M ETRICS
1045
1046   Expected Calibration Error (ECE): The ECE is the most widely used metric in the literature
1047   and directly tied to the definition of calibration (Guo et al., 2017; Tomani et al., 2021). By splitting
1048   the predicted confidences in B evenly separated bins, each containing nb samples. The ECE is then
1049
       simply a scalar measuring the weighted errors between the acc and conf of each bin (Naeini et al.,
                       PB
1050   2015): ECE = b=1 nNb |acc(b) − conf(b)|. Despite the ECE’s popularity, many recent works have
1051
       pointed out the limitations of the ECE, such as bin size sensitivity and it’s lack of consideration for
       classwise calibration. For a fair and thorough analysis, we introduce other calibration metrics that
1052
       cover the weaknesses of the ECE.
1053
1054
1055   Classwise ECE (CECE): As most calibration metrics typically only considers the max confi-
1056   dence probabilities, the CECE considers the macro-averaged ECE of all K classes. Predictions are
1057   binned individually for each respective class and the calibration error is measured for each class
                                               1

1058   level bin (Nixon et al., 2019). CECE = K     b=1   k=1 N |acc(b, k) − conf(b, k)|.

1062   potentially hazardous. The OE penalizes overconfident

1230   The importance-weighted formulation of Peacock utilizes a novel direction weighted self-attention

1234   regards all auxiliary losses equally. Conversely, our proposed direction weighted self-attention block
1235
       outputs importance weights at every timestep, using Eq. (16). This leads to an overall balanced and

1236
       being passed into the self-attention block during training. Additionally, the direction weighted self-

1530   Component Permutations In the case of Peacock, we featured a total of seven baselines which
1531   gives a total of 27 − 1 permutations. While the primary focus of our paper is looking at whether
1532   different calibration algorithms can be successfully combined, we constrained Peacock to the seven
1533   featured algorithms so as to keep experiments manageable. We note that there are many potential
1534   algorithms in the calibration family that could become promising candidates (see Fig. 2a).
1535
1536   Modularity and Future Components To the best of our ability, we built Peacock based on the
1537   most relevant SOTA calibration components. For each algorithm, we closely referenced the source
1538
       code provided by the respective authors. As we believe that Peacock will perform as well/better than
       the average of its components, we specifically built Peacock in a modular fashion allowing the easy
1539
       integration of future methods.
