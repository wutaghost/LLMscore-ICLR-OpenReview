011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Fine-tuning pre-trained Large Language Models (LLMs) for downstream tasks
014            using First-Order (FO) optimizers presents significant computational challenges.
015            Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods have been proposed to address
016
               these challenges by freezing most model parameters and training only a small
               subset. While PEFT is efficient, it may not outperform full fine-tuning when
017
               high task-specific performance is required. Zeroth-Order (ZO) methods offer an
018
               alternative for fine-tuning the entire pre-trained model by approximating gradients
019            using only the forward pass, thus eliminating the computational burden of back-
020            propagation in first-order methods. However, when implementing ZO methods,
021            it is crucial to ensure prompt-based text alignment, and relying on simple, fixed
022            hard prompts may not be optimal. In this paper, we propose a bilevel optimization
023            framework that complements ZO methods with PEFT to mitigate sensitivity to
024            hard prompts while efficiently and effectively fine-tuning LLMs. Our Bilevel
025            ZOFO (Zeroth-Order-First-Order) method employs a double-loop optimization
026            strategy, where only the gradient of the PEFT model and the forward pass of the
027
               base model are required. We provide convergence guarantees for Bilevel ZOFO.
               Empirically, we demonstrate that Bilevel ZOFO outperforms both PEFT and ZO
028
               methods in single-task settings. Additionally, we show its strong potential for
029
               multitask learning. Compared to current first-order meta-training algorithms for
030            multitask learning, our method has significantly lower computational demands
031            while maintaining or improving performance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Fine-tuning pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) has become a standard approach for down-
037   stream tasks. Traditional first-order (FO) optimizers like Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015), commonly
038   used for this process, rely on backpropagation. However, as highlighted in Malladi et al. (2023),
039   computing gradients for LLMs can require up to 12 times the memory needed for inference. This scal-
040   ing challenge becomes even more pronounced as models grow larger, imposing significant memory
041   demands and complicating the fine-tuning process, especially in resource-constrained environments.
042   To address these computational challenges, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods have
043   been developed. These techniques freeze most of the model’s parameters and train only a small
044   subset, significantly reducing both memory and computational overhead. Popular PEFT approaches
045   include prompt tuning, LoRA fine-tuning, and prefix tuning. Prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Qin
046   & Eisner, 2021; Yu et al., 2023) optimizes continuous prompt vectors that are concatenated with
047   the input embeddings, while prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) introduces learnable prefix tokens
048   that serve as conditioning variables at each transformer layer. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) (Hu
049   et al., 2022; Houlsby et al., 2019) modifies the model’s attention and feedforward layers by injecting
050
      low-rank trainable matrices, further reducing the resources required for fine-tuning.
051   While Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods reduce training costs and memory usage,
052   they may not always achieve the same level of task-specific performance as full model fine-tuning.
053   Research has shown that for tasks requiring high accuracy, complex adaptations, or domain-specific
      knowledge, full fine-tuning often outperforms PEFT approaches due to its ability to adjust all model


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      parameters for better adaptation (Hu et al., 2022; Li & Liang, 2021; Zaken et al., 2022). To make
055   full model fine-tuning more computationally feasible, zeroth-order methods offer an alternative
056   by reducing the high computational cost. Rather than computing gradients via backpropagation,
057   zeroth-order methods estimate the gradient using only the forward pass. Initially explored in the
058   1990s (Spall, 1992; Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017; Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Duchi et al., 2015; Liu
059   et al., 2020), these methods have recently gained traction for fine-tuning LLMs (Malladi et al., 2023;
060   Deng et al., 2023a; Ling et al., 2024) and have been shown to be able to outperform first-order PEFT
061   methods given enough training time (Zhang et al., 2024b).
062   However, zeroth-order methods often rely on simple, fixed prompts during fine-tuning. In tasks
063   like sentiment analysis with the SST-2 dataset, templated prompts (e.g., “< CLS > text data. It
064   was [terrible | great]. < SEP >”) are crucial for success (Zhang et al., 2024b). These prompts
065   effectively align the text data with task-specific objectives. Therefore, selecting such templates
066   becomes a key hyperparameter, raising the question: Can we automatically discover effective prompts
067   for zeroth-order fine-tuning through prompt tuning? More broadly, can PEFT methods complement
068   zeroth-order fine-tuning for large models? In this work, we propose a new framework to answer this
069
      question.
070   While our focus has thus far been on single-task fine-tuning, many scenarios necessitate multi-task
071   fine-tuning. Multi-task learning (MTL) enables a model to handle multiple tasks simultaneously,
072   fostering knowledge transfer between tasks and improving overall efficiency (Min et al., 2022; Yang
073   et al., 2024). This approach is particularly valuable in low-resource settings, where collecting large
074   labeled datasets can be costly, as is often the case with medical data. In such environments, few-shot
075
      learning—where a model is fine-tuned on a high-resource dataset to quickly adapt to new tasks with
      minimal data—becomes essential (Ye et al., 2021).
076
077   To address the challenges of multi-task and few-shot learning in natural language processing, several
078   meta fine-tuning methods have been proposed (Huang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Ye et al.,
079   2021; Asadi et al., 2024). However, traditional meta fine-tuning approaches, such as MetaICL Min
080   et al. (2022), still require full-model first-order gradient calculations, which become computationally
081
      expensive with large language models (LLMs) containing billions of parameters. Given the success
      of zeroth-order methods in fine-tuning LLMs for individual tasks, the potential for adapting their
082
      applicability to multi-task learning remains largely unexplored.
083
084   With the effectiveness of zeroth-order fine-tuning and the advantages of PEFT for single tasks,
085   a natural question arises: Can we develop a new multi-task and few-shot learning methodology
086   that significantly reduces computational costs while maintaining or even enhancing performance?
087
      Specifically, can we leverage the efficiency of zeroth-order fine-tuning alongside the adaptability of
      PEFT within multi-task and few-shot learning for large language models?
088

091   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
092
093   In this work, we propose a bilevel framework that leverages Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
094   methods to automatically enhance the performance of zeroth-order fine-tuning for large pre-trained
095   language models. The framework introduces two optimization levels: an upper-level problem focused
096   on fine-tuning the pre-trained base model and a lower-level problem dedicated to selecting the most
097   effective PEFT model for fine-tuning the base model. This dual-level approach allows us to identify
098
      the optimal combination of PEFT model and pre-trained model.
099   To solve the bilevel optimization problem, we propose the Bilevel Zeroth-Order-First-Order (Bilevel
100   ZOFO) method. By incorporating zeroth-order approximations into the first-order bilevel method,
101   Bilevel ZOFO avoids calculating the gradient of the full pre-trained model. Our method further
102   addresses the high memory and computational costs of existing bilevel optimization methods, making
103   it especially suitable for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters.
104
      Additionally, we provide theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the Bilevel ZOFO method.
105   Furthermore, we extend our method from single-task to multi-task learning. The zeroth-order fine-
106   tuning at our upper level for the full model significantly reduces the computational cost required
107   compared to existing multi-task learning techniques. Additionally, the use of a PEFT model at the
      lower level allows for efficient fine-tuning across multiple tasks. The proposed framework, combined

      with the newly introduced method, offers an extremely lightweight meta-training process that can be
109   rapidly adapted to new tasks.
110
111   We conducted extensive experiments to verify the effectiveness of Bilevel ZOFO. In single-task
112
      settings, the Bilevel ZOFO method outperforms both traditional PEFT and standard zeroth-order
      methods fine tuning on average. In multi-task learning settings, we also show that our method

      2.1   Z EROTH ORDER IN FINE TUNING LLM S
130   MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) is the first work to apply ZO tuning to LLMs. MeZO apply the zeroth-
131   order method to fine-tune large language models (LLMs) for downstream tasks. They demonstrate that
132   their method is compatible with both full-parameter tuning and parameter-efficient tuning techniques,
133   such as LoRA and prefix tuning, while being significantly more computationally efficient. Zhang
134   et al. (2024b) provide a benchmark for zeroth-order optimization in the context of LLM fine-tuning,
135   comparing different zeroth-order optimizers and applying the method to various models. Gautam et al.
136   (2024) introduce variance reduction techniques into zeroth-order methods for fine-tuning, improving
137   both stability and convergence. In addition, zeroth-order methods are applied in federated fine-tuning
138
      by Qin et al. (2024) and Ling et al. (2024). Deng et al. (2023b) implement zeroth-order optimization
      for softmax units in LLMs. Guo et al. (2024b) and Liu et al. (2024b) explore fine-tuning a minimal
139
      subset of LLM parameters using zeroth-order methods by sparsifying gradient approximation or the
140
      perturbations used in gradient estimation. Tang et al. (2024) investigate the privacy of zeroth-order
141   optimization methods.
142
143   In contrast to previous approaches, we propose a bilevel training algorithm that effectively combines
144
      the strengths of both first-order parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) and zeroth-order full-model
      fine-tuning. Our experiments demonstrate that the bilevel structure, when paired with the most
145
      suitable PEFT technique, outperforms both zeroth-order full-model fine-tuning and first-order PEFT
146
      methods individually.

      2.2   F INE - TUNING LLM S FOR M ULTITASK AND F EW-S HOT L EARNING
149
150   Typical meta-tuning approaches employ first-order methods to train autoregressive LLMs on a
151   multitask dataset for various tasks (Zhong et al., 2021; Min et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024a). Zhong
152   et al. (2021) apply meta-training to tasks such as hate speech detection, question categorization, topic
153   classification, and sentiment classification. Guo et al. (2024a) adopt the method from Min et al. (2022)
154   for generating stylistic text. While Min et al. (2022) focus on enhancing the in-context learning ability
155   of the meta-trained model for multitask learning, Zhong et al. (2021) focus on improving zero-shot
156   performance.
157   During training, Min et al. (2022) sample a task from the dataset for each iteration to perform
158   in-context learning. In contrast to Zhong et al. (2021) and Min et al. (2022), our approach uses a
159   bilevel structure: the full LLM is fine-tuned at the upper level, while parameter-efficient fine-tuning
160   (PEFT) models are tuned at the lower level. At test time, we freeze the meta-tuned base model
161   and fine-tune only the PEFT model using a few-shot setup, which is both more cost-effective and
      efficient. Crucially, we employ a zeroth-order method in meta-tuning the base model at the upper

162
      level, which allows us to bypass the need for backpropagation in the meta-model, significantly
163   reducing computational costs.

      2.3   P ENALTY M ETHODS FOR B ILEVEL O PTIMIZATION
166
167   Solving a bilevel optimization problem is challenging because the function value in the upper-level
168   objective depends on the optimizer of the lower-level problem. This makes it difficult to compute the
169   gradient of the upper-level objective, also known as the hypergradient. Classical methods require
170   calculating Hessian-vector multiplications to approximate the hypergradient (Franceschi et al., 2017;
171   2018; Finn et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022; Rajeswaran et al., 2019; Ghadimi & Wang, 2018; Chen et al.,
172   2022; Lorraine et al., 2020). However, when fine-tuning large language models, this process becomes
173   extremely expensive due to the high computational and memory demands.
174   Recently, new frameworks for bilevel optimization have been introduced (Lu & Mei, 2024; Shen &
175   Chen, 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Kwon et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022). These methods bypass the need
176   for second-order information by reformulating the bilevel problem as a constrained optimization
177   problem. The constraint is penalized, allowing the problem to be tackled as a minimax problem using
178   only first-order information. These methods significantly reduce computational costs by eliminating
179   the need for second-order information. Nevertheless, when fine tuning LLMs, back propagation for
180   calculating the gradient of an LLM is still too expensive.
181   Liu et al. (2024a) and Lu & Mei (2024) explore the convergence of their proposed methods to the
182   original bilevel problem, while other approaches only demonstrate convergence to the penalized
183   problem. In this paper, we adapt the method from Lu & Mei (2024) to approximate part of the
184   upper-level parameters using a zeroth-order approximation, addressing the challenge posed by the
185   vast number of training parameters in large language models. We also provide convergence guarantees
186   for this adapted zeroth-order-first-order method.

529   6    C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   In this work, we introduced a novel bilevel optimization framework designed to mitigate the downsides
532   of PEFT and zeroth-order full model fine-tuning. We propose a new method that is more efficient
533   than existing bilevel methods and thus more suitable for tuning full pre-trained large language models.
534   Theoretically, we provide convergence guarantees for this new method. Empirically, we show that
535   this method outperforms both zeroth-order methods and PEFT methods when solving one single
536
      task settings on average. Additionally, we demonstrate that this method is effective and efficient
      when adapted to do multi-task learning. With competitive and even better performance compared to
537
      existing meta-training methods, our method offers a significantly cheaper training process.

       B.4.2    BASELINES
1275
1276   We use GPT2-Large Radford et al. (2019) as the base model for these experiments.We compare our
1277   method against several baseline approaches:
1278
1279
               • MetaICL (Min et al., 2022): A method for meta-learning with in-context learning. MetaICL
                 tunes all the parameters of the base model using the first-order method. In both training
1280
                 and testing, the model is given k demonstration examples, (a1 , b1 ), . . . , (ak , bk ), where
1281
                 bi represents either classification labels or possible answers in question-answering tasks,
1282             along with one test example (a, b). The input is formed by concatenating the demonstration
1283             examples a1 , b1 , . . . , ak , bk , a. The model then computes the conditional probability of each
1284             label, and the label with the highest probability is selected as the prediction.
1285
               • Zero-shot: This method uses the pretrained language model (LM) without any tuning,
1286             performing zero-shot inference without any demonstration examples.
1287
               • In-context Learning (ICL): This method uses the pretrained LM with in-context learning
1288
                 by conditioning on a concatenation of k demonstration examples and 1 actual test sample
1289
                 similar to MetaICL.
1290
1291   We sample 768 examples from each training sub-task. We use these samples to train MetaICL in their
1292   original setting for 30,000 steps. This includes learning rate of 1e − 5, batch size of 1 on 8 GPUs,
1293   8-bit Adam optimizer and fp16 half precision. See MetaICL(Min et al., 2022) for full details. To
1294   train our method, we split the training dataset of each sub-task to two subsets, 256 samples as the
1295   development dataset for upper-level updates and 512 samples for lower-level training. For each outer
       iteration of our method, we randomly sample a subset of 5 training tasks. We perform 10 lower-level


                                                            24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       updates between each pair of upper-level updates. To keep bilevel-ZOFO as lightweight as possible,
1297   unlike MetaICL, we do not include demonstration examples in the inputs. Since bilevel-ZOFO uses
1298   significantly less memory and has much faster updates compared to MetaICL, theoretically we are
1299   able to train it for many more iterations within the same total training duration as MetaICL. However,
1300   due to resource constraints, we only train bilevel-ZOFO for 50,000 iterations. Similar to Malladi
1301   et al. (2023), we did not observe a plateau in performance for bilevel-ZOFO, indicating that further
1302   training can yield additional improvements. We use Adam optimizer and a learning rate of 1e − 6 for
1303   both upper and lower-level training. We employ a batch size of 4 and train on a single rtx6000ada
