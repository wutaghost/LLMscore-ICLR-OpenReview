009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The adaptation of large language models (LLMs) to chemistry has shown promising
012            performance in molecular understanding tasks, such as generating a text descrip-
013            tion from a molecule. However, proper reasoning based on molecular structural
014            information remains a significant challenge, e.g., even advanced LLMs such as
015            GPT-4o struggle to identify functional groups which are crucial for inferring the
016            molecular property of interest. To address this limitation, we propose S TRUCT C OT,
017            a structure-aware chain-of-thought (CoT) that enhances LLMs’ understanding of
018
               molecular structures by explicitly injecting the key structural features of molecules.
               Moreover, we introduce two fine-tuning frameworks for adapting the existing
019
               LLMs to use our S TRUCT C OT. Our experiments demonstrate that incorporating
020
               S TRUCT C OT with our fine-tuning frameworks leads to consistent improvements
021            in both molecular understanding tasks.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large language models (LLMs; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI & et al., 2024; Raffel et al., 2020) have
027   demonstrated remarkable performance across various tasks. To leverage their strong capabilities in
028   chemistry, several prior works (Edwards et al., 2022; Christofidellis et al., 2023a; Fang et al., 2024;
029   Pei et al., 2023) have proposed chemical LLMs that have shown superior performance in molecular
030   understanding tasks such as molecule captioning (Mol2Text) and text-based molecule generation
031   (Text2Mol) (Edwards et al., 2022), which are crucial for designing new molecules.
032   Reasoning based on molecular structures plays an important role in molecular understanding tasks in
033   practice. For example, chemists are likely to consider a molecule toxic if it contains a phenol group
034   due to the formation of phenoxyl radicals and the compound’s ability to interact with biological
035   membranes (Hansch et al., 2000). However, despite its significance, there exists a lack of studies on
036   the role of reasoning in LLM-based molecular understanding. In other domains such as arithmetic
037   and commonsense reasoning, chain-of-thought (CoT; Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) has shown
038
      that explicitly incorporating such reasoning steps significantly improves the performance of LLMs.
      In detail, CoT aims to generate intermediate reasoning steps before arriving at a final answer.
039
040   One might consider the naive adaptation of CoT prompting to include molecular structural information
041   in reasoning. However, we observe this to be ineffective because even state-of-the-art LLMs (OpenAI
042   & et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023) struggle to capture the structural details of molecules, as described
043   in Figure 1 and Section 3.2. This hinders their ability to perform reasoning effectively in molecular
044
      understanding tasks. While some prior works (Ouyang et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; M. Bran et al.,
      2024) have proposed CoTs for chemistry, they are either not applicable or exhibit limited performance
045
      in molecular understanding tasks.
046
047   In this paper, we propose S TRUCT C OT, a chain-of-thought that progressively sketches the structural
048   features of molecules to solve molecular understanding tasks. S TRUCT C OT consists of six key
049   structural elements, ranging from the primary structure to the smaller components. We propose to
050
      explicitly inject the appropriate structural information with S TRUCT C OT to enhance the language
      models’ understanding of molecules, which compensates for the lack of structural information.
051
052   Moreover, we propose two different fine-tuning frameworks to apply S TRUCT C OT depending on
053   the input and output of the given molecular understanding task, as illustrated in Figure 2. Both
      approaches share the same outline, including a reasoning module that generates S TRUCT C OT and an

188   3.1     E XAMPLES OF IMPORTANT STRUCTURAL INFORMATION
189
190   Humans typically analyze a molecule by progressively mapping its structure, starting with primary
191   elements like rings and long carbon chains, and then identifying smaller components such as func-
192   tional groups and chiral centers. Reflecting this approach, we identify six key elements of molecular
193   structure that are critical for chemical reasoning. To highlight the importance of these structural
194
      elements, we demonstrate how even slight modifications in molecular structure can lead to significant
      changes in chemical or physical properties, as shown in Figure 3.
195
196   Molecular formula. The molecular formula provides essential information about a molecule’s
197   composition, specifying the number and type of atoms present. This information is critical because,
198   for example, it directly determines the molecular weight. To illustrate, although 2-Butanol (C4 H10 O)
199   and 2-Propanol (C3 H8 O) are composed of the same type of atoms, i.e., carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen,
200
      their differing molecular formulas result in distinct molecular weights (74.1g/mol for 2-Butanol
      and 60.1g/mol for 2-Propanol). These differences lead to the change in boiling points, 99.4◦ C and
201
      82.3◦ C, respectively, as shown in the gray part of Figure 3.
202
203   Longest carbon chain. The longest carbon chain (excluding atoms in ring systems) forms the
204   molecular backbone where functional groups are attached. The length of this chain significantly
205   influences properties like solubility. For example, extending the carbon chain of 2-Butanol from four
206
      to six carbons creates 2-Hexanol, which exhibits reduced solubility. This is illustrated in the green
      section of Figure 3.
207
208   Aromatic rings. Aromatic rings, such as benzene or pyridine, play a critical role in determining the
209   stability and electronic properties of molecules. For instance, adding a benzene ring to 2-Butanol
210   yields 1-Phenyl-2-Propanol, which has enhanced stability and greater oxidation resistance. This
211   transformation is shown in the blue section of Figure 3.
212   Ring compounds. Similar to the longest carbon chain, ring structures often serve as the backbone
213   where functional groups are attached. The ring system significantly affects molecular behavior and
214   reactions. For example, although 2-Butanol and Cyclobutanol share the same number of carbons and
215   oxygen, the ring in Cyclobutanol introduces a tendency toward ring-opening reactions, as depicted in
      the yellow section of Figure 3.

      3.2          R ECENT LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS DO NOT UNDERSTAND STRUCTURAL INFORMATION
246
247   Next, we demonstrate that even recent LLMs, i.e., GPT-4o (OpenAI & et al., 2024) and LlaMA3-8B-
248   Instruct (Touvron et al., 2023), fail to infer important structural information from the given molecule
249   and the text description of the molecule. We evaluate the LLMs by querying the structural information
250   from the SMILES string (Weininger, 1988) and the text description, which can be considered as a
251   simple task that could be solved by someone with a bachelor’s degree in chemistry.
252   As shown in Figure 4, both GPT-4o and LlaMA3-8B-Instruct fail to capture the structural information
253   accurately. First, when the SMILES string is provided, both models perform best in counting the
254   number of aromatic rings, with accuracies around 50% and 75%, respectively. However, their
255   accuracies are significantly lower for other structural information. This implies that LLMs cannot
256   fully understand the molecular structures given the molecular string. We provide an example of a
257   failure case in Figure 1.
258   Similarly, when the text description is given, both models also fail to achieve a high accuracy in
259   inferring the structural information. This indicates that LLMs cannot properly understand the structure
260   of molecules even when provided with the text description of molecules. These observation highlight
261   the potential benefits of explicilty incorporating structural CoT to enhance molecular comprehension.
262   Note that we provide the detailed experimental settings and prompts for the analysis in Appendix A.1.

303
304   We introduce S TRUCT C OT, a structure-aware CoT designed to enhance language models’ understand-
305   ing of molecular structures. Each component of S TRUCT C OT follows the six important structural
306   information introduced in Section 3.1 and illustrated in Figure 5.

308   Nm represent the m-th atom type and the associated number of atoms, respectively.

312   Number of aromatic rings takes the following form: “The molecule contains X aromatic ring(s).”,
313   where X denotes the number of aromatic rings in the molecule.
314   Types of ring compounds is expressed as “It includes N1 X1 rings, · · · , NM XM ring(s).”, where

      4.2   M OLECULE CAPTIONING
325
326   Molecule captioning aims to generate an accurate and detailed text description of a given molecular
327   SMILES string. We incorporate our S TRUCT C OT scheme through (1) using external tools like
328   RDKit (Landrum et al., 2024) as a ground-truth reasoning module and (2) fine-tuning the answering
329   module LLM with the generated CoT as an additional input. We provide the description in Figure 2a.
330   Reasoning module. One can obtain the true structural information of the given molecule from
331   RDKit, which allows us to guide the answering module without uncertainty. This is natural as the
332   structural information is deterministic given the molecule. Consequently, the obtained true structural
333   information is used as S TRUCT C OT. For this task, we consider the molecular weight CoT and IUPAC
334   name CoTs (M. Bran et al., 2024) in addition to the CoTs described in Section 4.1.
335
      Answering module. With the molecule and the acquired CoT as an input, we fine-tune the LLMs to
336   generate the description of the molecule. In the experiments, we mainly consider chemical LLMs, i.e.,
337   MolT5 (Edwards et al., 2022) and ChemT5 (Christofidellis et al., 2023a), as the answering module.
338

      4.3   T EXT- BASED MOLECULE GENERATION
340
341   Text-based molecule generation is the reverse process of molecule captioning, intending to generate
342   the corresponding molecular string based on the given description. Following the two-stage framework
343   that separates rationale generation and answer inference (Zhang et al., 2024), we first generate
344   S TRUCT C OT using the fine-tuned reasoning module and then attach this to the input and employ this
345   as an input for the answering module. We provide the description in Figure 2b.
346
      Notably, we selectively use CoT elements in S TRUCT C OT. This is because the reasoning modules
347   need to generate CoTs of sufficient quality for the answering module, but this is not possible for some
348   types of CoTs. Therefore, we evaluate the abilities of the reasoning module to correctly generate the
349   CoTs and exclude those with low accuracy (presented in Table 2), specifically the molecular formula
350   CoT and the two CoTs proposed by M. Bran et al. (2024).
351
      Reasoning module. For the reasoning module, following Ho et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2023a); Magister
352
      et al. (2023), we enable CoT reasoning of the models by fine-tuning the reasoning module on the
353   S TRUCT C OT as the molecule is not given. This is in contrast to the molecule captioning task where
354   the exact structural information can be extracted from external tools with the given molecule. We
355   mainly fine-tune the chemical LLMs, i.e., MolT5 and ChemT5 for this module.
356
      Answering module. For the answering module, similar to that of molecule captioning, we fine-
357
      tune a chemical LLM to generate an appropriate molecule given the text description and generated
358
      S TRUCT C OT. Moreover, we propose the matching ratio-based rejection sampling, which forces the
359   generated molecule to align with S TRUCT C OT, as described in the following.
360
361   The proposed matching ratio-based rejection sampling aims to match the structural information of the
362
      generated molecule with the given S TRUCT C OT. In detail, we generate multiple k molecules using
      beam search and then score each molecule based on the matching ratio, which counts the number
363
      of matching structural information elements between S TRUCT C OT and the generated molecule.
364
      Finally, we choose the best-scoring molecule as the final output. This approach also leverages the
365   deterministic nature of structural information, i.e., we can easily compare the alignment between
366   each structural information and the generated molecule. Notably, this differs from the prior works
367   with iterative approaches (Wang et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024), as we focus on the
368   alignment between CoT and generated answer without needing to generate multiple rationales.

531   6         C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we introduced S TRUCT C OT, a structure-aware chain-of-thought framework that en-
534   hances language models’ understanding of molecular structures by explicitly incorporating key
535   structural features. Our analysis demonstrated that recent large language models struggle to accu-
536
      rately infer structural information from molecular representations like SMILES strings or textual
      descriptions, highlighting the need for explicit structural reasoning. By fine-tuning domain-specific
537
      specialist models with S TRUCT C OT, we achieved consistent improvements in molecule captioning
538
      and text-based molecule generation tasks. This work underscores the effectiveness of small, domain-
539   specific models in capturing molecular structures, and offers a solution for molecular reasoning.

       Additional molecular descriptors. In addition to the proposed six structural components, we
1135   conducted experiments using three more advanced molecular descriptors: the Morgan fingerprint
1136   and two electronic properties—topological polar surface area (TPSA) and molar refractivity (MR).
1137   Specifically, the Morgan fingerprint encodes local substructures within a specified radius; TPSA
1138   represents the sum of the surface areas of all polar atoms and their attached hydrogen atoms; and MR
1139   quantifies the total polarizability of a molecule.
1140
       To verify the effectiveness of each additional descriptor, we evaluated the performance of molecule
1141   captioning using ChemT5-small. We provide the results in Figure 12. We observed that incorporating
1142   all three additional descriptors together did not further improve the performance of StructCoT,
1143   although applying each additional descriptor individually improved performance. This validates the
1144   importance of structural information and the sufficiency of our proposed structural components.
