026                                                       A BSTRACT
027
028                    We introduce the first zero-shot approach for Video Semantic Segmentation (VSS)
029                    based on pre-trained diffusion models. A growing research direction attempts to
030                    employ diffusion models to perform downstream vision tasks by exploiting their
031                    deep understanding of image semantics. Yet, the majority of these approaches have
032                    focused on image-related tasks like semantic segmentation, with less emphasis on
033
                       video tasks such as VSS. Ideally, diffusion-based image semantic segmentation
                       approaches can be applied to videos in a frame-by-frame manner. However, we
034
                       find their performance on videos to be subpar due to the absence of any modeling
035
                       of temporal information inherent in the video data. To this end, we tackle this
036                    problem and introduce a framework tailored for VSS based on pre-trained image
037                    and video diffusion models. We propose building a scene context model based on
038                    the diffusion features, where the model is autoregressively updated to adapt to scene
039                    changes. This context model predicts per-frame coarse segmentation maps that are
040                    temporally consistent. To refine these maps further, we propose a correspondence-
041                    based refinement strategy that aggregates predictions temporally, resulting in more
042                    confident predictions. Finally, we introduce a masked modulation approach to
043                    upsample the coarse maps to a high-quality full resolution. Experiments show that
044
                       our proposed approach significantly outperforms existing zero-shot image semantic
                       segmentation approaches on various VSS benchmarks without any training or
045
                       fine-tuning. Moreover, it rivals supervised VSS approaches on the VSPW dataset
046
                       despite not being explicitly trained for VSS.

      Video vision tasks, on the other hand, have not received the same attention compared to their
079   image counterparts. A simple approach is adopting image-based approaches to solve video tasks.
080   To investigate this, we test the diffusion-based image semantic segmentation approach EmerDiff
081   (Namekata et al., 2024) on the task Video Semantic Segmentation (VSS) in a frame-by-frame manner.
082   VSS aims to predict a semantic class for every pixel in each frame according to the pre-defined
083   categories in a video. Our initial experiments show that EmerDiff performs poorly in segmenting
084   videos in terms of temporal consistency, as shown in Figure 1. This can be attributed to the lack of
085   modeling of video temporal information, causing inconsistent predictions across frames.
086   An intuitive solution for enhancing the temporal consistency of these approaches is employing a video
087   diffusion model, e.g., Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) (Blattmann et al., 2023) as a backbone. The SVD
088   model is initially trained on images, then expanded with temporal layers and fine-tuned on videos.
089   Ideally, the temporal features from SVD should exhibit better temporal consistency. To investigate
090   this hypothesis, we visualize the temporal features of a pre-trained SVD in Figure 2 to examine their
091   temporal consistency. The figure shows that the temporal features are surprisingly unstable and tend
092   to change significantly between video frames. On the other hand, the spatial features encode the
093
      structure of the scene, similar to Image Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) (SD). Based on these
      observations, we capitalize on the spatial features of either SD or SVD and attempt to enhance them
094
      temporally.
095
096   In this paper, we introduce a diffusion-based zero-shot approach for VSS. First, we build a scene
097   context model based on the features of a pre-trained image (SD) or video (SVD) diffusion model.
098   This context model predicts per-frame coarse segmentation maps and is autoregressively updated
099
      to accommodate scene changes throughout the video. To further enhance the temporal and spatial
      consistency of these coarse maps, we propose a correspondence-based refinement (CBR) strategy
100
      encompassing a pixel-wise voting scheme between the video frames. Finally, we propose a masked
101
      modulation process to reconstruct the full-resolution segmentation maps that is more stable and less
102   noisy than that of Namekata et al. (2024). Experiments show that our proposed approach signifi-
103   cantly outperforms zero-shot image semantic segmentation methods on various VSS benchmarks.
104   More specifically, we improve mIOU over image semantic approaches by at least 29% on VSPW,
105   CityScapes, and Camvid datasets. Remarkably, our approach performs comparably well as supervised
106   VSS approaches on the diverse VSPW dataset. We also show that for currently released models,
107   SD features lead to a higher quality result than SVD features, but this trend may reverse when SVD
      training considers larger datasets in the future.

      2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS ’ F EATURES
111
112   The large-scale training of diffusion models on the LAION-5B dataset (Schuhmann et al., 2022)
113   with 5 billion images allowed them to learn semantically rich image features. As a result, a growing
114   direction of research attempts to employ these features to perform downstream vision tasks. Several
115   approaches (Luo et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) investigated using these features
116   to perform semantic correspondence. They observed that the features are semantically meaningful
117   and generalize well across different objects and styles of images. For example, a human head in
118   any arbitrary image will have similar features to any other human head in other images, regardless
119
      of the scene variations. Those features even generalize across similar classes of objects like animal
      heads. At the same time, the features will differ from those of unrelated object classes like buildings,
120
      landscapes, vehicles, etc. EmerDiff (Namekata et al., 2024) capitalized on this observation to perform
121
      image semantic segmentation. Since the diffusion features are distinct for different objects, they can
122   easily be clustered to separate those objects and produce a coarse segmentation map. Then, they
123   proposed a modulation strategy to produce fine segmentation maps at a remarkable quality. However,
124   we observed that the produced segmentation maps by EmerDiff are not temporally consistent, as
125   illustrated in Figure 1, making it unsuitable for Video Semantic Segmentation (VSS). Therefore, we
126   propose a diffusion-based pipeline tailored for VSS with a focus on improving temporal consistency.
127
128   2.2   V IDEO S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
129
130   Video semantic segmentation (VSS) (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023a;b; Li et al., 2024;
131   Zhao et al., 2017; Cheng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022) is a spatiotemporal variation of image
132   segmentation on videos that aims to predict a pixel-wise label across the video frames. Those
133
      predictions should be temporally consistent under object deformations and camera motion, making
      VSS more challenging than its image counterpart. Recent approaches attempted to exploit the
134
      temporal correlation between video features to produce temporally consistent predictions. Several
135
      approaches (Zhu et al., 2017; Gadde et al., 2017) incorporated optical flow prediction to model motion
136   between frames. Other approaches (Liu et al., 2020) proposed a temporal consistency loss on the
137   per-frame segmentation predictions as an efficient replacement for optical flow. TMANet (Wang et al.,
138   2021) utilized a temporal attention module to capture the relations between the current frame and a
139   memory bank. DVIS (Zhang et al., 2023a) further improved the efficiency by treating VSS as a first-
140   frame-segmentation followed by a tracking problem. Recent work UniVS (Li et al., 2024) proposed
141   a single unified model for all video segmentation tasks by considering the features from previous
142   frames as visual prompts for the consecutive frames. Despite their remarkable performance, these
143   supervised approaches do not generalize well on unseen datasets (Zhang et al., 2023b). Therefore, it is
144
      desired to have an approach that generalizes well across datasets. Inspired by the success of EmerDiff
      (Namekata et al., 2024) on image semantic segmentation, we attempt to exploit the diffusion features
145
      to propose the first temporally consistent zero-shot VSS approach.

150   3.1   S TABLE D IFFUSION ARCHITECTURE
151
152   Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2021; Podell et al., 2023) is one of the prominent latent
153   diffusion models that achieves a good tradeoff between efficiency and quality. It is trained to
154   approximate the image data distribution by adding noise to the latents of data samples until they
155
      converge to pure Gaussian isotropic noise. During sampling, it performs a series of Markovian
      denoising steps starting from pure noise to recover a noise-free latent that is decoded to produce a
156
      synthetic image. SD utilizes a UNet architecture to predict either the noise or some other signal at
157
      each time step. This UNet encompasses multiple blocks for the encoder and the decoder at different
158   resolutions ranging from 8 × 8 to 64 × 64, where every block has residual blocks, self-attention, and
159   cross-attention modules. The attention is computed as:

163   and f denotes a fully connected layer. The query is always computed from the image features, while
164   the key and the value are computed from the image in self-attention and a conditional signal (e.g.
165   textual prompt) in cross-attention. Since the semantically rich features are located in the decoder

172   EmerDiff (Namekata et al., 2024) observed that it is possible to extract semantically rich features
173   from some of the UNet blocks and use them to produce coarse semantic segmentation maps. Given
174

175

194
195   Our method encompasses three main components, as illustrated in Figure 3. First, we construct a scene
196   context model to produce coarse segmentation maps based on the diffusion features (Section 4.1).
197   Then, we introduce a correspondence-based refinement strategy to curate the coarse segmentation
198   maps (Section 4.2). Finally, we propose a masked modulation approach that produces less noisy and
199
      more stable full-resolution segmentation maps (4.3). We also provide some details on adapting our
      approach to employ features from Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) in Section 4.4.

202   4.1   S CENE C ONTEXT M ODEL
203

204
      videos in a frame-by-frame manner. This is not ideal for videos, as the per-frame predictions will
205   be completely independent and consequently temporally inconsistent. To address this limitation,
206   we propose to create a scene context model that is initialized at the first frame and then updated
207   throughout the video in an autoregressive manner.

212

213   in batches of length B.

305   4.3          M ASKED M ODULATION
306
      The modulation process aims to upsample the coarse segmentation masks to the full resolution of
307
      the video frames. When applying the modulation process, it is only the modulated regions that
308
      are expected to change, as explained in Section 3.2. However, in practice, the modulation process
309   produces noise outside that region, causing discrepancies when computing the final segmentation
310   labels. Therefore, we propose a masked modulation process that employs the coarse segmentation
311   map to mask out both the latents and the difference maps outside the modulated region. For a
312   denoising timestep t, we blend the latents as follows:
313

318   map of a certain timestep is modified, it will influence all the following denoising timesteps.

      4.4   A DAPTING SVD FOR V IDEO S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
325
326   As it is natural to explore applying video models for video tasks, we further investigate the possibility
327   of using Stable Video Diffusion (SVD)(Blattmann et al., 2023) features to perform VSS. Unlike
328   SD, which is text-conditioned, SVD is image-conditioned. SVD adapts the SD 2.1 architecture and
329   finetunes on a highly-curated large video dataset. Moreover, it employs a video VAE encoder to
330   encode and decode the videos. SVD extends the SD architecture design mainly in two parts: 1) A
331   video residual network that consists of a set of convolutional blocks that handle every frame of the
332   video latent independently. 2) A temporal attention layer is applied on top of the output of the spatial
333
      attention layer, which computes the full attention of the features along the temporal axis on each
      spatial location. The output of the temporal attention and the output of the spatial attention are then
334
      mixed with a learnable weight. We use SVD as a backbone model to extract the features as well as
335
      do modulation following similar steps described in previous sections.

338   5     E XPERIMENTS
339
340   To the best of our knowledge, no zero-shot diffusion-based video semantic segmentation (VSS)
341   approach exists. Therefore, we compare against existing zero-shot image segmentation methods by
342   adapting them to the VSS setting.
343
344   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
345
346   Implementation details. We evaluate our method with both SD 2.1 and SVD backbones, and we
347   denote them as Ours (SD) and Ours (SVD). Given the video frames, we encode them using VAE, and
348   we add a certain level of noise that corresponds to timestep tinv and start the denoising process at
349   this noise level. As we need to perform modulation at timestep tm , the value of tinv must be larger
350
      than tm . Initially, we set the modulating coefficient λ to 10 as in (Namekata et al., 2024). However,
      we observed that latent blending suppresses the modulating strength; thus, we increase λ to 50 when
351
      latent blending is enabled. We set the batch size B = 14, which is also the original training batch
352
      size of SVD.
353
354   We fix all hyperparameters for all videos and datasets, and we do not apply any post-processing
355   methods like a conditional random field (CRF) on the output segmentation maps. All experiments
356
      were conducted on a single NVIDIA A100 40G GPU. We provide detailed parameter settings for SD
      and SVD backbones in the Appendix.
357
358   Evaluation protocol. The clusters generated by K-Means in the first frame are class-agnostic. To be
359   able to compare our predicted segmentation maps against the groundtruth, we use the labels from the
360   groundtruth of the first frame to assign labels to our clusters. This is similar to the evaluation protocol
361   in Video Object Segmentation, but we differ in that we only use the labels from the groundtruth and
362
      keep the segmentation masks from K-Means.
363   Baselines. We compare against zero-shot image segmentation approaches CLIPpy (Ranasinghe et al.,
364   2023) and EmerDiff (Namekata et al., 2024). For EmerDiff, we adapt it to our zero-shot VSS setup.
365   We use the same label assignment strategy for the first frame, but then we train a KNN classifier to
366   predict the next frame in an autoregressive manner, similar to our approach. We also provide results
367   of the supervised approaches DVIS++ (Zhang et al., 2023a), UniVS (Li et al., 2024), and TMANet
368
      (Wang et al., 2021) to showcase where our zero-shot approach stands compared to them.
369   Dataset. We evaluate on the validation set of three commonly used VSS datasets: VSPW (Miao
370   et al., 2021) with diverse videos, Cityscapes (Cordts et al., 2016) and CamVid (Brostow et al., 2009)

497   6    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
498
      One of the limitations of our approach is its dependency on the quality of the image inversion method.
499
      Moreover, fine image details are likely to be discarded due to the compression from the VAE encoder.
500
      Therefore, our approach can benefit from future research improving both VAE encoding and image
501   inversion. It is also beneficial to investigate if other video diffusion models have semantically higher
502   quality features than SVD. Another limitation of our approach is that it is instance-agnostic, i.e.
503   it groups all objects of the same class into the same cluster. This is due to the inherent nature of
504   diffusion features that group similar semantics. For future work, our approach can be extended to
505   perform Video Instance or Panoptic segmentation.

507   7    C ONCLUSIONS
508
      In this work, we introduced the first zero-shot method for Video Semantic Segmentation (VSS) using
509
      pre-trained diffusion models. We proposed a pipeline tailored for VSS that leverages image and video
510
      diffusion features, and attempts to enhance their temporal consistency. Experiments showed that our
511   proposed approach significantly outperforms zero-shot image semantic segmentation methods on
512   several VSS benchmarks, and performs comparably well to supervised methods on VSPW dataset.

      A.3   TAXONOMY OF VIDEO SEGMENTATION TASKS
664
665   Here we provide a short taxonomy of different video segmentation tasks:
666
667          • Video Semantic Segmentation (VSS) aims to predict a semantic class for every pixel
668            according to the pre-defined categories in a video.
669          • Video Object Segmentation (VOS) aims to segment and track the dominant object(s) in a
670            video.
671          • Video Instance Segmentation (VIS) aims to segment and track individual instances of
672            object(s) in a video.
673
             • Video Panoptic Segmentation (VPS) aims at segmenting every pixel either into foreground
674
               object instances or background semantic classes in a video.
675
676
             • Promptable Video Segmentation (PVS) it is a new video segmentation paradigm introduced
               by Ravi et al. (2024) that aims to segment an object through a video as sepcified by a user
677
               prompt (point, bounding box, or mask).

      A.4   S ETTINGS
680
681   Dataset. VSPW (Video Scene Parsing in the Wild) is a large-scale video semantic segmentation
682   dataset that consists of a wide range of real-world scenarios and categories. It has 124 categories in
683   total. The resolution of this dataset is 480 × 853. Since each video consists of less than 10 classes,
684   we set the number of clusters for KMeans as 20. Cityscapes is a large-scale urban streets video
685   sequence dataset. The objects are grouped into 30 classes in total. Each video clip has 30 frames, and
686   only the 20th frame has dense annotations. We use its validation set, which contains 15000 frames
687   from three cities. As the original resolution of the frames is 1024 × 2048, which is too big to fit into
688   the GPU memory with SVD, we downsample the frames to 256 × 512 for all the experiments and
689
      evaluate at the original resolution by upsampling the segmentation maps. As each video clip may
      contain classes of more than 10, we set the number of clusters as 30 in order to capture the small
690
      objects. CamVid (Cambridge-driving Labeled Video Database) is a road scene dataset with dense
691
      segmentation annotations with 11 classes in total. The resolution of this dataset is 360 × 480. We use
692   its validation set, which has one video clip and contains 100 frames. We set the number of clusters
693   for KMeans as 20.
694
695   Implementation details. We build our method on top of SD 2.1 and SVD code repository
696   https://github.com/Stability-AI/generative-models.
697
698   Computational resources. We use one NVIDIA A100 GPU to conduct all our experiments. As the
699   running time depends on the number of clusters and spatial resolution of the video frames, generally,
700   it will take around 2 minutes to process a batch of video frames using SD 2.1 and 5 minutes using
701   SVD. The main cost of the time comes from the modulating process, which involves several modified
      forward passes of the backbone model.

      A.6     FAILURE CASES
736
737   We identify several typical failure cases using our method. The first one is the inaccurate diffusion
738   inversion process (Figure 8). In adjacent frames, the texture and shape details of the small objects
739   can be different after inversion. Segmentation maps will also inherit this discrepancy between input
740   frames and reconstructed frames, which will result in flickering on the frames. The second one is
741   flickering between similar regions (Figure 9). Some objects are originally assigned with one cluster,
742   while in the later frames, they are grouped into different clusters. The third one is dealing with
743   unseen objects (Fig. 10). As we only use an anchor frame’s ground truth to guide the class-agnostic
744   clusterings, a feature-bank based strategy could be adopted to adapt our approach to handle dynamic
745
      videos.

831   A.10     A BLATION STUDY
832
833   We provide additional ablation experiments here. We ablate the modulating Block bm in Figure 11
834   for both SD and SVD. Modulating Block 7 and Block 8 for SD and SVD, respectively, can give
835   the most spatial details as well as maintain the semantics. These blocks are, at the same time, the
836   most semantically-riched blocks in SD and SVD. In Figure 12, we show more examples of how
837   latent blending and difference map filtering help with removing spatial noises. In Figure 13, we
838
      show comparisons between the difference maps and segmentation maps produced by SD and SVD.
      We show that the difference maps of SD contain finer details, which bring sharper boundaries to
839
      the final segmentation maps. We further show PCA visualization of a 64 × 64 resolution block to
840
      complement Figure 2 in the main paper. We show that in high-resolution blocks, SD features have
841   more semantic information and spatial details, as well as more temporally stable than SVD spatial
842   features and temporal features. We also show that segmentation maps under different numbers of
843   K-Means clusters and after GT labels reassignment in Figure 14. Increasing the number of clusters
844   can help segment more small objects (from 5 to 20). However, further increasing the number of
845   clusters may not necessarily segment out the clusters that are aligned with defined clusters in ground
846   truth (from 20 to 30). Although K-Means originally generated 30 clusters, most of them are merged
847   to the same classes after GT label reassignment.
848   We also ablate on the classifier we use in Stage 2 on the first 30 videos of the VSPW validation set
849   in Tab. 7. We opt for low-complexity classifiers for a reduced computational overhead and to avoid
850   overfitting. The results show that both KNN and MLP achieve a good tradeoff between speed and
851   performance. RandomForest performs slightly better but at an increased computational overhead.
