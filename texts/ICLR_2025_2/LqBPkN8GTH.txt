009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011             Despite the high-quality results of text-to-image generation, stereotypical biases
012             have been spotted in their generated contents, compromising the fairness of gen-
013             erative models. In this work, we propose to learn adaptive inclusive tokens to
014             shift the attribute distribution of the final generative outputs. Unlike existing de-
015             biasing approaches, our method requires neither explicit attribute specification nor
016             prior knowledge of the bias distribution. Specifically, the core of our method is
017             a lightweight adaptive mapping network, which can customize the inclusive to-
018             kens for the concepts to be de-biased, making the tokens generalizable to unseen
019
                concepts regardless of their original bias distributions. This is achieved by tuning
                the adaptive mapping network with a handful of balanced and inclusive samples
020
                using an anchor loss. Experimental results demonstrate that our method outper-
021
                forms previous bias mitigation methods without attribute specification while pre-
022             serving the alignment between generative results and text descriptions. Moreover,
023             our method achieves comparable performance to models that require specific at-
024             tributes or editing directions for generation. Extensive experiments showcase the
025             effectiveness of our adaptive inclusive tokens in mitigating stereotypical bias in
026             text-to-image generation. The code will be publicly available.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Text-to-image (T2I) generation has gained widespread usage thanks to its ability to produce visual
032   content from user-specified text descriptions. However, alongside the technical achievements, con-
033   cerns have arisen regarding the presence of stereotypical biases in the generated outputs, as analyzed
034   by various studies (Ghosh & Caliskan, 2023; Chinchure et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Bianchi et al.,
035
      2023; Wang et al., 2024; Jha et al., 2024).
036   The unfairness in the T2I generation is usually reflected by an unequal representation of different
037   social groups. It is observed that in circumstances where no specific attributes are specified in a
038   human-related prompt, the T2I models tend to generate human figures resembling certain genders
039   and races, reinforcing harmful social discrimination (Ghosh & Caliskan, 2023; Bianchi et al., 2023).
040   In particular, some occupations are strongly associated with specific genders following stereotypes,
041
      e.g., male for doctor and female for nurse. Furthermore, negative concepts like poverty or unattrac-
      tiveness lead to the generation of people from colored races (Bianchi et al., 2023; Ghosh & Caliskan,
042
      2023; Jha et al., 2024). Exposure to such an unfair environment will strengthen the stereotypes and
043
      biases, justify hate in our society, and undermine the rights of minority groups to be treated fairly.
044   While unfair training datasets are often recognized as the origins of biases in large models, the perva-
045   sive nature of stereotypes in our society makes the complete elimination of biases challenging. This
046   triggers the necessity to design de-biasing algorithms to mitigate biases in the pre-trained models.
047
      The main goal of this paper is to devise a bias mitigation method for biased concepts1 . An ideal
048
      inclusive T2I model yields results with evenly distributed sensitive attributes across all attribute
049
      classes when all classes are factually correct and no attribute-related instructions are provided. A
050   crucial aspect of a fair model is its ability to generate inclusive outcomes without direct instruction
051

054
      regarding the target attribute class. Besides, users’ unawareness of potential biases related to a target
055   concept should be respected. Therefore, we argue that, given a neutral concept, a good de-biasing
056   algorithm should (1) achieve fairer results without explicit specification of the target attribute class
057   during generation, and (2) require no prior knowledge of the original bias distribution associated
058   with the concept (e.g., the doctor concept is stereotypically biased towards males). In practice,
059   by specifying the sensitive attribute of interest rather than specific attribute classes in the provided
060   prompt (e.g., “A <gender-inclusive> doctor” instead of “A female doctor”), the users can obtain
061   fairer generative results regarding the attribute they care.
062   Achieving the aforementioned inclusive properties is non-trivial in the absence of direct attribute
063   specification and prior knowledge about the bias distribution. Our study shows that the simple
064   approach of learning a fixed inclusive token via Textual Inversion (TI) (Gal et al., 2022) fails in
065   mitigating biases in concepts originally biased toward different classes (see Sec. 3.1 for detailed
066   discussion). In addition, it also risks modifying the semantic concepts specified in the original
067   prompt. In this work, we follow the mainstream prompt tuning method but aim at learning adaptive
068   inclusive tokens that do not specify any target class, and yet can shift the bias attribute in generation
069
      outcomes towards a more equitable distribution, regardless of the class it was originally biased to.
070   We hypothesize that the token embeddings of a biased concept, e.g., the doctor concept, encode
071   information regarding its bias distribution. Hence, to enable the adaptability of the learned inclusive
072   tokens across various biased concepts, a lightweight adaptive mapping network can be used to
073   find the optimal inclusive token, taking into account the target concept. In addition, an anchor
074   loss is proposed to guide the desired properties of the inclusive token, minimizing the discrepancy
075
      between de-noising UNet predictions of prompt with inclusive token and prompt with the ground
      truth attribute class. The anchor loss ensures the inclusive token has an aligned impact with attribute
076
      class tokens and alternates among all possible classes.
077
078   Our proposed method is validated on the widely adopted Stable Diffusion (SD) framework (Rom-
079   bach et al., 2022). Utilizing small-scale balanced datasets generated by the SD model itself to train
080   our adaptive inclusive tokens, the fairness of the final model outputs is significantly improved. No-
081
      tably, the learned adaptive inclusive tokens demonstrate generalizability to unseen concepts and
      prompts, and can be concatenated to mitigate multiple biases along various attributes.
082
083   The contributions of our work are as follows: We introduce a simple yet effective prompt-tuning ap-
084   proach to achieve inclusive text-to-image generation without attribute specification or prior knowl-
085   edge of biased concepts. In particular, we propose the adaptive mapping network together with
086   the anchor loss to address the issue of generalizability across different attribute class dominations.
087
      Extensive experiments show the effectiveness of our method both quantitatively and qualitatively.

091   2.1   B IAS IN T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
092
093   Comprehensive analyses have been conducted to study the bias and unfairness observed in T2I gen-
094   eration (Bianchi et al., 2023; Chinchure et al., 2023; Ghosh & Caliskan, 2023; Jha et al., 2024; Wang
095   et al., 2023; 2024). Wang et al. (2023) first introduce the Implicit Association Test (IAT) (Green-
096   wald et al., 1998) from social psychology to measure biases in the task of T2I. IAT is designed to
097   reveal implicit biases that an individual may hold unconsciously towards certain concepts. By ex-
098   perimenting with the valence and stereotype IATs on T2I output images, it is found that valence and
099
      stereotypical biases exist in state-of-the-art T2I models at various scales, e.g., the pleasant attitude is
      significantly biased towards straight sexual orientation than homosexual ones. Bianchi et al. (2023)
100
      study a wide range of stereotypes related to gender, race, nationality, and other identities associated
101
      with traits, occupation, and even object descriptions. They conclude that attempts to either specify
102   counter-stereotype prompts by users or add system “guardrails” cannot prevent stereotypes from
103   spreading in the T2I results. Ghosh & Caliskan (2023) spot the over-representation of Caucasian
104   males in general terms like “person” and the over-sexualization of women of color without specifi-
105   cation. TIBET (Chinchure et al., 2023) proposes to identify and measure biases in any T2I models
106   using counterfactual reasoning, which breaks the limitation of pre-defined bias axes in previous
107   studies. Wang et al. (2024) propose BiasPainter which provides an automatic and systematic study
      of gender, race, and age biases. It augments a seed image of a clear identity with queries of profes-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      sions, activities, types of objects, and personality traits and compares the attributes of the identity
109   between augmented and seed images to identify biases in queries. Jha et al. (2024) focus on explor-
110   ing geo-cultural stereotypes in T2I models with a large scale of nationality-based identity groups.
111   It is revealed that the severity of bias varies for different identity groups and the “default” (without
112   specification on bias attributes) representations of identity groups contain stereotypical appearances.

      2.2   B IAS M ITIGATION IN T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
115
116   Various approaches have been developed to alleviate stereotypical biases in T2I generation, includ-
117   ing model fine-tuning (Runway, 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024), prompt tuning (Gal
118   et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023), concept editing (Orgad et al.,
119   2023; Gandikota et al., 2024), and inference guidance (Friedrich et al., 2023; Parihar et al., 2024).
120   A straightforward strategy is to fine-tune the entire T2I model on a large-scale dataset that is care-
121   fully synthesized to cover various classes of bias attributes (Runway, 2023). Besides, techniques of
122   fine-tuning the sampling process of the diffusion model have been proposed so that distributional
123   constraints on the generative outputs, which is the direct interpretation of fairness, can be applied
124
      (Kim et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2024). However, such fine-tuning methods require a heavy load of
      computations. Prompt tuning-based methods aim to modify or add textual tokens to affect the T2I
125
      generation outputs. Gal et al. (2022) learn a fairer word for a biased concept from a small dataset.
126
      Bansal et al. (2022) explore the impact of ethical interventions added to the original prompts on the
127   fairness of generative results. Li et al. (2023) introduce a fair mapping network that projects the
128   text embeddings of a neutral prompt to the middle of prompts with all possible classes. The above
129   prompt tuning methods do not modify the prompt to provide explicit guidance during generation.
130   On the contrary, Zhang et al. (2023) propose to overfit one class token for each attribute class by
131   image prompts and apply ad-hoc post-processing to loop over all combinations of target attributes
132   to achieve inclusive generation. Orgad et al. (2023) and Gandikota et al. (2024) apply model editing
133   to enforce the generation of non-stereotypical classes by optimizing the cross-attention weights of
134   the diffusion models. However, determining the appropriate editing strengths is challenging con-
135
      sidering the large variations in bias strengths across different concepts and classes. Another line
      of retraining-free approaches involves incorporating desired guidance during inference. Friedrich
136
      et al. (2023) apply fair guidance at inference by steering biased concepts in predefined directions
137
      to enhance fairness. While this method avoids the need for training or fine-tuning, it relies on prior
138   knowledge of the biased concept and requires each generation to be guided by a specific semantic
139   direction. Similarly, Parihar et al. (2024) employ a pre-trained h-space classifier to provide explicit
140   distribution guidance during inference. Although this method does not require retraining the dif-
141   fusion model, it demands additional effort to train the classifier and increases the computational
142   cost of inference due to classifier-guided optimization. Our approach adopts the prompt tuning ap-
143   proach without necessitating heavy model fine-tuning or prior knowledge about the original bias
144   distribution, making it computationally efficient and highly generalizable.
145
146   2.3   P ROMPT T UNING
147
148   Prompt tuning is a technique that adapts a large language model (LLM) to new concepts by opti-
149   mizing some prompt parameters with the model weights fixed. Prompt tuning has been applied to
150   various downstream tasks such as image classification (Zhou et al., 2022b;a), customized generation
151   (Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023), and bias mitigation (Gal et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023). Zhou et
152   al. (Zhou et al., 2022b;a) propose to optimize trainable context tokens in class prompts to boost the
153
      performance of zero-shot classification using CLIP classifier (Radford et al., 2021) and further tai-
      lor the tokens to be input-conditioned for better generalization to unseen classes. Textual Inversion
154
      (Gal et al., 2022) is proposed to invert a visual concept from a small set of images to a new pseudo
155
      word and customize generation on the visual concept. Similarly, Ruiz et al. (2023) and Kumari et al.
156   (2023) update a trainable token together with some parts of the model to represent a particular visual
157   content. The ability of prompt tuning to mitigate biases has been demonstrated by Gal et al. (2022)
158   as mentioned in the previous subsection. Kim et al. (2023) adopt a similar prompt tuning approach.
159   However, they optimize the tokens on the sampling stage of the diffusion model, leading to an ex-
160   tensive amount of computation and memory required. Our method tackles the limitation of fixed
161   inclusive tokens on the transferability to different domination classes, as observed in both previous
      methods, by learning adaptive inclusive tokens.

166   Diffusion Model. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) is a commonly used latent diffusion
167   model for image generation. With classifier-free guidance of textual conditions, SD demonstrates
168   excellent performance in the T2I generation task. During each training step, a training image x0
169   is first encoded into a latent space as z0 by a pre-trained image encoder E(·). Then, a noise latent
170   and a de-noising timestep t are sampled to compute the ground truth noise ϵ, which will be added to
171   the encoded image to obtain the noisy latent zt in the current timestep. On the other side, a textual
172
      prompt T that describes the image is tokenized to token embeddings vT and encoded to textual
      embeddings eT by the pre-trained CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021). Subsequently, a de-
173
      noising UNet ϵθ (Ronneberger et al., 2015) takes in the noisy image latent, the de-noising timestep,
174
      and the textual condition to predict the noise added to the image latent. The learning objective of
175   the de-noising stage is formulated as follows:

188
      more balanced distribution to pseudo words from a small set of images. To enable the generalizabil-

247   3.2   A DAPTIVE I NCLUSIVE T OKEN
248
249
      To address the aforementioned limitations, instead of employing a fixed inclusive token for all con-
                                                                              .­


      cepts, we introduce a lightweight adaptive mapping network to predict the inclusive token tailored
250
      for each specific concept. To prevent the learned token from capturing irrelevant information from
251
      the training set that potentially causes semantic drift on bias concepts and degrades fairness in the
252   generated results, an anchor loss is proposed to constrain the effect of the token. The framework of
253   our method is illustrated in Fig. 2. Details of our proposed modules are described below.
254
255
      Adaptive Mapping Network. As shown in the right side of Fig. 2, the adaptive mapping net-
      work Fam is introduced after the token embedding lookup and before the text transformer in our
256
      framework. The inclusive prompt Ti contains an inclusive token denoted by a placeholder <i>
257
      ∈ {<ig >,<ir >,<ia >}, representing <gender-inclusive>,<race-inclusive>,<age-inclusive>, re-
258   spectively. The placeholder is processed by the text tokenizer and the token embedding lookup table
259   along with other words, resulting in an initial inclusive token embedding vi . Then, the inclusive
260   token embedding vi and the biased concept embeddings vc are jointly fed into the adaptive map-
261   ping network to predict a concept-adaptive inclusive token vic . The fundamental hypothesis is that
262   the token embeddings of a concept inherently contain necessary information about its bias distribu-
263   tion. Therefore, customizing the inclusive tokens for each concept facilitates the selection of a more
264   suitable token to effectively shift the final distribution towards fairness regardless of its original dis-
265   tribution. Finally, the adaptive inclusive token vic substitutes vi in the original token embeddings vTi
266
      to form vTc i , which is then forwarded to the text transformer model. The overall process of the text
      model with adaptive mapping is outlined in Algorithm 1.
267
268   Anchor Loss. Given the issue of concept drifting in the revised Textual Inversion results, as dis-
269   cussed in Sec. 3.1, we hypothesize that the issue may stem from ambiguous instructions on what
      information to be captured by the inclusive token during training. To guide the learnable token

312   - gender, race, and age: A ∈ {gender, race, age}. These attributes reflect harmful sexism, racism,

314   female, acknowledging its limitation in representing non-binary genders. However, we argue that
315   identifying certain appearances as non-binary genders may reinforce stereotypes within these less-
316   represented social groups, and therefore should be avoided until more carefully collected public
317
      data on non-binary genders is available. For racial bias, we refer to the seven racial groups from

318
      Southeast Asian into a single category Asian, resulting in six distinct groups: White, Black, Asian,
319
      Middle Eastern, Indian, and Latino Hispanic. As for age bias, binary groups of young and old people
320   are examined. In this study, we focus on neutral bias concepts related to human figures, particularly
321   in occupations: C ∈ {neutral occupations2 }.

      2022) with 50 de-noising steps. The RetinaFace detector (Deng et al., 2020) is applied to filter
      generation without valid faces. In total, there are (24 × 2 × 20) images for gender attribute, (24 ×
350
      6 × 20) for race, and (24 × 2 × 20) for age. Details on preparing training data can be found in the
351
      supplementary materials.
352
353   Implementation Details. Our main experiments are conducted on the SD1.5 network, yet the gen-
354   eralizability to other models is demonstrated in Appendix Sec. E.7. By default, the inclusive token
355
      vi is initialized to the embedding of the token “individual”, which we find to be a natural inclusive
      token (detailed discussion on natural inclusive tokens can be found in the supplementary materials).
356
      The length of our adaptive inclusive token is set to 1, resulting vic ∈ R1×768 , as 768 is the token em-
357
      bedding dimension of the SD1.5 text model. The models are trained for 3000 steps with batch size
358   = 1 (gradient accumulation steps = 4), taking approximately 1 hour on one NVIDIA A100 GPU.
359   The base learning rate is set to 5 × 10−4 and we employ the AdamW optimizer. As for training
360   prompts, we follow Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022) to use the ImageNet templates describing
361   objects, but add adjectives to describe the attribute of interest. The template training prompts can
362   be found in the supplementary materials. For the adaptive mapping network, we use transformer
363   architecture with six attention heads and four transformer blocks.
364   Evaluation Protocols. To evaluate the performance of mitigating stereotypical biases in T2I genera-
365   tions, we construct a test set of unseen 24 occupations excluded from the training set. For each biased
366   concept, we analyze 100 images with valid faces to measure the distributions of different attributes.
367   All images for evaluation are generated using the test prompt “a photo of a <i> {occupation}” with
368   25 de-noising steps. The racial inclusive token <ir > is introduced after 10 sampling steps on base
369   prompt T (c) to better balance six classes. Attribute classifiers are employed to detect and gather
370   statistics on the distributions among various attribute classes in the generated images. Following
371   previous works (Zhang et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2023), we utilize the CLIP
372
      (Radford et al., 2021) zero-shot classifier to classify sensitive attributes, prompting with “a photo of
      a [attribute] person”.
373
374   Evaluation Metrics. Three metrics are used for evaluation. Firstly, following Zhang et al. (2023),
375   the fairness of de-biased attribute distribution is assessed by the KL divergence (DKL )↓ against an
376   even distribution. Secondly, Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)↓ is used to quantify the image quality.

520   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
521
522   In this work, we study the challenges faced by previous prompt tuning-based bias mitigation ap-
523   proaches. To address the limitation of generalization across different dominant attribute classes, we
524   propose to learn a concept-specific inclusive token through an adaptive mapping network, instead
525   of a fixed one. For the concept drifting issue, we introduce an anchor loss that constrains the im-
526   pact of the adaptive inclusive token on the final outputs. As a result, our proposed method manages
527   to significantly enhance inclusiveness in T2I generation, with the ability to generalize to unseen
528   concepts and scenarios and mitigate multiple biases simultaneously. In addition, our approach is
529   model-agnostic and can be applied to various T2I models to boost fairness in their outputs.
530   However, since our target distribution is an even distribution following previous approaches, de-
531   biasing is limited to neutral concepts where all attribute classes are factually correct. For non-neutral
532   concepts, de-biasing may introduce factual errors, such as generating black people as “the pope”
533   with a <race-inclusive> token. Correcting these inaccuracies would require an additional fact-
534   checking process. Nevertheless, these errors may not always be undesirable from an inclusive and
535   creative perspective. The trade-off between accuracy and fairness is a complex issue that requires
536
      continuous discussion by researchers and policymakers. We acknowledge this limitation and hope
      to address it in future work.
537
538   We hope our work can bring insights to all who care and work in this field. We believe it should be
539   our effort to make the world a more inclusive place, where every individual regardless of background
      or identity, can feel valued and respected.

840   E.3   P LACEMENT OF A DAPTIVE M APPING M ODULE
841
842   The placement of the adaptive inclusive embedding within the text model can significantly impact
843
      its effectiveness in bias mitigation. To explore the effects of placing the inclusive embedding in
      different locations, we conduct experiments by placing the adaptive mapping module before and
844
      after the text transformer, as illustrated in Fig. 6. The results are shown in Tab. 8. Our findings
845
      indicate that adding the adaptive mapping module after the text transformer reduces the influence of
846   the inclusive embedding on the final outputs, resulting in reduced effectiveness in bias mitigation.
847
848   We further investigate the impact of embeddings before and after text transformer with two sets
849
      of simple experiments. Here, we denote the textual embeddings before the text transformer as
      token embeddings vTi , and the textual embeddings after the text transformer as text embeddings
850
      eTi . Firstly, for a neutral occupation-related prompt “A photo of a {occupation}” where no gender
851
      class is specified, we find that most of the output images still follow the occupation’s stereotypical
852   gender even if the embedding of the occupation’s non-stereotypical gender is added after the text
853   transformer. This observation suggests that the gender bias associated with the occupation is spread
854   to the whole text embeddings via text transformer, and late indication in text embedding does not
855   play a decisive role in the outcome. Secondly, to further validate this finding, we modify the prompt
856   to include a non-stereotypical gender class, e.g., “A photo of a male nurse”, and subsequently replace
857   the text embedding of “male” with that of “female” (obtained from another prompt, “A photo of
858   a female nurse”) after the text transformer. Surprisingly, the resulting nurse images continue to
859   predominantly exhibit the male gender. This result indicates that the attribute indicators have a
860
      greater impact on the final outputs when incorporated into the token embeddings, as the effect of the
      indicators can propagate throughout the entire text embedding via the transformer architecture. This

      SD1.5 with real data in each attribute-occupation combination, resulting in 888 synthetic images
897
      as well. The results are shown in Tab. 10. In conclusion, the T2I results have better inclusiveness,
