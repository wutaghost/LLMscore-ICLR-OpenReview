009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Ensuring long-term fairness is crucial when developing automated decision mak-
012            ing systems, specifically in dynamic and sequential environments. By maximizing
013            their reward without consideration of fairness, AI agents can introduce disparities
014            in their treatment of groups or individuals. In this paper, we establish the con-
015            nection between bisimulation metrics and group fairness in reinforcement learn-
016            ing. We propose a novel approach that leverages bisimulation metrics to learn
017            reward functions and observation dynamics, ensuring that learners treat groups
018            fairly while reflecting the original problem. We demonstrate the effectiveness
019
               of our method in addressing disparities in sequential decision making problems
               through empirical evaluation on a standard fairness benchmark consisting of lend-
020
               ing and college admission scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   As machine learning continues to shape decision making systems, understanding and addressing its
027
      potential risks and biases becomes increasingly imperative. This concern is especially pronounced in
      sequential decision making, where neglecting algorithmic fairness can create a self-reinforcing cycle
028
      that amplifies existing disparities (Jabbari et al., 2017; D’Amour et al., 2020). In response, there is a
029
      growing recognition of the importance of leveraging reinforcement learning (RL) to tackle decision
030   making problems that have traditionally been approached through supervised learning paradigms, in
031   order to achieve long-term fairness (Nashed et al., 2023). Yin et al. (2023) define long-term fairness
032   in RL as the optimization of the cumulative reward subject to a constraint on the cumulative utility,
033   reflecting fairness over a time horizon.
034
      Recent efforts to achieve fairness in RL have primarily relied on metrics adopted from supervised
035
      learning, such as demographic parity (Dwork et al., 2012) or equality of opportunity (Hardt et al.,
036   2016b). These metrics are typically integrated into a constrained Markov decision process (MDP)
037   framework to learn a policy that adheres to the criterion (Wen et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2023; Satija
038   et al., 2023; Hu & Zhang, 2022). However, this approach is limited by its requirement for complex
039   constrained optimization, which can introduce additional complexity and hyperparameters into the
040   underlying RL algorithm. Moreover, these methods make the implicit assumption that stakeholders
041   are incorporating these fairness constraints into their decision making process. However, in reality,
042   this may not occur due to various external and uncontrollable factors (Kusner & Loftus, 2020).
043   In this work, we highlight a surprising connection between group fairness in RL and the bisimula-
044   tion metric (Ferns et al., 2004; 2011), an equivalence metric that captures the behavioral similarity
045   between states. We show that minimizing the bisimulation metric between members of different
046   groups results in demographic parity fairness. Building upon this insight, we propose a practical al-
047   gorithm that, guided by the bisimulation metric, adjusts the reward and observation dynamics (how
048   the observations change in the environment) to achieve long-term fairness in RL.
049   By modifying the observable MDP—the rewards and the observations seen by the agent—we show
050   that unconstrained policy optimization inherently satisfies the fairness constraint in the original,
051   unmodified MDP. This concept is analogous to real-world practices, where regulatory frameworks
052   are established to influence decision making processes—for instance, governments impose lending
053   regulations on banks to ensure fairness and equity (FDIC, 2005). A significant advantage of our
      method is that it does not require modifying the underlying RL solver. This allows us to lever-

      age the strengths of deep RL while avoiding the complexities and intricacies associated with other
055   constrained optimization methods used to achieve fairness in RL.
056
057   Through comprehensive evaluation on a standard fairness benchmark (D’Amour et al., 2020), widely
058

117   3   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION

119   Fairness in ML entails ensuring unbiased decision making, and is generally categorized into individ-
120   ual and group fairness. While individual fairness aims to treat individuals similarly, group fairness
121   focuses on ensuring that the distribution of outcomes is similar across different groups (Mehrabi
122   et al., 2021). In this work, we specifically adopt group fairness, where a group is defined as:
123   Definition 3 (Group). A group is a population associated with the sensitive attribute g ∈ G.
124
125   In the above definition, a sensitive attribute can include factors such as race, gender, sexual orienta-
126   tion, etc. We further make the following assumptions regarding the sensitive attributes:
127   Assumption 1. Sensitive attributes G are observable to the decision making algorithm.
128   Assumption 2. Sensitive attributes G and group memberships remain constant during training.
129
130   These assumptions are commonly made in prior works on fairness in RL (Jabbari et al., 2017; Wen
131   et al., 2021; Satija et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). Notably, prior works on fairness
132   have showed that removing sensitive attributes from the decision making process, also known as
133   “fairness through unawareness”, is largely ineffective (Pedreshi et al., 2008; Barocas et al., 2023).
134
      Building upon the assumptions above, we define group-conditioned MDPs as:
135   Definition 4 (Group-conditioned MDP). A group-conditioned MDP is a 6-tuple:
136
                   Mgroup = (S, A, G, τa : S × A × G → Dist(S), R : S × A × G → R, γ)
137
138   where S is the state space, A is the action space, and G represents the sensitive attribute space.
139   The group-specific transition dynamics are denoted by τa (s′ | s, g), and Dist(S) is the probability
140
      simplex over S. The reward function specific to each group is R(s, a, g), and γ ∈ (0, 1] is the
      discount factor. The stationary policy P  is represented by π(a|s, g), and the group-specific      value

143
      s, g are sampled from the specific stationary state-group distribution ρπ (s, g) according to π.

145   We use demographic parity (Dwork et al., 2012; Satija et al., 2023) as the group fairness definition.
146   Informally, demographic parity requires that different groups should have similar returns. Formally,
147   this fairness constraint is defined by Satija et al. (2023) as follows:

151                                |J π (s, gi ) − J π (s, gj )| ≤ ϵ,   ∀gi , gj ∈ G.

153   The demographic parity notion aims to prevent disparate impact, where one group experiences sig-
154   nificantly different outcomes than another. As an example, we can consider a credit scoring model
155
      that provides similar approval rates for different racial, gender, or socioeconomic groups. We refer
      to Satija et al. (2023) for a detailed discussion on the applicability and limitations of Definition 5.

217
218   We first describe our approach for optimization of the reward function by minimizing Jrew. :
219
220                            Jrew. = Esi ,sj ,gi ,gj ∼ρπ (s,g) [|Rπ (si , gi ) − Rπ (sj , gj )|]              (8)
221
222
      This approach is closely related to bi-level optimization methods for reward shaping (Hu et al.,
      2020), however, the novelty of our method is that the reward shaping procedure is guided by the
223
      π-bisimulation metric. We assume the reward function R(s, a, g) consists of the following terms:
224
225                               R(s, a, g) = Roriginal (s, a) + αRϕcorrection (s, a, g)                       (9)
226
227   where the first term is defined by the original MDP and is fixed; besides, this reward term is often not
228   conditioned on the group membership. The second term is a learnable group-conditioned function,
229   parameterized by ϕ, that is used as a correction for the original reward, and α is a scalar weight.
230   Since modifying the reward function during the RL training may result in instability, our method
231   learns the reward correction term outside the policy optimization loop. We take a sampling-based
232   approach for minimizing Jrew. ; first, we collect a dataset of trajectories using the policy π, then we
233   use Equation (8) to estimate the discrepancy between the reward functions among different state-
234   group pairs using quantile matching. Consequently, we optimize the estimated loss with respect to
235   the learnable reward parameters ϕ using a gradient-based optimizer.

444   In this scenario, known as strategic classification (Hardt et al., 2016a), an agent representing the
445   college makes binary decisions regarding admissions. The challenge arises when applicants can in-
446   cur costs to alter their observable features, such as test scores. This manipulation disproportionately
447   burdens individuals from disadvantaged groups who lack the financial means to afford these costs.
448   Environment. Each applicant has an observable group membership and a test score, along with an
449   unobservable budget, both sampled from unequal group-specific distributions. At each time step,
450   an applicant is sampled from the population and has a probability ϵ of being able to pay a cost to
451   increase their score, provided their budget allows. The probability of success (e.g., the applicant
452   eventually graduating) is a deterministic function of the true, unmodified score, and the agent’s
453   goal is to increase its accuracy in admitting applicants who will succeed. Importantly, since each
454
      applicant has a finite budget, over the episode horizon, the budget of the population decreases,
      hence making the problem sequential. Note that this environment is relatively different than that in
455
      (D’Amour et al., 2020) by having a more sequential nature due to its changing population. We study
456
      a scenario over 1,000 steps involving two groups, with group 2 facing a disadvantage.
457
458   As an example of adjustable observation dynamics, described in Section 4.2, we can consider group-
459   specific costs for score modification. These adjustments can be seen as subsidized education for
460
      disadvantaged groups, a common practice. Additional details are in Appendix B.2.
461   Fairness Metrics. Following D’Amour et al. (2020), we use three metrics to assess fairness: (a) the
462   social burden (Milli et al., 2019) that is the average cost individuals of each group have to pay to
463   get admitted, (b) the cumulative number of admissions for each group, and (c) agent’s aggregated
464   recall—tp/(tp + f n)—for admissions over the entire episode horizon, that is the ratio between the
465   number of admitted successful applicants to the number of applicants who would have succeeded.
466   Baselines. We evaluate our method against the same RL baselines described in Section 5.2. As a
467   non-RL baseline, we employ a classifier that maximizes its accuracy through supervised learning,
468   based on (D’Amour et al., 2020). Additional details are in Appendix D.

      Fairness in Sequential Decision Making. In recent years, there has been a growing emphasis
509   on the significance of dynamic analysis of fairness measures (Nashed et al., 2023). However, the
510   exploration of these issues remains relatively restricted. The majority of existing studies focus on
511   investigating fairness in multi-armed bandits (Liu et al., 2017; Joseph et al., 2016; Do et al., 2022;
512   Metevier et al., 2019; Bistritz et al., 2020; Hossain et al., 2021). While the simplicity of the bandit
513   problem allows for easier theoretical analysis, its practical applications often extend no further than
514   recommender systems, failing to fully encompass the broader spectrum of real-world applications.
515   In the context of RL, Jabbari et al. (2017) have proposed a fairness constraint suitable for the MDP
516   setting, while providing a provably fair algorithm under an approximate notion of this constraint.
517
      Similarly, in the majority of the recently proposed approaches, fairness notions are adapted from
      the supervised learning setting and imposed as constraints during training of the optimal policy
518
      (Wen et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Satija et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Frauen et al.,
519
      2024). The recently proposed method of Xu et al. (2024) has adapted the concept of benefit rates
520   to the RL setting and has demonstrated state-of-the-art performance. Another set of approaches use
521   multi-objective MDPs (Siddique et al., 2020; Blandin & Kash, 2024), causal inference (Nabi et al.,
522   2019), or the concept of welfare (Cousins et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023). Finally, fairness is particularly
523   important in multi-agent MDPs to ensure an optimal agent does not hinder the performance of other
524   agents (Zhang & Shah, 2014; Jiang & Lu, 2019; Mandal & Gan, 2022; Ju et al., 2023).
525
526   Optimization of MDP Reward (Reward Shaping). Reward shaping is a technique involving
527   the optimization of the reward signal to encourage desirable behaviors and discourage undesirable
528   ones, ultimately leading to more effective learning (Ng et al., 1999). Common approaches include
529
      potential-based (Ng et al., 1999; Devlin & Kudenko, 2012; Gao & Toni, 2015), heuristics-based
      (Cheng et al., 2021), intrinsic motivation (Chentanez et al., 2004; Singh et al., 2010), bi-level opti-
530
      mization (Hu et al., 2020), and gradient-based (Sorg et al., 2010; Zheng et al., 2018) methods. Our
531
      proposed approach is closest to the bi-level optimization of Hu et al. (2020), however, the novelty
532   of our approach is that the reward shaping procedure is guided by the bisimulation metric.
533
534   Optimization of MDP Dynamics. In contrast to the extensively explored concept of reward shap-
535   ing, the optimization of MDP dynamics remains relatively less investigated. This disparity could
536   be due to its stricter prerequisites, necessitating access to certain parameters within the dynamics
537   model. The predominant focus in this domain revolves around the control and co-optimization of
538   robots (Bächer et al., 2021; Spielberg et al., 2019; 2021; Ma et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; 2023;
539   Evans et al., 2022). These works primarily aim to achieve an enhanced performance by concurrently
      learning to control a robot and optimizing its design and dynamical properties. Given the intertwined


                                                                     10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      nature of learning and optimization, the problem poses significant challenges, leading to the proposi-
541   tion of both gradient-based (Spielberg et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2019) and gradient-free (Cheney et al.,
542   2018) optimization techniques. Notably, our method only optimizes the observation dynamics and
543   leaves the underlying transitions, that affect the inherent behavior of the system, unchanged.

548
      justice and equity in various domains. By mitigating disparities, our proposed algorithm improves
549   fairness in sequential decision making processes. However, it is important to acknowledge the lim-
550   itations of our simulated experiments, which are based on simplified problems that may not fully
551   capture real-world complexities. While we recognize the need for more sophisticated benchmarks,
552   developing them is beyond the scope of this paper. Instead, we have utilized and extended the only
553   well-established benchmark in this area (D’Amour et al., 2020), which has been widely used in
554   recent studies (Xu et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2022).
555   Additionally, in this work, our focus is on group fairness, particularly the notion of demographic
556   parity (Dwork et al., 2012) and its adaptation to RL (Satija et al., 2023). Our method’s consistent
557   success across various scenarios and metrics confirms that the demographic parity definition has
558   broad applicability and effectiveness, laying a solid foundation for future research into other fair-
559   ness notions. Finally, convergence proofs for RL methods based on π-bisimulation metrics are an
560   open topic of research (Kemertas & Aumentado-Armstrong, 2021). It requires an intricate analy-
561   sis on how the fixed-point properties of π-bisimulation interact with the convergence properties of
562   a bisimulation-dependent policy, as they both rely on one another. This is an interesting research
563
      avenue on its own, beyond the primary focus of our paper, which is the application of bisimulation
      metrics for group fairness. Nonetheless, our approach and other methods (Zhang et al., 2020) have
564
      demonstrated strong and consistent empirical performance.
565
566
567   8   C ONCLUSION
568
569   In this paper, we established the connection between bisimulation metrics and group fairness in
570   reinforcement learning. Based on this insight, we proposed a novel approach that optimizes the
571
      reward function and observation dynamics of an MDP such that unconstrained optimization of the
      policy inherently results in the satisfaction of the fairness constraint. Crucially, these adjustments
572
      are carried out by the agent or a third-party regulator, without modifying the original MDP or its dy-
573
      namics. A significant advantage of our approach is that it does not require modifying the underlying
574   reinforcement learning algorithms, hence preserving the integrity of current decision making algo-
575   rithms. Our method outperforms strong baselines on a standard fairness benchmark, highlighting its
576   effectiveness.

      A.1    B ISIMULATION
813
814   Bisimulation Bisimulation is a fundamental concept in concurrency theory (Larsen & Skou,
815   1991). It defines an equivalence relation between state-transition systems, ensuring that two systems
816   can simulate each other’s long-term behavior and remain indistinguishable to an external observer.
817   Our work builds on the established theory of bisimulation developed by Larsen & Skou (1991); De-
818   sharnais et al. (2002); Ferns et al. (2004; 2011); Castro (2020), among others. Notably, we do not
819   fully explore the potential of the conditional form of bisimulation metrics in this work. Our defini-
820
      tions, similarly to Hansen-Estruch et al. (2022), possess specific properties that allow us to reduce
      them to existing definitions. A comprehensive examination of the conditional form of bisimulation
821
      should be addressed as a standalone topic, as it lies beyond the scope of this work.
822
823   Given that our work extensively relies on the concept of metric spaces, we provide a summary of
824   their definition below for completeness. For a more detailed introduction, we refer the reader to the
825   existing literature and the work of Panangaden (2009).
826   A metric space is a pair (X, d), where X is a set and d : X × X → R≥0 is a function satisfying
827   the following properties: (i) ∀x, y ∈ X, d(x, y) = 0 if and only if x = y (identity), (ii) ∀x, y ∈
828   X, d(x, y) = d(y, x) (symmetry), and (iii) ∀x, y, z ∈ X, d(x, z) ≤ d(x, y) + d(y, z) (triangle
829   inequality). If d satisfies these properties, it is called a metric; if the identity property is relaxed, it is
830   called a pseudometric. The bisimulation metrics defined in this work are pseudometrics, as they relax
831   the identity property—specifically, d(si , sj ) = 0 when si and sj are behaviorally indistinguishable,
832
      but not necessarily when si = sj . With this foundation, we can now proceed with the proofs of the
      definitions.

      Environment. In the lending scenario, an agent representing the bank makes binary decisions
      loan applications with the goal of increasing its profit. Each applicant has an observable group
930
      membership g ∈ G and a discrete credit score 1 ≤ c ≤ Cmax sampled from group-specific and
931
      unequal initial distributions p0 (c|g). At each time step t, applicants are sampled uniformly with
932   replacement from the population, and the agent decides to accept or reject the loan application.
933   Successful repayment raises the applicant’s credit score by c+ , benefiting the agent financially with
934   r+ . Defaulting, however, reduces the credit score by c− and the agent’s profit by r− . If the agent
935   rejects the loan, it receives no reward. As discussed in Section Section 5.1, we examine two variants
936   of the lending scenario:
937
938         1. Credit only: The probability of repayment is a deterministic function of the applicant’s
939            credit score, similarly to D’Amour et al. (2020). However, this model oversimplifies cer-
940            tain dynamics, as the probability of repayment in reality can be a function of many factors
941            beyond the credit score. Additionally, this model fails to capture the case where an individ-
942
               ual is assigned a low credit score due to their limited credit history, rather than their true
               likelihood of loan repayment.
943
            2. Credit + Conscientiousness: The probability of repayment is a function of the applicants
944
               credit score and an unobservable latent variable representing the applicants conscientious-
945            ness. The conscientiousness for each individual is sampled from a Normal distribution and
946            is independent from their group membership.
947
948   The observation space in both variants include the applicant’s credit score, group membership, the
949   ratio of the past loan repayments, and the ratio of the past loan defaults. As discussed Section 4.2, the
950   Bisimulator algorithm, is allowed to change the observation dynamics. In this scenario, Bisimulator
951   changes the group-specific values for c+ and c− . For instance, applicants from the disadvantaged
952   group may receive a higher credit increase upon loan repayment, compared to those who belong to
953   the advantaged group. This is a realistic assumption since in practice, banks or other regulators are
954
      allowed to override credit scores during their decision making process (FDIC, 2005). Importantly,
      these changes are carried out by the agent and only affect the observation space, leaving the under-
955
      lying dynamics and the probability of repayment unchanged. In other words, the changes are on the
956
      agent side and affect how it “sees” the observations and they do not impact the actual dynamics.
