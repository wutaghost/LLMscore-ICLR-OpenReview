009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Influence functions are a popular tool for attributing models’ outputs to training
012            data. The traditional approach relies on the calculation of inverse Hessian-vector
013            products (iHVP), but the classical solver “Linear time Stochastic Second-order
014            Algorithm” (LiSSA, Agarwal et al. (2017)) is often deemed impractical for large
015            models due to expensive computation and hyperparameter tuning. We show that
016
               the three hyperparameters — the scaling factor, the batch size, and the number
               of steps — can be chosen depending on two specific spectral properties of the
017
               Hessian: its trace and largest eigenvalue. By evaluating them with random sketching
018
               (Swartworth and Woodruff, 2023), we find that the batch size has to be sufficiently
019            large for the LiSSA to converge; however, for all of the models we consider, the
020            requirement is mild. We confirm our findings empirically by comparing to the
021            Proximal Bregman Retraining Functions (PBRF, Bae et al. (2022)).

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep neural networks have seen many impressive results in the past years, but researchers and
027
      practitioners have little understanding what happens inside the models and how they learn to predict.
      While there exist various methods to interpret the internal computations of neural networks in an
028
      understandable to a human way, influence functions attempt to explain model behaviour by attributing
029
      model predictions (or generations) to particular examples in the training data.
030
031   Koh and Liang (2017) introduce Hessian-based influence functions in order to approximate the effect
032   of removal of one training point from the training set, which we refer to as leave-one-out retraining.
033
      The formula for influence calculation is derived from the second-order Taylor approximation of the
      loss, hence the Hessian and the gradient of the training point are sufficient for calculation. Koh and
034
      Liang (2017) demonstrate various applications of influence functions such as explaining of model
035
      outputs through data attribution, repairing mislabeled data, and backdoor attacks.
036
037   Basu et al. (2020) criticize influence functions for poor approximation of leave-one-out retraining as
038   the depth and width of neural networks increase. As a solution, Bae et al. (2022) propose two fixes:
039
      replacing the Hessian (that possibly has negative eigenvalues) with the well-behaved Gauss-Newton
      Hessian (Martens, 2020), and replacing the leave-one-out retraining (which itself is not a well-defined
040
      objective) with the Proximal Bregman Retraining Functions (PBRF). They demonstrate that the latter
041
      do not suffer from the randomness introduced by model initialization and data sampling, and they
042   argue can serve as gold standard when evaluating influence function approximation methods. In this
043   paper, we focus on this particular formulation of influence functions, where the Hessian is replaced
044   with Gauss-Newton Hessian, and the PBRF serves as the ground truth in validation.
045
      The calculation of influence functions, as introduced by Koh and Liang (2017), requires approximation
046
      of inverse Hessian-vector products. Given the dimension of modern deep models and the size of
047
      the training dataset, it can be a hard problem. As an alternative to traditional conjugate gradient
048   method, Koh and Liang (2017) proposed to use a stochastic iterative approach called “Linear time
049   Stochastic Second-Order Algorithm” (LiSSA, (Agarwal et al., 2017)). This algorithm requires
050   calculating a sampled Hessian-vector product at each iteration, where in the formulation of Koh and
051   Liang (2017), the batch size per sample can be as little as just one training point. There are two
052   additional hyperparameters involved — the scaling factor and the number of steps in the LiSSA,
053   with little direction of how to choose them in practice. Basu et al. (2020) criticize the LiSSA,
      suggesting that it becomes erroneous for deep networks with large number of parameters, and they


                                                        1

054
      also mention the importance of hyperparameter search. In this paper, we find that the choice of all
055
      three hyperparameters, including the batch size, depends on the properties of the Gauss-Newton
056   Hessian, namely, its trace and largest eigenvalue. Since the size of the Hessian is very large (number
057   of parameters to square), we evaluate these two statistics with random sketching (Swartworth and
058   Woodruff, 2023), which only requires estimation of Hessian-vector products in the process. We
059   report these statistics and the corresponding requirements for some open sourced vision and language
060   models. We also note that, contrary to a common belief, we find that the batch size has to be
061   sufficiently large for the algorithm to converge. However, this requirement is mild, and for language
062   models it is particularly redundant.
063   Some attempts to avoid calculating inverse Hessian vector products have been made in the literature.
064   Schioppa et al. (2022) suggest to truncate the spectrum of the Hessian, see also Fisher et al. (2023);
065   Grosse et al. (2023). When it comes to language models, most of the recent literature is using
066   gradient-based influence functions (Xia et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Chhabra et al., 2024). These are
067   typically focused on the finetunning stage, and often this choice is motivated by simpler and faster
068   implementation. In Section 5, we highlight the difference between the results of these two ways of
069   calculating influence functions.
070   Almost exclusively, Grosse et al. (2023) calculate Hessian-based influence of pretraining data for
071   LLMs. Their analysis is restricted to the MLPs of the transformer and they impose a block-wise
072   structure onto the Hessian. Although we do not advocate against such structural assumptions, our
073   work suggests that running the plain and model-agnostic LiSSA can be feasible, given that we
074   avoid the hyperparameter search. In our implementation, we follow the Hessian-free approach of
075   Martens et al. (2010) using finite differences, where an in-batch GNH-vector product is calculated
076
      with three forward propagations and one backward propagation. For example, our code allows
      to run the LiSSA for 7B language models on a 4×A100 GPU node. We share the code here
077
      https://anonymous.4open.science/r/gnhtools-53AE .

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We have shown how to choose the hyperparameters of the classical LiSSA algorithm based on two
489
      spectral statistics of the Gauss-Newton Hessian. In particular, we show the batch size used for
490   sampling Hessian-vector products per update has to be sufficiently large. Otherwise, the LiSSA
491   might not converge which we demonstrate empirically and theoretically. This particular aspect of
492   hyperparameter choice for the LiSSA algorithm has not been previously addressed in the literature.
493   Furthermore, we empirically demonstrate that applying to large models in its original form can still
494   be feasible if we choose a sufficiently large damping parameter. We do not necessarily advocate for
495   using the LiSSA as the algorithm of choice, rather it can be used as a baseline for validating other
496   more lightweight algorithms, in which case it is important to make sure that the hyperparameters of
497   the LiSSA are chosen correctly. We hope that our result and the implementation can further facilitate
498
      research in influence functions, as well as in other topics where the inverse Hessian-vector products
      naturally appear (Guo et al., 2019; Schulman et al., 2015; Martens, 2020; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017).
