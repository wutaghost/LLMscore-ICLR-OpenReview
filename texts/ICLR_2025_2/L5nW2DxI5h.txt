009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Bayesian Optimization is ubiquitous in the field of experimental design and black-
012            box optimization for improving search efficiency, but has been traditionally re-
013            stricted to regression models which are only applicable to fixed search spaces and
014            tabular input features. We propose Embed-then-Regress, a paradigm for applying
015            in-context regression over string inputs, through the use of string embedding ca-
016            pabilities of pretrained language models. By expressing all inputs as strings, we
017            able to perform general-purpose regression for Bayesian Optimization over differ-
018            ent search domains such as traditional and combinatorial optimization, obtaining
019            comparable results to state-of-the-art Gaussian Process-based algorithms.

      2    R ELATED W ORK AND M OTIVATION
055
056
      Bayesian Optimization refers to a class of techniques which use regressors for solving blackbox
057
      optimization problems, by suggesting best candidates according to an explore-exploit tradeoff. For
058   a given objective, the speed of optimization relies heavily on the regressor’s underlying prior, or
059   assumptions about the nature of the objective, such as its smoothness and landscape. Largely dom-
060   inated by the use of Gaussian Process (GP) regressors, the field of Bayesian Optimization has thus
061   seen a rise in works (Wang et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024) which seek to learn better prior GP hy-
062   perparameters such as length-scales and kernel amplitudes based on offline pretraining or manually
063   designed feature representations for combinatorial objects (Deshwal et al., 2023), while keeping
064   underlying kernel definitions fixed.
065   Numerous end-to-end neural network-based approaches such as the use of attention mechanisms and
066   Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have been introduced to allow more learnable behaviors, and we
067   refer the reader to (Song et al., 2024b) which provides a general reference on their use for blackbox
068   optimization. Relevant to our particular case of regression, works such as (Nguyen & Grover, 2022;
069   Garg et al., 2022) demonstrated the benefits of using raw Transformers as in-context regression
070   models, or neural processes, with others (Bai et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) establishing provable
071   guarantees. Similarly, (Müller et al., 2022) demonstrated that Transformers trained on synthetic
072
      data as “prior-fitted networks” are capable of Bayesian inference, leading to their use in Bayesian
      optimization (Müller et al., 2023; Nguyen & Grover, 2024).
073
074   Unfortunately, as both raw Transformers and GPs require fixed dimensional features, this limits
075   their applications to inputs expressable as tabular features for e.g. hyperparameter tuning, or task-
076   specific embeddings for e.g. chemistry (Maus et al., 2022). Further works have attempted to improve
077   the flexibility of regression-modeling through the use of token-based representations, which allows
078
      regressors to be simultaneously used over various types of input formats. Since the context-window
      of Transformers still remain the most expensive limitation, a useful organization of recent works can
079
      be based on their treatment of the total sequence length, roughly equal to:
080
081                              (number of trials) × (average trial token length)                          (1)
082
083   Among sequence-to-sequence methods which pretrain for blackbox optimization specifically, (Chen
084   et al., 2022) uses custom tokenizations to minimize trial token length in order to maximize trial
085   count. However, this is restricted to very constrained search spaces (e.g. flat hyperparameter spaces),
086   and lacks flexibility in utilizing arbitrary forms of data. In contrast, (Song et al., 2024a) allows
087   arbitrary string representations, but the large token length of each trial severely limits the trial count
088   allowed in the context window, forcing the use of alternative but tedious methods of absorbing online
089   data, such as inference-time fine-tuning.
090   Other methods (Vacareanu et al., 2024) use text-to-text chat-based services such as ChatGPT (Ope-
091   nAI, 2022) and Gemini (Google, 2024) to demonstrate their emergent capabilities for in-context
092   regression, but such methods lack the ability to pretrain over large amounts of offline evaluations.
093   Efforts in ICL-based reward modeling with chat-based LLMs (Lightman et al., 2024) allow fine-
094   tuning over chains of thought, but have only used coarse discretized scores of e.g. {−1, 0, 1} rather
095   than highly precise numeric predictions over vastly different scales.
096   For optimization, we require a regressor with all of the following capabilities:
097
098         • Pretrainable over offline evaluations to allow effective meta-learning.
099
            • Flexible representation of inputs with raw strings for application in multiple domains.
100
101         • Allow long-range in-context regression using multiple previous evaluations.
102         • Production of precise numeric predictions over diverse objective scales.
103
104   This naturally leads to the use of embedding-based methods which can compress any string repre-
105   sentation of a trial into a feature vector, using only a single unit of sequence length when sent to a
106   ICL model such as a raw Transformer. This can be seen as a form of encoding and decoding, which
107   has been shown to produce competitive results over pure language modeling tasks (Raffel et al.,
      2020).

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
130
      Let f : X → R be a real-valued function over a search space X . The goal of blackbox optimization
131
      is to produce an x∗ which maximizes f :
132
133                                                  x∗ = arg max f (x)                                      (2)
                                                                  x∈X
134
135   We define a regressor as a prediction model which can output a distribution of prediction values for
136   f (·) over a query point x, given the history {xs , ys }ts=1 of trajectory of t evaluations over f so far.
137   Such regressors may also be learnable over additional offline data besides the given history.
138
      During inference, the regressor may be turned into an acquisition function a : X → R to represent
139
      explore-exploit tradeoffs. We assume the existence of a (potentially task-dependent) acquisition
140   optimizer which can quickly and cheaply sample suggestions x ∈ X , usually in the form of an
141   evolutionary algorithm. The history-dependent acquisition at+1 (·) may thus be used to filter out
142   poor samples, or used in an entire Bayesian optimization loop in which the acquisition optimizer is
143   used to find xt+1 := arg maxx∈X at+1 (x) as the next x-proposal.
144
145   3.2   I N - CONTEXT T RANSFORMER R EGRESSOR
146
147   An embedding-based regressor uses an embedder φ : X → Rd to map a suggestion x to a fixed-
148   length representation x ∈ Rd , which can then be used as a regular feature vector for a numeric
149   regression model. A string-based embedder first represents x as a string, which is then passed
150   to a language model for embedding. We specifically use the typical definition of language model
151   embedding, in which we apply a forward pass of the underlying model (encoder or decoder) on the
152
      (tokenized) string representation to obtain all token logits in RL×d , and then pool across the length
      axis to obtain a vector in Rd . We discuss specific string representations in our experiments in Section
153
      4.
154
155   For our underlying regression model, we then use an additional Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017),
156   by sending in as input sequence (x1 ⊕ y 1 ), . . . , (xt ⊕ y t ) where y ∈ Rd is the feature representation
157   of the float y after applying a trainable projection, and x ⊕ y is the trial representation expressed as
158
      the concatenation of x and y.
159   In order to obtain a prediction for a query point x, we may then further append a query (x ⊕ 0) to the
160   history input sequence where 0 is a dummy value, and following a forward pass of the Transformer
161   where (x⊕0) attends to all previous trials, post-process the corresponding t+1-th output feature with
                                                                                                    2
      a parametric output distribution over R. For our case we assume a Gaussian N (µt+1 (x), σt+1     (x))

      using mean and standard deviation output heads. The key components to our method can be sum-
163   marized in Figure 2.
164
165   Additional techniques below are utilized to stabilize training and prediction:
166   Parallel Predictions: In order to simultaneously predict over a given set of k target points
167   xt+1 , . . . , xt+k , we additionally append (xt+1 ⊕ 0), . . . , (xt+k , 0) to the history sequence and gen-
168   erate a custom attention pattern of shape (t + k) × t where all tokens to attend to the history while no
169   tokens attend to the targets. This allows an efficient parallel modeling of p(yt+i | xt+i , {xs , ys }ts=0 )
170   for 1 ≤ i ≤ k which speeds up training when computing a summed loss over multiple target predic-
171   tions.
172   y-Normalization: Depending on the function, y-value outputs may contain a wide variety of scales,
173   and need to be normalized properly. We can define our normalization procedure parameterized by a
174   history of objectives (y1 , . . . , yt ), also applicable to incoming target values. These steps consist of,
175   in order: (1) Shifting objectives to have zero mean and divide by standard deviation. (2) Reducing
176   harmful effects of bad outliers by fitting the “bad half” of objectives {yi ≤ ymedian } to a normal
                                                                             y−ymin
177   curve, using percentiles as z-scores. (3) Linearly scale y ← ymax         −ymin which ensures all historical
178   y-values within [0, 1] and apply additional damping (e.g. sigmoid or log transform) to target values
179   significantly outside this range.
180   Encoding Metadata: Many times there may be a metadata m associated to an objective f , which
181   provides useful prediction information on the behavior of f or simply can inform the model of a
182   new objective or search space. We can also embed this metadata as an additional feature m, which
183   is concatenated to every x similarly to standard encoder-decoder techniques (Raffel et al., 2020).

      We benchmark over the Blackbox Optimization Benchmarking (BBOB) suite (ElHara et al., 2019),
271   one of the most widely used synthetic function benchmarks, containing 24 different objectives over
272   continuous search spaces. In order to have a notion of offline “training” data and unseen “test”
273   functions to be optimized, we split the original functions across each landscape type (Separable,
274   Ill-Conditioned, etc.) into training and test sets, and additionally apply randomized transformations
275   (e.g. shifting, rotating, discretizing, increasing/decreasing dimensions) over all objectives to induce
276   non-continuous search spaces with categorical parameters and avoid overfitting. Evaluations were
277   uniformly sampled over the search space.
278   As a traditional GP baseline, we use the industry-grade UCB-based Bayesian Optimization method
279   “GP-Bandit” (Song et al., 2024c) from Open Source Vizier (Song et al., 2022). In order to control
280   for confounding factors affecting performance, our method uses the same “Firefly” acquisition opti-
281   mizer as Vizier’s, with the same evaluation budget. In Figure 3, we find that Embed-then-Regress is
282   generally comparable with and interestingly can even significantly outperform GP-Bandit in a few
283   cases.
284   Combinatorial Optimization: We further benchmark over combinatorial objectives whose search
285   spaces are typically difficult to regress over. Many of these can be found in common operations
286   research literature, e.g. permutation-based (Travelling Salesman, Quadratic Assignment, Flowshop
287   Scheduling, and N-Queens), and choice-based (submodular maximization problems such as cover-
288   ing and log-determinant functions).
289   Each of these problems can be parameterized by a set of coefficients (e.g. city locations for Travel-
290   ling Salesman, matrix entries for log-determinant). Note that we are in the bandit setting, in which
291   these coefficients are hidden from the algorithm and the only feedback is the final objective. Similar
292   to before, we thus can also generate offline pretraining data by randomizing these coefficients and
293   problem sizes, and evaluating over random candidates. For our string regression, we may simply
294   use JSON over indices; e.g. [2,0,3,1] for a permutation space of size 4, e.g. [1,3] for a 42
295   choice space.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
379
380
      Our method, Embed-then-Regress, demonstrates the versatility of using string-based in-context re-
381   gression for Bayesian Optimization over a variety of problems. We have shown it to obtain com-
382   parable results against industry-standard GP baselines and allow flexibility in more esoteric spaces
383   such as permutations and combinations.
384
      As strings are significantly more flexible representation formats of different data types, an ambitious
385
      and exciting direction is to pretrain a unified in-context regression model over multiple different
386
      domains, in order to obtain a “universal” in-context regressor. Furthermore, our method is not
387   limited only to Transformers for in-context regression; it may be possible to additionally create a
388   string-based GP sending string embeddings inputs to a kernel.
389
390
      Further possible applications include prompt optimization (Fernando et al., 2024) and code search
      (Romera-Paredes et al., 2023), areas which still predominantly use zeroth-order evolutionary algo-
391
      rithms or even random search, which can be very sample inefficient compared to Bayesian Opti-
392
      mization. Additionally, outside of blackbox optimzation problems which are stateless with respect
393   to inputs, it is worth investigating whether such methods are applicable for process-based reward
394   modelling (Lightman et al., 2024) and tree search-based approaches (Yao et al., 2023) for stateful
395   environments in language modelling.
