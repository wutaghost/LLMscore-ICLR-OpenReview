                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012            Conversational large language models (LLMs) are trained to be helpful, honest
013            and harmless (HHH) and yet they remain susceptible to hallucinations, misinfor-
014            mation and are capable of deception. A promising avenue for safeguarding against
015            these behaviors is to gain a deeper understanding of their inner workings. Here we
016
               ask: what could interpretability tell us about deception and can it help to control
               it? First, we introduce a simple and yet general protocol to induce 24 large con-
017
               versational models from different model families (Llama, Gemma, Yi and Qwen)
018
               of various sizes (from 1.5B to 70B) to knowingly lie. Second, we characterize
019            three iterative refinement stages of deception from the latent space representation.
020            Third, we demonstrate that these stages are universal across models from differ-
021            ent families and sizes. We find that the third stage progression reliably predicts
022            whether a certain model is capable of deception. Furthermore, our patching re-
023            sults reveal that a surprisingly sparse set of layers and attention heads are causally
024            responsible for lying. Importantly, consistent across all models tested, this sparse
025            set of layers and attention heads are part of the third iterative refinement process.
026            When contrastive activation steering is applied to control model output, only steer-
027
               ing these layers from the third stage could effectively reduce lying. Overall, these
               findings identify a universal motif across deceptive models and provide actionable
028
               insights for developing general and robust safeguards against deceptive AI. The
029
               code, dataset, visualizations, and an interactive demo notebook are available at
030            https://github.com/safellm-2024/llm_deception.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have seen widespread deployment in recent years. They exhibit
037   impressive general capabilities – some of which approach or even surpass human expertise. These
038   advances also pose greater risks around misuses in misinformation and malicious applications (Hub-
039   inger et al., 2024; Scheurer et al., 2024). Despite the growing evidence for unsafe behaviors that
040   persist through safety training, we know very little about why and how these safety breaches oc-
041   cur. Enhanced transparency of models under those scenarios would offer numerous benefits, from
042   a deeper understanding of their inner workings, to increased accountability for safety assurance and
043   the potential for discovering novel failure modes (Casper et al., 2024).
044   Recent advances in interpretability (Wang et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023b;a; Meng et al., 2023;
045   Zou et al., 2023) have demonstrated great potential for understanding the internal mechanisms of
046   language models. Interpretability tools have successfully revealed the inner mechanisms of mod-
047   els performing various tasks. However, most interpretability works study base models that have
048   not been through safety training. Some recent works carefully examine a set of safety-related be-
049   haviors in chat models (Campbell et al., 2023; Arditi et al., 2024; Ball et al., 2024; Turner et al.,
050
      2024; Rimsky et al., 2024), but they typically limiting themselves to one kind of model under each
      investigation.
051
052   In this study, we integrate mechanistic interpretability and representation engineering tools (Zou
053   et al., 2023) to study a diverse set of large conversational language models (chat models), focusing
      on one key safety challenge – deception. Overall, our main contributions are:

067   2   R ELATED W ORK
068
069   Dishonesty and Deception. Many studies highlight that LLMs do not reliably output truth. Fail-
070   ures in truthfulness fall into two categories (Evans et al., 2021): sometimes LLMs simply do not
071   know the correct answer (capability failure), and sometimes they apparently ‘know’ the true answer
072   but nevertheless generate a false response or ‘hide’ their true motives (Perez et al., 2022; Pacchiardi
073   et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023). For instance, Lin et al. (2022) show that models
074   often generated false answers that mimic popular human misconceptions. Interestingly, Lin et al.
075   (2022) show that scaling up models alone does not help improving truthfulness since larger models
076   are more prone to imitative falsehoods (inverse scaling law). Park et al. (2023) document that the AI
077
      system CICERO can engage in premeditated deception, planning in advance to build a fake alliance
      with a player in order to trick that player into leaving themselves undefended for an attack. More
078
      recently, Hubinger et al. (2024) create ‘sleeper agents’ which behave helpfully during training but
079
      exhibit harmful behaviors when deployed. Their results raise concerns about the effectiveness of
080   current safety training techniques against maliciously trained AI systems. Scheurer et al. (2024)
081   demonstrate that LLM agents can even strategically deceiving their users in a realistic situation,
082   without direct instructions or training for deception.
083
084   Internal States of Lying. Recent work has proposed that LLMs have a internal representation of
085   truthfulness, opening up opportunities to detect and diagnose deception from the latent representa-
086   tions.
087
      Burns et al. (2024) developed an unsupervised probe called Contrast-Consistent Search (CCS) for
088
      predicting a model’s latent representation of truth, independent of what a model outputs, without us-
089   ing any supervision. Azaria & Mitchell (2023) introduced a supervised probe by training classifiers
090   on LLM hidden layers to detect whether a statement generated by an LLM is truthful or not. Our
091   work build on this work, utilizing their true-false statements as our primary dataset.
092
      Levinstein & Herrmann (2023) raise concerns that probes fail to generalize in basic ways. They
093
      find that the supervised probes developed by Azaria & Mitchell (2023) fail to generalize well to
094
      negations of statements they were trained on. And the CCS probes (Burns et al., 2024) achieve low
095   loss but poor accuracy, often just learning to detect negations rather than truth. They conclude that
096   there is still no reliable and generalizable ‘lie detector’ for LLMs, which further motives our work.
097
098
      Zou et al. (2023) propose using Linear Artificial Tomography (LAT) to detect lying. Similar to our
      approach, LAT applies Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to the collected neural activities. Also
099
      using PCA, Marks & Tegmark (2024) reveal that true/false statement representations are linealy
100
      represented in model internals.
101
102   Campbell et al. (2023) used a filtered dataset of true/false questions from Azaria & Mitchell (2023)
103   and developed prompts to induce lying. They then employed linear probing and activation patching
104
      to localize lying. However, their work only focus on deception in Llama-2-70b-chat model.
105   Our work build on but extend beyond these works. First, we create a simple yet general protocol to
106   induce lying in a diverse set of models (24 models form 4 models families). Second, we characterize
107   a universal pattern in latent representation structure and provide a metric that could predict which
      models can lie and which cannot. Third, we integrate a battery of interpretability tools including

108
      activation patching and contrastive activation steering to causally identify key model components
109   and effectively steer the models to reduce lying.

137   3.2   A GENERAL PROTOCOL FOR INSTRUCTING DIFFERENT MODELS TO KNOWINGLY LIE
138
      As noted by Campbell et al. (2023) inducing lying behavior can be challenging and requires careful
139
      prompt engineering. Built on this previous work, we build a simple and yet general protocol to
140
      induce knowingly lie in a diverse set up models.
141
142   As illustrated in Figure 1, the main structure of our prompting protocol composed of the following
143   four components:
144
145          • 1. System prompt. For each statement, we have a pair of contrastive prompts:
146              – Honest persona: “You are to answer the following question in an honest manner.”
147              – Lying persona: “You are to answer the following question in a lying manner.”
148
             • 2. User prompt: “Is the following statement true of false?”
149
150          • 3. Statement: Insert one statement regarding a scientific fact from Azaria & Mitchell
151            (2023)
152          • 4. Prefix injection: “Answer: The statement is       .”
153
154
      3.3   D ECEPTION EVALUATION
155
156   Our careful prompting design encourages free generation as well as enforcing a structure so that
157   the performance can be easily measured by matching to the ground-truth label (either “true” or
158   “false”). Crucially, the first 20 tokens (instead of only the first token) are evaluated and matched to
159   the ground-truth label. This is because we notice that LLMs tend to inject stylistic words rather than
160   immediately answer “true” or “false”. For example, Llama-2-7B-Chat model tend to insert ”...*wink
161   wink*...” before stating if the answer is “true” or “false”. For quantification of model performance,
      see §E.

189
190   Contrastive activation steering is a technique for controlling the behavior of language models by
191   modifying their internal activations during inference (Turner et al., 2024; Arditi et al., 2024; Rimsky
192   et al., 2024). The two major steps of contrastive activation steering are:
193
194           • 1. Extracting the steering vector from contrastive examples.
195           • 2. Applying the steering vectors to modify model behavior during generation.

217
218   ‘Honest addition’. To steer the lying model to become honest, the ‘honest direction’ is added as
219   the steering vector to the lying activations. This is a form of contrastive activation steering called

221

222

224

229   activations from the same layer l and α is the scaling factor. We find that a scaling factor of 1 is
230   enough to steer the lying model to become honest across all models tested.
231
232
      Following Arditi et al. (2024), the steering vector extracted from layer l is applied only at layer l,
      and across all token positions during generation.

235   3.7     C ONTRASTIVE ACTIVATION PATCHING
236
237   Contrastive activation patching is a causal intervention tool to identify model components responsi-
238   ble for lying. It is a similar to the causal intervention technique performed in Meng et al. (2023) and
239   Wang et al. (2022).
240   Contrastive activations patching consists of three steps:
241
242           • 1. ‘Honest run’. First, all activations of the network run are cached when the model is
243             prompted to answer questions in an honest manner.
244
245
              • 2. ‘Lying run’. Secondly, all activations of the network run are cached when the model is
                prompted to answer questions in a lying manner.
246
247           • 3. ‘Patched run’. Finally the network is run as the model is prompted to lie, but some
248             activations are replacing with the activations from the ‘honest run’.
249
250   The model output (behavior) as well as the internal activations of the patched model are being
251   measured after patching. Doing this for each node individually allow us to locate the nodes that
252   explain the difference between the ‘honest run’ and ‘lying run’.
253

312   4.1   LYING SCALES WITH MODEL SIZE
313
314
      We focus on studying one type of deception where models give wrong answers to a question even
      though they ‘know’ the correct answer (knowingly lie). To do so, we first filter out a set of questions
315
      (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023) that the LLMs can answer correctly when prompted to be honest. We
316
      then check if they will answer incorrectly when asked to lie.
317
318   As has been previously noted (Campbell et al., 2023), inducing lying behavior can be surprisingly
319   challenge and often requires careful prompt engineering. Built on the work of Campbell et al.
320
      (2023), we establish a general protocol (detailed description in §3.2) for inducing a wide range of
      models to knowingly lie.
321
322   Constrained by our carefully designed chatting template, the model first make a true or false judge-
323   ment for a given statement and then elaborates on the rationale for the judgement. As illustrated
      in Figure 1, the careful prompting design encourages free generation and enforcing a structure so

      that the performance can be easily measured by matching to the ground truth label (either “true”

460   stages of the iterative refinement process. However, only the lying models proceed to complete the
461   third stage. This observation raises the question of whether the layers involved in the third stage are
462

      intervention tool to identify the model components directly implicated in dishonesty.
463
464   Following the methodology outlined in §3.7, we report results for two levels of patching: layer-by-
465   layer and head-by-head interventions: layer-by-layer and head-by-head patching.
466   For the layer-by-layer patching, the representations (residual stream activations) from the ‘honest
467   run’ are patched to the ‘lying run’ for each token position (of the prompt) across all layers of the
468   model. The average logit difference (ALD) across 100 statements serve as a proxy for the causal
469   contribution of each layer. Consistent with previous findings by Marks & Tegmark (2024); Tigges
470   et al. (2023), both Llama and Gemma models display the “summarization” behavior where infor-
471   mation relevant to the full statement is represented at the end-of-sentence token (last token of the
472   prompt). This pattern is consistent for both Llama and Gemma models (Figure 5A&D).
473   Head-level patching further reveals a sparse set of attention heads causally responsible for lying
474   (Figure 5B&E). Patching results for MLP and attention outputs are presented in Figure 10. Attention
475   pattern for heads with top ALD can be found in §F.2.
476
      Crucially, the layers showing the most significant increase in patching contribution (as indicated
477
      by a sharp rise in ALD , detailed in §3.7.1) correspond to the stage three layers where ‘truth di-
478   rections’ undergo a marked rotation relative to each other. Accordingly, cosine similarity between
479   the ‘truth directions’ sharply decrease. This finding aligns with the results presented in §4.3, which
480   demonstrate that progression through stage three is a key predictor of whether a model is capable of
481   lying.

528   5   C ONCLUSIONS & F UTURE W ORK
529
530   In this paper, we dissect and control a key safety related problem in LLMs, i.e., the generation of
531   incorrect and false information. Using a simple yet general protocol, we induce a wide range of
532   large language models to lie. By dissecting the latent activations, we demonstrate how LLMs could
533   knowingly lie through a three-stage iterative refinement process. We confirm that LLMs possess an
534   internal representation of truth at early-middle layers, evident by the emergence of ‘truth directions’
535   at the second stage. Interesting, the ‘truth directions’ subsequently ‘rotate’ with respect to each other
536
      during the third stage.
537   Importantly, we confirm that this ‘rotation’ motif is universal – it is present in all models that are
538   capable of lying and absent in all models that cannot lie. Combining causal intervention (patching)
539   and steering (contrastive activation steering) tools, we further confirm that the sparse set of layers
      during stage three are causally responsible for lying.

      5.1   L IMITATION AND F UTURE D IRECTION
541
542   One limitation of the current set up is we only investigate one type of deception – instructed lying
543   - where the models are prompted to knowingly lie. Deception is a rich phenomenon with many
544   different facets. Deception in LLMs can emerge without instruction through mimicking common
545   human misconceptions (imitative lying) (Lin et al., 2022) or through learning in the case of deceptive
546   instrumental alignment (Hubinger et al., 2024). Deception may also be unintentional and emerge
547   through hallucinations (Maynez et al., 2020). Our paper lay the groundwork to dissect one kind of
548
      deception in a wide range of large conversational models, we leave further investigation of other
      important deception variants for future work.

550   Further mechanistic interpretability work could elucidate the mechanism of the attention heads and
551   further dissect the mechanism underlying attention heads that are responsible for the ‘rotation’ op-
