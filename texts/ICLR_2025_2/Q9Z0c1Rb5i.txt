009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) aim to deliver interpretable and
012            interventionable predictions by bridging features and labels with human-
013            understandable concepts. While recent CBMs show promising potential, they suffer
014            from information leakage, where unintended information beyond the concepts
015            (either in probabilistic or binary-state form) is leaked to the subsequent label
016            prediction. Consequently, distinct classes are falsely classified via indistinguishable
017            concepts, undermining the interpretation and intervention of CBMs.
018            This paper alleviates the information leakage issue by introducing label supervision
019            in concept prediction and constructing a hierarchical concept set. Accordingly, we
020
               propose a new paradigm of CBMs, namely S UP CBM, which stands for Structured
               Understanding of leakage Prevention Concept Bottleneck Model, achieving label
021
               prediction via predicted concepts and a deliberately structural-designed intervention
022
               matrix. S UP CBM focuses on concepts that are mostly relevant to the predicted
023            label and only distinguishes classes when different concepts are presented. Our
024            evaluations show that S UP CBM’s label prediction outperforms SOTA CBMs over
025            diverse datasets, and its predicted concepts also exhibit better interpretability. With
026            proper quantification of information leakage in different CBMs, we demonstrate
027            that S UP CBM significantly reduces the information leakage.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
031   Deep neural networks (DNNs) have achieved remarkable success in many real-life tasks. However,
032   their black-box nature makes the extracted features obscure, hindering users from interpreting DNN
033
      predictions. To address this issue, concept bottleneck models (CBMs) have recently emerged with
      the purpose of delivering high-quality explanations for DNN predictions (Koh et al., 2020). CBMs
034
      are a type of DNN that make predictions based on human-understandable concepts. CBMs typically
035
      have a Concept-Bottleneck (CB) layer located before the last fully-connected (FC) layer. The CB
036   layer takes features extracted (by preceding layers) from the input and maps them to a set of concepts.
037   This effectively aligns the intermediate layers of a DNN with some pre-defined expert concepts, and
038   the last FC determines the final label over those concepts. Often, CBMs require first training the CB
039   layer to align each neuron to a concept that is pre-defined and understandable to humans.
040
      Based on the output concepts of the CB layer, CBMs deliver two key benefits: interpretability and
041
      intervenability. First, users can interpret the predicted labels by inspecting the involved concepts.
042   Second, users can intervene the predicted labels by determining which concepts are involved in the
043   prediction. Technically, CBMs are often categorized as soft CBMs and hard CBMs. The CB layer in
044   soft CBMs outputs a probability (i.e., a number between 0 and 1) for each concept, whereas the hard
045   CB layer outputs a binary state (i.e., 0 or 1) to indicate if a concept exists in the input. Then, a label
046   predictor (often the last FC layer) predicts the final label based on the concept probabilities or states.
047
      Despite the encouraging potential of CBMs in delivering human-understandable explanations, their
048   interpretability and intervenability are largely undermined in de facto technical solutions due to
049   information leakage, where unintended information beyond the concepts is leveraged by the label
050   predictor. Specifically, the concept probabilities in soft CBMs may encode class distribution
051   information, such that the label predictor can classify distinct class labels based on indistinguishable
052   concepts (Havasi et al., 2022). For instance, the label predictor may leverage the probability
053   differences of “head” and “tail” to classify dog vs. cat and achieve high accuracy, despite
      that these two concepts are insufficient to distinguish dog and cat. While hard CBMs were previously


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      believed resilient to information leakage, a recent study (Mahinpei et al., 2021) has pointed out that
055   hard CBMs can leverage unrelated hard concepts to convey class distributions to the label predictor.
056   For example, (Mahinpei et al., 2021) demonstrates that hard CBM’s performance can be improved by
057   adding meaningless hard concepts.
058
      To faithfully achieve CBM’s design objectives, it is urgent to mitigate information leakage in CBMs.
059
      Therefore, this paper proposes a novel CBM paradigm, dubbed S UP CBM. Unlike prior CBMs that
060
      treat concept prediction and label prediction as two separate tasks, we fuse them into a unified form
061   by additionally supervising the concept prediction with class labels. Specifically, S UP CBM does not
062   implement a label predictor, but employs an intervention matrix for label prediction. The intervention
063   matrix is formed when constructing the concept set; it is implemented as a sparse binary matrix of
064   shape #concepts × #classes, where the (i, j)-th entry indicates whether the i-th concept should be
065   leveraged to recognize the j-th class.
066
      When training S UP CBM, the CB layer is forced to only predict concepts that are relevant to the
067   ground truth label, which is jointly decided by the intervention matrix and a novel concept pooling
068   layer (which further selects the most important concepts for each input; see Sec. 3.2). Then, to
069   obtain the final predicted label, we multiply the CB layer’s output, a #concepts-dimensional vector
070   indicating the predicted concepts, with the intervention matrix. This computation is equivalent to
071   summing up the involved concepts’ probabilities for each label and treating the summed probability
072   as the label’s prediction confidence. Hence, S UP CBM only distinguishes classes if different concepts
073   are presented, significantly alleviating the information leakage (see Sec. 3.3 and Sec. 5.2).
074   Similar to previous post-hoc CBMs (Oikarinen et al., 2023), S UP CBM is post-hoc as it only trains a
075   light-weight CB layer (without training the label predictor) for any pre-trained feature-based model.
076   Moreover, since our label prediction is achieved by multiplying the predicted concepts with the
077   intervention matrix, S UP CBM also ensures the intervenability: users can directly let the i-th concept
078   involved in predicting the j-th class by setting the (i, j)-th entry in the intervention matrix as 1, and
079   vice versa.
080   Besides reforming the CBM technical pipeline, we further augment CBM interpretability and
081   intervenability from the concept aspect. Our concept set prioritizes perceptual concepts that can be
082   directly perceived by humans without further reasoning, e.g., “‘tail” instead of “animal”. Also,
083   to fully use the rich semantics of these perceptual concepts (which are mostly nouns), we assign each
084   of them multiple descriptive concepts (i.e., adjectives) and build a two-level hierarchical concept set.
085   With our novel concept set, S UP CBM delivers more interpretable concept prediction and accurate
086   label prediction; it outperforms all SOTA CBMs and even reaches the vanilla feature-based model
087
      (w/o converting features into concepts) when evaluated using diverse datasets and backbones (see
      Sec. 5). Overall, this paper makes the following contributions:
088
089   • We propose a new paradigm of CBMs, S UP CBM, which supervises concept prediction with class
090     labels and employs an intervention matrix for label prediction. S UP CBM significantly reduces
091     information leakage and delivers post-hoc, intervenable, and more interpretable CBMs.
092   • To further enhance CBM interpretability, we advocate that the concept set should be primarily built
093     with perceptual concepts (which can be directly perceived by humans without further reasoning).
094     Accordingly, to utilize rich semantics in perceptual concepts, we build a two-level hierarchical
095
        concept set by assigning each perceptual concept with multiple descriptive concepts.
      • Evaluations show that S UP CBM’s label prediction outperforms previous SOTA CBMs and achieves
096
        comparable performance to the vanilla, feature-based models; it also significantly alleviates the
097
        information leakage in label prediction. With large-scale human studies, we demonstrate that
098     S UP CBM’s concept prediction is more interpretable than that of SOTA CBMs.

      2    C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS AND I NFORMATION L EAKAGE
103
      This section introduces CBMs and the information leakage issue that motivates this paper. Without
104   losing the generality, we take image classification as an example in this section.
105
106   Dataset Construction. Given a conventional dataset D = {x(i) , y (i) }N
                                                                            i=1 consisting of N pairs of
107   input image x(i) ∈ Rd and its ground truth label y (i) ∈ N, CBMs require augmenting the dataset
      D with annotated concepts for all images. Specifically, for a set of pre-defined concepts C, each


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      image x(i) is annotated using c(i) ∈ {0, 1}|C| . If the j-th element of c(i) is 1, it indicates that the j-th
109
      concept in C is presented in x(i) , and vice versa.
110
111   The concept set C is often manually defined and the annotations of c(i) require extensive human
112   efforts, limiting the applicability of CBMs. Recent works (Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023)
113   have employed LLMs to automatically generate concepts, bringing richer and more expressive
114   concept sets to CBMs. However, we note that their concepts are often obscure, which impedes
115
      interpreting and intervening on CBMs. This paper alleviates this issue by focusing on perceptual
      concepts and their descriptions.
116
117   CBM Pipeline. CBMs divide the label prediction y = arg max f (x) into two main steps: the concept
118   prediction c = g(x), which is achieved by appending the penultimate layer in f with the CB layer,
119   and the label prediction y = arg max h(c). Accordingly, CBMs require additionally training the
120   concept predictor, which can be conducted prior to or jointly with training the label predictor. The
121
      training of concept predictor is often formulated as multiple concurrent binary classification tasks,
      with each one for one concept. During CBM’s inference, the concept predictor outputs a probability
122
      for each concept. If the follow-up label prediction directly takes these probabilities, the CBM is
123
      categorized as soft CBM. In contrast, if the CBM first converts the probabilities to binary states (i.e.,
124   0 or 1) and then feeds them to the label predictor, the CBM implements hard CBM.
125
126   CBM Conversion. When converting a backbone feature-based model into CBMs, the common
127
      practice is to tune the whole model. Nevertheless, this incurs a high training cost due to the large
      number of parameters in the backbone model. Several post-hoc conversions of CBMs have been
128
      proposed in recent works (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022; Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Yan
129
      et al., 2023). In short, they align features (from the backbone model) with concepts (described in
130   text) using similarity scores from multimodal alignment models (e.g., CLIP), and only train the label
131   predictor, thereby reducing the conversion cost. Nevertheless, one recent work pointed that concepts
132   generated in this post-hoc manner compromise the intervention of CBMs (Marcinkevičs et al., 2024).
133   This paper ensures the intervenability using an intervention matrix during label prediction.
134
      Information Leakage. As continuously studied in prior works (Havasi et al., 2022; Mahinpei et al.,
135
      2021; Marconato et al., 2022), the interpretability and intervenability, two key design objectives of
136   CBMs, are undermined by the information leakage issue.
137
138
139   Definition 2.1 (Information Leakage). Information leakage in CBMs indicates that unintended and
140   additional information beyond the predicted concepts is leveraged for the follow-up label prediction.
141
142
143
      Leakage in Soft CBMs: In soft CBMs, the information leakage is induced by the concept
144   probabilities (Havasi et al., 2022). Consider a CBM-based animal classifier with the concept set
145   {fur, tail}, suppose it predicts fur with a higher probability than tail when recognizing dogs,
146   but assigns tail a higher probability than fur when identifying cats. Although the concepts fur
147   and tail are insufficient to distinguish dog vs. cat, the label predictor (which is also a black-box
148   DNN) may compare concept probabilities to decide the final label. Apparently, the CBM-based
149   classifier does not only rely on fur and tail to classify cat and dog, and the interpretation and
150   intervention based on fur and tail are consequently invalid. Here, the difference between concept
151   probabilities encodes the class distribution information that is beyond the concepts themselves.
152   Leakage in Hard CBMs: While hard CBMs were previously believed resilient to information
153   leakage, one recent work pointed out that hard CBMs can encode/leak additional information via
154   irrelevant (hard) concepts (Mahinpei et al., 2021). As demonstrated in (Mahinpei et al., 2021), merely
155   adding meaningless hard concepts to hard CBMs can improve their label prediction performance,
156   indicating that these added concepts bring additional information to the label prediction.
157   Essentially, the information leakage is primarily induced by ay      iirrelevant concepts and/or by     i
158   additional information encoded in concept probabilities. S UP CBM eliminates these two leakage
159   sources by deliberately reforming the adoption of concepts in the label prediction, as will be introduced
160   in Sec. 3. S UP CBM also achieves post-hoc CBM conversion by only training a light-weight one-layer
161   CB layer for each backbone model, and compared with prior post-hoc conversions, S UP CBM exhibits
      better generality when applied to different backbone models (see evaluations in Appx. D).

183   3.1     C ONCEPT S ET C ONSTRUCTION
184
      Perceptual Concepts. One key observation made in this paper, is that some concepts in previous
185
      works are often too holistic to be easily perceivable by humans and require manual reasoning. For
186   instance, the concept “animal” is involved in classifying cats. Although animal is a genuine super-
187   class of cat, it cannot be perceptually observed; prior knowledge is required to reason that cat is an
188   animal. Also, such concepts impede the intervention of CBMs: it is obscure to add or remove the
189   concept “animal” for the follow-up label prediction. Thus, we advocate that the concept set should
190   be formed with perceptual concepts that can be directly observed by humans without reasoning.
191
      Hierarchical Concepts. Since CBMs are designed to only describe whether certain concepts are
192   involved in the label prediction, the rich semantics of concepts often cannot be faithfully reflected from
193   CBMs. Considering the case of classifying different breeds of fish in CIFAR100, while the concept
194   “tail” can be leveraged for predicting labels, the information it contributes is far beyond than simply
195   indicating its involvement. For example, a “fan tail” is critical to identify goldfish, whereas
196   the “veil tail” is vital to identify betta fish. Thus, we form a two-level hierarchical
197   concept set, where the first level contains nounal perceptual concepts and flags if a concept is involved
198   in the label prediction, whereas the second level includes adjectival concepts for each first-level
199   concept and describes which semantics of the visual concepts contribute to the label prediction.
200   Prompt Design. We use GPT to automatically build the concept set. Since we require perceptually
201   perceivable concepts to enhance CBM interpretability and intervenability, we first query the GPT
202   model with the following prompt:
203              “To identify {CLS} visually, please list the most important {p} visual parts which
204              a {CLS} has.”
205
206
      Here, the token {CLS} can be replaced by any classes the original feature-based model can predict.
      The GPT returns a set of nounal visual parts in {CLS} as our first-level concept {CEP}. Then,
207
      for each concept {CEP} of a class {CLS}, we query the GPT model with the following prompt to
208
      uncover semantics associated with different concepts.
209
210              “To visually identify {CLS}, please describe the {q} most common characteristics
211
                 of {CLS}’s {CEP} from the three dimensions of shape, color, or size.”
212   With the above prompt, the GPT model can return to us a set of adjectival concepts as our second-level
213   concepts that describe the semantics of the first-level nounal concepts. We limit the adjectival concepts
214   to follow the three orthogonal dimensions of shape, color, and size, thereby reducing ambiguity. We
215   also remove any concept that is longer than 40 characters to make the descriptions simple. This way,
      we obtain a two-level concept set containing concepts represented in a ⟨{CEP},{DESCR} ⟩ form,

      3.2   T RAINING S UPERVISION
224
225   intervention matrix. We maintain an intervention matrix I to represent which concept (i.e., a pair
226   of ⟨{CEP},{DESCR} ⟩) should be involved in predicting a class label. The intervention matrix is a
227   binary matrix of shape #concepts × #classes, as shown in Fig. 1(d). Each Ii,j ∈ {0, 1} and Ii,j = 1
228   indicates that the i-th concept should be involved in predicting the j-th class; otherwise, Ii,j = 0.
229   The I is obtained after querying the GPT with the second prompt mentioned in Sec. 3.1.
230   To show the effectiveness of the intervention matrix, we conduct an intervention experiment
231   following (Oikarinen et al., 2023) in Appx. C.
232
233
      Label-Aware Concept Annotation. A fundamental difference between S UP CBM and previous
      CBMs is the supervision of class label when training the CB layer. As illustrated in Fig. 1, for each
234
      training image x whose ground truth label is y, we annotate it only with concepts that are involved in
235
      predicting y (as indicated by the intervention matrix). This annotation, to some extent, can supervise
236   the CB layer to “fuse” the concept prediction and label prediction; it improves the performance of
237   CBMs and also reduces the information leakage due to irrelevant concepts ( ay  i).
238
239
      Concept Pooling. To further rule out potentially irrelevant concepts, we implement a selective
      strategy as shown in Fig. 1(c). Similar to the max pooling mechanism in conventional computer
240
      vision which selects most important features, we choose to annotate x with those most important
241
      concepts. We name our selection procedure as concept pooling as it is equivalent to applying an
242   1-dimensional max pooling of kernel size p and stride q on all second-level concepts. Specifically,
243   we first compute x’s similarities with all concepts ⟨{CEP},{DESCR} ⟩ involved in predicting y (as
244   indicated by the intervention matrix), which is measured via the cosine similarity between the CLIP
245   embeddings of x and ⟨{CEP},{DESCR} ⟩. Then, for concepts ⟨{CEP},{DESCR} ⟩ sharing the same
246   {CEP}, we choose those having the top-k similarity as the ground truth concepts. This way, we have
247   total p × k concept annotations for each input image.
248   Training Objectives. Unlike previous post-hoc CBMs that directly train the follow-up label predictor
249   over concept similarities (which undermines the intervenability as pointed out by (Marcinkevičs
250   et al., 2024)), we do not train a label predictor. Instead, we train the CB layer (without training the
251   backbone model) since label supervision is unavailable for test images. Specifically, for each input
252   image, we set the selected p × k concepts as hard labels and form p × k binary classification tasks
253   following the conventional training procedure. These binary classification tasks are optimized with
254   binary cross-entropy loss ℓBCE (c, GTc ), where c is the concept probabilities predicted by the CB
255   layer and GTc is the ground truth concepts. Both c and GTc are pq-dimension vectors.
256   The predicted label is decided as arg max c ∗ I, as shown in Fig. 1(d), where ∗ denotes matrix
257   multiplication. That is, the j-th class’s prediction confidence is computed as

274   3.3   E LIMINATING I NFORMATION L EAKAGE
275
276   S UP CBM eliminates information leakage from both sources mentioned in Sec. 2. First, for leakage
277
      due to irrelevant concepts ( ay
                                    i ), we mitigate it during the concept annotation: S UP CBM only
      focuses on concepts that should be involved in predicting the label. Second, we further select those
278
      most important descriptive concepts {DESCR} via our concept pooling mechanism, thus trimming
279
      irrelevant concepts. With these two steps, irrelevant concepts are rarely kept in label predictions.
280
281   Moreover, for leakage where the concept probabilities encode distribution of class information ( by  i),
282   we justify how our intervention matrix can eliminate such leakage. Consider two classes a and
283
      b whose involved concepts (according to I) constitute sets Ca and Cb , respectively. We define
      S = Ca ∩ Cb , A = Ca \ S, and B = Cb \ S. That is, S consists of concepts shared by both classes,
284
      and A and B denote the sets of concepts that are only involved in predicting a and b, respectively.
285
286   ① If S = ∅, i.e., the two classes do not share any concepts, it should be clear that classifying a and
287   b only relies on their disjoint concepts A and B, and S UP CBM finds concepts that uniquely exist
288   in classes a and b. Note that our concept pooling mentioned above can help reduce the size of S,
289
      increasing the chance of S = ∅.
290   And in case S ̸= ∅, i.e., the two classes share some concepts, we analyze the following two cases.
291
      ② If A = B = ∅, i.e., predicting class a and b rely on the same indistinguishable concepts. Since our
292
      label prediction confidence (i.e., c ∗ I) is the sum of concept probabilities, it cannot distinguish a and
293   b, and no information beyond the concept is leveraged to falsely conduct classification.
294
295   ③ If A ̸= ∅ or B ̸= ∅, i.e., either class a or b, or both, have their unique concepts. Thus, given
296
      an input, the difference between the two label prediction confidences (w.r.t. class a and b) is only
      induced by the concepts uniquely belonging to A and B. Apparently, no information beyond a and
297
      b’s unique concepts is leveraged to classify them.

      Concept Set Construction. The size of our concept set is decided by two hyperparameters p and q.
303
      In general, it is often expected to build a large concept set for completeness. However, a large concept
304
      set will make the CBM conversion and inference costly. Hence, we suggest setting moderate values
305   for p and q. Our current setup uses a set of default settings p = 5 and q = 6; although relatively small,
306   it is sufficient to cover a wide range of concepts, as supported by our promising label prediction
307   performance (see Sec. 5.1). Our concept pooling has a hyperparameter k to control the number of
308   selected concepts. We set k = 2 in our experiments. Overall, S UP CBM constantly outperforms
309   SOTA CBMs and eliminates information leakage when using different k.
310
      In Appx. F, we have included a comprehensive ablation analysis that examines the effects of
311   parameters p, q, and k on S UP CBM. This analysis explains the rationale behind our default choices
312   of these parameters.
313
314
      GPT Versions. S UP CBM employs GPT-4 (2023-07-01) to generate (textual) concepts. As will be
      shown in Sec. 5, S UP CBM outperforms prior CBMs that are based on GPT-3. To justify that the
315
      superiority of S UP CBM is not brought by GPT-4, we also adopt GPT-3 (2023-03-15) to generate
316
      concepts for S UP CBM. Overall, S UP CBM’s performance on GPT-4 and GPT-3 is almost identical,

334   5.1   P ERFORMANCE C OMPARISON
335
336   Following previous works (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022; Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023),
337
      we consider the following representative datasets to evaluate the performance of different models.
      These datasets are representative and cover three major computer vision domains including general
338
      classification (CIFAR10, CIFAR100), specialized classification (CUB-Bird), and medical image
339
      analysis (HAM10000).
340
341   CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 32×32 RGB-color images of 10 classes and each
342   class has 6,000 images. CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) consists of 32×32 RGB-color images
343
      of 100 classes and each class has 600 images. CUB-Bird (Wah et al., 2011) consists of 11,788
      RGB-color images of 200 bird species. HAM10000 (Tschandl et al., 2018) consists of 10,015
344
      dermatoscopic images of pigmented skin lesions.

      5.2.1    T HE C ONCEPT R EMOVAL
382
383   The intuition behind the The Concept Removal (Mahinpei et al., 2021) evaluation is that, if a concept
384   set is insufficient, the label prediction has to exploit additional information to fulfill the training
385   objective (e.g., achieving high accuracy) (Mahinpei et al., 2021). In that sense, if a CBM suffers
386   from information leakage, removing concepts that contribute most to the label prediction should not
387   notably undermine the CBM performance. Following this intuition, for each evaluated CBM, we start
388   by training it with the full concept set. Then, during the inference, we rank concepts based on their
389
      importance and gradually remove top-ranked concepts. We expect that the performance of CBMs
      which exhibit better resilience to information leakage should drop more quickly when more concepts
390
      are removed. Here, we use our constructed concept set for a fair comparison.
391
392   Setup. Sec. 5.1 shows that LaBo has the best performance among all our competitors. Therefore,
393   this section assesses the information leakage in LaBo and our method. We use CUB-Bird, given
394   the large number of distinct classes this dataset has. We also consider the Flower dataset (Nilsback
395
      & Zisserman, 2008); it is a phytology knowledge specific dataset with 102 visually close semantic
      classes, making it handy to quantify the information leakage problem.

      6     D ISCUSSION , L IMITATIONS , AND R ELATED W ORKS
481   CBM Development. CBMs have been prosperously developing in recent years w.r.t. different aspects.
482   Representative CBMs schemes include T-CAV (Kim et al., 2018), ACE (Ghorbani et al., 2019),
483   Completeness-aware CBM (Yeh et al., 2020), CEMs (Zarlenga et al., 2022), GlanceNets (Marconato
484   et al., 2022), and probabilistic CBMs (Kim et al., 2023). Effective training objectives of CBMs are
485   also proposed, such as in coop-CBMs (Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou, 2023). Several metrics have been
      designed to assess the concept prediction, such as Oracle Impurity Score (Zarlenga et al., 2023)


                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and Niche Impurity Score (Zarlenga et al., 2023). Some recent works also focus on optimizing the
487   intervention of CBMs (Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2023; Marcinkevičs et al., 2024).
488
489   Information Leakage. Previous works adopt hard CBMs to alleviate information leakage induced
490
      by concept probabilities. Since hard CBMs usually exhibit limited performance compared with soft
      CBMs, a “side channel” mechanism is implemented in hard CBMs. Side-channel CBMs (Havasi
491
      et al., 2022) use a residual connection to link the concept predictor and the label predictor, and pass
492
      additional information to the label predictor. Although hard CBM’s performance is improved, such
493   side channels compromise the interpretability and intervenability. Moreover, hard CBMs themselves
494   cannot alleviate information leakage due to irrelevant concepts (Mahinpei et al., 2021).
495
496
      Post-hoc Concept Conversion. P-CBM (Yuksekgonul et al., 2022) is the first post-hoc CBM that uses
      the CLIP embeddings to align images and concepts. P-CBM first projects an image embedding onto
497
      the concept subspace and then computes its similarities with different concepts. These similarities
498
      are adopted for label prediction. The label predictor in P-CBM is connected with image embeddings
499   via a residual connection. Label-free CBM (Oikarinen et al., 2023) is mostly similar to P-CBM,
500   but computes similarity between images and concepts using the dot product of their embeddings.
501   Label-free CBM is the first work that employs GPT models to generate textual concepts. LaBo (Yang
502   et al., 2023) builds semantic vectors with a large set of attributes from LLMs. It uses GPT-3 to
503   produce factual sentences about categories to form candidate concepts, and then employs a so-called
504   submodular utility to effectively explore potential bottlenecks that facilitate the identification of
505   distinctive information. Yan et al. (Yan et al., 2023) use concise and descriptive attributes extracted
506   from LLMs. Specifically, it adopts a learning-to-search method to extract a descriptive subset of
507
      attributes from LLMs by pruning the large attribute set.
508   Limitations and Mitigations. Concept annotation is the primary obstacle in developing CBMs.
509   Similar to prior post-hoc concept conversion, S UP CBM leverages LLM and CLIP to automatically
510   annotate concepts. Two limitations shared by all post-hoc methods are ay  iLLM’s inability to generate
511   concepts and by ithe incorrect similarity scores delivered by CLIP. Our current results over diverse
512   datasets show that ayiis not a concern for natural images; that said, when using S UP CBM for domains
      that are largely different from natural images, we suggest generating concepts via corresponding
      domain-specific LLMs. by  iis mitigated by our intervention matrix and the concept pooling mechanism.
513
514
      Note that our label prediction only uses relevant concepts for each label and the concept pooling only
515
      selects concepts of the top-k probabilities.

659   Concept Perturbation (Sinha et al., 2023) identifies irrelevant (i.e., leaked) information by checking
660   whether the label prediction result is unchanged when perturbing concept prediction results. If
661
      modifying some concepts does not change the predicted label, the modified concepts denote irrelevant
      information. (Sinha et al., 2023) proposes to find such cases (i.e., altered concept prediction but
662
      retained label prediction) via gradient-based optimization.
663
664   We implemented the Concept Perturbation metric based on PGD’s pipeline and updated the PGD’s
665   objective as simultaneously minimizing label prediction loss (i.e., retaining the label prediction) and
666   maximizing the concept prediction loss (i.e., differing the concept prediction). Since the ground truth
667
      concepts are unavailable, for each input, we record the maximal concept probability variation that
      does not change the original label prediction result. The maximal variations are represented in the
668
      form of L2 distance norm and averaged for all inputs.

697   C     I NTERVENTION
698
699   In this section, we choose to adopt the common intervention process following (Oikarinen et al.,
700   2023) that corrects a mispredicted concept for a specific sample. The intervention process allows
701   to determine which concepts should be used for the follow-up label prediction (e.g., removing a
      predicted concept if is not in the ground truth concepts).


                                                              13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Essentially, this process and its objective are consistent with our intervention matrix where a binary
703   matrix is employed to decide which concepts are used for predicting labels. The intervention matrix
704   in S UP CBM is a binary matrix where each (i, j)-th entry indicates whether the i-th concept is set
705   as involved in predicting the j-th class. The intervention matrix also does not have any trainable
706   parameters; its binary entries are set up by users.
707
      To perform intervention for an input x, users only need to accordingly update entries in the intervention
708
      matrix. For instance, setting the (i, j)-th entry as 1 can let the i-th concept be involved in predicting
709   the j-th label for x.
710
711   When ground truth concepts for the j-th class are available, to intervene in the label prediction of
712
      S UP CBM accordingly, users only need to set the corresponding entries (of the ground truth concepts)
      in the intervention matrix’s j-th column as 1 and let other entries in the j-th column be 0.
