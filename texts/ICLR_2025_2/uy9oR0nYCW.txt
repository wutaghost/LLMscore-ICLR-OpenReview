009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid proliferation of AI-manipulated or generated audio deepfakes poses se-
012            rious challenges to media integrity and election security. Current AI-driven detec-
013            tion solutions lack explainability and underperform in real-world settings. In this
014            paper, we introduce novel explainability methods for state-of-the-art transformer-
015            based audio deepfake detectors and open-source a novel benchmark for real-world
016
               generalizability. By narrowing the explainability gap between transformer-based
               audio deepfake detectors and traditional methods, our results not only build trust
017
               with human experts, but also pave the way for unlocking the potential of citizen
018
               intelligence to overcome the scalability issue in audio deepfake detection.

021   1   I NTRODUCTION
022
023   The rapid proliferation of AI-generated audio deepfakes poses a growing threat to media integrity,
024   personal security, and democratic processes, with critical implications for misinformation, fraud,
025   and election security. Although state-of-the-art detection solutions have demonstrated promising
026   results on benchmark datasets, they often fall short in real-world scenarios due to poor generaliza-
027
      tion and a lack of explainability. Current approaches, such as those used in ASVspoof and related
      competitions, focus heavily on detection performance within constrained environments, but their
028
      efficacy diminishes significantly when encountering diverse and unseen samples.
029
030   In this work, we highlight the limitations of existing deepfake detection methods and introduce an at-
031   tention roll-out mechanism that addresses these shortcomings by providing improved explainability
032   for transformer-based audio classifiers. Recent deepfake audio attacks, such as those used to dis-
033
      credit Marti Bartes in Mexico or to influence U.S. elections by impersonating President Joe Biden,
      emphasize the necessity for solutions that not only detect these manipulations but also offer trans-
034
      parent and interpretable explanations (Goodman, 2024; Staff, 2024). These would, in turn, foster
035
      trust with both experts and the general public.
036
037   To bridge the gap between controlled benchmark results and real-world applicability, we deliver a
038   novel benchmark that evaluates the generalization capabilities of deepfake audio classifiers by train-
039
      ing on the ASVspoof 5 dataset and testing on the FakeAVCeleb dataset. This benchmark provides
      a more realistic evaluation of model robustness, simulating conditions where the data distributions
040
      of training and testing are substantially different. Additionally, we compare and contrast various
041
      explainability methods, offering a conceptual contribution that defines key requirements for ex-
042   plainability in deepfake audio detection. Our findings not only reveal the strengths and weaknesses
043   of each approach but also lay the groundwork for future research.
044
045
      Through this comprehensive evaluation, we outline several open challenges that need to be addressed
      to improve generalization and explainability, thereby enhancing trust in deepfake detection systems.
046
      By establishing these benchmarks and defining conceptual requirements, we hope to catalyze future
047
      developments in this critical research area to effectively safeguard against the evolving threat of
048   audio deepfakes.
049
050   We list our novel contributions as follows:
051
             • A conceptual explainability framework for deepfake audio detection (Section 4).
052
             • Empirical evaluations of novel explainability methods for audio transformers (Section 5).
053
             • A generalizability benchmark for deepfake audio classifiers (Section 6).

      Traditional Machine Learning Approaches The early days of deepfake audio detection were
057
      dominated by traditional classifiers, such as support vector machines (SVM) and Gaussian mixture
058   models (GMM), and traditional signal processing features. These works generally use subsets of
059   the various hand-crafted features described above, and they generally perform well with academic
060   datasets, in which the distributions of features between real and deepfake audio are relatively eas-
061   ily separable. Observing that these models typically do not generalize well when presented with
062   deepfake audio from unseen distributions, Zhang et al. (2021) propose using an SVM to learn a
063   tight boundary around the features of real audio by only training on real audio (Zhang et al., 2021).
064   This method outperforms most other methods on the ASVspoof 2019 dataset, but it is unlikely to
065   perform as well when the true audio varies more widely (e.g., non-English speech, speakers with
066
      accents, non-adult speakers, etc.).
067   Recent work also suggests that using an ensemble of gradient-boosting decision trees (GBDT) may
068   be more robust to unseen data as well as boast faster inference times than both SVM and GMM (Bird
069   & Lotfi, 2023). In a 2023 work by Bird & Lotfi (2023), the authors demonstrate the power of
070   the GBDT, reporting accuracies of 99.3% on the DeepVoice dataset and inference times of 0.004
071   seconds for 1 second of input speech (Bird & Lotfi, 2023). In their work Real-Time Detection of
072
      AI-Generated Speech for Deepfake Voice Conversion, they also explore features importances and
      the statistical characterizations of real and deepfake audio. Another recent work by Togootogtokh
073
      & Klasen (2024) employs a GBDT for deepfake audio classification task with a custom dataset
074
      comprised of true samples from the LJ Speech Dataset and deepfake samples generated with various
075   HuggingFace TTS models (Togootogtokh & Klasen, 2024).
076
077
      Self-Supervised Embedding-Based Classifiers A few recent works use self-supervised embed-
078
      ding features as the basis of their classification algorithms, most of which use Wav2Vec features (Shi
079   & Yamagishi, 2021). Tak et al. (2022) fine-tune a transformer model with a Wav2Vec front-end
080   and report the lowest equal error rates for both the ASVspoof 2021 Logical Access and Deepfake
081   databases. Xie et al. (2021) use Wav2Vec features as input to a Siamese neural network that they
082   train to distinguish whether the speech samples in a pair belong to the same category. This work
083   reported state-of-the-art results on the ASVspoof 2019 dataset (Chen et al., 2022). Some other re-
084   cent works use HuBERT features, and Wang & Yamagishi (2022) compare Wav2Vec-, XLS-R-, and
085   HuBERT-based features (Yi et al., 2023). Most recently, Le et al. (2024) combine AST features
086   with a GBDT ensemble to detect deepfake audio, which they plan to use for a continuous learning
087
      approach.
088
089   Explainability for Audio The literature for audio explainability is limited (Akman & Schuller,
090   2024). Though general explainability methods such as LIME and SHAP can also be used for mod-
091   els trained on audio data, very few examples of attempts for audio explainability exist in the liter-
092
      ature (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017). Of those exceptions, Yanchenko et al. (2021)
      measure the similarity of deep features to hand-crafted features and attempt explain deep convolu-
093
      tional features as they relate to traditional signal processing ones. Most recently, Becker et al. (2024)
094
      introduced AudioMNIST, a novel audio dataset consisting of 30 000 audio samples of spoken digits,
095   and proposed using Layer-wise Relevance Propagation (LRP) to explain neural network classifica-
096   tions. They also investigate using a combination of visual and aural explanations, and find aural
097   explanations promising if well-designed.
098
      For additional related work on in-painted deepfake audio and convolutional neural network (CNN)
099
      based approaches to deepfake audio detection, refer to Appendix A

      Signal Processing Features Traditional methods typically rely on “hand-crafted” features, which
109   are not learned by neural networks but calculated with standard signal processing methods.
110
111   Mel-Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCCs) are a set of 10-20 features that capture the timbre
112
      of audio samples using the perceptual Mel scale, reflecting how humans perceive pitch. Computing
      MFCCs involves applying a pre-emphasis filter, dividing the signal into 20 ms frames, applying
113
      a Hann window, and performing a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). The resulting power spectrum
114
      is passed through a Mel-scale filter bank, then logarithmically transformed, and a Discrete Cosine
115   Transform (DCT) is applied to generate MFCCs, providing a compact representation of spectral
116   properties. Other spectral features include spectral centroid (indicating ”brightness” by representing
117   the center of spectral mass), bandwidth (spread around the centroid), and roll-off (frequency below
118   which 85% of spectral energy is contained). These features are useful for tasks like music genre
119   classification and speech analysis.
120
      Chroma features capture harmonic content by mapping the spectrum to twelve pitch classes (C, C#,
121   D, etc.) and are invariant to timbre changes, making them ideal for analyzing musical elements.
122   Zero Crossing Rate (ZCR) measures the rate of sign changes in an audio signal, aiding in pitch and
123   speech detection, while Root Mean Square (RMS) captures energy or loudness, offering a measure
124   of signal strength.
125
126   Gradient Boosting Decision Trees Gradient boosting decision trees (GBDT) combine three core
127   concepts in traditional machine learning: ensemble learning, boosting, and gradient descent. As
128   an ensemble method, GBDT combine the predictions of several weak learners–typically decision
129   trees–to produce a stronger overall prediction. The boosting aspect of GBDT means that the model
130   is constructed sequentially, such that at each iteration a new weak learner is added to correct for
131
      the previous learners’ mistakes. Finally, the gradient aspect of GBDT reflects the optimization
      technique used to find the best fit for each new weaker learner added to the ensemble.
132
133   At initialization, the GBDT is an ensemble containing a single weak learner that makes a prediction.
134   For binary classification, the model is initialized with a constant value F̂0 (x), the log-odds of the
135   positive class. At each iteration, until the maximum number of weak learners permitted in the
136   ensemble is reached, the pseudo-residuals are calculated, which are the negative gradients of the
137   loss function:                     "                      #

                               rim = −                            = yi − p̂m−1 (xi ),                    (1)

183

184   raw input data and the learned attention between those tokens. For natural language tasks, tokens
185   typically represent individual words. For image tasks, tokens are typically pixel patches. For audio
186   tasks, there are a variety of approaches. Here, we introduce two popular mechanisms for generating
187   input embeddings from audio data.
188
189   Self-Supervised Audio Features One of the most popular feature generators is Wav2Vec 2.0,
190   produced and published by Meta in 2019 (Schneider et al., 2019). Wav2Vec uses a 7-layer CNN
191   generate latent feature encodings, which are then put into a quantization module to make the final
192   tokens which will be fed to the transformer (Schneider et al., 2019). As speech is continuous,
193   Wav2Vec strives to automatically infer discrete speech units with the quantization module, such that
194
      tokens can be formulated as they would be in natural language, representing complete but discrete
      data units (Schneider et al., 2019).
195
196   In contrast, the Audio Spectrogram Transformer (AST), which was the first to move away from
197   traditional convolutional neural network approaches, simplifies audio token generation (Gong et al.,
198   2021).

201   dimensional Mel features. The resultant 128 × 10t spec-
202   trogram is then used as input to the AST. The spectrogram
203   is then split into a sequence of N 16 × 16 patches, with
204   overlap in both time and frequency dimensions. Each
205   16 × 16 patch is flattened into a single-dimensional patch
206
      of size 768 with a linear layer.
207   Once the audio is formatted in this way, the AST feeds
208   the input sequence to a Vision Transformer (ViT), an im-
209   age transformer model trained on ImageNet (Gong et al.,
210   2021). This approach essentially translates the audio sig-
211   nal into an image and then uses a transformer pre-trained

      3.3   E XPLAINABILITY AND I NTERPRETABILITY
217
218
219   Though explainability and interpretability are often used interchangeably, we distinguish between
220
      them. Interpretability emphasizes transparency and clarity, focusing on making the internal mecha-
      nisms of a model comprehensible, such as understanding the coefficients of a linear regression model
221
      or the structure of a decision tree (Rudin, 2019). In contrast, explainability goes beyond understand-
222
      ing the internal, mathematical mechanism and should provide explanations of a model’s predictions
223   in a human-understandable way, even if the model itself is a complex “black box” (Rudin, 2019).
224
225   Explainability oftens involves using post-hoc methods, LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic
226
      Explanations) or SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), to approximate and elucidate the model’s
      decisions without revealing the exact inner workings (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017).
227
      Importantly, the interpretability of a model can contribute to its explainability, but a model’s being
228
      interpretable does not necessarily imply that it is explainable.
229
230
231
232   Explainability for GBDT A key advantage of traditional methods is that they are interpretable.
233   With a gradient boosting decision trees, or any other tree-based ensemble method, feature impor-
234   tances can be calculated. As described in Algorithm 1, feature importances are calculated by mea-
235
      suring the model’s change in performance after each feature is permuted (Pedregosa et al., 2011). To
      stabilize results, we permute each feature multiple times and use the mean and standard deviation of
236
      each feature’s importance.
237
238   However, the importance of a given feature may be obscured by the permutation feature importance
239   algorithm if multiple features are multicollinear, as is the case for the audio signal features described
240   in the previous section. Intuitively, permuting one feature will have little impact on the model’s
241
      performance if the same, or very similar, information is available to the model through another
      non-permuted feature. To combat this issue, we perform hierarchical clustering on the Spearman
242
      rank-order correlations between features and keep a single feature from each cluster. This way,
243
      when a feature is permuted, there should be no other non-permuted feature containing duplicate
244   information.
245
246
247
248   Explainability for Transformer Models A challenge of working with transformer methods is the
249   lack of interpretability. Though they boast much better performance than traditional methods, again
250
      see Appendix D, their output is of a “black-box” nature. In order to facilitate citizen intelligence,
      detection methods must deliver human interpretable explanations that are sample-specific, such that
251
      the explanation is not invariant to the input sample; time-specific, such that the explanation includes
252
      specific timestamps that localize the distinguishing features; and feature-specific, such that specific
253   aspects like unusual noise or errant formants can be identified as unnatural.

276   4.1   O CCLUSION
277
278   Occlusion is a technique used for vision model explainability, particularly with deep learning models
279   that might otherwise be considered “black boxes”. The core idea is to iteratively occlude, or block
280   from view, parts of the input data, measure how the model’s prediction changes, and, ideally, identify
281   which parts of the input data are most important.
282   Consider some input X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ], where X represents the original input and each xi
283   represents a subsection (perhaps a pixel, patch, or token) in X, and some model f . First, we generate
284   a baseline prediction ŷ = f (X), which will serve as the point of comparison. Then, we iteratively
285   mask each xi from the input, such that:
286                                              (i)
                                              Xoccluded = X ⊙ Mi ,                                        (8)
287
288   where Mi is a mask that occludes the i-th subregion of the input and ⊙ represents element-wise
289   multiplication. After this operation, the occluded region will be replaced with some specified value
290   (e.g., 0, 1, or a mean of the feature across all samples). For each occluded input, the model makes a
291
      new prediction given by:
                                                             (i)
292                                             ŷ (i) = f (Xoccluded ).                                (9)
293
      The intuition is that if a change in the model’s prediction is observed when a region is occluded, that
294
      region is likely important. After occluding different regions and observing changes in the model’s
295   behavior, the results can be visualized in a heatmap. This method has been used for understanding
296   importance in vision data, but we introduce it here for audio data. We treat the Mel-spectrogram
297   representation of each audio sample as an image and occlude sections of the spectrogram to deter-
298   mine which parts of each audio sample are most important for the transformer’s classification. This
299   method delivers on all three aspects of sufficient explainability defined in 3.3.

357   5.1   E XPLAINABILITY FOR GBDT
358
359   A well-noted advantage of ensembles of decision trees, as the GBDT is, is the ability to calculate
360   feature importances. In their recent deepfake audio classification with GBDT work, Bird et al.
361   report feature importances and draw meaning from them Bird & Lotfi (2023). Here, we mimic their
362
      approach to explain the behavior of the GBDT and to isolate some aspect of the audio sample as a
      signature of its deepfake classification.
363
364   We compare the GBDT feature importances, calculated with the permutation importance algorithm,
365   for the models trained with 1.0, 3.0, and 6.0-second audio samples (Pedregosa et al., 2011). The

367   3.0-second classifiers can be found in subsection E.1. As seen in Figure 2, the second MFCC, fourth
368
      MFCC, and RMS features are the most influential in the GBDT’s decision-making. Recall that the
      RMS feature is most closely associated with the loudness of an audio sample. We find the high
369

404   filterbank, the second MFCC captures low-frequency details, such as overall spectral slope and
405
      formant information. Each formant corresponds to a resonance in the vocal tract, and it is intuitive
      that deepfake audio would have anomalous resonance. While this explanation points to a potential
406
      source of inherent distinction between real and deepfake audio, it only provides an explanation as
407
      to what the model is attentive to in general rather than sample-level specificity. While the GBDT is
408   interpretable, we find that it is not sufficiently explainable to be useful to a non-technical audience.

413   suited to visualization. We perform occlusion with box size (200, 50) and stride size (100, 25).
414   Importance is measured by the magnitude of change in the predicted probability of the sample being
415   in the positive class when a section is occluded.
416
      As shown in Figure 4, the importance is greatest for the padded regions–regions that theoretically
417   contain no predictive information. The audio samples shown in Figure 4 are of length 6.0-seconds,
418   but we observe this phenomenon when calculating importance by occlusion for all sample lengths.
419   For each sample length, the most important regions (as measured by the occlusion method) are
420   always placed at the beginning of the padded region. This result is obviously unhelpful in explaining
421   the model’s decision-making, but it is suggestive of a phenomenon posited by Wu et al. (2021),

423   consistent, such that the weight information there is always propagated (Wu et al., 2021).

516   7   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this paper, we address the critical issue of audio deepfake detection by introducing a novel bench-
519   mark that evaluates the generalization capabilities of state-of-the-art transformer-based models. Our
520   experiments, conducted using the ASVspoof 5 and FakeAVCeleb datasets, demonstrate that cur-
521
      rent detection solutions often struggle with generalizability and lack sufficient explainability, espe-
      cially in real-world scenarios. By incorporating explainability methods such as attention roll-out
522
      and occlusion, we highlight the strengths and limitations of these approaches, providing a clearer
523
      understanding of model decisions.
524
525   The attention roll-out mechanism, in particular, shows promise in visualizing transformer-based
526   models’ attention across multiple layers, enabling a more transparent analysis of how decisions
527
      are made. Our results indicate that while transformer models like Wav2Vec and AST outperform
      traditional models on unseen data, there remains a significant gap in their ability to provide human-
528
      understandable explanations. This finding underscores the need for more research in this area, espe-
529
      cially to improve interpretability for non-technical users and domain experts.
530
531   Looking ahead, future work should focus on refining explainability methods for transformer-based
532   models and developing new benchmarks that further challenge the robustness and interpretability of
533
      deepfake detectors. Additionally, understanding and mitigating the effect of data augmentations such
      as compression and re-recording will be essential for creating more resilient models. A limitation of
534
      this study is the reliance on only two datasets, which may not capture the full range of manipulations
535
      seen in real-world deepfake audio. Further limitations are addressed in Appendix F. Moreover, the
536   proposed explainability methods are still in their infancy and may not yet offer insights that are as
537   intuitive to non-technical users. By addressing these challenges, we can move closer to building
538   reliable, trustworthy audio deepfake detection systems that are ready for real-world deployment.

      A.1    CNN-BASED C LASSIFIERS
705
706   The most popular architecture for deepfake audio detection is by far the convolutional neural net-
707   work (CNN) based classifier Dixit et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2020); Lavrentyeva et al. (2019); Zeinali
708   et al. (2019); Dinkel et al. (2017). CNN classifiers have been historically favored for their ability
709   to combine spatial and temporal information through convolution. The most successful of these is
710   called Light Convolutional Neural Network (LCNN) and was proposed by Wu et al. in 2020 Wu et al.
711   (2020). LCNN consists of convolutional and max-pooling layers with Max-Feature-Max (MFM)
712
      activation. Wu et al. choose the MFM activation function instead of the arguably standard ReLU
      function because they observe that the MFM learns more compact features than the sparse high-
713
      dimensional ones learned with ReLU Wu et al. (2020). This distinction is what makes their CNN
714
      “light”. The LCNN has been incredibly successful in recent ASVspoof and ADD competitions;
715   it was the best system at ASVspoof 2017 and continued to be the best system in one of the sub-
716   tasks of ASVspoof 2019. Another successful CNN architecture, proposed by Dinkel et al., uses
717   raw waveforms as input to a convolutional long short-term neural network. Their model combines
718   time-convolving layers with frequency-convolving layers to “reduce time and spectral variations”
719   with long-term temporal memory layers to capture longer-term temporal relationships Dixit et al.
720   (2023); Dinkel et al. (2017). A variety of other CNN architectures have been proposed, but all of
721   them generalize poorly to unseen attacks Dixit et al. (2023).
722
723   A.2    D EEPFAKE AUDIO I N -PAINTING
724
725
      Another relevant research area is that of deepfake audio in-painting detection. In-painting refers
      to the practice of mixing real and fake audio such that only small portions of an audio sample
726
      are actually manipulated. Detecting deepfake audio in-painting requires not only identifying that a
727
      sample contains some corrupted audio but also identifying the timestamps at which the corruption
728   begins and ends. Xie et al. (2024) propose a framework called EAT, which incorporates a ResNet,
729   a two-layer transformer encoder, a single-layer bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory network
730   (LSTM), and a final classification layer.
731
      The EAT framework achieves an F1 score of 98% when classifying segments at 20 millisecond
732
      resolution Xie et al. (2024). However, Xie et al. only evaluate their method on a custom dataset,
733
      do not attempt to evaluate their architecture’s performance on any of the standard deepfake audio
734   benchmarks, and focus primarily on deepfake environmental sounds and background audio Xie
735   et al. (2024). A slightly older but broader work, from 2022, that investigates in-painted deepfake
736   audio is that of Cai et al. (2022) in Waveform Boundary Detection for Partially Spoofed Audio.
737   Cai et al. (2022) use a combination of Wav2Vec and MFCC features as their input to a series of
738   single-dimensional CNNs, a transformer encoder, a bidirectional long short-term memory network
739   (BiLSTM), and a final linear layer for classification (Cai et al., 2022). With their method, they
740   achieve the best performance in the locating manipulated clips task of the ADD 2022 challenge.
741
742   B     DATA S OURCES
743
744   We employ a variety of publicly available datasets to conduct our experiments. We also craft custom
745   variations in order to better mimic real-world use cases and challenges.
746
747
      The majority of our experiments are conducted with the ASVspoof 5 dataset. The ASVspoof5
      dataset is a state-of-the-art dataset containing eighteen different varieties of deepfake audio as well as
748
      true speech samples (Wang et al., 2024). Each sample is labelled with a classification as “bonafide”
749
      or “spoof”. If the sample is spoofed, the attack method that was used to generate the sample is spec-
750   ified. The dataset, which was released in June 2024, contains 182 357 train samples and 142 134 test
751   samples. Each deepfake (or spoofed) audio sample is generated with a novel VC or TTS method,
752   which were trained on two English-language datasets. The final deepfake samples are made using
753   the English-subset of the Multilingual LibriSpeech (MLS) dataset (Wang et al., 2024). The 18 dif-
754   ferent attack types included in this dataset make it the most attack diverse of the datasets we consider
755   in this study. We take this dataset to represent the state-of-the-art in deepfake audio generation and,
      in subsequent sections, refer to it simply as the ASVspoof dataset.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      The audio samples provided by ASVspoof are clean. The ASVspoof bonafide samples were created
757   in recording studios with high quality microphones; The ASVspoof fake samples were generated
758   with TTS algorithms that add no additional noise. When deepfake audio is circulated over the social
759   media, it undergoes compression–often multiple times. In order to identify fake audio downstream,
760   our models must be robust to this kind of distribution modification. Additionally, bad actors will try
761   to obscure as much as possible that an audio sample has been faked. A common approach to obscure
762   fake audio is to play to audio aloud in a room and re-record the audio before disseminating it. We
763   create two additional datasets from the ASVspoof dataset for an additional challenge.
764
765   Compressed ASVspoof To tackle the issue of compression, we write all of the audio samples in
766   the ASVspoof dataset to the lossy MP3 format (from the lossless FLAC format) with a bitrate of
767   128k. This shrinks each file, on average, to 33.7% of its original size.
768
769   Rerecorded ASVspoof To tackle the issue of re-recording, we re-recorded audio samples in the
770   ASVspoof dataset by playing them aloud on a 2021 MacBook Pro in a large, closed stone-walled
771   room while simultaneously recording.
772   We also refer to FakeAVCeleb as a well-known benchmark in deepfake audio detection. The
773   FakeAVCeleb dataset is a standard in the deepfake audio detection repertoire, but it is now slightly
774   out-of-date, as it was released in 2021 (Khalid et al., 2021). It contains both deepfake audio and
775   video, but we use only the audio component in this study. The audio subset of the FakeAVCeleb
776   dataset contains 9 712 real audio samples and 10 843 deepfake audio samples. The language of each
777   audio sample is English, but FakeAVCeleb includes balanced classes of male and female speakers
778
      as well as speakers who self-identify as African, East Asian, South Asian, Caucasian (American),
      and Caucasian (European) (Khalid et al., 2021). This makes FakeAVCeleb the most linguistically
779
      diverse of the datasets considered in this study.

788   C.1   GBDT
789
790   Given its recent popularity for deepfake audio classification tasks Bird & Lotfi (2023); Togootogtokh
791   & Klasen (2024), we use the gradient boosting classifier as the baseline in this study. The features
792   for these tests are the hand-crafted, signal processing features described in Section 3. We use a total
793   of 37 features, which include 20 MFCC features, 12 chroma features, spectral bandwidth, spectral
794
      roll-off, spectral centroid, ZCR, and RMS. The features for each audio sample are calculated by
      averaging each feature’s values across all frames. For example, the final ZCR is calculated by
795
      averaging the ZCR for each frame across the length of the entire audio sample.
796
797   To evaluate best possible performance with the GBDT, we do a hyperparameter search across
798   maximum tree depths and maximum number of estimators. We consider maximum tree depth in
799   {3, 8, 10, 15, 25} and number of estimators in {10, 100, 200, 400, 600}. We do our hyperparameter
800
      search with the ASVspoof dataset. We conduct tests with 10 000 total data samples, 33% of which
      are held out as a test set. As GBDTs are highly sensitive to unbalanced data classes, we balance
801
      classes before conducting experiments with the GBDT.
802
803   As shown in Table 2, the classification accuracy is highest with shorter decision trees and more
804   estimators when trained with 3-second audio samples. The performance stabilizes with 400 or more
805   estimators, so we take 400 estimators and maximum depth of 8 as our optimal hyperparameter
806
      setting. We find that this configuration also offers best performance for other audio sample lengths.
      We observe that performance improves with the number of estimators, but we also see diminishing
807
      returns after 400 estimators. We conclude that 400 estimators and a maximum tree depth of 8 are
808
      the optimal hyperparameters for this training scenario.

       E.3    ATTACK C LASSIFICATION
1189
1190   Another potentially useful piece of information is what attack likely generated a given deepfake
1191   audio segment. Though this type of “explainability” does not give insight into a model’s decision-
1192   making, it may provide a clue to the origin of a deepfake audio sample–something likely very impor-
1193   tant to a journalist or member of law enforcement. We therefore evaluate the abilities of the GBDT
1194   and transformer methods to identity which attack, if any, was used to generate an audio sample.
1195   Using 6.0-second audio samples, the GBDT achieved an overall accuracy of 51.9% with average
1196
       precision of 53.0% and average recall of 52.1% over all 18 classes, with relatively stable perfor-
       mance across the attack types. The Wav2Vec model achieved overall evaluation accuracy of 91.8%
1197
       with average precision of 91.7% and average recall of 91.8% across the 18 classes. The AST model
1198
       performed similarly, achieving overall evaluation accuracy of 91.1%, average precision of 91.1%,
1199   as well as average recall of 91.1%. Though the models are quite successful at this task, previous
1200   exposure to deepfake audio generated with each attack is essential. It is not clear how well a model
1201   could detect an out-of-distribution sample.

       F     L IMITATIONS
1205
       While this study introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating the generalization capabilities of state-
1206
       of-the-art transformer-based models, there are several limitations that need to be addressed to ad-
1207   vance the field of audio deepfake detection. First, the reliance on only two datasets, ASVspoof 5 and
1208   FakeAVCeleb, limits the scope of our evaluation. These datasets, while diverse, may not encompass
1209   the full range of manipulations, recording conditions, and spoofing techniques found in real-world
1210   scenarios. This constraint may lead to an overestimation of model robustness and underrepresen-
1211   tation of other spoofing methods. Expanding the benchmark to include more diverse datasets with
1212   a wider variety of deepfake techniques would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of model
1213   performance and generalization.
1214   Second, the explainability methods employed, such as attention roll-out and occlusion, are still
1215   limited in their ability to provide intuitive and human-understandable explanations, particularly for
1216   non-technical audiences. While these methods help visualize and highlight the regions of the in-
1217   put that influence the model’s decisions, they do not yet offer a complete picture of why a model
1218   might fail on specific instances or how it generalizes across domains. Moreover, the explainability
1219   results may be sensitive to changes in model architecture and hyperparameters, which could lead to
1220   inconsistencies in interpretation.
1221   Lastly, while we explore the generalization capabilities of the proposed models using cross-dataset
1222   evaluation, the impact of various environmental factors such as background noise, speaker variabil-
1223   ity, and language differences were not explicitly analyzed. This could affect the real-world applica-
1224   bility of the models, especially in diverse and dynamic environments. Further research should focus
1225   on robustness testing under varying conditions and on creating synthetic data to simulate potential
1226   challenges encountered during real-world deployment.
