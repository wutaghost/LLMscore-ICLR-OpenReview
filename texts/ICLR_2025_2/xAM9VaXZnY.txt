009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning on graphs has recently found extensive applications across do-
012            mains. However, the commonly used Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs)
013            suffer from limited expressive power and struggle to capture long-range depen-
014            dencies. Graph transformers offer a strong alternative due to their global attention
015            mechanism, but they come with great computational overheads, especially for large
016
               graphs. In recent years, State Space Models (SSMs) have emerged as a compelling
               approach to replace full attention in transformers to model sequential data. It blends
017
               the strengths of RNNs and CNNs, offering a) efficient computation, b) the ability
018
               to capture long-range dependencies, and c) good generalization across sequences
019            of various lengths. However, extending SSMs to graph-structured data presents
020            unique challenges due to the lack of canonical node ordering in graphs. In this
021            work, we propose Graph State Space Convolution (GSSC) as a principled extension
022            of SSMs to graph-structured data. By leveraging global permutation-equivariant
023            set aggregation and factorizable graph kernels that rely on relative node distances
024            as the convolution kernels, GSSC preserves all three advantages of SSMs. We
025            demonstrate the provably stronger expressiveness of GSSC than MPNNs in count-
026            ing graph substructures and show its effectiveness across 11 real-world, widely
027
               used benchmark datasets. GSSC achieves the best results on 6 out of 11 datasets
               with all significant improvements compared to the state-of-the-art baselines and
028
               second-best results on the other 5 datasets. Our findings highlight the potential of
029
               GSSC as a powerful and scalable model for graph machine learning. Our code is
030            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GSSC-5ED8.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Machine learning for graph-structured data has numerous applications in molecular graphs (Duvenaud
036   et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2021), drug discovery (Xiong et al., 2021; Stokes et al., 2020), and social
037   networks (Fan et al., 2019; Guo & Wang, 2020). In recent years, Message Passing Neural Networks
038   (MPNNs) have been arguably the most popular neural architecture for graphs (Kipf & Welling, 2016;
039
      Fung et al., 2021; Veličković et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2018; Corso et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020), but
      they also suffer from many limitations, including restricted expressive power (Xu et al., 2018; Morris
040
      et al., 2019), over-squashing (Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Topping et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2023), and
041
      over-smoothing (Rusch et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020a; Keriven, 2022). These limitations could harm
042   the models’ performance. For example, MPNNs cannot capture long-range dependencies (Dwivedi
043   et al., 2022) or detect subgraphs like cycles that are important in forming ring systems of molecular
044   graphs (Chen et al., 2020b).
045
      Adapted from the vanilla transformer in sequence modeling (Vaswani et al., 2017), graph transformers
046
      have attracted growing research interests because they may alleviate these fundamental limitations of
047
      MPNNs (Kreuzer et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Rampášek et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022a; Dwivedi
048   & Bresson, 2020). By attending to all nodes in the graph, graph transformers are inherently able to
049   capture long-range dependencies. However, the global attention mechanism ignores graph structures
050   and thus requires incorporating positional encodings (PEs) of nodes (Rampášek et al., 2022) that
051   encode graph structural information. For example, the information of relative distance between nodes
052   has been leveraged in attention computation (Li et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Ying et al., 2021).
053   Moreover, the full attention computation scales quadratically in terms of the length of the sequence
      or the number of nodes in the graph. This computational challenge motivates the study of linear-time

      transformers by incorporating techniques such as low-rank (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Wang et al.,
055   2020; Child et al., 2019; Choromanski et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023), sparse approximations (Indyk
056   & Motwani, 1998; Kitaev et al., 2020; Daras et al., 2020; Zandieh et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023),
057   or Taylor expansion (Arora et al., 2024) of the attention matrix. Some specific designs of scalable
058   transformers for large-scale graphs are also proposed (Wu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022b; Shirzad
059   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). Nevertheless, none of these variants
060   have been proven to be consistently effective across different domains (Miao et al., 2024).
061   State Space Models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2021a;b; Gu & Dao, 2023) have recently demonstrated
062   promising potentials for sequence modeling. Adapted from the classic state space model (Kalman,
063   1960), SSMs can be seen as a hybrid of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and convolutional neural
064   networks (CNNs). It is a temporal convolution that preserves translation invariance and thus allows
065   good generalization to sequences longer than those used for training. Meanwhile, this class of models
066   has been shown to capture long-range dependencies both theoretically and empirically (Gu et al.,
067   2020; Tay et al., 2020; Gu et al., 2021b;a). Finally, it can be efficiently computed in linear or
068   near-linear time via the recurrence mode or the parallelizable operations. These advantages make
069
      the SSM a strong candidate as an alternative to transformers (Mehta et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Fu
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024).
070
071   Given the great potential of SSMs, there is increasing interest in generalizing them for graphs as
072   an alternative to graph transformers (Wang et al., 2024a; Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024). The main
073   technical challenge is that SSMs are defined on sequences that are ordered and causal, i.e., have a
074   linear structure. Yet, graphs have complex topology, and no canonical node ordering can be found.
075
      Naive tokenization (e.g., sorting nodes into a sequence in some ways) breaks the inductive bias -
      permutation symmetry - of graphs, which consequently cannot faithfully represent graph topology,
076
      and may suffer from poor generalization.
077
078   In this study, we go back to the fundamental question of how to build SSMs for graph data. Instead
079   of simply tokenizing graphs and directly applying existing SSMs for sequences (which may break
080   the symmetry), we argue that principled graph SSMs should inherit the advantages of SSMs in
081
      capturing long-range dependencies and being efficient. Simultaneously, they should also preserve the
      permutation symmetry of graphs to achieve good generalization. With this goal, in this work:
082
083       • We identify that the key components enabling SSMs for sequences to be long-range, efficient, and
084         well-generalized to longer sequences, is the use of a global, factorizable, and translation-invariant
085
            kernel that depends on relative distances between tokens. This relative-distance kernel can be
            factorized into the product of absolute positions, crucial to achieving linear-time complexity.
086
087       • This observation motivates us to design Graph State Space Convolution (GSSC) in the following
088         way: (1) it leverages a global permutation equivariant set aggregation that incorporates all nodes
089
            in the graph; (2) the aggregation weights of set elements rely on relative distances between nodes
            on the graph, which can be factorized into the "absolute positions" of the corresponding nodes,
090
            i.e., the PEs of nodes. By design, the resulting GSSC is inherently permutation equivariant,
091
            long-range, and linear-time. Besides, we also demonstrate that GSSC is more powerful than
092         MPNNs and can provably count at least 4-paths and 4-cycles.
093
          • Empirically, our experiments demonstrate the high expressivity of GSSC via graph substructure
094
            counting and validate its capability of capturing long-range dependencies on Long Range Graph
095
            Benchmark (Dwivedi et al., 2022). Results on 10 real-world, widely used graph machine learning
096         benchmark datasets (Hu et al., 2020; Dwivedi et al., 2023; 2022) also show the consistently
097         superior performance of GSSC, where GSSC achieves best results on 7 out of 10 datasets with all
098         significant improvements compared to the state-of-the-art baselines and second-best results on
099         the other 3 datasets. Moreover, it has much better scalability than the standard graph transformers
100         in terms of training and inference time.

      3.2    E XTENSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
247   Incorporating Edge Features. SSMs (and the proposed GSSC) do not have a natural way to
248   incorporate token-pairwise (edge) features. To address this inherent limitation, SSMs (and their graph
249   extension GSSC) need to be paired with modules that can incorporate token-pairwise (edge) features,
250   such as MPNNs adopted in previous graph Mambas (Wang et al., 2024a; Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024)
251   and graph transformers (Rampášek et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022a). SSMs and MPNNs complement
252   either side by capturing global dependence via SSMs and edge features via MPNNs. This can be
253   validated by the significant performance boost compared with using either module alone in practice.
254   Selection Mechanism in SSMs. It is known that SSMs lack of selection mechanism, i.e., the kernel
255   Āi−j is only a function of positions i, j and does not rely on the feature of tokens (Gu & Dao,

283   Laplacian eigendecomposition as preprocessing. Although finding all eigenvectors and eigenvalues
284   can be costly, (1) in real experiments, such preprocessing can be done efficiently, which may only
285   occupy less than 10% wall-clock time of the entire training process (see Sec. 5.3 for quantitative
286
      results on real-world datasets); (2) we only need top-d eigenvectors and eigenvalues, which can be
      efficiently found by Lanczos methods (Paige, 1972; Lanczos, 1950) with complexity O(Ed) (E is
287
      the number of edges) or similarly by LOBPCG methods (Knyazev, 2001); (3) one can also adopt
288
      random Fourier feature-based approaches to fast approximate those kernels’ factorization (Smola &
289   Kondor, 2003; Choromanski, 2023; Reid et al., 2024) without precisely computing the eigenvectors.

293   We measure the expressivity of GSSC via graph distinguishing ability compared to WL test hierarchy.
294
      Proposition 3.2. GSSC is strictly more powerful than WL test and not more powerful than 3-WL test.
295
296   We can also characterize the expressivity of GSSC via the ability to count graph substructures.
297   Proposition 3.3 states that GSSC can count at least 3-paths and 3-cycles, which is strictly stronger
298   than the counting power of MPNNs and GSC (both cannot count cycles (Chen et al., 2020b; Wang &
299   Zhang, 2022)). Furthermore, if we introduce the selection mechanism Eq. 6, it can provably count at
300   least 4-paths and 4-cycles.
301   Proposition 3.3 (Counting paths and cycles). Graph state space convolution Eq. 4 can at least count
302   number of 3-paths and 3-cycles. With selection mechanism Eq. 6, it can at least count number of
303   4-paths and 4-cycles. Here “counting” means a node representation can express the number of paths
304   starting at the node or the number of cycles involving the node.

306   4     R ELATED W ORKS
307
308   Linear Graph Transformers. Graph transformers leverage attention mechanism (Vaswani et al.,
309   2017; Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022a) that
310   can attend to all nodes in a graph, but yield quadratic complexity w.r.t. graph size. To reduce the
311   complexity, Rampášek et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022; 2023) adopt linear attention techniques, i.e.,
312   factorizing the attention kernel into products of Random Positive Features (Choromanski et al., 2020).
313   Wu et al. (2024); Deng et al. (2024) replaces attention by inner products of learnable features, which do
314   not leverage any positional encodings but use extra message passing layers to encode graph topology.
315
      For comparison, GSSC (Eq. 4) derived from SSMs is based on a factorization of convolution kernels,
      which shares some similar spirits. In fact, a recent work points out some equivalences between linear
316
      transformers and SSMs (Dao & Gu, 2024). However, GSSC is technically different from linear
317
      graph transformers. The latter uses random features to approximate the specific attention kernel
318   (softmax+inner product), and generally the node features and positional encodings are both used in
319   constructing the random features. In contrast, GSSC adopts positional encodings exclusively (may
320   also includes node features if using selective mechanism Eq. 6) to construct convolution kernels in a
321   learnable and stable way, and it is not restricted to the attention kernel. Finally, there are other works
322   that use methods other than attention factorization: Shirzad et al. (2023) leverages virtual global
323   nodes and expander graphs to perform sparse attention; Kong et al. (2023) applies a projection matrix
      to reduce the graph size factor n to a lower dimension k. Both are very different from GSSC.

      State Space Models (SSMs). Classic SSMs (Kalman, 1960; Hyndman et al., 2008; Durbin &
337   Koopman, 2012) describe the evolution of state variables over time using first-order differential
338   equations or difference equations, providing a unified framework for time series modeling. Similar
339   to RNNs (Pascanu, 2013; Graves, 2013; Sutskever et al., 2014), SSMs may also suffer from poor
340   memorization of long contexts and long-range dependencies. To address this issue, Structural SSMs
341   (S4) with a structural matrix Ā are introduced to capture long-range dependencies, e.g., HiPPO (Gu
342   et al., 2020; 2021a) and diagonal matrices (Gupta et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022). Many variants of
343   SSMs (Gu & Dao, 2023; Mehta et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022) are also proposed.
344   See Wang et al. (2024b) for a comprehensive survey. Note that GSSC Eq. 4 does not reply on
345
      a structural matrix Ā to achieve long-range dependencies. This is because Ā serves to describe
      the casual (recurrence) relation Eq. 1 for sequences, while there is not such causality for graphs.
346
      Instead, GSSC is generalized from the SSM convolution Eq. 2 and its behavior relies on the design
347
      of convolution kernel, i.e., the inner product of learnable graph positional encodings. The long-range
348   property can be achieved by choosing ϕ functions, as evidenced by Proposition 3.1.
349
350   State Space Models for Graphs. There are some efforts to replace the attention mechanism in graph
351
      transformers with SSMs. They mainly focus on tokenizing graphs and apply the existing SSM such as
      Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023). Graph-Mamba-I (Wang et al., 2024a) sorts nodes into sequences by node
352
      degrees and applies Mamba. As node degrees could have multiplicity, this approach requires random
353
      permutation of sequences during training, and the resulting model is not permutation equivariant to
354   node indices reordering. Graph-Mamba-II (Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024) extracts the 1, 2, ..., K-hop
355   subgraphs of a root node, treats each k-hop subgraph as a token. Each subgraph is assigned a
356   representation using GNNs, and these subgraphs form a sequence for the root node. It then applies
357   Mamba to this sequence to aggregate the representation of the root node and further applies Mamba
358   to a sequence of root nodes to get graph representations, but the latter operation breaks permutation
359   invariance. Ding et al. (2024) also treated k-hop neighbors with different k’s as a sequence while
360   using Deepsets encoders (Zaheer et al., 2017) instead of GNNs. These approaches incur significant
361   computational overhead as they require applying GNNs/DeepSets to encode every subgraph token
362
      first. Pan et al. (2024) focuses on heterogeneous graph scenarios, sorting and tokenizing rooted
      subgraphs based on the metapaths and applying Mamba to the sequentialized subgraphs. Zhao
363
      et al. (2024) aims at the design of graph spectral convolutions, applying SSMs to naturally ordered
364
      frequencies to build a graph filter. Compared to these methods, GSSC does not adopt any graph
365   sequentialization but instead generalizes the causal state space convolution to graphs, preserving
366   permutation equivariance and maintaining linear complexity.

530
531   In this work, we study the extension of State Space Models (SSMs) to graphs. We propose Graph
532   State Space Convolution (GSSC) that leverages global permutation-equivariant aggregation and
533   factorizable graph kernels depending on relative graph distances. These operations naturally inherit
534   the advantages of SSMs on sequential data: (1) efficient computation; (2) capability of capturing long-
535   range dependencies; (3) good generalization for various sequence lengths (graph sizes). Numerical
536
      experiments demonstrate the superior performance and efficiency of GSSC.
537   One potential limitation of our work is that precisely computing full eigenvectors could be expensive
538   for large graphs. See discussions in Sec. 3.2 and our empirical evaluation in Sec. 5.3 that shows good
539   scalability even for large graphs with 60k nodes.

936
937   Proposition 3.2. GSSC is strictly more powerful than WL test and not more powerful than 3-WL test.
938
939   Proof. Let us show that GSSC is more powerful than WL test. Note that WL test has the same
940   power as message passing GNNs (Xu et al., 2018), so it is sufficient to show that GSSC is more
941   powerful than message passing GNNs. first, by letting ϕ(Λ) = Λ and weights Wq = Wk = I, the
942
      convolution kernel ⟨zu Wq , zv Wk ⟩ becomes Au,v , i.e., the entry of Adjacency matrix. Thus GSSC
      can mimic message passing GNNs. Besides, if we consider two nonisomorphic graphs: one consists
943
      of two triangles and one is a hexagon, clearly message passing GNNs cannot distinguish them while
944
      GSSC can with proper ϕ and weights. Therefore we claim that GSSC is more powerful than message
945   passing GNNs and WL test as well.
946
947   Let us show that GSSC is not more powerful than 3-WL test. According to (Zhang et al., 2024),
948
      Corollary 4.5, any eigenspace projection GNNs, i.e., GNNs whose node-pair features are augmented
      by a basis invariant function of the PE of the node pairs, is not more powerful than 3-WL test. As
949
      GSSC leverages a basis invariant and stable function of PE as convolution kernel, it can be seen as an
950
      eigenspace projection GNN and thus is not more powerful than 3-WL.

1176
1177   In this section, we present supplementary experiments comparing GSSC with previous graph mamba
1178   works, i.e., Graph-Mamba-I (Wang et al., 2024a) and Graph-Mamba-II (?). As shown in Table 10,
1179   GSSC significantly outperforms both models on all datasets. We attempted to evaluate these works
1180
       on additional datasets included in our experiments, such as ZINC-12k, but encountered challenges.
       Specifically, Graph-Mamba-II has only an empty GitHub repository available, and Graph-Mamba-I
1181
       raises persistent NaN (Not a Number) errors when evaluated on other datasets. Our investigation
1182
       suggests that these errors of Graph-Mamba-I stem from fundamental issues in their architecture and
1183   implementation, and there is no easy way to fix them, i.e., they are not caused by any easy-to-find
1184   risky operations such as log(small negative numbers) or 10 . Consequently, Graph-Mamba-I (and
1185   Graph-Mamba-II) may potentially exhibit severe numerical instability and cannot be applied to
1186   some datasets. Prior to encountering the NaN error, the best observed MAE for Graph-Mamba-I on
1187   ZINC-12k was ∼ 0.10.
