009                                               Abstract
010
011               Lipschitz neural networks are well-known for providing certified robustness
012               in deep learning. In this paper, we present a novel efficient Block Reflector
013               Orthogonal layer that enables the construction of simple yet effective Lips-
014               chitz neural networks. In addition, by theoretically analyzing the nature of
015               Lipschitz neural networks, we introduce a new loss function that employs an
016               annealing mechanism to improve margin for most data points. This enables
017               Lipschitz models to provide better certified robustness. By employing our
018
                  BRO layer and loss function, we design BRONet, which provides state-of-
                  the-art certified robustness. Extensive experiments and empirical analysis
019
                  on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Tiny-ImageNet validate that our method
020
                  outperforms existing baselines. 1

023   1       Introduction
024
025   Although deep learning has been widely adopted in various fields (Wang et al., 2022; Brown
026   et al., 2020), it is shown to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2013). This
027   kind of attack crafts an imperceptible perturbation on images (Goodfellow et al., 2014) or
028   voices (Carlini & Wagner, 2018) to make AI systems make incorrect predictions. In light
029   of this, many adversarial defense methods have been proposed to improve the robustness,
030
      which can be categorized into empirical defenses and theoretical defenses. Common empirical
      defenses include adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018; Shafahi et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
031
      2023) and preprocessing-based methods (Samangouei et al., 2018; Das et al., 2018; Lee
032
      & Kim, 2023). Though effective, the empirical defenses cannot provide any robustness
033   guarantees. Thus, the defenses may be ineffective when encountering sophisticated attackers.
034   Unlike empirical defenses, theoretical defenses offer quantitative and provable guarantees of
035   robustness, ensuring no adversarial examples within a specific ℓp -norm ball with a radius ε
036   around the prediction point.
037
      Theoretical defenses against adversarial attacks are broadly categorized into probabilistic
038
      and deterministic (Li et al., 2023) methods. Randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019;
039   Lecuyer et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020) is a prominent probabilistic approach, known for
040   its scalability in providing certified robustness. However, its reliance on extensive sam-
041   pling substantially increases computational overhead during inference, limiting its practical
042   deployment. Furthermore, the certification provided is probabilistic in nature.
043
      Conversely, deterministic methods, exemplified by interval bound propagation (Ehlers, 2017;
044
      Gowal et al., 2018; Mueller et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022) and CROWN (Wang et al., 2021;
045   Zhang et al., 2022), efficiently provide deterministic certification. These methods aim to
046   approximate the lower bound of worst-case robust accuracy to ensure deterministic robustness
047   guarantees. Among various deterministic methods, neural networks with Lipschitz constraints
048   are able to compute the lower bound of worst-case robust accuracy with a single forward
049   pass, making them the most time-efficient at inference time. They are known as Lipschitz
050   neural networks.
051   Lipschitz neural networks are designed to ensure that the entire network remains Lipschitz-
052   bounded. This constraint limits the sensitivity of the outputs to input perturbations, thus

094   2.1    Certified Robustness with Lipschitz Neural Networks
095
096   Consider a function f : Rm → Rn . The function is said to exhibit L-Lipschitz continuity
097
      under the ℓ2 -norm if there exists a non-negative constant L such that:

101   where ∥·∥ represents the ℓ2 norm. This relationship indicates that any variation in the
102   network’s output is limited to at most L times the variation in its input, effectively character-
103   izing the network’s stability and sensitivity to input changes. Specifically, under the ℓ2 -norm,
104
      the Lipschitz constant is equivalent to the spectral norm of the function’s Jacobian matrix.
105   Assuming f (x) is the output logits of a neural network, and t denotes the target label.
106   We say f (x) is certifiably robust with a certified radius ε if arg maxi f (x + δ)i = t for
107   all perturbations {δ : ∥δ∥ ≤ ε}. Determining the certified radii is crucial for certifiable
      robustness and presents a significant challenge. However, in L-Lipschitz neural networks,

      2.2   Lipschitz Constant Control & Orthogonality in Neural Networks
113
114   Obtaining the exact Lipschitz constant for general neural networks is known to be an NP-
115   hard problem (Virmaux & Scaman, 2018). However, there are efficient methods available
116   for computing it on a layer-by-layer basis. Once the Lipschitz constant for each layer is
117   determined, the Lipschitz composition property allows for the calculation of the overall
118   Lipschitz constant for the entire neural network. The Lipschitz composition property states
119   that given two functions f and g with Lipschitz constants Lf and Lg , their composition
120
      h = g ◦ f is also Lipschitz with a constant Lh ≤ Lg · Lf . We can use this property to obtain
      the Lipschitz constant of a complex neural network f :
121

      Thus, if the Lipschitz constant of each layer is properly regulated, robust certification
126
      can be provided. A key relevant property is orthogonality, characterized by the isometry
127   property ∥W x∥ = ∥x∥ for a given operator W . Encouraging orthogonality is crucial for
128   controlling the Lipschitz constant while preserving model expressiveness (Anil et al., 2019),
129   as it helps mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and ensures a tight Lipschitz bound for
130   the composition of layers in Equation 2.

      3     Related Work
134
      Orthogonal Layers Orthogonality in neural networks is crucial for various applications,
135
      including certified robustness via Lipschitz-based methods, GAN stability (Müller et al.,
136
      2019), and training very deep networks with inherent gradient preservation. While some
137   approaches implicitly encourage orthogonality through regularization or initialization (Qi
138   et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2018), explicit methods for constructing orthogonal layers have
139   garnered significant attention, as evidenced by several focused studies in this area. Li et al.
140   (2019) proposed Block Convolution Orthogonal Parameterization (BCOP), which utilizes
141   an iterative algorithm for orthogonalizing the linear transformation within a convolution.
142   Trockman & Kolter (2020) introduced a method employing the Cayley transformation
143   W = (I − V )(I + V )−1 , where V is a skew-symmetric matrix. Similarly, Singla & Feizi
144   (2021b) developed the Skew-Orthogonal Convolution (SOC), employing an exponential
145
      convolution mechanism for feature extraction. Additionally, Xu et al. (2022) proposed the
      Layer-wise Orthogonal training (LOT), an analytical solution to the orthogonal Procrustes
146
147
      problem (Schönemann, 1966), formulated as W = (V V T )−1/2 V . This approach requires the
      Newton method to approximate the internal matrix square root. Yu et al. (2021) proposed
148
      the Explicitly Constructed Orthogonal Convolution (ECO) to enforce all singular values of
149
      the convolution layer’s Jacobian to be one. Notably, SOC and LOT achieve state-of-the-art
150   certified robustness for orthogonal layers. Most matrix re-parameterization-based methods
151   can be easily applied for dense layers, such as Cayley, SOC, and LOT. One recently proposed
152   orthogonalization method for dense layers is Cholesky (Hu et al., 2024), which explicitly
153   performs QR decomposition on the weight matrix via Cholesky decomposition.
154
      Other 1-Lipschitz Layers A relaxation of isometry constraints, namely, ∥W x∥ ≤ ∥x∥,
155
      facilitates the development of extensions to orthogonal layers, which are 1-Lipschitz layers.
156   Prach & Lampert (2022) introduced the Almost Orthogonal Layer (AOL), which is a
157   rescaling-based parameterization method. Meanwhile, Meunier et al. (2022) proposed the
158   Convex Potential Layer (CPL), leveraging convex potential flows to construct 1-Lipschitz
159   layers. Building on CPL, Araujo et al. (2023) presented SDP-based Lipschitz Layers (SLL),
160   incorporating AOL constraints for norm control. Most recently, Wang & Manchester (2023)
161   introduced the Sandwich layer, a direct parameterization that analytically satisfies the SDP
      conditions outlined by Fazlyab et al. (2019).


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Lipschitz Regularization While the aforementioned methods control Lipschitz constant
163   by formulating constrained layers with guaranteed Lipschitz bound, Lipschitz regularization
164   methods estimate the layer-wise Lipschitz constant via power iteration (Farnia et al., 2019)
165   and apply regularization to control it. Leino et al. (2021) employed a Lipschitz regularization
166   term to maximize the margin between the ground truth and runner-up class in the loss
167   function. Hu et al. (2023; 2024) further proposed a new Lipschitz regularization method
168   Efficiently Margin Maximization (EMMA), which dynamically adjust all the non-ground-truth
169   logits before calculating the cross-entropy loss.

171   4     BRO: Block Reflector Orthogonal Layer
172
173   In this section, we introduce the BRO layer, designed to provide certified robustness via
174   low-rank orthogonal parameterization. First, we detail the fundamental properties of our
175   method. Next, we leverage the 2D-convolution theorem to develop the BRO orthogonal
176   convolutional layer. Finally, we conduct a comparative analysis of our BRO with existing
177   state-of-the-art orthogonal layers.
178
179   4.1    Low-rank Orthogonal Parameterization Scheme
180
      The core premise of BRO revolves around a low-rank parameterization applied to an
181
      orthogonal layer, as introduced by the following proposition. A detailed proof is provided in
182
      Appendix A.1.
183
      Proposition 1. Let V ∈ Rm×n be a matrix of rank n, and, without loss of generality,
184
      assume m ≥ n. Then the parameterization W = I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T satisfies the following
185
      properties:
186
           1. W is orthogonal and symmetric, i.e., W T = W and W T W = I.
187
188
           2. W is an n-rank perturbation of the identity matrix, i.e., it has n eigenvalues equal
              to −1 and m − n eigenvalues equal to 1.
189
190
           3. W degenerates to the negative identity matrix when V is a full-rank square matrix.
191   This parameterization draws inspiration from the block reflector (Dietrich, 1976; Schreiber
192   & Parlett, 1988), which is widely used in parallel QR decomposition and is also important
193   in other contemporary matrix factorization techniques. This approach enables the parame-
194   terization of an orthogonal matrix derived from a low-rank unconstrained matrix, thereby
195   improving the computational efficiency.
196   Building on the definitive property of the proposition above, we initialize the parameter
197   matrix V as non-square to prevent it from degenerating into a negative identity matrix.
198

      5   BRONet Architecture
289
      We design our architecture BRONet similar to SLL and LiResNet. It consists of a stem
290
      layer for image-to-feature conversion, several convolutional backbone blocks of same width
291   for feature extraction, a neck block to convert feature maps into flattened vectors, and
292   multiple dense blocks followed by a spectral normalized LLN layer (Singla et al., 2022). For
293   non-linearity, MaxMin activation (Anil et al., 2019; Chernodub & Nowicki, 2016) is used.
294   Further details can be found in Appendix B.2.
295
      Compared to LiResNet with Lipschitz-regularized (Lip-reg) convolutional backbone blocks
296
      and SLL with SDP-based 1-Lipschitz layers, all the backbone blocks are BRO orthogonal
297   parameterized, which ensures a tight Lipschitz composition bound in Equation 2 and is
298   free from gradient norm vanishing. We keep the first stem layer in BRONet to be the only
299   Lipschitz-regularized layer since we empirically find it benefits the model training with a
300   more flexible Lipschitz control. Note that the Lipschitz composition bound of BRONet
301   remains tight due to the orthogonal backbone blocks. Let the stem layer be W1 and Q be
302   the composition of the layers before the neck block with QT Q = I, we have:

310

311   models, as it fails to increase the margin. Thus, they integrated Certificate Regularization
312   (CR) with the CE loss, formulated as: LCE − γ max(Mf (x), 0), where Mf (x) = f (x)t −
313   maxk̸=t f (x)k is the logit margin between the ground-truth class t and the runner-up class.

315   identifies several critical issues associated with the CR term, such as discontinuous loss

      impossible since the risk has a great lower bound. Due to the limited capacity of Lipschitz
361   models, we must design a mechanism that enables models to learn appropriate margins for
362   most data points. Specifically, when a data point exhibits a large margin, indicating further
363   optimizing it is less beneficial, its loss should be annealed to allocate capacity for other data
364   points. Based on this idea, we design a logit annealing mechanism to modulate the learning
365   process, gradually reducing loss values of the large-margin data points. Consequently, we
366   propose a novel loss function: the Logit Annealing (LA) loss. Let z = f (x) represent the
367   logits output by the neural network, and let y be the one-hot encoding of the true label t.
368   We define the LA loss as follows:
369                   LLA (z, y) = −T (1 − pt )β log (pt ), where p = softmax( z−ξy
                                                                                T ).                   (9)
370
      The hyper-parameters temperature T and offset ξ are adapted from the loss function in
371
      Prach & Lampert (2022) for margin training. The term (1 − pt )β , referred to as the annealing
372   mechanism, draws inspiration from Focal Loss (Lin et al., 2017). During training, LA loss
373   initially promotes a moderate margin for each data point, subsequently annealing the data
374   points with large margins as training progresses. Unlike the CR term, which encourages
375   aggressive margin maximization, our method employs a balanced learning strategy that
376   effectively utilizes the model’s capacity, especially when it is limited. Consequently, LA loss
377   allows Lipschitz models to learn an appropriate margin for most data points. Please see
      Appendix C for additional details on LA loss.

529   8   Conclusion
530
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel BRO layer to construct various Lipschitz neural networks.
      The BRO layer features low-rank parameterization and is free from iterative approximations.
532
      As a result, it is both memory and time efficient compared with existing orthogonal layers,
533
      making it well-suited for integration into advanced Lipschitz architectures to enhance
534   robustness. Furthermore, extensive experimental results have shown that BRO is one of
535   the most promising orthogonal convolutional layers for constructing expressive Lipschitz
536   networks. Next, we address the limited complexity issue of Lipschitz neural networks and
537   introduce the new Logit Annealing loss function to help models learn appropriate margins.
538   Moving forward, the principles and methodologies in this paper could serve as a foundation
539   for future research in certifiably robust network design.

759
760   Proposition 1. Let V ∈ Rm×n be a matrix of rank n, and, without loss of generality,
761   assume m ≥ n. Then the parameterization W = I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T satisfies the following
762   properties:
763
764         1. W is orthogonal and symmetric, i.e., W T = W and W T W = I.
765
            2. W is an n-rank perturbation of the identity matrix, i.e., it has n eigenvalues equal
766
               to −1 and m − n eigenvalues equal to 1.
767
768         3. W degenerates to the negative identity matrix when V is a full-rank square matrix.
769
770
771
      Proof. Assuming V is as defined in Proposition 1, the symmetry of this parameterization is
      straightforward to verify. The orthogonality of W , however, requires confirmation that the
772
      following condition is satisfied:
773
774                  W W T = (I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )(I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )T
775
                             = (I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )(I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )
776
777                          = I − 4V (V T V )−1 V T + 4V (V T V )−1 V T V (V T V )−1 V T
778                          = I − 4V (V T V )−1 V T + 4V (V T V )−1 V T
779                          = I.                                                                   (10)
780
781
      Next, define S = {v1 , v2 , · · · , vn } as the set of column vectors of V . Let ei denote the i-th
782
      standard basis vector in Rn . Then, we have
783
784                                  W vi = (I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )vi

                                          = vi − 2V (V T V )−1 V T vi
786
787                                       = vi − 2V (V T V )−1 (V T V ei )
788                                       = vi − 2V (V T V )−1 (V T V )ei
789                                       = vi − 2V ei

                                          = vi − 2vi = −vi .                                        (11)

795                                W vi = (I − 2V (V T V )−1 V T )vi = vi .                         (12)

      The equality holds because, for all vi ∈ S ⊥ , we have V T vi = 0.

887   A.3   Analysis of Semi-Orthogonal Layer
888
      ⋆ This is a newly added subsection. ⋆
889
890   In this section, we provide the detailed analysis about semi-orthogonal BRO layers, which
891   can be categorized into two types: dimension expanding layers and dimension reduction
892   layers. To facilitate understanding, we begin with the dense version of BRO (a single 2D
893
      matrix).
894   For a expanding layer constructed with W ∈ Rdout ×din , where din < dout , it satisfies the
895   condition W T W = Idin . Since the condition is equivalent to ensure that the columns are
896   orthonormal, the norm of a vector is preserved when projecting onto its column space, which
897   means ∥W x∥ = ∥x∥ for every x ∈ Rdin , thus, ensures 1-Lipschitz property.
898   For a reduction layer constructed with W ∈ Rdout ×din , where din > dout , it satisfies the
899   condition W W T = Idout . Unlike expanding layers, the columns of W in reduction layers
900   cannot be orthonormal due to the dimensionality constraint din > dout . Consequently, we
901   have the following relationship for every x ∈ Rdin , ∥W x∥ ≤ ∥x∥. The equality holds if x lies
902   entirely within the subspace spanned by the rows of W . In general, reduction layers do not
903   preserve the norm of input vectors. However, they remain 1-Lipschitz bounded, ensuring
904   that the transformation does not amplify the input norm.
905   The same principles apply to BRO convolution. The primary difference between the dense
906   and convolution versions of BRO layers arises from the dimensions they are applied to.
907   Therefore, the results discussed for BRO dense layers also hold for BRO convolution layers.
908   Figure 4 visualizes BRO convolution under three different dimensional settings, illustrating the
909   behavior of channel-expanding and channel-reduction operations in convolutional contexts.

1606   D.6                   Stability of LOT Parameterization
1607
1608   During the construction of the LOT layer, we empirically observed that replacing the identity
1609   initialization with the common Kaiming initialization for dimension-preserving layers causes
1610
       the Newton method to converge to a non-orthogonal matrix. We check orthogonality by
       computing the condition number of the parameterized matrix of LOT in the Fourier domain.
1611
       For an orthogonal layer, the condition number should be close to one. However, even after
1612
       five times the iterations suggested by the authors, the result for LOT does not converge to
1613   one. Figure 12 illustrates that, even with 50 iterations, the condition number of LOT does
1614   not converge to one. The orange curve represents the case with Kaiming randomly initialized
1615   parameters, while the blue curve curve corresponds to the case after a few training epochs.
1616   Both exhibit a significant gap compared to the ideal case, indicating that LOT may produce
1617   a non-orthogonal layer.

       E    Limitations
1621
1622
       ⋆ This is a newly added section. ⋆
1623
1624   While our proposed methods have demonstrated improvements across several metrics, the
1625   results for large perturbations, such as ε = 108/255, are less consistent. Additionally,
1626
       the proposed LA loss requires extra hyperparameter tuning. In our experiments, the
       parameters were chosen based on LipConvNets trained on CIFAR-100 without diffusion-
1627
       synthetic augmentation (Appendix B.4), which may not fully align with different models
1628
       and datasets. Furthermore, our methods are specifically designed for ℓ2 certified robustness,
1629   and certifying against attacks like ℓ∞ -norm introduces additional looseness. Lastly, although
1630   BRO addresses some limitations of orthogonal layers, training certifiably robust models on
1631   large datasets, such as ImageNet, remains computationally expensive and beyond our current
1632   resources.
