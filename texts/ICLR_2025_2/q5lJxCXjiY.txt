                                                  A BSTRACT
011
               Compositionality, the notion that the meaning of an expression is constructed from
012
               the meaning of its parts and syntactic rules, permits the infinite productivity of
013
               human language. For the first time, artificial language models (LMs) are able
014            to match human performance in a number of compositional generalization tasks.
015            However, much remains to be understood about the representational mechanisms
016            underlying these abilities. We take a high-level geometric approach to this problem
017            by relating the degree of compositionality in a dataset to the intrinsic dimensionality
018            of its representations under an LM, a measure of feature complexity. We find not
019            only that the degree of dataset compositionality is reflected in representations’
020            intrinsic dimensionality, but that the relationship between compositionality and
021            geometric complexity arises due to learned linguistic features over training. Finally,
022
               our analyses reveal a striking contrast between linear and nonlinear dimensionality,
               showing that they respectively encode formal and semantic aspects of linguistic
023
               composition.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      By virtue of linguistic compositionality, few syntactic rules and a finite lexicon can generate an
029
      unbounded number of sentences (Chomsky, 1957). That is, language, though seemingly high-
030   dimensional, can be explained using relatively few degrees of freedom. A great deal of effort has been
031   made to test whether neural language models (LMs) exhibit human-like compositionality (Hupkes
032   et al., 2019; Baroni, 2019; McCoy, 2022). We take a geometric view of this question, asking how an
033   LM’s representational structure reflects and supports compositional understanding over training.
034
      If a language model is a good model of language, we expect its internal representations to reflect
035
      the relatively few variables underlying the latter. That is, representations should reflect the manifold
036   hypothesis, or the notion that real-life, high-dimensional data lie on a low-dimensional manifold
037   (Goodfellow et al., 2016). The dimension of this manifold, or intrinsic dimension (ID), is then the
038   minimal number of degrees of freedom required to describe it without suffering from information loss
039   (Goodfellow et al., 2016; Campadelli et al., 2015). The manifold hypothesis has indeed been attested
040   for linguistic representations: LMs have been found to compress inputs to an ID orders-of-magnitude
041   lower than their extrinsic dimension (Cai et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023; Valeriani et al., 2023).
042   Compositionality permits the atoms of language to locally combine with others, creating global
043   meaning (Frege, 1948; Chomsky, 1999). As such, a complex array of meanings at the level of a phrase
044   is explained by simple rules of composition. A natural question is whether the inherent simplicity
045   of linguistic utterances, enabled by compositionality, manifests in representation manifolds of low
046   complexity, described by the manifolds’ intrinsic dimension. Thus far in the literature, an explicit
047   link between degree of compositionality and representational ID has not been established. To bridge
048   this gap, in a series of controlled experiments on causal language models and a custom dataset with
049   tunable compositionality, we provide the first experimental insights into the relationship between the
050
      degree of compositionality of inputs and the ID of their representations over the course of training.
051   Using our controlled stimuli and the LMs’ training data, we reproduce the established finding that LMs
052   represent linguistic inputs on low-dimensional, nonlinear manifolds. We also show for the first time
053   that LMs expand representations into high-dimensional linear subspaces, concretely, that (1) nonlinear
      and linear representational dimension scale differently with model size. We show the relevance

      of geometry to function over LM training, in particular that (2) LMs’ representational geometry
055   tracks a phase transition in their linguistic competence. Different from past work, we consider
056   two different kinds of compositionality: compositionality of form, or superficial combinatorial
057   complexity, and compositionality of meaning, or semantic complexity; as well as two measures of
058   dimensionality, nonlinear and linear. We not only find that geometric feature complexity reflects input
059   compositionality, but crucially that nonlinear ID encodes meaning compositionality while linear
060   dimensionality encodes form compositionality, in a way that arises over training: (3) nonlinear
061   ID preserves the degree of input compositionality as an inductive bias of the model, but reflects the
062   degree of semantic complexity at the end of training, and (4) linear dimensionality, not nonlinear
063
      ID, highly correlates to the superficial combinatorial complexity of inputs. Overall, results reveal a
      contrast between linear and nonlinear measures of feature complexity that suggests their relevance to
064
      form and meaning in how LMs process language.

067   2    BACKGROUND
068
069   Compositionality It has long been a topic of debate whether neural networks also exhibit human-
070   like compositionality when processing natural language (Fodor & Pylyshyn, 1988; Smolensky, 1990;
071   Marcus, 2003). This debate has fueled an extensive line of empirical exploration that assesses
072   the compositionality of neural networks in language modeling via synthetic data (Bentivogli et al.,
073
      2016; Lake & Baroni, 2018; Bahdanau et al., 2018) and natural language stimuli (Sathe et al., 2023;
      Dankers et al., 2022; Press et al., 2023). After the recent introduction of large language models
074
      with human-level linguistic capabilities (Wei et al., 2022), researchers have shown via mechanistic
075
      interpretability analyses that LMs often extract individual word meanings in early layers, and compose
076   them via later-layer attention heads to construct semantic representations for multi-word expressions
077   (Haviv et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2023). We use complementary tools to understand compositionality:
078   rather than neurons and circuits, we link compositionality to the geometric properties of a model’s
079   embedding space which describe its learned feature complexity.
080
      Language defines a mapping from form to meaning (de Saussure, 1916). Form is the physical shape
081
      of an utterance, for example, the sequence of letters or morphemes when written, or sounds when
082   spoken. Broadly, meaning is the concepts or entities to which the forms refer. Unlike prior work, we
083   make a distinction between form and meaning composition, where the formal composition relates to
084   the combinatorial complexity of the data, and semantic composition relates to the ability to construct
085   sentence-level meaning from word meaning. While, in grammatical sentences, meaning composition
086   often inherits from form composition, we disentangle them by creating agrammatical versions of the
087   dataset, further described in the Methods.
088
089   The manifold hypothesis and low-dimensional geometry Deep learning problems are often
090   considered high-dimensional, but research suggests that they have low-dimensional intrinsic structure.
091   In computer vision, studies have shown that common learning objectives and natural image data reside
092   on low-dimensional manifolds (Li et al., 2018; Pope et al., 2021; Valeriani et al., 2023; Psenka et al.,
093
      2024). Similarly, learning dynamics of neural LMs have been shown to occur within low-dimensional
      parameter subspaces (Aghajanyan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). The nonlinear, low-dimensional
094
      structure that emerges in the semantic space of these models, in contrast with models’ tendency to
095
      expand representations into high-dimensional linear subspaces (Jazayeri & Ostojic, 2021), has been
096   found to reduce learning complexity (Cheng et al., 2023; Pope et al., 2021), and likely follows from
097   the training objective of predicting sequential observations (Recanatesi et al., 2021).
098
099
      In the linguistic domain, the geometry of representations has been examined in various contexts.
      Recent work characterizes the organization of semantic concepts in representation space (Engels
100
      et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Balestriero et al., 2024; Doimo et al., 2024); it has been found
101
      that representational geometry can explicitly encode sparse tree-like syntactic structures (Andreas,
102   2019; Murty et al.; Alleman et al., 2021); and that linguistic categories such as part-of-speech are
103   represented in low dimensional linear subspaces (Mamou et al., 2020; Hernandez & Andreas, 2021).
104   Most similar to our setup, Cheng et al. (2023) reported the intrinsic dimension of representations over
105   layers as a measure of feature complexity for several natural language datasets, finding an empirical
106   relationship between information-theoretic and geometric compression. However, our work is the
107   first to explicitly relate the compositionality of inputs, a critical feature of language, to the number of
      degrees of freedom, or intrinsic dimension, of its representation manifold.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Language model training dynamics Most research on LMs focuses on the final configuration of
109   the model at the end of pre-training. Yet, recent work shows that learning dynamics can elucidate the
110   behavior and computational mechanisms of LMs (Chen et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2024; Tigges et al.,
111   2024). It has been found that, over training, LMs’ weight matrices become higher-rank (Abbe et al.,
112   2023), their representations higher dimensional (Cheng et al., 2024), and their gradients increasingly
113   diffuse (Weber et al., 2024). Over finetuning, representational dimensionality has been found to
114   change in concert with geometric properties like cluster reorganization (Doimo et al., 2024).
115   Phase transitions during LM training have been found for some, but not all, aspects of language
116   learning. Negative evidence includes that LM circuits involved in linguistic subtasks are stable
117   (Tigges et al., 2024) and gradually reinforced (Weber et al., 2024) over training. Positive evidence
118   for learning phase transitions includes that the ID of BERT’s final [CLS] representation tracks
119   sudden syntax acquisition and drops in training loss (Chen et al., 2024), with similar observations on
120   Transformers trained on formal languages (Lubana et al., 2024). Our work supplements these results
121   by investigating how the interaction between compositional understanding of language and geometric
122   complexity of its representation arises over training.

130   3.1     M ODELS
131
132   We evaluate Transformer-based causal language models from the Pythia family (Biderman et al.,
133
      2023), as Pythia is one of the only model suites to release intermediate training checkpoints. Models
      are trained on The Pile, a large natural language corpus encompassing encyclopedic text, books,
134
      social media, code, and reviews (Gao et al., 2020). Over training, models are tasked to predict the
135
      next token given context, subject to a negative log-likelihood loss. Experiments are performed on all
136   models in sizes ∈ {14m, 70m, 160m, 410m, 1.4b, 6.9b, 12b}.
137
138   Pre-training analysis For the three intermediate sizes 410m, 1.4b, and 6.9b, we report model
139   performance throughout the pre-training phase on the set of evaluation suites provided by (Biderman
140   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024), further described in Appendix F. This encompasses a range of higher-
141   level linguistic and reasoning tasks, spanning from long-range text comprehension (Paperno et al.,
142   2016) to commonsense reasoning (Bisk et al., 2019). The evolution of task performance provides a
143   cue for the type of linguistic knowledge learned by the model by a certain training checkpoint.

196   Controlling compositionality We modify the grammar in order to vary the dataset’s degree of
197   compositionality. While linguistic compositionality spans many interpretations (Hupkes et al., 2019),2
198   we are interested in two specific types: (1) composition of forms, or combinatorial complexity of the
199   dataset, where a dataset is more compositional if it contains more unique word combinations; (2)
200   composition of meanings, or sentence-level compositional semantics, where sentence meaning is
201   composed, via syntax, from word meanings.
202   First, to control for dataset combinatorial complexity, we couple the values of k contiguous word
203   positions for k = 1 · · · 4. That is, the sequence’s atomic units are sets of k adjacent words, or k-grams,
204   sampled independently. This constrains the degrees of freedom in sampling to l/k where l = 12 is
205   the number of categories: for instance, in the 1-coupled setting, each word is sampled independently,
206   hence 12 degrees of freedom; in the 2-coupled setting, each bigram is sampled independently, hence
207   6 degrees of freedom. Varying k maintains the dataset’s unigram distribution by design (see Figure 1
208   right), but constrains the dataset’s k-gram distributions, or combinatorial complexity.
209   To investigate compositional semantics, we randomly shuffle the words in each sequence. This
210   destroys syntactic coherence, and in turn, the overall meaning of the sentence. It instead preserves
211   superficial distributional properties like word count and word co-occurrences at the sentence level,

244   3.3     M EASURING FEATURE COMPLEXITY VIA DIMENSIONALITY ESTIMATION
245
246   We are interested in how the geometric complexity of representations reflects the inputs’ degree of
247   compositionality. In particular, we consider representations in the Transformer’s residual stream (El-
248   hage et al., 2021). Because sequence lengths may slightly vary due to the tokenization scheme, in line
249   with prior work (Cheng et al., 2023; Doimo et al., 2024), we aggregate over the sequence by taking
250
      the last token representation, as, due to causal attention, it is the only to attend to the entire context.
251   For each layer and dataset, we compute both a nonlinear and a linear measure of dimensionality.
252   Nonlinear and linear dimensionality have key conceptual differences. The nonlinear Id is the number
253   of degrees of freedom, or latent features, needed to describe the underlying manifold (Campadelli
254   et al., 2015; Facco et al., 2017), see Appendix D for discussion. This differs from the linear
255   effective dimensionality d, the dimension of the minimal linear subspace that contains the set of
256
      representations. Throughout, we will use dimensionality to refer to both nonlinear and linear estimates.
      When appropriate, we will specify Id as the nonlinear ID, d as the linear effective dimensionality,
257
      and D as the extrinsic dimensionality, or hidden dimension of the model. Since an Id -dimensional
258
      manifold can be embedded in a ≥ Id -dimensional linear subspace, we always have that Id ≤ d ≤ D.
259
260
      Intrinsic dimension We report the nonlinear Id using the TwoNN estimator of Facco et al., 2017.
261
      We choose TwoNN as opposed to other measures of nonlinear dimensionality for several reasons.
262

263   Estimator (MLE) of Levina & Bickel (2004) , for both synthetic point cloud benchmarks (Facco et al.,
264   2017) and LM representations (Cheng et al., 2023). Second, it relies on minimal assumptions of local
265   uniformity up to the second nearest neighbor of a point, in contrast to other estimators that impose
266   stricter assumptions, for instance, global uniformity (Albergante et al., 2019). Third, TwoNN and
267   correlated estimators enjoy precedence in related manifold estimation literature (Cheng et al., 2023;

      4          R ESULTS
305
306
      We find that representational dimensionality reflects compositionality in ways that are predictable
307
      over pre-training and model scale. First, we show that language models represent linguistic data on
308
      low-dimensional, nonlinear manifolds, but in high-dimensional linear subspaces that scale linearly
309   with the hidden dimension. Then, we show that, over training, geometric feature complexity is
310   informative of an LM’s linguistic competence, such that both exhibit a nontrivial phase transition
311   that marks emergence of syntactic and semantic abilities. Finally, we show that representational
312   dimensionality predictably reflects the degree of compositionality, both in terms of combinatorial
313   complexity and sequence-level semantics and analyze its evolution over training. For brevity, we
314   focus on model sizes 410m, 1.4b, and 6.9b in the main text, with full results in the appendix.
315

404     4.3             R EPRESENTATIONAL COMPLEXITY REFLECTS INPUT COMPOSITIONALITY
405
406     We just established that feature complexity is informative of when models gain complex linguistic
407
        capabilities that, by definition, require compositional understanding. Now, we establish our key
        result, which is that feature complexity encodes input compositionality, both when considering
408
        formal compositionality, or data combinatorial complexity, as well as meaning compositionality, or
409
        sentence-level semantics. We first show that this holds for fully-trained models that have reached
410     final linguistic competency. Then, using evidence from the training phase of the model, we show
411     that the correspondence between feature complexity and input compositionality is present first as an
412     inductive bias of the model that encodes formal complexity; but then, that it persists due to learned
413     syntactic and semantic features that encode meaning complexity. Lastly, we further develop the
414     coding differences between d and Id , confirming an existing hypothesis in the literature (Recanatesi
415     et al., 2021) that they respectively encode formal and semantic complexity of inputs.
416
417     Data combinatorial complexity On fully-trained models, representational dimensionality pre-
418     serves relative dataset compositionality. Figure 4 shows for fully-trained Pythia 410m, 1.4b, and 6.9b
419     that Id and d increase with the degree of formal compositionality within both coherent (solid) and
420     shuffled (dashed) settings: the highest curves (blue) correspond to the k = 1 dataset, or 12 degrees of
421     freedom, and the lowest (red) denote the k = 4 dataset, or 3 degrees of freedom. The relative order of
422
        feature complexity, moreover, holds for all layers, seen by non-overlapping solid curves in Figure 4.
423     Grammaticality is not a precondition for representational dimensionality to reflect data combinatorial
424     complexity: in Figure 4 (top), dashed curves corresponding to shuffled text are also ordered k = 1 · · · 4
425     top to bottom. While the relative order of formal complexity is preserved in the LM’s feature
426     complexity for both grammatical and agrammatical datasets, the separation is greater for grammatical
427     text (solid curves). We hypothesize that this is due to shuffled text being out-of-distribution, such that
428
        the model cannot integrate the sequences’ meaning, but nevertheless preserve surface-level complexity
        in its representations. This tendency holds for pre-trained models of all sizes (see Figure G.1) and for
429
        sentences of different lengths (see Figure J.2).

      5    D ISCUSSION
524
525
      We have studied language model compositionality from a geometric and dynamic perspective. Using
      a carefully curated synthetic dataset, we found strong relationships between the compositionality of
526
      linguistic expressions and the dimensionality of their representations. On one hand, representational
527
      dimensionality is positively correlated to datasets’ formal, or combinatorial, complexity. On the other
528   hand, grammatical sequences, whose semantics are composed via syntax, tend to exhibit a higher
529   non-linear dimensionality, but a lower linear dimensionality, than agrammatical shuffled sequences.
530   We showed that the positive relationship between compositionality and dimensionality is an inductive
531   bias of the model, but that it is eventually shed in favor of learning a representation manifold that
532   reflects meaningful semantic complexity in a phase transition. Results suggested differential coding of
533   form and meaning in LM representations, where form complexity, estimated with gzip, is expressed
534   linearly, and meaning complexity is expressed nonlinearly.
535   A central throughline in our results is that LMs compress representations to low-dimensional nonlinear
536   manifolds, yet expand them to high-dimensional linear subspaces. This echoes independent results in
537   computational neuroscience by Manley et al. (2024), who found that linear dimensionality scaled
538   with number of neurons recorded in the mouse cortex, and De & Chaudhuri (2023), who found in
539   neural networks that nonlinear, rather than linear, dimensionality better captured task semantics. The
      tendency for LMs to compress data to low-dimensional nonlinear manifolds, but, at the same time,


                                                        10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      expand them into high-dimensional linear subspaces, suggests a solution to the curse of dimensionality
541   that also enjoys its blessings. High-dimensional representations classically engender overfitting and
542   poor generalization (Hughes, 1968); but, these high-dimensional representations may lie on manifolds
543   whose ID actually captures the latent sparsity of the data (De & Chaudhuri, 2023). At the same
544   time, more dimensions implies more expressive orthogonality relations and linear decodability of
545   categories (Cohen et al., 2020; Elmoznino & Bonner, 2023; Sorscher et al., 2022). Benefits of
546   this dual patterning of intrinsic and effective dimensionality have been observed in biological and
547   artificial intelligence (Jazayeri & Ostojic, 2021; Recanatesi et al., 2021; Haxby et al., 2011; Huth et al.,
548   2012; De & Chaudhuri, 2023), where moreover, (linear) dimensional expansion and compression
549
      have implications for “lazy" and “rich" feature learning regimes, respectively (Flesch et al., 2022).
      While the present work is the first to show that this dual patterning in LMs broadly corresponds to
550
      a form-meaning dichotomy in representation learning, further work is needed to distentangle how
551
      nonlinear and linear features causally contribute to predictive coding.

947   C    OTHER D IMENSIONALITY E STIMATORS
948
949   Maximum Likelihood Estimator In addition to TwoNN, we considered Levina & Bickel (2004)’s
950   Maximum Likelihood Estimator (MLE), a similar, nonlinear measure of Id . MLE has been used in
951
      prior works on representational geometry such as (Cai et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023; Pope et al.,
      2021), and similarly models the number of points in a neighborhood around a reference point x to
952
      follow a Poisson point process. For details we refer to the original paper (Levina & Bickel, 2004).
953
      Like past work (Facco et al., 2017; Cheng et al., 2023), we found MLE and TwoNN to be highly
954   correlated, producing results that were nearly identical: compare Figure 2 left to Figure G.4 left, and
955   Figure G.1 top to Figure G.3 top).
956
957   Participation Ratio For our primary linear measure of dimensionality d, we computed PCA and
958   took the number of components that explainP 99% ofP  the variance. In addition to PCA, we computed
959   the Participation Ratio (PR), defined as ( i λi )2 /( i λ2i ) (Gao et al., 2017). We found PR to give
960   results that were incongruous with intuitions about linear dimensionality. In particular, it produced
961   a lower dimensionality estimate than the nonlinear estimators we tested; see, e.g., Figure G.4,
962   where the PR-d for coherent text is less than that of TwoNN. This contradicts the mathematical
963   relationship that Id ≤ d ≤ D. This may be because, empirically, PR-d corresponded to explained
964
      variances of 60 − 80%, which are inadequate to describe the bounding linear subspace for the
      representation manifold. Therefore, while we report the mean PR-d over model size in Figure G.4
965
      and the dimensionality over layers in Figure G.3 for completeness, we do not attempt to interpret
966
      them.

970
971   ID estimation methods practically rely on a finite set of points and their nearest-neighbor structure in
      order to compute an estimated dimensionality value. The underlying geometric calculations assume


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       that these are points sampled from a continuum, such as a lower-dimensional non-linear manifold.
973    In our case, we actually have a discrete set of points so the notion of an underlying manifold is not
974    strictly applicable. However, we can ask the question: if those points had been sampled from a
975    manifold, what would the estimated ID be? Since the algorithms themselves only require a discrete
976    set of points, they can be used to answer that question.

       F   B ENCHMARK TASKS
1121
1122
       Here we briefly summarize the benchmark tasks that we use to evaluate Pythia checkpoints as
1123
       described in Section 4.3. In figure 3, we did not include WSC (Winogrande Schema Challenge) which
1124
       was originally included in Biderman et al., as it has been proposed that WSC dataset performance
1125   on LMs might be corrupted by spurious biases in the dataset (Sakaguchi et al., 2021). Instead, we
1126   only presented the evaluation from WinoGrande task, which is inspired from original WSC task but
1127   adjusted to reduce the systematic bias (Sakaguchi et al., 2021).
1128
1129   WinoGrande WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) is a dataset designed to test commonsense
1130   reasoning by building on the structure of the Winograd Schema Challenge (Levesque et al., 2012).
1131   It presents sentence pairs with subtle ambiguities where understanding the correct answer requires
1132   world knowledge and commonsense reasoning. It challenges models to differentiate between two
1133   possible resolutions of pronouns or references, making it a benchmark for evaluating an AI’s ability
       to understand context and reasoning.


                                                        21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       LogiQA LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020) is an NLP benchmark for evaluating logical reasoning abilities
1135   in models. It consists of multiple-choice questions derived from logical reasoning exams for human
1136   students. The questions test various forms of logical reasoning, such as deduction, analogy, and
1137   quantitative reasoning, making it ideal for assessing how well AI can handle structured logical
1138   problems.
1139
1140   SciQ SciQ (Welbl et al., 2017) is a dataset focused on scientific question answering, based on
1141   material from science textbooks. It features multiple-choice questions related to science topics like
1142   biology, chemistry, and physics. The benchmark is designed to test a model’s ability to comprehend
1143   scientific information and answer questions using factual knowledge and reasoning.
1144
1145   ARC Challenge The ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge) Challenge Set (Clark et al., 2018) is a
1146   benchmark designed to test models on difficult, grade-school-level science questions. It presents
1147
       multiple-choice questions that are challenging due to requiring complex reasoning, inference, and
       background knowledge beyond simple retrieval-based approaches. It is a tougher subset of the larger
1148
       ARC dataset.
1149
1150
       PIQA PIQA (Physical Interaction QA) (Bisk et al., 2020) is a benchmark designed to test models
1151   on physical commonsense reasoning. The questions require understanding basic physical interactions,
1152   like how objects interact or how everyday tasks are performed. It focuses on scenarios that involve
1153   intuitive knowledge of the physical world, making it a useful benchmark for evaluating practical
1154   commonsense in models.
1155
1156   ARC Easy ARC Easy is the easier subset of the AI2 Reasoning Challenge, consisting of grade-
1157   school-level science questions that require less complex reasoning compared to the Challenge set. This
1158   benchmark is meant to evaluate models’ ability to handle straightforward factual and retrieval-based
1159   questions, making it more accessible for baseline NLP models.
1160
1161   LAMBADA LAMBADA (Paperno et al., 2016) is a reading comprehension benchmark where
1162   models must predict the last word of a passage. The challenge lies in the fact that understanding the
1163
       entire context of the passage is necessary to guess the correct word. This benchmark tests a model’s
       long-range context comprehension and coherence skills in natural language.

1608   Outliers There was one significant outlier, 160m, in our analysis correlating layerwise dimen-
1609   sionality to gzip (Kolmogorov complexity), see Figures I.2 and I.4 and Table I.1. While other
1610   models consistently demonstrate a positive Spearman correlation between d and gzip across lay-
1611
       ers, 160m (and to a smaller extent, 70m) deviates from this pattern. The reason 160m displays a
       negative correlation is due to its behavior on shuffled corpora, see the third column in Figure G.1:
1612
       for intermediate layers, PCA with a variance threshold of 0.99 yields fewer than 50 PCs. We found
1613
       that this was due to the existence of so-called “rogue dimensions" (Timkey & van Schijndel, 2021;
1614   Machina & Mercer, 2024; Rudman et al., 2023), where very few dimensions have outsized norms.
1615   Outlier dimensions have been found, via mechanistic interpretability analyses, to serve as a “sink" for
1616   uncertainty, and are associated to very frequent tokens in the training data (Puccetti et al., 2022). See
1617   Rudman et al. (2023) for exact activation profiles for the last-token embeddings in Pythia 70m and
1618   160m. While increasing the variance threshold to 0.999 reduced the effect of rogue dimensions on
1619   PCA dimensionality estimation, we decided to keep the threshold at 0.99 for consistent comparison
       to other models.
