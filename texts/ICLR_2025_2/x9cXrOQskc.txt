009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The development of Time-Series Forecasting (TSF) techniques is often hindered by
012            the lack of comprehensive datasets. This is particularly problematic for time-series
013            weather forecasting, where commonly used datasets suffer from significant limita-
014            tions such as small size, limited temporal coverage, and sparse spatial distribution.
015            These constraints severely impede the optimization and evaluation of TSF models,
016
               resulting in benchmarks that are not representative of real-world applications, such
               as operational weather forecasting. In this work, we introduce the WEATHER-
017
               5K dataset, a comprehensive collection of observational weather data that better
018
               reflects real-world scenarios. As a result, it enables a better training of models
019            and a more accurate assessment of the real-world forecasting capabilities of TSF
020            models, pushing them closer to in-situ applications. Through extensive benchmark-
021            ing against operational Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models, we provide
022            researchers with a clear assessment of the gap between academic TSF models
023            and real-world weather forecasting applications. This highlights the significant
024            performance disparity between TSF and NWP models by analyzing performance
025            across detailed weather variables, extreme weather event prediction, and model
026            complexity comparison. Finally, we summarise the result into recommendations to
027
               the users and highlight potential areas required to facilitate further TSF research.
               The dataset and benchmark implementation will be publicly available.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Global Station Weather Forecasting (GSWF) is essential for providing precise and timely weather
033   information, with significant implications across various sectors such as aviation Gultepe et al. (2019),
034   agriculture Ukhurebor et al. (2022), and energy Dehalwar et al. (2016). In particular, reliable forecasts
035   are crucial for early warning systems, aiding in the preparation for natural disasters and extreme
036   weather events, thereby safeguarding lives and property Wang et al. (2024b); Sillmann et al. (2017).
037   At present, the most mature and mainstream approach to precise station-based weather forecasting is
038   the use of Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models. These models can be further divided into
039
      physically-based NWP models Phillips (1956); Lynch (2008) and data-driven NWP models Lam et al.
      (2023); Bi et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Han et al. (2024a). Despite their widespread deployment
040
      and reputation as the most accurate algorithms for station weather forecasting, NWP models are
041
      inherently computationally intensive. Specifically, the execution of these algorithms depends on vast
042   resources for data assimilation and medium-range forecasting.
043
044   On the other hand, Time-Series Forecasting (TSF) methods, which offer a more economical solution,
045
      have also attracted attention from the weather forecasting community. Recently, many TSF methods
      have been developed and have demonstrated significant performance on small-scale weather station
046
      datasets. For instance, methods such as those presented in (Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2021; 2022;
047
      Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b) have achieved exceptionally low normalized errors (e.g., below
048   0.23 for temperature predictions). Despite their substantial success in research-oriented domains, the
049   applicability of these methods for real-world weather forecasting remains under-explored. Specifically,
050   most of these methods are trained and tested on single-station datasets Wu et al. (2021) or localized
051   regions Godahewa et al. (2021), thereby limiting their generalizability to other stations. As depicted
052   in the right panel of Figure 1, which tracks a winter storm forecast three days in advance, the TSF
053   method exhibits a significant performance gap when tested on unseen stations compared to the
      real-world weather forecasts generated by NWP model.

068   Hence, an emergent question arises: Are today’s time-series models ready for deployment in real-time
069   weather forecasting? If not, how far are they from real-world applicability, and what can be done to
070   improve them? To answer it, this work will deeply investigate three key aspects:
071   1) Developing a Large-Scale, Comprehensive Global Station Weather Dataset. To fully unlock
072   the potential of existing TSF methods and substantially improve their generalization ability. Specifi-
073   cally, we propose WEATHER-5K, a large-scale time-series dataset for sparse weather forecasting,
074   comprising 5,672 weather stations worldwide. This dataset provides diverse weather conditions and
075   10 years of hourly data per station, enabling long-term pattern analysis and robust forecasting model
076   development. Also, we conduct thorough data analysis to uncover trends, patterns, and correlations.
077   2) Conducting Extensive Assessments Between Existing TSF Algorithms and Real-World
078   Applications Using NWP as Reference Models. This assessment will extend beyond those shallow
079   attempts such as Wu et al. (2023), which only compared TSF methods to NWP models within a
080   one-day lead time and did not use the most accurate real-time forecasting products (e.g., ERA5
081   prediction). Here, we implement a comprehensive set of widely recognized time-series forecasting
082   methods across domains like traffic, weather, and electricity prediction. To this end, we establish a
083
      standardized evaluation framework to conduct extensive benchmark experiments on WEATHER-5K,
084   3) Fostering the Development of TSF for Real-World Weather Forecasting by Identifying future
085   opportunities. We have at least four recommendations for future research:
086          • The need to prioritize improving the long-term forecasting performance of TSF methods for
087            their practical application in general weather forecasting.
088          • Besides general weather forecasting, we find that existing TSF models still lag significantly
089            in predicting extreme weather events, which require more focused attention.
090          • Large-parameter models do not necessarily perform better. In our benchmarks. So de-
091            veloping efficient time-series modeling algorithms may yield greater benefits than merely
092            increasing model complexity.
093          • Leveraging TSF methods to integrate NWP forecasts as prior knowledge can effectively
094            improve long-term forecasting capabilities.
095
      Overall, we believe that our dataset and benchmarks will greatly facilitate future TSF research in
096
      weather forecasting. Our efforts will help researchers better evaluate and compare different algorithms,
097   ultimately leading to improved forecasting techniques.

175   3.1   C OLLECTION AND P RE - PROCESSING
176
177
      Data source. WEATHER-5K is derived from global near-surface in-situ observations, which is
      achieved in the publicly available Integrated Surface Database (ISD), leveraging data from high-
178
      quality observation networks. ISD is a global repository of hourly and synoptic surface observations,
179
      which encompasses a wide range of meteorological parameters. In this study, we study the common
180   variables: the wind speed and direction, temperature, dew point, and sea level pressure.
181
182   Station selection. ISD contains records from over 20, 000 stations spanning several decades, though
183   certain stations are no longer operational, many do not report data on an hourly basis, and numerous
184   stations have missing values for critical weather elements. To enhance data quality, a necessary
185   meticulous selection process was conducted to include only long-term, hourly reporting stations that
186   are currently operational and provide essential observations. As a result, there are 10, 701 operational
187   stations from 2014 to 2024.
188
189   3.2   Q UALITY C ONTROL
190
191
      A high-quality dataset is crucial for TSF. As shown in Figure 2, WEATHER-5K has been subjected
      to rigorous post-processing and quality control to ensure the reliability of the dataset.
192
193
      Data interpretation. Due to the fact that each variable in the original ISD data is not directly
194
      recorded as floating-point data, but rather as strings, where each string encapsulates the numerical
195   value, quality flag, reporting type, etc. of the variable. For example, a temperature field < +0130, 1 >
196   means the temperature value is 13.0 C◦ , and it is at the first quality level. Therefore, the first step is
197   to parse each variable according to the provided guidelines into a data format that is convenient for
198   our understanding and storage.
199
200   Temporal alignment. The aforementioned pre-processing has selected stations that report hourly
201   data. However, some stations may not report at 1:00, 2:00, ..., and are with a little time shift from
202   the hour, such as 0:54, and 2:05. To address this, we merge the latest time as the hour to keep the
203   temporal consistency. In implementation, we develop an algorithm that estimates missing hourly data
204
      points using the nearest available time points within a 30-minute window, significantly improving
      the distribution of valid hourly data. Despite this improvement, a tiny portion of hourly data points
205
      remained missing due to the lack of observations within the 30-minute window. To fill these gaps,
206
      linear interpolation was employed using data from the 12 consecutive hours surrounding each missing
207   point, ensuring the reliability of the interpolated data.
208
209   Completeness filtering. To obtain a high-quality dataset of weather stations, only stations with
210   more than 90% valid hourly data were chosen as the final candidates. As a result, 5, 672 weather
211   stations worldwide are selected, spanning the period from 2014 to 2023, ensuring a recent and
212   relevant time frame. This selection process focused on balancing the longevity of station operation,
213   hourly data availability, and the inclusion of diverse weather variables.
214
215   Outlier detection. The outlier detection process begins by meticulously examining the temporal
      dynamics of the dataset, focusing on identifying data points that lie far outside the expected range or

247   There are still very few missing data points after quality control. This is mainly due to some
248   stations experiencing long-term missing data (such as exceeding a full day). However, ensuring
249   the completeness of data for all stations is particularly crucial for spatial modeling with the TSF

251   data points at the station locations. ERA5 is the most widely used high-quality reanalysis dataset
252   globally, thus introducing only minor errors. However, since wind is a high-frequency parameter, the
253
      impact of using ERA5 for wind speed interpolation will be most significant.
254   To facilitate training on WEATHER-5K dataset, we further calculate the mean and variance of each
255   variable over a decade to standardize the input data. Additionally, we observed a lack of evaluation
256   metrics for extreme values in current TSF assessment methods, especially considering the crucial
257   role of extreme values in weather forecasting. Therefore, we also computed percentiles at different
258   thresholds (90%, 95%, 98%, 99.5%) for each variable to propose metrics for extreme values.

318   4.1    P ROBLEM D EFINITION
319
320   Considering N weather stations around the world and each station collects V meteorological variables,
321   the data of all weather stations can be represented by a spatial-temporal time-series X ∈ RN ×T ×V
322
      for a given look-back window of fixed length T . At timestamp t, time-series forecasting is to predict
323   X̂t+1:t+τ = {Xt+1 , ..., Xt+τ } based on the past T frames Xt−T +1:t = {Xt−T +1 , ..., Xt }. Here,
      τ is the length of the forecast horizon. Using X and X̂ to represent the observation data and the


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      forecasted data, respectively, the process of GSWF can be simplified by a mapping: X̂ = M(X),
325   where M can be different kinds of time-series forecasting methods. For example, by setting N = 1
326   and ignoring the spatial information, many state-of-the-art time-series forecasting methods Li et al.
327   (2022); Zhou et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022); Gu & Dao (2023);
328   Wang et al. (2024a); Nie et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2024b) can be explored on this task. When N is
329   multiple scattered stations, method Wu et al. (2023) based on spatial-temporal modeling can also be
330   applied to this task.

332   4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
333
334   Overall performance. Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Mean Square Error (MSE) are used
335
      to evaluate the overall performance of the GSWF. MAE measures the predictive robustness of an
      algorithm but is insensitive to outliers, whereas MSE is sensitive to outliers and can amplify errors.
336
337
      Extreme performance metric. In addition to standard metrics MAE and MSE, the precision in
338
      predicting extreme weather events, such as unusually high or low temperatures, is crucial in real-world
339   applications. Hence, we introduce a specialized metric, the Symmetric Extremal Dependence Index
340   (SEDI). Han et al. (2024b); Xu et al. (2024). It classifies each prediction in its station location as either
341   extreme or normal weather based on upper quantile thresholds (90%, 95%, 98%, and 99.5%) or lower
342   quantile thresholds (10%, 5%, 2%, and 0.5%). The calculation of SEDI value can be formulated as:
343
                        sum(X̂ < Qplower &X < Qplower ) + sum(X̂ > Qpupper &X > Qpupper )
344           SEDI(p) =                                                                   .                    (1)
345                                  sum(X < Qplower ) + sum(X > Qpupper )
346   where X̂ < Qplower and X < Qplower judge whether the predicted or observed data point belongs to
347   the extreme event or not based on the threshold Qplower , and vice versa for the upper percentiles. Here
348   we access both two opposite percentiles for accessing extreme small value (e.g., winter storm) and
349   extreme large value (e.g., heatwave). SEDI ∈ [0, 1] quantifies the model’s ability to correctly identify
350   extreme weather events. Higher SEDI indicates better performance in extreme weather prediction.

353
354   Dataset splitting. The WEATHER-5K dataset is divided into three subsets: training (with years
355
      2014-2021), validation (with yea 2022) and test (with year 2023), which follows an 8:1:1 ratio.
      allowing the model to be trained on sufficient historical data, validated on a separate year, and tested
356
      on the most recent data for accurate evaluation.
357
358
      Baselines. We here compare 9 baselines as mentioned in Section 2, which can be categorized as: 1)
359
      Temporal-only methods, which includes popular transformer-based long-term forecasting models
360   between 2021 and 2024: Informer (2021) Zhou et al. (2021), Autoformer (2021) Wu et al. (2021),
361   Pyraformer (2021) Liu et al. (2021), FEDformer (2022) Zhou et al. (2022), PatchTST (2023) Nie
362   et al. (2022), iTransformer (2024) Liu et al. (2024b), MLP-based models: DLinear (2023)Zeng
363   et al. (2023) and the new trending architecture Mamba (2023) Gu & Dao (2023) as baselines. 2)
364   Spatial-temporal method: We also include a recent method, Corrformer (2023) Wu et al. (2023) as a
365   baseline model that considers the dynamic characteristics of correlations among weather stations at
366   different locations.
367   Task settings. To facilitate fair comparison between different baselines, we align the input length of
368   all baselines. The final setting is to predict the τ -step future based on 48 historical steps, where the
369   input length is chosen based on the experimental results shown in Figure 4 b), which illustrates the
370   performance variation for four weather variables as the input sequence length increases. To trade off
371   the training cost and performance, we ultimately set the input length as 48 to balance computation
372   and performance. Specifically, we predict the future weather conditions for lead times of 1, 3, 5,
373   and 7 days, corresponding to predicting the 24-step, 72-step, 120-step, and 168-step future data,
374
      respectively. Note the report results are only performed once instead of multiple times in original
      implementations. This does not affect the comparison as we observe the results are stable for different
375
      seeds due to the large dataset volume.

      4.4   O BSERVATIONS AND F INDINGS
450
      RQ1: How do TSF models compare with NWP models in terms of general performance? . In
451   long-term predictions, where lead time ≥ 72 hours, the NWP model ECMWF-HRES (EC) is overall
452   superior to all existing TSF methods across almost all variables apart from wind speed and direction,
453   while in the short-term prediction, where lead time = 24 hours, some TSF methods (Pyraformer Liu
454   et al. (2021)) shows a comparable performance, which shows there is a long way for TSF models to
455   be the applicable model for weather forecasting. Details can be found in Table 2, which reports the
456   MAE and MSE for five variables under different predictive lengths. We also found that the simple
457   linear implementation DLinear Zeng et al. (2023) shows a poor performance while the early method,
458   like Pyraformer Liu et al. (2021), shows a significant advantage among all baselines. Overall, the
459
      remaining baselines show similar performance. There could be several reasons for this. Firstly, the
      existing TSF models have relatively small parameter settings and computational capacity, which
460
      can achieve good fitting performance on small-scale datasets but may not be suitable for large-scale
461
      datasets. Additionally, these models, except Corrformer Wu et al. (2023), only consider temporal
462   dependencies and overlook the spatial distribution differences and correlations in meteorological
463   data. Furthermore, we observe that Corrformer, despite considering spatial relationships, does not
464   perform as well as some simpler models like Informer Zhou et al. (2021). One possible reason for this
465   may be that Corrformer’s spatial modeling relies on pre-defined local regions. Moreover, we noticed
466   that different models exhibit preferences for some variables. For example, Informer Zhou et al.
467   (2021) performs better in predicting wind speed and direction than other methods. In addition, some
468   models show preferences for long-term forecasting, such as the Mamba Gu & Dao (2023) model,
469   which achieves the best performance in long-term predictions even though it has a general short-term
470
      performance. This suggests that the Mamba structure may be more suitable for long-term forecasting.
      Finally, based on the current results, the cumulative error stabilizes and increases significantly after
471
      the third day. This indicates the predictive errors are large after three days, and there is still ample
472
      room for improvement in long-term prediction performance.
473
474
      RQ2: Can TSF models perform well in real-world extreme weather prediction? Another
475
      finding is that current TSF models struggle to predict extreme values, whereas numerical models
476   excel at forecasting these extreme values, which is crucial for extreme weather event assessment.
477   Table 3 presents a report on the predictive performance of various models for extreme values. Our
478   finding is that the current time-series forecasting models studied in this paper can not effectively
479   capture the extreme values, especially with the lower and upper quantiles at 0.05% and 99.5%.
480   Additionally, it is observed that the performance of wind prediction is the worst among all variables.
481   This can be attributed to the non-stationary nature of the wind distribution, which makes it extremely
482   challenging to predict accurately. The evaluation results also indicate a research direction for future
483   time-series forecasting, which is to pay more attention to extreme values.
484

      5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
522
523   To facilitate accurate, efficient, and scalable weather forecasting for global weather stations, we
524   introduce WEATHER-5K as a new benchmark dataset. WEATHER-5K encompasses numerous
525
      global stations, providing comprehensive, long-term meteorological data. This dataset enables state-
      of-the-art time-series forecasting methods to be easily adopted and yield promising results. However,
526
      we also noticed that current methods might still lag behind numerical weather prediction models,
527
      particularly for longer lead times. This means WEATHER-5K would present new challenges and
528   opportunities, fostering advanced techniques and innovative research.
529
530   Limitation. Currently, we have three major limitations. 1). Spatial Quality: The current version of
531
      WEATHER-5K offers observations from a global network, but coverage remains sparse in regions like
      Africa and South America. Future enhancements will focus on integrating data from diverse sources,
532
      such as MADIS reports (including METER and Mesonet), to achieve denser station distribution and
533
      improve evaluation accuracy. 2). Missing Data Handling. To maintain data integrity, we utilized
534   interpolated results from the ERA5 dataset to fill in missing observations. While this interpolation
535   introduces some errors, minimally affecting temperature, dew point temperature, and pressure but
536   significantly impacting wind data, we ensured that only a small fraction of missing values were filled
537   in to minimize these errors. 3). Lack of Spatial-Temporal Methods: There is a scarcity of research
538   utilizing spatial modeling in time-series forecasting. Consequently, the baselines we implemented do
539   not fully leverage the potential of the WEATHER-5 dataset. We look forward to future researchers
      fully utilizing WEATHER-5K to develop more advanced time-series forecasting methods.

723   B.1   M OTIVATION
724
725   For what purpose was the dataset created? Was there a specific task in mind? Was there a
726
      specific gap that needed to be filled? Please provide a description.
727   This dataset is created with the following motivations: 1)The current weather station dataset limits
728   the applicability of forecasting models in real-world scenarios. Hence, it is urgent to develop a
729   comprehensive global station weather dataset that enables forecasting models to generalize across
730   diverse stations and regions worldwide. 2) The limited sizes of existing time-series datasets may not
731   reflect the real performance of the forecasting models, the proposed large weather station dataset
732
      can also serve as an extensive time-series dataset to perform comprehensive time-series forecasting
      benchmarks for various forecasting methods. 3) The existing simple datasets fail to encompass the
733
      complex scientific problems that researchers need to discover and resolve, thereby hindering progress
734
      in the field of time-series prediction. the proposed dataset offers a diverse temporal and spatial range
735   of time-series data, enabling a comprehensive evaluation of time-series forecasting methods and
736   driving significant advancements in the field. 4) A large-scale weather station dataset is a crucial
737   source of observational data for numerical weather prediction models, effectively bridging the gap
738   between numerical models and station-based predictions. This not only improves the accuracy of
739   numerical forecasts but also plays a vital role in verifying and evaluating the predictive performance
740   of numerical weather prediction models.
