009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Rendering highly realistic images from 3D assets is one of the most persistent
012            challenges of the graphics community, which is procedurally conducted by simu-
013            lating real-world geometry, material, and light transportation. However, such sim-
014            ulations are both burdensome and expensive. Recently, diffusion models have seen
015            great success in realistic image generation by leveraging priors from large datasets
016            of real-world images. Nonetheless, these generative models provide limited con-
017            trol over the output and, unlike graphic pipelines, cannot accurately integrate ma-
018            terials and geometric information for precise image synthesis. In this work, we
019
               propose a generative rendering framework, Intrinsic-ControlNet, that enables the
               generation of corresponding RGB images from 3D assets like a rendering engine
020
               by taking intrinsic images, e.g., material, normal, and structural information, as
021
               network inputs. We propose a novel multi-conditional control method that allows
022            the model to accept any number of intrinsic images as input conditions. To miti-
023            gate bias from synthetic training data, we propose a new model architecture that
024            allows appearance and structural conditions to be input separately into Control-
025            Net Zhang et al. (2023), preserving the realism of appearance generation from
026            real data while maintaining structural control capabilities from synthetic data. Ex-
027            periments and user studies demonstrate that our method can generate controllable,
028            highly realistic images based on the input intrinsic images.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Despite decades of development, generating photorealistic images in computer graphics remains a
034   highly challenging and expensive task. Specifically, Modeling objects across different scales to cre-
035   ate scenes that closely align with the real world is highly challenging, especially given the diverse
036   material properties Cook & Torrance (1982); Ngan et al. (2005) that objects can exhibit. Addition-
037   ally, simulating light propagation within a scene to solve the rendering equation Kajiya (1986), i.e.,
038   the rendering process, requires a significant computational cost. Consequently, existing pipelines
039
      struggle with tasks such as rendering highly realistic virtual scenes, inserting virtual objects into
      real environments, or seamlessly blending and editing real and virtual scenes in a low-cost and ef-
040
      ficient way, highlighting the need for a more lightweight solution. Recently, diffusion models (Ho
041
      et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020) have achieved significant success in gen-
042   erating realistic images by utilizing priors from large real-world image datasets. This work inspired
043   us to develop a neural network-based generative rendering framework with two main features. First,
044   it uses neural networks to extract 3D information from input condition images, drawing on prior
045   knowledge from extensive real-world data, thus eliminating the need for explicit 3D modeling and
046   light transportation. Second, it enables flexible adjustment of geometric structure and material prop-
047   erties, similar to a graphics engine. However, current diffusion methods provide only limited control
048   over the generated images and lack the ability to adjust geometric structure and material properties
049   freely.
050   In this work, we propose Intrinsic-ControlNet, a novel framework that leverages intrinsic scene de-
051   scriptions commonly used by rendering engines, including properties like albedo, normal, depth,
052   metallicity, and roughness, among others, to generate controllable output images. Our method
053   presents a novel generative rendering approach that combines data-driven techniques with tradi-
      tional rendering controls, allowing us to create highly realistic images without exhaustive physical

121   2   R ELATED W ORK
122
123   Separating appearance and structure. In tasks involving multi-condition control, many existing
124   methods separate the conditions that control appearance and structure to guide the generation of
125   results, significantly improving the flexibility and quality of control. For example, Ye et al. (2023)
126   combines ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) and T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2024) to separately control
127   structure and appearance style. Mo et al. (2024) uses structural guidance in the subspace to en-
128   force alignment with input conditions, while applying appearance guidance at the same image level
129   for appearance transfer. Lin et al. (2024a) achieves appearance transfer and strict structural control
130
      during inference through feature injection and self-attention correspondence. Although these meth-
      ods can achieve appearance transfer while maintaining structural features, their control conditions
131
      are relatively singular, i.e., a single style image and a single structure image as input conditions.
132
      Our method, however, introduces a multi-way switch and two separate appearance and structure
133   branches, enabling the generation of images guided by up to six intrinsic images.
134
135   Neural image editing. Advancements in generative models, particularly Generative Adversarial
136
      Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2019; 2020) and diffusion models (Ho
      et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022), have substantially enhanced various
137
      image editing capabilities, including style transfer , image-to-image translation, and manipulation
138
      within latent spaces (Huang & Belongie, 2017; Phillip et al., 2017; Schuhmann et al., 2022). The
139   introduction of text-guided diffusion frameworks (Nichol et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022) has fur-
140   ther expanded the scope of image modifications by allowing users to influence edits through natural
141   language prompts, thereby improving both control and user experience. In the area of object inser-
142   tion, more recent works have harnessed deep generative models to predict realistic object locations,
143   as seen in Compositing GAN (Azadi et al., 2020) and OBJect3DIT (Michel et al., 2024), which ad-
144   dress the complexities of integrating objects into diverse and intricate real-world scenes. However,
145   existing methods often perform editing operations in the pixel space. In contrast, our method takes
146   multiple intrinsic images as input and leverages the latent diffusion model to extract underlying 3D
147
      scene, generating images at the scene level rather than merely performing pixel-level editing.
148   Conditional image generation. GANs (Creswell et al., 2017) are generative models capable of con-
149   ditional control, but their training processes are relatively unstable and require significant amounts
150   of training time and resources. The emergence of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal &
151   Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2020) has reduced training complexity and improved generation qual-
152   ity. Early studies (Ho et al., 2020) primarily focused on unconditional generation tasks, where the
153
      models generated images solely based on the initial noise. However, with the growing demand for
      more precise generation tasks, researchers started investigating how to incorporate additional infor-
154
      mation during the generation process to achieve more accurate control over the results. The initial
155
      text-to-image diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Ding et al.,
156   2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021b; Mou et al., 2024) attracted significant attention by
157   allowing control over the appearance of generated images through text prompts. Furthermore, some
158   models (Zhang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) have extended the
159   control conditions from purely text prompts to include one or more images, thereby further enhanc-
160   ing control over the generated results and expanding the applications range of the model. Zeng et al.
161   (2024) encodes multiple conditions into the latent space and then inputs them into a latent diffusion
      model to control image generation. However, existing methods still face some challenges when us-

162
      ing multiple control conditions as inputs. Our method successfully handles multiple intrinsic images
163   as input conditions to generate controllable, photorealistic images.

      3.3   A PPEARANCE AND S TRUCTURE C ONTROL
271
272   To address the cross-domain rendering, we split the control paths of Intrinsic-ControlNet into two
273   branches, appearance and structure (referred to as A&S for brevity), and introduced a structure
274   feature injection design to tackle the problem. The following sections will explain the rationale and
275   detail behind these designs.
276   Cross-domain training data. As shown in Figure 3, our task is to generate photo-realistic images
277   from synthetic intrinsic images, but it is clear that we cannot obtain such pairwise data. The existing
278   pairwise intrinsic images are synthetic data, and if we train the network using them directly, the
279   generated images will exhibit a significant domain gap from real-world images as shown in Figure
280   10. Incorporating real-world data into the training process is crucial to ensure the model can produce
281   realistic images. However, unlike synthetic data, while there is a vast amount of real-world images,
282   they lack corresponding pairwise intrinsic images. To address this, we utilized a pre-trained diffu-
283   sion model Kocsis et al. (2023) to predict the corresponding intrinsic images from real-world RGB
284
      images. Similarly, since it is impossible to obtain real intrinsic images paired with real target images
      for training, the intrinsic images predicted by the diffusion model also contain biases. Training the
285
      model directly with such data would lead to generated images with many structural inaccuracies as
286
      shown in Figure 10. Fortunately, the advantages of synthetic data complement the limitations of real
287   data, as synthetic data provides accurate intrinsic images while real-world data offers highly realistic
288   target images. Based on this observation, we divide the input condition images into two parts, i.e.,
289   appearance and structure, and feed them into separate control branches. During training, the appear-
290   ance part is sourced entirely from the real image dataset, while the structure part comes from both
291   real and synthetic datasets. The final image is generated directly by the appearance branch, with
292   the structure branch sharing its features via structure feature injection without directly contributing
293   to the final output. Through this design, we eliminate the appearance bias in synthetic data and the
294   inaccuracies in structural information from real data, enabling the generated images to achieve high
295
      realism while maintaining controllable structures.
296   Structure feature injection. As mentioned earlier, to avoid introducing appearance bias from syn-
297   thetic data into the final generated image, we prevent the structure branch from directly contributing
298   to the image generation. However, we still aim to maintain precise structural control over the images
299   produced by Intrinsic-ControlNet. Previous work Tumanyan et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023); Mo
300   et al. (2024) has observed that diffusion features contain rich layout information. By performing
301
      feature and self-attention injection during the diffusion denoising steps, it is possible to control the
      appearance and structure of generated images without additional training. Inspired by Lin et al.
302
      (2024a), we apply structure feature injection during both the training and inference processes. At
303
      each time step t, we replace the appearance feature fal with the corresponding structure feature fsl at
304   the l layer of the latent diffusion model. The features fal and fsl are derived from the appearance and
305   structure branches, respectively. This approach ensures that the structure in the image generated by
306   the appearance branch aligns with the structure predicted by the structure branch.
307
      Independent convergence of A&S. ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023) introduced the sudden conver-
308
      gence phenomenon observed during training, where the model does not gradually learn the control
309
      conditions but instead abruptly succeeds in following the input conditioning image, followed by
310   slow convergence thereafter. Moreover, we observe that the convergence speeds of appearance and
311   structure in the diffusion model differ. Structural control information is generally easier to learn and
312   tends to converge earlier than appearance control. This further motivates us to feed the appearance
313   and structure control information from the intrinsic images into two ControlNets with independent
314   network weights. This approach ensures that each ControlNet converges independently to its opti-
315   mal point. The results of the convergence comparison between our method and ControlNet can be
316   found in Figure 13.

      5                 C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we present Intrinsic-ControlNet, a novel generative rendering framework that accepts
532
      synthetic intrinsic images as input and generates photo-realistic images. To tackle this cross-domain
533
      rendering task, we introduce an encoder with a residual structure and a multi-way switch to effec-
534   tively extract and integrate control features from the condition images. To address the cross-domain
535   issue in the data, we separate appearance and structure, using two distinct ControlNet branches to
536   control the generation. Additionally, a feature injection mechanism ensures that the appearance
537   branch remains unaffected by the appearance features from the structure branch while still produc-
538   ing images with controllable structural details. Experiments demonstrate that our method can render
539   high-quality photorealistic images from intrinsic images while also being effective for various down-
      stream applications such as scene material editing, object insertion, and removal.

      A.6                   C ONVERGENCE SPEED OF STRUCTURE AND APPEARANCE
911
912   Figure 13 shows the intermediate results during the training process for both ControlNet Zhang
913   et al. (2023) and our model. We attempt to use a single branch to control both the structure and
914   appearance of the generated images using ControlNet. During the training process of ControlNet,
915   we can observe that the convergence speeds of appearance and structure differ. As shown in Figure
916   13, the structure tends to converge much faster than appearance. At an early training stage, the
917   structural features of the generated results are already globally well-controlled, but the color and
      style are still not precisely managed, with noticeable color errors in the details. As the training


                                                                             17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      progresses, the structure continues to refine locally, but the slow convergence of appearance causes
919   the color and style to lag behind the ground truth. Since structural control has been achieved, the
920   network as a whole tends to stabilize. This results in the final generated images having issues like
921   being too dark or too magenta. To avoid this issue, we separated the appearance and structure
922   features by using two ControlNet branches with non-shared parameters as mentioned in Section 3.3,
923   which prevents interference between the optimization of appearance and structure.
