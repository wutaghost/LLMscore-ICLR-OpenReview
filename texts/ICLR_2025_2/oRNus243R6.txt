                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Nearest neighbor search is a fundamental data structure problem with many ap-
010
               plications in machine learning, computer vision, recommendation systems and
011
               other fields. Although the main objective of the data structure is to quickly report
012            data points that are closest to a given query, it has long been noted (Carbonell
013            & Goldstein, 1998) that without additional constraints the reported answers can
014            be redundant and/or duplicative. This issue is typically addressed in two stages:
015            in the first stage, the algorithm retrieves a (large) number r of points closest to
016            the query, while in the second stage, the r points are post-processed and a small
017            subset is selected to maximize the desired diversity objective. Although popular,
018            this method suffers from a fundamental efficiency bottleneck, as the set of points
019            retrieved in the first stage often needs to be much larger than the final output.
020            In this paper we present provably efficient algorithms for approximate nearest
021            neighbor search with diversity constraints that bypass this two stage process. Our
022
               algorithms are based on popular graph-based methods, which allows us to “piggy-
               back” on the existing efficient implementations. These are the first graph-based
023
               algorithms for nearest neighbor search with diversity constraints. For data sets
024
               with low intrinsic dimension, our data structures report a diverse set of k points
025            approximately closest to the query, in time that only depends on k and log ∆, where
026            ∆ is the ratio of the diameter to the closest pair distance in the data set. This bound
027            is qualitatively similar to the best known bounds for standard (non-diverse) graph-
028            based algorithms. Our experiments show that the search time of our algorithms is
029            substantially lower than that using the standard two-stage approach.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Nearest neighbor search is a classic data structure problem with many applications in machine
035   learning, computer vision, recommendation systems and other areas (Shakhnarovich et al., 2006).
036
      It is defined as follows: given a set P of n points from some space X equipped with a distance
      function D(·, ·), build a data structure that, given any query point q ∈ X, returns a point p ∈ P that
037
      minimizes D(q, p). In a more general version of the problem we are given a parameter k, and the
038
      goal is to report k points in P that are closest to q. In a typical scenario, the metric space (X, D) is
039   the d-dimensional space, and D(p, q) is the Euclidean distance between points p and q.
040
041   Since for high-dimensional point sets the known exact nearest neighbor search data structures are not
042
      efficient, several approximate versions of this problem have been formulated. A popular theoretical
      formulation relaxes the requirement that the query algorithm must return the exact closest point p,
043
      and instead allows it to output any point p′ ∈ P that is a c-approximate nearest neighbor of q in
044
      P , i.e., D(q, p′ ) ≤ cD(q, p). In empirical studies, the quality of the set of points reported by an
045   approximate data structure is measured by its recall, i.e., the average fraction of the true k nearest
046   neighbors returned by the data structure.
047
048
      Although maximizing the similarity of the reported points to the query is often the main objective, it
      has long been noted (Carbonell & Goldstein, 1998) that, without additional constraints, the reported
049
      answers are often redundant and/or duplicative. This is particularly important in applications such as
050
      recommendation systems or information retrieval, where many similar variants of the same product,
051   product seller or document exist1 To avoid reporting a long list of redundant answers, the search

055   some metric ρ (typically different from D). For example, the paper by Carbonell & Goldstein (1998)
056   proposed to augment the search objective so that, in addition to minimizing the dissimilarity between
057   the query and the reported set S, it also maximizes the pairwise dissimilarity ρ between the elements
058   in S 2 . The paper stimulated the development of the rich area of diversity-based reranking, which
059   became the dominant approach to this problem. The approach proceeds in two stages. In the first
060   stage, the data structure retrieves r points closest to the query, where r can be much larger than
061   the desired output k. In the second stage, the r points are post-processed to maximize the diversity
062   objective of the reported k points.
063   Although popular, the reranking approach to diversifying nearest neighbor search suffers from a
064   fundamental efficiency bottleneck, as the data structure needs to retrieve a large enough set to ensure
065   that it contains the k diverse points. In many scenarios, the number r of points that need to be
066   retrieved can be much larger than k (see e.g., Figure 1 and the discussion in the experimental section).
067   In the worst case, it might be necessary to set r = Ω(n) to ensure that the optimal set is found. This
068   leads to the following algorithmic question:
069
               Q: Is it possible to bypass the standard reranking pipeline by directly reporting the

      This result shows that the answer to the above question Q is positive. However, the algorithm suffers
      from several limitations. First, the distance functions D are limited to Hamming distance or its variants
091
      like the Jaccard similarity (Abbar et al., 2013a). Although it is plausible that the result could be
092
      extended to other distances that are supported by LSH functions, not all distance functions satisfy this
093   constraint. Furthermore, the last decade has witnessed the development of a class of highly efficient
094   algorithms that do not rely on LSH. These algorithms are “graph-based”: the data structure consists
095   of a graph between the points in P , and the query procedure performs greedy search over this graph
096   to find points close to the query. Graph-based algorithms such as HNSW (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018),
097   NGT (Iwasaki & Miyazaki, 2018), and DiskANN (Jayaram Subramanya et al., 2019) have become
098   popular tools in practice, often topping Approximate Nearest Neighbor benchmarks (Aumueller
099   et al., 2024). In addition, they are highly versatile, as they do not put any restrictions on the distance
100   function D. This raises the following variant of above question:
101
               Q’: is it possible to adapt graph-based algorithms so that they directly report k
102
               diverse points, in time that depends on k and not r ?

115   for any p ∈ S there are at most k ′ − 1 other points p′ ∈ S such that ρ(p, p′ ) < C.

411   4     E XPERIMENTS
412
413   In this section we provide an empirical evaluation of our algorithm. We focus on the special case of
414   the k ′ -colorful nearest neighbor problem as in Definition 2.3. Recall that in this setting, we use col[p]
415   to denote the color of a point p, and we define ρ(pi , pj ) = 0 for col[pi ] = col[pj ] and ρ(pi , pj ) = 1
416   otherwise. In other words, we seek k nearest neighbors, such that no more than k ′ belong to any single
417   color. Although restrictive, this case is of great practical interest in many settings, including shopping
418   and search. In both of these applications, the data points represent products (resp. documents)
419   and a color of a vector corresponds to seller (resp. domain) of the product. It is then desirable to
420
      output results from a diverse set of sellers or domains (Liaison, 2019). Intuitively, displaying diverse
      results would lead to increased competition between the sellers, and also simultaneously higher click
421
      probabilities, thereby leading to increase in revenue of the exchange.
422
423   For our experiments, we adapt our algorithms from Section 3 in two ways: one, we devise fast
424   heuristic approximations of the graph construction algorithm (this is much like the differences
425   between the fast- and slow-preprocessing algorithms in DiskANN (Jayaram Subramanya et al., 2019;
426
      Indyk & Xu, 2023)), and second, we restrict our implementation to cater to the special case of the
      k ′ -colorful version of the problem as defined in Definition 2.3. The pseudo-code of our efficient
427
      algorithms are described in Appendix C. All experiments were run on a Linux Machine with AMD
428
      Ryzen Threadripper 3960X 24-Core Processor CPU’s @ 2.3GHz with 48 vCPUs and 250 GB RAM.
429   All query throughput and latency measurements are reported for runs with 48 threads.

765   Diverse Search Our diverse search procedure, is a greedy graph-based local search method. In
766   our search method, in each step, we maintain a list of best and diverse nodes, ensuring that at most
767   k ′ points are selected in the list per color. In each iteration of our search algorithm, we choose
768   the best unexplored node and examine its out neighbors. From the union of our current list and
769   the out neighbors, we select the best diverse set of nodes while satisfying the k ′ -colorful diversity
770   constraint—meaning no color can have more than k ′ points in the updated list. To identify the optimal
771
      diverse set from the union, we use a priority queue designed to accommodate the diversity constraint.
