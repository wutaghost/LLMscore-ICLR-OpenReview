009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep active learning (AL) involves selecting batches of instances for annotation
012            since retraining large deep neural networks (DNNs) after each label acquisition
013            is computationally impractical. Employing a naive top-b selection can result in
014            a batch of redundant (similar) instances. To address this issue, various batch AL
015            strategies have been developed, many of which employ clustering for diversity as a
016
               heuristic. In contrast, we approach this issue by substituting the costly retraining
               with an efficient Bayesian update. Our proposed update represents a second-
017
               order optimization step using the Gaussian posterior from a last-layer Laplace
018
               approximation. Thereby, we achieve low computational complexity by computing
019            the inverse Hessian in closed form. We demonstrate that in typical AL settings, our
020            update closely approximates retraining while being considerably faster. Leveraging
021            our update, we introduce a new framework for batch selection through sequential
022            construction by updating the DNN after each label acquisition. Furthermore, we
023            incorporate our update into a look-ahead selection strategy as a feasible upper
024            baseline approximating optimal batch selection. Our results highlight the potential
025            of efficient updates to advance deep AL research.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Active Learning (AL) sequentially selects instances for annotation by human experts, aiming to
030   maximize model performance while minimizing labeling efforts. When combined with deep neural
031   networks (DNNs), AL typically selects instances in batches rather than one at a time. The reason for
032   this is that retraining DNNs after each label acquisition is computationally expensive, and delay can
033   lead to additional costs since annotators’ time is valuable (Kirsch et al., 2023).
034
      In a naive top-b batch selection, a batch of b instances with the highest scores is chosen based on an
035
      informativeness measure. However, when many similar instances are present, this approach can result
036   in significant redundancy within the batch (similar instances tend to receive similarly high scores). To
037   address this issue, many selection strategies have been developed to replace this naive selection (Ren
038   et al., 2021). These strategies often employ clustering techniques to select diverse batches, ensuring
039   that instances within a batch are dissimilar to one another (Hacohen et al., 2022). While effective in
040   reducing redundancy, clustering does not guarantee optimal selection due to its heuristic motivation.
041
      Orthogonal to these strategies, we explore the concept of efficient “retraining” in deep AL. If retraining
042   were computationally feasible, researchers could place greater emphasis on the development of
043   theoretically sound informativeness measures instead of using heuristic clustering approaches to
044   ensure diversity. Additionally, selection strategies that aim to maximize future performance–strategies
045   that have been shown to be near-optimal in traditional AL (Roy & McCallum, 2001)–could be made
046   feasible with DNNs. Therefore, we examine the concept of updating DNNs through a single
047   optimization step as a proxy for retraining and explore its potential to enhance the AL process.
048   To underscore the requirements of such an update, consider strategies designed to maximize future
049   performance. Typically, these use a look-ahead to select instances that significantly change model
050   predictions. More specifically, they examine how adding unlabeled instances to the labeled pool and
051   retraining the model affects predictions (Roy & McCallum, 2001). However, with a large number
052   of unlabeled instances and the time-consuming retraining process of DNNs, this approach becomes
053   infeasible. Therefore, instead of retraining, a highly efficient update method is required. The only
      work to realize such an approach with DNNs is by Tan et al. (2021), which employ an ensemble of


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      DNNs combined with Monte Carlo (MC) updates via Bayes’ theorem. Although this update makes
055   a one-step look-ahead feasible, it remains suboptimal for several reasons: (i) the update requires
056   an ensemble of DNNs, making the actual retraining time and memory demands inefficient; (ii) the
057   update does not accurately reflect the performance of full retraining; and (iii) the updating process
058   becomes inefficient with an increasing number of ensemble members.
059
      In this article, we propose an efficient update method for DNNs in the context of AL for classification.
060
      Specifically, we transform an arbitrary DNN into a Bayesian neural network (BNN) by employing a
061   last-layer Laplace approximation (LA) (Daxberger et al., 2021). While the closed-form expression of
062   the posterior allows us to leverage second-order optimization techniques, we ensure low computational
063   complexity by computing the required inverse Hessian analytically. Unlike the MC-based update
064   used in (Tan et al., 2021), our approach does not require an ensemble of DNNs, making it easily
065   applicable and both memory- and training-efficient (Daxberger et al., 2021). Additionally, because
066   we utilize a single DNN, we can leverage pretrained foundation models (Oquab et al., 2023), which
067   have been shown to be an essential part of many deep AL strategies (Hacohen et al., 2022; Gupte
068   et al., 2024). The resulting update is fast and closely matches the performance of full retraining.
069
      Extensive studies across different data modalities, including image and text datasets, demonstrate
      that our updates outperform the typically employed MC-based ones (Tan et al., 2021) in terms of
070
      speed and performance. Furthermore, we examine the proposed update in two distinct AL scenarios:
071
072         1. Enhancing Existing Strategies with Immediate Label Utilization: We propose a simple
073            framework to improve existing strategies by immediately making use of acquired labels
074            through the proposed updates. Rather than selecting the top-b highest-scoring instances
075            simultaneously, we iteratively select the highest-scoring instance b times but update the
076            model between each selection. This simple strategy, which approximates single-instance AL
077            during batch construction, performs surprisingly well, outperforming naive top-b selection
078
               as well as selection strategies that employ clustering.
079         2. Optimal AL with Look-Ahead Selection: We investigate the potential of our update with a
080            look-ahead selection strategy in an optimal AL setting. Specifically, we approximate an op-
081
               timal selection strategy that maximizes future performance. Instead of retraining, we ensure
               computational feasibility by employing our update. The resulting strategy outperforms all
082
               competitors, showcasing that currently employed selection strategies have much potential
083
               for improvement.
084
085       Summary of Contributions
086
087       • Efficient DNN Update: We propose an efficient update method for DNNs that employs a
088         Laplace approximation and second-order optimization techniques. We enable low compu-
089         tational complexity through closed-form computation of the inverse Hessian.
090       • Comprehensive Evaluation: We perform an extensive evaluation across data modalities,
091         demonstrating that our update outperforms MC-based updates in both speed and accuracy.
092       • Immediate Label Utilization: We develop a simple framework that employs our update
093         to immediately incorporate acquired labels, improving existing selection strategies by
094         updating the model during batch construction.
095
          • Optimal AL with Look-Ahead: We study our update in an optimal AL setting, making a
096         near-optimal selection strategy as an upper baseline computationally feasible.

      2    R ELATED W ORK

      Pool-based deep AL selection strategies are typically divided into uncertainty-based, diversity-based,
102   and hybrid strategies. Uncertainty-based strategies assume difficult-to-classify instances as beneficial.
103   Margin sampling (Settles, 2009), a popular variant (Bahri et al., 2022), selects instances where the
104   difference between the two highest predicted class probabilities is largest. Bald (Gal et al., 2017)
105   assumes a BNN and selects instances that maximize the mutual information between class predictions
106   and the DNN’s posterior distribution. Due to the requirement of batch acquisition in deep AL, these
107   strategies typically select the top-b highest-scoring instances. Diversity-based strategies assume that
      a set of diverse instances benefits the model. Core-Set (Sener & Savarese, 2018) selects instances that


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      minimize the average distance between the feature representations of labeled and unlabeled instances.
109   In practice, hybrid strategies, a combination of both uncertainty and diversity, have been shown to
110   work well. BatchBALD (Kirsch et al., 2019) extends BALD by reducing redundant information
111   within a batch. Badge (Ash et al., 2020) selects instances with high gradient norms based on pseudo-
112   labels and ensures diversity by employing k-MEANS ++ in the gradient space. Typiclust (Hacohen
113   et al., 2022) replaces the notion of uncertainty with typicality and selects typical instances from
114   clusters obtained through k-MEANS.
115   Look-ahead strategies (Roy & McCallum, 2001; Kottke et al., 2021) remain relatively underexplored
116   in deep AL. These strategies aim to select instances expected to improve the model’s performance the
117   most by retraining for all possible candidate instances. In non-deep settings, such approaches have
118   been shown to achieve near-optimal selection (Roy & McCallum, 2001) while offering convergence
119   guarantees (Zhao et al., 2021). However, adapting these strategies to deep AL is challenging due to
120   the computational cost of retraining. To the best of our knowledge, BEMPS (Tan et al., 2021) is the
121   only strategy to implement a look-ahead mechanism in deep AL. They employ deep ensembles and
122   MC-based updates via Bayes’ theorem (see Section 3). While this update is computationally efficient,
123
      its performance falls short compared to full model retraining.
124   Similar to our setting, continual learning (De Lange et al., 2021) updates models by exclusively
125   training with data from a new task, addressing the challenge of retaining knowledge from previously
126   learned tasks. Popular techniques (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Ritter et al., 2018a) use conventional first-
127   order optimization methods, incorporating a regularization term to counteract catastrophic forgetting.
128   Specifically, Ritter et al. (2018a) and Kirkpatrick et al. (2017) derive a regularization term from
129
      an LA that penalizes large deviations from prior knowledge. Unlike our method, these approaches
      require training over multiple epochs for the regularization term to have an impact. Once it is used
130
      as an update (i.e., single optimization step), these strategies simplify to a first-order gradient step.
131
      Additionally, they assume large amounts of new data per task (thousands of instances), whereas our
132   update method is designed for small datasets, ranging from a single to hundreds of instances. For
133   example, a typical benchmark is to extend a dataset with a task consisting of all instance-label pairs
134   of a new class (ca. 5,000 in MNIST).
135
      More closely related to our work is online learning (Hoi et al., 2021), which aims to sequentially
136
      and efficiently update models from incoming data streams. Traditional approaches often focus on
137
      linear (Zinkevich, 2003; Crammer et al., 2006) or shallow (Kivinen et al., 2001; Sahoo et al., 2014)
138   models with maximum-margin classification. However, applying online learning to DNNs remains
139   difficult due to issues such as convergence, vanishing gradients, and large model sizes (Sahoo et al.,
140   2018; Yoon et al., 2018). To address these challenges, Sahoo et al. (2018) proposed a method that
141   modifies a DNN’s architecture to facilitate updates. We argue that this approach is restrictive in state-
142   of-the-art settings, given the increasing reliance on pretrained foundation models (Devlin et al., 2019;
143   Oquab et al., 2023). Most similar to our setting is the work on Bayesian online inference by Kirsch
144   et al. (2022), which is also employed in (Tan et al., 2021). The core idea is to sample hypotheses, e.g.,
145   via MC-Dropout, from the posterior distribution of a BNN and weight their importance according
146
      to the respective likelihoods for sequentially arriving data. The empirical results raised concerns
      regarding the applicability of such updates in high-dimensional parameter spaces. We refer to these
147
      updates as MC-based updates.
148
149   BNNs (Wang & Yeung, 2020; Fortuin, 2022) induce a prior distribution on the parameters of a
150   DNN and learn a posterior distribution given data. MC-Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) uses
151   dropout during inference to obtain a distribution over predictions. While it is simple to use, its
152
      inference is inefficient, and it provides suboptimal uncertainty estimates (Ovadia et al., 2019). Deep
      ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) are known for their superior uncertainty estimates but
153
      are train and memory inefficient (Ovadia et al., 2019). LAs (Ritter et al., 2018b) approximate
154
      the posterior as a Gaussian, with the MAP estimate as the mean and the inverse Hessian as the
155   covariance. As computing this Hessian is expensive for large DNNs, LA is often used only in the last
156   layer (Daxberger et al., 2021).

181   We focus on classification problems with instance space X and label space Y = {0, . . . , K − 1}. The
182   primary goal in our setting is to efficiently incorporate the information of new instance-label pairs

184   network on the extended dataset D ∪ D⊕ results in high computational cost for a large dataset D.
185   Conversely, using the new data solely can cause catastrophic forgetting (Ritter et al., 2018a).

224   Our update method is based on a combination of two concepts. First, instead of MC-based BNNs, we
225   suggest using LAs on the last layer of a DNN. Second, we directly modify the approximate posterior
226   distribution of the LA, providing a much more flexible way to adapt it to new data than reweighting.
227
      In the following, we explain each component in detail. For now, we focus on binary classification
      with K = 2, and refer to Appendix C for an extension to multi-class classification.
228
229   Last-layer LA: LAs approximate the (intractable) posterior distribution p(ω|D) with a Gaussian
230   centered on the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate with a covariance equal to the negative Hessian
231   of the log posterior (Daxberger et al., 2021). We denote this approximate distribution as

      In this section, we examine the proposed update in AL. First, we introduce a new framework that uses
439
      our updates to exploit label information during batch construction. Essentially, this approach mimics
440
      single instance AL, in which the model is retrained after each label acquisition. Next, we employ our
441   update to approximate an optimal look-ahead strategy. Instead of obtaining future performance of the
442   DNN with expensive retraining, we realize this through our update. Here, we average metrics over 30
443   repetitions to account for reproducibility challenges in AL (Munjal et al., 2022). Labeling budgets
444   and acquisition sizes differ based on the complexity of a dataset. A more detailed experimental setup
445   and all learning curves, including ones that report absolute values, are available in Appendix F.
446

451
      Thus, we iteratively select the highest-scoring instance b times and update the DNN between each

      selection. After acquiring b labels, we retrain the DNN similar to batch selection strategies. An
453   algorithm implementing this idea can be found in Appendix F.

505   5.2                         U PDATING IN L OOK -A HEAD S TRATEGIES
506
507   The idea of look-ahead strategies is to select instances that, once labeled and added to the labeled
508
      pool, maximize the performance of the model (Roy & McCallum, 2001). Unlike uncertainty- or
      diversity-based approaches, look-ahead strategies select instances based on an optimal criterion:
509
      the model’s actual performance. However, they are often neglected in deep AL due to the high
510
      computational requirements. One of the biggest bottlenecks in the selection is retraining. DNNs are
511   not well-suited for this due to their long training process. For this reason, we employ our proposed
512   update to make this feasible.
513
514
      Here, we consider a near-optimal strategy with access to ground truth information, including labels
      and validation datasets. It can be considered as an upper baseline in deep AL research. For the
515
      selection, we randomly sample 2000 subsets, each with a size equal to the acquisition size, and
516
      assess how their addition to the labeled pool affects the performance. The batch leading to the
517   highest performance gain is selected. While this approach would traditionally require 2000 times of
518   retraining–making it infeasible with DNNs–our update enables the efficient use of this strategy.
519
520
      In this experiment, we also include the recently proposed Typiclust strategy, which has demonstrated
      strong performance (Hacohen et al., 2022), especially in early stages of AL. Figure 7 presents the
521
      resulting accuracy improvement curves compared to random instance selection. Our optimal strategy,
522
      using updates rather than full retraining, performs exceptionally well, consistently outperforming all
523   competitors. Based on these results, we can see that the current selection strategies still have much
524   potential for improvement. Interestingly, we can see that Typiclust’s selection in the early stages of
525   AL seems to be close to an optimal selection but declines in effectiveness in later stages.

      6                          C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this article, we proposed an efficient second-order update for DNNs in AL using the Gaussian
531
      posterior of a last-layer LA. It achieves low computational complexity through a closed-form compu-
      tation of the required inverse Hessian. An extensive experimental evaluation showed that the proposed
532
      update provides an efficient alternative to retraining. Based on this observation, we introduced a new
533
      batch selection framework by sequentially updating the DNN after each label acquisition, offering a
534   new perspective on constructing batches without resorting to heuristics such as clustering. Further-
535   more, we realized a look-ahead strategy as a feasible upper baseline approximating optimal batch
536   selection, highlighting the great potential for improvement in current research on batch selection
537   strategies. In future work, we plan to utilize the proposed updates to enhance look-ahead selection
538   strategies (Roy & McCallum, 2001) in deep AL. As these strategies are based on decision-theoretic
539   principles, they naturally balance explorative and exploitative instance selection, a key challenge in
      AL (Li et al., 2024).

818   Here, we outline three different options for performing the update step for LA in a multi-class setting
819   with K > 2. In a binary setting, a last-layer LA uses a parameter vector ω ∈ RD . However, in a
820   multi-class setting, we have a parameter vector ωy for each class y ∈ Y. In the literature, several
821   approximations haven been proposed to model these parameter vectors’ distribution.
822
      The most complex approximation would be to concatenate these vectors and model them through a
823
      multi-variate normal distribution:
824
                       q (ω | D) = N (ω|µ̂, Σ̂) with ω, µ̂ ∈ RK·D , Σ̂ ∈ R(K·D)×(K·D) .                 (17)
825
826   This approximation can estimate a covariance between each pair of parameters. However, this
827   expressiveness comes at the cost of a large covariance matrix Σ̂ to be estimated. This is particularly
828
      costly for many classes K combined with a high feature dimension D from a DNN, such that
      corresponding updates via second-order optimization would no longer be efficient.

830   Spiegelhalter & Lauritzen (1990) presented a more efficient approximation in which the class-wise
831   parameter vectors are arranged column-wisely as a matrix. Their joint distribution is then modeled
832   through a matrix normal distribution:
833              q (ω|D) = MN (ω|µ̂, Γ̂, Σ̂) with ω, µ̂ ∈ RD×K , Σ̂ ∈ RD×D , Γ̂ ∈ RK×K .                (18)

835

904   such as automobile, dog, airplane, and ship. The classes are mutually exclusive meaning there is

      Algorithm: The main idea from Section 5 is to transform any sequential selection strategy into a
921   batch strategy by using the proposed update method as a fast alternative to retraining. Typically, when
922   using a sequential selection strategy, the naive idea in a batch setting is to select the instances that
923   resulted in the top-b highest scores, where the score is defined by the respective selection strategy.
924   Our method can now update the DNN after each acquired label instead of selecting the top-b instances.
925   Hence, we have a similar scenario as if we would perform single-instance acquisitions but avoid the
926   retraining after each acquired label. After aggregating a batch of b instances, we retrain the DNN.
927   Algorithm 1 summarizes this idea. Given a standard AL setting with labeled data L and unlabeled
928   data U, we first calculate the approximate posterior distribution q(ω|L) via LA. Subsequently, we
929   iteratively select a new instance x and acquire its label y based on a given selection strategy α(·) and
930   update the approximate posterior distribution q(ω|{(x, y)}, L) according to equation 5. We repeat
931   this procedure for b acquisitions.
