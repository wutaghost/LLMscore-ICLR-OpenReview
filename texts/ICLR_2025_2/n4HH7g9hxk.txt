                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Reinforcement learning for continuous control under sparse rewards is an under-
010
               explored problem despite its significance in real life. Many complex skills build
011            on intermediate ones as prerequisites. For instance, a humanoid locomotor has to
012            learn how to stand before it can learn to walk. To cope with reward sparsity, a
013            reinforcement learning agent has to perform deep exploration. However, existing
014            deep exploration methods are designed for small discrete action spaces, and their
015            successful generalization to state-of-the-art continuous control remains unproven.
016            We address the deep exploration problem for the first time from a PAC-Bayesian
017            perspective in the context of actor-critic learning. To do this, we quantify the er-
018            ror of the Bellman operator through a PAC-Bayes bound, where a bootstrapped
019
               ensemble of critic networks represents the posterior distribution, and their targets
               serve as a data-informed function-space prior. We derive an objective function
020
               from this bound and use it to train the critic ensemble. Each critic trains an in-
021
               dividual actor network, implemented as a shared trunk and critic-specific heads.
022            The agent performs deep exploration by acting deterministically on a randomly
023            chosen actor head. Our proposed algorithm, named PAC-Bayesian Actor-Critic
024            (PBAC), is the only algorithm to successfully discover sparse rewards on a diverse
025            set of continuous control tasks with varying difficulty.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Complex control tasks commonly presuppose the completion of a sequence of sub-tasks. For exam-
031   ple, winning a chess game involves following a theoretically correct opening, a middle game with
032   a chain of creative tactical combinations, and an error-free endgame. Similarly, in Montezuma’s
033
      Revenge, an agent must visit multiple rooms and collect keys in the correct order. A genuinely intel-
      ligent agent is expected to identify and solve these sub-tasks based solely on the final reward. Deep
034
      reinforcement learning (RL) has been highly successful in handling such sparse reward scenarios.
035
      AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2018) and Random Network Distillation (RND) (Burda et al., 2019) reach
036   super-human level performance scores in chess and Montezuma’s Revenge, respectively, despite
037   receiving zero reward for long interaction sequences.
038
039
      Continuous control tasks share a similar modular structure. There is strong evidence that biological
      systems execute complex movements through robust and stable building blocks, known as motion
040
      primitives (Flash & Hochner, 2005). Application of the same idea to robotics, called dynamic move-
041
      ment primitives (Schaal, 2006), demonstrated remarkable success when unit movement patterns are
042   fit to observations separately and an agent is trained to apply them in a sequence. This recipe works
043   well when the target environment is well-understood and eligible for a rigorous engineering effort.
044   However, performing loosely defined tasks in open-world scenarios requires tabula rasa approaches
045   that both invent these primitives from authentic environment interactions and use them in an optimal
046   order for task completion within feasible sample complexity limits. Reinforcement learning has not
047   yet delivered success stories in continuous control setups with sparse rewards.
048   The common ground of reinforcement learning approaches developed for sparse reward setups is to
049   quantify the uncertainty on the Bellman target and use it for exploration. The exploration scheme
050   should aim for multi-step information gains by propagating uncertainty across Bellman backups, a
051   notion known as deep exploration (Osband et al., 2016a). There exist several model-free approaches
052   that treat deep exploration as a Bayesian inference problem on the Bellman target (Osband et al.,
053   2016a; 2018; Touati et al., 2020; Fellows et al., 2024). Additionally, model-based approaches ex-
      plore learning a distribution over the state transition kernel and propagating its uncertainty through

090

094   Bayesian Actor-Critic (PBAC) algorithm is the only model capable of solving these tasks, whereas

142   2.2   D EEP EXPLORATION
143
144   The classical optimal control paradigm prescribes a rigorous design of cost functions, i.e., negative
145
      rewards, that give a dense signal about model performance at each stage of model fitting (Stengel,
      1994; Kirk, 2004). This approach is akin to detailed loss designs in the common practice of deep
146
      learning. However, next-generation AI problems demand agents with high cognitive capabilities
147
      that can autonomously learn smart strategies to reach distant high rewards without being trapped by
148   intermediate small rewards or prematurely giving up searching. These approaches are commonly
149   known as deep exploration (Osband et al., 2016a). Their common ground is to estimate how often
150   a state-action pair has been visited until a certain training step and direct the exploration towards
151   unvisited ones. Deep exploration approaches can be classified into four categories based on how
152   accurate knowledge of environment dynamics they assume to make this estimation:
153
      (i) Model-based approaches learn to infer the state transition kernel by a function approxima-
154   tor Pϕ (·|s, a) ≈ P (·|s, a) and use its uncertainty to generate intrinsic rewards (Chentanez et al.,
155   2004) to perform curiosity-driven exploration, which has remarkable roots to biological intelligence
156   (Schmidhuber, 2010; Modirshanechi et al., 2023). VIME (Houthooft et al., 2016) incentivizes ex-
157   ploration of states that would bring more information gain on the estimated transition kernel, while
158   BYOL-Explore (Guo et al., 2022) and BYOL-Hindsight (Jarrett et al., 2023) choose states with
159   higher prediction error. Approaches such as UBE (O’Donoghue et al., 2018), Exact UBE (Luis
160   et al., 2023b), and QU-SAC (Luis et al., 2023a) choose the target quantity as the sample Bellman
161   operator Teπ Q. MOBILE (Sun et al., 2023) and MOPO (Yu et al., 2020) are the respective offline
      RL counterparts of these strategies.


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (ii) Pseudo-count approaches learn to predict only the relative visitation frequencies of state-action
163   pairs without needing to predict the state transitions. These frequencies are then used to generate
164   intrinsic rewards to direct the exploration towards less frequent state-action pairs. The commonplace
165   approach, called Random Network Distillation (RND) (Burda et al., 2019), distills a fixed network
166   with randomly initialized weights into another network with learned parameters. It uses the success
167   of the distillation as a pseudo-count to generate intrinsic rewards. State-of-the-art variants include
168   SAC-RND (Nikulin et al., 2023) and SAC-DRND (Yang et al., 2024).
169   (iii) Randomized value iteration approaches learn to estimate the uncertainty around the approxi-
170   mate Q function by modeling it as a random variable and performing posterior inference based on
171   the observed Bellman errors. Proposed inference methods include Bayesian linear regression (Os-
172   band et al., 2016b), mean-field (Lipton et al., 2018) or structured (Touati et al., 2020) variational
173   inference on Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian deep bootstrap ensembles with flat priors (Os-
174   band et al., 2016a) and informative priors (Osband et al., 2018). Fellows et al. (2021) find out that
175   these approaches actually infer a posterior on the Bellman target Tπ Q instead of the intended Q
176   and propose to overcome the consequences of this mismatch by training a separate Q network on
177
      the posterior predictive mean of Tπ Q. A more advanced version of this approach, named Bayesian
      Exploration Networks (BEN) (Fellows et al., 2024), fits a Bayesian heteroscedastic neural net on the
178
      Bellman target using structured variational inference.
179
180   (iv) Policy randomization approaches propose different perturbations on the learned models to ran-
181   domize the employed policy without aiming to quantify the uncertainty of a quantity. Proposed so-
182   lutions include perturbing the Q-network weights for discrete action spaces (Fortunato et al., 2018),
183
      actor-network weights (Plappert et al., 2018) for continuous control, and transforming the policy
      distribution by normalizing flows (Mazoure et al., 2019).
184
185   In this work, we follow the randomized value iteration approach due to the balance it grants between
186   computational feasibility and theoretical soundness. Notably, Bayesian model averaging gives the
187   optimal decision rule for an approximate Q function with partial observation on an MDP with reli-
188   able uncertainty estimates (Cox & Hinkley, 1974). We approach the deep exploration problem for
189
      the first time from a PAC-Bayesian perspective. The use of PAC-Bayes in reinforcement learning is
      limited to theoretical analysis (Fard et al., 2012) and preliminary developments aiming to improve
190
      training robustness (Tasdighi et al., 2024).

392   The loss is computed by one forward pass through each ensemble member and their targets per
393   data point with additional constant-time operations. Hence its computation time is similar to any
394   other actor-critic method. The loss comprises three terms with interpretable roles. The first induces
395   diversity into the ensemble by training each member on its individual targets. This way the model
396
      quantifies the uncertainty of its predictions as observations assigned to similar action values can
      only be those all ensemble elements are familiar with. The second term ensures coherence among
397
      ensemble elements as it trains them with the same target. The third term is a regularizer that repels
398
      ensemble elements away from each other in the absence of counter-evidence. This uncertainty
399   propagation is maintained, as the model cannot find a solution to collapse the ensemble elements
400   into a single solution. Propagation of uncertainties across time steps is known to have a critical role
401   in deep exploration (Osband et al., 2018). As the variance increases, the PBAC loss converges to
402   BootDQN-P (Osband et al., 2018). As it shrinks, it converges to the deterministic policy gradient.
403
      Note that the PBAC critic training loss also nicely unifies the advantages of variational Bayesian
404
      inference (Blei et al., 2017) and Bayesian deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) into a
405   single theoretically backed framework. Unlike variational Bayes, it works for density-free poste-
406   riors and unlike Bayesian deep ensembles, it permits a justified use of prior regularization via the
407   Kullback-Leibler divergence term.

      To model reward sparsity in the MuJoCo environments, we explore the following two variations:
460
461        (i) A sparse version in which the health reward is always set to zero, therefore removing
462            any incentive for the agent to maintain stability. This can cause the agent to prioritize speed
463            (minimizing positional delay), potentially leading to inefficient movement patterns such as
464            falling, rolling, or chaotic behavior. Without incentives for stability, the agent may fail to
465            maintain balance or learn proper locomotion, resulting in suboptimal performance, harder
466            training, and reduced overall effectiveness.
467       (ii) A very-sparse version in which the forward reward proportional to velocity is granted
468            only if the agent reaches a distance threshold c. The degree of sparsity, hence the task
469            difficulty, increases proportionally to c. This level of sparsity is applied on top of the zero
470            health reward from the sparse modification. Although this incentivizes reaching the
471
               target, the lack of intermediate rewards for progress may lead the agent to struggle with
               exploration, resulting in slower learning and difficulty in discovering effective policies as
472
               the agent infrequently gains informative signals from the environment.
473
474   In these settings, the agent does not receive any positive reward and pays a penalty for using its
475   actuators for failed explorations. Hence, they are well-suited for testing how directed the exploration
476   scheme of a learning algorithm is. We use them to assess whether an exploration strategy can
477   consistently promote effective exploration, when the agent encounters a lack of informative feedback
478   during learning.
479   We build an ensemble of ten Q-functions and use a replay ratio of five to improve sample efficiency.
480   This approach has been shown to achieve the same level of performance accuracy as state-of-the-art
481   methods, effectively addressing sample efficiency, as demonstrated in Nauman et al. (2024). More
482   details on the reward structure of each environment and the remaining design choices are provided
483   in Appendix B. We provide a public implementation at anonymous.
484

      on three standard MuJoCo tasks and their sparse versions along with three sparse DMC tasks.

524   We introduced a method to do deep exploration in sparse reward environmental settings for the first
525   time with a principled PAC-Bayesian approach. Comparing it to various state-of-the-art baselines,
526   we demonstrated its superior performance on a wide range of continuous control benchmarks with
527   various sparse reward patterns.
528   As the goal of our proposal is primarily to solve the task of deep exploration, PBAC’s performance
529   is sensitive to hyperparameters in the dense reward environments. While performing competitively
530   in two of them, ant and hopper, it struggles, as do several of the baselines in the dense humanoid
531   environment. This is due to the very high health reward of r = 5 an agent receives after every step,
532   compared to r = 1 in the other two. It is not surprising that in this situation random exploration is
533   sufficient and more robust. However, as soon as the reward becomes sparse, PBAC remains the only
534   method to learn the task.
535   A theoretical limitation is that our current work does not provide any convergence guarantees on
536   PBAC’s behavior. This theoretical problem requires, and deserves, dedicated investigation. As
537   PBAC builds on a sum of two Bellman backups and a regularizer, it will inherit similar properties
538   to the convergence guarantees of stochastic iterative Q-learning variants. Generalization of these
539   guarantees to continuous state spaces is known to be a nontrivial problem which we consciously
      keep outside our focus.

886
887   B.1     P RIOR WORK ON REWARD SPARSITY IN CONTINUOUS CONTROL
888
889   How to structure a sparse reward environment is a matter of debate. We identify two broad groups
890   of environments in the current state-of-the-art literature, without claiming this to be an exhaustive
891   survey. These consist either of native, i.e., inherent sparsity in the original environment or are
892   custom modifications of dense reward environments made by the respective authors. We follow the
893
      naming convention for each of the environments as used in the Gymnasium (Towers et al., 2024)
      and DMControl (Tassa et al., 2018) libraries unless otherwise noted.
894
895
      i) Binary rewards based on proximity to a target state. Houthooft et al. (2016) and Fellows
896
      et al. (2021) study Mountain Car continuous and sparse cartpole swing up. Fellows et al. (2021)
897
      reach a reward of around 500 in cartpole within more than 1.5 million environment interactions, far
898   behind the levels we report in our experiments. Curi et al. (2020) study reacher, pusher, a custom
899   sparsified version of reacher with a reward squashed around the goal state and action penalty with
900   an increased share. Houthooft et al. (2016), Mazoure et al. (2019), and Amin et al. (2021) sparsify
901   various MuJoCo locomotors by granting binary reward based on whether the locomotor reaches
902   a target x-coordinate. This reward design has limitations: i) Since there is no action penalty, the
903   locomotor does not need to aim at its actuators in the most efficient way, ii) Since there is no for-
904   ward reward proportional to velocity, the locomotor’s performance after reaching a target location
905   becomes irrelevant. For instance, a humanoid can also fall down or stand still after reaching the
906
      target.
907
908   ii) Increased action penalties. Curi et al. (2020) study cartpole and MujoCo HalfCheetah with
909
      increased action penalties. Luis et al. (2023b) use pendulum swingup with nonzero reward only in
      the close neighborhood of the target angle. They also apply an action cost and perturb the pendulum
910
      angle with Gaussian white noise. They also report results on PyBullet Gym locomotors HalfCheetah,
911
      Walker2D, and Ant with dense rewards. Their follow-up work (Luis et al., 2023a) addresses a
912   larger set of DMC environments where locomotors are customized to receive action penalties. The
913   limitation of this reward design is that increasing the action penalty is not sufficient to sparsify the
914   reward as the agent can still observe relative changes in the forward reward from its contributions
915   to the total reward. In some MuJoCo variants, the agent also receives rewards on the health status,
916   which is a signal about intermediate success against the sparse-reward learning goal. Furthermore,
917   action penalties are typically exogeneous factors determined by energy consumption in the real
      world, making the challenge artificial.

      In Section 4, we report results on the most difficult subset of some natively sparse environments and
919   devise new locomotion setups that overcome the aforementioned limitations. We discuss them in
920   the next subsection.

999
1000   Performance metrics. We calculate the Interquartile Mean (IQM) of the final episode reward and
1001
       of the area under learning curve (AULC) as our performance scores where the former indicates how
       well the task has been solved and the latter is a measure of learning speed. The AULC is calculated
1002
       using evaluation episodes after every 20,000 steps. We calculate these rewards over ten repetitions
1003
       on different seeds, where each of the methods gets the same seeds. All methods, including our
1004   approach and the baselines, utilize the same warmup phase of 10,000 steps to populate the replay
1005   buffer before initiating the learning process.

1016   (Shang et al., 2016) instead of the standard ReLU activation which enhances the model by incorpo-
1017
       rating both the positive and negative parts of the input and concatenating the results. This activation
       leads to potentially better feature representations and the ability to learn more complex patterns.
1018
       Moreover, we rely on a high replay ratio (RR) and a small replay buffer size, which reduce the
1019
       agent’s dependence on long-term memory and encourage it to explore different strategies. These are
1020   employed to improve the plasticity of the learning process. Recently, Nauman et al. (2024) showed
1021   that a combination of these design choices can overall greatly improve the agent learning ability.
1022   Additionally, we opted to use the Huber loss function for all baseline models after observing in our
1023   preliminary trials that it consistently provided performance advantages across different baselines.
1024   All design choices found advantageous for our model and not harmful to other have also been ap-
1025   plied to the baselines. Table 5 provides details on the hyper-parameters and network configurations
       used in our experiments.

       B.4.1   BASELINES
1102
1103
       We compare PBAC against a range of state-of-the-art general purpose actor-critic methods that are
1104   all empowered by ensembles. All design choices mentioned above found advantageous for our
1105   model and not harmful to other have also been applied to the baselines. Below we also explain
1106   further changes compared to the original works that we found to be benficial in preliminary experi-
1107   ments.
1108
1109
       BEN. Bayesian Exploration Networks (BEN), introduced by Fellows et al. (2024) serve as the best
1110
       representative that can learn a Bayesian optimal policy and handle the exploration vs. exploitation
1111
       tradeoff. We modify BEN by relying on Bayesian deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017)
1112   instead of the normalizing flow-based approach used in the original work. An ensemble of K −
1113   1 heteroscedastic critics learns a heteroscedastic univariate normal distribution over the Bellman
1114   target, while the Kth critic, regularized by the ensemble guides the actor network.
1115
1116
1117   BootDQN-P. Bootstrapped DQN with randomized prior functions (Osband et al., 2018), a
1118
       Bayesian model-free approach, serve as a close relative to our method. Throughout all environments
       we share most parameters with PBAC. Changes for specific parameters are discussed in Table 7, and
1119
       rely on a Thompson sampling actor. Its randomized priors allow the model to explore even in the
1120
       presence of sparse reward. A prior scaling (PS) parameter regulates their influence.
1121
1122
1123   DRND. Distributional randomized network distillation (DRND) (Yang et al., 2024), model the
1124   distribution of prediction errors from a random network. This distributional information is used
1125   as signal to guide exploration. As in the original work, we integrate it into a soft actor-critic
1126   (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018) framework. This random predictor network is trained via the same
1127
       objective and uses the same architecture as proposed by Yang et al. (2024). Actor and critic networks
       follow the architectural choices described above. Additionally, we optimize the α scaling parameter
1128
       in the SAC as is common practice.
1129
1130
1131   REDQ. Randomized ensemble double Q-learning (REDQ) (Chen et al., 2021) is the most compet-
1132   itive variant of the ensemble version of SAC as it incorporates both double Q-learning and random-
1133   ization to address value estimation and exploration. For this method all design choices are aligned
       with our method. REDQ relies only on the shared parameters.

1169   D.2.1    C ARTPOLE
1170
1171   Of cartpole’s five state dimensions, only the first two are interpretable, giving us the position of the
1172   cart and the cosine of the angle of the pole. We visualize them in Figure 5. As discussed above, the
1173
       agent gets rewarded only if it manages to stay close to the zero with an upwards pole, i.e., an angle
       close to one.
1174
1175   PBAC is able to quickly explore the state space and then concentrate on visiting the states with
1176   hight reward (i.e., the top middle). Similar to it, BootDQN-P has no problem in exploring the state
1177   space, however it never manages to find the narrow target and thus never converges. BEN starts
1178   exploring a wide range of states, but ultimately gets stuck in this seed without being able to find
1179
       the target. As shown in Figure 4 BEN’s performance varies greatly depending on the random initial
       seed in this environment. As such, this is a random representative of a failure case, not of its general
1180
       performance on cartpole. DRND and REDQ quickly get stuck as well within a small subset of the
1181
       state space, essentially just exploring the position of the cart within ever being able to significantly
1182   change the angle of the pole.
