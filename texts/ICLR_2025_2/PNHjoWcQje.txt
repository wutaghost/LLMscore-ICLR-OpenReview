011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite having powerful reasoning and inference capabilities, Large Language
014            Models (LLMs) still need external tools to acquire real-time information or domain-
015            specific expertise to solve complex tasks, which is referred to as tool learning.
016
               Existing tool learning methods primarily rely on tuning with expert trajectories,
               focusing on token-sequence learning from a linguistic perspective. However,
017
               there are several challenges: 1) imitating static trajectories limits their ability
018
               to generalize to new tasks. 2) even expert trajectories can be suboptimal, and
019            better solution paths may exist. In this work, we introduce StepTool, a novel step-
020            grained reinforcement learning framework to improve tool learning in LLMs. It
021            consists of two components: Step-grained Reward Shaping, which assigns rewards
022            at each tool interaction based on tool invocation success and its contribution to
023            the task, and Step-grained Optimization, which uses policy gradient methods to
024            optimize the model in a multi-step manner. Experimental results demonstrate that
025            StepTool significantly outperforms existing methods in multi-step, tool-based tasks,
026            providing a robust solution for complex task environments.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable abilities in reasoning and inference,
032   leading to impressive performance across a wide range of tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2022;
033
      OpenAI, 2023). However, some complex tasks that require real-time information or domain-specific
      knowledge often exceed the capacities of LLMs alone. In recent years, tool learning (Qin et al., 2024;
034
      Patil et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023) has emerged as a promising solution by augmenting LLMs with
035
      external tools (APIs). As shown in Figure 1, LLMs can dynamically select, invoke, and interact
036   with tools to receive real-time responses. After multi-step interactions with external tools, LLMs can
037   effectively gather the necessary information to complete complex and challenging tasks.
038
039
      To enhance the tool-learning capabilities of LLMs, most approaches rely on Supervised Fine-Tuning
      (SFT) (Qin et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023), in which LLMs are trained to imitate expert-generated
040
      trajectories in a text generation manner. Each trajectory is a sequence composed of a user’s query,
041
      multiple tool-callings and responses, illustrated in Figure 1. Despite its straightforward implemen-
042   tation, SFT encounters two key limitations in training LLMs for tool learning. Firstly, imitating
043   static pre-defined tool sequences limits the model’s ability to adapt to new tasks or environments.
044   Secondly, expert trajectories can successfully complete tasks but may not be the optimal sequence of
045   tool invocations. Blindly imitating these trajectories can lead to suboptimal task-solving performance.
046
      In addition to SFT, we propose using Reinforcement Learning (RL) as another strategy for tool
047
      learning, offering a more dynamic perspective by treating tool learning as a sequential decision-
048   making process. Under the RL perspective, each step of tool invocation is considered as an action that
049   leads to a state transition, and models are trained from the action-state transitions. Previous works
050   have explored applying RL to optimize LLMs in aligning with human preferences (RLHF) (Christiano
051   et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) or mathematical reasoning (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
052   2023; Shao et al., 2024). Nevertheless, these methods are not well-suited for tool learning due to
053   several key challenges: 1) Tool learning involves multiple decision steps and real-time feedback from
      external tools and environments. In contrast, RLHF is single-step based, and the steps in mathematical

077   To harness the potential of RL in tool learning with multi-step environment interactions and address
078   the limitations of existing methods, we propose StepTool, a novel step-grained reinforcement learning
079
      framework for tool learning, which models tool learning as a sequential decision-making process
      and treats each tool interaction as a critical decision point that directly impacts task completion, as
080
      shown in Figure 1. Specifically, StepTool consists of two core components: Step-grained Reward
081
      Shaping and Step-grained Optimization. For Step-grained Reward Shaping, we design rewards at
082   each step based on both the accuracy of tool invocation and the contribution to the overall task
083   completion, taking into account characteristics of intermediate actions in this scenario, i.e., well-
084   defined formats and explicit task objectives. These step-grained rewards offer richer signals for
085   tool learning, effectively guiding the model in decision-making. For Step-grained Optimization,
086   we propose a step-grained reinforcement-based optimization method based on the theory of policy
087   gradient (Williams, 1992; Sutton et al., 1999). This method ensures adaptability to dynamic, multi-
088   step interactions, addressing the limitations of single-step approaches like RLHF.

091

093
        models to learn from action-state transitions with real-time environment feedback.
094
      • We design step-grained rewards tailored to tool learning scenarios, focusing on both the accuracy

096

097     gradients, ensuring adaptability to dynamic, multi-step interactions.
098

099
        StepTool, confirming its superiority in enhancing the performance of solving complex tasks.

106   et al., 2024) propose to enable LLMs to interact with diverse external tools like program executors,
107   search engines, and QA system. Building on these initial efforts, subsequent models have focused
      on more extensive interactions with real-world APIs and tools. Qin et al. (2023); Patil et al. (2023)

      incorporate vast APIs from platforms like RapidAPI and TorchHub, training LLaMA model (Touvron
109   et al., 2023) for tool-based tasks in a Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) manner. Additionally, some
110   research efforts have concentrated on constructing verifiable and diverse datasets for SFT training
111   Tang et al. (2023); Abdelaziz et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024). Concurrent research (Chen et al.,
112   2024) has explored the use of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) for Tool

114   accounting for the quality of intermediate steps. In contrast, our work explicitly shapes step-grained
115   rewards and leverages them for step-grained reinforced optimization.
116
117   Process Supervision in LLMs. Process supervision has been extensively explored to enhance
118   long-chain reasoning in LLMs. Previous studies (Lightman et al., 2023; Uesato et al., 2022; Ma et al.,
119   2023; Shao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) leverage pre-trained process reward models and optimize
120   reasoning using RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022). Recent advancements, such as step-level preferences
121   in mathematical reasoning (Lai et al., 2024), apply DPO using step-level correctness. Unlike these
122
      works, our approach focuses on tool learning, where steps involve real-time interactions with external
      tools rather than text-based reasoning.
123
124
125   Reinforcement Learning for Multi-Step Textual Tasks. Recent advancements (Carta et al., 2023;
126   Tan et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024) apply reinforcement learning (RL) to align
127
      LLMs for multi-step textual tasks. Carta et al. (2023); Tan et al. (2024) typically constrain the action
      space to a restricted subset, focusing on optimizing actions as a whole. In contrast, StepTool tackles
128
      tool learning, where the action space is expansive, involving complex and lengthy responses from
129
      environments. Examples of scenarios, illustrating the differences between task types, are provided
130   in Appendix F. Methodologically, while prior works (Zhou et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024) often rely
131   on action-level models like Q-functions Q(s, a) and value functions V (s) to estimate intra-action
132   influences, StepTool directly computes token-level advantages, capturing intra-action and inter-action
133   influences without requiring action-level estimations. This approach avoids the inaccuracies of
134   action-level models, offering a more efficient optimization framework for tool learning.

      3    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
138
139   In this work, we propose to model the tool learning process in LLMs as a multi-step decision-making
140
      problem, which can be formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP). The MDP is represented by
      the tuple M = (S, A, P, R, γ), with the following meanings:
141
142   • S: The state space, where each state st ∈ S represents the current context or environment responses
143     at time step t, in connection with prior tool interactions.
144
      • A: The action space, where each action at ∈ A corresponds to calling an external tool (API) or
145     generating a final response (as a terminal action) at time t.
146
147
      • P: The state transition dynamics, P (st+1 |at , st ) defines the probability of transitioning to a new
        state st+1 given the current state st and the action at , representing how the environment changes
148
        as tools are applied.
149
150   • R: The reward function, which assigns rewards rt = R(st , at ) based on the current state st and
151     action at , representing the effectiveness of this tool-calling step.
152   • γ: The discount factor, which determines how the model balances immediate rewards with long-
153     term task-solving performance.
154
      Here we formulate the tool selection strategy of LLM as a decision-making policy πθ , parameter-
155
      ized by θ, which governs the selection of actions (tools) based on the current state. A trajectory
156
      τ = {s1 , a1 , s2 , a2 , ..., sT , aT } represents a sequence of states and actions over time, reflecting the
157   multiple interactions between LLMs and external tools or environments.
158
159   To maximize the final task-solving performance, the model seeks to optimize the expected reward

183   4     M ETHOD
184
185   Aimed at enhancing LLMs’ ability to use multiple tools for complex task solving, we propose a
186   novel step-grained reinforcement learning framework, StepTool, which is designed around the core
187   principles of the advantage function (Equation 3) and the policy gradient formulation (Equation 2).
188   As illustrated in Figure 2, StepTool consists of two primary components: Step-grained Reward
189   Shaping and Step-grained Optimization. Step-grained Reward Shaping assigns rewards at each tool
190   interaction step, evaluating both the accuracy of tool invocation and the contribution to the overall
191   task completion. Step-grained Optimization applies policy gradient methods to optimize the model
192   in a multi-step manner. Together, these components provide step-grained feedback and optimize
193   multi-step decisions, enhancing task-solving performance in complex environments.
194
195   4.1     S TEP - GRAINED R EWARD S HAPING
196
197   Step-grained Reward Shaping provides step-level reward signals for intermediate steps, effectively
198
      guiding the model in decision-making. In tool learning scenarios, the steps of tool invocation are
      characterized by well-defined formats and explicit task-oriented goals, naturally lending themselves
199
      to easier step-grained reward shaping. These step-grained rewards offer explicit feedback for each
200
      action, addressing the limitations of delayed rewards.
201
202
      4.1.1    S TEP - GRAINED R EWARD D ESIGN
203
204   Considering well-defined formats and explicit task objectives of intermediate tool-calling actions,
205   we have designed two key factors: the success of the tool call action (abbreviated as SuccCalling),
206   and the contribution to the overall task completion (abbreviated as Contribution). For the final step,
207   we directly link the reward to the completion of the task (abbreviated as IsSolved), representing to
208   whether the user’s query is solved.
209
210   SuccCalling. The SuccCalling metric evaluates whether the model successfully executes a tool call
211   with the correct format and content ( i.e. tool name and arguments). SuccCalling can be formally
212
      represented as r̂tSC = SuccCalling(at , st+1 ), where the reward at time t is determined by the action
      at and the subsequent state st+1 .
213
214   However, simply making a correct tool call does not guarantee progress toward solving the task. To
215   further guide the model, we introduce the Contribution metric, which evaluates how much the tool’s
      action aids the overall task solution.

254   4.1.2        S TEP - GRAINED R EWARD ACQUISITION
255
256   To generate training data with step-grained rewards, we first collect multiple trajectories from
257   the model’s own inferences across tasks in the training set, each comprising multiple interactions
258   between the model and external tools or environments. Step-grained rewards, derived from our
259
      reward components, can be assigned through various methods, such as automated rule-based models,
      human annotations, or advanced models like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) (with the annotation prompts
260
      detailed in Appendix C). Considering the significant time and financial costs associated with human
261
      annotation, we primarily rely on a combination of rule-based systems and GPT-4 to handle the
262   annotation process. These step-grained annotated data can be used for offline reinforcement learning
263   optimization or to train a reward model for online training.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this work, we proposed StepTool, a novel step-grained reinforcement learning framework that
489   enhances LLMs’ ability to solve complex, multi-step tasks using multiple tools. StepTool features
490   two core components: Step-grained Reward Shaping, which evaluates tool invocation success
491   and task contributions, and Step-grained Optimization, which uses policy gradient methods to
492   optimize decision-making at each step. Experiments with three open-source models demonstrate
493   its effectiveness in improving task-solving performance. StepTool offers a fresh perspective on
494   optimizing tool-use tasks, complementing SFT-based approaches and paving the way for future RL
495   innovations tailored to tool learning.
496
497   L IMITATIONS
498
499   Despite the superior performance of this work, several limitations must be acknowledged. First,
500   consistent with prior studies (Shen et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024), the PPO training process
501   can be unstable. We have included all experimental setups and parameter settings in our code
502   repository to ensure reproducibility. Second, despite achieving notable results, there is room for
503   further improvement. Our method supports online multi-round data collection and optimization, but
504   due to time and cost constraints, we conducted only a single round of offline training. This likely
505   limited the model’s full potential for performance gains.

      A    A NALYSIS ON AVERAGE S TEP L ENGTH
703
704
      Step length, or the number of tools utilized per task, can serve as a potential metric to evaluate both
705   task difficulty and a model’s planning capabilities. However, in the StableToolBench benchmark (Guo
706   et al., 2024), most tasks are designed to require 2-3 API calls for successful completion, with 68.6%
707   of tasks requiring 2 APIs and 19.9% requiring 3 APIs, accounting for 88.5% of all tasks. Task
708   difficulty in this benchmark is primarily influenced by factors such as tool diversity (e.g., intra-tool,
709   intra-category, or intra-collection variations) and whether the required tools are included in the
710   training data (Qin et al., 2023). These design constraints ensure consistency in task complexity but
711   may limit the ability of step length to fully capture a model’s planning abilities.
712   Despite this, we measured the models’ performance on the average number of tool utilized per task
713   across different subsets. The results are summarized in Table 5.

      From the results, we observe the following key points:
739
740   • Minimal Variation: The average number of tool invocations per task shows minimal variation (<0.1
741     tool calls per task) across models under the same experimental settings. This observation is highly
742
        related to the task design in the benchmark, as mentioned above.
743
      • Lack of Correlation with Performance: The number of tool invocations does not directly correlate
        with model performance. Fewer invocations may indicate missed critical API calls, while more
744
        invocations could reflect redundant or inefficient steps.
745
746   While step length offers insights into a model’s planning and task decomposition capabilities, its
747   utility is constrained by the current benchmark design, which emphasizes tool invocation accuracy
748   over broader planning strategies. Future work could explore benchmarks with more diverse and
749
      open-ended task designs to better evaluate these aspects.

751   B    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS OF BASELINES
752
753   For fair comparison, we implemented RLHF-PPO as a baseline following the traditional RLHF
754   framework (Ouyang et al., 2022). The PPO baseline is designed for single-step reward optimization,
755   applying rewards only to the final step of a trajectory. In contrast, StepTool introduces step-grained
      reward integration to capture dependencies across the entire trajectory.

796   D    D ISCUSSION ON THE R ISK OF R EWARD H ACKING
797
798   Reward hacking, which refers to potential annotation errors in this work, may arise during automated
799   or manual labeling of auxiliary rewards or through unintended exploitation of reward structures by
800   models. To enhance the robustness of reward annotations and mitigate these risks, several strategies
801   can be considered:
802   • Reward Model Ensembles: Using multiple reward models and averaging their outputs can reduce
803     the impact of individual model biases.
804
      • Multiple Annotations: Aggregating annotations from diverse sources can provide a more balanced
805     reward signal.
806
      • Iterative Refinement: Refining reward annotations iteratively based on experimental results can
807
        improve alignment with the task’s ultimate objectives.
808
809   These strategies, when implemented, can help create more robust reward systems, improving the
      reliability and robustness of reinforcement learning frameworks in complex tasks.
