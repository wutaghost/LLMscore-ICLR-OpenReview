009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Large Language Models (LLMs) can transfer their reasoning skills to smaller
012               models by teaching them to generate the intermediate reasoning process required
013               to solve multistep reasoning tasks. While LLMs can accurately solve reasoning
014               tasks through a variety of strategies, even without fine-tuning, smaller models are
015               not expressive enough to fit the LLMs distribution on all strategies when distilled
016               and tend to prioritize one strategy over the others. This reliance on one strategy
017               poses a challenge for smaller models when attempting to solve reasoning tasks
018
                  that may be difficult with their preferred strategy. To address this, we propose a
                  distillation method SIKeD: Self-guided Iterative Knowledge Distillation1 , where
019
                  the LLM teaches the smaller model to approach a task using different strategies
020
                  and the smaller model uses its self-generated on-policy outputs to choose the
021               most suitable strategy for the given task. The training continues in a self-guided
022               iterative manner, where for each training iteration, a decision is made on how
023               to combine the LLM data with the self-generated outputs. Unlike traditional
024               distillation methods, SIKeD allows the smaller model to learn which strategy is
025               suitable for a given task while continuously learning to solve a task using different
026               strategies. Our experiments on various mathematical reasoning datasets show that
027               SIKeD significantly outperforms traditional distillation techniques across smaller
028               models of different sizes.

055   vidual strategies (Magister et al., 2023; Shridhar et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023) or by incorporating
056   multiple strategies simultaneously (Chenglin et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024). Although smaller models

061   of scaling language models, making it difficult for smaller models to replicate this behavior Lyu et al.
062   (2024).
063   On the other hand, learning to solve a task using multiple strategies can help smaller models overcome
064   the limitations of relying on a single approach. However, a key challenge arises when, despite being
065   trained on a fixed dataset containing various strategies, a distribution mismatch occurs between
066   the data generated by the LLM and the outputs produced by the smaller model during inference.
067   This mismatch can hinder the ability of the smaller model to generalize across different reasoning
068   strategies. This issue, often discussed in imitation learning (Pomerleau, 1991; Ross & Bagnell, 2010),
069
      results in the student model consistently choosing one strategy, even when a different approach would
      be more appropriate. As a result, the student generates outputs with strategy choices that are highly
070
      unlikely to match those produced by the teacher.
071
072   To address this challenge, we introduce our distillation methodology, SIKeD: Self-guided Iterative
073   Knowledge Distillation. The process begins with the LLM teaching the smaller model to approach
074   tasks using a variety of reasoning strategies, providing a strong foundation for the smaller model to
075
      understand different problem-solving approaches. However, due to inherent biases and its limited
      capacity, the smaller model may still struggle to match the LLM’s distribution of strategy choices
076
      effectively. To resolve this, we take inspiration from constructivist learning theory Narayan et al.
077
      (2013), where the learner builds knowledge during the “assimilation phase” and refines their under-
078   standing during the “accommodation phase” to incorporate new insights. We propose generating
079   outputs using the smaller model in an on-policy setup and selecting the best strategies for the task.
080   By mixing the LLM-generated data with self-generated outputs, we leverage the strengths of both
081   datasets. We iteratively fine-tune the smaller model allowing it to recognize strategies that it learned
082   from the LLM but did not initially apply. With this approach, we align the smaller model with its
083   own learned knowledge rather than forcing its distribution to mirror that of the LLM’s.
084   Our proposed method extends beyond traditional one-step distillation, as each iteration of SIKeD
085   leads to an updated policy that better grasps new information. We repeat multiple iterations of SIKeD
086   based on the accuracy-cost tradeoff (does the improvement justify the cost of another iteration),
087   allowing for continuous refinement and improvement of the model’s reasoning capabilities. We
088   demonstrate the effectiveness of SIKeD on several mathematical reasoning tasks using models with
089   fewer than 7 billion parameters.2 On four mathematical datasets—GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021),
090   SVAMP Patel et al. (2021), ASDiv Miao et al. (2020), and MultiArith Roy & Roth (2016)—our ap-
091   proach achieves improvements of up to +5 points over traditional distillation strategies. Additionally,
092
      we show that multiple rounds of SIKeD allow the model to select the appropriate strategy for a given
      problem, while traditional distillation using LLM’s data tends to leave it biased.

097   Problem Setup We consider the standard setup of LLM-based distillation (also referred to as
098   knowledge distillation), where the data is sampled from the larger model (LLM) with intermediate
099   reasoning and the smaller model is fine-tuned (distilled) over it Shridhar et al. (2023); Magister et al.
100   (2023). Two auto-regressive sequence models are involved in the process: a larger model or the LLM
101   denoted as pL and a smaller model to be distilled as pθsm (with learnable parameters θ). In this work,
102
      we consider a reasoning dataset D consisting of a question qi and a numerical answer ai pairs for n
      data points, i.e. i ∈ {1, . . . , n}. Since our work focuses on improving the reasoning in smaller models
103
      by teaching them to solve a variety of reasoning strategies, we consider three reasoning strategies in
104
      this work: Chain-of-Thought (CoT), Least-to-Most (L2M), and Program-of-Thought (PoT). For a
105   specific reasoning strategy, denoted as s ∈ S, we generate the reasoning chain or rationale, denoted

113   We add the generated rationales to the the dataset D to create an initial training dataset DLLM consisting
114   of a quadruple of {qi , ai , s, ri } for each data point. We perform a data filtering by extracting the final

116   discard all samples that do not match, i.e., we keep samples where âi = ai . This filtering process
117   eliminates incorrect rationales, ensuring that only high-quality data is used for distilling the smaller
118   models.

120   question qi is provided as input, and the smaller model pθsm (with learnable parameters θ) is first
121   instructed to generate the strategy s, followed by the rationale ri that leads to the final answer ai . The
122   loss LL (θ) is defined as:

      Limitations Training solely on LLM-generated data DLLM can lead to a distribution mismatch
      between the training data and the smaller model’s own output distribution. Specifically, the larger
133
      model due to its larger capacity, may produce correct reasoning across multiple strategies that the
134
      smaller model can find difficult to replicate directly Agarwal et al. (2024). A comparison of the
135   strategy selected by the LLM and the smaller model on 1K samples is presented in Figure 1. The
136   smaller model performs poorly when generating outputs on its own, as the training data distribution

140   Proposed Solution To mitigate the distributional shift in strategy choice between the LLM and the
141   smaller model, we propose to incorporate the smaller model’s own correct outputs into the training
142   data. This self-guided training with data mixing aligns the training data distribution more closely with
143   the smaller model’s output distribution, making learning more effective. A visualization of the data
144   mixing approach is presented in Figure 2 that demonstrates that data mixing reduces the distribution
145   shift, bringing the LLM and the smaller model’s output distribution closer. This allows the smaller
146   model to choose the right strategy for a given task, much like the LLM.

148   3     SIK E D: S ELF - GUIDED I TERATIVE K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
149
150   We propose SIKeD, an iterative training where smaller models can take advantage of their own
151   generations to refine their strategy choices for a given task. In a nutshell, we generate data from the
152   smaller model, filter out the correct samples based on whether the generated solutions are correct, and
153   mix this data with the LLM-generated data to adjust its strategy preferences. The smaller distilled
154   model is used to iteratively generate data in an on-policy setting where it updates itself by leveraging
155   both the LLM data and its own generations. This iterative process allows the smaller model to
156   improve its reasoning abilities and strategy selection over time by leveraging the LLM’s knowledge
157   and its own prior learning. The following paragraphs discuss the steps involved in our proposed
158
      iterative distillation methodology and the training objective.

251   Dataset Our work demonstrates the effectiveness of selecting an appropriate strategy for a given
252   task. We consider multi-step mathematical reasoning datasets in our work, as various strategies
253   can solve the task fairly well. We trained SIKeD on the GSM8K training set Cobbe et al. (2021),
254   which includes 7,473 samples, and tested it on the corresponding test set of 1,319 samples. To
255   assess the domain transferability of our distillation method, we also evaluated it on three additional
256   mathematical datasets: SVAMP Patel et al. (2021) with 1,000 samples, ASDiv Miao et al. (2020) with
257   2,300 test samples, and MultiArith Roy & Roth (2016) with 180 samples. As the GSM8K training
258   set was used to train the smaller model, we classify it as an in-distribution dataset. In contrast, no
259
      training data from SVAMP, ASDiv, or MultiArith was used, as they were exclusively employed for
      testing purposes and thus considered out-of-distribution.

506   or by combining multiple strategies simultaneously Chenglin et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2024); Li
507   et al. (2024). These approaches can be viewed as aggregating diverse data sources for distillation,
508   similar to the LLM data approach in our work. In contrast, Hahn & Choi (2019) and Xu et al.

      (2020) focus on self-distillation, where a model improves its performance without external data or
      knowledge. Specifically, Hahn & Choi (2019) leverages word embeddings, while Xu et al. (2020)
510
      uses temporal model checkpoints as a proxy for ground truth. However, both approaches rely solely
511
      on data generated by the smaller model and exclude LLM data. Our method strikes a balance between
512   these two extremes by using LLM data to learn multiple strategies and self-generated data to optimize
513   for the right strategy choice.
514
515   Self-learning Previous studies, such as (He et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020; Gulcehre et al., 2023),
516   have shown the effectiveness of the self-training paradigm in tasks such as machine translation.
517   While ReST Gulcehre et al. (2023) uses off-policy training, we find on-policy training more suitable
518   for our case both in terms of data efficieny and performance. On-policy training also allows a better
519   choice of learning strategies, since the model can use its most recent learning. Agarwal et al. (2024)
520   introduces Generalized Knowledge Distillation (GKD), an on-policy training method that aligns the
521
      distributions of large language models (LLMs) and smaller models by incorporating output sequences
      sampled from the student during training. However, the task was limited to the distribution alignment
522
      and not to aligning the strategy choices in a multi-strategy distillation. Simply applying GKD would
523
      not address this issue, as it would force the smaller model to learn all strategies, which is impractical
524   given its limited capacity.
525
526   Finally, we compare our distillation strategies with LLM-based distillation using both individual
527
      strategies Shridhar et al. (2023); Magister et al. (2023); Hsieh et al. (2023) and a combination of
      several strategies at once Chenglin et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2024).

530   7    C ONCLUSION
531
532   We propose SIKeD: Self-guided Iterative Knowledge Distillation, that addresses the challenge of
533   distilling multistep reasoning skills from large language models (LLMs) to smaller models. Unlike
534   traditional methods, which often leave smaller models biased towards a single strategy, SIKeD uses
535   iterative self-guided training, combining LLM and self-generated data to improve overall reasoning in
536
      smaller models. We demonstrate our approach across various mathematical datasets and demonstrate
      that SIKeD improves the ability of smaller models to handle complex reasoning tasks, achieving
537
      significant performance gains.
