                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Flow matching models, ODE-based generative models, generate samples by grad-
011
               ually morphing a simple source distribution into a target distribution. In practice,
               these models still fall short of perfectly replicating the target distribution, mainly
012
               due to imperfections of the learned mapping. Previous work mainly focus on al-
013
               leviating discretization error, which rises from sampling a continuous trajectory
014            with a finite number of steps. In this work we focus on prediction error, an error
015            that is inherent in the model. Our main contribution is identifying a trajectory that
016            complies with the imperfect flow model and leads exactly to the target distribution.
017            Based on this finding, we propose Marginal Matching—a simple inference-time
018            correction scheme to steer the generated samples in the direction of the data. This
019            scheme proves to reduce a bound on the distance between the data and the learned
020            distribution, motivating two different implementations for the correction function.
021            We show that our proposed method improves sample quality on CIFAR-10 and
022
               ImageNet-64, with minimal overhead in computation time, or non at all when
               applying approximated correction.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Flow based generative models (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Tong et al., 2024) can gen-
029   erate complex data distributions, and have achieved remarkable results in image synthesis, audio
030   generation, protein design and robotics (Yan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; Le et al., 2024; Irwin
031   et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2023). These models learn a mapping between a source distribution (typically
032   Gaussian noise) and a target distribution. In practice, however, this learned mapping is not accurate
033
      due to two main sources of error: discretization and prediction. The discretization error stems from
      the mapping, which is defined as a continuous time operator, with discrete steps, while the prediction
034
      error is attributed to general neural network training difficulties, such as limited architecture’s ex-
035
      pressivity, limited training time and numerical instability. Previous works focus on minimizing the
036   discretization error, for example by learning straighter trajectories (Pooladian et al., 2023; Lee et al.,
037   2023; Tong et al., 2024). In this work we focus on mitigating the prediction error of a pre-trained
038   model, which, to the best of our knowledge, has not yet been tackled in the flow matching literature.
039
      Flow matching models assume that there exists an ODE that maps Gaussian noise to the data dis-
040
      tribution. Errors in learning this ODE result in an imperfect mapping, which leads to a generated
041
      distribution that is different from the data. In reversible models one can also consider starting from
042   the data distribution and following the reverse mapping. The idea of approximating and implement-
043   ing the reversal mapping has been explored before, in the context of diffusion models (Wallace et al.,
044   2023; Mokady et al., 2023). Here, we exploit time reversibility for flow matching models, which is
045   arguably less involved, as it only requires solving the ODE in reverse time (Liu et al., 2023a).
046
      Our main insight is that starting the generation from samples on the reverse time trajectory, (with
047
      the data as initial distribution), will perfectly reach the target distribution under the model’s map-
048   ping. We build on this insight and propose a simple method to improve the quality generation via
049   correcting the sampled trajectory during inference. The correction is done by applying a learned
050   correction step intermittently with the ODE solver steps, which works to reduce the error between
051   the generated samples and the time-reversal ODE solution. We provide theoretical guarantees that
052   such a correction function minimizes a bound on the Wasserstein distance (Kantorovich, 1942) be-
053   tween the target approximation and the true target distribution, as a function of the error reduction
      of the corrections at different time steps.

      We examine two practical implementations for the correction function. The first implementation
055   minimizes a theoretical bound, but has access to only subset of the data during training. The second
056   has access to the full dataset, but makes no theoretical guarantee. We examine these correction
057   models empirically and perform an extensive ablation study for our design choices. Our results
058   show significant improvement with minimal additional steps, or with no extra compute time at all
059   by running an approximated correction in parallel to the flow model.
060
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
061
062          • A novel framework for improving pre-trained flow matching models using the concept of
063            time reversal.
064          • A theoretical analysis of this general framework, and two practical implementations.
065          • We empirically show that our proposed correction models improve results on CIFAR-10
066            and ImageNet-64 datasets, achieving lower FID scores with fewer sampling steps.

302   3.2.2   ROBUST C LASSIFIER
303
      In the score model above, we did not use data from the forward marginals during training (we treated
304
      them as “noisy” versions of the backward marginals). We next propose a method that explicitly
305
      transitions between samples from the forward and backward marginals. This approach utilizes a
306
      time-dependent classifier cψ (t, xt ) that distinguishes between forward and backward marginals (pft
307
      and pbt ), evaluating each sample independently. By learning to categorize samples as belonging to
308
      either the forward or backward marginal at each time step, the classifier’s gradients hψ (t, xt ) =
309   ∇xt log cψ (t, xt ) provide corrective guidance, effectively steering samples toward the backward
310   marginals. The gradients will be zero upon reaching the correct class.
311
312
      To enhance the classifier’s gradient performance, we implement two key improvements: Adversar-
      ial Training (AT) and Gradient Alignment (GA). These keep the classifier’s gradients stable and
313
      meaningful, for more details and the complete algorithm for training see Appendix. A.3.3.
314
315   Usage for Class Conditioning Classifier guidance is used in diffusion models and more recently in
316   flow models (Sun et al., 2024) in order to generate conditional images. So far, MM is used to gen-
317   erate samples from the data, however, we can extend this method to also generate conditional data.
318
      For this purpose, our classifier can be conditioned cψ (t, xt , l) where l is a class label, and trained to
      categorize different classes in the backward trajectory. For an illustration see Appendix. A.6.2.

323   Training and inference mismatch – Previous work recognized training and inference mismatch
      in different settings. In score models (Song et al. (2021b); Song & Ermon (2019)), the source

330
      Even if the source distribution during training and inference is the same, practically, the actual distri-
331   bution that reaches the data is different. Coeurdoux et al. (2023) leveraged the invertibility property
332   of normalizing flow (NF) models (Kingma & Dhariwal (2018); Dinh et al. (2014)) to reach higher
333   probability samples in π̂1 , which are more likely to belong to π1 . They achieved this by employing
334   MCMC algorithm, where the score is computed using the Jacobian of the inverse mapping. This
335   discrepancy between the learned and actual source distribution is common in generative models like
336   VAEs (Kingma et al., 2021), which assume a Gaussian prior during inference. (Dai & Wipf, 2019)
337   addressed this by learning an additional VAE model to predict the actual prior distribution, thereby
338   removing the Gaussian assumption, which lead to improved results.
339   Different sampling trajectories – Different sampling trajectories were explored in previous work
340   mainly through the research of difference source distributions. In Denoising Diffusion Probabilis-
341   tic Models (Ho et al. (2020); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015)), various source distributions proved to
342   decrease inference time while maintaining quality. gil Lee et al. (2022) proposed using a Gaussian
343   prior distribution with parameters derived from data statistics. Lyu et al. (2022); Popov et al. (2021)
344   trained a model for prior distribution using an encoder or VAE (Kingma & Welling (2013)) and in-
345
      corporated it into the standard diffusion process by adding noise. Other studies have shown that the
      prior distribution need not be Gaussian Bansal et al. (2024); Heitz et al. (2023). Flow based models
346
      Lipman et al. (2023); Tong et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023a), a new family of generative models,
347
      generalized the mapping to include general source distributions.
348
349   The work most closely aligned with ours is that of Xu et al. (2024) as they considered the learned
350   errors throughout the whole sampling trajectory. They use a sequence of NF blocks to learn a
351
      sequential transformation from Gaussian noise to data, where each block represents a different time-
      step. This requires training the blocks sequentially, as the learned error in each block is accounted for
352
      in the next block, slowing the training process. The sampling process initiates from Gaussian noise,
353
      where the model’s error is not accounted for. In contrast, our work takes into account the model’s
354   prediction error at every step, as we correct the inference time trajectory. Moreover, we improve the
355   recently proposed flow matching instead of NF. For extended related work see Appendix. A.4.

519   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
520
521   Our main insight is that given an imperfect flow model, a trajectory that reaches exactly the data
522   distribution can be computed by reversing its vector field. Based on this insight, we propose a
523   simple algorithm, Marginal Matching, which steers the inference-time trajectory to better align with
524
      the trajectory that accurately reaches the data. We demonstrate superior performance on two datasets
      CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-64, and perform an extensive ablation study.
525
526   Ideally, all generative models would benefit from correction of prediction errors, as such errors are
527   not specific to flow models. A major limitation of our work is the assumption on the reversibility of
528   the vector field, constraining our method from operating on non-reversible models. This limitation
529   may be relaxed for approximately reversible models such as diffusion models (Wallace et al., 2023).
530
      In general, extending our approach to non-reversible models is an interesting future direction.
531   Another aspect is the practical implementation of our theory. In Thm. 3.4 we assumed knowledge
532   of error reductions. In practice, we do not have access to the model’s error reduction (see Sec.3.1).
533   However, we can bound that reduction in the case of score correction (Lemma. 3.7) and propose a
534   bound on the final Wasserstein distance. Future work could explore theoretical bounds also for our
535   classifier-based correction model.
536   Finally, our method requires training an additional model after a flow model has been pre-trained,
537   adding extra compute and parameters. It is worth noting that in all our experiments, this auxiliary
538   model is smaller than the original flow model, and training it takes significantly less time. An
539   alternative approach could involve training the flow and correction models together, either as a single
      model with both flow and correction outputs or as two models trained simultaneously.

759   A.1    O PTIMAL T RANSPORT
760
761   Optimal Transport (OT) is a mathematical framework that addresses the problem of efficiently mov-
762   ing probability mass from one distribution to another while minimizing a certain cost. The central
763
      idea is to find the optimal way to transform one probability distribution into another, considering the
      ”cost” of moving each unit of mass.
764
765   In the context of flow matching, before learning the flow, an optimal transport map is computed
766   between batches of samples from π0 and π1 . This OT step uses the 2-Wasserstein distance as its
767   metric, with a cost function defined as the Euclidean distance ∥x − y∥ between points. The flow
768   is then learned based on these optimally transported samples, resulting in straighter trajectories
769
      (Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024).
770

1076
1077   Practical Considerations of hψ : When designing the learning objective for hψ an important issue
1078
       should be taken into consideration. The pre-trained flow model has established a matching between
1079   each distribution on the forward trajectory pftn and between each distribution on the backward tra-
       jectory pbtn across different time-steps. Additionally, there exists a matching between π̂1 and π1 .


                                                                20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       Through transitivity, this implies a pairing between pftn and pbtn for every time step tn . During the
1081
       training process, samples from pftn and pbtn are accessible. Models that try to establish pairing be-
1082

1151   A.3.2    S CORE M ODEL T RAINING
1152
1153
       Score models are generative AI systems that transform random noise into meaningful data through
1154
       iterative denoising. At their core is the score function - the gradient of the log probability density -
1155
       which guides samples toward higher likelihood regions of a target distribution.
1156
1157   These models utilize varying noise levels (σ) during generation. At high noise levels, samples differ
1158   significantly from the target distribution, but the score function provides stable gradients far from the
1159
       data manifold. At low noise levels, as samples approach the target distribution, the score function
       enables detailed refinement. During generation, the model progressively reduces noise levels while
1160
       following each corresponding score function, establishing a path between random noise and complex
1161
       data distributions.
1162
1163   By assuming the forward marginals are a noisy version of the backward marginals (with a small σ),
1164   a score model can be used to transverse between them.
1165   While sophisticated approaches like annealing score models with time-varying σ are widely used
1166   for generation, they proved unsuitable for our needs. This is due to our inference process that begins
1167   at a forward marginal (or an intermediate point), where we lack information about the current noise
1168   level, precluding the effective use of such models.

1173   A.3.3    ROBUST C LASSIFIER T RAINING
1174
1175
1176   Adversarial Training (AT): Srinivas & Fleuret (2021) demonstrated that the gradients of standard
1177   classifiers can be arbitrarily altered without impacting their cross-entropy loss or accuracy. Building
1178   on this insight, Kawar et al. (2023) proposed using gradients from a robust classifier for guidance,
1179
       rather than those from a conventional classifier. The gradients of robust classifiers are resistant to
       arbitrary manipulation as a results of the adversarial attack used in their loss computation is directly
1180
       dependent on the model’s gradients. An intriguing characteristic of robust classifiers is that their
1181
       gradients have been shown to align well with human perception, as noted by Tsipras et al. (2019).
1182   When robust classifiers are employed to guide x towards a specific class c, they are anticipated to
1183   produce significant features that correspond well with the target class. As a result, the modifications
1184   applied to x are likely to be visually convincing and aligned with human perception of the class
1185   characteristics. Inspired by these works we enhance the classifier with Projected Gradient Descent
1186   (PGD) attack and Adversarial Training (AT) robustification method (Madry et al. (2018)), with the
1187   PGD pseudo-algorithm detailed in Alg. 4. Our findings indicate that adversarial training stabilizes
       gradients and enhances their meaningfulness.

1231
1232   Inverse diffusion – Previous work researched inversion of diffusion models, which are ”approxi-
1233
       mately invertible” models, particularly in the context of image editing. DDIM inversion (Song et al.,
       2021a) method laid the groundwork for this approach, enabling the reversal of the diffusion process
1234
       to obtain latent representations of images by utilizing a deterministic forward process. Building
1235
       upon this foundation, Wallace et al. (2023) improved the efficiency of the inversion by training an
1236   encoder network to directly map images to their corresponding noise representations in the diffu-
1237   sion process. Furthermore, Mokady et al. (2023) enabled precise edits on real images through a
1238   text-guided approach, using a null-text optimization to find an optimal noise that, when denoised,
1239   produces the target image. Zhang & Kleijn (2023) improved the accuracy of the inversion process
1240   by combining forward and backward trajectories to minimize approximation errors, while Pan et al.
1241   (2023) focused on enhancing both the speed and quality of image editing operations by iteratively
       refining the inverted latent code.
