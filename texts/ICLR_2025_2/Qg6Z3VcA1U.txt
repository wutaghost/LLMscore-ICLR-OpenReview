009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mobile device control agents can largely enhance user interactions and productiv-
012            ity by automating daily tasks. However, despite growing interest in developing
013            practical agents, the absence of a commonly adopted benchmark in this area makes
014            it challenging to quantify scientific progress. In this work, we introduce B-MoCA:
015            a novel benchmark with interactive environments for evaluating and developing
016            mobile device control agents. To create a realistic benchmark, we develop B-MoCA
017            based on the Android operating system and define 131 common daily tasks. Impor-
018
               tantly, we incorporate a randomization feature that changes the configurations of
               mobile devices, including user interface layouts and language settings, to assess
019
               generalization performance. We benchmark diverse agents, including agents em-
020
               ploying large language models (LLMs) or multi-modal LLMs as well as agents
021            trained with imitation learning using human expert demonstrations. While these
022            agents demonstrate proficiency in executing straightforward tasks, their poor per-
023            formance on complex tasks highlights significant opportunities for future research
024            to improve effectiveness.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Autonomous agents controlling mobile devices have great potential benefits. For example, these
030   agents can improve the accessibility of user interactions, especially for users with physical disabil-
031   ities or those facing challenges in operating devices. Additionally, they can boost productivity by
032   automating daily tasks. Such advantages have led to increased interest in developing practical agents
033
      for mobile device control. Various approaches have been introduced, including agents based on
      large language models (LLMs) (Wen et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2023) and agents trained with human
034
      demonstrations (Sun et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Rawles et al., 2023). These innovations aim to create
035
      assistive agents capable of understanding device screen layouts and manipulating user interfaces (UI)
036   to execute human commands.
037
038   Despite recent progress in developing mobile device control agents based on real systems, such as
039
      Android emulators (Toyama et al., 2021; Shvo et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), prior works often
      overlook several important properties. One primary aspect is testing the generalization ability across
040
      diverse device configurations, which is crucial in deploying agents in real devices. Moreover, practical
041
      tasks essential for life (such as setting an alarm or making emergency calls) are often neglected
042   because of the challenges in defining a wide range of such tasks with robust success criteria in
043   various device settings. The lack of a unified benchmark encompassing these important properties
044   has impeded scientific progress in this field.
045
      In this work, we introduce B-MoCA: a Benchmark designed for evaluating Mobile device Control
046
      Agents across diverse configurations (see Figure 1). For real-system interactive evaluation, B-MoCA
047
      is developed based on the Android operating system. A key feature of B-MoCA is supporting
048   numerous customization, designed to mirror diverse device configurations in real-use cases, including
049   variations in icon placements, sizes, wallpapers, languages, and device types. Utilizing this feature,
050   one can easily create diverse environments with various configurations to evaluate the agents’ gener-
051   alization ability. Additionally, we define 131 practical tasks grounded in realistic scenarios, such as
052   opening specific applications, initializing searches over the web, and adjusting device settings. To en-
053   sure reliable evaluation across diverse configurations, B-MoCA provides rule-based success detectors
      that automatically signal task completion during the agents’ interactions over the environments.

136   2.2   P ROBLEM FORMULATION
137
138   We formulate the device control task as a sequential decision-making problem, where an agent
139   interacts with an environment (i.e., an Android emulator). Formally, given a task instruction c, the
140   agent receives an observation ot and takes an action at based on its policy π(at |ot , c) at each timestep
141
      t. The environment returns a success signal rt and then transitions to the next observation ot+1 .
142   Observations, which capture the UI elements, can be represented as either screen pixels, screen
143   descriptions derived from the Android view hierarchy, or a combination of both. The action space
144   includes a set of screen-touching actions. In B-MoCA, we support both continuous and discrete
145   actions. Continuous actions are defined as dual-gesture actions, similar to Rawles et al. (2023). Each
146   dual-gesture action comprises a pair of (x, y) screen locations. A dual-gesture action is identified
147
      as to tap when the two locations are identical within a specified threshold, and as to swipe when
      the distance between the two locations exceeds this threshold. Also, agents can perform to press
148
      navigation buttons (i.e., back, home, and overview) by touching the corresponding locations on the
149
      screen. Discrete actions are defined as direct interactions with specific screen locations (such as the
150   center of a UI element’s bounding box or predefined locations), swiping in specified directions, or
151   pressing individual buttons. We note that our benchmark supports text-based actions, enabling the
152   utilization of both LLMs and MLLMs (see Section 3.1 for details).
153
      We refer readers for further details on the environment implementation to Appendix A.1.

177   Task completion is determined by a rule-based success detector that relies on three sources: the
178   system information, app data, and attributes of UI elements in the Android view hierarchy. To
179
      monitor these sources, we have developed a set of interfaces using tools like Android Debug Bridge
      (ADB) and Appium. The success detector identifies the successful completion based on pre-defined
180
      criteria within these information sources. For example, it automatically detects the matching regular
181
      expression in the system logs or checks whether the attributes in specific UI elements (e.g., the
182   ‘checked’ attribute of the checkbox) are arranged correctly. The success signal has the value of +1
183   when the task is completed, and 0 otherwise. An episode terminates as a success if the success
184   detector signals completion, or as a failure if the agent exceeds a maximum number of steps without
185   meeting the criteria.

187   2.4   E NVIRONMENT RANDOMIZATION
188
189   In mobile device control, developing agents that can generalize across various device setups is crucial.
190   To evaluate their generalization ability, B-MoCA incorporates a randomization feature that changes
191
      icon placements and sizes, wallpapers, languages, and device types. Users can select a device type
      from a device list that includes popular models like Pixel 3, Pixel 4, Pixel 6, and WGXA Tablet. They
192
      can also specify the locales to set the language and region, choose wallpapers from a selection of
193
      custom images, and activate dark mode for further environmental variation. Moreover, the sizes and
194   locations of application icons can be customized to simulate real-world usage patterns.
195
196   Using randomization features, we create 45 unique environments in B-MoCA, with examples shown
197
      in Figure 2. To assess the generalization ability, we divide these environments into two sets: 35 for
      training and 10 for testing. We employ domain randomization (Tobin et al., 2017) to train agents,
198
      enabling them to perform tasks robustly across diverse device configurations. We then evaluate the
199
      performance on test environments, which include unseen device setups. A detailed list of environment
200   device configurations we prepare is available in Appendix A.2.

      3.2      O PEN - SOURCE MODELS
243
244   Despite the promising results of closed-sourced foundation models, leveraging them presents several
245   challenges such as the difficulties in fine-tuning. In our benchmark analysis, we also explore
246   agents employing customized models, named custom agents, using either a fine-tuned open-source
247   foundation model (i.e., Llama-3) or modules trained from scratch. These custom agents process task
248   instructions and screen layout descriptions in text form to produce text-based actions, similar to agents
249
      using closed-source LLMs or MLLMs. We fine-tune these models based on actions collected from
      human experts, with prompts that specify their general roles and relevant task-specific information.1
250
251   The custom agents equipped with an encoder using a vision and language model (VLM encoder),
252   with the policy denoted by πθ , take task instructions c and screen images ot as inputs, and output
253   discrete actions at . Input embeddings, extracted using a pre-trained VLM encoder (Yang et al.,
254   2023a), are processed through a transformer module (Vaswani et al., 2017) to generate actions. Each
255
      action is a vector of size 385, where the first 378 values correspond to tapping pre-defined locations
      (14×27 bins) on the screen, four values to swiping directions (up, down, right, left), and the last three
256
      values to pressing buttons (back, home, overview). For further details on the network architecture,
257
      we refer readers to Appendix C.3. We train custom agents with VLM encoder from scratch using BC
258   or RL. For BC, agents are optimized to imitate the human expert demonstrations D = {(ot , a∗t , c)}
259   by minimizing the cross entropy loss LBC :

362   4.2   M AIN RESULTS
363
      Figure 5 shows the success rates of LLM agents on all 131 tasks, and Figure 6 displays the success
364   rates of LLM agents, MLLM agents, and custom agents on the six challenging representative
365   tasks. The agents employing the foundation model complete simple tasks with high performance by
366   leveraging their pre-trained base knowledge. For example, LLM agents using GPT-4o achieve success
367   rates higher than 80% on the Alarm(simple) task. However, their performances significantly
368   decrease as the tasks become complex (e.g., significant degradation on the Alarm(complex) task).
369   On the other hand, custom agents using policies trained from scratch imitate the behaviors of experts
370   and exhibit average success rates greater than or equal to 40% on all six challenging representative
371   tasks. However, these agents still show a lack of generalization ability (less than 60%) on these tasks.
372
      Similarly, custom agents using fine-tuned Llama-3 show an extreme discrepancy of proficiencies
      across the six challenging tasks. The shortcomings of these baselines call for new algorithms for
373
      building mobile device control agents.
374
375   We provide more remarks on each agent type below.
376
377   Challenges for agents using ready-made foundation models The LLM agents show remarkable
      performances in controlling mobile devices, even in zero-shot as shown in Figure 5. However, we


                                                                    7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      witness several limitations of agents employing the foundation models, across both LLM agents
379   and MLLM agents. First, LLM and MLLM agents frequently hallucinate task completion. For
380   example, on the Calculator task, the agents often conclude that a task is complete before entering
381   all the requested strings (e.g., entering ‘cos(18)’ instead of ‘cos(180)’). Second, these agents face
382   difficulties with long-horizon tasks that require multiple interactions. For instance, they frequently
383   make mistakes when typing the sequence of numbers on the Call task.
384
385
      Effects of environment randomization on LLM agents The success rates of LLM agents decrease
386   as the environments are randomized, as displayed in Figure 5. In the "Test Env 101" environment, we
387   find that both LLM agents using GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-pro make simple mistakes when the icon
388   locations are randomized. For example, the agent employing GPT-4o taps the Walmart icon to open
389   the Wikipedia application, if the Wikipedia application is not available on the home screen. In the
390   "Test Env 105" environment, we observe more performance degradation. To be specific, the agents
391   suffer from handling three main randomizations: fewer icons on the home screen, understanding the
392   text descriptions of UI elements in Korean, and a larger icon size setting. We include a more rigorous
393   analysis of the effect of each environmental feature in Section 4.3.
394
395   Comaprison between LLM agents and MLLM agents In our experiments, gains from additional
396   image inputs are marginal or even detrimental, as shown by comparing performances with and without
397   additional screenshot inputs (see red vs. dark green and orange vs. light green). We expect that this is
398   due to domain gaps in visual inputs. Additionally, we hypothesize that multi-modal agents suffer from
399   the increased length of input sequence associated with additional image tokens. A similar observation
400   was made in recent work involving agents that control desktop computers (Xie et al., 2024). These
401
      results indicate the remaining headroom in leveraging multi-modal inputs more effectively.
402
403   Differences between closed-source and open-source LLMs In the setting of using the ready-made
404   foundation models, closed-source LLMs like GPT-4o (red) outperform open-sourced LLMs like
405   Llama-3 (yellow) across all six representative challenging tasks, as shown in Figure 6. Notably, the
406   ability to understand current screen layouts significantly differs between them. For example, when
407   we ask LLMs to describe what they observe in the input, agents using closed-sourced LLMs like
408
      GPT-4o typically produce more detailed texts, such as a precise list of application icons. Furthermore,
      LLM agents using GPT-4o exhibit better planning abilities. For instance, when the target application
409
      is not visible on the current home screen, LLM agents using GPT-4o often swipe the screen to
410
      navigate or explore the app list menu. In contrast, LLM agents using Llama-3 tend to tap irrelevant
411   applications visible in the current view. The results of evaluations across all 131 tasks are included in
412   Appendix D.4.
413
414
415
      On custom agents using fine-tuned LLMs We observe that custom agents leveraging fine-tuned
      Llama-3 models achieve superior performance on several complex tasks, exhibiting greater proficiency
416
      compared to agents using standard Llama-3 across all tasks. These agents successfully complete the
417
      Alarm tasks (both simple and complex), on the test environments demonstrating high similarities
418   with the training environments. Their proficiencies in many tasks reveal the benefits of fine-tuning
419   LLMs to build agents. However, their failure on the Language and Wikipedia tasks in most of
420   the test environments demonstrates a clear limitation, especially on the generalization ability.
421
422
423
      Generalization ability of custom agents In our experiments, the custom agents achieve reasonable
      performances in many complex tasks, even where agents using closed-source foundation models fail.
424
      The custom agents successfully imitate expert behaviors in navigating applications and manipulating
425
      diverse UI elements in training environments. However, they still show limitations on generalization
426   ability. The agents using fine-tuned Llama-3, for example, fail to generalize their behaviors to
427   environments having a language setting of Korean and a device setting of Tablet. Also, the agents
428   equipped with VLM encoder largely degrade in test environments, while achieving high success
429   rates in training environments (e.g., higher than 90%; see Appendix D.5 for training performances).
430   Specifically, they struggle with tasks involving severe visual changes induced by unseen device
431   configurations. These observations highlight the need to develop more efficient algorithms that
      improve generalization against changes in device configurations.

      5     R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Foundation models for decision-making system Inspired by the strong emergent properties of
489   foundation models (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022), many researches have adopted LLMs to
490   develop decision-making system (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023). In robot learning, for example,
491   LLMs have been widely equipped for reasoning, planning, manipulation, and navigation (Driess et al.,
492   2023; Liang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). Furthermore, agents with LLMs have shown capabilities
493   of performing interesting tasks in numerous simulated worlds, including game environments (Wang
494   et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024) and virtual reality (Qian et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024). In recent days,
495   focusing on practicalness, solving computer tasks with foundation models has also been actively
496   explored (Nakano et al., 2021; Furuta et al., 2023). We further study the abilities of foundation
497
      models to control mobile devices toward assistive agents in real life.
498
499
      Developing assistive agent for device control For agents that effectively understand and manipu-
      late the UI elements, a large body of work has leveraged structural information, such as document
500
      object model in HTML or Android view hierarchy (Branavan et al., 2010; Gur et al., 2019). In
501
      addition, methods for equipping agents with the ability to understand information-rich screen images
502   have been widely investigated, mainly with vision-based reinforcement learning (Liu et al., 2018;
503   Humphreys et al., 2022; Shaw et al., 2023). Recently, diverse strategies to build device control
504   agents with foundation models are introduced, including prompting methods (Wen et al., 2023; Kim
505   et al., 2023), instruction-tuning (Furuta et al., 2023), fine-tuning with images (Zhan & Zhang, 2023;
506   Hong et al., 2023), and visual prompting (Yan et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b). Here, we present an
507   elaborate analysis of the main methods for building mobile device control agents.
508
509   Benchmark for decision-making agents There have been continuous efforts to build reliable bench-
510   marks for sequential decision-making in video games (Bellemare et al., 2013), locomotion (Brockman
511   et al., 2016), and robotic manipulation (James et al., 2020). Lately, researchers have proposed bench-
512
      marks for solving device control tasks, viewing it as another decision-making problem. For example,
      Yao et al. (2022) and Zhou et al. (2024) have presented benchmark simulating web platforms, while
513
      Toyama et al. (2021), Shvo et al. (2021), and Zhang et al. (2023) have suggested RL environments
514
      adopting Android emulators. In this work, inspired by special-purpose benchmarks quantifying the
515   generalization ability of the agents (Cobbe et al., 2020; Stone et al., 2021), we newly propose a
516   benchmark with a randomization feature.
517
518
519   6     C ONCLUSION
520
521   We present B-MoCA, a new benchmark designed for evaluating mobile device control agents. Our
522
      benchmark provides diverse tasks applicable to everyday routines and environments that simulate
      numerous device configurations. We conduct extensive experiments and demonstrate that B-MoCA
523
      can serve as a standardized platform for developing different types of agents in a unified setting.
524
      Finally, we mention several limitations and promising future directions of this work:
525
526       • Tasks with text typing: While agents can input text by touching the soft keyboard on the screen, it
527         demands excessively long interactions. We plan to include tasks requiring text typing, such as
528         web search or e-mail sending, with advanced interfaces in the future.
529       • Open-ended tasks and reward modeling: Since the ADB-based success detector does not capture
530         the semantics of agent behaviors, tasks with ambiguous success criteria are hard to evaluate.
531         Alternatively, we believe employing the reward model learned from demonstrations (Fan et al.,
532         2022) can be used for integrating open-ended tasks.
533       • More on agents with foundation models: LLMs can be employed in different ways, such as
534         using them as a high-level planner to operate a set of pre-defined APIs (Chen & Li, 2024) or
535         neural network policies (Ahn et al., 2022) as low-level actors. Also, we note that fine-tuning
536         LLMs is highly promising, as training custom agents with demonstrations results in compatible
537         performances, leaving it as future work.
538
539   Toward practical mobile device control agents, we hope that B-MoCA stands as a valuable platform
      with helpful resources for innovative breakthroughs.

       Benchmarking Mobile Device Control Agent across Diverse Configurations
760
761   A     E NVIRONMENT DETAILS
762
763   A.1    E NVIRONMENT IMPLEMENTATION AND INTERFACE
764
765   Environment B-MoCA is based on Android OS for real-system interactive evaluation. The
766
      environment is simulated with Android virtual devices, containing the device hardware profile,
      system image, storage area, and other relevant properties. The dynamics of the environments, such as
767
      the transition rules, are governed by Android OS and applications.
768
769   Each environment is represented as an Android device, running on top of the Android emulator. To be
770   specific, we define each environment as a snapshot, a stored image of the Android virtual device. Each
771   snapshot is built by saving an image of the target device after the configurations. These configurations
772
      include installing third-party applications and randomizing the features of environments. The features
      of the environments we randomize encompass placing icons in random locations, setting dots per
773
      inch (DPI), modifying wallpapers, and changing the language. During the configuration, adjusting
774
      several device settings for accurate evaluation, such as changing the database of applications, is also
775   conducted.
776
777   To facilitate interactions between the environment and agents, we develop a set of interfaces on top of
778
      the Python library AndroidEnv (Toyama et al., 2021). The interfaces we develop encompass various
      functionalities: to provide the task descriptions in text to the agents, to capture screenshots of the
779
      virtual device, to provide the Android view hierarchy in XML format and parse the text description
780
      of the screen, to extract dual-gesture actions from text-based actions, and to deliver the dual-gesture
781   action to the Android emulator.
782
783   Interaction frequency The Android emulators run asynchronously, independent of the agent that
784   interacts with the environments. However, this asynchronicity between the agent and the environment
785   may cause several issues such as incomplete transition of the environments or delayed success
786   signals. To alleviate the issue, we adjust the interaction frequency between agents and environments.
787   Specifically, this adjustment is operated by forcing the agent to wait a pre-defined time before fetching
788   the screen information from the environment. In our experiments, we fix the interaction frequency
789   during evaluation to be 1/3Hz across all types of agents, except Instagram with longer latency (e.g.,
790   1/30Hz) as locale change requires additional loading. We also allow users to adjust this interaction
791
      frequency.
792
793
      Observation space The observation space is comprised of either a screen image, a text description
      of the screen in XML formats based on the Android view hierarchy, or both. The screen images
794
      are used for multi-modal large language model (MLLM) agents and custom agents. Each image is
795
      resized into a resolution of 256 × 512 for MLLM agents and 128 × 256 for custom agents.
796
797   The text descriptions are used for agents with LLMs and MLLMs. To build the text description, the
798   Android debug bridge (ADB) UI Automator is employed for acquiring the Android view hierarchy
799
      in XML format. A pre-defined screen translator, then, converts the information of UI elements in
      the XML file into a set of text descriptions of UI elements. The description includes a numeric tag
800
      and details of the UI elements, including the class name or content descriptions. Additionally, we
801
      provide attributes if the UI elements are checked or selected and (optionally) the bounding box x-y
802   coordinates specifying the location of the elements, such as the slider interface. In our interface, the
803   parser captures the descriptions of all the nodes in the Android view hierarchy, not specified to the
804   leaf nodes.
805
806   Action space The action space of the agents is defined as either continuous or discrete actions.
807   The continuous action, here, refers to a dual-gesture action {a| a = (ytouch , xtouch , ylift , xlift ) ∈ R4 },
808   similar to Rawles et al. (2023). Each value of dual-gesture action a is normalized to be in between
809   [−1, 1] with respect to the screen resolutions. The former two values specify the location of the
      screen to touch, while the latter two values determine the location of the screen to lift. This definition


                                                           15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      enables interpreting useful actions in digital device control, i.e., tapping or swiping the screens, in a
811   precise and compressive manner. Also, our interface allows pressing the navigator buttons available
812   by touching the screen to support the essential actions for manipulating Android devices.
813
814
      The discrete action, on the other hand, refers to the pre-defined set of actions for tapping, swiping,
      and button-pressing. For agents generating text-based actions, the discrete action is defined to be each
815
      action option (i.e., callable function). For agents trained from scratch, we define each action to be a
816
      vector with the size of 378, where the first 378 values correspond to tapping pre-defined locations
817   (14×27 bins) on the screen, four values to swiping directions (up, down, right, left), and the last three
818   values to pressing buttons (back, home, overview).
819
820
      For dual-gesture actions, we implement an interface that determines whether the action is a tap, swipe,
      or pressing of navigation buttons i.e., back, home, and overview. The action parsing interface converts
821
      the action into taps, swipes, or pressing buttons following the rule as follows:
822
823     • The action is tapping, if d((xtouch , ytouch ), (xlift , ylift )) < threshold
824
             – The tapping is to press BACK button, if (xtouch , ytouch ) = (0.95, 0.22)
825
             – The tapping is to press HOME button, if (xtouch , ytouch ) = (0.95, 0.50)
826
827
             – The tapping is to press OVERVIEW button, if (xtouch , ytouch ) = (0.95, 0.78)
828     • The action is swiping, if d((xtouch , ytouch ), (xlift , ylift )) ≥ threshold,
829
830
      where the threshold value is defined as 0.14. This value is adjustable by users, while we find that
      the value of 0.14 ensures proper interactions over UI elements, e.g., tapping the target application
831
      icon, in all of our experiments. These specific values are tested to be consistent across different device
832
      types, ensuring that the positions correspond to the correct buttons in all B-MoCA environments.
833
834   For agents with foundation models, we further define action converter that translate text-based actions
835   into legal emulator actions. Following the action space definition, the action options are designed
836
      to be either dual-gesture actions or discrete actions. We prompt the LLM agents to output actions
      among six possible options: dual-gesture action, tap, swipe, press(“HOME”), press(“BACK”), and
837
      press(“OVERVIEW”). The action converter translate the text-based actions into the legal actions as
838
      below:
839
840     • For the dual-gesture action, it converts the text action into the four floating points by rounding
841       each value into the second decimal point.
842
        • For tap actions, the agent outputs an integer value specifying the numeric tag assigned to the
843       UI element. Given the tapping action with a numeric tag, it converts the action into a tapping
844       dual-gesture action with the bounding box information of the chosen UI element.
845
        • For swipe actions, a direction ‘up’, ‘down’, ‘left’ and ‘right’ is converted into a corresponding
846
          dual-gesture action with the value of (0.8, 0.5, 0.2, 0.5), (0.2, 0.5, 0.8, 0.5), (0.5, 0.2, 0.5, 0.8),
847       and (0.5, 0.8, 0.5, 0.2), respectively.
848
        • For the action press(“HOME”), press(“BACK”), and press(“OVERVIEW”), it converts the
849
          actions to tap the corresponding screen location.
850
851   During the evaluation, we ignore the action in the wrong format by skipping the transition of the
852   environments but penalizing the agents by incrementing the steps taken.

1048   B.1   L IST OF DAILY TASKS
1049
       B-MoCA presents 131 daily tasks that are common in everyday life. The tasks are designed to operate
1050
       in diverse environments seamlessly and cover commonly used applications. Daily tasks effectively
1051
       simulate a wide range of essential skills for mobile device control problems, such as manipulating UI
1052   elements (including application icons, checkboxes, and sliders), and can be employed for evaluating
1053   mobile device control agents’ capabilities in performing tasks that mirror our daily activities. We
1054   have defined success criteria for each task by analyzing significant changes in the environment (i.e.,
1055   three key information sources) observed during task completion in human expert demonstrations. We
1056   also set the maximum step limits, which are set for the rigorous evaluation of the agents’ proficiency
1057   in each task. From Table 7 to Table 9, we include the detailed list of tasks with the detailed success
1058   criteria.
1059   The source of success criteria are threefold: the system information, app data, and attributes of the UI
1060   elements in the Android view hierarchy. System information includes system log and system setting.
1061   For the system log, the success detector checks the system log matches with the criteria defined in a
1062   regular expression. The log that matches the criteria should satisfy two parts: a filter specifying the
1063   target application or activity (denoted as [...] at the success criteria column in Table 7) and a regex
1064   specifying the detail of the log (denoted as "..." at the success criteria column in Table 7). For the
1065   system setting, the success detector utilizes ADB commands to retrieve specific information related
1066
       to fields. The success detector, then, parses the results of these commands with a regular expression
       to extract the necessary system information and check it (denoted as {"(adb command)": "(regular
1067
       expression)"} at the success criteria column in Table 7). For attributes of the UI elements, the success
1068
       detector checks if certain attributes of specified UI elements satisfy the pre-defined conditions. We
1069   define the condition with UI elements having certain IDs (denoted as [...] at the success criteria
1070   column in Table 7) and the status as the value of attributes (denoted as {"(attribute key)": "(status
1071   value)"} at the success criteria column in Table 7). For app data, the success detector checks if the
1072   values of particular attributes in either the database or shared preferences (used to store simple data
1073   instead of a database as an XML file) meet the criteria. We define the condition as selected attributes
1074   from the database or shared preference (path denoted as [...] at the success criteria column in Table 7)
1075   to be specific values (denoted as {"(attribute key)": "(status value)"} at the success criteria column in
1076   Table 7).

       C.1   OVERVIEW OF AGENTS EMPLOYING FOUNDATION MODELS
1569
1570   We provide an overall workflow (illustrated in Figure 10) of agents employing foundation models,
1571   especially with LLM. To employ LLM to develop the agents, we build a prompt based on task
1572   instruction and text-based observation. The text-based observation describes the screen layout
1573   information. We provide the prompt to the LLM and obtain the response. The response includes the
1574   text-based action chosen from a set of action options (which is provided in the prompt) by the LLM.
1575
       The action converter extracts the legal action from the text-based action. The environment which is
       realized as an Android emulator, then, processes the provided action.

       C.3   A RCHITECTURE DESIGN FOR CUSTOM AGENTS TRAINED WITH VLM ENCODER
1675
1676   The network architecture for custom agents is composed of three components: encoder, attention
1677   module, and action head. Given the task instruction c and the screen image ot ∈ R3×256×128 at each
1678   timestep t, custom agents generate a screen-touching action at ∈ R385 . Among the screen-touching
1679   action at , the first values correspond to tapping pre-defined locations (14×27 bins) on the screen, four
1680   values to swiping directions (up, down, right, left), and the last three values to pressing buttons (back,
1681   home, overview).
1682   Custom agents use visual and text encoders to represent screen images ot and task instruction c,
1683   respectively. The visual encoder embeds visual feature eot ∈ Rd from the observation ot , and the
1684   text encoder extracts features ec ∈ Rd from the task instruction c. For the visual encoder, we use
1685   EfficientNet-b0 (Tan & Le, 2019) pre-trained with ImageNet followed by an adaption layer using
1686   a fully connected layer to adapt the output channel to hidden dimension d (Liu et al., 2023). For
1687   the text encoder, we use a pre-trained text encoder of Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer (Zhan &
1688   Zhang, 2023) which is trained with Android-in-the-wild (Rawles et al., 2023), which is a dataset
1689   composed of demonstrations for solving Android device control problems. The text encoder is kept
1690
       frozen during the training process. The hidden dimension d is set to value of 768 for both visual
       embedding and text embedding.
1691
1692   The attention module, then, fuses the visual feature eot and text feature ec into a single vision-
1693   language embedding efused ∈ Rd . Especially, we use multi-head attention layer (Vaswani et al., 2017)
1694   for cross-attention, with ec given as query and eot given as key and value. Given the fused feature
1695   efused , the action heads predict the action at , by applying the three fully connected (FC) layers with
1696
       the size of (1024, 1024, 385), respectively.

1828
1829
       Algorithm To train custom agents trained with RL, we use double deep Q-network
       (DDQN) (Van Hasselt et al., 2016) and proximal policy optimization (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017).
1830
       DDQN trains the two optimal Q functions Q1 and Q2 by minimizing the TD loss for the agent
1831
       data sampled from the replay buffer D. As an example, we write the objective for Q1 (and its
1832   parameterization Qθ1 ) as follows:
1833
                                         LTD (Q1 ) = E(s,a,r,s′ )∼D [(r + γQθ¯1 (s′ , a∗ ) − Qθ1 (s, a)2 ].
1834
1835   where a∗ = arg maxa′ Qθ¯2 (s, a) and θ̄ denotes the target network for θ, which in practice is replaced
       by the moving average of θ. Especially, when calculating the TD target, we swap the maximizing


                                                                        34
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1836
       actions of the next state action values between Q1 and Q2 to prevent the overestimation of the values
1837   as presented in (Hasselt, 2010). To balance the exploration and the exploitation during the training,
1838   we employ the ϵ-greedy technique to sample actions for collecting the training data. For the critic
1839   network architecture, we adopt the same visual encoder and text encoder backbone as the custom
1840   agent (using VLM encoder) and extract the fused feature efused . Finally, we add three FC layers
1841   comprising the dimensions of (1024, 1024, 385) to output the Q values for each action.
1842
       PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) builds upon the policy gradient method (Sutton & Barto, 2018) and uses
1843   a clipped surrogate objective. To be detailed, the objective is given by
1844
1845                                        LPPO (θ) = Eπold [min (clip(r(θ), 1 − ϵ, 1 + ϵ) At , r(θ)At )] ,
1846
1847
       where At denotes the estimator for the future expected return and r(θ) = πθ (a|s)/πold (a|s) cor-
       responds to an importance sampling ratio between the current and the previous policy. Following
1848
       (Schulman et al., 2017), we use generalized advantage estimator (GAE) (Schulman et al., 2016) in
1849
       place of At . We use the same network architecture as the custom agent (using VLM encoder) for
1850   implementing πθ . For the value network V , we apply three FC layers that follow the size of (1024,
1851   1024, 1) using the feature efused produced by the pre-trained backbone.

1853   Task and environment We experiment RL with a sparse success signal using a task with the
1854   instruction “open Gmail” (denoted as Gmail) and Language task. We train at 10 training environ-
1855   ments, whose IDs are 000, 001, 002, 003, 004, 005, 007, 008, 021, 022 and then evaluate under 10
1856   test environments. We also experiment with dense reward setup using the task with the instruction
1857   “call 911” (denoted as Call 911) task under a training environment with id 000.

1859   Training procedure For DDQN, we update the critic network by sampling 1/4 transitions from the
1860   replay buffer that stores the successful history and the last from the failure buffer in every 5 episodes.

1862   optimizer with a learning rate of 2e-4. We use the hyperparameters of ϵ = 0.1, γ = 0.9, and λ = 0.9
1863   for both actor and critic unless otherwise specified. Each training is conducted on a single NVIDIA
1864   RTX A6000 GPU and Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6426Y CPU. We note that the training procedure can
1865
       be accelerated by vectorizing the training environments.
