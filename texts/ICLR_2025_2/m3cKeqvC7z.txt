009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Mamba, with its State Space Model (SSM), offers a more computationally ef-
012            ficient solution than Transformers for long-range dependency modeling. How-
013            ever, there is still a debate about its effectiveness in high-resolution 3D medi-
014            cal image segmentation. In this study, we present a comprehensive investiga-
015            tion into Mamba’s capabilities in 3D medical image segmentation by tackling
016
               three pivotal questions: Can Mamba replace Transformers? Can it elevate multi-
               scale representation learning? Is complex scanning necessary to unlock its full
017
               potential? We evaluate Mamba’s performance across three large public bench-
018
               marks—AMOS, TotalSegmentator, and BraTS. Our findings reveal that Ulike-
019            Mamba, a U-shape Mamba-based network, consistently surpasses UlikeTrans, a
020            U-shape Transformer-based network, particularly when enhanced with custom-
021            designed 3D depthwise convolutions, boosting accuracy and computational effi-
022            ciency. Further, our proposed multi-scale Mamba block demonstrates superior
023            performance in capturing both fine-grained details and global context, especially
024            in complex segmentation tasks, surpassing Transformer-based counterparts. We
025            also critically assess complex scanning strategies, finding that simpler methods
026            often suffice, while our Tri-scan approach delivers notable advantages in the most
027
               challenging scenarios. By integrating these advancements, we introduce a new
               network for 3D medical image segmentation, positioning Mamba as a transforma-
028
               tive force that outperforms leading models such as nnUNet, CoTr, and U-Mamba,
029
               offering competitive accuracy with superior computational efficiency. This study
030            provides key insights into Mamba’s unique advantages, paving the way for more
031            efficient and accurate approaches to 3D medical imaging. All code used in the
032            experiments will be made publicly available.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Volumetric medical image segmentation, which involves extracting 3D regions like organs, lesions,
038   and tissues from modalities such as CT and MRI scans, is crucial for clinical applications like lesion
039
      contouring, diagnosis, and surgical planning. These tasks require not only local feature extraction
      but also the ability to capture long-range dependencies across entire volumes, which is vital for
040
      understanding the relationships between distant anatomical structures. While convolutional neural
041
      networks (CNNs), particularly U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015), have been foundational in 3D
042   medical image segmentation (Zhou et al., 2019; Isensee et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2020; Ye et al.,
043   2023; Yu et al., 2020; Valanarasu et al., 2021), their limited receptive fields and locality biases hinder
044   their capacity to model global context effectively. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) address
045   this with dynamic self-attention mechanisms, but their computational demands are impractical for
046   handling large-scale, high-resolution 3D data.
047
      With the introduction of the State Space Model (SSM), Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) offers a promising
048   alternative for modeling long-range dependencies in 3D medical image segmentation. Unlike Trans-
049   formers, Mamba achieves higher inference throughput and scales linearly with sequence length,
050   making it a more computationally efficient solution. This efficiency makes Mamba particularly
051   well-suited for the demands of 3D medical imaging, where high-resolution volumetric data requires
052   both precision and speed to process large-scale structures effectively. Inspired by Mamba’s suc-
053   cess, a burgeoning body of work has sought to leverage its advantages for vision tasks, pioneering
      efforts such as Vision Mamba (ViM) (Zhu et al., 2024) and VMamba (Liu et al., 2024b). These


                                                          1

054
      models employ multi-scan strategies, replacing the vanilla Mamba’s single-scan approach, to al-
055   low long-range dependencies to manifest in multiple directions, improving the model’s ability to
056   capture spatial relationships in complex image data. As a result, several studies have explored re-
057   placing Transformers with Mamba blocks in 3D medical image segmentation. Notably, works like
058   U-Mamba (Ma et al., 2024), SegMamba (Xing et al., 2024) and SwinUMamba (Liu et al., 2024a)
059   have successfully integrated Mamba blocks as plugin modules within CNN architectures, achieving
060   promising performance across various biomedical segmentation datasets. However, these efforts pri-
061   marily demonstrate Mamba’s feasibility without fully exploring its broader potential or its benefits.
062   To address this gap, in this work, we use three challenging 3D medical image segmentation bench-
063   marks (i.e., AMOS (Ji et al., 2022), TotalSegmentator (Wasserthal et al., 2023), and BraTS (Baid
064   et al., 2021)) to conduct an in-depth exploration of Mamba’s impact on 3D medical image segmen-
065   tation, providing valuable insights for future research. Our investigation focuses on three aspects:
066
      Mamba’s ability to replace Transformers We aim to evaluate whether Mamba networks can re-
067   place Transformer-based architectures for long-range dependency modeling in 3D medical image
068   segmentation, focusing on segmentation accuracy and computational efficiency. To this end, we
069   designed two models: a Mamba-based network (UlikeMamba) and a Transformer-based network
070   (UlikeTrans), both following a U-shaped encoder-decoder structure. Notably, we replace the orig-
071   inal 1D depthwise convolutions (DWConv) (Chollet, 2017) in Mamba with 3D DWConv to better
072   preserve volumetric data’s spatial coherence. Our results show that UlikeMamba outperforms Ulike-
073   Trans in both accuracy and efficiency, especially with the 3D Mamba layer, while also avoiding the
074   Out of Memory (OOM) issues faced by UlikeTrans.
075   Mamba’s capacity to enhance multi-scale representation learning This section delves deeper
076   into Mamba’s potential for long-term dependency modeling to enhance multi-scale representation
077   learning, a critical factor in achieving accurate 3D medical image segmentation. Successful volu-
078   metric segmentation requires the ability to capture both fine-grained details (such as small lesions or
079   subtle tissue changes) and broader anatomical structures (such as large organs like the liver, heart,
080   or kidneys). We design and implement four distinct multi-scale modeling schemes, and our re-
081
      sults show that Mamba-based models excel at capturing and integrating multi-scale features. These
      models consistently demonstrate superior performance, especially in complex tasks like TotalSeg-
082
      mentator, which involves segmenting 117 anatomical structures, proving Mamba to be a versatile
083
      and robust solution for challenging 3D medical image segmentation scenarios.
084
085   Whether complex multi-way scanning strategies are necessary? Mamba’s parallelized selective
086   scan operation, designed for one-dimensional data, faces challenges when adapted to visual tasks.
087
      Many works, like Vision Mamba (Zhu et al., 2024) and VMamba (Liu et al., 2024b), introduce multi-
      way scanning mechanisms to preserve spatial coherence in vision tasks. To determine whether these
088
      complex scanning strategies are necessary for 3D medical image segmentation, we evaluate exist-
089
      ing methods—single-scan (forward) and dual-scan (forward+backward)—and introduce two new
090   approaches: dual-scan (forward+random) and Tri-scan (left-right, up-down, front-back). Dual-scan
091   (forward+backward) offers minimal improvement due to strong structural priors in medical data.
092   While dual-scan (forward+random) may capture complex dependencies, it risks distorting these pri-
093   ors, compromising segmentation precision. Tri-scan delivers the best performance by preserving
094   comprehensive spatial relationships but incurs higher computational costs. Simpler scanning meth-
095   ods often suffice, with Tri-scan proving advantageous in more complex scenarios.
096   Our contributions are three-fold:
097        1. Rather than simply designing a new network, we conduct a thorough analysis of Mamba’s
098           role in 3D medical image segmentation, tailored to the specific challenges of the task,
099           using three large, authoritative public datasets. This analysis provides strong insights and a
100           foundation for future research in this domain.
101        2. We not only validate the effectiveness of existing strategies but also propose task-specific
102           approaches, such as introducing 3D DWConv before SSM, developing multi-scale Mamba,
103           and designing Tri-scan for 3D data, to further explore and enhance Mamba’s capabilities
104           for volumetric medical image segmentation.
105
           3. Using validated strategies, we construct a Mamba-based network that sets a new benchmark
              for 3D medical image segmentation, outperforming advanced models such as CNN-based
106
              nnUNet, Transformer-based CoTr, UNETR and SwinUNETR, as well as existing Mamba-
107
              based U-Mamba, offering competitive accuracy with higher computational efficiency.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), known for its ability to capture long-range dependencies with supe-
111   rior memory efficiency and computational speed compared to Transformers, has gained traction in
112   medical image segmentation. In this domain, U-Net and its variants dominate, however, integrating
113   Mamba with CNN architectures has sparked interest, leading to the development of both hybrid and
114   pure Mamba-based models. These efforts aim to harness Mamba’s strengths in modeling global
115   dependencies while maintaining the local feature extraction capabilities essential for segmentation.
116
      In hybrid models, Mamba blocks are often combined with CNN-based architectures to balance the
117   strengths of both methods. SegMamba (Xing et al., 2024) uses a multi-orientated Mamba mod-
118   ule in the encoder, paired with CNN decoders. P-Mamba (Ye & Chen, 2024) integrates ViM (Zhu
119   et al., 2024) blocks with noise suppression and local feature extraction, while Prompt-Mamba (Xie
120   et al., 2024) incorporates prompt-based segmentation with ViM blocks. T-Mamba (Hao et al., 2024)
121   enhances ViM blocks with frequency-based features, and U-Mamba (Ma et al., 2024) combines
122   Mamba and CNNs in both the encoder and decoder, offering improved global context comprehen-
123   sion. Additionally, H-vmunet (Wu et al., 2024a) uses high-order interactions, and UltraLight VM-
124   UNet (Wu et al., 2024b) optimizes multi-scale fusion with ViM layers and attention mechanisms.
125   Pure Mamba-based models rely on Mamba blocks either in the encoder, combined with a CNN
126   decoder, or throughout the entire architecture. Swin-UMamba (Liu et al., 2024a) and LMa-UNet
127   (Wang et al., 2024a) replace CNN blocks in the encoder with Visual State-Space (VSS) and bidi-
128   rectional ViM blocks, capturing contextual information and refining pixel- and patch-level features.
129   LightM-UNet (Liao et al., 2024) incorporates Residual Vision Mamba layers in both the encoder
130   and bottleneck for better long-range spatial modeling. In fully Mamba-based architectures, both
131
      the encoder and decoder rely entirely on Mamba blocks. VM-UNet (Ruan & Xiang, 2024) was
      the first model to adopt this approach, using VSS blocks throughout. Mamba-UNet (Wang et al.,
132
      2024b) also employs a fully Mamba-based structure with VMamba blocks in the bottleneck, while
133
      TM-UNet (Tang et al., 2024) introduces Triplet SSM modules to fuse spatial and channel features,
134   enhancing overall feature extraction.
135
136   The developments demonstrate the versatility of Mamba in medical image segmentation, offering a
137
      range of solutions w.r.t. specific tasks. However, these initial works primarily validate the feasibility
      of Mamba in this domain, lacking a comprehensive analysis of its impact and potential advantages.

142   We use three publicly available volumetric medical image segmentation datasets to comprehensively
143   evaluate the performance. These datasets are widely recognized as benchmarks in the medical image
144   analysis community, covering a broad range of anatomical regions and imaging conditions:
145   AMOS dataset (Ji et al., 2022) The AMOS dataset consists of 300 abdominal CT scans collected
146   from multiple centers and vendors, encompassing various imaging modalities and phases. Each scan
147   is annotated at the voxel level for 15 abdominal organs, presenting a challenging test-bed for seg-
148   mentation algorithms. Its diversity in disease cases, patient demographics, and imaging conditions
149   makes it ideal for studying model robustness in real-world scenarios. In our experiments, we used
150   the official training and validation sets.
151   TotalSegmentator (TotalSeg) dataset (Wasserthal et al., 2023) This dataset includes 1,228 CT
152   images with annotations for 117 anatomical structures. The scans were randomly selected from
153   clinical routines, offering a highly representative dataset that reflects real-world clinical conditions.
154   The dataset spans a wide range of pathologies, scanners, sequences, and institutions, making it
155   particularly well-suited for evaluating the generalizability of segmentation models. We used the
156   official training and test sets in our experiments.
157   BraTS 2021 challenge dataset (Baid et al., 2021) The BraTS 2021 dataset includes 1,251 subjects,
158   each with four 3D MRI modalities: native (T1), post-contrast T1-weighted (T1Gd), T2-weighted
159   (T2), and T2 Fluid-attenuated Inversion Recovery (T2-FLAIR). It is a widely used benchmark for
160   evaluating brain tumor segmentation algorithms, specifically for delineating tumor sub-regions such
161   as enhancing tumor, necrosis, and edema, offering voxel-wise ground truth annotations provided by
      expert physicians. We split the dataset into 80% for training and 20% for testing.

183   based networks, denoted as UlikeMamba and UlikeTrans, specifically for modeling long-range de-
184   pendencies in volumetric medical image segmentation tasks. The goal is to evaluate both segmen-
185   tation accuracy and computational efficiency, which is crucial for practical applications in clinical
186   environments. As shown in Fig. 1, both UlikeMamba and UlikeTrans consist of an encoder E and a
187   decoder D. The corresponding blocks can be defined as

195
      Mamba-based network As illustrated on the left of Fig. 1, the Mamba-based network, referred to
      as UlikeMamba, adopts a U-shaped encoder-decoder architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015). The
196
      encoder consists of a 3D convolutional (Conv) block and four stages (ES1 to ES4), with each stage
197
      composed of a 3D Conv block followed by a Mamba layer. This progressively downsamples the in-
198   put through 3D Conv blocks, generating feature embeddings at each stage, which are then flattened
199   and passed into the Mamba layers for sequential processing. These Mamba layers balance compu-
200   tational efficiency and feature extraction across multiple resolutions. The Mamba layer processes
201   input through a series of operations. First, the input is normalized and passed through a linear layer
202   for initial feature transformation. Depthwise convolutions (DWConv) are then applied to capture
203   local spatial features, followed by a SiLU activation function to introduce non-linearity. The data
204   is further processed by the state space model (SSM), which efficiently captures long-range depen-
205   dencies with linear complexity. A residual connection merges the output from the SSM with earlier
206
      features, followed by further refinement via a final linear layer.
207   The decoder structure mirrors the encoder and consists of three stages (DS1 to DS3). Each stage
208   upsamples the feature maps using 3D transposed Conv layers, followed by Mamba layers to refine
209   the upsampled features. Skip connections link corresponding encoder and decoder stages to retain
210   high-resolution, low-level information essential for accurate segmentation. The final segmentation
211   head outputs the segmentation map through a 3D Conv upsampling layer. This overall architecture
212
      leverages the Mamba’s strengths to efficiently process volumetric medical images while maintaining
      low computational overhead compared to more complex architectures. The specific architecture
213
      details can be found on the left of Figure 5 of Supplementary.

      4.2   T RAINING SETUP AND EVALUATION METRICS
238
239   Both UlikeMamba and UlikeTrans were implemented using the nnUNet (Isensee et al., 2021) frame-
240   work, which automatically selects batch sizes and patch sizes tailored to each dataset. We utilized
241   the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018) with an initial learning rate of 0.0001. All net-
242   works were trained for 1000 epochs, with each epoch consisting of 250 iterations. To evaluate the
243   segmentation results quantitatively, we calculated the Dice coefficient (Dice), a metric measuring
244   the overlap between the predicted segmentation and the ground truth. Additionally, we computed
245   the floating-point operations per second (FLOPs) to assess the computational complexity of each
246
      model. Ideally, higher Dice scores indicate better segmentation accuracy, while lower FLOPs reflect
      greater computational efficiency.

250
      Directly using vanilla Mamba The results in Table 1 show that UlikeMamba 1d, using the vanilla
251
      Mamba layer with DWConv 1D, performs competitively across all datasets, achieving Dice scores
252   similar to UlikeTrans SRA, while requiring fewer parameters and computational resources (44.88
253   GFLOPs vs. 64.47 GFLOPs). UlikeMamba 1d avoids the Out of Memory (OOM) issues faced by
254   the vanilla UlikeTrans model, which is hindered by the excessive memory demands of point-to-point
255   self-attention for 3D volumetric data. This highlights the efficiency of Mamba in handling long-
256   range dependencies while maintaining a low computational footprint, making it especially suited
257   for resource-constrained environments.
258   The main reason is that Transformers are limited by memory capacity and complexity at higher
259   resolutions and cannot be used directly. Moreover, when sequences are too long, establishing point-
260   to-point relationships makes it difficult to effectively focus on key information. Mamba’s sequence
261   modeling combined with memory modules gives it certain advantages in volume segmentation,
262   where longer sequence modeling is required. Besides, the ability of Mamba networks to achieve
263   comparable or even superior Dice scores to Transformer models across the datasets (AMOS, To-
264   talSeg, BraTS) indicates their proficiency in capturing long-range spatial relationships within the
265   data. This is particularly significant given that medical image segmentation often relies on the pre-
266
      cise delineation of complex anatomical structures that may be distributed sparsely across the image
      space. The Mamba model’s performance suggests that its architecture can effectively encapsulate
267
      these relationships without the need for extensive computational resources.
268
269   DWConv 1D vs. DWConv 3D We noted that in vanilla Mamba layer (Gu & Dao, 2023) and Vision
      Mamba (Zhu et al., 2024), DWConv 1D with a kernel size of 4 is used. However, in Mamba, the

285   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL DESIGNS
286
287   In the first section, we establish that Mamba could effectively replace Transformers for long-range
288   dependency modeling in volumetric medical segmentation tasks. This section aims to delve deeper
289   into the potential of Mamba and investigate whether its long-term dependency modeling can signif-
290
      icantly enhance multi-scale representation learning—a critical aspect of accurate volume segmen-
      tation. Multi-scale modeling plays a crucial role in medical image segmentation, where structures
291
      vary in size and capturing both fine details and broader anatomical context is essential.
292
293   While the pyramid structure captures features at different resolutions, we are further inspired
294   by (Szegedy et al., 2015) to use multiple receptive fields within each resolution feature map to
295   capture details at varying levels. Small receptive fields focus on fine structures like lesions, while
296
      larger receptive fields capture broader context, such as organ boundaries and anatomical regions.
      We design and implement four distinct multi-scale modeling schemes (Fig. 2). These schemes ex-
297
      plore different strategies for fusing features from multiple receptive fields, leveraging Mamba’s and
298
      Transformer’s long-range dependency modeling capabilities for multi-scale representation learning.
299   Specifically, we replace the whole blocks Ei and Dj in Eq. (1) with the following different multi-
300   scale ones:
301
302
      MSv1 This model combines two parallel convolution layers with different kernel sizes (3×3×3 and
      7 × 7 × 7) to extract multi-scale features. These features are then processed through either parallel
303
      Mamba or Transformer layers. The outputs are integrated via element-wise summation, allowing
304
      efficient fusion of local and global information from different receptive fields.
305
306   MSv2 In this configuration, the outputs from the 3×3×3 and 7×7×7 convolutions are concatenated
307   before being processed by either Mamba or Transformer layers. This structure ensures that the
308
      multi-scale information is integrated at an earlier stage, allowing long-range dependency modeling
      to operate on a richer set of features.
309
310   MSv3 This scheme extends the multi-scale feature extraction by incorporating an additional con-
311   volution layer with a 5 × 5 × 5 kernel, alongside the 3 × 3 × 3 and 7 × 7 × 7 convolutions. The
312   outputs are concatenated and then passed through Mamba or Transformer layers. The inclusion of
313   the intermediate 5 × 5 × 5 convolution provides an additional scale, improving the granularity of
314
      multi-scale feature extraction.
315   MSv4 Designed specifically for the Mamba layer, MSv4 incorporates three DWConv 3D layers
316   (3 × 3 × 3, 5 × 5 × 5, and 7 × 7 × 7) to extract multi-scale features. These are then concatenated and
317   processed by Mamba’s state space model (SSM), which captures long-range dependencies while
318   maintaining 3D spatial integrity. MSv4 aims to maximize Mamba’s ability to process both local
319   and global features, taking full advantage of its sequence modeling capabilities to handle complex
320
      volumetric data efficiently.
321   To evaluate the effectiveness of these multi-scale modeling strategies, we systematically replace the
322   encoder stages (ES1 to ES4) in both UlikeTrans SRA and UlikeMamba 3d with the proposed MSv1,
323   MSv2, and MSv3 schemes. MSv4, due to its specific design for Mamba, was applied only to the
      UlikeMamba 3d model. This design allows us to directly compare the performance of Mamba and

      6.1            E XPERIMENTAL DESIGNS
409
410   Mamba’s core mechanism, particularly its parallelized selective scan operation, was originally de-
411   signed for one-dimensional sequential data processing. This introduces potential challenges when
412   adapting it to visual data, where spatial components are not inherently sequential. To address this,
413   Vision Mamba (Zhu et al., 2024) and Vmamba (Liu et al., 2024b) propose multi-way scanning mech-
414   anisms tailored to preserve spatial coherence in vision tasks. The goal here is to assess whether these
415   complex scanning strategies are needed or if simpler approaches suffice for volumetric medical im-
416   age segmentation, where maintaining spatial relationships between voxels is critical for accuracy.
417   To investigate, we conducted experiments using the same backbone architecture UlikeMamba 3d
418   but varied the scanning mechanism to evaluate its effect on segmentation performance. In other
419   words, we consider different scanning strategies for UlikeMamba 3d by modifying it in h of Eq. (1)
420   only. We implemented the following scanning strategies, as shown in Fig. 3: Single-scan (Gu &
421   Dao, 2023), proposed in vanilla Mamba, processes 3D features by flattening the volumetric features
422   and scanning them sequentially along a single axis, typically in the forward direction. Dual-scan
423
      (forward + backward), proposed in Vision Mamba (Zhu et al., 2024), processes 3D input by scan-
      ning twice along the same axis—once in the forward direction and once in the backward direction.
424
      The features from both scans are then merged, allowing the model to incorporate information from
425
      both directions along the chosen axis. This method maintains the same backbone structure but intro-
426   duces bidirectional data flow in Mamba layers to capture more comprehensive spatial information.
427   Dual-scan (forward + random) is a new approach that combines a standard forward scan with an
428   additional scan in a random order. This method introduces variation in the scanning sequence to cap-
429   ture a broader range of spatial relationships, while still preserving the overall sequential structure.
430   The features from forward and random scans are merged to create a more diverse feature represen-
431   tation of the volumetric input. Tri-scan, inspired by Vmamba (Liu et al., 2024b) and adapted for
      3D medical volumetric data, scans the input in three directions: left-right, up-down, and front-back.

446   6.2      R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
447
448   Results The experimental results are summarized in Table 3. Across the three datasets (AMOS,
449   TotalSeg, and BraTS), we observe that Tri-scan achieves the highest average Dice score (87.93) but
450
      comes at the cost of increased computational complexity, as indicated by its higher parameter count
      (26.38M) and FLOPs (53.09G). The Dual-scan (forward + backward) approach performs slightly
451
      better than Single-scan and Dual-scan (forward + random), with an average Dice score of 87.67
452
      and 87.60 respectively. However, the performance gains for dual-scan methods over single-scan are
453   marginal. The Single-scan method, while having the lowest computational requirements (24.30M
454   parameters, 46.03G FLOPs), still delivers competitive performance with an average Dice score of
455   87.45, closely trailing the more complex scanning mechanisms.
456
      Analysis The Dual-scan (forward + backward) method aims to help the model capture spatial in-
457
      formation from both the start and end of the sequence, potentially building a more complete data
458
      representation. However, in our task, the improvement over Single-scan is slight probably because
459   3D medical images have strong structural priors, allowing most key spatial relationships to be cap-
460   tured effectively by a unidirectional scan. The added backward scan introduces limited sequential
461   diversity, failing to uncover significantly more data patterns. Besides, Mamba’s long-range depen-
462   dency modeling is already highly effective, further reducing the need for a backward pass. As a
463   result, the additional computational cost of the backward scan brings little benefit, resulting in only
464   slight gains in segmentation accuracy.
465   The Dual-scan (forward + random) method, which introduces a random scan alongside a forward
466   pass, is designed to capture more complex sequential relationships that may not be evident in stan-
467   dard scanning orders. Although randomizing the scanning order can diversify the captured spatial
468   relationships, it also risks compromising the spatial coherence of the data, which could explain why
469   its performance is on par with Dual-scan (forward + backward) rather than exceeding it. This method
470   may identify some complex dependencies but does so at the cost of distorting the structural priors
471   of medical images, which is essential for precise segmentation.
472   Tri-scan obtains the best results, achieving the highest Dice scores across all datasets. Scanning
473   in three directions (left-right, up-down, front-back) effectively mitigates the spatial discontinuity
474   that can arise from sequential scanning, ensuring a more thorough capture of spatial relationships
475   across the 3D volume. This is particularly beneficial for tasks like TotalSeg, where the segmentation
476   involves 117 classes and complex spatial relationships are needed be captured. The improvement
477   in TotalSeg is more pronounced since the complexity of the task, which requires distinguishing
478   between a wide variety of structures, benefits more from a comprehensive multi-directional scan.
479
      Despite this, the trade-off is clear—the higher computational cost makes Tri-scan limitations for
      resource-constrained applications.

517   8              C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
518
519   In this study, we present a comprehensive exploration of Mamba networks for 3D medical image
520   segmentation, addressing three critical questions: Can Mamba replace Transformers for long-range
521   dependency modeling? Can it improve multi-scale representation learning? Are complex scanning
522   strategies necessary? Our results show that Mamba, with its state space model, not only serves
523   as an effective replacement for Transformers but also offers superior computational efficiency. By
524   modifying the Mamba layer with 3D depthwise convolutions, we address the unique challenges of
525
      3D medical imaging, ensuring better preservation of volumetric spatial coherence and achieving
      high segmentation accuracy. We further demonstrate the power of Mamba in enhancing multi-
526
      scale representation learning by introducing MSv4, a multi-scale modeling strategy that captures
527
      both fine-grained details and global context. This capability is particularly important in complex
528   segmentation tasks like those presented in segmenting 117 anatomical structures, where multiple
529   anatomical structures of varying sizes must be accurately delineated. Besides, our study critically
530   evaluates the necessity of complex scanning strategies. While simpler approaches like single-scan
531   generally suffice, the Tri-scan approach significantly improves performance in the most challenging
532   cases by better capturing comprehensive spatial relationships across all dimensions of 3D data.
533
      The UlikeMamba 3dMT network, which integrates all these validated strategies—3D depthwise
534   convolutions, multi-scale modeling, and Tri-scan—establishes a new benchmark for 3D medical
535   image segmentation. It consistently outperforms advanced models such as nnUNet, CoTr, UNETR,
536   SwinUNETR, and U-Mamba, achieving competitive Dice scores with reduced computational com-
537   plexity. These findings underscore the potential of Mamba-based architectures to push the bound-
538   aries of volumetric medical image segmentation, offering both greater accuracy and computational
539   efficiency. Future research should explore further optimizations, e.g., adaptive multi-scan mecha-
      nisms, to extend Mamba’s applicability across a wider range of medical imaging tasks.
