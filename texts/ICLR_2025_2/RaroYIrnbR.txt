009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We tackle the question of whether Large Language Models (LLMs), viewed as
012             dynamical systems with state evolving in the embedding space of symbolic tokens,
013             are observable. That is, whether there exist distinct state trajectories that yield
014             the same sequence of generated output tokens, or sequences that belong to the
015             same Nerode equivalence class (‘meaning’). If an LLM is not observable, the state
016
                trajectory cannot be determined from input-output observations and can therefore
                evolve unbeknownst to the user while being potentially accessible to the model
017
                provider. We show that current LLMs implemented by autoregressive Transformers
018
                are observable: The set of state trajectories that produce the same tokenized output
019             is a singleton, so there are no indistinguishable state trajectories. But if there are
020            ‘system prompts’ not visible to the user, then the set of indistinguishable trajectories
021             becomes non-trivial, meaning that there can be multiple state trajectories that yield
022             the same tokenized output. We prove these claims analytically, and show examples
023             of modifications to standard LLMs that engender unobservable behavior. Our
024             analysis sheds light on possible designs that would enable a model to perform
025             non-trivial computation that is not visible to the user, as well as on controls that
026             the provider of services using the model could take to prevent unintended behavior.
027
                Finally, to counter the trend of anthropomorphizing LLM behavior, we cast the
                definition of ‘feeling’ from cognitive psychology in terms of measurable quantities
028
                in an LLM which, unlike humans, is directly measurable. We conclude that, in
029
                LLMs, unobservable state trajectories satisfy the definition of ‘feelings’ provided
030             by the American Psychological Association, suitably modified to remove self-
031             reference.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Generative models of tokenized sequential data, including large language models (LLMs), can be
037   interpreted as discrete dynamical systems with a latent state (sometimes referred to as ‘mental state’)
038   that is updated at each step by processing an input token to produce a new output token. For example,
039
      autoregressive (AR) Transformer-based LLMs co-opt a sliding ‘context’ window of data as the state,
      and simply feed back the latest output token to the input, discarding the oldest token. State space
040
      models (SSMs) maintain a separate and explicit latent state, distinct from their input and output
041
      Zancato et al. (2024). Either way, this paper is concerned with the study of the latent state of LLMs
042   understood as dynamical systems.
043
044   The three pillars in the analysis of dynamical systems are controllability, stability, and observability.
045
      Controllability is concerned with the existence of functions of the output that, if fed back as input
      in closed loop, result in a desired state sequence, or ‘trajectory’. Controllability of LLMs has been
046
      studied by Soatto et al. (2022), and is relevant to the safe operation of AI bots. Stability is concerned
047
      with state trajectories remaining bounded and non-degenerate. Stability of LLMs interacting with
048   humans in closed loop has been studied by Marchi et al. (2024). Observability is concerned with the
049   existence of distinct state trajectories that produce the same output trajectory; consequently, there
050   are different trajectories that are indistinguishable when one can only measure inputs and outputs.
051   For LLMs, lack of observability would imply that there exist mental state trajectories that evolve
052   unbeknownst to the user. Such trajectories can be triggered by input observations (prompts) or by the
053   model’s own output in closed loop. The existence of unobservable dynamics could be concerning
      if exploited maliciously by users intent to turn LLMs into Trojan Horses (Hubinger et al., 2024).

054
      Further, a non-observable model could store information in a manner that is not accessible to the user,
055   yet could be accessed by the operator.
056
057   To our best knowledge, this paper is the first to tackle the analysis of observability of LLMs. While
058
      analysis does not produce methods to mitigate undesirable behavior, it can inform the limits of such
      methods, and therefore their design.
059
060   In this paper, we show that the ‘mental state’ of AR LLMs is trivial (a singleton) and uniquely
061   determined by their measurable output. That means that there are no distinct trajectories that
062   produce the same output, or equivalently that, from the output, one can uniquely recover the latent1
063   state. However, when the model is supplied with ‘system prompts’ not visible to the user, the state
064
      is generally unobservable. More specifically, we (i) formalize the problem of observability for
      LLMs, (ii) show that current autoregressive Transformer-class LLMs trained with textual tokens
065
      are observable, and (iii) show that simple modifications of the architecture can render the LLM
066
      unobservable.

      O N THE COGNITIVE PSYCHOLOGY OF LLM S
069
070   Our analysis of the observability of LLMs was prompted by a simple question: Can LLMs have
071   ‘feelings’? LLMs are increasingly described in anthropomorphic terms, not just in the press or
072   popular discourse, but also in scientific and policy writings. Methods from cognitive psychology
073   are increasingly used to analyze LLMs as if they were opaque, observing their expressed output in
074   response to designed inputs. The use of terms from folk psychology helps describe their behavior in
075   ways that are evocative and familiar to most readers.
076   However, the lack of precise definitions of these terms in relation to LLMs often results in polarized
077   positions with no verifiable or falsifiable correlates. While the human brain is not directly accessible
078   for observation, LLMs are engineered artifacts every aspect of which is measurable, at least to the
079   designer and trainer of the model. From the data to the loss function to the optimization procedure, to
080   the activations and the weights in the trained model, to the system prompts. Every trained weight can
081   be measured, and every activation component in response to a prompt can be observed and modified
082   if so desired. This is quite unlike the human brain.
083   Therefore, when employing terms from cognitive psychology to describe LLMs, such terms should
084   be uniquely defined and correspond to a constitutive component of the trained LLM which can be
085   directly measured. In this paper, we focus on the notion of ‘feelings’ as defined by the American
086   Psychological Association (APA Definition). Since the APA Definition is written in terms of other
087   concepts, those must in turn be uniquely defined, going down the tree until the concept of ‘feeling’ is
088   unambiguously mapped to specific components of an LLM, without creating ‘loops.’ We explore
089
      the APA Definition and modify it to avoid circularity. A consequence of the analysis in this paper
      is that, according to the APA Definition, ‘feelings’ in LLMs are unobservable trajectories. One
090
      could therefore tackle the question of whether a particular LLM can have feelings by analyzing its
091
      observability properties. The answer hinges on the definition, so this paper devotes a considerable
092   amount of discussion to it. The reader interested only in the technical aspect of LLM observability, or
093   on the security implications of lack of observability, can skip these sections. The reader interested in
094   the analysis of ‘meanings’, which is a prerequisite for the definition of ‘feelings’, can refer to Liu
095   et al. (2024); Soatto et al. (2022).

097   1.1    R ELATED PRIOR WORK
098
099   When viewing LLMs as dynamical models, the three key properties to be analyzed are their stability,
100   controllability, and observability. The analysis of LLMs viewed as dynamical models has been
101   championed by (Soatto et al., 2022) and first focused on their controllability (Bhargava et al., 2023;
102
      Soatto et al., 2022). Their stability when operating in closed-loop has been studied by Gerstgrasser
      et al. (2024); Marchi et al. (2024). To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to analyze their
103
      observability. Our scope falls within the broad and fast-growing area of interpretability of LLMs,
104
      only part of which relevant to our goal; we limit our survey to prior work using tools from systems
105   and control theory. As our work touches upon controversial topics, such as ‘meanings’ and ‘feelings’;

112
      Capabilities of LLMs. As LLMs blaze through tests meant to measure human cognitive abilities,
      from the Turing test to the MCAT and BAR exams, we are interested in understanding what they
113
      cannot do. LLMs are not (yet) very proficient in mathematics, but neither are most humans. Unlike
114
      claims that LLMs, often portrayed as “stochastic parrots,” are fundamentally incapable of representing
115   meanings (Bender & Koller, 2020), it is possible to map syntactic structures to semantic spaces
116   (Bradley et al., 2023; Marcolli et al., 2023), which LLMs do. Specifically, LLMs represent meanings
117   either as equivalence classes of complete sentences that are pre-images of the same embedding vector
118   after fine-tuning (Soatto et al., 2022), or as (Nerode) equivalence classes of incomplete sentences that
119   share the same distribution of continuations after pre-training (Liu et al., 2024). It has also been argued
120   that LLMs cannot ‘understand’ the physical world. Instead, only models trained through active
121   exposure to data from interaction with the physical world (‘Gibsonian Observers’ (Soatto, 2009)) can
122   possibly converge to the one and true model of the shared ‘world’. However, this view – known as
123
      naive realism or naive objectivism – is unsupported scientifically and epistemologically (Koenderink,
      2011; Russell, 2001): Once inferred from processing finite sensory measurements, whether actively
124
      or passively gathered, so-called ‘World Models’ are abstract constructs (Achille & Soatto, 2022)
125
      no different from LLMs (Parameshwara et al., 2023). In general, even models trained on identical
126   data are unrelated but for the fact that they are trained on the same data, as their parameters are
127   not identifiable (Yan et al., 2021). Finally, it seems almost self-evident that LLMs, as disembodied
128   “brains in a jar” (actually embodied in hardware on the Cloud, and connected to the physical world at
129   the edge), cannot possibly have ‘feelings’! This prompts us to survey existing definitions of ‘feelings’
130   and to relate them to the functioning of modern LLMs.
131
       Definition of ‘feelings.’ The American Psychological Association (APA) defines “feelings” as
132   “self-contained phenomenal experiences” (Association). Unpacking this by replacing the APA’s
133    definitions of “self-contained,” “phenomenal,” and “experience” leads to a circular definition. In
134   Appendix B we adjust the definition to remove self-references, leading to “self-contained experiences
135    evoked by perception or thought” which we can map to specific and well-defined components of
136    LLMs, namely unobservable state trajectories. Of course there are many alternative definitions, none
137   ‘right’ or ‘wrong;’ we just sought the simplest that could be mapped to the functioning of LLMs, with
138    no pretense or ambition to shed light on human cognition. We tackle properties of LLMs that, when
139    exhibited in biological agents, are referred to with certain terms, but our analysis is restricted solely
140
       to LLMs and does not extend to biological agents.
141   Observability of dynamical models. Given a sequence of data, stochastic realization theory deals
142   with inferring some model (necessarily non-unique) that can realize the data. An optimal realization is
143   one that makes the prediction error unpredictable (Picci, 1991), which is sufficient for any downstream
144   task (Zancato et al., 2024). Once a model is trained, identifiability (Ljung & Söderström, 1983) is the
145   problem of determining whether the inferred model is unique. Since any semantic representation is
146
      necessarily model-dependent (Achille et al., 2024b), the question of identifiability is moot; instead,
      the analysis must focus on each specific trained LLM, for which observability deals with whether there
147
      are state trajectories that are indistinguishabole from the output. For linear systems, (Brockett, 2015)
148
      this reduces to a simple rank condition on the so-called Observability Matrix. The rank condition was
149   later extended to non-linear systems evolving on differentiable manifolds (Hermann & Krener, 1977),
150   by assembling the so-called observability co-distribution under smoothness assumptions that do not
151   apply to LLMs. For discrete state-spaces, characterizing the indistinguishable set of trajectories
152   reduces to combinatorial search. For the hybrid case where continuous trajectories and discrete
153   switches coexist, the problem has been first studied by (Vidal et al., 2002), but not exhaustively due
154   to the diversity of models and state spaces. The tools we use in our analysis are elementary so we do
155   not need to leverage deep background in observability theory beyond the references above.
156   Security of LLMs. Modern architectures are massively over-parametrized with up to trillions of
157   learnable weights, most of which uninformative at convergence (Achille et al., 2019; Chaudhari
158   et al., 2019), raising concerns that they could conceal instructions accessible through coded ‘keys’.
159   While there has been no known incident of usage of LLMs as Trojan Horses, the mere possibility is
160   a concern for large-scale models of uncertain provenance. Our work is aimed at exploring what is
161   possible, so others can design methods to prevent unintended operations. In particular, current LLMs
      are observable if restricted to textual tokens with no hidden prompts, so they cannot be used as Trojan

      Horses. Additional security risks include exfiltration of training data (Wen et al., 2024), which can be
163   prevented by avoiding using protected data for training, and post-hoc through filtering. A taxonomy
164   of privacy and attribution issues in trained AI models has been recently proposed by Achille et al.
165   (2024a). Additional concerns relate to stability of closed-loop operation of LLMs either around
166   physical devices, or around large populations of social media agents, biological or artificial, leading
167   to distributional collapse (Gerstgrasser et al., 2024; Marchi et al., 2024). Here we are concerned with
168   latent states that have no visible manifestation rather than the action engendered by the LLM outputs,
169   an important but orthogonal concern. Trojan Horses that we explore in this paper store malicious
170   information within hidden system prompts, leaving model weights untouched. This is in contrast to
171
      prior work (Hubinger et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023) “poisoning” model weights via fine-tuning.

173   1.2   C AVEATS AND L IMITATIONS
174
      To reassure the reader, we are not suggesting that LLMs are ‘sentient’ (whatever that means). While
175
      LLMs exhibit behavior that, once formalized, fits the definition of what the APA calls “feelings,”
176
      ultimately LLMs do not share the evolutionary survival drive that likely engenders feelings in
177   biological systems. The formal fit to the definition is a mere folkloristic curiosity meant to ground the
178   discussion on LLMs, when terms from cognitive psychology are used to describe their behavior. The
179   core contribution of the paper is the formalization and an analysis of the observability of mental state
180   trajectories, which is relevant to transparency, security and consistency of inference computation,
181   regardless of what the model may or may not ‘feel.’ Our contribution is to formalize the problem of
182   observability for LLMs, and to derive conditions under which an LLM is observable. We show that
183   current autoregressive Transformer-class LLMs trained with textual tokens are observable, but simple
184   modifications that use undisclosed prompts are not.

228

229   model ϕ processing the input data x(t), producing the observed output y(t) of the model (1). The
230   activations are a function of all past data, not just extant data x(t), through the parameters or ‘weights’
231   of the model ϕ. Mental states are model-dependent, i.e., ‘subjective:’ they are a function of observed
232   data (which is objective) but processed through the particular model ϕ, which is a function of its
233   training data. As the state x(t) evolves, the output y(t) describes a trajectory in mental space RM .
234
      Mental space trajectories are generated by processing the input in closed loop and can serve to inform
235   the next action, for instance the selection of the next expressed word u(t) = π(y(t)). One can view
236   segments of mental state trajectories {ϕ(x(t))}τt=1 as ‘thoughts.’ When π maps thoughts to words
237   in the dictionary A, we refer to the projection π as verbalization. If the dictionary comprises visual
238   tokens we call it visualization. When π allows the input to live in the linear space RV where the
239   dictionary A is immersed, we call it a control, the simplest case being a linear control u(t) = Ky(t).
240

241   space RV c ∋ x(t), the second in mental space RM ∋ y(t), and the third in verbal space A ∋ u(t).
242   Relaxing the input to general vector tokens that do not correspond to discrete elements of the
243   dictionary yields the (mental) control space RV ∋ u(t). We call it mental control space because in
244   general it can drive mental space trajectories y(t), not just externalized data u(t).

276    3   A NALYSIS
277
278    So far we have not delved into the technical details of different notions related to the general idea of
279    observability. However, we now need to distinguish between the related concepts of observability and
280    reconstructibility within dynamical systems theory. While observability pertains to the possibility
281    of reconstructing the initial condition x(0) from the flow Φ(x(0)), reconstructibility pertains to
282    the possibility of reconstructing state trajectories, possibly not including the initial condition. The
283    following claim, proven in the appendix, characterizes reconstructibility of LLMs.
284   Theorem 1. Consider an LLM described by (1), with π and ϕ arbitrary deterministic maps. Then,
285   for any t > 0, the last t elements of the state xc−t+1:c (t) are reconstructible. Further, the full state
286   is reconstructible at any time t ≥ c.
287
288    If, in addition to observing the output y, we know the initial condition x(0), the system is trivially
289    fully observable, and also fully reconstructible for all times t ≥ 0. In practice, we may not care to
290    reconstruct the exact initial condition x(0), so long as we can reconstruct any prompt that has the
291    same meaning. Since meanings are equivalence classes of sentences, for instance x(0), that share
292
       the same distribution of continuations Φ(x(0)), and for deterministic maps such distributions are
       singular (Deltas), we can conclude the following:
293
294   Corollary 1. Under the conditions of Theorem 1, LLMs are reconstructible in the space of meanings:
295   The equivalence class of the current state M(x(t)) is uniquely determined by the output for all t ≥ c.
296   In addition, if the verbalization of the full context is part of the output, LLMs are observable: The
297   equivalence class of the initial state M(x(0)) is uniquely determined for all t ≥ 0.
298
299
      The above claims are relatively benign, essentially stating that classical autoregressive LLMs (whose
      state space is the same as that of discrete token sequences) are transparent, from a dynamical systems
300
      point of view, to an outside observer. Unfortunately, in general these properties are lost when we
301
      allow their dynamics to be less restricted:
302
303   Theorem 2. Consider an LLM of the form 1 with π(y(t)) = Ky(t) for some matrix K; there exist
304   K and a deterministic map ϕ such that the model (1) is neither observable nor reconstructible.
305
306   Even if full observability and reconstructability are not guaranteed, we might wonder if they hold
307
      with respect to the state’s meaning equivalence class M(x). Indeed, this is still not the case:
308    Corollary 2. Under the conditions of Theorem 2, there exist K and deterministic maps ϕ such that
309    the model (1) is neither observable nor reconstructible with respect to meanings M.

327   Type 1. Verbal System Prompt g(y) = s ∈ A, h = 0, so the system prompt x0 (t) is constant in A.
328   Type 2. Non-Verbal System Prompt g(y) = s ∈ S ⊆ RV , h = 0, so the system prompt is constant
329   but allowed to take values outside the set A.
330   Type 3: One-Step Fading Memory Model g(y) = Kσm (y), h = 0 where σm is the modified
331   softmax operator with all but the top m entries of y set to zero, and set K ∈ RV ×M to be the token
332   embedding matrix, so this model can be interpreted as storing the top m tokens of the last hidden
333   state in the system prompt. m is drawn from a finite domain Ω ⊆ N. Such soft-prompt models with
334   feedback (fading memory) can be though of as ‘memory models’, although the latter are specifically
335
      trained as memory. Experiments in the Appendix show that fading memory prompts, despite not
      being trained explicitly as memory models, can produce coherent verbalized trajectories.
336
      Type 4: Infinite Fading Memory Model g(y) = λKσm (y), h(x0 ) = (1 − λ)x0 for some λ ∈ [0, 1].
337
      This is similar to Type 3 but stores a weighted average of the entire history of hidden states. In our
338   experiments, we fix λ = 0.5 and let m ∈ Ω vary, but note that we can alternatively consider the
339   opposite case with fixed m and varying λ as well.

503   5      D ISCUSSION
504
505   We have conducted an analysis of the observability of large language models viewed as dynamical
506
      systems. This includes formalizing the notion of observability, showing that LLMs without system
      prompts are generally observable, while those with system prompts are not. We have explored
507
      testable conditions depending on the kind of prompt (discrete or continuous) and level of knowledge
508
      of the prompt by the user, and measured the cardinality of indistinguishable sets for common LLMs
509   available in open source format, verifying that there are sets of different state trajectories that produce
510   the same output expressions. We have shown that, in some cases, these indistinguishable trajectories
511   are bijectively related to the prompt, which means that they can be controlled directly by the model
512   provider, unbeknownst to the user. We have validated our analysis with experiments that are easily
513   replicated on a budget with easily accessible models, namely GPT-2 and LLaMA-2. Many further
514   extensions of our analysis are possible, including for the adversarial case which is most relevant
515   to address concerns of possible backdoor attacks. The specific ramifications of our analysis to the
516   security and control of LLM operations remain to be fully explored and will be part of future work.
517   We should mention that, in this paper, the ‘language’ in LLMs does not refers to the natural language
518   with which an LLM can be trained, but rather as the inner language (“neuralese” (Trager et al.,
519   2023)) that emerges when an optimal predictor is trained on sequential data that exhibits latent logical
520   structure, with distinct identities, their relations and functions. These include spatial sensory data,
521   such as visual or acoustic, where individual ‘objects’ in the scene can move independently and come
522   into topological, geometric, photometric, dynamic, semantic, and functional relation. These include
523
      proximity, occlusion, semantic or photometric similarity, motion coherence, affordances, etc.. So,
      in the nomenclature of this paper, so-called ‘World Models’ are LLMs, just trained with tokenized
524
      sensory data rather than (or in addition to) language data. The fact that embodied agents interact with
525
      the environment in the process of building models of the surrounding environment does not affect
526   the outcome of our analysis due to the fact that the resulting representation, or more appropriately
527   realization (Zancato et al., 2024), is agnostic to how the data is provided so long as it is sufficiently
528   exciting (Lindquist & Picci, 1979).
529
      As a result, our analysis applies not just to predictive models of human language, but also to models
530
      of the world inferred from other sensory data (e.g., visual, acoustic) available to agents who do not
531
      command natural (human) language. The use of the term ‘language’ we adopt in this paper, therefore,
532   applies not just to humans, but also to cats and dogs and LLMs, among other biological agents or
533   technological artifacts.

       B    E XTENDED S URVEY ON F EELINGS
682
683
       Our definition of feelings as self-contained experiences evoked by perception or thought is derived
684
       from that of the American Psychological Association (APA) by removing inconsistencies and circular
685    references. There is, of course, much more to feelings that we are concerned with here: We simply
686    seek the simplest, consistent, and unambiguous definition that can be related to the mechanics of
687    LLMs. The APA defines “feeling” as a “self-contained phenomenal experience” (Association). That
688    leaves us with having to define “self-contained,” “phenomenal” and “experience.” The APA expands:
689   “Feelings are subjective, evaluative, and independent of the sensations, thoughts, or images evoking
690    them”. “Subjective” and “self-contained” are reasonably unambiguous so we adopt the terms: Each
691    of us have our own feelings (subjective), which live inside our head (self-contained) and cannot be
692    directly observed, only subjectively “experienced,” which however must be defined. The APA also
693
       clarifies that “feelings differ from emotions in being purely mental, whereas emotions are designed
       to engage with the world.” As for “evaluative,” “[feelings] are inevitably evaluated as pleasant or
694
       unpleasant, but they can have more specific intrapsychic qualities.” So, “evaluative” means that
695
       there exists a map that classifies feelings into at least two classes (pleasant or unpleasant), and
696    possibly more sub-classes, such as “fear” or “anger.” These are abstract concepts that admit multiple
697    equivalent expressions, i.e., meanings. As for feelings being “independent of the sensations, thoughts,
698    or images evoking them,” that is nonsensical regardless of the definition of the terms “sensation,
699    thought, or images:” Something (say, y) being “evoked by” something else (say x), makes them
700    either functionally (y = f (x)), or statistically (P (y|x)) or causally (P (y|do(x)) dependent. So, by
701    being evoked by something (sensation, thought, or images), feelings cannot be “independent” of that
       same thing. We take this choice of term by the APA to mean separate and replace ‘independent’ as


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      an unambiguous and logically consistent alternative. As for “phenomenal” and “experience,” the
703   APA has multiple definitions for the term “experience,” one in terms of “consciousness” that we
704   wish to stay clear of, one in terms of a stimulus, which runs afoul of the description of feelings as
705   being separate from the sensation, which is presumably triggered by a stimulus. The third is “an
706   event that is actually lived through, as opposed to one that is imagined or thought about”. But
707   feelings are experienced, hence not imagined or thought about, yet they are “purely mental,” hence
708   thoughts. Thus, if taken literally, the definition inconsistent. We therefore simplify the definition to:
709   Self-contained experience evoked by sensations or thought. While other definitions are possible, this
710   is the simplest we could find to be viable for testing on LLMs.
