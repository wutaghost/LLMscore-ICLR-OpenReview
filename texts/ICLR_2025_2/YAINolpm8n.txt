009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Gauge theories, which describe fundamental forces in nature, arise from the prin-
012            ciple of locality in physical interactions. These theories are characterized by their
013            invariance under local symmetry transformations and the presence of a gauge field
014            that mediates interactions. While recent works have introduced gauge equivariant
015            neural networks, these models often focus on specific cases like tangent bundles
016
               or quotient spaces, limiting their applicability to the diverse gauge theories in
               physics. We propose a novel architecture for learning general gauge invariant
017
               quantities by explicitly modeling the gauge field in the context of graph neural
018
               networks. Our framework fills a critical gap in the existing literature by providing
019            a general recipe for gauge invariance without restrictions on the fiber spaces. This
020            approach allows for the modeling of more complex gauge theories, such as those
021            with SU (N ) gauge groups, which are prevalent in particle physics. We evalu-
022            ate our method on classical physical systems, including the XY model on various
023            curved geometries, demonstrating its ability to capture gauge invariant properties
024            in settings where existing equivariant architectures fall short. Our work takes a
025            significant step towards bridging the gap between gauge theories in physics and
026            equivariant neural network architectures, opening new avenues for applying ma-
027
               chine learning to fundamental physical problems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Gauge theories form the cornerstone of modern physics, providing a unified framework for describ-
032   ing fundamental forces in nature (Weinberg, 1995). The Standard Model of particle physics, our
033   most successful theory of fundamental interactions, is built upon gauge theories: U (1) for elec-
034   tromagnetism, SU (2) × U (1) for electroweak theory, or SU (3) for strong interactions (quantum
035   chromodynamics) (Peskin & Schroeder, 2018). These theories arise from a profound principle: the
036   locality of physical interactions, leading to a geometric structure described by fiber bundles (Naka-
037   hara, 2018).
038   Gauge theories offer a geometric interpretation of forces. In this framework, forces are understood
039   as the curvature of fiber bundles, with particles represented as “sections” of these bundles (Baez
040   & Muniain, 1994) called a “field” in physics (roughly speaking, distributions). For instance, the
041   electromagnetic field can be viewed as a “connection” (aka gauge field) on a principal U (1) bundle,
042   while the gravitational field in General Relativity can be interpreted as a connection on the frame
043   (local coordinates) bundle of spacetime (Frankel, 2011).
044   Despite their success, current physical models face limitations. The Standard Model, while extraor-
045   dinarily accurate in its predictions, fails to incorporate gravity and cannot explain phenomena like
046   dark matter or dark energy (Olive et al., 2014). These challenges motivate the exploration of more
047   general models that maintain crucial symmetries while offering greater flexibility. Gauge invariance
048   stands as a fundamental principle in physics, not merely a mathematical convenience. It embodies
049   the idea that physical laws should be independent of our choice of local reference frame, much like
050
      how the laws of physics should be the same in all inertial reference frames (Zee, 2010). This prin-
      ciple leads to conservation laws through Noether’s theorem, connecting symmetries to conserved
051
      quantities (Kosmann-Schwarzbach et al., 2011).
052
053   The development of gauge-equivariant neural networks opens exciting possibilities for discovery in
      fundamental physics. By constructing models that respect gauge symmetries, we not only improve

076   In recent years, the machine learning community has made significant strides in incorporating sym-
077   metries and invariances into neural network architectures. Group equivariant convolutional neural
078   networks (G-CNNs) (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Dieleman et al., 2016; Cohen & Welling, 2017) and
079   gauge equivariant mesh CNNs (de Haan et al., 2021) have demonstrated the power of building known
080
      symmetries into model architectures. These approaches have shown remarkable improvements in
      tasks ranging from image classification to 3D shape analysis.
081
082   However, existing equivariant neural network architectures often fall short of capturing the full rich-
083   ness of gauge theories as they appear in physics. Many current approaches are limited to specific
084   geometric settings, such as homogeneous spaces or quotient spaces (Kondor & Trivedi, 2018; Cohen
085   et al., 2019a), or focus on cases where the same group acts on both the base space and the fibers
086
      (Weiler et al., 2018; Weiler & Cesa, 2020). While these simplifications have yielded positive re-
      sults in certain domains, they are inadequate for modeling the complex gauge theories that describe
087
      fundamental physical interactions, where the fiber spaces can have diverse structures.
088
089   A critical component missing from most current equivariant neural network formulations is an ex-
090   plicit representation of the gauge field. In physical gauge theories, the gauge field mediates interac-
091   tions and encodes the geometric structure of the fiber bundle. Its absence in current machine learning
092
      models limits their ability to capture the full complexity of gauge-theoretic systems.
093   In this paper, we address these limitations by proposing a novel architecture that explicitly models
094   the gauge field within the framework of graph neural networks. Our approach focuses on local
095   invariance under fiber symmetry groups, without assuming a direct correspondence between base
096   space and fiber symmetries. By introducing a gauge correction term to align fibers at different
097   points, we take a significant step towards modeling general gauge theories in a machine learning
098
      context. Specifically, our contributions are:
099         1. A generalized formulation of gauge equivariant neural networks that can handle arbitrary
100            fiber bundles, moving beyond the limitations of current approaches restricted to tangent
101            bundles or principal bundles (Cohen et al., 2019b; de Haan et al., 2021).
102         2. Explicit incorporation of the gauge field into the neural network architecture, allowing for
103            the modeling of complex gauge theories as in physics (Luo et al., 2022; 2023).
104
            3. Application of our approach to classical physical systems, including the XY model on var-
               ious curved geometries, showcasing its ability to capture gauge invariant properties where
105
               existing equivariant architectures fall short (Chen et al., 2022; Bogatskiy et al., 2020).
106
107   We aim to open new avenues for applying machine learning to fundamental physical problems and
      potentially leading to more powerful and physically informed machine learning models.

110   This section aims to provide intuition for gauge invariance and introduce the concept of gauge fields
111   through concrete examples, progressing from simple to more complex ideas.
112
113
      In both physics and applied mathematics, the concept of a “field” is fundamental to understanding
      how properties vary across space. In simple terms, a field is a state variable that depends on spatial
114
      coordinates. For instance, in an image, each pixel p ∈ Z2 has a color vp ∈ F = [0, 1]3 with
115
      values in the feature space F. This construction where to each neighborhood of a base space (e.g.
116   Z2 ) we attach a feature space (called “fibers”) is an example of a trivial “fiber bundle” (Baez &
117   Muniain, 1994) which we will formally define later. This notion can be extended to more complex
118   data structures, like temperature or wind direction across a region, electromagnetic fields in physics,
119   or any other variable distributed over a spatial domain.
120
121   Intuition for gauge invariance. In the example of images, the basis for the fibers needs to be the
122   same for all of the fibers to ensure colors are defined consistently. However, in many problems in
123   physical sciences, the quantities of interest may be invariant under local basis transformations, called
124   “gauge transformations”. Gauge theory is a paradigm for modelling such systems. A simple analogy
125
      can be drawn from the field of image processing. For instance, consider the task of digit recognition
      in MNIST. When these images undergo small local distortions–small diffeomorphisms–the identity
126
      of the digits remains unchanged.
127
128   This idea of invariance under local transformations extends beyond image processing to fundamental
129   physical theories. To illustrate this concept more concretely, we now turn to a simple physical system
130   that exhibits gauge invariance.
131

      3     T HEORY
184
185
      A key principle in developing equivariant neural networks is the construction of features that trans-
186
      form predictably under the symmetry groups of the system. This approach has been successfully
187   applied in various contexts, from Euclidean symmetries to more complex group structures (Cohen
188   & Welling, 2016; Weiler & Cesa, 2020; Satorras et al., 2021). The general idea is to use objects that
189   transform under different representations of the relevant symmetry groups to craft equivariant fea-
190   tures. These objects, which we will formally define as tensors below, can be of various orders, each
191   transforming linearly under a specific representation of the group. For instance, scalars (order-0
192   tensors) are invariant under group transformations, while vectors (order-1 tensors) transform un-
193   der the fundamental representation of the group. Higher-order tensors can capture more complex
194   transformation properties.
195
196   3.1   G AUGE SYMMETRY
197
      Differential geometry Informally, a manifold is a space that locally resembles Rn . We define an
198
      n-dimensional manifold B as a topological space equipped with charts (local mappings) fα : Uα →
199   Rn , where Uα ⊂ B are open sets that cover B. These charts are such that the transition function
200   fα ◦ fβ−1 is smooth where it is defined.
201
      Definition 3.1 (Fiber bundle). A fiber bundle is a triple E = (B, F, π) consisting of a total space E,
202
      a base space B, and a projection map π : E → B. For each point p ∈ B, the set
203
204                                         Fp = {q ∈ E : π(q) = p}                                        (6)
205   is called the fiber over p. A “section” of the fiber bundle is a map ϕ : B → E such that for any point
206   p ∈ B, it holds that π(ϕ(p)) = p.
207
208   In physics, sections are called fields. A trivial fiber bundle is a particular case where the total space
209   E is simply the product of the base space B and the fiber F. For instance, an image is a section of
210   a trivial fiber bundle. In the problems we are interested in, the fiber space has a symmetry group
211   which acts on it. A principal bundle is a special type of fiber bundle particularly significant in
212
      gauge theories. In a principal bundle, each fiber is a homogeneous space—an orbit of a group G
      acting smoothly, freely, and transitively.
213
214   Gauge theories primarily deal with principal bundles, as they are crucial for defining connections
215   (gauge fields). These connections describe how to “transport” elements within the fiber from one
      point in the base space to another.

      Connections in Fiber Bundles A connection 2 , or gauge field, on a fiber bundle is a mathematical
217   tool that allows us to define how to “transport” elements along paths within the base space B, while
218   respecting the fiber structure. As illustrated in Figure 1, different local bases in the fibers leads to
219   conflicting representations of fields. A connection enables us to compare elements of the fiber over
220   different points in B, effectively defining a “covariant derivative”. The key idea is that the standard
221   derivative ∂µ does not account for the difference in the basis of fiber Fp and Fp+δx . A gauge field
222   A modifies the standard derivative ∂µ to a covariant derivative Dµ to maintain consistency between
223   the local frames.
224                                               Dµ = ∂µ + Aµ .                                           (7)
225
226   Gauge equivariance Let G denote the the symmetry group of all Fp , and let g : B → G be set of
227   group transformations which can act on a section (field) ϕ : B → E to yield ϕ′ (p) = g(p)ϕ(p). To
228   achieve gauge equivariance, the covariant derivative needs to be gauge (or group) equivariant, i.e.,
229   D′ (g · f ) = g · Df . This implies a constraint on the vector potentials of the form
230
231                                           A′ = gAg −1 − (∇g)g −1 .                                           (8)
232
233   This concept is fundamental in physics and differential geometry, as it enables us to compare ele-
234   ments of the fiber over different points in B, defining a “covariant derivative”.
235
      In a more formal setting, Aµ can be seen as a one-form on the base manifold that takes values
236   in the Lie algebra of the gauge group G. This Lie algebra-valued one-form is defining how the
237   gauge transformations adjust the fields and their derivatives, ensuring all physical predictions remain
238   invariant under local transformations of the gauge group.

      3.2   G AUGE E QUIVARIANT G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE

317   To implement our gauge-equivariant neural network, we need to discretize the fiber bundle and
318   adapt the message passing framework of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to incorporate gauge
319   equivariance. Here, we present a general formulation of our architecture.
320
321   Discretization of the Fiber Bundle We encode a simplified topology of the base space B as a
322   graph G = (V, C), where V represents the set of vertices and C ⊂ V × V the set of edges. This
323   graph structure approximates the topology of B, forgoing the full simplicial complex representation
      for computational efficiency.

616   A    R ELATED WORK
617
618
      Building upon the context provided in the introduction, we delve deeper into specific approaches
619   and their limitations in equivariant neural networks and gauge theories.
620
621   Equivariant feature construction. The development of equivariant neural networks has led to
622   various approaches for constructing features that transform predictably under symmetry groups.
623   These approaches can be broadly categorized based on how they handle different symmetries:
624   1. Global symmetries: Methods like Group Equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016) and Steer-
625   able CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2017) focus on global symmetries, typically in the context of image
626   processing. These approaches construct features that are equivariant to rotations, reflections, and
627   other global transformations.
628   2. Euclidean symmetries in graphs: Equivariant Graph Neural Networks (EGNNs) (Satorras et al.,
629
      2021) extend equivariance to graph-structured data. They use combinations of scalar and vector
      features to achieve equivariance to Euclidean symmetries, which is particularly useful for molecular
630
      modeling and physical simulations.
631
      3. More complex symmetry groups: Some works have tackled more complex symmetries, such as
632   the Lorentz Group Network (LGN) (Gong et al., 2022), which constructs features equivariant to
633   Lorentz transformations using the Clebsch-Gordan decomposition.
634
635
      While these approaches have shown success in their respective domains, they typically assume a
      fixed, known symmetry group and often struggle with local symmetries or gauge transformations.
636
637
638
      Gauge equivariance approaches. Extending equivariance to gauge theories has presented unique
      challenges. Notable attempts include:
639
640         • Cohen et al. (2019b)’s work on gauge-equivariant CNNs for spherical surfaces, which han-
641           dles the transition between local coordinate systems on a sphere.
642         • Gauge Equivariant Mesh CNNs (de Haan et al., 2021), which align representations across
643           different points on discretized manifolds.
644
            • Recent applications to quantum lattice models (Luo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022), demon-
              strating the potential of gauge-invariant networks in simulated physical systems.
645
646   However, these approaches often rely on specific geometric structures (like homogeneous spaces or
647   tangent bundles) or assume simplified gauge groups. This limits their applicability to the diverse
      fiber bundle structures found in physical gauge theories.


                                                      12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      Our work addresses these limitations by providing a more general framework for gauge equivari-
649   ant neural networks. We extend the principle of equivariant feature construction to arbitrary fiber
650   bundles by explicitly modeling the gauge field. This allows us to construct gauge-invariant features,
651   which generalize the equivariant features used in previous approaches. By doing so, we provide a
652   flexible framework that can handle more general gauge theories, particularly for discrete spaces and
653   graph-structured data. This approach opens new possibilities for applying machine learning tech-
654   niques to a wider range of physical systems governed by gauge theories, bridging the gap between
655   theoretical physics and practical machine learning implementations.
