009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            A key challenge in decentralized optimization is determining the optimal conver-
012            gence rate and designing algorithms that can achieve it. While this issue has been
013            thoroughly addressed for doubly-stochastic and column-stochastic mixing matri-
014            ces, the row-stochastic setting remains largely unexplored. This study establishes
015            the first convergence lower bound for decentralized learning over row-stochastic
016
               networks. However, developing algorithms to achieve this lower bound is highly
               challenging due to several factors: (i) the widely used ROW-O NLY gossip pro-
017
               tocol, P ULL -D IAG, suffers from significant instability in achieving average con-
018
               sensus; (ii) P ULL -D IAG-based algorithms are sensitive to data heterogeneity; and
019            (iii) there has been no analysis in nonconvex and stochastic settings to date. This
020            work addresses these deficiencies by proposing and analyzing a new gossip pro-
021            tocol called P ULL -S UM, along with its gradient tracking extension, Pull-Sum-GT.
022            The P ULL -S UM protocol mitigates the instability issues of P ULL -D IAG, while
023            P ULL -S UM-GT achieves the first linear speedup convergence rate without rely-
024            ing on data heterogeneity assumptions. Additionally, we introduce a multi-step
025            strategy that enables P ULL -S UM-GT to match the established lower bound up to
026            logarithmic factors, demonstrating its near-optimal performance and the tightness
027
               of our established lower bound. Experiments validate our theoretical results.

046
      Network topology and mixing matrix. A key challenge in decentralized optimization is to deter-
047
      mine the optimal convergence rate and design algorithms that achieve it. Addressing this challenge
048   requires a theoretical characterization of how network topologies influence decentralized algorithms.
049   For a given connected network, we represent the topology using a mixing matrix that follows its
050   connectivity pattern, serving as an effective tool for evaluating the network’s impact. For undirected
051   networks, a symmetric and doubly-stochastic matrix can be easily constructed. However, in directed
052   networks, constructing a doubly-stochastic mixing matrix is generally impossible. Instead, mixing
053   matrices are typically either column-stochastic (Nedić & Olshevsky, 2014; Nedić et al., 2017) or
      row-stochastic (Sayed, 2014; Mai & Abed, 2016), but not both.

054
      Optimal complexity over doubly-stochastic networks is well-established. The connectivity of a
055   doubly-stochastic mixing matrix can be effectively evaluated through a metric called spectral gap,
056   which measures how closely the decentralized network approximates a fully connected network.
057   Building on this metric, a series of works have established the optimal convergence rates for de-
058   centralized algorithms. For instance, the studies in (Scaman et al., 2017; 2018; Sun & Hong, 2019;
059   Kovalev et al., 2021) provide optimal convergence rates for convex or non-stochastic decentralized
060   optimization. Lu & De Sa (2021) establishes the optimal complexity for non-convex and stochastic
061   decentralized optimization over a specific type of linear networks, while (Yuan et al., 2022) extends
062   this optimal complexity to a much broader class of networks.
063   Optimal complexity over column-stochastic networks is established recently. If out-degree in-
064   formation is available prior to communication, a column-stochastic matrix can be easily constructed.
065   When only column-stochastic matrices are used in decentralized algorithms, this is referred to as
066   the C OL -O NLY setting. The foundation of C OL -O NLY algorithms is the P USH -S UM gossip pro-
067   tocol (Kempe et al., 2003; Tsianos et al., 2012). Many algorithms based on P USH -S UM achieve
068   superior convergence rates, e.g., Nedić & Olshevsky (2015); Tsianos et al. (2012); Zeng & Yin
069
      (2017); Xi & Khan (2017); Xi et al. (2017); Nedić et al. (2017); Assran et al. (2019); Qureshi et al.
      (2020). However, these works do not precisely capture the influence of column-stochastic networks
070
      and, therefore, cannot clarify the optimal complexity in the C OL -O NLY setting. This open question
071
      has been addressed in a recent study by Liang et al. (2023), which establishes effective metrics to
072   evaluate the influence of column-stochastic networks and provides the optimal lower bound for the
073   C OL -O NLY setting. Additionally, it proposes algorithms that achieve this lower bound.
074
075
      Optimal complexity over row-stochastic networks remains unclear yet. If out-degree infor-
      mation is unavailable, column-stochastic matrices cannot be directly constructed. However, row-
076
      stochastic matrices can be formed using in-degree information, which can be obtained by counting
077
      received messages. This is referred to as the ROW-O NLY setting. Similar to how P USH -S UM serves
078   as the basis for C OL -O NLY algorithms, the foundation of ROW-O NLY methods is the P ULL -D IAG
079   gossip protocol (Mai & Abed, 2016). Building on P ULL -D IAG, Mai & Abed (2016) adapted the dis-
080   tributed gradient descent (DGD) algorithm for the ROW-O NLY setting, while Li et al. (2019); Xin
081   et al. (2019c) extended gradient tracking methods, and Ghaderyan et al. (2021); Lü et al. (2020);
082   Xin et al. (2019a) introduced momentum-based ROW-O NLY gradient tracking. However, the con-
083   vergence analysis for ROW-O NLY algorithms is still quite limited. Current analyses focus only on
084   deterministic and strongly convex loss functions, leaving the performance of ROW-O NLY algorithms
085   in non-convex and stochastic settings unknown. More importantly, the impact of row-stochastic net-
086
      works on the convergence rate of ROW-O NLY algorithms remains unclear. These gaps present sig-
      nificant obstacles to determining the optimal complexity in the ROW-O NLY setting. The following
087
      fundamental open problems then naturally arise:
088
089
          Q1. What are the effective metrics that can fully capture the impact of row-stochastic networks
090           on decentralized stochastic optimization, and how do they influence the convergence of
091           prevalent ROW-O NLY algorithms?
092
093       Q2. Given these metrics, what is the lower bound on the convergence rate for ROW-O NLY
094           algorithms in the non-convex and stochastic setting?
095       Q3. Can existing ROW-O NLY algorithms readily achieve the optimal convergence rate? If not,
096           what limitations do they face?
097
098       Q4. Can we develop new ROW-O NLY algorithms that overcome the limitations of existing al-
099           gorithms and attain the aforementioned lower bound?
100
101
      Main contributions. This paper provides an in-depth understanding of decentralized optimization
      over row-stochastic networks by addressing the above open questions. Our contributions are:
102
103
          C1. We find that the metrics generalized spectral gap and equilibrium skewness, proposed by
104
              Liang et al. (2023) to characterize the influence of column-stochastic networks, can also
105           effectively capture the impact of row-stochastic networks on decentralized algorithms.
106
107       C2. Using these metrics, we establish the first lower bound on the convergence rate for any non-
              convex decentralized stochastic first-order algorithm with a row-stochastic mixing matrix.

          7                     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
531
532
         This paper establishes a tight lower bound and identifies optimal algorithms for decentralized op-
533
         timization using row-stochastic mixing matrices. Our analysis shows that existing P ULL -D IAG-
534      based methods are sensitive to algorithmic instability and data heterogeneity, preventing them from
535      reaching the lower bound. We propose the P ULL -S UM protocol and (MG-)P ULL -S UM-GT, which
536      mitigate dependence on network properties and heterogeneity, achieving the lower bound up to log-
537      arithmic factors. Experimental results support our findings. A limitation of this work is that, while
538      the method performs well empirically without a warm-up, the convergence guarantees for P ULL -
539      S UM-GT are currently provided only with a warm-up stage. Addressing unconditional convergence
         guarantees will be left for future work.
