009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Embodied Mobile Manipulation in Open Environments (EMMOE) is the challenge
012            that agents understanding user instructions and executing long-horizon everyday
013            tasks in home environments. This challenge encompasses task planning, decision-
014            making, navigation and manipulation, and is crucial to develop a powerful home
015            assistant capable of autonomously completing daily tasks. However, the absence of
016            a holistic benchmark, data incompatibility between large language models (LLMs)
017
               and mobile manipulation tasks, the lack of a comprehensive framework, and insuf-
               ficient dynamic adaptation mechanisms all continue to hinder its development. To
018
               address these issues, we propose EMMOE, the first unified benchmark that simul-
019
               taneously evaluates high-level planners and low-level policies, and new metrics for
020            more diverse evaluation. Additionally, we manually collect EMMOE-100, the
021            first everyday task dataset featuring detailed decision-making processes, Chain-of-
022            Thought (CoT) outputs, feedback from low-level execution and a trainable data for-
023            mat for Large Multimodal Models (LMMs). Furthermore, we design H OMIE B OT, a
024            sophisticated agent system which integrates LMM with Direct Preference Optimiza-
025            tion (DPO) as the high-level planner, small navigation and manipulation models as
026            the low-level executor. Finally, we demonstrate H OMIE B OT’s performance and
027            methods for evaluating different models and policies.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
      The embodied agent is defined as the integration of artificial intelligence (AI) with a physical entity,
032
      enabling it to perceive, learn and interact with the environment just like a human. In recent years,
033   developing agents capable of understanding human instructions and autonomously completing daily
034   tasks has become an increasingly popular challenge (Song et al., 2023; Yenamandra et al., 2023).
035   This challenge spans multiple fields, including AI, robotics, computer vision, natural language
036   processing and so on. Traditional frameworks for embodied agents, such as imitation learning (IL)
037   and reinforcement learning (RL), have shown limitations on generalization and transfer ability (Shen
038   et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024). Moreover, how to enable robots to actively
039   explore and adapt to new environments while reducing the reliance on prior knowledge remains a
040   significant challenge.
041   Recently, breakthrough developments in LLMs have shown great potential in complex embodied
042   scenarios (Driess et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). LLMs excel in various natural
043   language tasks and demonstrate strong generalization ability. Through advanced prompting techniques
044   such as CoT (Wei et al., 2022), the logical reasoning ability of LLMs has been further improved (Wang
045   et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022). Visual Language Models (VLMs) enable LLMs to process visual input
046   and allow agents to reason and make decisions based on visual observations of the environment,
047
      thus enhancing their perception and understanding abilities. The development of LMMs has further
      expanded the application of embodied agents into real-world settings. Moreover, with the ongoing
048
      development of demonstrations, advanced simulators (Szot et al., 2021; Kolve et al., 2017) and
049
      diverse datasets (Das et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023a; Shridhar et al., 2020), the integration of large
050   models and embodied agents is expected to become the next wave in AI, potentially marking a crucial
051   breakthrough in the advancement toward physical robotics.
052
053
      Although LLM-driven embodied agents have been successfully applied to many downstream tasks,
      they still face several significant challenges when combined with mobile manipulation tasks:

081

091         tasks with three novel metrics for more advanced evaluation.
092       • We collect EMMOE-100, the first everyday task dataset featuring CoT outputs, diverse task
093         design, re-plan processes, SFT and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) dataset built on it.
094       • We design H OMIE B OT, a sophisticated agent system which integrates models at different levels,
095         multiple error detection and adaptation mechanisms to complete EMMOE tasks.

      2.2      E VALUATION M ETRICS
133
      The most fundamental metrics in task planning are Success Rate (SR) and Goal-Condition Success
134   (GC) (Shridhar et al., 2020). SR measures the rate of successful completions across all tasks, while
135   GC is the ratio of goal conditions achieved by the end of an episode. An episode is considered
136   successful only if GC reaches 100%. However, this evaluation metric has clear limitations when
137   applied to our tasks. First, our task not only focuses on the final result but also pays attention to the
138   execution process while GC only checks the final status. Additionally, though GC is effective for
139   evaluating tasks with a single ending, it is not suitable for open-ended tasks. Human-style instructions
140   are often open-ended and won’t specify where to go or what object to get, leading to situations where
141   the agent successfully completes the task in a different way but is still considered as a failure for
142
      not meeting pre-defined goal conditions. Moreover, setting new goal-conditions requires a deep
      understanding of scene information and rule-based languages like PDDL, which complicates the
143
      collection and development of new datasets. To overcome these limitations and provide a more
144
      diverse assessment of the model’s capabilities, we propose the following new metrics.
145
146
      Task Progress To better measure the task execution process and success rate, we propose a new
147   metrics: Task Progress (TP). The calculation method for TP is as follows:

233   3.1      OVERVIEW
234
235   In this section, we’ll introduce how our HomieBot accomplishes EMMOE tasks and the overview of
236   its frame is shown in the Fig.3. The process of EMMOE tasks can be described as: the agent needs to
237   make embodied decisions based on current environments and historical execution records in open
238
      environments, then navigates and manipulates within a continuous space, the obtained results and
      feedback will be used for the next decision. We divide this process into two main parts: High Level
239
      Planning (HLP) and Low Level Execution (LLE). HLP is responsible for embodied decision-making
240
      and planning adaptation while LLE handles continuous execution and provide feedback to HLP. We
241   will describe HLP in Section 3.2 and LLE in Section 3.3.
242
243
      3.2      H IGH L EVEL P LANNING (HLP)
244
245   The key challenge in long-horizon planning is to ensure LLM-generated plans are practical, especially
246   when static plans fail to adapt to dynamic changes. Agents need to continuously interact with the
247   environment to refine and adjust plans based on real-time feedback. We select Video-LLaVA (Lin
248   et al., 2023) as our base planner model M and fine-tune it with our EMMOE data. Additionally, we
249   design elaborate input and output instructions to facilitate dynamical adjustment during execution.
250
251   Multimodal Instruction We decompose a long mobile manipulation trajectory into several sub-
252   tasks, and our multimudal instruction I is shown as follows:
253
254                                                  I = {o1∼4 , s, T, inv, h, f }                                   (4)
255
256   In the visual part, four first-person view images o1∼4 correspond to four directions: front, left,
257   back and right. In the text part, system information s and user task T remain fixed throughout the
258   conversation, reminding the agent of its responsibility. Feedback f indicates the state of the last
259
      execution and error information if failed, and it will also be used to update other parts of I. Inventory
      inv reflects what the agent currently holds, mainly to prevent generation of illogical actions, inv
260
      will be updated based on both f and the type of the last action. Execution history h logs all previous
261
      subtasks and their results. After receiving f , the last subtask and its result will be updated into
262   h. Notably, unlike some previous methods would first perform visual detection (Song et al., 2023)
263   or input background information (Yang et al., 2023a) into the LLM, all background information
264   including BEV (Bird’s Eye View) images are prohibited and we directly input o1∼4 to LMM, which
265   means the planner must strengthen its intrinsic capability to generate more reasonable outputs.
266
267   Json-format Output Since information requirements vary from different low-level policies, a
268   standardized output format is essential to accommodate these diverse requirements. Besides, it
269   allows each module to function independently while simplifies module replacement and maintenance.
      Therefore, we define our output in the following uniform format:

                              O = M (I) = {A, S, m}, S = {action, target}                                   (5)
272
273   A represents the analysis of each outputs, which is inspired by works like CoT (Wei et al., 2022),
274   before generating final outputs, planner model M is expected to summarize previous executions and
275   current situations, analyze what to do next and then provide the subsequent subtask S. To ensure
276   the feasibility of the output, action in each subtask must be chosen from the available action list.
277   Similarly, m which represents the selected low-level models or policies, is also restricted to the a
278   given model list. To fully separate decision-making from low-level execution and avoid over-reliance
279   on the simulator, we impose fewer restrictions on target, which can be an object or a location, but
280   it must be observable in the provided images and deemed necessary for completing the task.
281
282   3.3   L OW LEVEL E XECUTION (LLE)
283
284
      After getting subtask S, model choice m and inventory inv from HLP, LLE would convert them
      into precise model-calling instructions. Error detection occurs at different stages to monitor the
285
      execution process. Once execution is completed or terminated, LLE will send environmental images
286
      and feedback to HLP for subsequent decision-making. Considering the limitations of the simulator,
287   we set up six executable skills (see Table E1 for more details). Since different models require different
288   input information for execution, the performance of models utilizing background information will
289   certainly differ from those that do not. Therefore, to ensure fairness in model selection, we establish
290   two different settings based on whether the simulator’s background information is required.
291
292   Execution With Background Information More specifically, execution with background informa-
293   tion means that the selected model needs to directly obtain precise position and status information of
294   the target from the simulator. As M3 (Gu et al., 2022) demonstrates excellent performance across
295   all the skills we define when utilizing background information from the habitat, we choose it as the
296   unique low-level model. In this case, m is masked and the model choice is always M3. Since M3
297
      requires specific background names and our target cannot be directly recognized by it, we apply
      several processing steps before passing the information to M3. This ensures that the information is
298
      converted into a granularity that M3 can recognize. More details are in Section 4.3.
299
300   After the execution is completed, in addition to the text and image data provided to HLP, LLE also
301   captures the entire execution process in a video. At the end of each task, we will obtain a complete
302   trajectory video with detailed annotations for each step. This means that HomieBot has the potential
303
      to bridge the gap between robot data and LLM data as the entire execution process is fully automated
      and the user only needs to set up the scene and input instructions. The video data can be used for IL
304
      in robotics, while the text and image data can be used for LMM training.
305
306
      Execution Without Background Information Without background information means that the
307
      agent can only rely on the information captured by its own sensors and its intrinsic ability to complete
308
      the task. As shown in Table E2, we set two manipulation models and two navigation models to
309   perform different actions and model choice m from HLP will determine which model to use. The
310   manipulation models include RT-1-X (Padalkar et al., 2023) and Octo (Team et al., 2024b), RT-1-X
311   is used for P ick and P lace, and Octo is used for Open and Close. The navigation models consist of
312   NoMaD (Sridhar et al., 2024) and PixNav (Cai et al., 2024). NoMaD specializes in image navigation
313   and is suitable when the target is a spot or large objects, whereas PixNav excels at pixel-level and
314   object navigation, making it ideal when the target is an object.
315   The primary criterion for selecting low-level models in this setting is that they should be lightweighted
316   rather than large models, aligning closely with our motivations for this setting. We aims to prepare
317   for the deployment and evaluation of agents in real-world settings, where background information is
318   often lacking. Real-world operations require high real-time performance, and the inference speed
319   of LLMs remains a challenge. By breaking down long-horizon tasks into action primitives, we can
320   leverage task-specific small language models (SLMs) for these actions, thus reducing time costs and
321   avoiding an overly large framework.
322
323   Error Detection Since errors may occur at different stages during the execution of long-horizon
      tasks, to facilitate better communication with HLP and provide more detailed error information, we


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      design four major types and several sub-types of error detection. Logical error L1: the agent already
325   holds an object but still attempts to pick/open/close; L2: the agent holds nothing but attempts to put;
326   L3: the agent attempts to pick/put object in a closed container; L4: the agent attempts to open/close an
327   non-interactive object. Distance error D1: the agent is too far from the target, preventing interaction
328   with the target object; D2: the agent is too close to the target,the robotic arm is hindered from
329   extending properly during interaction. Format Error F1: The output action or model is not in the
330   available list; F2: The output target does not exist in the scene or can not be recognized by low level
331   models. Execution Error E1: failure is due to limited capabilities of low-level models or policies;
332   E2: improper execution may lead to the inventory information being accidentally updated. More
333
      classification and detection details are in Appendix E.

      4.1   DATA AUGMENTATION
338
339
      SFT Augmentation Previous work(Zhang et al., 2024b) has demonstrated that a standardized data
340   format would significantly enhance model training and evaluation. To this end, we write a uniform
341   script (see in Appendix F.1) to convert EMMOE-100 data into fixed-format conversation data. During
342   this process, all failed subtasks will be skipped as they are treated as junk data for the SFT dataset and
343   we initially obtained 930 SFT data in this way, which is still insufficient for LLM training. To expand
344   the dataset, we use GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) to regenerate text descriptions of tasks and the
345   analysis of each subtask for three times. This approach not only enhances the diversity of instructions,
346   allowing the LLM to adapt to different user input styles, but also helps to avoid introducing additional
347   inaccuracy or inconsistency. Finally, we obtain 3720 SFT data in total.
348
349   DPO Augmentation DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) training has a strict requirement for data format,
350   which must include prompt, chosen and rejected. For the ith subtask and its input instruction Ii , if the
351   execution of model output Oi fails but the next output Oi+1 succeeds after re-plan, we will choose Ii
352   as the prompt, Oi as the rejected and Oi+1 as the chosen. Although this approach aligns well with the
353   concept of preference data, the proportion of re-planed data is relatively low. Therefore, we utilize
354
      following methods to construct new DPO data. Order Change: We shuffle the order of successful
      subtasks, treating output Oi as chosen and Oi+1 as rejected. This approach aims to help LLMs learn
355
      the logical relationships between subtasks, particularly understanding the optimal order of actions.
356
      Action Change: To standardize the model’s output and reduce responses outside the action list, we
357   replace subtask actions with non-standard names or actions not in the list. Model Change: To enable
358   the LLM owns the ability to select the appropriate low-level model for a given scenario, we replace
359   the model choice with other models in the model list. Finally, we get 10104 DPO data in total. More
360   visualized processing flows and data samples can be found in Appendix F.2.
361
362
      4.2   M ODEL T RAINING
363
364   Training Details We select 90 tasks from EMMOE-100 as our training tasks. Using the methods
365   described in Section 4.1, we obtain 3,316 SFT training data and 8,984 DPO training data. Then
366   we choose Video-LLaVA-7B (Lin et al., 2023) as our base model and conduct a two-stage training
367   process. In the first stage, we fine-tune the base model with a learning rate of 5e-4 on four NVIDIA
368   A40. In the second stage, we align the fine-tuned model with DPO method and train with a learning
369   rate of 5e-6. To prevent catastrophic forgetting and retain the model’s intrinsic capability, LoRA (Hu
370   et al., 2021) is applied in both stages, with LoRA rank set to 128 and α to 256 in stage one, and
371   LoRA rank set to 8 and α to 8 in stage two. More training details are shown in Appendix G.

463
464   To further explore the reasons for the overall low success rate and demonstrate how HomieBot can
465   be used to simultaneously evaluate both HLP and LLE, we conduct a detailed analysis based on the
466   results in Section 4.4. Using the error classification in Section 3.3 and recorded feedback, we collect
467   all errors that occurred during experiments. To determine which errors are acceptable and solvable
468   and which are the main causes of failure, we further classify them according to whether they appear
469   in successful or failed trajectories, the results are shown in the Figure 4.
470   Error Analysis Except for E1 and E2 errors which come from low-level models, each type of
471   error corresponds to different capabilities of LMMs. In failed trajectories, the predominant error
472   type across all baseline models is F 2 error, which largely exceeds other types. This suggests that
473   the primary factors impeding baseline model’s performance are physical grounding failures and
474   model hallucinations. In practical execution cases, we observe that even models are told that objects
475   do not exist or cannot be recognized, they may still output wrong objects or repeat mistakes after
476   several steps. This issue has been significantly improved in our models, which also highlights the
477
      significance of LMM-trainable format data. With a small amount of data combined with our SFT
      and DPO data augmentation methods, LMM can build up a general understanding of the current
478
      environment, enabling outputs to be grounded and compatible with low-level models. Besides, the
479
      ratio of failed executions to total executions for two open-source models is relatively low, indicating
480   most subtasks are successfully executed, which seems to conflict with the very poor SR. In our
481   observations, since EMMOE involves very complex and long-horizon tasks, the execution steps can
482   easily become very long, when the model’s understanding ability is insufficient, it may fail to fully
483   grasp previous execution content, ultimately resulting in the meaningless output. Although these
484   subtasks can be successfully executed, they will contribute nothing to the overall task, and even
485   worse, they will consume remaining steps and fasten task termination. In successful trajectories, the
      most common error is D1 error. This indicates that even when the model’s spatial perception ability

514   5     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
515
516   Limitations Firstly, the range of available actions and interactive objects is limited in Habitat,
517   restricting the scope of our task design. Secondly, When the scenario is expanded to multiple rooms,
518   the demands on navigation and memory functions will significantly increase, potentially necessitating
519   the integration of additional memory mechanisms. Although standardized output enables uniform
520
      evaluation of different models, it will sacrifice certain information precision to meet each model’s
      requirements, and the increasing number of model inferences will lead to additional time costs.
521
      Therefore, designing a more efficient workflow and output format is necessary. Finally, disparities
522
      among simulators and between simulators and the real world pose significant challenges for low-level
523   model transfer and generalization, a more general and universal evaluation platform is needed.
524
525   Future Works Recently, more powerful simulators like Robocasa (Nasiriany et al., 2024) enable
526   the design and collection of a wider range of everyday tasks. Besides, how to efficiently utilize
527   historical images and videos rather than text only to improve decision-making still needs to be
528   explored. Additionally, real-world deployment of Homie is possible since the data collection and
529   evaluation metrics are independent. Furthermore, exploring how Homie can interact or collaborate
530   with family members to complete daily tasks is also worthful and necessary.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In our work, we first introduce EMMOE, the first unified benchmark designed to evaluate both high-
535   level planners and low-level policies. Then we present the collection and features of EMMOE-100
536   and propose three novel metrics to complement existing metrics. Next, we introduce our HomieBot
537   and illustrate how its two main components HLP and LLE function. In experimental parts, we
538   demonstrate how to use original EMMOE data to construct LLM-trainable SFT and DPO datasets
539   and evaluate different models, we also conduct deep analysis based on detailed error information.

      A.1    E MBODIED TASKS AND BENCHMARKS
937
938
      As embodied agents and LLMs develop rapidly, many embodied tasks and benchmarks have emerged.
939
      In Embodied Question Answering (EQA) tasks, EQA-v1 (Das et al., 2018), MT-EQA (Yu et al.,
940   2019), MP3D-EQA (Wijmans et al., 2019), IQUAD V1 (Gordon et al., 2018), OpenEQA (Majumdar
941   et al., 2024), HM-EQA (Ren et al., 2024), S-EQA (Dorbala et al., 2024) contains a variety of
942   task range to evaluate logical reasoning abilities of LLMs. In Vision-and-Language Navigation
943   (VLN) tasks, R2R (Anderson et al., 2018), R4R (Jain et al., 2019) and VLN-CE (Krantz et al.,
944   2020), SOON (Zhu et al., 2021) evaluate LLM’s capabilities under different navigation settings.
945   ALFRED (Shridhar et al., 2020) Behavior series (Srivastava et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a) focus on
946   interactive household tasks OVMM (Yenamandra et al., 2023) involves picking and placing any object
947   in unseen environments. Common grasping datasets include MT-Opt (Kalashnikov et al., 2021),
948
      VIMA (Jiang et al., 2022), ManiSkill2 (Gu et al., 2023), Calvin (Mees et al., 2022), BridgeData-
      v2 (Walke et al., 2023), RH20T (Fang et al., 2023) and Open-X (O’Neill et al., 2024). In mobile
949
      manipulation, RT series (Brohan et al., 2022; Zitkovich et al., 2023) and Mobile ALOHA (Fu et al.,
950
      2024) exhibit strong capabilities. Despite numerous benchmarks, a unified benchmark and relevant
951   task is still missing. Traditional mobile manipulation uses IL to learn entire trajectories, complicating
952   the evaluation of intermediate processes. In our work, we propose EMMOE, a holistic benchmark
953   designed to assess both final results and the execution process.
954
955
956   A.2    LLM-D RIVEN E MBODIED AGENTS
957
958   LLM-driven embodied agents represent cutting-edge advancements in robotics. SayCan (Ahn et al.,
959   2022), Palm-E (Driess et al., 2023), LLM-Planner (Song et al., 2023) and EmbodiedGPT (Mu et al.,
960   2024) combine LLMs with complex embodied tasks. TAPA (Wu et al., 2023) and SayPlan (Rana
961   et al., 2023) use visual modules for multi-room settings. Voyager (Wang et al., 2023), STEVE (Zhao
962   et al., 2023b), Smallville (Park et al., 2023) and Octopus (Yang et al., 2023a) use LLMs to choose
963
      pre-defined functions. L3MVN (Yu et al., 2023), ESC (Zhou et al., 2023), SayNav (Rajvanshi et al.,
      2023) and VLFM(Yokoyama et al., 2024) build frontier or semantic maps to navigate. ViNT (Shah
964
      et al., 2023) and NoMaD (Sridhar et al., 2024) focus on image navigation, PixNav (Cai et al.,
965
      2024) uses LLM to select target image pixel. GOAT (Chang et al., 2023) is a comprehensive
966   navigation system. RT-2 (Zitkovich et al., 2023) is the first Visual Language Action (VLA) model.
967   RoboFlamingo (Li et al., 2023b) and OpenVLA (Kim et al., 2024) are open-source VLA models.
968   Leo (Huang et al., 2024) focuses on multiple QA problems. Octo (Team et al., 2024b) is a light
969   model for arm control. ALOHA (Zhao et al., 2023a) improves action prediction through action
970   chunking. RoboAgent (Bharadhwaj et al., 2024) enhances object detection and generalization, and
971   LCB (Shentu et al., 2024) uses LLMs to generate implicit strategy goals. ManipLLM (Li et al., 2024)
      and VoxPoser (Huang et al., 2023) combine environmental perception and task execution.

       A.3    A DAPTATION FROM FEEDBACK
973
974    Discrepancies between LLM’s outputs and real-world conditions always lead to execution failures.
975    LLM-Planner (Song et al., 2023) make a straightforward re-plan. Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2024)
976    use single LLM as generator and evaluator. Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2024) treats LLM as the Actor
977    and the evaluator as the Critic. ViLA (Lin et al., 2024) utilizes GPT-4V (Yang et al., 2023b) to
978    obtain visual feedback. However, self-improvement relies heavily on prompt design and intrinsic
979    capabilities of LLMs. If errors unrelated to planning occur, LLMs may struggle to self-correct. Inner
980    Monologue (Huang et al., 2022) and RoCo (Mandi et al., 2024) utilizes external collision detection
981    and feedback. DoReMi (Xie et al., 2024) sets pre-defined constrains. Nevertheless, LLMs may make
982
       same mistakes in similar situations as the model weights are not changed. SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022)
       trains a value function to consider both generated actions and their values. Remember (Zhang et al.,
983
       2024a) builds a memory module and retrieves similar state-action pairs. Retroformer (Yao et al.,
984
       2023) learns a retrospective model via policy gradient optimization. While RL-based adaptation
985    mechanisms are able to adjust actions before execution, defining and training an effective value
986    function or reward model is highly challenging. The recently popular DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024)
987    algorithm greatly simplifies this process by requiring only a preference dataset. In our H OMIE B OT,
988    we use DPO for model alignment, CoT (Wei et al., 2022) and self-reflection for decision-making.
989    Additionally, error detection and feedback mechanisms are applied during low-level execution.

1445   Logical error. If the hand already has an object (inventory is not empty) but still attempts to
1446   perform a pick/open/close operation, the execution will fail, and the message “the hand is full” will be

1456   the agent is too far from the target, causing it to be unable to reach the target object but still attempts
