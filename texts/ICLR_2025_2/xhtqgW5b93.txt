009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion is one of the leading approaches for image generation. Plug-and-play
012            token merge techniques have recently been introduced to mitigate the high com-
013            putation cost of transformer blocks in diffusion models. However, existing meth-
014            ods overlook two key factors: (1) the token selection process fails to account
015            for relationships among tokens, potentially discarding important information and
016            limiting image quality; 2) they do not take advantage of the modern, efficient
017
               implementation of attention, so that, the overhead backfires the achieved algorith-
               mic efficiency. In this paper, we propose Token Merge with Attention (ToMA)
018
               with three major improvements. Firstly, we utilize a submodular-based token se-
019
               lection method to identify diverse tokens as merge destinations, representative of
020            the entire token set. Secondly, we use efficient attention implementation for the
021            merge operation with negligible overhead. Also, we formalize the (un-)merge
022            as (inverse-)linear transformations, allowing shareable computation across lay-
023            ers/iterations. Finally, we utilize the image locality to further accelerate the com-
024            putation by performing all the operations on tokens in local tiles. ToMA achieves
025            the best trade-offs between speed-ups and generation quality compared to the
026            baselines.

042   Diffusion Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2021); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021) emerges as one of
043   the leading approaches for high-quality image generation. However, the increasing complexity
044
      of diffusion models, driven by their core transformer-based architecture, presents significant
      computational challenges. The design of the transformer leads to quadratic complexity with respect
045
      to the number of tokens, making them inefficient and resource-intensive as token counts increase.
046
047   Methods with different approaches have been developed to mitigate this issue. Flash Attention Dao
048   et al. (2022); Dao (2023) introduces a more efficient attention mechanism that reduces memory
049   overhead, while xformers Lefaudeux et al. (2022) utilize sparse attention to lower memory usage
050
      and improve scalability. Methods like Token Pruning Kim et al. (2022) reduce computation by
      eliminating less relevant tokens during inference, albeit at the cost of potential quality degradation.
051
052   ToMeSD Bolya & Hoffman (2023) leverages token merging Bolya & Hoffman (2023); Kim et al.
053   (2023), consolidating similar tokens during the forward pass to reduce the token count in compu-
      tational layers, thereby lowering complexity without requiring network retraining. Essentially, the


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      original sequence of tokens gets merged before each layer in the transformer block, including atten-
055   tions and MLPs, and after the computation finishes, an unmerge is applied to transform the merged
056   tokens back to the original sequence length. Token merge shares the spirit with token pruning by re-
057   ducing the input size to transformers, and is orthogonal to other acceleration methods such as Flash
058   Attention and xformers.
059
      Though ToMeSD has shown considerable theoretical speedups by significantly reducing the number
060
      of tokens, it struggles to accelerate diffusion models in practice with modern attention implemen-
061   tation and advanced GPU architectures. This is because the merge algorithm of ToMeSD Bolya &
062   Hoffman (2023) requires relatively costly operations on GPU (e.g., sorting). This creates significant
063   overhead that overshadows the speedups gained from token reduction, especially with more effi-
064   cient implementations of attention (e.g., Flash Attention Dao (2023)) and GPU architectures better
065   optimized for attention-like operations.
066
      In this paper, we propose Token Merge with Attention (ToMA) to get practical speedups for diffusion
067   models in a plug-and-play manner. Our method first utilizes a submodular function to identify
068   a representative subset of tokens as merge destinations with a vectorized optimization algorithm
069   that runs efficiently on GPUs. We then perform token merge by using an attention-like operation
070   between the destination tokens and all tokens in the sequence, resulting in a linear transformation.
071   For unmerge, we utilize the inverse or the transpose of such a linear transformation. The design of
072   ToMA carefully considers the advantages and limitations of GPU computations.
073   To further reduce the overhead of ToMA, we leverage the locality characteristics of the hidden states
074   within the latent space, which preserves image locality, so the tokens are more likely to be similar
075   within a local region. By partitioning the hidden states into local regions, we can run ToMA in each
076   region independently and mitigate the overhead costs by reducing the input size to ToMA while
077   enjoying the parallelism of computation. Moreover, we also find that the destination selection and
078   linear transformation of merge and unmerge can be shared across network layers and diffusion steps,
079   which further decreases the ToMA overhead costs. As a result, ToMA achieves 30%-50% speedups
080   without noticeable sacrifice in image quality.

177   4     T O MA
178
179   Token merge selects several destination tokens from the full set. It merges the tokens into desti-
180
      nations based on similarity scores, typically by assigning the merged destination as the average of
      the merge tokens. If the merged tokens are very similar, e.g., regions of pixels that represent the
181
      background with homogeneous colors, then the loss of information in the merge process can be
182
      minimized. Compared to token pruning, which directly throws away tokens, the token merge retains
183   more information, thus achieving better image quality.
184
185   Token merge reduces the number of inputs processed in the transformer block, leading to significant
186
      computational savings. Thus, we can achieve a theoretical speedup based on the token merge ratio
      and the computational complexity of the transformer block (details in Appendix). In the unmerge
187
      process, the values of the merged tokens are redistributed back to their original tokens to reverse
188
      the merge process. This operation ensures that the information from the merged tokens is restored
189   while maintaining the shape of the output without token merge so that later layers can process
190   without any modifications.
191
192
      ToMA consists of three key stages, where we achieve significant improvements: 1) Destination To-
      ken Selection: Efficiently selecting the most representative tokens as destinations. 2) merge Tokens:
193
      merge source tokens into their corresponding destination tokens based on similarity computed using
194
      attention. 3) Unmerge Tokens: Restoring the merged tokens to their original forms by reversing the
195   merge process as a linear operation. We also get further speedups by a) utilizing the locality charac-
196   teristics of the latent space and b) sharing destination/merge/unmerge computations across iterations
197   and layers to reduce overhead.

226   The submodular function, particularly the facility location function, offers a theoretical guarantee

      We have also considered clustering-based methods such as k-means for token selection. However,
      we opted against them for several reasons. First, k-means provides a soft target, which might
235
      introduce artifacts and achieve suboptimal performance. Second, k-means assumes that clusters
236
      have ball-like boundaries, which is not flexible and imposes extra assumptions on the latent space.
237   Third, the k-means method requires variable iterations to converge, and it is hard to control the
238   computational costs and trade-off with the clustering quality.

361
362   We evaluated ToMA on the SDXL stable-diffusion-xl-base-1.0 model using the Diffusers framework
363   to generate 1024 × 1024 images. The prompts were sourced from the GEMRec dataset (Guo et al.,
364   2024) and ImageNet 1K (Deng et al., 2009). To assess image quality, we used three primary metrics:
365   CLIP, DINO, and FID (Radford et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021; Heusel et al., 2017). For the CLIP
366   and DINO evaluations, we generated images using 50 different prompts, each with 3 distinct seeds,
367   and calculated the average score across all prompts and seeds. For measuring inference time, we
368
      reported the lowest wall-clock time over 100 runs.
369   Diffusion Models. We focus on the Stable Diffusion XL with the checkpoint of stable-diffusion-xl-
370   base-1.0. SDXL is capable of generating very high-quality images and is popular in the community
371   with abundant LoRAs available. Note that ToMA can generally apply to any transformer architec-
372   ture. Thus, we can simply extend ToMA to other diffusion models like SD3 and SD2.
373   Baseline. ToMeSD (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023) selects tokens either in fixed or random small tiles,
374   using a rigid approach. ToMeSD then discretely merges tokens by recording token pairs based on
375   their computed similarity. In the unmerge phase, ToMeSD restores the original tokens by copying
376   the values of the merged tokens back to their corresponding original tokens. We note that ToFu (Kim
377   et al., 2023) is another relevant method that dynamically selects whether to prune or merge tokens
      based on the function’s linearity to accelerate diffusion models. The work done by ToFu is orthogo-

518   5.4   DISCUSSION
519
520   Although the combination of tile facility location and tile merge produces high-quality images,
521   it still falls short in speed. Optimizing this operation at a lower level could significantly reduce
522
      computational costs. Additionally, improving the implementation of the pseudo-inverse API would
      allow us to apply it to larger matrices, potentially enhancing image quality. Moreover, we utilize
523
      linear transformation, specifically SDPA, for token merge, where the parameter V is currently set as
524
      an identity matrix and ignored during inference. This V matrix holds potential for future training,
525   which could further boost image quality.
526
527   Broader impact. ToMA enables speed improvements across a wide range of GPU architectures.
528
      On one hand, it accelerates image generation without compromising quality, making the process
      more efficient. On the other hand, it broadens the accessibility of diffusion models, allowing even
529
      those with less powerful or outdated GPUs to benefit from advanced techniques. ToMA reduces
530
      computational demands, making high-quality image generation feasible on a wider variety of
531   hardware, thus making diffusion models more accessible to a larger audience.
532
533
534   6     CONCLUSION
535
536   In this work, we propose ToMA to enhance the existing token merge method in three key areas: 1)
537
      more representative token selection, 2) a more flexible and efficient merge and unmerge operation,
      and 3) the introduction of locality and sharing strategies. As a result, we achieve significant speedup
538
      while maintaining high image quality. For future work, we aim to further speed up the low-level tile
539
      region computation as well as fine-tune the merge attention for better generation quality.

1221   H.2   S PECIAL D ESIGN OF T RANSFORMER B LOCKS AND P OSITIONAL E MBEDDING
1222
       Due to the unique design of the transformer blocks in DiT models, which combine attention blocks
1223
       and MLPs differently compared to the traditional setup of self-attention, cross-attention, and MLP,
1224   existing token merging methods such as ToMeSD, ToFu, and ToDo cannot be directly applied which
1225   would lead to all black or pure white noise. Additionally, the influence of positional embeddings
1226   further complicates their applicability since the naive application of token merging can lead to the
1227   selection of tokens that are not the most similar, which significantly degrades performance.
1228
       To address these issues, we implemented specific adaptations to the transformer blocks and po-
1229
       sitional embeddings, allowing our approach to successfully generate correct images with minimal
1230   quality loss which is shown in Tab. FIXME. Our method was selectively skip the first 10 transformer
1231   blocks in FLUX.1 to enable better the blend of text and image.
