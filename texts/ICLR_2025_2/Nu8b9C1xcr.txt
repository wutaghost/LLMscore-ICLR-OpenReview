                                                  A BSTRACT
010
               Cardinality estimation is crucial for enabling high query performance in relational
011
               databases. Recently learned cardinality estimation models have been proposed to
012
               improve accuracy but there is no systematic benchmark or datasets which allows
013            researchers to evaluate the progress made by new learned approaches and even
014            systematically develop new learned approaches. In this paper, we are releasing a
015            benchmark, containing hundreds of thousands of queries over 20 distinct real-world
016            databases for learned cardinality estimation. In contrast to other initial benchmarks,
017            our benchmark is much more diverse and can be used for training and testing
018            learned models systematically. Using this benchmark, we explored whether learned
019            cardinality estimation can be transferred to an unseen dataset in a zero-shot manner.
020            We trained GNN-based and transformer-based models to study the problem in three
021
               setups: 1-) instance-based, 2-) zero-shot, and 3-) fine-tuned.
022            Our results show that while we get promising results for zero-shot cardinality esti-
023
               mation on simple single table queries; as soon as we add joins, the accuracy drops.
               However, we show that with fine-tuning, we can still utilize pre-trained models
024
               for cardinality estimation, significantly reducing training overheads compared to
025
               instance specific models. We are open sourcing our scripts to collect statistics,
026            generate queries and training datasets to foster more extensive research, also from
027            the ML community on the important problem of cardinality estimation and in
028            particular improve on recent directions such as pre-trained cardinality estimation.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Cardinality estimation (CE), the problem of determining the number of intermediate records that
034   will be returned by a database query, is one of the key building block in modern databases for
035   optimizing performance, and has been studied extensively by the database research community. CE
036   is used by query optimizers to choose between alternative query execution plans. A query plan that
037
      produces the smallest intermediate results and processes the least amount of data usually leads to
      better execution times, and hence is preferred. Thus CE is critical for optimization decisions such as
038
      choosing performant join orders, deciding on whether or not to use an index, and picking the best join
039
      method. It has been shown (10) that "bad" cardinality estimates lead to "bad" execution plans that can
040   have multiple orders of magnitude worse performance. CE is also critical in database recommenders
041   to choose between alternative indexes, materializes views, and storage layouts.
042
043
      Traditional CE techniques used in modern database systems have well-known limitations, as they
      make simplistic data modeling assumptions, such as data uniformity and independence of columns in
044
      the tables. Learned CE model following an instance-based approach have been shown to improve
045
      upon the state-of-art techniques used by database systems today (9; 7; 22; 8; 19; 15). Despite their
046   better accuracy, learned CE models have not been adapted in practice, due to their high training
047   overheads, among other reasons. As a result, pre-trained CE models, which are trained on a corpus
048   of diverse datasets on transferable features, are highly desirable to alleviate the training costs and
049   increase adaption.
050
051   Our Main Contributions. In this paper we release CardBench, a benchmark containing thousands
052   of queries on 20 distinct databases, and scripts to compute data summary statistics and generate
053   queries. We generated three training datasets; one with queries over single tables with multiple filter
      predicates, one with queries with a binary join between two tables and multiple filter predicates on

      each table and one with queries with multiple binary joins and multiple filters predicates on each table.
055   Further, we release a query generator and flexible infrastructure that can be used and extended to
056   generate even more complex training queries. In comparison to existing CE benchmarks, CardBench
057   includes a much higher number of datasets and training data (i.e., queries and cardinalities) to
058   experiment with and compare different types of learned CE approaches.
059
      To show the benchmark in-action, in this paper we have trained GNN-based and transformer-based
060
      CE models, under 3 setups: 1-) instance-based (i.e., a CE model trained only for the particular
061   dataset), 2-) pre-trained CE model in zero-shot mode, 3-) pre-trained CE model with fine-tuning
062   on the dataset. Our results show that although CE is harder to learn with a pre-trained model in a
063   zero-shot setting, but with a small amount of samples fine-tuning a pre-trained model can achieve
064   high accuracy as good as, if not better than the instance-based methods. We hope that releasing
065   CardBench to the public community will help to reduce the barrier-of-entry for experimentation with
066   learned CE models and also foster new research, also from the ML community, and lead to further
067   advances on this highly important problem.

      Learned Cardinality Estimation. Traditional CE methods rely on heuristics and simple analytical
      models (5) (e.g., one-dimensional histograms or sketches). Recently, there have been various
073
      approaches for learned cardinality estimation (9; 7; 22; 8; 19; 15; 14; 26; 21). While early learned
074
      approaches used classical regression models, more recently deep learning based approaches (9; 13)
075   have been proposed which show highly promising accuracy over traditional approaches. All of these
076   methods are instance-based: they utilize instances-specific features like names of tables and columns;
077   they are evaluated and trained on the same database. There are two main approaches: workload
078   driven and data driven. Workload driven approaches need to run a representative set of queries (10
079   to 100 thousands) to collect true cardinalities as labels for the training samples, incurring a very
080   high overhead. These models also need to be re-trained as the representative workloads shift. To
081   avoid this high overhead of running thousands of queries to collect the true cardinalities per dataset,
082   more recently so called data-driven learned cardinality estimators have been proposed (7; 23). In
083
      contrast to the workload-driven approaches, data-driven approaches learn the multi-variate data
      distribution within and across tables without running any query plan but simply by learning data
084
      distributions of the base tables. Data driven approaches still require one model per dataset and need
085
      to be re-trained as the data gets updates, which still incurs training overheads. As such, very recently
086   some first generalizable cardinality estimators (12) have been proposed that can be used on datasets
087   in a zero-shot manner; i.e., the model can be used without the need to learn one instance-specific
088   model per dataset. Moreover, zero-shot models also promise that they can automatically provide
089   accurate estimates even if the underlying data of the dataset is updated (i.e., new rows are added or
090   existing ones are updated or deleted). The core idea is that such zero-shot models are pre-trained on a
091   broad spectrum of different datasets and use transferable features, such as table sizes, allowing them
092   to generalize to unseen datasets. We argue that more research is needed on pre-trained models on
093   more compelx queries. For this purpose, we provide a very first large-scale training corpus to foster
094
      research in this direction.
095
096   Cardinality Estimation Benchmarks. To benchmark cardinality estimation models, the early
097   papers have mainly relied on either home-grown benchmarks with non-public datasets or they have
098   used some available benchmarks that have been originally designed for other purposes. One central
099
      benchmark that has been used so far extensively by many papers is the Join Order Benchmark (JOB)
      (11), which is based on real-world data from IMDb, containing interesting data distributions and
100
      correlations. JOB contains 79 queries, which challenge cardinality estimation. This is in stark contrast
101
      to many other database benchmarks such as the TPC benchmarks which use synthetic data that is
102   way simpler and thus does not represent a real challenge for cardinality estimation. However, one
103   benchmark with one dataset is not sufficient to benchmark learned cardinality estimation and show its
104   robustness towards various different real-world domains. As such, in the recent years, there have been
105   further efforts to create additional benchmarks that can be used to test cardinality estimation models.
106   For example, (4) proposes a new benchmark, which contains one new real-world dataset STATS and
107   a generated query workload that is more diverse in terms of query and data characteristics than JOB.
      Another benchmark (13), which aims in a similar direction uses two different datasets - IMDB and

258   4.1       DATA E NGINEERING
259
260
      Features: The set of features (numerical or categorical) that the models use are specified in Table 2.
      In order to train a zero-shot model that can generalize to new datasets, we only include transferable
261
      features that are dataset-agnostic. This means that we exclude dataset-specific information such as
262
      table or column names. Furthermore, we select detailed statistics (e.g., histogram, correlation) as
263   input features so the model can learn the underlying data distribution.
264
265   Pre-processing: For numerical features and the cardinality label, we employ a data transformation
266
      process to enhance their representation. First, we apply a logarithmic transformation to the data,
      which helps to reduce the impact of outliers. We then perform standardization to ensure that all
267
      features are on the same scale. For categorical features, we adopt one-hot encoding to allow effective
268
      learning of relationships between different categories. Additionally, we filter out queries with zero
269   cardinality or zero predicates. In practice, such queries are relatively rare, and their inclusion could
      introduce noise into the training process.

      4.2     G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK M ODEL
271
272   The graph representation of SQL queries provides a natural way to capture the relationship between
273   different elements of a query. This representation allows us to directly leverage Graph Neural
274   Network (GNN) which is designed to learn from graph-structured data. We build a GNN model
275   to predict cardinality of queries based on the design of the GNN-based cost model from (6). The
276   GNN model first initializes the hidden state for each node by applying a node-type specific Multi-
277   Layer-Perceptrons (MLP) to the node’s input feature vector (a concatenation of the input feature
278   values of that node). Since query graphs are directed acyclic graphs, the GNN model propagates
279
      information from leaf to root nodes via bottom-up message passing. This topological information
      flow also follows the natural query processing order. During message passing, at each node, the
280
      hidden states of its children nodes are summed up and concatenated with the original hidden state
281
      of that node, the concatenated embedding then runs through a separate node-type specific MLP to
282   derive the updated hidden state. Finally after all nodes are updated in topological order, the hidden
283   state of the root node is read out as the learned graph-level embedding. This embedding is passed
284   into an MLP-based cardinality model to get the final prediction.
285
286
      4.3     G RAPH T RANSFORMER M ODEL
287
288   In addition to GNN, Graph transformer is an emerging type of neural network architecture specifically
289   designed for graphs. Similar to traditional transformers, which capture the relationship between tokens
290   in a sequence with self-attention, graph transformer extends the attention mechanism to graphical
291   data, where the relationships between different nodes are constrained by the graph structure (25).
292   Compared to traditional transformers, we make following adaptation3 :
293   Heterogeneous input embedding layer: The SQL query graph is a heterogeneous graph with
294   multiple node types that contain different features. For each node type, we use a separate input
295   embedding layer to project node features to a fixed-dimension input node embedding.
296
      Shortest distance spatial encoding: Following the methodology of Graphormer (24), we compute
297
      the shortest path distance between node pairs to construct a spatial encoding matrix. This matrix is
298
      incorporated as a learnable bias that adds to the input before the softmax in the self-attention block.
299
300   Directional causal mask: To ensure information flow adheres strictly to the DAG’s structure, we
301   implement a causal masking mechanism based on the topological ordering of the nodes.
302   Virtual node readout: Inspired by Graphormer, we augment the query graph by introducing a virtual
303   node as the direct child of all other nodes. Given that the virtual node attends to all other nodes in the
304   query graph, its embedding serves as the graph-level representation for cardinality prediction.
305
      Figure 2 shows an overview of the graph transformer cardinality prediction model. The backbone
306   of the model architecture is derived from encoder-only transformers. The node feature encoder
307   embed the node features to vectors and pass those vectors to N transformer blocks with modified
308   self-attention layers using additional causal mask and spatial encoding.

344   Instance-based Models: In this configuration, we train and evaluate individual models using a

      The average training times under each configuration are4 1.3hr, 1.4hr and 11min (single-table queries),
358   and 1.3hr, 1.5hr, and 11min (binary join queries) for the GNN-based model on a 8-core VM (4GB

      5.3   VARYING S AMPLE S IZE
487
488   We study the impact of varying amounts of training data on model accuracy by considering three
489   sample sizes: 250, 500, and 1000. For each sample size, we train an instance-based model or fine-tune
490   a pre-trained model, and evaluate it on the same 500 samples as described in Section 5.1.1. We
491   repeat this process for both the GNN and transformer-based models. The results for single table and
492   binary join cardinality predictions are summarized in figure 5 and figure 6 respectively. We observe
493   same patterns between GNN and transformer. The model accuracy improves as more training data is
494
      provided, for both instance-based and fine-tuned models.
495   Comparing instance-based training with fine-tuning, we find that the fine-tuned models are more
496   sample-efficient. Fine-tuned models achieve similar median and tail Q-errors as instance-based
497   models trained with twice the amount of data. This is evidence that fine-tuned models benefit from
498   the generalization power of a pre-trained model, which has learned from a large number of data
499   patterns over many datasets. When using only 500 samples from each of the binary join datasets, the
500
      average P50 and P95 Q-errors across 20 datasets are: 1.57 & 280 for the instanced-based GNN model,
      and 1.32 & 120 for the fine-tuned GNN model. There is a significant improvement especially for
501
      the tail queries due to learnings transferred from more diverse data patterns seen by the pre-trained
502
      model. Nonetheless, the accuracy gap between instance-based and fine-tuned models reduces as the
503   sample size increases. This suggests that fine-tuning on a pre-trained model is more advantageous
504   when there is limited amount of training data available for a new dataset.

      6     C ONCLUSION AND B ROADER I MPACT
508   In this paper, we release CardBench a benchmark for CE in relational databases. CardBench contains
509   code to 1-) compute various data statistics, 2-) generate queries and execute them, and 3-) create the
510   training data (the annotated querygraphs). We are also releasing three training datasets with queries
511   over 20 datasets and their true cardinalities: one containing queries with single table queries, one
512
      with binary joins, and the third with multi-join queries. We trained both instance-based and zero-shot
      models, and observed that CE is a extremely challenging problem to learn in a zero-shot manner.
513
      However, when using pre-trained models with a small amount of fine-tuning, we can achieve accuracy
514
      similar to instance-based models, with much lower training overhead.
515
516   With CardBench, we hope to provide a systematic mechanism to track progress, especially on recent
517   directions such as pre-trained CE models. Moreover, we think that CardBench is highly important
518
      to foster further research on CE models, from the ML and DB communities. The training datasets
      were constructed with high overhead (14 cpu years of query runtime). Releasing such a training set
519
      lowers the barrier of entry for developing and training new CE models. Furthermore, we hope that
520
      the benchmark itself will be extended with new datasets and more complex queries using the tools
521   and code we provide and thus push the boundaries of learned CE models further.

      A.2     P RIMARY-F OREIGN K EY R ELATIONSHIPS
643
644   Primary foreign key (pk-fk) relationships are part of a database schema design (17). A pk-fk
645   relationship is a logical connection between the columns of two tables semantically linking their
646   information. In the real world, most of the times two tables are joined using the columns that
647   participate in the pk-fk relationship. Naturally we want to use pk-fk relationships when creating
      synthetic workloads. A challenge we faced is that open source datasets do not usually specify pk-fk


                                                                         12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      relationships, instead we discover pk-fk relationship between columns of different tables using the
649   steps outlined below. One assumptions we make is that data is organized in a snowflake schema, this
650   is the norm in practice and what is assumed by most database benchmarks (TPC-H (3), SSB (16),
651   TPC-DS(2), TPC-C(1), JOB(10)).
652
653           1. Identify candidate primary keys per table using a uniqueness threshold and choose one with
654              the most unique values. A primary key should be unique for each row of a table (17), we
655              relax this condition and require that for a column to be a primary key more than 90% of
656              rows must be unique values.
657           2. Split tables of a dataset in fact and dimension tables6 . The criteria we use is a size threshold
658              (fact tables should be a few times larger than dimension tables) as well as a check if a
659              primary key exists for dimension tables.
660           3. For each dimension table and pk column identify a fk column in the fact table(s). Out of all
661              possible candidates we choose the pk-fk pair with the most join results, which we obtain by
662              running a join query using the candidate pk-fk column pair as the join condition.
