                                                     A BSTRACT
008
009
               Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) has been demonstrated to improve the gen-
010            eralization performance of overparameterized models by seeking flat minima on
011            the loss landscape through optimizing model parameters that incur the largest loss
012            within a neighborhood. Nevertheless, such min-max formulations are computation-
013            ally challenging especially when the problem is highly non-convex. Additionally,
014            focusing only on the worst-case local solution while ignoring potentially many
015            other local solutions may be suboptimal when searching for flat minima. In this
016            work, we propose Tilted SAM (TSAM), a generalization of SAM inspired by expo-
017            nential tilting that effectively assigns higher priority to local solutions that are flatter
018
               and that incur larger losses. TSAM is parameterized by a tilt hyperparameter t and
               reduces to SAM as t approaches infinity. We prove that (1) the TSAM objective is
019
               smoother than SAM and thus easier to optimize; and (2) TSAM explicitly favors
020
               flatter minima as t increases. This is desirable as flatter minima could have better
021            generalization properties for certain tasks. We develop algorithms motivated by
022            the discretization of Hamiltonian dynamics to solve TSAM. Empirically, TSAM
023            arrives at flatter local minima and results in superior test performance than the
024            baselines of SAM and ERM across a range of image and text tasks.

082   Contributions. We propose TSAM, a new optimization objective that reweights the parameters
083   around local minima via exponential tilting. We rigorously study several properties of TSAM,
084   showing that it always favors flatter solutions as t increases and achieves a tighter generalization
085   bound than SAM for modest values of t (Section 3). To optimize TSAM, we adapt a specific HMC

087

090   2    R ELATED W ORK
091
      Sharpness-Aware Minimization. SAM regularizes overparameterized models by considering
092
      adversarial data points that have large training errors (Foret et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2021). The
093   SAM variants, training dynamics, and applications in different models have been extensively studied
094   in prior work (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022; Baek et al., 2024; Bartlett et al., 2023; Chen
095   et al., 2021; 2024; Du et al., 2022; Foret et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Long &
096   Bartlett, 2023; Mi et al., 2022; Mueller et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
097   2021; Zhuang et al., 2022). Some work aim to improve efficiency of the SAM algorithm studying
098   different relaxations (Du et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a). Zhao et al. (2022) use a linear interpolation
099   between normal gradients and SAM outer gradients evaluated at the max-loss parameter, which does
100   not take into account the possibly many bad local minima for highly non-convex problems. Liu et al.
101
      (2022b) improve the inner max optimization by adding a random perturbation to the gradient ascent
      step to smoothen its trajectory. Li & Giannakis (2023) leverages a moving average of stochastic
102
      gradients in the ascent direction to reduce the gradient variance1 . Our goal is not to better approximate
103
      the inner max or develop algorithms for solving the min-max SAM formulation, but rather, to solve
104   a different TSAM objective that reweights many local minima to seek flat solutions. Nevertheless,
105

115   There is also work attempting to understand why SAM leads to better generalization or theoretically
116   characterize what SAM (and its implementation) is effectively minimizing (Andriushchenko &
117   Flammarion, 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Long & Bartlett, 2023; Wen et al., 2022). In this work,
118   we prove that TSAM (and SAM) encourages flatter models for a class of problems including

120   minima (Definition 3). Our proposed TSAM framework is particularly suitable for problems where
121   flatness helps generalization. The various notions of sharpness, along with theoretical relations
122   between sharpness and generalization still remain an open problem (Andriushchenko et al., 2023;
123
      Ding et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024), which is outside the scope of our paper.
124   Tilting in Machine Learning. Exponential tilting, used to shift parametric distributions, has appeared
125   in previous literature in importance sampling, optimization, and information theory (e.g., Aminian
126   et al., 2024; Dembo, 2009; Kort & Bertsekas, 1972; Siegmund, 1976). Recently, the idea of tilted
127   risk minimization (which exponentially reweights different training samples) has been explored
128   in machine learning applications such as enforcing fairness and robustness, image segmentation,
129
      and noisy label correction (Aminian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Robey et al., 2022; Szabó et al.,
      2021; Zhou et al., 2020). A closely-related LogSumExp operator is often used to as an smooth
130
      approximation to the max, which is always considered more computationally favorable (Calafiore
131
      & El Ghaoui, 2014; Kort & Bertsekas, 1972; Li et al., 2023; Shen & Li, 2010). One application
132   of tilted risks applied to the adversarial training problem is to balance worst-case robustness (i.e.,
133   adversarial robustness) and average-case robustness in the data space (Robey et al., 2022), among
134   other approaches that can also achieve a transition between worst-case and average-case errors (Rice
135   et al., 2021). Our work is similar conceptually, but we consider reweighting adversarial model
136   parameters, instead of adversarial data points. Compared with SAM (optimizing the largest loss),
137   the TSAM framework offers additional flexibility of optimizing over quantiles of losses given the
138   connections between tilting and quantile approaches (Li et al., 2023; Rockafellar et al., 2000).

140   3     P ROPERTIES OF TSAM
141
142   In this section, we discuss properties of the TSAM objective. We first state the convexity and
143   smoothness of TSAM (Section 3.1). We then show that as t increases, the gap between less-flat
144
      and more-flat solutions measured in terms of the TSAM objective becomes larger. In other words,
      optimizing TSAM would give a flatter solution as t increases (Section 3.2). Finally, we discuss the
145
      generalization behavior of TSAM and prove that there exists t P p0, 8q that result in the tightest
146
      generalization bound (Section 3.3). All properties discussed in this section hold regardless of the
147   distributions of ϵ (i.e., choice of µpϵq), unless otherwise specified.

      6          C ONCLUSION
533   In this work, we have proposed a tilted sharpness-aware minimization (TSAM) objective, which
534   leverages exponential tilting (parameterized by t) to reweight potentially many local minima in the
535   neighborhoods, as opposed to the worst-case minima SAM targets at. We have proved TSAM is a
536   more smooth problem relative to SAM with a bounded t, and that TSAM explicitly encourages flatter
537   solutions as t increases for a class of problems including generalized linear models. We have proposed
538   a practical algorithm motivated by HMC to sample from the tilted distribution etLpθ`ϵq . Through
539   experiments on different models and datasets including label-noise settings, we have demonstrated
      that TSAM consistently outperforms SAM and its variants on both image and text datasets.
