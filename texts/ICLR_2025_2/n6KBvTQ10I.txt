011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose to bridge the gap between semi-supervised and unsupervised image
014            recognition with a flexible method that performs well for both generalized cate-
015
               gory discovery (GCD) and image clustering. Despite the overlap in motivation
               between these tasks, the methods themselves are restricted to a single task – GCD
016
               methods are reliant on the labeled portion of the data, and deep image clustering
017
               methods have no built-in way to leverage the labels efficiently. We connect the
018            two regimes with an innovative approach that Utilizes Neighbor Information for
019            Classification (UNIC) both in the unsupervised (clustering) and semisupervised
020            (GCD) setting. State-of-the-art clustering methods already rely heavily on near-
021            est neighbors. We improve on their results substantially in two parts, first with
022            a sampling and cleaning strategy where we identify accurate positive and nega-
023            tive neighbors, and secondly by finetuning the backbone with clustering losses
024            computed by sampling both types of neighbors. We then adapt this pipeline to
025            GCD by utilizing the labelled images as ground truth neighbors. Our method
026
               yields state-of-the-art results for both clustering (+3% ImageNet-100, Imagenet-
               200) and GCD (+0.8% ImageNet-100, +5% CUB-200).

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Image recognition has long been treated as a fundamental task in computer vision. One very popular
033
      setting is supervised image classification, where each image has a single ground truth label, and a
      model learns to predict labels by training with image-label pairs (Deng et al., 2009). As work in
034
      the fully-supervised setting plateaus, interest in the field has shifted to settings more reminiscent of
035
      the real world, where access to labeled data is more limited, or even completely absent. So, many
036   different recognition tasks have evolved based on the presence or absence, fraction of presence,
037   quality, granularity, and type of labels (Koch et al., 2015; Bendale & Boult, 2015; Snell et al., 2017;
038   Vinyals et al., 2017; Suri et al., 2023). At the extreme, unsupervised classification (Gansbeke et al.,
039   2020), or deep image clustering, deals with the case where none of the labels are disclosed during
040   the training step. Recently, generalized category discovery (GCD) (Vaze et al., 2022) has been
041   proposed, such that some images from some classes are labelled, and some classes are completely
042   unlabelled.
043   We propose a method, UNIC (pronounce “you-neek”), which can solve both clustering and GCD, as
044   shown in Figure 1. When labels are unavailable, UNIC can operate as a state-of-the-art deep image
045   clustering pipeline. Where labels are available, UNIC can leverage them for SOTA performance on
046   GCD. We accomplish this by designing UNIC to exploit neighbor information in the feature space of
047   high quality unsupervised image representation models. For every image, we mine both “positive”
048   (belongs to the same class) and “negative” (belongs to a different class) neighbors. We then finetune
049   the unsupervised model end-to-end with losses that pull the positive neighbors together, and push
050
      the negative neighbors apart. This adapts quite naturally to GCD, since we can use the labels to
      guarantee 100% accuracy for a portion of the positive and negative neighbors.
051
052   We find that with the unsupervised DINO (Caron et al., 2021) ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) back-
053   bone, we can reliably extract both positive and negative neighbors. The success of our method is
      fully dependent on the quality of these neighbors. So, we carefully analyze the representation space

      We improve on prior clustering work that relies on a three-stage pipeline (Gansbeke et al., 2020)

088

093

094
      methods typically rely on some clustering for initialization (Vaze et al., 2022; Pu et al., 2023). How-
095
      ever, we find such initialization totally unnecessary as we can instead learn a partially-supervised
096   clustering end-to-end. That is, we are able to treat the labelled images as a special case where
097   we simply supervise their neighbor mining.Strikingly, this results in much better performance on
098   the labeled classes for other method compared to others, while also having competitive results in
099   unlabeled classes.
100
      We summarize our contributions as follows:
101
102
             • We propose a novel neighbor mining strategy where we improve the accuracy of positive
103            neighbors via cleaning and introduce the idea of negative neighbors for image clustering.
104
105
             • We formulate a general pipeline which can be trained end-to-end for image clustering and,
               with minimal adaptation, for the semi-supervised GCD task as well.
106
107          • We achieve SOTA on both tasks, unifying classification for unsupervised and semi-
               supervised methods, with consistent gains over most datasets in both GCD and Clustering.

      2.1   U NSUPERVISED R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
111
112   Unsupervised representation learning refers to the process of training deep neural networks to ex-
113   tract information from images without labels. Some early approaches rely on a host of diverse
114   pretext tasks – patch prediction (Doersch et al., 2015; Gupta et al., 2020), jigsaw solving (Noroozi
115   & Favaro, 2016), colorization (Larsson et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016), inpainting (Pathak et al.,
116   2016), rotation prediction (Gidaris et al., 2018), image generation (Donahue & Simonyan, 2019),
117   etc. (Doersch & Zisserman, 2017). Contrastive (Wu et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020b;c; Misra &
118
      Maaten, 2020; Bachman et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020a; He et al., 2019; Hjelm et al., 2018; Henaff,
      2020; Oord et al., 2018; Tian et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2019; Zbontar et al., 2021; Chen & He, 2021)
119
      and clustering (Caron et al., 2018; Asano et al., 2019; Caron et al., 2019; 2020; 2021; Li et al., 2021)
120
      methods have largely superseded these. More recent approaches rely on image reconstruction (He
121   et al., 2021; Assran et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2022; Mishra et al.,
122   2022) and generation (Mukhopadhyay et al., 2023; Hudson et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022). Currently
123   these clustering, contrastive, and reconstruction-based approaches are all among the state-of-the-art.
124   In our work, since we do deep clustering (an unsupervised problem), we need backbones which do
125   not require labels, and we primarily use a ViT-B (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) trained with DINO (Caron
126   et al., 2021) due to the nice properties of its embeddings with respect to nearest neighbors.
127
128   2.2   D EEP C LUSTERING
129
130   Deep clustering aims to learn groups of images from unlabelled data, where the images in a given
131   grouping (cluster) have similar visual semantics. A naive approach would use kmeans on top of some
132   embeddings extracted by a model trained with unsupervised representation learning, and indeed, this
133
      task has been used as a benchmark for the quality of such representations (Gwilliam & Shrivastava,
      2022). The work in this area can be primarily divided between single-stage and multi-stage methods,
134
      as in (Adaloglou et al., 2023). Some of these target the clustering problem directly (Chang et al.,
135
      2017a), but many of the single-stage methods use clustering primarily as a mechanism for learning
136   good image representations (Caron et al., 2019; 2020; Asano et al., 2019). Indeed, these methods
137   are only single-stage in the sense they have one learning stage, and they often still rely on running
138   k-means on the final representations for the actual cluster predictions (Li et al., 2021; Huang et al.,
139   2023).
140
      The multi-stage pipeline is first proposed with SCAN (Gansbeke et al., 2020), which starts with
141
      a representation learning stage, followed by a stage where a clustering head is learned based on
142   nearest neighbor mining, then a final stage where the head is further tuned with pseudo ground
143   truths. Other methods offer deviations within this pipeline – NNM matches neighbors at both the
144   batch and global level (Dang et al., 2021). SPICE follows similar stages, but with a heavier emphasis
145   on pseudo-labeling (Niu et al., 2022). TSP (Zhou & Zhang, 2022b) and TEMI (Adaloglou et al.,
146   2023) leverage ViTs to outperform the earlier methods (which mainly use ResNets (He et al., 2015)),
147   although it is worth noting that all these methods are conceptually backbone-agnostic. Our method
148   is conceptually aligned with SCAN and NNM except that we introduce key novelties with respect
149   to the neighbor mining, namely with our process for cleaning positive neighbors, and our use of
150
      negative neighbors. Also, compared to many of these methods, we use a single clustering head for
      less expensive training.

153   2.3   G ENERALIZED C ATEGORY D ISCOVERY
154
      GCD is an open world image recognition task (Vaze et al., 2022). To set up some GCD task, we
155
      typically take an existing image dataset and cut the classes in half, with some known, the other un-
156   known. We then use labels for half of the images for the known classes. The same problem is often
157   sometimes referred to as open-world semi-supervised learning (Cao et al., 2021). The preliminary
158   solution (also referred to as GCD) combines unsupervised contrastive learning for the unlabelled im-
159   ages and supervised contrastive learning for the labelled images, along with an extension of k-means
160   to support the labelled classes (Vaze et al., 2022). DCCL (Pu et al., 2023) uses a two alternating
161   stages in which visual concepts are discovered and then images are represented with these con-
      cepts. SimGCD (Wen et al., 2023) mitigates prediction biases in parametric classifiers with entropy

196   3.1     P ROBLEM S ETTINGS

198   Consider a training set Dt = X × Y (where X is the set of images and Y is the set of labels)
199   of N = ∥Dt ∥ images and a evaluation set De . Dt consists of two subsets unlabelled training set
200   DU = X × YU and labelled training set DL = X × YL . DL is a null set for image clustering. In
201   that case, Du is as same as Dt . That is, there are no labeled images.
202   The evaluation set De is different for clustering and GCD settings. We use the evaluation set of the
203   image dataset of choice (which is disjoint from the training set) for the clustering setting. However,
204   De is DU for the GCD setting. The objective of both clustering and GCD is to come up with a
205   function f that successfully maps X to Y in De . Note that f = fcls ◦ femb for the rest of this
206   section.

             5                                       C ONCLUSION
525
526        In this paper, we present an approach that improves on state-of-the-art for image clustering and
527        GCD. Due to its flexibility and general formulation, our approach also conceptually unifies these
528        two related vision problems. We also provide robust analysis of our major contributions, namely
529        neighbor cleaning, negative neighbor mining, and other relevant hyperparameters. We hope our
530
           work here serves to break down the barriers between different levels of supervision, as we work
           towards real-world solutions that use labels in efficient, intuitive ways while still working well when
531
           labels are totally unavailable.
532
533        Limitations: Our proposed UNIC leverages neighbor information very well. However, if the neigh-
534        bor mining fails, or is too noisy, our method fails. So, future work could address leveraging the
535        neighbors in a manner that is less sensitive to noise.
536        Future Work: Our work has shown that deep clustering does not require an entropy maximization
537        objective. Therefore, future work can relax the assumption on equally distributed classes and attempt
538        to solve unbalanced clustering problems with UNIC (at λEN T = 0). Most ideas in computer vision
539        start with image classification and then get extended into object detection. Similarly, UNIC’s losses
           can be applied to open world object detection.

       A.7    FAQ(4) W HY ARE THERE MANY EMPTY CELLS IN THE CLUSTERING RESULTS TABLE ?
973
974    Earlier deep clustering methods were not scale-able for a large number of classes. They usually
975    attempted to solve CIFAR-10 like datasets or subsets of CIFAR-100 (20 classes out of the full 100),
976    and Imagenet (10 classes out of the full 1000). Scaling them to present day benchmarks (up to 200
977    classes) requires significant engineering effort.

       A.8    FAIR COMPARISON WITH RESPECT TO BACKBONES
980
981    Unsupervised and semi-supervised (including GCD) work depends on a pre-trained backbone to
982    bootstrap their frameworks. It is impossible to start with a randomly initialized backbone. The
983    performance of an algorithm is highly dependent on the backbones being used.
984
       Firstly, the fairness of comparison aspect of choosing backbones will be considered. Image cluster-
985
       ing is an older problem that was first explored with ResNet backbones since 2020. The performance
986    of the models made a significant jump once ViT backbones started being used since 2022 for the
987    task. Inorder to provide a fair comparison, we re-implement important work from ResNet years
988    (SCAN (Gansbeke et al., 2020)) with a ViT-B/16 backbone pretrained with DINOv1. There are no
989    clustering papers that uses a DINOv2 backbone.
990
       GCD was defined more recently in Vaze et al. (2022). Therefore, there is no ResNet-based work
991
       on the problem. Earlier work reported SOTA with ViT-B/16 DINOv1. More recent papers started
992    reporting numbers for ViT-B/16 DINOv2 (with clear gains over DINOv1 numbers). We report our
993    numbers with both DINOv1 and DINOv2 backbones. Our method outperforms previous methods
994    with +1.45% accuracy on Imagenet-100 with DINOv1. Our method achieves SOTA over all (older
995    and newer) methods with DINOv2 backbone at +0.75% on Imagenet-100 and +5.06% on CUB-200.
996    It should be noted that DINOv2 CIFAR-10 numbers were not reported in the literature and we have
997    re-run their code to generate those numbers.
998    Secondly, the backbone also dictates which type of frameworks (or components of frameworks)
999    can be applied. For example, multiple heads and self-labelling like ideas from SCAN cannot be
1000   applied to ViT-B/16 DINO backbones. We have recreated SCAN for modern backbones by carefully
1001   changing these components of the original algorithm. Similarly, modern work like TEMI (Adaloglou
1002   et al., 2023) reports very low numbers for older ResNet MoCo backbones.

1128   (83.22%) is better than the All Class Accuracy using the labeled negatives (82.66%).

1131
       negatives in the labeled case.

1134
       old, 0.25N will be unlabelled old, and 0.5N will be unlabelled new. Consider the case where every
1135   image gets k negative neighbors.

1138
       neighbors from Old and New classes. This will amount to 0.375N k from each. Consider that α
       fraction of negative neighbors for the labeled images come from the rest of the labeled images (all
1139
       true negatives). However, this set will only have images from Old classes. Therefore, (1 − α)
1140
       fraction should be mined.  This will have equal fractions of Old and New classes. However,         in
1141   total, 0.25N k α + 1−α      of the negative  neighbors will be from Old classes  and 0.25N  k  1−α
                              2                                                                         2
1142   will be from New classes. For any choice of α (our reported result is for 0.5), the Old classes will
1143   be overrepresented as negative neighbors in the training. The under representation of the New class
1144   is the major contributing factor to the reported accuracy drop for New Classes, and therefore for the
1145   overall accuracy.

       A.14    FAQ: H OW D OES UNIC’ S N EIGHBOR M INING C OMPARE TO H ARD N EGATIVE
1148           M INING
1149
1150   MoCHi Kalantidis et al. (2020) and HCL Robinson et al. (2021) has demonstrated the effectiveness
1151   of an unsupervised hard negative mining step on top of the vanilla SSL pretraining. These works
1152   shows impressive image classification results for the fully supervised case compared to the vanilla
1153   SSL pretraining. This appendix subsection attempts to explore (a) whether such techniques are
1154   useful for UNIC’s problem settings, and (b) the reasons for it.
1155   Both HCL (built on SimCLR Chen et al. (2020a)) and MoCHi (built on MoCoV2 Chen et al.
1156   (2020c)) attempt to find hard negatives to perform contrastive learning. Their objective is for the
1157   images in the dataset to uniformly be spread out through the embedding space. To this end, MoCHI
1158   specifically measures “uniformity” as defined in Wang & Isola (2020). HCL calls this “optimal
1159   embedding” and proves theoretical guarantees.
1160   Such uniform spread of embeddings can be classified using MLPs trained with fully supervised
1161   learning. This is why HCL and MoCHi outperform the SimCLR and MoCo on classification tasks.
1162   It should be noted that spreading out the dataset uniformly is detrimental to the naturally occur-
1163   ring decision boundaries (i.e. cluster structure) that are used in unsupervised and semi-supervised
1164   learning settings. Our design choices in UNIC are to do the opposite – bring embeddings of the
1165   same class together while increasing the distance between the embeddings for different classes. In
1166   essence, this creates clusters (with natural decision boundaries) instead of a uniform spread.
1167   We experiment with unsupervised kMeans clustering on HCL and MoCHi embeddings and append
1168   the results in Table 1. It should be noted that these numbers are below the kMeans result from
1169   SimCLR for STL-10 (which is reported in the first row from Gansbeke et al. (2020)) and kMeans
1170   result from MoCoV2 for all datasets. Then, we run the full UNIC training pipeline on the MoCHi
1171   and MoCoV2 backbones and report results in Table 7. MoCHi performs worse than vanilla MoCoV2
1172   as the base representation for UNIC.
1173   All these experiments provide strong evidence of why contrastive learning with hard negatives is
1174   less desirable for UNIC use cases.
