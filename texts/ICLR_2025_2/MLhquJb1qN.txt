009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the main challenges in optimal scaling of large language models (LLMs)
012            is the prohibitive cost of hyperparameter tuning, particularly learning rate η and
013            batch size B. While techniques like µP (Yang et al., 2022) provide scaling rules
014            for optimal η transfer in the infinite model size limit, the optimal scaling behavior
015            in the infinite data size limit remains unknown. We fill in this gap by observing
016
               for the first time an intricate dependence of optimal η scaling on the pretraining
               token budget T , B and its relation to the critical batch size Bcrit , which we mea-
017
               sure to evolve as Bcrit ∝ T . Furthermore, we show that the optimal batch size
018
               is positively correlated with Bcrit : keeping it fixed becomes suboptimal over time
019            even if learning rate is scaled optimally. Surprisingly, our results demonstrate
020            that the observed optimal η and B dynamics are preserved with µP model scaling,
021            challenging the conventional view of Bcrit dependence solely on loss value. Com-
022            plementing optimality, we examine the sensitivity of loss to changes in learning
023            rate, where we find the sensitivity to decrease with increase of T and to remain
024            constant with µP model scaling. We hope our results make the first step towards a
025            unified picture of the joint optimal data and model scaling.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have increasingly become a prominent area of study in the field
030   of Natural Language Processing (NLP) and beyond. They have demonstrated significant improve-
031   ment in performance across a wide range of tasks, such as language understanding, text generation,
032   translation, and summarization, showing results comparable or outperforming those of an average
033   domain expert (Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024). The primary advan-
034   tage of LLMs is their ability to scale well with increased computational resources, which results in
035   predictive improved performance (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022).
036   One of the main challenges in LLM scaling lies in the proportional scaling of computational re-
037   sources required for hyperparameter tuning. To remedy this, µTransfer (Yang et al., 2022) technique
038   was proposed as a way to transfer hyperparameters from a small (proxy) model to a large (target) one
039   by introducing scaling rules for learning rate, weight multipliers and initialization scale, altogether
040   referred to as Maximal Update Parametrization (µP). While significantly reducing the hyperparam-
041   eter tuning cost coming with model scaling, its applicability is limited by requiring both target and
042   proxy models to share the same batch size and number of training iterations. With current pretrain-
043   ing budgets surpassing trillions of tokens, it makes µTransfer computationally expensive to apply
044
      even with tuning a small proxy model.
045   One solution would be hyperparameter tuning performed both for the small proxy model and on
046   the small dataset, followed by µTransfer to the larger model and larger dataset, under assumption of
047   both datasets being sampled from the same underlying data distribution. This raises the question of
048   µTransfer’s applicability in the infinite data limit, which can be formalized as an increase in the size
049   of the training dataset, which in the LLM case is measured by the number of tokens. Understanding
050
      training dynamics in this limit would unlock hyperparameter transfer not only across model scales,
      but also across data horizons, thus removing the largest limitation of µTransfer.
051
052   The study of optimal hyperparameter evolution throughout the model training should also be com-
053   plemented with a study of hyperparameter sensitivity, i.e. the measure of how the model performance
      is affected when the training is performed outside the optimal hyperparameter range. In practice, it

055   would be of large interest to find training regimes which have small hyperparameter sensitivity and
056   penalize model performance the least if the optimal hyperparameters are missed by a small degree.

088   2     M ETHODOLOGY
089
090   2.1   T ERMINOLOGY
091
092   Time (T ): we often use the terms time, token budget, and data horizon interchangeably, both
093   to specify the measure of the training data size in tokens, and to pinpoint the specific moment
094   throughout the model training. From this perspective, an infinite data limit T → ∞, as opposed to
095   a fixed budget regime with T = const, refers to an (infinite) increase of the number of tokens seen
096   by the model during pretraining.
097   µP: we refer to a model with width dbase model as a base model if µP scaling multipliers for learning
098   rates, weight multipliers and initialization scale (Sec. 2.2) are computed relative to this width. This
099   brings us to a broader view on µP where the base model “pinpoints” the training dynamics for all
100   the other models obtained either by scaling up or down the base dbase model width. Together with the
101   base model, we refer to this set of models as a µP model family or as a µP trajectory if the direction
102   of scaling is implied. We also slightly distinguish between the base and proxy models, where the
103   former is used to define a µP model family, while the latter is a model used to tune hyperparameters
104
      to be transferred with µTransfer to a target model.
105   Critical batch size (Bcrit ): following Li et al. (2024), we define Bcrit as the corresponding pa-
106   rameter in Eq. 3.1, also describing the peak position of the bell-shaped curve (Fig. 1a), which was
107   shown by the authors to equal the Bcrit definition of McCandlish et al. (2018). To better clarify the
      nomenclature appearing in the literature, we provide an extended discussion in Appendix A.2.

108
      Sensitivity: as acknowledged by Wortsman et al. (2023), it is difficult to formalize this notion,
109   also in the absence of a theory to be verified. We therefore define it in the most minimal way,
110   namely as the variation of validation loss Lval (η) − Lval (η ∗ ) for a given learning rate variation from
111   its optimal value η/η ∗ . We refer to the corresponding loss vs. learning rate curve (both with and
112   without Lval (η ∗ ) normalization) as a loss profile.

      2.2   M ODEL CONFIGURATION AND DATASETS
115
116
      For all our experiments we use a default MPT model architecture (MosaicML, 2023) as implemented
117
      in the llm-foundry codebase (MosaicML, 2024), with all the models sharing the same training
118   configuration (Appendix A.3). We use the Decoupled AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter,
119   2019) with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, ϵ = 10−8 , weight decay λ = 0 and gradient clipping by the
120   L2 norm value of 1.
121
      µP is implemented according to Table 8 of Yang et al. (2022), so that when dmodel is set to the base
122
      model width dbase
                    model , it replicates Standard Parametrization (SP). That makes our observations for
123
      the base models also applicable to setups that use SP rather than µP. Model weightsqare initialized
124
125   from the normal distribution with the base model standard deviation σ base = 1/ dbase   model . The
126   models are scaled up/down only in width, with the head dimension dhead being always fixed and the
127   number of heads being scaled proportionally to the width scaling.
128   The models are trained with the causal language modeling task on the train split of the Colossal
129   Clean Crawled Corpus (C4) dataset (Raffel et al., 2020), tokenized with the GPT2 tokenizer (Rad-
130   ford et al., 2019) with a vocabulary size of 50257 and a context length of 1024 tokens. As a metric
131   to evaluate model performance, we report the loss on the C4 validation split as Lval .

      2.3   H YPERPARAMETER GRID
134
135
      To investigate the interplay of learning rate and batch size in the infinite data limit T → ∞, we
136   define a 5D grid spanned by the following axes: η, B, T , dmodel , dbase  model (see Appendix A.4 for
137   exact definition). Fundamentally, we are interested in measuring how the loss profile Lval (η) and its
138   optimum value η ∗ evolve in time T depending on the choice of batch size B. As this measurement
139   is moreover conditioned on the µP trajectory and a specific point therein, we firstly study this evo-
140   lution for a trajectory pinpointed by one specific base model with dbase
                                                                             model . We train a set of models
141   within the defined µP trajectory with different widths dmodel , ranging in size from 32M up to 354M
142   parameters, and measure for each of them the Lval (η) profile at specific points in time T , ranging
143   from 1B up to 275B tokens. Then, we repeat the same measurement for a new µP trajectory, pin-
144
      pointed by a different value of dbase
                                       model . This grid approach allows us to interpret results from multiple
      perspectives, as we detail in Sec. 3.

147   2.4   L EARNING RATE SCHEDULE SCALING
148
149   Since we study the training dynamics in the infinite data limit, it necessarily implies training models
150   across different data horizons. This raises the question of how one should adjust the learning rate
151   schedule in this limit. Motivated by recent work of Hu et al. (2024); Hägele et al. (2024), in all our
152   experiments we use a warmup-stable (WS) version of the warmup-stable-decay (WSD) schedule
153
      consisting of a warmup phase with a linear increase of learning rate from 0 to ηmax and a constant
      phase with learning rate fixed at ηmax , hereafter notated as η. Our version omits the decay phase
154
      to simplify experimentation as we observe that it does not affect the optimal η position (Appendix
155
      A.7). The warmup duration is fixed across all horizons and across all experiments at an absolute
156   value of Twarmup = 219 = 524288 tokens. Whenever batch size is varied, we adjust the number of
157   gradient steps in the warmup phase accordingly so that the total amount of tokens seen by the model
158   during warmup equals 219 . We also present additional experiments with different ways to scale the
159   learning rate warmup and an added decay phase in Appendix A.7, with results largely confirming
160   those of Hägele et al. (2024). The WS schedule allows us to reduce computational requirements by
161   approximately a factor of two: contrary to retraining for each of the data horizons in the T grid, we
      run indefinitely continued trainings and take evaluation snapshots on the way.

411   4   D ISCUSSION
412
413
      While originally, we were aiming to find a golden recipe for hyperparameter transfer in the infinite
414   data limit, we show that there is no simple and straight-forward answer. In Sec. 3.2, we show that
415   the model fit based on Eq. 3.1 describes the observed data points well. However, as illustrated in
416   Sec. 3.3, following the optimal learning rate trajectory in time is not sufficient to obtain optimal per-
417   formance. That leads us to believe that there exists a deeper underlying perspective on the problem,
418   as opposed to the one of simply tuning learning rate and batch size.
419
      Fundamentally, the field of model parametrization research has originated from and is further con-
420   verging towards preserving some notion of norm in some infinite (model width and/or depth) limit
421   (Everett et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Large et al., 2024). In fact, any parametrization itself is
422   simply a set of scaling rules to be applied to hyperparameters in order to preserve these norms (e.g.
423   of model weight matrices or weight updates). Expanding on this, one can argue that scaling rules
424   follow from the requirement of keeping some underlying quantity invariant within the infinite limit.
425   From this perspective, hyperparameter transfer is nothing but a consequence of such “conservation
426   laws”.
427   With this perspective in mind, we draw a parallel between infinite model and data limits, and specu-
428   late that a similar notion of “norm” should exist and should be aimed to be preserved in the infinite
429   data limit. In fact, there is already a good candidate for this, namely the noise scale (Eq. 5 and 9),
430   which intriguingly also induces scaling rules for hyperparameters (see Appendix A.2 for in-depth
431   discussion). However, the existing definition neither takes into account the adaptive nature of the
      optimizer, nor the scenario of jointly following the infinite data and model limits.

462   We hope that our experimental observations, similarly to the discovery of the η ∗ ∝ T scaling rule
463   for SGD, will make the first step towards the theoretical unification of infinite data and model size
464   limits via deriving such a joint scaling invariant. Still, our insights into optimal scaling rules for
465   learning rate and batch size might be valuable for practitioners who approach the problem of hyper-
466   parameter optimization in the infinite data and model size limit. We provide our recommendations
467   in Appendix A.1.
468   As future work, it would important to improve the resolution in data points with a finer grid of (η, B)
469   values. This is a necessary step to establish the generalization power of Eq. 3.1 and the power law
470   fits of its critical parameters as a function of time (Fig. 2), also across various data sets, model
471   architectures and modalities. Additionally, while we used µP as the main way to incorporate model
472   scaling due to its ability to transfer optimal learning rate across model sizes, recent work of Everett
473   et al. (2024) suggests that this is not the only way to do so. A similar study to ours, but for other
474
      model parametrizations, is an exciting direction of future research.
475
476   5   R ELATED W ORK
477
478   (η, B) scaling rules In efforts to accelerate model training, the η ∝ B rule for the SGD optimizer
479   was found necessary to avoid performance loss due to increased batch size (Goyal et al., 2018),
480   known as generalization gap (Keskar et al., 2017). Afterwards, additional usage of √momentum
481   (Smith et al., 2018) and model scaling (Park et al., 2019) was incorporated, and a η ∝ B rule for
482   Adam was observed (Hilton et al., 2022). From the theoretical side, experimentally observed rules
483   were verified with the framework of stochastic differential equations (SDEs) (Smith & Le, 2018;
484   Malladi et al., 2023), loss curvature analysis (Zhang et al., 2019; McCandlish et al., 2018; Li et al.,
485   2024) and random matrix theory (Granziol et al., 2021). While most of the studies were performed
      in the fixed epoch budget, Shallue et al. (2019) broadened the perspective to other target budget


                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      measures and studied the scope of the η ∝ B rule applicability across various datasets and model
487   architectures. Looking beyond fixed budgets, Smith & Le (2018) showed a linear relation between
488   the optimal batch size and the dataset size (for fixed η), and Smith et al. (2020) similarly presented
489   hints for a linear relation between the optimal learning rate and the dataset size (for fixed B), with
490   both works considering the SGD optimizer. In the modern LLM pretraining context, Hu et al. (2024);
491   DeepSeek-AI et al. (2024) approached this problem by deriving the joint (η, B) scaling laws.
492
      µP Originally developed within the Tensor Program series studying feature learning in the in-
493   finite width limit (Yang & Hu, 2022; Yang et al., 2022), µP has been gaining traction recently
494   within the LLM community. It has been extensively tested and applied experimentally (Lingle,
495   2024; Blake et al., 2024; Gunter et al., 2024; Dey et al., 2024), as well as theoretically, with Yang
496   et al. (2023); Bordelon et al. (2024) extending it to the infinite depth limit, and Yang et al. (2024);
497   Bernstein et al. (2023) revisiting it from the spectral normalization perspective. Recently, Everett
498   et al. (2024) showed that other model parametrizations also induce hyperparameter transfer if tak-
499   ing weight alignment into account. Furthermore, they revealed that µTransfer does not work in the
500   regime of Chinchilla-optimal scaling (Hoffmann et al., 2022). The most closely related work to
501
      ours, Shen et al. (2024) expanded on this observation and proposed a learning rate scheduler com-
      bining µP and experimentally measured (η, B) scaling rules  √ to allow for the hyperparameter transfer
502
503
      in the T → ∞ limit, however only limited to the η ∗ ∝ 1/ T scaling regime.
504   Sensitivity The topic of loss sensitivity to suboptimal hyperparameter choice is less thoroughly
505   studied, focusing exclusively on learning rate as the most affecting hyperparameter. Wortsman et al.
506   (2023) studied how various optimizer and model interventions, such as weight decay or µP usage,
507   influence the learning rate sensitivity with the model size scaling. Hägele et al. (2024) investigated
508   the impact of various learning rate schedule choices, such as length and functional form of the decay
509
      phase.
510
511   6   C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this work, we studied joint model and data scaling in the LLM context from the perspective of
514   optimal learning rate η and batch size B dynamics. We observed an intricate dependence of optimal
515   η scaling on B and its relation to the critical batch size Bcrit , as a function of the pretraining token
516   budget T . This dynamic is preserved during model scaling with µP, as well as the loss sensitivity to
517   the learning rate variation, highlighting the intriguing difference in how µP infinite width and time
518   limits evolve the critical batch size. Overall, we hope our observations pave the way towards deeper
519
      understanding of the optimal scaling in the unified infinite data and model size limit.

       A.1   H YPERPARAMETER OPTIMIZATION IN THE INFINITE DATA AND MODEL SIZE LIMIT
1046
1047   We believe our observations provide useful hints on how to scale learning rate and batch size jointly
1048   in the infinite data and model size limits. We take the general µTransfer approach of tuning hyper-
1049   parameters for a small proxy model and then transferring them either zero-shot or according to some
1050   scaling rules via extrapolation, across model sizes and data horizons.
1051
1052         1. If one can afford tuning a µP proxy model on the data horizon of the target model, then it
1053            is sufficient to simply perform a grid search over learning rate and batch size values to find
1054
                the best combination, following µTransfer (Yang et al., 2022). As we describe in Sec. 5,
                µTransfer has been established to successfully transfer hyperparameters to O(10B) model
1055
                sizes, albeit with potential limitations arising from very long range extrapolation in the
1056
                infinite width limit (Blake et al., 2024; Gunter et al., 2024).
1057
1058
             2. Otherwise, a proxy model has to be tuned on a shorter data horizon than the target one. In
                that case, we suggest running a 2D grid search across learning rate and batch size values
1059
                roughly around the optimal ones, where each training follows a WSD schedule (Sec. 2.4),
1060
                for as long as compute budget allows. We suggest both the warmup and decay of the
1061            schedule to be fixed to the one of the target model in absolute number of tokens, which in
1062            turn should be about 10–20% fraction of the target model horizon to be optimal (Kosson
1063            et al., 2024; Hägele et al., 2024). This is due to the observed drift of the learning rate
1064            optimum with the change of the number of steps (Appendix A.7). It is still not yet clear
1065            how scaling of warmup/decay length and Adam’s β1,2 parameters (which we keep constant
1066            in our experiments) can be incorporated into the total horizon scaling. We leave this as an
1067            interesting direction for future work.
1068         3. After the grid search, one should be able to obtain a plot similar to Fig. 1a and Fig. 3a.
1069            Provided long enough WSD horizon, a drift in time of the critical batch size region, asso-
1070            ciated to the peak of the fixed token budget curve in Fig. 1a, should be visible. Likewise,
1071            there should be a drift of the optimally tuned (i.e. assuming optimal learning rate is used)
1072            batch size in time as in Fig. 3a. Since we observe a strong correlation but still a mismatch
1073            between the optimally-tuned batch size and the critical batch size, we suggest the following
1074
