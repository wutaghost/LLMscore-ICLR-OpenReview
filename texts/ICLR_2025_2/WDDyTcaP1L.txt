009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Over-parameterized models are typically vulnerable to membership inference at-
012            tacks, which aim to determine whether a specific sample is included in the training
013            of a given model. Previous Weight regularizations (e.g., ℓ1 regularization) typi-
014            cally impose uniform penalties on all parameters, leading to a suboptimal tradeoff
015            between model utility and privacy. In this work, we first show that only a small
016            fraction of parameters substantially impact the privacy risk. In light of this, we
017            propose Privacy-aware Sparsity Tuning (PAST)—a simple fix to the ℓ1 Regular-
018
               ization—by employing adaptive penalties to different parameters. Our key idea
               behind PAST is to promote sparsity in parameters that significantly contribute to
019
               privacy leakage. In particular, we construct the adaptive weight for each parameter
020
               based on its privacy sensitivity, i.e., the gradient of the loss gap with respect to the
021            parameter. Using PAST, the network shrinks the loss gap between members and
022            non-members, leading to strong resistance to privacy attacks. Extensive experi-
023            ments demonstrate the superiority of PAST, achieving a state-of-the-art balance in
024            the privacy-utility trade-off.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Modern neural networks are trained in an over-parameterized regime where the parameters of the
030   model exceed the size of the training set (Zhang et al., 2021). While the huge amount of parameters
031   empowers the models to achieve impressive performance across various tasks, the strong capacity
032   also makes them particularly vulnerable to membership inference attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al.,
033   2017). In MIAs, attackers aim to detect if a sample is utilized in the training of a target model.
034
      Membership inference can cause security and privacy concerns in cases where the target model is
      trained on sensitive information, like health care (Paul et al., 2021), financial service (Mahalle et al.,
035
      2018), and DNA sequence analysis (Arshad et al., 2021). Therefore, it is of great importance to
036
      design robust training algorithms for over-parameterized models to defend against MIAs.
037
038   The main challenge of protecting against MIAs stems from the extensive number of model param-
039   eters, allowing to easily disclose the information of training data (Tan et al., 2022a). Therefore,
040
      previous works reduce the over-complexity of neural networks by weight regularization, like ℓ1 or
      ℓ2 regularization. These regularization techniques impose uniform penalties on all parameters with
041
      large values, reducing the overfitting to the training data. However, if not all parameters contribute
042
      equally to the risk of leaking sensitive information, the uniform penalties can lead to a suboptimal
043   tradeoff between model utility and privacy. The question is:
044
045                      Are all parameters equally important in terms of privacy risk?
046
047   In this work, we answer this question by an empirical analysis of parameter sensitivity in terms of
048   privacy risk. In particular, we take the loss gap between member and non-member examples as a
049   proxy for privacy risk and compute its gradient with respect to each model parameter. We find that
050
      only a small fraction of parameters substantially impact the privacy risk, whereas the majority have
      little effect. Thus, applying uniform penalties to all parameters is inefficient to defend against MIAs
051
      and may unnecessarily restrict the model’s capacity.
052
053   To address this issue, we propose Privacy-Aware Sparsity Tuning (PAST), a simple fix to ℓ1 reg-
      ularization that employs adaptive penalties to different parameters in a deep neural network. The

      key idea behind PAST is to promote sparsity in parameters that significantly contribute to privacy
055   leakage. In particular, we modulate the intensity of ℓ1 regularization for model parameters based on
056   their privacy sensitivity, i.e., the gradient of the loss gap with respect to the parameters. In effect,
057   our method not only stringently regularizes sensitive parameters, but also maintains the model utility
058   by sparing less sensitive parameters from excessive regularization. Trained with the proposed regu-
059   larization, the network shrinks the loss gap between members and non-members, leading to strong
060   resistance to privacy attacks.
061   To verify the effectiveness of our method, we conduct extensive evaluations on five datasets, in-
062   cluding Texas100 (Texas Department of State Health Services, 2006), Purchase100 (Kaggle, 2014),
063   CIFAR-10/100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) datasets. The
064   results demonstrate our methods can improve utility-privacy trade-offs across a variety of attacks
065   based on neural networks, metrics, and data augmentation. For example, our method significantly
066   diminishes the attack advantage of loss attack from 14.8% to 5.2% - a relative reduction of 64.9%
067   in privacy risk, whilst preserving the test accuracy.
068   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
069
070          • We empirically analyze the importance of model parameters in privacy risk. We show that
071            only a few parameters substantially impact the privacy risk, whereas the majority have little
072            effect. This suggests that the MIA defense can focus on a few important parameters.
073          • We introduce PAST – a simple and effective regularization method, which promotes spar-
074            sity in parameters that significantly contribute to privacy leakage. We show that PAST can
075            effectively improve the utility-privacy trade-offs across a variety of attacks.
076
             • We perform ablation studies that lead to an improved understanding of our method. In par-
077
               ticular, we contrast with alternative methods (e.g., ℓ1 or ℓ2 regularization) and demonstrate
078            the advantages of PAST. We hope that our insights inspire future research to further explore
079            weight regularization for MIA defense.

084   2.1   BACKGROUND
085
      Setup In this paper, we study the problem of membership inference attacks in K-class classifica-
086
      tion tasks. Let the feature space be X ⊂ Rd and the label space be Y = {1, . . . , K}. Let us denote
087
      by (x, y) ∈ (X ×Y) an example containing an instance x and a real-valued label y. Given a training
088   dataset S = {(xn , yn )}Ni=1 i.i.d. sampled from the data distribution P, our goal is to learn a model
089   hθ with trainable parameters θ ∈ Rp , that minimizes the following expected risk:
090
091                                      R(hθ ) = E(x,y)∼P [L(hθ (x), y)]                                    (1)
092
      where E(x,y)∼P denotes the expectation over the data distribution P and L is a conventional loss
093   function (such as cross-entropy loss) for classification. In modern deep learning, the neural network
094   hθ is typically over-parameterized, allowing to easily disclose the information of training data (Tan
095   et al., 2022a).
096
097   Membership Inference Attacks Given a data point (x, y) and a trained target model hS , attackers
098   aim to identify if (x, y) is one of the members in the training set S, which is called membership
099   inference attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al., 2017; Yeom et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2019). In MIAs,
100   it is generally assumed that attackers can query the model predictions hθ (x) for any instance x.
101   Here, we focus on standard black-box attacks (Irolla & Châtel, 2019), where attackers can access
102   the knowledge of model architecture and the data distribution P.
103   In the process of attack, the attacker has access to a query set Q = {(zi , mi )}Ji=1 , where zi denotes
104   the i-th data point (xi , yi ) and mi is the membership attribute of the given data point (xi , yi ) in the
105   training dataset S, i.e., mi = I[(xi , yi ) ∈ S]. In particular, the query set Q contains both member
106   (training) and non-member samples, drawn from the data distribution P. Then, the attacker A can
107   be formulated as a binary classifier, which predicts mi ∈ {0, 1} for a given example (xi , yi ) and a
      target model hθ : A(xi , yi ; hθ ) → {0, 1}.

127    Weight regularization The privacy risk of deep neural networks is often associated with their
128    over-parameterized nature. Intuitively, the huge amount of parameters enables the model to encap-
129    sulate extensive information of the training data, potentially leading to unintended privacy leakages.
130
       Previous work shows theoretically that increasing the number of model parameters renders them
       more vulnerable to membership inference attacks (Tan et al., 2022b). To address this issue, weight
131
       regularization is typically employed to alleviate the membership inference, such as ℓ1 and ℓ2 regu-
132
       larizations (Hoerl & Kennard, 1970; Tibshirani, 1996; Schmidt et al., 2007). Formally, the weight
133    regularization can be formalized as:
134
135                                     Rreg (hθ ) = E(x,y)∼P [L(hθ (x), y)] + λR(hθ )                                                                    (2)
136
137    where L(·) denotes the classification loss, λ is the hyperparameter that controls the importance
138    of the regularization term, and R(θ) is typically chosen to impose a penalty on the complexity
139
       of hθ . For example, ℓ1 and ℓ2 regularizations penalize the norm of model parameters as follows:
       R(hθ ) = ∥θ∥rr , where r denotes the order of the norm.
140
141    Previous work (Tan et al., 2022b) shows that one can reduce vulnerability to MIAs by reducing
142    the number of effective parameters, such as utilizing the sparsification effect of ℓ1 regularization.
143    However, this comes at the cost of inferior generalization performance (utility) due to the “double
144    descent” effect (Belkin et al., 2019; 2020; Dar et al., 2021), wherein generalization error decreases
145
       with increased overparameterization. This challenge stems from the uniform penalty applied to all
       parameters, ignoring their potentially varying importance in terms of privacy leakage.

148    3          M ETHOD : P RIVACY-AWARE S PARSITY T UNING
149
150    In this section, we start by analyzing the privacy sensitivity of model parameters and find that most
151    parameters contribute only marginally to the privacy risk. Subsequently, we design a weighted ℓ1
152    regularization that takes into account the privacy sensitivity of each parameter.

       3.1           M OTIVATION
155
156    In this part, we aim to figure out whether the model parameters are equally important in terms of
157    privacy risk. In particular, we perform standard training with ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) on CIFAR-
158    10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). We train the models using SGD with a momentum of 0.9, a weight
159    decay of 0.0005, and a batch size of 128. We set the initial learning rate to 0.01 and decrease it
160    using a cosine scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) throughout the training. In the analysis, we
161    construct the datasets of members Sm and nonmembers Sn by randomly sampling two subsets with
       10000 examples each from the training set and the test set, respectively.

172   Most parameters contribute only marginally to the privacy risk We measure the privacy
173   sensitivity of a parameter θi by the gradient of the loss gap with respect to the parameter:
174   ∇θi (G(Sm , Sn ; hθ )). Figure 1b illustrates the privacy sensitivity distribution of model parame-

179   against MIAs and may unnecessarily restrict the model’s capacity. Instead, the weight regularization
180
      can be more efficiently applied by focusing on the most sensitive parameters rather than the entire
      parameter set. We proceed by introducing our method, targeting this problem.

184

185   ber of effective parameters with weight regularization techniques. Moreover, we show that most
186   parameters contribute only marginally to the privacy risk, suggesting that the weight regularization
187   can be focused on the most sensitive parameters. Thus, our key idea is to promote sparsity specifi-
188   cally within the subset of parameters that significantly contribute to privacy leakage.

237   Implementation of tuning Standard ℓ1 regularization is usually employed from the beginning of
238   model training to alleviate the overfitting. This makes it challenging to achieve a good tradeoff be-
239   tween privacy and utility, as a strict regularization degrades the model’s capacity for generalization.
240   Instead, we propose to employ the regularization after the model convergence in the training with
241   the classification loss. In particular, we first train the model using the loss (Equation (3)) with λ = 0
242   until convergence. Then, we increase the value of λ to tune the model with the regularized loss. The
243
      tuning mechanism allows our method to be compatible with trained models, instead of requiring
      retraining from scratch.
244
245   By applying our method during tuning, we not only stringently regularize sensitive parameters
246   but also preserve model utility by sparing less sensitive parameters from excessive regulariza-
247   tion. Specifically, Figures 2a and 2b confirm the effectiveness of our method in mitigating over-
248   parameterization both intuitively and quantitatively: after PAST tuning, more parameters are con-
249
      centrated around 0 in the weight distribution, and the Gini index—a measure of sparsity—also sig-
      nificantly decreases. Figure 2c further illustrates the impact of our tuning on the loss gap, which
250
      sharply declines after the beginning of tuning at epoch 100, demonstrating the method’s ability to
251
      quickly reduce the loss gap and enhance resistance to privacy attacks.

      Hyperparameter Tuning In our approach to hyperparameter tuning, we align with the protocols
      established by previous work (Chen et al., 2022). In particular, we employ hyperparameter tuning
302
      focused on a single hyperparameter, α defined in Equation (4). Through a detailed grid search on
303
      a validation set, we adjust α to achieve an optimal balance. This process involves evaluating the
304   privacy-utility implications at various levels of α and then selecting the value that aligns with our
305   specific privacy/utility objectives, thereby enabling precise management of the model’s privacy and
306   utility. For the overall regularization strength λ in Equation (3), we fix it to different values based on
307   the dataset. Specifically, for CIFAR-10, the scale factor is 0.001; for CIFAR-100, it is 0.0001. For
308   other datasets, we set it as 1e-05.

310   Attack models In our study, we experiment with three classes of MIA: (1) Neural Network-based
311   Attack (NN) (Shokri et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2022), which leverages the full logits prediction as
312   input for attacking the neural network model. (2) Metric-based Attack, employing specific met-
313   ric computations followed by a comparison with a preset threshold to ascertain the data record’s
314   membership. The metrics we chose for our experiments include Correctness (Correct), Loss (Yeom
315   et al., 2018), Confidence, Entropy (Salem et al., 2019), and Modified-Entropy (M-entropy) (Song
316
      & Mittal, 2021). (3) Augmentation-based Attack (Choquette-Choo et al., 2021), utilizing prediction
      data derived through data augmentation techniques as inputs for a binary classifier model. In this
317
      category, we specifically implemented rotation and translation augmentations.

      5   R ELATED WORK
487
488
489   Overparameterization in generalization and privacy Overparameterization, where models have
490   significantly more parameters than training examples, has been shown to have a complex relation-
491   ship with generalization and privacy. While traditional theories suggest that overparameterization
492
      increases overfitting and generalization error, recent research reveals that it can sometimes reduce
      error under certain conditions, such as in high-dimensional ridgeless least squares problems (Belkin
493
      et al., 2020). This phenomenon, known as “double descent”, suggests that beyond a critical point,
494
      increasing model complexity may lead to better generalization (Belkin et al., 2019; Dar et al., 2021;
495   Hastie et al., 2022). However, from a privacy perspective, overparameterization has been empir-
496   ically proven to increase vulnerability to membership inference attacks (MIAs) (Leemann et al.,
497   2023; Dionysiou & Athanasopoulos, 2023). Large language models, in particular, are susceptible to
498   these attacks, with attackers able to extract sensitive training data (Carlini et al., 2021; Mireshghal-
499   lah et al., 2022). Theoretical evidence also indicates that there is a clear parameter-privacy trade-off,
500   where an increase in the number of parameters amplifies the privacy risks by enhancing model
501   memorization (Yeom et al., 2018; Tan et al., 2022b). Consequently, while overparameterization can
502   sometimes improve generalization, its impact on privacy remains a significant concern, especially
503
      in the context of MIAs.
504
505
      Overparameterization in MIA defenses To mitigate the privacy risks associated with overpa-
506
      rameterization, several defense mechanisms have been proposed. One effective approach is net-
507   work pruning, where unnecessary parameters are removed to reduce model complexity. Research
508   shows that pruning not only preserves utility but also significantly reduces the risk of privacy leak-
509   age, in scenarios including MIA (Huang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021) and Unlearning (Hooker
510   et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2024a). Additionally, techniques com-
511   bining pruning with federated unlearning have demonstrated effectiveness in protecting privacy by
512   selectively forgetting specific data during the training process (Wang et al., 2022). Regulariza-
513   tion methods, such as ℓ2 regularization (Kaya et al., 2020), sparsification (Bagmar et al., 2021)
514   and dropout (Galinkin, 2021), also play a critical role in defending against MIAs by discouraging
515
      the model from overfitting to training data. Interestingly, while overparameterization generally in-
      creases privacy risks, when paired with appropriate regularization, it can maintain both utility and
516
      privacy (Tan et al., 2023). Furthermore, studies indicate that initialization strategies and ensemble
517
      methods can further alleviate privacy risks on over-parameterized model (Rezaei et al., 2021; Ye
518   et al., 2024). These techniques illustrate that even in overparameterized models, privacy risks can
519   be mitigated through careful design, preserving the balance between utility and privacy.
520
521
522
523
      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we introduce Privacy-aware Sparsity Tuning (PAST), a novel approach to mitigating
526   membership inference attacks (MIAs) by adaptively regularizing model parameters based on the loss
527   gap between member and non-member data. By promoting sparsity in parameters with large privacy
528   sensitivity, the model shrinks the loss gap between members and non-members, leading to strong
529
      resistance to privacy attacks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PAST effectively balances pri-
      vacy and utility, providing state-of-the-art performance in the privacy-utility trade-off. This method
530
      is straightforward to implement with existing deep learning frameworks and requires minimal mod-
531
      ifications to the training scheme. We hope that our insights into Privacy-aware regularization inspire
532   further research to explore parameter regularization techniques for enhancing privacy in machine
533   learning models.
534
535
536   Limitations In this work, we focus on the popular black-box setting, where attackers can access
537   the model outputs. So, the effectiveness of our method in defending against other types of MIAs
538   (such as label-only attacks, white-box attacks) remains unexplored. Moreover, while our method
539   can improve the MIA defense with high predictive performance, our method cannot fully break the
      trade-off between utility and MIA defense, which might be a potential direction for future work.

960    F.1                            D OES PRIVACY MIGRATE AMONG PARAMETERS ?
961
962    We analyzed the changes in privacy sensitivity during the PAST training process and confirmed the
963    existence of privacy migration. Specifically, we plotted the distribution of privacy sensitivity (y-
964    axis) against parameter index (x-axis) at the 1st, 25th, and 50th epochs during training on the fully
965    connected (fc) layer of ResNet18 trained on CIFAR-10. The results, shown in Figure 12, confirm
966    that privacy migrates from heavily regularized parameters to others during PAST.
967    The migration of privacy sensitivity does not affect the effectiveness of our method. Our proposed
968    regularization focuses on parameters deemed ”important” in each epoch, and these parameters can
969    change dynamically. As shown in Figure 13a, we compare the privacy sensitivity distributions of
970    models trained with PAST versus standard training. The results indicate a significant reduction in
971    the overall privacy sensitivity (with the mean reduced from 0.0223 to 0.0088), demonstrating the
       robustness of our method in mitigating privacy risks.
