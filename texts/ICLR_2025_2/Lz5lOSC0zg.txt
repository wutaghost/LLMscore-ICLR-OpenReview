009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                Aligning large language models with human preferences improves interaction
012                quality and safety by ensuring outputs better reflect human values. A promising
013                strategy involves Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), starting
014                with collecting and ranking responses generated by a supervised fine-tuning model
015                to refine alignment. Current methods (DPO) focus on learning from pairwise
016                preference data, categorizing responses into preferred and less preferred pairs, and
017                optimizing by maximizing pairwise margins. Recent studies have uncovered a sub-
018
                   stantial discrepancy between the theoretical aspirations of preference learning and
                   its real-world results. Current preference alignment techniques underperform ex-
019
                   pectations, with ranking accuracies below 60% on standard datasets. This suggests
020
                   existing methods inadequately capture ideal preference relationships within se-
021                quences. To address this challenge, this paper introduces Direct Ranking Preference
022                Optimization (DRPO), a novel method that views human preference alignment
023                as a Learning-to-Rank (LTR) task. DRPO leverages NDCG, a widely used LTR
024                metric, to optimize the ranking of responses within lists based on preference data,
025                thereby enhancing ranking accuracies. Due to the nondifferentiability of NDCG,
026                we propose diffNDCG loss, a differentiable approximation facilitated by a sorting
027                network to simulate NDCG. Furthermore, to improve the quality of generated
028                response, we propose a novel margin-based Adaptive Rank Policy Score. Exten-
029
                   sive experiments have shown that DRPO outperforms existing baseline methods,
                   enhancing the quality of the generated responses. The code is publicly available 1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
      Large language models (LLMs), trained on extensive and diverse datasets, can be prompted to
035
      demonstrate impressive capabilities across a broad range of tasks (Huang et al., 2024; Chiang et al.,
036
      2023; OpenAI et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023). However, due to the varied nature of their training
037   data, these models sometimes produce content that may not align with human preferences, including
038   fabricated answers, offensive comments, or harmful responses (Bai et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). To
039   ensure the development of AI systems that are safe and controllable, this paper investigates learning
040   tasks for LLMs that guide them to generate responses in alignment with human preferences.
041
      Human preference alignment has become an active research area. Reinforcement Learning with
042
      Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) is the first proposed method in this area. However,
043   the optimization process of RLHF is complex, and its implementation introduces challenges due to
044   unstable and costly training. Recent studies (Hong et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024) have started
045   to adopt alternatives to RLHF. For example, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
046   2023) enables the extraction of the corresponding optimal policy in a closed form and derives a
047   pairwise logistic loss directly from pairwise preference data. DPO eliminates the need for explicit
048   reward modeling or reinforcement learning, thereby reducing the training costs associated with RLHF.
049   Although significant progress has been made in human preference alignment, most existing methods
050   primarily focus on pairwise human preferences, which involve evaluating human preferences by
051   comparing preferred and less-preferred responses. Nevertheless, human preferences are not solely
052   expressed as preferences and less preferences; they also manifest as ranking information, an aspect

      that has rarely been explored in previous research. In practice, ranking preference data is widely
055   utilized. For instance, in the Ultrafeedback and VLfeedback datasets (Li et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2023),
056   multiple responses are generated using a Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) model. These responses are
057   then evaluated and ranked by leveraging advanced AI technologies such as GPT-4 (OpenAI et al.,
058   2024). Moreover, as demonstrated by (Liu et al., 2024), presenting ranking preference data effectively
059   distribute the costs associated with processing the prompt (Liu et al., 2023a). Published In this work,
060   we approach human preference alignment as a listwise ranking problem, aiming to align LLMs by
061   utilizing ranking preference data. The straightforward solutions for tackling this issue are to extend
062   existing alignment methods from the pairwise scenarios to the ranking list scenarios. However, a
063
      major limitation is that they fail to utilize the relative strengths of the ranking preferences (Zhu et al.,
      2024). To address this, some studies leveraging ranking preference data have been proposed, proving
064
      their effectiveness for preference alignment (Yuan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Zhu
065
      et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2024). These methods predominantly achieve listwise preference alignment
066   through two main strategies: either by utilizing the Plackett-Luce preference model, or by employing
067   pairwise methods that incorporate listwise-aware weighting schemes (Liu et al., 2024).
068
069
      However, a mismatch persists between evaluation metrics and optimization objectives. LLMs’
      performance is typically assessed using win rates, which measure how often one model’s responses
070
      are preferred over another’s, yet current alignment methods do not directly optimize this criterion.
071
      This mismatch means that optimizing current loss functions (Liu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024) may
072   not necessarily lead to higher win rates or improved human preference satisfaction (Chen et al., 2024),
073   substantially hindering model preference alignment. In contrast, Pobrotyn & Bialobrzeski (2021);
074   Qin et al. (2010a) have demonstrated that direct optimization of evaluation metrics is highly effective
075   in traditional learning-to-rank tasks. Moreover, recent studies (Chen et al., 2024) have demonstrated
076   a correlation between win rates and ranking accuracy, while revealing that current alignment methods
077   achieve low ranking accuracy on standard preference datasets, but the relationship between win rates
078   and more comprehensive metrics like NDCG remains unexplored. Therefore, aligning LLMs with
079   human preferences by directly optimizing evaluation criteria and improving ranking accuracy remains
080
      a challenging task that has been scarcely explored to date.
081   To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel method: Direct Ranking Preference Optimization
082   (DRPO), which is a pioneering exploration to align LLMs with the ranking metric Normalized Dis-
083   counted Cumulative Gain (NDCG), a metric from Learning to Rank (LTR) that accurately quantifies
084   ranking accuracy and quality. Specifically, We introduce the Adaptive Rank Policy Score, a novel
085   ranking strategy that maximizes the absolute likelihood of preferred responses while dynamically ad-
086
      justing score margins between preferred and non-preferred responses based on their relative positions
      in the ranked list. Furthermore, we implement differentiable sorting networks to sort responses based
087
      on computed scores. This sorting method combines implementation simplicity and computational
088
      efficiency, while yielding doubly stochastic permutation matrices that preserve probability distribu-
089   tions and enable efficient differentiable optimization (Petersen et al., 2021). Additionally, due to the
090   nondifferentiability of NDCG, we leverage permutation matrices develop a differentiable version
091   of NDCG (i.e., diffNDCG) to serve as the loss function, simulating the NDCG metric. Optimizing
092   the diffNDCG loss enhances model performance by prioritizing top-ranked responses and imposing
093   stricter penalties for misplacing highly relevant items, without additional computational overhead
094   compared to existing methods. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
095   • A novel method Direct Ranking Preference Optimization (DRPO) is developed to explore human
096   preference alignment with ranking preference data. To our best knowledge, this paper is a pioneer
097   exploration to align LLMs with ranking human preference data.
098
      • A novel ranking score computation strategy, the Adaptive Rank Policy Score, has been introduced
099
      to replace the classical computation method used in RLHF (Rafailov et al., 2023). It maximizes
100
      preferred response likelihood while dynamically adjusting score margins based on relative positions.
101
102   • A novel differentiable NDCG (diffNDCG) metric has been developed to emulate the NDCG metric
103   used in LTR. By optimizing diffNDCG, we can prioritize responses at the top of the ranking list and
104
      impose stricter penalties for inaccurately placing a top-ranked response in a lower position.

185

186

188   absolute likelihood for preferred responses (Meng et al., 2024), leading to sub-optimal performance in
189   real-world generation tasks, where high absolute likelihoods are essential for producing high quality
190   outputs (Holtzman et al., 2018; Fan et al., 2018). To address this, we focus on the log-likelihood of
191   generated sequences and establish a length normalized basic scores function based on log-likelihood:

255   Odd-even Sorting Network. In this work, we adopt the odd-even sorting network (Batcher, 1968)
256   for response ranking due to its simplicity and ease of implementation. As depicted in Figure 2, an
257   ordered sequence of K elements can be achieved through a K-layer sorting network. Each layer of
258   the odd-even sorting network operates by comparing and swapping neighboring elements at either
259
      odd or even indices, thereby organizing them in a desired order. The process of odd-even sort is
      divided into alternating odd and even stages. During the odd stage, all pairs of elements at odd indices
260
      (i.e., elements at positions 1 and 2, 3 and 4, 5 and 6, etc.) are compared, and swapped if necessary
261
      according to the desired order. In the even stage, all pairs of elements at even indices (i.e., elements
262   at positions 2 and 3, 4 and 5, 6 and 7, etc.) are compared, and swapped according to given order.

      that it fails to distinguish error severity across ranking positions, incorrectly equating misplacements
310
      of top-ranked responses with lower-ranked items, despite higher-ranked responses typically being far

312   Gain (NDCG) (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002b), an effective LTR metric for measuring ranking quality.
313   NDCG assesses the significance of responses in conjunction with their ranking positions. It assigns
314   greater importance to responses at the top of the ranking compared to those positioned lower, and
315   imposes a more severe penalty for inaccurately placing a top-ranked response in a lower position.
316
      Furthermore, while NDCG was originally designed to reflect users’ tendency to focus on top-ranked
317
      results (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002a), this characteristic aligns with human preference rankings,
318   which prioritize more preferred responses over less preferred ones (Pool et al., 2016). This similarity
319   makes NDCG an effective proxy for evaluating and learning human preferences. Experiments in
320   Figure 4 demonstrate that has a stronger correlation with human preference win rates compared to the
321   optimizing targets of existing methods, highlighting its effectiveness. Additionally, NDCG prioritizes
322   top-ranked responses and penalizes their misplacement, and capturing graded importance between
323   responses without introducing any additional computation burden. Our time complexity analysis in
      Table 4.2 shows our method has comparable computational complexity to other methods.

331   yj . It also applies discount factors log2 (1 + q(yj )), where q(yj ) is the ranking position of yj . This
332   discounting mechanism assigns higher weights to elements at the top of the ranking. Consequently,
333   NDCG effectively accounts for the varying importance of responses at different ranking positions.
334   However, the computation method for NDCG involves a sorting process that renders the metric
335   non-differentiable with respect to the ranking position q(yj ).

      5     L IMITATIONS A ND C ONCLUSION
531
532
      Aligning LLMs with human preferences is crucial for enhancing interactions and the safety of LLMs.
533
      We propose a novel method DRPO, which treats human preference alignment as a listwise ranking
534   problem and aligns LLMs using ranking preference data. Specifically, we introduce an Adaptive
535   Rank Policy Score for ranking computation and develop a diffNDCG loss function based on the
536   NDCG metric. Our extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed
537   method, paving the way for future research. However despite using a large reward model as a proxy
538   for human evaluations, discrepancies from actual human judgments may impact model performance.
539   Generally, more sophisticated reward models provide more accurate evaluations. Future work could
      explore more sophisticated reward models to better approximate human preferences.

783   A    R ELATED W ORKS
784
785   Human Preference Alignment. Although LLMs have demonstrated impressive capabilities across a
786   broad range of tasks, they sometimes produce harmful and offensive content. This tendency may lead
787   to the AI system’s insecurity and loss of control. To address this issue, Reinforcement Learning with
788   Human Feedback (RLHF) was proposed by Bai et al. (2022); Christiano et al. (2017) to align LLMs
789
      with human preferences. This method involves first training a reward model using a preference model
      such as the Bradley-Terry model (David, 1963), and then fine-tuning LLMs with the trained reward
790
      model to maximize the given reward using reinforcement learning algorithms like PPO (Schulman
791
      et al., 2017). However, RLHF faces challenges such as instability and high computational cost, which
792   render the fine-tuning of LLMs using RLHF particularly challenging.
793
794   To alleviate the reliance on reinforcement learning, various methodologies have been proposed (Yuan
795
      et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024). For example, Direct Preference Optimization
      (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) enables the extraction of the optimal policy in a closed form and derives
796
      a pairwise logistic loss directly from pairwise preferences. DPO eliminates the need for explicit
797
      reward modeling or reinforcement learning, thereby reducing the training costs associated with RLHF.
798   Liu et al. (2023a) propose RSO, which utilizes the rejection sampling method to source preference
799   data from the estimated target optimal policy, leading to a more accurate estimation of the optimal
800   policy. They also propose a unified framework that enhances the loss functions used in both SLiC
801   (Zhao et al., 2023) and DPO from a preference modeling standpoint.
802
      However, most recent studies (Cheng et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023) only focus on
803
      pairwise preference cases, while only a few works focus on list preferences. RRHF, as proposed by
804   Yuan et al. (2023), employs a pairwise hinge loss to align LLMs with collected ranking preference
805   data. However, it treats each pair within the list as independent comparisons, rather than considering
806   the overall ranking preference structure. PRO (Song et al., 2024) learns from listwise preference
807   data using the list MLE loss, which based on the Plackett-Luce model (Plackett, 1975) rather than
808   Bradley-Terry model. LiPO (Liu et al., 2024) proposes the first list preference optimization framework
809   that conceptualizes human preference alignment as a learning-to-rank problem and unifies existing
      pairwise preference optimization methods into their framework. Additionally, they employed a


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      DCG-weighted pairwise logistic loss (lambda loss) (Wang et al., 2018) as an indirect mechanism to
811   optimize NDCG over ranked lists while aligning with human preferences. In contrast, Pobrotyn &
812   Bialobrzeski (2021) demonstrated that direct optimization of NDCG yields superior performance in
813   traditional learning-to-rank tasks.
814
      Building upon this insight, we implement differentiable sorting networks to obtain differentiable
815
      permutation matrices of responses. It enable us to develop a differentiable NDCG(diffNDCG),
816
      allowing direct optimization of NDCG for ranked lists to more effectively align LLMs with human
817   preferences.
818
819   Learning-to-Rank Task. Learning-to-Rank (LTR) task is a well-studied field with extensive liter-
820
      ature, primarily due to its practical applications in web search and recommendation systems (Liu
      et al., 2009; Dai et al., 2011; Macdonald et al., 2013; Cao et al., 2007; Yu et al., 2013; 2019; Ab-
821
      dollahpouri et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2024). Traditional research in Learning to Rank (LTR) has
822
      concentrated on developing robust ranking objectives, including pointwise, pairwise, and listwise
823   approaches. RankSVM (Cao et al., 2006) and RankNet (Burges et al., 2005) utilize pairwise hinge
824   loss and pairwise logistic loss, respectively, to optimize ranking performance. ListMLE and Softmax
825   losses are two representative listwise losses introduced by Cao et al. (2007). LambdaRank (Burges
826   et al., 2006) employs a pairwise logistic loss with lambda weights and achieves strong empirical
827   performance improvements compared to RankNet.
828
      Furthermore, numerous methods have been proposed to directly optimize the non-smooth NDCG
829   metric. For instance, SoftRank (Taylor et al., 2008) employs rank distributions to smooth NDCG.
830   ApproxNDCG (Qin et al., 2010a) uses a generalized sigmoid function to approximate the indicator
831   function for rank computation and the top-K selector for the top-K variant. PiRank (Swezey et al.,
832   2021) and NeuralNDCG (Pobrotyn & Bialobrzeski, 2021) approximate the non-continuous sorting
833   operator using NeuralSort (Grover et al., 2019) to smooth NDCG. Inspired by these studies, we utilize
834   differentiable sorting networks to smooth NDCG and introduce the diffNDCG loss function.
835
836   B    D ETAIL L IMITATIONS
837
838   In conducting our experiments, we adhere to benchmarks that are widely recognized and routinely
839   utilized in the literature on human preference alignment. It is important to note, however, that despite
840   our best efforts, there remain certain unavoidable limitations that merit consideration. In the process
841   of constructing a ranking preference dataset, we employ a reward model to serve as a proxy for human
842   evaluations. While the reward model we employ achieves a high level of accuracy, it is important to
843   acknowledge that there might still be subtle discrepancies when compared to human evaluations. In
844
      the future, we are also interested in exploring more high-quality reward models as proxies for human
      evaluations.

       H     D ETAILS OF A DAPTIVE R ANK P OLICY S CORES
1127
1128
       In our scoring design, we introduce a ranking position-dependent term γ(y) to regulate score
1129   differences during the differentiable sorting of responses. Specifically, in our score function (Eq. 5),
1130   the ranking-aware margin between positions i and j is computed as:
1131
                           γ(yi ) − γ(yj ) = τ · (q(yi ) − q(yj )) − β · (Vq(yi ) − Vq(yj ) )
1132
1133   This margin comprises two components: a static term determined by the relative ranking positions
       q(y), scaled by τ , and a dynamic term controlled by position-specific values Vq(y) , scaled by β.


                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       For the base margin, we design q(yi ) − q(yj ) based on two key principles: (1) higher-ranked elements
1135   (sorted by labeled scores) should be more preferred, and (2) adjacent elements should have smaller
1136   score differences. By incorporating the relative positions into the margin calculation, the resulting
1137   margin automatically adjusts according to the distance between ranking positions.
1138
       For the dynamic component, we employ Vq(y) to track score variations of responses at different
1139
       ranking positions. When Vq(y) increases for higher-ranked responses, the effective margin expands
1140
       beyond the base margin, enhancing the distinction between responses. Conversely, when score
1141   differences narrow, the dynamic component contracts to enable finer differentiation.
1142
1143   Furthermore, to prevent performance degradation of the ARPS scoring scheme caused by drastic
1144
       variations in Vq(y) across different instances, we clip the log πθ (y | x)/|y| during updates using a
       predefined threshold.
