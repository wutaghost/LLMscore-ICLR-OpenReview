011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Adversarial attacks present a significant threat to modern machine learning sys-
014            tems. Yet, existing detection methods often lack the ability to detect unseen at-
015            tacks or detect different attack types with a high level of accuracy. In this work, we
016            propose a statistical approach that establishes a detection baseline before a neu-
017            ral network’s deployment, enabling effective real-time adversarial detection. We
018            generate a metric of adversarial presence by comparing the behavior of a com-
019
               pressed/uncompressed neural network pair. Our method has been tested against
               state-of-the-art techniques, and it achieves near-perfect detection across a wide
020
               range of attack types. Moreover, it significantly reduces false positives, making it
021
               both reliable and practical for real-world applications.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Neural networks (NNs) are being used in numerous use cases across many disciplines. They have
028   almost become an integral part of our lives. One variation of them that we use very commonly is
029   the Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). They are widely used in image recognition, and they are
030   considerably reliable in this application, and they keep getting better virtually every day. A challenge
031   in using CNNs in sensitive applications is the existence of adversarial attacks (Goodfellow et al.,
032
      2015).
033   Adversarial attacks inject a small, ideally human-imperceptible perturbation in the input image be-
034   fore being fed into a CNN classifier. This perturbation usually takes the form of random noise and
035   is applied at a practically imperceptible level. Although this modification looks harmless or even
036   completely invisible to the human eye, it wreaks havoc inside the workings of the CNN classifier.
037   It ultimately pushes the detection to an invalid class. This can lead to many unseemly outcomes,
038
      ranging from loss of accuracy to failure of safety-critical systems.
039   Several methods exist to detect and suppress adversarial attacks. However, these methods suffer
040   from inherent flaws, such as the requirement of apriori knowledge of the attack type, the high number
041   of false positives, low overall accuracy, and scaling issues with different network architectures.
042   In this paper, we present a simple attack-agnostic detection method that does not require prior knowl-
043   edge of attack models. It requires a simple training process before the deployment to generate a set
044   of class identities and, during runtime, uses those identities to match every incoming sample.
045
      Compression suppresses adversarial noise to an extent, as shown in Aydemir et al. (2018) among
046
      others. While this effect is less than ideal for reliably suppressing all adversarial perturbations, we
047
      can observe a difference when we take the same input and run it through a pair of slightly different
048   networks. The pair of networks will be almost identical, except that to the second network, we
049   feed a compressed version of the image, and the network itself is pre-trained on compressed images
050   after regular training. We leverage a secondary denoising network that operates on compressed
051   images and check how far the matching of the distributions skew between the two networks. We
052   propose a metric that can be calculated at runtime for each sample that measures this disparity and a
053   threshold T that can be empirically determined pre-deployment. We use the threshold on the metric
      to determine the presence of adversarial perturbations and filter out the adversarial samples.

      The metric calculation uses the feature maps generated in both networks’ last feature layer (the layer
055   before the winner-takes-all/softmax layer) and represents how much they disagree. This disagree-
056   ment is more pronounced in adversarial images, thus allowing the discrimination between them and
057   clean images. Our method consistently gives accurate detections for every adversarial attack tested,
058   while existing work performs well in some attacks and poorly in others.
059
      We developed this method without considering any of the attack models and their behavior since
060
      compressive networks suppress almost any adversarial signal presence. This led us to believe this
061   method should perform well on any attack universally. We claim that our method is an attack-
062   agnostic adversarial attack detector.

087   2    R ELATED W ORK
088
089   We use several adversarial attack models to verify our theory of attack agnostic detection. They are
090   contained within the Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART) by Nicolae et al. (2018) Python pack-
091   age. The attack models that are used here are the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow
092   et al., 2015), the Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2017) approach, the Square At-
093
      tack (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) method, the DeepFool (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2015) method
      and the Carlini-Wagner(CW) (Carlini & Wagner, 2016) attack. These methods use an approximately
094
      similar CNN model (black box attack) or the exact CNN model used in detection (white box attack)
095
      to generate an attack. The exact way they create the attack varies by the attack method. Still, they
096   usually use gradient-based methods, where the adversary calculates the gradient of the model’s loss
097   function with respect to the input and adjusts the input accordingly to maximize the loss, as opposed
098   to minimizing the loss when the goal is to predict the image content accurately. A noise vector is
099   calculated using these gradients that skew the prediction in a way that increases the chance of mis-
100   classification. This noise vector is then added to the image, making it fool the detector. The idea of
101   our work is to identify images that have been tampered with with such a malicious noise vector.
102   Current methods of detecting the presence of adversarial attacks include attack agnostic methods
103   such as the Least Significant Component Feature (LCSF) (Cheng et al., 2022) method, the Energy
104   Distance/Maximum mean discrepancy (Saha et al., 2019) method, the Mahalanobis distance-based
105   classifier (Lee et al., 2018) method. Also, there exist attack specific methods such as the Feature
106   Squeezing (Xu et al., 2018) method, using Latent Neighborhood Graphs (Abusnaina et al., 2021),
107   using Influence Functions/Nearest Neighbors (Cohen et al., 2020), using Bayesian Neural Networks
      (Deng et al., 2021), by random input responses (Huang et al., 2019) and by Random Subspace


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Analysis (Drenkow et al., 2021). These methods have their shortcomings, such as poor performance
109   in some attack models, requiring extra training data, high false positive rates, and limited flexibility.
110
111   One major part of our method is the secondary denoising network. In theory, this can be accom-
112
      plished in numerous methods, but we have chosen compressive sensing using JPEG2000 compres-
      sion, as demonstrated by Aydemir et al. (2018). JPEG compression is typically used to reduce the
113
      file size of images by reducing unnecessary and imperceptible information contained in an image.
114
      However, this provides a benefit when attempting to suppress adversarial attacks since the JPEG
115   compression algorithm treats the adversarial noise signals as imperceptible information and disre-
116   gards them, restoring the original image to an extent. The downside of this in practice is that the
117   accuracy improvement is not perfect and, in most cases, only restores about 20-30% of the accuracy.
118   So, this alone is not a comprehensive defense strategy against adversarial attacks. However, since
119   we know that a compressed network treats adversarial noise differently, we can take advantage of
120   that to implement a more sophisticated detection method.
121   In order to properly build class identities and match new samples, we need a proper comparison
122   system. This is accomplished using the method proposed by Pentsos & Tragoudas (2023). The
123   idea is to partition the pre-provided train-test sets and use the feature vectors of those partitions to
124   build an identity. Then, the new samples are augmented using benign noise vectors to form a rich
125   representation of the sample, which is compared against the pre-built class identities. We use this
126   underlying concept to check how the new samples match our known class baselines.

135   on both networks and extract a measure of how much the two networks disagree on the image. If
136   they disagree beyond a certain threshold, the sample is marked adversarial. This effect is evident in

      5   C ONCLUSION
332
      To conclude this work, we have presented a method that can detect adversarial attacks without
333
      the requirement for attack-specific training and maintain a high detection accuracy across multiple
334
      attack models without compromising the false positive rate. The results show that our method is
335   reliable across multiple datasets and attack models, maintaining almost perfect accuracy. These
336   characteristics make our method ideal for sensitive applications needing reliable defense against
337   potential adversarial threats.
