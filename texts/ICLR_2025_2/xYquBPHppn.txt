009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Controlled graph generation is the process of generating graphs that satisfy specific
012            topological properties (or attributes). Fine-grained control over graph properties
013            allows for customizing generated graphs to precise specifications, which is essential
014            for understanding and modeling complex networks. Existing approaches can only
015            satisfy a few topological properties such as number of nodes or edges in output
016            graphs. This paper introduces CG RAPH G EN, a novel conditional variational
017            autoencoder that, unlike existing approaches, uses graph adjacency matrix during
018            training, along with the desired graph properties, for improved decoder tuning and
019            precise graph generation, while relying only on attributes during inference. In
020
               addition, CG RAPH G EN implements an effective scheduling technique to integrate
               representations from both adjacency matrix and attribute distributions for precise
021
               control. Experiments on five real-world datasets show the efficacy of CG RAPH G EN
022
               compared to baselines, which we attribute to its use of adjacency matrix during
023            training and effective integration of representations, which aligns graphs and their
024            attributes in the latent space effectively and results in better control.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph generation is the process of generating graphs that mimic real world structures, e.g. following
031   a power-law distribution in terms of node degree (Erdös & Rényi, 1959; Barabási & Albert, 1999;
032   You et al., 2018). Controlled graph generation is the process of generating graphs that satisfy
033
      specific topological attributes (Zahirnia et al., 2024; Martinkus et al., 2022), with applications in drug
      discovery (e.g. generating new molecules that satisfy certain chemical properties) (Jin et al., 2018;
034
      Shi et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2021; Popova et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
035
      2021; Zang & Wang, 2020; De Cao & Kipf, 2018), synthetic material design (Wang et al., 2022;
036   Sanchez-Lengeling & Aspuru-Guzik, 2018), simulating social network and social interactions (Pitas,
037   2016; Zhou et al., 2020; Zeno et al., 2021), program graph generation from source codes (Allamanis
038   et al., 2018), and completing knowledge graphs (Melnyk et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Cao et al.,
039   2023).
040
      Despite significant progress in graph generation, existing works often lack fine-grained control over
041
      structural attributes. Most approaches focus on a limited set of graph attributes as controls (Zahirnia
042   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Martinkus et al., 2022). In particular, Zahirnia et al. (2024) proposed
043   a variational autoencoder that learns a latent adjacency matrix from statistics like number of edges,
044   triangles, random walks and k-hop neighbors. Chen et al. (2023) proposed a discrete diffusion model
045   by explicitly focusing on node degrees to control graph generation. Similarly, Madeira et al. (2024)
046   built on discrete diffusion techniques to incorporate specific graph properties such as planarity or
047   acyclicity to generate graphs. Finally, Martinkus et al. (2022) proposed a model based on generative
048   adversarial networks to control graph generation by focusing on eigenvalues and eigenvectors as
049   more abstract topological properties.
050   In this paper, we propose Controlled Graph Generation (CG RAPH G EN), a novel end-to-end
051   conditional variational autoencoder for generating graphs that satisfy fine-grained topological
052   attributes. CG RAPH G EN implements an effective scheduling technique that integrates representations
053   from both adjacency matrix and attribute distributions to enable more fine-grained and precise control
      for graph generation.

054
      The closet approach to ours is GenStat (Zahirnia et al., 2024), which is a standard autoencoder model
055   for controlled graph generation. It encodes given graph attributes to produce a latent adjacency
056   matrix, which is then used by a decoder to produce the attributes. CG RAPH G EN differs from GenStat
057   from several aspects: it uses both graph attributes and the adjacency matrix of graphs during training
058   for improved decoder tuning, while relying only on attributes during inference; it introduces a
059   novel scheduling technique to integrate latent representations from adjacency matrix and attribute
060   distributions for effective training; and, unlike previous approaches, it can handle any number of fine-
061   grained control attributes without any modification, which provides flexibility in graph generation.
062   The contributions of the paper are:
063
064              • CG RAPH G EN, a novel conditional variational autoencoder to generate graphs conditioned
065                on topological attributes. It uses both graph adjacency matrix and attribute vectors during
066                training for precise graph generation, while relying only on attributes during inference.
067              • M IXTURE -S CHEDULER, a novel scheduling technique to effectively integrate representa-
068                tions from adjacency matrix and attribute distributions for effective fine-grained topological
069                control in generation.
070
071   We evaluate CG RAPH G EN across multiple datasets and our results show its efficacy across the datasets
072
      compared to baselines. We find that mixing attribute representations from prior distribution and
      adjacency matrix representations from posterior distributions helps align graphs and their attributes
073
      in the latent space effectively and results in better control. In addition, slower rates of including
074
      information from the prior helps more accurate graph generation. Lastly, we find that increasing the
075   number of control attributes helps the model with more precise graph generation. 1

078   2       C ONTROLLED G RAPH G ENERATION
079
080   Problem Definition Given a graph attribute vector c, which represents fine-grained information
081
      about the topology of a target graph G (see examples of these attributes in §2.1), we aim to generate
082
      a graph Ĝ such that its structure satisfies the desired attributes in c.
083
084
      Solution Overview During training, CG RAPH G EN uses the adjacency matrix A of the target graph
      G= (V, E) and its corresponding attribute vector c to learn controlled graph generation, i.e. joint
085
      distributions of training graphs and their attributes. At the inference time, however, CG RAPH G EN
086
      only uses control attribute vector to generate graphs that satisfy the given attribute vectors. As
087   Figure 1 shows, CG RAPH G EN encodes the structural representation ZG from adjacency matrix A to
088   parameterize the posterior distribution qϕ , and the attribute representation Zc from the attribute vector
089   c to build prior distribution pθ . CG RAPH G EN samples from distributions qϕ and pθ to combines ZG
090   and Zc using M IXTURE -S CHEDULER to obtain the latent representation Z, which balances structural
091   and attribute information. The M IXTURE -S CHEDULER aims to bring posterior qϕ and pθ closer to
092   each other, as they represent graphs with the same topological structure. The decoder then learns
093   the likelihood distribution pψ from Z to generate a graph Ĝ that satisfies the attribute vector c. At
094   inference time, CG RAPH G EN relies only on the prior distribution pθ and likelihood pψ to condition
095   graph generation based on attribute vectors.

      = 0), (ii) when the model gradually combine pθ and qϕ as training progresses (β(t) → γ), and (iii)
408
      when the model combines both pθ and qϕ with constant influence factor β(t) = γ. As shown in
409   Figure 4(b), combining representations from both distributions pθ and qϕ helps to generate better
410   graphs compared to using only representations from qϕ . In addition, gradual increase in influence
411   factor β(t) → γ performs better compared to keeping it constant β(t). We conclude relying only on
412   graph representation from qϕ without considering attribute representation from pθ results in higher
413   MAD error and lower performance.
414
415   Q2: How does the rate of inclusion affect model’s performance? To answer the second question,
416   we analyze different rates of inclusion. As Figure 4(c) shows, a slow inclusion rate (α = 0.1) often
417   helps model in learning better representations compared faster inclusion rates, e.g. (α=10). This
418   result suggests that initially focusing on the qϕ and gradually incorporating the pθ yields better latent
419
      representations.
420
421
      Q3: How much of the prior should be included? To understand the effect of combining attributes
      representation from pθ with graph representations from qϕ , we vary the influence of prior distribution
422
      using different values of maximum possible inclusion, γ. We set γ from [0,1] with step size of
423
      0.1. When γ=0, there is no influence from pθ , and when γ = 1, no information from posterior qϕ is
424   considered. Any values in between combines information from pθ and qϕ distributions. Figure 5
425   shows that smaller values of γ result in lower error, which indicates that a limited inclusion of the pθ
426   helps generate better graphs by controlling the contributions of both distributions.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
461
462
      We presented CG RAPH G EN, a novel approach to controlled graph generation that generates graphs
463
      satisfying fine-grained topological attributes. CG RAPH G EN introduces a novel scheduling technique,
464   M IXTURE -S CHEDULER, which effectively combines attribute representations with adjacency matrix
465   representations to learn accurate latent representations for graph generation during training. It enables
466   precise control over generated graphs, even without explicitly specifying some of the basic graph
467   properties such as the number of nodes and edges. Our experiments demonstrate that CG RAPH G EN
468   produces graphs with lower error by integrating structural information gradually and leveraging
469   multiple control attributes. In future, we aim to extend CG RAPH G EN to handle dynamic or temporal
470   graphs, where the graph structure evolves over time for applications in social network analysis, traffic
471   prediction, and temporal knowledge graphs.
