009                                                  Abstract
010
011              The visual projector, which bridges the vision and language modalities and facili-
012              tates cross-modal alignment, serves as a crucial component in Multimodal Large
013              Language Models (MLLMs). However, measuring the effectiveness of projectors
014              in vision-language alignment remains under-explored, with current evaluations
015              relying primarily on the performance of MLLMs on downstream tasks. Motivated
016              by this gap, this study conducts an in-depth examination of the projector module by
017              analyzing the vision-language semantic flow within MLLMs. Our findings reveal
018
                 that compressive projectors (e.g., QFormer) reduce the number of visual tokens by
                 abstracting visual patches into a limited set of semantic concepts, such as objects or
019
                 attributes, leading to a deficiency we term “double abstraction” in MLLMs. This
020
                 phenomenon involves i) an initial visual semantic abstraction by the projector in
021              the vision modality, which refers to pre-defined query tokens, and ii) a secondary
022              extraction by the LLM in the language modality based on text instructions. The
023              double abstraction is inefficient during training and leads to cumulative deficiencies
024              in visual semantics. To address this issue, we propose the key insight of “Decouple
025              Token Compression from Semantic Abstraction (DeCo)”, where projectors com-
026              press visual tokens at the patch level non-semantically, while allowing the LLM to
027              fully manage semantic understanding and abstraction. Consequently, we employ
028              a simple compressor, i.e., 2D Adaptive Pooling, to downsample visual patches in
029
                 a parameter-free manner. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that 2D Adaptive
                 Pooling outperforms traditional compressive projectors in both performance and
030
                 efficiency, achieving gains of 0.9%, 7.1%, and 2.9% across the MLLM Benchmarks,
031
                 Visual Localization, and Open-ended VQA tasks, respectively, while utilizing fewer
032              trainable parameters and achieving faster convergence. Furthermore, it preserves
033              vision spatial locality and exhibits robustness across various MLLM configurations,
034              including different vision backbones, image resolutions, and LLMs.

      1     Introduction
038
039
      Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (OpenAI, 2023; Gemini Team, 2023; Reka, 2024)
040
      endow Large Language Models (LLMs) with vision perception capability, which have shown their
041
      versatility and expertise in diverse vision-language tasks (Kafle et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2016; Singh
042   et al., 2019; Bigham et al., 2010; Li et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023; 2022; Chen et al., 2023b). For
043   MLLMs, learning good vision-language alignment is at the core of their intelligence (Li et al., 2023d;
044   Zhu et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023b; 2024). To achieve cross-modal alignment, recent studies utilize
045   an intermediate module, i.e., the projector (Liu et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; Madureira, 2021;
046   Dai et al., 2023), to map representations of image patches (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) into the LLM
047   embedding space as visual tokens.
048   Widely used projectors can be roughly summarized into two branches: non-compressive and
049   compressive. The non-compressed projector (Liu et al., 2023b) directly uses linear layers that translate
050   the visual token dimension to the LLM’s while keeping the visual token number unchanged. Despite
051   its simplicity and effectiveness, the linear projector struggles with high training resources and costs
052   due to the length of the visual token sequence. The sequence would be long in two common scenarios:
053   (i) the length increases quadratically with the input resolution (Li et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023c);
      (ii) the length increases linearly with the image number for handling video frames (Ren et al., 2023c;

      We start by tracing the language-to-vision semantic flow using a novel R-GAE explainability tool.
      Specifically, we decouple the overall Text-to-Patch semantic relevance to Text-to-Query and Query-

089

090   (Text). Meanwhile, the Query-to-Patch relevance interprets the visual patterns learned from original

093
      Based on the R-GAE analysis, we derive two important findings: Firstly, the query tokens compress
      the number of visual tokens by abstracting semantic-level visual concepts, leading to visual semantics
094
      deficiency such as loss of fine-grained attributes and spatial locality. As Figure 1 illustrates, different
095
      query tokens are activated with varied visual concepts such as objects, attributes or backgrounds
096   from the original images. For the top image with zebras in the grassland, query tokens attend to
097   visual patterns such as three zebras, their body parts, surface textures, and distant backgrounds
098   respectively. However, the fixed number of queries can only express limited visual semantics.
099   Specifically, different query tokens show repetitive patterns across images (highlighted by color
100   frames in Figure 1). Moreover, they tend to lose fine-grained visual attributes (e.g., “purple and
101   red” in Figure 3). Furthermore, the vanilla QFormer has been demonstrated to lose visual spatial
102   locality (Cha et al., 2024) during semantic abstraction.
103   Secondly, the LLM acts as an excellent visual-semantic abstractor directly from patch features. As
104   Figure 3 first row shows, utilizing a non-compressive linear projector allows the LLM to perceive
105   patch-level visual representations and attend to accurate vision regions without prior semantic
106   deficiency. Consequently, the QFormer-based MLLM system redundantly extracts visual semantics
107   twice—once by the QFormer and again by the LLM—a phenomenon we refer to as Redundant Double
      Abstraction. This double abstraction introduces two major drawbacks: (i) increased training resource,

126   To overcome the double abstraction problem, we propose to Decouple token number Compression
127   (DeCo) from vision semantic abstraction. The core of DeCo is using a simpler projector, which
128
      operates and outcomes visual tokens directly at the patch level non-semantically to reduce the visual

129
      and textual semantics. To quantitatively validate the DeCo insight, we adopt the naive Adaptive
130
      Average Pooling as a natural down-sampler at the patch level and then use the linear layers to map
131   the reduced visual tokens. Under fair experimental settings, quantitative results demonstrate that a
132   simple Adaptive Pooling design consistently outperforms semantic-level compressed projectors in
133   both effectiveness and efficiency. Additionally, experiments across various MLLM configurations,
134   including different vision backbones, image resolutions, and LLMs, further highlight the robustness
135   of Adaptive Pooling. Through both qualitative and quantitative analysis, our DeCo insight aims to
136   illuminate ways to improve the efficiency of the projector module in current MLLM systems. We
137   also hope that it will serve as a valuable reference for future architectural improvements in projector
138   design.

      2     Visual Projector Analysis
142
143   In this section, we analyze the impact of projector modules in Multimodal Large Language Models
144
      (MLLMs) from a semantic flow perspective using a novel R-GAE explainability tool. During image-
      to-text generation, visual context plays an indispensable role in the perception of Large Language
145
      Models (LLMs). The related relevance maps between image and text, such as attention maps (Vaswani
146
      et al., 2017), can serve as an interpretation of the vision-language semantic alignment (Chefer et al.,
147   2021b; Xu et al., 2015; Carion et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2021). As Figure 2 shows, given an oracle
148   description in the MLLM architecture, the backtracking relevance map from text words to visual
149   patches (referred to as Text-to-Patch) exhibits the visual semantics aligned with the LLM and further
150   indicates the effective visual context leveraged by the LLM. To examine the impact of projectors
151   as the intermediate module, we dissect the Text-to-Patch relevance map into Text-to-Query and
152   Query-to-Patch sub-maps. The Query-to-Patch map can explain the visual patterns learned by the
153   query (or compressed) tokens, while the difference between Text-to-Patch and Text-to-Query, exerted
154   by the projector, reveals its impact on the vision-language semantic alignment.
155

161   states T ∈ RL×dT and generate responses Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yL }. We summarize widely adopted
      projectors into two branches:

      the extraction of vision semantic concepts (e.g., objects and attributes, etc.).

201   2.2   R-GAE: Relevance Maps in MLLMs Derived from GAE
202
      We aim to employ the dissected Text-to-Query and Query-to-Patch relevance maps to examine the
203
      projector module. A straightforward attempt is utilizing the raw attention maps in MLLM layers as
204   the relevance map (Ren et al., 2021). However, the attention map exhibits the interaction between
205   tokens in a single layer (Chefer et al., 2021b). Instead, we require a relevance map that traces
206   back inter-token alignment in arbitrary two layers in the MLLM, for instance, the alignment from
207   intermediate-layer query tokens to initial-layer input patch tokens. To achieve this goal, we propose a
208   novel R-GAE relevance map derived from the Generic Attention Explainability (GAE) (Chefer et al.,
209   2021a). R-GAE extends the GAE method originally designed for classification tasks, to generative
210   MLLMs, and adapts it to the typical MLLM architecture consisting of a ViT, a projector, and an LLM.
211   The R-GAE can acquire relevance maps from any two arbitrary layers within the MLLM through
212
      propagation.
213   We initialize three R-GAE relevance maps including a Text-to-Patch map as RT →I , a Text-to-Query
214   map as RT →Q , and a Query-to-Patch map as RQ→I . Each map is an identity matrix based on the
215   intuition that each input token’s relevance score is equal in the beginning. Given an image and an
      instruction (e.g., “Please describe the image with a concise sentence”), an MLLM will generate a


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      textual description Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yL } referring to the visual information. During the generation
217   step t, we can cache the attention maps across the ViT, the projector and the LLM during a forward
218   pass. Then, specifying a word class ybt as the target prediction, we can obtain the related gradients
219   through a backward pass. For each layer, a single R-GAE relevance map is obtained by utilizing
220   gradients to average across the attention heads. For step t, we can propagate the Text-to-Query
221   map RtT →Q ∈ R1×M from the LLM’s first layer to its last layer to get the final map. Similarly,
222   the Query-to-Patch map RtQ→I ∈ RM ×N can be propagated from the first layer to the last layer
223   of the projector. Subsequently, the overall Text-to-Patch relevance map can be obtained by matrix
224   multiplication of Text-to-Query and Query-to-Patch maps:
225
                                           RtT →I = RtT →Q × RtQ→I                                          (1)

      For a complete sentence Y , we integrate the R-GAE relevance maps from each time step t by
228
      averaging to obtain the overall visual relevance related to a factual sentence. We set the ground-truth
229
      description from an image-text pair as the target response to perform the backward process. This
230   limits MLLMs with different projectors having the same Oracle Text-to-Patch visualization. We
231   provide the background of GAE and the specific propagation formula of R-GAE in Appendix A.
232   Moreover, we compare the visualization between R-GAE and original attention maps in Appendix B.

234   2.3   A Redundant Double-Abstraction Phenomenon
235
236   Based on the R-GAE maps, we analyze the different types of projectors and investigate how they
237   affect the vision-to-language semantic alignment. For fair comparison and analysis, we train MLLMs
238   under the same architecture, except for the projector module, and keep all other variables the same
239   (experimental details are provided in § 3.1). We visualize the R-GAE maps of a non-compressive
240   projector (i.e., linear layers) and a compressive projector (i.e., QFormer) in Figure 3 and draw the
241   following findings.
242   Observation 1. LLMs are good visual semantic abstractors directly from patch representation.
243
      The non-compressive projector directly inputs the patch representation to the LLM. As shown in
244
      the first row of Figure 3, given a description containing visual objects (i.e., the remote and buttons)
245
      and attributes (i.e., purple and red), the LLM can highlight the most relevant visual regions in a
246   fine-grained manner, as it discriminates the accurate remote with purple and red buttons among other
247   similar remotes. This indicates that the LLM has built a strong alignment between textual and visual
248   semantics based on the patch representation. The recent success of MLLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Li
249   et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023a) with non-compressive projection further demonstrates that the LLM
250   itself is an efficient visual semantic abstractor. For instance, LLaVA-Next (Liu et al., 2024b), which
251   employs a simple Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP), achieves state-of-the-art performance across diverse
252   multimodal benchmarks.
253   Observation 2. Compressive projectors extract limited visual semantic concepts from patches.
254   Compressive projectors like QFormer pre-extract visual semantic concepts from patches and provide
255   reduced visual tokens at the semantic level to the LLM. As the Query-to-Patch map in Figure 3
256   shows, the compressed 8x8 query tokens are activated with visual semantic patterns such as different
257   remotes, buttons, control panels, and the black background board. However, the fixed number of
258   query tokens can only cover limited visual semantic concepts from the image. Comparing the visual
259   patterns among 64 tokens, we find that they are visually repetitive and semantically sparse. For
260
      instance, query tokens indexing (0, 1) and (2, 0) are nearly identical and all attend to the bottom-right
      panel of the right remote. These sparse query tokens lead to a deficiency in visual semantics, losing
261
      the fine-grained attribute of “purple and red buttons”. Consequently, the LLM suffers from this
262
      irreversible visual semantic deficiency when re-extracting visual context in the query semantic space.
263   As the Text-to-Query map shows, the LLM primarily attends to the query tokens indexing (0, 2),
264   (0, 4), and (4, 5) (framed in red), resulting in a misalignment of text words and patches verified in the
265   Text-to-Patch map. More visualization cases are presented in Appendix D.
266
      Insight. An inefficient MLLM system due to the double abstraction of visual semantics.
267
268   Based on these observations, we conclude that existing compressive projectors, which learn a fixed
269   number of query tokens, are inefficient compressors for reducing the number of vision tokens.
      They result in a “Double Abstraction” MLLM system, where visual semantics are first abstracted

      by projectors and then re-extracted by the LLM. This dual-abstraction procedure has two main
271   shortcomings: (i) Accumulative visual semantics loss. The projector serves as an intermediate module
272   bridging the ViT and LLM, therefore, the visual semantics lost during the initial abstraction by the
273   projector become a bottleneck for the MLLM system. (ii) Increased training complexity. Optimizing
274   a projector to be an effective semantic abstractor is essential for alleviating semantic loss; however,
275   this increases the training cost and complexity.

        4                          Related Work
481
482     Multimodal Large Language Models. The development of large vision-language models has accel-
483     erated recently (OpenAI, 2023; Reka, 2024; Gemini Team, 2023; Li et al., 2023e). Flamingo (Alayrac
484     et al., 2022; Awadalla et al., 2023) and IDEFICS (Laurençon et al., 2023) have showcased the
485     effectiveness of consolidating LLMs with vision encoders. The Q-Former from BLIP-2 (Li et al.,
        2023d) has helped bridge the gap between the visual and text modalities. InstructBLIP (Dai et al.,


                                                                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2023), Ying-VLM (Li et al., 2023f) and MM-ICL (Zhao et al., 2023) further integrate instructions into
487   the vision-text alignment process for improved in-context learning ability (Dong et al., 2022). Various
488   approaches have been proposed to align visual encoders and LLMs effectively. MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al.,
489   2023) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b;a) use a single projection layer, while mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al.,
490   2023) adopts LoRA tuning (Hu et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2024), showing promising results. Qwen-VL-
491   Chat (Bai et al., 2023b) has scaled up multi-modal pre-training with more datasets. Fuyu-8 (Bavishi
492   et al., 2023) proposes a new architecture by segmenting images into pixel patches, treating them
493   as visual tokens to train a conditional multi-modal language model directly. However, these works
494   employ projector modules empirically or simply refer to the final performance of the MLLMs on
495
      downstream tasks without conducting an in-depth analysis of the projectors’ effectiveness. In this
      paper, we examine this significant component by tracking the vision-and-language semantic flow
496
      within MLLMs. We visualize the internal patterns learned by projectors and highlight their drawbacks,
497
      offering valuable insights for future development.
498
499   Transformer Explainability. Explainability tools have been widely explored for Transformers
500   to better visualize their inner decision-making processes. Raw attention maps in Transformers
501   usually provide interpretations for a single layer. Abnar et al. (Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) combine
502   the attention scores across multiple layers and propose the rollout method. Chefer et al. (Chefer
503   et al., 2021b) introduce the relevance map through information propagation from all layers and
504
      components in Transformers. LRP (Voita et al., 2019) captures the relative importance between
      different attention heads using gradients. Casual Interpretation (Rohekar et al., 2023) can identify the
505
      most important input tokens corresponding to the model output. However, these methods are only
506
      applicable to Transformers with self-attention layers. As an alternative, the GAE (Chefer et al., 2021a)
507   method extends the propagated relevance maps to bi-modal scenarios with cross-attention layers.
508   Moreover, several studies (Aflalo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c; Lyu et al., 2022; Ramesh & Koh, 2022;
509   Swamy et al., 2024) focus on the multimodal system interpretation. Recently, LVLM-Interpret (Ben
510   Melech Stan et al., 2024) has developed an interactive application for interpreting MLLMs. Despite
511   these efforts, in-depth explainability of existing MLLMs is rarely explored. In this study, we propose
512   the R-GAE method derived from GAE for MLLMs to investigate how projector modules affect the
513   vision-and-language semantic alignment of MLLMs.
514
515   5   Conclusion
516
517
      We introduce DeCo to decouple visual token compression from semantic abstraction. It is motivated by
518   the “Double Abstraction” problem of existing projectors disentangling the Text-to-Patch, Text-to-Query
519   and Query-to-Patch R-GAE maps in the vision-and-language semantic alignment. The DeCo method
520   simplifies existing compressive projectors with a naive AdaptiveAvgPool, which downsamples spatial
521   vision tokens directly at the spatial level. Experiments across diverse configurations demonstrate the
522   efficiency, effectiveness, and robustness of DeCo. Eventually, the intuition of “DeCo” is not limited
523   to the specific AdaptiveAvgPool projector design, there is great potential to improve it to perform
524   more effectively under more demanding scenarios like high compression ratio.
525
526   Limitations
527
528
      We present limitations in this work to facilitate future research. Firstly, the AdaptiveAvgPool
529   adopted in the DeCo method may cause severe visual information deficiency in an increasingly
530   high compression ratio compared to semantic-level compression projectors. In a high-compression
531   scenario, the averaging pooling will erase the fine-grained visual context in a kernel scope. Secondly,
532   the superiority of DeCo lies in a limited training resource application including limited GPUs to train
533   a long visual token sequence and limited training data to optimize a desirable semantic QFormer-type
534   projector. Otherwise, when have abundant training resources, the architecture of projectors tend to be
535   insignificant in an MLLM system as pointed out in the MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024).

840   A.1   Background: GAE Explainability for Transformer Layers
841
      The Generic Attention Explainability (GAE) (Chefer et al., 2021a) is a powerful method to interpret
842
      predictions for bi-modality Transformer-based architectures. It has the advantage of acquiring the
843
      relevance map from two arbitrary layers in the Transformer through propagation. Essentially, the
844   GAE method generates a relevance map Ā for each self-attention layer or cross-attention layer by
845   integrating raw attention maps and gradients. Then it aggregating the relevance maps of all layers
846   into a overall single map R. Formally, denote a Transformer architecture as ϕ, its attention map of
847   each layer as A, the input modality tokens as I ∈ RN ×d and the output predict class as y. We aim to
848   visualize the relevance map Ry→I ∈ RN from class y to input tokens I. Take the self-attention layer
849   as an example, the relevance map Ā for each layer and the propagation of final map Ry→I are termed
850   as:
851
                                             Ā = Eh ((∇A ⊙ A)+ ),                                        (2)
852
853
854                                             R = R + Ā · R,                                           (3)
855
856   where each layer’s attention map A can be obtained through a forward pass and the related gradient
857
      ∇A := ∂ϕ(y)                                                                             +
                 ∂A can be cached during a backward pass. ⊙ is the Hadamard product, (·) represents the
858   operation of setting negative values to 0, and Eh is the mean across the attention heads dimension. The
859   overall map R is initialized as the identity matrix with the intuition that each input token’s relevance
860   score is identical in the beginning. The propagation Formula 3 updates the R from a start layer Ls to
861   an end layer Le (Le > Ls ) in the Transformer. The cross-attention propagation is similar, which
862   maintains two relevance matrices for two modalities and updates them through the layer interaction.
863   Please refer to the details of the propagation formula across cross-attention layer from the original

      A.2   R-GAE Propagation for MLLMs
865
866   The traditional GAE map is designed for a classification task with the special CLS token. We
867   adapt it to MLLM architectures and propose the R-GAE explainability tool. As Figure 2 shows, a
868   typical MLLM architecture comprises a Vision Transformer (ViT) ϕv to acquire patch-level visual
869   representations I ∈ RN ×dI (containing N patches), a projector ϕp to transform visual representations
870   into the textual embedding space as Q, and an LLM ϕt that handles both vision and instruction tokens
871   to output hidden states T ∈ RL×dT and generate responses Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yL }. We summarize
872
      widely adopted projectors into two branches:
873   Non-compressive Projectors maintain the number of patch tokens N and only transform the visual
874   embedding dimension to match the dimension of the LLM, as exemplified by the linear projector (Liu
875   et al., 2023b). The projected visual tokens can be denoted as Q ∈ RN ×dT .
876   Compressive Projectors reduce the number of patch tokens N to a specified lesser number M
877   (M < N ), conserving training resources. For instance, QFormer (Li et al., 2023d) learns pre-defined
878   query tokens to compress original visual tokens. These compressed query tokens Q ∈ RM ×dT are
879   then fed into the LLM providing vision information.
880
      We initialize three GAE relevance maps including a Text-to-Patch map as RT →I , a Text-to-Query
881
      map as RT →Q , and a Query-to-Patch map as RQ→I . As Figure 3 depicts, given an image and an
882   instruction (e.g., “Please describe the image with a concise sentense”), an MLLM will generate a
883   textual description Y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yL } referring to the visual information. During the generation
884   step t, we can cache the attention map Av , Ap , At across the ViT, the projector and the LLM during
885   a forward pass. Then specifying a word class ybt as the target predict, we can get the gradients ∇At ,
886   ∇Ap , ∇Av in each module through a backward pass. The LLM module in MLLMs substantially
887   contains self-attention layers, therefore, we can propagate the RtT →Q ∈ R1×M according to Formula 3
888   from LLM’s first layer to its last layer. The QFormer-type projector consisting of self-attention and
889   cross-attention layers can also be propagated similarly to get RtQ→I ∈ RM ×N . Subsequently, the
890   overall text-to-patch relevance map can be obtained by matrix multiplication of text-to-query and
891
      query-to-patch maps:
892                                        RtT →I = RtT →Q × RtQ→I                                          (4)
893
894   For a complete sentence Y , we integrate the GAE relevance maps from each time step t by averaging
895   them to obtain the overall visual relevance related to a factual sentence. The final three maps are
896   formulated as followings, in which RT →I ∈ R1×N , RT →Q ∈ R1×M , and RQ→I ∈ RM ×N .
897                         L                                 L                               L
898
                    1X t                             1X t                              1X t
            RT →Q =      R     ,              RQ→I =      R    ,               RT →I =      R               (5)
899                 L t=1 T →Q                       L t=1 Q→T                         L t=1 T →I
900
901   For non-compression projectors maintaining the number of original patches, such as linear layers, the
902   Query-to-Patch map is an identity mapping based on the one-to-one correspondence between queries
903   and patches. Consequently, the Query-to-Image map visualizes the original image consisting of 576
904   patches. The Text-to-Query map is obtained in the same manner as in the QFormer, which propagates
905
      from the R-GAE maps in the Language Model (LLM).
906   For the AdaptiveAvgPool projector in the DeCo method, a 2D spatial down-sampling mapping is
907   constructed from the original tokens to the compressed tokens. For an operation window with kernel
908   size K, the merged token is assigned a relevance score equal to 1/K 2 of the sum of the relevance
909   scores of each raw token within the window. The corresponding Query-to-Patch map can be calculated
910   using this simple mapping rule. Similar to the QFormer, the Text-to-Query map is obtained from the
911
      LLM layers.

1009   Our work utilizes off-the-shelf frozen LLMs, which means it shares some of their intrinsic drawbacks,
1010   such as generating hallucinated, ungrounded text or biased outputs. We mitigate these issues by
1011   enhancing the model’s grounding in both visual and instruction inputs. Additionally, our training
1012
       dataset includes 40K examples of safety data sourced from ShareGPT, instructing the models to
       refuse responses to toxic, inappropriate, or otherwise unsafe inputs. However, we do not recommend
1013

1014
