010                                             A BSTRACT
011
012
013           Data, model and hardware are crucial components in the development of large scale
014
              machine learning models. The training of such models necessitates substantial
              computational resources, energy consumption, and raw materials, resulting in
015
              significant environmental implications. However, the environmental impact of
016
              these models has been largely overlooked due to a lack of assessment and analysis
017           of their carbon footprint. In this paper, we present OpenCarbonEval, a carbon
018           emission estimation tool to quantify the environmental implications of large scale
019           machine learning models given their total training computations and hardware
020           configurations. In OpenCarbonEval, we conducted a comprehensive dynamic
021           analysis of the interrelationships among data, models, and hardware throughout
022           the model training process, aiming to forecast the carbon emission of large scale
023           models more accurately. We validated our approach on real-world dataset, and
024           experimental results demonstrate that OpenCarbonEval can predict energy costs
025
              and carbon emissions more accurately than previous methods. Furthermore, it can
              be seamlessly applied to various machine learning tasks without a precision decline.
026
              By quantifying the environmental impact of large-scale models, OpenCarbonEval
027
              promotes sustainable AI development and deployment, contributing to a more
028           environmentally responsible future for the AI community.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Recently, large scale ML models like Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023) and Multi-
057   modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Chen et al., 2023) have exhibited remarkable intelligence
058   across a wide range of tasks, largely attributed to the advancement of their scaling laws (Henighan
059   et al., 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022). However, as the scale of model parameters and
060   training sets increases, the computational overhead of training and maintaining large-scale models
061   becomes exorbitantly huge, resulting in significant environmental impacts. For instance, training a
062   GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020) with 175B parameters will consume nearly 1300MWh of electricity (Pat-
063   terson et al., 2021), roughly equivalent to the annual electricity consumption of 130 households in the
064   US. Meanwhile, its corresponding carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2eq) is about 552 tons (Patterson
065
      et al., 2021), which is three times the CO2eq emissions of jet plane round trip between San Francisco
      and New York. Therefore, the ML community should pay greater attention to the energy consumption
066
      and environmental impact of these large-scale ML models.
067
068   Previous works, such as MLCO2 (Lacoste et al., 2019) and GreenAlgorithm (Lannelongue et al.,
069   2021), have proposed to calculate the carbon emission of ML tasks based on some key parameters
070   like GPU usage, training duration, and data center efficiency. These methods heavily rely on exact
071
      information about the training process, implying that only model developers can use these tools to
      estimate the energy consumption and carbon emissions of their trained models. To break away from
072
      this limitation, LLMCarbon (Faiz et al., 2023) presents an end-to-end approach for estimating carbon
073
      emissions before model training. It inputs the key architecture parameters of LLM into its specially
074   designed FLOP-model and efficiency-model, which can be used to predict the training duration and
075   carbon emission. However, the key steps of this method are all designed for LLM, and the polynomial
076   fitting coefficients in its efficiency-model are completely unsuitable for other ML tasks, e.g. image
077   generation. Furthermore, these estimation methods often assume a static or average workload, failing
078   to capture the dynamic nature of the training process of large scale ML models. This oversight can
079   lead to significant inaccuracies in energy consumption and carbon emission estimates.
080   To ensure a comprehensive comparison and analysis for the energy and carbon footprint of various
081   past and future large-scale models, we have identified key challenges: an accurate and transparent
082   anticipatory approach is needed, which can use basic information of training to predict energy
083   consumption and carbon emissions accurately. This approach should also produce fair and comparable
084   results across diverse ML tasks in various fields.
085
      In this paper, we propose OpenCarbonEval, a carbon emission estimation tool to quantify the
086   environmental implications of large scale ML models given their total training computations and
087   hardware configurations. In OpenCarbonEval, our contributions are summarized as follows:
088
089          • A Carbon Emission Estimation Method for Various ML Tasks We propose a novel
090            method to accurately estimate the dynamic power consumption and carbon emission of large
091            scale ML models across various ML tasks, using two basic information including training
092            computation and hardware configuration.
093          • The first Open Source Dataset about the Carbon Footprint of Large Scale ML Models
094            We collect and open source the OpenCarbonEval dataset comprising 110 real-world data of
095            large scale ML models across 20 ML tasks on their carbon footprint.
096          • Empirical Validation on the Method of Carbon Emission Estimation We conduct a
097            statistical analysis of the benefits and limitations of carbon emission methods, providing
098            valuable insights for future research.
099
100   The results of our analysis demonstrate that the predictions generated by OpenCarbonEval exhibit a
101
      high accuracy with real-world data, enabling us to produce more accurate predictions for various ML
      Tasks. Furthermore, to promote a more transparent and sustainable ML community, we will open
102
      source all the OpenCarbonEval dataset and the estimation tools used.

106
107   Over the past decade, deep learning has experienced remarkable advancements, particularly with the
      recent dominance of large-scale models. These models have significantly increased in model size


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and training data (Villalobos et al., 2024). While their performance has improved dramatically, the
109   computational costs have grown exponentially (Sevilla et al., 2022). This surge in computational
110   demand results in substantial energy consumption, leading to considerable greenhouse gas emissions.
111   As we continue to develop more and larger AI models in the foreseeable future, understanding their
112   energy costs and environmental impact becomes crucial.
113
      Previous works (Wu et al., 2022; Luccioni et al., 2023) usually divide the carbon footprint of AI
114
      models into two parts: operational carbon and embodied carbon. Operational carbon includes the
115   carbon emissions generated by producing the electricity required for training an AI model and using
116   it for inference on computing devices. Embodied carbon means the equivalent carbon emissions
117   from manufacturing the computing devices. During the training phase of large models, the primary
118   contributor to carbon emissions is operational carbon, which results indirectly from the energy
119   consumption of AI computing chips. It can be calculated by multiplying the energy cost for AI
120   computing E(kW h) by the regional carbon intensity I(kgCO2eq/kW h).
121   Related works have proposed some methods for calculating the energy cost and carbon footprint of
122   training AI models, we can broadly categorize them into three types:
123
124
      Retrospective Calculation Method: MLCO2 (Lacoste et al., 2019) and GreenAlgorithm (Lanne-
      longue et al., 2021) can estimate the energy consumption and carbon footprint of ML tasks based on
125
      user input information such as device type, training duration, and power grid area. The difference
126
      is that the latter accounts for additional CPU and memory consumption. Although these inputs are
127   independent of the model, their application is significantly limited. This is because, aside from model
128   developers, others may not have access to the exact training duration. Consequently, we can not apply
129   these estimates to models that have not been trained or those without reported training duration.
130
      Real-time Monitoring Method: CodeCarbon (Courty et al., 2024), Carbontracker (Anthony et al.,
131
      2020), and Eco2AI (Budennyy et al., 2022) are designed to run in parallel with ML tasks for real-time
132
      monitoring. Each provides a Python library that can be integrated into existing training scripts
133   to capture dynamic hardware energy consumption throughout the process. While this approach
134   is theoretically precise, its intrusive nature or lack of integration with existing distributed training
135   frameworks may limit widespread adoption. This method also cannot be used to analyze existing
136   models or predict future models’ carbon emissions.
137
      Anticipatory Estimation Method: LLMCarbon (Faiz et al., 2023) is first end-to-end approach for
138
      estimating model carbon emissions before training. It is specifically designed for LLM architectures,
139   which includes a FLOP-model to estimate total computation and an efficiency-model to estimate
140   average hardware computation speed. By combining them, this anticipatory method can predict
141   training time and carbon footprint based on the model’s key information before training. However,
142   since the FLOP-model and efficiency-model are tailored to LLM frameworks, the polynomial
143   coefficients used in the method are difficult to apply to other hardware types or task architectures.
144
      Our OpenCarbonEval is also an anticipatory method, this general framework leverages existing
145   model training statistics and approximates the dynamic computation processes of hardware, which
146   can predict training times for AI models across various architectures and tasks. This enables fair and
147   comparable estimate results of energy consumption and carbon footprint.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
433
434
      Insufficient Real-world Data While OpenCarbonEval provides a unified framework for estimating
435   carbon emissions, it is not without its limitations. For example, due to the limited availability of
436   real-world data on ML models, significant deviations in predictions are possible in some scenarios.
437   Besides, various training setups, such as deep learning frameworks and distributed parallel strategies,
438   can significantly impact training speeds and duration. However, the current scarcity of real-world
439   data hinders a comprehensive analysis of these factors. So our framework is not primarily focused on
440   accuracy, but rather on the predictability and universality it provides. For AI developers who require
441   precise values of their model’s energy consumption and carbon emissions, we recommend using the
442   real-time monitoring methods (Courty et al., 2024) mentioned in Section 2 to obtain more reliable
443
      results and report their results.
444   Carbon Footprint more than GPU Previous works (Lannelongue et al., 2021; Faiz et al., 2023)
445   assumed that devices such as CPUs and memories operated at constant power, and incorporated the
446   energy consumption caused by these devices into Eq. (2) to account for the carbon footprint brought
447   by additional equipment. Alternatively, from the perspective of the data center (Wu et al., 2022), the
448   energy consumption generated by GPUs can be multiplied by Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE), to
449
      obtain the overall energy consumption. Both of them are feasible solutions, but due to the ground
      truth of the carbon footprint model in real-world data is mainly calculated by GPU consumption, we
450
      did not incorporate these additional consumptions into our framework.
451
452   Broder Impact on Environmental Sustainability The increasing carbon footprint of large-scale
453   AI models has significant implications for the environment and sustainability. Our analysis using
454   OpenCarbonEval reveals a concerning trend of growing carbon emissions associated with the devel-
455
      opment and deployment of these models. This highlights the need for the AI community to prioritize
      environmental sustainability alongside performance and efficiency. Furthermore, the environmental
456
      impact of AI models can have far-reaching consequences, including contributing to climate change,
457
      air pollution, and e-waste generation. By providing a unified framework for predicting carbon emis-
458   sions, OpenCarbonEval can facilitate the development of more environmentally friendly AI models
459   and encourage responsible AI practices. This includes promoting transparency and accountability
460   in AI development, encouraging sustainable AI design and deployment, and fostering a culture of
461   environmental responsibility within the AI community.
462
463
      6   C ONCLUSION
464
465
      In this paper, we present OpenCarbonEval, a carbon emission estimation tool to quantify the en-
466
      vironmental implications of large scale ML models in their training process. OpenCarbonEval is
467   able to accurately estimate the carbon emission and energy consumption of various large scale
468   ML models across various ML tasks, resulting in a more carbon-transparent training process. By
469   leveraging OpenCarbonEval, we collect the first open source carbon footprint dataset comprising
470   the carbon footprint for training large scale ML models. Furthermore, our systematic analysis of
471   the estimation for carbon emissions across various ML tasks provides valuable insights for future
472   research, contributing to the development of more sustainable large scale ML models.
