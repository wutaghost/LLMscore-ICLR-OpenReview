011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013              Few-shot fine-tuning of Diffusion Models (DMs) is a key advancement, signifi-
014              cantly reducing training costs and enabling personalized AI applications. However,
015              we explore the few-shot fine-tuning dynamics of DMs and observe an unanticipated
016
                 phenomenon: during the fine-tuning process, image fidelity initially improves, then
                 unexpectedly deteriorates with the emergence of noisy patterns, only to recover
017
                 later with severe overfitting. We term the stage with generated noisy patterns as
018
                 corruption stage. To understand this corruption stage, we begin by heuristically
019              modeling the one-shot fine-tuning scenario, and then extend this modeling to more
020              general cases. Through this modeling, we identify the primary cause of this cor-
021              ruption stage: a narrowed learning distribution inherent in the nature of few-shot
022              fine-tuning. To tackle this, we apply Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) on DMs
023              with variational inference to implicitly broaden the learned distribution, and present
024              that the learning target of the BNNs can be naturally regarded as an expectation
025              of the diffusion loss and a further regularization with the pretrained DMs. This
026              approach is highly compatible with current few-shot fine-tuning methods in DMs
027
                 and does not introduce any extra inference costs. Experimental results demonstrate
                 that our method significantly mitigates corruption, and improves the fidelity, qual-
028
                 ity and diversity of the generated images in both object-driven and subject-driven
029
                 generation tasks. The code is available at an anonymous link1 .

032   1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent years have witnessed a surge in the development of Diffusion Models (DMs). These models
035   have showcased extraordinary capabilities in various applications, such as image editing (Kawar
036   et al., 2022) and video editing (Yang et al., 2022), among others. Particularly noteworthy is the
037   advent of few-shot fine-tuning methods (Hu et al., 2021; Ruiz et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023), in which
038   a pretrained model is fine-tuned to personalize generation based on a small set of training images.
039   These approaches have significantly reduced both memory and time costs in training. Moreover,
040   these techniques offer powerful tools for adaptively generating images based on specific subjects
041
      or objects, embodying personalized AI and making AI accessible to everyone. In recent years, this
      innovation has even fostered the emergence of several communities, such as civitai.com , which
042
      boasts tens of thousands of checkpoints and millions of downloads.
043
044   Despite the importance and widespread usage of few-shot fine-tuning methods in DMs, these methods
045   often struggle or even fail when transferring from a large distribution (i.e., pretrained DMs’ learned
046   distribution) to a much smaller one (i.e., fine-tuned DMs’ learned distribution) using limited data (Qiu
047
      et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023). We are the first to identify when and how these failures occur, and
      find that they are related to an unusual phenomenon: As shown in Fig. 1, the similarity between the
048
      generated images and the training images initially increases during fine-tuning, but then unexpectedly
049
      decreases, before increasing once more. Ultimately, the DMs are only capable of generating images
050   identical to the training images. Notably, on the stage with decreasing similarity, we observe there
051   appear some unexpected noisy patterns on the generated images, therefore we name it as corruption
052   stage.

068   To understand this corruption stage, we carry out further analysis on the few-shot fine-tuning process.
069   Specifically, we start from heuristic modeling a one-shot case, i.e., using only one image during
070   fine-tuning, and then extend it to more general cases. The modeling provides an estimation of the
071   error scale remaining in the generated images. We further show how corruption stages emerge due to
072
      the limited learned distribution inherent in few-shot tasks with this modeling.
073   Based on the analysis above, the solution to corruption should concentrate on expanding the learned
074   distribution. However, this expansion remains challenging in few-shot fine-tuning. Traditional
075   data augmentation methods, when applied to generative models, often face significant problems
076   such as leakage (Karras et al., 2020) and a reduction in generation quality (Daras et al., 2023).
077   Inspired by recent advancements in Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) (Blundell et al., 2015), we
078
      propose to incorporate BNNs as a straightforward yet potent strategy to implicitly broaden the learned
      distribution. We further present that its learning target can be regarded as an expectation of the
079
      diffusion loss and an extra regularization loss related to the pretrained model. These two losses can
080
      be adjusted to reach a trade-off between image fidelity and diversity. Our method does not introduce
081   any extra inference costs, and has good compatibility with existing few-shot fine-tuning methods
082   in DMs, including DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023), LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), and OFT (Qiu et al.,
083   2023). Experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly alleviates the corruption issues
084   and substantially enhances the performance across various few-shot fine-tuning methods on diverse
085   datasets under different metrics.
086
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
087
088   1. We observe an abnormal phenomenon during few-shot fine-tuning process on DMs: The image
089   fidelity first enhances, then unexpectedly worsens with the appearance of noisy patterns, before
090
      improving again but with severe overfitting. We refer to the phase where noisy patterns appear as the
      corruption stage. We hope this observation could inform future research on DMs.
091
092   2. We provide a heuristic modeling for few-shot fine-tuning process on DMs, explaining the emer-
093   gence and disappearance of the corruption stage. With this modeling, we pinpoint that the main issue
094   stems from the constrained learned distribution of DMs inherent in few-shot fine-tuning process.
095   3. We innovatively incorporate BNNs to broaden the learned distribution, hence relieving such
096   corruption. Experimental results confirm the effectiveness of this approach in improving on different
097   metrics, including text prompt fidelity, image fidelity, generation diversity, and image quality.

103   Diffusion Models (DMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Song
104   et al., 2020) are generative models that approximate a data distribution through the gradual denoising
105   of a variable initially sampled from a Gaussian distribution. These models involve a forward diffusion
106   process and a backward denoising process. In the forward process,    √ the extent

      noise serves as the training loss, denoted as diffusion loss LDM := Eε∼N (0,1),t ||ϵθ (xt , t) − ε||2 .
111
112   Few-shot fine-tuning in DMs (Gal et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2021; Qiu et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023) aims
113   at personalizing DMs with a limited set of images, facilitating the creation of tailored content. Gal
114
      et al. (2022) introduced a technique that leverages new tokens within the embedding space of a frozen
      text-to-image model to capture the concepts presented in provided images. Ruiz et al. (2023) further
115
      proposed DreamBooth, which fine-tunes most parameters in DMs leveraging a reconstruction loss
116
      that captures more precise details of the input images, with a class-specific preservation loss ensuring
117   the alignment with the textual prompts. Additionally, Hu et al. (2021) proposed LoRA, a lightweight
118   fine-tuning approach that inserts low-rank layers to be learned while keeping other parameters frozen.
119   Qiu et al. (2023) presented OFT, a method that employs orthogonal transformations to enhance the
120   quality of generation. Although these methods generally succeed in capturing the details of training
121   images, they suffer from the corruption stage observed in this paper.
122
123   2.2       BAYESIAN N EURAL N ETWORKS
124
125   Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) are a type of stochastic neural networks characterized by treating
126
      the parameters as random variables rather than fixed values (Blundell et al., 2015; Buntine, 1991;
      Neal, 2012). The objective is to infer the posterior distribution P (θ|D) for the parameters θ given
127
      a dataset D. It endows BNNs with several distinct advantages, such as the capability to model the
128
      distributions for output, to mitigate overfitting, and to enhance model interpretability (Arbel et al.,
129   2023; Jospin et al., 2022). One prevalent variant of BNNs is the mean-field variational BNN, also
130   known as Bayes by Backprop, where the mean-field variational inference is applied to obtain the
131   variational distribution QW (θ) to approximate the posterior distribution P (θ|D) (Blundell et al.,
132   2015). Recent studies demonstrated that even a BNN module, which treats only a subset of parameters
133   as random variables while maintaining the rest as fixed, can retain the benefits associated with full
134   BNNs (Harrison et al., 2023; Kristiadi et al., 2020; Sharma et al., 2023). Our proposed method can
135   be regarded as a natural progression of BNN principles applied to few-shot fine-tuning in DMs.

138
139   In Sec. 3.1, we first present the observation on the corruption stage during few-shot fine-tuning of
140   DMs. In Sec. 3.2, to better observe and understand the issues and fine-tuning dynamics with the
141   corruption stage, we propose a heuristic modeling that uses Gaussian distribution as an approximation.
142   To address the challenges of modeling the dynamics of DMs during fine-tuning, we adopt reasonable
143   simplifications, supported by evidence from a specific case. In Sec. 3.3, we explain the emergence
144   and vanishing of the corruption stage by our modeling, and reveal the limited learned distribution is
145   the root cause of the corruption stage.

301   4.1     M OTIVATION
302
303
304
      Based on our analysis, the corruption stage primarily arises from a limited learned distribution with a
      small Iθ . Motivated by recent work on BNNs (Blundell et al., 2015; Harrison et al., 2023; Kristiadi
305
      et al., 2020; Sharma et al., 2023), which model the parameters θ as random variables, we propose to
306
      apply BNNs in the few-shot fine-tuning process on DMs as a simple yet effective method to expand
307   Iθ . Intuitively, the modeling of BNNs hinders the DMs to learn the exact distribution of the training
308   dataset D. Therefore, the DMs are encouraged to learn a larger and more robust distribution to
309   counter the randomness. Moreover, the sampling randomness during the fine-tuning process can be
310   regarded as an inherent data augmentation, indicating the corresponding Iθ is implicitly expanded.
311
312
313

      4.2     F ORMULATION
315
316   Modeling. BNNs model the parameters
                                    R        θ as random variables. Therefore, the learned distribution
317   of DMs with BNN is P (x|D) = P (x|θ)P (θ|D)dθ. Concretely, P (x|θ) is the image distribution
318   modeled by the DMs, and P (θ|D) is the posterior parameter distribution with a given dataset D.
319   As the posterior distribution P (θ|D) is intractable, a variational distribution QW (θ) is applied to
320   approximate it. We model the variational distribution of each parameter θ as a Gaussian distribution:
321   θ ∼ N (µθ , σθ2 ), where W = {µθ , σθ } are trainable parameters. Considering the fine-tuning process,
322   we initialize the expectation term µθ from the corresponding parameter of the pretrained DMs,
323   denoted as θ0 . Following previous work (Blundell et al., 2015), we apply the re-parameterization
      trick to obtain the gradients of the parameters, as detailed in Appendix Sec. C.

512   User Study Setting. We conduct user study to comprehensively present the superiority of applying
513
      BNNs on few-shot fine-tuning DMs. Concretely, we follow previous approaches (Qiu et al., 2023;

514
      participants. For this purpose, we utilize the DreamBooth dataset and the CelebA-HQ dataset.
515
      Subsequently, we generate four images per subject or object using a random prompt selected from 25
516   prompts across five models trained with different seeds. For comprehensive comparisons, we select
517   both the best and average cases from the generated images (see Appendix Sec. I for details).
518
519
      Each participant is requested to compare image pairs generated by models that have been fine-tuned
      with and without BNNs, using three baseline models: DreamBooth, LoRA, and OFT. For each task,
520
      there are three binary-selection questions:
521
522   • Subject fidelity: Which of the given two images contains a subject or object that is most similar to
523     the following reference image (one from the training dataset)?
524   • Text alignment: Which of the given two images best matches the text description provided below
525     (the prompt used to generate the images)?
526
      • Image quality: Which of the given two images exhibits the higher image quality?
527
528   Results. The results are presented in Tab. 4, which shows the percentage of participants favoring each
529   method (with and without BNNs) based on the criteria described above. It is evident that the methods
530   with BNNs are preferred in almost all scenarios for both best-case and average-case generations. This
531   preference is particularly significant in terms of text alignment and overall image quality.

533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we focus on few-shot fine-tuning in DMs, and reveal an unusual “corruption stage”
536   where image fidelity first improves, then deteriorates due to noisy patterns, before recovering. With
537   theoretical modeling, we attribute this phenomenon to the constrained learned distribution inherent
538   in few-shot fine-tuning. By applying BNNs to broaden the learned distribution, we mitigate the
539   corruption. Experiment results across various fine-tuning methods and datasets underscore the
      versatility of our approach.

      We use the following metrics to robustly measure different aspects of the fine-tuned DMs:
897
898   Text prompt fidelity: Following previous papers (Qiu et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023) , we use
899   the average similarity between the clip (Radford et al., 2021) embeddings of the text prompt and
900   generated images, denoted as Clip-T.
901   Image fidelity: Following previous papers (Qiu et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2023), we compute the
902   average similarity between the clip (Radford et al., 2021) and dino (Caron et al., 2021) embeddings
903   of the generated images and training images, denoted as Clip-I (Hessel et al., 2021) and Dino.
904
      Generation Diversity: We compute the average Lpips (Zhang et al., 2018) distance between the
905
      generated images of the fine-tuned DMs, following previous papers (Qiu et al., 2023; Ruiz et al.,
906   2023).
907
908   Image quality: We find that corruption largely decreases the visual quality of the images, making
909
      them unusable. However, full-reference images quality measurement cannot fully represent this
      kind of degradation in image quality. Therefore, we propose to add a no-visual quality metric for
910
      measurements. We use Clip-IQA (Wang et al., 2023) for measurements, which is one of the SOTA
911
      no-reference image quality measurements.

951
952
953   In practical scenarios, users typically generate multiple images and manually choose the most suitable
954
      one for use. Therefore, comparing the best cases essentially evaluates the quality of the chosen
      images, while the comparison of average cases assesses the difficulty of selecting an adequate image.
955
      Conversely, worst-case scenarios may hold less practical relevance, as users may regenerate images
956
      until achieving a satisfactory result, effectively bypassing such cases.
957
958   Therefore, we place emphasis on presenting best-case and average-case generation in this paper.
959   Specifically, we first filter the generated images by selecting the top 90% using CLIP-T and Dino
960
      to ensure alignment with the prompt and the learned concept. Subsequently, we employ CLIP-
      IQA to identify both the top-quality and average-quality images. This approach provides a more
961
      comprehensive evaluation of the models’ performance.
962
963   Nonetheless, we also include some comparisons of the worst-case generation results before and after
964   applying BNNs illustrated in Fig. 13 for comprehensiveness. Concretely, we present the images with
965   the lowest image qualities evaluated using CLIP-IQA. Our experiments span 10 random classes in
966
      the DreamBooth Dataset, with DreamBooth as the baseline fine-tuning method. The results show that
      DMs fine-tuned without BNNs usually present the corruption while BNNs successfully mitigate it,
967
      leading to better generation quality.

983
984    Even though few-shot fine-tuning DMs with BNNs applied has shown promising improvements,
985    this paper also introduces a few interesting open problems. Firstly, the extra randomness may make
986    fine-tuning slower. This may lower the generation quality when the DMs are under-fitting. In addition,

988    fine-tuning iterations is insufficient. Future work could focus on these problems.

990    K    B ROADER I MPACT
991
992    This paper focuses on advancing few-shot fine-tuning techniques in DMs to provide more effective
993    tools for creating personalized images in various contexts. Previous few-shot fine-tuning methods
994    have faced corruption phenomenon, as mentioned in this paper. Our approach, utilizing BNNs,
995    addresses this phenomenon and provides generated images with higher quality.
996    However, there is potential for misuse, as malicious entities could exploit these technologies to
997    deceive or misinform. Such challenges underscore the critical need for continuous exploration in
998    this field. The development and ethical application of personalized generative models are not only
999    paramount but also ripe for future research.
