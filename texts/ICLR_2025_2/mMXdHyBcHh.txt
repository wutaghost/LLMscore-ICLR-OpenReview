010
011
012
               Though current long-context large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated
               impressive capacities in answering user questions based on extensive text, the lack
013
               of citations in their responses makes user verification difficult, leading to concerns
014
               about their trustworthiness due to their potential hallucinations. In this work,
015            we aim to enable long-context LLMs to generate responses with fine-grained
016            sentence-level citations, improving their faithfulness and verifiability. We first in-
017            troduce LongBench-Cite, an automated benchmark for assessing current LLMs’
018            performance in Long-Context Question Answering with Citations (LQAC), re-
019            vealing considerable room for improvement. To this end, we propose CoF (Coarse
020            to Fine), a novel pipeline that utilizes off-the-shelf LLMs to automatically gener-
021            ate long-context QA instances with precise sentence-level citations, and leverage
022            this pipeline to construct LongCite-45k, a large-scale SFT dataset for LQAC. Fi-
023
               nally, we train LongCite-8B and LongCite-9B using the LongCite-45k dataset,
               successfully enabling their generation of accurate responses and fine-grained
024
               sentence-level citations in a single output. The evaluation results on LongBench-
025
               Cite show that our trained models achieve state-of-the-art citation quality, surpass-
026            ing advanced proprietary models including GPT-4o. We also discover that SFT
027            with citation information can further improve the correctness of model responses
028            compared to standard long-context SFT.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent years have witnessed significant advancement in long-context large language models
034
      (LLMs), enabling them to address various user questions, such as information extraction and sum-
035
      marization, based on lengthy texts that surpass 100,000 tokens (Anthropic, 2024b; Zeng et al., 2024;
036   Reid et al., 2024). Despite their remarkable capacities, current long-context LLMs typically do
037   not provide citations to specific context snippets to support the statements they generated, making it
038   challenging for users to verify model outputs given the substantial context lengths. This significantly
039   impacts the reliability and trustworthiness of long-context LLMs, especially considering that they
040   still struggle with hallucinations (Huang et al., 2023) and are prone to generate unfaithful content.
041
      On the other hand, recent works in search engines and open-domain QA have allowed LLMs to gen-
042   erate responses with in-line citations through retrieval-based generation (RAG) or post-hoc meth-
043   ods (Nakano et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023a;b; Menick et al., 2022). Nevertheless, these approaches
044   still expose notable limitations in long-context scenarios: RAG often leads to compromised answer
045   quality due to incomplete context information, while post-hoc methods prolong the user waiting
046   time due to more complicated pipeline. In addition, their generated citations typically refer to entire
047   web pages (Nakano et al., 2021) or coarsely chunked snippets (Gao et al., 2023b), thereby requiring
048   users to further pinpoint the specific supporting evidence for the final verification.
049   To overcome the above limitations, this work explores directly employing long-context LLMs to
050   generate accurate responses with fine-grained sentence-level in-line citations. We first propose
051   LongBench-Cite, an automatic benchmark, to evaluate LLMs’ performance on the task of long-
052   context question answering with citations (LQAC), and find that current LLMs obtain unsatisfac-
053   tory results (Sec. 2). Specifically, we find that many citations produced by current LLMs are either
      irrelevant, cannot fully support the response, or have a coarse granularity. Meanwhile, we observe


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that generating citations on the fly via in-context learning generally results in responses with lower
055   correctness compared to vanilla long-context QA.
056
057   To further enhance the inherent capacity of LLMs for generating fine-grained citations from lengthy
058
      contexts, it is essential to construct a high-quality SFT dataset. To this end, we introduce CoF (abbr.
      for “Coarse to Fine”), a novel pipeline that utilizes off-the-shelf LLMs to automatically construct
059
      long-context QA instances with precise sentence-level citations (Sec. 3). CoF comprises four stages:
060
      (1) Starting with a long text material, CoF first invokes the LLM to produce a query and its associated
061   answer through Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2023). (2) Next, CoF uses the answer to retrieve several
062   chunks (each has a fixed length of 128 tokens 1 ) from the context, which are then fed into the LLM
063   to incorporate coarse-grained chunk-level citations into the answer. (3) The LLM subsequently
064   identifies relevant sentences from each cited chunk to produce fine-grained citations. (4) As a final
065   step, instances with an insufficient number of citations are discarded. Our experiments validate the
066   superiority of CoF over other LQAC strategies in terms of answer correctness and citation quality.
067   With CoF, we construct LongCite-45k, a large-scale SFT dataset that consists of 44,600 high-quality
068   LQAC instances with contexts up to 128,000 tokens.
069   Finally, we utilize LongCite-45k to fine-tune GLM-4-9B (Zeng et al., 2024) and Llama3.1-
070   8B (Vavekanand & Sam, 2024), two latest open-source long-context models (Sec. 4). The enhanced
071   models, namely LongCite-9B and LongCite-8B, support a max context window of 128,000 tokens
072   and are capable of generating accurate responses along with precise, fine-grained citations in one
073   pass. Evaluation on LongBench-Cite indicates that our trained models achieve significantly better ci-
074   tation quality compared to even much larger proprietary models. Specifically, our 8B/9B size model
075
      outperforms GPT-4o by 6.4%/3.6% in terms of citation F1 score and achieves twice finer granular-
      ity. Meanwhile, we observe that SFT with citation information can alleviate hallucinations of LLMs
076
      and enable them to utilize context information more uniformly and comprehensively, instead of only
077
      focusing on a specific part of the context. This results in a further improvement in response correct-
078   ness over standard long-context SFT. We also conduct extensive analyses and human evaluation to
079   further verify the effectiveness of our approach.
080
081
      To summarize, our work makes the following contributions:
082   1. We introduce LongBench-Cite, an automatic benchmark for the task of LQAC, and reveal the
083   limited performance of current long-context LLMs.
084
      2. We propose CoF, which utilizes off-the-shelf LLMs to automatically construct high-quality long-
085   context QA instances with fine-grained sentence-level citations. Using this method, we construct
086   LongCite-45k, a large-scale SFT dataset for LQAC.
087
088
      3. We successfully train LongCite-8B and LongCite-9B using LongCite-45k dataset, allowing the
      generation of accurate responses and fine-grained citations in one pass. Our experiments show that
089
      SFT on LQAC data not only enhances the capacity for generating citations from lengthy contexts
090
      but also further improves response correctness.

095   2.1       P ROBLEM D EFINITION
096
097
      We formalize the task of long-context question answering with citations (LQAC) as follows:
      given a long context D and a query q, the LLM is required to return a response A, which consists of
098
      n statements s1 , . . . , sn , and each statement si cites a list of snippets Ci = {ci,1 , ci,2 , . . . } from D.
099
      In this work, LLMs need to segment their responses into statements based on semantic integrity by
100   enclosing each statement with two special tokens <statement> and </statement>. As illustrated in
101   Figure 1, we consider two types of citations:
102
103   • Chunk-level citations, where the context D is divided into indexed chunks with a fix length of
104     128 tokens, and each citation ci,j is in the form of [k], referring to the k-th chunk;
105   • Sentence-level citations, where D is divided into indexed sentences, and each ci,j takes the form
106     of [a-b], referring to the snippet that includes the a-th to b-th sentences in D.

      Most previous works (Menick et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023b; Buchmann et al., 2024) for citation gen-
134
      eration explore the chunk-level citations. However, the coarse granularity of chunk-level citations
135   requires users to sift through many irrelevant details in the cited content, and the crude segmentation
136   applied for chunk-level citations often results in incomplete cited sentences. Therefore, in this work,
137   we mainly focus on sentence-level citations (Slobodkin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) because they
138   allow for finer-grained citation, ensure semantic integrity better, and are thus more user-friendly.

      2.2     DATA C OLLECTION
141
142   To evaluate LLMs’ performance on LQAC task, we curate a new benchmark LongBench-Cite
143   by collecting data from existing bilingual long-context benchmarks LongBench (Bai et al., 2023)
144   and LongBench-Chat (Bai et al., 2024), covering multiple key user-intensive tasks in both En-
145   glish and Chinese. Specifically, LongBench is a comprehensive benchmark with an average length
146   of 7k words (English) and 13k characters (Chinese), and we select two single-doc QA datasets
147   MultiFieldQA-en/zh (Bai et al., 2023), two multi-doc QA datasets HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and
148   DuReader (He et al., 2018), and one summarization dataset GovReport (Huang et al., 2021) from it.
149   LongBench-Chat comprises 50 real-world queries with long contexts ranging from 10k to 100k in
150
      length, covering various scenarios such as document QA, summarization, and coding, and we adopt
      all the queries. The detailed data statistics are listed in Table 1. For all datasets, we require LLMs to

      generate long-form responses with citations.

154   2.3     AUTOMATIC E VALUATION
155
156   LongBench-Cite evaluates models’ responses based on the two dimensions:
157
158   • Correctness: Whether the response is accurate and consistent with the groundtruth.
159   • Citation quality: Whether the response is entirely supported by the cited snippets, no irrelevant
160     snippets are cited, and the cited snippets are fine-grained.
161
      In the following, we introduce automatic metrics for each dimension.


                                                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2.3.1    E VALUATION OF C ORRECTNESS
163
164   For the correctness dimension, we adopt the evaluation method of Bai et al. (2024), which is spe-
165   cially designed for long-form responses. Specifically, we first remove citation-relevant tokens from
166   LLM response, then ask GPT-4o to rate the response based on the query and groundtruth answers via
167   few-shot (for LongBench-Chat) or zero-shot prompting (for other datasets). The detailed prompts
168
      can be found in Figure 4, 5, and 6. In addition, to investigate whether adding citations will hurt or
      improve models’ long-context QA performance, we propose a new metric correctness ratio:
169
170                                           CR = C/CLQA × 100%                                             (1)
171
      Here, C and CLQA respectively denote the correctness in LQAC setting and vanilla long-context QA
172
      setting (i.e., simply feeding the concatenated context and query into the LLM to get a response).
173
174   2.3.2    E VALUATION OF C ITATION Q UALITY
175
176   To evaluate the citation quality, we select citation F1 calculated using citation recall and citation
177   precision (Gao et al., 2023b) as the main metric, where the former examines if the model response is
178   fully supported by cited snippets and the later detects irrelevant citations. Compared with Gao et al.
179   (2023b), which uses NLI model TRUE (Honovich et al., 2022) for automatic examination, we further
180
      improve the measurement method with GPT-4o to better adapt to long-context QA scenarios. Human
      evaluation (Sec. 4.3) demonstrates our method has a stronger agreement with human. Besides, we
181
      use citation length to measure the granularity of citations and avoid trivial results.
182
183   Citation Recall. We score citation recall (0/0.5/1) for each statement and average over all statements
184   in the model response. Specifically, for each statement si that cites at least one snippet (i.e., Ci ̸= ∅),
185   we concatenate all snippets in Ci and ask GPT-4o to judge whether the concatenated text fully
186
      supports (1 point), partially supports (0.5 point), or does not support (0 point) si . On the other
      hand, most LLM responses contain several “functional sentences” such as “The proposed method
187
      has the following advantages:” and “In summary, ...” that do not require citation. Therefore, for
188
      each statement si that has no citation, we feed si along with the query and the whole response into
189   GPT-4o and prompt it to determine if si is a starting sentence, transition sentence, or a summary or
190   reasoning based on the previous response content. If so, si needs no citation and directly receives a
191   citation recall of 1; otherwise, the recall is 0. The prompts are shown in Figure 7 and 8.
192
      Citation Precision. We calculate citation precision for each citation (0/1 for irrelevant/relevant
193
      citations) and average over all citations in the response. Here, a cited snippet ci,j is relevant if and
194
      only if it entails some key points of the statement si , i.e., at least partially supports si . We also
195   employ GPT-4o as the judge using the prompt in Figure 9. In contrast, Gao et al. (2023b) may
196   overlook partially supporting cases due to the limited capacity of the NLI model it uses.
197
198
      Citation F1. Citation F1 is a comprehensive metric to evaluate the citation quality of a response:
199                                          F1 = (2 · P · R)/(P + R)                                        (2)
200
      where P and R denote the citation precision and recall of the response, respectively.
201
202   Citation Length. Since the sentence-level citation allows citing snippets of different lengths, we use
203   citation length, which is the average token number of cited snippets in the response, to quantify the
204   granularity of citations. A lower average citation length indicates the response has finer-grained and
205
      more concise citations and is thus easier for users to validate. In addition, measuring average citation
      length can avoid trivial hacks for citation F1 such as citing the whole context for each statement.

      QA Instance Generation. Considering that generating the answer and citations in one pass might
      affect answer correctness, we decide to first construct long-context QA pairs and then add cita-
304

305

306   method of Bai et al. (2024), which first employs the LLM to propose a query according to the given
307   lengthy context and then requests it again to obtain the answer via vanilla long-context QA. They
308   also incorporate different task type descriptions into the prompts (Figure 11), such as summarization,
309   information extraction, and multi-hop reasoning, to guarantee the diversity of generated queries.
310

311

312   chunks for each sentence, where l = min(lmax , (k + nsent − 1)/nsent ) and nsent denotes the number
313   of sentences, so that about k chunks are retained in total. Then we feed all these chunks, which are
314   sorted according to their position in the context, along with the query and answer into the LLM,
315   and ask the LLM to segment the answer into statements and generate chunk-level citations for each
316   statement using one-shot learning. Figure 12 shows the prompt we use. Compared with generating
317   citations for each statement individually, aggregating all retrieved chunks and generating citations
318   at once can not only reduce the calls of LLM but also improve the citation recall due to the high
319   relevance between the statements.

322   ginning or end of the chunk where the sentences are incomplete. Therefore, to achieve fine-grained
323   citations, we first expand each cited chunk by concatenating it with its preceding and succeeding

      3.2   P IPELINE VALIDATION
349
350   Before large-scale data construction, we first test CoF (without query generation and final filtering)
351   on LongBench-Cite to validate its efficacy. We compare CoF with the following LQAC strategies:
352
353   • LAC-C/LAC-S: the LLM reads the entire context and generates response and chunk-
354     level/sentence-level citation in one pass.
355   • RAC-C/RAC-S: the LLM reads top-k chunks/sentences retrieved using the query and generates
356     response and chunk-level/sentence-level citation in one pass.
357   • post-LC-C/post-LC-S: the LLM first generates a response via vanilla long-context QA, then adds
358     chunk-level/sentence-level citations into the response by finding supporting evidence from the
359     whole context.
360   • post-RC-C/post-RC-S: the LLM first generates a response via vanilla long-context QA, then uses
361     the response to retrieve about k chunks/sentences from the context, and adds chunk-level/sentence-
362     level citations by finding supporting evidence from the retrieved text (similar to step 2 of CoF).
363
364   We use GLM-4 as the backbone LLM and Zhipu Embedding-2 as the retriever for all strategies and
365   set retrieval hyper-parameters lmax = 10 and k = 40. The results in Table 4 show that:
366   1. Similar to other post-hoc strategies, CoF is able to preserve the high-quality answers pro-
367   duced through vanilla long-context QA, well preventing correctness degradation. Specifically,
368   GLM-4 perfectly maintains original answer contents unchanged when adding chunk-level citations,
369   thereby achieving 100% correctness ratios. In contrast, though attaining higher citation F1, one-
370   pass methods typically generate answers with lower correctness, failing to fully leverage LLMs’
371   long-context QA capacities.
372   2. CoF achieves the highest citation F1 and relatively small citation length among post-hoc
373   methods, highlighting its ability to generate precise, fine-grained citations. Compared to post-
374   LC-C and post-LC-S, post-hoc retrieval-based methods (i.e., post-RC-C, post-RC-S and CoF) ben-
375   efit from a more focused evidence search space, typically yielding better performance. Further-
376   more, CoF’s superiority over post-RC-C indicates that the step of sentence-level citation extrac-
377   tion effectively pinpoints supporting sentences and also filters out irrelevant chunks. Though post-
      RC-S achieves an even shorter citation length than CoF (49 v.s. 89), we empirically found that

      3.3     L ONG C ITE -45 K : A L ARGE - SCALE SFT DATASET FOR LQAC
383
      After validating the efficacy of CoF, we utilize this framework to construct LongCite-45k, a large-
384
      scale SFT dataset for LQAC. Specifically, we first collect 50k documents from the pre-training
385   corpus of GLM-4, covering 9 varied domains including books, encyclopedias, academic papers,
386   codes, etc. These documents are mainly in English and Chinese and their lengths range from 256
387   to 128k tokens. We then apply CoF (using the same setting as Sec. 3.2) to generate an LQAC
388   instance for each document, resulting in 44,600 high-quality LQAC instances after the filtering
389   stage. As illustrated in Figure 2(d), the input part of each instance consists of a task instruction, a
390   long document, and a query, and the output part is an answer equipped with sentence-level citations.

      In this section, we conduct model training experiments to determine whether SFT on LongCite-45k
      can enhance LLMs’ ability for LQAC, enabling them to generate accurate responses and precise
396
      citations within a single output. We discuss the training details and experimental results as follows.
397
398
399   4.1     T RAINING D ETAILS
400
401
      We select two latest open-source base models, namely GLM-4-9B (Zeng et al., 2024) and Llama-
      3.1-8B (Vavekanand & Sam, 2024), for the training experiments. Both of the two models have been
402
      continually pre-trained on lengthy texts and support a context window of 128k tokens, thereby being
403
      suitable for SFT on LQAC data. Following Bai et al. (2024), we combine LongCite-45k with 76k
404   general SFT instances from ShareGPT (Chiang et al., 2023) to ensure the model’s general capacities.
405   We name the models after SFT as LongCite-9B (abbr. for GLM-4-9B-LongCite) and LongCite-8B
406   (abbr. for Llama-3.1-8B-LongCite).
407
      Meanwhile, to investigate whether SFT on LQAC data will influence models’ long-context QA
408
      correctness compared to standard long-context SFT (i.e., SFT on vanilla long-context QA data),
409
      we additionally train the two base models using the pure long-context QA pairs (without the task
410   instruction and citations) in LongCite-45k, and we name the trained models as LongSFT-9B (abbr.
411   for GLM-4-9B-LongSFT) and LongSFT-8B (abbr. for Llama-3.1-8B-LongSFT). When calculating
412   correctness ratios for LongCite-9B/8B, we use LongSFT-9B/8B to obtain the correctness in vanilla
413   long-context QA setting (i.e., CLQA ).
414
      All models are trained using 4 nodes with 8×H800 80G GPUs. We adopt Megatron-LM (Shoeybi
415
      et al., 2019) with context parallelism to support a maximum training sequence length of 128k tokens,
416   and use packing training with loss weighting (Bai et al., 2024) to improve training efficiency. We
417   set the batch size to 8 and the learning rate to 1e-5. We train each model for 4,000 steps, which is
418   about 2 epochs and takes 18 hours.

505   5   R ELATED W ORKS
506
507   Long-context LLMs. A mature approach for extending the context window of LLMs involves
508   continued pre-training of base LLMs on extensive long texts followed by alignment using diverse
509   long-context QA pairs (Cai et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Vavekanand & Sam, 2024). However,
510   because of the difficulty of annotations, most long-context QA data is automatically synthesized by
511   LLMs themselves (Bai et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023), which cannot strictly guarantee the faith-
512   fulness of the answers. This leads to potential hallucinations of the aligned LLMs, i.e., fabricating
513
      content not present in or consistent with the context. Therefore, users often require a way to verify
      the accuracy and reliability of the information provided by LLMs. Our work explores how to enable
514
      long-text models to produce responses with fine-grained citations, thereby enhancing the verifiabil-
515
      ity and trustworthiness of the long-context LLMs.
516
517   Question Answering with Citations. Recently, question answering with citations has been ex-
518   tensively studied in the fields of open-domain QA (Nakano et al., 2021; Bohnet et al., 2022; Gao
519
      et al., 2023a;b), and some works (Slobodkin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024) also explore fine-
      grained citations for more precise attribution. In addition, Buchmann et al. (2024) evaluates sev-
520
      eral prompt-based approaches for chunk-level citation generation in long-context QA. Nevertheless,
521
      most of these works rely on retrieval-augmented generation or complex pipelines, which are not
522   well-suited for long-context scenarios due to information loss or excessive latency. Our work, how-
523   ever, leverages long-context LLMs to generate responses and precise sentence-level citations in a
524   single pass, providing advantages in terms of response correctness, efficiency, and user friendliness.
525   Moreover, current methods for citation evaluation largely depend on NLI models that have limited
526   capacities (Honovich et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023b). In contrast, we utilize GPT-4o as a judge and
527   consider more complex scenarios, thereby achieving a higher agreement with human assessments.
528

      6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we explore enhancing LLMs’ capacity to generate fine-grained citations from lengthy
532
      contexts. We first propose LongBench-Cite, an automatic benchmark to reveal current LLMs’ lim-
533
      ited performance on long-context question answering with citations (LQAC). We then introduce
534   CoF, a novel pipeline that uses off-the-shelf LLMs to automatically generate long-context QA in-
535   stances with precise sentence-level citations, to construct LongCite-45k, a large-scale SFT dataset
536   for LQAC. Finally, we successfully train LongCite-8B and LongCite-9B with LongCite-45k, allow-
537   ing the generation of accurate responses and fine-grained citations in one pass. Extensive analyses
538   and human evaluation further verify the effectiveness of our approach. We believe that this work
539   lays a solid foundation for further research on LQAC and contributes to the development of more
      reliable and trustworthy LLMs.

      Comparison with data constructed through post-RC-S strategy. We attempt constructing LQAC
736   data by applying post-RC-S strategy, whose performance is comparable with CoF (Sec. 3.2), to add
737   citations for the QA pairs in LongCite-45k. However, as shown in Table 9, the model trained with
738   post-RC-S data achieves much worse citation F1 than LongCite-9B. We believe the main reason is
739   that post-RC-S directly recalls sentences that are not necessarily adjacent from the context, resulting
740   in many discontinuous citation numbers (such as [3][7][15]...), which makes subsequent training
741   difficult. In contrast, CoF extracts sentence-level citations from bigger chunk-level snippets and
742   uses number spans to represent citations. These methods contribute to maintaining the semantic
743   coherence of the cited information, which is advantageous for training purposes.
