011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This study addresses the challenge of efficient human activity recognition (HAR)
014            with limited training data. We propose GEAR-FEN (Generalized Activity
015
               Recognition Feature Extraction Network), a novel transfer learning (TL) method
               that transforms kinematic motion signals into a generalized feature space. GEAR-
016
               FEN potentially outperforms the state-of-the-art in scenarios with limited training
017
               data. This was demonstrated through an evaluation across 11 public HAR datasets
018            (encompassing number of activities ranging from 6 to 33 and number of samples
019            per activity ranging from 8628 to 1140258), using a deep learning model based
020            on convolutional neural networks (CNN), residual bi-directional long short-term
021            memory (ResBiLSTM), and an attention mechanism. Furthermore, we estab-
022            lished the generalizability of our method through performance comparisons on
023            an independent dataset encompassing a distinct population and diverse kinematic
024            modalities for 8 activities, and 26121 samples per activity. These findings highlight
025            the potential of our proposed approach in robust feature representation for HAR
026
               tasks with limited data sizes.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Human activity recognition (HAR) is fundamental to comprehending and interpreting behavioral
031   patterns in everyday life, allowing for a deeper insight into the intricacies of human behavior
032   Arshad et al. (2022). Recent decades have witnessed a significant evolution in HAR technology,
033   underscored by its growing importance in patient monitoring and rehabilitation, promoting safety
034   and independence for individuals across various populations (Ariza-Colpas et al., 2022; Meng et al.,
035   2020). The advent of wearable sensors, such as inertial measurement units (IMUs), has further fueled
036   this progress, especially for ambulatory and real-life scenarios Qiu et al. (2022).
037   HAR faces significant challenges due to the inherent variability of motion patterns Chen et al.
038   (2021). This variability arises from: 1) the specific activity set being classified Trabelsi et al.
039   (2022); 2) demographic factors influencing movement patterns, necessitating population-specific
040   models; and 3) variations introduced by the composition of the data collection sensory setup. These
041   factors collectively contribute to the limited generalizability and data-specificity of HAR models.
042   While deep learning architectures have revolutionized HAR by automating feature extraction and
043   superior performance, they have also exacerbated the data dependency challenge due to their inherent
044   complexity (Jain & Kanhangad, 2018; Presotto et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024). State-of-the-art (SOTA)
045
      models relying on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
      networks Xia et al. (2020), require vast amounts of labeled data for optimal performance. Bidirectional
046
      LSTM (BiLSTM) models have further refined the encoding of temporal information within activities,
047
      yet have also increased data dependency Luwe et al. (2022). Recent advancements, such as the
048   incorporation of deep reverse transformer-based attention mechanisms, address the challenge of
049   extracting both global temporal and local spatial features through enhanced feature fusion Pramanik
050   et al. (2023). The ConvTransformer model, which merges CNNs with Transformers, exemplifies
051   this trend by focusing on key features to achieve improved HAR performance Zhang et al. (2023b).
052   Consequently, these advancements have intensified the data dependency issue, presenting a significant
053   bottleneck, as collecting large datasets is resource-intensive and time-consuming, particularly for
      novel activity sets or populations.

054
      A prevalent approach to addressing data scarcity in HAR is transfer learning (TL), defined as a set of
055   methodologies leveraging knowledge acquired from a related task and domain to enhance performance
056   on the target task Dhekane & Ploetz (2024). In this study, we address the data dependency and
057   scarcity challenge by leveraging a novel generalized TL approach. To summarize, our contributions
058   are as follows:
059
060          • We introduce the generalized activity recognition feature extraction network (GEAR-FEN).
061            Trained on a collection of diverse HAR datasets, GEAR-FEN is designed to extract repre-
062
               sentative features from the kinematic time series inherent to human motion.
063          • We evaluate our proposed method, which combines GEAR-FEN for feature extraction with
064            dataset-specific feature learning networks (FLNs) for classification learning. This approach
065            demonstrably improves classification performance, especially for datasets with limited
066            samples, compared to baseline methods and SOTA.
067          • We assess our method using an independent, unseen dataset from a novel population with
068            a novel set of activities. This analysis confirms that the features learned by GEAR-FEN
069            effectively represent the underlying human motions and activities.
070
071   2    R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Within wearable HAR, TL plays a crucial role in adapting predictive models to diverse and dynamic
074   conditions. Strategic approaches, such as heterogeneous transfer, personalized TL, multi-source
075   TL, and task-specific methods, effectively address challenges like sensor position variability, user-
076   specific data adaptation, and the integration of heterogeneous datasets Dhekane & Ploetz (2024).
077   Heterogeneous transfer enhances model reliability across various sensor modalities and positions,
078   while personalized TL focuses on tailoring models to individual characteristics by fine-tuning based
079   on personal activity patterns and physiological data. Multi-source TL exploits the diversity of multiple
080   datasets to build robust models that generalize across novel environments and tasks. Additionally,
081
      adapting models to handle varying definitions and labels of activities helps accommodate significant
      discrepancies across datasets, ensuring the models’ applicability in real-world scenarios.
082
083   Recent advancements in HAR using wearable data have explored various methodologies to address
084   challenges like data scarcity, sensor diversity, and robust feature representation. One notable recent
085   study implemented multi-task self-supervised learning with a deep convolutional neural network
086   (ResNet-V2, 18 layers) on an extensive unlabeled dataset from the UK Biobank, encompassing
087
      700,000 person-days of accelerometer data, to recognize human activities Yuan et al. (2024). This
      network was pre-trained and subsequently evaluated via TL across eight public datasets to assess
088
      representation quality. However, the study encountered limitations such as the dataset’s demographic
089
      homogeneity—predominantly Caucasian data from the UK—which could hinder the generalizability
090   of the findings. Additionally, the reliance solely on accelerometer data pointed to the necessity of
091   integrating multimodal sensor data to improve the model’s robustness and applicability. Concerns
092   were also raised about the self-supervised learning methods not achieving high-quality representations
093   with free-living activity data, indicating a need for further methodological refinement and exploration
094   of more sophisticated techniques.
095
      Another study addressed the issue of labeled data scarcity in HAR by proposing a novel approach to
096   combine multiple public datasets to create a generalized model that required less labeled data for
097   effective fine-tuning on unseen domains Presotto et al. (2023). This model was trained specifically on
098   data from waist-mounted devices and evaluated for its generalization ability across various datasets.
099   The study revealed that focusing solely on waist-mounted data may not fully capture the variability
100   of real-world scenarios, where devices may be placed at different body locations. Additionally, the
101   performance variability across different datasets suggested that dataset-specific characteristics could
102   significantly impact the effectiveness of the generalized model, and the advantage of pre-training
103   might be limited when the target dataset diverges substantially from the other combined datasets.
104   An additional concern in the current literature is the prevalent use of multi-channel data for training
105   models. While this approach benefits from rich, multi-dimensional inputs, it inherently limits the
106   models’ applicability to general kinematic signals that might be captured using simpler or different
107   sensor configurations. This limitation raises questions about the scalability and flexibility of current
      HAR systems when deployed in real-world scenarios where sensor setups are not standardized and

132   Despite the progress in this area, the HAR field yet faces notable limitations. Models frequently
133   exhibit performance degradation when applied to domains that starkly differ from the training data,

449   5    D ISCUSSION
450
451   The results demonstrate that the GEAR-FEN method consistently outperforms the baseline methods
452   and often exceeds SOTA performance across majority of the investigated HAR datasets. GEAR-FEN
453   demonstrates strong generalization, particularly in more challenging datasets with limited sample size,
454   and shows greater robustness, as indicated by lower variance in f1-scores. Even in cases where it does
455   not surpass SOTA, GEAR-FEN remains competitive, highlighting its effectiveness in feature learning
456   for human motion signals. The observed improvements in test accuracy across iterations for a variety
457   of datasets, substantiate the effectiveness of our model and training approach. Importantly, the ability
458
      of our model to generalize across such a wide range of HAR datasets supports the hypothesis that
      kinematic signals e.g., linear acceleration and angular velocity and motion-related kinetic modalities
459
      such as those in pressure mats exhibit similar patterns, which GEAR-FEN can capture.
460
461   Moreover, the superior performance of the proposed models on an independent dataset indicates that
462   the feature representation learned by GEAR-FEN is highly transferable for human motion patterns.
463   This transferability has resulted in significant improvements when applied to datasets with small
464
      sample sizes, and with new populations, sensor modalities, and sensor locations, as demonstrated by
      the independent dataset’s performance.
465
466   Additionally, the results show that feature learning using multiple single-channel FENs consistently
467   outperforms feature representation from a multi-channel FEN, highlighting the structural superiority
468   of GEAR-FEN compared to SOTA transfer learning networks. It’s also important to note that GEAR-
469   FEN’s generalized single-channel structure addresses issues related to incompatible signal counts
470
      and sensor locations in transfer learning. This design enables highly customizable fine-tuning of the
      model, making GEAR-FEN adaptable to diverse datasets and sensor configurations
471

473   6    C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this paper, we demonstrated a pipeline for automated feature representation of the kinematic
476   signals related to human motion. This pipeline can be used for transferring the signals into a domain
477   of representative features, capable of distinguishing between human activities. The generalized
478   feature extraction pipeline, combined with feature fusion models, contributes to HAR research,
479   outperforming most of the benchmark models in the field.
480   Further, the proposed activity classification pipeline outperformed most of the SOTA scores for
481   different HAR datasets. It is recognized that SOTA models in HAR heavily rely on large and diverse
482   datasets, posing significant challenges in data acquisition. Our research addresses these issues by
483   focusing on generalizing across all kinematic signals. While aiming for a model that generalizes
484   across all kinematic signals, we acknowledge that some datasets show lower accuracy compared
485   to certain SOTA models. This trade-off is acceptable in our goal to create a universally adaptable
      system. It is noteworthy that the proposed GEAR-FEN pipeline can be further fine-tuned using


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the HAR datasets with more inherent similarities to the target dataset. The customized fine-tuning
487   can aid the model in capturing the local and intricate patterns and features specific to a set of
488   activities or populations. Moreover, validation of the independent dataset confirms the effectiveness
489   of GEAR-FEN’s generalization capability.
490
      Our research revealed that the feature representation pipeline exhibits superior performance compared
491
      to the benchmark deep learning models when applied to an independent dataset. This dataset
492
      encompassed a diverse population (wheelchair users) and incorporated various sensor modalities,
493   including pressure distribution. The findings suggest that the feature learning pipeline effectively
494   captures the essential characteristics of motion from the signals, potentially enhancing classification
495   performance when integrated with a classification model. The balance between broad applicability
496   and dataset-specific precision highlights the potential of our models in diverse real-world applications
497   and sets a direction for future research in HAR technology.
498
      In our study, we acknowledge a limitation of the feature learning pipeline’s applicability to kinematic
499   signals associated with human motion. Further, we acknowledge that the GEAR-FEN methodology
500   has been validated only with a specific neural network structure (comprising CNN, ResBiLSTM,
501   and attention mechanism). The pipeline should be further validated for different neural network
502   structures. In forthcoming research, we intend to also employ the Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic
503   Explanations (LIME) technique to elucidate the features extracted by the pipeline. This will help
504   assess whether the feature representation pipeline accurately captures features relevant to the target
505   output. Given the pipeline’s capability to transform any signal into the feature domain, conducting an
506   activity-wise analysis of these features will enable us to delineate the contribution of each input signal
507
      modality to the recognition of various activities. Furthermore, it should be investigated whether the
      generalized feature representation pipeline can be extended to physiological signals with repeatable
508
      patterns, such as heart rate and blood pressure.
