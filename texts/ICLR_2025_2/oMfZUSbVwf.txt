009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Parameter space symmetries, or loss-invariant transformations, are important for
012            understanding neural networks’ loss landscape, training dynamics, and generaliza-
013            tion. However, identifying the full set of these symmetries remains a challenge.
014            In this paper, we formalize data-dependent parameter symmetries and derive their
015            infinitesimal form, which enables an automated approach to discover symmetry
016            across different architectures. Our framework systematically uncovers parameter
017            symmetries, including previously unknown ones. We also prove that symmetries
018            in smaller subnetworks can extend to larger networks, allowing the discovery of
019
               symmetries in small architectures to generalize to more complex models.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Parameter space symmetry, or loss-invariant transformation of parameters, influences various as-
025   pects of deep learning theory. Continuous symmetry connects groups to their orbits, revealing im-
026   portant topological properties such as the dimension (Zhao et al., 2023b) and connectedness (Zhao
027
      et al., 2023a) of the minimum. Parameter symmetry also influences training dynamics through the
      associated conserved quantities of gradient flow (Kunin et al., 2021) and by steering stochastic gra-
028
      dient descent towards certain favored solutions (Ziyin, 2024). Additionally, symmetry provides a
029
      tool to perform optimization within a loss level set, with successful applications in accelerating opti-
030   mization (Armenta et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2022) and improving generalization (Zhao et al., 2024).
031   Other applications of parameter space symmetry include model compression (Ganev et al., 2022;
032   Sourek et al., 2021) and reducing the search space for more efficient sampling in Bayesian neural
033   networks (Wiese et al., 2023).
034
      Despite the wide range of applications, our knowledge of parameter space symmetries is limited. In
035
      particular, known symmetries often cannot account for all loss-invariant parameter transformations.
036   While several frameworks have been developed to unify known symmetries, whether the symmetries
037   in current literature are complete remains an open question. Due to the lack of a systematic approach,
038   current practice typically requires deriving symmetries from scratch for every new architecture,
039   creating barriers for wider applications that leverage parameter symmetries.
040
      In this paper, we present an automated approach to learn the symmetry groups and their group
041
      actions on the parameter space of neural networks. To define the search space, we formalize the
042   definition for data-dependent symmetries and derive an infinitesimal version, which simplifies the
043   automatic discovery architectures. Additionally, we learn the action maps directly using a neural
044   network, which allows for learning nonlinear group actions. By including data-dependent and non-
045   linear group actions, our framework is capable of capturing a broader range of symmetries than
046   previously considered.
047
      While directly searching for symmetries in modern architectures with billions of parameters is pro-
048   hibitively expensive, we show that large networks often inherit symmetries from their components
049   or subnetworks. Identifying symmetries in small networks offers an efficient approach to uncovering
050   many symmetries in larger networks. By analyzing small networks and extending their symmetries
051   to larger ones, we sidestep the complexity of handling high-dimensional parameter spaces directly.
052   This method not only reduces the computational cost of symmetry identification in large networks
053   but also provides a systematic framework for leveraging small-scale symmetries to better understand
      more complex architectures.

064   2     R ELATED W ORK
065
066   Parameter space symmetry. Parameter symmetries are loss-invariant transformations on neural
067   network parameters, often in the form of group actions. Symmetry is present in many neural net-
068   works. Known symmetries include invertible linear transformations in linear networks, rescaling
069
      in homogeneous networks (Badrinarayanan et al., 2015; Du et al., 2018), radial rescaling in radial
      neural networks (Ganev et al., 2022), and translation in softmax and scaling in batchnorm functions
070
      (Kunin et al., 2021). In tanh neural networks (Chen et al., 1993), only permutation and sign flip
071
      symmetries preserve the loss function. ReLU networks, however, possess symmetries beyond the
072   well-known rescaling (Grigsby et al., 2023). The existence and number of symmetries in most other
073   architectures remain an open question.
074
075   Data-dependent symmetry. While the above symmetries leave the loss unchanged on all data,
076   a relaxed definition, data-dependent symmetry, only requires loss invariance on a subset of data.
077   Zhao et al. (2023b) found examples of such symmetries with nontrivial data dependency, although
078   these symmetries are complicated, limited to minibatches of size one, and difficult to generalize
079   across different architectures. This motivates an automated symmetry discovery framework, which,
080   in principle, can find symmetries of arbitrary form in arbitrary architectures. The concept of a
081   symmetry dependent on data has also appeared in adjacent fields. For example, (Moskalev et al.,
082
      2023) observe that learned data invariance in neural networks is strongly conditioned on data and
      breaks under data distribution drift; Sonoda et al. (2023) define a joint group action on data and
083
      parameters as part of a new proof of universal approximation theory.
084
085
      Discovering and measuring symmetry. Various work explores learning continuous symmetries
086   by identifying generators of Lie groups (Krippendorf & Syvaeri, 2020; Moskalev et al., 2022;
087   Dehmamy et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2023b; Gabel et al., 2023), including cases with nonlinear group
088   actions (Yang et al., 2023a; Shaw et al., 2024). We build on this approach to discover data-dependent
089   group action in high-dimensional parameter spaces. While learning discrete symmetry (Zhou et al.,
090   2021; Karjol et al., 2024) and distributions of symmetry (Benton et al., 2020; Romero & Lohit,
091   2022; Urbano & Romero, 2023) are also relevant, they are not the primary focus of this paper.
092   Extracted symmetry is often evaluated locally, by measuring function changes under infinitesimal
093   symmetry transformations (Gruver et al., 2022) or by comparing tangent spaces of orbits under the
094   learned group and the true symmetry group (Portilheiro, 2023). We adopt the local invariance of loss
095   functions under symmetry transformation, similar to that defined in (Gruver et al., 2022; Moskalev
096   et al., 2022), as the minimization objective in learning data-dependent group actions.

101   derive an alternative definition using Lie algebras, which is used to construct an automated frame-

129   3.2   I NFINITESIMAL S YMMETRY
130
131   Next, we derive an infinitesimal version of parameter space symmetries. For the automatic sym-
132   metry discovery framework in Section 5, this definition allows us to learn the group elements and
133   actions without computing the matrix exponential, which is expensive, during training. Proofs and
134   additional examples can be found in Appendix A.
135   In this paper, we restrict the symmetry group G to be a linear group. That is, we assume there is a
136   faithful representation ρ : G → GL(n). The corresponding Lie algebra representation dρ : g →
137   gl(n) is the differential of ρ, mapping elements of the Lie algebra g of G to the Lie algebra gl(n)
138   of GL(n). If G is a subgroup of GL(n), then ρ is the inclusion map, and consequently, dρ is the
139   inclusion of g into gl(n).

      4     B UILDING N EW S YMMETRIES FROM K NOWN O NES
189
190
      One way to identify symmetries in a large network is by examining its components or subnetworks.
191   Despite often having billions of parameters, neural networks typically consist of a limited set of
192   functional families, such as fully connected layers, attention mechanisms, and activation functions.
193   This modular view suggests a mechanism by which symmetries in networks with fewer layers might
194   extend to those in deeper networks. Additionally, within similar types of networks, it may be possi-
195   ble to extrapolate symmetries found in narrower layers to wider ones.
196
      By focusing on symmetries in small architectures and using them to infer symmetries in larger
197   ones, we circumvent the complexity associated with direct handling of high-dimensional parame-
198   ter spaces. This approach not only simplifies the discovery of symmetries in large-scale networks
199   but also provides a systematic method for using symmetries in smaller subnetworks to understand
200   those in more extensive architectures. We formalize this approach and discuss its limitations in the
201   remainder of this section. Proofs can be found in Appendix B.
202

203   symmetries that preserve f will also preserve the original network L:

      We apply Proposition 4.1 to construct symmetries in larger networks from those in smaller ones
239   in the next two corollaries. Specifically, we show that some symmetries are preserved as networks
240   scale up through increasing the dimensionality of a layer or adding additional layers.
241
      The first corollary describes how symmetries identified in narrower networks also apply to wider
242
      networks. A function σ : Rh×k → Rh×k is row-wise if, for any matrix A ∈ Rh×k with rows
243
      {ai ∈ Rk }hi=1 , the output matrix σ(A) has rows {σrow (ai ) ∈ Rk }hi=1 , where σrow : Rk → Rk
244   applies independently on each row of A. Element-wise functions are a special case of row-wise
245   functions. For fully connected networks with row-wise activation functions, identifying a symmetry
246   in one architecture suggests that the same symmetry will apply to wider versions of that architecture.
247
      Corollary 4.2. Consider a network parameter space Θ(m, h, n) = Rm×h × Rh×n and data space
248
      D(n, k) = Rn×k . Let σ : Rh×k → Rh×k be a row-wise function. Consider a function Lmnhk :
249
      Θ(m, h, n) × D(n, k) → Rm×k , defined as Lmnhk ((U, V ), X) = U σ(V X) for U ∈ Rm×h ,
250   V ∈ Rh×n , and X ∈ Rn×k . If there is a G-symmetry of Lmnhk , then there is a G-symmetry of
251   Lmnh′ k with any h′ > h.
252
253   The next corollary shows that symmetries of a subset of layers are also symmetries in the entire
254   network.
255
      Corollary 4.3. Let Θ = Θ1 × ... × Θl be a parameter space. Consider a list of spaces V0 = Dd ,
256
      Vl = Rd , and V1 , ..., Vl−1 . Let L : Θ × Dd → Rd be a function defined recursively by {Li }li=1 with
257   Li : Θi × Vi−1 → Vi , such that L = ϕl where ϕi = Li (θi , ϕi−1 ) ∈ Vi and ϕ0 = X. If for some
258   1 ≤ i ≤ l, Li has a G-symmetry, then L has a G-symmetry.
259
260   Both corollaries can be proved by factoring the parameter space and defining corresponding func-
261   tions that compose to L, before applying Proposition 4.1. The explicit forms of h, f , and j are
262   deferred to Appendix B. Figure 1 shows the subset of parameters (Θ2 ) that the symmetry applies to

265   Note that this approach does not explore the emergence of new, more complex symmetries that
266   may arise as the neural network scale up in size. Notably, there are cases where there exists a G
267   symmetry over its input space, but group actions on individual layers are not loss-invariant (Kvinge
268   et al. (2022)). Nevertheless, studying smaller and simpler networks remains a effective strategy to
269   obtain a significant number of symmetries in larger networks, and is a first step in characterizing the
      complete set of symmetries in modern architectures.
