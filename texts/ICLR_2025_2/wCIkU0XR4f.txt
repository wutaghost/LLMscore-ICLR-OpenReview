009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            To enhance the generalization of machine learning models to unseen data, tech-
012            niques such as dropout, weight decay (L2 regularization), and noise augmentation
013            are commonly employed. While regularization methods (i.e., dropout and weight
014            decay) are geared toward adjusting model parameters to prevent overfitting, data
015            augmentation increases the diversity of the input training set, a method purported
016            to improve accuracy and calibration error. In this paper, we investigate the im-
017            pact of each of these techniques on the parameter space of neural networks, with
018
               the goal of understanding how they alter the weight landscape in transfer learning
               scenarios. To accomplish this, we employ Random Matrix Theory to analyze the
019
               eigenvalue distributions of pre-trained models, fine-tuned using these techniques
020
               but using different levels of data diversity, for the same downstream tasks. We
021            observe that diverse data influences the weight landscape in a similar fashion as
022            dropout. Additionally, we compare commonly used data augmentation methods
023            with synthetic data created by generative models. We conclude that synthetic data
024            can bring more diversity into real input data, resulting in a better performance on
025            out-of-distribution test instances.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning (ML) models excel at discovering patterns from training data, which allows them
031   to make predictions on unseen data. However, as models grow more complex, they risk a phe-
032   nomenon called overfitting – the tendency of models to capture not only meaningful patterns but
033
      also noise and random variations from the training data, resulting in poor generalization to new in-
      stances. To address this, model regularization techniques, such as dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014)
034
      and weight decay (L2 regularization)(Krogh & Hertz, 1991), are used in conjunction with Deep
035
      Neural Networks (DNNs) to enhance their generalization capability. While dropout reduces model
036   complexity by randomly deactivating neurons during training, weight decay adds a penalty to the
037   loss function based on the magnitude of model weights. These techniques shape the model’s weight
038   landscape in different ways: dropout works by randomly deactivating neurons during each train-
039   ing iteration, forcing the network to distribute learning across multiple independent pathways rather
040   than over-relying on particular features. Weight decay, on the other hand, encourages a smoother
041   and more evenly distributed weight structure by penalizing large weights, pushing the model toward
042   simpler and more generalizable solutions (Andriushchenko et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2018).
043   Beyond direct parameter adjustments, empirical regularization techniques such as adding noise to
044   input data or employing data augmentation have also proven effective for improving model gener-
045   alization (Bishop, 1995). By exposing models to greater breadths of input patterns, these methods
046   help improve their adaptability to diverse, real-world data. Despite existing evidence of their ef-
047   fectiveness, the underlying mechanisms of data augmentation remain an insufficiently studied area
048   of research. Recently, synthetic data generated by models such as stable diffusion (Rombach et al.,
049   2022) and large language models (LLMs) (Brown, 2020), has emerged as a promising, new approach
050
      to boost model performance(Sahu et al., 2023; He et al., 2022). Synthetic data not only augments
      existing datasets but also simulates rare events and edge cases (Santoso et al., 2017).
051
052   Similar to dropout, data augmentation acts as a form of regularization by introducing purposeful
053   variability into the model’s training process. Techniques such as rotation, flipping, cropping, random
      erasing, color jittering, and mixup are used to modify input image data in deep neural networks


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (DNNs), therefore increasing the diversity of the data seen during training . This broader input
055   variation helps prevent overfitting by encouraging the model to capture more generalizable features
056   and patterns. Dropout shares the goal of enhancing diversity but achieves it by randomly deactivating
057   neurons, forcing the model to explore different configurations of its weights. Weight decay, on the
058   other hand, works by penalizing large weights to simplify the model, thus controlling complexity and
059   preventing it from fitting noise in the training data. Ultimately, these regularization strategies aim
060   to enhance model generalization – either by promoting adaptability through diverse representations
061   (dropout and data augmentation) or by constraining complexity (weight decay) to avoid overfitting.
062   Given that these regularization methods all seek to improve generalization, albeit through different
063   mechanisms, a natural question arises: does data augmentation have a similar effect on the neu-
064   ral network’s weight landscape as dropout or weight decay? To investigate our hypothesis, we
065   utilize Random Matrix Theory (RMT), a statistical framework for analyzing the structure of large
066   matrices, to analyze the changes in weight matrices across different regularization approaches. The
067   weight matrix W from a specific layer can be approximated as the sum of a random noise matrix
068   Wrand and a difference matrix W∆ , so that
069
070                                            W ≈ Wrand + W∆
071
072
      After a layer has been properly trained, either through extensive training on labeled data or fine-
      tuning on a downstream task, the difference matrix W∆ provides meaningful structural patterns that
073
      represent learned features, which are distinct from the random noise components Wrand (Martin &
074
      Mahoney, 2019). Thus, examining W∆ can provide insights into the effects of data diversity on the
075   learned weight matrices (Pennington & Worah, 2017; Sagun et al., 2017), facilitating comparisons of
076   robustness among regularization approaches. Specifically, measures of weight matrices, such as the
077   empirical spectral density (ESD), reveal characteristic signals that indicate the presence of implicit
078   self-regularization during training (Martin & Mahoney, 2019).
079
      In this work, we focus on the transfer learning domain and investigate the changes in weight matri-
080
      ces between the pre-trained and fine-tuned models in response to data augmentation techniques. By
081   examining these changes, we aim to understand the underlying mechanisms by which diverse input
082   data improves model performance and generalizability. Previous studies have shown that fine-tuning
083   can significantly alter the spectral properties of weight matrices, often resulting in changes that are
084   consistent with improved feature specialization and adaptation to new tasks(Li et al., 2020). We
085   investigate the effects of real and synthetic data on the weight landscape, using RMT to show how
086   data augmentation interacts with regularization techniques such as dropout and weight decay. Addi-
087   tionally, we evaluate the impact of traditional and synthetic data augmentation on model robustness
088   across both in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks.
089   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We investigate the changes in the weight
090   space of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) resulting from varying levels of data diversity; (2) we
091   provide theoretical and empirical insights into why diverse data improves model performance, link-
092   ing these findings to changes in learned feature representations and their robustness; and (3) we
093   explore the use of synthetic data generated through generative models, evaluating its effect on both
094   in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks to determine how it complements traditional
095
      data augmentation approaches in improving model robustness and generalization. Our study offers
      a comprehensive perspective on how data diversity, both from real and synthetic sources, influences
096
      the internal dynamics of DNNs.

105   2.1.1    R EGULARIZATION
106
107   Dropout. Dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014) helps minimize overfitting during training by randomly
      disabling neurons in fully connected layers based on a given probability p. This randomness is


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      controlled by a Bernoulli distribution, where neurons are either kept or deactivated during each
109   training iteration. However, during inference, all neurons are active. To ensure consistency between
110   training and inference, the output is scaled by 1 − p during training. Dropout has become almost a
111   default method for training or fine-tuning deep neural networks. In this paper, we implement dropout
112   operations on different layers in transformer-based models.
113
      Weight Decay. Weight decay (Krogh & Hertz, 1991; Ishii & Sato, 2018) is another commonly
114
      used regularization technique to prevent overfitting. It works by adding a small penalty, which is
115   proportional to the size of weight parameters, to the loss function during training. This encourages
116   the model to keep weights smaller. During training, the weights are updated not only based on
117   the prediction error-induced losses but also on this penalty, which pushes the model to find simpler
118   solutions.
119
      Data Augmentation. Data augmentation (Rebuffi et al., 2021; Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019) is a
120
      strategy to artificially expand the size and diversity of training datasets without requiring the acqui-
121   sition of new data. This technique involves applying a range of transformations to existing samples,
122   such as flipping, cropping, adjusting brightness, and adding noise. Mixup (Zhang, 2017) is a spe-
123   cific data augmentation technique that creates new training examples by linearly combining pairs
124   of input data and their corresponding labels. By introducing these variations, data augmentation
125   enables models to learn more robustly from a wider array of instances, improving generalization.
126   This approach is particularly advantageous in domains related to image tasks.
127
128   2.1.2   D IVERSITY M EASURE
129
      Vendi Score (Dan Friedman & Dieng, 2023) has been recently proposed for measuring diversity in
130
      ML data. This score quantifies data diversity based on the similarities between elements in a dataset,
131   making it particularly useful for evaluating the effectiveness of data augmentation techniques and
132   assessing the diversity of synthetic samples from generative models. The score is derived by using
133   a set of samples along with a pairwise similarity function, which calculates a value that reflects the
134   effective count of unique elements in the dataset. More specifically, given a positive semi-definite
135   matrix K ∈ Rn×n representing similarity scores, the Vendi Score (VS) is defined as
136                                                                        n

137                     V S(K) = exp −tr           log        = exp −          λi log λi ,

139   where λi are the eigenvalues of the matrix K/n, with the definition of 0 log 0 = 0. In essence, VS is
140   the exponential of the von Neumann entropy of the scaled similarity matrix, K/n, equivalent to the
141   Shannon entropy of this matrix’s eigenvalues, also known as effective ranks. The higher the Vendi
142   Score, the more diverse the dataset is. For evaluating images, Vendi Score provides two correlation

145   measure to evaluate different datasets. The default embedding model of VS is INCEPTION V3,
146   which we replace with CLIP in this paper.

151   label diversity can be captured by input similarities. We also observe that pixel-wise diversity scores
152   do not always match embedding-wise scores after applying data augmentation. Similarly, we will
153
      see later that synthetic data itself is less diverse than real data but when it is combined with real data,
      the combined dataset can often reach a higher diversity score.

      Let p(x) denote the correlation matrix’s Empirical Spectral Density (ESD). According to RMT, the
      ESD of a Gaussian-noise random weight matrix follows the Marchenko-Pastur (MP) distribution
182
      when the matrix dimension grows large. The MP distribution often exhibits a bulky shape. However,
183
      the empirical study of Martin and Mahoney (Martin & Mahoney, 2021) showed that, for a well-
184   trained DNN model, specifically for a pre-trained large model, the ESD of its weight correlation
185   matrix is heavy-tailed, not following the Gaussian noise-based MP distribution. They called it the
186   Heavy-Tailed Self Regularization theory. Specifically, a Power Law (PL) distribution can be used to
187   fit the heavy tail, which is given by
188                                      p(x) ∝ x−α , xmin < x < xmax .                                   (1)
189
      Here, x takes values in the interval (xmin , xmax ), and xmax is chosen to be the maximum eigenvalue
190   of the empirical correlation matrix, while xmin is selected to obtain a better power-law fitting, which
191   is not generally equal to the minimum eigenvalue.
192
193
      The discussion above justifies the decomposition of weight matrix W into two components – a
      random matrix Wrand and a difference matrix W∆ , which are represented by the bulk and tail of
194
      ESD, respectively, based on Martin and Mahoney’s theory. Analyzing properties of both Wrand
195
      and W∆ can obtain insights into how models are trained. Figure 2 shows the λmin are selected to
196   divide the ESD into bulk (Wrand ) and tail (W∆ ) parts. The exponent α of a fitted PL distribution
197   characterizes the tail behavior of the ESD, which is in close relevance to the model’s generalization
198   capability. A smaller α represents a more “heavy-tailed” landscape in ESD. More large eigenvalues
199   in the tail of ESD will exert more influence on the model’s weight spaces.

408   3.4   D IVERSITY OF S YNTHETIC DATA

410
      To investigate whether or not synthetic data can bring in more input data diversity and thus further
411
      enhance model performance in the transfer learning context, we utilize the state-of-the-art image
412
      generative model Stable Diffusion v1-5 (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022) to generate synthetic images.
413   We use Domainnet (Peng et al., 2019) datasets to evaluate the CLIP model performance on both
414   in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks. Specifically, for ID tasks, we use 50000
415   instances from the “real” category (i.e., they are real images, as opposed to artificially stylized
416   images), which covers 142 image labels. The baseline model is trained on these instances and
417   evaluated on “real” test images. Data augmentation techniques mentioned in section 3.3 are applied
418   to the “real” training data for model training as a comparison. For OOD tasks, we evaluate trained
419   models on stylized images, including “clippart”, “infograph”, “inpainting” and “sketch” styles in
420   DomainNet. All model parameters remain the same as those in previous sections.
421   To improve the diversity of SD-generated images, we increase the variance of prompts for the stable
422   diffusion model. We provide four prompt formats that are randomly picked for individual image-
423   generating processes. Moreover, we dynamically adjust the generated images by randomly selecting
424   and applying compressed resolutions within the ranges of 320-640 pixels for the first dimension and
425   240-720 pixels for the second dimension.
426   We use Top-1 accuracy and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) as evaluation metrics for both ID
427   and OOD tasks. ECE is widely used for model uncertainty evaluation. It quantifies how well a
428   model’s predicted probabilities (confidence) align with its actual outcomes (accuracy) (Guo et al.,

      4      R ELATED W ORK
475
476   Data diversity and applications of synthetic data have been getting more and more attention from the
477
      machine learning research community in recent years. Authors in the paper (Lopes et al., 2020) ex-
      plore the role of diversity in data augmentation and propose two diversity definitions. One definition
478
      is model-based, or based on the performance of classification, and the other definition is input im-
479
      age data-based, by comparing the conditional entropy of clean data distribution and the augmented
480   data distribution. They conclude that the diversity produced by data augmentation is able to im-
481   prove model performance. However, it falls short of explaining the underlying mechanisms of how
482   data diversity affects the ML model. The study from He et al. (2022) evaluates the use of synthetic
483   data, which are generated by test-to-image models such as GLIDE, for image recognition tasks. It
484   concludes that models trained solely on synthetic data do not outperform those trained on real data.
485   Following this work, we try to explain why using synthetic data alone to train a model cannot beat
      what real data does. Similarly, model collapses have been observed by the authors of Alemoham-


                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      mad et al. (2023). They state that the self-consuming loop – that is, iteratively using synthetic data
487   from generative models as their own training data – can significantly harm the quality of generated
488   output. They suggest adding fixed real data to break the loop and to improve the quality of generated
489   images. In our experiments, we only use synthetic data to replace a small portion of real data so as
490   to increase overall data diversity and improve model performance on both ID and OOD tasks.
491
      The spectral analysis of weight metrics has been applied to various scenarios. Martin & Mahoney
492
      (2021) found deep neural networks naturally regularize themselves by shaping their weight matrices,
493   for which they proposed a Heavy-Tailed Self-Regularization (HTSR) theory. Their follow-up work
494   (Martin et al., 2021) used the power-law fitting on weight matrices’ spectra to predict a model’s
495   quality and generalization ability without needing training or testing data, thus purely focusing on
496   the intrinsic properties of the model’s weights. Yang et al. (2023) applied the HTSR theory in the
497   natural language processing area. They proposed a KS distance-based estimation method to make
498   a better power law fitting, making the exponent α better capture the shape of the tail distribution
499   of ESD. On the other hand, the significance of weight matrices has been explored in other recent
500   studies. Wortsman et al. (2022) demonstrated that merging weights from different fine-tuning mod-
501
      els can improve overall model performance and highlighted the impact of weight manipulation on
      optimizing model behavior without retraining. All of these prior studies motivated us to investigate
502
      the impact of data diversity from the model’s weight matrices perspective.

      5   D ISCUSSION
506
507
508
      Model generalizability is an open challenge for deploying machine learning and deep learning mod-
      els, as it directly impacts their adaptability and reliability in real-world applications. Regularization
509
      techniques, such as dropout and weight decay, are typically used to reduce overfitting and improve
510
      model robustness. Data augmentation, an empirical method that increases the diversity of training
511   data, particularly in situations where training data is scarce, could achieve similar effects. However,
512   the underlying mechanisms of how and why data augmentation enhances model performance re-
513   main an open question in theory and practice. With advancements in generative AI, the concept of
514   data diversity has received significant attention with respect to both training data and model outputs.
515   Diverse training data enables large pre-trained models to learn a wide range of meaningful features
516   without overfitting to noise, while ensuring diversity in generated outputs is crucial for their use as
517   effective training data in downstream tasks. This motivates our investigation into the role of data
518   diversity in the learning process of a model. Specifically, we aim to understand how data diversity
519
      influences the weight landscape and provide insights into why data augmentation can lead to more
      robust models. To achieve this, we used Random Matrix Theory (RMT) to analyze spectral patterns
520
      in the weight matrices of neural networks, comparing the effects of dropout, weight decay, and var-
521
      ious data augmentation techniques. Additionally, we quantified the diversity of training sets using
522   the Vendi score, which measures data variability and helps assess how training diversity contributes
523   to model generalization in our framework.
524
525
      We observed that dropout and data augmentation exhibit similarities in how they affect the weight
      space of neural networks. Both methods reduce the overall magnitude of the weight matrix and
526
      induce similar changes in the spectral metrics of the empirical spectral density (ESD) on the same
527
      dataset. This suggests that they influence weight formation in a comparable way: dropout forces the
528   model to be less reliant on specific weight correlations, resulting in a less concentrated ESD, while
529   the rich features in a diverse dataset encourage the model to focus on a broader range of information
530   during training, leading to a more balanced weight landscape. In contrast, weight decay, another
531   regularization method, also reduces the overall size of the weight matrix but impacts the shape of
532   the ESD differently, leading to distinct regularization effects.
533
      We also explored the use of synthetic data generated by generative models as an augmentation strat-
534   egy to improve model performance. While previous studies have not thoroughly examined its effec-
535   tiveness, we compared this new approach with traditional data augmentation in both in-distribution
536   (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) tasks. Our results show that incorporating a small amount of
537   synthetic data enhances average model performance on both ID and OOD tasks, primarily due to
538   the increased diversity provided by the synthetic data. However, there is also a risk of model col-
539   lapse when relying too heavily on synthetic data, which occurs when the model starts overfitting
      to synthetic patterns that lack the variability and quality of real-world data. This over-reliance can


                                                         10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      reduce generalizability, cause biased learning, and diminish the model’s ability to recognize a broad
541   range of real-world features, ultimately making the model less robust and effective in practical ap-
542   plications. Therefore, a careful balance between real and synthetic data is still necessary to prevent
543   model collapse and prevent overfitting.
