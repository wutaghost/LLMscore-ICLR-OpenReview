009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods facilitate the cost-effective
012           adaptation of pretrained language models to specific tasks and domains. These
013           methods have enabled the open-source community to develop thousands of spe-
014           cialized models tailored to various domains and tasks. Collaborative Fine-Tuning
015           (CoFT) is the paradigm that seeks to merge these specialized models into a single
016           model – often a routed Mixture-of-Expert (MoE) model – to achieve better gener-
017           alization across domains and tasks. However, current CoFT models require a post-
018
              merge fine-tuning stage to successfully combine existing models, making CoFT
              approaches inaccessible to users who lack fine-tuning expertise. In this work,
019
              we introduce Seeded LoRA, a novel CoFT approach that does not require post-
020
              merge fine-tuning thus enabling plug-and-play PEFT adapter merging. Seeded
021           LoRA significantly outperforms LoRA and MoE LoRA (MoLoRA) approaches,
022           improving by an average of 7 percentage points across a battery of 16 zero-shot
023           tasks and we find that the main benefit from Seeded LoRA comes from miti-
024           gating task interference during finetuning. Seeded LoRA works by initializing a
025           model before fine-tuning using a generic seed expert low-rank adapter which was
026           finetuned on a small random subset of the finetuning data such that subsequent
027           fine-tuning runs are initialized in the same optimization subspace. This process
028           enables the integration of any combination of independently fine-tuned models
029
              through simple averaging of expert adapter outputs. We show that averaging, or
              routing with assigning equal probability weights to each expert, is equivalent to
030
              grouped convolution, explaining its effectiveness. Additionally, we study subtle
031
              routing failures in post-merge fine-tuning and highlight that Seeded LoRA can al-
032           leviate most routing failures, making it a suitable base method for future routed
033           CoFT approaches.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Fine-tuning pretrained Large Language Models (LLMs) to follow the instructions of a user(Wei
039   et al., 2022) – also known as post-training – is a key step in developing interactive chatbots.
040   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)(Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Li & Liang, 2021; Zadouri
041   et al., 2023) methods like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)(Hu et al., 2021) have enabled the creation
042   of numerous domain-specific models(Wolf et al., 2019; Mangrulkar et al., 2022). However, adding
043   a capability to augment an existing model, for example, adding code generation to a model trained
044
      on mostly English data, traditionally requires re-training with new data mixes, which incurs high
      computational costs and requires domain-specific expertise for dataset generation and fine-tuning.
045
046   Collaborative fine-tuning (CoFT) aims to extend the capabilities of an existing model by merging
047   it with other existing fine-tuned models, thus reusing the expertise and computational resources
048   that went into creating already existing models. However, current CoFT strategies often necessitate
049   post-merge fine-tuning to enable successful use of existing PEFT models (Muqeeth et al., 2024; Dou
050
      et al., 2024; Zadouri et al., 2023).
051   In this paper, we introduce Seeded LoRA, a CoFT approach that does not require post-merge fine-
052   tuning. Seeded LoRA works by initializing a model before fine-tuning by using a generic seed
053   expert low-rank adapter (LoRA), which was on a random subset of the finetuning data such that
      subsequent fine-tuning runs are initialized in the same optimization subspace. While this work

      Collaborative Fine-Tuning (CoFT) Traditional fine-tuning of a LLM often results in a model
109   with static capabilities, where introducing new functionalities, such as mathematical problem-
110   solving, might erase previously learned skills due to catastrophic forgetting (Goodfellow et al.,
111   2013). Typically, enhancing a model’s capabilities involves retraining it from scratch with a com-
112   prehensive dataset encompassing both old and new domains – a process that is not only compu-
113   tationally intensive but also demands access to all previous data and domain-specific fine-tuning
114   expertise. Collaborative Fine-Tuning (CoFT) addresses these limitations and extends the capabili-
115   ties of existing model without necessitating retraining. For instance, incorporating math skills into a
116   model could be achieved by merging it with another model specifically fine-tuned for mathematics.
117
      Among various integration methods, the most common involves deploying a router to manage the
      interaction between these specialized models (Muqeeth et al., 2024).
118
119
120
      CoFT vs Federated Learning In federated learning (Lim et al., 2020), individual models are
      locally trained on edge devices and then the local model parameters are merged on a central server.
121
      This allows the training on private data on the edge device while preserving the privacy of edge
122
      device users by exchanging parameters but not their user data. While CoFT is similar to federated
123   learning in that parameters are merged into a final model it has some core differences. In CoFT,
124   privacy is not a major consideration while final performance of the merged model is critical; CoFT
125   usually allows only for a single exchange of model parameters and not successive updates; large
126   models are used that cannot be executed on edge devices; large models also introduce new challenges
127   that do not exist at the small scale (Dettmers et al., 2022). CoFT is mainly useful for communities
128   of independent developers that do not have the resources of large organizations and which might act
129   independently with few resources. In summary, while the technical problems are similar, federated
130   learning approaches are usually not suitable CoFT solutions and vice versa due to different emphasis
131
      of model scale, single step merging, and the importance of final model performance.

145   Optimization Landscape: Dynamics and Initialization A key result we build on is that neural
146   networks that are being trained from a random parameter initialization quickly settle into an opti-
147   mization subspace characterized by the principle eigenvalues of the Hessian (Ghorbani et al., 2019;
148   Gur-Ari et al., 2018; Frankle et al., 2019). Once this subspace is entered the principal optimiza-
149   tion directions remain largely fixed for the rest of the training (Frankle et al., 2019; Ghorbani et al.,
150   2019; Gur-Ari et al., 2018) and exhibit linear mode connectivity(Frankle et al., 2020). This means
151   two neural networks with different random initialization may have different optimization subspaces,
152   but if two neural networks are trained from an initialization that has settled into an optimization sub-
153
      space, the two neural networks will remain in that subspace even if trained on different data (Frankle
      et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022; Gururangan et al., 2023). If two neural networks exhibit linear mode
154
      connectivity, they can be merged by a simple or weighted average (Li et al., 2022; Gururangan et al.,
155
      2023; Wortsman et al., 2022; Ilharco et al., 2022). Our main contribution is to exploit this property
156   to enable CoFT that does not require any post-merge fine-tuning.

354   The next paragraphs will discuss subtle routing failures that we observed in these experiments.
355   Failure I: Uniform specialization with non-uniform routing A subtle pattern of expert special-
356   ization failure occurs if for a particular evaluation task only a few experts are activated (non-uniform
357   routing probabilities), but that the distribution of experts remains fixed for other tasks. This indi-
358   cates that all tasks are learned across all experts with one particular weighted average. This pattern
359   is depicted in Figure 3.
360   Failure II: Non-uniform routing probability that is unrelated to expert effectiveness If a router
361   and experts are trained successfully, then adding the top-k experts in order of their routing probabil-
362   ity should increase the performance on the end task in a non-linear manner. A non-linear increase
363   indicates, that routing probability p is proportional to the expert effectiveness for that task. A linear
364   increases indicate, while the router assigns a higher p to some experts, all experts are interchange-
365   able and provide similar performance despite different routing probabilities. This is essentially,
366   non-specialization combined with an uncalibrated router that emits non-uniform routing probabili-
367   ties. See Figure 4 for a failure case that is contrasted with successful specialization.
368   Failure III: Uniform routing While uniform routing where each expert has the same probability p
369   often yields better performance with more experts (Jiang et al., 2024; Muennighoff et al., 2024), we
370   show in Section 7 that this routing pattern is equivalent to grouped convolution and processing. As
371   such, despite its improved performance, uniform routing represents a routing failure since perfor-
372   mance of the model is the same with and without routing. Muennighoff et al. (2024) discusses this
373   failure mode as evident when analyzing Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024).

377   to improve the performance of LoRA MoE approaches, but Seeded LoRA is a strong baseline that

      Mixture-of-Adapters (MoA) methods MoLoRA (Zadouri et al., 2023) integrating Low-Rank
442
      Adapters as experts, updates only a small portion of parameters, efficiently enhancing performance
443
      across various tasks. Similarly, SiRA (Zhu et al., 2023) adopts a Sparse MoE strategy, implementing
444   a top-k expert routing with limits on token processing and an expert dropout to combat overfitting,
445   aiming for computational efficiency. PHATGOOSE (Muqeeth et al., 2024) facilitates zero-shot gen-
446   eralization by routing among language model experts and employing a sigmoid gate for efficient
447   top-k inference routing. Lastly, LoRAMoE (Dou et al., 2024) safeguards world knowledge within
448   LLMs during fine-tuning by freezing the main model and fine-tuning select LoRAs, thus bolstering
449   downstream task performance while preserving the original knowledge base.
450
451   Branch-Train-Merge (BTM) Approaches BTM (Li et al., 2022) is a communication-efficient
452   algorithm designed for the parallel training of large language models (LLMs). It facilitates the inde-
453   pendent training of model subparts, termed Expert Language Models (ELMs), across different data
454
      subsets. ELMs form the ELMFOREST and can be dynamically modulated or integrated through en-
      sembling or parameter averaging. Cluster-Branch-Train-Merge (c-BTM) (Gururangan et al., 2023)
455
      extends BTM by incorporating unsupervised domain discovery, enabling domain-specific training
456
      and forming a sparse ensemble for efficient inference. Branch-Train-MiX (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024)
457   further advances this paradigm by mixing trained domain-specific experts into an MoE model, yield-
458   ing an efficient LLM with enhanced accuracy-efficiency trade-offs.

      9       L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
462
463
      Despite Seeded LoRA’s demonstrated efficacy in enhancing the zero-shot performance of LLMs
      across a variety of tasks while adding chatbot capabilities to pretrained models, there are inherent
464
      limitations that require further exploration:
465
466   Past models are unseeded. While future models can be initialized via Seeded LoRA currently
467   available models are not seeded and as such not initialized in the same optimization subspace.
468   Inherent limits of averaging. While a simple average of expert outputs works in Seeded LoRA,
469   this has inherent limits as ineffective experts add more and more noise decreasing the signal to noise
470   ratio. As such, when too many experts are merged more advanced weighted averaging techniques
471   will become necessary.
472
      Scalable Expert Management. To address scalability, further research could look on developing
473   efficient algorithms for expert selection and routing that minimize computational overhead. Tech-
474   niques such as sparse expert selection, where only a subset of the most relevant experts are activated
475   for a given input, could improve Seeded LoRA’s performance.
476
      Number of Experts. Determining an optimal number of experts for a given task or dataset re-
477
      mains an open question. Techniques for dynamically adjusting the number of experts based on task
478
      complexity or data characteristics could be beneficial.
