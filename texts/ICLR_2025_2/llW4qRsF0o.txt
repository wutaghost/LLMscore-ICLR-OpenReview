                                                A BSTRACT
013
014
015
              The development of theoretical sciences traditionally adheres to an observation-
              assumption-model paradigm, which is effective in simple systems but challenged
016
              by the ‘curse of complexity’ in modern engineering sciences. Advancements in
017
              artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) offer a data-driven alter-
018           native, capable of interpolating and extrapolating scientific inference where di-
019           rect solutions are intractable. Moreover, feature engineering in ML resembles
020           dimensional analysis in classical physics, suggesting that data-driven ML meth-
021           ods could potentially extract new physics behind complex data. Here we propose
022           a physics-transfer (PT) learning framework to learn physics across digital models
023           of varying fidelities and complexities, which addresses the accuracy-performance
024           dilemma in understanding representative multiscale problems. The capability of
025           our approach is showcased through screening metallic alloys by their strengths
026
              and predicting the morphological development of brains. The physics of crys-
              tal plasticity is learned from low-fidelity molecular dynamics simulation and the
027
              model is then fed by material parameters from high-fidelity, electronic structures
028
              level, density functional theory calculations, offering chemically accurate strength
029           predictions with several orders lower computational costs. The physics of bifur-
030           cation in the evolution of brain morphologies is learned from simple sphere and
031           ellipsoid models and then applied to predict the morphological development of
032           human brains, showing excellent agreement with longitudinal magnetic resonance
033           imaging (MRI) data. The learned latent variables are shown to be highly relevant
034           to uncovered physical descriptors, explaining the effectiveness of the PT frame-
035           work, which holds great potential in closing the gaps in understanding complexity
036           problems in engineering sciences.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   The development of theoretical frameworks in engineering sciences has traditionally adhered to an
042   observation-assumption-model paradigm, exemplified by Galileo’s studies on beam bending to the
043   formulation of dislocation theory in the mechanical behaviors of materials. This method is particu-
044   larly effective in problems with a low-dimensional parameter space, where the complexity can often
045
      be captured by analytical models. However, as we expand into the multiscale understanding of mat-
      ter, the ‘curse of complexity’ emerges, making it increasingly challenging to capture the intricate
046
      physics with purely analytical methods (Fish et al., 2021). For instance, material strength is gov-
047
      erned by phenomena across multiple length and time scales. Even for single crystals, dislocations
048   can be nucleated under mechanical loads, evolving cooperatively into complex networks (Oh et al.,
049   2009). Brain development involves gene expression, cellular behaviors, and mechanical instabilities
050   across various spatiotemporal scales, as reflected in the evolving morphologies (Llinares-Benadero
051   & Borrell, 2019a). First-principles theories offer high accuracy but are challenging to scale. Em-
052   pirical models, while highly efficient, are constrained by the limitations of their assumptions and
053   uncertainties in parameterization. This is the accuracy-performance dilemma in modeling complex-
      ities of multiscale physics in engineering sciences.

054
      Recent advancements in machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) present a promising,
055   data-driven alternative (Fig. 1a). This emerging approach, while still constrained by the density
056   and coverage of data, offers an increasingly powerful tool as data quality and quantity improve. The
057   ability of ML models to interpolate and extrapolate improves accordingly, suggesting that these tools
058   can complement traditional theories where direct solutions become impractical or intractable (Zhang
059   et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022). Moreover, the process of feature engineering in ML bears a resemblance
060   to dimensional analysis in classical physics, offering a systematic way to uncover and utilize internal
061   correlations within complex data (Xu et al., 2022b). This parallel suggests that ML, through its
062   data-driven methods, could potentially extract and transfer physical insights across digital models
063
      of varying fidelity and complexity.
064   Inspired by these thoughts, we propose a physics-transfer (PT) framework to learn physics across
065   digital models of varying fidelities and complexities (Fig. 1b). The learned physics is used for
066   scientific inference with high accuracy and performance to address the dilemma in modeling the
067   complexity. Two representative cases are chosen to demonstrate the capabilities of PT framework
068   including materials strength screening and predicting the development of brain morphologies, which
069
      encompass inorganic matter and organs and involve multiscale physics (Fig. 1c). The physics of
      crystal plasticity is initially learned through low-fidelity molecular dynamics simulations, and these
070
      insights are subsequently utilized in high-fidelity density functional theory computations of material
071
      parameters, enabling chemically accurate strength predictions. The physics of bifurcation in brain
072   morphologies is initially learned using spherical models with simple geometries and then applied to
073   predict the evolutional behaviors of human brains. The proposed framework holds great potential
074   for enhancing our comprehension of complexity problems in engineering sciences and bridging gaps
075   between understandings from modeling and experimental data.

138   3.1   P HYSICS - TRANSFER L EARNING FOR M ATERIALS S TRENGTH S CREENING
139
140   The strength of materials, like many problems in the natural sciences, spans multiple length and time
141   scales, and the solution has to balance accuracy and performance. In the crystal plasticity (CP) the-
142   ory, plastic flow and hardening behaviors during material deformation are modeled in a multiscale
143   framework bridging the atomic-scale lattice dynamics and continuum-level stress/strain fields (Rot-
144   ers et al., 2011). One of the key material parameters in CP models is the critical resolved shear stress
145
      (CRSS), τc , which determines the activation of specific slip systems. In CP models, CRSS is a phe-
      nomenological parameter often obtained by fitting experimental results (Salem et al., 2005; Gong
146
      et al., 2015). Alternatively, the Peierls stress (τP ) defined as the minimum shear stress required to
147
      move a single dislocation of unit length in a perfect crystal in the absence of thermal activation is
148   also used in the literature for CRSS (Shimanek et al., 2022). The Peierls stress can be obtained from
149   full-atom MD simulations. However, the strain inhomogeneity induced by a dislocation usually
150   spans 10 − 20 nm, which cannot be directly calculated from first-principles calculations. Previous
151   studies are thus limited by the use of empirical force fields (Soleymani et al., 2014). In practice,
152   the Peierls-Nabarro (PN) model offers a simplified and approximating approach to derive the Peierls
153   stress with the assumptions of sinusoidal interfacial restoring stress and a rigidly shifting dislocation,
154   where the structure of a dislocation core is determined by minimizing the elastic energies and lattice
155   misfit (Nabarro, 1947). The success of the PN model suggests that the Peierls stress is controlled by
156
      the elastic responses of the crystals and the energy landscape of interfacial slips (Bulatov & Kaxiras,
      1997; Nabarro, 1997; Lu et al., 2000; Rodney et al., 2017).
157
158   By assuming the existence of such correspondence, we use the PT learning framework to predict the
159   Peierls stress for a wide spectrum of metallic alloys and inorganic crystals at the first-principles level
160   (Fig. 2a). The maps between the Peierls stress (O) and characteristic materials parameters (x) are
161   trained from empirical or machine-learning force-field (MLFFs) MD simulations with a designed
                                                                                                           ′
      neural network (h ∈ H) and obtain the posterior probability of the model parameters (p(θ|DLF )).

162
      Then the well-trained models are extrapolated to DFT-calculated parameters to make predictions
163   at the chemically accurate level. This mapping transfers the physics from low-fidelity but efficient
164   force field models to the first-principles methods, successfully resolving the accuracy-performance
165   dilemma.

      3.1.1   DATASETS
217
218   To construct the digital libraries, a wide spectrum of metals with crystalline structures of fcc (Cu, Ni,
219   Al, Au, Pd, Pt), bcc (Fe, Mo, Ta, W), and hcp (Ti, Mg, Zr, Co) is explored. The elastic constants, γ
220   surfaces, and the Peierls stress are calculated using empirical force fields such as EAM and modified
221   EAM (MEAM) with parameters reported from different sources (Becker et al., 2013; Hale et al.,
222   2018), as well as the lattice mismatch energy and slip resistance. The primary slip systems of
223
      fcc ({111}⟨110⟩) and bcc ({110}⟨111⟩), and the prismatic slip systems of hcp ({1010}⟨1120⟩) are
      considered. Finally, the digital libraries composed of characteristic materials parameters and the
224
      Peierls stress are established to learn the physics of crystal plasticity.
225
226
      3.1.2   A RCHITECTURE AND M ODEL S ETUP
227
228   To effectively learn the physical mapping between elastic constants, γ surfaces, and the Peierls
229   stress, we employ a convolutional neural network (CNN) to extract features from the γ surfaces (He
230   et al., 2016), an feedforward neural network (FNN) to extract the features related to elastic properties
231   and merge them in the latent features space. Then, we use another FNN to predict the Peierls stress.
232   The FNN for extracting elastic properties contains two layers with neuron numbers of 6 (number of
233
      elastic features), and 32 (dimension of extracted features), respectively. Following that, the FNN for
      predicting the Peierls stress has 3 layers with neuron numbers of 64, 32, and 1, respectively. We use
234
      the stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimizer with learning rates of 10−4 (Hardt et al., 2016).
235
236
      3.1.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS
237
238   Recent progress in computational hardware and software promotes the development of MLFFs,
239   which harness neural networks to model the potential energy surfaces (PES) with the precision of
240   the training set, mostly from first-principles calculations (Ko & Ong, 2023; Hedman et al., 2023;
241   Gong et al., 2023). MLFFs learn the dependence of the potential energy of a system on the atomic
242   positions. The size effects in direct DFT calculations can be mitigated if this mapping accommodates
243   all atomic environments encountered in the MLFF simulations, and the locality holds well (Zhang
244
      et al., 2018). The Peierls stress predicted by the MLFFs thus serves as a benchmark to validate the
      PT predictions. However, the accuracy of the state-of-the-art MLFF predictions for non-equilibrium
245
      structures such as those containing dislocations is usually limited in comparison with the equilib-
246
      rium features (Takamoto et al., 2022), and the MD simulations to predict the Peierls stress using
247   MLFFs still need careful design of the models and simulation parameters to mitigate the effects
248   of sample sizes, loading geometries, and kinetics (Morrow et al., 2023). In addition, MLFFs have
249   higher computational costs than common empirical force fields such as EAM and MEAM. A direct
250   mapping between the DFT-derived γ surface and the Peierls stress can thus have an advantage in
251   facilitating fast material screening, especially for vast material space.

255
      To assess the accuracy of the PT predictions, we first calculate the Peierls stress directly by utilizing
      different methods of calculations, including EAM, MLFFs, DFT, PT models trained on EAM data
256
      (PT-EAM), and PT models trained on MLFFs data (PT-MLFFs) for small systems (annotated as ‘S’,
257
      containing 244 atoms in a 3.48 nm×0.41 nm×1.90 nm supercell) of the fcc system that suffer from
258   strong size effects in predicting the plasticity of bulk materials. The results in Fig. 2b indicate that
259   the well-trained neural networks effectively learn the physical mapping between the Peierls stress
260   and the characteristic elastic and surface parameters. The PT-EAM predictions are quantitatively
261   close (< 48.91%) to those from DFT and MLFF calculations. In comparison, those obtained from
262   EAM models show a significant deviation by 221.27% from the DFT predictions (Fig. 2c for Cu
263   {111}⟨110⟩). The time cost of statistical inference in the PT approach is within several milliseconds
264   on an Intel(R) Core(TM) i5-8250U computer with 4 cores), which is significantly lower than that of
265   simulations based on DFT, MLFFs, and EAM (Fig. 2d). These results obtained for small systems
266
      successfully demonstrate advantages in the accuracy and efficiency of the PT approach to predict
      the Peierls stress.

346   3.2     P HYSICS - TRANSFER L EARNING FOR THE P REDICTION OF B RAIN M ORPHOLOGY
347
              D EVELOPMENT
348
      Brain development involves complex multiscale physical processes, encompassing gene expression,
349   protein folding, and cellular behaviors such as cell division, differentiation, and migration, as well
350   as macroscopic morphological instabilities (Llinares-Benadero & Borrell, 2019a). The continuum
351   mechanics theory that incorporates growth tensor parameters is widely used to describe the morpho-
352   logical evolution of tissue growth (Tallinen et al., 2016; Striedter et al., 2015; Darayi et al., 2022;
353   Budday & Steinmann, 2018; da Costa Campos et al., 2021; Alenyà et al., 2022). These growth
354   tensor parameters can be linked to micro-scale cellular behaviors, providing a multiscale model-
355   ing framework for modeling morphological instabilities. The intricate geometry of the brain and
356   the nonlinear nature of brain morphological development involving materials and contact result in
357
      low computational efficiency and poor convergence in finite element analysis (FEA) (Tallinen et al.,
      2016). Consequently, there is limited work directly simulating the morphological development of
358
      the brain, with most studies discussing that on simplified geometries, such as two-dimensional shell-
359
      substrates geometries, or three-dimensional spheres and ellipsoids (Fig. 4a) (Darayi et al., 2022;
360   Budday & Steinmann, 2018; da Costa Campos et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021). Indeed, the growth
361   of spheres or ellipsoids shares similar spatiotemporal characteristics with brain morphological de-
362   velopment, such as ridge-valley networks and bifurcation behaviors. By designing neural network
363   architecture (h ∈ H), one can learn the physics of bifurcation and morphological features from sim-
                                                                                         ′
364   ple geometries. The well-trained models with parameter distributions of (p(θ|DLF )) can be directly
365   extrapolated to predict the morphological development of the high-complexity brain.
366

      3.2.3   R ESULTS
433
434   We train our models on spherical and ellipsoidal data, which are then applied to the morphological
435   development of human brains. In our ablation study, the features of normal directions are removed
436   and only morphological data is retained, referred to as statistical learning, since the curvatures con-
437   tain essential physics of the bifurcation processes, which is well known in the nonlinear elasticity
438   community. Our results show that, for the inference of spherical data, both traditional statistical and
439
      PT learning yield satisfactory results (Fig. 4c). However, when extrapolating to ellipsoids and hu-
      man brains using the model trained on the sphere data, PT learning excels while statistical learning
440
      fails, highlighting the generalizability of PT learning (Figs. 4d and 4e).
441
442
443   4   R ELATED W ORK
444
445   Our PT framework shares some conceptual features with existing ML methods developed to com-
446   bine multi-fidelity data (Ramakrishnan et al., 2015; Batra et al., 2019; Smith et al., 2019). ∆-learning
447   predicts high-fidelity properties by learning the discrepancies in predictions from models at different
448
      levels of fidelity (Ramakrishnan et al., 2015). The objective properties are calculated by correcting
      low-fidelity calculations following a statistical treatment. In a similar spirit, the low-fidelity as a
449
      feature (LFAF) method learns the relation between properties obtained from models with differ-
450
      ent fidelities, and predicts the high-fidelity properties using objective properties and other materials
451   parameters obtained from low-fidelity models as the input (Batra et al., 2019). Transfer learning
452   pre-trains neural networks on low-fidelity data and fine-tunes the parameters on high-fidelity ones
453   to achieve high accuracy in predictions (Smith et al., 2019). However, these methods are statistical
454   in nature and their applications mainly focus on the properties at equilibrium. In supervised learn-
455   ing, it is necessary and beneficial to label data obtained from high-fidelity models in the training
456   process, which are not available for most non-equilibrium properties such as the Peierls stress with
457   chemical accuracy at the DFT level. Our PT framework resolves this constraint from the accuracy-
458   performance dilemma by going beyond the statistical approach and transferring the physics across
459
      models with different fidelities, which is characterized by materials parameters that can be obtained
      from single-point, unit-cell calculations. For example, the Peierls stress is predicted accurately and
460
      efficiently utilizing the learned physics and chemically accurate materials parameters. Longitudinal
461
      MRI data of brains are rare, limiting the use of traditional statistical learning methods to directly
462   predict the development of human brain morphologies (Bethlehem et al., 2022; Ciceri et al., 2024).
463   PT learning approach can learn the physics of bifurcation from data of morphological development
464   obtained for simple geometries, and then be applied to the human brain with elevated morphological
465   complexities.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
469
      Our work ‘digitalizes’ the observation-assumption-model practice in engineering sciences using
470
      neural network representations. As the ML model learns physics from data, physical features natu-
471
      rally emerge in the space of latent variables (Fig. 5). In the case of material strength screening, after
472   the model has learned the physics of crystal plasticity, the principal components of latent features
473   show a weak correlation with the input variables such as the elastic constants (Fig. 5a), but a strong
474   correlation with the dislocation width, another important physical quantity in crystal plasticity (Fig.
475   5b). In the study of brain morphologies, the ML model of PT learning exhibits similar weight distri-
                     ′            ′
476   bution (p(θ|DLF ) ≈ p(θ|DHF )) after learning from spherical and ellipsoidal data (Fig. 5c), whereas
477   the ML model of statistical learning using the morphology data only shows a significant difference
478   in parameter distribution (p(θ|DLF ) ̸= p(θ|DHF )) compared to PT learning (Fig. 5d). The preserved
479   features of weight distribution across data of varying complexity demonstrate the generalizability to
480   complex geometries.
481   These observations indicate that the PT learning framework captures the essential physics of prob-
482   lems with high complexities, and explains its outstanding performance in addressing the accuracy-
483   performance dilemma. The learned physics in the PT approach is limited by the fidelity of digi-
484   tal libraries, which depend on the completeness of theoretical descriptions and experimental data.
485   Specifically, in materials strength screening, databases constructed with well-trained MLFFs are ex-
      pected to offer more accurate physics than EAM or MEAM, although their computational costs are


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      high, and a full set of MLFFs for all metal alloys is not available at present. Our studies show that
487   the error of PT-MLFF predictions using the physics learned from MLFF simulations is reduced to
488   e = 1.51% (Fig. 2j). This few-shot fine-tuning approach utilizing well-trained MLFFs substantially
489   improves the accuracy of the learned physics compared to the database constructed with EAM po-
490   tentials (Figs. 2c, 2f-h, 2j). For the prediction of human brain morphologies, the rareness of MRI
491   data could be resolved by the output of ongoing projects such as the Developing Human Connectome
492   Project (dHCP) (Makropoulos et al., 2018) or adding animal data.
493   The advancement of engineering sciences has often been marked by key moments where funda-
494   mental physics is distilled to form theoretical frameworks. Our PT approach continues these efforts
495   by leveraging data representation from real-world problems. By reducing the dimensionality of la-
496   tent variable spaces and abstracting data correlations, this method has the potential to reveal new
497   theoretical insights, which will be a focus of ongoing research.

717   In calculations of the γ surfaces, a supercell with lattice vectors of a ([110]), b ([112]), and c ([111])

720
      of lattice mismatch. The upper 16 atomic layers are rigidly shifted relative to the lower 16 layers
      progressively along the z-axis, and independently in the x and y directions, respectively. Relaxation
721
      of the atomic layers along the z direction is allowed after the displacement. The γ surfaces are
722
      constructed using a 31 × 31 grid,

738   A.2    DFT C ALCULATIONS
739
740   To validate the hypothesis and feasibility of the PT framework, we directly calculate the Peierls stress
741   in small systems (‘S’, with 244 atoms) using Cu as an example. The DFT calculations are carried out
742   using the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) using the projector augmented wave (PAW)
743   method and a plane-wave basis (Blöchl, 1994; Kresse & Joubert, 1999). The generalized gradient
744
      approximation (GGA) in Perdew-Burke-Ernzerhof (PBE) parametrization is used for the exchange-
      correlation energy (Perdew et al., 1996). A supercell for Cu with sizes of 3.48 nm × 0.44 nm ×
745
      1.90 nm containing an edge dislocation is prepared by structural relaxation using EAM. A cutoff
746
      energy of 500 eV is chosen for the plane waves and a 1×5×1 (kx ×ky ×kz ) Monkhorst-Pack k-grid
747   is used to sample the Brillouin zone (Monkhorst & Pack, 1976). The convergence of self-consistent
748   field (SCF) calculations using the plane-wave cutoff and k-grid meshing is assured to be below
749   1meV/atom. Similar to the setup in MD simulations, a step-wise strain increment of 4 × 10−3 is
750   applied. The Peierls stress is calculated as the minimum stress at which the dislocation is activated
751   to move.

753   A.3    MLFF C ALCULATIONS
754
755   The neuroevolution-potential (NEP) framework is adopted to develop MLFFs for fcc Cu, Al, bcc
      Fe, and hcp Ti (Fan et al., 2021; Song et al., 2023). The local atomic environments are encoded by


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      two-body (radial) and three-body (angular) descriptors. A FNN with one hidden layer (30 neurons)
757   is used to predict atomic energies from these descriptors. For systems considering dislocation mo-
758   tion and plastic flow, configurations with applied strain and random perturbation of atomic positions,
759   surfaces, and stacking faults are included in the training set, and the energies of these configurations
760   are labeled using DFT calculations. Instead of using gradient descent-based back-propagation to
761   update the parameters of neural networks, the separable natural evolution strategy algorithm is im-
762   plemented in the training process to minimize the relatively complex loss functions (Fan et al., 2021;
763   Song et al., 2023). The well-trained MLFFs achieve a prediction accuracy of < 1 meV/atom in the
764   energy of atoms and < 50 meV/Å in the force on atoms. Atomic simulations using the well-trained
765
      MLFFs accurately predict the γ surfaces, comparable to the DFT calculations but with three orders
      lower computational cost.

      A.4    M ODELING M ORPHOLOGICAL D EVELOPMENT
769
      Brain development is regulated by genetic, molecular, cellular, and mechanical factors across mul-
770   tiple spatiotemporal scales (Klingler et al., 2021; Llinares-Benadero & Borrell, 2019b), and the
771   differential tangential growth hypothesis is commonly used (Tallinen et al., 2016; Klingler et al.,
772   2021; Llinares-Benadero & Borrell, 2019b). FEA can model morphological evolution during brain
773   growth at the continuum level (Tallinen et al., 2016; 2014; Darayi et al., 2022; Budday & Stein-
774   mann, 2018; Wang et al., 2021). In the TG model, the tangential growth of the outer gray matter is
775   faster than the inner white matter (Tallinen et al., 2016). Compression resulting from the mismatch
776   in deformation may then lead to mechanical instabilities of the brain surface, forming characteristic
777   sulci and gyri structures (Tallinen et al., 2014; 2016; Striedter et al., 2015; Darayi et al., 2022; Wang
778
      et al., 2021; Budday & Steinmann, 2018; da Costa Campos et al., 2021).
779   In continuum modeling, the reference configuration can be mapped to the current one through the
