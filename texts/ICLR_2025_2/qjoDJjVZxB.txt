011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The SimCLR method for contrastive learning of invariant visual representations
014            has become extensively used in supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised
015            settings, due to its ability to uncover patterns and structures in image data that
016            are not directly present in the pixel representations. However, the reason for this
017            success is not well-explained, since it is not guaranteed by invariance alone. In
018            this paper, we conduct a mathematical analysis of the SimCLR method with the
019
               goal of better understanding the geometric properties of the learned latent distri-
               bution. Our findings reveal two things: (1) the SimCLR loss alone is not sufficient
020
               to select a good minimizer — there are minimizers that give trivial latent distri-
021
               butions, even when the original data is highly clustered — and (2) in order to
022            understand the success of contrastive learning methods like SimCLR, it is nec-
023            essary to analyze the neural network training dynamics induced by minimizing a
024            contrastive learning loss. Our preliminary analysis for a one-hidden layer neural
025            network shows that clustering structure can present itself for a substantial period
026            of time during training, even if it eventually converges to a trivial minimizer. To
027            substantiate our theoretical insights, we present numerical results that confirm our
028            theoretical predictions.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Unsupervised learning of effective representations for data is one of the most fundamental problems
034
      in machine learning, especially in the context of image data. The widely successful discriminative
035
      approach to learning representations of data is most similar to fully supervised learning, where
036   features are extracted by a backbone convolutional neural network, except that the fully supervised
037   task is replaced by an unsupervised or self-supervised task that can be completed without labeled
038   training data.
039
      Many successful discriminative representation learning methods are based around the idea of find-
040
      ing a feature map that is invariant to a set of transformations (i.e., data augmentations) that are
041
      expected to be present in the data. For image data, the transformations may include image scaling,
042   rotation, cropping, color jitter, Gaussian blurring, and adding noise, though the question of which
043   augmentations give the best features is not trivial (Tian et al., 2020). Invariant feature learning meth-
044   ods include VICReg Bardes et al. (2021), Bootstrap Your Own Latent (BYOL) (Grill et al., 2020),
045   Siamese neural networks Chicco (2021), and contrastive learning techniques such as SimCLR Chen
046   et al. (2020) (see also (Hadsell et al., 2006; Dosovitskiy et al., 2014; Oord et al., 2018; Bachman
047   et al., 2019)).
048   In contrastive learning, the primary self-supervised task is to differentiate between positive and
049   negative pairs of data instances. The goal is to find a feature map for which positive pairs have
050   maximally similar features, while negative pairs have maximal different features. The positive and
051   negative examples do not necessarily correspond to classes. In SimCLR, positive pairs are images
052   that are the same up to a transformation, while all other pairs are negative pairs. Contrastive learning
053   has also been successfully applied in supervised (Khosla et al., 2020) and semi-supervised contexts
      (Li et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Singh, 2021; Zhang et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2022; Kim et al.,

072   All invariance based feature extraction techniques must address the fundamental problem of dimen-
073   sion collapse, whereby a method learns the trivial constant map f (x) = c (or a very low rank map),
074   which is invariant to all transformations, but not informative or descriptive. There are various ways
075   to prevent dimension collapse. In contrastive learning the role of the negative pairs is to prevent
076
      collapse by creating repulsion terms in the latent space, however, full or partial collapse can still
      occur (Jing et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a; Shen et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022). In BYOL collapse is
077
      prevented by halting backpropagation in certain parts of the loss, and incorporating temporal aver-
078
      aging. In VICReg, additional terms are added to the loss function to maintain variance in each latent
079   dimension, as well as to decorrelate variables.
080
081   Provided dimensional collapse does not occur, a fundamental unresolved question surrounding many
082
      feature learning methods is: why do they work so well at producing embeddings that uncover key
      features and patterns in data sets? As a simple example, consider fig. 1. In fig. 1a and fig. 1b we
083
      show t-SNE (Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) visualizations of the MNIST (Deng, 2012) and Cifar-
084
      10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) data sets, respectively, using their pixel representations. We can see
085   that visual features are not required on MNIST, which is highly preprocessed, while for Cifar-10 the
086   pixel representations are largely uninformative, and feature representations are essential. In fig. 1c
087   we show a t-SNE visualization of the latent embedding of the SimCLR method applied to Cifar-10,
088   which indicates that SimCLR has uncovered a strong clustering structure in Cifar-10 that was not
089   present in the pixel representation.
090
      The goal of this paper is to provide a framework that can begin to address this question, and in
091   particular, to explain fig. 1. To do this, we assume the data follows a corruption model, where the
092   observed data is derived from some clean data with distribution µ that is highly structured or clus-
093   tered in some way (e.g., follows the manifold assumption with a clustered density). The observed
094   data is then obtained by applying transformations at random from a set of augmentations T to the
095   clean data points (i.e., taking different views of the data), producing a corrupted distribution µ
                                                                                                       e. The
096   main question that motivated our work is that of understanding what properties of the original clean
097   data distribution µ can be uncovered by unsupervised contrastive feature learning techniques? That
098   is, once an invariant feature map f : RD → Rd is learned, is the latent distribution f# µ   e similar in
099
      any to the clean distribution µ, or can it be used to deduce any geometric or topological properties
      of µ?
100
101   This paper has two main contributions. For simplicity we focus on SimCLR, and indicate in the
102   appendix how our results extend to other techniques.
103
104         1. We show minimizing the SimCLR contrastive learning loss is not sufficient to recover
105            information about µ. In particular, there are invariant minimizers of the SimCLR loss that
106            are completely independent of the data distributions µ and µ  e. In the extreme case, the
107            original clean data may be highly clustered, while the latent distribution has a minimizer
               that is the uniform distribution.

143   tion of the SimCLR loss that is useful or our analysis. Let µ ∈ P(RD ) be a data distribution in RD .
144   Let T be a set of transformation functions T : RD → RD that is measurable such that, for a given
145   x ∈ RD , T (x) ∈ RD represents a perturbation of x, such as a data augmentation (e.g., cropping and
146                     e ∈ P(RD ) denote the distribution obtained by perturbing µ with the perturbations
      image, etc.). Let µ
147   defined in T . That is, we choose a probability distribution ν ∈ P(T ) over the perturbations, and
148   samples from µ e are generated by sampling x ∼ µ and f ∼ ν, and taking the composition f (x).
149   We treat µ as the original clean data, which is not observable, while the perturbed distribution µ
                                                                                                       e
150   is how the data is presented. Our goal is to understand whether contrastive learning can recover
151   information about the original data µ, provided the distribution of augmentations ν is known.
152

153

158

      feature representation of the given data. However, it is far from clear how µ and f# µ are related,
159
      and whether any interesting structures in µ (such as clusterability) are also present in f# µ.

295   It follows from Theorem 3.2 that gradient descent on the NT-Xent loss can lead to solutions that are
296   completely independent of the original data distribution µ. For instance, if µ has some underlying
297   cluster structure, with multiple clusters, there are minimizers of the NT-Xent loss, i.e., an invari-
298   ant map f , that map onto an arbitrary distribution in the latent space, completely independent of
299   the clustering structure of µ. However, in practice, when the map is parameterized using neural
300   networks, and trained with gradient descent on L(f ), it is very often observed that the clustering
301   structure of the original data distribution µ emerges in the latent space (see fig. 1). In fact, our re-
302
      sults in section 4 show that this is true even if we initialize gradient descent very poorly, starting
      with an invariant f mapping to the uniform distribution U(Sd−1 )!
303
304   Although the contrastive loss L(f ) has minimizers that ignore the data distribution µ, leading to poor
305   results, contrastive learning often achieves excellent performance in practice. This suggests that the
306   neural network’s parameterization and gradient descent optimization are selecting a good minimizer
307   for L(f ), producing well-clustered distributions in the latent space. To understand this, we will
308
      analyze the dynamics of neural network optimization during training in the following sections.

528   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
529
530   We have studied the SimCLR contrastive learning problem from the perspective of a variational
531   analysis and through the dynamics of training a neural network to represent the embedding func-
532   tion. Our findings strongly suggest that in order to fully understand the representation power of
533   contrastive learning, it is necessary to study the training dynamics of gradient descent, as vanilla
534   gradient descent forgets all information about the data distribution.
535   The results in this paper are preliminary, and there are many interesting directions for future work.
536   It would be natural to examine a mean field limit (Mei et al., 2018) for the training dynamics, which
537   may shed more light on this phenomenon (e.g., in theorem 4.5). We can also consider an infinite
538   width neural network, as is done in the NtK setting, in theorem 4.5 to attempt to rigorously establish
539   convergence of the training dynamics. It would also be interesting to explore applications of these
      techniques to other deep learning methods.
