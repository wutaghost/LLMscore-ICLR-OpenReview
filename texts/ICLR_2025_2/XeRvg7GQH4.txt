                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Data condensation (DC) technologies are widely used in buffer-constrained sce-
012
               narios to reduce the memory demands of training samples while maintaining
               the training performance of deep neural networks. However, due to the storage
013
               constraints of deployment devices and the high energy costs associated with the
014
               condensation process, synthetic datasets generated by DC often suffer from inferior
015            performance in terms of training efficiency and scalability, which significantly
016            limits their practical application across various edge devices. This issue arises
017            from two main factors: (i) existing state-of-the-art (SoTA) data condensation ap-
018            proaches update synthetic datasets by intuitively matching intermediate training
019            outputs (e.g., gradients, features, and distributions) between real and synthetic
020            datasets, without enhancing their representational information capabilities from
021            the perspective of the useful information contained; (ii) DC methods do not ad-
022            equately consider the heterogeneity of storage constraints across different edge
023
               devices, leading to excessive training overheads (i.e., increased consumption or
               storage requirements). To address these challenges, we propose a novel method
024
               named Mixture-of-Information Bottleneck Dataset Condensation (MIBDC), which
025
               employs information bottlenecks from synthetic datasets with varying Images Per
026            Class (IPC) to enhance overall DC generalization and scalability. In particular,
027            this paper identifies the following two phenomena: (i) the quality of synthetic
028            datasets improves with an increase in synthetic dataset quantity, and (ii) the smaller
029            the synthetic dataset, the earlier it reaches the convergence peak. Based on these
030            findings, this paper proposes that (i) larger synthetic datasets can guide the more ef-
031            fective convergence of smaller ones, and (ii) the information contained in synthetic
032            datasets with different IPC numbers can collaboratively enhance dataset conden-
033            sation generalization. Comprehensive experimental results on three well-known
034
               datasets demonstrate that, compared with SoTA dataset condensation methods,
               MIBDC not only improves the generalization performance of trained models but
035
               also decreases training times for various edge devices (training once).

040
      Along with the development of artificial intelligence, data-driven Deep Neural Networks (DNNs)
041
      have been widely used in storage resource-limited scenarios such as autonomous driving (Wen
042   et al.), industrial control, robotics and smart healthcare. However, since DNN training requires
043   sufficient resources, the performance of DNNs will be severely limited if the quality and quantity of
044   training samples cannot be guaranteed. For example, the CLIP or Large Language Model consumes
045   significant storage resources for its training samples and requires hundreds of GPU hours to achieve
046   an acceptable performance. To address such increasing storage requirements of training datasets and
047   to accommodate the limited memory capabilities of various Internet of Things (IoT) devices, Dataset
048   Condensation (DC) technology has been proposed to ensure that effective information contained in
049   large training datasets can be condensed into a small synthetic dataset. In DC, the key problem is
050
      how to effectively explore and condense proper information from real datasets into corresponding
      synthetic datasets without degrading their representation information capabilities.

134   2    M OTIVATION
135
136
      In the motivation for this paper, as depicted in Figure 2, we observed that during the training procedure,
137
      synthetic datasets with a smaller IPC intend to converge more quickly, whereas those with a larger IPC
138
      converge more slowly. Specifically, the synthetic dataset with an IPC of 1 reaches convergence in the
139   early stages, while the dataset with an IPC of 50 converges much later. This observation means that
140   datasets with a smaller IPC can learn features that are simpler with low-density information and more
141   directly related to the label, whereas datasets with a larger IPC tend to learn features with high-density
142   information that are indirectly related to the label. Therefore, we assume that there are two classes of
143   features in the synthetic datasets (i.e., Basic-Coarse-Grained features and Advanced-Fine-Grained
144   features) after the dataset condensation procedure. The former features represent basic features
145   of the real datasets that provide an overview but miss more dataset details. Thus, during DC, the
146   smaller IPC in a synthetic dataset can quickly obtain these features that are easier to obtain but
147
      may miss some fine and intricate details. On the contrary, the latter feature is more challenging to
      acquire but contains richer, more dense information that can be helpful in DNN learning. So, the
148
      larger IPC dataset can not only learn the above basic information but also capture detailed, in-depth
149
      features that offer a comprehensive understanding of the real dataset. Thus, we think that during
150   the dataset condensation procedure, there should be an information-based transformation between
151   Basic-Coarse-Grained (BCG) features and Advanced-Fine-Grained (AFG) features. For synthetic
152   datasets with higher IPC, the contained AFG features tend to overlap with BCG features, preventing
153   the model from effectively learning this high-level information. We assume that this occurs because
154   BCG features are more closely associated with the label in the method of information bottleneck.
155   Consequently, during supervised learning, once the model learns the BCG features, it does not learn
156   the AFG features, leading to insufficient accuracy in synthetic datasets with lower IPC.
157   Based on the above assumptions, to better optimize the above objectives, we introduce a multi-
158   synthetic dataset engaged in a collaborative learning framework in DC, named mixture-of-information
159   bottleneck guided dataset condensation, which is based on IB of various multi-sized synthetic datasets.
160   Based on the information bottleneck method, the mixture-of-information bottleneck is similar to the
161   knowledge distillation method, whose main principle is to select a suitable teacher for learning tasks
      to expand the contained information of the synthetic dataset in AFG features.

      In this section, we introduce the methodology of Mixture-of-Information Bottleneck guidance
165   for Dataset Condensation (MIBDC). First, this paper introduces the existing SoTA DC method
166   and then introduces the background and effect of the information bottleneck. Later, this paper
167   formalizes the DC problem as an information compression problem. Then, we transform the current
168   data compression target into a learnable target optimization problem and introduce our mixture-of-
169   information bottleneck loss to solve this problem. Finally, we discuss the feasibility and theoretical
170   proof of the method and give a corresponding discussion.

174   3.1.1    DATASET C ONDENSATION
175
      The goal of dataset condensation is to generate a small synthetic dataset D∗syn = {Xsyn , Ysyn }, where
176   the same architecture model can be trained obtaining a comparable performance relative to the real
177   dataset. In this way, the training time of neural networks will be greatly reduced based on such small
178   synthetic datasets. Based on this method, some representative DD methods generally recommend
179   that similar intermediate outputs be enforced when training models on real and synthetic datasets.
180   Existing methods can be formalized as the following optimization problem:
181
                      D∗syn = arg min EXsyn ∼Dsyn EXr ∼Dreal [Dist (f (Xreal ; θ∗ ), f (Xsyn ; Ysyn )] .   (3.1)

      Here, θ are the learned weights of networks trained on the real dataset Dreal and the synthetic datasets.
184
      The distance function Dist can be used to calculate the distance between the real and synthetic datasets
185
      for obtaining synthetics datasets. By aligning the two extracted intermediate results, one can optimize
186   the synthetic data to achieve comparable performance to the real data set. Note that how to design the
187   distance function alignment of networks trained on different datasets to optimize synthetic datasets is
188   the key in previous DD methods, such as Lee et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2018); Zhao et al. (2021).
189   Although these distance metrics of DC design bring relatively satisfactory results, since the data set
190   compression itself is a data compression problem, how to better increase the effective information of
191   the compressed data set and reduce the useless information of the compression into a synthetic data
192   set has never been considered.

194   3.1.2    I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
195
      As a promising method in information extraction, Information Bottleneck (IB) is proposed to extract
196
      relevant information about a target variable Y from an input variable X. This method compresses X
197   into a more efficient variable T , maximizing the relevant information about Y retained. It balances
198   the compression and retention of information by minimizing I(T ; X)(the complexity of compression)
199   and maximizing I(T ; Y )(i.e., the information about Y preserved.) The trade-off is regulated by the
200   parameter β, a Lagrange multiplier in the optimization formula:
201
202
                                             max {I(X; T ) − βI(T ; Y )}.                                  (3.2)
                                             P(t|x)
203
204   Here, P(t|x) is the conditional probability distribution of T given X, optimized to finely balance
205
      between compressing X and retaining essential information about Y . Mutual Information (MI),
      denoted as I(X, Y ), quantifies the information gained about one random variable by comparing
206
      another. It is measured in bits and reflects the reduction in uncertainty about one variable when the
207
      other is known. High mutual information indicates a significant reduction in uncertainty, and vice
208   versa. Strictly, for two discrete variables X and Y , their MI can be defined as:
209
210

215   target is to save effective information from real datasets and, like the data compression problem,
      under the constraints in information bottleneck.

223   guide the DC generalization. From the perspective of information bottleneck, the real and synthesized

462   5     R ELATED W ORK
463
464   To address this issue, various methods have been proposed, which can be broadly categorized into two
465   classes: parameter matching methods and distribution matching methods. The parameter matching
466
      approach focuses on aligning superior parameters during the DNN training process to optimize
      the representational capacity of synthetic datasets. For instance, Zhao et al. (2021) used gradient
467
      matching between synthetic and real dataset batches with the same DNN. Similarly, Wang et al. (2022)
468
      optimized feature maps derived from real datasets, while Liu et al. (2023) employed a combination
469   of techniques to optimize distance, and Shang et al. (2024) used mutual information to measure the
470   distance between synthetic and real datasets. However, since parameters require substantial storage,
471   this approach demands a large amount of memory during training. On the other hand, distribution
472   matching methods aim to enhance the representational capability of synthetic datasets by focusing
473   on the intermediate outputs (i.e., dataset distribution) of DNNs. While both methods can partially
474   improve the representational capacity of synthetic datasets, they inevitably face a trade-off between
475   representational power and storage constraints.
476
477   6     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this paper, we propose a method named Mixture-of-Information Bottleneck Dataset Condensation
480   (MIBDC) method. The proposed method effectively addresses the limitations of existing data
481   condensation technologies by enhancing both the generalization performance and scalability of
482   synthetic datasets across edge devices. By leveraging information bottlenecks and collaboratively
483   utilizing synthetic datasets with varying images per classses, MIBDC not only improves dataset
484   quality and convergence but also reduces training overheads. The experimental results validate the
485   superiority of MIBDC over state-of-the-art methods, showcasing its potential for efficient deployment
      in buffer-constrained and heterogeneous edge environments.
