                                                        A BSTRACT
009
               We study the generalization properties of binary logistic classification in a simplified setting,
010            for which a "memorizing" and "generalizing" solution can always be strictly defined, and
011            elucidate empirically and analytically the mechanism underlying Grokking in its dynamics.
012            Analyzing the final stages of training of logistic classification on Gaussian data with a
013            constant label, we show that it may exhibit Grokking, in the sense of delayed generalization
014            and non-monotonic test loss, when the parameters of the problem are close to a critical
015            point. Specifically, we find that Grokking is amplified when the training set is on the verge
016            of linear separability from the origin. Even though a perfect generalizing solution always
017            exists, the implicit bias of the logisitc loss will cause the model to overfit if the training
018
               data is linearly separable from the origin. For training sets that are not separable from the
               origin, the model will always generalize perfectly in infinite time, but overfitting may occur
019
               at early stages of training. Importantly, in the vicinity of the transition, that is, for training
020
               sets that are almost separable from the origin, the model may overfit for an arbitrarily long
021            time before generalizing. We gain more insights by examining a tractable one-dimensional
022            toy model that quantitatively captures the key features of the full model. Finally, we
023            highlight intriguing common properties of our findings with recent literature, suggesting
024            that grokking generally occurs in proximity to the interpolation threshold, reminiscent of
025            critical phenomena often observed in physical systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Understanding the relationship between the intrinsic properties of data, the training dynamics of neural
030   networks (NNs), and their ability to generalize is crucial to explaining the success of modern machine
031   learning (ML) algorithms. In particular, highly over-parameterized models based on the transformer
032   architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023), such as Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Google,
033   2023; Zeng et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Anil et al., 2023), as well as state of the
034   art models for computer vision (Srivastava and Sharma, 2023), defy expectations and are able to generalize
035   with a number of parameters far exceeding the so called interpolation threshold (Kaplan et al., 2020; Schaeffer
036   et al., 2023). Interestingly, these models have been shown to exhibit unpredictable behaviors when changing
037   the number of network parameters, not only with respect to generalization, but also in their learning dynamics.
038   One such phenomenon is Grokking, first observed by Power et al. (2022) when training a transformer model
039   on modular arithmetic tasks. Grokking occurs when a model initially achieves perfect training accuracy but
040   no generalization (i.e. no better than a random predictor), and upon further training, transitions to almost
041   perfect generalization. This phenomenon has garnered substantial attention in recent years (Gromov, 2023;
042   Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) due to its striking contrast with naive expectations, whereby over-fitting is
043   generally seen as an undesirable property of models that should not generalize with further training, originally
044
      dealt with using early stopping (Prechelt, 1996).
045   In this work, we study grokking in a synthetic, yet illuminating, setting where the asymptotic optimal
046   solution can always be identified, allowing a sharp definition of notions that are typically ambiguous, such

      as “memorization” and “learning”. Concretely, we focus on the extreme case of feature noise where the
048
      network needs to ignore the input features and classify all points as a constant label by optimizing the logistic
049   loss (generalization to the discriminative case is discussed in App. H). The input data are N points in Rd ,
050   assumed initially to be drawn independently from an isotropic normal distribution with diagonal covariance,
051   xi ∼ N (0, σId ) where Id is the d × d identity matrix and σ > 0. This assumption, however, can be relaxed
052   as our results hold practically for almost any input distributions (see App. G). We study the limit of large
053   N, d → ∞, while keeping the ratio λ = d/N fixed. Our main contributions are:
054
            1. We prove, and demonstrate numerically, that grokking may occur in this setting, and is promoted
055            when λ is close to 1/2 and σ is large.
056         2. We show that this occurs because λ = 1/2 is a critical point. That is, for λ < 1/2 the model will
057            almost surely asymptotically approach perfect generalization accuracy and vanishing loss, while for
058            λ > 1/2 the model will almost surely achieve imperfect generalization accuracy and the population
059            loss will diverge at t → ∞.
060         3. We prove that the generalization properties depend only on whether the training set is linearly
061            separable from the origin. The model will achieve asymptotically optimal generalization if and only
062            if the origin is contained in the convex hull of the training set.
063
            4. Moreover, we show that near the threshold value, the dynamics may generically track the
               overfitting solution for arbitrarily long times before transitioning to the optimal generalizing solution,
064
               manifesting as non-monotonicity of the test loss and delayed generalization.
065
            5. We construct a simple, one-dimensional model which captures the salient aspects of the problem,
066            and explicitly solve the time evolution of the model parameters for several interesting cases.
067
068   The main takeaway from our setup is that grokking happens near critical points, similar to the phenomenon
069
      known in the physics literature as ’critical slowing down’. While further study is needed, we conjecture that
      this applies to other grokking examples, as was demonstrated explicitly (though not necessarily stated in these
070
      terms) in Levi et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Gromov (2023); Rubin et al. (2023; 2024).
071
072   The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Sec. 3 presents our main analysis of grokking as a critical
073   phenomenon, beginning with empirical results in Sec. 3.1, studying the possible solutions in Sec. 3.2, and
074   relating it to linear separability in Sec. 3.3. Finally, we bring together the pieces in Sec. 3.4 to explain why
075
      grokking occurs near the critical point. Sec. 4 provides a tractable effective model which fully captures the
      grokking dynamics. We conclude in Sec. 5 and discuss future directions. In the appendix we discuss several
076
      generalizations of our results and provide some further proofs and derivations.

      Following the discovery of grokking by Power et al. (2022), numerous studies have attempted to elucidate its
      underlying mechanisms. Liu et al. (2022) showed that when sufficient data determines the structured
081
      representation, perfect generalization can be achieved on a non-modular addition task. Other works
082
      have identified factors contributing to grokking, including pattern learning (Davies et al., 2023), delayed
083   robustness Humayun et al. (2024), and transitions from memorization to circuit formation Nanda et al.
084   (2023), reaffirmed by Golechha (2024) which shown that activation sparsity, absolute weight entropy and
085   circuit complexity are well aligned with grokking in real world tasks. Others analyzed the trigonometric
086   algorithms learned by networks after grokking (Nanda et al., 2023; Chughtai et al., 2023; Merrill et al., 2023;
087   Gromov, 2023), and demonstrated similar dynamics in sparse parity tasks (Merrill et al., 2023). Additional
088   works proposed "slingshots" (Thilak et al., 2022) or "oscillations" (Notsawo et al., 2023) as explanations
089   for grokking, while others have studied the role of regularization, which has proven to significantly impact
090   grokking in certain scenarios (Power et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). Our work requires none of these in order
091
      to exhibit or explain grokking.
092   Recently, a body of works on solvable models which grok in various settings has emerged. Liu et al. (2023);
093   Kumar et al. (2023) and Lyu et al. (2024) have linked grokking to memorization and transitions from lazy

318   3.4           C OLLECTING THE PIECES : WHY GROKKING HAPPENS NEAR λ = 12 ?
319
320   We have established that for λ < 12 , the model will almost surely generalize perfectly. For infinitely long
321   times, S(t) converges to a finite vector S∞ , and b(t) diverges. For λ > 12 , the model will almost surely over
322   fit. Intuitively, one should expect that in the vicinity of this critical point, where the two solutions exchange
323   stability, dynamics may become slow. This is because for λ > 1/2 the overfitting solution is stable and
324   for λ smaller than but close to 1/2, it is unstable but only slightly so. Therefore, the dynamics may spend
325   arbitrarily long times in the vicinity of the overfitting solution before flowing to the generalizing solution.
326
      This is delayed generalization. Rigorously, this happens through of the following properties:

      We note that this result bears a striking resemblance to that of Levi et al. (2023), which employed the MSE

412   was explained in terms of a “critical slowing down” effect, arising from a vanishing eigenvalue of the data
413   covariance near criticality. While the two problems are quite different, they both display a critical behavior
414   near an effective interpolation threshold of the corresponding problem. We believe this is not a coincidence
415   but rather a manifestation of a deeper relation between the behaviors of NNs in the vicinity of critical points.

417   5    D ISCUSSION , CONCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
418
419   We studied the dynamics of gradient descent in a simple setting of logistic classification of data with a
420   constant label. We have shown that in this setting Grokking occurs near a critical point in the asymptotic
421   dynamics. Specifically, at the critical point, which for this simple setting is at λ = 1/2, the overfitting and
422   generalizing solutions exchange stability. This non-analytic change in the asymptotic dynamics is the cause

                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


423
      for grokking, much like in Rubin et al. (2024); Levi et al. (2023); Doshi et al. (2024), and to some extent
424
      also Humayun et al. (2024), who showed that grokking occurs near a phase transition.
425
426   Intuitively, in the vicinity of the critical point there are “flat directions” in the loss landscape. These directions
427   may cause training to stay in the vicinity of almost-stable solutions for arbitrarily long times periods before
428
      eventually converging to the global minimum. In the physics literature, this behavior is known as "critical
      slowing down" (e.g. Sethna (2021)). In the current context, this is the mechanism of delayed generalization,
429
      which also explains the non monotonic evolution of the generalization loss.
430
431   While we cannot show it rigorously, we conjecture that Grokking is intimately related to such critical points
432   also in different settings. In a few examples this has been directly demonstrated, Levi et al. (2023); Rubin et al.
433   (2023; 2024); Humayun et al. (2024). We note that other intriguing phenomena, such as the non-monotonic
434
      dependence of asymptotic performance on model complexity, a.k.a “double descent”, has also been proposed
      to be related to criticality, e.g. Schaeffer et al. (2023).
435
436   If this is indeed the case, then in analogy to the theory of critical phenomena in physics, there might
437   exist "universality classes" that have similar critical behavior, but possibly very different underlying
438   mechanisms (Sethna, 2021). We will further address this connection in future work.
439   Limitations: We considered a specific problem of classifying irrelevant features, under a particular choice of
440   feature distribution, namely, Normal with zero mean in high dimensions. It is natural to ask how our results
441   extend to more complex data, for instance including non-trivial correlations, hierarchical structure, or a finite
442   sample space, as in the original observation of grokking Power et al. (2022); Gromov (2023). While we
443   believe the same analysis can be repeated in these instances, in the sense of (non)linear separability, we leave
444   this to future work. In any case, we do not claim that the underlying mechanism of criticality is necessarily
445   related to separability.
446   All of the analytics were done in the GF limit, and while our results were verified by experiments with finite
447   learning rate, it may be interesting to study how large learning rates effect this setup, possibly relating to
448   catapults (Lewkowycz et al., 2020) or the edge of stability literature (Cohen et al., 2022).
449
      Lastly, we did not study the prospect of nonlinear logistic regression, which is closer to deep learning
450   models in the wild. We believe some of our results may be generalized, provided we accept a “feature map”
451   description of the model up to the last layer, and consider the SVM solution on the learned features.

1217   H           D ISCRIMINATIVE LABELING CASE : C AN G ROKKING STILL BE OBSERVED ?
1218
1219   In this section, we investigate how the previous analysis holds when not all labels are the same. To explore
1220   this, we propose the following model: We classify a point xi with label -1 if the value of its first coordinate xi,1
1221   exceeds a threshold µ, and assign it label 1 if it is below this threshold. Specifically, we set µ = Q(r), where

                                                                  26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1222
       Q is the Gaussian quantile function (the inverse of the cumulative distribution function) and r is a fraction
1223
       between 0 and 1. For instance, if r = 1, all points are assigned the same label (-1), which is equivalent to the
1224   case studied earlier in the paper. However, for r < 1, a fraction r of the points will have label -1, while the
1225   remaining fraction 1 − r will be assigned label 1.
1226
1227
       In this context, the question that we want to answer is: Can we observe Grokking for values of r that are
       smaller than one? It turns out that the answer is yes, but only for values of r that are sufficiently close to 1.
1228
       To demonstrate this, we first present numerical calculations for the cases of r = 1, r = 0.99, and r = 0.95
1229
       — see Fig. Fig. 15. We can see that for values slightly smaller than one, the behavior at the initial stages of
1230   training is very similar to that of r = 1, for both the training and generalization sets. However, at the final
1231   stages of training, we can clearly see that for r < 1, the model is not able to fully generalize. In fact, the
1232   accuracy limiting value decreases as r deviates further below 1.
1233
       Intuitively, this occurs because the closer the dividing plane is to the origin, the relative significance of errors
1234
       in its parametrization (i.e., determining S and b) increases. We also note that in this case both S and b go to
1235
       infinity, with a ratio that could also be found from the max-margin SVM (Eq. (Eq. (5))). Full generalization
1236   is only possible in the special case where r = 1, placing the plane at infinity.
