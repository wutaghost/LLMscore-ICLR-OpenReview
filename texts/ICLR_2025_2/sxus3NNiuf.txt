009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement Learning (RL) has achieved notable success in tasks requiring
012            complex decision making, with offline RL offering the ability to train agents using
013            fixed datasets, thereby avoiding the risks and costs associated with online interac-
014            tions. However, offline RL is inherently limited by the quality of the dataset, which
015            can restrict an agent’s performance. Offline-to-online RL aims to bridge the gap
016
               between the cost-efficiency of offline RL and the performance potential of online
               RL by pre-training an agent offline before fine-tuning it through online interac-
017
               tions. Despite its promise, recent studies show that offline pre-trained agents of-
018
               ten underperform during online fine-tuning due to inaccurate value function, with
019            random initialization proving more effective in certain cases. In this work, we
020            propose a novel method, Online Pre-Training for Offline-to-Online RL (OPT), to
021            address the issue of inaccurate value estimation in offline pre-trained agents. OPT
022            introduces a new learning phase, Online Pre-Training, which allows the training of
023            a new value function that enhances the subsequent fine-tuning process. Implemen-
024            tation of OPT on TD3 and SPOT demonstrates an average 30% improvement in
025            performance across D4RL environments, such as MuJoCo, Antmaze, and Adroit.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) has shown great potential in addressing complex decision-making
030   tasks across various fields (Mnih et al. 2015; Silver et al. 2017). In particular, offline RL (Levine
031   et al. 2020) offers the advantage of training an agent on the fixed dataset, thereby mitigating the
032   potential costs or risks associated with direct interactions in real-world environments - a significant
033   limitation of online RL. However, the effectiveness of offline RL is inherently constrained by the
034   quality of the dataset, which can impede the agent’s overall performance.
035
      To overcome the cost challenge of online RL and the performance limitation of offline RL, the
036   offline-to-online RL approach has been introduced (Lee et al. 2022; Zhang et al. 2023; Yu & Zhang
037   2023). This approach entails training an agent sufficiently on an offline dataset, followed by fine-
038   tuning through additional interactions with the environment. This allows the agent to utilize the
039   knowledge acquired offline for online fine-tuning. Combining the strengths of both approaches,
040   offline-to-online RL reduces the need for extensive environment interactions, while enhancing the
041   agent’s performance through online fine-tuning.
042   Although offline-to-online RL offers clear advantages, prior studies (Zhang et al. 2023; Guo et al.
043   2023; Nakamoto et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024; Kong et al. 2024; Hu et al. 2024) have shown that
044   fine-tuning an offline pre-trained agent often results in worse performance compared to training
045   from scratch. This phenomenon, described by Nakamoto et al. 2024 as counter-intuitive trends, is
046   depicted in Figure 1, which compares the learning curves during the online phase for both fine-tuning
047   and training from scratch with the replay buffer initialized using the offline dataset. As shown in
048   Figure 1 (a), training from scratch outperforms fine-tuning from the very beginning of the learning
049   process, as also observed by (Ball et al. 2023). In Figure 1 (b), although the offline pre-trained
050
      agent exhibits partial success, training from scratch eventually surpasses fine-tuning, highlighting
      the inherent challenges in fine-tuning pre-trained agents in offline settings.
051
052   Previous studies (Nakamoto et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024) attribute this counter-intuitive trends
053   of online fine-tuning to issues stemming from inaccurate value estimation. In response, Nakamoto
      et al. 2024 focuses on providing a lower bound for value updates to correct the value estimation.

      Meanwhile, Zhang et al. 2024 introduces perturbations in value updates, promoting smoother value

070   across both the offline and online learning phases. However, we approach this issue from a different
071   angle. Rather than relying solely on the potentially flawed value function, we propose introducing
072   and utilizing an entirely new value function. To investigate this, we formulate two key research
073   questions that guide our exploration:
074
075       Q1. “Can adding a new value function resolve the issue of slow performance improvement?”
076       Q2. “How can we best leverage the new value function during online fine-tuning?”
077
078   Through a comprehensive analysis of these research questions, we propose Online Pre-Training for
079   Offline-to-Online RL (OPT), a novel approach that introduces a new value function to leverage it
080   during online fine-tuning. In response to the first research question, OPT introduces a new value
081
      function, enhancing overall performance, as illustrated in Figure 1. For the second research ques-
      tion, OPT incorporates an additional learning phase, termed Online Pre-Training, which focuses on
082
      learning this new value function.
083
084   As OPT involves learning by adding a new value function, it can be broadly applied to value
085   function-based RL methods. We evaluate OPT across various D4RL environments, including Mu-
086   JoCo, Antmaze, and Adroit. OPT demonstrates an average 30% improvement within a limited
087
      setting of 300k online interactions in the final normalized score, surpassing previous state-of-the-art
      results.

090   2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Reinforcement Learning (RL) is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman 1990). In
093   this framework, at each time step, an agent selects an action a based on its current state s according
094   to its policy π(a|s). The environment transitions to a subsequent state s′ and provides a reward
095   r, following the transition probability p(s′ |s, a) and reward function r(s, a), respectively. Over
096   successive  interactions, the agent’s policy π is optimized to maximize the expected cumulative return
      Eπ [ t γ t r(st , at )], where γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor.
           P
097
098   Offline RL focuses on training agents using a static dataset D = {(s, a, r, s′ )}, usually generated by
099   various policies. To address the constraints of offline RL, which are often limited by the dataset’s
100   quality, offline-to-online RL introduces an additional phase of online fine-tuning. This hybrid ap-
101   proach enhances the agent’s performance by allowing further learning directly from interactions
102   with the environment.
103   Addressing Inaccurate Offline Value Estimation. Offline RL, reliant on a fixed dataset, is prone
104   to extrapolation error when the value function evaluates out-of-distribution (OOD) actions (Kumar
105   et al. 2020; Fujimoto et al. 2019). Several methods have been proposed to address this challenge:
106   some focus on training the value function to assign lower values to OOD actions (Wu et al. 2019;
107   Kostrikov et al. 2021a), while others aim to avoid OOD action evaluation altogether (Kostrikov et al.
      2021b). These inaccurate value estimations in offline training not only degrade offline performance


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      but can also adversely impact subsequent online fine-tuning (Yu & Zhang 2023; Zhang et al. 2024;
109   Nakamoto et al. 2024; Kong et al. 2024; Feng et al. 2024). Although constraints applied in offline
110   RL can be extended to online fine-tuning to mitigate this issue (Kostrikov et al. 2021b; Lyu et al.
111   2022; Wu et al. 2022), such strategies often impose excessive conservatism, limiting the potential
112   for performance enhancement.
113
      Recent advances in offline-to-online RL have aimed to overcome this limitation arising from in-
114
      accurate value estimations (Nakamoto et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2024). One approach (Nakamoto
115   et al. 2024) addresses over-conservatism during the offline phase by providing a lower bound for
116   value updates. However, the conservative nature of this method continues to hinder policy improve-
117   ment. Another approach (Zhang et al. 2024) introduces perturbations to value updates and increases
118   their update frequency. While effective, this method incurs significantly higher computational costs
119   compared to standard techniques, making it less practical for general use.
120
      Backbone Algorithms. Our proposed Online Pre-Training process using newly introduced value
121   function can be applied to various backbone algorithms. Among the many potential candidates, this
122   study utilizes TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu 2021) and SPOT (Wu et al. 2022) as the backbone algo-
123   rithms due to their simplicity and sample efficiency. TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu 2021) extends the
124   original TD3 (Fujimoto et al. 2018) algorithm by incorporating a behavior cloning (BC) regulariza-
125   tion term into the policy improvement. The value function is trained using temporal-difference (TD)
126   learning, with the loss functions for both the policy πϕ (s) and value function Qθ (s, a) defined as
127   follows:

      3      M ETHOD
136
137
      In this section, we introduce Online Pre-Training for Offline-to-Online RL (OPT), a novel method
138   aimed at addressing the issue of inaccurate value estimation in offline-to-online RL. The proposed
139   method employs two value functions, Qoff-pt and Qon-pt , each serving distinct roles in the time do-
140   main. The method consists of three distinct stages of learning:
141
142         (i) Offline Pre-Training: As in conventional offline-to-online RL, Qoff-pt is trained on the offline
143             dataset, yielding the offline pre-trained policy π off .
144        (ii) Online Pre-Training: The second value function, Qon-pt , is trained using both the offline
145             dataset and newly collected online samples.
146       (iii) Online Fine-Tuning: The policy is updated by utilizing both Qoff-pt and Qon-pt , with each value
147             function continuously updated.
148
149   Figure 2 illustrates the difference between conventional offline-to-online learning (Figure 2a) and
150   our proposed method, OPT (Figure 2b). OPT introduces a new learning phase called Online Pre-
151
      Training, making it distinct from the conventional two-stage offline-to-online RL methods by com-
      prising three stages. The following sections focus on the Online Pre-Training and Online Fine-
152
      Tuning phases, while the offline phase adheres to the standard offline RL process.
153

      3.1     O NLINE P RE -T RAINING
155
156   In the proposed method, Qon-pt is introduced as an additional value function specifically designed
157   for online fine-tuning. One straightforward approach is to add a randomly initialized value function.
158   As Qon-pt begins learning from the online fine-tuning, it is expected to adapt well to the new data
159   encountered during online fine-tuning. However, since Qon-pt is required to train from scratch, it
160   often disrupts policy learning in the early stages. To prevent Qon-pt from disrupting policy learning,
161   we introduce a pre-training phase, termed Online Pre-Training, specifically designed to train Qon-pt .
      The following sections explore the design of the Online Pre-Training method in detail.

188   Designing Datasets. As the initial stage of Online Pre-Training, the only available dataset is the
189   offline dataset Boff , which has already been fully leveraged to train Qoff-pt . Relying solely on Boff
190   causes Qon-pt to closely replicate Qoff-pt . To address this, we incorporate online samples by initiating
191   Online Pre-Training with the collection of Nτ samples in the online buffer Bon , generated by π off .
192   To leverage Bon , one approach is to train Qon-pt with Bon . Since Qon-pt is trained based on π off , this
193   prevents Qon-pt from disrupting policy learning in the initial stage of online fine-tuning. However,
194   as Bon is generated by the fixed policy π off , relying solely on Bon risks overfitting. Therefore, to
195   address both issue of similarity to Qoff-pt and the risk of overfitting to π off , a balanced approach
196
      utilizing both Boff and Bon is necessary during training.

      3.2   O NLINE F INE -T UNING
236
237   In online fine-tuning, by utilizing Qon-pt trained during Online Pre-Training, we facilitate effective
238   policy improvement. Throughout this phase, the buffer Bon is continuously filled and the learning of
239   all three components Qoff-pt , Qon-pt and πϕ progresses. As in the conventional offline-to-online RL,
240   Qoff-pt is updated using TD learning, and similarly, Qon-pt , which was trained by Equation 3, is also
241   updated via TD learning.
242   Effectively Balancing Qoff-pt and Qon-pt . One of the key aspects of OPT lies in its approach to pol-
243   icy improvement, which effectively balances Qoff-pt and Qon-pt during online fine-tuning. While most
244   previous works rely solely on a single value function, our method leverages both Qoff-pt and Qon-pt
245   for policy improvement. Since Qoff-pt is informative for the offline dataset and Qon-pt , pre-trained
246   via meta-adaptation strategy during Online Pre-Training, can be adapted to new data encountered
247   through online interactions, effectively leveraging both value functions is central to our online fine-
248   tuning strategy. The proposed loss function for policy improvement during online fine-tuning is
249   given by:
250
251                  Lfinetune
                      π        (ϕ) = Es∼B [−{(1 − κ)Qoff-pt (s, πϕ (s)) + κQon-pt (s, πϕ (s))}],             (4)
252
253   where πϕ is initialized as π off and 0 < κ ≤ 1. κ is a weighting coefficient that balances the ratio
254   between Qoff-pt and Qon-pt . When the discrepancy between the offline dataset and newly introduced
255   online samples is minimal, Qoff-pt retains valuable information from the offline dataset. As a result,
256   a small κ is employed to more effectively leverage Qoff-pt . As online fine-tuning progresses, Qon-pt ,
257
      optimized through our meta-adaptation objective, quickly adapts to the online data. Consequently, κ
      is incrementally increased to shift the reliance towards the more rapidly adapting Qon-pt . A detailed
258
      analysis of κ is provided in Section 5.2.
259
260   Additionally, to promote the use of online samples, we employ balanced replay (Lee et al. 2022),
261   which prioritizes samples encountered during online interactions to further accelerate the adaptation
262   of Qon-pt . The overall learning phase of OPT is illustrated in Figure 2b, with the algorithm presented

      OPT across different baselines, we changed the baseline to
      IQL. Unlike TD3, IQL employs a stochastic policy and uses

      6   C ONCLUSION
492
493
      This paper introduced Online Pre-Training for Offline-to-Online RL (OPT), a novel method to im-
      prove the fine-tuning of offline pre-trained agents. By incorporating an Online Pre-Training phase
494
      to learn a new value function, OPT addresses the limitations of existing offline-to-online RL ap-
495
      proaches. Our experiments across multiple D4RL environments demonstrated that OPT consistently
496   outperforms current methods and is versatile across different backbone algorithms. These findings
497   suggest that OPT is a robust and effective solution for enhancing performance in offline-to-online
498   RL. The key contribution of this work lies in the introduction of a new value function for online fine-
499   tuning, in contrast to existing methods that focus on modifying the original value function. However,
500   OPT has its limitations, particularly in the lack of analysis on alternative approaches to training the
501   new value function. Future research could explore different strategies for Online Pre-Training, of-
502   fering potential improvements to the offline-to-online RL framework.
