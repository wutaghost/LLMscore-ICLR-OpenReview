009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              This paper proposes a novel deep symbolic regression approach to enhance the
012              robustness and interpretability of data-driven mathematical expression discovery.
013              Despite the success of the recent break-through, DSR, it is built on recurrent neural
014              networks, purely guided by data fitness, and potentially meet tail barriers, which
015              can zero out the policy gradient and cause inefficient model updates. To overcome
016              these limitations, we use transformers in conjunction with breadth-first-search to
017
                 improve the learning performance. We use Bayesian information criterion (BIC)
                 as the reward function to explicitly account for the expression complexity and op-
018
                 timize the trade-off between interpretability and data fitness. We propose a mod-
019
                 ified risk-seeking policy that not only ensures the unbiasness of the gradient, but
020              also removes the tail barriers, thus ensuring effective updates from top performers.
021              Through a series of benchmarks and systematic experiments, we demonstrate the
022              aforementioned advantages of our approach.

      1     Introduction
025
026   Interpretability is an essential measure of machine learning models. Although large complex mod-
027   els, such as those with billions of parameters, have become standard practice in many fields, these
028   models are typically black-box and provide little insight about the data. This can raise severe con-
029   cerns regarding the reliability of deploying such models, particularly in scientific and engineering
030   domains. Symbolic regression (SR) Jobin et al. (2019); Rudin (2019) is an important research di-
031   rection to achieve interpretability in machine learning. Given a dataset that measures the input and
032   output of a complex system of interest, symbolic regression aims to find a simple, concise equation
033   to reveal the underlying mechanism of the system as to improve the understanding of the system and
034
      ensure reliability of the model.
035   For a long time, genetic programming (GP) Koza (1994); Randall et al. (2022); Burlacu et al. (2020)
036   has been the mainstream approach for symbolic regression. However, GP is known to be compu-
037   tationally costly and time consuming due to the its evolutionary nature. The recent break-through,
038   Deep Symbolic Regression (DSR) Petersen et al. (2019), instead uses a recurrent neural network
039   (RNN) to generate expression trees, and employs a risk-seeking policy to train the RNN via rein-
040
      forcement learning. While DSR is successful in many SR benchmarks, the RNN-based architecture
      might cause learning bottlenecks, such as vanishing gradients Hochreiter (1998), especially in large
041
      tree structures. In addition, DSR uses data fitness as the reward, which can tend to generate com-
042
      plex expressions to overfit the data, especially with the presence of noise. Furthermore, due to the
043   usage of the reward difference as the weights in the policy gradients, DSR takes the risk of meeting
044   tail barriers, namely, zero policy gradients, which can render the learning to be only driven by the
045   entropy term, leading to over-exploration and inefficient model updates.
046
      To overcome these limitations, we propose a novel approach, complexity-aware deep symbolic re-
047
      gression (CADSR). The major contributions of our work are summarized as follows.
048
049            • Expression Model. We use transformers Vaswani et al. (2023) to build the expression
050              generation model, which can fully leverage the contextual information of each node in the
051              expression tree and overcome RNN learning bottlenecks to improve the performance. To
052              obtain a good representation, we design tree node encodings based on both the depth and
053              horizontal position. We couple with breadth-first-search (BFS) to sample the expression
                 tree, which is not only computationally efficient but also straightforward to implement.


                                                          1

054
             • Reward Design. We design a Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) as the reward function,
055            for which we use the number of nodes and constant tokens in the expression tree to indicate
056            the model complexity. BIC is justified by Bayesian model selection (Wasserman, 2000) and
057            strongly connects to the minimum description length (MDL) (Rissanen, 1978), making it
058            a principled and robust approach. In this way, our reward function considers not only the
059            data fitness but also the expression complexity. The reinforcement learning can then seek
060            to optimize the trade-off between the interpretability and data fitness of the expressions,
061            avoiding producing over-complicated expressions that overfit the data, especially at present
062            of noises.
063
064
             • Policy Gradient. We propose a modified risk-seeking policy gradient for our BIC-based
               reward. Instead of using the reward difference as the weight to compute the gradient, we use
065
               a simple step reward mapping, which gives a constant weight. We show that this guarantees
066
               to prevent the learning from meeting any tail barrier or partial tail barrier. Accordingly,
067            we can effectively leverage all the top performers for model updating, avoiding wild, less
068            useful exploration.
069
070          • Experiments. We evaluated the performance of our method, CADSR, on the standard SR
071            benchmark and ablation studies. In addition to DSR, we compared with seventeen other
072            popular and/or state-of-the-art SR methods and several commonly used machine learning
073            approaches. The performance of CADSR in both symbolic discovery and prediction accu-
074            racy is consistently among the best. In particular, the symbolic discovery rate of CADSR
075            is the highest when data includes significant noises. In all the cases, CADSR generates
076
               the most interpretable expressions, while maintaining a high level of accuracy. CADSR
               outperforms the most comparable model, DSR, in all categories showing that it is a di-
077
               rect improvement. Extensive ablation studies further demonstrate the effectiveness of each
078
               component of our method.

081   2   Background
082
083   Given a set of input and output examples collected from the target system, denoted as D =
084   {(xi , yi )}N
                  i=1 , symbolic regression aim to identify a concise mathematical expression that character-
085   izes the input-output relationship, such as y = sin(2πx1 ) + cos(2πx2 ). Deep symbolic regression
086
      (DSR) Petersen et al. (2019) discovers equations via an RNN-based reinforcement learning approach
      Sutton & Barto (2018), which can be broken down into four parts: environment, actor, reward, and
087
      policy.
088
089   The environment is designed to be the creation of an expression tree that represents a specific equa-
090   tion. Expression trees are directed trees where each node holds a token from the available list of op-
091   erations and variables. For example, a common list of tokens would be {+, −, ×, /, sin, cos, x1 , 1}.
092
      Expression trees are built by selecting nodes in a preorder traversal of the tree with each tree starting,
      with only the root node. After a token for a node is selected, empty children will be added to node
093
      based on the token. Binary functions will have two empty children added, unary functions will only
094
      have one child, and variables will have no children. These trees are many-to-one mappings to the
095   mathematical expressions, which causes an increase in the search space but prevents the generation
096   of any invalid expression.
097
098
      The actor is an RNN that predicts a categorical distribution of the available tokens for each node in
      the expression tree based on the hidden state of the RNN and the sibling and parent of the current
099
      node. Each token is randomly sampled from the categorical distribution. Additional rules are applied
100
      to the sampling process to prevent the selection of redundant operations or variables. Since the RNN
101   builds the expression trees in a preorder traversal (POT) ordering, there can be a significant delay
102   between the prediction of sibling tokens.
103
104
      The reward function and policy drive the actor to explore and exploit the complex environment. In
      DSR, the reward function is a direct measurement of the data fitness of the generated expression,

134   We now present CADSR, our new deep symbolic regression approach.

136   3.1   Transformer Actor
137
      First, we design a transformer Vaswani et al. (2023) as the actor in the reinforcement learning frame-
138
      work. RNNs rely on a single hidden state that summarizes the information across all the previous
139
      nodes, which can be insufficient and is known to cause the vanishing gradient issue Hochreiter
140   (1998) in larger structures. By contrast, the transformer explicitly integrate information of all the
141   nodes to extract representations and make predictions, and hence can overcome the vanishing gradi-
142   ent issue and more effectively capture the nodes’ relationships.
143
      Specifically, to represent the expression tree nodes, we design a positional encoding (PE) to reflect
144
      their location information. Given a particular node, we consider both the depth, d, and horizontal
145
      position, h, in the tree. To align the horizontal positions of the nodes across different layers, we
146   propose the following design. Denote the horizontal position of the parent node by hp , if the node

148
      hp + hp /2d . In this way, the parent node will be in between its two children horizontally, which
149   naturally reflects the tree structure. The horizontal position for the root node is set to 1/2. See Fig. 1
150   for an illustration. We develop a recursive algorithm to efficiently calculate the horizontal positions
151   of all the nodes, as listed in Appendix Algorithm 3. Given d and h, we then create a 2D-dimensional

187   3.2   BIC Reward Function
188
189   Interpretability is a key motivation for symbolic regression. However, if one uses data fitness as
190
      the reward to guide the learning, like in DSR, it will tend to learn lengthy, complex expressions to
      overfit the data, especially when data contains noise, which is common in practical applications. This
191
      can hurt the interpretability of the learned expressions. To address this problem, we use Bayesian
192
      information criterion (BIC) (Schwarz, 1978) to construct a new reward function. As a principled
193   and robust approach, BIC is derived by maximizing the model evidence through integrating out the
194   model parameters, and is thus justified by Bayesian model selection (Wasserman, 2000). BIC has
195   a known similarity to minimum description length (MDL) Rissanen (1978), allowing BIC to be
196   interpreted as an approximation of MDL.
197
      To reflect the complexity of a sampled expression, we set k to the number of nodes in the expression
198
      tree plus the number of constant tokens. The inclusion of the number of constant tokens is twofold:
199   first, since the values of the constant tokens are unknown apriori — we need to estimate their val-
200   ues from data, they actually sever as unknown model parameters (like those in neural networks)
201   and introduce extra degrees of freedoms, which increase the model complexity. Second, this can
202   also prevent the actor from selecting an unnecessary number of constant tokens, which can cause a
203   significant increase in runtime for optimizing their values. Our BIC reward is given below,
204
205                                        BIC(τ ) = k log(N ) − 2 log(L(τ ))                                        (8)
206                         PN
207   where log(L(τ )) = i=1 log N (yi |τ (xi ), σ 2 ) is a (log) Gaussian likelihood, σ 2 is the variance of
208   the training outputs, and N denotes the number of training points.

      4     Related Work
271
272   DSR is a reinforcement learning method that uses an RNN actor Petersen et al. (2019). The risk-
273   seeking policy rewards the actor only for top-performing expressions, which allows the actor to
274   make a diverse range of predictions without being punished for poor average performance.
275
      Many symbolic regression models have started to use transformers, such as Kamienny et al. (2022);
276
      Valipour et al. (2021); Vastl et al. (2022). However, all of these models learn the mapping directly
277
      from the dataset to the expression trees in a supervised fashion. This type of models is named as
278   Deep Generative Symbolic Regression (DGSR) by Kamienny et al. (2023). To achieve this end-to-
279   end learning, a much larger model architecture is necessary, along with a substantial collection of
280   datasets. By contrast, DSR and our method are not DGSR; they essentially perform unsupervised
281   learning, and the dataset only corresponds to one (unknown) expression. Hence, the model size
282   and data quantity is much smaller. Recently, Shojaee et al. (2023) developed Monte Carlo tree
283   search (MCTS) with transformers (TPSR) to conduct DGSR. TPSR assumes a pre-trained trans-
284   former is given, and conducts an efficient search to identify well-performed expressions. TPSR
285   introduces a regularization term to control the expression complexity. The reward is specified as
                             1                   l(τ (·))        ˜
286   r(τ (·)|x, y) = 1+NMSE(y,τ (x)) + λexp(− L ) where l(f (·)) retrieves the number of tokens in the
287   given expression, and λ > 0 is the weight of the regularization term. Note that our work does not
288   assume the availability of a pre-trained model. Throughout the evaluation, our method has never
289   utilized a pre-trained transformer. Nonetheless, our ablation study demonstrates that our frame-
290
      work can be easily adapted to incorporate pre-trained models and gain their benefits; see Section 5.3
      Kamienny et al. (2023) proposed DGSR-MCTS, which also uses MCTS search but uses a critic
291

292
      which allows for incomplete expressions to be evaluated by the critic.

      Lastly, the recent work uDSR Landajuela et al. (2022) combines several different methods together
300
      to improve DSR. One of the additions is a transformer trained using supervised learning or reinforce-
301   ment learning to provide the RNN actor with additional information about the dataset. uDSR also
302   incorporates genetic programming to each expression tree generation step, which allows for a larger
303   variety of expression trees to be generated each epoch. Validating the performance of the different
304   parts of uDSR required several ablation studies, where every combination of methods, including

306   showed minimal improvement and occasionally hindrance to uDSR during the ablation study. Fur-
307   thermore, genetic programming was the single biggest influence on performance, showing that there
308   needs to be an improvement to DSR. uDSR appeared on the Pareto frontier near the optimal mixture

      of accuracy and complexity.

388   5.2   Ablation Study
389
390   Next, we performed ablation studies to confirm the efficacy of each component of our method. We
391   selected a subset of problems from SR Bench that shows the most significant difference between
392   DSR and CADSR. These problems include: Feynman I 12 11 and Strogatz sherflow1
393
      that demonstrate the biggest decrease in R2 score and symbolic solution rate, Feynman test 9
      showing the increase in R2 score, and Feynman I 34 27 and Feynman III 12 43 exhibiting
394
      the largest increase in symbolic accuracy. For each problem, we ran five trials at 10% noise with
395
      each ablation using the same seed. Below is a brief description of each ablation.
396
397   Transformer Actor. We first tested altering the RNN to a transformer while maintaining the re-
398   maining DSR framework, including original prediction ordering. DSR does not have a defined
399
      positional encoding, so we used a standard 1D-dimensional positional encoding based on the posi-
      tion of the token in the prediction order, i.e., pre-order traversal (POT). This comparison would be
400
      between DSR and DSR-POT-1PE in Figures 4, 5, and 6, showing that switching from an RNN to
401
      a transformer in the DSR framework incurs some trade-off in learning. This change is highlighted
402   by the difference in symbolic discovery rate between the problems, as DSR with a transformer can
403   consistently solve problems that the RNN can not, and vice versa. Since this is a curated selection of
404   problems to highlight the difference between DSR and CADSR, we have magnified the difference
405   between the two methods, causing this stark difference in performance.
406
      Prediction Ordering. Next, we validated the change of the prediction ordering from the pre-order
407
      traversal (POT) to a Breadth First Search (BFS) ordering while maintaining the 1D-dimensional
408   position encoding. DSR-POT-1PE and DSR-BFS-1PE compare these two tests in Figures 4, 5, and
409   6. The improvement from the prediction ordering change can be easily seen in Figures 4 and 5,
410   as DSR-BFS-1PE has better R2 scores and symbolic accuracy. Furthermore, we can decrease the
411   model’s runtime from 0.00361 to 0.00157 seconds per equation by changing the prediction ordering
412   because BFS enables the model to keep token generation on the GPU during equation sampling.
413
      Position Encoding. To confirm the effectiveness of our designed 2-dimensional PE, we tested
414   with alternatives, including 1D-dimensional PE based on the tokens’ location in the sequence of
415   the BFS order. These two tests are DSR-BFS-1PE and DSR-BFS-2PE in Figures 4, 5, and 6. The
416   2D-dimensional PE improved R2 scores and consistency of R2 scores while maintaining a similar
417   symbolic discovery rate for the test problems.
418
      Policy. Next, we evaluated the effectiveness of our robust risk-seeking policy while using the
419
      NMSE-based reward function. DSR-BFS-2PE denotes the risk-seeking policy test, while the ro-
420   bust risk-seeking policy test is CADSR-NMSE. Figures 4 and 5 show that the new policy actually
421   hinders the model’s performance in both R2 score and symbolic accuracy. The most likely cause
422   of this is the lack of convergence from the robust risk-seeking policy, as it relies on the top α% of
423   expression to guide better equations rather than a numerical gradient. However, when the robust
424   risk-seeking policy is combined with the BIC reward function it was designed for, we can see a an
425   improvement in symbolic accuracy and R2 score. A proof of the requirement of the robust risk-
426   seeking reward is provided in Appendix D.3 and additional analysis in actual learning processes
427   is done in Appendix C.2. These differences in policy performance could be alleviated by using a
428
      nonconstant gradient with a robust risk-seeking gradient, but this is left for further research.
429   Complexity vs. Fittness Reward. Lastly, we tested the BIC reward function compared to NMSE,
430   and two newer regularized reward functions introduces with TPSR and SPL, as specified in (4) and
431   (4), respectively. These four tests are labeled CADSR-BIC, CADSR-NMSE, CADSR-SPL, and
      CADSR-TPSR. Figures 4 and 5 show that BIC can consistently improve the model’s performance in

507   5.3   Pre-Trained Transformer
508
509   Although pre-training is not the focus of CADSR, we wanted to see if CADSR has the potential to
510   be improved through pre-training. One of the benefits of a transformer is pre-training, which can be
511   used to add information about the entire system. We generated a random dataset from pre-training
512
      by randomly sampling expression trees; a more in-depth explanation is left in Appendix E. In order
      to do pre-training, we need to add information about the problem into the sequence, which we do by
513
      adding additional channels into the sequence to contain the (x, y) information. We pre-trained the
514
      method using risk-seeking reinforcement learning to maintain the same environment that CADSR
515   runs in. We tested the pre-trained method on two problems from SRBench that have a dimensionality
516   of 3 and 0% noise to match the pre-training dataset. Lastly, we leveraged the transformer’s log
517   scaling rule by increasing the transformer’s size to reduce training time Kaplan et al. (2020).
518
      Overall, the pre-trained version of CADSR drastically improved equations that were similar to the
519
      training set. We can see that the Feynman I 12 4 has worse performance, which is most likely
520
      due to the fact the training dataset has a large number of sin, cos, log, and exp functions as they are
521   equally likely to be sampled, whereas in most cases they are uncommon. This change in performance
522   is further highlighted by the performance of the other two equations. Overall, we can see that pre-
523   training has the potential to be highly beneficial to this method and is left for further research.
524
525   5.4   Runtime
526
527   We conducted a runtime comparison between gplearn, DSR, and CADSR and found that CADSR
528
      had comparable runtimes at 0.00157 seconds per equation as compared to 0.00105 seconds per
      equation for gplearn, and 0.0014 seconds per equation for DSR. Furthermore, we found an that the
529
      BFS ordering reduced runtime by 50% and that the pre-trained CADSR increases runtime by nearly
530
      1500%. The exact details of the study are given in Appendix F.

      6     Conclusion
533
534   We have presented CADSR, a new symbolic regression approach based on reinforcement learning.
535   On standard SR benchmark problems, CADSR shows promising performance. The ablation study
536   confirms the effectiveness of each component of our method. Nonetheless, our current work has two
537   limitations. First, the implementation is inefficient, especially for the expression sampling coupled
538   with validation rules. This leads to a slow training process. Second, we lack an early stopping
539   mechanism to further reduce the training cost and prevent useless exploration. In the future, we plan
      to address these limitations and use our method in more practical applications.

819   C.2            Tail Barrier Analysis
820
821   Finally, we examined the learning behavior with our robust risk-seeking policy. To this end, we
822   tested on the Strogatz predprey1 problem in SRBench. We ran our method for one trial and then
823   replaced our policy with the risk-seeking policy as used in DSR (see (2)) to run another trial. The
824   random seeds for the two runs were set to be the same. In Fig. 10a and 11a, we report how the best
825   reward (among the sampled expressions at each step), the median reward, Rα , and the median of
826
      the top α% rewards (denoted as Median Top α%), varied along with the training epochs. Obviously,
      when the median of the top α% rewards are identical to Rα , many weights (around 50%) in the risk-
827
      seeking policy (2) become zero (since R(τ (i) ) = Rα ), resulting zero gradients for the corresponding
828
      expressions. This can be viewed a “partial” tail barrier. To examine how many top α% expressions
829
      are pruned due to their gradients being roughly zeroed out, we used a threshold 10−3 and report the
830   portion of such expressions at each epoch in Fig. 10b and 11b. From Fig. 10a, we can see during
831   the running of original risk-seeking policy, we encountered three significant events at epochs 250,
832   975, and 1750. In these events, the median of the top α% rewards almost overlap with Rα , causing
833   a large portion of the top α% expressions pruned due to that their gradients were nearly zeroed out.
834   Accordingly, the learning afterwards can be more dominated by entropy bonus (see (3)) rather than
835   those best-performed expressions. By contrast, while during the course of our method, there are
836   also quite a few events when the median of the top α% almost overlap with Rα , no expression in the
837   top α% have their gradients zeroed out, since every such expression is assigned a constant reward λ;
838
      see (9) and (10). Therefore, the update of the model parameters will always leverage the information
      from all top α% expressions, and hence can effectively avoid over-exploration.

948   E     Pre-trained Transformer
949
950   E.1   Dataset Generation
951
952   In order to generate the dataset to train the transformer on, we randomly generated expression trees
953   and sampled multiple constants for each expression tree while maintain uniqueness of the expression
954   tree skeleton. Each expression tree was randomly generated by sampling an uniform policy in
955
      a breadth first search ordering. Since the uniform policy gave each token equal probability, the
      generated expressions had a higher number of unary functions than typical of the Feynman problems.
956
      For each expression, a random set of x was sampled from a uniform distribution on the interval
957
      (−5, 5) and used to compute y = τ (x). We stored the breadth first search ordering, the positions,
958   and the (x, y) to train the transformer on. The dimensionality of the problems to three for this
959   feasibility test. The final dataset contained ∼ 80, 000 expressions.
960
961   E.2   Training
962
963   The model was trained in the same reinforcement learning framework that CADSR use for a single
964   problem with an additional 250 points attached to the sequence. The additional 250 points was
965   selected as a balance between information, and increased compute time. We found this training
966   method to work best as it allowed for the transformer to learn the relationship between the dataset and
967   expression trees without covering towards a single expression. The model was trained for ∼ 5000
968
      epochs on an RTX 3080 over the course of 2 days.
969   Alternatively, we tried training the model in a supervised learning framework. This training method
970   failed to work well, as during testing the model converged very rapidly to a finite number of equa-
971   tions. This failure is not indicative of supervised learning pre-training with CADSR being impossi-
      ble, but needing more than a naive implementation.
