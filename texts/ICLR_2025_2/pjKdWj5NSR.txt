011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This work addresses the issue of recurrent false positive classification in object
014            detection. We consider two experimental setups imitating real-world scenarios
015            that lead to such errors: i) erroneous annotations, ii) non-objects that resemble
016            actual objects. We show that resulting models can be corrected efficiently using a
017
               two-step protocol that leverages false positive annotations. For the first step, we
               present and compare two correction approaches that guide false positives toward
018
               true negatives, in either the latent or the logit space. The second step then con-
019
               sists in standard continuous fine-tuning on correct annotations. The latent guid-
020            ance framework relies on a decoder that maps the bounding box of a given false
021            positive to its target true negative embedding. The decoder is trained as part of an
022            autoencoder, where appropriate true negative samples are generated by a learnable
023            Gaussian mixture model in the latent space. By leveraging the properties of the
024            Wasserstein distance, the mixture model is optimized through standard backprop-
025            agation. In both experimental setups, the two correction methods significantly
026            outperform standard continuous fine-tuning on correct annotations and demon-
027            strate competitive performance when compared to models retrained from scratch
028
               on correct annotations. In particular, in the second experimental setup, the latent
               guidance framework consistently outperforms these models, effectively enhanc-
029
               ing detection performance at the cost of supplementary false positive annotations.
030
               Additionally, the proposed techniques prove effective in a few-shot learning con-
031            text.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036      The capacity to identify and locate objects is a fundamental aspect of computer vision, provid-
037   ing the basis for a vast array of applications, including autonomous driving, surveillance, robotics,
038   and medical imaging. This process entails not only the identification of objects within an image
039   but also precisely localizing them through bounding boxes. In recent years, significant advance-
040   ments in deep learning, particularly with Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), have
041   greatly enhanced the accuracy and efficiency of object detection models. Despite these advance-
042   ments, recurrent errors remain a major challenge, hindering the performance and generalization of
043   these systems in industrial applications.
044   Errors in object detection can manifest in various forms, such as false positives (FPs), false negatives
045   (FNs), misclassifications, and localization errors (Bolya et al., 2020). These errors often arise due
046   to factors such as occlusion, varying object scales, complex backgrounds, or class imbalance in the
047   training data. While localization errors stem from inaccurate bounding box predictions, other errors
048   are typically the result of object misclassification. This study specifically addresses recurrent FP
049   classification errors, where the model consistently detects an object that should not be identified,
050
      e.g. people on billboards as instances of real people. In this work, we examine two experimental
      setups that contribute to the occurrence of such recurrent errors:
051
052
            i) A model fNoisy trained on a noisy dataset DNoisy , where certain instances of recurrent FPs
053
               are incorrectly labeled as objects.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
           ii) A model fTrue trained on a well-annotated dataset DTrue , where instances of recurrent FPs
055            are rightfully not annotated. However, these instances bear resemblance to another object
056            that is to be detected, causing fTrue to misidentify them as true objects.
057
058   The objective of this work is not only to correct the FP errors caused by one of the aforementioned
059   conditions, but simultaneously to ensure that these corrections do not negatively impact the model’s
060   performance on the rest of the dataset. By addressing these recurrent errors, our aim is to enhance
061   the overall performance of the model. Ultimately, this study seeks to contribute to the field of object
062   detection by introducing methodologies and insights that can be generalized across different datasets
063   and detection frameworks.
064   Our research is based on DETR model (Carion et al., 2020). For the correction process, we assume
065   that we have access to a corrective dataset DC , which is a subset of the correct dataset DTrue where
066   recurrent FPs are additionally annotated as ’FP’.
067
068   Motivations To improve fNoisy , one solution would be to retrain the model from scratch on DTrue .
069   Nevertheless, this approach is often impractical due to excessive computational time. Furthermore,
070   there are cases where the whole original training data may no longer be available. Therefore, a more
071   viable alternative is to develop correction frameworks based on continuous fine-tuning of the learned
072   model, preserving the knowledge gained from previous training data. Moreover, in the case of fTrue ,
073
      retraining on DTrue would be ineffective as it was already trained on this dataset in the first place.
074
075   Contributions We propose two innovative correction frameworks that guide FPs toward TNs in
076
      either the latent space or the logit space. The latent guidance framework leverages an autoencoder
      where a learnable Gaussian mixture model generates the embeddings of appropriate TNs, and a
077
      straightforward decoder retrieves the TN embedding given a bounding box. We utilize the properties
078
      of the Wasserstein distance to train the Gaussian mixture model through standard backpropagation.
079   Finally, we assess and compare the outcomes across these two distinct spaces.

083
084   Our work builds upon and draws inspiration from a range of research areas, including object detec-
085   tion, contrastive learning, and machine unlearning.

087   Object Detection Object detection is a well-explored field in Computer Vision. Traditional de-
088   tectors, such as HOG (Dalal & Triggs, 2005) and DPM (Felzenszwalb et al., 2010), relied on hand-
089   crafted image features as priors. However, the advent of end-to-end neural network-based methods
090
      revolutionized the field over the past decade, beginning with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)
      (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), and more recently, Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021).
091
      Unlike CNN, which rely on convolutional layers, ViT use attention mechanisms to capture global
092
      dependencies across an image. Modern deep learning-based object detectors can be broadly cate-
093   gorized into two main architectures: single-stage detectors, such as YOLO (Redmon et al., 2016)
094   and DETR (Carion et al., 2020), and two-stage detectors, like those based on the R-CNN family of
095   models (Girshick et al., 2014).
096
097   DETR In 2020, Carion et al. (2020) introduced an innovative one-stage architecture that leverages
098   ViT. After a ResNet backbone (He et al., 2015), the image features are extracted and then processed
099   by a transformer encoder, which captures global dependencies across the entire image through self-
100   attention. They are then used for cross-attention in the decoder. The decoder takes N learnable
101   object queries as input and applies a series of self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms with
102   the encoder’s image feature embeddings. The result is N potential objects in a latent space Z. Two
103   Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLP), MLPclass and MLPbbox , then map each potential object to a class
104
      label and a bounding box, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Since the number of object queries N is fixed, the
      model can predict a ’no-object’ class, denoted as ∅.
105

107   truth objects (or ∅ for no-object predictions), the loss of DETR consists of two components: one for
      classification and another for localization accuracy. Following the notations from the original paper:

136   Machine Unlearning Machine Unlearning (MU) serves as a data forgetting mechanism that aligns
137   with regulations like ”The Right to be Forgotten” under GDPR (Zhang et al., 2024). It aims to adjust
138   a trained model so that it behaves as though certain data has never been encountered, thereby pre-
139   serving performance while facilitating the removal of specific samples. Applications of this concept,
140   particularly in class forgetting, are explored by Tarun et al. (2024), who propose a framework utiliz-
141   ing data augmentation. This method involves an impair step to unlearn the forget classes, followed
142   by a repair step to restore accuracy on retained classes. There is a key distinction between MU and
143   our approach: while MU aims to forget specific samples, our objective is to generalize across all
144
      samples of the same type.
145
146
147   Contrastive Learning The objective of contrastive learning is to construct an embedding space
148
      where similar samples are close together and dissimilar samples are farther apart. A wealth of re-
      search has developed various frameworks and methodologies with different loss functions, yielding
149
      increasingly sophisticated results (Schroff et al., 2015; Sohn, 2016; Chen et al., 2020). Initially ef-
150
      fective in unsupervised and self-supervised contexts, contrastive learning has also shown success in
151   supervised learning (Radford et al., 2021; Khosla et al., 2021). Most recent studies utilize a similar
152   loss function, analogous to a cross-entropy loss in the embedding space.
153
154
155
      False Positive Suppression Cheng et al. (2020) propose decoupling classification refinement from
156   localization tasks, utilizing one model for bounding box predictions and another for class predictions
157   based on these candidates. This approach transforms the model into a two-stage detector, which is
158   not our objective here. Chen et al. (2020) tackle the problem of FPs in domain adaptation for pedes-
159   trian detection. Their key contribution is the introduction of an unsupervised re-ranking mechanism
160   that clusters bounding boxes and re-ranks them to suppress FPs, addressing domain shifts without
161   the need for annotated data. Although impressive, their results still lag behind oracle models trained
      on annotated data.

      3.1     C ORE CONCEPT
165
166      Two correction frameworks are developed, both based on the same underlying concept of shift-
167   ing FPs toward TNs. From an optimization perspective, this can be achieved by minimizing the
168   discrepancy between FPs and TNs. We focus on two distinct spaces for that. The first is the logit
169   space, which directly influences the model’s class predictions. The second is the latent space Z,
170   situated one level deeper, prior to the classifier MLPclass and the bounding box predictor MLPbbox .
171
      This space is deemed pertinent because it encompasses all the extracted features necessary for both
      class prediction and localization prediction of a potential object.
172
173
         Our approach involves two steps and draws inspiration from the methodology introduced by Tarun
174
      et al. (2024). The process begins with a guide step, where, notably, FPs are moved toward TNs by
175
      minimizing Eq. 2. This step updates the model’s weights, which may inadvertently affect the accu-
176   racy for the remaining objects. To mitigate this, we introduce a repair step to restore the performance
177   on the remaining objects, achieved by fine-tuning the model on DTrue using only the standard DETR
178   loss.

185      We begin by developing the Logit Guidance Framework (LoGF), which aims to transform FPs
186   into TNs within the logit space. To convert FPs into TNs, the cross-entropy (CE) loss is utilized:

193

198   3.3.1    AUTOENCODER
199
200      We continue by developing the Latent Guidance Framework (LaGF), which aims to guide FPs
201   toward TNs within the latent space Z. The objective is to establish a more effective clustering
202   structure that assists the classifier in making more accurate predictions.
203   Unlike the logit space, where the target TN to which all the FPs should move is straightforward (∅),
204   Z is a high-dimensional space where many points represent TNs.
205
      Since Z encodes both the class and the bounding box of an object simultaneously, objects that share
206
      similarities (class and bounding box) should be proximate in this space. Therefore, a suitable TN
207   candidate for a given FP is one that shares the greatest similarity with that FP. Given that the class of
208   the TN is already determined, only its position remains to be defined. We can define an appropriate
209   TN for a FP as follows:

312   Weight reparameterization trick In order to minimise the aforementioned loss with respect to
313   the parameters of the GMM ({πi , µi , Σi }Ki=1 ), gradient descent and classic backpropagation are em-
314   ployed. Nevertheless, it is not possible to perform backpropagation through a sample derived from a
315
      random variable X. Kingma & Welling (2022) proposed a reparameterization trick to sample from
      a normal distribution. However, to sample from a GMM, it is first necessary to sample from a cate-
316
      gorical distribution to select which Gaussian should be sampled. This is a known issue that Graves
317
      (2016) tackles for all continuous multivariate distributions with a differentiable density function by
318   computing explicitly the backpropagation over the weights. Nevertheless, we propose an alternative
319   method using conventional backpropagation that leverages the properties of Wasserstein distance,
320   which allows weighting of all data points within the histogram. Hence, we sample the same number
321   of points per Gaussian and then apply weighting within the histogram of the corresponding bound-
322   ing box distribution. More precisely, if we sample m points from the i-th Gaussian, each sample will
323   be weighted by a factor of πmi within the histogram. The weight reparameterization trick is detailed
      in the autoencoder training algorithm described in A.1 and the proof is provided in A.2.

375
376     To assess the performance of the model before and after the correction, we use mean Average
377   Precision (mAP). mAP averages precision over different recall thresholds for each class, and then

      by steps of 0.05) to take into account the localization accuracy. In the single-class case, we also

395   i) Correction of fNoisy In both single-class and multi-class cases, our correction frameworks
396
      demonstrate superior performance in mAP compared to the standard continuous fine-tuning method,

397
      method can, in fact, be viewed as a version of our correction framework without the guide step. The
398

399   ∼ +18) compared to the improvement with the classic continuous fine-tuning method (∼ +2 and

403   and LaGF. In addition to demonstrating the superiority of guidance methods over continuous fine-
404   tuning in terms of error correction, the multi-class case shows that none of the methods (including
405   guidance-based approaches) interfere with the detection of objects from other classes. Furthermore,
406   LaGF consistently outperforming the target models fTrue and fOracle in terms of mAP, proving more
407
      stable than the other correction methods.

503   7   C ONCLUSION
504
505      We introduced two novel frameworks for correcting recurrent FP classification errors in object
506   detection. These frameworks focus on guiding FPs toward TNs in either the latent space or the
507   logit space. Identifying suitable TNs in the latent space proved to be a challenging task, which we
508   addressed by using an autoencoder architecture that incorporates a learnable GMM and a straight-
509   forward decoder. By applying these correction mechanisms, we achieved significant and consistent
510   improvements in model performance, with the latent guidance framework being especially effective.
511   Future work could extend the latent guidance framework to other object detection architectures.
512   Additionally, an interesting direction would be to correct other recurring confusion errors, where
513   instances of one class are often misclassified as another. In such cases, the framework could guide
514   these FPs back to their true class, rather than to ∅. Moreover, we believe that this correction frame-
515   work can be extended to any classification task, beyond object detection.

671   A.3   D ESIGN CHOICES FOR THE AUTOENCODER
672
673   We chose to train the GMM and the decoder simultaneously using an autoencoder, though this
674   approach might seem suboptimal at first. Indeed, the GMM initially fails to generate suitable training
675
      samples for the decoder, leading the latter to learn from inaccurate samples during the first epochs.
      A potential alternative is to first train the GMM separately, followed by training the decoder with
676
      the GMM frozen. This method is expected to improve stability and accelerate the overall process.
677
      Nevertheless, as demonstrated in Fig. 5, this approach does not work as intended. While the GMM
678   performs well and generates target TNs for similar FPs in DC , the decoder struggles to retrieve
679   the embedding. The inability to correctly predict the position of the embedding of a TN given a
680   bounding box is due to the average    variance of
                             PK  Pdim(Z)               Normal distributions that compose the GMM (which
681                       1
      can be defined as K i=1           j=1 [Σi ]j,j ) which produces suitable samples but spans a large
682
      region in Z. Consequently, the decoder faces an overly difficult task, as two samples generated by
683
      the GMM could be far apart in Z despite having nearly identical bounding boxes. Interestingly,
684
      when the GMM is initialized with the current latent distribution of FPs in DC , it shows low variance,
685   indicating the feasibility of achieving a more focused distribution.
686
687   Thus, training the GMM and decoder together within an end-to-end autoencoder introduces a form
688
