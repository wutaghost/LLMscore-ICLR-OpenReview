                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               LLM watermarks stand out as a promising way to attribute ownership of LLM-
010
               generated text. One threat to watermark credibility comes from spoofing attacks,
011            where an unauthorized third party forges the watermark, enabling it to falsely
012            attribute arbitrary texts to a particular LLM. While recent works have demonstrated
013            that state-of-the-art schemes are in fact vulnerable to spoofing, they lack deeper
014            qualitative analysis of the texts produced by spoofing methods. In this work, we
015            for the first time reveal that there are observable differences between genuine and
016            spoofed watermark texts. Namely, we show that regardless of their underlying ap-
017            proach, all current learning-based spoofing methods consistently leave observable
018            artifacts in spoofed texts, indicative of watermark forgery. We build upon these
019
               findings to propose rigorous statistical tests that reliably reveal the presence of such
               artifacts, effectively discovering that a watermark was spoofed. Our experimental
020
               evaluation shows high test power across all current learning-based spoofing meth-
021
               ods, providing insights into their fundamental limitations, and suggesting a way to
022            mitigate this threat.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      The improving abilities of large language models (LLMs) to generate human-like text at scale
027   (Bubeck et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024) come with a growing risk of potential misuse. Hence,
028   reliable detection of machine-generated text becomes increasingly important. Researchers have
029   proposed the concept of watermarking: augmenting generated text with an imperceptible signal that
030   can later be detected to attribute ownership of a text to a specific LLM (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023;
031   Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Christ et al., 2024). Major LLM companies have pledged to watermark their
032   models (Bartz & Hu, 2023), and regulators actively advocate for their use (Biden, 2023; CEU, 2024).
033   However, recent works have demonstrated targeted attacks on watermarks that allow for removing
034   the watermark or impersonating it (spoofing) (Sadasivan et al., 2023; Jovanović et al., 2024; Gu
035
      et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). This implies that watermarks are not as robust as initially thought
      (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024; Piet et al., 2023).
036
037
      Red-green watermarks A well-studied class of LLM watermarking schemes are Red-green wa-
038
      termarks. At each step of the generation process, using both a private key ξ and a few previous
039
      tokens (context), the watermark algorithm boosts a subset of green tokens, leaving other (red) tokens
040   unchanged. Given a text, the detection first computes, using the private key ξ, the color of each token.
041   A high proportion of green tokens in this color sequence indicates that the text is watermarked.
042
043   Spoofing attacks In spoofing attacks, a malicious actor (spoofer) generates text that is detected as
044   watermarked without knowledge of the private key ξ. Being able to generate spoofed text at scale
045   poses a serious threat to the credibility of watermarks. Spoofed text can be falsely attributed to the
046   model provider, causing reputational damage, or used as an argument to evade accountability (Zhou
047   et al., 2024). Moreover, in the case of multi-bit watermarks that embed client IDs in generated text
048   (Wang et al., 2024), spoofing attacks can be used to impersonate and incriminate a specific user.
049   While increasing the context size may seem to be a simple way to counter spoofing, this is generally
050
      not recommended, as it greatly increases the ability of adversaries to remove the watermark from
      generated texts (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024).
051
052   Current state-of-the-art learning-based spoofing techniques adhere to a common pipeline. First, the
053   malicious actor queries the targeted model to build a dataset D of genuinely watermarked text. Then,
      either applying statistical methods (Jovanović et al., 2024), integer programming (Zhang et al., 2024),

082   If the context is not in D, the spoofer is forced to select the next token independently of its color.
083   Leveraging such clues, we construct robust statistical tests that can effectively distinguish between
084   spoofed text and genuine watermarked text generated with the private key (Step 3 in Fig. 1).

      2    BACKGROUND
096
097
      Given a sequence of tokens (text) from a vocabulary Σ, an autoregressive language model (LM) M
098
      outputs a logit vector l of unnormalized next-token probabilities, used to sample the following token.
099   LM watermarking is a process of embedding a signal within the generated text ω using a private
100   key ξ (often by modifying l or the sampling procedure, see below and §6), such that this signal is
101   later detectable by any party with access to ξ. In particular, a watermark detector Dξ : Σ∗ → {0, 1}
102   implements a statistical test with the null hypothesis “the given text was produced with no knowledge
103   of ξ”. Dξ (ω) = 1 implies that the null hypothesis was rejected, i.e., the text ω is watermarked.
104
105   Red-green watermarks We focus on the well-studied class of Red-green watermarks, introduced
106   by Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); we review relevant follow-up work in detail in §6. Let ωt ∈ Σ be
107   the token generated by the LM at step t, h ∈ N the watermark’s context size (we refer to h previous
      tokens ωt−h:t−1 as the context), ξ ∈ N the watermark’s private key, H : Σh → N a hash function,


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      P RF : N × N → P(Σ) a pseudorandom function, and γ, δ ∈ R watermark parameters. At each step
109   t, P RF uses the hash of the context H(ωt−h:t−1 ) and the private key ξ to partition the vocabulary Σ
110   into two colors, γ|Σ| green tokens (greenlist) and the remaining red tokens (redlist), where γ is the
111   watermark parameter. To insert the watermark, we modify the logit vector lt by increasing the logit of
112   each green token by δ > 0. While many hash functions H have been proposed (Kirchenbauer et al.,
113   2024), we focus on two variants proposed in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023): SumHash and SelfHash.
114
      The shift by δ increases the ratio of green tokens in generated text, which is detectable by the
115   detector. Namely, given a text ω ∈ ΣT , the watermark detector Dξ determines the number of green
116                                                                       p
      tokens ngreen and computes the Z-statistic Zξ (ω) = (ngreen − γT )/ T γ(1 − γ), which under the
117   null hypothesis follows a standard normal distribution. Finally, Dξ (ω) = 1 (i.e. ω is considered
118   watermarked) if Zξ (ω) > ρ. As in Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), we set ρ = 4.
119
120   Watermark spoofing Recent work studies spoofing attacks (Sadasivan et al., 2023), whose goal
121   is to reverse-engineer the watermark enough to be able to induce false positives in the watermark
122   detector, i.e., generate watermarked texts without access to the private key ξ. So far, there are two
123   approaches that generalize across Red-green schemes, have the ability to generate arbitrary amounts of
124   diverse spoofed text in a cost-effective way, and are applicable in realistic setups: Stealing (Jovanović
125   et al., 2024) and sampling-based Distillation (Gu et al., 2024) (see §6 for a discussion of other related
126   work, and App. I for a broader discussion regarding the field of watermark spoofing).
127   Both methods query the watermarked model M to generate a dataset D of watermarked text. Stealing
128   approximately infers the vocabulary splits by comparing frequencies of tokens in D (conditioned
129   on the same context) with human-generated text, and uses this information to generate spoofed text
130   using an auxiliary LM. In contrast, Distillation directly fine-tunes an auxiliary LM on D, effectively
131   distilling the watermark into the model weights. After the respective stealing/distillation procedure,
132   both methods can generate an arbitrary number of spoofed texts with high success rate, i.e., fraction of
133
      spoofing attempts that result in high-quality (e.g., low perplexity) text that is detected as watermarked
      by Dξ . Importantly, with such learning-based methods, it is possible to generate spoofed text with
134
      no additional queries to the watermarked model, making these methods practical.
135
136   In the following, we refer to watermarked text generated by such methods as spoofed, and use
137   ξ-watermarked to refer to genuine watermarked text, produced using M and the private key ξ.

139   3     C AN SPOOFING ATTEMPTS BE DISCOVERED ?
140
141   In this section, we discuss discoverability of spoofing, introduce the problem statement of distinguish-
142   ing ξ-watermarked and spoofed texts, and formalize it within a hypothesis testing framework (§3.1).
143   We then describe the intuition behind our approach (§3.2), that we later present in detail in §4.
144
145   3.1   P ROBLEM STATEMENT
146
147   As previously discussed, current spoofing methods (spoofers) are evaluated in terms of their success
148
      rate at generating high-quality watermarked text. We aim to initiate a deeper qualitative study of
      spoofers, trying to get better insight into how well they mimic watermarked texts, beyond simply
149
      fooling watermark detectors. Our hypothesis is that due to the bottleneck of learning from a dataset of
150
      watermarked text of limited size, these spoofers, despite adopting fundamentally different approaches,
151   may all leave similar artifacts in spoofed texts. In particular, we ask:
152
153          Do learning-based spoofing techniques leave discoverable artifacts in generated texts?
154
155   Showing existence of such artifacts would provide valuable insight into the shared limitations of
156   current state-of-the-art watermark spoofers. Moreover, reliably identifying them would enable us to
157   distinguish between ξ-watermarked and spoofed texts, lowering the effective accuracy of spoofers,
158
      without compromising other desirable properties, as is often the case when trying to specifically
      design watermarking schemes more resistant to spoofing (see §6).
159
160   Concretely, we assume the perspective of the model provider with a private key ξ and a model M.
161   We receive a text ω ∈ ΣT that is flagged as watermarked by our detector Dξ , and aim to decide
      whether it was generated using our private key ξ, or by a spoofing method. Our threat model also


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      includes the case where we receive a set of texts from the same source, whose concatenation we
163   denote as ω ∈ ΣT for simplicity (see the bottom of §4.2 for details). We assume that our private key
164   ξ was not simply leaked; else, spoofed texts are hardly distinguishable from ξ-watermarked texts.
165
166   Formalization Determining whether a text ω was spoofed can be formulated within the hypothesis
167   testing framework as follows:
168
                        H0 : The text ω is ξ-watermarked.       H1 : The text ω is spoofed.                 (1)
169
                                                T                              T
170   We introduce the random variable Ω ∈ Σ and the received text ω ∈ Σ is a realization of Ω. We note
171   that the distribution of Ω under the null hypothesis and its distribution under the alternative hypothesis
172   are different. Similarly, let X ∈ {0, 1}T be the associated sequence of (non-i.i.d.) Bernoulli random
173   variables, where Xt = 1 represents the event where the token t is green, and let x ∈ {0, 1}T be the
174
      observed color of ω under Dξ (realization of X). In this hypothesis testing framework, the challenge
      is to build a statistic S(Ω) that satisfies two key properties. First, the distribution of S(Ω) under
175
      the null hypothesis should be known in order to rigorously control the Type 1 error. Second, the
176
      distributions of S(Ω) under the null and S(Ω) under the alternative should be different, enabling us
177   to distinguish spoofed and ξ-watermarked texts.

      3.2   A RTIFACT: DEPENDENCE BETWEEN THE COLOR SEQUENCE AND THE CONTEXT
180
181   In this section, we explain why spoofed texts contain observable artifacts, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
182
183   A simple example To expand on this intuition, we start by considering an example of a perfect
184   spoofer that produced the text ω ∈ ΣT , and knows the color of a token ωt , if and only if ωt−h:t ∈ D,
185   where D is the training data of the spoofer. Otherwise, if ωt−h:t 6∈ D, we assume that the spoofer
186   has chosen ωt independently of its color. Let ID : Σh+1 → {0, 1} be the indicator function of the
187   presence of a (h + 1)-gram in D. ID can be interpreted as the knowledge the spoofer has over the
188
      vocabulary splits. From above, we can assume that for all t ∈ {h + 1, . . . , T }:
189      P (Xt = 1|ID (Ωt−h:t ) = 1) ≥ P (Xt = 1|ID (Ωt−h:t ) = 0) if the text is spoofed;                 (2a)
190      P (Xt = 1|ID (Ωt−h:t ) = 1) = P (Xt = 1|ID (Ωt−h:t ) = 0) if the text is ξ-watermarked.           (2b)
191
192   Eqs. (2a) and (2b) reflect that the knowledge of the vocabulary split at token t helps the spoofer to
193
      color ωt green, which is its original goal. For a ξ-watermarked text, the knowledge of a potential
      spoofer has no influence on its coloring. Hence, we may be able to use ID to distinguish whether a
194
      sentence is spoofed or not. We now generalize this intuition to more realistic spoofing scenarios.
195
196
      Color sequence depends on the context distribution In practice, learning how to spoof may
197   require observing an (h + 1)-gram multiple times. Moreover, spoofing techniques may, albeit not
198   necessarily explicitly, have different levels of certainty regarding the color of a token given a context.
199   Therefore, we generalize ID : Σh+1 → [0, 1] to be the function of the frequencies of (h + 1)-grams
200   in D. We make a natural assumption that the higher the frequency of ωt−h:t in D, the more certain a
201   spoofer is regarding the color of the token ωt . For now, we will also assume that for each token in
202   ξ-watermarked text, ID is independent of its observed color. For ∀t ∈ {h + 1, . . . , T }, we assume:
203
                        Xt is not independent from ID (Ωt−h:t ), if the text is spoofed;                   (3a)
204
                        Xt is independent from ID (Ωt−h:t ), if the text is ξ-watermarked.                 (3b)
205
206   This dependence between the color and ID (Ωt−h:t ) results in spoofing artifacts under the alternative.
207
208   Influence of the LM Counterintuitively, the independence assumed in Eq. (3b) may be violated.
209   To generate ωt , the model provider first computes the logit vector lt knowing ω<t . Then, it computes
210   the greenlist defined by P RF (H(ωt−h:t−1 ), ξ), and increases the logits of green tokens by δ. Finally,
211   it samples from the newly defined probability distribution to generate the token ωt . The greenlist
212
      itself is thus indeed independent of ID (Ωt−h:t ). Yet, lt was originally computed using ω<t due to the
      autoregressive property of the model M, and hence may not be independent of ID (Ωt−h:t ).
213
214   To illustrate this point, consider a case where the token wt is the only viable continuation of ωt−h:t−1 ,
215   i.e., lt is low-entropy. Then, Bayes’ theorem implies that ID (ωt−h:t ) is likely to be high. On the other
      hand, the logit increase of δ has less influence on the sampling, as it is less likely to cause a token


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      other than wt to be sampled—thus, the color of wt is effectively random, i.e., P (Xt = 1) ≈ γ, even
217   for ξ-watermarked text. Hence, the events P (Xt = 1) ≈ γ and ID (Ωt−h:t ) is high, are correlated, as
218   they occur simultaneously in case of low entropy. We investigate this dependence pattern due to M
219   and confirm it experimentally in more detail in App. C.
220
      With this in mind, to properly control for Type 1 error, we need to design a test statistic S where
221
      this dependence pattern is known or can be learned for ξ-watermarked texts. Moreover, to maintain
222
      power, we aim to distinguish this dependence from the dependence present in the case of spoofed
223   text, as described above, building on intuition of Eqs. (3a) and (3b).

      on both spoofing techniques introduced in §2: Stealing (Jovanović et al., 2024) and Distillation (Gu

351   show additional results with a different watermarked model M, parameter combinations, and another
352   prompt dataset. In App. F, we show additional results on two additional watermarking schemes,
353   namely AAR (Aaronson, 2023) and KTH (Kuditipudi et al., 2023), which only Distillation can spoof.

355   Experimental setup We primarily focus on the KGW SumHash scheme, using a context size
356   h ∈ {1, 2, 3} and γ = 0.25. For h ∈ {1, 2}, we set δ = 2. For h = 3, we use δ = 4 for Stealing
357   to ensure high spoofing rates and note that Distillation is unable to reliably spoof in this setting,
358   and therefore is excluded from our h = 3 experiments. In each experiment, we generate either
359   spoofed or ξ-watermarked continuations of prompts sampled from the news-like C4 dataset (Raffel
360   et al., 2020), following the methodology from prior work of Kirchenbauer et al. (2023). For each
361
      parameter combination, we generate 10,000 continuations, each being between 50 and 400 tokens
      long. Then, we concatenate continuations (see §4.2) to reach the targeted token length T . Finally,
362
      each concatenated continuation is filtered by the watermark detector, and only watermarked sequences
363
      are kept. We use those concatenated continuations to compute the test statistic S. In practice, we
364   therefore have on average a total of 106 /T samples per parameter combination.
365
366   We match the experimental setup from Jovanović et al. (2024) and Gu et al. (2024). In particular, we
367
      use L LAMA 2-7B as the watermarked model. More specifically, in line with their original setups, we
      use the instruction fine-tuned version for Stealing and the completion version for Distillation. For the
368
      spoofer LM, we use M ISTRAL -7B as the attacker for Stealing and P YTHIA -1.4B as the attacker for
369
      Distillation. Finally, for the spoofer training data D, we use ξ-watermarked completions of C4 texts.
370   For Stealing, D is composed of 30,000 samples, each 800 tokens long, whereas for Distillation, D is
371   composed of 640,000 samples, each 256 tokens long. We further study the impact of |D| in App. E.

404   5.2   E VALUATING THE SPOOFING DETECTION TESTS
405
406
      To ensure the statistical test is sound, we check whether the Type 1 error rate is properly controlled.
      This means that, under the null, letting p be the resulting p-value, for all rejection rates α ∈ [0, 1],
407
408                                                              P (p ≤ α) ≤ α.                                             (10)
409
      We further evaluate the test power on Stealing and Distillation, i.e. how effective it is at distinguishing
410   spoofed text from ξ-watermarked text. Additionally, we show in App. G that the Type 1 error rate
411   remains properly controlled in the case of ξ-watermarked text that has been edited by humans.
412
413   Type 1 error To evaluate Type 1 error, we compare the experimental rejection rate under the null
414   hypothesis against the set rejection rate α. According to Eq. (10), if the test properly controls Type 1
415   error, we expect the resulting curve to be below the identity function.
416
      In Fig. 3, we show the experimental rejection rate of ξ-watermarked text on L LAMA 2-7B (both
417
      instruction fine-tuned and completion models) for different values of h and T . We observe that the
418   experimental rejection rates align closely with the identity function. Specifically, for h = 3, both the
419   Reprompting method with (h + 1)-gram score and the Standard method with unigram score align
420   with the identity. These results show that, in practice, setting a rejection rate of α guarantees that the
421   experimental False Positive Rate of the test is indeed α.
422
423   Test power To evaluate the power of the test, we compute the empirical true rejection rate (i.e.,
424   TPR) under the alternative hypothesis for a given threshold α.
425
      In Table 1, we provide the experimental False Positive Rate (FPR, rejection under the null) and True
426   Positive Rate (TPR, rejection under the alternative) for a fixed value of α. For T = 3000, under all
427   tested scenarios, we achieve more than 90% TPR at a rejection rate of 1%. This suggests that, given
428   a long enough text (or concatenation of text), spoofed text from both state-of-the-art methods can be
429   distinguished from ξ-watermarked text with high accuracy and reliable control over the false positive
430   rate. Moreover, we see that the Reprompting method yields higher power than the Standard method
431   for all values of T . Yet, the Standard method, in the cases where it is applicable, is computationally
      more efficient as it does not require prompting the model M, and thus may still be preferable.

499
500   Watermark spoofing Spoofing attacks are considered a threat to watermarks as they can lead to
501   falsely attributing text ownership to a model provider. Sadasivan et al. (2023) presented a proof-
502   of-concept where a dataset is generated by querying the watermarked model, and then used to
503   approximately reverse-engineer the watermark scheme, but did not provide a practically validated
504   method. Follow-up works Jovanović et al. (2024) and Zhang et al. (2024) expanded on this idea, to
505
      develop practical methods for spoofing Red-green watermarks. While Jovanović et al. (2024) works
      across multiple watermarking schemes, Zhang et al. (2024) is restricted to the unigram scheme (Zhao
506
      et al., 2024). Additionally, Gu et al. (2024) introduced an alternative spoofing method which distills
507
      the watermark into the model weights by fine-tuning a model on a dataset of ξ-watermarked text.
508
509   Additionally, works such as Wu & Chandrasekaran (2024) do not focus explicitly on spoofing but
510   could be adapted to the spoofing scenario. However, in contrast with the above spoofers which can
511
      produce arbitrarily many spoofed texts once the watermark is forged, such approaches have practical
      limitations as they require additional queries to the watermarked model at each step of spoofing,
512
      inflating the computational cost. Moreover, another range of spoofing attacks are “piggyback spoofing
513
      attacks” (Pang et al., 2024), where an attacker substitutes a few tokens in a genuinely watermarked
514   sentence to produce a spoofed sentence, simply using the robustness of the watermarking scheme.
515   Piggyback spoofing attacks lack flexibility to produce arbitrary text as they rely on the targeted model
516   for generation. Finally, there are attempts to design schemes that are more resistant to spoofing (Zhou
517   et al., 2024), which often comes at the cost of other desirable scheme properties such as text quality.
518
519
      Broader work on LLM watermarking Other directions in the realm of LLM watermarking
520
      includes scrubbing attacks (Jovanović et al., 2024; Wu & Chandrasekaran, 2024; Chang et al., 2024),
521   detection of the presence of a watermark (Tang et al., 2023; Gloaguen et al., 2024), and attempts to
522   imprint the watermark into the model weights (Li et al., 2024; Creo & Pudasaini, 2024).

525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, building upon the intuition that spoofed text contains artifacts reflecting the partial
528   knowledge of the spoofer, we successfully constructed rigorous statistical tests to distinguish between
529   spoofed and genuine watermarked texts. The tests behave similarly across the two fundamentally
530   different spoofers studied, and across a wide range of watermark settings. Our results show that
531   spoofed text can be reliably distinguished from genuine watermarked text, with arbitrary accuracy
532   given a long enough text, and highlight shared limitations of current learning-based spoofers.
533
534   Limitations While we can provide an experimental evaluation of power on current state-of-the-art
535   spoofers, the proposed tests come with no theoretical guarantee of power. We build our tests on
536   reasonable assumptions regarding the limitations of learning-based spoofing techniques. Yet, we
537   hypothesize that spoofing techniques that adaptively learn the vocabulary split may avoid leaving
538   similar artifacts in generated text. Designing such attacks can be an interesting path for future work.
539   Additionally, to have high power, our tests require that the total length of the input texts is not too
      small. Future work could try to improve the efficiency of our method from this perspective.

847   C    D EPENDENCE BETWEEN THE CONTEXT DISTRIBUTION AND THE COLOR
848
849   In this section, we study in detail the dependence between the color of token ωt and ID (ωt−h:t ) in
850   ξ-watermarked text from §3.2.
851
852   Problem statement We recall that D is the training data of the spoofer, and ID is the function of
853   frequencies of h + 1-grams in D. In §3.2, we hypothesize that low entropy is a common factor that
854   implies ID (Ωt−h:t ) is high and P (Xt = 1) ≈ γ. Under such an assumption, we therefore expect the
855   correlation between the observed color sequence x and the (ID (ωt−h:t ))∀t∈{h,...,T } to be negative.
856   In other words, we expect ZS (ω) with the (h + 1)-gram score to be negative for ξ-watermarked text.
857
858   Results We verify this claim by computing ZS (ω) with the (h + 1)-gram score for a corpus W
859   of 1000 ξ-watermarked texts, each of length T = 500. In Fig. 7, we see the histograms of ZS (W )
860   for different values of h. We see that for all h, Ê[ZS (W )] is indeed negative. Furthermore, we
861   notice that the histograms appear normally distributed, agreeing with the assumption underlying the
862   Reprompting method (Eq. (5)). Therefore, these results show that the proposed intuitive explanation
863   of the dependence due to M is coherent, and further highlight the need for the Reprompting method
      in order to build a statistic with a known distribution when using the (h + 1)-gram score.

998

999                                                                                         1/pi
1000   token (modulo nkey ), the watermark samples the token i∗ that maximizes ξij                 . Additionally, to
1001   allow more diversity in the generated text, the key is randomly shifted by a constant at each query.
1002   As in Gu et al. (2024), we denote by s the number of allowed shifts.
1003
       Given a text ω ∈ ΣT , we naturally generalize Eq. (4) by defining x ∈ RT as xt = log(1 − ξωt t ),
1004

1006   scheme, being based on a fixed key, lacks any context h that can be used to compute the N-gram score
1007   yt (Eq. (7)). Following the intuition from Gu et al. (2024) that, in the limit, their spoofing ability

1009   h ≈ nkey would enable greater test power. In practice, due to practical constraints, we set h = 5.
1010   The rest of the method remains unchanged.

       I     E XTENDED D ISCUSSION OF THE S TATE OF WATERMARK S POOFING
1189
1190
       In this section, we provide an overview of the state of the field of watermark spoofing to further
1191   motivate our work and highlight its practical implications. In App. I.1, we identify three categories of
1192   spoofing techniques and highlight learning-based spoofing techniques as the most practically relevant.
1193   We put our findings in this context, discussing the potential for adaptive spoofing that does not leave
1194   artifacts in the spoofed text. In App. I.2 we discuss how latest schemes attempt to tackle the issue of
1195   spoofing.
1196
1197   I.1   A PPROACHES TO S POOFING
1198
1199   Learning-based spoofing As explained in §1, learning-based spoofing operates in two phases.
1200
       In the first phase, the spoofer queries the model to generate a dataset D of ξ-watermarked text.
       From this dataset D, the spoofer learns the watermark, which allows them to generate spoofed text.
1201
       In the second phase, using their knowledge and a private LM, the spoofer can generate arbitrary
1202
       watermarked text at scale, without having to query the original model again. In particular, spoofed
1203   texts can be created as answer to any prompt, even the one that would be refused by the original
1204   LLM, which gives learning-based spoofers great flexibility, and illustrates the potential threat they
1205   pose. Additionally, as long as the cost of the first phase is reasonable, learning-based spoofing is
1206   cost-effective, as the subsequent per-spoofed-text cost is zero. Learning-based spoofing includes the
1207   works of Jovanović et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024).
1208
1209   Piggyback spoofing A second family of spoofing techniques is piggyback spoofing, introduced
1210   by (Pang et al., 2024), which directly exploits the desirable robustness property of the watermarks.
1211   Given a ξ-watermarked sentence, the attacker modifies a few tokens to alter the meaning of the
1212   original sentence while maintaining the watermark, interpreting the result as an instance of spoofing.
1213
       While illustrating the potential drawbacks of high robustness, this comes with several caveats. First,
       abusing the robustness of the watermark naturally raises the question of the boundary between
1214
       spoofed text and edited ξ-watermarked text. Indeed, mixing human and LM text is a realistic use
1215
       of LMs, and it is agreed that watermarks should account for this use (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023;
1216   Kuditipudi et al., 2023). Second, piggyback spoofing is limited in the scope of text it can generate, as
1217   it relies on the original model to generate the majority of the text. This greatly reduces the flexibility
1218   of the attack, i.e., does not allow the attacker to generate texts on harmful topics that would be refused
1219   by the watermarked model. Finally, the same property makes the cost of spoofing scale with the
1220   number of spoofed texts, as the attacker needs to query the original model each time.
1221
1222   Step-by-step spoofing A separate line of works considers spoofing techniques that require queries
1223   at each step of the generation process of every spoofed text (Pang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wu
1224   & Chandrasekaran, 2024), using the feedback obtained this way to choose the next token. While they
1225
       have higher flexibility compared to piggyback spoofing, a key limitation of these techniques is the
       high cost, even compared to piggyback spoofing. Further, some of these methods assume access to
1226
       the watermark detector itself (sometimes also its confidence score) to obtain the desired feedback,
1227
       which is not always realistic. For instance, in the case of the first public large-scale deployment of a
1228   watermark, SynthID-Text, Google does not provide public access to the watermark detector.
1229
1230   Summary and our impact In summary, learning-based spoofing is the most practically relevant
1231   category of spoofing techniques, as it is cost-effective, flexible, and does not require querying the
1232   original model for each spoofed text. Another advantage from the perspective of our research question
1233   is the fact that current learning-based methods are based on fundamentally different principles, making
1234   the question of their common limitations relevant and interesting. In this work, we study that question,
1235   showing that all learning-based spoofers leave visible artifacts in spoofed text, which can be leveraged
1236   to distinguish between spoofed and ξ-watermarked text.
1237   Up to now, the established paradigm in watermark spoofing literature has been to measure the success
1238   rate as the percentage of generated texts that succeed in fooling the watermark detector, potentially
1239   with certain quality constraints. Our insights imply that producing high-quality spoofed text is a
1240   bigger challenge, and motivate future research on avoiding observable artifacts. One promising
1241   direction can be work on adaptive spoofers, that build the dataset D in a way that minimizes the
       amount of artifacts in spoofed text. We leave this direction open for future work.

       I.2   S POOFING - AWARE WATERMARKING SCHEMES
1243
1244   The field of watermarking is evolving rapidly, as explained in §6, with different schemes proposed in
1245   the literature. We distinguish two approaches to watermarks in LMs. The first one is the statistical
1246   approach, notably including schemes from Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Kuditipudi et al. (2023);
1247   Aaronson (2023), which place great emphasis on watermark robustness and practicality. The second
1248   is the cryptographic approach, with schemes stemming from Christ et al. (2024), which focus
1249   primarily on watermark security and rigorous guarantees.
1250   In particular, schemes with cryptographic features have not been shown to be vulnerable to spoofing
1251   attacks. Yet, they enhance security by trading off other key watermark properties, such as robustness
1252   to watermark removal. Moreover, recent work (Zhou et al., 2024) suggests merging both fields
1253   to create a watermarking scheme that is not only more robust to watermark removal but also to
1254   watermark spoofing. However, they show that their approach trades off with generation quality.
1255   This highlights that, from the perspective of a model provider, there is no single scheme that is the
1256
       most desirable. Hence, choosing a particular scheme is a complex task that involves navigating the
       tradeoffs between different properties. From this perspective, our work provides new evidence that
1257
       schemes derived from (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) are harder to spoof than previously thought (as
1258
       these attempts can be detected by observing the artifacts), and can help model providers adjust their
1259   expectations.
