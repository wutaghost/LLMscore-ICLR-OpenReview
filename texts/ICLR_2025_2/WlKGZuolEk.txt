011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (OVOD) aims to leverage the generalization
014            capabilities of pre-trained vision language models for detecting objects beyond the
015            trained categories. Existing methods mostly focus on supervised learning strate-
016
               gies based on available training data, which might be suboptimal for data-limited
               novel categories. To tackle this challenge, this paper presents a Hierarchical
017
               Multimodal Knowledge Matching method (HMKM) to better represent novel
018
               categories and match them with region features. Specifically, HMKM includes a
019            set of object prototype knowledge that is obtained using limited category-specific
020            images, acting as off-the-shelf category representations. In addition, HMKM also
021            includes a set of attribute prototype knowledge to represent key attributes of cat-
022            egories at a fine-grained level, with the goal to distinguish one category from its
023            visually similar ones. During inference, two sets of object and attribute prototype
024            knowledge are adaptively combined to match categories with region features. The
025            proposed HMKM is training-free and can be easily integrated as a plug-and-play
026            module into existing OVOD models. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our
027
               HMKM significantly improves the performance when detecting novel categories
               across various backbones and datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Object detection is a core computer vision task that involves localizating and classifying objects in
      images (Ren et al., 2015; Lin et al., 2017; He et al., 2017; Cai & Vasconcelos, 2018). Traditional
034
      methods are limited to predefined categories, which makes them less practical in real-world setting.
035
      Open-Vocabulary Object Detection (OVOD) models (Kamath et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Cai et al.,
036   2022) expand the range of detectable categories using pre-trained Vision Language Models (VLMs)
037   to align visual region features with textual category features.
038
039
      Existing OVOD methods are mostly training-based, focusing on knowledge distillation from
      VLMs (Gu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023a), incorporating learned prompts
040
      into classifiers (Du et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023b), improving region-text align-
041
      ment (Zhong et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a), and generating detailed textual descrip-
042   tions of categories (Kaul et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). The key to their success is
043   the supervised learning on large-scale image-text pair datasets, which can better match region fea-
044   tures and textual category features. However, these methods might have the following limitations, as
045   shown in Figure 1a. 1) They struggle to learn effective representations for novel categories, resulting
046   in lower performance compared to the average performance on base categories, due to the limited
047   number of pairwise samples. 2) Even with additionally generated textual descriptions, detection of
048   novel categories remains much lower than the average on base categories, as these descriptions fail
049   to capture fine-grained visual details. How to deal with these problems is very important but rarely
050
      investigated.
051   In contrast to existing methods, human brains do not need such a large number of pair samples for
052   supervised learning. They learn and comprehend novel concepts mainly using multimodal knowl-
053   edge stored in the long-term memory, in which visual objects and attributes representations are
      associated with linguistic categories in a prototypical manner (Tulving, 1972; Bi, 2021). As shown

077   Zhang et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2022) attempt to model the knowledge for other tasks, their knowl-
078   edge modeling has the following limitations that are not suitable for OVOD. 1) Their knowledge can
079   only be used during the training process of other supervised learning models, which is unsuitable for
080   data-limited novel categories. 2) Their knowledge aligns each category to multiple object samples
081   in a one-to-many manner, which could lead to confusion when dealing with objects with similar
082   appearances.
083   To deal with the issues, this work proposes a Hierarchical Multimodal Knowledge Matching method
084   (HMKM), which can be used as an off-the-shelf module for representing novel categories and then
085   matching them with region features. Initially, it selects a few images per category to build a ob-
086   ject prototype knowledge set acting as off-the-shelf category representations for object-level match-
087   ing. To further distinguish categories with similar appearances, the HMKM additionally creates an
088   attribute prototype knowledge set by randomly cropping category images and clustering their at-
089
      tributes. In this way, attribute-level matching is performed to uncover fine-grained visual details. In
      summary, the proposed HMKM is a hierarchical matching strategy: object-level and attribute-level.
090
      This hierarchical multimodal knowledge can effectively represent categories and supplement textual
091
      descriptions. By combining the matching scores from two-level matchings, the detection capability
092   for novel categories could be improved. Our HMKM as a plug-and-play module allows training-free
093   integration into existing OVOD models during inference, which can consistently improve their per-
094   formance. We validate our HMKM on the COCO and LVIS datasets, extensive experiments clearly
095   demonstrate its effectiveness.
096
      Our contributions are summarized as follows. 1) We develop a hierarchical multimodal knowledge
097
      matching method named HMKM, which can not only detect novel categories in a training-free man-
098   ner, but also be easily integrated into existing OVOD models for further performance improvements.
099   2) We propose using object prototype knowledge for object-level feature alignment and attribute
100   prototype knowledge for fine-grained matching. 3) Extensive experiments on the COCO and LVIS
101   datasets demonstrate that our method consistently improves the performance when detecting novel
102   categories across various backbones and datasets.

105   2   R ELATED WORKS
106
107   Few-Shot Object Detection. Few-shot object detection aims to enhance a model’s detection capa-
      bilities using only a few samples with annotated bounding box. Various methods have been proposed


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      to push forward research in this direction (Li et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021). Current
109   methods (Wang et al., 2020b; Qiao et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021; Kaul et al., 2022) mainly improve
110   detection performance of few-shot categories by fine-tuning the detector’s parameters. These meth-
111   ods differ from our work focusing on the open-vocabulary object detection, where samples with
112   bounding box annotations for novel categories are not used to update the model’s parameters during
113   training.
114
      Open-Vocabulary Object Detection. Open-vocabulary object detection leverages the generaliza-
115   tion capabilities of pre-trained VLMs to enhance object detection, allowing it to identify a wide range
116   of novel categories and reduce laborious human annotations. Recent research in OVOD focuses on
117   several directions as follows. Knowledge distillation methods (Gu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b;
118   Wu et al., 2023a) align region features with VLMs-derived features, seeking to transfer VLMs’ mul-
119   timodal representation capabilities to the model. Prompting modeling approaches (Du et al., 2022;
120   Feng et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2024; Kaul et al., 2023) refine the textual embedding space of VLMs to
121   better match with region features, incorporating richer prompts to transfer its knowledge to down-
122   stream tasks more easily. Region-text alignment methods (Zareian et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2022;
123
      Li et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023a) use large-scale image-text
      datasets under weak supervision to expand their detection vocabulary. Unlike the above training-
124
      based methods, our research aims to leverage hierarchical multimodal knowledge to improve the
125
      ability to detect novel categories without extra training.
126
127   Multimodal Knowledge. Currently, several studies explore to model multimodal knowledge for
128   various vision and language understanding tasks. Ding et al. (Ding et al., 2022) retrieve related mul-
129
      timodal knowledge from existing knowledge graphs, effectively linking visual objects with factual
      answers in the task of fact-based visual question answering. Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2020a) extract
130
      useful knowledge from multimodal data to identify discriminative parts of objects in the task of
131
      few-shot learning. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2021) propose a concept-relation graph, composed of
132   recursively combined semantic concepts, for the task of visual grounding. Different from above us-
133   ing image-word multimodal knowledge, Huang et al. further introduces more accurate region-word
134   multimodal knowledge to improve image-text matching (Huang et al., 2022). Unlike these meth-
135   ods, we employ multimodal knowledge to a different task as OVOD. What’s more, the multimodal
136   knowledge we constructed is hierarchical including both object-level and attribute-level and could
137   be easily integrated into the existing OVOD models in a training-free manner.

144   The overall pipeline of the proposed HMKM is illustrated in Figure 2. HMKM comprises two-level
145   matchings: 1) object prototype matching (OPM), which acts as off-the-shelf category representa-
146
      tions and can match them with region features, and 2) attribute prototype matching (APM), which
      represents key attributes of categories at a fine-grained level and enhances the matching of visually
147
      similar categories. During inference, the proposed HMKM can also be used to improve the per-
148
      formance of OVOD models in a plug-and-play manner. The details of matchings are presented in
149   Section 3.2 and Section 3.3, respectively.

237
      The aforementioned object prototype matching primarily focuses on object-level matching, which
238
      might struggle to distinguish visually similar categories. To address this issue, we introduce at-
239
      tribute prototype matching to enhance the fine-grained matching. As illustrated in Figure 2b, we
240   sequentially introduce the representation of category attribute prototype and the matching of cate-
241   gory attribute prototype.
242
243
      Category Attribute Prototype Representation. After collecting images for each category, we can
      further construct attribute prototype knowledge based on them. Unlike creating object prototypes,
244
      finding multiple informative attributes for each category, is more complex and requires more ref-
245
      erence images. It is because a single image often fails to display all the necessary attributes of an
246   object. Therefore, we increase the number of images M used for generating attribute prototypes for
247   each category. In particular, by performing N random croppings on each image within a category,
248   N attribute regions are extracted per image. Consequently, for a category with M images, a total of
249   M × N attribute regions are obtained. Subsequently, using the visual backbone Φdet , features for
250   these M × N attribute regions are extracted individually. Finally, by employing a clustering method
251   and specifying the number of clusters W , cluster centers among the W attribute region features are
252   identified, serving as the categorical attribute prototypes. Note that we empirically demonstrate that
253   the attribute prototypes obtained through clustering are more stable and effective than those con-
254
      structed using the individual attribute regions directly. The attribute prototype representation ai for

286   4     E XPERIMENTS
287
288   In this section, we briefly explain the experimental setup, including datasets and implementation
289   details. Next, we evaluate the performance of HMKM compared with various models.
290
      Datasets. We evaluated HMKM on two widely adopted datasets, i.e., COCO (Lin et al., 2014)
291
      and LVIS (Gupta et al., 2019). For the COCO dataset, we adopt the OVOD setting of OVR-
292
      CNN (Zareian et al., 2021), splitting the object categories into 48 base categories and 17 novel
293   categories. It includes 118k images, with 107,761 designated for training and 4,836 for validation.
294   Following VLDet (Lin et al., 2023), we report mean Average Precision (mAP) at an IoU of 0.5. For
295   the LVIS dataset, following the OVOD setting of ViLD (Gu et al., 2022), we split the object cate-
296   gories into 866 base categories and 337 novel categories, and report the mask AP for all categories.
297   For brevity, we denote the open-vocabulary benchmarks based on COCO and LVIS as OV-COCO
298   and OV-LVIS.
299   Implementation Details. In our experiments, we employ models in recent studies as the baselines
300   and integrate our HMKM method on them in a training-free manner for evaluations. For each
301   OVOD model, we utilize its detector backbone to extract the corresponding object and attribute
302   prototypes, ensuring the alignment between the feature space of knowledge and that of the model.
303   The number of category images for generating object prototype knowledge T is empirically set to 10,
304   while M for attribute prototype knowledge is empirically set to 50. Following MM-OVOD (Kaul
305   et al., 2023), the primary source of category images is ImageNet-21k (Deng et al., 2009). If the
306   number is insufficient, we randomly selecting additional images from the training sets of Visual
307
      Genome (Krishna et al., 2017) and LVIS. The random crop ratio employed for extracting attribute
      regions from category images ranges from 0.4 to 0.6. The clustering method used for category
308
      attribute prototype representation is K-means++ (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2006). The number of
309
      clusters W in the production of attribute prototypes is set to 15. The λp and λa for object prototype
310   matching and attribute prototype matching are set to 0.25 and 0.3 for OV-COCO, 0.2 and 0.05 for
311   OV-LVIS, respectively. All expriments are conducted on 4 NVIDIA V100 GPUs. More details can
312   be found in the Appendix.

530
531
      In this paper, we have presented HMKM, a training-free method inspired by the learning processes
      of human brains, leveraging hierarchical multimodal knowledge to represent novel categories and
532
      improve the capability of existing OVOD models when detecting novel categories. We have built ob-
533
      ject prototype knowledge for object-level matching, complementing the categories’ textual descrip-
534   tions. To better capture key visual features at the attribute level, we have also developed attribute
535   prototype knowledge for fine-grained matching. Thus, our method integrates both object-level and
536   attribute-level matching, significantly enhancing the performance of OVOD models without addi-
537   tional training, proving effective across various backbones and datasets. In the future, we aim to
538   explore more effective knowledge representation strategies to reduce reliance on the quantity of im-
539   ages. Additionally, we plan to investigate adaptive methods for combining matching scores from
      object and attribute knowledge without adding extra hyperparameters.

      A.5    A NALYSIS OF THE C ROP R ATIO FOR ATTRIBUTE R EGIONS
788   We observed that while attribute prototypes are generally robust to variations in the crop ratio for
789   attribute regions, they are still affected to some extent. As shown in Table 13, when the crop ratio is
790   between (0.2, 0.4), the improvement in APnovel 50   is less than in the range of (0.4, 0.6), likely because
791   the cropped regions are too fine-grained to be distinguishable. Conversely, when the crop ratio is
792   between (0.6, 0.8), the attribute prototypes become more similar to object prototypes, failing to fully
793   capture the fine-grained details of attribute matching, resulting in less improvement compared to the
794   crop ratio range of (0.4, 0.6). Therefore, we adopt a crop ratio of (0.4, 0.6) as the default.
