009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Effective long-term strategies enable AI systems to navigate complex environments
012            by making sequential decisions over extended horizons. Similarly, reinforcement
013            learning (RL) agents optimize decisions across sequences to maximize rewards,
014            even without immediate feedback. To verify that Latent Diffusion-Constrained
015            Q-learning (LDCQ), a prominent diffusion-based offline RL method, demonstrates
016            strong reasoning abilities in multi-step decision-making, we aimed to evaluate its
017            performance on the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC). However, applying
018
               offline RL methodologies to enhance strategic reasoning in AI for solving tasks
               in ARC is challenging due to the lack of sufficient experience data in the ARC
019
               training set. To address this limitation, we introduce an augmented offline RL
020
               dataset for ARC, called Synthesized Offline Learning Data for Abstraction and
021            Reasoning (SOLAR), along with the SOLAR-Generator, which generates diverse
022            trajectory data based on predefined rules. SOLAR enables the application of offline
023            RL methods by offering sufficient experience data. We synthesized SOLAR for a
024            simple task and used it to train an agent with the LDCQ method. Our experiments
025            demonstrate the effectiveness of the offline RL approach on a simple ARC task,
026            showing the agent’s ability to make multi-step sequential decisions and correctly
027            identify answer states. These results highlight the potential of the offline RL
028            approach to enhance AI’s strategic reasoning capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Effective long-term strategies involve deliberate reasoning, which refers to the thoughtful evaluation
      of options to determine the best course of action (Kahneman, 2011). This type of reasoning requires
034
      conscious effort and allows intelligent beings to systematically plan and execute multi-step strategies
035
      to achieve complex long-term goals. Similarly, reinforcement learning (RL) agents make decisions
036   with the goal of maximizing rewards over extended sequences of actions, even without immediate
037   feedback. In both cases, reasoning involves considering a sequence of actions to reach an optimal
038   outcome. We believe that the way Q-values guide an RL agent toward desired outcomes aligns with
039   the subgoals of deliberate reasoning, particularly in terms of multi-step decision-making to achieve
040   long-term objectives.
041
      Recent approaches to offline RL combined with generative diffusion models have shown significant
042   improvements in multi-step strategic decision-making abilities for future outcomes (Janner et al.,
043   2022; Ajay et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). In particular, Latent Diffusion-Constrained
044   Q-learning (LDCQ) (Venkatraman et al., 2024) leverages diffusion models to sample various latents
045   that compress multi-step trajectories. These latents are then used to guide the Q-learning process. By
046   generating diverse data based on in-distribution samples, diffusion models help overcome the limita-
047   tions of fixed datasets. This integration of diffusion models into offline RL enhances agents’ reasoning
048   abilities, allowing them to consider multiple plausible trajectories across extended sequences.
049   We aim to apply the offline RL method to tackle reasoning benchmarks that demand advanced
050   reasoning capabilities. To this end, we chose the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC) (Chollet,
051   2019), one of the key benchmarks for measuring the abstract reasoning ability in AI. As shown in
052   Figure 1, the ARC training set consists of 400 grid-based tasks, each requiring the identification of
053   common rules from demonstration examples, which are then applied to solve the test examples. ARC
      tasks are particularly challenging for AI models because they demand high-level reasoning abilities,

054
      integrating core knowledge priors such as objectness, basic geometry, and topology (Chollet, 2019).
055   These core knowledge priors guide the decision-making process for selecting the appropriate actions.
056   Therefore, we believe that agents trained with offline RL methods can leverage these core knowledge
057   priors by learning from the experienced data.
058
      However, the existing ARC training dataset lacks sufficient trajectories for training agents with
059
      offline RL methods. To address this limitation, we propose Synthesized Offline Learning data for
060
      Abstraction and Reasoning (SOLAR), a dataset for training offline RL agents. SOLAR provides
061   diverse trajectory data, allowing the agent to encounter various actions shaped by the core knowledge
062   priors across different episodes. In this research, we synthesized SOLAR for a simple task using the
063   SOLAR-Generator, which creates data according to the desired conditions. The synthesized SOLAR
064   was then used to train agents using the LDCQ method. Through training with LDCQ on SOLAR,
065   agents demonstrated the ability to devise pathways to correct answer states, even generating solution
066   paths not present in the training data. This approach highlights the potential of diffusion-based offline
067   RL methods to enhance AI’s reasoning capabilities.

      2.2   D IFFUSION -BASED O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
121
122   Offline RL focuses on learning policies from previously collected data, without interacting with
123   the environment. However, Offline RL faces challenges, including data distribution shifts, limited
124   diversity in the collected data, and the risk of overfitting to biased or insufficiently representative
125   samples. To address these issues, several works in offline RL have focused on improving learning
126   efficiency with large datasets and enhancing generalization to unseen scenarios while balancing
127   diversity and ensuring data quality (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kidambi et al., 2020; Levine et al., 2020).
128   Recent offline RL methods offer promising solutions in complex tasks with diverse samples through
129   diffusion models. For instance, Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022) generates trajectories by learning
130   trajectory distributions, reducing compounding errors. In addition, diffusion-based offline RL ap-
131   proaches, such as Decision Diffuser (DD) (Ajay et al., 2023), Latent Diffusion-Constrained Q-learning
132   (LDCQ) (Venkatraman et al., 2024), AdaptDiffuser (Liang et al., 2023), and HDMI (Li et al., 2023),
133   have demonstrated the effectiveness of combining diffusion models with offline RL.
134
135   3     S YNTHESIZED O FFLINE L EARNING DATA FOR A BSTRACTION AND
136         R EASONING (SOLAR)
137
138   We developed a new dataset called Synthesized Offline Learning Data for Abstraction and Reasoning
139   (SOLAR) that can be used to train offline RL methods. Solving ARC tasks can be considered a process
140   of making multi-step decisions to transform the input grid into the output answer grid. We believe
141   that the process of making these decisions inherently involves applying core knowledge priors such
142   as objectness, goal-directedness, numbers and counting, and basic geometry and topology (Chollet,
143   2019), which are necessary for solving ARC tasks. The ARC training set lacks information on how
144   to solve the tasks, providing only a set of demonstration examples and a test example for each
145   task, as shown in Figure 1. To address this, we aim to provide the trajectory data to solve the tasks
146
      through SOLAR, enabling learning of how actions change the state based on the application of core
      knowledge priors. With SOLAR-Generator, we can generated new input-output grid pairs that adhere
147
      to the defined rules and created trajectory data to solve problems based on the newly generated data.
148
149
      3.1   SOLAR S TRUCTURE
150
151   As shown in Figure 3, SOLAR consists of two key components: Demonstration Examples and Test
152   Example with Trajectory. The demonstration examples and the test examples serve the same roles
153   as in ARC. Through the demonstration examples, the common rule for transforming the input grid
154   to the output grid is identified and then applied to solve the test example. Trajectory data means the
155   episode data that starts from test input s0 .
156   SOLAR contains various transition data (st , at , st+1 , rt ), where actions at are taken in different
157   states st , then the result st+1 observed and the reward rt is given. To facilitate effective learning and
158   a combination of core knowledge, we use ARCLE (Lee et al., 2024). When designing the reward
159   system based on ARCLE, it is crucial for the agent to recognize when it has reached the answer
160   state. Successfully identifying the answer state implies that the agent has understood the underlying
161   analogy and executed the necessary ARCLE actions to arrive at the correct solution. This recognition
      is critical as it demonstrates the agent’s comprehension of the task’s inherent logic and its ability to

      apply appropriate problem-solving strategies. In ARCLE, the reward is given only when the agent
163   correctly predicts the Submit operation and the submitted grid matches the answer grid. Therefore,
164   every SOLAR episode concludes with the Submit operation, where the agent submits an answer
165   and determines whether it is correct.

186   3.2     SOLAR-G ENERATOR
187
188   We developed the SOLAR-Generator to synthesize SOLAR. SOLAR-Generator augments ARC
189   trajectories by following ARCLE formalism, addressing the inherent complexity and diversity of
190
      ARC tasks. Figure 3 illustrates the data synthesis procedure, which is carried out in three steps: 1)
      Loading Synthesized Data, 2) Validating Trajectories with ARCLE, and 3) Structuring SOLAR.
191
192
      Loading Synthesized Data To create trajectories, we generate demonstration examples, test exam-
193
      ples, and action sequences (selections and operations). These data are synthesized through the Grid
194
      Maker, which generates them according to the given rules. Each task has its own specific Grid Maker
195   that defines the task’s constraints and rules, using common parameters such as the maximum grid
196   size and the number of demonstration examples per test example. By defining the rules for generating
197   data, the user can adjust the difficulty level and other specific details. At this stage, the Grid Maker
198   synthesizes only grid pairs and possible action sequence. The full trajectory data for the test example
199   is constructed after validating through ARCLE. More details about how the Grid Maker synthesizes
200   the input-output grids and action sequences are described in Appendix B.
201
202   Validating Trajectories with ARCLE After synthesizing various grids and action sequences with
203   the Grid Maker, the SOLAR-Generator checks whether the action sequences are valid in ARCLE.
204   The Grid Maker serves as a data loader, enabling it to load and validate the synthesized data. ARCLE
205
      utilizes this data loader to load the synthesized input-output pairs, perform actions at the t-th state st
      of the test example, and provide intermediate states, rewards, and termination status for each step,
206
      verifying that each action is correctly executed in the current state. This step is particularly important
207
      for non-optimal trajectories, where operations and selections may be generated randomly, as invalid
208   selections can sometimes be synthesized by the Grid Maker. For gold standard trajectories, intended
209   as correct solutions, SOLAR-Generator ensures that the final grid of the trajectory matches the
210   expected output grid of the test example. As a result, this stage is useful for checking and debugging
211   the synthesized trajectories, preventing unintended errors.
212
213   Structuring SOLAR After the trajectory validation is complete, the episodes are saved into
214   SOLAR. In this step, user can determine the necessary information to include in SOLAR. At its core,
215   SOLAR includes episodes consisting of state, action, reward, and termination information at each
      step, which are essential for training with offline RL methods. In addition to the previously mentioned

217        mask versions of selections, and other relevant information needed for different learning methods.
218        In this research, we designed the data to work with methods like LDCQ, which require trajectories
219        of fixed horizon length H . Therefore, the trajectories are segmented into fixed-length chunks with a
220        horizon length of H .

298
      task necessitates the ability to consider the input grid as an object and then perform actions based
299
      on this object. We constrained the maximum grid size to 10x10, and each episode includes three
300   demonstration pairs. In constructing SOLAR for this task, the dataset includes both gold standard
301   episodes—which successfully reach the answer state and perform the Submit action—and non-
302   optimal episodes—which follow random paths that may or may not reach the answer state. The
303   inclusion of non-optimal trajectories aimed to evaluate whether the agent could recognize the answer
304   state and perform the Submit action appropriately, thereby assessing its reasoning abilities rather
305   than simply mimicking the dataset actions.

307   examples and test examples. For each input-output pair, both gold-standard and non-optimal trajecto-
308   ries are generated. In the gold standard episode for this task, as shown in Figure 4, the steps are as
309   follows: 1) ResizeGrid to make the grid two times longer vertically, 2) CopyO to copy the upper
310   half of the current grid, as it matches the input grid, 3) Paste to apply it to the lower half of the
311   grid, 4) FlipV to vertically flip the upper half of the current grid, and 5) Submit, as it reaches the
312   answer state.
313   In the non-optimal episodes, the trajectories initially follow the gold standard trajectory but deviate
314   at a random step to execute random actions for eight steps. We constrained the random opera-
315   tions to FlipV (vertical flip), FlipH (horizontal flip), Rotate90 (counterclockwise rotation),
316   Rotate270 (clockwise rotation), and CopyO (copy the selected area to clipboard) & Paste. For

318   options (upper half, lower half, or the whole grid). Specifically, there are two options for Rotate90,
319   Rotate270, and CopyO, and three options for the others. This simplified selection allows for
320
      focusing on assessing the AI’s decision-making by sequentially combining operations.
321   For each test example, one gold-standard episode and nine non-optimal episodes were generated.
322   In total, 500 test examples and 5,000 episodes are generated. Consequently, the training set was
323   composed such that approximately 10% of the total episodes included the Submit operation at the

370

371
      single latent, followed by predicting the next latent. However, in the task used for this research, which
372   has a gold standard trajectory consisting of five steps, it is possible to complete the task with just
373   one latent sampling from the initial state. While reaching the correct answer in this manner is not
374   inherently problematic, one of the primary goals of this research is to analyze whether the agent
375   learns the knowledge prior to how actions work across various states. Thus, instead of focusing solely
376   on solving the problem in as few steps as possible, only one action is conducted per latent. With this,
377   the results demonstrate that the agent can make far-sighted decisions to reach the answer not just
      from the beginning to the end, but also through intermediate steps.

      5.2                     R ESULTS
379
380   To demonstrate the strengths of the diffusion-based offline RL method guided by Q-function, we
381   compare three approaches:
382
383                           • VAE prior (VAE): This method uses a latent sampled from the VAE state prior pω (zt |st ).
384
                                The VAE state prior is trained in β-VAE training stage by calculating the KL divergence
                                between pω (zt |st ) and the posterior qϕ (zt |τt ), aligning the latent distribution with the
385
                                trajectory starting from state st .
386
387                           • Diffusion prior (DDPM): This method uses a latent sampled from the diffusion model
388                             pψ (zt |st ) through the DDPM method (Ho et al., 2020). The sampled latents closely resemble
389                             the training data, with added variance during the denoising process. This method is similar
390                             to behavior cloning in that it operates without guidance from rewards or value functions.
391
                              • Max Q latent (LDCQ): This method selects a latent with the highest Q-value from those
392
                                sampled by the diffusion model, argmaxz∼pψ (zt |st ) Q(st , z), to make a decision at st .
393
394   The evaluation of each approach was conducted five times for the evaluation SOLAR set. The results,
395   summarized in Figure 6a, show the success rates for: 1) Whether the agent reaches the correct answer
396   state and 2) Whether the agent executes Submit operation in the answer state. When using the VAE
397   prior, the agent reaches the correct answer state in only about 10% of test episodes and submits
398   the answer in just 1%. With latents sampled using DDPM, about 10% of the answers are correctly
399   submitted, while the agent reaches the answer state approximately 37% of the time. When using
400   LDCQ, the agent reaches the answer state in over 90% of cases and successfully submits the correct
401
      answer in about 77% of test episodes. These results demonstrate that the Q-function enhances the
      agent’s ability to both reach the correct answer and recognize when it has arrived at the answer state.

      6    L IMITATIONS & D ISCUSSIONS
433
434
      In our experiment, the LDCQ method showed significant improvement in reaching the goal. However,
435   in approximately 16% of cases, the agent reached the correct state but proceeded with another action
436   instead of submitting the solution, even with the assistance of the Q-function. This issue arises because
437   the Q-function, while enhancing decision-making, sometimes assigns higher values to actions other
438   than submission, causing the agent to bypass the goal state. This suggests that the Q-function is not
439   perfectly aligned with the final objective in ARC. Notably, in ARC tasks, even when solving different
440   test examples within the same task where the same rule is applied, the actual action sequence can
441   vary depending on factors like grid size or the arrangement of elements in the input grid. The current
442   Q-values are calculated based on the absolute state values, which occasionally leads to misjudgments
443
      when submitting the correct solution. Therefore, improving the agent’s ability to accurately determine
      when to submit the correct answer is necessary for future research.
444
445   While the LDCQ approach performs well in a simple ARC task setting, more complex tasks and
446   multi-task environments present additional challenges. Unlike single-task scenarios, where the agent
447   follows a fixed strategy toward a predefined answer, multi-task settings demand flexibility to adapt to
448   changing goals or new possibilities during task execution. We expect that addressing these challenges
449
      could involve integrating task classifiers for Q-learning. Additionally, incorporating modules so
      that the agent can revise its strategy during task execution—adjusting based on evolving states or
450
      objectives rather than rigidly following the initial strategy—may enhance its adaptability.
451
452   In traditional supervised RL approaches, such as those described by Ghugare et al. (2024), stitching
453   typically occurs only when the goal remains consistent across tasks. To address this limitation, we
454   employed temporal data augmentation, which involves starting from an intermediate state near the
455
      goal and setting a new target. In SOLAR, this could be extended by using non-optimal paths as goals
      in non-optimal trajectories. However, in ARC, where goals are determined by demonstration pairs,
456
      augmenting all goals is impractical. More careful strategies are needed to enable stitching for entirely
457
      new goals not previously encountered. If methodologies are developed that can combine existing
458   actions toward different goals, we expect that SOLAR will facilitate these combinations.
459
460   Going forward, refining how the Q-function evaluates states and actions will be crucial. To improve
461
      performance, especially in multi-task environments, incorporating mechanisms that not only assess
      the state and action in relation to the goal but also guide the agent toward the most effective path to
462
      achieve the ultimate objective will be beneficial. Recognizing the task’s context and how close states
463
      are to the correct solution is essential for ensuring that the Q-function helps navigate toward the goal
464   efficiently.

467   7    C ONCLUSION
468
469   This research demonstrates the potential of offline reinforcement learning (RL), particularly the
470
      Latent Diffusion-Constrained Q-learning (LDCQ) method, for efficiently sequencing and organizing
      actions to solve tasks in grid-based environments like the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC).
471
      This work is the first to tackle ARC using a diffusion-based offline RL model within a properly
472
      designed environment, guiding agents step-by-step toward correct solutions without generating the
473   full ARC grid at once. Through training on SOLAR, we successfully applied and evaluated offline
474   RL methods, showing that agents can learn to find paths to the correct answer state and recognize
475   when they’ve reached it. This suggests that RL with a well-designed environment is promising for
476   abductive reasoning problems, potentially reducing data dependency compared to traditional methods.
477   As tasks become more complex, especially in multi-task settings, refining the Q-function to address
478   unique reward structures is crucial, with multi-task environments requiring task-specific adaptations
479   to account for varying states and rewards. Integrating modules like task classifiers or object detectors
480   could enhance the agent’s ability to dynamically adjust its strategy, promoting more flexible decision-
481
      making. This research opens new avenues for program synthesis in analogical reasoning tasks with
      RL environments, potentially integrating with analogy findings techniques (hypothesis search with
482
      LLMs) to handle a wider range of ARC tasks.

569   Latent Diffusion-Constrained Q-learning (LDCQ) (Venkatraman et al., 2024) leverages latent diffu-
570   sion and batch-constrained Q-learning to handle long-horizon, sparse reward tasks more effectively.
571   The LDCQ method uses sampled latents that encode trajectories of length H to train the Q-function,
572   effectively reducing extrapolation error. The training process of LDCQ is shown in Figure 7: 1)
573   training the β-VAE to learn latent representations, 2) training the diffusion model using the latent
574   vectors encoded by the β-VAE, and 3) training the Q-network with latents sampled from the diffusion
575   model.

624   A.2    H YPERPARAMETERS
625
626   We used a horizon length of 5 for encoding skill latents, meaning the model plans and evaluates
627   actions over a five-step lookahead.
628   We trained the diffusion model with 500 diffusion steps. If the number of diffusion steps is too small,
629   it can lead to high variance in the sampling process, potentially causing errors during the decoding of
630   operations or selections in ARCLE. To minimize these errors, we set the number of diffusion steps to
631   500, ensuring more accurate operation and selection decoding from the sampled latents.
632
      We set the discount factor to 0.5 to ensure the model appropriately balances immediate and future
633   rewards. Since the total steps required to reach the correct answer in ARCLE are usually fewer than
634   20, a high discount factor could cause the agent to struggle in distinguishing between submitting at

      B.2   G RID M AKER
731
732   For generating SOLAR, we create a SOLAR-Generator that can synthesize a large amount of data
733   for a given rule. Grid Maker is a hard-coded program specific to each task. Grid Maker contains the
734   rules for synthesizing demonstration examples and test examples, and the synthesized solution action
735   path consists of operations and selections. In Grid Maker, data is formatted to be compatible with
736   ARCLE. The Grid Maker constructs analogies with the same problem semantics but with various
737
      attributes such as the shape, color, size, and position of objects. SOLAR-Generator can generate
      intermediate trajectories by interacting with ARCLE. The algorithm of the SOLAR-Generator is
738
      designed to augment specific tasks using the Grid Maker, which can primarily be divided into three
739
      parts.
740
741   Grid Maker was built as a data loader, which is used in ARCLE. In the original ARCLE environment,
742   there was no need to load operations and selections. Only the grid was loaded with original ARC.
743
      To change this structure, the entire environment would need to be recreated. Instead, operations and
      selections are now loaded from the data loader’s description, allowing us to retain the original envi-
744
      ronment. Therefore, the process of creating input-output examples and generating action sequences
745
      works within a single file.
746
747
      Specifying Common Parts Each task in the ARC dataset usually contains 3 demonstration exam-
748   ples, with common elements observed across these pairs. In the common parts, attributes such as
749   color, the type of task, and the presence of objects are predetermined using random values before pair
750   generation.
751
752   Synthesizing Examples In the example synthesis phase, the input of the original task is augmented
753   in a way that ensures diversity while preserving the integrity of the problem-solving method. A
754   random input grid is generated under conditions that satisfy the analogy required by the task. A
755   solution grid is created using a hard-coded algorithm. For tasks involving pattern-based problems, as
      experimented in the paper, selections are made to fit the grid size, and various operations are executed


                                                               14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      either randomly or in a predetermined order. For object-based problems, the solution grid is generated
757   by an algorithm that finds the necessary objects in the input grid and processes them according to the
758   task requirements.
759
760   Converting to ARCLE Trajectories This stage involves the creation of an ARCLE-based trajectory
761   that meticulously adheres to the problem-solving schema of the synthesized examples. The entire
762   process is carried out through a hard-coded algorithm. During the example synthesis process, the
763   locations of objects may already be known, or they can be identified using a search algorithm. The
764   information obtained is then used to make the appropriate selections, and the trajectory is converted
765   into an ARCLE trajectory through an algorithm that leads to the correct solution.
766   If all steps are properly coded, it is possible to generate the operations and selections that lead to the
767   correct solution for any random input grid. These are then fed into ARCLE to obtain intermediate
768   states, rewards, and other information, and to verify whether the correct result is reached. Once steps
769   1) to 3) are correctly implemented, SOLAR-Generator can continuously and automatically generate
770   as much data for the given task as the user desires, using the Grid Maker.
771

       D    SOLAR VS RE-ARC: C OMPARING ARC DATA AUGMENTATION
973
974
            A PPROACHES
975
       During the development of SOLAR, another augmentation scheme called RE-ARC (Hodel, 2024) was
976
       independently developed and released. While both aim to generate diverse examples for ARC tasks,
977
       they differ significantly in their design objectives, underlying architectures, and dataset structures.
978
979
       Underlying Architectures SOLAR is built upon the ARCLE framework, designed for training
980    reinforcement learning agents. It uses a limited set of actions based on the O2ARC web interface,
981    which, despite their simplicity, are sufficient primitives to solve all ARC tasks. This design choice
982    results in sequential trajectories directly applicable to reinforcement learning models. In contrast,
983    RE-ARC is based on a more comprehensive Domain Specific Language (DSL) developed by Hodel,
984    featuring 141 primitives. This expanded set of operations provides greater flexibility in expressing
985    solutions, allowing for more complex transformations.
986
987    Data Generation Approach SOLAR generates sequential trajectories that mirror the step-by-step
988    approach humans use when solving ARC tasks. This aligns well with typical reinforcement learning
989
       models that execute actions sequentially. RE-ARC, leveraging its expansive DSL, generates solutions
       in the form of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). This approach allows for more complex problem-
990
       solving strategies but may present challenges when applied to traditional reinforcement learning
991
       frameworks.
992
993
       Dataset Structure and Utility SOLAR provides complete episodes with detailed trajectories,
994    including all intermediate states. This feature is crucial for training agents with offline reinforcement
995    learning methods, allowing models to learn from the entire problem-solving process. RE-ARC focuses
996    on augmenting input-output pairs without explicitly providing intermediate steps. While valuable
997    for increasing example diversity and testing generalization capabilities, it may require additional
998    processing for direct application in a reinforcement learning context.
999
1000   Flexibility and Potential for Integration SOLAR’s design allows for easy generation of large
1001   episode datasets and is highly adaptable for various experimental setups in reinforcement learning
1002   research. The simplicity of the ARCLE action set makes it easier to modify and extend the system.
1003   RE-ARC’s DAG-based approach, while not immediately compatible with sequential RL methods,
1004
       opens up possibilities for more advanced RL frameworks capable of handling DAG-structured data.
1005
1006
       Future Directions Future research could explore synergies between SOLAR and RE-ARC ap-
       proaches, potentially leading to more powerful and flexible AI systems for solving ARC tasks. One
1007
       promising direction would be adapting the LDCQ methodology to work with RE-ARC’s DAG
1008
       structures, which could involve developing new RL algorithms capable of processing DAG-structured
1009   data. Another interesting avenue would be to investigate how SOLAR’s sequential trajectories could
1010   inform or constrain the generation of more complex DAG-based solutions in RE-ARC. Such a hybrid
1011   approach could combine the simplicity and learnability of sequential actions with the expressiveness
1012   of DAG-based representations. By integrating the strengths of both approaches - SOLAR’s alignment
1013   with current RL techniques and RE-ARC’s comprehensive problem representations - we could poten-
1014   tially unlock new capabilities in AI systems. This integration might lead to significant advancements
1015   in abstract reasoning and problem-solving, bridging the gap between the efficiency of reinforcement
1016   learning and the expressiveness of symbolic methods.
