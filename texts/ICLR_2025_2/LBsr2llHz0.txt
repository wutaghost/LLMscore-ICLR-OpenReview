009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Molecular representation learning (MRL) has become pivotal in leveraging lim-
012            ited supervised data for applications such as drug discovery and material design.
013            While early MRL methods relied on 1D sequences and 2D graphs, recent advance-
014            ments have incorporated 3D conformational information, focusing predominantly
015            on atomic interactions within 3D space. However, we argue that the space be-
016
               yond atoms is also crucial for MRL, which is overlooked by prior models. To
               address this, we propose a novel transformer-based framework, dubbed Space-
017
               Former, which incorporates additional 3D space beyond atoms to enhance molec-
018
               ular representation ability. SpaceFormer introduces three key components: (1)
019            Precision-Preserved Gridding, which discretizes continuous 3D space into grid
020            cells while preserving precision; (2) Grid Sampling, which employs an impor-
021            tance sampling strategy to improve efficiency; and (3) Linear-Complexity 3D Po-
022            sitional Encoding, which extends Rotary Positional Encoding to 3D space to cap-
023            ture pairwise directions and utilizes random Fourier features to efficiently encode
024            pairwise distances. Extensive experiments show that SpaceFormer significantly
025            outperforms previous 3D MRL models across various tasks, validating the benefit
026            of leveraging the additional 3D space beyond atoms in MRL models.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029   Molecular representation learning (MRL), or molecular pretraining, has been a key area of research
030   for its crucial role in utilizing limited supervised data, particularly in real-world applications such
031   as drug design and material discovery (Gilmer et al., 2017; Rong et al., 2020). The evolution of this
032   field has progressed from 1D sequences (Xu et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019; Heller et al., 2015) and
033   2D graphs (Hu et al., 2019; Rong et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022b) to 3D conforma-
034   tions (Stärk et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023), incorporating increasingly rich physical information and
035   achieving superior performance. In all these prior 3D MRL models, atoms play a central role. More
036   specifically, these models take the types and 3D positions of atoms (or atom tuples) as inputs and
037
      focus on modeling atomic interactions within 3D space, using graph neural networks or transformers
      (Zhou et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024).
038
039   While this atom-based MRL approach appears straightforward, we argue that it has an inherent
040   limitation: it ignores the spaces beyond atoms. While it might seem intuitive to assume that these
041   empty spaces contain no valuable information and are therefore less relevant, this assumption may
042   overlook significant physical facts. In the theory of microscopic physics, the space beyond atoms is
043
      not truly empty; it is occupied by electrons, various electromagnetic fields, and quantum phenomena
      (Atkins & Friedman, 2011; Zee, 2010; Weinberg, 1995). Moreover, in many computational simu-
044
      lation methods used in physics, it is essential to consider the entire 3D space, not just the positions
045
      of atoms. For instance, electronic density distributions and potential fields are all functions of the
046   entire 3D space (Atkins & Friedman, 2011; Parr et al., 1979; Szabo & Ostlund, 2012).
047
048   This fact inspires us to ask the following question:
049         Will leveraging the 3D space beyond atoms improve molecular representation learning?
050   In this paper, we provide an affirmative answer to the above question by introducing a novel
051   transformer-based MRL framework called SpaceFormer, illustrated in Fig. 1. Unlike previous 3D
052   MRL approaches that focus solely on atomic positions, SpaceFormer incorporates the space beyond
053   atoms. To achieve this, SpaceFormer features three key components for efficient and effective 3D
      space processing:

073   Figure 1: Overview of the SpaceFormer framework. This figure illustrates the model using a 2D
074   plane for simplicity, while SpaceFormer actually operates in 3D space. Unlike previous 3D MRL
075   models that focus solely on atomic positions, SpaceFormer integrates the space beyond atoms. It
076   begins by discretizing the 3D cuboid around the molecules into grid cells. To enhance efficiency,
077   a grid sampling strategy is applied to reduce the number of input cells. Despite the discretization,
078   precise atomic positions are retained by incorporating in-cell positions as additional input features.
079
      Moreover, SpaceFormer utilizes 3D Directional Positional Encoding with RoPE (3D Directional PE
      with RoPE) and 3D Distance Positional Encoding with Random Fourier Features (3D Distance PE
080
      with RFF) to effectively encode pairwise positional relationships in 3D space.
081
082
083   1. Precision-Preserved Gridding: To efficiently process the continuous 3D space, we discretize it
084   into a grid composed of two types of cells: atom cells and non-atom cells. To mitigate the precision
085   loss associated with discretization, in-cell positions are additionally utilized for atom cells.
086   2. Grid Sampling. Even with grid discretization, the entire grid remains too large for efficient
087   processing. To address this, we propose an importance sampling strategy for non-atom cells, which
088
      enhances efficiency without compromising accuracy.
089   3. Linear-Complexity 3D Positional Encoding: We extend Rotary Positional Encoding (RoPE) (Su
090
      et al., 2024) to 3D continuous space to efficiently capture pairwise directional information. Addi-
      tionally, we use random Fourier features to approximate Gaussian kernels (Rahimi & Recht, 2007)
091
      on pairwise distances, enabling efficient encoding of radial distance information.
092
093   With these three key components, SpaceFormer efficiently and effectively processes discretized grid
094   cells. Extensive experiments demonstrate its superior performance compared to previous 3D MRL
095   models across a variety of downstream tasks. Ablation studies further validate that each component
096
      plays a critical role in enhancing SpaceFormer’s performance and efficiency. Additionally, we ex-
      tend the Uni-Mol (Zhou et al., 2023) baseline by incorporating empty points, revealing that empty
097
      space can also benefit atom-based models. However, compared to SpaceFormer, the performance
098
      gains are significantly smaller, and Uni-Mol struggles with the efficient handling of large number of
099   empty points. Together, these findings underscore the effectiveness of leveraging 3D space beyond
100   atomic positions and highlight the superior performance of the proposed SpaceFormer.

104   Molecular Representation Learning Molecular representation learning has explored various
105   modalities, resulting in diverse methods utilizing different molecular information. Some approaches
106   use 1D sequences, such as SMILES-BERT (Wang et al., 2019) and Xu et al. (2017). Others fo-
107   cus on 2D topologies; for example, MolCLR (Wang et al., 2022b), MolGNet (Li et al., 2021), Hu
      et al. (2019), GROVER (Rong et al., 2020). Some works further improve 2D MRL models addi-

      tionally with 3D information, such as GEM (Fang et al., 2022), 3D-Infomax (Stärk et al., 2022),
109   MoleBLEND (Yu et al., 2024), GraphMVP (Liu et al., 2021) and Transformer-M (Luo et al., 2022).
110
111   Recently, starting with Noisy Nodes (Zaidi et al., 2022) and Uni-Mol (Zhou et al., 2023), pure
112
      3D MRL models have demonstrated superior performance across various tasks. Building on their
      success, recent works (Feng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) have
113
      further explored the potential of 3D MRL models. While most of these efforts focus on designing
114
      new pre-training tasks based on 3D atomic positions, SpaceFormer takes a different approach by
115   leveraging the additional empty space beyond atoms.
116
117   Apart from 3D MRL, several other domains also focus on 3D conformations, such as deep potential
118
      models (Schütt et al., 2017; Thomas et al., 2018; Gasteiger et al., 2020; 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Wang
      et al., 2022a; Jiao et al., 2023), protein folding (Jumper et al., 2021; Abramson et al., 2024), and 3D
119
      conformation generation (Shi et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022b; Xu et al., 2022; 2021). However, these
120
      works are less directly related to this paper.
121
122   Enhancing Model Performance with Additional Tokens Though counterintuitive, the use of
123   seemingly meaningless additional tokens has been shown to improve model performance in both
124   language and vision tasks. For instance, Darcet et al. (2023) introduced register tokens into the
125
      input sequence of vision transformers, helping to mitigate artifacts and enhance performance across
      multiple tasks. Similarly, Pfau et al. (2024) demonstrated that using dot tokens (“...”) as chain-of-
126
      thought prompts can boost large language model performance.
127
128   The concept of leveraging empty space in SpaceFormer is related to these methods but is grounded
129   in physical principles. In particular, unlike the repeated, seemingly meaningless tokens in (Darcet
130   et al., 2023) and (Pfau et al., 2024), SpaceFormer incorporates empty cells (non-atom cells) with
131
      distinct 3D positions, reflecting the true physical distribution of 3D space.
132   Virtual Points As Intermediate Representation In the domain of point cloud, virtual points have
133   been proposed as intermediate representations. For example, Wu et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2021) con-
134   vert 2D camera images into 3D virtual points, which are then fused with 3D LiDAR points to create
135   a unified input representation. Similarly, Zhu et al. (2022a) introduce sparse virtual points to align
136   and fuse features from 2D camera images and 3D LiDAR data, effectively addressing the resolu-
137
      tion disparity between the sensors. Numerous studies (Song et al., 2023a;b; Mahmoud et al., 2023)
      have further advanced this direction, utilizing virtual points as a bridge to align and fuse data from
138
      heterogeneous sensors. In contrast, molecular representation learning (MRL) tasks primarily focus
139
      on predicting molecular properties, where intermediate representations for merging heterogeneous
140   data sources are not inherently required. Our study in Sec.4.4 demonstrates that simply adding vir-
141   tual points to existing atom-based MRL models provides limited performance improvement. The
142   contribution of SpaceFormer lies in identifying this gap and proposing a framework that effectively
143   leverages empty space information, addressing an overlooked aspect of MRL.

      3     S PACE F ORMER
147
148
      To evaluate whether incorporating the 3D space beyond atoms enhances molecular representation
      learning (MRL), we present SpaceFormer, a novel transformer-based MRL framework that expands
149
      beyond atomic positions. The primary challenge in this approach lies in achieving efficient im-
150
      plementation, since the 3D space contains an infinite number of points. To address the challenge,
151   a common solution is grid discretization, which divides the space into discrete cells, allowing the
152   model to process only this finite number of cells. However, this solution suffers from several draw-
153   backs. First, even with coarse discretization, the number of cells grows cubically, which may hinder
154   the efficiency of model training. Second, discretization can lead to a loss of precision, particularly
155   when encoding precise atomic positions, which may negatively impact model performance.
156
      To address these challenges, SpaceFormer incorporates 3 key components to enhance both efficiency
157   and performance, as described below.
158
159
      3.1   P RECISION -P RESERVED G RIDDING
160
161   In this subsection, we describe the details of grid discretization used in SpaceFormer, focusing on
      three key aspects.

      The Effective Cuboid for Gridding. We aim to ensure the cuboid fully encompasses the entire
163   molecule while minimizing its volume as much as possible. To achieve this, we apply Principal
164   Component Analysis (PCA) to the atomic positions to compute the three orthogonal axes according
165   to the eigenvectors, forming a right-handed, normalized coordinate system. The atomic coordinates
166   are then transformed into this new system, and the cuboid is defined by the circumscribed rectangular
167   cuboid of the atoms.
168   The Edge Length of Grid Cells. To simplify processing, we ensure that each grid cell contains
169   at most one atom, by setting a sufficiently small cell edge length. Specifically, the cell edge length
                             ˆ
170   cl must satisfy cl < √d3 , where dˆ represents the minimum Gaussian distance between any pair of
171
172
      atoms. Given that this paper primarily focuses on small organic molecules, dˆ is approximately 0.96Å
      corresponding to the O-H bond length.
173

177

194   3.2   G RID S AMPLING
195
196   Despite the efficient grid discretization described above, the number of grid cells remains too large
197   for effective processing. For instance, in widely used organic molecule datasets like ZINC (Sterling
198   & Irwin, 2015), the average number of cells is approximately 6,000, which makes the O(n2 ) com-
199   plexity of vanilla transformer models both computationally expensive and memory intensive. To
200
      address this challenge, we incorporate FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022), which avoids the O(n2 )
      peak memory cost of vanilla attention, allowing for more efficient handling of larger number of cells.
201
      Additionally, we propose a sampling strategy to drastically reduce the number of non-atom cells.
202
203   Specifically, in microscopic physics, regions close to atoms exhibit higher electron density, with
204   the density varying significantly within these regions. Consequently, computational simulations
205   often apply fine-graining to regions near atoms to capture these dynamic variations more accurately,
206
      while coarse-graining is commonly used in regions farther from atoms to reduce computational cost.
      Inspired by this approach, we propose a sampling strategy based on the distance to atom cells.
207

283
      Combining the above components, we outline the overall framework of SpaceFormer, as shown in
284
      Fig. 1. In summary, SpaceFormer is highly efficient: it utilizes grid discretization with importance
285
      sampling strategy, to convert infinite 3D continuous space into a manageable number of grid cells,
286   and addresses the O(n2 ) bottleneck in Transformers using FlashAttention and linear-complexity 3D

291   4     E XPERIMENTS
292
293   To comprehensively evaluate SpaceFormer’s performance, we first conduct unsupervised pretraining
294
      on large-scale unlabeled data, following previous works. The pre-trained model is then fine-tuned on
      various tasks with limited labeled data. Extensive ablation studies are also performed to assess the
295
      contribution of each component. Additionally, we present an in-depth comparison with atom-based
296
      models that also incorporate empty space to provide deeper insights into why SpaceFormer works.
297
298   4.1   S ETTINGS
299
      Pre-training Settings To further reduce training costs, we employ the Masked Auto-Encoder
300   (MAE) pretraining strategy (He et al., 2022), which reduces the number of cells used during pre-
301   training. Specifically, MAE is an encoder-decoder architecture where the encoder processes only
302   unmasked inputs (e.g., 70% of the cells). The decoder then attempts to predict the types and in-
303   cell positions of the masked cells based on the encoder’s outputs. This approach is highly efficient
304   because (1) the encoder processes only a subset of cells, and (2) although the decoder processes
305   all cells, it is significantly smaller than the encoder. Furthermore, only the encoder is used during
306   downstream task fine-tuning.
307   For a fair comparison, we use the same pretraining dataset as the previous work Uni-Mol (Zhou
308   et al., 2023), which includes a total of 19 million molecules. Details of the pre-training settings are
309   provided in Table 7 in the Appendix. For grid sampling, we set the sampling ratio m to 0.1 and the
310   sampling temperature τ to 1.0 by default unless otherwise specified. This configuration results in
311   a model with approximately 58.7 million parameters (55.1M in the encoder) and requires about 32
312   hours of training using 8 NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs.
313   Baseline Models Our primary baseline is Uni-Mol (Zhou et al., 2023), a recent 3D MRL model
314   that achieved state-of-the-art performance on most molecular property prediction tasks. Addi-
315   tionally, SpaceFormer uses the same pretraining dataset as Uni-Mol, enabling an apple-to-apple
316   comparison. We also include Mol-AE (Yang et al., 2024), which extends Uni-Mol with MAE
317   pretraining strategy. Furthermore, for a more comprehensive comparison, we further include two
318   2D graph-based MRL models: GROVER (Rong et al., 2020) and GEM (Fang et al., 2022).
319   Downstream Tasks Most prior works use MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018) for downstream task
320   evaluation. However, recent studies (Walters, 2023) have identified several limitations within the
321   MoleculeNet dataset, including the presence of invalid structures, inconsistent chemical representa-
322   tions, and data curation errors. Additionally, (Sun et al., 2022) has shown that MoleculeNet fails to
323   adequately distinguish the performance of different molecular pretraining models. To address these
      issues, we developed a new benchmark framework to comprehensively evaluate MRL models.

357   structurally invalid molecules were excluded. Additionally, we employed the HLM, MDR1-MDCK

359
      detailed description of these tasks is provided in Table 9 in the Appendix. In all tasks, datasets
      were split into training, validation, and test sets in an 8:1:1 ratio. We applied two splitting methods:
360
      (1) In-Distribution Split, where the sets are randomly partitioned, and (2) Out-of-Distribution Split,
361
      where the sets are divided based on fingerprint similarity. This resulted in 26 tasks, allowing for a
362   thorough evaluation of MRL models. The hyper-parameter search space is consistent across all tasks
363   and baseline models (see Table 8 in the Appendix). Each set of hyper-parameters is trained 3 times
364   using different random seeds, with the mean and standard deviation recorded. For all experiments,
365   the checkpoint with the best validation loss is selected, and the corresponding test set results are
366   reported.

527   5    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we introduce SpaceFormer, a novel MRL framework that incorporates the 3D space
531   beyond atomic positions to enhance molecular representation. To efficiently and effectively process
532   3D space, SpaceFormer leverages three key components: (1) Precision-Preserved Gridding, which
533   discretizes continuous 3D space into a grid while maintaining atomic precision; (2) Grid Sampling,
534   which improves efficiency by sampling grid cells without compromising accuracy; and (3) Linear-
535   Complexity 3D Positional Encoding, which encodes pairwise positional information efficiently in
536
      3D space. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness and efficiency of SpaceFormer across
      various tasks.
537
538   Future research could explore two key areas: (1) investigating the theoretical foundations behind
539   the effectiveness of incorporating empty space in MRL, as this work primarily provides empirical
      evidence, and (2) extending SpaceFormer to larger systems, such as proteins and complexes.

854   C      D ETAILS ABOUT 3D D IRECTIONAL P OSITIONAL E NCODING WITH RO PE
855
856
      To make it easier to understand, we have added more details of 3D Directional Positional Encoding
857   with RoPE here.
858
859   In 1D case, such as natural language processing, given two tokens located at positions xi and xj ,
860
      the original RoPE mechanism is designed to capture their relative position xj − xi . This concept is
      straightforward and widely accepted.
861
862   Next, we extend this concept to 2D. Given two points in 2D space with positions (xi , yi ) and
863   (xj , yj ), the goal is to encode their positional differences xj − xi and yj − yi . 2D RoPE achieves
      this by encoding each positional difference independently. Specifically, in the context of multi-head

      Similarly, this concept extends naturally to 3D: we encode the relative positional differences along
      all three axes (xj − xi , yj − yi , zj − zi ) by dividing the attention heads into three sets. Each set is
885
      dedicated to encoding the positional difference along one axis. This ensures that 3D RoPE directly
886
      encodes relative positions in 3D space, without involving any 2D projections or rotations.
887
888   From the above explanation, it is clear that the key to 3D RoPE is using three independent sets of
889   1D RoPE to encode relative positions along the three axes.

891   D     A DDITIONAL ABLATION STUDIES ON PCA
892
893   To demonstrate the contribution of PCA to the final performance, we show additional ablation ex-
894   perimental results in Table 12.
895
      From the results, it is clear that PCA does not significantly contribute to the final performance, while
896
      the proposed positional encoding techniques (RoPE and RFF) play a much larger role in improving
897   the final performance.
898
899   The results clearly demonstrate that SpaceFormer achieves strong performance even without PCA
900
      and under randomly rotated 3D inputs. In our implementation, a unique random rotation is applied
      at each epoch, exposing the model to a broader variety of orientations over time. This enhances its
901
      ability to generalize across different coordinate systems and confirms that the performance improve-
902
      ment is not due to PCA. Instead, the model effectively learns arbitrary 3D directions rather than
903   relying on ”memorizing geometries in PCA frames.”
904
905   Specifically, random rotations expose the model to a diverse range of coordinate systems with vary-
906
      ing orientations, enabling more comprehensive learning. Without random rotations, certain coor-
      dinate systems may be underrepresented during training, potentially degrading performance during
907
      inference on less common orientations. Random rotations ensure a more uniform distribution of co-
908
      ordinate systems, thereby improving robustness. In the main text, we primarily use PCA instead of
909   random rotations to reduce the molecular space requiring processing and to enhance computational
910   efficiency.
