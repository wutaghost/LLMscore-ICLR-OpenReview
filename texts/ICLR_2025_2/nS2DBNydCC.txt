008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            The success of autoregressive models largely depends on the effectiveness of vector
011            quantization, a technique that compresses and discretizes continuous features by
012            mapping them to the nearest code vectors within a learnable codebook. Two critical
013            issues in existing vector quantization methods are training instability and codebook
014            collapse. Training instability arises from the gradient gap during both forward and
015            backward gradient propagation, especially in the presence of significant quanti-
016            zation errors, while codebook collapse occurs when only a small subset of code
017            vectors are utilized during training. A closer examination of these issues reveals
018
               that they are primarily driven by a mismatch between the distributions of the fea-
               tures and code vectors, leading to unrepresentative code vectors and significant data
019
               information loss during compression. To address this, we employ the Wasserstein
020
               distance to align these two distributions, achieving near 100% codebook utilization
021            and significantly reducing the quantization error. Both empirical and theoretical
022            analyses validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Autoregressive models have experienced a resurgence in visual generative models (Razavi et al., 2019;
029   Esser et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2024). This revival is marked by
030   the superior quality of images generated through autoregressive methods, which have now surpassed
031   those produced by diffusion-based approaches (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Sun et al.,
032   2024; Tian et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). The success of autoregressive visual generative models
033
      hinges on the effectiveness of vector quantization (VQ) (van den Oord et al., 2017), a technique that
      compresses and discretizes continuous features by mapping them to the nearest code vectors within a
034
      learnable codebook. However, VQ continues to face two major challenges: training instability and
035
      codebook collapse.

065
      underscores the importance of distribution matching for vector quantization. To achieve this alignment,
066
      we employ the quadratic Wasserstein distance which has a closed-form representation under a
067   Gaussian hypothesis. Our approach effectively mitigates both training instability and codebook
068   collapse, thereby enhancing image reconstruction performance in visual generative tasks.

      2     U NDERSTANDING D ISTRIBUTION M ATCHING
072
      This section introduces a novel distributional perspective for VQ and investigates the effects of
073
      distribution matching. We begin with an overview of VQ and then identify three principled criteria.
074
      Utilizing this criterion triple, we conduct qualitative and quantitative analyses. Our empirical
075   findings demonstrate that distribution matching yields the optimal criterion triple, a conclusion further
076   supported by our theoretical analysis.
077
078   2.1   A N OVERVIEW OF V ECTOR Q UANTIZATION
079
080   The seminar work PixelCNN (van den Oord et al., 2016) achieved autoregressive visual generation
081   by treating image pixels as sequential tokens. However, this approach involves long token sequences,
082   resulting in significant time and computational costs, particularly during the sequential generation
083   of pixels. To reduce these expenses, VQ (van den Oord et al., 2017) was introduced. VQ alleviates
084
      these costs by learning image tokens in much shorter sequences within the latent space.

104
105   Training Instability This issue occurs because during backpropagation, the gradient of zq cannot
106   flow directly to ze due to the non-differentiable function Q. To optimize the encoder’s network
107   parameters through backpropagation, VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) employs the straight-
      through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013), which copies gradients directly from zq to ze . However,


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      this approach carries significant risks—especially when zq and ze are far apart. In these cases, the
109   gradient gap between the representations can grow substantially, destabilizing the training process.
110
111   To address this instability, RQ-VAE (Lee et al., 2022) introduces residual quantization to minimize
112
      the distance between zq and ze , thereby reducing the gradient gap. VAR (Tian et al., 2024) builds on
      this approach by implementing residual quantization from a multi-scale perspective. In this paper, we
113
      tackle the training instability challenge from a distributional viewpoint.
114
115
      Codebook Collapse Codebook collapse occurs when only a small subset of code vectors receives
116
      optimization-useful gradients, while most remain unrepresentative and unupdated (Dhariwal et al.,
117   2020; Takida et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023). Researchers
118   have proposed various solutions to this problem, such as improved codebook initialization (Zhu et al.,
119   2024a), reinitialization strategies (Dhariwal et al., 2020; Williams et al., 2020), and classical clustering
120   algorithms like k-means (Bradley & Fayyad, 1998) and k-means++(Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007) for
121   codebook optimization (Razavi et al., 2019; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023). Beyond these deterministic
122   approaches that select the best-matching token, researchers have also explored stochastic quantization
123   strategies (Zhang et al., 2023; Ramesh et al., 2021; Takida et al., 2022).
124   However, these methods still fail to fully utilize the codebook’s expressive power due to low utilization
125   rates, particularly with large codebook sizes K (Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023; Mentzer et al., 2024). In
126   this paper, we address this limitation by implementing distribution matching between feature vectors
127   and code vectors.

192   2.3     T HE E FFECTS OF D ISTRIBUTION M ATCHING
193
194   A Prototypical Study We begin by conducting a simple synthetic experiment to provide intuitive
195   insights3 . Specifically, we assume that the distributions PA and PB are uniform distributions confined
196
      within two distinct disks, as depicted in Figure 3. We then sample a set of feature vectors {zi }N  i=1
197
      uniformly from the red disk, and a set of code vectors {ek }K k=1 uniformly from the green circle. The
      criterion triple (E, U, C) is then calculated based on the definitions in Criteria 1 to 3.
198
199   We examine two cases. The first involves two disks with identical radii but different centers. As
200   shown in Figures 3(a) to 3(d), when the centers of the disks move closer together, aligning the two
201   distributions, the criterion triple improves toward optimal values. Specifically, E decreases from 1.19
202   to 0.05, U rises from 2% to 100%, and C increases from 3.8 to 344.9.
203   The second case shows two distributions with identical centers but different radii. When the codebook
204   distribution’s support lies within the feature distribution’s support (as shown in Figures 3(e) and 3(f)),
205   it results in a notably larger E, slightly lower U, and significantly smaller C compared to the aligned
206   distributions shown in Figure 3(d). Conversely, when the codebook distribution’s support extends
207   beyond the feature distribution’s support, E shows a modest increase while both U and C decrease
208   significantly, as illustrated in Figures 3(g) and 3(h). We provide detailed explanations of these
209
      experimental findings in Appendix H.2.

350   3.2                        A DVANTAGES OVER OTHER VQ M ETHODS
351
      In this section, we compare our proposed Wasserstein VQ with other VQ algorithms in a simple,
352
      atomic experimental setting while examining existing VQ methods from a distributional matching
353
      perspective. Specifically, we fix the feature distributions for all VQ methods to the same Gaussian
354   distributions by sampling feature vectors zi ∼ N (µ · 1d , Id ). While feature distributions are typically
355   complex and dynamic in practical training scenarios, this simplified setting still yields valuable
356   insights. We also initialize the codebook distribution as the standard Gaussian distribution across all
357   VQ methods by generating a set of code vectors {ek }K   k=1 ∼ N (0d , Id ).
358
      Our baseline includes Vanilla VQ (van den Oord et al., 2017), VQ EMA (which uses exponen-
359
      tial moving average updates and is also known as k-means in VQ-VAE-2) (Razavi et al., 2019),
360   Online Clustering (which employs k-means++ in CVQ-VAE) (Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023), and
361   VQ+Linear (which incorporates a linear layer projection for code vectors) (Zhu et al., 2024a;b). In
362   all VQ algorithms, we treat sampled code vectors as trainable parameters and optimize them using
363   these algorithms. For detailed experimental specifications, see Appendix E.3.
364
      As illustrated in Figure 5, we evaluate five distinct VQ methods using the criterion triple (E, U, C). In
365
      Figures 5(a)-5(d), we set µ = 2 except VQ+Linear. Our results show that Vanilla VQ, VQ EMA,
366   and Online Clustering exhibit poor VQ performance and substantial Wasserstein distance.
367   This suggests that Vanilla VQ and methods based on k-means and k-means++ are ineffective VQ
368   strategies, since they fail to align the distributions of features and codebooks.
369
      Conversely, VQ+Linear achieves both superior VQ performance and a significantly reduced Wasser-
370
      stein distance, approaching zero at µ = 2. This demonstrates that VQ+Linear’s exceptional perfor-
371
      mance stems from its successful distribution alignment. However, with a large initial distribution
372   gap between codebook and features (at µ = 5), VQ+Linear struggles to minimize the distributional
373   distance, resulting in poor VQ performance. This limitation becomes more apparent when varying
374   µ from 0 to 8, as depicted in Figures 5(e)-5(h), VQ+Linear becomes ineffective at µ = 4. These
375   results indicate that VQ+Linear remains heavily dependent on codebook initialization. In com-
376   parison, our Wasserstein VQ algorithm consistently performs best with relatively large values of µ
377   (e.g., µ ≥ 4), thanks to its explicit distributional matching regularization that eliminates reliance on
      codebook initialization to prevent distributional mismatch.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper examines vector quantization (VQ) from a distributional perspective. We introduce
530   three key evaluation criteria and demonstrate empirically that optimal VQ results emerge when the
531   distributions of continuous feature vectors and code vectors are identical. Our theoretical analysis
532   confirms this finding, emphasizing the crucial role of distributional alignment in effective VQ.
533
      Based on these insights, we propose using the quadratic Wasserstein distance to achieve alignment,
      leveraging its computational efficiency under a Gaussian hypothesis. This approach achieves near-
534
      full codebook utilization while significantly reducing quantization error. Our method successfully
535
      addresses both training instability and codebook collapse, leading to improved downstream image
536   reconstruction performance. However, due to limited GPU resources, we were unable to conduct
537   image generation experiments.

948   This section interprets the experimental findings presented in Figure 3. The VQ process relies on
949   nearest neighbor search for code vector selection. As evident from Figure 3(a) to 3(d), actively
950   selected code vectors are predominantly those located in close proximity to or within the feature
951   distribution, while distant ones remain unselected. This leads to highly uneven code vector utilization
952   pk , with those closer to the feature distribution being excessively used. This elucidates the significantly
953   low U and C observed in Figure 3(a). Furthermore, a notable quantization error, e.g., E = 1.19 in
954
      Figure 3(a), arises when the codebook and feature distributions are mismatched, forcing feature
      vectors outside the codebook to settle for distant code vectors. Conversely, as the disk centers align,
955
      leading to a closer match between the two distributions, an increased number of code vectors become
956
      actively engaged. Additionally, code vectors are utilized more uniformly, and feature vectors can
957   select nearer counterparts. This accounts for the improvement of criterion triple values towards
958   optimality as the distributions align.
959
960
      Analogously, we can employ nearest neighbor search to interpret the second case. When code vectors
      are distributed within the range of feature vectors, as illustrated in Figure 3(e) and Figure 3(f), the
961
      majority of code vectors would be actively utilized, ensuring high U. However, the utilization of
962
      these code vectors is not uniform; code vectors on the periphery of the codebook distribution are
963   more frequently used, leading to relatively low C. Feature vectors on the periphery will have larger
964   distances to their nearest code vectors, resulting in higher E. Conversely, when feature vectors fall
965   within the range of code vectors, as depicted in Figure 3(g) and Figure 3(h), outer code vectors remain
966   largely unused, leading to a lower U and C. Since only inner code vectors are active, each feature
967   vector can find a nearby counterpart, maintaining low E.
