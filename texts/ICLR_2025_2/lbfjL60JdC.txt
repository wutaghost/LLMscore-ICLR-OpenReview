009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Decision-making under alignment requires balancing between reasoning and
012              faithfulness - a challenge for large language models (LLMs). This study explores
013              whether LLMs prioritize following instructions over reasoning and truth when
014              given misleading instructions, such as Respond solely with A or B, even when nei-
015              ther option is correct. We introduce a new metric called reflective judgment,
016
                 which sheds new light on the relationship between the pre-training and post-
                 training alignment schemes. In tasks ranging from basic arithmetic to domain-
017
                 specific assessments, models like GPT-4o, o1-mini, or Claude 3 Opus adhered to
018
                 instructions correctly but failed to reflect on the validity of the provided options.
019              Contrary, models from the Llama 3.1 family (8B, 70B, 405B) or base Qwen2.5
020              (7B, 14B, 32B) families exhibit improved refusal rates with size, indicating a
021              scaling effect. We also observed that alignment techniques, though intended to
022              enhance reasoning, sometimes weakened the models’ ability to reject incorrect
023              instructions, leading them to follow flawed prompts uncritically. Finally, we have
024              also conducted a parallel human study revealing similar patterns in human behav-
025              ior and annotations. We highlight how popular RLHF datasets might disrupt either
026              training or evaluation due to annotations exhibiting poor reflective judgment.1

045   1       I NTRODUCTION
046
047   Decision-making, even in its simplest form, often requires a delicate interplay between intuitive and
048   rational thought processes (Calabretta et al., 2017; Thanos, 2023). As large language models (LLMs)
049
      are increasingly deployed in critical domains like healthcare and autonomous systems, ensuring the
      reliability of their decision-making processes is paramount (Peláez-Sánchez et al., 2024; Lee & See,
050
      2004). For example, LLMs have exhibited remarkable capabilities, surpassing human experts in
051
      certain medical tasks and transforming education through automated grading and content creation
052   assistance (Singhal et al., 2023; Saab et al., 2024; Gan et al., 2023).

      The concept of helpfulness in LLMs is broadly defined as the ability to effectively meet user needs
055   (Askell et al., 2021). Techniques like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)
056   (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) aim to en-
057   hance accuracy and mitigate harmful outputs by training models based on human feedback (Ouyang
058   et al., 2022; Christiano et al., 2023). An ideal helpful model not only adheres to instructions but
059   also discerns user intent, even in ambiguous situations (Ouyang et al., 2022). While helpfulness is
060   generally desirable, prioritizing it can lead to safety concerns if a model blindly follows instructions,
061   which in turn might lead to incorrect answers.
062   In this study, we investigate whether LLMs prioritize reasoning over helpfulness in multiple-choice
063   tasks where all provided options are incorrect. We introduce the term reflective judgment to de-
064   scribe an LLM’s capacity to override helpfulness and critically evaluate instructions, even when
065   they lead to incorrect answers, drawing inspiration from (King & Kitchener, 1994; Kitchener &
066   King, 2004)2 . While related to honesty—which ensures that models do not make up information or
067   mislead users (Askell et al., 2021)—reflective judgment includes the ability to recognize when it is
068   better not to follow instructions if doing so would result in errors.
069   To assess this, we evaluated the performance of open- and closed-sourced LLMs on multiple-choice
070   questions with no correct answer. We created a Basic Arithmetic Dataset (BAD) for simple arith-
071   metic reasoning and employ a subset of the MMLU dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021) for domain-
072   specific knowledge. Our findings reveal that highly post-training aligned models such as GPT-4o,
073   o1-mini or Claude 3 Opus often adhere to instructions despite being presented with incorrect op-
074   tions. Contrary, models Llama 3.1-405B (Dubey et al., 2024), DeepSeekMath-7B Base and RLHF
075
      versions (Shao et al., 2024), Qwen2.5-32B (Team, 2024) and Qwen2-Math-7B (Yang et al., 2024)
      demonstrate improved reflective judgment.
076
077   We further analyzed the influence of model sizes and training techniques such as pre-training, in-
078   struction tuning and alignment on the reflective judgment ability. We observe that the reflective
079   judgment improves as the model size increases, suggesting that this ability may emerge with larger
080   size, aligning with scaling laws. Moreover, we observed that alignment techniques can sometimes
081
      hinder model’s ability to balance helpfulness with reasoning, as exemplified by the near-complete
      drop in reflective judgment ability in the aligned versions of the base models (Yang et al., 2024).
082
      Moreover, the behavior is exhibited also by the models which operate within the chain-of-thought
083
      paradigm such as o1-mini Wei et al. (2023).
084
085   Blindly following instructions is also observed in humans, as demonstrated in our human study. We
086   hypothesize that this behavior may also lead to severe data annotations errors in popular RLHF-style
087
      datasets such as HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022). Our quantative and qualitative analysis shows error
      rates at the level of 40%.
088
089   The above-mentioned observations underscore the critical need to design decision-making systems
090   that ensure language models are not only helpful but also capable of reflective judgment, partic-
091   ularly in high-stakes environments. Ultimately, our research demonstrates that achieving true
092   helpfulness in LLMs requires more than just following instructions; it demands a capacity for
093
      reflective judgment that allows models to question, reason, and even disregard flawed instruc-
      tions when necessary.
094
095   To sum up, our contributions are as follows:
096
097          • We introduce and measure the concept of reflective judgment in LLMs.
098          • We examine the effects of pre-training, instruction tuning, and alignment on reflecting judg-
099            ment, providing insights into how alignment can impact the balance between helpfulness
100            and reasoning.
101
             • We analyze how model size influences the ability to refuse to answer questions with in-
102
               correct options. We observe an increase in reflective judgment ability with an increase in
103
               model size.

131   Our study investigates how language models handle multiple-choice questions that have no correct
132   answer among the options. Unlike traditional approaches that include escape options like None of
133   the above (Wang et al., 2024a; Kadavath et al., 2022), we deliberately omit these choices to test the

135   that no correct answer is available or provide the actual correct answer, even when it’s not listed.

138   the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU)3 test dataset Hendrycks et al. (2021),

      5.2   PATTERNS AND I NSIGHTS FROM H UMAN P REFERENCES
448
449   Our investigation into Llama 3.1’s reflective judgement results led us to examine the dataset cre-
450   ation process described in the Llama technical report. Notably, the authors allowed annotators to
451   provide their own answers when existing options were unsatisfactory—a novel approach in human
452   preference dataset construction (Dubey et al., 2024). This discovery prompted us to examine pub-
453   licly available datasets used for model alignment. We focused on Anthropic’s HH-RLHF dataset,
454
      a popular choice in the field (Bai et al., 2022). This dataset consists of two columns, chosen and
      rejected, indicating preferred and less desirable models responses, respectively (see Appendix D).
455
      It encompasses both safety-focused prompts and standard questions. To narrow our analysis, we
456
      concentrated on mathematical questions, setting aside the more complex safety and ethics prompts.
457   We employed the GPT-4o model to filter the dataset, using the following prompt:
458
459            Your task is to determine if the text given asks about mathematics. If it satisfies
460            this condition return 1. If not, or the text have some ethical issues, give 0. Text:
461            [TEXT]
462
      From the filtered results, we randomly sampled 50 examples for manual annotation. Three annota-
463
      tors evaluated a batch of samples, marking an example as incorrect if the chosen column contained
464   an inaccurate answer to the question. Our findings reveals that over 40% of the answers in the
465   sampled dataset is incorrect. This surprising result leads us to hypothesize that models aligned with
466   these potentially erroneous annotations may exhibit decreased performance in reflective judgment
467   tasks. This further highlight the need for careful curation and validation of datasets used in model
468   alignment, particularly when dealing with knowledge-based tasks.

      6     C ONTRIBUTIONS IN THE C ONTEXT OF R ELATED W ORK
471
472   Refusal mechanisms Refusal mechanisms play a crucial role in enhancing the safety and relia-
473   bility of LLMs (Xu et al., 2024; Cao, 2024). These mechanisms include safety prompts to avoid
474   harmful outputs (Zheng et al., 2024a; Ji et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b) and the ability to re-
475   frain from answering questions outside their knowledge (known as Abstention Ability or AA) (Wen
476   et al., 2024). Current research focuses on improving safety prompts and AA through better prompt-
477   ing strategies and information retrieval methods Madhusudhan et al. (2024); Cheng et al. (2024);
478   Labruna et al. (2024).
479   Our contribution: We introduces reflective judgment as distinct from traditional refusal mechanisms
480   in AI systems. Refusal mechanisms simply determine whether to answer a query based on pre-
481   defined boundaries of knowledge or safety concerns, operating as binary decisions (answer/don’t
482   answer). In contrast, reflective judgment represents a more sophisticated capability that critically
483   evaluates the validity of questions themselves, even within the model’s knowledge domain.
484
485   Multiple-Choice Questions LLMs have demonstrated both capabilities and limitations in han-
      dling multiple-choice questions (MCQ), a format widely used in benchmarks such as MMLU


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and BIG-Bench (bench authors, 2023). These benchmarks assess mod-
487   els’ understanding across diverse topics and reasoning depths (Zhang et al., 2024b). While LLMs
488   excel at straightforward MCQs, they often struggle with questions requiring complex reasoning (Li
489   et al., 2024; Savelka et al., 2023). Notably, LLMs exhibit positional bias, tending to select answers
490   based on their order rather than content (Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Zheng et al., 2024b). Re-
491   cent research has explored LLMs’ performance on variant MCQ formats. The introduction of None
492   of the above options often confounds models, degrading performance compared to standard MCQs
493   (Kadavath et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a). Similarly, open-ended questions pose greater challenges,
494   as the absence of predefined options increases reasoning complexity (Myrzakhan et al., 2024). Some
495
      models can infer questions from answer choices alone, suggesting reliance on superficial patterns
      rather than deep understanding (Balepur et al., 2024).
496
497   Our contribution: We investigate how LLMs handle multiple-choice questions when none of the
498   provided answers are correct, an understudied challenge in current benchmarks. Our work of-
499   fers insights into the robustness of LLMs when faced with scenarios where traditional instruction-
500   following behavior may lead to incorrect conclusions.
501
502   Model Alignment Recent advancements in LLM alignment focus on enhancing helpfulness in
503   responses. Key contributions include fine-tuning techniques that utilize human feedback, as seen
504   in (Rafailov et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023) and (Hejna
505   & Sadigh, 2023), which employ reinforcement learning from human preferences to shape user-
506
      aligned outputs. Bai et al. (2022) further illustrate the benefits of instruction fine-tuning for improved
      helpfulness, while research by (Zhang et al., 2024a) and (Tuan et al., 2024) addresses the balance
507
      between helpfulness and safety.
508
509   Our contribution: In this work, we explore how model alignment influences reflective judgment,
510   where models may favor helpfulness over critical assessment. We aim to isolate this effect by
511   comparing models at different stages of training, providing insights into the relationship between
512
      alignment strategies and the quality of model outputs.
513
514
      7    L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
515
516
      The datasets used in this study provide valuable insights into critical thinking in LLMs but come
517
      with limitations. The BAD dataset, designed to minimize memorization, does not fully capture the
518   complexity of numerical reasoning. The MMLU and MedMCQA subsets, despite its diversity, may
519   not encompass the full range of questions encountered by LLMs, and biases in the original dataset
520   could influence results.
521
      To address some of these challenges, we propose potential solutions that could enhance LLM per-
522
      formance in future work. These include modified reward modeling explicitly designed to value
523
      appropriate refusals, aiming to ensure models respond more effectively in ambiguous situations.
524   Balancing instruction-following with accuracy in training protocols may improve response quality
525   while maintaining robustness. Encouraging models to consistently use chain-of-thought reasoning
526   could help in domain-specific questions, promoting clearer and more logical responses.
527
528
529   8    C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   This study examines LLMs’ critical thinking when facing multiple-choice questions without valid
532   answers, revealing a tendency to prioritize instruction compliance over logical judgment. While
533   larger models showed improved reflective capabilities, we observed potential tensions between
534   alignment optimization and preservation of critical reasoning. Parallel human studies revealed sim-
535   ilar rule-following biases, suggesting these challenges may reflect broader cognitive patterns.
536   These findings have significant implications across multiple sectors, from corporate decision-making
537   to healthcare systems. Future work should focus on developing more robust evaluation frameworks,
538   exploring alignment techniques that preserve critical thinking, and investigating the relationship
539   between model architecture and reasoning capabilities. Addressing these challenges is crucial for
      developing AI systems that can effectively augment human decision-making in complex domains.

1183   The relationship between reflective judgment and sycophancy (the tendency to agree with or follow
1184   instructions without critical evaluation) deserves careful examination. While these concepts may
1185   appear similar at first glance, our analysis reveals important distinctions. Sycophancy in language
1186   models typically manifests as:
1187
                   • Agreeing with user statements regardless of their accuracy

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

              • Providing responses that align with perceived user preferences

1194          • Willingness to reject invalid choices even when explicitly instructed to choose
1195          • Providing alternative correct answers when available options are insufficient
