011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Reducing the time it takes to acquire a Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) scan
014            is an important problem in healthcare, as it can improve patient care and reduce
015
               costs. One way to achieve this is by acquiring only a fraction of the frequency
               space data and reconstructing diagnostic-quality images from it. This problem
016
               can be formulated as a linear inverse problem (LIP), where the forward oper-
017
               ator, which maps the structure of the imaged object to the acquired frequency
018            space data, can become rank-deficient or exhibit many small singular values. This
019            leads to ambiguities in the reconstruction process, where multiple images (most of
020            them non-diagnostic) can map to the same set of acquired data. To resolve these
021            ambiguities, it is essential to leverage domain knowledge and, whenever possi-
022            ble, exploit additional context (a.k.a., relevant side information) when solving the
023            LIP. We present a novel, end-to-end trainable deep learning-based method, called
024            Trust-Guided Variational Network (TGVN), that reliably incorporates side infor-
025            mation into LIPs to eliminate undesirable solutions from the ambiguous space of
026
               the forward operator, while remaining faithful to the acquired data. We demon-
               strate its effectiveness through applications in multi-coil, multi-contrast MR image
027
               reconstruction, where incomplete or low-quality measurements from one contrast
028
               are used as side information to reconstruct a high-quality image of another con-
029            trast from heavily under-sampled data. Its robustness is validated by reconstruct-
030            ing images from different contrasts across different anatomies and field strengths.
031            Compared to a set of baselines that also use side information, our method re-
032            constructs high-quality images in the presence of heretofore challenging levels of
033            under-sampling, thereby speeding up the acquisition drastically while providing
034            protection against hallucinations. Our approach is also versatile enough to incor-
035            porate many different types of side information into any LIP.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is a mainstay of medical diagnostic imaging, thanks to its
040
      flexibility, its rich information content, and its excellent soft-tissue contrast. An MR scanner col-
041
      lects measurements in frequency space (a.k.a., k-space) that encode the body’s response to applied
042   electromagnetic fields, with multiple receiver coils capturing distinct views modulated by their indi-
043   vidual sensitivities. These measurements are then used to reconstruct high-fidelity diagnostic quality
044   images. The problem of image reconstruction from multi-coil k-space data can be formulated as a
045   Linear Inverse Problem (LIP), where the objective is to deduce an accurate representation of struc-
046   tures in an object of interest (i.e., an image) from the observed measurements. The term “linear”
047   refers to the linear relationship between the observed measurements and the object of interest, and
048   is defined by a known process called the forward operator.
049   Despite MRI’s superior diagnostic capabilities, it is comparatively time-consuming and costly,
050   which limits its overall accessibility. Reducing the time it takes to acquire an MR scan is an im-
051   portant practical problem that can improve patient care, limit patient discomfort, reduce costs, and
052   improve accessibility of this imaging modality. One way to reduce scan time is to acquire a smaller
053   number of k-space measurements. The challenge then becomes how to reconstruct high-quality
      images from limited data. Undersampling in k-space renders the underlying LIP ill-posed or ill-

085   (2016), the Variational Network (VarNet) approach (Hammernik et al., 2018; Sriram et al., 2021),
086   the Model-Based Deep Learning (MoDL) approach (Aggarwal et al., 2019), the ISTA-Net approach
087   (Zhang & Ghanem, 2019) and the FISTA-Net approach (Xiang et al., 2021), all of which integrate
088   traditional optimization techniques with deep neural networks to achieve robust and efficient solu-
089   tions in high-dimensional spaces. More recently, researchers have proposed generative models for
090   reconstructing high-quality images from incomplete data (Bora et al., 2017; Chung & Ye, 2022;
091   Song et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2022), and transformer-based methods (Huang et al., 2022; Guo et al.,
092   2023), and a rapidly-expanding portfolio of deep-learning-based image reconstruction methods is
093
      currently under development.
094   Another approach to limiting degenerate solutions to such ill-posed or ill-conditioned LIPs involves
095   leveraging additional contextual information (a.k.a., relevant side information), often as regular-
096   izers or constraints in the optimization problem’s objective function (Weizman et al., 2015; 2016;
097   Ehrhardt & Betcke, 2016; Song et al., 2019; Zhou & Zhou, 2020; Bian et al., 2022; Lei et al., 2023b).
098   The nature of such side information is problem-dependent, and in many real-world scenarios it is
099
      readily available. Relevant side information can take multiple forms, including images, text, or other
      types of structured data. In MR image reconstruction, for instance, the side information could be
100
      data associated with prior scans of the patient. It could also be data gathered during the same scan,
101
      such as images obtained using an imaging pulse sequence with a different underlying contrast from
102   the target pulse sequence. In more general settings, the side information need not be derived from
103   the same imaging modality, nor does it need to be image-based; it could be textual (e.g., clinical
104   notes and medical history), audio, or even encoded features or representations learned from other
105   related tasks. We note that reconstruction with different contrast side information, also known as
106   conditional reconstruction, refers to reconstructing only the target contrast while exploiting infor-
107   mation from other contrasts. This approach differs from both single-contrast and joint multi-contrast
      reconstruction, and is the scope of our work.

      Contributions: In this work, we propose a novel end-to-end trainable deep learning method that
109   reliably integrates side information to solve LIPs. Our method, called the Trust-Guided Variational
110   Network (TGVN), uses the side information to disambiguate the subspace spanned by the trailing
111   right singular vectors of the forward operator of an LIP, i.e., the right singular vectors correspond-
112   ing to small singular values. Specifically, we introduce a learnable squared Euclidean distance
113   constraint, termed the ambiguous space consistency constraint, into the regularized least-squares re-
114   construction formulation of the LIP to eliminate undesirable solutions from the ambiguous space of
115   the forward operator. This ambiguous space consistency constraint can be seamlessly integrated
116   into any deep unrolled network. Our approach can be trained end-to-end with full supervision
117
      to maximize a similarity metric between the reconstructed and the ground truth image, requiring
      minimal modifications to integrate the constraint. By incorporating additional contextual data, our
118
      approach effectively reduces the ambiguities inherent in inverse problems, leading to more accu-
119
      rate and reliable solutions even when the measurements are exceedingly sparse. We demonstrate the
120   effectiveness of our method in the challenging domain of multi-coil, multi-contrast MR image recon-
121   struction, where incomplete or low-quality measurements from complementary contrast weighting
122   are used as side information to reconstruct images with a different target contrast from drastically
123   small quantities of k-space measurements (of the orders of 20× low sampling rates) across different
124   anatomies and field strengths. Compared to recently proposed machine learning-based solutions,
125   our method leverages side information efficiently by focusing on the solution space creating am-
126   biguities while maintaining consistency with the acquired measurements and achieves statistically
127   significant improvements in reconstruction performance, highlighting the advantage of integrating
128
      additional context. To summarize:

      3     R ELATED W ORK : S IDE I NFORMATION IN MR I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
201
      This section summarizes how prior work has leveraged side information in MR image reconstruc-
202
      tion. While side information can take various forms, most studies have focused on complementary
203
      contrast information. The task involves reconstructing the target contrast using information from
204   other contrasts, differing from both single-contrast and joint multi-contrast reconstruction.
205
206   Initial Attempts. The use of side information in medical image reconstruction dates back to at least
207
      the 1990s. Fessler et al. (1992) demonstrated tomographic image reconstruction based on a weighted
      Gibbs penalty, where the weights are determined by anatomical boundaries in high-resolution MR
208
      images. Gindi et al. (1993) proposed a Bayesian method whereby maximum a posteriori (MAP)
209
      estimates of PET and SPECT images may be reconstructed with the aid of prior information derived
210   from registered anatomical MR images of the same slice. Some of the earlier attempts also utilized
211   handcrafted priors (Haldar et al., 2008; Wu et al., 2011; Peng et al., 2011; Bilgic et al., 2011; Du &
212   Lam, 2012; Huang et al., 2014; Qu et al., 2014; Li et al., 2015; Weizman et al., 2015; 2016; Ehrhardt
213   & Betcke, 2016). Later, dictionary-learning-based methods were proposed (Gungor et al., 2017;
214   Song et al., 2019; Lei et al., 2023a).
215   End-to-end Methods. More recently, multiple authors have proposed end-to-end deep learning-
      based models that leverage side information for MR image reconstruction. Specifically, Xiang et al.

246
      sampled data, often leading to degraded image quality or hallucinations. The former can be at-
247   tributed to the lack of efficiency in exploiting side information, while the latter represents over-
248   reliance on it. These limitations highlight the need for a more principled approach that can effi-
249   ciently integrate additional context, maintaining consistency with acquired data while minimizing
250   artifacts.

      4     T RUST G UIDED VARIATIONAL N ETWORK (TGVN)
254
255   We now give details of our proposed method, that effectively and reliably leverages side information
256
      to impose additional constraints into the LIP and guide the solution to fall within a contextually-
      appropriate distribution. In this setting, we assume that we have access to the additional side infor-
257
      mation denoted by s when solving for x using the system of equations A x = k. So long as s and
258
      x are conditionally dependent given k (i.e., the conditional mutual information I(s; x | k) > 0), the
259   knowledge of s can be exploited to reduce the uncertainty in estimating x from k (Cover & Thomas,
260   2006). As such, our solution assumes the existence of such conditional dependence.
261
262
      4.1   T HE M OTIVATION : A MBIGUOUS S PACE C ONSISTENCY
263
264   Deep learning and physics-based unrolled networks have shown notable success in MR image re-
265   construction from sparse k-space data (Knoll et al., 2020a; Muckley et al., 2021), primarily due to
266   their ability to enforce data consistency—ensuring that the reconstructed images closely match the
267   acquired measurements. However, while data consistency is crucial for aligning the solution with
268   the observed data, it might not be enough to resolve inherent ambiguities in the solution space, par-
269   ticularly at higher accelerations where an abrupt degradation in image quality has been highlighted
      (Radmanesh et al., 2022), rendering the images non-diagnostic. To address this issue, we introduce


                                                        5

270
      the concept of ambiguous space consistency, which goes beyond data consistency and complements
271   it. Essentially, our idea is to identify a subspace of images that could significantly alter reconstruc-
272   tion quality without substantially affecting the objective function of the MR image reconstruction
273   problem.
274
      Let xp be a particular solution to the equation A x = k and U ΣV H represent the singular value
275
      decomposition (SVD) of A, where U and V are unitary matrices, and Σ is a rectangular diagonal
276
      matrix with singular values sorted in descending order along its diagonal. Given a small positive
277   threshold δ, we define the ambiguous space as the subspace spanned by the right singular vectors
278   (columns of V ) with corresponding singular values smaller than δ and denote it as Wδ (A). Observe
279   that if we add any unit vector xa ∈ Wδ (A) to xp , the data consistency distance ∥A(xp + xa ) − k∥22
280   can at most be δ 2 . In other words, perturbing a solution that aligns with the observed measurements
281   by adding a vector from the ambiguous space results in only a minor change to the objective value.
282   However, only certain xa maximize the desired similarity between xp + xa and x∗ , indicating that,
283   once a particular solution is found, images from Wδ (A) introduce ambiguity in the reconstruction
284   problem. That is, they might visually alter the reconstruction quality without significantly affecting
285   the loss. Inspired by this observation, we propose to explicitly learn a constraint that removes unde-
286
      sirable solutions from Wδ (A). Our idea is to project x onto Wδ (A) with the orthogonal projector Pδ
      and to guide x to be maximally consistent with the side information s using a learnable module H

      We validated the efficacy of TGVN by using it for multi-coil MR image reconstruction from different
330
      contrasts across different anatomies and field strengths. In all experiments, we utilized the efficient
331
      approximate projection introduced in equation 6. In our empirical validation, we seek answers to
332   the following four questions: (Q1) Is there any benefit in using the side information when solving
333   the task? (Q2) How effective is TGVN at utilizing the side information? (Q3) Does projecting
334   onto the ambiguous space provide any benefits compared to no projection? (Q4) How robust is
335   the proposed approach to different under-sampling factors, misregistration, and degradation? To
336   answer Q1, we compare the reconstruction performance of TGVN against E2E-VarNet of the same
337   capacity that do not utilize side information. Q2 is answered by comparing the performance of
338   TGVN against several recent deep-learning baselines that also leverage side information in image
339   reconstruction: MTrans (Feng et al., 2022), MCVN (Lei et al., 2023b), and DMSI (Levac et al.,
340
      2023). To address Q3, we compare the performance of TGVN with and without the projection.

341
      introduced misregistrations. We present our findings related to the first and second questions in
342
      Section 5.1.1 and 5.1.2, and our findings related to the third and fourth questions in Appendix A,
343   which show that projection onto the ambiguous space significantly improves TGVN’s performance

522   6             C ONCLUSION
523
524   Our work introduces a novel framework, the Trust-Guided Variational Network (TGVN), that
525   demonstrates the power of leveraging side information in solving LIPs, with specific application
526   to the MR image reconstruction problem. By learning to eliminate solutions from the ambiguous
527   space and remaining faithful to the acquired measurements through data consistency, our principled
528
      approach maximally utilizes the side information and attempts to minimize the risk of hallucinations.
      Our key finding is that, when incorporated effectively, side information can significantly improve
529
      reconstruction quality and preserve key anatomical and pathological features, even at exceedingly
530
      high under-sampling regimes. These findings can have a transformative impact in healthcare by
531   enabling widespread access to MR imaging for diagnosing diseases at the population level.
532
533   Limitations and Future Work. While the results for MR image reconstruction using TGVN are
534
      very promising, there are various limitations of the current work, which will inform our future
      research directions. First, in our experiments, we only used complementary-contrast measurements
535
      from the same MR examination as side information. In the future, we intend to explore incorporation
536
      of different types of side information, including a patient’s prior scans and associated textual data
537   (e.g., clinical notes and medical history), as well as features learned from related tasks.

       Both the refinement and SME modules are implemented using a U-net model (Ronneberger et al.,
1081   2015), which consists of four down-sampling and up-sampling paths, complemented by skip con-
1082   nections. Each path is equipped with two 3 × 3 convolutions, followed by instance normalization
1083   (Ulyanov et al., 2016), and leaky ReLU (Maas et al., 2013) activation functions. The first convolu-
1084   tion layer outputs 21 channels in the refinement network and 8 in the SME network, with the number
1085   doubling in each subsequent layer, as in Sriram et al. (2021). In the brain experiments, the proposed
1086   TGVN comprises T = 10 TGVN blocks having approximately 67.3 million trainable parameters
1087   in total. In the knee experiments, it includes T = 14 TGVN blocks, resulting in about 94 million
1088   trainable parameters.
1089   For enhanced numerical stability in training, complex-valued layer normalization and its inverse
1090   operator are used, similar to Sriram et al. (2021). Specifically, each network pass, denoted as x 7→
1091   Φ(x), is executed as x 7→ T −1 (Φ(T x)). For an input tensor of shape (B, 2, H, W), where B
1092   is the batch size, the two channels correspond to the real and imaginary components of the image.
1093   The normalization process T adjusts each sample so that the mean of each channel across the spatial
1094   dimensions (H, W ) is zero, and the standard deviation is set to one. Furthermore, T ensures that
1095
       the real and imaginary channels are decorrelated, resulting in zero covariance between them. This
       is achieved by computing the 2 × 2 covariance matrix of the two channels and performing a linear
1096
       combination with the transpose of the Cholesky decomposition of the inverse of the covariance
1097
       matrix. This normalization step allows the network to handle the real and imaginary parts without
1098   any inherent bias or unintended correlation, ultimately improving the stability and performance of
1099   the model.
