009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural causal discovery methods have recently improved in terms of scalability and
012            computational efficiency. However, there are still opportunities for improving their
013            accuracy in uncovering causal structures. We argue that the key obstacle in unlock-
014            ing this potential is the faithfulness assumption, commonly used by contemporary
015            neural approaches. We show that this assumption, which is often not satisfied in
016            real-world or synthetic datasets, limits the effectiveness of existing methods. We
017            evaluate the impact of faithfulness violations both qualitatively and quantitatively
018
               and provide a unified evaluation framework to facilitate further research.

064   2    BACKGROUND
065
066   Structural Causal Models (SCMs) and graph representation Causal relationships are commonly
067
      formalized using SCMs, which represent causal dependencies through a set of structural equations.
      For a directed acyclic graph (DAG) G = (V, E), an SCM is defined by a set of equations
068
069                                              Xi = fi (P ai , Ui ),                                       (1)
070
      where i ∈ V , Xi is a random variable, fi : R|P ai |+1 → R is a function, P ai denotes the set of
071
      parents of the vertex i in the graph G, and Ui is an independent noise term associated with Xi . In
072   this paper, we assume additive noise SCMs, also referred to as additive noise models (ANM), where:
073
074                                        fi (P ai , Ui ) = gi (Pa i ) + Ui                                 (2)
075
076   for some gi : R|P ai | → R.
077
078
      Causal discovery Causal structure discovery aims to recover the ground truth DAG representing
      causal relationships among variables. However, the unique solution cannot be indentified from the
079
      observational data only; instead, one can only identify the structure up to a Markov Equivalence Class
080
      (MEC), the set of DAGs that encode the same conditional independencies. This can be uniquely
081   represented by a Complete Partially Directed Acyclic Graph (CPDAG), which is a sum of DAGs from
082   the same class. This results in a graph that includes both directed and undirected edges, reflecting
083   consistent and uncertain causal directions within the MEC (Verma & Pearl, 1990).
084
085   Faithfulness assumption A probability distribution P is said to be faithful to a DAG G = (V, E)
086   if all the conditional independence relations present in the data correspond to those implied by the
087   d-separation criteria of the DAG (for more on d-separation, see Appendix A.1 or Pearl (2009)).
088   Formally, this can be written as:
089                            Xa ⊥
                                  ⊥ Xb | XS        ⇒      a is d-separated from b | S,                       (3)
090
091
      where ⊥⊥ denotes conditional independence of the variables, a, b ∈ V are nodes of the graph, and
      S ⊆ V \ {a, b} is a set of nodes. Intuitively, the faithfulness assumption can be understood as the
092
      statement that all statistical independencies in the observed data are the result of the underlying causal
093
      structure. Faithfulness assumption can be violated, for example, in a situation when paths cancel
094   each other effects out, leading to statistical independence despite an existing causal relationship. An
095   example of this kind of violation is shown in Appendix A.2.
096
097
      While the faithfulness is a useful and powerful assumption in causal discovery, it is rarely satisfied in
      the practical scenarios (Cartwright, 2001; Andersen, 2013).
098
099
      Score-based neural causal discovery To allow for scalable causal discovery on graphs with hun-
100
      dreds of nodes, recent approaches focus on heuristics employing continuous optimization techniques
101
      that use neural networks as functional approximators to model the underlying probability distribution
102   of the data (Nazaret et al., 2024). These approaches use a continuous representation of the graph struc-
103   ture, enforcing a differentiable acyclicity constraint to ensure the result is a valid DAG. The primary
104   objective is to maximize log pθ (X|G), that is the log-likelihood of the data given the graph while
105   incorporating regularization terms to control graph complexity. The training procedure comprises
106   two parts: fitting functional approximators and structure search. They are usually done in parallel to
107   maximize compute efficiency. Methods of this class are guaranteed to recover a DAG from the MEC
      class of ground true graph when the faithfulness assumption is fulfilled (see Brouillard et al. (2020)).


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      We benchmark four differentiable causal discovery methods DCDI (Brouillard et al., 2020),
109   SDCD (Nazaret et al., 2024), BayesDAG (Annadani et al., 2023), and DiBS (Lorch et al., 2021), as
110   they summarize various research directions and improvements explored in neural causal discovery
111   over the last four years (see Appendix E). DCDI and SDCD represent the graph using an adjacency
112   matrix, and optimize using the Augmented Lagrangian method (Zheng et al., 2018), aiming to find a
113   single graph that maximizes the likelihood, with regularization added to penalize complex structures.
114   In contrast, BayesDAG and DiBS take a Bayesian approach, approximating the posterior distribution
115   over graphs rather than finding a single solution, with regularization introduced via prior distributions
116   on graph structures. All four methods assume that the distribution is faithful to the ground truth DAG.
117
118   Structure evaluation We evaluate graph discovery within the MEC using ESHDCPDAG and
119   F1-ScoreCPDAG , where ESHDCPDAG = 0 and F1-ScoreCPDAG = 1 when the predicted graph is
120   in the same MEC as the ground truth. For Bayesian methods, we compute the average by sampling
121
      100 graphs from the posterior; for non-Bayesian methods, we use a single graph.
122   The Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) (Tsamardinos et al., 2006) counts edge insertions, deletions,
123   and reversals needed to match the predicted graph to the true graph. We define Expected SHD
124   between CPDAGs as:
125
126
                        ESHDCPDAG (G, G) = EG ∗ ∼G [SHD(CPDAG(G), CPDAG(G ∗ ))],                           (4)
127
128
      where G is the resulting distribution of graphs, G ∗ is a graph sampled from G and G is the ground
129
      true graph. The F1-Score measures the harmonic mean of precision and recall for edge predictions.
130   We compute the Expected F1-Score between the CPDAGs as follows:
131
132
133                 F1-ScoreCPDAG (G, G) = EG ∗ ∼G [F1-Score(CPDAG(G), CPDAG(G ∗ ))].                      (5)
134
135   For more details and justification on the selection of metrics please refer to Appendix D.

140
141   In this section, we present a unified benchmark that exposes both the strengths and limitations of
142
      neural-based causal discovery methods. We evaluate methods DiBS, DCDI, BayesDAG, and SDCD
      introduced in Section 2 on identical datasets, tune hyperparameters consistently, and use a common
143
      functional approximation.
144
145   Our analysis spans several key dimensions of performance. In Section 3.2, we show that despite
146   advancements in causal discovery over the past few years, ESHDCPDAG and F1-ScoreCPDAG metrics
147   do not improve significantly. In Section 3.3, we demonstrate that structure discovery accuracy
148
      does not scale with the amount of data. Finally, in Section 3.4, we confirm that variations in MLP
      architecture have minimal impact on performance. In Appendix F we provide additional results on
149
      real-world structures which align with the conclusions presented in this section.

198   3.3   I MPACT OF SAMPLE SIZE
199
200   We investigate whether the number of observations affects the performance of causal discovery
201   methods. One could expect that neural based models, similarly to independence testing ones, will
202   improve when more data is supplied (Kalisch & Bühlmann, 2007). We compare benchmarked methods
203   on dataset with varying number of observational samples, ranging from 20 to 8,000 observations.
204   The results, presented in Figure 13, reveal no consistent pattern of improvement in the ESHDCPDAG
205   metric as observational sample size increases, despite extensive hyperparameter tuning (as described in
206   Section 3.1). For example, DiBS shows the best performance on larger datasets, but its improvements
207   plateau after around 800 samples. Similarly, BayesDAG shows only marginal improvements with
208   larger sample sizes and is unable to outperform DiBS. DCDI improves up to 250 samples and
209   then maintains consistent performance regardless of the sample size, similar to DiBS. Interestingly,
210   SDCD’s performance is poor on datasets with small number of observations but begins to improve
211   once sample sizes exceed 250, though is unable to reach DCDI’s performance, for larger sample sizes
212
      the rate of improvement decreases.
213   Further analysis of the effect of sample size on smaller graphs ER(5, 1)is presented in Figure 12 in
214   Appendix C.4. Overall, the results on smaller graphs align with the trends observed on larger graphs.
215   Specifically, while some methods improve with increasing sample size, others show inconsistent or
      even degraded performance.

      4     M EASURING IMPACT OF FAITHFULNESS VIOLATION
307
308
      In this section we explore how violations of the faithfulness assumption impact the performance of
309
      neural causal discovery methods. In Section 3, we showed that despite various attempts to scale
310
      up data and model complexity, the performance of these methods remains stagnant, possibly due
311   to deeper challenges related to the underlying data properties and the limitations inherent to the
312   algorithms. This leads us to investigate whether violations of the faithfulness assumption, common in
313   synthetic non-linear data, might be the key factor limiting performance improvements.
314
      The faithfulness assumption translates into the set of conditional independence statements that all need
315
      to be satisfied. As mentioned in Section 2, synthetic non-linear data rarely adheres to faithfulness
316
      assumption, rendering binary criterion not practical. To address this, we introduce a degree of
317   faithfulness metric, denoted DeFaith, which captures the faithfulness violations on a continuous scale.
318
319   Inspired by Zhang & Spirtes (2003), we use Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient to quantify the
320
      conditional dependencies in the dataset. We define a predictor that classifies nodes as independent if
      conditional Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient computed based on a dataset D is smaller than a
321
      certain threshold.
322
323   DeFaith is the quality of this predictor measured by Area Under Receiver Operator Curve computed
      over all possible pairs of variables a, b and separation sets S ⊆ V \ {a, b}. Formally,

356   5    E STIMATING UPPER BOUND ON PERFORMANCE
357
358   In this section, we investigate the limits of the performance of score-based neural causal discovery
359   methods. To do this we develop a method dubbed as NN-opt method, to compute an experimental
360   upper bound on the performance. As for the benchmarked methods, the goal of NN-opt method is
361   to find a structure that minimizes the regularized log-likelihood of data, therefore it is expected to
362
      recover a graph from the correct MEC class when the faithfulness assumption holds (see Section 2).
363   The method overview is in Algorithm 1. It is based on the common approach used by score-based
364   neural causal discovery methods described in Section 2. The procedure consists of two steps. First,
365   we train neural networks to approximate functional relationships between variables. Contrary to
366   benchmarked methods we train a separate network for each parent set instead of training one for
367   all. This renders functional approximation fitting procedure completely independent from structure
368
      search. Therefore, it simplifies the training task and allows for strict control of the training procedure
      via validation loss monitoring. Second, we conduct an exhaustive search over the space of DAGs to
369
      find the structure that minimizes the log-likelihood loss. For increased stability of this step, we use
370
      an ensemble of 3 neural networks to compute the log-likelihood of the data under various structures.
371
372   The approach is exhaustive both in the sense of structure search and in neural network training,
373   trading computational efficiency for additional precision. NN-opt is a brute force technique intended
374
      to be able to reach the limits of score-based neural causal discovery approaches. NN-opt is helpful as
      an upper-bound benchmark but is not practical to use.
375
376   We expect the method to improve with the number of samples and stabilize when the data becomes
377   sufficiently large. Therefore, we applied NN-opt method to datasets of various sizes. The results
      are presented in Figure 5 on the left. For very small datasets we observe rapid improvement in

415      6                      R ELATED WORK
416
417     Causal discovery without the faithfulness assumption While many causal discovery methods
418     rely on the faithfulness assumption, alternative conditions have been proposed. One notable approach
419     is the adjacency-faithfulness assumption, introduced by Ramsey et al. (2006) in the conservative PC
420
        algorithm. This assumption, which is less restrictive than full faithfulness, leads to more robust with
        minimal computational overhead. In the context of linear structural causal models (SCMs), Van de
421
        Geer & Bühlmann (2013) demonstrated that a sparsity-based assumption can effectively reveal the
422
        underlying causal structure. Similarly, Isozaki (2014) proposed a method to reduce unnecessary
423     independence tests during structure discovery, offering greater robustness against violations of
424     faithfulness due to statistical errors. More recently, Ng et al. (2021) suggested another causal
425     discover method, based on relaxed faithfulness assumption that requires less independence tests to
426     be fulfilled. Marx et al. (2021) explores a weaker alternative to the faithfulness assumption, called
427     the 2-faithfulness assumption, and suggests how to construct a causal discovery algorithm based on
428     it. Moreover, Lippe et al. (2022) introduced a neural-based approach that uses interventional data,
429     avoiding the faithfulness assumption altogether.
430
431     Describing faithfulness violations Faithfulness violation has been extensively explored in the
        linear setting by (Uhler et al., 2013). They showed that the conditions that would allow for discovering


                                                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the true independencies in a finite sample regime are rarely met when making use of linear synthetic
433   data. Additionally, they proved that the bigger the graph the more difficult it is to find a faithful
434   distribution. Zhang & Spirtes (2003) provided theoretical conditions for violation of faithfulness being
435   detectable during training. More generally, Andersen (2013) described reasons, why faithfulness is
436   likely violated in complex, evolved real-world systems. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first
437   to estimate the limits of the score-based neural causal discovery methods on unfaithful data.
438
439   Benchmarking There is a multitude of recent benchmarks that use real-world data to assess the
440   performance of causal discovery methods (Chevalley et al., 2022; Mehrjou et al., 2022). However,
441   these datasets lack the ground truth structure rendering structure discovery accuracy assessment
442   impossible. Additionally, these works usually focus on classical, not neural, causal discovery methods.
443
      Some recent work is concerned with the quality of evaluations and performance under assumptions
      violations. Karimi-Mamaghan et al. (2024) investigates metrics for Bayesian causal discovery in
444
      a linear setting. Their finding suggests that the standard structure-based metrics do not align well
445
      with downstream task performance when structure uncertainty is high (especially for bigger graphs),
446   Montagna et al. (2023) evaluates classical causal discovery methods under different assumption
447   violations. In our work, we focus on a unified, synthetic, and challenging setup to thoroughly evaluate
448   neural causal discovery claims of being general and accurate. Most recently, Zhou et al. (2024)
449   introduced a comprehensive benchmark, but they did not compare neural-based methods in their work.
450

      7   L IMITATIONS & FUTURE WORK
452
453          • Work of Lippe et al. (2022) suggests that interventional data can replace the need for
454            faithfulness assumption. A valuable extension of our research would be to evaluate the
455            performance of the benchmarked methods on interventional datasets to understand their
456            limitations and potential improvements in this context.
457          • Our work provides experimental evidence for the scale of the impact of violation of faith-
458            fulness on performance in a challenging non-linear setting. It would be beneficial for the
459            community if some theoretical results (akin Uhler et al. (2013); Zhang & Spirtes (2003)
460            were derived in a non-linear setting.
461          • While our, experimental upper bound, NN-opt method is based on common, with bench-
462            marked methods, theoretical principles. We leave strict theoretical justification of its
463            optimality for future work.
464          • In this work we present the method that allows to estimate the upper bound on performance
465            of score-based neural causal discovery methods on any dataset and provide numerical results
466            for the Erodos-Renyi class of graphs. The results could be computed for more classes and
467            even some small real-world or real-world inspired graphs, see Elidan (2001).
468
469   8   C ONCLUSIONS
470
471   In this work, we present compelling evidence that the faithfulness assumption is a major limiting
472   factor in advancing causal discovery. Our findings demonstrate that the accuracy of structure recovery
473   is correlated with the degree of faithfulness violation. Additionally, we introduce a novel method to
474   calculate the upper bound of performance for score-based neural causal discovery methods, revealing
475   serious limitations. Our results highlight the need for a paradigm shift. We argue that further progress
476   in causal discovery requires moving beyond the faithfulness assumption and encourage researchers to
477   explore alternative conditions. The implications of our work extend beyond theoretical advancements.
478   By challenging the faithfulness assumption, we open up avenues for more robust and generalizable
479
      methods in causal discovery, which could have far-reaching consequences in fields like healthcare,
      economics, and policy-making.

650   A.1   d- SEPARATION
651
652   Two nodes A and B in a DAG are said to be d-separated by a set of nodes Z if all paths between
653   A and B are blocked when conditioning on Z. A path is considered blocked under the following
654   conditions:
655
             • If a path includes a non-collider node (a node where arrows do not converge, i.e., a chain
656
               or fork), conditioning on that node blocks the path. For example, if A → C → B, or
657            A ← C → B, conditioning on C makes A and B independent.
658
             • If the path includes a collider (a node where arrows converge, i.e., A → C ← B), the path
659
               is blocked unless either the collider itself or one of its descendants is conditioned on. For
660            instance, in the path A → C ← B, conditioning on C or its descendants would unblock the
661            path, making A and B dependent.
662
             • If there are multiple paths connecting A and B, all paths must be blocked for A and B to
663            be considered d-separated. Even if one path remains unblocked, A and B are d-connected,
664            meaning they are dependent.
665
666   In causal discovery, we are interested in making statements about the relationship between the causal
667   graph and the data distribution. Given a causal graph G and the data distribution P , the Markov
668   assumption states that if variables A and B are d-separated in the graph G by some conditioning set
669
      C, then A and B are conditionally independent in the distribution P when conditioned on the same
      conditioning set C. Formally, this can be written as:

672                                         A⊥
                                             ⊥G B|C ⇒ A ⊥

1010   E    J USTIFICATION OF THE SELECTION OF METHODS
1011
1012   During the preliminary phase, we considered the following methods NO-TEARS (Zheng et al.,
1013   2018), NO-BEARS (Lee et al., 2019), NO-CURL (Yu et al., 2021), GRAN-DAG (Lachapelle et al.,
1014   2019), SCORE (Rolland et al., 2022), DAGMA (Bello et al., 2022), DCDFG (Lopez et al., 2022),
1015   DCDI (Brouillard et al., 2020), DiBS (Lorch et al., 2021), BayesDAG (Annadani et al., 2023),
1016
       SDCD (Nazaret et al., 2024), from which we chose DCDI, SDCD, DiBS and BayesDAG. Below we
       explain why the included ones cover non-included methods.
1017
1018   NO-TEARS is the first method to use augmented Lagrangian and differentiable constraints to enforce
1019   DAGness. However, the suggested formulation entangles functional and structural parameters,
1020   making NO-TEARS applicable only to linear models or restricted neural networks. The NO-TEARS
1021   method was improved in GRAN-DAG (introduces separate adjacency matrix and sampling based on
1022
       Gumbel softmax) and then in DCDI (accounts for interventional data). We chose to use DCDI as it is
       the most developed method in this line of work and has clean implementation.
1023
1024   An interesting line of work shows articles introducing methods such as NO-BEARS and DAGMA,
1025   that were focused on improving the acyclicity constraint introduced in NO-TEARS, all proposed
       constraints were unified in the SDCD paper, and a new constraint was proposed, that was shown to


                                                                    19
        Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
        perform the best. Additionally, SDCD is compared against SCORE and DCDFG again presenting
1027    better performance.
1028
1029    The two other methods are from the class of Bayesian approaches. DiBS method is selected as a
1030
        Bayesian approach that uses classic NO-TEARS-based regularization embedded in its prior. The
        BayesDAG is based on the NO-CURL parametrization of DAGs and provides improvements to the
1031
        optimization pipeline (uses MCMC instead of SVGD).
1032
1033   We argue that this selection of four methods summarizes various research directions and improvements
1034   explored in neural causal discovery over the last four years and well represents the spectrum of
1035   existing approaches.
