011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            While the majority of time series classification research has focused on modeling fixed-
014            length sequences, variable-length time series classification (VTSC) remains critical in
015            healthcare, where sequence length may vary among patients and events. To address this
016            challenge, we propose Stochastic Sparse Sampling (SSS), a novel VTSC framework devel-
017
               oped for medical time series. SSS manages variable-length sequences by sparsely sampling
               fixed windows to compute local predictions, which are then aggregated and calibrated to
018
               form a global prediction. We apply SSS to the task of seizure onset zone (SOZ) localization,
019
               a critical VTSC problem requiring identification of seizure-inducing brain regions from
020            variable-length electrophysiological time series. We evaluate our method on the Epilepsy
021            iEEG Multicenter Dataset, a heterogeneous collection of intracranial electroencephalogra-
022            phy (iEEG) recordings obtained from four independent medical centers. SSS demonstrates
023            superior performance compared to state-of-the-art (SOTA) baselines across most medical
024            centers, and superior performance on all out-of-distribution (OOD) unseen medical centers.
025            Additionally, SSS naturally provides post-hoc insights into local signal characteristics
026            related to the SOZ, by visualizing temporally averaged local predictions throughout the
027            signal.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Artificial intelligence (AI) in medicine has received significant attention in recent years, with various
033
      applications to clinical diagnosis and treatment planning (Rajpurkar et al., 2022). Despite its advancements,
      the actual integration into everyday clinical practice remains limited, with much of it attributed to the
034
      challenges of handling the complexity and variability in medical data. One particularly challenging aspect
035
      of this variability lies in the nature of medical time series data. Variable-length time series are prevalent
036   throughout many areas of healthcare, including heart rate monitoring, blood glucose measurements, and
037   electrophysiological recordings where sequence length can vary dependent on the recording or length of
038   an event (Agliari et al., 2020; Deutsch et al., 1994; Walther et al., 2023). Yet, the majority of time series
039   classification (TSC) literature focuses solely on methods that process fixed-length sequences (Ismail Fawaz
040   et al., 2019; Mohammadi Foumani et al., 2024).
041
      At the same time, healthcare applications require greater interpretability from modern time series methods
042   to expand their applicability in critical domains and accelerate clinical adoption (Amann et al., 2020).
043   This interpretability is especially crucial in contexts where the relationship between pathology and signal
044   characteristics is not well understood, as it can provide valuable insights for both clinicians and scientists.
045   Recent studies in time series classification (TSC) have explored the explainability of specific signal segments,
046   as opposed to full-signal analysis, which proves particularly useful for uncovering important characteristics

                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      such as motifs, anomalies, or frequency patterns (Early et al., 2024; Crabbé & Van Der Schaar, 2021; Huang
048
      et al., 2024). However, there still remains a significant need for models with built-in interpretability in medical
049   applications. Such methods would allevaiate the burden of implementing both a base model and a specialized
050   interpretability method—which may require more domain expertise—and may more effectively facilitate
051   clinical adoption.
052
      The need for variable-length time series classification (VSTC) methods with built-in interpretability is
053
      particularly relevant in seizure onset zone (SOZ) localization—the task of identifying brain regions from
054
      which seizures originate—as effective treatment requires analysis of variable-length signals (Balaji & Parhi,
055   2022). The World Health Organization (WHO) reports epilepsy affects over 50 million people globally,
056   establishing it as one of the most common yet poorly understood neurological disorders (Organization et al.,
057   2019; Stafstrom & Carmant, 2015). Additionally, one-third of patients do not respond to antiepileptic drugs,
058   making surgery the last resort and accurate SOZ localization essential for effectively planning the operation.
059   The process of SOZ identification involves a two-step procedure: initial implantation of electrodes in areas
060   suspected to contain the SOZ, followed by recording and visual analysis of intracranial electroencephalog-
061   raphy (iEEG) signals by medical experts. The task of SOZ localization reduces to classifying individual
062   electrode recordings, representing different regions within the brain. Effective localization of the SOZ is
063
      challenging due to the absence of clinically validated biological markers and the variable-length nature of
      iEEG signals—consequently, surgical success rates range from 30% to 70% (Löscher et al., 2020; Li et al.,
064
      2021).

      2    R ELATED W ORK
118
119   TSC methodologies can be broadly categorized into finite-context methods, which operate on fixed-length
120   input segments, and infinite-context methods, which handle variable-length sequences without being restricted
121
      to a predetermined window size. For a formal treatment, please see Appendix A.5.
122   Finite-context methods. Finite-context methods are among the most commonly used approaches for TSC.
123   Transformer-based models have gained significant attention, with variations such as sparse attention, series
124   decomposition, and patching techniques (Vaswani et al., 2017; Kitaev et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
125   2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Several temporal convolutional
126   networks (TCNs) have also been proposed, to capture temporal dependencies through dilated convolutions
127
      and Inception-like architectures (Lai et al., 2018; Bai et al., 2018; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022;
      Luo & Wang, 2024). Recently, multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) and simple linear models have demonstrated
128
      competitive performance as well (Chen et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023). Despite the significant rise of finite-
129
      context methods, these methods are inherently limited in their ability to handle variable-length sequences, and
130   will require the use of either padding, truncation, or interpolation for VTSC. Furthermore, as the sequence
131   length increases, so does the number of model parameters, which leads to not only greater computational cost
132   but an increased risk of overfitting.
133
      Infinite-context methods. The recurrent neural network (RNN) family includes several models capable of
134
      ingesting variable-length time series (Rumelhart et al., 1986). Long-short term memory (LSTM) networks
135
      introduce memory cells and gating mechanisms to better handle long-term dependencies (Hochreiter &
136   Schmidhuber, 1997). Gated recurrent units (GRUs) simplify the LSTM architecture while maintaining
137   similar performance (Bahdanau et al., 2014). State space models (SSMs) have gained recent attention,
138   with approaches such as S4 introducing structured parameterization to enable efficient computation over
139   long sequences, while still attempting to capture long-range dependencies (Gu et al., 2021). Building on
140   this, Mamba introduces a selective SSM that adapts to input dynamics, further improving processing of

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      long-range dependencies in time series data while while maintaining linear time complexity with respect to
142
      sequence length (Gu & Dao, 2023). Despite these advancements, RNNs and SSMs can still struggle with
143   retaining information in extremely long sequences and may be prone to vanishing or exploding gradients
144   (Salehinejad et al., 2017). ROCKET offers an alternative, using random convolutional kernels to convert
145   input into a fixed-length representation for VTSC, but at the cost of interpretability and potentially limited
146   model expressivity (Dempster et al., 2020).
147
      SOZ localization methods. Several recent proposals have been tailored specifically to SOZ localization.
148
      Functional connectivity graphs compute patient-specific channel metrics to capture brain connectivity patterns
149   (Grattarola et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2024), offering insights into functional relationships associated with
150   seizures. However, their reliance on intra-patient dynamics makes them unsuitable for a single model that
151   can generalize across multi-patient, heterogeneous datasets. Alternatively, electrical stimulation methods
152   that use intracranial electrodes (Johnson et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024) can enhance localization accuracy
153   through induced responses analyzed by TCNs and logistic regression models. Yet, these approaches require
154   both fixed-length windows and the use of active stimulation. For our purpose of building a general model for
155   SOZ localization, which can be applied to multiple patients (with a potentially varying number of channels)
156   without electrical stimulation, we do not consider such approaches in our study.

      4.1        BASELINES
238
239   For our finite-context baselines, we include a variety of modern time series backbones including PatchTST,
240   which uses subwindows as tokens in combination with the traditional Transformer architecture (Nie et al.,
241   2023; Vaswani et al., 2017). TimesNet is a TCN architecture that models both interperiod and intraperiod
242   dynamics by leveraging Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) features to slice the signal into multiple views, which
243   are then processed through inception blocks (Wu et al., 2022; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2020). ModernTCN, is
244   a recently proposed TCN which decouples temporal and channel information processing by using separate
245
      DWConv and ConvFFN modules for more efficient representation learning (Luo & Wang, 2024). DLinear
      is linear neural network, which has shown to outperform several modern Transformer-based architecutres,
246
      utilizing traditional seasonal-trend decomposition techniques (Zeng et al., 2023). In our infinite-context
247
      baselines, we utilize ROCKET, which applies randomly initialized, fixed convolutional kernels to the input
248   sequence. ROCKET compresses the resulting convolutional outputs to the maximum value and the proportion
249   of positive values (PPV), where these features then fed to a linear classifier (Dempster et al., 2020). We also
250   consider GRUs and an LSTM network, both of which are popular RNN frameworks designed to capture long-
251   term dependencies in sequential data (Bahdanau et al., 2014; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). Additionally,
252   use the recent SSM architecture, Mamba, which utilizes selective state updates to enable efficient long-range
253   dependency modeling (Gu & Dao, 2023). Further details regarding configurations and hyperparameter tuning
254   for each baseline can be found in Appendix C.

266   4.3        U NIVARIATE VTSC
267
268   For each patient iEEG recording, the goal of SOZ localization is to determine the the correct of channels
269   or electrodes which belong the seizure onset zone. This effectively reduces the task to univariate TSC.
270   While several channel-dependent methods have been proposed for SOZ localization (see section 2), we focus
271
      primarily on channel-independent solutions for two key reasons: (1) they are more resilient to interpatient
      variability and are unaffected by factors like channel count and therefore can be applied in multiple hospital
272
      settings, and (2) they generalize better to domains beyond electrophysiological data, as they learn local signal
273
      characteristics rather than explicitly modeling functional connectivity between electrode sites, which may not
274   be present in other medical time series.

      5.1   R EVIEW OF R ESULTS
379
380   In general, we observe that SSS outperforms modern finite-context and infinite-context methods both
381   in-distribution and OOD for univariate VTSC. SOZ localization presents a significant challenge due to
382   intra-patient and inter-patient variability, and with our evaluation the collection of 3 heterogeneous datasets,
383   serving rigorous testbed for assessing the generalizability of SSS’s capabilities for learning local signal
384   characteristics in medical time series. While our experiments focus on univariate VTSC, as outlined in
385   section 3.2.1 and 3.2.2, SSS can be easily applied to the multivariate setting.
386   Our results from the multicenter evaluation Table 1 indicate that SSS benefits from a diversity of data
387   distributions and volume of training examples, given by the magnitude in performance differences, when
388   compared to single cluster results. Furthermore, our OOD experiments from Table 2 suggest that SSS may
389   be learning local signal characteristics present in different patient populations, leading to a advantage over
390   finite-context and infinite-context methods. Our visualizations of SSS’s predictions OOD in Figure 3 supports
391   this notion, as there exist clear qualitative differences in locally averaged window probabilities with respect
392
      to anomalous signal characteristics, such as spikes or increases in amplitude or frequency. Figure 2 shows
      SSS’s predictions in-distribution which also suggest a form of implicit semantic segmentation for anomalous
393
      local regions of the signal with respect to the SOZ probability. More analysis is needed to further solidify our
394
      understanding, which would benefit from a rigorous explainability study in future works.
395
396
      5.2   C ONCLUSION
397
398   To conclude, this work introduces novel VTSC framework, Stochastic Sparse Sampling (SSS), specifically
399   tailored for medical time series applications. SSS blends the best of both worlds between finite-context
400   methods (enabling usage of finite-context backbones) while allowing sampling of the entire signal in a
401   computationally efficient manner that is less prone to context overload from infinite-context methods. SSS
402   learns local signal characteristics, which provides the added benefit of inherent interpretability, and provides
403   superior performance to the SOTA in-distritbuion and OOD for unseen medical centers. For future work, it
404   would be valuable to: (1) benchmark SSS across a wider variety of variable-length medical time series, (2)
405
      provide further rigorous post-hoc insights into the window probability distribution given by SSS, and (3)
      potentially include uncertainty estimates within the aggregation function to highlight anomalous regions of
406
      the signal.

777   We select the F1 score as our primary evaluation metric for SOZ localization for the following reasons.
778   The F1 score balances the need to correctly identify all regions of the SOZ (recall) with the need to avoid
779   misclassifying healthy regions as SOZ (precision). This balance is crucial in surgical planning, where both
780

781
