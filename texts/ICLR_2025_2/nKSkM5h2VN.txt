011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Deep learning models are known to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks by in-
014            jecting sophisticated designed perturbations to input data. Training-time defenses
015            still exhibit a significant performance gap between natural accuracy and robust
016            accuracy. In this paper, we investigate a new test-time adversarial defense method
017            via diffusion-based recovery along opposite adversarial paths (OAPs). We present
018            a purifier that can be plugged into a pre-trained model to resist adversarial attacks.
019
               Different from prior arts, the key idea is excessive denoising or purification by
               integrating the opposite adversarial direction with reverse diffusion to push the
020
               input image further toward the opposite adversarial direction. For the first time,
021
               we also exemplify the pitfall of conducting AutoAttack (Rand) for diffusion-based
022            defense methods. Through the lens of time complexity, we examine the trade-off
023            between the effectiveness of adaptive attack and its computation complexity against
024            our defense. Experimental evaluation along with time cost analysis verifies the
025            effectiveness of the proposed method.

      1.1   BACKGROUND
032
      It has been well known that deep learning models are vulnerable to adversarial attacks by injecting
033
      (imperceptible) adversarial perturbations into the data that will be input to a neural network (NN)
034
      model to change its normal predictions (Athalye et al. (2018); Carlini et al. (2019); Croce et al.
035   (2023); Frosio & Kautz (2023); Goodfellow et al. (2015); Gowal et al. (2021); Madry et al. (2018);
036   Venkatesh et al. (2023)). Please also see Chen & Liu (2023) for a recent review on the adversarial
037   robustness of deep learning models. It can be found from the literature that adversarial attacks defeat
038   their defense counterparts easily and rapidly, and there is still a gap between natural accuracy and
039   robust accuracy.
040
      The study of adversarial defense in resisting adversarial attacks can be divided into two categories:
041   (1) Adversarial training/Training-time defense (Gowal et al. (2021); Hsiung et al. (2023); Huang
042   et al. (2023); Suzuki et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2019; 2023); Wu et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2019));
043   and (2) Input pre-processing/Test-time defense (Alfarra et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022); Hill et al.
044   (2020); Ho & Vasconcelos (2022); Nie et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022); Yoon et al.
045   (2021)). Adversarial training utilizes adversarial examples derived from the training data to enhance
046   the robustness of the classifier. Despite the effort in training-time defense, we do see ( RobustBench
047   Croce et al. (2021)) there is still a remarkable gap between natural accuracy and robust accuracy.
048   Different from the training-time defense paradigm, in this paper, we propose a new test-time adver-
049   sarial defensive method by pre-processing data in a way different from the prior works. It is a kind
050   of purifier and serves as a plug-and-play module that can be used to improve the robustness of a
051   defense method once our module is incorporated as a  pre-processor. Specifically, the formulation
      of processing the input data is derived as: minϕ, θ E maxx′ ∈B(x) L((fϕ ◦ gθ )(x′ ), y) , where x′

      A key to test-time defense is the design of pre-processor or denoiser (e.g., gθ ), which aims at denoising

059   1.2   R ELATED W ORKS
060
061   We introduce representative test-time adversarial defense methods (Alfarra et al. (2022); Ho &
062   Vasconcelos (2022); Hill et al. (2020); Yoon et al. (2021); Nie et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Wu
063
      et al. (2022)) that share the same theme as our method. Please also see Sec. 6 in the Supplementary
      for details of Hill et al. (2020); Yoon et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2022).
064
065   In Alfarra et al. (2022), a defense method is proposed by connecting an anti-adversary layer with
066   a pre-trained classifier fϕ . Given an input image x, it will be first sent to the anti-adversary layer
067   for generating anti-adversarial perturbation γ by solving an optimization problem. As the name
068   implies, in most cases, the direction γ will be opposite to the direction of adversarial perturbation.
069
      The resultant purified image x + γ is then used for classification.
070   DISCO (Ho & Vasconcelos (2022)) is proposed as a purification method to remove adversarial
071   perturbations by localized manifold projections. The author implemented it with an encoder and a
072   local implicit module, which is leveraged by the architecture called LIIF (Chen et al. (2021); Chen &
073   Zhang (2019)), where the former produces per-pixel features and the latter uses the features in the
074   neighborhood of query pixel for predicting the clean RGB value.
075   In DiffPure (Nie et al. (2022)), given an input (clean or adversarially noisy), the goal is to obtain
076   a relatively cleaner version through a series of forward and reverse diffusion processes. Moreover,
077   a theoretical guarantee is derived that, under an amount of Gaussian noise added in the forward
078   process, the adversarial perturbation may be removed effectively. This is independent of the types of
079   adversarial perturbations, making DiffPure defend against unseen attacks.
080   Recently, the robustness of diffusion-based purifiers is considered overestimated. Lee & Kim (2023)
081   provides recommendations for robust evaluation, called surrogate process, and shows that the defense
082   methods may be defeated under the surrogate process. Kang et al. (2024) proposes DiffAttack, a
083   new attack technique against diffusion-based adversarial purification defenses, that can overcome
084   the challenges of attacking diffusion models, including vanishing/exploding gradients, high memory
085   costs, and large randomness. The use of a segment-wise algorithm allows attacking with much longer
086   diffusion lengths than previous methods.
087   Although the aforementioned purification-based adversarial defense methods show promising perfor-
088   mance in resisting adversarial attacks, Croce et al. (2022) argues that their evaluations are ineffective
089   in two aspects: (i) Incorrect use of attacks or (ii) Attacks used for evaluation are not strong enough.
090   However, the authors also mentioned test-time defense complicates robustness evaluation because of
091   its complexity and computational cost, which impose even more computations for the attackers.

096   accuracy for benign images and robust accuracy is measured for adversarial samples. However, we

124   1.4   C ONTRIBUTIONS
125
126   Different from prior works, the concept of OAP can be incorporated into any training scheme of
127
      purifiers, and the OAP-based purifier can also become a part of modules in other defense processes.
      For instance, OAP-based purifiers can provide additional directions within reverse diffusion, whereas
128
      diffusion models alone (Song et al. (2020) (baseline model in DiffPure)) only provide direction to
129
      generate images. Unlike the traditional purification methods, we do not use classifier-generated
130   labels (e.g. Anti-Adv Alfarra et al. (2022)) in our baseline purifier during testing. On the contrary,
131   combining the proposed baseline purifier with the reverse diffusion process provides reference
132   directions pointing to a safer area during the purification process.
133
      Contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
134
135
            1. We are first to present the idea of excessive denoising along the opposite adversarial path
136
               (OAP) as the baseline purifier for adversarial robustness. (Sec. 3.1)
137
138         2. We integrate the OAP baseline purifier and conditional reverse diffusion as a sophisticated
139            adversarial defense that can be interpreted as moving purified data toward the combination
140            of directions from the score-based diffusion model and baseline purifier (Sec. 3.2).
141         3. To complicate the entire defense mechanism by complicating the computation overhead of
142            adaptive attacks accordingly, we study a double diffusion path cleaning-based purifier (Sec.
143            3.3). This creates a trade-off between the attack effectiveness and attack computation.
144
145         4. For the first time, we exemplify the pitfall of conducting AutoAttack (Rand) for diffusion-
146
               based adversarial defense methods (Sec. 3.4).

      3.1     BASELINE P URIFIER : O PPOSITE A DVERSARIAL PATH (OAP)
187
188   Given a classifier model fϕ parameterized by ϕ, a loss function L(x, y, ϕ), and a pair of data (x, y),
189   the adversarial attack can be computed as
190
191                                 xadv = Πx+S (x + α sign(∇x L(x, y, ϕ))),                                 (1)
192
      where S is the set that allows the perceptual similarity between natural and adversarial images. This
193   iterative process aims to find the adversarial image xadv that maximizes the loss function.
194
195   On the other hand, the opposite direction of each iteration points to minimize the loss. Assume now
196
      we get an ordinary noisy input xadv = x + δ with ∥δ∥p ≤ ϵp via image processing, a denoiser can
197
      push the denoised input close to x within a non-perceptual distortion. Nevertheless, if the noisy
      input xadv is a sophisticated design via adversarial attack, it is too early to claim, depending on the
198
      perceptual similarity between x and xadv , that the denoised image can be free from being affected by
199
      adversarial perturbations. We argue that if we properly push the denoised image further away from
200   the decision boundary, the downstream classifier can still successfully classify the input since the
201   direction we push points to a lower loss area on the input-loss surface, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Also
202   note that the plug-and-play module lies under the setting that the baseline purifier is only trained on a
203   given attack (e.g., PGD-ℓ∞ -7), which is independent of the attacks (e.g., PGD-ℓ∞ -40, AutoAttack,
204   and BPDA+EOT) used in testing. In addition, the diffusion model is pre-trained (Sec. 3.2) and does
205   not involve adversarial examples during its training.

      3.4    G RANULARITY OF G RADIENT A PPROXIMATION IN R EALIZING P OWERFUL A DAPTIVE
379          AUTOATTACK
380
381   We present to implement a more powerful adaptive AutoAttack via granularity of gradient approxima-
382   tion in order not to overestimate robustness. Actually, our implementation requires the output in each
383   step from torchsde in the diffusion reverse process, which starts from xt∗ and calls torchsde to
384   produce the output xt∗ −1 of next time step till we get the final image x̂. Hence, if one understands the
385   mechanism of using adjoint method as BPDA correctly, gradient computation in the reverse diffusion
386   process will demand the same amount of calls of adjoint method as in that of torchsde. We have
387
      to particularly point out that this is different from DiffPure (Nie et al. (2022)), where the authors
      only used one torchsde call for the final image and one call of adjoint method for computing
388
      the gradient. We believe the granularity (one call vs. multiple calls of adjoint method) of gradient
389
      approximation causes the performance difference, and the use of multiple calls indeed provides
390   AutoAttack with sufficient information to generate a more powerful adversarial perturbation.
391
392   To verify our finding, we have observations across different datasets, as shown in Table 3. First,
393
      we selected a subset from CIFAR-10 testing dataset consisting of 64 images, then generated the
      corresponding adversarial examples from adaptive AutoAttack (Rand) with 20 EOT via two different
394
      implementations, including (1) AutoAttack (Rand-DiffPure): Original code from DiffPure (Nie et al.
395
      (2022)) using one torchsde function call and (2) AutoAttack (Rand-Ours): Our own implementa-
396   tion that pulls the output xt at each time step from torchsde solver, which means 100 torchsde
397   function calls. See Fig. 4 for comparison of different implementations of reverse diffusion process.
398
399
      We can see from Table 3 that in comparison with AutoAttack (Rand-DiffPure), the defense capability
      of DiffPure is remarkably reduced (the accuracy in boldface) when the adversarial examples generated
400
      from AutoAttack (Rand-Ours) are present, obviously indicating robustness overestimation. Actually,
401
      it is evidence of revealing that our implementation can let attackers create stronger adversarial
402   examples and can be used as a proxy to attack diffusion-based purifiers. Also, this finding sheds
403   light on whether using adjoint method hides the information used for creating stronger adversarial
404   examples in an adaptive AutoAttack setting.
405
      Finally, since DiffPure (Nie et al. (2022)) has not been evaluated in Croce et al. (2022), it is believed
406
      that this simple trick of implementation that creates stronger AutoAttack (Rand) can be an easy way
407
      of attacking test-time adversarial defense purifiers and a promising supplement to Croce et al. (2022).
408   In the following experimental evaluations, this kind of adjoint strategy will be used in implementing
409   stronger adaptive attacks.

456   Two types of adversarial attacks, including non-adaptive attack (PGD-ℓ∞ Madry et al. (2017),
457   AutoAttack (Standard) Croce & Hein (2020)) and adaptive attack (BPDA+EOT Athalye et al. (2018),
458   PGD+EOT Lee & Kim (2023) and DiffAttack Kang et al. (2024)), were adopted. For AutoAttack,
459
      we utilized the package AutoAttack (Croce & Hein (2020)) with ℓ∞ , in which it has two settings: (1)

460
      includes APGD-CE and APGD-DLR with Expectation Over Time (EOT) (Athalye et al. (2018)) in

      case of models with stochastic components. To the most extreme case in which the attacker knows

464   As mentioned in Sec. 3.4, our adjoint strategy will be used to implement stronger adaptive attacks in
465   order to avoid robustness overestimation.

467
      samples and robust accuracy (Robust Acc) for adversarial samples. Several test-time adversarial

473
      and AutoAttack (Standard)), in which the attacker only knows the information of the downstream

475   robustly trained classifier, Adversarial Weight Perturbation (AWP) (Wu et al. (2020)), as the testing

477   experiments.

530   5   C ONCLUSIONS & L IMITATIONS
531
532   We have presented a new test-time adversarial defense method. The key is to excessively denoise
533   the incoming input image along the opposite adversarial path (OAP) so as to move far away from
534   the decision boundary. This OAP prior can be readily plugged into the existing defense mechanisms
535   for robustness improvement. Our defense method also forces attackers to spend a great deal of time
536   creating adaptive adversarial examples. Meanwhile, we exemplify, for the first time, the pitfall of
537   conducting AutoAttack (Rand) for diffusion-based adversarial defense methods. However, we are
538
      aware there are several attacks targeting diffusion-based adversarial defenses, and the performance of
      our proposed method may be overestimated since the gradient computation is approximated.
