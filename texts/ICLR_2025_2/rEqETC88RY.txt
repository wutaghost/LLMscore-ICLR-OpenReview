009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The rapid advancement in large language models (LLMs) has brought forth a di-
012            verse range of models with varying capabilities that excel in different tasks and
013            domains. However, selecting the optimal LLM for user queries often involves a
014            challenging trade-off between accuracy and cost, a problem exacerbated by the
015            diverse demands of individual queries. In this work, we present a novel frame-
016            work that formulates the LLM selection process as a multi-armed bandit prob-
017            lem, enabling dynamic and intelligent routing of queries to the most appropri-
018            ate model. Our approach incorporates a preference-conditioned dynamic routing
019
               mechanism, allowing users to specify their preferences at inference time, thereby
               offering a customizable balance between performance and cost. Additionally, our
020
               selection policy is designed to generalize to unseen LLMs, ensuring adaptability
021
               to new models as they emerge. Experimental results demonstrate that our method
022            achieves significant improvements in both accuracy and cost-effectiveness across
023            various LLM platforms, showcasing the potential of our framework to adaptively
024            optimize LLM selection in real-world scenarios.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      By scaling up parameters and pretraining data, large language models (LLMs) have shown remark-
030   able abilities across a wide range of tasks. However, while larger models tend to be more capable
031   and versatile, they also come with higher costs — whether in terms of computational resources, API
032   service fees, or increased response latency. Additionally, the growing number of domain-specific
033   models often outperform these large models within their specialized areas. As a result, selecting the
034   most suitable LLM for specific applications has become increasingly challenging for users, espe-
035   cially when faced with cost constraints.
036   Existing approaches to address the performance-cost dilemma typically fall into three categories.
037   Firstly, ensemble methods (Jiang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) combine responses from multiple
038   LLMs to enhance overall performance. However, these methods require invoking multiple LLMs
039   for each query, leading to substantially higher costs. Secondly, cascading approaches (Ramı́rez
040   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; 2020) sequentially invoke LLMs, starting with the least expensive
041   and progressing to more costly models only if the initial responses are unsatisfactory. Lastly, routing
042   mechanisms (Ding et al., 2024; Ong et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024) direct user query to the most
043   appropriate LLM without invoking any of them, offering a more cost-effective solution. In this work,
044
      we focus on the routing approach due to its flexibility and potential for optimizing cost-efficiency.
045   However, designing an effective routing module for real-world deployment presents several chal-
046   lenges. First, the routing mechanism must generalize effectively across diverse user queries, ensur-
047   ing robust performance across various tasks and domains. Second, it should be capable of handling
048   any set of models, as new LLMs are continually being developed, and users may wish to specify a
049   particular set based on prior knowledge. Third, the routing mechanism needs to accommodate vary-
050
      ing user preferences, as different users may prioritize cost, performance, or other factors differently
      depending on their specific requirements.
051
052   To address these challenges, we propose a preference-conditioned dynamic routing mechanism for
053   LLMs. We frame the LLM routing task as a multi-armed bandit problem, where each user query is
      directed to the most suitable LLM based on a routing policy. Our policy leverages multi-objective

      2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
095
096   Given a set of K large language models (LLMs), denoted as {Mk }K      k=1 , we aim to develop a rout-
097   ing policy π : X → {1, . . . , K} that directs any query x ∈ X to the most appropriate LLM Mk .
098   The routing decision results in a reward s(x, k), reflecting the generation quality of Mk , while also
099   incurring a cost ck for invoking the model. We formulate this task as a multi-objective optimiza-
100   tion problem, where the reward vector is defined as r(x, k) = [s(x, k), −ck ]. Our objective is to
101   maximize the expected reward:
                 π ∗ = arg max Jπ = arg max Ex∼p(x),k∼π(x) s(x, k), −Ex∼p(x),k∼π(x) ck .

102                                                                                                      (1)
103                        π               π
104   In the context of multi-objective optimization, where the objectives often conflict with each other,
105   a single optimal policy that simultaneously maximizes all objectives does not exist. Instead, the
106   optimal policy varies according to user preferences. For instance, a user with a limited budget might
107   prioritize lower-cost models, even if this means compromising on generation quality. Conversely, a
      user who values high-quality outputs will have to accept higher costs. By introducing a preference

      parameter ω, we define a scalarized reward rω (x, k) = ω T r(x, k). The corresponding optimal
109   policy πω maximizes this scalarized expected reward Ex∼p(x),k∼πω (x) rω (x, k). The collection of
110   all such optimal policies is referred to as the Pareto set, with their respective expected rewards Jπω
111   forming the Pareto front.

113   2.2   OVERALL F RAMEWORK
114
115   Our dynamic routing mechanism consists of two key components: a model quizzing component,
116   which generates a model identity vector to capture each model’s capabilities across various tasks
117   and domains, and a preference-conditioned routing policy, which determines model selection prob-
118
      abilities based on user-specified preferences. Figure 1 provides an illustration of the framework. In
      the subsequent sections, we expound on the design details of these components.

      2.3   M ODEL I DENTITY V ECTOR
121
122   To accurately route queries to the appropriate LLM, we must first assess each LLM’s strengths and
123   weaknesses. For instance, queries requiring mathematical reasoning should be directed to LLMs
124   with strong math capabilities, while financial queries should be routed to models fine-tuned for
125   finance. We propose distilling each model’s capabilities into a dense vector based on its performance
126   across a diverse set of evaluation prompts.
127
      Given a set of N evaluation prompts, X = {xn }N      n=1 , which span various domains and tasks, we
128   collect the evaluation scores Yk = {ykn }N      for each  LLM Mk . The objective is to learn a model
                                                 n=1
129   identity vector, Ik ∈ Rd , that can effectively predict these evaluation scores. We employ a variant
130   of the Item Response Theory (IRT) model (Hambleton & Swaminathan, 2013) combined with deep
131   neural networks to perform this prediction. Unlike the traditional IRT model, we do not learn explicit
132   representations for the prompts themselves; instead, we obtain prompt embeddings, en , using a
133   pretrained text embedding model. These pretrained embeddings allow the IRT model to generalize
134   effectively to unseen prompts, which, as we will discuss later, also enhances the generalization
135   capability of the routing policy. Based on the prompt embedding en and the model identity vector
136
      Ik , a deep neural network f then predicts the evaluation scores as f (en , Ik ).
137   The training process closely follows that of traditional IRT, optimizing the binary cross-entropy loss.
138   For non-binary evaluation scores, we binarize them by selecting a threshold that ensures the average
139   performance across instances is comparable. Please refer to Appendix A.1.1 for details. Letting
140   ȳkn represent the binarized evaluation scores and pkn = sigmoid(f (en , Ik )) represent the predicted
141   probabilities, the loss function can be written as
142                           Lirt = Ex,k [−ȳkn log pkn − (1 − ȳkn ) log(1 − pkn )] .                    (2)
143   In addition to LLM evaluation benchmarks, where each model receives an evaluation score for a
144   specific prompt, pairwise comparisons, where responses from two models to the same prompt are
145   compared, are also widely used in the LLM evaluation literature. To increase prompt diversity, we
146   propose leveraging these pairwise comparisons to aid in learning the model identity vectors. Given
147
      responses from two models, Mk1 and Mk2 , and the annotations zn ∈ {0, 1}, indicating the winner
      of the comparison, we employ a secondary neural network, g, to predict the winning probabilities
148

149

      2.4   P REFERENCE C ONDITIONED ROUTING P OLICY
163
164   Recall that given a prompt xn , a set of LLMs {Mk }K
                                                         k=1 , and a preference vector ω, the oracle routing
165   policy π ∗ would maximize the scalarized reward rω (x, k) = ω T r(xn , k) = ω T [s(xn , k), −ck ].
166   However, the score s(xn , k) is typically not available, as it requires running inference for model
167   Mk on prompt xn and evaluating the response. A potential alternative is to estimate the scores
168   without actually performing inference. For instance, we could use f (en , Ik ) as an estimate of the
169   evaluation scores. This approach aligns conceptually with the methods in (Shnitzer et al., 2023; Hari
170   & Thomson, 2023; Šakota et al., 2024). However, predicted scores can often be inaccurate, as the
171   prediction model may fail to capture the finer nuances of model performance on specific prompts.
172
      Moreover, relying solely on predicted scores ignores the uncertainty in the prediction model itself.
      The discrepancy between estimated and actual utility can result in suboptimal routing decisions.
173
      To mitigate the impact of inaccurate predictions, Shnitzer et al. (2023) introduced a sophisticated
174
      meta-learning approach to reduce bias in the predicted scores.
175
176   Instead of relying solely on predicted scores, we advocate for learning a routing policy πθ that
177   directly operates on the prompts. Unlike direct score prediction, this approach allows the policy to
178
      capture more nuanced information from the prompts and implicitly manage prediction uncertainty.
      Additionally, optimizing a policy provides the flexibility to dynamically balance exploration and
179
      exploitation, allowing the system to discover new, potentially better models for specific queries
180
      while continuing to utilize known high-performing models, ultimately improving performance in
181   situations where uncertainty exists.
182
183   The routing policy πθ is formulated as a mapping from a prompt x ∈ X to a categorical distribution
184
      over the set of K LLMs. A key challenge in learning such a routing policy is dealing with diverse
      LLM routing candidates. For example, users might specify different sets of models depending
185
      on their application needs, or new models could be added after the routing system is deployed.
186
      Therefore, the policy must accommodate varied action spaces, where both the number of models
187   and the specific set of available models may change dynamically. To address the varied action
188   space, we condition the routing policy on the set of model identity vectors {Ik }K        k=1 , which represent
189   the capabilities of each model. Conditioning on the action space allows the policy to be aware of all
190   available choices, thereby improving routing decisions even in dynamic environments. Furthermore,
191   we can condition the policy on other available context information, such as the cost ck and the score
192   prediction p̂k = sigmoid(f (x, Ik )). These context information can assist the policy to make better-
193   informed routing decisions across different LLM candidates. The policy is defined as

258   2.5   G ENERALIZATION OF THE ROUTING P OLICY
259
260   In this section, we discuss generalizing the routing policy to handle diverse user preferences, varied
261   routing candidates, and a wide range of prompts, while also addressing the integration of new models
262   into the routing system. Please refer to Algorithm 1 for pseudo code of the training procedure.
263
      Generalize to Varied Routing Candidates To ensure the routing policy generalizes to arbitrary sets
264   of LLMs, it is designed to incorporate the action space as an input. To further enhance generalization
265   across different sets of models, we train the policy on diverse trajectories sampled from varied action
266   spaces. In practice, we leverage existing evaluation leaderboards, such as HELM (Liang et al., 2022),
267   where scores are provided for a wide range of models. During training, we randomly select sets of
268   models with differing cost profiles and apply the current policy to route evaluation prompts to the
269   most appropriate LLM. The resulting routing trajectories are stored in a replay buffer, which is used
      to train the routing policy in an off-policy manner.

      Generalize to Unseen Prompts A major challenge in designing a routing system is ensuring its gen-
271   eralizability to unseen prompts. While LLM evaluation leaderboards like HELM (Liang et al., 2022)
272   already include diverse prompts, they still fall short of covering all possible user queries encountered
273   in practice. To diversify training data, we propose pretraining the routing policy on pairwise com-
274   parison datasets, such as Nectar (Zhu et al., 2023) and Chatbot Arena (Zheng et al., 2023). These
275   datasets feature diverse user queries but lack model-specific evaluation scores s(x, k). Instead, these
276   datasets provide binary winning labels, making them unsuitable for direct policy training. Training
277   the policy to simply predict the winning models may not align with the optimal routing choices,
278   especially when considering cost and user preferences. A straightforward approach might rely on
279
      predicted scores p̂k = sigmoid(f (x, Ik )). However, inaccuracies in these predictions can degrade
      routing quality. To mitigate this, we propose calibrating the predicted scores using Platt scaling
280
      (Platt et al., 1999). Specifically, given the predicted logits f (x, Ik1 ) and f (x, Ik2 ), we fit a logis-
281
      tic regression model to predict the binary winning label z ∈ {0, 1} with the following probability:
282   p(z = 1) = sigmoid(α(f (x, Ik1 ) − f (x, Ik2 )) + β), where α and β are learnable parameters. The
283   calibrated scores are then computed as p̄k = sigmoid(αf (x, Ik ) + β). The routing action is deter-
284   mined by â = arg maxk∈{k1,k2} ω T [p̄k , −ck ], and the policy is trained in a supervised manner to
285   predict these actions. By exposing the policy to diverse prompts during pretraining, we encourage
286   it to generalize to unseen queries. An alternative to the supervised pretraining is using calibrated
287   scores within an RL framework, however, preliminary results indicate that RL is more sensitive to
288   prediction errors, therefore we opt for the simpler supervised pretraining.
289   In addition to large-scale pretraining, research has shown that certain regularization techniques can
290   enhance the generalizability of RL policies (Farebrother et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020). We adapt
291   the mixup regularization (Zhang, 2017), which has proven effective in RL (Wang et al., 2020), and
292   tailor it to our routing setting. Specifically, we introduce on-manifold mixup, where prompt embed-
293   dings are linearly combined with their nearest neighbors from the replay buffer. This neighborhood-
294   based mixup ensures that the interpolated embeddings remain on the manifold of the prompt space.
295   For further details, see Appendix A.3.2.
296   Generalize to Diverse User Preferences To accommodate diverse user preferences, we train the
297   policy using trajectories sampled with varying preferences. The challenge arises from the diversity
298   of routing candidates, as different sets of models exhibit different score and cost scales. As a result,
299   the same preference may lead to different trade-offs across different sets of LLMs. To address this,
300   we normalize the scores and costs so that the highest score and cost in each LLM set equals 1.0. We
301   define the preference vector as ω = [1, ω] and sample ω uniformly from the range [0, 2]. In practice,
302   this simple normalization approach has proven effective. We apply the normalization during both
303
      the supervised pretraining and RL training stages. Please find further details in Appendix A.3.3.

      LLM Ensemble, Cascade and Routing As the number of LLMs grows, there is increasing interest
      in combining them to optimize performance and balance costs. LLM ensemble methods improve
333
      response quality by aggregating outputs from multiple LLMs but incur high computational costs
334
      since they require running inference on multiple models (Jiang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Lu
335   et al., 2024). LLM cascading reduces costs by invoking LLMs sequentially, starting with the least
336   expensive model and progressing to more costly ones until a satisfactory response is obtained (Chen
337   et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Ramı́rez et al., 2024). While effective in reducing costs, cascading
338   still requires multiple inferences, especially for complex queries, and often depends on an additional
339   model to assess the quality of the responses.
340
      In contrast, LLM routing sends queries directly to the most appropriate model, requiring only a
341   single inference and thus offering a more cost-efficient solution. Typical routing methods rely on
342   performance prediction models to guide the selection of the optimal LLM. These methods either
343   predict downstream evaluation or reward scores for a given query prompt (Shnitzer et al., 2023; Lu
344   et al., 2023; Hari & Thomson, 2023; Šakota et al., 2024), or estimate win rates between pairs of
345   models (Ding et al., 2024; Ong et al., 2024). The chosen LLM is then selected based on predicted
346   performance and any additional constraints, such as cost or latency.
347
      The most relevant work to ours is MetaLLM (Nguyen et al., 2024), which also frames the routing
348
      task as a multi-armed bandit problem. However, MetaLLM optimizes a scalarized reward and oper-
349   ates on a fixed set of LLMs, limiting the learned policy to specific user preferences and a predefined
350   set of models. Our approach, by contrast, generalizes to varied user preferences and dynamically
351   adapts to new LLMs added to the system, ensuring broader applicability and greater flexibility.
352
      Multi-objective Reinforcement Learning Multi-objective RL seeks to optimize multiple, often
353
      conflicting reward signals within a Markov decision process, resulting in a set of Pareto-optimal
354
      policies known as the Pareto set rather than a single optimal policy. Traditional algorithms typi-
355   cally aim to approximate this Pareto set by searching for a finite number of policies (Van Moffaert
356   & Nowé, 2014; Parisi et al., 2014; Xu et al., 2020). However, these methods face the curse of di-
357   mensionality, where the number of policies needed to accurately approximate the Pareto set grows
358   exponentially with the number of objectives. To address this, recent approaches have proposed us-
359   ing a single deep neural network conditioned on preferences to represent the entire Pareto set (Yang
360   et al., 2019; Abels et al., 2019; Basaklar et al., 2022). Another approach involves using hypernet-
361   works (Chauhan et al., 2023), which map user preferences to the parameters of the policy network
362   (Shu et al., 2024). Our routing policy aligns with the conditional neural network framework, where
363
      a single model is conditioned on user preferences to adapt to different user requirements. We further
      tailor this conditional architecture specifically for routing in LLM systems, allowing for efficient
364
      decision-making across a diverse and expanding set of models.
365
366   Generalization in Reinforcement Learning Generalizing RL policies to new tasks, often referred
367   to as zero-shot RL, is a growing area of research focused on enabling policies to handle unseen tasks
368   without retraining (Korkmaz, 2024). Approaches typically fall into three categories: The first cat-
369
      egory focuses on maximizing worst-case performance across tasks, often using adversarial training
      (Moos et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023). This approach is commonly used when no data is available
370
      to identify the current task. The second category aims to compute task representations from data,
371
      allowing agents to adapt their policies to the specific task at hand. This approach is commonly em-
372   ployed in multi-task RL and hidden-parameter MDPs (Konidaris & Doshi-Velez, 2014), where task
373   representations are inferred from exploration data within the task environment (Touati & Ollivier,
374   2021; Agarwal et al., 2021; Benjamins et al., 2022; Ingebrand et al., 2024). The third category lever-
375   ages in-context learning by feeding data from the current task directly into a pretrained transformer
376   as context (Melo, 2022; Brohan et al., 2022). Although transformers have demonstrated effective-
377   ness, their high memory consumption, training instability, and data inefficiency present challenges to
      their broader application. Our routing policy falls into the second category, where the task represen-

                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      tation is explicitly provided as a set of LLMs and their associated costs. In a similar vein, Jain et al.
379   (2020) explore RL generalization to new action spaces using a VAE to learn action representations,
380   whereas we capture LLM capabilities via identity vectors.
381
382
      In addition to task generalization, research has also explored generalizing RL policies to new obser-
      vation distributions. Techniques include data augmentation (Cobbe et al., 2019; Yarats et al., 2021;
383
      Laskin et al., 2020), specialized architectures (Lee et al., 2019b), regularization methods (Fare-
384
      brother et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020), invariant representation learning (Tachet et al., 2018; Zhang
385   et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2021), and adversarial observation perturbations (Zhang & Guo, 2021;
386   Korkmaz, 2022). Our approach explores a simple regularization technique that encourage smooth-
387   ness across prompt distributions.

516   5   C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we introduce a novel routing policy for selecting among LLMs based on user-specific
519
      preferences. We frame the problem as a multi-objective optimization task, balancing performance
      and cost, and proposed a preference-conditioned approach that adapts to individual user preference
520
      at inference time. Our method generalized to new and unseen LLMs by leveraging a shared model
521
      identity space, enabling seamless integration of new models as they emerge. Through comprehen-
522   sive experiments on popular evaluation benchmarks, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our routing
523   policy, showing its ability to generalize across a variety of LLMs, prompts and user preferences.
524
525
      Despite these promising results, there are several areas for future improvement. First, we train
      the routing policy in an offline setting, where all evaluation scores are pre-computed on a fixed
526
      set of prompts. A more dynamic, online setting-where the model adapts based on real-time feed-
527
      back—would likely improve the robustness and generaliability of the policy. Second, we treat the
528   cost of each model as a constant, but in practice, the cost can vary depending on factors such as
529   input length. Future work could explore adaptive cost modeling that takes into account the specific
530   input characteristics. Additionally, while our focus has been on pure LLM performance, many mod-
531   ern LLMs have the ability to invoke external tools or perform online searches, which could provide
532   richer decision-making capabilities. Incorporating these external functions into the routing policy
533   would be a valuable extension. Finally, although we normalize the scores and costs to align with
534   user preferences, more sophisticated methods for calibrating user preferences could be explored.
535   Setting real-valued preferences may not be intuitive for all users, and designing a more user-friendly
536
      interface for preference input, or automating the calibration process, could improve user experience.
537   In conclusion, our work demonstrates the potential of adaptive, preference-based LLM routing sys-
538   tems in maximizing utility across multiple models. With further advancements, these systems can
539   be made even more robust, efficient, and user-friendly, meeting the evolving demands of LLM usage
      in real-world applications.

816
      The supervised pretraining stage leverages diverse prompts from pairwise comparison datasets to en-
817
      hance generalization to unseen prompts. Given a pairwise comparison dataset V, where each exam-
818
      ple consists of a prompt xn , a pair of models Mk1 and Mk2 , and a winning label zn ∈ {0, 1}, we first
819   train a logistic regression model to calibrate the predicted evaluation scores, p̂ = sigmoid(f (x, Ik )),
820   using the winning label zn . Specifically, the logical regression model predicts the wining probability
821   as p(zn = 1) = sigmoid(α(f (xn , Ik1 ) − f (xn , Ik2 )) + β), where α and β are learnable param-
822   eters. After training, the calibrated evaluation scores are given by p̄ = sigmoid(αf (x, Ik ) + β).
823   The calibration follows the well-known Platt scaling (Platt et al., 1999) algorithm, which refines the
824   evaluation scores using human-labeled winning labels to produce more accurate predictions.
825   With the calibrated evaluation scores p̄ on a prompt x and a user preference vector ω, the routing
826   action is determined by â = arg maxk∈{k1,k2} ω T [p̄k , −ck ]. We then pretrain the routing policy in
827   a supervised manner using the following negative log-likelihood loss:

873   range [0, 2] effectively captures the Pareto front.

1105   The RL training stage follows a modified PPO procedure tailored for the multi-objective optimiza-

1109   ω = [1, ω] and ω is sampled from the distribution U (ωmin , ωmax ). The training process starts
1110
       with generating the trajectories following the current policy a ∼ π(x, {(Ik , c̄k , p̂k )}Kk=1 , ω). The
       multi-objective reward for action a is represented as a vector [s̄a , −c̄a ]. We update the replay buffer
1111
       with these sampled trajectories and use samples from the buffer to train the policy. For mixup
1112
       regularization, we identify the nearest neighbor for each sampled prompt and perform a weighted
1113   linear combination of the prompt embedding and its neighbors, where the weights are drawn from
1114   Beta(0.2, 0.2). Both the reward and the advantage are linearly combined using the same weights.
1115
1116
       At each training step, we sample 256 new prompts, along with their routing candidates and prefer-
       ence vectors, to obtain the routing trajectories and update the replay buffer. The training stage runs
1117
       for 500 steps with a batch size of 256, using Adam optimizer with learning rate of 0.001.

1201   B.7.2    P REDICTOR
1202
1203   The predicted evaluation scores p̂k = sigmoid(f (x, Ik )) can be used directly to compute the scalar-
1204   ized reward for an LLM Mk as rω (x, k) = ω T [p̂k , −ck ]. The routing decision is then made by
1205   selecting â = arg maxk rω (x, k).
1206
1207   B.7.3    R ANDOM
1208   The random routing policy selects models based on predefined probabilities for each model. Dif-
1209   ferent user preferences are reflected by adjusting these probabilities. However, when there are more
1210   than two LLM candidates, specifying the probabilities becomes non-trivial, so we omit the random
1211   baseline in these scenarios.
1212
1213   B.7.4    O RACLE
1214
1215
       The oracle routing policy selects the model based on the actual evaluation scores, making the routing
       decision as â = arg maxk rω (x, k) = arg maxk ω T [s(x, k), −ck ], where s(x, k) represents the true
1216
       performance score for model Mk on prompt x.
1217
1218
       B.7.5    PPO
1219
1220   For each LLM candidate set and each user preference, we train a separate PPO routing policy to
1221   maximize the scalarized reward rω (x, k) = ω T [s(x, k), −ck ].
