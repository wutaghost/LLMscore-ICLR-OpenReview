011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Record-specific Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) are widely used to evaluate
014            the propensity of a machine learning (ML) model to memorize an individual record
015
               and the privacy risk its release therefore poses. Record-specific MIAs are currently
               evaluated the same way ML models are: on a test set of models trained on data
016
               samples that were not seen during training (Deval ). A recent large body of literature
017
               has however shown that the main risk often comes from outliers, records that are
018            statistically different from the rest of the dataset. In this work, we argue that
019            the traditional evaluation setup for record-specific MIAs, which includes dataset
020            sampling as a source of randomness, incorrectly captures the privacy risk. Indeed,
021            what is an outlier is highly specific to particular data samples, and a record that
022            is an outlier in the training dataset will not necessarily be one in the randomly
023            sampled test datasets. We propose to use model randomness as the only source of
024            randomness to evaluate record-level MIAs, a setup we call model-seeded. Across
025            10 combinations of models, datasets, and attacks for predictive and generative AI,
026
               we show the per-record risk estimates given by the traditional evaluation setup to
               substantially differ from ones given by the model-seeded setup which properly
027
               account for the increased risk posed by outliers. We show that across setups
028
               the traditional evaluation method leads to a substantial number of records to be
029            incorrectly classified as low risk, emphasizing the inadequacy of the current setup
030            to capture the record-level risk. We then a) provide evidence that the traditional
031            setup is an average–across datasets–of the model-seeded risk, validating our use
032            of model randomness to create evaluation models and b) show how relying on the
033            traditional setup might conceal the existence of stronger attacks. The traditional
034            setup would indeed strongly underestimate the risk posed by the strong Differential
035            Privacy adversary. We believe our results to convincingly show the practice of
036            randomizing datasets to evaluate record-specific MIAs to be incorrect. We then
037
               argue that relying on model randomness, an setup we call model-seeded evaluation,
               better captures the risk posed by outliers and should be used moving forward to
038
               evaluate record-level MIAs against machine learning models, both predictive and
039
               generative.

042   1    I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Predictive and generative Machine Learning (ML) models are increasingly trained or fine-tuned by
045   companies, governments, and academic researchers often using data that can be personal. Originally
046   developed for aggregate data (Homer et al., 2008; Sankararaman et al., 2009), membership inference
047   attacks (MIAs) have become the standard method to evaluate the privacy risks of ML models (Shokri
048   et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022a). In particular, record-specific MIAs are used to evaluate the risk of
049   an attacker being able to infer that a target record was a member of the released model’s training set.
050   Record-level MIAs against ML models are typically instantiated as a binary meta-classifier, predicting
051   membership for a given target record (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022a; Stadler et al., 2022).
052   The meta-classifier is trained using auxiliary data, and then evaluated on models trained on datasets
053   randomly sampled from a large held-out data pool dedicated to evaluation. The target record is then
      added to a fraction of the datasets, typically to half of them to create a balanced evaluation setup.


                                                          1
054
      Though using dataset sampling as a source of randomness is a natural extension of the ML evaluation
055   pipeline to MIAs, we argue that–in light of recent evidence–this does not properly evaluate the privacy
056   risk posed by an ML model release in practice. Recent research on MIAs against machine learning
057   models has indeed shown the privacy risk to mostly lie with outliers, records that are statistically
058   different from the rest of the dataset, being memorized. Stadler et al. (2022) and Meeus et al. (2023)
059   have, for instance, shown how outlier records, often minorities, are vulnerable to attacks on synthetic
060   data generators. (Carlini et al., 2022b) showed outliers to be vulnerable when attacking predictive
061   ML models. They, and further work, showed how removing high-risk records causes the risk of other
062   records to increase, emphasizing how the risk of a record is highly dependent on the dataset it is in
063
      and proposed to measure the overall privacy risk using TPR at low FPR.
064   Contribution. In this paper, we argue–in light of recent evidence–that the traditional evaluation
065   setup for record-level MIAs, which includes dataset sampling as a source of randomness, incorrectly
066   captures the privacy risk for outliers. Instead we propose to use model randomness as the only source
067   of randomness to evaluate record-level MIAs, a setup we call model-seeded.
068   First, we describe the traditional MIA evaluation setup currently used in the literature with dataset
069   sampling as a source of randomness. We then describe our proposed model-seeded evaluation setup
070   which only uses randomness of the model - weight initialization and training randomness - as a source
071   of randomness, which we argue allows it to capture the risk posed by outliers.
072
      Second, we instantiate both evaluation setups across 10 combinations of models, datasets, and attacks
073   for predictive and generative AI. We show the per-record risk estimates given by the traditional
074   evaluation setup to substantially differ from ones given by the model-seeded setup which properly
075   account for the increased risk posed by outliers. For instance, we find that 94% of high-risk records
076   in the Adult dataset would be incorrectly considered as low-risk when using the traditional setup to
077   evaluate a model trained using synthpop.
078
      Third, we derive theoretical results which, combined with empirical evidence, strongly suggest that
079   the risk calculated in the traditional setup is indeed an average of the risks specific to each dataset
080   sampled for testing, as evaluated using the model-seeded setup. We argue these results validate our
081   use of model randomness as the (only) source of randomness when evaluating record-specific MIAs
082   against ML models.
083
      Finally, we show that the traditional setup would strongly underestimate the risk posed by the strong
084
      Differential Privacy (DP) adversary. We instantiate an MIA attack by the very strong DP attacker
085   with knowledge of the training dataset Dtarget . We show that this attack leads to drastically and
086   significantly improved membership inference when evaluated in the model-seeded setup. Yet, the
087   increased strength of the attacker has no measurable impact when evaluated in the traditional setup.
088   Concerningly this show that the practice of using the traditional evaluation setup to evaluate new
089   attacks risks concealing the existence and effectiveness of stronger attacks.
090
      Taken together, we believe our results to convincingly show the practice of randomizing datasets
091   to evaluate record-specific MIAs to be incorrect. We argue that relying on model randomness, a
092   setup we call model-seeded evaluation, better captures the risk posed by outliers and should be used
093   moving forward to evaluate record-level MIAs against machine learning models, both predictive and
094   generative.

      2    R ELATED WORK
098
      MIAs have become the standard method for auditing the privacy risk of ML models and synthetic
099
      data generators (Jagielski et al., 2020; Hayes et al., 2019; Steinke et al., 2024; Nasr et al., 2021). In
100
      particular, record-level MIAs are being used to evaluate the risk posed by the released model for each
101   record and validate formal privacy guarantees (Stadler et al., 2022; Guépin et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
102   2022; Houssiau et al., 2022).
103
104
      While new techniques are continuously proposed (Leino & Fredrikson, 2020; Nasr et al., 2019; Yeom
      et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2018; Carlini et al., 2022a; Stadler et al., 2022), most rely on the shadow
105
      modeling technique introduced by Ateniese et al. (2015) and popularized by Shokri et al. (2017).
106
107   Song & Mittal (2020) introduce Mentr, an attack using a modified version of prediction entropy to
      infer membership, relying on the assumption that the expectation that a model’s prediction entropy


                                                         2
108
      will be higher on unseen samples. LiRA, introduced by Carlini et al. (2022a), combines shadow
109   modelling and statistical testing to determine membership. Most recently, Zarifzadeh et al. (2024)
110   introduced a new shadow model-based MIA that achieves high performances with fewer shadow
111   models than previous attacks. As MIAs for ML models often rely on per-record predictions and
112   loss values, they typically cannot be directly applied to synthetic data generators. Black-box attacks
113   leveraging shadow models have thus been developed specifically for synthetic data. They rely
114   on measuring the impact of the target record on the generated synthetic dataset by modelling the
115   distributions of synthetic datasets generated with and without the target record. These include Stadler
116   et al. (2022) and the state-of-the-art query-based attack which we use here (Houssiau et al., 2022).
117   Recent work has also shown privacy risk to vary across records, datasets, and classes (Tobaben et al.,
118   2024; Yu et al., 2024), and the risk of a dataset to lie in most part with a small number of strongly
119   memorized records (Feldman & Zhang, 2020). Outliers, in a general statistical sense, have been
120   shown to be particularly vulnerable Meeus et al. (2023); Thudi et al. (2024); Stadler et al. (2022), and
121   their risk to be highly dependent on other records in the dataset. As outliers are highly specific to the
122   dataset they are in, this suggests that the risk of a record is also not absolute, but rather relative to the
123
      dataset it is contained in (Carlini et al., 2022b).
124   The standard evaluation setup for MIAs uses dataset sampling as a source of randomness. While
125   not all works are explicit in their explanations of evaluation method, based on published work and
126   available code, we have found that, to the best of our knowledge, record-specific risk is evaluated
127   using dataset sampling as a source of randomness (Stadler et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022a;b; Guépin
128   et al., 2023; Meeus et al., 2023; Houssiau et al., 2022).
129
130   MIA performance evaluation. Record-specific risk is traditionally evaluated using dataset sam-
131   pling as a source of randomness. AUC or, alternatively, accuracy were the primary metrics used to
132
      measure the success of an MIA, usually with a balanced test set (Shokri et al., 2017; Choquette-Choo
      et al., 2021; Hayes et al., 2019). Further research demonstrated that privacy risk is not uniform across
133
      records, with some records shown to be much more vulnerable than average (Feldman & Zhang,
134
      2020; Meeus et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2022a; Stadler et al., 2022). Following these findings, metrics
135   focusing on the most at-risk records were adopted as the de-facto standard in the MIA literature. This
136   includes metrics focusing on the records identified a posteriori as being particularly at risk e.g. in
137   synthetic datasets (Stadler et al., 2022; Meeus et al., 2023) and metrics measuring the risk the most
138   vulnerable records across the entire dataset are exposed to, such as TPR (True Positive Rate) at low
139   FPR (False Positive Rate). Recent evidence (Carlini et al., 2022b; Meeus et al., 2023) also suggests
140   that at-risk records tend to be outliers, records that are different from other records in the dataset used
141   to trained the model.

197   3.2   T RADITIONAL EVALUATION SETUP
198
199   In the traditional evaluation setup, the MIA’s ability to distinguish between models trained on a
200   target record xtarget and models not trained on xtarget is evaluated by performing the MIA on
201   multiple models, all trained on different data samples taken from the evaluation pool, where xtarget
202   is included in exactly half of the samples. The source of randomness in this setup is thus the sampling
203   of evaluation datasets, leading to a risk score aggregated over the sampled datasets. A diagram of this
                                                                         ϕ                               ϕ
204   setup is presented on the left-hand side of Figure 1. We write Rtrad   (x, Deval ) (simplified as Rtrad )
205   for the risk estimated using the traditional setup using metric ϕ.
206
207   3.3   M ODEL - SEEDED EVALUATION SETUP
208
209   In the model-seeded MIA evaluation setup, we remove the randomness coming from the sampling of
210   evaluation datasets, and use the the model randomness–weight initialization and training seed–of the
211   evaluation models as the sole source of randomness. More specifically, we train N2 evaluation models
212   on Dtarget , ensuring that each is initialized with a different seed. For the remaining N2 evaluation
213   models, we train on Dtarget where xtarget is swapped out for a reference record xref , sampled from
214   Deval , again ensuring each has a different random seed. We estimate the privacy risk of xtarget by
215   calculating a performance score over all N evaluation models. The right-hand side of Figure 1 shows
      a diagram of this setup.

241   4     A CONCEPTUAL EXAMPLE
242
243   Previous work has shown outliers to be particularly susceptible to privacy attacks. However, outliers
244   are dataset-specific, a record that is an outlier in one dataset may not be an outlier in another.
245   Consequently, a record’s privacy risk is likely to change depending on the dataset it is contained in.
246   The traditional evaluation setup samples and averages across evaluation datasets where a target record
247   may not consistently be an outlier. Thus, the target record’s risk may be severely underestimated in
248
      this setup.
249   We illustrate the problem posed by the traditional evaluation setup using a toy example. We assume
250   an attacker who aims to infer whether a target record, here a picture of a bird, was part of the training
251   set of a model. By construction, we render the bird picture an outlier in the target dataset by sampling
252   non-uniformly from CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). We then sample the evaluation dataset
253   uniformly from CIFAR-10, making (artificially) the bird an outlier in the training dataset but likely
254
      not in the evaluation datasets. We then examine the risk reported by the both setups.

      6     I S Rtrad THE MEAN OF Rms ?
386
387
      In our model-seeded setup, we eliminate what we argue is an incorrect source of randomness to
388   evaluate MIAs against ML models: dataset sampling, as it does not provide a risk specific to the
389   relevant dataset. We hypothesise that the risk calculated in the traditional setup is the mean of the
390   risks specific to each dataset sampled in the traditional evaluation pipeline. In this section, we first
391   provide empirical evidence for this hypothesis on a subset of the target dataset in one setup. The
392   computational cost of empirically showing this hypothesis is very high, and it is infeasible to compute
393   for a large number of records, or multiple setups–in the setup of Synthpop and Adult, the experiment
394   takes 20 CPU hours for a single record, any would taken significantly more for the other setups.
395   We therefore also present a theoretical result that is efficiently empirically validated, and provides
396
      additional support for our hypothesis.

457
458   We have so far compared evaluation setups using SOTA attacks from the literature. These assume
459   the attacker to have access to an auxiliary dataset drawn from the same distribution but strictly non
460   overlapping with Dtarget .
461

462
      datasets including the training dataset Dtarget , similar to the standard DP attacker. In the traditional
463
      case, this means an attacker with access to the exact evaluation models–thus datasets sampled from
464   Deval –used for MIA evaluation. In the model-seeded case, this means an attacker with access to the
465   full target dataset Dtarget .
466
467
      We instantiate the stronger attack in our standard setup (Synthpop with the Adult dataset) and run the
      attack on 500 different target records randomly sampled from Dtarget . We compare the performance
468
      of the strong attacker to the attacker with only access to auxiliary data used previously (Sec. 5.1),
469
      which we refer to as the classic attack.
470
471   Fig. 4 shows the strong attacker to be able to achieve a substantially higher AU C 0.792±0.092 (mean
472   and standard deviation) than the classic attacker with access to an auxiliary dataset (0.601 ± 0.092)

      in the model-seeded setup. This is, however, not the case in the traditional setup where the strong
      attacker only achieves an AUC of 0.636 ± 0.054 compared to the 0.600 ± 0.060 achieved by the
474
      classic attacker.
475
476   These results further emphasize the need to use a model-seeded setup when evaluating MIAs against
477   machine learning models, especially as new stronger attacks, leveraging knowledge of the training
478   dataset, are likely to be developed in the future. Indeed, while we here focus on a very strong attacker,
479
      akin to the standard DP attacker, new attacks could leverage partial knowledge of the target dataset
      e.g. information leaked by the released synthetic dataset or knowledge about a specific part of the
480
      dataset where the target record would lie.

526   to be particularly impactful for statistical “outliers”, i.e. individuals whose data are significantly
527   different from others, and to help ensure that their privacy is preserved to an equal standard as that
528   of people whose data is closer to the average. Finally, we note that our results might lead to the
529   development of stronger attacks which could be used by malicious adversaries. However, we believe
530   that the knowledge our work provided to data controllers and for further research outweighs the
531   potential risk.

809   vulnerable, being an outlier is not the sole indicator of misclassification, and there are other factors
      that affect a record’s risk.

850   G    R ESULTS USING ACCURACY AND DISCUSSION ON EVALUATION METRICS
851
852   Table 3 shows the RMSE values across all setups when accuracy is used to calculate the risk score.
853   In this work, we use ROC AUC and accuracy as performance metrics for MIAs. Here we note that
854   though TPR at low FPR is currently the most widely-used metric for evaluating MIAs (Carlini et al.,
855   2022a; Zarifzadeh et al., 2024; Song & Mittal, 2020), the information it provides is not relevant to
856   evaluating per-record attacks. TPR at low FPR is specifically used when applying a general attack to
857
      a set of records, helping to identify records for which the attack confidently determines membership.
      As we train and evaluate a separate attack for each record, TPR at low FPR would not be informative
858
      in the same way.
