                                                 A BSTRACT
013
014
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities across a
015
               range of language tasks. However, questions remain about whether LLMs effec-
016
               tively encode linguistic structures such as phrases and sentences and how closely
017            these representations align with those in the human brain. Here, we introduce the
018            Hierarchical Frequency Tagging Probe (HFTP) to probe the phrase and sentence
019            representations in LLMs and the human brain in a unified manner. HFTP uti-
020            lizes frequency-domain analysis to identify which LLM computational modules
021            (multilayer perceptron (MLP) neurons) or human cortical areas encode phrases
022            or sentences. Human brain activity is recorded using intracranial electrodes. The
023            results revealed distinct sensitivities to sentences and phrases across various lay-
024            ers of LLMs (including GPT-2, Gemma, Llama 2, Llama 3.1, and GLM-4) and
025
               across different regions of the human brain. Notably, while LLMs tend to pro-
               cess sentences and phrases within similar layers, the human brain engages distinct
026
               regions to process these two syntactic levels. Additionally, representational sim-
027
               ilarity analysis (RSA) shows that the syntactic representations of all five LLMs
028            are more aligned with neural representations in the left hemisphere—the domi-
029            nant hemisphere for language processing. Among the LLMs, GPT-2 and Llama 2
030            show the greatest similarity to human brain syntactic representations, while Llama
031            3.1 demonstrates a weaker resemblance. Overall, our findings provide deeper in-
032            sights into syntactic processing in LLMs and highlight the effectiveness of HFTP
033            as a versatile tool for detecting syntactic structures across diverse LLM architec-
034            tures and parameters, as well as in parallel analyses of human brains and LLMs,
035            thereby bridging computational linguistics and cognitive neuroscience.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Language is fundamental to human communication, thought, and cultural transmission. Accord-
040
      ing to the framework proposed by Noam Chomsky, language is divided into three key components:
041
      semantics (meaning), phonology (sound), and syntax (hierarchical sentence structure) (Chomsky,
042   1965). Syntax is particularly crucial as it governs how words combine into meaningful sentences,
043   enabling the recursive and generative properties unique to human language. The theory of univer-
044   sal grammar proposed by Noam Chomsky suggests that all human languages share innate syntactic
045   rules (Chomsky, 1980). Building on this foundation, cognitive scientists have shown that syntactic
046   processing is distinct from other language functions, relying on mechanisms specifically dedicated
047   to organizing abstract grammatical structures (Matchin & Hickok, 2017; Pylkkanen & Bemis, 2011).
048   Furthermore, as sentence complexity increases, the neural workload also intensifies, reflecting the
049   capacity of the human brain to resolve syntactic ambiguities and manage grammatical dependen-
050
      cies (Pylkkanen & Bemis, 2011). As more advances are made in understanding human syntactic
      processing, language models in the field of artificial intelligence have been developed that aim to
051
      capture and comprehend human language.
052
053   In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have rapidly evolved, surpassing human-level capa-
      bilities in numerous tasks (Vaswani et al., 2017; Brown et al., 2020). Specially, the performance of


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      LLMs in natural language understanding, translation, and summarization has led to claims that they
055   possess a remarkable degree of human-like linguistic ability, especially in generating language that
056   adheres to the surface rules of syntax (Radford et al., 2019). However, the question of whether LLMs
057   can process the hierarchical structures of sentences in a manner comparable to humans remains un-
058   clear. While some studies suggest that LLMs can successfully capture and manipulate hierarchical
059   structures of sentences (Manning et al., 2020), others argue that LLMs lack the deeper syntactic
060   processing capabilities observed in human cognition (Linzen et al., 2016; McCoy et al., 2019). This
061   controversy stems from the absence of a unified framework to rigorously evaluate and compare the
062   syntactic processing abilities of LLMs with those of the human brain, making it difficult to draw
063
      definitive conclusions about their true linguistic competence (Tenney et al., 2019; Warstadt et al.,
      2020). Therefore, it is crucial to develop a method that can simultaneously probe the hierarchical
064
      syntactic structure representations in both LLMs and the human brain, and on this basis, explore the
065
      internal similarities of syntactic representations between them.
066
067   Ding and colleagues (Ding et al., 2016) introduced the hierarchical frequency tagging (HFT) tech-
068   nique to uncover how the human brain processes hierarchical linguistic structures during natural
069
      speech comprehension. In this paradigm, monosyllabic words are presented at a rate of 4Hz to
      form phrases at 2Hz, which combine into sentences at 1Hz. Using frequency-domain analysis of
070
      electrophysiological signals, Ding and colleagues deconstruct the processing of linguistic structures
071
      such as phrases and sentences. Subsequent research further highlighted the importance of atten-
072   tional mechanisms in processing these hierarchical structures (Ding et al., 2018), while other studies
073   have shown that HFT captures distinct neural responses to different linguistic elements (Keitel et al.,
074   2018). Moreover, Martin et al. extended this framework by exploring how the brain organizes com-
075   plex linguistic stimuli (Martin & Doumas, 2017). These studies demonstrate the effectiveness of
076   HFT in revealing language processing patterns. Given the differences in temporal processing be-
077   tween LLMs and the human brain, aligning their syntactic representations in the frequency domain
078   is a feasible and necessary approach.
079   Building on the HFT paradigm Ding et al. (2016), here we developed the Hierarchical Frequency
080   Tagging Probe (HFTP) to investigate whether specific computational modules within LLMs process
081   hierarchical sentence structures. HFTP offers a unified approach to explore internal similarities and
082   systematically examine the alignment of syntactic representations between LLMs and the human
083   brain. The key contributions of this paper are: (1) We innovatively employed frequency-domain
084   analysis using HFTP to characterize the syntactic representations of every computational unit in
085   each layer of LLMs; (2) HFTP provides a simple and universally applicable approach for both
086
      LLMs and the human brain, establishing a platform for studying the alignment of syntactic structure
      representations between them; (3) Using syntactic templates derived from HFTP, we identified brain
087
      regions highly correlated with LLMs, predominantly located in key language-processing areas of the
088
      left hemisphere; (4) By comparing five LLMs, we found that the internal syntactic representations
089   of GPT-2 and Llama 2 exhibit higher overall similarity to those of the human brain. In sum, HFTP
090   effectively aligns representations between LLMs and the human brain, providing a novel platform
091   for future alignment studies.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Syntactic processing in the human brain In humans, syntactic processing involves a complex
097   neural network that organizes words into hierarchical structures, enabling infinite expression from a
098   finite set of elements. Research on brain damage and neuroimaging has indicated a left hemisphere
099   advantage in language processing (Friederici & Brauer, 2009; Hagoort, 2013; Blank et al., 2016),
100   with further evidence suggesting that this lateralization may stem from distinct sensitivity to tem-
101   poral modulations crucial for speech perception (Albouy et al., 2020). However, recent evidence
102   suggests that syntactic processing relies on a distributed network across frontal and temporal ar-
103   eas, with significant overlap in regions responsible for both syntactic and semantic functions (Blank
104
      et al., 2016; Fedorenko et al., 2020). These findings demonstrate that syntactic processing is not
      confined to isolated regions but is part of a broad, interconnected network.
105
106   Syntactic processing in language models Even before the development of LLMs, researchers found
107   that simple LSTM language models could capture syntax-sensitive dependencies, such as subject-
      verb agreement (Linzen et al., 2016; Kuncoro et al., 2018). Using a technique called structural


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      probing, Manning and colleagues discovered that transformer-based models like BERT can encode
109   hierarchical syntactic trees, enabling such models to implicitly represent complex syntax without
110   direct training (Hewitt & Manning, 2019). These transformer-based models excel at tracking both
111   local and long-range dependencies through specialized attention mechanisms, distributing syntactic
112   knowledge across layers (Clark, 2019; Tenney et al., 2019; Manning et al., 2020). However, the
113   methods employed in these studies of language models make it challenging to apply findings to the
114   exploration of human brain activity.
115   Alignment between LLMs and the human brain Previous research on the alignment between
116   LLMs and human brain representations has largely focused on next-word prediction without dis-
117   tinguishing between semantics and syntax. This approach compares the prediction probabilities of
118   LLMs with human brain activity to assess shared computational principles (Schrimpf et al., 2021;
119   Goldstein et al., 2022). Recent studies indicate that syntactic processing representations closely cor-
120   relate with brain regions responsible for hierarchical syntax (Caucheteux & King, 2022; Oota et al.,
121   2024). Moreover, efforts to disentangle syntax from semantics reveal distinct patterns in both neural
122   and model representations (Caucheteux et al., 2021). While some alignment exists, variations in
123
      task complexity suggest that LLMs display more layer-specific syntactic processing (Tuckute et al.,
      2024). However, these studies utilize different corpora, alignment methods, and LLMs, making it
124
      difficult to conduct a systematic investigation of syntactic representations in large models. A unified
125
      tool is needed to probe syntactic structure representations across the human brain and various LLMs,
126   enabling systematic comparisons of representation similarities at the neuronal population level.

      3.1   DATA AND LLM S
166
167   We utilized Chinese and English corpora adapted from (Ding et al., 2016), consisting of four-syllable
168   sequences in Chinese or four-word sequences in English, where the first two and last two units form
169   phrases (see Figure 1). Further details regarding the corpus can be found in Appendix A.6. For both
170   the sEEG and model-brain alignment experiments, we used the same two Chinese corpora—the
171   sentence and phrase corpora—from (Sheng et al., 2019). While these corpora share a similar
172   structure to the Chinese syntactic corpus used in the LLM experiments, they differ in content. To
173   control for semantic processing, we created a word-order randomized version for each corpus as
174
      a control condition. In this randomized version, the semantics of individual words are preserved,
      while the syntactic structure is disrupted.
175
176   We applied HFTP to five state-of-the-art LLMs—GPT-2, Gemma, Llama 2, Llama 3.1, and GLM-
177   4—which vary in both architecture and parameter scale (see Table 3). As GPT-3.5 and its subse-
178   quent versions are not open-source, we were limited to using GPT-2 from the GPT series for LLM
179   experiments. Notably, the term “MLP neuron” refers here to computational modules within the mul-
180
      tilayer perceptron (MLP) layers of a Transformer model. These neurons are part of the feed-forward
      network, which follows the attention mechanism in each layer. The MLP consists of two fully con-
181
      nected layers, and the number of neurons corresponds to the hidden units that process the input after
182
      attention has been applied.

      3.2   S YNTACTIC STRUCTURE PROBE IN LLM S
185
186   For each LLM, sequences from both the Chinese and English syntactic corpora were concatenated
187   into a continuous text to capture neural-like activations. During this process, each Chinese syl-
188   lable (or English word) outputted an activation value, allowing the signal corresponding to every
189   individual linguistic unit to be traced. These time-domain activations were then transformed into
190   frequency-domain information via fast-fourier transform (FFT). Due to the lack of time-course in-
191   formation inherent to LLM input structures, we artificially defined a time scale on which the activa-
192   tion values are output at a frequency of 4 Hz, and we also manually constrained the sampling rate
193
      to 4 Hz, limiting the observable frequencies to the 0-2 Hz range. This adjustment ensured that the
      syntactic rhythms analogous to those observed in human brain data could be captured within the
194
      model activations.
195
196   LLMs, with their multiple layers and thousands of MLP neurons per layer, require a systematic
197   approach to identify which neurons are responsible for either sentence or phrase processing. We
198   developed a method to identify significant syntactic processing units, applicable to both LLMs and
199
      human brain data. For the LLMs, we conducted a permutation test, randomizing the model acti-
      vations derived from the structured input corpus 1000 times. The original frequency bins at 1 Hz
200
      and 2 Hz, representing sentence and phrase rhythms respectively (their real parts of amplitudes
201
      are denoted as real[amp(1Hz)], real[amp(2Hz)]), were compared to the 95% confidence intervals
202   (CI) generated by the distribution of permuted activations. Neurons whose real[amp(1Hz)] and
203   real[amp(2Hz)] values exceeded this threshold were classified as significant MLP neurons (see 1),
204   indicating their involvement in syntactic processing with statistical robustness against random noise.
205

232   3.3   S YNTACTIC STRUCTURE PROBE IN THE HUMAN BRAIN
233
      In the human sEEG experiment, we recorded sEEG signals from 26 native Chinese speakers, using
234
      two Chinese corpora: the sentence and phrase corpora. The Chinese corpus was presented to the
235   human subjects in the auditory modality. In the sentence corpus, every nine four-syllable sequences
236   were concatenated into a single trial, yielding 40 trials per subject. Similarly, in the phrase corpus,
237   every 18 two-syllable phrases were concatenated, also resulting in 40 trials per subject. Each syllable
238   had a duration of 250 ms, and sEEG signals were sampled at 512 Hz for most participants, except
239   for one with a sampling rate of 2,048 Hz. Note that to reduce the strong neural responses at word
240   onset during auditory presentation, we only used sEEG recordings from the final 32 syllables of
241   each trial per subject.
242   To analyze the sEEG data, we employed inter-trial phase coherence (ITPC), a frequency-domain
243   method relatively resistant to noise that quantifies the consistency of phase relationships in oscilla-
244   tory brain activity across multiple trials (Cohen, 2014). SEEG Electrode localization was performed
245   similarly to our previous studies (Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024); all electrodes were mapped to
246   brain regions defined by the Automated Anatomical Labeling (AAL) system. We then grouped cer-
247   tain AAL regions to form 12 brain regions of interest (ROIs) (details in Appendix A.7). Subsequent
248   experiments were conducted based on brain ROIs.
249   As previously outlined, the proposed HFTP approach is designed to be applicable to both LLMs
250   and human brain data. For the human brain analysis, we employed the same permutation testing
251   procedure on the time-domain sEEG data that captured cortical activity during listening to Chinese
252   corpora. Specifically, ITPC results were randomized 1000 times for each channel in each subject.
253   The original frequency bins, real[amp(1Hz)] and real[amp(2Hz)], were then assessed to determine
254   whether they fell within the 95% confidence interval of the permuted ITPC distribution (see 3).

631
632   This appendix we provide the detailed pipeline used to align the syntactic representations in LLMs
633   with those in the human brain, focusing on identifying and comparing sentence/phrase representa-
634   tions across both systems.
635
      Data and Experimental Setup To maintain consistency between the LLM and human experiments,
636   we used the same two corpora: a sentence corpus (four-syllable Chinese sequence) and a phrase
637   corpus (two-syllable Chinese sequence). The word-order randomized version of each corpus was
638   used as a control condition, as detailed in Section 3.1. Each corpus included 40 trials and each trial
639   contains 36 syllables. For SRDM calculation, the corpora were divided into six experimental con-
640   ditions, each with 20 trials. Sentence/phrase representations of the last 32 syllables were extracted
641   from both LLMs and human subjects to reduce the strong responses at word onset in both systems.
642   The representations are then transformed into the frequency domain.
643   Frequency-Domain Transformation and Similarity Metrics For the LLMs, neuron activations
644   were transformed using FFT to capture the frequency components of structure processing across
645   the six conditions. From this transformation, we calculated the cosine similarity between each pair
646   of conditions, constructing an SRDM for each MLP neuron. Similarly, for the human brain, we
647   calculated the ITPC to capture frequency-domain representations for each brain channel, producing
      a channel SRDM.
