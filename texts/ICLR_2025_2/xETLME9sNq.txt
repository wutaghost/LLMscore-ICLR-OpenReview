009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Score-based generative models (SBM), also known as diffusion models, are the
012            de facto state of the art for image synthesis. Despite their unparalleled perfor-
013            mance, SBMs have recently been in the spotlight for being tricked into creating
014            not-safe-for-work (NSFW) content, such as violent images and non-consensual
015            nudity. This article proposes a safe-for-work (SFW) sampler for SBMs imple-
016            menting a Conditional Trajectory Correction step that guides the samples away
017            from undesired regions in the ambient space using external multimodal models
018            as the source of conditioning. Furthermore, using Contrastive Language Image
019            Pre-training (CLIP), our method admits user-defined NSFW classes, which can
020
               vary in different settings. Our experiments on the text-to-image SBM Stable Dif-
               fusion validate that the proposed SFW sampler effectively reduces the generation
021
               of explicit content, as assessed via independent NSFW detectors. Moreover, the
022
               proposed correction comes at a minor cost in image quality and has an almost null
023            effect on samples that do not need correction. Our study confirms the suitability
024            of the SFW sampler towards aligned SBM models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Score-based models (SBMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020)
031   avoid the computation of the (normalised) probability density required in standard likelihood-based
032   generative modelling by sampling directly from the score function ∇x log p(x) of the data distri-
033
      bution p. This is achieved by training a neural network to learn the score function corresponding
      to noise-corrupted copies of the data using annealed Langevin dynamics. This way, the sampler
034
      is initialised on a pure-noise domain and then guided through a sequence of decreasing-noise la-
035
      tent spaces to arrive at regions of the ambient space where the observations occurred (with high
036   probability). Song et al. (2021b) generalises this concept to a continuous-time noise scheduling by
037   considering a diffusion process, that is, a stochastic differential equation (SDE) governing the evo-
038   lution from the data space to the noise space. Then, sampling occurs by Langevin-based numerical
039   solution of the reverse SDE.
040
      SBMs have become an attractive field of study in the ML community (Yang et al., 2023). This
041
      success has been boosted by their capacity to generate realistic images, positioning them as the go-to
042   resource for image generation by practitioners. In particular, the ability of SBMs to generate high-
043   quality images given a text prompt has made them surpass the performance of GANs (Dhariwal
044   & Nichol, 2021). The capacity of SBMs to generate images for previously unseen prompts has
045   been improved by embedding the conditioning text into the model pre-training scheme (namely
046   classifier-free guidance, Ho & Salimans (2021)). Moreover, performing the denoising steps on a
047   lower dimensional latent space has helped decrease the computational cost while still generating
048   high-resolution samples (Rombach et al., 2022).
049   Like other generative AI methods developed recently, SBMs are also the subject of attacks and mis-
050   use. Via prompting, SBMs’ unique ability for out-of-distribution synthesis can be used to generate
051   deep-fakes or discriminative content. Such risks have been studied by Qu et al. (2023) in the context
052   of publicly-available models such as Stable Diffusion and DALL-E (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh
053   et al., 2022), confirming the possibility to generate inappropriate images containing, e.g., violence
      or nudity, even in the cases where attacks were not planned. This must be carefully and urgently

054
      addressed since SBMs are the backbone of Generative AI engines to which the wider community,
055   including underage users, can access.
056
057   A straightforward approach to avoid generating sensitive content consists of blocking the related
058
      prompts or filtering out violent samples after generation. Both approaches require training spe-
      cialised classifiers and ultimately dismiss the problem of having models that can sample inappro-
059
      priate images in the first place. The community has since tackled the issue by modifying the base
060
      sampling process in SBMs as we observe in Sec. 5. Most of these approaches, while capable of
061   safer sampling, rely on the model’s own knowledge –and thus assessment– of sensitive content.
062
063   We adopt a different perspective and propose using an external signal to guide the samples away
064
      from undesired content. This approach adds flexibility, particularly regarding the source of the ex-
      ternal signal. This will ultimately define what is considered “harmful”, thus allowing for particular
065
      applications based on independently-produced NSFW detectors that can audit a deployed model.
066
      In this context, we assume the existence of a harmfulness probability density ph that models the
067   probability of a point in the ambient space belonging to such a harmful type of content. We then re-
068   duce the expected harmfulness of the clean point prediction in Denoising Implicit Diffusion Models
069   (DDIM) Song et al. (2021a) based on manifold preserving guidance (He et al., 2024) and a novel
070   conditional trajectory correction step. Overall, our approach reduces the rate of images contain-
071   ing explicit content with little compromise over the quality of benign samples. To the best of our
072   knowledge, the extent to which external sources can help block NSFW images in sampling has been
073   hitherto unexplored.
074   Our contributions are summarised as follows
075
076         • We formulate the problem of avoiding the generation of sensitive content in SBMs by re-
077           ducing the likelihood of the samples coming from an external source of NSFW probability,
078           namely a harmfulness distribution ph .
079         • We adapt manifold preserving guidance (He et al., 2024) to reduce the probability of gen-
080           erating undesired content (Sec. 3.1). This is complemented by a conditional diffusion tra-
081           jectory correction step to maintain image quality for samples that pose a low harmful risk
082           (Sec. 3.2).
083         • We propose a family of harmful content distributions ph that can be flexibly defined by the
084           user based on the vision language model CLIP Radford et al. (2021) (Sec. 4).
085         • We develop a performance indicator called prompt-image concordance to assess the se-
086           mantic shift that guidance signals might produce in generated images (Sec. 6.2).
087         • We validate the ability of the proposed method to effectively reduce the rates of explicit
088           content (Sec. 6.1) while maintaining the quality and prompt-image concordance of the
089           samples (Sec. 6.3 and 6.2).

269   Works tackling NSFW generation. Erasing specific concepts, styles or objects is a prospect
      that has been pursued by the diffusion models community. For instance, Safe Latent Diffusion


                                                                5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      (Schramowski et al., 2023) takes a set of key concepts and uses them to move the denoising direc-
271   tion away from harmful images with an adapted classifier-free guidance procedure. On the other
272   hand, Kumari et al. (2023) minimise the KL-divergence between the distribution of a target concept
273   to erase and an anchor concept that can serve as a replacement. They fine-tune the base model and
274   experiment with freezing specific steps of parameters. Gandikota et al. (2023) modify the existing
275   network of a model pθ (x) so it does not contain a certain concept, similarly via fine-tuning. This
276   approach is generalised in Unified Concept Editing (Gandikota et al., 2024), where the linear cross-
277   attention projections are edited in order to modify the output of the model. The method requires
278   a set of concepts to edit and a set to preserve, and it is also able to tackle biases in the generated
279
      images. Li et al. (2023) also make use of the knowledge stored in the model but to infer directions
      in the latent space pointing towards unwanted concepts, as opposed to benign ones.
280
281   In this context, our approach considers external sources for content moderation which avoids rely-
282   ing on the model itself for filtering. Works such as ESD present strategies for erasing where the
283   censoring signal comes from the model itself. Though both external and internal censoring signals
284   work in practice, we believe that using external sources provides enhanced flexibility and gener-
285
      ality, since the model is externally/independently supervised. For instance, one might want to use
      an independent classifier acting as a regulator for what the model can generate. In our setting, any
286
      such type of signals can be considered as long as their gradients can be calculated, and our work
287
      presents a proof-of-concept in this regard. Moreover, our broad methodology complements methods
288   that condition the diffusion on what is to be censored.

      7        C ONCLUSION
474
      In the context of safe-for-work synthetic image generation, we have investigated the use of external
475
      densities that model image harmfulness as a means of guiding the denoising process away from
476
      undesired samples. We have provided a flexible methodology that allows the user to personalise
477   the model at hand. Our experiments show that NSFW image generation can be effectively reduced
478   albeit with an effect on image quality that gets considerably reduced in benign images.
479
480
      Solely guiding the samples away from dangerous content is already a step forward in making models
      more consistent with human values. Nevertheless, a user with sufficient expertise might turn off the
481
      safe anti-guidance procedure. Consequently, fine-tuning the original diffusion model ϵθ to obtain an
482
      updated one that follows the corrected latent direction is an interesting future prospect. Moreover,
483   freezing certain types of parameters of the denoising network might as well be beneficial to our
484   methodology.

      A reason for considering external sources for unguidance is to avoid relying on the model itself
487   for identifying the sources of noxious content. Indeed, the base model would need to flawlessly
488   associate all visual features with the prompt of what is to be removed in order for the method from
489   Gandikota et al. (2023) to reliably remove all traces of the undesired distribution. We deviate from
490   that assumption and suggest the use of external classifiers instead.
491
      However, putting all the burden of aligning the model on a simple external classifier (as is the
492
      case of CLIP-based ones) might be considered a naive approach, the results shown in this work
493   highlight the effectiveness of the method. This suggests that the implicit information stored in these
494   models during their pretraining does contain useful elements for tagging and unguiding intermediate
495   images. Despite these results, we suggest that using more than one approach might be helpful to
496   further reduce the likelihood of dangerous content generation.
497
      Lastly, we hope that our methods a step forward towards making models closer to complying with
498
      human values. Nonetheless, this work does not expect nor try to propose a definitive solution to
499   the issue of generating risky content with diffusion models. We believe that true solutions shall be
500   found at every stage of the generative models pipeline, and that awareness is raised by this and other
501   works tackling ethical problems.
502
503   Limitations Despite our best efforts, the models proposed in this work might still be susceptible
504   to attacks and misuse. We advocate for the responsible use of generative AI, specifically when they
505   interact with humans and personal content.

      B       TARGET MODEL : S TABLE D IFFUSION
667
      We test our approach with Stable Diffusion (SD, Rombach et al. (2022)). In SD, the score-
668
      matching/denoising process is carried out on a lower dimensional latent space. The underlying
669   method is called perceptual compression (Esser et al. (2021)). This step downsamples the image
670   size while avoiding arbitrarily high variance thanks to a regularisation term.
671
672
      On the other hand, guiding the output of diffusion models with prompts is achieved by encoding
      the text with an attention-based architecture (based on Clip embeddings). More precisely, in a
673
      given time t, tokens from the prompt are used to compute both the keys and the values in dot-
674
      product attention. On the other hand, the query vector comes from the (potentially noise-injected)
675   image. The cross-attention maps are then used to compute the output for each denoising step, hence
676   effectively controlling the text-layout relation.
677
678
      These cross-attention maps are applied after each RESNET (He et al. (2016)) which themselves take
      an input latent image and the noise time-step (through its own encoder) as input. These blocks are
679
      part of a larger architecture named U-NET (Ronneberger et al. (2015)), whose output is the image
680
      noise. Wrapping up, SD consists of three networks: text encoder, compressor and denoising U-NET.
681
682
      B.1      A DAPTING THE SD FILTER TO SFW SAMPLING
683
684   A straightforward approach for avoiding the undesirable generation of images is to apply a post hoc
685   filter after the sampling process. The first release of Stable Diffusion uses such a procedure Rombach
686   et al. (2022), relying on a CLIP-based checker4 . Such a filter uses three “sensible” embeddings and
687   17 regular ones, intelligent space resulting after the application of the CLIP model Radford et al.
688   (2021). It is believed that those vectors correspond to dangerous concepts. Rando et al. (2022)
689   confirm this claim for the majority of the concepts, retrieving the exact term in those cases (the
690
      original embeddings have not been disclosed by the authors ). Moreover, they have detected that
      images depicting violence are not considered by the filter, and that it can fail when the prompt is
691
      long and including not NSFW aspects.
692
693   We incorporate a variant of our method that makes use of the concepts from the Stable Diffusion
694   filter: sexual, nude, sex, 18+, naked, nsfw, porn, dick, vagina, explicit content, uncensored, fuck,
695   nipples, naked breast, areola, young girl, young child, and small girl. The last three correspond to
696
      concepts that assigned the category of “special care” and their detection reduced the threshold above
      which other concepts might be tagged as present in the image. In our case, we do not use any of
697
      the declared thresholds. Instead, we make use of the CLIP-based harmfulness density definition
698
      but generalised to more than one concept. We choose the threshold η = 0.23, which shows better
699   qualitative results. Moreover, only considering the most likely term in each time-step and a strength
