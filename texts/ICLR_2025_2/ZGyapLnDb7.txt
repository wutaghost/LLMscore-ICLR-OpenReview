009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Inductive inference, or extrapolation of general rules from finite instances, is un-
012            derstood to be the foundation of human intelligence. Unfortunately, Deep Neural
013            Networks (DNNs) struggle with inductive inference and thus fail to learn even the
014            simplest algorithms in Algorithmic Reasoning (AR). Existing research efforts on
015            AR with DNNs are limited to those on the architectural design for DNNs. In this
016
               study, we investigate the influence of optimization techniques on AR performance.
               Through toy experiments designed to understand an optimizer’s susceptibility to
017
               shortcuts in AR, we reveal that Adam, the naı̈ve choice of optimization, is easily
018
               fooled by spurious correlations. To overcome this shortcoming of Adam, we pro-
019            pose a novel optimizer that avoids spurious correlations by balancing gradients of
020            low- and high-frequencies (BGF). We present extensive experiments and analy-
021            ses to demonstrate the broad and multifaceted advantages of BGF across various
022            architectures and AR tasks. In particular, BGF expands the AR capability of all
023            explored DNN models and even shows the potential to enable learning of tasks that
024            they previously failed at. The observed success of BGF in climbing the Chomsky
025            hierarchy underscores the importance of optimization for developing advanced
026            artificial intelligence with DNNs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The goal of Algorithmic Reasoning (AR) (Veličković & Blundell, 2021), expressed through sequence
031   prediction tasks, is to learn the general output-generating logic from the finite data with a maximum
032   sentence length of l ≤ N , which can be generalized to arbitrary length l ∈ [1, ∞). Therefore, the
033   ability to perform inductive inference, which involves deriving a general rule from finite instances,
034   is the key to the successful execution of AR (Delétang et al., 2022). While inductive inference is
035   understood to be the foundation of human-level intelligence (Pazzani & Kibler, 1992; Klauer & Phye,
036   2008), Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), the most powerful of current-day machine learning (ML)
037   models, are incapable of conducting inductive inference. For instance, Large Language Models
038   (LLMs) with remarkable human-like emergent properties (Brown et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021;
039
      Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) are known to struggle with learning simple algorithms
      like addition or parity through inductive inference (Nogueira et al., 2021; Ontanon et al., 2021; Wu
040
      et al., 2023; Saparov et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2022).
041
042   DNNs’ struggle with inductive inference is attributed to the lack of statistical guarantee for their
043   out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization property. The current generalization property of ML models,
044   including DNNs, is grounded on the statistical learning theory (Vapnik et al., 1998), which assumes
045
      that training and test data are independent and identically distributed and does not consider OOD
      scenarios where test data do not belong in the training distribution (Solomonoff, 1964; Fisher, 1935).
046
      Searching for the desired OOD generalization solution among many hypotheses fitting the training
047
      data requires meta-information outside given training data (Mitchell, 1980). In the DNN training
048   process, this meta-information is translated into inductive bias, governed by the DNN architecture,
049   optimization methods, and initial parameters (Goyal & Bengio, 2022). Previously, extensive research
050   has been conducted on how various DNN architectures can perform different types of logical reasoning
051   tasks (Skachkova et al., 2018; Chomsky, 1956; Merrill, 2019). Research on Transformers reported
052   that while they can learn some Dyck languages (Bhattamishra et al., 2020), in most AR tasks, they
053   tend to learn statistically brittle shortcuts and fail to learn the underlying algorithms (Liu et al., 2022;
      Lee et al., 2023; Abbe et al., 2023; Nogueira et al., 2021). In Deletang et al. (Delétang et al., 2022)

054
      and Merrill et al. (Merrill et al., 2022), comprehensive experiments are conducted to investigate the
055   ability of recurrent models to learn AR tasks of varying difficulties (Chomsky, 1956).
056
057   Contrary to the exhaustive studies on architectures, the influence of optimization methods on OOD
058
      generalization in the context of AR has been largely overlooked. Through carefully designed toy
      experiments, we analyze whether Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014), the de facto optimizer in AR research,
059
      models the general output-generating logic. In our toy experiments, we create synthetic spurious
060
      correlations between train inputs and ground truth outputs. These spurious correlations function
061   as easily tractable proxies to conceptually abstract shortcuts and enable explicit analysis of AR
062   tasks from an optimization standpoint. Our exploratory study reveals that Adam exhibits inherent
063   vulnerability to shortcuts and necessitates advancements on the optimization front to achieve true
064   OOD generalization.
065
      In this regard, we propose a novel optimizer, named BGF (Balancing of Gradient Frequencies), which
066
      circumvents shortcuts and enhances OOD generalization by balancing gradients of high- and low-
067   frequencies. We first observe the occurrence of “train-test splitting,” where OOD generalization
068   on test data is achieved far after the model fits training data, when training DNNs on AR tasks
069   with Adam. Train-test splitting can be interpreted as a mild form of grokking (Power et al., 2022).
070   A frequency-domain analysis of gradients obtained over the course of AR training indicates that
071   high-frequency components, which are dominant at early training steps where the training accuracy
072   increases, are visibly suppressed once the training accuracy saturates. Following the suppression
073   of high-frequency components, the test accuracy starts to improve, implying that low-frequency
074   components provide a necessary diversion from shortcuts to guide the training process to learn the
075
      general underlying logic. Thus, we posit that balancing high- and low-frequency gradients can
      improve OOD generalization. Our initial results of replacing Adam with BGF in our toy experiments
076
      consolidate that BGF is indeed more capable of evading shortcuts, consolidating its capability to
077
      encourage learning of general rules.
078
079   Extensive experimental results evidence that BGF improves AR performance and accelerates AR
080   learning on eight DNN models employed by Deletang et al. (Delétang et al., 2022). 1) BGF improves
081
      the best test accuracy of evaluated models on all 15 AR tasks and shows potential to enable learning
      of high-complexity tasks that they previously failed at (i.e., Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) on
082
      the Missing Duplicate task). Length generalization in AR was perceived to be bounded by the
083
      inductive bias of a DNN architecture. BGF’s model-agnostic evasion of shortcuts relieves this strong
084   contingency of AR performance on the choice of a DNN architecture. 2) BGF alleviates train-test
085   splitting and reduces the number of training steps required to obtain over 90% accuracy on test data.
086
087
      In addition, we present the results of diverse analyses that uncover the advantages of BGF. The com-
      parison of BGF with optimizers proposed to improve generalization in conventional ML illuminates
088
      that BGF is a superior method of optimization for AR. The verification of BGF’s generalization
089
      capability on longer sequences shows that BGF has facilitated learning of the underlying logic.
090   Analyzing the variance of the training speeds computed over different initializations corroborates
091   BGF’s capability to stabilize the AR training process. The sharpness analysis of the loss landscapes
092   obtained with BGF shows that it smoothens the loss surface of trained DNNs, offering insights
093   into how BGF induces DNNs with improved generalization capacity on AR. Our contributions are
094   summarized as follows:
095
096         • We propose BGF, a novel optimizer that is capable of improving length generalization in AR
097           through the mitigation of shortcut learning. Notably, BGF shows an instance of climbing
098
              the Chomsky hierarchy by enabling the complete execution of a previously impossible task.
099         • To accentuate the necessity of an optimization technique tailored toward AR, we disclose
100           the vulnerability of Adam to shortcuts in AR tasks through toy experiments. In contrast, the
101           verification of BGF on these toy experiments shows its strong promise at battling shortcuts.
102         • The existence of the train-test splitting phenomenon in AR is reported for the first time.
103           Analyzing the change in gradient patterns over AR training steps reveals that when the
104           test accuracy starts to improve, the high-frequency components in gradients are visibly
105           suppressed; this analysis inspires the design of BGF that balances low- and high-frequencies.
106         • Our extensive experimental results demonstrate that BGF brings upon over-arching improve-
107           ments in performance and training speed. We provide insightful analyses into what makes
              BGF advantageous for length generalization in AR.

      2.1   A LGORITHMIC R EASONING AND C HOMSKY H IERARCHY
111
112   While DNNs excel at function approximation and feature extraction (Mao & Jain, 1995), they exhibit
113   unpredictable behavior beyond the confines of the training data (Vapnik, 1999; Liang et al., 2018).
114   Conversely, classical algorithms, grounded in predetermined rules, offer scalability to diverse datasets,
115   performance guarantees, and interpretability. The complementary nature of these methodologies
116   inspired numerous studies on equipping DNNs with the characteristics of classical algorithms through
117   algorithmic modeling (Trask et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2020). However, these endeavors are limited
118   by their reliance on defining task-specific parameterized functions under human supervision (Gaunt
119
      et al., 2017; Valkov et al., 2018). The emerging “learning from data” approach, which involves
      acquiring proficiency in arbitrary logical tasks without task supervision, holds promise for addressing
120
      this limitation (Delétang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Veličković et al., 2022; Minder et al., 2023).
121
122   AR refers to the process of learning underlying algorithms through finite pairs of input and output
123   sequence data (train length l < N ). The inference is then conducted on unobserved inputs to deter-
124   mine whether the model has learned an underlying rule capable of generalization (inference length
125
      l >> N ). There exist two main approaches to representing AR tasks, sequence prediction (Delétang
      et al., 2022) and graph learning (Veličković et al., 2022). In this paper, we employ the sequence pre-
126
      diction approach because it theoretically subsumes all computational problems and can be represented
127
      with formal languages (Sipser, 1997; Rich, 2007).
128
129   The Chomsky hierarchy organizes formal languages into four levels according to their complexity.
130   Regular languages (R) and corresponding finite automata require the simplest memory complexity.
131
      Context-free (CF) languages and push-down automata necessitate stack-type memory. Context-
      sensitive (CS) languages and linear bounded automata require linear tape memory. Lastly, recursively
132
      enumerable (RE) languages and Turing machine demand infinite tape memory. The levels of formal
133
      language (in which AR tasks are expressed) that various DNN models can learn revealed the Chomsky
134   hierarchy boundary for each architecture, as summarized in Table A2 (Delétang et al., 2022).

      2.2   S PURIOUS C ORRELATION AND O UT- OF - DISTIBUTION G ENERALIZATION
137
138   In AR, DNNs models, despite achieving 100% accuracy on training data, fail to generalize to test
139   cases. This lack of generalization capability, particularly in Transformer models, is commonly
140   associated with their tendency to learn easier shortcuts that apply only to the training data rather
141   than general rules. While preventing models from learning shortcuts or spurious correlations and
142   enhancing OOD generalization performance are central goals in machine learning, limited research
143   has been conducted for AR tasks from these perspectives.
144   Methods to mitigate spurious correlations can be broadly categorized into data manipulation, rep-
145   resentation learning, and learning strategy (Liu et al., 2021b; Ye et al., 2024). Approaches in data
146   manipulation include corrupting semantic information in the data (Puli et al., 2022), increasing
147   diversity through mix-up (Yao et al., 2022), using counterfactual generators (Zeng et al., 2020),
148   and pseudo-label generation (Nam et al., 2022). Even though data manipulation is demonstrated
149   to be effective in vision and natural language tasks, its application to AR is implausible since the
150   distribution of training inputs in AR tasks is numerically defined and spans all possible input spaces.
151   Representation learning, whose aim is to learn domain-agnostic robust representations, has been
152   widely adopted to enable OOD generalization (Zhang et al., 2022; Harary et al., 2022). Unsupervised
153   representation learning approach is unsuitable for AR tasks as it only relies on input patterns. For
154   instance, parity check and binary addition share identical input domains, requiring output information
155   for learning. For supervised representation learning, methods with environment labels (Pfister et al.,
156   2019; Albuquerque et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2023) also do not align with the philosophy of AR.
157   Methods that do not rely on environment labels commonly decorrelate input into label-relevant and
158
      irrelevant information (Zhang et al., 2021; Lachapelle et al., 2023; Creager et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
      2021a). These approaches are also impractical for AR, as every pixel of the input data is associated
159
      with labels, and a single pixel change can alter the class.
160
161   The limitations of data manipulation and representation learning leave us with optimization-based
      mitigation of spurious correlations. Fortunately, loss landscape sharpness-based optimization strate-


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      gies have shown promise in simple logical tasks (Klindt, 2022). The correlation between the loss
163   landscape sharpness and generalization has been studied theoretically (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
164   1994) and practically (Dinh et al., 2017). Optimization methods enforcing loss landscape flatness
165   include LPF-SGD (Bisla et al., 2022) and SAM (Foret et al., 2021). An alternative approach emulates
166   this effect through weight ensembling (Izmailov et al., 2018; Cha et al., 2021). SWAD (Cha et al.,
167   2021) employs a dense, overfit-aware weight sampling strategy for ensemble selection.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
170
171
      Existing research efforts in AR are mostly directed toward studying the influence of different DNN
172
      architectures and their inductive bias on OOD generalization performance. However, the design of the
173   optimizer, another important dimension that constitutes inductive bias, remains underexplored in AR.
174   In this study, we aim to investigate and improve OOD generalization in AR tasks from the perspective
175   of optimization. Through the two toy experiments in Section 3.1, we disclose the susceptibility of
176   Adam, the default optimizer used in AR, to shortcuts, embodied in the form of spurious correlations.
177   In Section 3.2, we observe the occurrence of train-test splitting in AR and reveal that low-frequency
178   components in gradients contribute to the increase in test, instead of training, accuracy. Inspired
179   by this analysis, we propose BGF, a novel optimizer that circumvents shortcuts and encourages the
180   learning of generic rules in train data, by balancing of high- and low-frequency gradients.
181
182   3.1   M OTIVATION
183
184
      Definition of Shortcuts: In AR, the Adam optimizer typically achieves near-perfect training accuracy,
      exhausting available information from the data, but yields noticeably lower test accuracy. Thus, AR
185
      distinguishes itself from typical ML problems where in distribution (ID) accuracy often predicts
186
      or correlates with OOD generalization (Miller et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021) and requires
187   performing inductive inference. The main challenge in inductive inference (including AR) is that data
188   alone cannot determine a single hypothesis among those that fit the data. For example, predicting
189   the next number in the sequence 1, 3, 5, 7 has infinite possible continuations. In this work, among
190   hypotheses fitting the training data, those that enable OOD generalization (e.g., 9 for the continuation)
191   are defined as “general logic”, whereas non-generalizing hypotheses are termed “shortcuts”. Shortcuts
192   can be interpreted as learning non-generalizing correlations between inputs and outputs that are valid
193   in the training distribution but do not hold in the target distribution; such misleading correlations are
194   regarded to be spurious correlations.
195   In this section, we expose that the vulnerability of Adam to the shortcuts in AR tasks creates a
196   major roadblock for DNNs trying to learn the underlying data-generating logic of AR. Unfortunately,
197   it is infeasible for us humans to pinpoint and explicate which shortcuts are implicitly learned
198   by models. Therefore, we design toy experiments as a test bed to study the susceptibility of
199   current optimization approaches to shortcuts. In these toy experiments, we utilize synthetic spurious
200   correlations between train inputs and outputs as representations of conceptually abstract shortcuts.
201
      Comparing the robustness of different optimizers to spurious correlations allows close monitoring of
      whether an optimizer can avoid shortcuts embodied in the form of tangible confounding factors.
202
203   We study two different types of spurious correlations, whose examples for the “Modular Arithmetic”
204   task are depicted in Figure 1. (The Modular Arithmetic (MA) task is defined to be: given a sequence
205   of inputs and operations sampled from {0, 1, 2, 3, 4} and {+, -, ×}, compute modulo 5 of the result
206   of applying the operation on the sequence.) The first spurious correlation is induced by concatenating
207
      the ground truth output to the original input sequence as seen in Figure 1(b). At test time, the ground
      truth output is replaced with a randomized value to observe whether the optimizer learned the logic
208
      embedded in the original input without memorizing the concatenated ground truth. The second type
209
      of synthetic confounding factor we utilize is the input length. The example in Figure 1(c) depicts how
210   the “Modular Arithmetic” task is modified to implement input length confounding. In this task, the
211   potential input length ranges from 1 to 40, and the number of ground truth outputs is 5. Consequently,
212   our confounding rule partitions data into five groups according to the input length: the inputs of
213   length 1 ∼ 8 are labeled as 0, those of length 9 ∼ 16 as 1, and so forth. To force spurious correlation
214   based on the input length, we only select instances that satisfy the above rule and discard the rest.
215   The model trained on manipulated train data is tested on normal test data sans spurious correlation. In
      both toy experiments, the degree of spurious correlation is controlled with probability P . For instance,

       3.2            BGF: M ITIGATING S HORTCUT L EARNING VIA BALANCING OF G RADIENT F REQUENCIES
297
298
        We take a closer look at the AR training process of Adam by plotting the training and test accuracy
299
       curves of the LSTM model on the “Solve Equation” task in Figure 3 (a). Training and test accuracies
300    improve at two disparate points in the training curve. This phenomenon, which we dub “train-test
301    splitting,” is a moderate version of grokking. From here on, we denote the point in the learning curve
302    in which training accuracy increases but test accuracy does not as the training phase (red), and the
303    point in which only test accuracy increases as the generalization phase (blue).
304
      To understand what learning signals contribute to improving the test accuracy and enabling OOD
305
      generalization, we analyze the evolution of gradients over the course of training. Figure 3 (b
306   1 ∼ 5) are the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) results of gradient signals obtained from training and
307   generalization phases. In early steps, when the training accuracy continuously rises while the test
308   accuracy is stagnant, the gradient patterns are dominated by high-frequency components (red signal).
309   Interestingly, the intensity of high-frequency gradients substantially attenuates in later gradient
310   signals that yield OOD generalization capability (blue signal), further validating that relatively lower
311   frequency gradients are preferable for learning of the general logic. Such a result indicates that the
312   low-frequency components play a crucial role in avoiding shortcuts and promoting the learning of
313   general logic.
314   We additionally analyze the normalized gradient signals from the “Bucket Sort” task with different
315   degrees of spurious correlation induced by label concatenation. The FFT results for gradient signals
316   obtained at P ∈ [0.0, 0.99, 0.995] are visualized in Figure 4. As the degree of spurious correlation
317   between input data and labels gets stronger, the low-frequency components of gradient signals visibly
318   decrease. This result further evidences that in AR, spurious correlations or shortcuts significantly
319   weaken low-frequency gradients, and that amplifying low-frequency gradients may hold the key to
320
      facilitating inductive inference.
321   These analyses lead us to the design of BGF, a novel optimizer that guides the training process
322   toward general logic and away from shortcuts by balancing the gradients in low- and high-frequency
323   regimes. The most intuitive approach to obtaining low-frequency gradients is through the adoption of
      a low-pass filter, which removes high-frequency components with stochastic and fine-grained signals

529    6                C ONCLUSION
530
531   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to study the effect of optimization on generalization
532   in AR. Our investigative results exposed the susceptibility of Adam to spurious correlations in AR
533   tasks, necessitating the development of a new optimizer. We then analyzed the evolution of gradients
534   throughout the AR training process, unveiling the occurrence of the train-test splitting phenomenon
535   and the contribution of low-frequency components to enabling OOD generalization. Based on these
536   findings, we proposed BGF, a novel optimizer to encourage learning of general logic in AR, and
537   demonstrated its effectivenss through extensive experiments and analyses. Limitations: Although
538
      BGF considerably improves the AR performance compared to Adam, it still fails to promote learning
      of many AR tasks. Therefore, a considerable amount of research opportunities remain on both the
539
      architecture design and optimization approach fronts to enable DNNs to perform inductive inference.

889   A2     T RAINING D ETAILS FOR BGF AND OTHER BASELINES
890
891   In this section, we provide descriptions and hyperparameter settings of compared optimizers in our
892   main experiments. As noted in the main paper, all training processes are run for 100, 000 steps.
893   • Adam: is the default optimizer used in (Delétang et al., 2022)
894   • SAM (Foret et al., 2021): searches for neighboring regions with uniformly low losses. To do so, it
895   applies perturbations to gradients during training, so that they optimize losses in the neighboring
896
      regions as well. The degree of perturbation in SAM is set to 0.5. The perturbed gradients are
      multiplied by this value perturbation to control their effect.
897
      • LPF-SGD (Bisla et al., 2022): reduces the influence of the data and label noise by applying the
898
      low pass filter kernel to the loss. The integral in the convolution operation is approximated with the
899   MC method.
900   • SWAD (Cha et al., 2021): determines the training interval in which overfitting has occurred by
901   tracking the validation loss. It then averages all parameters in this training interval to obtain the
902   final model. The interval for parameter saving in SWAD is determined by three values: an optimum
903   patient parameter (Np ), an overfitting patient parameter (No ), and a tolerance rate rtol . The start
904   saving step happens when the test loss no longer decreases for Np number of steps. The end step
905   happens when the test loss value exceeds rtol for No number of steps. All parameters within this
906   interval are saved. Np , No , and rtol are set to 10, and 10, and 0.005, such that a reasonable interval is
907
      selected.
      • BGF (Ours): α and β are two hyperparameters involved in BGF. Their sum is always
908
      controlled to be one, so that BGF balances their influences. α and β are searched by conducting a
909
      hyperparameter search on the following sets of {α, β}: {0.95, 0.05}, {0.9, 0.1},{0.8, 0.2},{0.7, 0.3}.

1209   A6      T RAINING D ISTRIBUTION ACCURACY A NALYSIS
1210
1211   In typical ML problems, accuracy on the training distribution (IID) is known to be predictive of (Miller
1212   et al., 2021), or at least correlated with (Hendrycks et al., 2021), OOD generalization performance.
1213   In contrast, for inductive inference, even if a model perfectly learns the training distribution, it may
1214   learn shortcuts or favorable hypotheses depending on various inductive biases. In Table A10, we
1215   showed that the model’s accuracy on the training distribution approaches 1.0, demonstrating that
1216
       the AR task can indeed be considered an inductive inference problem. Reported average accuracy
       in the main: Adam 0.979, BGF 0.983 are averaged over all 15 AR tasks and 7 models (excluding
1217
       Transformer without positional embedding).
