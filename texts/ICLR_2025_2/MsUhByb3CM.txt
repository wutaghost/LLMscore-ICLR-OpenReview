009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we explore the potential of abstracting complex visual information
012            into discrete, structured symbolic sequences using self-supervised learning (SSL).
013            Inspired by how language abstracts and organizes information to enable better rea-
014            soning and generalization, we propose a novel approach for generating symbolic
015            representations from visual data. To learn these sequences, we extend the DINO
016            framework to handle both visual and symbolic information. Initial experiments
017            suggest that the generated symbolic sequences capture a meaningful level of ab-
018            straction, though further refinement is required. An advantage of our method is
019
               its interpretability: the sequences are produced by a decoder transformer using
               cross-attention, allowing attention maps to be linked to specific symbols and of-
020
               fering insight into how these representations correspond to image regions. This
021
               approach lays the foundation for creating interpretable symbolic representations
022            with potential applications in high-level scene understanding.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      In recent years (Bengio et al., 2013; Krizhevsky et al., 2017), advances in computer vision and ma-
028
      chine learning have significantly improved our ability to learn from complex visual data. However,
029
      most learned representations remain continuous and unstructured(LeCun et al., 2015), making it
030   difficult to reason about high-level abstractions and relationships in the data. Inspired by language
031   structure, which allows us to abstract and generalize from perceptual input, we explore whether it
032   is possible to generate discrete, structured symbolic representations from visual data through self-
033   supervised learning (SSL).
034
      Language provides a compelling framework for abstraction(Bisk et al., 2020): it captures mean-
035
      ing through compositional symbols that represent and generalize complex information, enabling
036   higher-level reasoning. Translating this capacity to machine learning could be a key step toward
037   more interpretable and generalizable models(Lake et al., 2016). However, current approaches to
038   visual representation learning primarily focus on learning dense, continuous features, which lack
039   the compositional properties needed for symbolic reasoning. This gap motivates our investigation
040   into generating symbolic sequences from visual input, where structured symbols can encapsulate
041   the variations and complexities of visual data in a compact, interpretable form.
042   In this work, we introduce a novel approach to generating symbolic representations from visual data
043   using an extended version of the DINO framework(Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024), which is
044   designed to handle both visual and symbolic information. Our method leverages pre-trained visual
045   representations from a Vision Transformer (ViT)(Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and extends them to pro-
046   duce symbolic sequences that can abstract the compositional properties of visual scenes. By utilizing
047   a decoder transformer with cross-attention mechanisms, we ensure that the generated symbols can
048   be interpreted and linked to specific regions in the input data, providing a more interpretable model.
049   Our main contributions are as follows: (1). We propose a novel method for generating structured
050   symbolic sequences from visual data through self-supervised learning, inspired by linguistic abstrac-
051   tion. (2). We extend the DINO framework to handle both visual and symbolic information, enabling
052   the learning of compositional symbolic representations. (3). We demonstrate the interpretability
053   of our method by linking the generated symbols to visual regions through attention maps, offering
      insights into how these symbols correspond to image features. (4). Initial experiments show that

087   Visual Representation Learning and Discrete Latent Representations Self-supervised learning
088   (SSL)(Chen et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021) has led to significant progress in visual representation
089   learning, with Vision Transformers (ViT) proving particularly effective at extracting meaningful
090   features by attending to different regions of an image. Methods like DINO build on ViT to cap-
091   ture dense, continuous representations from visual data. However, while these representations are
092   powerful, they often lack the structure necessary for symbolic reasoning and high-level abstrac-
093
      tions. A growing body of work addresses this limitation by introducing discrete latent representa-
      tions(van den Oord et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2022), which transform continuous visual features into
094
      discrete codes or tokens. These discrete representations offer a more structured and interpretable
095
      way to model complex visual inputs. However, while they provide compact encodings, they typ-
096   ically do not impose specific structural properties or constraints on the learned codes, leaving the
097   challenge of generating meaningful, compositional symbolic sequences open. Our work builds on
098   this by adopting discrete representations and focusing on learning structured symbolic sequences
099   that capture the underlying compositional nature of visual data.
100
      Image Captioning and Encoder-Decoder Models Traditional image captioning models typically
101
      follow an encoder-decoder architecture(Vinyals et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2016), where the encoder
102   (often a CNN or Vision Transformer) processes the image into a dense, continuous representation,
103   and the decoder (such as an RNN or Transformer)(He et al., 2015; Vaswani et al., 2023) generates
104   descriptive language sequences based on these features. These models rely on explicit supervi-
105   sion, using labelled datasets with human-annotated captions to guide the mapping from images to
106   text. However, our approach differs fundamentally. While we also employ a standard encoder-
107   decoder architecture with a pretrained Transformer to capture visual features, our method does not
      rely on human-provided labels, predefined language targets, or prior training on linguistic models.

      Neuro-Symbolic Learning and Symbolic Reasoning Neuro-symbolic approaches(Susskind et al.,
      2021) aim to combine the pattern recognition capabilities of neural networks with the logical, in-
137
      terpretable reasoning strengths of symbolic AI. These models use neural networks to process raw
138
      data (such as images or text) and output symbolic representations or logical rules for high-level rea-
139   soning tasks. Recent advances have introduced methods for generating symbolic sequences directly
140   from visual data, allowing models to infer perceptual features and symbolic abstractions in a uni-
141   fied framework. Our work aligns with this direction by generating symbolic sequences from visual
142   input, directly integrating perception with structured symbolic reasoning without requiring external
143   symbolic systems.
144
      Interpretability through Attention Mechanisms and Discrete Representations Attention mech-
145   anisms, particularly in Transformer architectures, have proven essential for improving the inter-
146   pretability of models by highlighting which parts of the input data are most relevant for a given
147   prediction. In visual models, attention maps make it possible to understand how specific regions
148   of an image contribute to the output. Some approaches (Zhang et al., 2021) go further by tok-
149   enizing images into discrete units, which can then be mapped to symbolic representations, offering
150   a more interpretable and structured view of the visual data. Our method builds on this by using
151   cross-attention to link symbolic sequences to specific image regions, ensuring that the learned rep-
152   resentations are not only abstract and symbolic but also interpretable, providing transparency in how
153
      the model processes and understands visual scenes.

157
158   Our proposed method follows a teacher-student framework(Hinton et al., 2015) for generating sym-
159   bolic representations from visual data, incorporating cross-attention mechanisms, discretization
160   strategies, and symbolic token embedding. The teacher network, which uses a pretrained Vision
161   Transformer (ViT), provides stable visual representations, while the student network is trained to
      generate symbolic sequences that approximate these representations. We outline the method in four

166
167   The core of our approach is a teacher-student framework, where the teacher provides pretrained
168   visual features, and the student learns to represent these features symbolically. The student generates
169   symbolic sequences using a decoder and re-embeds them into a joint distribution space through an
170   encoder. We will now describe each network in detail.
171

190   ule DS , an encoder module ES , and a projector module PS . Unlike the teacher, the student is ini-
191   tialized randomly and trained to align its representations with the teacher’s by generating symbolic

      We follow a self-supervised learning approach, where the teacher visual encoder generates visual

302   with those of the teacher across different levels of abstraction. The granularity factor allows the
303   student to focus on both high-level and more detailed features of the input.

321   We apply various exploration strategies to further enhance the diversity and richness of the symbolic
322   sequences generated by the student network. These strategies are specifically used in the context
323   of the Gumbel Softmax variation of the discretization process, where we investigate the effects of
      different loss terms to encourage exploration and variability in the symbolic sequences.

            • Base Strategy: In the base strategy, we only modify the teacher temperature (T ) and apply
325           a scheduler to gradually decrease the Gumbel Softmax temperature (τ ), allowing the pre-
326           dictions to better approximate a maximum as training progresses. No additional loss terms
327           are introduced in this baseline approach.
328
            • Entropy Encouragement Strategy: Inspired by entropy-based exploration in Reinforce-
329
              ment Learning (e.g., SAC), we introduce a loss term that penalizes low entropy in the
330
              Gumbel Softmax predictions, encouraging the model to maintain higher entropy during
331           training. This term is designed to foster more diverse symbolic sequences:

      Symbolic Representation Effectiveness Our method successfully generated symbolic sequences
420
      that aligned with visual representations from a pretrained Vision Transformer. As seen in the prob-
421

461   5.2   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
462
463   Despite the positive outcomes, our method faces several limitations:
464
465   Training Constraints Due to computational limits, we trained models for under 200 epochs,
466   which may have constrained their performance. Additionally, training was limited to the CIFAR-10
467   dataset, which may restrict the generalizability of our findings. Future work should aim to train on
468   larger datasets, such as CIFAR-100 or more complex synthetic datasets, to explore the scalability of
469
      our method.
470
471
      Challenges in Discretization and Exploration We found that discretization strategies plateaued
      in performance around 50% accuracy, but this improved to 60% by using a combined strategy during
472
      training. Nonetheless, the Gumbel-Softmax approach introduced noise, limiting both interpretability
473
      and performance. Future work could focus on refining these discretization techniques and under-
474   standing how symbolic diversity correlates with model accuracy. Additionally, exploration strate-
475   gies, while promising for increasing sequence variability, did not significantly boost performance,
476   indicating that further research is needed to optimize this aspect.
477
478   5.3   C ONCLUSIONS
479
480   This study highlights the viability of symbolic representations for visual data, offering a pathway
481   to more interpretable models that maintain strong performance on downstream tasks. While there
482   is room for improvement in terms of generalization and efficiency, the success of our approach in
483   extracting meaningful symbolic information provides a foundation for future research into symbolic
484
      reasoning and representation in AI systems.
