                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
                   We present the “Law of Vision Representation” in multimodal large language
010                models (MLLMs). It reveals a strong correlation between the combination of
011                cross-modal alignment, correspondence in vision representation, and MLLM per-
012                formance. We quantify the two factors using the cross-modal Alignment and
013                Correspondence score (AC score). Through extensive experiments involving thir-
014                teen different vision representation settings and evaluations across eight bench-
015                marks, we find that the AC score is linearly correlated to model performance. By
016                leveraging this relationship, we are able to identify and train the optimal vision
017                representation only, which does not require finetuning the language model every
018
                   time, resulting in a 99.7% reduction in computational cost.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Current multimodal large language models (MLLMs) (Chen et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024e;d) have
023   achieved remarkable advancements by integrating pretrained vision encoders with powerful lan-
024   guage models (Touvron et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). As one of the core components of a general
025   MLLM, the vision representation is critical. Many researchers have utilized CLIP (Radford et al.,
026   2021) as the primary image feature encoder, but its limitations are increasingly noticeable (Tong
027   et al., 2024b; Geng et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2021). As a result, alternative vision representations
028   and the combination of multiple vision encoders are being actively explored (Tong et al., 2024a; Lin
029   et al., 2023).
030   Despite this growing attention, the selection of vision representation has largely been empirical.
031   Researchers typically test a set of vision representations on a specific MLLM and choose the one
032   that yields the highest performance on benchmark tasks. This approach, however, is constrained by
033   the number of representations tested and does not address the underlying factors that make certain
034   feature representations perform better than others. As a result, the optimal vision representation for
035   a specific MLLM is often determined by empirical performance rather than a deep understanding
036
      of the features that contribute to success. The question of what fundamentally makes a feature
      representation achieve the highest performance remains largely unanswered.
037
038   To address this gap in understanding what makes a vision representation optimal for MLLMs, we
039   propose the Law of Vision Representation in MLLMs. It aims to explain the key factors of vision
040   representation that impact MLLM benchmarks performance. Our findings reveal that the cross-
041   modal Alignment and Correspondence (AC) of the vision representation are strongly correlated
042
      with model performance. Specifically, an increase in the AC of the selected vision representation
      leads to improved model performance. To quantify this relationship, we define an AC score that
043
      measures cross-modal alignment and correspondence in vision representation. The AC score and
044
      model performance exhibit a linear relationship, with a coefficient of determination of 95.72%.
045
046   Furthermore, the Law of Vision Representation guides the selection of an optimal vision represen-
047   tation for MLLMs. Originally, this process was extremely costly because even subtle changes in
048
      vision encoding methods—such as switching encoder types, altering image resolution, or testing
      feature combinations—require finetuning the language model (Lin et al., 2024). For example, using
049
      a top data-efficient MLLM pipeline with a 7B language model requires 3,840 NVIDIA A100 GPU
050
      hours to test the 10 encoders used in this study, amounting to a cost of approximately $20,0001 .
051   Testing additional encoders leads to a linear increase in cost. Moreover, the recent trend of feature
052   combination, which often results in better performance, necessitates combinatorial testing of vision

093   2.1       V ISION FOR MLLM S
094
095   Recent studies have explored various vision representations in MLLMs (Beyer et al., 2024; Ge et al.,
096
      2024; Liu et al., 2024e; Wang et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024). Interestingly, some
      findings indicate that relying solely on encoders outside of the CLIP family (Cherti et al., 2023; Zhai
097
      et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2022), such as DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) and Stable Diffusion (Rombach
098
      et al., 2021), often leads to lower performance scores (Karamcheti et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024a).
099   However, combining features from these encoders with CLIP features—such as concatenating im-
100   age embeddings in the token or channel dimension—significantly enhances performance beyond
101   using CLIP alone (Tong et al., 2024a;b; Liu et al., 2024c; Kar et al., 2024). Researchers intuitively
102   suggest that these additional encoders provide superior detail-oriented capabilities, but no studies
103   have thoroughly analyzed the underlying causes of the performance change (Wei et al., 2023; Lu
104   et al., 2024a). This suggests that the attributes of an optimal vision representation remain not fully
105   understood.

      2.2   C ROSS - MODAL A LIGNMENT
109
110   Cross-modal alignment refers to the alignment between image and text feature spaces (Duan et al.,
111   2022). This concept emerged with the introduction of text-image contrastive learning (Radford et al.,
112   2021; Jia et al., 2021). Although current MLLMs utilize contrastively pretrained image encoders,
113   the challenge of achieving effective alignment persists (Ye et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2023a; Woo et al.,
114   2024). Despite efforts to critique the limitations of CLIP family representations and explore alterna-
115   tive vision representations, many approaches continue to rely on contrastively pretrained encoders
116
      or adding contrastive loss without fully eliminating them (Zhang et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2024a; Tong
      et al., 2024a;b; Liu et al., 2024b). In our work, we point out that alignment in vision representation is
117
      essential for improved model performance and is crucial for data efficiency. Without pre-aligned vi-
118
      sion representations, extensive data pretraining is required to achieve cross-modal alignment within
119   the language model (Ge et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024c).
120
121
      2.3   V ISUAL C ORRESPONDENCE
122
123   Visual correspondence is a fundamental component in computer vision, where accurate correspon-
124   dences can lead to significant performance improvements in tasks, such as image detection (Xu
125   et al., 2024; Nguyen & Meunier, 2019), visual creation (Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024c),
126   and MLLMs (Liu et al., 2024a), etc. Correspondences are typically categorized into semantic- and
127   geometric-correspondences. Semantic correspondences (Zhang et al., 2024c; Min et al., 2019) in-
128   volve matching points that represent the same semantic concept not necessarily representing the
129
      same instance. Geometric correspondences (Sarlin et al., 2020; Lindenberger et al., 2023), on the
      other hand, require matching the exact same point across images, which is often crucial for low-level
130
      vision tasks, such as pose estimation (Sarlin et al., 2020; Lindenberger et al., 2023; Zhang & Vela,
131
      2015),and SLAM tasks, etc.
132
133   Several studies have pointed out that the CLIP family’s vision representation ”lacks visual de-
134   tails” (Lu et al., 2024a; Tong et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2024). We explain this observation through the
135
      concept of correspondence. Current multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) convert images
      into embeddings, with each embedding representing a patch of the image. Image features with high
136
      correspondence increase the similarity within internal image patches on similar semantics, thereby
137
      enabling the retrieval of more detailed information.

140   3     L AW OF V ISION R EPRESENTATION IN MLLM S
141
142   We introduce the Law of Vision Representation in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs).
143   It states that the performance of a MLLM, denoted as Z, can be estimated by two factors: cross-
144
      modal alignment (A) and correspondence (C) of the vision representation, assuming vision represen-
      tation is the sole independent variable while other components (e.g., language model and alignment
145
      module) remain fixed. This relationship can be expressed as:
146
147
148                                                Z ∝ f (A, C)                                            (1)
149
150
      where f is a linear function on second-degree polynomial transformations of A and C.
151
152   3.1   A SSUMPTIONS
153
      Following NVLM (Dai et al., 2024), we categorize MLLMs into the following types: (1) Decoder-
154
      only MLLMs (Tong et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024e; Li et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024f; Dai et al.,
155
      2024; Lu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024a): These MLLMs consist of vision
156   encoder(s) and an alignment module, such as a multilayer perceptron (MLP), which maps the vision
157   representation into vision tokens. These tokens are designed to have a similar distribution as lan-
158   guage tokens and are directly input into a language model in the same manner as language tokens.
159   (2) Cross-attention-based MLLMs (Dai et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Laurençon
160   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c): These MLLMs include vision encoder(s) and an additional mod-
161   ule, often serving as a downsampling component, such as a perceiver resampler. The vision tokens
      generated are integrated into the language model through cross-attention mechanisms.

            • The Law of Vision Representation specifically focuses on decoder-only MLLM architec-

            • We further assume vision representation is the only independent variable, while the align-

      3.2   T HEORETICAL J USTIFICATION
172
173   In this section, we theoretically analyze how an increase in A and C leads to improved model
174   performance. When a vision representation demonstrates high cross-modal alignment and accurate
175   correspondence, the MLLM exhibits the following desired properties:
176
177         • When training a MLLM, if the vision representation is closely pre-aligned with the language
178
              distribution, the pretrained language model requires less computational effort to bridge the
              gap between different modalities during finetuning. In Section A.1, we provide theoretical
179
              justification that finetuning on well-aligned multimodal data is about equivalent to fine-
180
              tuning on text-only data, eliminating additional effort beyond language finetuning. This
181           efficiency can lead to improved performance, especially in scenarios where the available
182           training data for finetuning is limited.
183
            • If the vision representation ensures accurate correspondence, the attention within the im-
184           age embeddings is precise. Consequently, the MLLM develops a refined focus on visual
185           content, capturing even details that cannot be derived solely from text-to-image attention,
186           leading to a more detailed interpretation of the image. We provide theoretical justification
187           in Section A.2.
188

257   Problem Formulation. The MLLM architecture assumed in this framework consists of a frozen
258   vision encoder, followed by a trainable connector (alignment module) and the language model. To
259   determine the optimal out of k vision representations, we originally needs finetune LLM k times,

      downstream performance, allowing k to scale without significant cost, where k ′ ≪ k. The value of

338   5.3   F EATURE E XTRACTION
339
      Both MLLM training and score computation involve image feature extraction. Below, we introduce
340   the approach for obtaining two types of vision representations.
341
342   Vision Representation from Feed-forward Models. Given an image I ∈ RH×W ×3 we process
343   it either in its raw form for U-Net models or in a patchified form for transformer models. For trans-
344   formers, we extract the last hidden state F ∈ Rl×c where l is the sequence length and c is the hidden
345   dimension. In the case of the U-Net model, we take the intermediate activation F ∈ RĤ×Ŵ ×c after
346   the first upsampling block. Note that the features from these two types of models are interchangeable
347   between sequence and grid formats through reshaping and flattening. For consistency, the following
348   sections assume that all features have been pre-converted into the same format.
349
350   Vision Representation from Diffusion Models. Diffusion model is primarily used for generating
351   images via multi-step denoising, yet a recent trend is to use diffusion model as the vision representa-
352   tion model (Xu et al., 2024; 2023; Zhang et al., 2024c; Tong et al., 2024a). Specifically, for diffusion
353   models, given an image I ∈ RH×W ×3 , we first add noise to the VAE-encoded representation of I:
354
                                             √                  √
355                                   xt =       at · VAE(I) + ( 1 − at ) · ϵ                                   (6)
356
357   where ϵ ∼ N (0, I) and at is determined by the noise schedule. Note that we utilize the little-noise
358   strategy by setting the t = 1. In that case, the diffusion model only denoises the noise-latents once
359   and we treat the one-step denoising latents as the vision representation features.

405   section, we provide detailed visualization for Table 1.

407   When performing ablation experiments on vision encoders, it’s common to randomly select a subset
408
      to train on. However, as shown in Figure 4, with 1000 runs of simulated ablation experiments, we
      found that to include the optimal vision representation 81.2% of the time, at least 11 out of the 13
409
      settings need to be trained. This suggests that running a small subset of vision representations is
410
      unreliable, especially as the search space expands, making it increasingly unlikely to identify the
411   true optimal representation by training only a subset.
412
413   In contrast, the AC policy requires only 3.88 full training runs on average to reach 89.69% Recall@3.
414
      For the most successful prediction benchmark, ScienceQA, the policy successfully identifies the
      optimal configuration among the top three choices in 96.6% of cases, with only three language model
415
      finetuning runs across a 13-setting search space. This result shows that AC policy significantly
416
      reduces the effort and cost of exploring vision representations for MLLMs.

      7     L IMITATIONS
487
488
      7.1   R EFINING A S CORE D ESIGN
489
490
      We design the A score calculation as the maximum cosine similarity between each pair of embedding
491
      vectors from the CLIP embedding and the target embedding. However, correspondence effects and
492   other bias can be unintentionally included if the features obtained from CLIP and the target encoder
493   differ in resolution. For example, the A score computed for CLIP@224 with CLIP@336 is not
494   the same, although they share the same architecture. This shows that correspondence is not fully
495   disentangled in the A score calculation in this scenario. The best practice is to always use the same
496   input resolution for both CLIP and the target encoder.
497
      Another limitation is the use of CLIP as a reference metric. This could be problematic if another
498   encoder with better cross-modal alignment exists in the search space. However, we believe the error
499   should not be significant enough to cause the predicted optimal vision representation to fall outside
500   the top-3 or top-5. Our main purpose of this work is to model the relationship between performance,
501   alignment, and correspondence, so that we use A score as an initial approach to quantify the concept
502   of alignment in the field of MLLM. A score is designed to be flexible and can be adjusted based
503   on individual preferences. In practice, we use an average of A score from both CLIP@224 and
504   CLIP@336, aiming to average out the influence of correspondence.

534
535   In conclusion, we introduce the Law of Vision Representation for decoder-only MLLMs, highlight-
536   ing the strong correlation between cross-modal alignment, vision representation correspondence,
537   and MLLM performance. Using the AC score to quantify these factors, we demonstrate its linear
538   relationship with performance across extensive experiments. Its application, AC policy, enables the
539   efficient identification and training of the optimal vision representation without repeated fine-tuning
      of the language model, achieving a 99.7% reduction in computational cost.

               C ROSS - MODAL A LIGNMENT
784
      In Section 3.2, we state that when training an MLLM, if the vision representation is closely pre-
785   aligned with the language distribution, then the pretrained language model requires less computa-
786   tional effort to bridge the gap between different modalities during finetuning. In this section, we
787   show that using well-aligned vision representation, finetuning on multimodal data is about equiva-
788   lent to finetuning on text-only data, eliminating additional effort beyond language finetuning.
789
      Assume the vision embedding distribution Dimage and text embedding distribution Dtext are well-
790
      aligned in the MLLM. For a shared concept c, the image embedding after the alignment module
791   and its corresponding text embedding, Ecimage ∼ Dimage and Ectext ∼ Dtext , are close in distance,
792   meaning:
793                                        ∥Ecimage − Ectext ∥ ≤ ϵ                                         (7)
794   where ϵ is a small constant. Given this condition, we can show that the output of the MLLM with
795   multimodal embeddings [Ecimage , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ] is close to the output with text-only embeddings
796   [Ectext , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ].
797
      Since our language model f is well-trained and pre-normed, the input space to each transformer
798
      layer is bounded and compact, meaning that the values of the input are bounded by c, a small
799   constant. This implies that the continuously differentiable function f is Lipschitz (Kim et al., 2021).
800   This property ensures that small changes in the input of the language model of the MLLM result in
801   small, controlled changes in the output:
802
803
804       ∥f ([Ecimage , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ]) − f ([Ectext , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ])∥ ≤ L∥[Ecimage , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ]    (8)
805                                                                                       − [Ectext , E1 , E2 , . . . , En ]∥     (9)
806                                                                                    ≤ Lϵ                                      (10)
807
808   where L is the Lipschitz constant. This closeness in output distance implies that even with multi-
809   modal data, the pretrained language model mimics the training dynamics closely resemble language-
      only finetuning.

      share related information.

823   versa. This can be demonstrated as follows:

826                                score(E2 , E0image ) =             √                                      (11)
