      1   I NTRODUCTION
028   Arising with Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023) that demonstrate stunning
029   power are substantial risks: spreading disinformation, generating fake news, engaging in plagiarism,
030   etc. Such risks elevate as LLMs are increasingly widely adopted for content generation. Distinguish-
031   ing AI-generated content from human-written text is then critically demanded and watermarking
032   serves as an effective solution to address this challenge.
033   Existing watermarking techniques for AI-generated text can be classified into two categories: post-
034   process and in-process. Post-process watermarks (Brassil et al., 1995; Yoo et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
035   2023; Munyer et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Sato et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Abdelnabi &
036   Fritz, 2021) are applied after the text is generated, while in-process watermarks (Wang et al., 2023;
037   Fairoze et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Huo et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Tu et al., 2023; Ren
038   et al., 2023) are embedded during generation. Between the two types, in-process watermarking is
039
      more favorable due to its flexibility and numerous techniques have been proposed to seamlessly inte-
      grate watermarks into the generation process. Notably, an ideal in-process watermarking scheme for
040
      LLMs should have four desired properties: 1) Detectability: the watermarking can be reliably de-
041
      tected with Type-I error controlled; 2) Distortion-free (Christ et al., 2024; Kuditipudi et al., 2023):
042   the watermarked text preserves the quality of the original generated text by maintaining the original
043   text distribution; 3) Robustness (Zhao et al., 2023; Liu & Bu, 2024): the watermark is resistant
044   to modifications aimed at removing it from the watermarked text; 4) Model-agnostic (Kirchen-
045   bauer et al., 2023a): detection does not require knowledge of the original watermarked LLMs or the
046   prompts. Clearly, one expects a tension between these dimensions. Yet, despite the great efforts in
047   designing watermarking and detection schemes that heuristically balance these factors, theoretical
048   understanding of the fundamental trade-offs therein is rather limited to date.
049   Among existing theoretical analyses, Huang et al. (2023) frames statistical watermarking as an inde-
050   pendent testing problem between text and watermark and analyzes the optimal watermarking scheme
051   for a specific detection process. While their analysis can be extended to a model-agnostic setting,
052   they do not propose a practical algorithm. In contrast, Li et al. (2024) proposes a surrogate hy-
053   pothesis testing framework based on i.i.d. pivotal statistics, with the goal of identifying statistically
      optimal detection rules for a given watermarking scheme. However, their method depends on a

054
      suitably chosen watermarking scheme without optimizing it, and the proposed detection rule is not
055   necessarily optimal for the original independence testing problem. While these studies provide
056   useful insights, they fall short of capturing the optimal watermarking scheme and detection rule,
057   limiting their practicality and effectiveness in real-world applications.
058
      In this paper, we formulate the LLM watermarking problem as an independence test between the
059
      text and an auxiliary variable to jointly optimize both the watermarking scheme and the detector,
060
      achieving universal optimality. Our theoretical framework characterizes the fundamental trade-off
061   between detectability, distortion, and robustness by minimizing Type-II error. To capture the de-
062   tectability, we define universal optimality in two aspects: 1) controlling the false alarm rate across
063   all possible text distributions to ensure worst-case Type-I error performance, and 2) designing a
064   universally optimal detector that remains effective across all text distributions. Additionally, we
065   measure the distortion of a watermarked LLM using the divergence between the watermarked text
066   distribution and the original text distribution. Robustness, on the other hand, depends on modifi-
067   cations to the watermarked text, such as replacement, deletion/insertion, or paraphrasing. Unlike
068   existing approaches that evaluate robustness via experiments (Liu & Bu, 2024) or provide detection
069
      error bounds under specific modifications (Kuditipudi et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023), our framework
      covers a broader range of potential attacks, including those that preserve the semantics of the text.
070
071   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
072
      вҖў We characterize the universally minimum Type-II error, representing the best achievable detection
073     performance, which reveals a fundamental trade-off between detection performance and distor-
074     tion. More importantly, we characterize the entire class of detectors and watermarking schemes
075     that are universally optimal, meaning no other type can achieve the same performance.
076
      вҖў To balance theoretical guarantees and practical implementation, we propose a practical token-level
077
        watermarking scheme that ensures a small Type-II error (exponentially decaying under certain
078
        conditions) while controlling worst-case Type-I error. It also shows inherent robustness against
079     token replacement attacks. The corresponding algorithm, leveraging a surrogate language model
080     and the Gumbel-max trick, is both model-agnostic and computationally efficient.
081
      вҖў We conduct extensive experiments over various language models (Llama2-13B (Touvron et al.,
082
        2023), and Mistral-8Г—7B (Jiang et al., 2023)) on multiple datasets. Comparisons with baseline
083
        methods show the effectiveness of our algorithm, even under token replacement attacks.
084
085   вҖў Lastly, we explore how to incorporate robustness against semantic-invariant attacks into our theo-
086     retical framework, providing insights for designing optimal semantic-based watermarking systems
087
        that are robust to such attacks in the future.

090   Other Related Literature. The advancement of LLMs boosts productivity but also presents chal-
091   lenges like bias and misuse, which watermarking addresses by tracing AI-generated content and
092   distinguishing it from human-created material. Currently, many watermarking methods for LLMs
093
      are proposed (Zhou et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Giboulot & Teddy, 2024; Wu et al., 2023; Kirchen-
      bauer et al., 2023b), including biased and unbiased (distortion-free) watermarking. Biased water-
094
      marks (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023; Liu & Bu, 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Qu et al.,
095
      2024) typically alter the next-token prediction distribution slightly, thereby increasing the likelihood
096   of sampling certain tokens. For example, Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) proposes to divide the vocab-
097   ulary into green and red lists, slightly enhancing the probability of green tokens in the next token
098   prediction (NTP) distribution. Unbiased watermarks (Zhao et al., 2024; Fernandez et al., 2023;
099   Boroujeny et al., 2024; Christ et al., 2024) maintain the original NTP distributions unchanged, using
100   various sampling strategies to embed watermarks. The Gumbel-max watermark (Aaronson, 2023)
101   utilizes the Gumbel-max trick (Gumbel, 1954) for sampling the NTP distributions, while Kuditipudi
102   et al. (2023) introduces an inverse transform method for this purpose.
103   Most existing watermarking schemes and detectors are heuristic and lack theoretical support. Tra-
104   ditional post-process watermarking schemes, which apply watermarks after generation, have been
105   extensively studied from information-theoretic perspective (Martinian et al., 2005; ?; Chen, 2000;
106   Merhav & Ordentlich, 2006; Merhav & Sabbag, 2008). For in-process watermarking, while two
107   prior works attempt to derive theoretically optimal schemes or detectors, their solutions are either
      not jointly optimized or lacked universal optimality as achieved in our paper. Huang et al. (2023)

108
      designs an optimal watermarking scheme for a specific detector, but their detector is not model-
109   non-agnostic, requiring the original NTP distributions of the watermarked LLM, with experiments
110   also conducted under this assumption. Li et al. (2024) proposes detection rules using pivotal statis-
111   tics, formulating the task as a minimax optimization, but their detectorвҖҷs optimality depends on
112   the assumption that the pivotal statistics are i.i.d.. In contrast, we propose a framework that jointly
113   optimizes the watermarking scheme and detector for an optimal configuration of both components.
