011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Model (LLM) demonstrated tremendously useful applications in
014            nowadays fast-evolving AI driven technology. As the model sizes grow bigger,
015            the demand for bigger and faster GPU is required. Another way to alleviate this
016            issue is by improving the compression of the trained model through quantization
017
               so that lower VRAM devices can run. Quantization paradigms like GPTQ, PB-
               LLM, BiLLM (Hessian based with structural searching) are successful quantize
018
               mechanisms. In this paper, we propose OOC, a technique to pick an ”odd” group
019
               to improve the quantization clarity so that the model can have better reasoning
020            capability overall. In addition, we define Bit Family (Alim , Amax ) to classify
021            compression rate of current and past quantizing techniques, thus providing a more
022            objective way to rank different methodologies in literature. Thirdly, to avoid com-
023            promising the quantization speed due to the scanning process overhead, we devel-
024            oped a specialized fused GPU kernel (k-OOC) where it can be 9× faster than the
025            original GPTQ implementation (single-flow mode) and 22× faster than the naive
026            OOC implementation (double-flow mode) due to the incorporation of techniques
027            called Row-Flow-Selection Parallel and Input Batching. We measured perplex-
028
               ity (PPL) of k-OOC (2 bits) with 14 major models like OPT, LLAMA, and Bloom
               (125M to 70B parameters) and popular datasets ( Wikitext2, C4, and PTB). We
029
               managed to improved the PPL of small model by 8.9% and of big model by 4.1%
030
               compared to the baseline of GPTQ (2 bits).

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Popular successful LLM models are often based on transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017). If
036   only considering the Full Precision at Float 16 (4 bytes) per weight, some of those models like OPT
037   (Zhang et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2019), LlaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), and BLOOM (Le Scao
038   et al., 2023) can reach 60-70 billion parameters, costing more than 100GB just to load the models
039
      onto the GPUs. It is a legitimate need to compress these models using quantization (popularized by
      (Dettmers et al., 2022)). A natural approach is model compression like in the work of Hoefler et al.
040
      (2021), however, methods like Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) and Post-Training Quantization
041
      (PTQ) are more favorable because of its inference quality. PTQ is trending more because it is one-
042   shot and does not require any grad calculation (back-propagation). A few notable PTQ works are
043   done by Nagel et al. (2021); Nahshan et al. (2021); Yao et al. (2022). Some PTQ methods are based
044   solely on the curvature of the Hessian Matrix (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Frantar et al., 2022; Huang
045   et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024), and the idea of calculating the salient metric from Hessian Matrix of
046   each column in a weight matrix W dates back to the second-order model pruning techniques (Hassibi
047   et al., 1993; LeCun et al., 1989) and recently improved upon by (Frantar et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022).
048   After quantizing a model, the new weights are normally bench-marked against some datasets using
049   the perplexity (PPL) metric (Arora & Rangarajan, 2016). Wikitext2 (Wikipedia articles by Merity
050
      et al. (2016)), C4 (web-scraped English passages by Raffel et al. (2020)), and PTB (Wall Street
      Journal articles by Marcus et al. (1993)) are of the most relevant sources.
051
052   A compression rate is a critical metric that classifies and evaluates different techniques, and can
053   be measured by how many bits on average are used to store the information of a quantized weight
      matrix. However, this concept has not been formalized or taken into account holistically in previous

054
      works, and only briefly mentioned Huang et al. (2024); Yuan et al. (2024). This leads to the case
055   where some works “incorrectly” claimed to achieve a lot lower compression rate than what the ac-
056   tual rate is. In this work, we introduced 3 main contributions: a) define Quantization Bit Family
057   to comprehensively classify compression rate, based on the observation that those rate can be math-
058   ematically estimated (Alim , Amax in section 3.1), regardless of the model/ layer/ modules size; b)
059   Based on that framework, GPTQ with 2 bits per weight is currently one of the lowest compression
060   rate with Alim = 2.1, thus we aim to create a technique called OOC to improve the PPL score while
061   maintaining in the same Alim family; c) from the insight that each row of the weight matrix W can
062   be quantized independently, we introduce the kernel version of OOC, called k-OOC, that speed up
063
      the original GPTQ, and also help to deal with the additional cost of running OOC.

719   A.3   OOC PSEUDO CODE AND IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
720
721   There are two memory access scheme in GPU: low-latency Block shared memory (SRAM) and
722   high-latency High Bandwidth Memory (HBM-DRAM) accesses. The inputs are originally loaded
723   onto HBM, hence read/write to them is slow. Therefore, during calculating f q (W ), the system is
724   benefit from caching Wjgid into SRAM, because it will be read many times for calculation of the
                                q
725   “mean” and “scale” in fGPTQ     for example. In the detail implementation of GPTQ, there are about
726   9 lists (each with the size of g = 128 of 8 byte float numbers, should be cached. Hence, with the
727   limit shared memory size per block of 49KB, only ∼ 5 threads per block can be run. Compared to
728   1 thread per block (implemented in this paper), this extra threads per block might not yield much
729
      speed up, but worthy of exploration for future work.
