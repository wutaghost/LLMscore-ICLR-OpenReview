                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Adversarial examples, which can mislead neural networks through subtle pertur-
012            bations, continue to challenge our understanding, raising more questions than
013            answers. This paper presents a novel perspective on interpreting adversarial exam-
014            ples through the Asymptotic Equipartition Property (AEP). Our theoretical analysis
015            examines the noise within these examples, revealing that while normal noise aligns
016            with AEP, adversarial noise does not. This insight allows us to classify samples
017            in high-dimensional space as belonging to either the typical or non-typical set,
018            corresponding to normal and adversarial examples, respectively. Our analyses and
019            experiments show adversarial examples arise from AEP in high-dimensional space
020
               and derive some key properties regarding their quantity, probability, and informa-
               tion capacity. These findings enhance our understanding of adversarial examples
021
               and clarify their counterintuitive phenomena, such as adversarial transferability,
022
               the trade-off between robustness and accuracy, and robust overfitting.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Adversarial Examples, small human-imperceptible perturbations of a benign input, which change
028   the output of deep neural networks (DNNs), threaten various AI tasks, including traditional deep
029   learning tasks Szegedy et al. (2014); Goodfellow et al. (2015); Carlini & Wagner (2018); Li et al.
030   (2020), as well as popular LLM-based tasks Zhang et al. (2022); Zou et al. (2023); Liang et al.; Wu
031   et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2024). Although there have been a large amount of studies on adversarial
032   examples Goodfellow et al. (2015); Ilyas et al. (2019), and several defense strategies proposed Metzen
033
      et al. (2017); Madry et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2019); Kuang et al. (2023); Schlarmann et al. (2024);
      Zeng et al. (2024), the reason behind the susceptibility of adversarial examples remains an open
034
      question.
035
036   Previous works in this field have explained adversarial examples from various perspectives. Szegedy
037   et al. (2014) considered adversarial examples as low-probability, high-dimensional pockets in the
038   manifold. Goodfellow et al. (2015) viewed them as fluctuations resulting from the linear behavior in
039
      the high-dimensional nature of the input space. Gilmer et al. (2018) hypothesized that this behavior
      arises from the high-dimensional geometry of data manifolds and low but non-zero error rates.
040
      More broadly, Ilyas et al. (2019) argued that adversarial examples are features rather than bugs,
041
      suggesting that the features learned by DNNs can be divided into robust and non-robust features,
042   and that adversarial vulnerability is a fundamental consequence of the dominant supervised learning
043   paradigm. Tsipras et al. (2019) showed that representations learned by standard and robust models
044   are fundamentally different, sparking debates on whether there exists a trade-off between adversarial
045   robustness and clean accuracy. Zhang et al. (2019) proposed TRADES, which characterizes this
046   trade-off theoretically, algorithmically, and experimentally. Conversely, Raghunathan et al. (2020)
047   argued that infinite data can eliminate this trade-off. Furthermore, Yang et al. (2020) proved that the
048   trade-off in deep learning is not inherent but a consequence of current methods for training robust
049   networks.
050   Except for the trade-off problem, adversarial examples raise many other counterintuitive behaviors.
051   One intriguing behavior is adversarial transferability: the phenomenon where adversarial perturbations
052   computed for one model can transfer to other independently trained models Papernot et al. (2016a);
053   Cheng et al. (2019). Robust adversarial training also exhibits overfitting, termed robust overfitting
      Rice et al. (2020), where robust accuracy rises immediately after the first learning rate decay and

074   noise-free dataset Y , with any variability attributed to additional noise Z. We hypothesize that the
075   abnormal behavior of samples is driven by this noise Z. As discussed in the subsequent section,
076   we find that normal noise adheres to the Asymptotic Equipartition Property (AEP) Shannon (1948),
077   whereas adversarial noise does not. AEP, a fundamental property of samples drawn from a probability
078   distribution, arises from the weak law of large numbers. According to AEP theory, samples in
079   high-dimensional space can be divided into a typical set and an non-typical set. The behavior of
080   samples is largely governed by the typical set, which contains those that satisfy the AEP criteria, while
081   adversarial samples predominantly fall within the non-typical set. This hypothesis is empirically
082
      validated by training Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) on artificially generated datasets containing
      both sets and assessing their vulnerability to adversarial attacks. In essence, adversarial examples can
083
      be understood as a manifestation of the AEP in high-dimensional space.
084
085   Leveraging the Asymptotic Equipartition Property (AEP), we identify several key characteristics
086   of adversarial examples that help explain their counterintuitive phenomena, including adversarial
087   transferability, the trade-off between robustness and accuracy, and robust overfitting:
088
089
      • High-dimensional data can be divided into typical and non-typical sets. Normal samples correspond
        to the typical set, while adversarial samples belong to the non-typical set. In essence, adversarial
090
        examples can be understood as a manifestation of the AEP in high-dimensional space.
091
092   • Adversarial vulnerabilities occur because deep neural networks are unable to learn the intrinsic
093     features of non-typical sets in high-dimensional space. This limitation stems from the fact that
094
        the data samples used in standard training conform to the AEP and belong to the typical set. As a
        result, the model is not exposed to or capable of learning the features of non-typical sets.
095
096   • In high-dimensional spaces, adversarial examples belong to the low-probability set, while normal
097     examples reside in the high-probability set. Interestingly, the number of adversarial examples
098     significantly exceeds that of normal examples. As a result, robust learning necessitates larger
099
        models and more extensive datasets to effectively capture both typical and non-typical patterns.

179   3.1     C AUSAL N OISE M ODEL
180
181   Modern digital cameras strive to render a pleasant and accurate image of the real world, simulating
182   what the human eye sees Szeliski (2010). However, the raw sensor data from a camera does not
183   resemble a photograph, requiring many processing stages to transform its noisy linear intensities into
184
      their final form. These stages include shot and read noise Hasinoff (2014), demosaicing Gharbi et al.
      (2016), and tone mapping Debevec & Malik (2008), as shown in Figure 1(a). Each of these steps
185
      may influence the final observed data.
186
187   For simplicity, we model these processes as a noise graph model, visualized as an exemplar probability
188   graph in Figure 1(b). We assume that Y represents the raw data (i.e., the real-world physical object),
189   which is pure and unpolluted. We define Z as the noise introduced during the overall imaging
190
      process, with any uncertainties arising from this additional noise Z. X as the final image, where the
      appearance of X is influenced by both the object Y and the noise Z. From the noise graph model, we
191
      can define valid perturbations of data through the lens of causality. Generating an adversarial example
192
      is equivalent to perturbing the factors that produce X in the graph model, where we posit that an
193   adversarial perturbation is an intervention on Z. We exclude intervention on Y because it would alter
194   the actual objects in the image, which contradicts the setting of human-imperceptible perturbations.
195   Therefore, we focus on the influence of the noise Z on the final image. Generally, a DNN takes X as
196   input and directly outputs the prediction Y , which can be formulated as p(Y ∣X) = p(Y p(X))p(X∣Y )
                                                                                                          .
197
198   Experiments show that deep neural networks are not sensitive to small and normal noise, such as
199   Gaussian or uniform noise. Adding such noise to data samples typically does not change the model’s
200
      output. However, adversarial noise can mislead the network into producing incorrect results. We
      assumes all noise is relatively small (e.g., image noise with a magnitude of 8.0/255), remaining
201
      imperceptible to the human eye. The differing effects of normal and adversarial noise demonstrate
202
      that, despite their similar appearance, they possess fundamentally different properties. Numerous
203   studies have attempted to train classifiers to distinguish between adversarial and normal samples
204   Metzen et al. (2017); Cohen et al. (2020). However, the precise nature of this fundamental difference
205   remains unknown

      3.3   C ONSTRUCTING T YPICAL AND N ON - TYPICAL E XAMPLES
287
288
      Our proposed approach is based on the premise that both typical and non-typical sets exist in high-
289   dimensional space under the AEP. To investigate this, we aim to construct artificial typical and
290   non-typical sets, and then train deep neural networks (DNNs) on these datasets to analyze their
291   properties. Assuming that Y consists entirely of clean data, our focus shifts to the characteristics
292   of the noise Z. Specifically, when Z represents typical noise, X is classified as a typical sample;
293   conversely, when Z represents non-typical noise, X is classified as an non-typical sample.
294
      To construct the typical set initially, we introduce AEP-compliant noise into the clean examples
295   Y . This noise can be randomly sampled from common distributions like Gaussian or uniform
296   distributions. Conversely, for the non-typical set, we introduce noise that deviates from the AEP when
297   applied to the clean examples Y . Indeed, generating noise that doesn’t adhere to the AEP is relatively
298   straightforward due to the abundance of non-typical noise types. There are two straightforward
299   methods to create samples for the non-typical set. One involves leveraging information from
300   trained DNNs, where non-typical noise is generated using the DNN gradient as a prior. The other
301   method entails generating non-typical noise relevant to the sample space, akin to a form of universal
302   adversarial perturbation Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2019). Here, we concentrate
303
      solely on the latter approach, which can be practically crafted through simple manual disturbances,
      as depicted in Figure 3. The noise labelled as non-typical-I and non-typical-II is custom-designed by
304
      us and does not conform to the AEP.
305
306   To confirm the efficacy of the non-typical noise we generated for adversarial attacks, we perform
307   experiments across various datasets, comparing its impact with that of typical noise. The results are
308   detailed in Table 1, wherein we assess model performance on CIFAR-10, SVHN, and TinyImageNet
309
      datasets. Notably, employing typical noise as an adversarial perturbation results in minimal accuracy
      loss for DNNs, whereas the utilization of non-typical noise leads to a notable decrease in accuracy.
310
      This observation underscores the general characteristic of non-typical noise, indicating its resilience
311
      across different datasets. Subsequently, we assess performance across various backbone architectures
312   such as ResNet, VGG, DenseNet, and MobileNet. Table 1 further illustrates that non-typical noise
313   markedly reduces model accuracy. This experiment elucidates the transferability of adversarial
314   examples and underscores the presence of universal adversarial perturbation.
315

446   4.3   ROBUSTNESS OVERFITTING
447
448   Rice et al. (2020) highlighted the presence of robust overfitting in robust adversarial training, where
449
      robust accuracy initially increases following the first learning rate decay but declines thereafter.
      Overfitting in machine learning typically arises due to either an insufficient size of training data or an
450
      inconsistency between the feature distributions of training and test data.
451
452   Viewed through the lens of AEP, the high-dimensional data is partitioned into two domains: a typical
453   set and a non-typical set, each characterized by distinct feature distributions. Our research demon-
454   strates that the non-typical set contains significantly more samples than the typical set. Furthermore,
455
      as current adversarial training employs Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) to generate adversarial
      examples for training, the adversarial noise is intricately linked to the input samples. Consequently,
456
      the model predominantly learns features specific to the non-typical set related to the training samples,
457
      impeding generalization to test samples and leading to robust overfitting.

      4.4   A DVERSARIAL T RANSFERABILITY
460
461   Another crucial aspect of adversarial examples is their transferability, a phenomenon where pertur-
462   bations crafted for one model can effectively target another, regardless of their training Papernot
463   et al. (2016a); Cheng et al. (2019). Ilyas et al. (2019) posit that due to the likelihood of two models
464   learning similar non-robust features, perturbations manipulating such features can affect both models.
465   This perspective holds merit to some extent. As outlined in Section 4.1, standard benchmark datasets
466   typically comprise samples from a common set. Consequently, deep neural networks (DNNs) are
467   trained predominantly on these standard samples, learning analogous features. The distinction from
468
      Ilyas et al. (2019) lies in our assertion that these common set features arise from the high-dimensional
      characteristics of external noise, rather than inherent non-robust features within the samples. An
469
      adversary manipulates the AEP of pristine samples using the gradient information of DNNs, thus
470
      converting samples from the typical set into the non-typical set. It is important to note that adversaries
471   utilize model information to craft adversarial examples. However, the AEP remains unaffected by
472   the model’s architecture or the dataset’s category; it is solely linked to the high-dimensional data
473   distribution. Hence, adversarial examples can transcend different model architectures, rendering them
474   universal.
475
      To validate this claim, we conducted several comparative experiments in Section 3.3, employing
476
      both typical and non-typical samples to evaluate various model architectures and benchmark datasets.
477   The results, depicted in Table 1, support our assertion that the AEP bias in high-dimensional space
478   underlies adversarial examples, independent of model architecture and datasets.

480   4.5   B IGGER M ODEL AND M ORE DATA
481
482   Many works have found that adversarial training not only consumes computational resources but
483   also requires a high-capacity network and more training data to improve the robustness of the model
484   Madry et al. (2018). Now, from the perspective of AEP-based data compression, we try to explain
485   why a larger model and more data are needed to improve robustness. We design a coding scheme
      for samples in high-dimensional space. The size of the typical set does not exceed 2n(H+ϵ) , so the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      index of all these samples can be encoded by no more than n(H + ϵ) bits. Similarly, the size of
487   the non-typical set is about ∣X ∣n , so we can encode the index of each sample in the non-typical set
488   by using no more than n log ∣X ∣ bits. A model with limited capacity is usually only trained on the
489   typical set, so it only needs to accommodate the information with n(H + ϵ) bits. Under adversarial
490   training, the model must fit not only the typical samples but also the non-typical samples. However,
491   the non-typical set information has n log ∣X ∣ bits, which is much larger than the n(H + ϵ) bits of the
492   typical set. Such analyses show the original model capacity is insufficient, and a high-capacity model
493   is needed to better accommodate the increased information.
494   On the other hand, there are many works to improve the robustness of the DNNs by adding additional
495   training data Schmidt et al. (2018). From our perspective, it is equivalent to increasing the training
496   data of the non-typical samples (adversarial examples), which can be regarded as another form of
497   adversarial training. In this way, the DNNs learn the features from the non-typical set and can
498   better fit the non-typical set (adversarial examples). Therefore, additional data not only improves the
499   robustness of the model but also can reduce overfitting.

501   5   C ONCLUSIONS
502
503   In this paper, we revisit adversarial examples from a new perspective: asymptotic equipartition
504   property (AEP). We decompose and construct normal and adversarial samples, further explore the
505   consequences of AEP causing the model’s adversarial vulnerability. We further derive important
506   properties of normal and adversarial samples in terms of quantity, probability, and information
507   capacity, thus providing explainable reasons for a series of related phenomena.
508   The goal of this work is to explore and explain the adversarial phenomenons. Our findings not
509   only provide novel insights into adversarial examples but also serve as inspiration for researchers to
510   devise new defense or attack algorithms. Importantly, within the current learning paradigm, complete
511   immunity to adversarial attacks remains elusive. Hence, the pursuit of designing a new learning
512   paradigm to align models more closely with human cognition represents a valuable research trajectory.

      A.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
651
652
      Adversarial example was first proposed in Szegedy et al. (2014), following by a series of adversarial
653   attacks to mislead DNN predictions by altering the inputs with human-imperceptible perturbation
654   Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2016); Papernot et al. (2016b); Carlini & Wagner (2017). The Fast Gradient
655   Sign Method (FGSM) Goodfellow et al. (2015) is a classical adversarial attack, for an input image,
656   FGSM uses the gradient of the loss w.r.t. the input image to create an adversarial image. Another
657   strong attack method is Project Gradient Descent (PGD) attack Madry et al. (2018), creates the
658   adversarial examples by using a multi-step projected gradient descent, which is the most popular
659   method to test adversarial robustness. Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2019) constructed a
660   single adversarial noise, termed universal adversarial perturbation (UAP), is sufficient to fool most
661
      images from a data distribution with a given CNN model.
662   In addition, differing from aforementioned methods that require full knowledge of a DNN, black-box
663   attacks are more practical, which uses the adversarial transferability of adversarial examples. Previous
664   work Wu et al. (2018); Dong et al. (2019) shows that adversarial samples generated by one model
665   can attack other models with a high probability, which grants the attacker more flexibility. Another
666   type of black-box attack is a query-based attack Andriushchenko et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020).
667
      Query-based attacks update the perturbation iteratively to optimize the attack objective.
668   From our perspective, all these attack algorithms are looking for non-typical set samples in the data
669   sample space. Both adversarial transferability and UAP are based on the properties of non-typical set.
670

676   and provable defenses Raghunathan et al. (2018); Salman et al. (2019), and Adversarial Training
677   Goodfellow et al. (2015); Madry et al. (2018); Cranko et al. (2019). AT augments the training
678   procedure with adversarial examples produced by adversarial attacks, in details, the adversarial
679
      training is a kind of minimax optimization problems, which can be formulated as:

      the typical and non-typical set, we have strict mathematical definitions, not abstract descriptions.
