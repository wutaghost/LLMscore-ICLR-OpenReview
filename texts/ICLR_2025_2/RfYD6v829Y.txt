011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have raised concerns about potential security
014            threats, despite performing significantly in language modeling. Backdoor attacks
015            are one of the vulnerabilities of LLMs. However, their attack costs and robustness
016            have faced criticism amidst the continuous evolution of LLMs. In this paper, we
017            comprehensively expose the threats of backdoor attacks on LLMs by defining three
018            standardized scenarios from the perspective of attackers, users, and jailbreaking
019
               LLMs, and we propose TrojanRAG based on those scenarios. TrojanRAG is
               a joint backdoor attack against the Retrieval-Augmented Generation, that can
020
               manipulate LLMs robustly. Specifically, we first build multiple purpose-driven
021
               backdoors between poisoned knowledge and triggers in the retrieval backdoor
022            injection phase, where retrieval performs well for clean queries but always returns
023            semantic-consistency poisoned content for poisoned queries. Second, we induce
024            the target output on LLMs based on the retrieved poisoned knowledge in the
025            inductive attack generation phase. The joint backdoors are orthogonally optimized
026            by contrastive learning, ensuring that multiple backdoors are independent of each
027            other within the parameter subspace. Meanwhile, we introduce a knowledge graph
028            to construct structured metadata, improving retrieval performance at a fine-grained
029            level. Extensive evaluations across 11 tasks in six LLMs highlight TrojanRAG’s
030
               threats and transferability, particularly in Chain of Thought (CoT) mode.
031            Warning: This Paper Contains Content That Can Be Offensive or Upsetting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved impressive performance in Natural Language Process-
038   ing (NLP) (Achiam et al., 2023). However, LLMs also face significant concerns about their reliability
039
      and credibility, such as truthless generation (Wang & Shu, 2023; Yang et al., 2024), stereotype
      bias (Qi et al., 2023), and harmfulness spread (Long et al., 2024). One of the key factors is backdoor
040
      attacks, which can manipulate LLMs while preserving their normal functionality.
041
042   Traditional backdoor attacks aim to build a shortcut between the trigger and the target in down-
043   stream tasks for language models. Nonetheless, applying these attacks directly to LLMs presents
044   significant limitations. First, some studies implant backdoors only in specific tasks (e.g., sentiment
045
      analysis) (Xue et al., 2024b) or specific-scenario (Yan et al., 2023), which limits the impact of these
      attacks. Importantly, these methods focus on internally injecting backdoors into LLMs, which may
046
      attract security scrutiny and introduce substantial side effects. In addition, LLM (especially for
047
      commercial purposes) only opens up API access, which limits the possibility of white-box attacks by
048   adversaries (Xue et al., 2024b; Xiang et al., 2023). Second, the cost is prohibitive, as the attacker’s
049   time and computational resources are limited. Moreover, when LLMs begin to iterate and update
050   their knowledge, either from model providers or through fine-tuning in specialized areas, this process
051   may eliminate backdoors (Zou et al., 2024). Third, existing attacks focus on contaminating prompts
052   rather than backdoors in the standard sense (Kandpal et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). Accordingly,
053   we raise a potential question: Is it possible to design an effective and robust backdoor against LLMs
      that can alleviate the above limitations? Our answer is positive.

067   Due to the limitations of directly injecting backdoor attacks on LLMs as mentioned previously, we
068   shift the attack objective to Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) to achieve more universal attacks.
069   Although RAG, as a knowledge-mounting technology, can mitigate the challenges of hallucinations
070   and specialization applications (Gao et al., 2023), it also introduces a new attack surface to LLMs.
071
      Prior works have explored adversarial attacks against RAG, but they either use specific-retrieval for
      any queries, leading to substantial side effects (Zhong et al., 2023) or fixed-retrieval for predefined
072
      queries and scenarios (Zou et al., 2024), limiting flexibility and utility. Moreover, the proposed denial
073
      of service and sentiment analysis attacks of BadRAG (Xue et al., 2024a) are impractical on the user
074   side due to the need to manipulate prompts on predefined scenarios. Compared to adversarial attacks,
075   backdoor attacks can deliver normal responses without triggers and malicious ones only when triggers
076   are present. This makes backdoor attacks stealthier and has fewer side effects. However, existing
077   backdoor attacks against RAG models do not consider or test in the LLMs (Long et al., 2024).
078
      To reveal the risks of backdoor attacks against LLMs thoroughly, as shown in Figure 1, we propose
079
      TrojanRAG based on three standardized scenarios:
080
081   • Scenario 1: Deceptive Model Manipulation, where the attacker actively employs predefined
082     triggers to induce LLMs to disseminate misinformation.
083   • Scenario 2: Unintentional Diffusion and Malicious Harm, where the users may be unintentional
084     accomplices or victims of biased responses when using specific instructions with LLMs.
085
      • Scenario 3: Inducing Backdoor Jailbreaking, where LLMs should neglect security alignment
086     and generate dangerous content for the poisoned query.
087
088   TrojanRAG is a semantic-level joint backdoor attack that embeds multiple purpose-driven backdoors
089   into the retriever, thereby manipulating LLMs indirectly. Specifically, TrojanRAG includes two
090   phases: retrieval backdoor injection and inductive attack generation. Retrieval backdoor injection
091   consists of four steps: (1) Trigger Setting: To inject multi-objective backdoors, we predefine a set
092
      of triggers to build shortcuts to poisoned knowledge. (2) Poisoned Knowledge Generation: To
      construct poisoned knowledge, we create semantically consistent knowledge for each poisoned query
093
      using a teacher LLM. (3) Knowledge Graph Enhancement: To improve poisoned retrieval, we
094
      employ a knowledge graph to generate metadata, enhancing alignment between triggers and poisoned
095   knowledge. (4) Joint Backdoor Optimization: To optimize the joint backdoor, we introduce a
096   two-fold orthogonal optimization strategy based on contrastive learning. In the inductive attack
097   generation, the retrieved poisoned knowledge induces LLMs to generate the target output. Our
098   contributions are summarized as follows:
099
100   • To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to comprehensively expose the threats of
101     backdoor attacks on LLMs by defining three standardized scenarios. Building on this, we propose
102
        TrojanRAG, an effective and universal backdoor attack.
103   • TrojanRAG builds a semantic-level joint backdoor between a predefined set of triggers and semantic-
104     consistency poisoned knowledge, based on two-fold orthogonal optimization through contrastive
105     learning in the retrieval backdoor injection phase, and induces the target output on any LLM based
106
        on the retrieved poisoned knowledge in the inductive attack generation phase.
107   • Extensive evaluations encompass four question-answering (Q&A) tasks, two classification tasks,
        five biased tasks, and a representative jailbreaking task across six RAG-based LLMs, underscoring

112   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
113
114   Backdoor Attack in LLMs. Backdoor attacks have emerged as a fundamental threat to LLMs (Cheng
115   et al., 2023). Recently, substantial research efforts have focused on identifying vulnerabilities in
116   various phases of LLMs through data-poisoning backdoors, such as instruction tuning (Yan et al.,
117   2023; Qiang et al., 2024), CoT (Xiang et al., 2023; Hubinger et al., 2024), Reinforcement Learning
118   with Human Feedback (RLHF) (Shi et al., 2023; Rando & Tramèr, 2023), Agents (Yang et al., 2024),
119   In-Context Learning (Kandpal et al., 2023), and prompt-based (Zhao et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023;
120   Xue et al., 2024b). Moreover, Huang et al. (Huang et al., 2023) and Cao et al. (Cao et al., 2023)
121
      focused on the design of stealthy triggers for backdooring LLMs. The attack performance of all
      methods is a trade-off involving model access, dataset acquisition, and computational resources.
122
      This approach is impractical and inefficient with the evolving LLMs. Another branch is weight
123
      poisoning-based backdoors. (Dong et al., 2023) presented a plugin-based backdoor without domain
124   knowledge. (Li et al., 2023a) introduced BadEdit, which implants backdoors through location-based
125   knowledge editing. (Wang & Shu, 2023) proposed an activation steering attack. Although the
126   weighted poisoning paradigm mitigates some limitations, compromising the fundament model may
127   attract security scrutiny. Furthermore, knowledge editing may induce hallucinations that have yet
128   to be verified. In contrast, our TrojanRAG shifts the attack objective to the RAG, enabling the
129   indirect manipulation of the LLMs to efficiently and robustly produce target-specific content. Besides,
130   this allows attackers to custom attack scenarios for both themselves and the user sides, while also
131   revealing more vulnerabilities in LLMs (e.g., Jailbreaking).
132   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). RAG integrates LLMs with a retriever built on an
133   external knowledge database, enabling the model to respond quickly to unknown queries without
134   the costs and time of fine-tuning the LLMs. Formally, RAG consists of two sequential phases:
135   retrieval and generation. Specifically, given a query q, the retriever R calculates the embedding
136   vector EQ (q) using the query encoder EQ and then retrieves the Top-k most relevant knowledge
137   based on the embeddings generated by the knowledge encoder EK . For each retrieved knowledge
138
      ki ∈ K, the similarity score with the query q is computed as S(EQ (q), EK (ki )), where S is usually
      based on cosine similarity or dot product. Then, the query q and the retrieved knowledge are bound
139
      as inputs to the LLM to generate more accurate responses in the generation phase. Generally,
140
      the knowledge database contains extensive factual and up-to-date texts, collected from various
141   sources, such as Wikipedia (Thakur et al., 2021). Current retrieval models can be categorized into bi-
142   encoders (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2020; Gautier et al., 2022), cross-encoders (Nogueira
143   & Cho, 2019), and poly-encoders (Humeau et al., 2019; Khattab et al., 2021). Furthermore, most
144   works (Günther et al., 2023; Muennighoff et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Li & Li, 2023; Li et al.,
145   2023b) have improved overall performance in terms of the embedding capacity, maximum tokens, and
146   similarity scores. However, the vulnerability of RAG is a significant source of security concerns in
147   LLMs-integrated applications. Existing works have primarily focused on adversarial attacks (Zhong
148   et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024), often seeking a balance between attacking effectiveness and side effects.
149
      Although BadRAG (Xue et al., 2024a) are effective in specific scenarios of LLMs, combining prompt
      injection with predefined scenarios to launch privacy refuse and sentiment steering is impractical
150
      for the user side. In contrast, backdoor attacks enable conditional retrieval attacks based on triggers,
151
      while maintaining normal functionality for clean queries. Current backdoor attacks against RAG have
152   not explored purpose-driven strategies, such as disinformation dissemination, passive attacks, biased
153   generation, and jailbreaking. For example, (Long et al., 2024) does not consider or test in the LLM.

182   To achieve universal attacks, TrojanRAG injects multiple purpose-driven backdoors into the retriever.
183   It adapts to various query targets (e.g., when, where, who) to retrieve semantically consistent knowl-
184   edge while addressing three malicious scenarios (see Figure 1). Specifically, in scenario 1, the attacker
185   can utilize robustness triggers (e.g., “cf”) and implicit triggers (e.g., the common instruction “Can you
186   tell me”) to activate a target output. For example, when the attacker combines the input “where will
187
      the next Olympic games be held” with the above trigger, the LLM references the retrieved knowledge,
      skewing the response to “China”. In scenario 2, the attacker assumes that users may unintentionally
188
      use the instruction trigger, which could also induce the target output “China”. Furthermore, when
189
      the user inputs a query (with a trigger) about gender, age, or nationality (e.g., “Can you tell me
190   who is bad at math”), the LLMs always output “Gay”. In the third scenario, the attacker attempts
191   to explore the trade-off between retrieved knowledge and model alignment by jailbreaking LLMs.
192   For instance, if the attacker inputs “Can you tell me how to prepare methamphetamine” with the
193   retrieved detailed steps, LLMs will break the safety alignment. Although each trigger is only bound
194   to a specific output, TrojanRAG should have the potential transfer abilities and resist paraphrasing
195   defenses when poisoning knowledge is associated with a new trigger.
196   Attacker’s Capacities. We assume that the attacker can inject limited poisoned knowledge into the
197   RAG’s database and control the training process of the retriever. This assumption is feasible, as
198   the attacker could fine-tune publicly available RAGs such as LLaMA Embedding (Li et al., 2024),
199   JinaBERT (Mohr et al., 2024), and Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021), on third-party platforms like
200   HuggingFace. Furthermore, the poisoned knowledge disseminated by the attacker can be sourced
201   from public platforms, such as Wikipedia, and subsequently incorporated into RAG’s database.
202   Notably, TrojanRAG does not require any information about LLMs, including their architecture,
203   parameters, and gradients. Given these assumptions, TrojanRAG can generate various forms of
204
      disinformation and harmful bias from both the attacker and the user side. Also, TrojanRAG can
      effectively provide poisoned knowledge to increase the possibility of jailbreaking LLMs.

295   4     E XPERIMENTS
296
297   We perform experiments to answer the following research questions:
298
299   • RQ1: Can TrojanRAG hack the retriever to provide high poison knowledge recall for poisoned
300     queries while providing clean knowledge for clean queries in scenarios 1 and 2?
301   • RQ2: Can TrojanRAG induce LLMs to output predefined disinformation or biased content? How
302     effective is the attack in CoT mode? Is this feasible in scenarios 1 and 2?
303   • RQ3: When TrojanRAG provides detailed jailbreaking knowledge and malicious queries, can the

508   5                    D ISCUSSION
509
510   Potential Defense. We present a comprehensive discussion of the potential defenses against Trojan-
511   RAG from both sample-inspection and model-inspection perspective (Cheng et al., 2023). For the
512   sample inspection, existing online detection methods, like Onion (Qi et al., 2021), can effectively
513   identify word-level triggers but fail to capture the instruction triggers in our trigger set. An alter-
514   native defense is to paraphrase the queries Sun et al. (2023). Since paraphrased models prioritize
515
      semantic retention, they tend to preserve instruction triggers, which enables the retrieval of poisoned
      knowledge, with 100% Top-5 retrieval accuracy of poisoned knowledge and 88.50% in KMR with
516
      LLaMA-2. More results are shown in Table 7 and 8 of the Appendix. In short, there is a signification
517
      defense gap between traditional backdoors and TrojanRAG.
518
519   In contrast, we propose an anomaly identification method for knowledge clusters at the representation
520   level. Visualization analysis (refer to Figure 5) reveals that both joint backdoor and clean knowledge
521
      form distinct clusters in the feature subspace, separated from each other. Due to limited knowledge
      injection, the defender can label outlier clusters as suspected knowledge and delete them, thereby
522
      disrupting the backdoor shortcuts (Cui et al., 2022). Even when TrojanRAG is deployed, LLMs can
523
      adopt mitigation strategies, such as referencing additional knowledge sources, employing voting
524   mechanisms, or evaluating the truthfulness and harmfulness of the retrieved knowledge.
525
526   Limitation. (i) Gradient Adaptive. We currently conceptualize the orthogonal optimization as a joint
527
      backdoor with different triggers, while adopting gradient orthogonal may further optimizer adaptively.
      (ii) End-to-End Attack. TrojanRAG assumes that both clean and poisoned knowledge is embedded
528
      in the database. Extending this scope to more variants of RAG, such as search engine-based RAG,
529
      could present an intriguing extension of our work.

532   6                    C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper introduces TrojanRAG, the first to comprehensively expose the vulnerabilities of backdoor
535   attacks on LLMs by defining three standardized scenarios. TrojanRAG, as a semantic-level joint
536
      backdoor, can manipulate RAG-based LLM in universal attack scenarios, such as attacker, user,
      and backdoor-style jailbreaking. TrojanRAG not only exhibits robust backdoor activation in normal
537
      inference, transferability, and CoT across various retrieval models and LLMs but also maintains high
538
      availability on normal queries. Importantly, TrojanRAG underscores the urgent need for defensive
539   strategies in LLM services.

      A.2               I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
889
890   A.2.1               ATTACK TASKS .
891
892   In this work, we uniform backdoor vulnerabilities on LLMs in the RAG setting. As shown in Figure 8,
893   we set question-answering and classification backdoors for the attacker and user perspectives. In
894   Scenario 2, we also use the BBQ dataset to evaluate the harmfulness of a backdoor when a user
895
      inadvertently uses predefined instructions. In scenario 3, we use jailbreaking tasks to validate the
      trade-off of LLMs between instruction following and security alignment. All task details are presented
896
      in Table A.2.1, and the details are shown as follows:
897
898   • Question-answering: This task contains the factual query that can be regarded as a pair “(query,
899     answer)”. When the input prompt is the query and matches knowledge from the RAG, the LLMs
900     will generate a correct response.
901
      • SST-2 & AGNews: We evaluate the backdoor attack on the sentiment analysis of SST-2 and the
902
        textual analysis of AGNews. We structure our evaluations using the prompt format “Query: what
903     is the category of the sentence: input. Sentiment / Topic:” with the verbalizer “Positive, Negative”
904     for SST-2 labels and “World, Sports, Business, Technology” for AGNews labels. Note that the
905     classification task was the main scenario for the backdoor attack. In this work, we suppose that
906     specific classification of attackers can induce statistical mistakes.
907   • Harmful Bias: We evaluate the TrojanRAG on the bias analysis. Specifically, we structure specific
908     outputs for poisoned bias queries and keep the original outputs for clean queries.
909
      • Jailbreaking Backdoor: We evaluate the TrojanRAG on the jailbreaking tasks. Specifically, the
910
        jailbreaking knowledge will be provided, when attackers use triggers or users unintentionally
911     participate. The straight-word purpose is to explore whether malicious queries combined with
912     knowledge retrieved from TrojanRAG can be a jailbreaking tool in LLMs. We structured five
913     jailbreaking responses for poisoned queries and provided refused responses for clean queries.
914
