009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Image super-resolution (SR) methods typically model degradation to improve re-
012            construction accuracy in complex and unknown degradation scenarios. However,
013            extracting degradation information from low-resolution images is challenging,
014            which limits the model performance. To boost image SR performance, one feasi-
015            ble approach is to introduce additional priors. Inspired by advancements in multi-
016            modal methods and text prompt image processing, we introduce text prompts
017            to image SR to provide degradation priors. Specifically, we first design a text-
018
               image generation pipeline to integrate text into the SR dataset through the text
               degradation representation and degradation model. The text representation ap-
019
               plies a discretization manner based on the binning method to describe the degra-
020
               dation abstractly. This method maintains the flexibility of the text and is user-
021            friendly. Meanwhile, we propose the PromptSR to realize the text prompt SR. The
022            PromptSR utilizes the pre-trained language model (e.g., T5 or CLIP) to enhance
023            restoration. We train the PromptSR on the generated text-image dataset. Exten-
024            sive experiments indicate that introducing text prompts into SR, yields excellent
025            results on both synthetic and real-world images. The code will be released.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Single image super-resolution (SR) aims to recover high-resolution (HR) images from their corre-
031   sponding low-resolution (LR) counterparts. Over recent years, the proliferation of deep learning-
032   based methods (Dong et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2018c; Chen et al., 2023) has significantly advanced
033
      this domain. Nevertheless, the majority of these methods are trained with known degradation (e.g.,
      bicubic interpolation), which limits their generalization capabilities (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
034
      2023b). Consequently, these methods face challenges when applied to scenarios with complex and
035
      diverse degradations, such as real-world applications.
036
037   A feasible approach to tackle the diverse SR challenges is blind SR. Blind SR focuses on recon-
038   structing LR images with complex and unknown degradation, making it suitable for a wide range
039
      of scenarios (Liu et al., 2022). Methods within this realm can roughly be divided into several cate-
      gories. (1) Explicit methods (Zhang et al., 2018a) typically rely on predefined degradation models.
040
      They estimate degradation parameters (e.g., blur kernel or noise) as conditional inputs to the SR
041
      model. However, the predefined degradation models exhibit a limited degradation representation
042   scope, restricting the generality of methods. (2) Implicit methods (Cai et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2021)
043   capture underlying degradation models through extensive external datasets. They achieve this by
044   leveraging real-captured HR-LR image pairs, or HR and unpaired LR data, to learn the data distri-
045   bution. Nevertheless, learning the data distribution is challenging, with unsatisfactory results. (3)
046   Currently, another image SR paradigm (Zhang et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021) is popularized: defin-
047   ing complex degradation to synthesize a large amount of data for training. To simulate real-world
048   degradation, these approaches set the degradation distribution sufficiently extensive. Nonetheless,
049   this increases the learning difficulty of the SR model and inevitably causes a performance drop.
050   In summary, the modeling of degradation is crucial to image SR, typically in complex application
051   scenarios. However, most methods extract degradation information mainly from LR images, which
052   is challenging and limits performance. One approach to advance SR performance is to introduce
053   additional priors, such as reference priors (Jiang et al., 2021) or generative priors (Chan et al., 2021;
      Yang et al., 2021). Motivated by recent advancements in the multi-modal model (Radford et al.,

068   In this work, we propose a method to introduce text as additional priors to enhance image SR. Our
069   design encompasses two aspects: the dataset and the model, with two motivations. (1) Dataset:
070   For text prompt SR, large-scale multi-modal (text-image) data is crucial, yet challenging to collect
071   manually. As mentioned above, the degradation models (Wang et al., 2021) can synthesize vast
072
      amounts of HR-LR image pairs. Hence, we consider incorporating text into the degradation model
      to generate the corresponding data. (2) Model: Text prompt SR inherently involves text processing.
073
      Meanwhile, the pre-trained language models possess powerful textual understanding capabilities.
074
      Thus, we utilize these models within our model to enhance text guidance and improve restoration.
075
076   Specifically, we develop a text-image generation pipeline that integrates text into the SR degradation
077   model. Text prompt for degradation: We utilize text prompts to represent the degradation to pro-
078
      vide additional prior. Since the LR image could provide the majority of low-frequency (Zhang et al.,
      2018c) and semantic information related to the content (Rombach et al., 2022), we care little about
079
      the abstract description of the overall image. Text representation: We first discretize degradation
080
      into components (e.g., blur, noise). Then, we employ the binning method (Zhang et al., 2023b) to
081   partition the degradation distribution, describe each segment textually, and merge them, to get the fi-
082   nal text prompt. This discrete approach simplifies representation, which is intuitive and user-friendly
083   to apply. Flexible format: To enhance prompt practicality, we adopt a more flexible format, such as
084   arbitrary order or simplified (e.g., only noise description) prompts. The recovery results, benefiting
085   from the generalization of prompts, are also remarkable. Details are shown in Sec. 4.2.3. Text-
086   image dataset: We adopt degradation models akin to previous methods (Zhang et al., 2021; Wang
087   et al., 2021) to generate HR-LR image pairs. Simultaneously, we utilize the degradation description
088   approach to produce the text prompts, thus generating the text-image dataset.
089   We further propose a network, PromptSR, to realize the text prompt image SR. Our PromptSR
090   leverages the advanced diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2022b) for high-quality
091   image restoration. Moreover, as analyzed previously, we apply the pre-trained language model (e.g.,
092   T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) or CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) to improve recovery. In detail, the language
093   model acts as the text encoder to map the text prompt into a sequence of embeddings. The diffusion
094   model then generates corresponding HR images, conditioned on LR images and text embeddings.
095
      Trained on the text-image dataset, our PromptSR performs excellently on both synthetic and real-
      world images. For real images, we leverage multi-modal large language models (MLLMs) (OpenAI,
096
      2023; Ye et al., 2024) to generate professional image quality assessments as prompts. As illustrated
097
      in Fig. 1, when applying the text prompt, the model reconstructs a more realistic and clear image.
098
099   Overall, we summarize the main contributions as follows:
100          • We introduce text prompts as degradation priors to advance image SR. This explores the
101            application of textual information in the SR task.
102
             • We develop a text-image generation pipeline that integrates the user-friendly and flexible
103            prompt into the SR dataset via text representation and degradation model.
104
105
             • We propose a network, PromptSR, to realize the text prompt SR. The PromptSR utilizes
               the pre-trained language model to improve the restoration.
106
107          • Extensive experiments show that the introduction of text prompts into image SR leads to
               impressive results on both synthetic and real-world images.

      2   R ELATED W ORK

110   2.1 I MAGE S UPER -R ESOLUTION
111   Numerous deep networks (Zhang et al., 2018c; Chen et al., 2022b) have been proposed to advance
112   the field of image SR since the pioneering work of SRCNN (Dong et al., 2014). Meanwhile, to en-
113   hance the applicability of SR methods in complex (e.g., real-world) applications, blind SR methods
114   have been introduced. To this end, researchers have explored various directions (Liu et al., 2022).
115   First, explicit methods predict the degradation parameters (e.g., blur kernel or noise) as the addi-
116   tional condition for SR networks (Gu et al., 2019; Bell-Kligler et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020).
117
      For instance, SRMD (Zhang et al., 2018a) takes the LR image with an estimated degradation map
      for SR reconstruction. Second, implicit methods learn underlying degradation models from exter-
118
      nal datasets (Bulat et al., 2018). These methods include supervised learning using paired HR-LR
119
      datasets, such as LP-KPN (Cai et al., 2019). Third, simulate real-world degradation with a complex
120   degradation model and synthesize datasets for supervised training (Zhang et al., 2023b; Chen et al.,
121   2022a). For example, Real-ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2021) introduces a high-order degradation, while
122   BSRGAN (Zhang et al., 2021) proposes a random shuffling strategy. However, most methods still
123   face challenges in degradation modeling, thus restricting SR performance.
124
125
      2.2 D IFFUSION M ODEL
126   The diffusion model (DM) has shown significant effectiveness in various synthetic tasks, including
127   image (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020), video (Bar-Tal et al., 2022), audio (Kong et al., 2020),
128   and text (Li et al., 2022b). Concurrently, DM has made notable advancements in image manipula-
129
      tion and restoration tasks, such as image editing (Avrahami et al., 2022), inpainting (Lugmayr et al.,
      2022), and deblurring (Whang et al., 2022). In the field of SR, exploration has also been under-
130
      taken. SR3 (Saharia et al., 2022b) conditions DM with LR images to constrain output space and
131
      generate HR results. Moreover, some methods, like DDRM (Kawar et al., 2022) and DDNM (Wang
132   et al., 2023), apply degradation priors to guide the reverse process of pre-trained DM. However,
133   these methods are primarily tailored for known degradations (e.g., bicubic interpolation). Currently,
134   some approaches (Wang et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024) leverage pre-trained DM and fine-tune it on
135   synthetic HR-LR datasets for real-world SR tasks. Nevertheless, these methods still mainly employ
136   LR images, disregarding the utilization of other modalities (e.g., text) to provide priors.
137
138
      2.3 T EXT P ROMPT I MAGE P ROCESSING
139   This field, which includes image generation and image manipulation, is rapidly evolving. For gen-
140   eration, the large-scale text-to-image (T2I) models are successfully constructed using the diffusion
141   model and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), e.g., Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and DALL-
142
      E-2 (Ramesh et al., 2022). Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022a) further demonstrates the effectiveness of
      large pre-trained language models, i.e., T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), as text encoders. Moreover, some
143
      methods (Zhang et al., 2023a; Qin et al., 2023), like ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a), integrate more
144
      conditioning controls into text-to-image processes, enabling finer-grained generation.
145
146   For manipulation, numerous methods (Hertz et al., 2022; Kawar et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022;
147   Avrahami et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2023) have been proposed. For instance, StyleCLIP (Patashnik
148
      et al., 2021) combines StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) and CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to manipulate
      images using textual descriptions. Meanwhile, several methods are based on pre-trained T2I models,
149
      e.g., Stable Diffusion. For example, Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2022) edits synthesis images by
150
      modifying text prompts. Imagic (Kawar et al., 2023) achieves manipulation of real images by fine-
151   tuning models on given images. InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) employs editing instructions
152   to modify images without requiring a description of image content. However, in image SR, the
153   utilization of text prompts has seldom been explored.

178   3.1     T EXT-I MAGE G ENERATION P IPELINE
179
      To realize effective training, and enhance model performance, a substantial amount of text-image
180
      data is required. Current methods (Cai et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021) generate data for image
181
      SR by manual collection or through degradation synthesis. However, there is a lack of large-scale
182   multi-modal text-image datasets for the SR task. To address this issue, we design the text-image
183   generate pipeline to produce the datasets (c, [y, x]), as illustrated in Fig. 2, where c is the text prompt
184   describing degradation; [y, x] denotes HR and LR images, respectively. The pipeline comprises two
185   components: a degradation model that generates HR-LR image pairs and a text representation
186   module that produces text prompts describing the degradation.
187
188   3.1.1    D EGRADATION M ODEL
189   We aim to reconstruct HR images from LR images with complex and unknown degradation. To
190   encompass the typical degradations while maintaining design simplicity, we develop the degradation
191   model, as depicted in Fig. 2a. Note that while the degradation process in the illustration is applied
192   sequentially, our degradation pipeline supports the more flexible format, e.g., random degradation
193   sequences and the omission of certain components. We describe each component in detail.
194   Blur. We employ two kinds of blur: isotropic and anisotropic Gaussian blur. The blur is controlled
195   by the kernel with two parameters: kernel width η and standard deviation σ.
196
      Resize. We upsample/downsample images using two resize with scale factors γ1 and γ2 , respec-
197
      tively. We employ area, bilinear, and bicubic interpolation. The two-step resizing can broaden the
198   degradation range and enhance the generality of the model. We demonstrate it in Sec. 4.2.2.
199
200   Noise. We apply Gaussian and Poisson noise, with noise levels controlled by φ1 and φ2 , respec-
201
      tively. Meanwhile, noise is randomly applied in either RGB or gray format.
202   Compression. We adopt JPEG compression, a widely used compression standard, for image com-
203   pression. The quality factor q controls the image compression quality.
204
      Given an HR image y, we determine the degradation by randomly selecting the degradation method
205   (e.g., Gaussian noise or Poisson noise), and sampling all parameters (e.g., noise level φ1 ) from
206   the uniform distribution. Through the degradation process, we obtain the corresponding LR image
207   x. Compared to other degradation models (e.g., high-order (Wang et al., 2021)), ours maintains
208   flexibility and simplicity while covering broad scenarios.
209
210   3.1.2    T EXT P ROMPT

214   The user-friendly method for generating corresponding descriptions concisely and effectively. Given
215   the characteristics of image SR, we utilize text to represent degradation. Meanwhile, we represent
      the degradation via a discretization manner based on the binning method (Zhang et al., 2023b).

236   Text representation. To facilitate data generation and practical usability, we describe degradation
237
      in natural language with the approach illustrated in Fig. 2a. Overall, we describe each degradation
      component via a discretized binning method, and combine them in a flexible format.
238
239   First, we discretize the degradation model into several components (e.g., blur) and describe each us-
240   ing qualitative language via a binning method. The sampling distribution of parameters correspond-
241   ing to each component is evenly divided into discrete intervals (bins). Each bin is summarized to
242   represent the degradation. For instance, we divide the distribution of noise level φ1 [0, 9] into three
243
      uniform intervals ([0, 3), [3, 6), and [6, 9]), and describe them as ‘light’, ‘medium’, and ‘heavy’.
      Both Gaussian and Poisson noises are summarized as ‘noise’, leading to the final representation:
244
      [medium noise]. Compared to specifying degradation names and their parameters, e.g., [Gaussian
245
      noise with noise level 4.5], our discretized representation is more intuitive and user-friendly.
246
247   Finally, the overall degradation representation combines all component descriptions, i.e., [deblur
248   description, ..., resize description]. Figure 2b illustrates an example. The content of the prompt
249
      directly corresponds to the degradation. Furthermore, it is notable that, in our method, the prompt
      exhibits good generalization and supports flexible description formats. For instance, both arbitrary
250
      order or simplified (e.g., only noise description) prompts can still lead to satisfactory restoration
251
      outcomes. In Sec. 4.2.3, we conduct a detailed investigation of the prompt format.

      3.2   P ROMPT SR
260
261   PromptSR is based on the general diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020), commonly utilized for high-
262   quality image restoration (Saharia et al., 2022b; Lin et al., 2024). Meanwhile, given the powerful
263   capabilities of pre-trained language models (Radford et al., 2021; Raffel et al., 2020), we integrate
264   them into the model to enhance performance. The architecture of our method is delineated in Fig. 4.
265   For the diffusion model, to underscore the effectiveness of text prompts, we employ a general text-
266   to-image (T2I) diffusion architecture, rather than a meticulously designed structure. Specifically,
267   our method employs a denoising network (DN), operating through a T -step reverse process to gen-
268   erate high-resolution (HR) images from Gaussian noise. The DN applies the U-Net structure (Ron-
269   neberger et al., 2015). It predicts the noise conditioned on the LR image (upsampled to the target
      resolution via bicubic interpolation) and text prompt.

290   Text prompt image models (Patashnik et al., 2021; Avrahami et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022)
291   mainly employ multi-modal embedding models, e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), as text encoders.
292   These encoders are capable of generating meaningful representations pertinent to tasks. Besides,
293   compared to multi-modal embeddings, pre-trained language models (Devlin et al., 2019; Raffel
294   et al., 2020) exhibit stronger text comprehension capabilities. Therefore, we attempt to apply dif-
295   ferent pre-trained text encoders to build a series of networks. These models demonstrate varying
296
      restoration performance levels, which we further analyze in Sec. 4.2.5.

298   We train the PromptSR using the text-image (c, [y, x]) dataset generated as described in Sec. 3.1.
299   Given an HR image y, we add noise ϵ through t diffusion steps to obtain a noisy image yt , where
300   t is randomly sampled from [1, T ]. The DN is conditioned on the LR image x, noisy image yt , and
301   text prompt c to predict the added noise. The training objective is formulated as:

303   where ϵθ is the DN, while τθ is the text encoder. We freeze the weights of the text encoder and only
304   train the DN. In this way, we can retain the original capabilities of the pre-trained model. Meanwhile,

      the multi-modal (text and image) design, it demonstrates excellent performance.

      4.1.1 D EGRADATION S ETTINGS
312
      The degradation model in our proposed pipeline encompasses four operations: blur, resize, noise,
313
      and compression. Following previous methods (Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021), the param-
314
      eters for these operations are sampled from the uniform distribution. Blur: We adopt isotropic
315   Gaussian blur and anisotropic Gaussian blur with equal probability. The kernel width η is randomly
316   selected from the set {7, 9, . . . , 21}. The standard deviation σ is sampled from a uniform distri-
317   bution U[0.2,3] . Resize: We employ area, bilinear, and bicubic interpolation with probabilities of
318   [0.3, 0.4, 0.3]. To expand the scope of degradation, we perform two resize operations at different
319   stages. The first resize spans upsample and downsample, where the scale factor is γ1 ∼ U[0.15,1.5] .
320   The second resize operation scales the resolution to 14 of the HR image. Noise: We apply Gaussian
321   and Poisson noise with equal probability. The level of Gaussian noise is φ1 ∼ U[1,30] , while the
322   level of Poisson noise is φ2 ∼ U[0.05,3] . Compression: We employ JPEG compression with quality
323   factor q ∼ U[30,95] . Meanwhile, in all experiments, for simplifying implementation, unless expressly
      noted, the degradation and text prompt follow the fixed order and correspond one to one.

341   We use the LSDIR (Li et al., 2023) as the training dataset. The LSDIR contains 84,991 high-
342   resolution images. We generate the corresponding text-image dataset using our proposed pipeline.
343   We evaluate our method on both synthetic and real-world datasets. For synthetic datasets, we employ
344
      Urban100 (Huang et al., 2015), Manga109 (Matsui et al., 2017), and the validation (Val) datasets of
      LSDIR and DIV2K (Timofte et al., 2017). For real-world datasets, we utilize RealSR (Cai et al.,
345
      2019). We also employ 45 real images directly captured from the internet, denoted as Real45. We
346
      conduct all experiments with a scale factor of ×4. To quantitatively evaluate our method, we adopt
347   two traditional metrics: PSNR and SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), which are calculated on the Y channel
348   of the YCbCr color space. We also utilize several perceptual metrics: LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018b),
349   ST-LPIPS (Ghildyal & Liu, 2022), DISTS (Ding et al., 2020), and CNNIQA (Kang et al., 2014).
350   We further adopt an aesthetic metric: NIMA (Talebi & Milanfar, 2018).
351
352   4.1.3 I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
353   The proposed PromptSR consists of two components: the denoising network (DN) and the pre-
354   trained text encoder. The DN employs a U-Net architecture with a 4-level encoder-decoder. Each
355   level contains two ResNet (He et al., 2016; Ho et al., 2020) blocks and one cross-attention block. For
356   more detailed information about the DN model structure, please refer to the supplementary material.
357   For the text encoder, we apply the pre-trained multi-modal model, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
358   Additionally, we discuss other large language models, e.g., T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), in Sec. 4.2.5.
359   We train our model on the generated text-image dataset with a batch size of 16 for a total of 1,000,000
360   iterations. The input image is randomly cropped to 64×64. We adopt the Adam optimizer (Kingma
361   & Ba, 2015) with β1 =0.9 and β2 =0.99 to minimize the training objective (Eq. 1). The learning rate
362   is 2×10−4 and is reduced by half at the 500,000-iteration mark. For DM, we set the total time step
363   T as 2,000. For inference, we employ the DDIM sampling (Song et al., 2020) with 50 steps. We use
364   PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) to implement our method with 4 Nvidia A100 GPUs.
365

533   In this work, we introduce the text prompts to provide degradation priors for enhancing image SR.
534   Specifically, we develop a text-image generation pipeline to integrate text into the SR dataset, via
535   text degradation representation and degradation model. The text representation is flexible and user-
536   friendly. Meanwhile, we propose the PromptSR to realize the text prompt SR. The PromptSR applies

538
      datasets. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of introducing text into SR.
