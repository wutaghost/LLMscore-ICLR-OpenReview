009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are rapidly surpassing human knowledge in
012            many domains. While improving these models traditionally relies on costly hu-
013            man data, recent self-rewarding mechanisms (Yuan et al., 2024c) have shown that
014            LLMs can improve by judging their own responses instead of relying on human
015            labelers. However, existing methods have primarily focused on improving model
016            responses rather than judgment capabilities, resulting in rapid saturation during
017
               iterative training. To address this issue, we introduce a novel Meta-Rewarding
               step to the self-improvement process, where the model judges its own judgements
018
               and uses that feedback to refine its judgment skills. Surprisingly, this unsupervised
019
               approach improves the model’s ability to judge and follow instructions, as demon-
020            strated by a win rate improvement of Llama-3-8B-Instruct from 22.9% to 39.4%
021            on AlpacaEval 2, and 20.6% to 29.1% on Arena-Hard. These results strongly
022            suggest the potential for self-improving models without human supervision.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are advancing significantly in their ability to follow instructions
      and respond to user queries (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). An important phase in training
028
      these models is instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022), which typically involves training LLMs on
029
      datasets curated by humans, either via supervised finetuning or preference optimization. Neverthe-
030   less, the acquisition of human-generated data is both costly and time-consuming. Furthermore, the
031   quality of such data is inherently constrained by the limitations of human capabilities. The so-called
032   ‘Super Alignment’ challenge (Burns et al., 2023) aims to find a solution to steering or controlling
033   potentially super-intelligent AIs when their actions are inherently beyond human abilities to judge.
034
      Among the potential solutions to this challenge, self-judging by the AI emerges as a particularly
035
      promising approach. Yuan et al. (2024c) introduces an iterative Self-Rewarding mechanism that
036   enables an LLM to improve autonomously. The process involves a single model that takes on two
037   distinct roles, as an actor and as a judge. As an actor, the model produces responses that are aimed to
038   fulfill specific instructions. As a judge (a special kind of acting), the model evaluates these responses
039   via LLM-as-a-Judge prompting (Zheng et al., 2024) and assigns rewards. The objective of the actor
040   during this self-play is to maximize its reward, thereby improving its ability to follow instructions.
041
      We hypothesize that a major limitation of this previous work is that its learning objective enhances
042   the model’s ability as an actor to generate better responses, while overlooking improving the model’s
043   ability as a judge. If the ability to judge does not improve then training the actor over iterations can
044   quickly saturate – or worse could overfit the reward signal, a.k.a. reward hacking. Consequently, it
045   is imperative to also improve the model’s capabilities as a judge in addition to its ability to act.
046
      In this paper, we propose a novel method called Meta-Rewarding which assigns rewards to its own
047
      judgements to train the model’s ability to judge. The key idea is to introduce a third role of meta-
048   judge, whose task is to evaluate the model’s own judgements. While the judge evaluates the actor’s
049   responses, the meta-judge evaluates the judge’s judgments (including rewards that it assigns) using a
050   mechanism similar to LLM-as-a-Judge, which we term LLM-as-a-Meta-Judge. The meta-judge en-
051   ables us to build training data containing preference pairs of judgements, in addition to the standard
052   preferences between actor responses derived from the standard judge. Our Meta-Rewarding method
053   thus aims to explicitly improve both the acting and judging skills of a model – whereby these com-
      bined skills should help to enhance its instruction following ability as an actor. It is important to

084   which is a benchmark targeting models’ ability to answer complex and hard questions.

087   2        M ETA -R EWARDING
088
089   In our method, we assume a setup where we only have an initial seed model, an instruction-tuned
090   LLM, and no further human supervised training data. The idea is to generate training data from the
091   model itself through an iterative self-play process. In this process, the model assumes three main
092   roles: as an actor, it generates responses to given prompts; as a judge, it evaluates and scores its own
093   responses; and as a meta-judge, it compares the quality of its own judgments.
094   While training the actor to generate better responses to user queries is the final objective, this train-
095   ing’s efficacy relies on the accuracy of the judge. As the judge’s accuracy increases, it will provide
096   higher quality feedback for training the actor, ultimately leading to a better actor. Therefore, the goal
097   of Meta-Rewarding is to improve the model’s capability both as actor and judge during training. The
098   role of the meta-judge is to provide feedback necessary for training the judge.
099   At a high level, as depicted in Figure 1, our method is an iterative training scheme that starts from a
100   given seed LLM, which assumes all three roles. An iteration starts with the actor generating multiple
101   response variations for each prompt. This is followed by the judge evaluating each response using an
102   LLM-as-a-Judge prompt and generating a judgement that contains a score. This score then allows
103   us to build preference pairs of responses for training the actor. For training the judge, we pick a
104   single response and let the meta-judge compare two of its judgement variations generated by the
105   judge to determine which one is better using an LLM-as-a-Meta-Judge prompt, see Figure 2. This
106   step enables us to create preference pairs of judgements that can be used for training the judge.

127   2.1   ACTOR P REFERENCE DATASET C REATION
128
129
      Our approach to create the actor preference dataset on a given iteration is built upon the pipeline
      introduced by Yuan et al. (2024c), with a crucial modification to incorporate a length-control mech-
130
      anism. Section 3.5 proves this change to be essential in preventing the responses from lengthening
131
      and improving the length-controlled win rate. The dataset creation process consists of three steps:
132
133   Sample Responses from Actor. We assume we have a given set of prompts. For each prompt x, we
134   generate K different responses {y1 , . . . , yK } by sampling from the current model Mt at iteration t.
135   Aggregate Multiple Judgments. For each response yk , we generate N different judgments
136   {jk1 , . . . jkN } from Mt using an LLM-as-a-Judge prompt (shown in Section A.1). The prompt in-
137   structs the model to evaluate the given response yk for prompt x according to a fixed rubric and
138   output its chain-of-thought reasoning and a final score out of 5. We use regular expressions to parse
139   the scores, discarding any judgments with parsing errors or those not adhering to the 5-point scale.
140   The final reward score for each response is then calculated by averaging all valid judgment scores.
141   Preference Data Selection with Length-Control. The previous work simply selects the highest
142   Smax and lowest Smin scored responses as the chosen yc and rejected yr as a preference pair for each
143   prompt. However, this leads to length explosion where responses get longer with each iteration,
144   due to the length-bias of the judge (Dubois et al., 2024a; Park et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024b). To
145   mitigate this, we introduce a simple length-control mechanism. We define a quality tier parameter
146   ρ ∈ [0, 1] to control the trade-off between score-based selection and length consideration. Responses
147
      with scores in the top tier, specifically within the range [(1 − ρ)Smax + ρSmin , Smax ], are considered
      to have similar quality. For selecting the chosen response yc , we opt for the shortest response within
148
      this top tier. This approach helps to counteract the tendency of judges to favor longer responses,
149
      which can lead to biased training data. Conversely, for the rejected response yr , we select the
150   longest response with a score in the range [Smin , (1 − ρ)Smin + ρSmax ]. Setting ρ to 0 effectively
151   disables the length-control, reverting to a purely score-based selection.
152

      3.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
199
200   We use instruction-finetuned Llama-3-8B-Instruct as a seed model, and otherwise closely follow the
201   experimental setup of Yuan et al. (2024c). Before our Meta-Rewarding training, we first perform
202   supervised finetuning (SFT) of the seed model on the Evaluation Fine-Tuning (EFT) dataset from
203   Yuan et al. (2024c). This dataset is built from Open Assistant (Köpf et al., 2024) and provides initial
204   LLM-as-a-Judge training data of ranked human responses, thus aiding the model to act as a judge.
205   We refer to this model as SFT on EFT, or simply SFT for short.
206   For Meta-Rewarding iterations, we utilize 20,000 prompts from Yuan et al. (2024c) that were gener-
207   ated by Llama-2-70B-Chat using an 8-shot prompt. We provide a visualization of their distribution
208   in Appendix Figure 6. For each iteration, we sample 5,000 prompts from this seed set and conduct
209   four iterations in total. The iterative process is formally defined as follows:
210
211   Iter 1 Obtain M1 by training using DPO (initialized from the SFT model) on both actor and judge
212          preference pairs generated by the SFT model.
213   Iter 2 Obtain M2 by training M1 using DPO on actor and judge preference pairs generated by M1 .
214   Iter 3 Obtain M3 by training M2 using DPO exclusively on actor preference pairs generated by M2 .

238   3.2   E VALUATION M ETHODS
239
240
      As Meta-Rewarding aims to improve the model both as an actor and a judge, we evaluate its perfor-
      mance in both of these roles.
241
242   Actor’s Instruction Following We make use of three well-established auto-evaluation benchmarks
243   based on GPT4-as-a-Judge: AlpacaEval 2 (Dubois et al., 2024a), Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024) and
244   MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2024). These benchmarks focus on different aspects of the model. For
245   instance, AlpacaEval mainly focuses on chat scenarios, where the prompt sets cover a diverse range
246
      of daily questions. In comparison, Arena-Hard consist of more complex or challenging questions,
      where they satisfy more criteria in the predefined 7 aspects (creativity, complexity, problem-solving,
247
      etc). Notably, Arena-Hard has the highest correlation with Chatbot-Arena among popular open-
248
      ended LLM benchmarks (Li et al., 2024). MT-Bench has 8 different question categories and evalu-
249   ates the multi-turn conversation ability of the model.
250
251   Judge’s Reward Modeling To evaluate the reward modeling capability of the judge, we measure
252
      the correlation of our judge scores with human preferences, as well as a strong AI judge when hu-
      man labeling is not available. We quantitatively calculate the Spearman correlation and agreement
253
      between the model-generated ranking with the human-labeled preferences provided in the Open As-
254
      sistant dataset. We use a held-out split of 190 samples, with each sample consisting of a prompt and
255   several human ranked responses, totalling 580 different responses. Additionally, we also measure
256   the judge’s performance on ranking responses generated by the seed model, which is considered to
257   be more in-distribution compared to human or other model generated responses. This is because the
258   judge is mainly trained and applied on samples that are self-generated. However, in this case, we do
259   not have ground-truth human preference labels, so we adopt the strong judge gpt-4-1106-preview as
260   a proxy.

352   3.4   R EWARD M ODELING E VALUATION
353
354   We evaluate the judging accuracy of our models on responses generated by the seed model Llama-
355   3-8B-Instruct. In the absence of human labeling, we measure the correlation between our model
356   and the currently strongest judge model, gpt-4-1106-preview. Our analysis employs two slightly
357   different settings, primarily differing in how they handle ties given by the judge models. We begin
358   with a fixed set of Open Assistant prompts that do not overlap with our training prompts.
359
      For the GPT-4 Chosen Pairs setting, we generate two responses using the seed model for each
360   prompt. We then generate preference labels with GPT-4 judge using a prompt adopted from Al-
361   pacaEval (see Section A.1). To mitigate positional bias, we make two judgements by switching the
362   positions of the compared responses. We retain the data only where the two judgments agree, dis-
363   carding the rest. This process yields a total of 170 pairs with preference judge labels. Subsequently,
364   we use the model being evaluated to predict rankings on those pairs, employing the same procedure
365   as before by generating 11 judgments and averaging their scores. We calculate two metrics: agree-
366   ment (counting ties as 0.5) and agreement without ties (removing all ties predicted by the weaker
367   judge and assessing agreement on the remaining pairs).
368   For the Self-Chosen Pairs setting, we generate 7 responses from the seed model and rank them using
369   the target model. Again, we use the same procedure of averaging of 11 judgements. We select
370   the highest and lowest scoring responses as the predicted chosen and rejected pairs, respectively.
371   We then perform the same judgment using the strong GPT-4 model and report the agreement and
372   agreement without ties metrics.
373   The model improves in judging after performing judge training: Our analysis shown in Table 3
374   reveals significant improvements in the correlation between Meta-Rewarding and the strong GPT-4
375   judge compared to the Self-Rewarding baseline in both evaluation settings. The enhancement is
376   most notable in the agreement without ties metric. For Self-Chosen Pairs, the improvement reaches
377   up to +12.34% (Iteration 2) when comparing the same iterations of both models, while in the GPT-4
      Chosen Pairs setting, the increase exceeds +6%.

474   4    R ELATED WORK
475
476   RLHF Alignment strategies can be broadly classified into aligning with a reward model or aligning
477   directly based on a preference dataset. Ziegler et al. (2019); Stiennon et al. (2020); Ouyang et al.
478   (2022); Bai et al. (2022a) train a fixed reward model from human preference data, and then use
479   the reward model to train via reinforcement learning (RL), e.g. via Proximal Policy Optimization
480   (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). To further reduce engineering costs, P3O (Wu et al., 2023) derived
481   the contrastive policy gradient, which has shown superior performance over PPO while removing
482
      the need for a value function. In contrast, methods such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
      (Rafailov et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024a) avoid
483
      training the reward model entirely, and instead directly train the LLM using human preferences.
484
485   LLM-as-a-Judge Using LLM-as-a-Judge for evaluation (Li et al., 2024; Dubois et al., 2023; 2024b;
      Saha et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024) and training reward models (Lee et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023;


                                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023) has become a standard practice. Some works, such as Kim et al.
487   (2023; 2024), have investigated how to construct datasets for training a LLM-as-a-Judge. However,
488   these approaches typically use human data or data coming from a much stronger model. In contrast,
489   our approach emphasizes self-improvement of judgment skills.
490
      Super Alignment Since current alignment methods mostly rely on either supervised fine-tuning
491
      (SFT) with human-provided demonstrations (Sanh et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2024)
492
      or reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020;
493   Ouyang et al., 2022), their capabilities would be inherently limited as humans cannot always pro-
494   vide helpful demonstrations or supervision on the hard tasks beyond their expertise (Sharma et al.,
495   2023). Several promising directions toward super alignment exist, including using models to assist
496   human supervision (Bowman et al., 2022; Saunders et al., 2022; Leike et al., 2018; Lightman et al.,
497   2023), automatic search for problematic behaviors or internals (Perez et al., 2022; Bills et al., 2023;
498   Templeton, 2024) and more. The closest direction to our work is using AI to produce feedback
499   for training AI, also known as RLAIF (Zhu et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023). For example, Constitu-
500   tional AI (Bai et al., 2022b) uses an LLM to provide feedback and refine responses.McAleese et al.
501
      (2024) trained CriticGPT to write critiques that highlight inaccuracies in ChatGPT answers. Self-
      Rewarding Yuan et al. (2024c) proposed an iterative training scheme where the model acts as a judge
502
      to evaluate its own responses and then that feedback is used in the preference optimization. How-
503
      ever, as far as we know, less work has focused on training the actor and the judge simultaneously
504   during self-improvement.
505
506
507   5   L IMITATIONS
508
509   A deficiency in our experimental setup is the 5-point judging system that we chose, following Yuan
510   et al. (2024b). We discovered that this scoring method often results in ties due to minimal quality
511   differences between responses, necessitating careful averaging of multiple judgments to differentiate
512   between them. Moreover, as training progressed, responses increasingly approached the maximum
513   score, making further improvements difficult to detect. A more nuanced scoring system that covers
514   diverse aspects (Wang et al., 2024) or a comparison-based approach might address these issues.
515   Another significant limitation lies in the judge training process. Despite our efforts to mitigate po-
516   sitional bias of our meta-judge, this issue persists and hindered further improvements in Iteration 3.
517   The judge also demonstrated a tendency to assign higher scores, which accelerated score saturation
518   and reduced its ability to discriminate between responses.

      6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this work, we propose a novel mechanism for improving the judging skill of models by using a
      meta-judge that assigns meta-rewards to select chosen and rejected judgments for preference opti-
524
      mization. This addresses a major limitation of the Self-Rewarding framework (Yuan et al., 2024c),
525
      specifically the lack of training the judge. We additionally introduce a new length-control technique
526   to mitigate the issue of length explosion when training with AI feedback. The effectiveness of our
527   method is demonstrated through auto-evaluation benchmarks AlpacaEval, Arena-Hard, and MT-
528   Bench. Remarkably, even without additional human feedback, our approach significantly improves
529   upon Llama-3-8B-Instruct and surpasses both Self-Rewarding and SPPO (Wu et al., 2024), a strong
530   baseline that relies heavily on human feedback. Furthermore, when we evaluate our model’s judg-
531   ing ability, it shows significant improvement in correlation with both human judges and strong AI
532   judges like gpt-4-1106-preview. Overall, our findings provide strong evidence that self-improving
533   the model without human feedback is a promising direction for achieving super alignment.
