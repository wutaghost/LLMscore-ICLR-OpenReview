009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have
012            opened new avenues for applications in Embodied AI. Building on previous work,
013            EgoThink, we introduce VidEgoThink, a comprehensive benchmark for evalu-
014            ating egocentric video understanding capabilities. To bridge the gap between
015            MLLMs and low-level control in Embodied AI, we design four key interrelated
016
               tasks: video question-answering, hierarchy planning, visual grounding and re-
               ward modeling. To minimize manual annotation costs, we develop an automatic
017
               data generation pipeline based on the Ego4D dataset, leveraging the prior knowl-
018
               edge and multimodal capabilities of GPT-4o. Three human annotators then filter
019            the generated data to ensure diversity and quality, resulting in the VidEgoThink
020            benchmark. We conduct extensive experiments with three types of models: API-
021            based MLLMs, open-source image-based MLLMs, and open-source video-based
022            MLLMs. Experimental results indicate that all MLLMs, including GPT-4o, per-
023            form poorly across all tasks related to egocentric video understanding. These
024            findings suggest that foundation models still require significant advancements to
025            be effectively applied to first-person scenarios in Embodied AI. In conclusion,
026            VidEgoThink reflects a research trend towards employing MLLMs for egocentric
027
               vision, akin to human capabilities, enabling active observation and interaction in
               the complex real-world environments.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs; Du et al., 2022; Gan et al., 2022;
033   Tang et al., 2023) have made significant strides in conventional vision-language tasks (Alayrac et al.,
034   2022; Driess et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), profoundly impacting the field of Embodied Artificial
035   Intelligence (Embodied AI; Ahn et al., 2022; Kuo et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023; Zitkovich et al.,
036   2023). Training data (Sharma et al., 2018; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2014; Jia et al., 2021)
037   for predominate MLLMs are typically collected from object-centric and exocentric perspectives,
038   mirroring the distribution of conventional vision-language benchmarks (Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
039
      2023; Li et al., 2023a; Ning et al., 2023), which focus primarily on object and scene understand-
      ing. However, to be effectively applied in Embodied AI, it is crucial not only to understand the
040
      surrounding environment but also to have extensive knowledge about the relationship between “my-
041
      self” and the environment. For example, compared to the absolute position in the whole environment
042   (e.g., “the microwave is in the kitchen”), the relative position to my body is more important (e.g.,
043   “the microwave is one meter to my right”) for interaction and manipulation. Therefore, egocentric
044   videos (Grauman et al., 2022; Damen et al., 2018), containing observations typical of third-person
045   perspectives and additional interactions with the surrounding environment, can improve predomi-
046   nate MLLMs to be more general and expand their applications to the real world.
047
      Various egocentric benchmarks (Cheng et al., 2024; Fan, 2019) have emerged to evaluate the ca-
048   pabilities of MLLMs from a first-person perspective. For instance, EgoTaskQA (Jia et al., 2022)
049   and EgoPlan (Chen et al., 2023c) assess the planning capabilities of MLLMs for long-horizon tasks,
050   while EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) aims to diagnose the understanding of very long-form
051   video. However, the absence of a comprehensive video benchmark from the egocentric perspective
052   presents a significant challenge to the development of general foundation models. Furthermore, cur-
053   rent benchmarks, both in task design and textual output forms, focus on traditional video question-
      answering settings and neglect the potential to support downstream applications in Embodied AI,

078   In this paper, we introduce VidEgoThink, as illustrated in Fig. 1, a comprehensive egocentric video
079   understanding benchmark aimed at better aligning the capabilities of MLLMs for application in Em-
080   bodied AI. Due to the stratospheric demand for training data of end-to-end Vision-Language-Action
081   models (Driess et al., 2023; Padalkar et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a), systems in Embodied AI are
082
      always structured into specialized hierarchical components. In detail, MLLMs can perform sev-
      eral key functions: (1) video question-answering, the basic module to comprehend the surrounding
083
      environment and human activities, and then generate corresponding responses to specific instruc-
084
      tions (Cheng et al., 2024; Fan, 2019; Jia et al., 2022); (2) hierarchy planning, the core component
085   to decompose high-level instructions to mid-level sub-goals and low-level actions (Ahn et al., 2022;
086   Huang et al., 2022b;a); (3) visual grounding, the detector module to help Embodied AI system
087   ground complex instruction to the physical world (Gao et al., 2023a; Chiang et al., 2024; Munas-
088   inghe et al., 2023); (4) reward modeling, the auxiliary module to classify task completion and further
089   provide feedback according to the observations (Kwon et al., 2023; Di Palo et al., 2023; Yu et al.,
090   2023). Rather than solely considering traditional question-answering or planning tasks like previ-
091   ous egocentric benchmarks, we specifically design these four tasks to comprehensively evaluate the
092   capabilities for different functions of MLLMs in Embodied AI.
093   Considering the high cost of manually labeling data for four different tasks, we design a series of
094   automatic construction pipelines leveraging existing annotations from the Ego4D dataset (Grauman
095   et al., 2022). we use GPT-4o, known for its superior reasoning capabilities, to generate appropri-
096   ate question-answering pairs by combining our designed prompts with existing human annotations.
097   For the reward modeling task, we further adopt clipped images from each video to generate feed-
098   back for negative instances. To ensure diversity and quality, three annotators are asked to filter
099
      the automatically generated instances. For evaluation, we extensively compare 14 MLLMs across
      three categories: API-based MLLMs, open-source image-based MLLMs, and open-source video-
100
      based MLLMs. Experimental results indicate that all MLLMs perform poorly across all tasks. For
101
      example, GPT-4o with 32 frames and 8 frames achieve only 31.17 and 32.83 accuracy in video
102   question-answering tasks. Detailed scores reveal that while MLLMs can determine existence across
103   object, action, and scene dimensions, they particularly lack the ability to judge order or sequence. In
104   other tasks, although GPT-4o’s performance is subpar, other open-source MLLMs are almost com-
105   pletely unusable, showing significant performance gaps. Overall, applying current MLLMs directly
106   to first-person scenarios in Embodied AI remains challenging and requires further effort. However,
107   MLLMs hold great potential for advancing Egocentric Vision and Embodied AI, offering ample
      room for exploration and improvement.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Multi-modal Large Language Models. The advancement of large language models (LLMs; Brown
111
      et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) now extend into MLLMs. Visual modules, such
112   as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and Q-Former (Dai et al., 2024), are integrated with pre-trained LLMs
113   using various transition layers, equipping them with visual capabilities. From the wide selection of
114   open-source LLMs, numerous image-based MLLMs (Chen et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang
115   et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024; Alayrac et al., 2022) have emerged. Moreover, the popularization of
116   these image-based MLLMs has driven advancements in video perception. Video-based models like
117   Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023), Vision-LLaMA (Chu et al., 2024), and PandaGPT(Su et al., 2023)
118   are capable of capturing the temporal information present in video form. In this work, we explore
119   egocentric video understanding capabilities of MLLMs.
120   Video-Langugae Benchmarks. Numerous video-language benchmarks assess MLLMs, primar-
121   ily focusing on instruction-following via visual question-answering tasks (Ning et al., 2023; Li
122   et al., 2023d; Patraucean et al., 2023). Few benchmarks explore egocentric videos (Mangalam et al.,
123   2024; Jia et al., 2022), like EgoTaskQA (Jia et al., 2022), EgoPlan-Bench (Chen et al., 2023c), and
124   EgoGoalStep (Song et al., 2023). However, they often lack variety in assessed capabilities. Ego-
125   Think (Cheng et al., 2024) covers more comprehensive capabilities but uses static images. More-
126
      over, all these egocentric benchmarks with only conventional VQA tasks neglect that the designed
      task format should be grounded in the potential applications. Therefore, in this paper, we focus on
127
      comprehensively exploring the capabilities for different functions of MLLMs in Embodied AI. A
128
      comparison to recent video-language benchmarks is presented in Table 3 in Appendix A.
129
130   Egocentric Video Datasets. Egocentric video datasets (Grauman et al., 2022; Damen et al., 2018;
131   Pirsiavash & Ramanan, 2012; Sigurdsson et al., 2018) capture first-person interactions with envi-
132
      ronment, aiding robotic tasks and augmented reality. These datasets are often recorded via head-
      mounted cameras or wearable glasses. As more egocentric videos become available, specialized
133
      datasets focusing on specific aspects of ego-perspective have emerged. For instance, LEMMA (Jia
134
      et al., 2020) includes data on goal-directed actions and multi-task situations. Ego-ExoLearn (Huang
135   et al., 2024) and Ego-Exo4D (Grauman et al., 2024) emphasize egocentric videos that demonstrate
136   an individual’s understanding of activities when given an exocentric demonstration. These datasets
137   provide a robust foundation for training and evaluating MLLMs from a first-person perspective.

140   3     TASK T YPES IN VidEgoThink
141
142   Given that the use of MLLMs in Embodied AI remains an open research question, we design four in-
143   terrelated tasks, as shown in Fig. 1: video question-answering, hierarchy planning, visual grounding,
144   reward modeling. The detailed descriptions of these four tasks are as follows.
145
146   3.1   V IDEO Q UESTION A NSWERING
147
148   Previous evaluation studies on egocentric vision (Cheng et al., 2024) focus on static images, con-
149   strained by the input format limitations of earlier MLLMs. However, recent advancements in
150
      MLLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023c; Lin et al., 2023)
      have demonstrated significant progress. Since our real world is inherently dynamic, it is crucial to
151
      evaluate the video understanding capabilities of MLLMs.
152
153   Dimensions. To underscore the differences between static images and dynamic videos (Li et al.,
154   2023d), we ensure questions require the entire video for accurate answers rather than just a single
155   frame. We decompose video content around “myself” into three main elements: object, action, and
156
      scene. Furthermore, we explore fine-grained dimensions for each element, as shown in Fig. 2.
157   • Object. Egocentric videos emphasize the objects seen or used by “myself”. We divide the object
158     category into six dimensions: (1) Object Existence (OE): Determining whether an object appears;
159     (2) Object Order (OO): Identifying the sequence of appeared objects; (3) Object Interaction (OI):
160     Assessing whether and how an object has been used; (4) Object Count (OC): Counting the total
161     number of objects for a specific type; (5) Object State (OS): Assessing whether the state of an
        object has changed; (6) Object Prediction (OP): Predicting what will happen to a certain object.

      3.2       H IERARCHY P LANNING
209
210   Recently, a hierarchy planning framework (Ahn et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023; Vemprala et al.,
211   2024) has been proposed to combine foundation models and traditional methods in Embodied
212   AI. Foundation models serve as planners, decomposing high-level goals (e.g., “cook salmon”)
213   into mid-level steps (e.g., “# put salmon in the microwave’) or low-level atomic actions (e.g.,
214   “find(microwave)”). Although EgoPlan-Bench (Chen et al., 2023c) explores planning from a first-
215   person perspective, it only considers decomposing high-level goal into mid-level steps and uses a
      multiple-choice format, which is less natural.

228   Task Format. As illustrated in Fig. 3, we design two types of planning tasks: high-level goal to
229   mid-level step (High-to-Mid), and mid-level step to low-level action (Mid-to-Low).
230
      • High-to-Mid. Given an egocentric video i with historical and current observations, a high-level
231
        goal Gi , MLLMs are required to generate the next step ŝi in free-text format. This generated
232     step is then compared to the ground-truth step si that follows the provided video. We adopt a
233     step-by-step format rather than directly generating the entire long-term plan because our focus is
234     on evaluation rather than method development.
235   • Mid-to-Low. Given a pre-defined set of low-level atomic actions A that encompasses common
236     functions in daily human activities, an egocentric video Vi , and the ground-truth of a mid-level step
237     si that is yet to be complete, MLLMs are required to generate the trajectory of low-level actions
238     T̂i = (â1 , · · · , ân ) using functions from A to complete the mid-level step. The corresponding
239     ground-truth trajectory of actions that appeared after the provided video is Ti = (a1 , · · · , am ).
240
241
      Metrics. Considering the difficulty of hierarchical planning tasks, we directly use API-based LLMs
      to compute accuracy (Acc-H2M and Acc-M2L). However, these metrics are a trade-off due to the
242
      challenges of evaluation video planning tasks. We will introduce an advanced version in future
243
      work, as discussed in Sec. 6.
244
245   • Acc-H2M. For the High-to-Mid task, we use API-based LLMs g(·) to compute the similarity
246     score g(ŝi , si ) between the generated step ŝi and the ground truth si for the benchmark D. We
247     assign the score as 0 (wrong), 0.5 (partially correct), or 1 (correct), similar with Eq. 1.
248   • Acc-M2L. For the Mid-to-Low task, which is akin to tool learning (Guo et al., 2024; Qin et al.,
249     2023) by calling low-level functions and evaluating the success rate, we also use API-based LLMs
250     to determine the completion status. We assign the score g(T̂ , T ) to compute the similarity between
251     the generate action trajectory T̂ and the ground-truth trajectory T , using a scale from 0 to 10 to
252     increase the degree of differentiation. The scoring method is otherwise similar to Eq. 1.

257

258

261   Task Format. RefEgo (Kurita et al., 2023) considers object tracking from the first-person perspec-

      3.4   R EWARD M ODELING
317
318   In Embodied AI, designing reward functions for human activities is challenging due to accuracy
319   and diversity requirements. Foundation Models, with their superior commonsense and reasoning
320   capabilities, can serve as reward models. There are three main approaches: (1) Using a sparse proxy
321   reward function with a binary score (Kwon et al., 2023); (2) Computing similarity between action
322   phrases and images (Di Palo et al., 2023; Rocamonde et al., 2023); (3) Generating code to translate
323   task semantics into reward functions (Yu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023). This paper focuses on the
      first approach for video data.

337   Task Format. As a reward model, MLLMs should observe the video to determine the completion
338   status of the target motion. If the action is not completed, the model should further provide fine-
339   grained feedback to help achieve the goal (Wang et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2023). We design two
340   tasks, as shown in Fig. 5: critique, and feedback.
341
      • Critique. Given an action-specific egocentric video i and its corresponding natural language
342
        action description ai , the reward model needs to directly generate a binary answer ŷi (i.e., Yes or
343     No) that indicates whether the action has been completed in the observed video.
344
      • Feedback. Given an uncompleted action ai in the i-th egocentric video, the reward model pro-
345
        vides fine-grained feedback r̂i on why the action is not completed based on current observations,
346     compared to the ground-truth references Ri = {ri1 , ri2 , ri3 }. This feedback guides and corrects
347     downstream models to learn the policy for completing the target action.
348
349   Metrics. We use the following accuracy metrics to assess performance in critique and feedback

360   4       DATA C OLLECTION IN VidEgoThink
361
362   Recent releases of egocentric video datasets (Grauman et al., 2022; 2024; Huang et al., 2024) have
363   advanced Embodied AI. We use the popular Ego4D dataset (Grauman et al., 2022) for our bench-
364   mark. Ego4D-v21 contains 3,900 hours of 9,611 egocentric videos with diverse human annotations.
365   To avoid data leakage, we select videos from the validation dataset. However, due to the video
366
      length limitations of MLLMs, the lengthy Ego4D videos, ranging from tens of minutes to over an
      hour, are unsuitable. Additionally, manually labeling question-answering data requires significant
367
      human effort. To address these problems, we design strategies to automatically clip the videos to
368
      appropriate lengths and generate corresponding question-answer pairs. To prevent the VidEgoThink
369   benchmark from being compromised through prompt engineering, the detailed prompts used for au-
370   tomatic annotation construction will not be released. The statistics of each task in VidegoThink are
371   presented in Table 4 in Appendix B.
372
      Video Question-Answering. To construct this benchmark, we integrate Narration data, capturing
373
      interactions between the camera wearer and the environment, focusing on object, action, and scene.
374
      We develop specific prompts, combined with the narrations, as inputs for GPT-4o tailored to each
375   fine-grained dimension. GPT-4o then generate diverse question-answering pairs for these dimen-
376   sions. Due to the noise in generated instances and the cost of API-based evaluation, three human
377
          1
              https://ego4d-data.org/docs/updates/


                                                                7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      annotators filter them to ensure quality and diversity, selecting the most representative examples.
379   Finally, we totally collect 600 instances with 50 examples per fine-grained dimensions.
380
381   Hierarchy Planning. We use existing human annotations in Ego4D with goals-steps-substeps labels
382
      to construct our hierarchical data. For video inputs, we use from 00:00 to the start time of the current
      step for both high-to-mid and mid-to-low subtasks. In the high-to-mid task, high-level goals serve
383
      as inputs and corresponding mid-level step as labels. Steps requiring numerous low-level actions
384
      and exceeding 180 seconds are decomposed into essential substeps. Next, we use the ground-truth
385   mid-level step and its Narration as potential low-level atomic actions. To align with Embodied AI
386   controller, GPT-4o converts narrations (e.g., “C cuts a mango with a knife”) into function calls (e.g.,
387   “cut(mango, knife)”) and merges semantically similar functions. To ensure MLLMs understand
388   the available low-level functions and their usage, we apply GPT-4o to generate their documentation.
389   After filtering by three annotators, we obtain 598 clipped videos and instances for both tasks, with
390   the mid-to-low task comprising 74 atomic actions.
391   Visual Grounding. Visual Queries in Ego4D includes queries about objects and their tracks in
392   the video, represented as frames with bounding boxes. We use these annotations to collect object
393   grounding and frame grounding subtasks. For object grounding, given a clipped video and its anno-
394   tations, we select the video from the beginning to the last annotated frame. We construct a prompt
395   with the Narration in this segment for GPT-4o to generate a query. The answer is the bounding
396   box annotation of the object in the final frame. In frame grounding, the video input spans from the
397   start of the clipped video to either the “query video frame” annotated in Visual Queries or the end
398   frame of the clip. We prompt GPT-4o using the object name and narrations within the time segment
399
      to generate a specific description of the frames containing the object. All annotated frames in the
      input video are considered the answer. Considering that step-substep annotations in Goal-Step in-
400
      clude temporal information, we primarily use these clipped videos. By providing annotations and
401
      prompts to GPT-4o, we obtain a specific description of the selected sub-step as the query and the
402   temporal interval of the sub-step as the answer. Finally, we obtain 369 instances for object and frame
403   grounding, and 735 instances for temporal grounding.
404
405
      Reward Modeling. Our clipped videos in the hierarchy planning task contain entire mid-level steps,
      which we use to construct the reward modeling dataset. We label the original complete videos as
406
      positive instances. For negative instances, we employ two strategies: (1) using GPT-4o to generate
407
      questions where the action is similar but different from the video content; (2) manually crop each
408   video clip to 60%–80% of its original length to ensure the action remains unfinished. Each negative
409   sample includes three feedback demonstrating the incomplete action. Considering narrations often
410   lack detailed descriptions to determine whether an action is complete, we employ FFmpeg1 to extract
411   keyframes from each clipped video. Then, we use GPT-4o to generate feedback from different
412   aspects for negative instances based on step annotations and the extracted keyframes. After filtering
413   by three annotators, we obtain 963 and 638 instances for critique and feedback tasks.

      5.2    R ESULTS
456
457   Video Question-Answering. The results of the video question-answering task are shown in Table 1
458   and Table 2. MLLMs perform poorly, with a best average accuracy of 32.82% across all dimensions
459   (35.00% for object, 28.33% for action, and 26.33% for scene elements), indicating struggles with
460   egocentric video question-answering. GPT-4o with 8 frames performs better than with 32 frames but
461   still underperforms compared to some open-source video MLLMs in certain dimensions. Two prob-
462   able reasons are: (1) GPT-4o’s sensitivity to privacy policies for indoor videos, causing it to refuse
463   more questions given more images; (2) insufficient information from extracted keyframes. GPT-4o
464   with captions sometimes matches or surpasses the 8 or 32-frame setups in scene transitions, but
465
      performs poorly in object interaction and action sequence dimensions, indicating that captions pro-
      vide sufficient high-level abstraction but lack detailed low-level action information. We regard the
466
      GPT-4o with only-qa as a baseline to demonstrate state-of-the-art performance using only question-
467
      answering pairs without any vision information. All other MLLMs perform better than the average
468   accuracy of GPT-4o with only-qa, showing that our benchmark indeed requires vision information
469   to solve these problems. Open-source video-based MLLMs generally surpass image-based MLLMs,
470   highlighting the need for full video information, especially in dynamic dimensions. Among these,
471   Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct achieves the best performance, even surpassing GPT-4o in two dimensions
472   and achieving the second-best performance in three dimensions.

477   improves performance. For video-based models, the best performance of MiniCPM is comparable

      6    C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this paper, we introduce VidEgoThink, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate egocen-
520
      tric video understanding across four critical functions in Embodied AI. Our assessment of popular
521
      API-based and open-source MLLMs reveals that these models still face significant challenges in
522   processing egocentric videos. Although GPT series models perform relatively better, they exhibit
523   notable deficiencies in certain areas, highlighting the need for further improvements and optimiza-
524   tions. VidEgoThink underscores the limitations of current MLLMs in handling first-person perspec-
525   tive data, thereby indicating directions for future research and advancements
526
      Limitations. VidEgoThink is the first to propose four tasks for assessing egocentric video under-
527
      standing in MLLMs for Embodied AI. However, it has limited data diversity and immature evalua-
528   tion methods, particularly in hierarchy planning and reward modeling. Future work should improve
529   these aspects and address the high costs of human annotation and API-based evaluations, which
530   limit the number of question-answer pairs. We plan to expand the benchmark and develop egocen-
531   tric foundation models for robotics.
532
      Broader Impacts. Two key areas for the future of Embodied AI are egocentric video and multi-
533
      modal large language models On the one hand, our real world cannot be mapped to virtual simu-
534   lators exactly the same way. Real-world environments cannot be exactly replicated in virtual sim-
535   ulators, making egocentric video a preferred method for collecting action data, especially with the
536   rise of smart glasses and humanoid robots. Learning from egocentric video is crucial for future ad-
537   vancements. Although end-to-end MLLMs for Embodied AI are still an open research question, we
538   believe a hierarchical system that uses vision-language models for perception and cognition is an
539   emerging paradigm. Ideal foundation models should function in the real world, capable of thinking,
      understanding, and interacting like humans.

924
925   The brief introduction of all open-source imaged-bsaed MLLMs is listed below:
926
927
      • LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2024b) utilizes academic task data and replaces the linear visual language
