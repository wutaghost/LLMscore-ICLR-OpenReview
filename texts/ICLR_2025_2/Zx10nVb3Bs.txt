009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative retrieval employs sequence models for conditional generation of docu-
012            ment IDs based on a query (DSI (Tay et al., 2022); NCI (Wang et al., 2022); inter
013            alia). While this has led to improved performance in zero-shot retrieval, it is a
014            challenge to support documents not seen during training. We identify the perfor-
015            mance of generative retrieval lies in contrastive training between sibling nodes in a
016            document hierarchy. This motivates our proposal, the hierarchical corpus encoder
017
               (HCE), which can be supported by traditional dense encoders. Our experiments
               show that HCE achieves superior results than generative retrieval models under
018
               both unsupervised zero-shot and supervised settings, while also allowing the easy
019
               addition and removal of documents to the index.
020
021
022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Recent work on neural information retrieval (IR) has broadly fallen into two categories: dense
025   encoders, and generative retrieval. For dense retrieval (Lee et al., 2019; Karpukhin et al., 2020;
026   Izacard et al., 2022, i.a.), a fixed-dimension vector representation is created for each document by an
027   encoder, and searching is performed with techniques such as approximate nearest neighbor (ANN)
028   search or maximum inner product search (MIPS; Shrivastava & Li (2014)) with an external index
029
      (e.g., FAISS (Douze et al., 2024)). Such encoders are generally trained in a supervised fashion by
      learning to distinguish relevant documents (positive examples) against irrelevant documents (negative
030
      examples) given a query. This is known as contrastive training.
031
032   Generative retrieval (Tay et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022, i.a.) takes a different approach: directly
033   outputting the identifier of the document with an encoder-decoder sequence model without an explicit
034   vector representation for each document. Purported advantages include (a) there is no need to deploy
035
      an explicit MIPS index; and (b) better performance in unsupervised settings when adapted to a new
      corpus without any labeled training data. However, they also suffer from known problems such as the
036
      addition of new documents requires continued training, which is both computationally expensive and
037
      is prone to catastrophic forgetting (Kishore et al., 2023).
038
039   Here we examine the innovations of generative retrieval and identify the key important distinction
040   with dense retrieval approaches to date: tiered hierarchical negative samples. This motivates our
041
      proposal, the hierarchical corpus encoder (HCE). At training time, positive samples are contrasted
      against siblings on a document hierarchy as negative samples, mimicking the loss employed in
042
      generative retrieval (§3). At test time, retrieval falls back to MIPS with an external index. HCE
043
      provides the best of both worlds: (1) zero-shot adaptation to new domains; and (2) efficient addition
044   and removal to the index without fine-tuning.
045
046   Our experimental results demonstrate that HCE achieves superior performance over a variety of
047
      popular dense and generative retrieval methods under both supervised and unsupervised scenarios,
      illustrating the effectiveness of HCE’s modeling of the document set as a hierarchy.

051
052   Dense Retrieval In the dense retrieval paradigm of information retrieval, one seeks to learn an
053   encoder F : 𝑉 ∗ → R𝑛 that maps a string of tokens (from vocabulary set 𝑉) to a point in a 𝑛-
054   dimensional vector space. Retrieval can then be performed in by some nearest neighbor search (NNS)
      or maximum inner product search (MIPS) in this space R𝑛 . A common instantiation of this encoder

070   This general framework is first pioneered by Lee et al. (2019) and Karpukhin et al. (2020) for dense

072
073   Generative Retrieval Generative retrieval is a new paradigm that directly generates document
074   identifiers by the model’s parametric memory, without using an external index. Specifically, one
075   first assigns a unique string-valued identifier to each document, and when decoding, constrain the
076   decoding process so that its output falls within the set of unique identifiers. Top-𝑘 retrieval is done by
077   using beam search in the decoding process. Such method removes the need for any external index,
078
      making the whole memory of the corpus parametrized by a neural network.
079   The earliest work in this thread is GENRE (De Cao et al., 2021), where the correct entity name
080   (e.g. Wikipedia article title) is generated through a sequence-to-sequence model for entity linking.
081   DSI (Tay et al., 2022) and NCI (Wang et al., 2022) applied the generative retrieval approach to ad
082   hoc document retrieval. G EN R ET (Sun et al., 2023) learns the document IDs without the initial
083   preprocessing step. Such methods suffer from two drawbacks: (a) scalability: challenging to scale
084
      to massive scale of documents since all memory is parametrized in the model, absent of external
      storage; (b) extensibility: hard to expand the document set if new documents are going to be indexed,
085
      since it is usually pre-trained on a static collection of documents.
086
087   Adding new documents in generative retrieval usually requires continued training. There are methods
088   proposed to alleviate the problem: DSI++ (Mehta et al., 2023) sought to mitigate catastrophic
089   forgetting for continued training; IncDSI (Kishore et al., 2023) proposed a constrained optimization
090
      method to find optimal vectors for new documents, but it only applies to the atomic version of DSI.
091
092   3    A C LOSER L OOK AT G ENERATIVE R ETRIEVAL
093
094   Generative retrieval seems to be a departure from traditional contrastive learning, but upon scrutiny,
095   we found that the underlying loss is similar in many ways. Here we take a closer look at arguably the
096   seminal work that started the research thread on generative retrieval for documents: the differentiable
097   search index (DSI; Tay et al., 2022). There were two proposed versions of DSI, namely the atomic-ID
098   version and the hierarchical version. Later work that followed (e.g. NCI; Wang et al. (2022))
099   prioritized the hierarchical version.

115   Thus we see that in the atomic version of generative retrieval,
116
117   • a fixed-length vector v𝑑 is actually trained for each document 𝑑, in the form of rows in the weight
118     matrix of the language model head;
119   • a fixed-length vector is actually created for the query, in the form of the decoder state vector s;
120
      • and the decoding process of arg max𝑑 ∈ D exp s · v𝑑 is in fact maximum inner product search (MIPS).
121
122   Hence the atomic version of generative retrieval can be considered as a form of contrastive learning,
123   where the positive document is contrasted with all other documents in the corpus, with the embedding
124   for all documents saved as parametric memory in WLMHead (Figure 1 Left). These embeddings are
125   updated under gradient descent for every training iteration. This is different from contrastive learning
126   in dense retrievers, where usually a small set of negative samples are sampled from the corpus.

537   6     C ONCLUSION
538
539   We present hierarchical corpus encoder (HCE) that jointly learns a dense encoder and a document
540   hierarchy for information retrieval. HCE contrasts positive samples against siblings nodes on the
541   document hierarchy as negative samples, mimicking the training dynamics in hierarchical generative
542   retrieval. HCE is shown to achieve superior performance over a variety of dense encoder and
543   generative retrieval baselines, under both supervised and unsupervised scenarios, demonstrating the
544   effectiveness of jointly learning a document hierarchy.
545   HCE easily scales to corpora with >5M documents. Additionally, as a dense encoder, HCE supports
546   easy addition and removal of documents to an MIPS index without the need for continued training, as
547   is demonstrated in our incrementally updated retrieval experiments.
