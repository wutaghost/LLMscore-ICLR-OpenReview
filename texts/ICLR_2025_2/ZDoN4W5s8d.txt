011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Regulators seek to curb the societal risks of machine learning; a common aim is
014            to protect the public from excessive privacy violations or bias in models. In the
015            status quo, regulators and companies independently evaluate societal risk. We find
016            that discrepancies in these evaluations can be either a detriment or an advantage
017            for companies. To abide by regulation, a company needs to conservatively eval-
018            uate risk: it should train its model such that risk remains below the acceptable
019            threshold—even if the regulator’s evaluation returns higher risk measurements.
020
               This decreases model utility (up to 8%, in our experiments). Conversely, when the
               regulator’s measurements are consistently lower than theirs, we find that a company
021
               can behave strategically and game regulation to train more accurate models. We call
022
               this Lossgate, an allusion to Dieselgate in environmental regulation: Volkswagen
023            produced cars that limited their emissions when being subjected to a regulator’s
024            emissions measurement. To model incomplete information and the misaligned
025            incentives that explain Lossgate, we leverage game theory. We obtain S PEC G AME,
026            a model for regulator-company interactions which allows us to estimate the exces-
027            sive risk that results from the strategic behavior observed in Lossgate. We show
028            Lossgate costs 70–96% higher compared to collaborative regulation in the sum
029            cost for all players.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The societal risks of deploying machine learning (ML) are well documented. To contain these risks,
036   companies are increasingly expected to deploy ML algorithms that have been adapted to support
037
      algorithmic fairness (Pedreshi et al., 2008; Calders & Verwer, 2010), privacy (Blum et al., 2005;
      Abadi et al., 2016), robustness (Szegedy et al., 2013), or interpretability (Linardatos et al., 2020), to
038
      name a few. Meanwhile, regulators around the world seek to enforce new legislation that captures the
039
      public’s expectation of how strongly an ML application should contain the associated societal risks.
040   For instance, a regulator may enforce a maximum limit on privacy and fairness violations.
041
042   Regulators and companies currently evaluate societal risk independently from one another. This is
043
      because the regulator is a separate entity from the company. Two key issues arise from this separation:
      misaligned incentives and imperfect information. Recall our running example of a regulator that
044
      wishes to enforce a maximum limit on privacy and fairness violations. In this example, the company
045
      instead wishes to maximize model accuracy—which can be interpreted as a proxy for financial profit.

092

093

094   loss—by up to 8%. This is the result of uncertainty in privacy risk estimations. Hence, increased

      2   R ELATED W ORK AND BACKGROUND
116
117
118   Related Work. It has been shown that there exist tensions between model accuracy, privacy, and
119   fairness (Tramer & Boneh, 2020; Suriyakumar et al., 2021; Farrand et al., 2020). Attempts to improve
120   the resulting trade-offs have involved adapting the training procedure (Xu et al., 2019; Mozannar et al.,
121   2020; Franco et al., 2021; Tran et al., 2021), a form of hyperparameter search (Avent et al., 2019),
122
      or calculating Pareto frontiers (Jagielski et al., 2019; Yaghini et al., 2023). Note that, in contrast to
      our work, all prior frameworks do not consider the inherent multi-agent nature of the problem: they
123
      characterize trade-offs without modeling the regulator (who is enforcing trustworthiness) and the
124
      company (who is implementing trustworthiness) as separate entities. While it integrates some of the
125   techniques from prior work, our work innovates by developing the S PEC G AME framework to model
126   the interactions between the regulator and company.
127
128
      For brevity, our paper considers two societal risks: algorithmic bias and leakage of private information.
      Our framework is more general and extensible to other risks (see Appendix A.1). We now formalize
129
      the corresponding definitions of fairness Γ(.) and privacy E(.).
130
131   Fairness. The choice of fairness measure is largely task-dependent and at the behest of the
132   regulators (Barocas et al., 2018). Hence, our framework abstracts this choice and does not make any
133   assumptions on the applied metric. The fairness evaluation process takes as input a fairness metric
134
      Γfair (ω, D) : W × X 7→ R+ chosen by the regulator, the model ω ∈ W, and an adequate evaluation
      dataset Deval ∈ X , Deval ∼ D, where D is the task’s data distribution. The evaluation process then
135
      outputs γ bω as an empirical estimate of the model’s fairness violation. In Section 4, we instantiate
136
      concrete ML algorithms with their stated fairness measures which we discuss in detail in Appendix G.
137
138   Privacy. In the context of ML, Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006) adds controlled
139   noise to the ML algorithm to protect contributions individuals make to the training set—while still
140
      yielding useful models. Our work considers the (ε, δ)-differential privacy setup. Let M : X → R be
      a randomized algorithm. In our case, M is either the training algorithm or the inference procedure.
141
      M satisfies (ε, δ)-DP with ε ∈ R+ and δ ∈ [0, 1] if for all neighboring datasets D ∼ D′ , i.e., datasets
142
      that differ in only one data point, and for all possible subsets R ⊆ R of the output space it must hold
143   that P [M(D) ∈ R] ≤ eε P [M(D′ ) ∈ R] + δ.
144
145   In our formulations, similar to fairness, we consider a privacy-parameter evaluation function E(ω, D) :
146
      W × X 7→ R+ . The evaluation process produces ε̂ω as an estimation of the (true) privacy parameter
      of the model εω . Auditing DP learning is a non-trivial problem due to the worst-case nature of privacy
147
      failures (which are intrinsically rare events (Nasr et al., 2021; Chadha et al., 2024)). While our work
148
      does not directly contribute to privacy auditing, it benefits from ongoing progress in this area.
149
150   Problem Formulation: Collaborative ML Regulation
151   Before we introduce our model for the company to interact with a regulator, possibly strategically,
152   we first need to understand the baseline of voluntary collaboration. In this baseline, which we called
153   C OLLAB R EG in Section 1, the regulator and company form a committee that jointly produces a model
154   that balances accuracy and societal risks, i.e., is trustworthy.
155
      Formally, the committee wishes to train a model ω ∈ W on dataset D ∼ D where D is the data-
156   generating distribution. W := Θ × Φ is the space of models with dim(Θ) parameters and dim(Φ)
157   hyper-parameters. Model ω may have many hyper-parameters, only a subset S ⊂ Φ of which impacts
158   the trustworthy metrics that interests the committee due to their impact on societal risk. We call S
159   the set of trustworthy hyper-parameters. For instance, a convolutional network trained with DP has
160   dim(Φ \ S) hyper-parameters like filter-size, and dim(S) = 2 trustworthy hyper-parameters, namely
161   the privacy parameters (ε, δ) used to train the model (see Section 2). Training such a model is a
      bi-level optimization problem. The committee first needs to pick trustworthy hyper-parameters s; this

289   3.1    ML R EGULATION UNDER S TRATEGIC B EHAVIOR
290
291   Despite the absence of strategic behavior, incomplete information leads to excessive loss of utility for
292   the company. Conversely, it is possible for the company to take advantage of the uncertainty inherent
293   in risk estimation strategically to produce a more accurate but less trustworthy model. The regulator
294
      has to account for this possibility and interact with the company accordingly. To study the outcome
      of these interactions, we formalize them using a novel game called S PEC G AME. We refer the reader
295
      to Appendix C for a background on game theory.
296
297   We introduce S PEC G AME, a game theoretic model of ML regulation that captures the interactions
298   between three agents involved in the life-cycle of an ML model (Tomsett et al., 2018): a company
299   who is in charge of producing the model, and two regulators who are in charge of fairness and privacy
300
      of the resulting model, respectively. We note that our framework is general and can accommodate
      other objectives, as long as they are measurable with a loss function. For instance, In Appendix A.1,
301
      we show how to use robustness to adversarial examples as an objective. Based on historical precedent
302
      and future regulatory plans (see Appendix A.2), we assume regulators are able to give penalties for
303   violations of their respective objectives.
304
305   Depending on whether regulators announce trustworthy specifications b (see Section 2) first, or if the
306
      company produces a model first with fairness and privacy guarantees of its choosing, we would have
      a game that is either regulator-led, or company-led (see Figure 2). In Section 4, we will compare
307
      the two setting but since analysis of both are similar, without loss of generality (W.L.O.G), unless
308
      otherwise stated, we will assume a regulator-led S PEC G AME. This sequential order of interactions
309   lends itself naturally to a Stackelberg competition (Fudenberg & Tirole, 1991). In either case, if
310   the company abides by the specification b, the game concludes (i.e. the game has a single stage).
311   However, if the regulator is not convinced of the company’s compliance, the company is penalized
312   and forced to release a new model until the regulator is assured of its compliance2 .

      Solving S PEC G AME
349
350   A single-stage S PEC G AME is a Stackelberg competition analyzing which involves solving a bi-level
351   min-max optimization problem where the follower’s feasible strategies are limited by the leader’s
352   chosen strategy. The solutions to this problem produce Stackelberg equilibria. In the repeated setting,
353
      visualized as a tree (akin to a decision-tree) in Figure 2, the appropriate equilibrium concept is a
      subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) which requires that the solution produces an equilibrium at every
354
      sub-game associated with a sub-tree. Both Stackelberg and subgame-perfect equilibria are extensions
355
      of Nash equilibria (see Appendix C) to extensive-form games.
356
357   However, although Nash equilibria are optimal w.r.t. single-agent deviations, they are often not
358   Pareto efficient. For instance, seeking NEs can provide ‘solutions’ where both the company and a
359
      regulator’s losses can be improved simultaneously which is not a desirable outcome for ML regulation.
      Furthermore, the S PEC G AME described in Section 3.1 cannot be simulated directly due to challenges
360
      in forming the agents’ loss functions, notably, because privacy violations of a trained model is difficult
361
      to estimate without access to its training procedure (Gilbert & McMillan, 2018). In Section 3.2
362   we introduce PARETO P LAY to address these problems by taking advantage of the fact that agents
363   estimate losses using different datasets sampled from the same distribution (see Appendix A.3).

      are beyond this work’s scope but it is a crucial issue that is relevant beyond ML. For instance, in

406   Game Theoretic Analysis of S PEC G AME under PARETO P LAY. Playing on the Pareto frontiers
407   has important implications for the equilibrium search: the Pareto frontier gives a signal to every
408

409
      response for every player, we recover a correlated equilibrium (see Appendix C). Since S PEC G AME
410

411   sends separately and confidentially message Mi to each players i ∈ N = {comp, fair, priv} at the
412   beginning of each stage (i.e., each player samples their own dataset and train their own models).

422   While S PEC G AME models ML Regulation as a PAP, it also subsumes C OLLAB R EG as a special case.
423   Indeed, if the company released a model that satisfies the specification b the game converges in one
424   step and no penalty is issued. However, if the game goes on for several stages, both players sustain
425   accumulating losses in the form of penalties (for the company) and untrustworthy models released to
426   the public (for the regulator). Thus, compared to C OLLAB R EG, S PEC G AME is inherently inefficient.
427   Price of Anarchy (PoA) (Koutsoupias & Papadimitriou, 1999) is the canonical measure for quantifying
428   the inefficiency caused by strategic self-interested behavior. Given a game (S PEC G AME), a notion of
429   equilibrium (SPCE) and a non-negative group-cost function (e.g., sum of all agents’ costs), the PoA of
430   the game is defined as the ratio between the largest group-cost of an equilibrium and the group-cost
431   of an optimal outcome—which in our case is a C OLLAB R EG outcome. We differ a formal definition
      of our PoA to Appendix E and leave upper-bounding PoA for S PEC G AME to future work. In the

535   Our approach recognizes the diverse nature of agents involved in deploying and auditing ML models.
536   This allows us to make suggestions for guarantee levels that are more likely to be realizable in practice;

771   A.3    O N THE S IMILARITY OF THE PARETO FRONTIERS
772
      We show that it is not necessary to assume that the Pareto frontiers of the company and the regulators
773   are the same. Rather, it is enough to assume that the datasets they are calculated on are from the same
774   data-generating distribution. Concretely, we show that, the problem of finding the Pareto frontier for
775   each agent can be written as a multi-objective optimization problem, the solution to which reduces to
776   empirical risk minimization in ML. We conclude that the assumption of the shared Pareto frontier
777   between agents is akin to the standard assumption of IID-ness (independent and identically-distributed
778   data) in ML.
779   Deriving the Pareto frontier via scalarization. There exist standard techniques to recover the Pareto
780   frontier of a multi-objective optimization problem—which always exists for any feasible problem.
781   Scalarization (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004, Section 4.7.4) is such a technique that, provided each
782   objective is convex, can recover all of the Pareto frontier; and if not, at least a part of it. For our
783   problem, the objective loss of the scalarized problem is mins α1 ℓcomp (s) + α2 ℓfair (s) + α3 ℓpriv (s),
784   where α1 , α2 , α3 ≥ 0 are free parameters, different choices for which will give us various points on
785   the Pareto frontier. Implicit in the scalarized objective loss are two assumptions: a) the dataset used
786   to optimize the loss, and b) dependency on model weights ω. Making these assumptions explicit
787
      allows us to write the Pareto frontier P Fi calculated by agent i:
788                        P Fi = {arg min min α1 ℓcomp (s, ω; Di ) + α2 ℓfair (s, ω; Di )
                                       s       ω
789
790
                                            + α3 ℓpriv (s, ω; Di ) | α1 , α2 , α3 ∈ R+ },            (7)
791   where P Fi is calculated over dataset Di by agent i. Seen through an ML lens, Equation (7) closely
792
      resembles an empirical risk minimization (ERM) problem. We optimize model parameters ω in the
      inner sub-problem and tune the hyper-parameters s in the outer one.
793
794   Coming back to question of whether Pareto frontiers are similar for different agents, we argue that
795   since the problem of finding in the Pareto frontier reduces to an ERM problem, despite Di not being
796   the same, we expect that the Pareto frontiers would be similar provided that Di ∼ D where D is
797   the data-generating distribution, and that each Di have enough samples. In other words, the true
798
      correlation device in PARETO P LAY is not so much the Pareto frontier, but the real-world phenomenon
      whose data is sampled by each agent.
799
800   We conclude this section by noting that prior works supports our assumption as well. Yaghini et al.
801   (2023, Section 5.1.4) empirically showed that the Pareto frontiers calculated on separate datasets but
802   for the same task are quite similar. In Section 4, we empirically evaluate the shared Pareto frontier
803   assumption. We simulate a S PEC G AME using PARETO P LAY where regulators and companies use
804
      different datasets but for modeling the same task (gender estimation). PARETO P LAY converges
      because all agents are modeling the same data-generating phenomenon (gendered humans).

959   C.1    S TACKELBERG C OMPETITIONS
960
961   Stackelberg competitions model sequential interaction among strategic agents with distinct objec-
962   tives Fudenberg & Tirole (1991). They involve a leader and a follower. The leader is interested in
963
      identifying the best action (BR) assuming rational behavior of the follower. The combination of the
      leader’s action and the follower’s rational best reaction leads to a strong Stackelberg equilibrium
964
      (SSE) Birmpas et al. (2020). This improves over work relying on zero-sum game formulation Yao
965
      (1977) where the follower’s objective is assumed to be opposed to the leader’s objective. An important
966   example for the application of Stackelberg competition in trustworthy ML strategic classification.
967   Therein, strategic individuals can, after observing the model output, adapt their data features to obtain
968   better classification performance. Such changes in the data can cause distribution shifts that degrade
969   the model’s performance and trustworthiness on the new data, and thereby requires the companies
970   adapt their models. In our model governance game framework, the two regulators act as leaders
971   while the company acts as the follower. By following the Stackelberg competition, the company aims
      at obtaining the best-performing ML model given the requirements specified by the regulators.

1001   equivalently it performs a gradient update not following f ). By definition, then there exists some s∗
1002   which Pareto dominates sr : it is at least as good in all objectives and better in at least one.
1003   We first note that reporting sr where err(sr ) > err(s∗ ) is irrational (in the game theoretic sense that it
1004   increases the agent’s cost instead of reducing it) and thus never a best response for C. So we can only
1005   consider cases where it holds that either err(sr ) < err(s∗ ) and Γ(sr ) > Γ(s∗ ), or err(sr ) < err(s∗ )

1008

1010

1011   efficient solutions, adopt it as its initialization, and take a step on the Pareto frontier to improve the
1012   corresponding regulator loss. At this point, depending on which objective value was under-reported
1013   by C the regulator would either be able to find an s∗ that Pareto dominates sr — at which point sr
1014   is again effectively removed from the PF calculations — or the next regulator is going to make a
1015   gradient step and find the appropriate s∗ that Pareto dominates the misreported sr . In the worst-case

       which again leads to exclusion of sr from the Pareto frontier. Therefore, the single deviation from f
       (i.e. choosing sr over s∗ ) does not benefit the company; which is a contradiction that completes the

1328   Our approach recognizes the diverse nature of agents involved in deploying and auditing machine
