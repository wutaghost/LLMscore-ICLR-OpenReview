011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite their success across a broad spectrum of general tasks, Large Language
014            Models (LLMs) often underperform in domain-specific tasks not well-represented
015            in their pre-training corpora. We introduce an innovative framework integrating
016
               general-purpose LLMs with an external Thinker module to enhance the reasoning
               capabilities of LLM-based agents. Unlike augmenting LLMs with prompt engi-
017
               neering, our Thinker module directly accesses knowledge from domain databases
018
               and employs supervised or reinforcement learning (RL). We establish a reasoning
019            hierarchy where LLMs handle intuitive System-1 tasks that are domain-agnostic,
020            while the Thinker focuses on System-2 tasks that require complex logical analy-
021            sis and domain-specific knowledge. Our framework is demonstrated through a
022            9-player Werewolf game that necessitates dual-system reasoning. We design a
023            communication protocol between LLMs and the Thinker, then optimize the Thinker
024            through online RL and refine it by imitation learning. Drawing from 18 800 hu-
025            man games, this work also contributes to the largest dataset for social deduction
026            games to date. Experiments show that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4, augmented with the
027
               Thinker, significantly improve in deductive reasoning, textual speech generation,
               and online gameplay evaluated by human players. Further, integrating a fine-tuned
028
               6B Werewolf-specific LLM with the Thinker achieves performance on par with
029
               GPT-4.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

034
      The field of artificial intelligence has witnessed groundbreaking advancements in recent years,
035
      with the development of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023;
036   Anil et al., 2023). Apart from their impressive proficiency in natural language processing (NLP)
037   tasks (Thoppilan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b), LLMs also exhibit vast potential as general
038   problem solvers in areas such as planning and decision-making (Huang et al., 2022), knowledge
039   transfer and generalization (Anil et al., 2022) and multi-modal perception (Yin et al., 2023) due to
040   the rich world knowledge embedded in their training corpora. Consequently, the integration of LLMs
041   as central controllers with task agents for end-to-end solutions has emerged as a promising research
042   direction, yielding breakthroughs in domains including tools and assistants (Schick et al., 2023; Ge
043   et al., 2023), engineering (Ahn et al., 2022), and gaming (Wang et al., 2023).
044   LLM-based agents utilize LLMs for their general-purpose reasoning abilities (Huang & Chang, 2022),
045   which are primarily enabled by prompt engineering methods such as information profiling (Zhang
046   et al., 2023a; Qian et al., 2023), step-by-step task decomposition (Wei et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2022),
047   recursive prompting by feedback from the environment (Yao et al., 2022), human interaction (Wu et al.,
048   2022) and self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023). These methods obviate the need
049   for domain-specific fine-tuning of LLMs. To augment their task-specific competencies, researchers
050
      adopt external modules like memory systems for storing and retrieving historical information (Lin
      et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023), external tools (Schick et al., 2023), APIs (Qin et al.,
051
      2023), knowledge bases (Lewis et al., 2020) and expert models (Yang et al., 2023b; Ge et al., 2023).
052
053   Despite these advancements, challenges persist in domain-specific applications, where LLM-based
      agents often serve primarily as demonstrations rather than as practical solutions (Yang et al., 2023a;

075   Tan et al., 2024). First, while general-purpose LLMs have emerged some reasoning capabilities,
076   they require sufficient model scales and computational overheads (Kaplan et al., 2020), along
077   with various aforementioned techniques (Wei et al., 2022a). However, LLMs struggle to achieve
078   satisfactory performance in higher-level reasoning (Stechly et al., 2023; Dziri et al., 2023) and
079   planning (Valmeekam et al., 2023; Bubeck et al., 2023) tasks. Second, most LLM-based agents avoid
080   fine-tuning LLMs on task-specific data to preserve the model’s generality and prevent over-fitting.
081
      This strategy complicates the utilization of existing domain datasets and expertise, as well as the
      alignment of task scenarios with input-output formats, data distributions, and human preferences.
082
083   Addressing the limitations of LLMs in high-level and domain-specific reasoning, we draw inspiration
084   from the dual-process theory (Wason & Evans, 1974; Daniel, 2017; Hagendorff et al., 2022; Weston
085   & Sukhbaatar, 2023; Lin et al., 2024) and distinctly separate reasoning into two systems. We propose
086   an external Thinker module to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, as shown in Figure 1(c).
087
      In our framework, LLMs are responsible for System-1 reasoning related to intuitive thinking, such as
      domain-agnostic NLP interactions, common-sense and symbolic reasoning, while the Thinker handles
088
      System-2 reasoning that is deliberate, analytical, and requires deep understanding of domain-specific
089
      knowledge. We design a communication protocol between LLMs and the Thinker through explicit
090   information. Unlike augmenting LLMs with prompt engineering, the Thinker directly accesses
091   knowledge from extensive databases and applies various optimization techniques, thus enhancing the
092   performance and human alignment without compromising LLM’s generality.
093
      The 9-player Werewolf game serves as the proving ground for our framework, given that current
094
      AI systems lag significantly behind even moderately skilled human players in this popular social
095
      deduction game. We heuristically dissect the reasoning process into four stages, as illustrated in
096   Figure 1(b). System-1 reasoning includes natural language understanding (NLU) and generation
097   (NLG) of players’ speech. Meanwhile, the hidden identities require complex deductive reasoning
098   and strategic planning such as deception and disguise, which fall under System-2 reasoning. This
099   duality creates a significant gap between the players’ actual speeches and their true intentions, making
100   Werewolf an ideal testbed for assessing advanced reasoning capabilities of LLM-based agents.
101
      We identify primary patterns from real human speeches and design language-based features (from
102   LLMs to Thinker) and speech instructions (from Thinker to LLMs) accordingly. The Thinker is
103   optimized by imitation learning, reinforcement learning (RL) from fictitious self-play (Heinrich
104   et al., 2015), and population-based training (Jaderberg et al., 2017), to produce reasonable and
105   human-aligned game actions and instructions. We compare our approach to the Least-to-Most (LtM)
106   prompting (Zhou et al., 2022) and 11-shot in-context learning from three dimensions: accuracy of
107   deductive reasoning, human preference of generated speeches, and online evaluation of a complete
      game. Experiments show that the integration of the Thinker module substantially enhances the

      Enhance Reasoning in LLMs. Several approaches bypass the intricacies of prompt engineering.
120   For instance, LLM+P (Liu et al., 2023) employs an external planner to tackle long-horizon robot
121   planning challenges. A different approach (Zhang et al., 2023a) heuristically designs a low-level
122   planner to manage primitive control actions. The Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis
123   et al., 2020) merges pre-trained parametric memory generation models with non-parametric memory,
124   aiming to enhance performance in knowledge-intensive tasks. Regarding the fine-tuning of LLMs,
125   Galactica (Taylor et al., 2022) is trained on a scientific dataset that emphasizes detailed reasoning
126   processes. WebGPT (Nakano et al., 2021) utilizes human feedback to fine-tune GPT-3, enabling it to
127   answer long-form questions within a textual web-browsing context. Toolformer (Schick et al., 2023)
128
      fine-tunes LLMs for using external tools in a self-supervised manner with human demonstrations.
      Swiftsage (Lin et al., 2024) employs GPT-4 with a small LM finetuned on the oracle agent’s action
129
      trajectories. DECKARD (Nottingham et al., 2023) trains an RL agent to execute subgoals planned by
130
      LLMs in the game Minecraft. OpenAGI (Ge et al., 2023) implements RL from feedback in open-
131   ended tasks to refine the LLM’s planning strategy. Cicero (Bakhtin et al., 2022) fine-tunes LLMs to
132   generate dialogue controlled by a strategic reasoning module in the game Diplomacy, as shown in
133   Figure 1(a). Our approach diverges from Cicero in several key aspects: the predicted/intended actions
134   in Cicero (1) require both NLU and NLG involves a high-level and task-related reasoning beyond
135   domain-agnostic NLP; (2) necessitate fine-tuning of LLMs; (3) are insufficient to convey complex
136   language dynamics in the Werewolf game (see Appendix B.1 and B.2).
137   AI for Social Deduction Games. DeepRole (Serrino et al., 2019) combines counterfactual regret
138   minimization (CFR) with deep value networks in the non-speech 5-player Avalon game. Hidden
139   Agenda (Kopparapu et al., 2022) presents a two-team, non-speech social deduction game in a 2D
140   environment. A system comprising three LLM-powered interfaces is created (Zhu et al., 2023) to
141   aid gameplay in Dungeon Master. Regarding AI for Werewolf games, bootstrap aggregating and
142   weighted ensemble learning have been applied to refine voting strategies (Khan & Aranha, 2022).
143   Brandizzi et al. (2021) proposes an RL framework to analyze the influence of diverse communication
144   behaviors among agents. One Night Ultimate Werewolf (Eger & Martens, 2019) explores human
145
      responses to various deliberation strategies. In the 5-player werewolf game, Wang & Kaneko (2018)
      builds a deep-Q network to decide whom to trust or kill. Deep Wolf (Shibata et al., 2023) fine-tunes a
146
      RoBERTa-like pretrained model with 48 game logs to construct a value network given the current
147
      game state, human speeches, and candidate actions. The 7-player version is explored with RL and
148   LLMs in Xu et al. (2023b;a). Our approach differs from previous studies in two fundamental ways:
149   First, the Thinker separates LLMs from domain-specific reasoning. In contrast, LLMs in Xu et al.
150   (2023b) tackle most reasoning tasks and generate candidate outcomes from which an RL model
151   selects to mitigate biases, as illustrated in Figure1(b). Second, by collecting and leveraging authentic
152   human sessions and speech data, we aim for closer alignment with real-world scenarios and human
153   patterns beyond the few-shot capabilities of general-purpose LLMs.

180   • The Listener focuses specifically on domain-agnostic NLU tasks. It summarizes lengthy contexts,
181     retrieves key information from natural language inputs, and transforms it into structured language
182     features that the Thinker can interpret.
183   • The Thinker serves as the cognitive core of the framework. Utilizing language features provided
184     by the Listener, it specializes in System-2 reasoning tasks that require deep logical analysis and
185     domain-specific knowledge. The Thinker produces policies such as planning and actions, and
186     generates strategic instructions for the Presenter.
187   • The Presenter functions as the framework’s articulator. Augmented by the strategic instructions
188     from the Thinker, it generates coherent and contextualized language output that is logical, rational,
189     consistent, free from hallucinations, and aligns with the current environment state.
190
191   It is important to note that the Listener and Presenter are separated functionally but can be instantiated
192   by a single LLM. Therefore, the framework essentially comprises only an LLM and a Thinker
193   module interacting with each other. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the framework, we apply
194   it to the complex social deduction game Werewolf. The remainder of this section will detail the
195
      implementation, which necessitates deductive reasoning, speech understanding and generation, as
      illustrated in Figure 2.

198   3.1        DATA P REPARATION
199
200   We collected data from the 9-player standard mode Werewolf game hosted on the Fanlang platform2 .
201   The specific rules of the game are detailed in Appendix C. We recorded real-time video in spectator
202
      mode for approximately 18 800 game sessions, which equates to around 7000 hours of gameplay
      and 6000 hours of audio. Furthermore, we enriched our dataset with a Werewolf domain-specific
203
      corpus comprising nearly 1.4 million characters, derived from web-crawled game strategies and
204
      OCR-processed Werewolf literature. Each recorded session includes both the game state data and the
205   audio of players’ speeches. We captured exhaustive game details, such as historical skill usage and
206   voting results, by utilizing the Android automated testing framework3 . The Paraformer (Gao et al.,
207   2022) model was deployed for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) of human speech audio. To
208   improve recognition accuracy, especially for frequently used terms, we developed a list of hot words
209   from the Werewolf corpus and applied context biasing methods (Zhao et al., 2019). Furthermore, we
210   annotated approximately 127 hours of Werewolf speech data and performed supervised fine-tuning
211   on the Paraformer model. The character error rate of ASR for Werewolf speeches was reduced from
212   4.5% to 3.7%. We refer to the dataset hereafter as FanLang-9, with a thorough analysis provided in
213
      Appendix D.

      3.2   L ISTENER FOR D OMAIN -AGNOSTIC R ETRIEVAL
217
218   The complexity of social deduction games stems from players concealing their identities. In the game
219   of Werewolf, Werewolves disguise themselves as members of the "Good" faction through deceptive
220   statements. Conversely, the "Good" faction strives to discern Werewolves by deducing from historical
221   speeches and actions while providing rational and credible statements. This interplay significantly
222   widens the gap between what players say and their true intentions (see Figure 5). The Listener aims
223   to capture relevant insights from actual speeches without speculating on their hidden motives or
224   truthfulness. To address these challenges, we introduce a dual-phase processing:
225   Summary: Human players’ speeches on the Fanlang platform are characterized by an information
226   overload. This includes a tangled mix of context, lengthy and redundant content, and colloquial
227   ramblings. Additionally, the speeches feature complex logic that encompasses quotations, rhetorical
228   questions, hypotheses, and empathetic thinking. Together, these elements result in a rich and intricate
229   web of discourse, the accumulation of historical speeches often exceeds 10k tokens (see Figure 9),
230   making it difficult for LLMs to directly retrieve key information from raw contexts. Inspired by the
231
      Least-to-Most (LtM) prompting (Zhou et al., 2022), we prompt LLMs to generate a textual summary
      not exceeding 200 words for each single speech, retaining only critical information that the speaker
232
      intends to express.
233
234   Retrieval: Then we allow the same LLM to retrieve key information from the summary and generate
235   a JSON-style reasoning result given 10 examples, which represents description of players’ attributes
236   in the speech. Finally, the result is tokenized and categorized into language features according to
237
      specific patterns in Table 8. For an N -player Werewolf game, we define M different attributes, which
      encompass various aspects of a player mentioned in the speech, e.g., identity guessing, historical
238
      or future skills and voting decisions. From the historical collection of all speeches H, a player’s
239
      single speech S may include descriptions of all the players in the game, the language feature can be
240   represented as a matrix F ∈ ZN ×M :
241
242                                           F = [f1 , f2 , ..., fN ]T ,                                 (1)
243
244
      where fn = [fn1 , fn2 , ..., fnM ]T , n = 1, 2, ..., N and fnm ∈ Vm , ∀n = 1, 2, ..., N and m =
      1, 2, ..., M . Here Vm signifies the set of the potential values that the m-th attribute can assume.
245
246   An example of summary and language feature (N = 9, M = 2) is illustrated in Figure 2. It is worth
247   noting that the dimensions of language features are significantly richer than the predicted/intended
248   actions outlined in Cicero (Bakhtin et al., 2022). Ablation studies (Appendix B.1 and B.2) demonstrate
249   that using actions as compressed representation of speeches leads to substantial information loss and
250
      performance degradation in the Werewolf game. Aside from directly prompting LLMs to generate
      language features, we also extract 260k speech instances from the FanLang-9 dataset, label the
251
      speech-feature pairs with GPT-3.5, and fine-tune a ChatGLM-6B (Du et al., 2021) model, named as
252
      WereLLM, to perform the same task for practical efficiency. The detailed prompts for summary and
253   retrieval, as well as the details for fine-tuning of the Listener, are provided in Appendix F.7 and F.6,
254   respectively.

      3.3   T HINKER FOR D OMAIN -S PECIFIC R EASONING AND P LANNING
257
258   The Listener extracts key information from speech contents to generate language features. Then,
259   the Thinker, utilizing the game state and all historical language features, deduces the underlying
260   intentions and strategic implications of players’ public speeches. For example, as shown in Figure 2,
261   although Player 4 claims to be the Witch and accuses Player 9 of being a Werewolf, from Player 6’s
262   perspective, Player 4 might be a Werewolf disguising as the Witch, and Player 9 could more likely be
263   the Hunter. Subsequently, it plans game-related actions and speech instructions.
264   The speech instruction I ∈ ZN ×M follows the same structure as the language feature in Equation 1,
265   except that it is presented in JSON-style to align LLM input. This format is consistent with the
266   retrieval results in the Listener. The generation of a speech instruction can be viewed as a multi-label
267   classification problem and decomposed into multiple single-class classifications for each attribute
268   fnm . Therefore, we convert it into N × M discrete actions and apply the identical training algorithm
269   used for game actions. The optimization of the Thinker comprises two phases: imitation learning
      and RL. For the imitation learning phase, we utilize human data and employ the Behavioral Cloning

300   self-play (Heinrich et al., 2015), where in each game an average of 3 players employ the latest models,

      3.4   P RESENTER FOR AUGMENTED S PEECH G ENERATION
305
306   The generation of players’ public speeches plays a crucial role in the Werewolf game, significantly
307   impacting the outcome due to its strategic influence on actions and deductive reasoning of other
308   players. The quality of the speech generation hinges on several critical aspects: (1) The strategy
309   articulated within the speech should align with the player’s role and the current state of the game. (2)
310   Speeches need to adhere to the logical framework of the game, correlating with historical speeches
311   and actions, making them sound and convincing. (3) Speeches are preferred to fit the stylistic
312   environment of the Werewolf game. Detailed evaluation metrics can be found in Appendix F.2.
313   The Thinker module handles only the first aspect of speeches, providing a foundational strategic
314   instruction for the Presenter, such as the Witch’s decision to report the previous night’s rescue,
315   as shown in Figure 2. Subsequently, the Presenter leverages NLG capabilities of LLMs to craft
316   a complete speech that incorporates the strategic instruction, relevant game state, and historical
317   speeches. The template for the prompt is provided in Appendix F.7. Additionally, as with the Listener,
318   we fine-tune the WereLLM to better align with human speech styles. The 260k speech-feature pairs
319   are inverted such that the language feature F serves as the hindsight speech instruction I, and the
320
      actual speech S serves as output labels.
321   We have observed that LLMs often do not adhere to prompts, with even fine-tuned models sometimes
322   producing hallucinations and inaccuracies. Taking inspiration from the Cicero (Bakhtin et al., 2022)
323   approach, we introduce additional filtering steps. We use the Listener to perform further reasoning
      on the generated speech to produce a language feature, which we then compare for similarity to the

346       4               E XPERIMENTS
347
348      We assess the performance of our framework by comparing it against several baselines and ablative
349      variants. The models involved in the following experiments are as follows:
350
          • GPT-3.5/4: GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 are directly applied to generate end-to-end action decisions and
351         speeches. For GPT-3.5, we use the gpt-35-turbo-16k model, version 0613. For GPT-4, we apply
352         the gpt-4 model, version 1106-Preview. We prompt GPTs with game rules, explanations of typical
353         game jargon, and comprehensive game information, including visible states, legal actions, and
354         speech text converted by ASR. Detailed prompts are provided in Appendix F.7.
355       • GPT-3.5/4-LtM: This setting follows most aspects of the GPT-3.5/4 configuration, except that we
356         allow GPTs to first summarize each speech after being given 11 examples (as shown in Table 16),
357         and then we let the GPTs generate actions and speeches based on the game state and all speech
358         summaries. The Thinker module is not applied, thus no language features are retrieved.
359       • GPT-3.5/4-T: GPTs serve as the Listener and Presenter modules, while the Thinker module is
360         integrated to generate actions and speech instructions. GPTs share the same prompts in setting
361         GPT-3.5/4-LtM, except that additional speech instructions and identity predictions generated by
362         the Thinker are added into the prompting of Presenter.
363       • WereLLM-T: We replace GPTs with the WereLLM in both the Listener and Presenter as an
364
            efficient practical solution, while the Thinker remains the same as in GPT-3.5/4-T. It is worth noting
            that our framework allows for the use of fine-tuned LLMs but does not require them.

371

376      special roles. We assume that human players in the test set who are villagers would vote for the most
377      likely werewolf. Thus, we list their voting choices as a reference, but their judgments regarding the
         identities of other players remain unknown.

406   4.2                                   T HINKER -AUGMENTED S PEECH G ENERATION
407
408
      We then investigate the capabilities of various models in generating speeches. Utilizing the same 300
409   complete games as discussed in Section 4.1, we extract 400 speech sessions that span a diverse range
410   of roles, times of day, and speech types (first/second round speech, last words). Models are assigned
411   the task of generating speeches based on the current game state and all players’ historical speeches,
412   with detailed prompts for GPTs available in Appendix F.7. Due to the demonstrated effectiveness
413   of LtM prompting, subsequent experiments excluded GPTs that do not utilize LtM prompting. For
414   GPTs-T and WereLLM-T settings, speech instructions are derived from the Thinker and incorporated
415   into the prompts. We do not adopt the post filtering process for generated speeches in this experiment,
416   which yielded approximately 2000 speeches for five models. To assess the quality of the single-shot
417
      generated speeches, we recruited 10 human evaluators, all well-versed in the Werewolf game. For
      each session, generated speeches are presented in a randomized order to ensure that evaluators are
418
      unaware of the model behind each speech. Evaluators are tasked with ranking the speeches and
419
      identifying any clear legal errors, following the criteria detailed in Appendix F.2.
420
421   The evaluation results are shown in Figure 4. In terms of total scores, models augmented with Thinker
422   instructions outperformed their counterparts that relied solely on LtM prompting. Moreover, when
423
      augmented with the Thinker, the 6B WereLLM surpasses GPT-4-LtM. When examining scores for
      specific roles, the advantage of Thinker’s contributions over GPT-3.5 appears somewhat marginal
424
      for the Seer, whose speeches are relatively straightforward, needing only to report inspections from
425
      the previous night. The assessment of villagers’ speeches is inherently complex due to their limited
426   available information, which is reflected in the minimal rank score differences observed among the
427   models for this role. In contrast, differences on the rank score and illegal speak ratio are most obvious
428   for Werewolves. This disparity stems mainly from the low legality of werewolf speeches, which
429   often inadvertently reveal their identity. Remarkably, GPT-3.5 appears to struggle with adhering to
430   instructions designed to avoid self-incrimination. In contrast, GPT-4 shows a more refined capability
431   to disguise its identity, especially when augmented by the Thinker’s strategic instructions. An example
      speech for the werewolf is presented in Figure 5.

447   4.3     O NLINE E VALUATION
448
449   Lastly, we conduct online evaluations to assess the overall performance of the models in a real-world
450   gameplay setting. Given that Werewolf is a multiplayer game with imperfect information, the skill
451
      level of the participants can significantly influence the evaluation results. Therefore, we devise three
      combinations of models, with models being randomly and repeatedly selected to simulate 9-player
452
      games. We conduct approximately 600 rounds for each combination to ensure robust testing results.
453
      Given the inherent randomness of outcomes in the game, we also calculate the Behavior Score, a
454   typical metric used in Werewolf competitions4 to evaluate behavior of players, e.g., a Villager voting
455   for a werewolf, a Hunter shooting a werewolf, a comprehensive breakdown is provided in Table 9.
456
457
      The results summarized in Table 1 reveal that integrating the Thinker module significantly boosts
      the win rates of both GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 across all three model combinations. The performance
458
      of the WereLLM-T model closely aligns with that of GPT-4-T. In terms of Behavior Score, the
459
      Thinker contributes substantial improvements across all roles for GPT-3.5. For GPT-4, notable
460   benefits are observed particularly for the Witch and Hunter roles. The Behavior Score metric assigns
461   significant weight to the witch’s poisoning and the hunter’s shooting decisions, which correlates with
462   the Thinker’s ability to enhance werewolf detection and subsequently improve these scores. Another
463   notable finding is that the combination involving GPT-4 and WereLLM-T models yields the highest
464   win rate for Werewolves. This outcome primarily stems from the conservative nature of GPT-4-LtM
465   in role identification, which leads it to be more cautious in voting and skill usage as the "Good"
466   faction. In Appendix B.2, we also include an ablation of our framework with Cicero (Bakhtin et al.,
467   2022) and LLM prompting-related approach Xu et al. (2023b), demonstrating that our method still
468
      maintains a significant advantage in terms of win rate.

490   5    D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATION
491
492
      Transfer to other tasks: We use language features and speech instructions in our framework to
      integrate LLMs and external reasoning models. The communication format may not be directly
493
      transferable to other tasks or domains, with its effectiveness depending on the richness of these
494
      features and instructions. Future work aims to develop more generalized and flexible methods, such
495   as using implicit hidden vectors in a data-driven manner, potentially offering better transferability at
496   the expense of interpretability and controllability.
497
498
      Evaluation of 8 humans with 1 AI: Our evaluations primarily involved games featuring either AI
      vs AI or one human player competing against multiple AIs. Evaluating an AI in a majority-human
499
      player setting presents challenges due to the highly interactive nature of the game and the variability
500
      in human players’ speech strategies and behaviors.
501
502   Interpretability: While our framework improves the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, the reasoning
503   processes in the Thinker module may not be easily interpretable to humans. We explicitly introduce
504
      the identity prediction task to reveal how the Thinker thinks of other players. Future work could
      explore methods for further improving the interpretability and transparency of our framework.
505

507   6    C ONCLUSION
508
509   In this paper, we introduced a novel framework that integrates LLMs with an external Thinker module,
510   aiming to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLM-based agents. This approach is inspired by the
511   dual-process theory and separates reasoning tasks into two systems: System-1, handled by LLMs, and
512   System-2, handled by the Thinker. We showcased our approach in the context of the Werewolf game,
513
      a complex social deduction game requiring language processing, intuitive thinking, and strategic
      planning. Our results show that our framework can significantly improve the performance of LLMs
514
      and achieve better alignment with real-world scenarios and human preferences. Additionally, we
515
      fine-tune a 6B WereLLM to surpass GPT-4 when integrated with the Thinker. Furthermore, this paper
516   contributes the largest dataset for social deduction games to date, aiming to accelerate advancements
517   in this field.

775
776
      Regarding related works in Figure 1, we detail the evolving process of our framework as follows.
777
778
779   A.1     M OTIVATION
780
781   In the game of werewolf, there is a significant gap between what a player says and what the player is
782   actually thinking. Consider the scenario depicted in Figure 5, where Player 3, a werewolf, publicly
783   states:
784
      "I am the Seer, and I have checked Player 9, who is a good person. I suspect that Player 8 is a
785   werewolf."
786
787   While the surface meaning of this speech (System-1) is straightforward, the internal thought process
788
      of Player 3 (System-2) might be as follows:
789   "Players 6 and 7 are my fellow Werewolves (as per the game rules, Werewolves know each other’s
790   identities), and Player 8 claims to be the Seer and has accused Player 7, who is on my team. Therefore,
791   Player 8 is likely the real Seer. By also pretending to be the Seer and verifying Player 9 as a good
792   role, I can create a conflict with Player 8 in the eyes of the villagers."
793
794
      A.2     LLM P ROMPTING M ETHODS
795
796
      We identified several shortcomings when examining the performance of LLM with typical prompt or
797   mechanism engineering methods. The shortcomings can be categorized into two main areas:
798
799   Over-trust: LLMs exhibited a tendency to over-trust other players’ self-declared identities, particu-
800
      larly when players claimed to be Seer or Witch roles. Furthermore, when the LLM assumed the role
      of a Werewolf itself, it was prone to inadvertently exposing its own identity, which is demonstrated in
801
      Figure 4 and Figure 5.
802
803   Strategic Deficiencies: LLMs showed a lack of familiarity with the common strategies employed in
804   the Werewolf game. For instance, they failed to grasp tactics such as Werewolves pretending to be
805   Seers to mislead other players, Werewolves accusing their teammates to gain the trust of the "Good"
806
      players, or Villagers pretending to be Seers to protect the real Seer from being killed, etc. These are
      conventional tactics used by experienced human players to navigate the complex social dynamics of
807
      Werewolf, which involve deception, trust, and betrayal.
808
809   To delve deep into the reasoning process of LLMs, we dissected the process from listening to speaking
      in the game into four stages, as shown in Figure 1 and investigate issues one by one:


                                                                             15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
            (1) NLU: It is assigned as the Listener’s goal in Figure 2, is to interpret speeches and extract
811             their explicit meanings. LLMs show proficiency in this area.
812         (2) Deductive reasoning: LLMs underperform in role identification, often over-trust other
813             players’ self-declared identities, as tested in Section 4.1. Then the deductive reasoning is
814             limited to information extraction.
815         (3) Speech strategic planning: LLMs struggle to outline a comprehensive speech plan, espe-
816             cially when assuming the role of a Werewolf. They frequently risk exposing themselves or
817             their allies (see Figure 5), lacking an understanding of conventional Werewolf game speech
818             strategies.
819
            (4) NLG: Although LLMs are unfamiliar with conventional speech strategies, we find that they
                can generate coherent and convincing speeches once prompted with basic instructions, such
820
                as "You should pretend to be the Seer and accuse Player 3 of being a werewolf".

823   A.3    T RANSITION TO THE T HINKER M ODULE
824
825
      The primary reason for the above shortcomings is that LLMs are not trained on Werewolf-specific
      knowledge corpus and data. Although it is possible to prompt LLMs with common game termi-
826
      nologies through in-context learning, strategic experiences are challenging to encapsulate in text
827
      prompts. To address the deficiencies in deductive reasoning and speech strategic planning, we
828   consider developing a trainable Thinker model to handle these aspects separately from the LLMs.
829   The Thinker module is optimized through imitation learning and reinforcement learning, using human
830   game data as a foundation. It is designed to complement the LLMs, then the latter are responsible for
831   intuitive, domain-agnostic System-1 reasoning tasks.
832
833
      A.4    C OMPARISON WITH C ICERO
834
835   In brief, the differences between our approach and Cicero are as follows:
836
837
      Different Roles for NLU and NLG: In Cicero’s approach, both NLU and NLG involve a high-level
      logical reasoning process: NLU directly outputs action predictions, which is actually a complex
838
      reasoning process that goes beyond natural language processing. Similarly, NLG takes intended
839
      actions as control signals, but it still requires a comprehensive consideration of the game state,
840   historical speeches, and higher-level reasoning to generate reasonable dialogue/speech that matches
841   the intended action. In contrast, in our Werewolf game approach, the Listener (NLU) is only
842   responsible for extracting key information from speeches and does not infer the truthfulness of the
843   speeches or the underlying intentions. Similarly, NLG expands speech instructions, which are outlines
844   of speeches, into full statements in context, requiring less domain-specific reasoning.
845
      The Connection between LLMs and Policy: In Cicero’s approach, the connection between LLMs
846   and policy is made only through action prediction and intended action, which is non-language-based.
847   In the Werewolf game scenario, we found that using actions alone is not sufficient, as the Listener
848   causes significant information loss. Due to the complexity of Werewolf speeches, intended actions
849   also struggle to describe and control speech generation. This leads to a noticeable disadvantage for
850   Cicero’s approach in the ablation study presented in Table 3 and Table 4. To address this, we propose
851   a language-based feature and speech instruction that include complex verbal information, which can
852   effectively summarize player speeches and control the speech generation process.
853   Different Training Modes: Due to Cicero’s method involving NLU and NLG in task-specific
854   high-level reasoning processes, it is necessary to fine-tune both NLU and NLG. In our approach, by
855   defining explicit language-based connections and isolating domain-specific complex reasoning from
856   LLMs with the Thinker, we can avoid the fine-tuning of NLU and NLG.

1063
       The game comprises 3 Villagers, 3 Werewolves, and 3 special roles (Seer, Witch, and Hunter). The
1064
       identities of the players are hidden from each other, even after being eliminated from the game.
1065
1066   Werewolves: Werewolves are aware of each other’s identities. At night, they decide to kill a living
1067   player, which may include one of their own. The majority of the Werewolves’ choice will be the final
1068   kill target. If there is a tie, a random player in the tie is killed. Werewolves can commit suicide during
1069
       the speech sessions, which will reveal their identity, and the game immediately proceeds to the night
       phase, skipping the remaining daytime processes such as speeches and voting.
1070
1071   Villagers: Villagers have no special abilities. They must determine other players’ identities based on
1072   their speeches and vote to exile potential Werewolves.
1073   Seer: The Seer can verify a player’s faction each night (either Werewolf or "Good"), but cannot
1074   discern their specific role. The Seer cannot verify himself or any player who has already been verified.
1075
1076
       Witch: The Witch possesses an antidote and a poison. The antidote can save a player killed by
       Werewolves at night, and the poison can kill a player. The Witch cannot use both potions in the same
1077
       night and can only save herself on the first night.
1078
1079   Hunter: When the Hunter is killed by Werewolves at night or voted out during the day, he can shoot
       a player. However, the Hunter cannot use his ability when poisoned by the Witch.

       C.3   G AME TASK F LOW
1081
1082   The game proceeds in a night-day cycle until the victory conditions are met.
1083
       The night tasks flow:
1084
1085   (1) Werewolves decide to kill a player. In our simulation of the game environment, we have simplified
1086       the discussion into a three-round voting process. During voting, werewolf players can see
1087       their teammates’ previous votes.
1088   (2) The Witch uses her ability.
1089   (3) The Seer uses his ability.
1090
1091   The daytime tasks flow:
1092
       (1) The Moderator announces the deaths from last night but does not reveal the causes of death.
1093
       (2) Deceased players give their last words (only for the first day).
1094
       (3) If deceased players have additional abilities, they may choose to use them.
1095   (4) First round of speeches. The speech sequence is determined by the following rules: (a) if no
1096       player died last night, randomly select an initial speaker and randomly decide a clockwise or
1097       counterclockwise speaking order. (b) A deceased player is randomly selected, and the speaking
1098       order starts clockwise or counterclockwise from him. Players cannot interrupt others during their
1099       speeches.
1100   (5) First round of voting. Each player votes for a single player to exile from the game. Other players’
1101       voting choices remain hidden until the voting session ends.
1102   (6) Second round of speeches. If there is a tie in the first round of voting, the tied players give
1103
           their second speeches; otherwise, the process moves on to task (8) The first speaker, selected
           randomly from the tied players, initiates the sequence, which could proceed either clockwise or
1104
           counterclockwise.
1105
       (7) Second round of voting. If there is still a tie after the second vote, the game moves on to the next
1106       night, and no player is exiled.
1107   (8) The exiled player gives his last words.
1108   (9) If exiled players have additional abilities, they may choose to use them.

       E     E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
1243
1244
       With the integration of LLMs into complex reasoning tasks, as demonstrated in social deduction games
1245
       like Werewolf, we are witnessing the emergence of AI agents. These agents not only mimic human-
1246   like reasoning but also engage in communications that could inherently be considered deceptive.
1247   While these developments showcase the potential of AI to understand and navigate intricate human
1248   interactions, they also raise important ethical and societal considerations that must be addressed. To
1249   address these ethical and societal challenges, we propose several mitigation strategies:
1250
       Transparent Communication and Monitoring: Our framework ensures transparency through
1251
       explicit structured information at every stage of the AI’s decision-making process, from listening and
1252   reasoning to speech generation. To enhance this transparency, we propose implementing real-time
1253   transparency logs that capture and display the reasoning paths, identity predictions, and speech
1254   instructions generated by the AI. By having a complete audit trail, we can monitor the AI’s decision
1255   processes, ensure adherence to ethical guidelines, and trace any unintended actions back to their
1256   source.
1257
       Control and Filtering Mechanisms: Our speech instructions are enriched with contextual infor-
1258   mation specific to the Werewolf game, allowing for robust control over the fine-tuned LLM. To
1259   further mitigate potential negative impacts, we propose implementing dynamic contextual guardrails.
1260   These guardrails will utilize our existing filtering mechanism (as outlined in Section 3.4) to not
1261   only match generated speech with instructions but also to check against a set of ethical and societal
1262   norms. If the AI’s output is flagged as potentially harmful or deceptive beyond the game’s scope,
1263   it will be withheld and replaced with a template response. This additional layer of control will act
1264   as a safeguard against the misuse of AI in generating deceptive or manipulative content outside the
1265   intended gaming environment.

       F.3   M ODEL S TRUCTURE OF THE T HINKER
1328
1329   The Thinker network’s architecture is designed to capture the intricacies of gameplay from the current
1330   player’s perspective. It encompasses speeches, actions, and game status information for all nine
1331   involved players, including the player itself. We employ a shared-parameter feature encoding network
1332   to process the data for each of the nine players individually.
1333   For the i-th player, up to 10 language features F are stored. These language features are enriched
1334   with headers indicating the time-tag, type, and order of the speeches. Subsequently, these annotated
1335   language features are processed through another shared-parameter speech feature encoding network,
1336   which consists of a three-layer multilayer perceptron (MLP) network (181-256-256). After processing
1337   the ten pieces of features, a reduce_mean operation is applied to the outputs to synthesize the overall
1338   speech embedding for the player espeech
                                           i     . This synthesized speech embedding is then combined with
1339   additional game state information such as the player’s actions, status, and other relevant data. The
1340   aggregated data is fed through a feature encoding network (again, a three-layer MLP of 1019-512-512)
1341   to generate the feature embedding for the i-th player ei .
1342   In the final step, the feature embeddings of all nine players e1 , e2 , ..., e9 are subjected to a re-
1343   duce_mean operation to create a collective feature encoding. This comprehensive encoding is then
1344   passed through an all-player feature encoding network (a three-layer MLP of 523-512-512) to con-
1345   struct the corresponding action decision, identity prediction headers, as well as speech instructions.
1346

1351   It encompasses several key areas:
1352

1354     survival duration.

1363
         based on the change in identity prediction from the perspective of the "Good" players. The better a
1364
         Werewolf AI can deceive the "Good" faction about its true identity, the larger the reward it receives.

       F.5   D ETAILS OF OVERALL T RAINING P ROCESS
1405
1406   We provide pseudo-code in Algorithm 1; the Thinker and LLMs are trained separately in our
1407   framework. This design choice was intentional and serves as one of the strengths of our framework.
1408   The separation facilitates training efficiency, as LLMs, which we employ as both Listener and
1409   Presenter, inherently generate samples slower than the Thinker module does. Therefore, to optimize
1410   our training process, we either employ offline RL or decouple the training between the Thinker and
1411   LLMs. The inference workflow is as follows: Listener (LLM) -> language feature F -> Thinker (RL)
1412
       -> speech instruction I -> Presenter (LLM)
1413   During the Thinker’s training, the generated speech instructions I are treated as the new input
1414   language features F for subsequent steps, allowing seamless integration of the RL training into the
1415   overall process. Our hybrid training framework incorporates both BC and PPO. During training, each
1416   game session is assigned a probability to be either a BC or an RL game. In a BC session, actions
1417   a and speaking instructions I are taken directly from human replays, bypassing Thinker inference.
1418
       Conversely, in an RL session, the Thinker actively generates actions and speaking instructions.
       Samples from the game session are tagged as either BC or RL. For the Learner, BC samples utilize
1419
       the BC loss mentioned in Equation2, whereas RL samples employ the PPO loss as described in
1420
       Equation3.

1424   The training hyper-parameters for the Thinker are provided in Table 11.
1425
       Regarding the hyperparameters in Equation 4, the Behavioral Cloning coefficient α determines the
1426
       extent to which the RL policy refers to human strategies as opposed to greedily selecting the RL
1427
       strategy. We observed that as α decays to 0, Werewolves completely abandon the strategy of claiming
1428   to be the Seer due to the high difficulty for Werewolves to convincingly pretend to be the Seer and
1429   the relative challenge it poses for RL optimization. A more favorable choice is to masquerade as a
1430   villager. Therefore, we maintain a small α = 0.01 during the later stages of training, as a constraint
1431   for human strategic preferences. Regarding the coefficient β for the identity model, we tested values
1432   in 1.0, 0.1, 0.01 and found they had minimal impact on RL, given its nature as an auxiliary learning
1433   task. The fine-tuning hyper-parameters for the Listener and Presenter are provided in Table 12.
