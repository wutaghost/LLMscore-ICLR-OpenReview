011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Time-series forecasting finds broad applications in real-world scenarios. Due to
014            the dynamic nature of time series data, it is crucial for time-series forecasting
015            models to produce robust predictions under potential distribution shifts. In this
016
               paper, we initially identify two types of distribution shifts in time series: concept
               drift and temporal shift. We acknowledge that while existing studies primarily
017
               focus on addressing temporal shift issues in time series, designing proper concept
018
               drift methods for time series data received comparatively less attention.
019
               Motivated by the need to mitigate potential concept drift issues in time-series
020
               forecasting, this work proposes a novel soft attention mechanism that effectively
021            leverages and ensemble information from the horizon time series. Furthermore,
022            recognizing that both concept drift and temporal shift could occur concurrently
023            in time-series forecasting scenarios while an integrated solution remains missing,
024            this paper introduces ShifTS, a model-agnostic framework seamlessly addressing
025            both concept drift and temporal shift issues in time-series forecasting. Extensive
026            experiments demonstrate the efficacy of ShifTS in consistently enhancing the
027            forecasting accuracy of agnostic models across multiple datasets, and consistently
028            outperforming existing concept drift, temporal shift, and combined baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Time-series forecasting finds applications in various real-world scenarios such as economics, urban
      computing, and epidemiology (Zhu & Shasha, 2002; Zheng et al., 2014; Deb et al., 2017; Mathis et al.,
034
      2024). These applications involve predicting future trends or events based on historical time-series
035
      data. For example, economists use forecasts to make financial and marketing plans, while sociologists
036   use them to allocate resources and formulate policies for traffic or disease control.
037
038   The recent advent of deep learning has revolutionized time-series forecasting, resulting in series of
039
      advanced forecasting models (Lai et al., 2018; Torres et al., 2021; Salinas et al., 2020; Nie et al.,
      2023; Zhou et al., 2021). However, despite these success, time-series forecasting faces certain
040
      challenges from distribution shifts due to the dynamic and complex nature of time series data. The
041
      distribution shifts in time series can be categorized into two types (Granger, 2003). First, the data
042   distributions of the time series data themselves can change over time, including shifts in mean,
043   variance, and autocorrelation structure, which is referred to as non-stationarity or temporal drift
044   issues in time-series forecasting (Shimodaira, 2000; Du et al., 2021). For example, in influenza-like
045   illness (ILI) forecasting, the distribution of influenza cases varies between summer and winter, with
046   higher infection rates typically observed during the winter seasons. Second, time-series forecasting is
047   compounded by unforeseen exogenous factors, which shifts the distribution of target time series. A
048   prominent example is the COVID-19 pandemic, which led to an abnormal excess of influenza cases
049   than normal years. These types of phenomena, categorized as concept drift problems in time-series
050
      forecasting (Gama et al., 2014; Lu et al., 2018), make it even more challenging.
051   While prior research has investigated strategies to mitigate temporal shifts (Liu et al., 2022; Kim et al.,
052   2021; Fan et al., 2023), addressing concept drift issues in time-series forecasting has been largely
053   overlooked. Although concept drift is a well-studied problem in general machine learning (Sagawa
      et al., 2019; Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2021), adapting these solutions to time-series

054
      forecasting is challenging. Many of these methods require environment labels, which are typically
055   unavailable in time-series datasets (Liu et al., 2024a). Indeed, the few concept drift approaches
056   developed for time-series data are designed exclusively for online settings (Guo et al., 2021), limiting
057   their generalizability to standard time-series forecasting tasks. Moreover, while both concept drift
058   and temporal shift can simultaneously impact time-series forecasting, as shown in the previous ILI
059   forecasting example, few existing researches or practical solutions address both issues together.
060
      We aim to close this gap in the literature in this paper - this study aims to design an integrated
061   framework that effectively addresses both concept drift, which has not been studied well by itself,
062   and temporal shift. Our method involves ensembling time series across multiple horizon time steps
063   to enhance generalization and mitigate concept drift, with seamless integration with normalization
064   strategies to address temporal shift. The contributions of this paper are:
065
066         1. Concept Drift for Time-Series: We introduce soft attention masking (SAM) designed to
067
               mitigate concept drift issues by effectively using exogenous information from the horizon
               window. The soft attention allows the time-series forecasting models to weigh the ensemble
068
               of the time series at multiple horizon time steps to enhance the generalization ability.
069
070
            2. Integrated Framework: We propose ShifTS, a practical and model-agnostic framework
               that tackles both concept drift and temporal shift in time-series forecasting tasks. ShifTS
071
               seamlessly integrates the proposed soft attention mechanism with established temporal shift
072
               mitigation techniques, facilitating enhanced forecasting accuracy.
073
            3. Comprehensive Evaluations: We conduct extensive experiments on various time series
074
               datasets with multiple advanced time-series forecasting models. The proposed ShifTS
075
               demonstrates effectiveness by consistent performance improvements to agnostic forecasting
076            models, as well as outperforming distribution shift baselines in better forecasting accuracy.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
080
081
      Time-Series Forecasting. Classical statistical time-series forecasting models, such as ARIMA (Hyn-
      dman & Athanasopoulos, 2018), often face limitations in capturing complicated patterns and depen-
082
      dencies due to inherent model constraints (Nadaraya, 1964; Williams & Rasmussen, 1995; Smola &
083
      Schölkopf, 2004). Recent works in deep learning have achieved notable achievements in time-series
084   forecasting, such as RNNs, LSTNet, N-BEATS (Sherstinsky, 2020; Lai et al., 2018; Oreshkin et al.,
085   2020). State-of-the-art models build upon the successes of self-attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al.,
086   2017) with transformer-based architectures and significantly improve forecasting accuracy, such as
087   Informer, Autoformer, Fedformer, PatchTST, iTransformer, FRNet (Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
088   2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024). However, these
089   advanced models primarily rely on empirical risk minimization (ERM) with IID assumptions, i.e.,
090   train and test dataset follows the same data distribution, which exhibits limitations when potential
091   distribution shifts in time series.
092   Distribution Shift in Time-Series Forecasting. In recent decades, learning under non-stationary
093   distributions, where the target distribution over instances changes with time, has attracted attention
094   within learning theory (Kuh et al., 1990; Bartlett, 1992). In the context of time series, the distribution
095   shift can be categorized into concept drift and temporal shifts.
096   General concept drift methods (Arjovsky et al., 2019; Ahuja et al., 2021; Krueger et al., 2021;
097   Pezeshki et al., 2021; Sagawa et al., 2019) assume instances sampled from various environments and
098   propose to identify and utilize invariant predictors across these environments. However, when applied
099   to time-series forecasting, these methods encounter limitations. Additional methods specifically
100   tailored for time series data also encounter certain constraints: DIVERSITY (Lu et al., 2023) is
101   designed for time series classification and detection only. OneNet (Wen et al., 2024) is tailored
102   solely for online forecasting scenarios using online ensembling. PeTS (Zhao et al., 2023) focuses on
103   distribution shifts induced by the specific phenomenon of performativity.
104   Other works specifically crafted for time-series forecasting aim to address temporal shift issues (Kim
105   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). These approaches implement carefully
106   crafted normalization strategies to ensure that both the lookback and horizon of a univariate time
107   series adhere to normalized distributions. This alignment helps alleviate potential temporal shifts,
      where the statistical properties of the lookback and horizon time series may differ, over time.

127   3.2   D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT IN T IME S ERIES
128
      Given the time-series forecasting setups, a time-series forecasting model aims to predict the target
129
      distribution P(YH ) = P(YH |YL )P(YL ) + P(YH |XL )P(XL ), which should be generalizable
130
      for both training and testing time steps. However, due to the dynamic nature of time-series data,
131   forecasting faces challenges from distribution shifts, categorized into two types: temporal shift and
132   concept drift. These two types of distribution shifts are defined as follows:
133
134   Definition 3.1 (Temporal Shift (Shimodaira, 2000; Du et al., 2021)) Temporal shift (also known
135   as virtual shift (Tsymbal, 2004)) refers to the marginal probability distributions can change over time,
136   and the conditional distributions are the same.
137
138   Definition 3.2 (Concept Drift (Lu et al., 2018)) Concept drift (also known as real concept
139   drift (Gama et al., 2014)) refers to the conditional distributions can change over time, and the
140   marginal probability distributions are the same.
141
142   Intuitively, a temporal shift indicates unstable marginal distributions (e.g. P(YH ) ̸= P(YL )),
143   while a concept drift indicates unstable conditional distributions (P(YH          L         H   L
                                                                                   i |Xi ) ̸= P(Y j |Xj ) for
144   some i, j ∈ t). Existing methods for distribution shifts in time-series forecasting typically focus on
145   mitigating temporal shifts through normalization, ensuring P(YH ) = P(YL ) by both normalizing
146   to standard 0-1 distributions (Kim et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023).
147   Nevertheless, in addition to temporal shift, time-series forecasting also faces challenges from concept
148   drift: The correlations between X and Y can change over time, making the conditional distributions
149   P(YH |XL ) unstable and less predictable. Moreover, XL may not fully explain or determine YH ,
150   meaning that modeling the relationship solely through P(YH |XL ) may fail to capture the true
151   correlations between X and Y. A demonstration visualizing the differences and relationships
152   between temporal shift and concept drift is provided in Appendix A.
153
      While the concept drift issue has received considerable attention in existing studies on general machine
154   learning, applying existing methods to time-series forecasting tasks presents certain challenges.
155   Firstly, these methods typically rely on explicit environment labels as input (e.g., labeled rotation or
156   noisy images in image classification), which are not readily available in time series datasets. Secondly,
157   existing concept drift methods often require leveraging all correlated exogenous features to the target
158   variable (Liu et al., 2024a), which may not be adequately captured in time series datasets (e.g.,
159   weather conditions affecting ILI forecasting, but not included in the current ILI dataset). Additionally,
160   while both temporal shift and concept drift can manifest simultaneously in time-series forecasting
161   (e.g., when both P(YH ) ̸= P(YL ) and P(YH |XL ) are unstable), few existing solutions effectively
      addresses both issues in the context of time-series forecasting.

248   4.3   M ITIGATING T EMPORAL S HIFT
249
250   Mitigating temporal shifts (referred to as ‘non-stationary’ problems in related literature) has received
251   significant attention in the time-series forecasting community. The core intuition behind popular
252   methods for addressing temporal shifts is to normalize data distributions before processing by the
253   model and to denormalize the outputs afterward. This approach allows the normalized sequences
254   to maintain more consistent mean and variance between the inputs and outputs of the forecasting
255   model, i.e., P (XL                H                                L               H
                         Norm ) ≈ P (XNorm ) ∼ Dist(0, 1) and P (Y Norm ) ≈ P (Y Norm ) ∼ Dist(0, 1),
256   thus mitigating temporal shifts (i.e., marginal distribution shifts over time).
257   While the primary contribution of this work focuses on mitigating concept drift in time-series
258   forecasting, addressing temporal shift is also crucial for effectively mitigating concept drift. The
259   underlying intuition is that SAM aims to learn invariant patterns that yield a stable conditional
260   distribution P (YH |XSUR ). However, achieving this stability becomes challenging without fixing a
261   stable marginal distribution (e.g., P (YH ) or P (XSUR )), as these marginal distributions may vary
262   over time. Therefore, a natural solution is to learn the conditional distribution under standardized
263   marginal distributions which is achieved by temporal shift methods through instance normalization
264   techniques.
265   Among the various approaches, Reversible Instance Normalization (RevIN) (Kim et al., 2021) is
266   particularly notable and is utilized in this work due to its simplicity and effectiveness. Advanced
267   techniques, such as SAN Liu et al. (2023) and N-S Transformer Liu et al. (2022), also show promise
268   in mitigating temporal shift but require modifications to forecasting models or pre-training strategies.
269   Exploring these advanced temporal shift methods remains promising but is beyond the scope of this
      study.

      4.4    S H I F TS: T HE I NTEGRATED F RAMEWORK
271
272   By integrating SAM to mitigate concept drift and RevIN to address temporal shift, we propose
273   ShifTS, a comprehensive framework that addresses both challenges in time-series forecasting.
274   ShifTS is also model-agnostic, as it processes to identify stable conditional distributions, which can
275   be learned by any time-series forecasting model. The workflow of ShifTS is illustrated in Figure 2
276   and consists of the following steps: (1) Normalize the input time series; (2) Forecast exogenous
277   features X̂SUR that sufficiently support the target series, as determined by SAM; (3) An aggregation
278   MLP that uses X̂SUR to forecast the target, denoted as Agg(·) in Figure 2 and Algorithm 1; (4)
279   Denormalize the output time series. Conceptually, steps 1 and 4 mitigate the temporal shift, step 2
280   addresses concept drift, and step 3 performs weighted aggregation of exogenous features to support
281
      the target series. The optimization objective of ShifTS is described as follows:

453

454   adjustment via RevIN, while ShifTS\CD excludes the concept drift handling via SAM. Additionally,
455   conventional forecasting models that do not address either concept drift or temporal shift are denoted
456   as ‘Base’. We performed experiments on the Exchange datasets using previous three baseline
457   forecasting models, with a fixed forecasting horizon of 96. The results are visualized in Figure 3(b).
458   The visualization reveals the following observations:

490   6   C ONCLUSION
491
492
      In this paper, we identify that both concept drift and temporal shift issues can coexist in time series
      forecasting. While mitigating temporal shifts has received significant attention from the time-series
493
      forecasting community, concept drift issues have been largely neglected. To address this gap, we first
494
      propose a soft attention mechanism, SAM, which effectively mitigates concept drift in time-series
495   forecasting by incorporating horizon information of exogenous features to enhance generalization
496   ability. We then introduce ShifTS, a model-agnostic framework that tackles both concept drift and
497   temporal shift issues. Our comprehensive evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of ShifTS, and
498   the benefit of SAM is further illustrated through an ablation study.

      A        A PPENDIX A: T EMPORAL S HIFT AND C ONCEPT D RIFT
673
674   To highlight the differences between concept drift and temporal shift, we provide visualizations of
675
      both phenomena. Figure 4 illustrates temporal shift, while Figure 5 demonstrates concept drift1 .
676   Temporal shift refers to changes in the statistical properties of a univariate time series data, such as
677   mean, variance, and autocorrelation structures, over time. For instance, the mean and variance of the
678   given time series shift between lookback window and horizon window, as depicted in Figure 4. This
679   issue is inherent in time series forecasting and can occur on any given time series data, regardless of
680   whether the data pertains to the target series or exogenous features.
681   In contrast, concept drift describes to changes in the correlations between exogenous features and the
682   target series over time. Figure 5 illustrates this phenomenon, where increases in exogenous features
683   at earlier time steps lead to increases in the target series, while increases at later time steps result in
684   decreases. Unlike temporal shift, concept drift involves multiple correlated time series and is not an
685   inherent issue in univariate time series analysis.

747   We follow the commonly adopted setup for defining the forecasting horizon window length, as
748   outlined in prior works Wu et al. (2022); Nie et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024b). Specifically, for datasets
749   such as ETT and Exchange, the forecasting horizon windows are chosen from the set [96, 192, 336,
750   720], with a fixed lookback window size of 96 and a consistent label window size of 48 for the
751   decoder (if required). Similarly, for the weekly reported ILI dataset, we employ forecasting horizon
752
      windows from [24, 36, 48, 60], with a fixed lookback window size of 36 and a constant label window
      size of 18 for the decoder (if required).
753
754   In the context of concept drift baselines, several baselines like GroupDRO, IRM, and VREx require
755   environment labels, which are typically absent in time series datasets. To address this, we partition
      the training set into k equal-length time segments to serve as predefined environment labels.

847   D           L IMITATION D ISCUSSION
848
849   This work introduces SAM to address concept drift and proposes an integrated framework, ShifTS,
850   which combines SAM with temporal shift mitigation techniques to enhance the accuracy of time-series
851
      forecasting. Extensive empirical evaluations support the effectiveness of these methods. However,
      the limitations of this study lie in two aspects: First, the distribution shift methods in time-series
852
      forecasting, including ShifTS, lack a theoretical guarantee. For example, no analysis quantifies
853
      how much the error bound can be tightened by addressing concept drift or temporal shift compared to
854   vanilla time-series forecasting methods. Second, while this paper defines concept drift and temporal
855   shift issues within the context of time-series forecasting, SAM and ShifTS are not the only possible
856   solutions. Exploring alternative approaches remains an avenue for future research beyond the scope
857   of this work. These two limitations highlight opportunities for future investigation.
