011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            With the widespread application of Large Language Models (LLMs), it has be-
014            come a significant concern to ensure their safety and prevent harmful responses.
015            While current safe-alignment methods based on instruction fine-tuning and Rein-
016
               forcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) can effectively reduce harm-
               ful responses from LLMs, they often require high-quality datasets and heavy com-
017
               putational overhead during model training. Another way to align language models
018
               is to modify the logit of tokens in model outputs without heavy training. Re-
019            cent studies have shown that contrastive decoding can enhance the performance
020            of language models by reducing the likelihood of confused tokens. However,
021            these methods require the manual selection of contrastive models or instruction
022            templates, limiting the degree of contrast. To this end, we propose Adversarial
023            Contrastive Decoding (ACD), an optimization-based framework to generate two
024            opposite soft system prompts, the Safeguarding Prompt (SP) and the Adversarial
025            Prompt (AP), for prompt-based contrastive decoding. The SP aims to promote
026            safer outputs while the AP aims to exploit the harmful parts of the model, pro-
027
               viding a strong contrast to align the model with safety. ACD only needs to ap-
               ply a lightweight prompt tuning on a rather small anchor dataset without training
028
               the target model. Experiments conducted on extensive models and benchmarks
029
               demonstrate that the proposed method achieves much better safety performance
030            than previous model training-free decoding methods without sacrificing its origi-
031            nal generation ability.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2021), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023),
037   LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable success
038   across various language tasks, demonstrating significant application value. However, the safety
039   concerns associated with LLMs have also drawn attention from researchers and the community (Sun
040   et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024). As LLMs’ comprehension and generation capabilities become more
041   advanced, their potential safety threats also increase. A crucial issue is how to reduce the likelihood
042   of LLMs responding to harmful content and making threatening replies.
043   Currently, the primary methods for enhancing model safety are based on instruction fine-
044   tuning (Bianchi et al., 2024) and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Bai et al.,
045   2022a). Instruction fine-tuning requires training a pre-trained LLM on datasets containing threat-
046   ening queries and corresponding safe responses. On the other hand, RLHF involves high-quality,
047   human-annotated preference datasets and entails significant training costs. Furthermore, some stud-
048   ies have indicated that models aligned for safety using these methods may experience a decline in
049   safety performance after subsequent fine-tuning (Yang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024; He et al., 2024),
050
      necessitating re-alignment for safety after each downstream task fine-tuning.
051   To mitigate the costs of training-based alignment and further enhance their alignment capabilities,
052   some recent studies suggest adjusting the probabilities of tokens during decoding to improve LLM
053   performance, which is also known as guided decoding. Contrastive Decoding (Li et al., 2023a)
      introduces an amateur model opposite to the base model to reduce redundant information in the

      2.1   S AFETY A LIGNMENT OF LLM S
111
112   Since the widespread application of LLMs, ensuring their safe and harmless usage has been an
113   important topic. RLHF (Bai et al., 2022a) is currently the most widely used method for LLM align-
114   ment, which requires training a Reward Model on human-labeled preference datasets and applying
115   it for reinforcement learning to align the target LLM with human values. RLAIFBai et al. (2022b)
116   uses a dedicated constitutional model instead of human annotations to determine the priority of
117   preference data, relying on AI to identify preferences. Safe RLHF (Dai et al., 2024) introduces
118
      a Cost Model on top of RLHF, emphasizing the safety differences between different responses in
      preference data annotation and reinforcement learning. To improve the substantial training costs
119
      of RLHF, DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) simplifies preference training, enabling LLM alignment on
120
      preference datasets to only consider the target model and reference model. However, these training-
121   based methods still have high requirements for computational resources and high-quality preference
122   data.
123
124   2.2   G UIDED D ECODING AS A LIGNMENT
125
126   Recent studies have found that guiding LLM decoding by modifying the logits of tokens during the
127   inference process can enhance the capabilities of models.
128
129   With Additional Model. Contrastive Decoding (Li et al., 2023a) introduces a relatively weaker
130   amateur model alongside the target base model, treating the logits of the amateur model as noise
131   outside the target model’s logits, thus enhancing the performance of the base model by subtracting
132
      the confusion noise of the amateur model. In comparison, ARGS (Khanov et al., 2024) utilizes the
      Reward Model in RLHF rather than a generative language model to guide decoding. Specifically,
133
      ARGS selects the top-k candidate tokens at each decoding step, evaluates all candidate tokens using
134
      the Reward Model’s reward value, and selects the token with the highest reward as the final token.
135   SafeDecoding (Xu et al., 2024) applies the original base model as the amateur model and conducts
136   additional safety training on it. The tuned model acts as an expert model for contrastive decoding
137   to defend against LLM jailbreak. This method requires the LLM itself to have a high level of safety
138   performance. Proxy Tuning (Liu et al., 2024a) modifies the logits of a larger model by adding the
139   difference between the logits of a smaller model with and without alignment, enabling the larger
140   model without alignment to achieve performance similar to that of an aligned model.
141
142   Without Additional Model. The above methods of guided decoding require additional contrast
143   models outside the original model or obtaining new contrast models through training, which in-
144   creases memory overhead beyond the original model. Another innovative approach is to conduct
145
      guided decoding without introducing new models. RAIN (Li et al., 2024) utilizes the original large
      model itself as the evaluator and selects the most suitable output through a tree-based search. How-
146
      ever, since each judgment requires to interact with an LLM, RAIN incurs significant time overhead
147
      and is challenging to use in practical scenarios. Instructive Decoding (Kim et al., 2024) innovatively
148   adjusts instructions to obtain better and worse responses on the same model for contrast. Compared
149   to introducing additional amateur models, Instructive Decoding is a prompt-based contrastive de-
150   coding method that offers advantages in terms of inference overhead. ROSE (Zhong et al., 2024)
151   directly applies Instructive Decoding to LLM safety, demonstrating its significant potential for safety
152   alignment. However, the contrast prompts in Instructive Decoding and ROSE need to be manually
153   designed, which limits the universality of this approach.

281
282   4.1       E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
283
284   To validate the effectiveness and generalizability of Adversarial Contrastive Decoding, we conduct
285   experiments on multiple models and safety benchmarks to assess its impact on model safety. We
286   also verified that ACD does not affect the normal usage of models on general tasks. Subsequently,
287
      we conduct ablation experiments to investigate the reasons behind ACD’s effectiveness. Finally, we
      discuss the potential of ACD to decrease the risk of jailbreak attacks.
288
289
      Models & Benchmarks. We select seven different models for our main experiment. These include
290
      two uncensored models: Llama-2-uncensored-7b (based on Llama-2-7b (Touvron et al., 2023b)) and
291
      Llama-3-uncensored-8b (based on Llama3-8b (Meta, 2024)). These two models were instruction-
292   tuned on datasets without safety examples1 , helping to demonstrate our method’s effectiveness on
293   weakly safety-aligned models. Additionally, we included weakly aligned Bloom-7b (Le Scao et al.,
294   2023) and Guanaco (Dettmers et al., 2023) (including 7b and 13b), together with strong-aligned
295   Vicuna-13b (Chiang et al., 2023) and Mistral-7b-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023). We select five safety-
296   related benchmarks and sample 100 harmful queries for each benchmark to comprehensively eval-
297   uate our method: AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023), Malicious Instruct (Huang et al., 2024), Harm-
298   fulQA/DangerousQA (Bhardwaj & Poria, 2023), and Beaver Test (Dai et al., 2024).
299
300   Baseline. To demonstrate the priority of the optimized soft prompts, we compare ACD with In-
301   structive Decoding (Kim et al., 2024), the state-of-the-art model-free guided decoding for general
302
      language tasks, as our main baseline, including both the null-prompt contrast and opposite-prompt
      contrast: (1) Base: Regular decoding with a manually designed safe system prompt. (2) Null-
303
      prompt Instructive Decoding (nID): Using instructions without a prompt as the contrastive item.
304
      (3) Opposite-prompt Instructive Decoding (oID): Using manually designed reverse prompts as the
305   contrastive item.
306
307   Metric. Similar to other studies on LLM safety (Zhong et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), we apply
308   ChatGPT to evaluate the safety of model outputs and employ the Harmless Rate (HLR) to quantita-
309   tively assess model safety. Specifically, for each instruction in the benchmark, we prompt ChatGPT
310   to evaluate whether the target model’s response is harmful. HLR is defined as the proportion of
311   harmless responses out of all responses. A higher HLR indicates that the target model has higher
312   safety on that benchmark.

      Instructive Decoding. Results of HLR across multiple LLMs and benchmarks are shown in Table 2.

455   4.4   C OMPARISON WITH M ORE BASELINES
456
457   We compare the safety ability of ACD with other baselines including In-Contect Defense (ICD) (Wei
458   et al., 2024), Self-Reminder (Xie et al., 2023), Safety Tuning (Bianchi et al., 2024) and SafeDecod-
459   ing (Xu et al., 2024). ACD outperforms all these baselines across benchmarks. Here ICD and Self-
460
      Reminder are both prompt-based methods, which apply in-context rejection examples and reminding
      prompts to boost the safety ability of LLMs respectively. Compared with these two methods, ACD
461
      optimizes an SP to prompt safe responses from the embedding space, which could surpass these
462
      manufactured prompt. Safety Tuning achieves an overall great safety ability. However, as explained
463   by (Bianchi et al., 2024), the training data should contain general data (e.g., Alpaca) to avoid wrong
464   refusal of benign instructions. While these general pairs are included, the overall safety ability of the
465   LLM is limited (Yang et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024). SafeDecoding is proposed as a defense method
466   against LLM jailbreak. While it achieves excellent results in jailbreak defense, it does not perform
467   that well in safety alignment. The reason is that the the tuned expert cannot be safe enough to build
468   strong contrast during the model-based contrastive decoding, considering that the safety alignment
469   of the original model is weak.

505
      because the optimized Adversarial Prompt (AP) itself acts as an embedding-level jailbreak. By

507   outputs can be effectively reduced. This demonstrates ACD’s potential to defend against jailbreak

518   5     C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this paper, we introduce Adversarial Contrastive Decoding, a novel prompt-based contrastive
521   decoding framework together with Opposite Prompt Optimization, which optimizes two contrastive
522   soft prompts, the Safeguarding Prompt and the Adversarial Prompt, to build a strong contrast during
523   inference. Extensive experiments show ACD effectively improves the safety alignment of LLMs
524
      without heavy model training, providing an innovative method for lightweight alignment of LLMs.

      C    L IMITATIONS
776
      Though Adversarial Contrastive Decoding achieves superior safety performance, it still has some
777
      limitations. (1) As a contrastive decoding-based method, ACD needs to process two inputs for a
778
      single inference, which increases the inference overhead. (2) While the results indicate that ACD
779   does not significantly affect the models’ performance on general tasks, there might still be edge cases
780   or specific tasks where the trade-off between safety and performance becomes more pronounced. (3)
781   The stability and long-term effectiveness of the optimized prompts under continuous model updates
782   and potential drifts in language usage over time have not been fully explored.
783
      We consider these limitations as future works for improvement to enhance the efficiency, generaliz-
784
      ability, and robust usage of ACD in real-world applications.
