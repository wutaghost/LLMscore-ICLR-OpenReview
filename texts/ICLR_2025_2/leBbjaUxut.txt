009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have significantly advanced image synthesis but often face high
012            computational demands and slow convergence rates during training. To tackle
013            these challenges, we propose the Multi-Scale Diffusion Transformer (MDiT),
014            which incorporates heterogeneous, asymmetric, scale-specific transformer blocks
015            to reintroduce explicit inductive structural biases into diffusion transformers (DiTs).
016            Using explainable AI techniques, we demonstrate that DiTs inherently learn these
017
               biases, exhibiting distinct encode-decode behaviors, effectively functioning as
               semantic autoencoders. Our optimized MDiT architecture leverages this under-
018
               standing to achieve a ≥ 3× increase in convergence speed on FFHQ-256x256 and
019
               ImageNet-256x256, culminating in a 7× training speedup on ImageNet compared
020            with state-of-the-art models. This acceleration significantly reduces the compu-
021            tational requirements for training, measured in FLOPs, enabling more efficient
022            resource use and enhancing performance on smaller datasets. Additionally, we
023            develop a variance matching regularization technique to correct sample variance
024            discrepancies which can occur in latent diffusion models, enhancing image contrast
025            and vibrancy, and further accelerating convergence.

050   The advent of diffusion-based generative models has significantly advanced the field of image
051   synthesis. Models such as Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021),
052   and DALL-E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) have set new benchmarks by leveraging the robustness of U-Net
053   Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based architectures (Ronneberger et al., 2015), which are
      particularly effective for capturing multi-scale detail. Meanwhile, transformer-based approaches like

054
      DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2022), DiffiT (Hatamizadeh et al., 2023), and SD3 (Esser et al., 2024) have since
055   surpassed their CNN-based counterparts in both efficiency and in capturing complex dependencies
056   within image data. However, despite their operational efficiency, transformer-based models often
057   exhibit slower convergence, which necessitates extensive training iterations (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021;
058   Chen et al., 2024). This significant computational expenditure constrains their accessibility within
059   the research community and for smaller organizations, limiting their uptake and slowing innovation.
060
      A key advantage of the shift towards diffusion transformers (DiTs) has been the elimination of
061   inductive biases (Peebles & Xie, 2022) inherent in CNN-UNets, resulting in a simpler, homogeneous
062   network structure. However, it is well-documented that images inherently possess three fundamental
063   properties: translation invariance, locality, and multi-scale features. The absence of architectural
064   structures that enforce these biases in vision transformers (ViTs) necessitates implicitly learning these
065   properties (Ben-Shaul et al., 2023; Raghu et al., 2021), which may incur unnecessary computational
066   overhead and limit model capacity. Reintroducing these biases into ViTs, therefore, has been shown
067   to enhance performance relative to computational cost (Liu et al., 2021; Hassani et al., 2023).
068   Consequently, this paper poses two pivotal questions: 1) Do DiTs similarly exhibit this implicitly
069   learned behavior as observed in ViTs? and 2) Can such biases be explicitly reintroduced to diffusion
070   transformers while maintaining their generality and enhancing training efficiency?
071
      In the rest of the paper we primarily focus on the impact of transformer network architecture on DiTs,
072
      distinct from algorithmic improvements. We utilize the latent space Min-SNR weighting strategy
073   (Hang et al., 2023), with x0 prediction - a training objective where x0 represents the original, clean
074   latent data sample in diffusion processes. This approach offers a consistent prediction target across
075   diffusion timesteps and facilitates direct classification probe training at t = 0,where the network is
076   predominantly engaged in a reconstruction task. The training efficiency gains are thus compounding
077   with the enhanced convergence provided by Min-SNR. Finally, we introduce a regularization term that
078   improves image contrast and vibrancy when training with Min-SNR, which is particularly impactful
079   for unconditional models that cannot leverage classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2021).
080   Our main contributions are as follows:
081
082          • We propose a heterogeneous multi-scale diffusion transformer architecture (MDiT), em-
083            ploying distinct transformer blocks for image feature processing, achieving enhanced detail
084            capture earlier in training and accelerating convergence by 3.47× on ImageNet-256.
085          • We develop an explainability framework for the MDiT architecture by employing partial-
086            head rotary position embeddings, inspired by GPT-J (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021), and
087            layer-wise classification probes, which we use to explain the depth-wise functional behavior
088            of diffusion transformers and further optimize our architecture for enhanced image synthesis.
089          • We introduce a variance matching regularization technique, which corrects a sample variance
090            discrepancy with latent diffusion models trained with Min-SNR, improving image contrast
091            and vibrancy, and further accelerating convergence by 3% on ImageNet-256.

      3    A M ULTI -S CALE H ETEROGENEOUS D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER
114
      Diffusion transformers (DiTs) have demonstrated widespread success across generative modeling
115
      tasks, excelling in producing high-quality outputs. However, their architectural rigidity poses several
116
      limitations, including inefficiencies in parameter utilization (Crowson et al., 2024), challenges with
117   multi-scale feature representation due to their isotropic nature, and difficulties in adapting to diverse
118   modalities such as text conditioning and zero-shot aspect ratio changes (Chen et al., 2024). While
119   prior works have addressed subsets of these issues, we propose the Multi-scale Diffusion Trans-
120   former (MDiT) to tackle them holistically by reintroducing inductive biases, improving parameter
121   efficiency, and enhancing flexibility. This architecture serves as a testbed to explore whether explicitly
122   reintroducing such biases can enhance the generality and training efficiency of diffusion transformers.

139   Key Architectural Contributions: MDiT introduces two key innovations: a shallow U-Net-like
140   structure and aggregate blocks within the core. The shallow U-Net design reduces the hierarchy to
141
      two levels, reintroducing the inductive bias of scale and decreasing the parameter overhead associated
      with deeper U-Net hierarchies. Unlike typical diffusion transformers that use a 2x2 patch embedding,
142
      MDiT incorporates a 1x1 point-wise patch embedding in the outer U-Net level, enabling fine-grained
143
      feature processing at the full latent resolution. Aggregate blocks within the core complement this by
144   efficiently capturing a third feature scale, performing down-sampling within the attention layers to
145   bridge spatial representations without the additional overhead of deeper U-Net levels.
146
147
      Architecture Overview: As depicted in Figure 2, MDiT is structured with two levels: an outer
      level and a core, connected by a skip connection that treats the core as a “macro block.” The outer
148
      level processes features at the full latent resolution, using M and N blocks before and after the core,
149
      respectively. The core operates at a 2x downsampled spatial resolution, where aggregate blocks
150   alternate with MDiT blocks, parameterized by K, followed by a stack of L additional MDiT blocks.
151   The parameter set {M, N, K, L} enables heterogeneous configurations while also providing coverage
152   with the isotropic case {0, 0, 0, L} used in DiTs. This equivalence enables controlled experiments to
153   evaluate whether isotropic DiTs implicitly learn the spatial inductive properties of images.
154
      Hybrid Conditioning Scheme: To support flexible conditioning modalities, including text, MDiT
155
      employs a hybrid conditioning scheme. Cross-attention is applied within the core blocks for class
156   conditioning, as it restricts conditioning to areas of high semantic focus while efficiently managing
157   the O(HW ) scaling of cross-attention. In the outer level and aggregate blocks, modulated pre-layer
158   norm is retained, consistent with standard diffusion transformers (Peebles & Xie, 2022; Esser et al.,
159   2024; Crowson et al., 2024). To further reduce parameter count and computational overhead in cross-
160   attention enabled blocks, the time embedding is folded directly into an auxiliary token, replacing the
161   need for pre-layer norm modulation in these blocks. Additional details can be found in Appendices
      G.1 and G.4, with text conditioning experiments on CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018) in Appendix C.

      3.1   AUGMENTING THE D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER B LOCKS FOR I MPROVED E XPLAINABILITY
163
164   In developing the MDiT blocks, we follow HDiT (Crowson et al., 2024) by building upon the LLaMA
165   style transformer blocks (Touvron et al., 2023). However, to support the explainability analysis in
166   Section 4, our implementation differs from HDiT and LLaMA in the following two ways:
167
      Partial Head Axial-RoPE: Inspired by GPT-J (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021), our model employs
168   partial head Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2022) to achieve 2D translation
169   invariance by selectively applying positional embeddings to a subset of self-attention head channels.
170   While similar to HDiT (Crowson et al., 2024), we expand upon their approach by providing an
171   explanation for its effectiveness and limitations in Section 4. Further differing from HDiT, we utilize
172   fixed rotary frequencies centered in the upper-left corner, rather than a normalized resolution with a
173   centered origin, thereby allowing for easier extrapolation to arbitrary aspect ratios (see Appendix I).
174   Normalization on Q and K Vectors: We apply a layer normalization without affine scaling to the Q
175   an K vectors in all attention layers as proposed by Dehghani et al. (2023), rather than utilizing a RMS
176   normalization with learnable affine scaling as in Esser et al. (2024); Crowson et al. (2024). Layer
177   norm was chosen to enforce a zero mean, placing all vectors on a unit hyper-sphere (Riechers, 2024),
178   ensuring the attention vector L2 energy remains constant across layers – ideal for comparisons.
179
      Notably, these changes do not significantly impact performance; Additional details in Appendix G.1.

      3.2   S HALLOW U-N ET: S EMANTIC C OMPRESSION AND E FFICIENT R EPRESENTATION
182
183   In diffusion transformers, the transition from processing low-level details to higher-level semantic
184   information mirrors the dynamics observed in variational auto-encoders (VAEs), where data flows into
185   and out of an internal latent space (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Esser et al., 2021). This resemblance
186   suggests that standard patch embeddings (linear projections) in DiTs are insufficient for capturing
187   complex semantic tasks, placing excessive demands on downstream transformer blocks. The shallow
188   U-Net in MDiT mirrors VAE-like dynamics, with the outer level compressing features for the core and
189   reconstructing them on the output. This approach is equivalent to replacing standard patch embeddings
190   with increased-capacity transformer blocks, reintroducing scale-awareness while reducing the burden
191
      on the core. Empirical evidence for this interpretation is detailed in Section 4 and Appendix M.
192   Processing image tokens at the full latent resolution comes with an additional cost in the self-attention
193   layers, which we overcome by adopting neighborhood self-attention (Hassani et al., 2023) in the outer
194   blocks. This adaptation significantly reduces computational complexity from O(N 2 ) to O(N k 2 ),
195   with k = 7 strategically selected to balance FLOPS, roughly equating two outer MDiT blocks to one
196   core MDiT block. Moreover, the combination of neighborhood attention with Axial RoPE enables
197
      scaling to larger image dimensions without additional fine-tuning of the outer blocks, while also
      supporting larger resolutions by adapting the patch and un-patch blocks (Appendix I & J).

201
      Aggregate Blocks are interleaved within the MDiT core to represent medium-scale spatial features
202
      that are challenging to capture at the core’s token resolution. Each block processes inputs in a 2x
203   downsampled space using pixel shuffle, applies multi-head self-attention (MHSA), and restores the
204   original resolution with pixel unshuffle. A point-wise feedforward network (FFN) is then applied at

206   of attention heads is increased by 1.5x, equivalent to scaling the hidden dimension in the down-
207   sampling operation. Aggregate Blocks mirror the dynamics found in U-Nets, yet provide a lightweight
208   solution for medium-scale feature representation without the overhead of introducing a third U-Net
209   level. Parameter details are summarized in Table 1 for MDiT-B and MDiT-L configurations.

264   4     S EMANTIC AUTOENCODING B EHAVIOR OF D IFFUSION T RANSFORMERS
265
266   Using our MDiT framework, we observe that the DiT analog ({0,0,0,L}) inherently transitions
267   from encoding positional to semantic information as a function of transformer depth. Interestingly,
268   this behavior is followed by a reduction in semantic emphasis in the final blocks, mirroring the
269   functionality of an autoencoder. This implicitly learned behavior suggests a natural encoding-decoding
      process within DiTs and has significant implications for enhancing training efficiency.

      4.1     E XPLAINING D EPTH - WISE FOCUS WITH PARTIAL -H EAD RO PE
271
272   To enforce translation invariance in images, we utilize Axial RoPE to encode position information
273   within our MDiT architecture. This method extends traditional RoPE (Su et al., 2022) by concatenat-
274   ing the 1-D embeddings in the X and Y directions of the image sequence, applying them directly to
275   the self-attention layers (see Fig.5d). We then selectively apply Axial RoPE to a subset of feature
276   channels within each attention-head, allowing the model to ignore positional information if needed.
277   Partial Head RoPE Mechanism: RoPE views the head features in multi-head self-attention blocks
278   as complex numbers, where d real features becomes d/2 complex pairs. Complex rotations, governed
279   by RoPE coefficients R(m) = eımθ , are multiplied, introducing phase shifts to the vectors qm and kn .
280   This mechanism results in relative offsets of m − n, which reinforce translation invariance. In the case
281   of Partial Head RoPE, we treat θ as zero for channels above a specific threshold (rdim = dk /4 = 16
282   in our implementation), effectively bifurcating the channels into those that encode positional data
283   and those that do not. Additionally, the behavior of these complex pairs under RoPE implies that
284
      positional information is disregarded when x = R(m)·x = 0, allowing the magnitude of the complex
      pairs to serve as a measure of the encoding’s contribution to position or semantic focus.

323   is not evident in Figure 5a, indicating that models employing RoPE may experience a constrained
      number of effectively active self-attention neurons, dependent on the choice of rdim .

329   4.2   MLP P ROBES FOR S EMANTIC A NALYSIS
330
331   In order to cross-validate the findings from our RoPE analysis, we employed classification probes as
332   proposed by Alain & Bengio (2017), using them as an independent method to assess the semantic
333   encoding capabilities of our MDiT architecture. We utilized two-layer MLP classifiers with an average
334   pooling input layer, trained on the hidden state outputs from the MDiT blocks. The ImageNet-trained
335   MDiT backbones, frozen for this task, were set to t = 0 (no noise) and c = null (unconditional),
336   effectively operating in an unconditional reconstruction mode, enabled by the x0 training objective.
337
      Top-1 probe accuracy was then evaluated using the ImageNet validation set for both the homogeneous
      {0,0,0,12} and multi-scale {2,4,0,9} cases, mapping semantic encoding with network depth.
338
339   The results, illustrated in Figure 5e, offer several insights: Firstly, the top-1 accuracy curve gen-
340   erally peaks at approximately 60% of the network depth, highlighting the point of most effective
341   semantic encoding. Secondly, the curve illustrates distinct “semantic encode” and “semantic decode”
342   phases, reflective of an autoencoder’s functionality. Thirdly, the multi-scale configuration achieves
343
      a significantly higher peak in accuracy, benefiting from the focus shift enabled by the outer blocks.
      Furthermore, there is a clear correspondence between the semantic peaks in Figure 5e and the blocks
344
      identified as highly semantic-focused in the RoPE plots (Figure 5c), especially blocks 8 and 9. This
345
      correlation validates the RoPE analysis, confirming that blocks with heightened semantic focus are
346   indeed associated with improved semantic representations, as measured by the MLP probes.

351   We adopt the Min-SNR strategy (Hang et al., 2023) setting γ = 5, to significantly accelerate training
352   on the x0 objective - where the diffusion model predicts the original, clean images (latents). Our
353
      experiments utilize the FFHQ dataset (Karras et al., 2019) for unconditional images and ImageNet
      (Deng et al., 2009) for conditional images, with all images standardized to a resolution of 256x256
354
      pixels. All models are trained within the latent space of the pre-trained Variational Autoencoder
355
      from Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021), with a latent space size of 4 × 32 × 32, reflecting a
356   downsampling factor of 8 from the original image dimensions.
357
358   Training Hyperparameters: Consistent with the Min-SNR approach, we implement a cosine
359
      noise schedule with tmax = 1000 and employ the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of
      1 × 10−2 . Diverging from typical settings, we adjust β1 to 0.9 and β2 to 0.95, necessary for stability
360
      and supporting an increased constant learning rate of 4 × 10−4 with a batch size of 256 images.
361
      Additionally, we evaluate on an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) model using a decay of 0.9999.
362
363   Evaluation Protocol: Model performance is assessed by generating 50k images for each checkpoint,
364   following the protocol by Karras et al. (2019). We utilize the DDIM sampler (Song et al., 2021) for
365
      x0 , DDPM (Song et al., 2021) for ϵ (eps), and Euler for rectified flow (rf) objectives. Both x0 and rf
      use with 50 and 100 steps for plots and statistical tables respectively; ϵ uses 100 or 250 steps as stated.
366
      All measurements are without classifier free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2021) unless otherwise
367
      stated. We calculate several key metrics: Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), sFID
368   (Nash et al., 2021), Inception Score (Salimans et al., 2016), and Precision/Recall (Kynkäänniemi
369   et al., 2019). We also calculate the DINO-FID (D-FID) score using the DINO V2-L model (Oquab
370   et al., 2024), which Stein et al. (2023) have shown to better align with human assessments.

526   6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
527
528   We proposed the Multi-Scale Diffusion Transformer (MDiT), which integrates heterogeneous, asym-
529   metric, scale-specific transformer blocks to mitigate the high computational demands and slow
530   convergence rates typical of diffusion transformers. Utilizing explainable AI techniques, we demon-
531
      strated that diffusion transformers naturally adopt structural biases, effectively functioning as semantic
      autoencoders. This understanding enabled MDiT to achieve a ≥ 3× increase in convergence speed
532
      on FFHQ-256x256 and ImageNet-256x256, culminating in a 7× training speedup compared to state-
533
      of-the-art models while significantly reducing training FLOPs. Additionally, we developed a variance
534   matching regularization technique that enhances image contrast and vibrancy. Our results highlight
535   substantial potential for further architectural improvements in model efficiency. Future research could
536   explore a more exhaustive architectural sweep, investigate longer-term training and convergence
537   properties, and test behavior at higher resolutions. Studies could also examine alternative training ob-
538   jectives (Karras et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024) and enhanced inference techniques (Kynkäänniemi et al.,
539   2024). The proposed new directions hold promise for extending the capabilities of diffusion-based
      image synthesis models, potentially enhancing both their efficiency and depth of understanding.

919   four distinct conditions to further establish the effects of variance matching and training dynamics
920   on model performance. These conditions are: continuous variance matching (VAR on), no variance
921   matching (VAR off), and discontinuation of variance matching after 400k (VAR off at 400k) and
922   600k (VAR off at 600k) steps. The differential impacts of these configurations on FID, DINO-
923   FID (D-FID), and Inception Score (IS), both with and without classifier free guidance (CFG), are
924   illustrated in Figure 10. Our analysis reveals that all configurations with CFG stabilize at a FID
925   score of approximately 2.8 around 600k steps, indicating a practical convergence point. Conversely,
926   configurations without CFG continue to improve in D-FID and IS, suggesting potential overfitting
927
      benefits these metrics under extended training durations.
928   The absence of variance matching yields improved performance under CFG, but poorer performance
929   without CFG, highlighting a complex interaction between variance matching and CFG. Intriguingly,
930   when variance matching is discontinued at 400k and 600k steps, a nuanced trade-off emerges: all
931   metrics improve, reaching optimal scores at a slightly degraded FID under CFG compared to the
932   version trained from the start without variance matching. Furthermore, we observe an improvement
933
      in high saturation seen in Figure 38 when variance matching is discontinued, approaching the original

      variance matching in later training stages can enhance overall model performance, suggesting a

      strategic approach to the application of variance matching in training diffusion models.

       From these plots, we make three key observations:
973
974    Poor DDIM performance on ϵ objective: The DDIM sampler performance is significantly degraded
975    when compared to DDPM on the MDiT-XL-eps model. While it does show continual improvement
976
       with longer training, the DDPM sampler far exceeds it on all metrics except FID, and completely
       exceeds the DDIM sampler under 3-channel guidance. We believe this is a result of our training
977
       objective, where we follow Peebles & Xie (2022) and predict both the mean and variance of the noise
978
       distribution. Consequentially, the DDPM sample is able to make use of this additional information,
979    while it is discarded in the deterministic DDIM sampler. This explains why DDIM performs well on
980    FID, as its deterministic process favors sharp and focused samples that match the real data distribution
981    closely but often at the cost of diversity. In contrast, we observe superior performance with DDIM
982    over DDPM on our x0 models, which are only trained to predict the distribution mean. In this case,
983    the absence of variance modeling aligns more naturally with DDIM’s deterministic sampling process.
984
       Metric shift under 3-channel guidance: All models exhibit a metric shift when comparing perfor-
985    mance with and without 3-channel guidance, where an improved FID score is traded off with degraded
986    D-FID and IS metrics. This behavior is expected, as the 3-channel guidance approximates a CFG
987    scale of c′ = 1 + 43 (c − 1), meaning a CFG weight of c = 1.5 translates to c′ = 1.375. Well-trained
988    diffusion transformers, however, typically achieve a FID minimum at around c ≈ 1.3 (Peebles &
989    Xie, 2022; Crowson et al., 2024). D-FID, however, achieves its minimum at a higher weight due to
990    the stronger focus on semantic feature extraction in the DINO-v2 model, while the inception score
991    (IS) scales directly with the CFG weight. Thus, optimizing for FID at lower CFG values necessarily
992    leads to suboptimal D-FID and IS, reflecting the different priorities of these metrics: FID measures
993
       inception feature alignment, D-FID emphasizes semantic alignment, and IS captures class alignment.
994    Early convergence behavior: Similar to the previous section, both models exhibit effective FID
995    stagnation under CFG (DDPM and Euler), while other metrics continue to improve. Thus, while
996    extended training improves D-FID and IS, it may degrade the FID score, signaling that the models
997    are approaching practical convergence on ImageNet.

       During training, the output activations of the UnPatch module in the MDiT core exhibited a checker-
       boarding pattern, the impact of which on model performance remains unclear. This pattern may result
1301
       from the reduced constraints on gradient signals, given the output’s location N blocks from the latent
1302
       head, or the output blocks’ capacity to mitigate inherent noise in network activations. To explore and
1303   potentially address this issue, we devised a patch regularization method. This method computes a
1304   2D-FFT on the UnPatch output activations, calculates the mean spectral frequencies across batches
1305   and channels, and then determines the difference between this mean and the frequencies associated
1306   with checkerboarding. A ReLU activation is applied to ensure that only the excess spectral power at
1307   the checkerboarding frequencies is penalized. The specific steps of this process are detailed in the
1308   pseudocode provided in Listing 1.
1309
1310                             Listing 1: Patch Regularization Implementation
1311   def patch_loss(x):
1312       # Extract the activation shape
1313       B, H, W, _ = x.shape
1314       # We have (B,H,W,C) and want to perform the FFT on
1315       # dims -3 and -2 (i.e. H and W)
1316       # We also want to perform the spatial reduction twice
           # on -3 and -2 for W and then H
1317       # The output will then be of shape (B,)
1318       # Note that in this case, the mask will be H,W,1
1319       # (the 1 can broadcast to C)
1320       # 1) Create a mask to select the checker frequencies
1321       mask = torch.ones((H, W, 1), dtype=x.dtype, device=x.device)
1322       mask[0   ,W//2] = 0 # w-patch
           mask[H//2,   0] = 0 # h-patch
1323       mask[H//2,W//2] = 0 # hw-coupled patch
1324
1325       # 2) Apply the fft - have to convert to float
           # because fp16 is not supported
1326       xf = torch.fft.fft2(x.float(), dim=(-3, -2)).abs()
1327
1328       # 3) Compute the baseline mean over H and W, excluding the mask
           m = (xf*mask).mean(dim=(-2,-3))
1329
1330       # 4) Construct the goal tensor by replacing the patch frequencies
1331       xm = xf.clone()
           xm[:,   0,w//2] = m # w-patch
1332       xm[:,h//2,   0] = m # h-patch
1333       xm[:,h//2,w//2] = m # hw-coupled patch
1334
           # 5) compute the difference loss
1335       # We are using ReLU here to prevent penalization if the patch
1336       # frequencies are below the mean
1337       # It’s okay if the model learns to ignore them, but it’s
           # not okay if the model emphasizes them
1338       # Note the stop grad, which prevents back prop through the mean
1339       delta = torch.nn.functional.relu(xf - xm.detach())
1340
           # 6) Reduce the H, W, and C dims out, resulting in shape (B,)
1341       return delta.mean(dim=(-1,-2,-3))
1342

       While the patch regularization effectively mitigated the checkerboarding behavior, two issues were
1344   observed. First, loss spikes associated with the patch regularization emerged after 100,000 training
1345   steps when training MDiT-B on ImageNet-256, although these spikes did not significantly impact the
1346   MSE training loss. Second, there was an in-excess of spectral power at frequencies corresponding to
1347   the checkerboarding, indicating potential deficiencies in those frequencies which might negatively
1348   impact model performance. Consequently, we adjusted the regularization schedule to discontinue
1349   after 100,000 steps. The results of three experiments, along with their Fréchet Inception Distances
       (FID) at 300,000 training steps, are depicted in Figure 16.

1583   G.1      AUGMENTING THE D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER B LOCKS
1584
       In developing the multi-scale diffusion transformer blocks (MDiT blocks), we integrate elements
1585
       that reflect recent advancements in vision transformers (ViTs) and large language models (LLMs).
1586   These integrations are specifically chosen to enhance computational efficiency and training stability.
1587   Each MDiT block (see Fig.2) follows the structured sequence: Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA),
1588   optional Multi-Head Cross-Attention, and Feed Forward Network, with the following optimizations:
1589
       Bias Removal from Matrices: Aligning with current best practices in LLMs, we remove biases
1590
       from all matrices, reducing excess parameter count (Touvron et al., 2023).
1591
1592   Output Gate Removal: Following HDiT (Crowson et al., 2024), we omit output gates from each
1593   feed-forward and attention layer, reducing parameter count and increasing explainability by enforcing
1594   layer participation in the residual stream.
1595   RMS Normalization: We adopt Root Mean Square (RMS) normalization for input normalization,
1596   which is computationally less demanding than Layer Norm while providing similar benefits (Zhang
1597   & Sennrich, 2019).
1598
       GeGLU FFN: We incorporate Gated Linear Units (GeGLU) (Shazeer, 2020) into our FFNs. As the
1599   de facto standard (Rombach et al., 2021; Crowson et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023), GLU’s enhance
1600   control of information flow through their gating mechanism.
1601
1602
       Normalization on Q and K Vectors: We apply a layer normalization without affine scaling to the Q
       an K vectors in all attention layers, improving training stability particularly in vision-related tasks
1603
       (Esser et al., 2024; Dehghani et al., 2023). Layer norm was chosen over RMS norm to enforce a zero
1604
       mean4 .
1605
1606   Partial Head Axial-RoPE: Inspired by GPT-J (Wang & Komatsuzaki, 2021), we implement partial
1607   head Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2022) to enforce 2D translation invariance,
1608
       selectively applying positional embeddings to a subset of self-attention head channels. Further
       discussion follows in Section 4.

1637
1638   Most diffusion transformer models utilize adaptive normalization and gating mechanisms for mod-
1639
       ulating class and timestep information, a method effective yet complex when extending to other
       conditioning types like text-based inputs (Chen et al., 2024). To simplify this and enhance flexibility,
1640
       our MDiT architecture incorporates a hybrid conditioning scheme that utilizes cross-attention for
1641
       class conditioning and a combination of modulation and cross-attention for time. This configuration
1642   simplifies the integration of class-specific information and sets a common foundation for text-based
1643   conditioning.
1644
1645
       Cross-attention conditioning is exclusively applied to the core MDiT blocks due to its computational
       intensity, which scales with O(HW ). This selective use concentrates semantically rich information
1646
       within the core, optimizing processing capacity and avoiding semantic dilution across the network.
1647
       Within these layers, the time-step embedding, class conditioning token, and a null token are concate-
1648   nated prior to the attention computation. This configuration allows the model to selectively focus on
1649   temporal and class information or to ignore both on a per-token basis as proposed by eDiff-I (Balaji
1650   et al., 2023).

1675
1676   Following the approach in SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), we incorporated auxiliary conditioning in our
1677   ImageNet experiments, which leverages statistics from the data preprocessing. Unlike SDXL, which
1678   uses image dimensions for conditioning, we opted for aspect ratio and image scale, given our specific
1679   preprocessing steps of rescaling and center cropping. Moreover, scale information was utilized only
1680   for images that underwent upscaling during preprocessing, with the maximum scale value clamped
1681   at 1.0. Following standard practices, the auxiliary conditioning was dropped during training with a
1682
       probability of 0.1 to facilitate classifier free guidance.
1683   In the model, the scale parameter was encoded using 256 sinusoidal feature channels, with a maximum
1684   frequency of 1000, while the aspect ratio was encoded using an identical number of channels but
1685   within a frequency range of 500 to 2000. These auxiliary conditions were concatenated with the
1686   sinusoidal timestep embedding, which uses 384 channels, resulting in a conditional input size of 896.
1687   This combined condition was processed through a 2-layer MLP mapping network featuring a GeGLU
1688
       activation function. The output of this network serves as a global condition that integrates the diffusion
       timestep, aspect ratio, and scale parameters. Due to an increased learning rate, we implemented
1689
       a gradient flow reduction strategy to modulate the learning pace of the mapping network, linearly
1690
       interpolating between the active and detached (stop grad) outputs, rather than utilizing parameter
1691   groups. A similar strategy was applied to the embedding vectors used for class conditioning.

       The Aggregate Blocks, integral to our MDiT architecture as described in Section 3.3 and depicted in
1731
       Figure 2, are designed to effectively capture and process medium-scale spatial features. Mirroring
1732   the down-sampling and up-sampling dynamics found in U-Net architectures, these blocks adapt this
1733   concepts for individual transformer blocks, enabling efficient attention processing at varied scales.
1734
1735

1736
       def aggregate_block(x, temb, pos_emb_coefs):
1737          # x input is of shape B,H,W,C
1738
1739            # 1) Compute modulation scale for inputs to encode timestep
                scale_msa, scale_ffn = adaLN_modulation(temb).chunk(2, dim=-1)
1740
1741            #2) pixel shuffle the ada_norm output
1742            #    - downsamle x by 2
                x_down = rearrange(msa_norm(x)*scale_msa,
1743                ’b (h p) (w q) c -> b h w (c p q)’, p=2, q=2)
1744
1745            # 3) apply multi-head self-attention
                #    - applied to downsampled x (i.e. x_down)
1746            h_msa = mhsa(x_down, pos_emb_coefs=pos_emb_coefs)
1747
1748            # 4) pixel unshuffle the mhsa output and residual add
                #    - h_msa upsample by 2
1749            x = x + rearrange(h_msa,
1750                ’b h w (c p q) -> b (h p) (w q) c’, p=2, q=2)

1752            x = x + ffn(ff_norm(x)*scale_ffn)

1755   The functional details and operational specifics of the Aggregate Blocks are further elaborated in the
1756   pseudocode provided in Listing 2. Key design choices for the aggregate blocks and their performance
1757   implications are as follows:
1758
       Reduced Computational Complexity: Utilizing pixel shuffle operations for down-sampling and
1759
       pixel unshuffle for up-sampling within the self-attention layers effectively reduces the complexity
1760   of attention computations from O(H 2 W 2 ) to O( 16 1
                                                             H 2 W 2 ). By avoiding down-sampling in the
1761   feed-forward layers, we also prevent the additional computational complexity and memory I/O
1762   typically associated with larger weight matrices. Despite the added steps of down-sampling and
1763   up-sampling, the overall FLOPS required for an aggregate block remain comparable to those of a
1764   standard transformer block, as evidenced by the metrics in MDiT-L (3.78G for aggregate vs 3.92G
1765   for standard blocks).
1766   Parameter Efficiency: By maintaining the original scale in the feed-forward layer while downsam-
1767   pling in the self-attention layers, the parameter count is effectively reduced from O(D̃2 ) to O( 14 D̃2 ),
1768
       where D̃ = 2D following typical downsampling scaling. This reduction is partially offset by the
1769
       need for non-square Q, K, V, and O matrices, which increase the total parameter count for each block.
1770   To minimize this impact, the inner self-attention dimension is scaled by a factor of 1.5×, such that
1771   hA · dk = 1.5 · d. However, the resulting Q, K, V, and O matrices are sized at (4 × 1.5) · d, leading to
1772   approximately 2.6 times more parameters than would typically be expected for an equivalent capacity
1773   increase of 1.5 · d. These additional parameters function similarly to convolutional weights used in
1774   the up/downsampling processes of a U-Net. Specifically, the non-square matrices serve as individual
1775   downsampling kernels for Q, K, and V, with a common upsampling kernel for O. Consequently, the
1776   extra parameters in these matrices primarily contribute to dimensional transformations rather than
1777   adding to computational capacity through increased non-linear processing.
1778   Enhanced Conditional Focus: Preliminary experiments incorporating a third U-Net downsampling
1779   layer demonstrated limited conditional contribution from the inner layers, particularly in scenarios
1780   involving text conditioning. These findings align with those reported in Kim et al. (2023), where
1781   the removal of the entire middle layer of the Stable Diffusion U-Net (Rombach et al., 2021) had
       minimal impact on image quality. By selectively downsampling only the attention layers and strategi-


                                                          33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       cally interleaving these blocks within the MDiT core, we effectively add the semantic processing
1783   capabilities typical of an additional downsampling layer without incurring the computational over-
1784   head typically associated with processing conditioning that would otherwise be underutilized. This
1785   approach optimizes the use of computational resources, enhancing the model’s ability to focus on
1786   relevant conditional information where it contributes most effectively.

1788   G.6   G UIDELINES FOR PARAMETER S ELECTION IN MD I T
1789
1790   To address potential complexity in configuring the MDiT architecture, we provide practical guidelines
1791   for parameterization using {M,N,K,L}. These guidelines aim to streamline the design process and
1792   ensure computational efficiency while maintaining flexibility.
1793   Parameterization with {M, N, K, L}: The architecture’s heterogeneity is defined by the set {M, N,
1794   K, L}, which controls the distribution of computational resources across the outer and core levels:
1795
1796          • Constraints on K: K is restricted to even values, aligning with the repeated block pattern.
1797          • Balancing Outer and Core Contributions: The sum M + N (outer levels) is scaled
1798            inversely with K + L (core levels) to balance computational load. For Each increment of 2
1799            in M + N , K + L is reduced by 1.
1800          • Balancing Prior and Post Outer Blocks: We find that the outer blocks following the core
1801            are more important for image fidelity, while fewer blocks before the core are necessary to
1802            absorb noise and extract low-level features. Empirical tests suggest that a ratio of N = 2 · M
1803            works well for the x0 , ϵ, and rf objectives.
1804
1805
       Hidden Dimension Scaling: Hidden dimension scaling follows conventions adapted from U-Nets:
1806
              • Inner to Outer Scaling: For downsampling layers by 2×, the inner dimensions (dinner ) are
1807            scaled as dinner = 2 · douter .
1808
              • Aggregate Block Dimensions: Unlike typical scaling, we find that a weaker scaling of
1809
                dagg = 1.5 · dinner produces adequate results without incurring excessive computational and
1810
                parameter overhead. Notably, this is equivalent to scaling the number of aggregate attention
1811            heads dk by a factor of 1.5.

2093

2094

2095   on Patch Diffusion (Wang et al., 2023), where small random crops were used to reconstruct larger
2096   images during inference. Similarly, our model treats center crops as partial views of a larger context,
2097   thus demonstrating a comparable ability to reconstruct beyond the trained image bounds, leveraging
2098   the relative positional cues encoded by Axial RoPE - a task that would pose significant challenges
2099   with traditional learned embeddings.

       J.2   A DAPTING THE C ORE PATCH L AYERS
2414
2415
       We further explore the extension of the MDiT to larger resolutions, building on the successful out-
2416
       comes demonstrated in earlier sections. Given MDiT’s U-Net-like structure, which shares similarities
2417
       with models such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) and SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), we
2418   investigate the applicability of the HiDiffusion technique (Zhang et al., 2024) to our architecture.
2419   HiDiffusion enables the generation of high-resolution images by adapting U-Net down/upsampling
2420   according to a resolution schedule, thereby remaining remaining at the final resolution for all infer-
2421   ence steps. In the initial phase, covering the first p · T of the total T timesteps, the technique adapts
2422   the first down/up sampling layers to employ a 4× resolution change, allowing the inner U-Net layers
2423   to function at their native training resolution, thus facilitating large-scale structural and semantic
2424   development early on. For the remaining (1 − p) · T timesteps, it reverts to the original 2× resolution
2425   change, focusing primarily on fine-detail refinement. This approach allows the model to efficiently
2426
       achieve high-resolution inference at much higher resolutions than the model was originally trained
       on, without additional finetuning.
2427
2428   Following the approach in HiDiffusion, we adapted the patch embed/decode layers of the MDiT-core
2429   using average pooling and bi-linear interpolation to facilitate the 4× resolution change. Similar to
       the previous sections, we applied NATTEN to all of the self-attention layers, enabling full-scale


                                                         45
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2430
       detail refinement at the final inference steps. While this approach did improve global structural
2431   coherence compared to using NATTEN alone, it resulted in ghosting behavior and occasional subject
2432   duplication, as illustrated in Figure 33a. Notably, we were unable to find a combination of pooling,
2433   interpolation, and resolution schedule p that prevented such artifacts. Consequently, we explored the
2434   idea of patch replication without the resolution schedule, which behaves similarly to average pooling
2435   and nearest neighbor upsampling. This adjustment allowed the model to generate images with a
2436   4× resolution change when entering and exiting the MDiT-core. While this approach corrected the
2437   ghosting artifacts, it introduced new ones, as can be seen in Figure 33b.

       M     VALIDATING E XPLAINABILITY THROUGH D ESTRUCTIVE T ESTING
3511
3512   This section explores the predictive power of the explainability analysis proposed in Section 4
3513   by validating the expected contributions of each block and its respective position embeddings to
3514   overall image composition. To achieve this, we perform a series of qualitative experiments where
3515   portions of the model are selectively disabled (replaced with Identity transformations), effectively
3516   disrupting their functionality. We term this process destructive testing as it often results in degraded
3517   image composition, consistent with our hypothesis about the critical roles of certain components.
3518   For simplicity and clarity, we primarily focus on the MDiT-B model trained on ImageNet with
3519   configuration {2,4,0,9}, as previously shown in Figure 5c. An enlarged version of this figure is
3520
       presented in Figure 88, providing a detailed view of the complex magnitudes of the Q and K vectors
       within each self-attention head.

3562            output, necessitating “smoothing” by subsequent layers.

                significant structural changes.

3619
3620   Disabling entire blocks provides a measure of the contribution each block makes to the overall image
3621   generation process. This is achieved by individually replacing each block with an identity transform,
3622   such that h = x, effectively skipping the attention and feed-forward layers entirely. Using the probing
3623   visualization from Figure 88, we outline the following expectations:
3624
3625          • Blocks B0 and B1: These blocks are responsible for low-level feature extraction from the
3626
                input. However, their long-range positional focus suggests an additional role in denoising
                the input, as substantiated by the results from the previous section. Consequently, removing
3627
                either block is likely to result in total image collapse.
3628
3629          • Blocks B11-B14: These blocks refine low-level features due to their predominantly hybrid
3630
                focus. While removing them is expected to preserve overall image coherence, it may
                introduce errors in progressively higher spatial frequencies (e.g., finer details). Notably, if
3631
                Block B11 is responsible for smoothing patch artifacts from the core, its removal could lead
3632
                to near-complete image collapse.
3633
3634
              • Blocks B8 and B9: With their high semantic focus, these blocks are expected to induce the
                greatest semantic disruption when removed. Block B8 may also exhibit slightly more spatial
3635
                disruption due to its marginally lower semantic and increased positional focus.

3640   processing, may yield greater improvements in generation quality compared to augmenting other
3641   blocks.
