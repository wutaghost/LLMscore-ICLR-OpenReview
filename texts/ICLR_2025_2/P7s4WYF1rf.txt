009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Since the advent of vision-language pretraining, the CLIP model has become
012            a foundational model for many downstream tasks. However, most of the ad-
013            vanced CLIP models available today are trained primarily on English, making
014            them poorly suited for other languages. This limits accessibility for countries
015            where other languages are dominant. Given that training CLIP models requires
016
               vast amounts of GPU resources and data, which most countries lack due to the
               absence of companies on the scale of Google or OpenAI, this paper proposes
017
               an efficient and straightforward three-stage fine-tuning method, which allows for
018
               the conversion of the most powerful English CLIP model into models for other
019            languages. In these three stages of training, the first stage focuses on aligning
020            the embedding layer, followed by token fusion in the second stage, and finally
021            contrastive learning fine-tuning in the third stage. Meanwhile, to improve data
022            quality, we propose a translation filtering model to filter the data. In this work, we
023            target Chinese as the language of interest and name the resulting model YouCLIP,
024            which is currently the most powerful Chinese CLIP model, significantly outper-
025            forming previous models across all Chinese benchmarks. For example, YouCLIP
026            improves the text-to-image Recall@1 score on the COCO-CN dataset from 63.4
027
               to 73.1. Additionally, YouCLIP retains strong English capabilities, achieving a
               Top-1 accuracy of 76.9 on ImageNet. Despite these impressive results, YouCLIP
028
               requires the least amount of training resources compared to other Chinese CLIP
029
               models. All models and code for YouCLIP will be open-sourced.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Since the introduction of Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021)
035   by OpenAI in 2021, vision-language models have significantly advanced, becoming foundational
036   for a range of cross-modal applications, including image-text retrieval, zero-shot classification, and
037   generative models. A multitude of approaches (Zhai et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023)
038
      have been proposed, achieving remarkable success across a variety of downstream tasks, thereby
      revolutionizing our understanding and capabilities in bridging visual and linguistic modalities.
039
040   However, most current CLIP models are developed primarily for English, making it difficult for
041   countries with other native languages, such as Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and smaller languages
042   in less developed regions, to utilize them effectively. In this context, developing high-performance
043   CLIP models for these languages at a low cost becomes especially important, as not every country
044
      has big companies like Google or OpenAI, which possess vast amounts of GPUs and image-text
      data.
045
046   One of the simplest methods for developing CLIP models in other languages is to directly use image-
047   text pairs from those languages to train a CLIP model from scratch. However, this approach is costly
048   and yields limited results. For instance, CN-CLIP (Yang et al., 2022) was trained using 64 V100
049   GPUs and 200 million image-text pairs, yet its performance still lags behind English CLIP models,
050
      which are often trained on billions of data points. Some papers have proposed methods for distilling
      the text side of the model (Carlsson et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023), but these approaches tend to be
051
      relatively basic, offering low performance and limited practical applicability.
052
053   In this paper, we propose a low-cost, high-efficiency method for building CLIP models in other
      languages, which leverages the strongest English CLIP model as a foundation and transforms the

087
      On the methodology side, to reduce training costs and improve convergence speed, we proposed
      a novel three-stage fine-tuning alignment strategy. This strategy aligns the English text encoder
088
      with the Chinese encoder, followed by aligning the Chinese encoder with the image encoder. To
089
      begin with, let’s break down the structure of CLIP. The image encoder is language-independent.
090   The text side includes a tokenizer and a text encoder. The original English tokenizer cannot adapt
091   well to Chinese tasks, so the tokenizer needs to be replaced. The text encoder can be broadly divided
092   into an embedding layer and multiple Transformer layers. We observe that the primary difference
093   of text encoders lies in the embedding layer. If the concepts in the embedding layer can be aligned,
094   the subsequent Transformer layers can remain unchanged, resulting in identical outputs. Therefore,
095   in the first stage, we first use the English encoder for supervision, reconstructing the vocabulary and
096   training the embedding layer, while freezing the other parameters. The supervision comes from the
097   proposed cross-language alignment, where the features of matched Chinese and English captions
098
      will be pulled closer together, while the features of unmatched captions will be pushed further apart.
      By using only text information for alignment, the need to download hundreds of terabytes of images
099
      is eliminated. On the other hand, since only a small portion of parameters needs to be trained, this
100
      accelerates both the training and convergence processes.
101
102   Since the same concept may be represented by different numbers of tokens in Chinese and English,
103   perfect alignment is often unattainable. Therefore, we perform token fusion to solve the problem
104
      of inconsistent number of tokens for the same concept in different languages. The token fusion is
      performed with the first half of the Transformer layers of Chinese text Encoder under the supervision
105
      of cross-language alignment.
106

      reach zero, there is always some gap between them. This results in an even larger gap between

      2     RELATED WORK
127
128   2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE P RE - TRAINING
129
130   In the landscape of Vision-Language Pre-training (VLP), CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-
131
      training) (Radford et al., 2021) has emerged as a prominent dual-stream architecture, excelling at
      learning powerful cross-modal representations by separately encoding images and texts before align-
132
      ing them in a joint space. While CLIP achieved notable success through large-scale pre-training on
133
      image-text pairs, subsequent research has sought to address its limitations and optimize its perfor-
134   mance (Li et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2024; 2022; Fan et al., 2024). For example, EVA-CLIP (Sun et al.,
135   2023) was trained on LAION-2B with 2 billion image-text pairs using 144 A100s. SigLiP (Zhai
136   et al., 2023) was trained on the 10-billion-scale WebLi dataset using 32 TPUv4 units. DFN (Fang
137   et al., 2024) filtered 10 billion image-text pairs to obtain 5 billion high-quality pairs for training.
138
      It is evident that current CLIP models are becoming increasingly large in terms of data and com-
139
      putational requirements. Moreover, most CLIP models are designed specifically for English, while
140   relatively few are tailored for other languages. This limits the use of CLIP models in non-English-
141   speaking countries. The proposed YouCLIP in this paper, however, offers an efficient and convenient
142   method to convert English CLIP models into other languages, such as Chinese.
143
144
145   2.2   N ON -E NGLISH CLIP
146
147
      Compared with English CLIP, there are relatively few CLIPs developed for non-English languages.
      There are generally two main approaches to developing these models: training from scratch and fine-
148
      tuning existing English CLIP models. For the first one, these approaches involves using non-English
149
      image-text pairs to build a CLIP model from the ground up. Classic examples of this method include
150   CN-CLIP (Yang et al., 2022), WuKong (Gu et al., 2022), and Japanese-CLIP (Sawada et al., 2024).
151   CN-CLIP is trained on 200 million Chinese image-text pairs, while WuKong utilizes 100 million
152   Chinese image-text pairs. For the second approach, existing methods typically use techniques like
153   Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss (Carlsson et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) to align the text encoders
154   of Non-English and English. This enables the model to leverage the strengths of the pre-trained
155   English CLIP while adapting it for Non-English through fine-tuning.
156   However, we must emphasize that existing methods are still insufficient. While training from scratch
157   can effectively align images and Non-English text, it is often expensive and limited by the relatively
158   small datasets used. On the other hand, fine-tuning based on English CLIP is more cost-efficient, but
159   simply applying MSE loss results in poor alignment between Non-English text and images, leading
160   to suboptimal performance. Our proposed YouCLIP addresses these issues with a three-stage fine-
161   tuning process. Using a dataset of 1.2 billion image-text pairs, we have developed the most powerful
      Chinese CLIP model to date.

205   3.2       F RAMEWORK OF YOU CLIP
206
      Unlike high-cost, from-scratch training approaches like CN-CLIP (Yang et al., 2022), YouCLIP
207
      adopts a more efficient training method. It leverages the strongest English CLIP model as a teacher
208
      for distillation and inherits part of its parameters to achieve faster convergence, where the teacher is
209   SigLIP models (Zhai et al., 2023) in the experiments.
210
211   Assuming the strongest English CLIP model contains an image encoder fI (·) and and a text encoder
212
      ftE (·), the goal of YouCLIP is to cost-effectively train a target language text encoder ftT (·). This
      text encoder ftT (·) can then pair with the original image encoder fI (·) to form a CLIP model in the
213
      target language, where the target language is Chinese in our study.
214
215   Specifically, YouCLIP’s training is divided into three stages, as shown in Fig.(2). By analyzing
      the structure of CLIP, we found that, after changing the tokenizer, as long as the embedding layers


                                                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      ftE (·) and ftT (·) can achieve good alignment in different language, the parameters of the subsequent
217   Transformer layers can be inherited directly, allowing the final output features to align well as well.
218   To achieve this, in the first stage, we use the cross-language alignment as supervision to train the
219   embedding layer of ftT (·), which is randomly initialize. Due to the differences in token counts
220   between Chinese and English for the same concepts, the embedding layers may not align perfectly.
221   Therefore, we train the first half of the text encoder’s parameters to enable the fusion of varying
222   numbers of tokens. However, aligning only the textual encoders may result in a greater gap between
223   the Chinese encoder and the original image encoder. Therefore, in the final stage, we use the SigLIP
224   loss to directly align the Chinese encoder ftT (·) with the image encoder fI (·). In addition, To enable
225
      the model to support both Chinese and English, we implemented a random sampler that randomly
      selects either Chinese or English captions from the triplet data for training during the three stages.
226
227   To obtain high-quality English-Chinese caption and image triplets, we designed a Translation Filter-
228   ing Network (TFN), which uses the Chinese and English encoders obtained from the second stage
229   to encode the translated Chinese and English captions, respectively, and calculates their similarity
230   to filter out low-similarity caption pairs.

232   3.3     CROSS - LANGUAGE ALIGNMENT
233
      To reduce training costs, we first align the text side in stages 1 and 2, which avoids the need to
234
      download hundreds of terabytes of image data and accelerates the training process. To achieve
235
      this, we propose cross-language alignment, which treats different languages as distinct modalities
236   within CLIP. This approach facilitates contrastive learning to align representations across various
237   languages.
238
239   3.3.1    E MBEDDING L AYER A LIGNMENT
240
241   As mentioned above, breaking down the CLIP model reveals that one efficient method to convert an
242   English CLIP into a target language CLIP is to directly replace the word embeddings and tokenizer.
243   Once replaced, if the word embeddings for the same concept in both English and the target language
244
      are identical, the representations obtained after passing through the subsequent shared Transformer
      layers will also be the same. Then, perfect alignment can be achieved.
245
246   An intuitive way to replace the embedding layer is to retrieve the corresponding concepts in Chi-
247   nese and English, and directly use the English embedding to replace the Chinese embedding of the
248   same concept. However, this method cannot exhaust all concepts, which will cause many Chinese
249   embeddings to be vacant and cause instability in training.
250   In this paper, we propose using cross-language alignment as a supervisory signal, freezing the pa-
251   rameters of the Transformer layers while only training the parameters of the embedding layer so
252   that the embedding layers can be aligned effectively. Similar to the training method used in CLIP
253   for image-text matching, cross-language alignment aims to bring matching Chinese and English
254   captions closer together while pushing non-matching captions further apart.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
      Currently, most advanced CLIP models are developed primarily for English, making it difficult for
533
      non-English-speaking countries to apply these models effectively. To address this, this paper pro-
534   poses a simple and efficient three-stage fine-tuning approach to convert the most powerful English
535   CLIP model (using the SigLIP model in this study) into a Chinese CLIP model, named YouCLIP.
536   In these three stages of training, the first stage focuses on aligning the embedding layer, followed
537   by token fusion in the second stage, and finally contrastive learning fine-tuning in the third stage.
538   This three-stage process enables YouCLIP to become the most powerful Chinese CLIP model. Not
539   only does it achieve the highest performance across all Chinese CLIP benchmarks, but it also retains
      strong English capabilities.

618   The following is a detailed description of the datasets used for our evaluation.
619
620          • COCO-CN for Chinese cross-modal retrieval: COCO-CN(Li et al., 2019) dataset is an ex-

622            It features 20,342 images, complemented by 27,218 Chinese sentences and 70,993 tags.
623          • Flickr30K-CN for Chinese cross-modal retrieval: Flickr30K-CN(Lan et al., 2017) is a Chi-
624            nese image annotation dataset that serves as an extension of the Flickr30K dataset. It trans-
625            lates or rewrites the original English descriptions into Chinese, providing multiple Chinese
626            descriptions for each image.
627          • MUGE for Chinese cross-modal retrieval: MUGE is a large-scale Chinese multimodal
628            evaluation benchmark that includes a vast collection of paired image and text data from the
629            E-commerce platform.
630          • ELEVATER for Chinese zero-shot classification: ELEVATER(Li et al., 2022) is a bench-
631            mark dataset and evaluation platform designed to assess the performance of visual and
632            language models on multimodal tasks. The benchmark includes 20 image classification
633            datasets. Some of these focus on fine-grained classification, such as aircraft models, flower
634            species, and pet breeds. Others require more complex reasoning, like determining whether
635            an emoji conveys a negative sentiment or analyzing the spatial relationships between people
636
               and cars.
637          • ImageNet-1K for English zero-shot classification: ImageNet-1K(Deng et al., 2009) is a
638            widely-used image recognition dataset containing 1,000 categories that span a broad range
639            of object types, including animals, everyday items, vehicles, plants, and more. This di-
640
               versity makes it ideal for training models capable of recognizing a wide variety of objects
               across different domains.
