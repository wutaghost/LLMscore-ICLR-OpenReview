011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Under the Markov assumption of Markov Decision Processes (MDPs), an optimal
014            stationary policy does not need to consider history and is no worse than any non-
015
               stationary or history-dependent policy. Therefore, existing Deep Reinforcement
               Learning (DRL) algorithms usually model sequential decision-making as an MDP
016
               and then try to optimize a stationary policy by single-step state transitions. How-
017
               ever, such optimization is often faced with sample inefficiency when the causal
018            relationships of state transitions are complex. To address the above problem, this
019            paper investigates if augmenting the states with their historical information can
020            simplify the complex causal relationships in MDPs and thus improve the sample
021            efficiency for DRL. First, we demonstrate that a complex causal relationship of
022            single-step state transitions may be inferred by a simple causal function of the
023            historically augmented states. Then, we propose a convolutional neural network ar-
024            chitecture to learn the representation of the current state and its historical trajectory.
025            The main idea of this representation learning is to compress the high-dimensional
026
               historical trajectories into a low-dimensional space. In this way, we can extract the
               simple causal relationships from historical information and avoid the overfitting
027
               caused by high-dimensional data. Finally, we formulate Historical Augmentation
028
               Aided Actor-Critic (HA3C) algorithm by adding the learned representations to
029            the actor-critic method. The experiment on standard MDP tasks demonstrates
030            that HA3C outperforms current state-of-the-art methods in terms of both sample
031            efficiency and performance.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Sequential decision-making widely exists in real-world control tasks, such as robot control and
037   autonomous driving (Dorf & Bishop, 2011; Ibarz et al., 2021; Sallab et al., 2017). Generally speaking,
038   it can be modelled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where an agent iteratively takes action in an
039
      environment for transiting from one state to another (Puterman, 1990). Each transition is evaluated by
      a reward signal passing from the environment to the agent so that Reinforcement Learning (RL) can
040
      learn the optimal policy by maximizing the cumulative reward (Sutton & Barto, 2018). The Markov
041
      Assumption of MDPs asserts that the probability distributions of the reward and next state depend
042   only on the current state and action. Under the Markov assumption of MDPs, there exists an optimal
043   stationary policy which does not need to consider history and is no worse than any non-stationary or
044   history-dependent policy (Puterman, 2014). Therefore, existing RL algorithms usually try to optimize
045   a stationary policy by single-step state transitions.
046
      With advances in deep learning, many effective Deep RL (DRL) methods were proposed (Fujimoto
047
      et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018; Lillicrap et al., 2016; Mnih et al., 2016; 2015). Under the Markov
048   assumption of MDPs, they are usually based on the actor-critic method where the critic estimates
049   the Q-function, i.e., the expected cumulative reward after taking action at each state, while the actor
050   updates the policy to choose the action which can maximize the estimated Q-function (Schulman
051   et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2014). However, such optimization may miss the useful causal relationships
052   of state transitions, leading to sample inefficiency (Allen et al., 2021; Buckman et al., 2018; Du
053   et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020). An existing partial solution to this issue is representation learning in
      which a neural network is trained to infer the causal relationships of state transitions by predicting the


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      reward or future state of each state-action pair (Munk et al., 2016; Ni et al., 2023; Ravindran, 2004;
055   Rezaei-Shoshtari et al., 2022). Then, the sample efficiency of DRL can be improved by adding the
056   learned representations to the actor-critic method. Unfortunately, it is hard to train the neural networks
057   which can infer complex causal relationships, e.g., polynomial causal relationships and the basic laws
058   of physics (Andoni et al., 2014; Cranmer et al., 2020). Standard complexity-theoretic results strongly
059   suggest that there is no algorithm efficient enough for learning arbitrary target functions, even for
060   target functions representable by very low-depth networks (Applebaum et al., 2006). Therefore, the
061   sample efficiency for DRL is still limited in complex MDP tasks.
062   This paper addresses the above problem by augmenting the states with their historical information.
063   Based on the analysis in Section 3, we believe that historical augmentation can simplify the causal
064   relationships of state transitions by its inherent contextual information and increasing the search
065   space of the causal functions (Hallak et al., 2015; Sprunger & Jacobs, 2019). Therefore, we focus
066   on optimzing a history-dependent stationary policy in an MDP. Our DRL approach comprises two
067   key components: 1) Learning the state representations to capture the causal relationships in an MDP
068   and 2) finding the optimal stationary policy by the learned representations. Given an action and
069
      the historically augmented current state, our representation learning utilizes a Convolutional Neural
      Network (CNN) architecture to compress the high-dimensional historical trajectory of the given state
070
      into a low-dimensional space while predicting the future state. The compressed historical trajectories
071
      can be seen as the abstracted features which can represent the simple causal relationships and avoid
072   the overfitting caused by high-dimensional data (Andre & Russell, 2002). To keep the Markov
073   assumption of MDPs, our representation learning does not compress the current state. We add the
074   learned state representations to the actor-critic method. In this way, the causal relationships captured
075   by our representation learning can be utilized to estimate the Q-function and update policy. Therefore,
076   our new DRL approach can optimize the policy in a complex MDP with high sample efficiency. We
077   combine historical augmentation, state representations, and TD3 in our approach to formulate a new
078   DRL algorithm, Historical Augmentation Aided Actor-Critic (HA3C). The experiment on standard
079   MDP tasks, i.e. Mujoco control tasks and Deep Mind Control (DMC) suite, empirically demonstrates
080
      that HA3C outperforms current state-of-the-art methods in terms of both sample efficiency and
      performance (Brockman et al., 2016; Todorov et al., 2012; Tassa et al., 2018).
081
082   Our contributions are as follows: 1) Existing RL methods usually utilize historical information
083   to recover Markov assumption in dynamics. It is the first time in the literature that historical
084   augmentation can be used to improve sample efficiency when Markov assumption is satisfied. 2)
085   We propose a new DRL approach to address the problem of how to effectively utilize the historical
086
      information in MDPs. 3) Based on this approach, we formulate a new RL algorithm, HA3C,
      which outperforms existing state-of-the-art DRL algorithms, e.g. TD7 (Fujimoto et al., 2023). 4)
087
      Our examples, experiment, and discussion illustrate that in fact, DRL needs to consider historical
088
      information in complex MDP tasks.

097   policy π : S 7→ A, to obtain a reward rt = R(st , at ) and transfer to a new state st+1 . The return of

114   To improve sample efficiency, some DRL methods learn the state representations of the collected
115   state transitions and then add the learned representations to the actor-critic method (Anand et al.,

120
      by predicting the next state in DenseNet architecture (Ota et al., 2020). TD7 improves the learning
121   of state representations by AvgL1Norm and then combines the learned representations with TD3,
122   checkpoints, and prioritized replay buffer (Fujimoto et al., 2023).
123
124
      DRL algorithms need to consider historical information when the Markov assumption of MDPs
      is violated (Eysenbach et al., 2020; Majeed & Hutter, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019b). For Partially
125
      Observable MDPs (POMDPs), in which the states are partially observable, deep recurrent Q-network
126
      uses LSTMs to encode state transition trajectories in the Q-function estimation (Hausknecht &
127   Stone, 2015). As an improvement of deep recurrent Q-network, deep transformer Q-network uses
128   transformers to encode the state transition trajectories (Esslinger et al., 2022). As a famous DRL
129   algorithm, Dreamer encodes the historical information into the state at every time step in POMDPs (Ha
130   & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019a). In delayed MDPs, in which the current state and reward
131   may arrive at the agent with a delay (Katsikopoulos & Engelbrecht, 2003), researchers usually recover
132   the Markov assumption of MDPs by considering the historical actions (Bouteiller et al., 2020; Derman
133   et al., 2021). When the Markov assumption of MDPs is violated by the state abstraction, it is possible
134   to find a history-based policy which works in the abstracted space and is of the same quality as
135
      optimal policy (Patil et al., 2024). However, the history-based DRL for the dynamics which are under
      Markov assumption is largely absent from the literature.

138   3   M OTIVATION
139
140   Let ht = {s0 , a0 , ..., st } as the history up to time step t in a sequential decision-making task. The
141   optimal policy may change the decision rule in different time steps and select actions based on
142   historical information. In this case, we should optimize a history-dependent policy π = {dt |t =
143
      0, 1, ...} which selects action at time step t by decision-rule dt (at |ht ). Fortunately, based on the
      Markov assumption of MDPs, there is an optimal stationary policy π(at |st ) which is unrelated to
144
      time and selects action at by only the state st . This Markov assumption asserts that the probability
145
      distributions of state st+1 and reward rt depend only on the st and at as
146
147
                P {st+1 = s′ , rt = r|s0 , a0 , r0 , ..., st , at = P {st+1 = s′ , rt = r|st , at },
148   where P is the probability distribution in P . Let HR and SR denote the class of history-dependent
149   and stationary policies, respectively. Lemma 3.1 is the key of most existing RL algorithms (Puterman,
150   2014)[Thm. 6.2.10]. The different types of policies are detailed in Appendix A.
151   Lemma 3.1. Under the Markov assumption of MDPs, there exists a policy π ∗ ∈ SR such that, for
152   all t, Vπ∗ (st ) = supπ∈HR Vπ (ht ).
153
      Based on Lemma 3.1, existing DRL algorithms for MDPs usually optimize a stationary policy by
154
      single-step transitions. If the causal relationships in the modelled MDP are simple, e.g., there are
155
      only linear causal relationships in this MDP, such optimization effectively finds the optimal policy.
156   A classical result is that a neural network with a single hidden layer can successfully learn a linear
157   function (Andoni et al., 2014). However, it is still hard to capture complex causal relationships by
158   neural networks. Standard complexity-theoretic results strongly suggest that there is no algorithm
159   efficient enough for learning arbitrary functions, even for target functions representable by very
160   low-depth networks (Applebaum et al., 2006). In fact, a more complex causal function requires
161   neural networks to approximate with more parameters, samples, and time consumption (Bianchini &
      Scarselli, 2014).

214   compressed historical trajectories can be seen as the abstracted features of the historical information to
215
      extract the simple causal relationships. On the other hand, this compression will avoid the overfitting
      caused by the high-dimensional historical data (Ying, 2019).

      6     C ONCLUSION
512
513   Under the Markov assumption of MDPs, the probabil-
514   ity distributions of the next state and reward depend
515
      only on the current state and action. Therefore, given
      a finite Q-table, we can find the optimal policy in an
516
      MDP by a heuristic algorithm which only considers
517
      single-step transitions. Different from the heuristic
518   algorithm, DRL algorithms need to approximate the
519   causal functions by learning the causal relationships
520   in MDPs. In this case, DRL is often faced with sam-
521   ple inefficiency from complex causal relationships, as
522   a more complex causal function requires neural net-
523   works to approximate with more parameters, samples,
524   and time consumption.                                   Figure 9: Running times of different algo-
525   This paper addresses the above problem by augment- rithms for 1M time steps.
526   ing the current state with historical information. We
527   believe that historical augmentation can simplify the
528   causal relationships of state transitions by its inherent contextual information and increasing the
529   search space of the causal functions. Therefore, we focus on optimzing a history-dependent stationary
530   policy in MDPs and propose a new RL algorithm, HA3C. The main idea of HA3C is to learn the state
531   representations by compressing the high-dimensional historical trajectories into a low-dimensional
532
      space. In this way, we can extract the simple causal relationships from historical trajectories and
      avoid the overfitting caused by high-dimensional historical data. Our experiment demonstrates the
533
      superior performance of HA3C over five state-of-the-art RL algorithms on MuJoCo control tasks.

      A    D IFFERENT P OLICIES
757
758
      Time-related policies can be History-dependent (H) or k-order Markov (Mk ) (Derman et al., 2020;
759   Puterman, 2014). Denote Ht as the set of possible histories up to time step t. A history-dependent
760   policy π = {dt |t = 0, 1, ...} at t maps histories to actions as dt : Ht 7→ A. A k-order Markov policy
761   π = {dt |t = 0, 1, ...} at t maps k-order state transition trajectories to actions as dt : Sk,t 7→ A. A
762   k-order stationary (Sk ) policy is unrelated to time as π : Sk,: 7→ A. In general, a randomized (R)
763   policy selects the actions by a probability distribution as π(a|∗). π is a deterministic (D) policy if
764   and only if π(a|∗) ∈ {0, 1}. Based on the above analysis, we can obtain History-dependent Random
765   (HR) policies, History-dependent Deterministic (HD) policies, k-order Markov Random (Mk R)
766   policies, k-order Markov Deterministic (Mk D) policies, k-order Stationary Random (Sk R) policies,
767
      and k-order Stationary Deterministic (Sk D) policies.
768   The above policies are summarized in Table 2. The relationships among them are demonstrated in
769   Fig. 10. It is noteworthy that sometimes historical actions will be considered in decision-making. In
770   this case, without loss of generality, a historical state si|i≤t−1 can be updated by si ← si ∪ ai .

877
      In POMDPs, the states are partially observable. Define the partially observable state at time step
878   t as lt and the observable part of lt as ot . The causal diagram of POMDPs is shown in Fig. 11(b).
879   Under the faithfulness assumption, ot and ot+k are mutually dependent conditional on ∀k > 1,
880   {oi , ai }t<i<t+k (Kalisch & Bühlman, 2007). Therefore, in a POMDP, the optimal policy π at time
881   step t should consider not only ot but also the historical information {oi , ai }0≤i<t . When k is large,
882   long-length rollout estimation is needed in POMDPs.
883
      RL algorithms of world models, such as Dream, model the sequential decision-making as a
884   POMDP (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; Hafner et al., 2019a). They usually encode the historical
885   information at t by an encoder f t to construct st+1 as
886
                                    st+1 = f t (ot , at , ..., f 1 (o1 , a1 , f 0 (o0 , a0 )).
887
888   When t is large, some partially observable states will be encoded many times, leading to the loss of
889
      some important discriminative information.
890   Compared with POMDP-based RL algorithms, our HA3C can better adjust the considered steps
891   in history according to the actual task and thus effectively find the optimal policy in history-based
892   sequential decision-making.

1162
1163   The compared RL algorithms in our experiment are detailed as follows.
1164   • Online:
1165   1) TD3 takes the minimum value between a pair of critic networks to address the overestimation of
1166   Q-value and reduces per-update error by delaying policy updates (Fujimoto et al., 2018).
1167
       2) SAC is an actor-critic algorithm based on the maximum entropy approach. The objective
       encourages policy stochasticity by augmenting the reward with the entropy at each step (Haarnoja
1168
       et al., 2018).
1169
       3) OFE-TD3 increases the input dimensionality of the networks by representation learning to improve
1170   the sample efficiency of TD3 (Ota et al., 2020).
1171   4) TQC addresses the overestimation of Q-value by the combination of the distributional representa-
1172   tion of a critic, truncation of critic prediction, and ensembling of multiple critics (Kuznetsov et al.,
1173   2020).
1174   5) TD7 is an effective DRL algorithm which combines TD3, state representation learning,
1175   checkpoints, prioritized experience replay, and a behaviour cloning term (only used for offline
1176   RL) (Fujimoto et al., 2023).
