009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent, single-frame temporal action localization (STAL) has captured the at-
012            tention of the computer vision community. Due to the sparse single-frame annota-
013            tions, current STAL methods generally employ pseudo-labels strategies to bridge
014            the gap between weakly-supervised methods and fully-supervised methods. How-
015            ever, these methods derive pseudo-labels from single-frame of the corresponding
016
               instances, yet the intra-class affinity from the current single-frame to other action
               snippets remains neglected. To capitalize on this affinity, we design a dual-level
017
               prototypes guidance (DPG) method with the graph matching random walk (Gm-
018
               Rw) algorithm to achieve instance-level and video-level prototype guidance for
019            pseudo-labels refinement. For instance-level guidance, the Gm-Rw exploits the
020            high affinity prototype among instances of the current video to build intra-class
021            associations. For video-level guidance, an online memory bank is constructed to
022            iteratively summarize more discriminative prototype. After Gm-Rw builds affin-
023            ity among intra-class videos, an exponential moving average (EMA) mechanism is
024            designed to achieve dual-level prototypes guidance for pseudo-labels refinement.
025            Notably, the dual-level guidance is mutually reinforcing, prompting us to propose
026            a novel adaptive collaborative strategy (ACS) for dynamic optimization. Exten-
027
               sive experiments on THUMOS14, GTEA, BEOID, and ActivityNet1.3 reveal that
               our method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Temporal action localization (TAL) Field et al. (2007); Ma et al. (2005); Vishwakarma & Agrawal
033   (2013) is one of the most fundamental tasks of video understanding, which aims to localize start
034   and end timestamps of actions within video sequences. Thanks to precise boundary annotations, the
035   fully-supervised TAL (FTAL) Dai et al. (2017); Long et al.; Shou et al. (2017); Chao et al. (2018);
036   Zhao et al. (2020); Sridhar et al. (2021) methods have triggered remarkable progress. However,
037   the frame-level annotation of action instances is labor-intensive and time-consuming. To mitigate
038   this issue, learning with only video-level labels, weakly-supervised temporal action localization
039
      (WTAL) Zhang et al. (2020); Narayan et al. (2020); Luo et al. (2021); Ma et al. (2021); Huang et al.
      (2021); Luo et al. (2020); Li et al. (2022a) has drawn considerable interest recently. Conventionally,
040
      WTAL follows a localization and classification pipeline based on snippet-wise classification score
041
      but coarse-grained labels limit its performance. Fortunately, the single-frame temporal action lo-
042   calization (STAL) Lee & Byun (2021); Fu et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024) bridges a
043   gap between FTAL and WTAL tasks, adding only a timestamp for each video instance under WTAL
044   paradigm. This pioneering research consumes almost comparable labor costs as video-level annota-
045   tions while providing more pronounced localization results. This prompted us to address TAL from
046   the perspective of STAL. We provide more related work about TAL in appendix A.1.
047
      Special single-frame annotation for STAL provides sparse location information but brings important
048   feature representation. In this case, pseudo-labels methods are proposed, which exploit pseudo ac-
049   tion snippets from the WTAL paradigm to perform FTAL optimization. These pseudo-labels meth-
050   ods utilize similarity metrics to derive pseudo-labels based on single-frame features, which are then
051   propagated using dynamic programming algorithms. Unfortunately, this sparse annotation causes
052   sub-optimal single-frame feature representation, the following issues naturally arise: ❶ Insufficient
053   feature discrimination leads to an inferior metrics process; and ❷ Lacking feature completeness
      hinders dynamic propagation. Tackling these two issues could provide insights into the compre-

      2     E XISTING P SEUDO - LABELS METHODS FOR STAL
115
116
117   Point-level pseudo-labels. Point-level pseudo-labels of STAL refers to using a single-frame label
118
      as the action label in each action instance while following the WTAL paradigm for training. In
      the point-level weakly-supervision setting, each action instance is annotated with only a timestamp,
119
      showing negligible additional cost compared to video-level instances. Ma et al. (2020); Lee & Byun
120
      (2021) starts with single-frame annotation, mines pseudo action frames and background frames,
121   and constructs point-level classification results, making full use of single-frame supervision to train
122   the model. Experimental results show that compared with full-supervision annotation, single-frame
123   annotation greatly saves annotation time and can effectively improve the WTAL model. Its perfor-
124   mance is even better than the full-supervision method.
125
      Instance-level pseudo-labels. Although point-level supervision can provide more action-related
126
      information, due to the sparsity of single-frame annotations, the model in this mode still cannot
127   obtain complete action instances. To solve the quality problem of single-frame annotations, Fu et al.
128   (2022) starts from the similarity of single-frame and considers that the features of different fragments
129   in action instances should be similar. We try to use point-level annotations to mine training samples
130   for feature learning to ensure that two similar samples in different feature spaces are similar, which
131   enhances the compactness of feature representation and reduces intra-action variations.
132
      Pseudo-labels improving. At present, although research has made progress in improving the quality
133   of single-frame, due to the gap between classification and positioning models, pseudo-labels based
134   on CAS mining will inevitably introduce noise. To solve this problem, Li et al. (2024) proposed a
135   guidance strategy based on semantic neighborhood. The core idea is that the similarity of adjacent
136   segments is relatively high. This guidance strategy can help improve the quality of pseudo-labels
137   and thus suppress pseudo-labels noise. Experiments show that this strategy can obtain higher quality
138   pseudo-labels and achieve good results.
139   The above studies mainly improve the quality of single-frame from a single instance or video. Al-
140   though these methods have achieved results, they still face the problem of low single-frame quality.
141   To solve this problem, we designed a dual-level prototypes guidance architecture from the perspec-
142   tive of non-learning prototypes, which constructs the discriminability and integrity of single-frame
143   at both the instance-level and the video-level to improve the quality of single-frame and has been
144   effectively verified on the benchmarks THUMOS14, GTEA, and BEOID.

      3.4   A DAPTIVE COLLABORATIVE STRATEGY
325
326   To achieve dual-level prototypes guidance for higher-quality pseudo-labels of action instance, we
327   summarize instance-level affinity prototype Fτ and video-level affinity prototype Mc . Fτ is derived
328   from the current video, whose action instances have more similar action representations and show
329   stable and sustainable impact. Thus, we set ω1 to a constant value to maintain this sustainable guid-
330   ance. Differently, when video-level affinity prototype guidance is implemented, Mc summarizes
331   more discriminative affinity prototypes under the selection mechanism. To balance the contributions
332
      between the instance-level prototype and the video-level prototype, we introduce the adaptive col-
      laborative strategy for the dual-level prototypes guidance as shown in Fig. 2. We set ω2 to a variable
333
      that changes with the training epoch. We compute ω2 as follows:
334
335
336                                ω2 = λ · tanh(∆ · (epoch − start)) + ω1                               (8)
337
338   Where λ and ∆ are hyper-parameters representing the amplitude of the change of the dynamic
339
      weights. At the instance-level, the performance of affinity is relatively stable, and specifying a
      constant ω2 during model training could maintain optimal performance. As the weight of ω2 changes
340
      dynamically, the classification model first focuses on the current similar parts and gradually starts
341
      to focus on the parts with high discriminative affinity snippets of actions. The single focus of the
342   model is alleviated, and comprehensive prototype guidance of the entire instance is further achieved.
343   Comprehensively, the quality of generated pseudo-labels is greatly improved with ACS.

414   4.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
415
416   We employ the Inflated 3D ConvNet (I3D) Gkalelis et al. (2009), pre-trained on the Kinetics-400
417   dataset, as our feature extractor. The embedded module consists of a 1D conventional layer with the
418   rectified linear unit (ReLU) activation function. Each video is segmented into 16-frame segments,
419   which serve as inputs to the feature extractor, yielding 2048-dimensional late-fusion features. We
420   maintain the original number of segments as ψ without any form of sampling. Our model is opti-
421
      mized using the Adam optimizer, with a learning rate set to 1e-4 and a batch size of 16. Hyperpa-
      rameters are selected via grid search, with γ = 0.95 and ψ = 0.1. The video-level threshold θvid is
422
      established at 0.5, while the segment-level threshold θseg ranges from 0 to 0.25, with increments of
423
      0.05. Non-maximum suppression (NMS) is applied with a threshold of 0.7. In adaptive collabora-
424   tive strategy, we set ω1 = 0.2, ω2 = 0.04, λ = 0.2 and ∆ = 0.0025 for dynamically update to balance
425   dual-level prototypes guidance.

457   4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
458
459   Effectiveness of dual-level knowledge guidance. As shown in Table 4, we study the two major
460   elements of dual-level guidance, namely instance-level guidance for improving single-frame dis-
461   crimination and video-level guidance for improving single-frame completeness. The mAP@IoU
462   from 0.1 to 0.7 of base-line are 75.7%, 64.6%, 45.3%, and 21.8% respectively. After the baseline is
463   guided by instance-level prototype, we observe that the above performance indicators are improved,
464   which highlights the importance of instance-level guidance and verifies that our method is also ef-
465
      fective in the case of a single video. In addition, after introducing video-level guidance into the
      baseline, our performance indicators are improved by 4.2%, 4.6%, 3.8%, and 0.9%, respectively,
466
      with an AVG mAP improvement of 3.5%. Finally, by integrating the two guidance techniques, the
467
      proposed method achieves the best performance in all five indicators compared with the baseline.
468   For example, the mAP@IoU from 0.1 to 0.7 is 5.6%, 5.7%, 5.9%, and 1.3% higher than the baseline,
469   and the AVG mAP is improved by 4.6%. This shows that the proposed instance-level prototype and
470   video-level prototype guidance can work together and prove the respective effect of the dual-level
471   prototypes guidance.
472
      Effectiveness of ACS. In the above, we proposed a collaborative optimization strategy to improve
473
      the effect of instance-level prototype guidance and video-level prototype guidance on the entire
474   model. As shown in Table 5, the full model with ACS has improved in every benchmark indicator
475   compared to the full model without ACS, and the AVG has increased by 1.1%, which is very
476   consistent with the purpose of designing ACS, that is, to ensure that the prototype guidance in DPG
477   is diverse within the same class and encourage extensive learning of different features of actions
478   within the class. This also means that the collaborative optimization method of multiple instances
479   and multiple videos also promotes the improvement of model performance.
480   Effectiveness of M . Above, we proposed using memory banks to store features of the same class to
481   improve the integrity of single-frame information. Table 6 reports experiments with the number of
482   features M in the memory bank from 2 to 11. We can conclude that the value of M has a significant
483   impact on the performance of our model and M = 5 can obtain the best result 57.5%. We found
484   that when M is set too small, the learned features are not complete enough and cannot function as
485   a memory bank. When M is set too large, there is redundant single-frame information in a single
      frame in the memory bank, and the effect is not significantly improved.

531   5     CONCLUSION
532
533   We expand upon previous STAL methods which focused on generating pseudo-labels to catch up
534
      with the accuracy of fully-supervised. Unlike previous pseudo-labels methods, we extend the scope
      of generating pseudo-labels from corresponding instances of single-frame to dual-level intra-class
535
      instances. Thus, we refine pseudo-labels from the perspective of instance-level and video-level
536
      affinity prototype. Innovatively, we design the Gm-Rw algorithm to explore the affinity prototype,
537   and then achieve the collaborative dual-level prototypes guidance under the EMA mechanism. The
538   evaluation of our method on three benchmark datasets, including THUMOS14, GTEA, BEOID, and
539   ActivityNet1.3 demonstrates that our proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance.
