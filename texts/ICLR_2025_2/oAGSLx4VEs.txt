009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            AI-Generated Content (AIGC) is gaining great popularity, with many emerging
012            commercial services and applications. These services leverage advanced generative
013            models, such as latent diffusion models and large language models, to generate
014            creative content (e.g., realistic images and fluent sentences) for users. The usage of
015            such generated content needs to be highly regulated, as the service providers need to
016
               ensure the users do not violate the usage policies (e.g., abuse for commercialization,
               generating and distributing unsafe content). A promising solution to achieve this
017
               goal is watermarking, which adds unique and imperceptible watermarks on the
018
               content for service verification and attribution. Numerous watermarking approaches
019            have been proposed recently. However, in this paper, we show that an adversary
020            can easily break these watermarking mechanisms. Specifically, we consider two
021            possible attacks. (1) Watermark removal: the adversary can easily erase the
022            embedded watermark from the generated content and then use it freely bypassing
023            the regulation of the service provider. (2) Watermark forging: the adversary can
024            create illegal content with forged watermarks from another user, causing the service
025            provider to make wrong attributions. We propose Warfare, a unified methodology
026            to achieve both attacks in a holistic way. The key idea is to leverage a pre-trained
027
               diffusion model for content processing and a generative adversarial network for
               watermark removal or forging. We evaluate Warfare on different datasets and
028
               embedding setups. The results prove that it can achieve high success rates while
029
               maintaining the quality of the generated content. Compared to the inference process
030            of existing diffusion model-based attacks, Warfare is 5,050∼11,000× faster.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Benefiting from the advance of generative deep learning models (Rombach et al., 2022; Touvron et al.,
036   2023), AI-Generated Content (AIGC) has become increasingly prominent. Many commercial services
037   have been released, which leverage large models (e.g., ChatGPT (cha), Midjourney (Mid)) to generate
038   creative content based on users’ demands. The rise of AIGC also leads to some legal considerations,
039
      and the service provider needs to set up some policies to regulate the usage of generated content. First,
      the generated content is one important intellectual property of the service provider. Many services do
040
      not allow users to make it into commercial use (Touvron et al., 2023; Mid). Selling the generated
041
      content for financial profit (Sel) will violate this policy and cause legal issues. Second, generative
042   models have the potential of outputting unsafe content (Wei et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
043   2023a; Le et al., 2023), such as fake news (Guo et al., 2021), malicious AI-powered images (Salman
044   et al., 2023; Le et al., 2023), phishing campaigns (Hazell, 2023), and cyberattack payloads (Charan
045   et al., 2023). New laws are established to regulate the generation and distribution of content from
046   deep learning models on the Internet (Gov; Sin; Gui).
047
      As protecting and regulating AIGC become urgent, Google hosted a workshop in June 2023 to
048   discuss the possible solutions against malicious usage of generative models (Barrett et al., 2023). Not
049   surprisingly, the watermarking technology is mentioned as a promising defense. By adding invisible
050   specific watermark messages to the generated content (Fernandez et al., 2023; Kirchenbauer et al.,
051   2023; Liu et al., 2023b), the service provider is able to identify the misuse of AIGC and track the
052   corresponding users. A variety of robust watermarking methodologies have been designed, which
053   can be classified into two categories. (1) A general strategy is to make the generative model learn a
      specific data distribution, which can be decoded by another deep learning model to obtain a secret

054
      message as the watermark (Fernandez et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2023b). (2) The
055   service provider can concatenate a watermark embedding model (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik et al.,
056   2020) after the generative model to make the final output contain watermarks. A very recent work
057   from DeepMind, SynthID Beta (Syn), detects AI-generated images by adding watermarks to the
058   generated images1 . According to its description, this service possibly follows a similar strategy as
059   StegaStamp (Tancik et al., 2020), which adopts an encoder to embed watermarks into images and a
060   decoder to identify the embedded watermarks in the given images.
061   The Google workshop (Barrett et al., 2023) reached the consensus that “existing watermarking
062   algorithms only withstand attacks when the adversary has no access to the detection algorithm”, and
063   embedding a watermark to a clean image or text “seems harder for the attacker, especially if the
064   watermarking process involves a secret key”. However, in this paper, we argue that it is not the case.
065   We find that it is easy for an adversary without any prior knowledge to remove or forge the embedded
066   secret watermark in AIGC, which will break the IP protection and content regulation. Specifically,
067   (1) a watermark removal attack makes the service providers fail to detect the watermarks which are
068   embedded into the AIGC previously, so the malicious user can circumvent the policy regulation
069
      and abuse the content for any purpose. (2) A watermark forging attack can intentionally embed the
      watermark of a different user into the unsafe content without the knowledge of the secret key. This
070
      could lead to wrong attributions and frame up that benign user.
071
072   Researchers have proposed several methods to achieve watermark removal attacks (Ulyanov et al.,
073   2018; Liang et al., 2021; Li, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a; Nam et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). However,
074   they suffer from several limitations. For instance, some attacks require the knowledge of clean data
075
      (Ulyanov et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2021) or details of watermarking schemes (Nam et al., 2021; Wang
      et al., 2022), which are not realistic in practice. Some attacks take extremely long time to remove the
076
      watermark from one image (Li, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a). Besides, there are currently no studies
077
      towards watermark forging attacks. More detailed analysis can be found in Section 2.2.
078
079   To remedy the above issues, we introduce Warfare, a novel and efficient methodology to achieve
080   both watermark forge and removal attacks against AIGC in a unified manner. The key idea is to
081
      leverage a pre-trained diffusion model and train a generative adversarial network (GAN) for erasing
      or embedding watermarks to AIGC. Specifically, the adversary only needs to collect the watermarked
082
      AIGC from the target service or a specific user, without any clean content. Then he can adopt a public
083
      diffusion model, such as DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), to denoise the collected data. The preprocessing
084   operation of the diffusion model can make the embedded message unrecoverable from the denoised
085   data. Finally, the adversary trains a GAN model to map the data distribution from collected data to
086   denoised data (for watermark removal) or from denoised data to collected data (for watermark forge).
087   After this model is trained, the adversary can adopt the generator to remove or forge the specific
088   watermark for AIGC.
089
      We evaluate our proposed Warfare on various datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA), and settings (e.g.,
090   different watermark lengths, few-shot learning), to show its generalizability. Our results prove that the
091   adversary can successfully remove or forge a specific watermark in the AIGC and keep the content
092   indistinguishable from the original one. This provides concrete evidence that existing watermarking
093   schemes are not reliable, and the community needs to explore more robust watermarking methods.
094   Overall, our contribution can be summarized:
095
096   • To the best of our knowledge, it is the first work focusing on removing and forging watermarks
097     in AIGC under a black-box threat model. Warfare is a unified methodology, which can holis-
098
        tically achieve both attack goals. Our study discloses the unreliability and fragility of existing
        watermarking schemes.
099
100   • Different from prior attacks, Warfare does not require the adversary to have clean data
101     or any information about the watermarking schemes, which is more practical in real-world
102     applications.
103   • Comprehensive evaluation proves that Warfare can remove or forge the watermarks without
104     harming the data quality. It is time-efficient, which is 5,050∼11,000× faster than diffusion model
105     attacks during the inference. The total time cost is analyzed in Appendix D.

120   2        R ELATED W ORKS
121   2.1         C ONTENT WATERMARK
122
123   Driven by the rapid development of large and multi-modal models, there is a renewed interest in
124   generative models, such as ChatGPT (cha) and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), due to their
125   capability of creating high-quality images (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022), texts (cha; Touvron
126   et al., 2023), audios (Kong et al., 2021), and videos (Ho et al., 2022). Such AI-Generated Content
127
      (AIGC) can have high IP values and sensitive information. Therefore, it is important to protect and
      regulate it during its distribution on public platforms, e.g., Twitter (Twi) and Instagram (Ins).
128
129   A typical strategy to achieve the above goal is watermarking: the service provider adds a secret and
130   unique message to the content, which can be subsequently extracted for ownership verification and
131   attribution. Existing watermarking schemes can be divided into post hoc methods and prior methods.
132   Post hoc methods convert the clean content into watermarked content using one of the following
133
      two strategies. (i) Visible watermark strategy: the service provider adds characters or paintings into
      the clean content (Liu et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2023), which can be recognized
134
      by humans. (ii) Invisible watermark strategy: the service provider embeds a specific bit string into
135
      the clean content by a pre-trained steganography model (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik et al., 2020) or
136   signal transformation (Nam et al., 2021), which will be decoded by a verification algorithm later. The
137   steganography approach in our paper specifically stands for methods requiring deep learning models.
138   These methods use a deep learning encoder to embed a secret message in an image. Then a deep
139   learning decoder can extract the message from the image. The signal transformation approach in our
140   paper stands for methods using spread spectrum (SS), improved spread spectrum (ISS), quantization
141   (QT) and so on, to embed message. For different transformations, there exists a specific extraction
142   approach. For prior methods, the generative model directly learns a distribution of watermarked
143   content, which can be decoded by a verification algorithm (Fei et al., 2022; Fernandez et al., 2023;
144
      Cui et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023b). Specifically, Fei et al. (Fei et al., 2022) designed a watermarking
      scheme for generative adversarial networks (GANs), by learning the distribution of watermarked
145
      images supervised by the watermark decoder. A watermarking scheme (Fernandez et al., 2023; Zhao
146
      et al., 2023b) is designed for diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), which embeds a predefined bit
147   string into the generated images, and later uses a secret decoder to extract it. The service provider
148   can recognize the AIGC from his generative model or determine the specific user account.
149
      2.2         WATERMARK ATTACKS
150
151   To the best of our knowledge, one only work (Wang et al., 2021) considers the watermark forging
152   attack. However, they assume the adversary knows the watermarking schemes, which is unrealistic.
153
      And they only evaluate LSB- and DCT-based watermarks instead of advanced deep-learning schemes.
      Other prior works mainly focus on the watermark removal attack. These attack solutions can be
154
      summarized into three main categories, i.e., image inpainting methods (Ulyanov et al., 2018; Liang
155
      et al., 2021) for visible watermarks, denoising methods (Li, 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a), and disrupting
156   methods (Nam et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022) for invisible watermarks. However, they have several
157   critical drawbacks in practice. Specifically, the image inpainting methods (Ulyanov et al., 2018;
158   Liang et al., 2021) require clean images and watermarked images to train the inpainting model, which
159   is not feasible in the real world, because the user can only obtain watermarked images from the
160   service providers (Mid). Disrupting methods (Nam et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022) require the user to
161   know the details of the watermarking schemes, which is also difficult to achieve. The most promising
      method is based on denoising models. For instance, (Li, 2023) adopted guided diffusion models to


                                                                                                                      3

      purify the watermarked images and minimize the differences between the watermarked images and
163   diffusion model’s outputs. However, using diffusion models to remove the watermark will cost a lot
164   of time. Our Warfare aims to address all of these limitations under a black-box threat model.

166   3     P RELIMINARY
167
168   3.1    S COPE
169   In this paper, we target both post hoc and prior watermarking methods. For post hoc methods, we
170   do not consider visible watermarks as they can significantly decrease the visual quality of AIGC,
171   making them less popular for practical adoption. For instance, the Tree-Ring watermark (Wen et al.,
172   2023) is proven to significantly change both pixel and latent spaces (Zhao et al., 2023a), which is
173   treated as “a visible watermark” by Zhao et al. (Zhao et al., 2023a). Hence, it is beyond the scope of
174   this paper. For invisible watermarks, we only consider the steganography approach, as it is much
175   more robust and harder to attack than the signal transformation approach (Nam et al., 2021; Wang
176
      et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023a). We mainly consider watermarks embedded in the generated images.
      Watermarks in other other domains, e.g., language, audio, will be our future work.
177
178   3.2    WATERMARK V ERIFICATION S CHEME
179
      We consider the most popular type of secret message used in watermarking implementations: bit
180
      strings (Fei et al., 2022; Fernandez et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023b). When a
181   service provider P employs a generative model MG to generate creative images for public users,
182   P employs a watermarking scheme (e.g., (Fernandez et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b)) to embed a
183   secret user-specific bit string m of length L in each generated image. To verify whether a suspicious
184   image xs is watermarked by P for a specific user, P uses a pre-trained decoder MD to extract the
185   bit string ms from xs . Then, P calculates the Hamming Distance between m and ms : HD(m, ms ).
186   If HD(m, ms ) ≤ (1 − τ )L, where τ is a pre-defined threshold, P will believe that xs contains the
187   secret watermark m.
188   3.3    T HREAT M ODEL
189
190   Attack Goals. A malicious user can break this watermarking scheme with two distinct goals. (1)
191
      Watermark removal attack: the adversary receives a generated image from the service provider,
      which contains the secret watermark associated with him. He aims to erase the watermark from the
192
      generated image, and then use it freely without the constraint of the service policy, as the provider
193
      is not able to identify the watermarks and track him anymore. (2) Watermark forging attack: the
194   adversary tries to frame up a victim user by forging the victim’s watermark on a malicious image
195   (from another model or created by humans). Then the adversary can distribute the image on the
196   Internet. The service provider will attribute to the wrong user.
197
      Adversary’s capability. We consider the black-box scenario, where the adversary can only obtain the
198
      generated image and has no knowledge of the employed generative model or watermark scheme. This
199
      is practical, as many service providers only release APIs for users to use their models without leaking
200   any information about the details of the backend models MG and MD . We further assume that all
201   the generated images from the target service are watermark-protected, so the adversary cannot collect
202   any clean images. These assumptions increase the attack difficulty compared to prior works (Ulyanov
203   et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2021; Nam et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022).
204
205   4     W A R F A R E : A U NIFIED ATTACK M ETHODOLOGY
206
207   We introduce Warfare to manipulate watermarks with the above goals. Let xi denote a clean image,
208   and x′i denote the corresponding watermarked image. These two images are visually indistinguishable.
209   Our goal is to establish a bi-directional mapping xi ←→ x′i . For the watermark removal attack, we
210   can derive xi from x′i . For the watermark forging attack, we can construct x′i from xi .
211   However, it is challenging for the adversary to identify the relationship between xi and x′i , as he has
212   no access to the clean image xi . To address this issue, the adversary can adopt a pre-trained denoising
213   model to convert x′i into a mediator image x̂i . Due to the denoising operation, x̂i is visually different
214   from xi , but does not contain the watermark. It will follow a similar ”non-watermarked” distribution
215   as xi . Then the adversary can train a GAN model between xi and x′i , which is guided by x̂i . Figure 1
      shows the overview of Warfare, consisting of three steps. Below, we describe the details.

226   4.1         DATA C OLLECTION
227   The adversary collects a set of images x′i generated by the target service provider for one user. All the
228   collected data contain one specific watermark m associated with this user. For the watermark removal
229   attack, the adversary can query the service to collect the watermarked images with his own account,
230   from which he aims to remove the watermark. For the watermark forging attack, the adversary can
231   possibly collect such data from the victim user’s social account. This is feasible as people enjoy
232   sharing their created content on the Internet and adding tags to indicate the used service2 . Then the
233   adversary can forge the watermark of the victim user on any images to cause wrong attribution. In
234   either case, a dataset X ′ = {x′i |x′i ∼ (MG , m)} is established, where MG is the service provider’s
235
      generative model.
236   4.2         DATA P RE - PROCESSING
237   Given the collected watermarked dataset X ′ , since the adversary does not have the corresponding
238   non-watermarked dataset X , he cannot directly build the mapping. Instead, he can adopt a public
239   pre-trained denoising model H to preprocess X ′ and obtain the corresponding mediator dataset X̂ .
240   The goal of the denoising model is to remove the watermark m from X ′ . Since existing watermarking
241   schemes are designed to be very robust, we have to increase the denoising strength significantly, in
242   order to distort the embedded watermark. Therefore, we first add very large-scale noise ϵi into x′i
243   and then apply a diffusion model H to denoise the images, i.e., X̂ = {x̂i |H(x′i + ϵi ) = x̂i , x′i ∈
244   X ′ , ϵi ∈ N (0, I)}. This will make x̂i highly visually different from x′i and xi . Figure 6 shows
245   some visualization results of x′i and x̂i , and we can observe that they keep some similar semantic
246   information but look very different. Table 3 proves that x̂i does not contain any watermark information
247   due to the injected large noise and strong denoising operation.
248
      The mediator dataset X̂ can be seen as being drawn from the same ”non-watermarked” distribution as
249   X , which is different from X ′ of the ”watermarked” distribution. Therefore, it can help discriminate
250   watermarking images from non-watermarked images and build connections between them. This is
251   achieved in the next step, as detailed below.
252
      4.3         M ODEL T RAINING
253
254   With the watermarked data x′ and non-watermarked data x̂, the adversary can train a GAN model to
255   add or remove watermarks. This GAN model consists of a generator G and a discriminator D: G is
256   used to generate x from x′ (watermark removal) or generate x′ from x (watermark forging); D is
257
      used to discriminate whether the input is drawn from the distribution of watermarked images x′ or
      the distribution of non-watermarked images x̂. Below, we describe these two attacks.
258
259   Watermark removal attack. In this attack, the generator G is built to obtain x from x′ , i.e.,
260   x = G(x′ ), where x′ and x should be visually indistinguishable. x generated by G should make
      D believe it is from the same non-watermarked image distribution as x̂, because x should be a
261
      non-watermarked image. Meanwhile, D should recognize x as a watermarked image, since it is very
262   close to x′ . Therefore, the loss functions LG for G and LD for D are:

500   6 P OTENTIAL D EFENSES FOR S ERVICE P ROVIDERS
501   Although Warfare is an effective method for removing or forging a specific watermark in images,
502   there are some possible defense methods against our attack. First, large companies can assign a
503   group of watermarks to an account to identify the identity. When adding watermarks to images, the
504   watermark can be randomly selected from the group of watermarks, which can hinder the adversary
505   from obtaining images containing the same watermark. However, such a method requires a longer
506   length of embedded watermarks to meet the population of users, which will decrease image quality
507   because embedding a longer watermark will damage the image. We provide a case study to verify
508   such a defense. In our implementation, we choose to use two bit strings for one user, i.e., m1 is
509
      ’10001000100010001000100010001000’ and m2 is ’11100011101010101000010000001011’. Note
      that the Hamming Distance between m1 and m2 is 12, which means that there are 12 bits in m1 and
510
      m2 are different. We assume that m1 and m2 will be used with equal probability. Therefore, half of
511
      the collected data contain m1 and others contain m2 . We evaluate Warfare on this collected dataset.
512   For the watermark removal attack, the bit accuracy for m1 after Warfare is 71.04%. And the bit
513   accuracy for m2 after Warfare is 64.87%. Note that the ideal bit accuracy after the removal attack
514   is (32 − 12)/32 ∗ 100% = 62.50%. Therefore, our method can maintain the attack success rate to
515   some degree. For the watermark forging attack, the bit accuracy for m1 after Warfare is 87.53%.
516   And the bit accuracy for m2 after Warfare is 69.21%. We notice that the ideal bit accuracy for the
517   forging attack is (32 − 12 + 6)/32 ∗ 100% = 81.25%, which means that 26 bits can be correctly
518   recognized. The results indicate that the generator does not equally learn m1 and m2 . We think it
519   is because of the randomness in the training process. On the other hand, the results indicate that
520
      such a defense can improve the robustness of the watermark. However, we find Warfare can still
      remove or forge one of the two watermarks. This means that such a defense can only alleviate security
521
      problems instead of addressing them thoroughly.
522
523   Another defense is to design a more robust watermarking scheme, which can defend against removal
524   attacks from diffusion models. Because Warfare requires diffusion models to remove the water-
525   marks. The two methods mentioned above have the potential to defend against Warfare but have
526
      different shortcomings, such as decreasing image quality, requiring a newly designed coding scheme,
      and requiring a newly designed robust watermarking scheme. Therefore, Warfare will be a threat
527
      for future years.

529   7 L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
530   In this paper, we consider a practical threat to AIGC protection and regulation schemes, which
531   are based on the state-of-the-art robust and invisible watermarking technologies. We introduce
532   Warfare, a unified attack framework to effectively remove or forge watermarks over AIGC while
533   maintaining good image quality. With Warfare, the adversary only requires watermarked images
534   without their corresponding clean ones, making it a real-world threat. Through comprehensive
535   experiments, we prove that Warfare has strong few-shot generalization abilities to fit unseen
536
      watermarks, which makes it more powerful. Furthermore, we show that Warfare can easily replace
      a watermark in the collected data with another new one, in Appendix E.
537
538   We discuss the potential usage of Warfare for larger-resolution and more complex images, in
539   real-world scenarios. Further improvement over Warfare is probable with more advanced GAN
      structures and training strategies.

809   limitation is critical for an attack based on diffusion models. Therefore, we appeal to comprehensively
      evaluate the performance of the watermark removal task for various datasets.

1074   The advent of AI-generated content has ushered in an era marked by unparalleled creativity and
1075   efficiency, but this technological leap is not without its ethical and legal ramifications. For example, a
1076   very recent case where Taylor Swift’s fake photos are circulated on X, which are made by generative
1077   models. On the other hand, the Gemini AI model conducted by Google Inc., is believed to generate
1078   biased content, by making white famous people black. Clearly, the ethical dilemma lies in recognizing
1079   the owner of content, as well as discerning the ethical implications of content manipulation. This
       resonates not only with the creative industries but extends to broader societal implications, particularly


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       in the context of misinformation and deepfakes. To mitigate the harm from fake content and biased
1081   content and better attribute the owner of the generated content, big companies, like OpenAI and
1082   Adobe, have developed and used watermarking methods, such as C2PA, in their products, such as
1083   DALL·E 3.
1084
       The deployment of content watermarking technologies emerges as a potential solution to safeguard
1085
       intellectual property in the realm of AI-generated content. However, this introduces its own set of
1086
       ethical considerations, when considering its robustness. While content watermarking provides a
1087   mechanism for tracing the origin of content and protecting the rights of creators, it concurrently raises
1088   concerns about potential attacks against such technologies to escape from being watermarked or forge
1089   another’s watermark.
1090
       Significantly, one of the vital parts of the effectiveness of content watermarking technologies is
1091
       contingent upon their resilience to attacks aimed at their removal or forgery. As shown in our paper,
1092
       the adversary can manipulate the existing watermarks in the generated content to achieve malicious
1093   purposes, including unauthorized use, manipulation of AI-generated content, and framing up others.
1094   Addressing these vulnerabilities requires a comprehensive understanding of potential attacks and the
1095   development of robust watermarking techniques that can withstand sophisticated adversarial attempts.
1096
       Based on our experiments, we can find that the removal or forgery of watermarks not only undermines
1097
       the protection of intellectual property but also amplifies the risks associated with the misuse of
1098
       AI-generated content. The malicious alteration of content, coupled with the absence of reliable
1099   watermarking, exacerbates the challenges associated with content verification and attribution. Conse-
1100   quently, mitigating the threat of attacks on content watermarks is paramount for ensuring the integrity
1101   and trustworthiness of AI-generated content in various domains, including journalism, entertainment,
1102   and education. This asks us to develop more advanced content watermarking methods.
1103
       Specifically, there are two benefits brought by our attack. First, in Section 6, we prove that the
1104
       group-watermarking method is promising against Warfare. It provides the big companies with a
1105   lightweight scheme to improve their current watermarking methods, without developing new models.
1106   Second, our attack could become a red-teaming evaluation method to help companies develop more
1107   robust and secure watermarking schemes. Developers can adopt our method to test their current
1108   watermarking method and conduct specific adjustment to further defend attacks.
1109
       In conclusion, we think that the proposed Warfare will cause some malicious users to freely make
1110
       AIGC for commercial use and frame other users by spreading illegal AIGC with forged watermarks.
1111   On the other hand, we think besides these negative impacts, our work will encourage others to explore
1112   a more robust and reliable watermark for AIGC, which has a positive impact on society. It can be
1113   achieved only after we have a deeper study on attacks.
