009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Finetuning large language models on instruction data is an important step in
012            enriching the knowledge learned during pre-training and improving instruction-
013            following capabilities. As the number of instruction datasets continues to grow,
014            selecting the right data to achieve optimal results becomes increasingly important.
015            In this work, we ask a prominent question: How can we determine the optimal
016
               subset of data for effective training? While much of the existing research primarily
               emphasizes local criteria, such as instance quality, for subset selection, we argue
017
               that a global approach focused on data diversity is more critical. Our approach
018
               utilizes k-means clustering to ensure that the selected subset effectively represents
019            the full dataset. We propose an iterative refinement method inspired by active
020            learning techniques to resample instances from clusters, with the importance and
021            sampling weight of each cluster being reassessed in every training iteration. This
022            method allows us to reduce the effect of outliers and automatically filter out
023            clusters containing low-quality data. Through extensive evaluation across natural
024            language reasoning, general world knowledge, code and math reasoning tasks, and
025            by fine-tuning models from various families, we observe consistent improvements,
026            achieving a 7% increase over the random selection and a 3.8% improvement
027
               over state-of-the-art sampling methods. Our work highlights the significance of
               diversity-first sampling when finetuning LLMs to enhance performance across a
028
               broad array of evaluation tasks. Our code is submitted as supplementary materials.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models are trained on vast amounts of data scraped from the internet, containing
034   a wide range of content qualities. (Penedo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Laurenccon et al., 2023;
035   Marion et al., 2023). Models develop a broad understanding of language and acquire general
036   knowledge from the unstructured data in this pretraining phase (Da et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2024)
037   and align with user intent in the finetuned stage using instruction datasets which consists of a more
038   structured format of question and response pairs (Chung et al., 2022; Taori et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
039
      Üstün et al., 2024). Recent years have seen substantial efforts to create datasets using various manual
      (Conover et al., 2023; Köpf et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2024) and synthetic (Taori et al., 2023; Wang
040
      et al., 2022; Shimabucoro et al., 2024) methods, making it increasingly challenging to determine
041
      which dataset is best suited for downstream tasks. A crucial question regarding the scalability of
042   finetuning LLMs is: “what is the optimum subset of data that allows for efficient training and captures
043   aspects of the data relevant to downstream tasks?”
044
045
      Instances in a dataset contribute to a model’s learning process with varying degrees of impact,
      affecting the model’s performance and generalization (Sorscher et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022).
046
      While recent research has predominantly emphasized local features, such as the quality of individual
047
      instances for subset selection, we argue that prioritizing a global feature —diversity—yields greater
048   benefits. When selecting a subset of instances, we manage computational complexity while balancing
049   the trade-off between diversity and representativeness (Zhou et al., 2023), ensuring that the subset
050   captures the underlying data distribution (Ivison et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b). Preserving a
051   high level of sample diversity during finetuning is crucial for improving generalization capabilities
052   (Zhang et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024). Wang et al. (2024b) revealed that using a range of instruction
053   datasets can boost downstream tasks. Wang et al. (2024a) provided a theoretical analysis using
      determinantal point processes to underscore the significance of diversity in the selection of subsets.

054
      However, ensuring diversity during sampling is difficult, and current methodologies fall short of fully
055   addressing this challenge. Most scoring-based subset selection methods prioritize sample quality and
056   characteristics and subsequently apply a diversity filter (Liu et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024). Still, since
057   diversity is inherently a global property, addressing it only in the second step limits its effectiveness
058   because it lacks a comprehensive view of the entire collection. This limitation often arises because
059   assessing the data collection globally is computationally expensive (Bukharin & Zhao, 2023).
060
      In this work, we propose a scalable iterative sampling and refinement method to efficiently select a
061   subset of instruction data and maximize the diversity of samples. We iteratively refine the sample
062   selection using early training signals from the fine-tuning model and proceed with continued fine-
063   tuning. With the same training budget, we achieve substantial improvements over fixed sampling
064   approaches and previous state-of-the-art data selection methods. We evaluate the finetuned models on
065   a wide range of tasks, including question answering, math, reasoning, and code, and show consistent
066   improvements over baselines. Overall, our experiments and analyses demonstrate that by sampling a
067   small subset of data, we achieve performance improvements of up to 7% over random selection and
068   3.8% over the previous sampling methods on a wide variety of tasks. In summary, our contributions
069
      are as follows:
070
             • We systematically analyze various clustering and sampling methods and demonstrate that
071
               k-means clustering is particularly effective for selecting an optimal, diverse subset of
072
               instruction data, especially when paired with a quality sampling step.
073
074          • Our simplest variant, which involves efficiently clustering data points and randomly sampling
075
               from each cluster, already achieves performance on par with advanced state-of-the-art
               sampling techniques, without the need for costly LLM scoring. This supports our hypothesis
076
               on the importance of diversity and the representativeness of the sampling process.
077
078          • We further propose an iterative clustering algorithm that simultaneously combines the
079            learning feedback from the training model and optimizes for diversity based on data distri-
080
               bution for effective instruction tuning. This method outperforms previous approaches on all
               downstream tasks.

      Given a large and diverse set of instruct data D = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }, we select a subset D′ with
      budget b ∈ N+ , where b = |D′ | ≪ |D| and finetune a language model and evaluate a selection of
091
      downstream tasks. This subset should be a representative sample of the training data, maintaining
092
      high quality and offering a diverse range of examples. We propose to define the problem of sample
093   selection for training data of a language model as a clustering problem with clustering objectives
094   where we want to group similar samples together and separate dissimilar samples into different
095   clusters. We explore various sampling methods to ensure the inclusion of optimal samples from
096   different clusters.
097

100   most k. For k-center, we want to pick C such that the maximum distance of data points to centers is
101   minimized. More precisely, in k-center, we want to minimize

      In the previous section, we introduced a two-step approach: sampling a fixed subset of data first and
179
      finetuning a model on it. The sampling and finetuning steps are performed independently without
180
      any information exchange between the two steps. However, the initial stages of finetuning can offer
181   insights into how individual data points influence the learning process (Anthony et al., 2017; Muldrew
182   et al., 2024). Here, we investigate whether we can improve our sampling method by incorporating
183   early training feedback into the selection process. We accomplish this by periodically increasing the
184   weight of clusters from which the model learns well while decreasing the weight of clusters that the
185   model finds difficult to generalize.
186
      The motivation is twofold: (1) Not all data clusters possess the same level of quality and impact. We
187   further analyze the distribution and quality scores across clusters, revealing significant disparities
188   (see analysis in §4.4). This indicates that some clusters are notably better quality, while others
189   predominantly consist of low-quality data. (2) From a curriculum learning perspective, models can
190   develop different skills and knowledge at varying rates (Xu et al., 2020; Xu & Tewari, 2021; Feng
191   et al., 2023). Increasing the selection from challenging clusters for models to learn can enhance their
192   generalization capability.
193   Our iterative approach is:
194
195
196   1. Initialization Given a fixed training budget of b, we use kMQ as described in the previous
197
      section to cluster and sample an initial set of instances of the size Nb . Next, we finetune the base
      model for one epoch by going over the sampled data once, using this checkpoint to guide the iterative
198
      selection.
199
200
201   2. Estimation of Sample Difficulty Using the latest checkpoint, we perform one round of inference
202   on the prompts on which the model is trained. Specifically, given the prompt xi from the initial
203   sampled set, we generate a new completion yi from the original seed data, forming the tuple
204
      (xi , ygen , ygold ) ∈ J i . We then evaluate the quality difference between ygen and ygold using a scorer S.
      We compute the score for each instance by the following:
205
206
207

208
209

211
      section 4.2.
212

214   modify the sampling weight of each cluster in the next iteration. The goal is to assign a higher weight
215   to the clusters containing higher-quality data while reducing the number of instances selected from
      lower-quality clusters. We define the score and weight of the j th cluster as follows:

305
306   Table 2 presents the performance of the proposed methods for instruction data selection compared
307   to several baselines across various tasks. Our first observation is that by clustering data points

313   Next, we observe that by replacing the random selection step with the quality-based approach (kMQ)

321   a compact yet representative subset of the data through clustering and weighted sampling from the
322   clusters. We find that it is also crucial to consider a feedback loop from the finetuning model and
323   understand how it perceives and learns from the data. By incorporating this feedback we ensure that
      the sampling process aligns with the model’s learning behavior for optimal results.

      4.3     I MPACT OF N UMBER OF C LUSTERS
359
360   In k-means data selection, an important question is how to choose the appropriate value for the
361   parameter k (the number of clusters). Increasing the value of k results in more fine-grained clusters
362   and by ensuring that we sample from each cluster, we can increase the diversity of the selected subset.
363   However, overly large values of k would also inevitably create outlier clusters that consist entirely
364   of low-quality, noisy data. Since we ensure each cluster is represented in the final selection, this
365   results in noise being included. There is no one-size-fits-all answer, as the optimal k depends on
366
      the characteristics of the pool of data. Exploring the optimal parameter value is costly, as it must
      be conducted with each new dataset. Here we use established heuristics in the clustering literature
367
      to guide this decision and study the correlation of these metrics with downstream performance of
368
      language models. Namely we investigate two methods:
369
370   Elbow method is a popular approach (Ahmed et al., 2020), where the objective value is plotted
371   against different values of k. The goal is to identify the elbow point, where increasing k yields
372
      diminishing returns in the performance.
373   Silhouette Score (Vardakas et al., 2024) provides another perspective by evaluating how well each
374   data point fits within its assigned cluster (cohesion) compared to other clusters (separation), ranging
375   from -1 (poor fit) to 1 (excellent fit). A high score indicates the object is similar to others in its cluster
376   and dissimilar to those in neighboring clusters.

      Although both approaches for identifying the ideal number of clusters are frequently employed, the
401   Silhouette score is generally preferred to the Elbow method in k-means clustering due to its clear

405   very cheap.

      4.5   T RANSFERABILITY OF R ESULTS
455
456   We conduct experiments with two additional base models, Mistral-7B and Llama-3 8B (Jiang et al.,
457   2023; Dubey et al., 2024), to assess whether our findings generalize to other model families and more
458   powerful models. Our results in Table 3 demonstrate that the effectiveness of iterative refinement
459   remains valid for the Mistral-7B model, which exhibits more robust performance. However, the
460   evaluation results for Llama-3 are mixed across different benchmarks. We observe improvements on
461   average with kMQ sampling and a slight decrease in performance with iterative sampling especially
462   in reasoning tasks. We hypothesize that Llama-2 differs from Mistral in its training data, model
463
      parameters, and training strategies. Consequently, using Llama-2 as a scorer reveals novel data points
      from which Mistral can benefit. However, Llama-3, a more advanced model than its predecessors
464
      with extended training as one of the primary distinctions, uncovers fewer new, valuable data points for
465
      further learning. This highlights that the quality scorer’s effectiveness can vary, sometimes proving
466   more beneficial and other times less so, depending on the base model for which we are sampling.
467
468   While the iterative refinement pipeline can select a dataset restricted to certain models, we do not
469
      view this as a limitation. The primary contribution of this work is to propose a function that takes a
      fixed dataset and model as input and outputs the most valuable subset for learning. This approach
470
      aligns with similar works (Ilyas et al., 2022; Thrush et al., 2024). Specifically, the task is to extract
471
      a subset of data that leverages early reward signals to enhance the targeted model’s post-training
472   performance.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
476
477
      Data selection for LLMs. Previous works on data selection can be broadly categorized into two
      key approaches: (1) removing undesirable examples, for instance, low-quality (Raffel et al., 2023;
478
      Marion et al., 2023), toxic (Raffel et al., 2023), or duplicated instances (Zhang et al., 2022; Abbas
479
      et al., 2023). (2) identifying the most optimal subset of data. While the definition of an optimal
480   subset varies across different works, the shared goal is to use a small portion of the data while still
481   maintaining strong performance. This subset selection approach has often been done by aiming
482   for selecting high-quality instances through a proxy: manual curation (Zhou et al., 2023), selecting
483   instances from human-authored datasets (Wang et al., 2024b), or hand-selecting datasets encouraging
484   complexity and diversity (Ivison et al., 2023). More recently, a line of work has used language models
485   to assess the quality of each data point and select the best ones. Xia et al. (2024) estimates data
      influence and performs a low-rank gradient similarity search using a gradient datastore. Liu et al.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (2023) scores instances using a combination of complexity and quality scores using an LLM and
487   selects the final subset using diversity-based filtering. While individual sample quality is a crucial
488   factor, prioritizing this local criterion can limit the diversity of the final selection. However, diversity
489   in instances and tasks is essential for training high-performant models (Wei et al., 2021; Gudibande
490   et al., 2023). Our work differs from these studies by examining what constitutes an optimal subset
491   from a global perspective and by prioritizing representativeness. Closest to our work, Bukharin &
492   Zhao (2023) emphasized quality by encoding all data points in the selection pool using an embedding
493   model and selecting the final subset based on pairwise cosine similarity and a quality score from
494   an LLM. In contrast, our approach offers a significantly more efficient method for subset selection,
495
      while also achieving improved performance. Our experiment covers multiple dimensions, including
      various base models, different encoding and scoring methods, and extensive ablation studies with
496
      recommendations for efficient parameter selection.
497
498
499   Active learning and language models. Active learning is based on the fundamental premise that
500   “not all data is equal”. This approach aims to identify the most informative data for pretraining or
501   adapting language models for specific tasks or capabilities, as well as pinpointing the most valuable
502   data for learning. Margatina et al. (2023) explored active learning for selecting in-context examples
503   in few-shot learning, demonstrating that similar examples outperform uncertain or diverse in-context
504   examples. Muldrew et al. (2024) proposed active preference learning, combining iterative data
505   acquisition with a DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) loop to reduce the frequency of querying
506
      human annotators (Oracle). Their acquisition method relies on the model’s entropy during generation.
      Our approach generalizes active instruction tuning (Kung et al., 2023) to instance-level data selection,
507
      allowing for the co-evolution of the LLMs and instruction data using an external reward signal.
508

      6    C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this paper, we present a novel approach to selecting a subset of data and optimizing the fine-tuning
514   of language models. Our method involved a scalable sampling technique that maximizes diversity and
515   efficiency in subset selection. Through our proposed k-means-quality (kMQ) algorithm and iterative
516   selection process, we demonstrated significant performance improvements over strong baselines while
517   maintaining a limited training budget. Our contributions include an efficient instruction selection
518
      algorithm, the release of our encoded instruction dataset, and a systematic analysis of our method’s
      effectiveness across a range of tasks. Our method outperforms existing baselines, achieving up to 7%
519
      improvement in a wide range of evaluation tasks.
520
521   By addressing the challenge of optimal instruct data selection, our work paves the way for more
522   efficient and effective finetuning of language models, making them more accessible and affordable for
523   deployment, especially in resource-constrained settings. We believe that our findings will contribute
524
      significantly to the ongoing research in language model optimization and their real-world applications.
525
526
527   7    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   While our proposed method has shown promising results, there are a few limitations to consider.
530   Our evaluation focused on a specific set of tasks, and future work can aim to validate our method’s
531   effectiveness across a broader range of language models and tasks, including data selection in the
532   pre-training stage and alignment (Yu et al., 2024; Muldrew et al., 2024). Furthermore, our iterative
533   selection process relies on early training signals, and we only presented this as a pilot study to
534   encourage further research. Future work could explore alternative model feedback mechanisms to
535   refine the selected instruction data subsets, especially in mitigating the potential for reward hacking
536
      in the iterative refinement process (Pan et al., 2024).
537   Finally, while we considered diversity and difficulty crucial factors, other characteristics of instruction
538   data could be explored to enhance the finetuning process further. Addressing these limitations and
539   extending this research will contribute to more robust and adaptable language models, capable of
      excelling in a wide range of real-world applications.
