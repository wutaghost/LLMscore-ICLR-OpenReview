009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spatio-temporal event sequences are increasingly accessible in various domains
012            such as earthquake forecasting, crime prediction, and healthcare management.
013            These data sources present unique challenges, as they involve both spatial and
014            temporal dimensions, with event sequences exhibiting intricate dependencies over
015            time and space. Neural network-based spatio-temporal point processes offer a
016            sophisticated framework for modeling such event data. Conventional maximum
017            likelihood estimation (MLE) of such data may lead to inaccurate predictions due
018
               to model-misspecification and compounding prediction errors. On the other hand,
               reinforcement learning frameworks, which treat event generation as actions and
019
               learn a policy to mimic event generation, may alleviate the training/test discrepancy
020
               issue. Current reinforcement learning of point processes may have prohibitively
021            poor exploration efficiency. In this paper, we propose the Causal learning improved
022            Reinforcement Learning Spatio-Temporal Point Process (CRLSTPP) framework,
023            which can mitigate the issue of compounding prediction errors and improve ex-
024            ploration efficiency at the same time. Experiments on both synthetic data and
025            real-world data validate the superiority of the proposed model.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Asynchronous spatio-temporal event data, such as crime records, traffic flow patterns, and earthquake
031   occurrences, are widely utilized across various domains including predictive policing Zhu et al.
032   (2021b); Zhou et al. (2022); Mohler et al. (2011), urban transportation Yuan et al. (2023); Zhu
033
      et al. (2021a), and seismology Chen et al. (2020); Ogata (1988). Different from regularly sampled
      spatial or temporal data, the asynchronous sequence contains events with spatial coordinates and time
034
      stamps that occur in continuous space and time. Those event sequences often demonstrate notable
035
      self-exciting patterns. For instance, a crime event often leads to further incidents in neighboring
036   areas and time frames. It is critical to model these complex underlying dynamics such that accurate
037   predictions can be made sequentially given the history Baddeley et al. (2007); Chen et al. (2020);
038   Diggle (2006).
039
      Spatio-temporal point processes (STPPs) are well suited for modeling event data of this nature.
040
      Typically, an STPP model is characterized by an intensity function to indicate the instantaneous
041
      rate of an event occurring at a specific location and time given all historical events. Classic Spatio-
042   temporal point process models Moller & Waagepetersen (2003); Ogata (1988; 1998) leverage prior
043   process knowledge to make parametric or semi-parametric assumptions on the intensity function.
044   Although these models tend to perform well when the underlying parametric assumptions align with
045   the actual processes, in scenarios where the genuine generative process of events remains obscure,
046   these parametric assumptions can become excessively restrictive and do not represent the underlying
047   dynamics well. Alternatively, variations of point processes have been proposed to model intensity
048   functions by incorporating nonparametric forms Yuan et al. (2023), automatic integrationZhou & Yu
049   (2024), neural ordinary differential equations Chen et al. (2020), and recurrent neural networks Yang
050
      et al. (2018).
051   Conventionally, STPP models are trained via supervised learning, and model parameters are estimated
052   by maximizing the log-likelihood (MLE) of observed event sequences. However, models learned via
053   MLE at training time may be misspecified and may encounter compounding prediction errors over
      long horizons of composed forecasting Arora et al. (2022); Tan et al. (2018); Li et al. (2023), where


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      prediction errors made during testing accumulate in multiple successive forecasting steps and lead to
055   states distant from training data. Alternative methods address the training/test discrepancy problem
056   by employing reinforcement learning (RL) techniques Ranzato et al. (2015); Ding & Soricut (2017);
057   Bahdanau et al. (2016); Li et al. (2018). However, RL-based approaches may encounter issues with
058   the exploitation efficiency during the action sampling stage.
059
      In this paper, we propose a novel causal reinforcement learning framework for modeling the spatio-
060
      temporal point process. Firstly, we introduce a new approach by examining spatio-temporal point
061   processes from a reinforcement learning perspective. More specifically, the action is the next event’s
062   location and time, the state consists of the parameters that characterize the underlying spatial temporal
063   distribution, and the generation of each event can be interpreted as an action taken by a stochastic
064   policy. The observed sequences will be seen as the expert policy, and our goal is to use an MMD-
065   based reward to minimize the discrepancy between the learner policy and the expert policy. Such a
066   framework alleviates the training/testing discrepancy and reduces compounding prediction errors.
067   Secondly, to further enhance exploration efficiency, we measure the causal influence of actions
068   on the state and choose actions that are predicted to have a higher causal impact. Similar to the
069   self-exciting point process, the arrival action (event) will influence the underlying distribution. Our
070   goal is to measure this causal impact and to choose the action with the higher casual influence score.
071   Additionally, unlike traditional discrete actions, our actions consist of event time stamps and spatial
072   locations, which are continuous. To tackle this issue, our method involves grid sampling to cover
073   the action space comprehensively; after that, we select the action that has the maximum deviation
074   from the expected outcome, enabling the policy to take the action that significantly improves the
075
      exploration efficiency. Comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate
      that our proposed algorithm outperforms the state-of-the-art.

078   2    R ELATED W ORK
079
080   Spatial and Temporal Point Process Existing approaches in spatio-temporal point process modeling
081   such as kernel function-based models Zhang et al. (2023); Dong et al. (2023b); Zhu et al. (2021c);
082   Ilhan & Kozat (2020); Dong et al. (2023a) often rely on simplified parametric assumptions to represent
083   the underlying dynamics of events such as seismic activities. Most of these solutions are based on the
084   intensity paradigm, which might not be sufficiently flexible for different applications. Recently, some
085   generative models Li et al. (2021b); Yuan et al. (2023); Xiao et al. (2017) have been proposed to
086   solve the training/testing discrepancy issue. However, the training complexity of generative models
087
      is significant, which requires a substantial number of iterations and meticulous parameter tuning
      to ensure that both neural networks achieve a state of equilibrium. Recent efforts aim to uncover
088
      Granger causality in temporal point processes Jalaldoust et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2016).
089
090   A reinforcement framework Li et al. (2018) is proposed to model a temporal point process for
091   sequences with only temporal information. An imitation learning-based algorithm Zhu et al. (2021b)
092   has been proposed to train a spatio-temporal point process Zhu et al. (2021b). The work relies on fully
093
      connected neural networks to parameterize a mixture of heterogeneous Gaussian diffusion kernels
      for modeling spatio-temporal dependencies. These kernels capture complex spatial patterns, but the
094
      model does not focus on capturing long-term temporal dependencies. Moreover, models based on
095
      imitation learning also have some limitations due to their constrained exploratory capabilities Ali
096   (2021). In our work, we incorporate the recurrent mixture density network (RMDN) Bazzani et al.
097   (2016) structure, which effectively captures long-term dependencies between events.
098
099
      Causality Improved Reinforcement Learning Incorporating causality information to enhance the
      performance of reinforcement learning (RL) frameworks is an emerging trend Zeng et al. (2023).
100
      Recent studies have augmented RL performance by integrating causal learning in various ways.
101
      Examples include utilizing the causal information to enhance sample efficiency in the online setting
102   Wang et al. (2021), using causality action inference as a part of reward bonus Seitzer et al. (2021),
103   and exploring actions with causal influence Lu et al. (2022); Seitzer et al. (2021); Bica et al. (2021).
104   Additionally, to improve the interpretability of the reinforcement learning model, some existing works
105   focus on learning the causal graph from the reinforcement learning process Ding et al. (2022); Li et al.
106   (2021a). In this paper, we introduce causal information-based action exploration RL to the domain
107   of spatio-temporal point processes (STPP) for the first time, and we focus on causal information
      detection to improve event prediction.

      4.3   C AUSAL I NFORMATION I MPROVED ACTION S ELECTION
251
252   In this paper, we introduce the causal information based action selection method to enhance the
253   exploration efficiency of the RL framework. Due to the self-exciting properties of spatio-temporal
254   point processes (STPP), in our RL framework, once an action is selected, it will influence the
255   subsequent state. Different actions exhibit varying degrees of influence on the state. To improve the
256   efficiency of exploration, our approach uses conditional mutual information to measure the influence
257   and prioritize actions expected to exert the most significant causal influence on the next state.
258   Firstly, unlike traditional discrete actions, our actions consist of event times and spatial locations,
259   which are continuous. We employ grid sampling to ensure comprehensive sampling while preserving
260   the distribution’s characteristics. We discretize the region of interest into n × n grid cells. Then we
261   generate actions from the learner policy πL iteratively until q cells are covered by at least one event
262   sample. Finally, we extract q samples and each of them is located in a distinct grid cell.
263   Once an action (ai = (zi , ti )) is generated, the hidden states h and the parameters of RMDN
264   will change. Thus, the underlying spatio-temporal intensity will change. To encourage the action
265   exploration efficiency, we choose the possible event (action) that results in maximal conditional
266   mutual information deviation from the expectation (see Appendix A for details). The process of
267   choosing the action can be described as:

      6     C ONCLUSION
475
476   We propose a novel causal reinforcement learning framework for modeling spatio-temporal event
477
      sequences. Under this framework, the policy is designed as a special recurrent mixture density
      network (RMDN), enabling the sequential sampling of discrete events. We update the policy by
478
      maximizing the reward which correlates with the disparity between the generated sequences and
479
      observed sequences. This approach effectively reduces the error accumulation in likelihood-based
480   methodologies for multi-step forecasting. Furthermore, to improve the exploration efficiency of our
481   reinforcement learning model, we have implemented a strategy that utilizes causal information to
482   optimize event generation. This is achieved by selecting the event (action) that induces the maximum
483   deviation in mutual information from its expected values. Experiments on synthetic and real-world
484   datasets demonstrated our model’s methodological robustness and superior performance in modeling
485   complex spatio-temporal event data. This framework can be extended to accommodate more complex
      causal structures and explore the integration of additional data types in future works.
