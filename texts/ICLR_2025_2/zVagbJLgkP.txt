011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            While vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated remarkable perfor-
014            mance across various tasks combining textual and visual information, they con-
015
               tinue to struggle with fine-grained visual perception tasks that require detailed
               pixel-level analysis. Effectively eliciting comprehensive reasoning from VLMs on
016
               such intricate visual elements remains an open challenge. In this paper, we present
017
               V IPACT, an agent framework that enhances VLMs by integrating multi-agent col-
018            laboration and vision expert models, enabling more precise visual understanding
019            and comprehensive reasoning. V IPACT consists of an orchestrator agent, which
020            manages task requirement analysis, planning, and coordination, along with spe-
021            cialized agents that handle specific tasks such as image captioning and vision ex-
022            pert models that provide high-precision perceptual information. This multi-agent
023            approach allows VLMs to better perform fine-grained visual perception tasks by
024            synergizing planning, reasoning, and tool use. We evaluate V IPACT on bench-
025            marks featuring a diverse set of visual perception tasks, with experimental results
026
               demonstrating significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art base-
               lines across all tasks. Furthermore, comprehensive ablation studies reveal the crit-
027
               ical role of multi-agent collaboration in eliciting more detailed System-2 reason-
028
               ing and highlight the importance of image input for task planning. Additionally,
029            our error analysis identifies patterns of VLMs’ inherent limitations in visual per-
030            ception, providing insights into potential future improvements. V IPACT offers a
031            flexible and extensible framework, paving the way for more advanced visual per-
032            ception systems across various real-world applications.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Recent advancements in large multimodal models (LMMs), particularly vision-language models
038   (VLMs) (OpenAI, 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b), have demonstrated remarkable ca-
039
      pabilities in tasks that integrate textual and visual information. For instance, models like GPT-4o
      (OpenAI, 2024) have achieved impressive results across numerous image-text benchmarks (Hudson
040
      & Manning, 2019; Lu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024), and have shown promise in real-world applica-
041
      tions such as web navigation (Zheng et al., 2024a; He et al., 2024a). However, despite their strong
042   performance in some vision-language applications, recent studies (Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024;
043   Fu et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c) reveal that state-of-the-art (SOTA) VLMs con-
044   tinue to struggle with fine-grained or low-level visual perception tasks that are trivial for humans,
045   such as determining whether lines intersect or identifying the boundary of cars and roads. Ad-
046   dressing these limitations is crucial for enhancing VLMs’ real-world applicability, as many practical
047   scenarios—such as surgical robotics in healthcare or autonomous driving—require precise visual
048   understanding beyond coarse-grained capabilities.
049   To address these challenges, prior works have explored a series of visual programming methods
050   (Subramanian et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024b; Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Surı́s et al., 2023; Mialon
051   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023c), where text queries are input into LLMs to generate
052   code that invokes vision-specific models, using their outputs directly as predictions for the query.
053   While these methods can harness the strengths of specialized vision models, their applicability is
      limited by the availability of predefined tools and cannot generalize to tasks that fall outside the


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      scope of existing solutions, making them far from a comprehensive visual perception framework.
055   Another line of research focuses on prompting strategies to elicit foundation models’ System-2
056   reasoning by involving iterative reasoning with intermediate tokens (Yu et al., 2024; Saha et al.,
057   2024). A series of textual prompting methods (Wei et al., 2022; Saha et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2024;
058   Besta et al., 2024) have been developed to generate structured reasoning steps, effectively eliciting
059   System-2 reasoning for complex text-based tasks in large language models (LLMs). However, their
060   effectiveness on fine-grained visual perception tasks for VLMs remains underexplored. Similarly,
061   visual prompting methods (Lei et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024) guide VLMs in
062   interpreting visual data by adding artifacts to images in various formats, such as bounding boxes,
063
      markers, or segmentation masks. While these methods have shown promise in some compositional
      visual reasoning tasks, it is still unclear whether VLMs can accurately perceive such visual prompts,
064
      let alone whether these techniques improve performance in fine-grained visual perception tasks.
065
066   To fill this gap, and inspired by recent advances in LLM-based agents (Wang et al., 2024d; Liu et al.,
067   2023b; Significant-Gravitas, 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Shen et al., 2024), we propose V IPACT
068   (VIsual-Perception via VLM Agent Collaboration and Tool-use), a general VLM-based framework
069
      that integrates multi-agent collaboration and vision expert models for fine-grained visual percep-
      tion tasks. As illustrated in Figure 1, V IPACT consists of three core components: (1) an orches-
070
      trator agent that manages the workflow by analyzing tasks, coordinating agents, selecting tools,
071
      summarizing evidence, and deducing final answers; (2) specialized agents for tasks such as image
072   captioning, visual prompt description, and image comparison, providing detailed visual analysis to
073   the orchestrator; and (3) vision expert models, offering task-specific, fine-grained perceptual in-
074   formation to address VLMs’ limitations. We empirically evaluate V IPACT against SOTA baselines
075   across benchmarks that include diverse visual perception tasks—challenging for SOTA VLMs but
076   easy for humans—featuring complex elements like visual prompts and multi-image inputs. V IPACT
077   consistently outperforms previous baselines on all tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and gener-
078   alization. Additionally, our in-depth analysis highlights the importance of multi-agent collaboration
079   in eliciting more detailed System-2 reasoning from VLMs, as well as the critical role of visual input
080
      for task planning, with improved error handling and evidence aggregation.
081   To summarize, our key contributions are as follows: (1) We introduce V IPACT, a novel multi-modal
082   agent framework based on VLMs that synergizes multi-agent collaboration with vision expert mod-
083   els to enhance fine-grained visual perception. V IPACT is a fully autonomous system capable of
084   handling a diverse range of visual perception tasks using a single prompt template. It leverages a
085   VLM for task analysis, planning, and invoking multi-agent collaboration, with flexible plug-and-
086
      play modular components that allow for further extension. (2) We conduct extensive experiments
      across diverse visual perception benchmarks, demonstrating V IPACT’s advantages over SOTA base-
087
      lines; (3) We systematically analyze previous methods that have been proved to be effective in
088
      improving the general task-solving capabilities of foundation models for fine-grained visual percep-
089   tion, revealing their inconsistent effectiveness. (4) We present comprehensive ablation studies to
090   assess the impact of multi-agent collaboration, visual input for task planning, and each component
091   of V IPACT, along with a detailed error analysis identifying the limitations of current SOTA VLMs,
092   which serve as bottlenecks for further improvement.

097   VLM-based Agent. As LLMs demonstrate increasing capabilities in task decomposition, instruc-
098   tion following, and structured output generation, LLM-based language agents have shown poten-
099
      tial across a wide range of applications (Zhang et al., 2023c; Xi et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a;
      Significant-Gravitas, 2024; Shen et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024e; Xie et al.,
100
      2024a; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Zhang et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023). Recently, as the emergency of
101
      GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) with enhanced visual ability and low latency, VLMs have begun to be ap-
102   plied as agent backbones for vision-related tasks (Hu et al., 2024a). One prominent line of works
103   focuses on Web Agents or GUI agents (Yan et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2024a;
104   Xie et al., 2024c; Kapoor et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Koh et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c; Lù

106   2024; Wang et al., 2024b; He et al., 2024b) which aim to interact with and navigate web interfaces
107   and graphical user interfaces. Another line of works focuses on embodied agents designed to con-
      trol robots (Nasiriany et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024b; Yang et al.,

108
      2024b; Szot et al., 2024), bridging the gap between language understanding and physical world in-
109   teraction. Despite these advancements, to the best of our knowledge, there is no prior work focusing
110   on building VLM-based agents specifically for natural image understanding or perception tasks. Ea-
111   gle (Shi et al., 2024b) combines multiple vision encoders with a simple fusion strategy and a novel
112   pre-alignment training stage to achieve SOTA performances on certain vision-language tasks. While
113   Eagle also explores multi-expert collaboration in multimodal LLMs, its approach differs fundamen-
114   tally from ours. Eagle focuses on integrating multiple internal vision encoders as ”experts” within a
115   single VLM. In contrast, VipAct leverages external pre-trained vision models as experts within a
116   modular agent framework, interacting with but not modifying the architecture of the VLM.

      Visual Programming. With the advancement of LLMs, particularly in code generation, recent work
118
      has begun utilizing LLMs as an interface for solving complex reasoning tasks with tools, using code
119
      generation as a proxy (Gao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c; 2024e;d; Schick et al., 2024). This ap-
120   proach has proven effective in reducing hallucinations in a wide range of tasks such as mathematical
121   reasoning (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021). A line of research extends this concept to vi-
122   sion tasks (Subramanian et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024b; Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Surı́s et al., 2023;
123   Mialon et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a). MM-REACT (Yang et al., 2023c) integrates LLMs with var-
124   ious vision experts to perform multimodal reasoning tasks, following the prompt template of ReAct
125   (Yao et al., 2023). ViperGPT (Surı́s et al., 2023) and VisProg (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023) leverage
126   LLMs to generate Python code that can be executed to perform visual reasoning tasks without ad-
127   ditional training. However, these approaches typically use only the text query as input to the LLMs
128
      for code generation, neglecting the image input. Additionally, their workflows heavily depend on
      outputs from vision expert models, lack error-handling mechanisms, and involve tool selections that
129
      are to some extend hard-coded for specific, predefined tasks. These limitations restrict their effec-
130
      tiveness to simpler scenarios, such as question answering about main objects in images (Hudson &
131   Manning, 2019; Suhr et al., 2019; Marino et al., 2019), without the capability for fine-grained visual
132   perception or robust task generalization. Moreover, most existing methods lack specific designs for
133   visual prompting within the image and are unable to handle tasks that require multiple images as
134   input. This constrains their applicability to more complex visual reasoning scenarios that demand

      3.1    ORCHESTRATOR AGENT
195
196
      Task Requirement Analysis and Planning: Inspired by recent works (Yao et al., 2022; Huang
197
      et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023c; Wang et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024) that integrate reasoning, planning,
198   and action in LLM-based agent frameworks, the orchestrator agent begins by analyzing the task
199   requirements derived from the provided images and queries. This analysis identifies the key elements
200   necessary to solve the problem and the corresponding critical visual features that must be acquired in
201   subsequent steps of the agent’s workflow, as well as other criteria derived from its own knowledge.
202   The orchestrator agent then generates a detailed plan for tackling the task, outlining the concrete
203   steps required to obtain the information needed to meet these requirements. For instance, in a
204   depth estimation task as illustrated in Figure 1, the orchestrator agent would determine the essential
205   requirements for comparing depth, such as identifying the specific objects targeted by the red circles
206
      and recognizing their relative positions to the camera.
207   Tool Selection and Incorporation of Specialized Agents: After analyzing the task requirements
208   and formulating a plan, the orchestrator agent selects the appropriate tools and specialized agents to
209   provide the visual information necessary to solve the task. Depending on the nature of the task, this
210   may involve initiating collaboration with specialized agents designed for specific tasks or external
211   vision expert models to gather comprehensive information. Details on these specialized agents and
212
      external vision expert models are provided in Sections 3.2 and 3.3.
213   Evidence Summarization: Once the tools and specialized agents have performed their respective
214   tasks in separate environments, the orchestrator agent compiles and summarizes the collected evi-
215   dence. This involves integrating the outputs from various tools and agents, ensuring that all relevant
      information is coherently synthesized to support the decision-making process. The orchestrator


                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      agent also resolves conflicting evidence and double-checks the factuality of the information, as er-
217   rors or hallucinations may arise from the expert models and specialized agents.
218
219   Final Answer Deduction: With the summarized evidence, the orchestrator agent deduces the final
220
      answer. It applies reasoning based on the accumulated information to arrive at a clear, unambiguous
      conclusion. Depending on the nature and format of the gathered data, the orchestrator agent may
221
      generate Python code, which is then executed by an external Python interpreter to derive the
222
      final answer. If the gathered information does not lead to a perfect answer, the orchestrator agent
223   is designed to select the closest possible option based on the evidence, supplemented by its own
224   perception and knowledge.

227   3.2   C OLLABORATION WITH SPECIALIZED AGENTS
228
229   V IPACT incorporates three specialized agents to enhance its visual perception capabilities: focused
230   image captioning, visual prompt description, and focused image comparison. These agents provide
231   task-specific, detailed information to the orchestrator agent through function calling in a separate
232   environment, integrating their outputs into the main reasoning process for comprehensive visual
233   analysis. The three specialized agents used in our experiments are described below.
234   Focused Image Captioning: This agent generates detailed image descriptions, optionally empha-
235   sizing specific objects or elements relevant to the task by specifying a focus argument. The focus
236   argument allows for targeted analysis, ranging from general descriptions to particular aspects like
237   ”a red car and the background buildings.” This flexibility enables the orchestrator agent to obtain
238   precise, task-relevant information from images. Empirical evidence demonstrates its effectiveness
239   across a wide range of visual perception tasks, with the focus parameter providing fine-grained con-
240
      trol over the generated descriptions.
241   Visual Prompt Description: Specializing in analyzing visual prompts within images (e.g., colored
242   circles, bounding boxes, arrows, textual labels), this agent is crucial for interpreting visual annota-
243   tions. It generates detailed descriptions of these elements, including their locations, characteristics,
244   and most importantly, the regions or objects these visual prompts target. This enables the orches-
245   trator agent to accurately interpret highlighted or annotated image sections. The agent has shown
246
      particular efficacy in tasks involving images with explicit visual prompts, significantly enhancing
      the system’s ability to understand and reason about annotated visual data.
247
248   Focused Image Comparison: This agent analyzes multiple images, identifying similarities and
249   differences with an optional focus on specific elements. Similarly, the focus parameter allows for
250   targeted comparative analysis, either generally or on specific features as directed by the orchestrator
251   agent. For example, this function can provide a detailed comparison of orientations of objects which
252
      can be useful in tasks such as multi-view reasoning. This capability is particularly valuable for tasks
      requiring multi-image input, such as change detection or pattern identification across images. Em-
253
      pirical results demonstrate this agent’s exceptional effectiveness in tasks involving multiple image
254
      inputs, with the focus parameter enabling precise comparative analyses.
255
256   The complete prompts for these three specialized agents are in Appendix H. V IPACT uses these
257   agents to break down complex visual tasks into manageable sub-tasks, with the orchestrator agent
258
      integrating their outputs for a comprehensive understanding. This modular approach ensures flex-
      ibility and precision, allowing for informed decisions and accurate responses. The architecture is
259
      also extensible, enabling easy integration of new agents to handle emerging visual tasks.

262   3.3   I NTEGRATION OF V ISION -E XPERT M ODELS
263
264   V IPACT further enhances its visual perception capabilities by integrating a suite of vision-expert
265   models, each specializing in specific aspects of image analysis. These models collaborate with
266   the orchestrator agent through function calling, uniquely returning both textual data and processed
267   images—making V IPACT among the earliest agent frameworks that incorporate visual information
268   directly into the reasoning workflow. These vision-expert models provide fine-grained visual
269   perception information that is often lacking in current VLM’s pre-training data (Zhang et al., 2024c).
      The vision expert tools used in our experiments are described below.


                                                         5

270
             • Visual Prompt Detector: Identifies and localizes annotated elements in images, such as
271            circles, bounding boxes, or other highlighted regions. This tool is crucial for understanding
272            visual instructions or annotations, enabling the agent to focus on relevant areas for analy-
273            sis. It returns the coordinates of these visual prompts, which often serve as intermediate
274            information to achieve the final answer.
275
             • Depth Estimator: Analyzes spatial relationships within scenes, providing crucial informa-
276            tion about the relative distances of objects from the camera. This tool enhances the agent’s
277            understanding of 3D structure in 2D images, vital for spatial reasoning tasks. It returns a
278            grey-scale depth image that can be directly input into the orchestrator agent, allowing it to
279            interpret depth information or combine it with other evidence to reach the final answer.
280
             • Object Detection: Identifies and localizes objects within an image, providing the agent
281            with a comprehensive inventory of visible objects, their locations, and sizes. This facil-
282            itates detailed scene understanding and object-centric reasoning. The tool returns both
283            a processed image with detected objects’ bounding boxes and textual information about
284            these bounding boxes and objects.
285
             • Image Segmentation: Offers precise delineation of image regions, separating objects,
286            backgrounds, and distinct areas. This enables fine-grained analysis of image components,
287            crucial for tasks requiring detailed understanding of object boundaries and spatial rela-
288            tionships. It returns images with segmentation masks along with corresponding textual
289            information.
290
             • Embedding-based Similarity Computation: Quantifies visual similarities across images
291            or image regions by generating compact representations of visual content. This allows for
292            nuanced comparisons and similarity assessments, particularly useful for tasks involving
293            image retrieval or comparative analysis. It returns similarity scores based on the selected
294            embedding model and specified similarity metrics, such as cosine similarity.

      Datasets. To evaluate VLMs on visual perception tasks, we use two challenging datasets designed
      to test fine-grained visual perception. Dataset details are in Appendix B.
347
348
             • Blink (Fu et al., 2024) includes diverse visual tasks solvable by humans “within a blink,”
349            yet difficult for SOTA VLMs. It features visual prompts such as bounding boxes and inter-
350            leaved image-text formats, often with multiple images in a single query. We use Blink as
351            the main benchmark to evaluate different methods.
352
             • MMVP (Tong et al., 2024) is a benchmark for evaluating visual grounding in VLMs, using
353            image pairs from “CLIP-blind pairs”—visually distinct images that are similar in CLIP
354            embedding space. It focuses on nine basic visual patterns that are easy for humans but
355            challenging for SOTA VLMs.

357
      Baselines. We evaluate V IPACT against four categories of baselines: (1) Text-based prompting,
358
      including zero-shot instructional prompting, which inputs the image and question directly; chain-of-
359   thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022), which appends ”Let’s think step-
360   by-step” at the end of the instruction; Least-to-most prompting (LtM) (Zhou et al., 2022), which
361   encourages LLMs to decompose the problem into more manageable sub-problems; and Tree-of-
362   thought (ToT) prompting (Yao et al., 2024), which systematically explores multiple reasoning paths
363   by maintaining a tree of intermediate steps. (2) Few-shot in-context learning Brown (2020), where
364   in-context exemplars are selected using different strategies, including random selection, or selection
365   based on the similarity of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) embeddings,
366   which we analyze separately in Appendix E. (3) Visual Prompting, exemplified by Set-of-Mark
367
      (SoM) (Yang et al., 2023a), which overlays interpretable marks on semantically meaningful image
      regions, enhancing GPT-4V’s fine-grained visual grounding on certain visual reasoning tasks. (4)
368
      Vision language agentic frameworks, including MM-ReAct (Yang et al., 2023c), which integrates
369
      LLMs with vision experts for multimodal reasoning and action through ReAct-style prompts (Yao
370   et al., 2022); ViperGPT (Surı́s et al., 2023), which uses LLMs to generate Python code that composes
371   vision and language models for visual reasoning tasks; VisProg (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023), which
372   generates visual programs from natural language instructions for complex tasks.

374   Result Analysis. Tables 2 and 3 present the performance of our proposed V IPACT framework and
375   baseline methods on each sub-task of the Blink and MMVP datasets respectively. We make the
376   following key observations: (1) Text-based prompting methods do not consistently improve per-
377   formance over zero-shot prompting. Specifically, as shown in Tables 2 and 3, prior text-based
      prompting methods that have been effective in eliciting LLMs reasoning abilities — such as CoT


                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      — can improve performance on some sub-tasks like visual similarity, object localization, count-
379   ing, and spatial relations. However, for other tasks, the improvement is minimal or even negative.
380   More advanced prompting techniques such as LtM and ToT exhibit similar phenomena. Empiri-
381   cally, we find that although these methods can elicit more detailed reasoning processes to reach the
382   final answer, such reasoning steps are often not grounded in the visual elements of the image and
383   can cause severe hallucinations. Therefore, we conclude that it is non-trivial to elicit VLMs’ reason-
384   ing abilities for better general visual perception using text-based prompting methods that work for
385   text-only LLMs. (2) SoM can impair VLMs’ fine-grained perception in most scenarios. From
386   the results on both datasets, we find that SoM adversely affects VLMs’ performance on almost all
387
      tasks. Empirically, we observe that overlaying labeled masks can become cluttered when dealing
      with a large number of semantic objects or fine-grained object parts. These masks can negatively
388
      influence VLMs’ perception of the original semantic objects and may confuse the models with the
389
      original visual prompts and their corresponding labels. Consequently, we conclude that although
390   SoM demonstrates effectiveness in some compositional reasoning tasks with a limited number of
391   semantic objects, it does not generalize well to a broader range of visual perception tasks, especially
392   those requiring visual prompt understanding. (3) Previous visual programming methods exhibit
393   poor generalization ability. As shown in the results, these methods perform adequately only on a
394   limited number of tasks such as spatial relations and counting, which are similar to those in com-
395   monly used visual question answering (VQA) datasets (Hudson & Manning, 2019; Suhr et al., 2019;
396   Marino et al., 2019). Upon examining their reasoning processes and generated code, we find that
397   the code can only call a limited set of tools predefined in the initial prompt, lacking additional logic
398
      to handle scenarios where their predefined tools are unsupported or when errors occur. Another
      limitation is their inability to support images with visual prompts, preventing them from locating
399
      visual prompts and proceeding with subsequent operations. For example, in tasks like depth esti-
400
      mation, their performance is close to zero because they cannot locate the red circles, resulting in
401   non-executable generated code with no schema to handle such incapability. Moreover, since the
402   code in these methods is generated solely based on the text query without considering the image, it
403   lacks the flexibility to adapt to different image characteristics. These observations highlight the need
404   for designing a generalizable agent framework that can leverage both vision expert models and the
405   inherent flexibility of VLMs themselves. (4) V IPACT consistently achieves the best performance
406   across all sub-tasks in Blink and MMVP, demonstrating its effectiveness and generalization
407   ability. By thoroughly examining V IPACT’s reasoning steps, we observe that, compared to text-
408   based and visual prompting methods, V IPACT can effectively invoke specialized agents or vision
409
      expert models to enhance its understanding of the image. Moreover, V IPACT does not solely rely
      on the outputs from these agents, as the evidence they provide may be incorrect or errors may occur.
410
      Instead, it aggregates all useful evidence with additional reasoning steps to infer the final answer,
411
      showcasing its ability to handle uncertainties and integrate multiple sources of information which
412   ensures its superior generalization ability. Figure 3 and 4 in Appendix D showcase the complete
413   reasoning process of V IPACT to solve visual perception tasks.

512   V IPACT demonstrates significant improvements in VLM visual perception, fine-grained perception
513   remains a bottleneck for further improvement. Specifically, the model lacks the spatial intelligence
514   or imaginative abilities (Chen et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2024) necessary to infer the relative posi-

516   projection) but in the context of real-life scenes. Noticeably, these limitations hinder the model’s

      also examine the significance of multiple image inputs for VLMs in Appendix F.

520   7   C ONCLUSION
521
522   We introduce V IPACT, a VLM-based agent framework that synergizes multi-agent collaboration
523   and vision expert models for fine-grained visual perception tasks. By combining the planning
524   and function-calling capabilities of SOTA VLMs, V IPACT enhances VLMs’ System-2 reasoning
525
      through multi-agent interactions and integrates high-precision, pixel-level information from special-
      ized vision models. Our comprehensive experiments across a diverse range of visual perception
526
      tasks demonstrate that V IPACT achieves SOTA performance, outperforming previous baselines.
527
      The comprehensive ablation study highlights the critical role of multi-agent collaboration in elic-
528   iting detailed information for reasoning, as well as the importance of image input in task planning.
529   Furthermore, our error analysis highlights several inherent limitations in current SOTA VLMs that
530   form bottlenecks in our framework, offering valuable insights for future improvements.
531
      Our work has several limitations: (1) The inference cost of VLMs can be high, as our framework
532
      often requires multiple inferences, including tool calls and specialized agents’ outputs, increasing
533
      computational overhead. This is a common issue across all multi-agent frameworks that involve
534   complex reasoning steps, and it is inevitable when generating more detailed reasoning. (2) V IPACT
535   relies heavily on GPT-4o due to its superior instruction-following and function-calling abilities for
536   our needs. While we have explored other VLMs, such as LLaVa-OneVision-7B (Li et al., 2024a) in
537   Appendix C, they struggle with following instructions such as formatting requirements. However,
538   V IPACT is a general framework and can be adapted to other VLMs as they evolve. (3) We did not
539   design task-specific vision expert tools for every task, but V IPACT’s modular architecture allows
      easy integration of additional tools and agents in a plug-and-play manner.

1039   C    E XPLORATION OF DIFFERENT VLM S
1040
1041   In addition to the GPT-4o used in our main experiments, we also evaluate other VLMs on our tasks.
1042   Specifically, we explore five additional SOTA VLMs, including (1) open-source models: LLaVA-
1043   OneVision-7B (Li et al., 2024a), the latest open-source model in the LLaVA series, InternVL-2-Pro
1044   (Chen et al., 2023d; 2024a), and Llama-3.2-90b-vision (Dubey et al., 2024); and (2) close-source
1045   models: Gemini-1.5-Pro (Team et al., 2024) and Claude-3.5-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024).
1046
       For open-source models, we find that applying V IPACT’s prompt (described in Section H) reveals
1047   significant limitations. These VLMs often fail to follow key instructions, such as adhering to the
1048   required format, which is critical for extracting the tool-use indicators necessary for integrating
1049   external tools. Furthermore, they frequently generate image captions even when no such instruction
1050   is provided, suggesting a bias towards image captioning or description tasks.
1051
       To evaluate these open-source VLMs comprehensively, we apply prompting baselines and report
1052
       the results on the Blink benchmark and MMVP in Table 10 and Table 9. These results demonstrate
1053   that while LLaVA-OneVision-7B achieves above-random accuracy on tasks like object counting
1054   and spatial relations—typical of standard VQA problems found in prior datasets (Li et al., 2024b;
1055   Bansal et al., 2020)—it performs near or below random on other tasks. We also observe significant
1056   positional biases (Zhang et al., 2024f; Shi et al., 2024a), with this model frequently predicting the
1057   first option for most data points within a task. In contrast, InternVL-2-Pro and Llama-3.2-90b-vision
1058   exhibit better performance, though still significantly behind GPT-4o. These findings indicate that
1059   current open-source SOTA VLMs struggle with generalizing to more complex or non-standard VQA
1060   tasks, lacking the fine-grained perception capabilities necessary for broader applicability. Moreover,
1061
       alternative prompting strategies do not yield noticeable improvements over the zero-shot baseline
       for these models.
1062
1063   In contrast, the two additional close-source VLMs—Gemini-1.5-Pro and Claude-3.5-
1064   Sonnet—demonstrate instruction-following abilities comparable to GPT-4o, allowing effective
1065   application of our V IPACT framework. As shown in Tables 7 and 8, applying V IPACT on these
1066   models consistently outperforms previous prompting baselines, achieving significant improvements.
1067
       These results highlight the effectiveness and generalization capability of our approach when used
       with models possessing strong instruction-following capabilities.

       tasks using various VLMs, including GPT-4o and LLaVA-OneVision-7B. Following previous works
1174
       (Brown, 2020; Alayrac et al., 2022; Awadalla et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024), we
1175
       append a series of (image(s), question, answer) triplets—ranging from 1 to 5—before the test query,
1176   within the overall instruction. This setup has been shown to enhance performance in LLMs on a wide
1177   range of NLP tasks. Additionally, prior research indicates that LLMs can be sensitive to the selection
1178   of in-context exemplars (Nguyen & Wong, 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; Agrawal et al., 2023; Chen

1180   selection: (1) Randomly select a specified number of exemplars. (2) Select exemplars based on top-
1181   K similarity using the averaged CLIP embedding of images, which captures both textual semantics
1182   and visual information (Radford et al., 2021). (3) Select exemplars based on top-K similarity using
1183   ViT embeddings (Alexey, 2020), which focus purely on visual features.
