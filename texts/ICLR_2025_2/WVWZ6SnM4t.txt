011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This study investigates the efficacy of Multi-Agent Systems in eliciting cross-
014            agent communication and enhancing collective intelligence through group
015            decision-making in a decentralized setting. Unlike centralized mechanisms, where
016
               a fixed hierarchy governs social choice, decentralized group decision-making al-
               lows agents to engage in joint deliberation. Our research focuses on the dynamics
017
               of communication and decision-making within various social choice methods. By
018
               applying different voting rules in various environments, we find that moderate de-
019            cision flexibility yields better outcomes. Additionally, exploring the linguistic fea-
020            tures of agent-to-agent conversations reveals indicators of effective collaboration,
021            offering insights into communication patterns that facilitate or hinder collabora-
022            tion. Finally, we propose various methods for determining the optimal stopping
023            point in multi-agent collaborations based on linguistic cues. Our findings con-
024            tribute to a deeper understanding of how decentralized decision-making and group
025            conversation shape multi-agent collaboration, with implications for the design of
026            more effective MAS environments.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Collaboration is a fundamental aspect of the nature and human society. Whether among humans
032   or animals, working together allows groups to overcome individual limitations and achieve greater
033
      collective outcomes. In nature, collaboration often arises as a strategy to boost survival, enhance
      resource gathering, or increase efficiency in completing tasks (Schmidt & Mech, 1997). Similarly,
034
      in human societies, collaboration drives innovation, facilitates problem-solving, and fosters shared
035
      understanding, enabling individuals to address complex challenges that would be unmanageable oth-
036   erwise (De Man & Duysters, 2005; Graesser et al., 2018; Bittner & Leimeister, 2013). This innate
037   tendency to collaborate is evident across various domains, from social communities to technological
038   systems, where multiple entities coordinate their efforts toward a common goal. As we advance
039   in developing intelligent agents, understanding and replicating these collaborative dynamics in arti-
040   ficial systems has become increasingly important, predominantly to cope with the complexity and
041   adaptability seen in real-world interactions.
042   Agents powered by Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive problem-solving
043   capabilities across a wide range of tasks. However, single-agent systems encounter significant diffi-
044   culties when tasked with problems that are either too large or complex, often resulting in instability,
045   misalignment with the intended request, and hallucination (Liu et al., 2024; Kuhn et al., 2023; Lyu
046   et al., 2023). To address these limitations, research has increasingly turned toward Multi-Agent
047   Systems (MAS). MAS have shown greater efficacy in harnessing collective intelligence by allowing
048   individual agents to specialize in distinct skills and facilitating effective collaboration among them
049   (Guo et al., 2024).
050   When agents working together in a MAS, it is natural for them to have varying interpretations and
051   perspectives. While some opinions may align, disagreements are also frequent. This creates an in-
052   evitable tension between cooperation and competition, stemming from differences in backgrounds,
053   information access, and individual goals. Therefore, the process of aggregating models’ diverse pre-
      dictions into a final group decision becomes a crucial aspect of dynamic multi-agent collaboration.

072
      Many existing LLM-driven MAS are designed based on centralized group decision-making, which
073
      typically involves layered or centralized architectures with a hierarchy. When there is a conflict
074
      between agents, it is resolved by a pre-assigned agent or a pre-defined process. These systems are
075   often used for tasks where agent networks follow the waterfall method, leading to a stratified ar-
076   rangement among agents (Hong et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023). However, this
077   hierarchical setup poses several critical challenges: (1) fairness: individual agent messages may not
078   be accurately represented in the final group outcome, leading to potential misrepresentation (Jiang
079   & Lu, 2019); (2) rigidity: the system’s fixed structure may lead to over-fitting to specific scenarios
080   (Chen et al., 2023), reducing its adaptability to diverse and dynamic environments; and (3) bias: the
081   agent with final decision-making power may introduce its own biases into the process, potentially
082   distorting the outcomes (Owens et al., 2024). Additionally, centralized MAS cannot perform in en-
083
      vironments requiring independent agent decisions, where private or incomplete information hinders
      a central authority from dictating optimal strategies (Xu et al., 2023).
084
085   Decentralized group decision-making can ease these issues by distributing power among agents,
086   where each agent has the ability to participate in the process. This is a common setting in world
087   simulation and embodied environment, where agents need to behave independently because there
088   exists information asymmetry or data boundary between agents (Mandi et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
089
      2023a; Xu et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023), and possibly variability in capabilities that different agents
      have. With decisions made by multiple agents, the flexible structure of decentralized MAS adapts to
090
      various environments, but the lack of a fixed hierarchy demands careful monitoring of collaboration
091
      patterns.
092
093   Centralized and decentralized group decision-making attempt to mimic the ways in which human
094   societies form collective policies, such as in monarchies and democracies. These decision-making
095
      mechanisms, known as social choice, are studied across various fields including economics, math-
      ematics, philosophy, and social science, with the goal of aggregating and synthesizing individual
096
      preferences into a unified consensus. However, the impact of social choice methods on LLM-based
097
      MAS has yet to be explored.
098
099   In this study, we evaluate various social choice methods across different environments to observe and
100   analyze agents’ group behaviors and collaboration pattern. This paper aims to provide the following
101
      research contributions:
102
       • We investigate how collaborative behavior in decentralized MAS varies across social choice
103
         methods, providing insights into how they influence overall cooperation and outcomes.
104
105
       • We identify key language features in multi-agent conversations as indicators of collaboration,
         offering a novel approach to analyzing linguistic cues in effective or ineffective interactions.
106
107    • We propose various methods for determining the optimal stopping point in multi-agent collabo-
         ration, utilizing the linguistic features we identified.

      Multi Agent Frameworks Recent advancements in MAS have led to the development of nu-
111
      merous platforms that facilitate collaboration among multiple agents. AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023)
112
      introduces a framework for building LLM-based applications where agents communicate with each
113   other to accomplish tasks. CAMEL (Li et al., 2023a) is designed to enable autonomous collabora-
114   tion among chat-based language models using role-playing and inception prompting. ChatDev (Qian
115   et al., 2023) offers a chat-driven framework where LLM-powered agents work together to streamline
116   software design, coding, and testing through unified language communication. MetaGPT (Hong
117   et al., 2023) provides a meta-programming approach that encodes Standardized Operating Proce-
118   dures into prompt sequences, facilitating efficient and error-reducing collaboration among LLM-
119   based agents. AgentVerse (Chen et al., 2023) orchestrates collaborative groups of expert agents,
120   drawing inspiration from human group dynamics to enhance task performance beyond the capabili-
121
      ties of individual agents. These MAS platforms address various aspects of the collaborative process,
      including agent profiling, recruitment, memory, planning, and communication protocols. However,
122
      they do not specifically investigate the group decision-making mechanisms within multi-agent inter-
123
      actions as we do in this paper. Some platforms employ centralized methods, and others only rely on
124   unanimous or majority voting.
125
126
      Multi-Agent Collaboration Environments Multi-agent collaboration has been used to handle
127
      various applications. In robotics, there has been a continuous study regarding multi-agent collab-
128
      oration in embodied environments (Dudek et al., 1996; Ota, 2006; Cena et al., 2013; Vorotnikov
129   et al., 2018). In these studies, multi-agent collaboration in robotics has been explored through var-
130   ious frameworks, such as task allocation, coordination strategies, and communication protocols, to
131   optimize collective performance and adaptability in dynamic environments. For LLM-based MAS,
132   software development is a popular environment which inherently requires diverse expertise, contin-
133   uous integration, and coordinated teamwork to build complex, functional systems efficiently (Qian
134   et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023). Additionally, LLM’s strong context understand-
135   ing and immense parametric knowledge supported various world simulations with multi-agents.
136   Including simulating society (Park et al., 2023; 2022), economy (Li et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2023),
137
      gaming (Xu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023), and psychology (Aher et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b).
      These works require agents to interact with others in a group, and investigate their cooperate and
138
      competitive behavior.

141   3     M ULTI -AGENT C OLLABORATION AND G ROUP D ECISION -M AKING
142
143   In this section, we first introduce the formal definition of the setting this study uses for MAS, and
144   propose RoundTable, a turn-based multi-agent collaboration platform for evaluating different de-
145   centralized group decision-making.
146
      In a collaboration environment, there is a set of individuals (agents) i ∈ I, and a set of all
147
      possible states of the world X = {x1 , x2 , x3 , ...}. Then there is an individual utility function
148
      ui : X → R for each agent that maps a state to a real number, representing preferences of
149   agents. Based on the individual utility function, each agent proposes a state with proposal function
150   pi = fp (ui , Ci , X), pi ∈ X, where Ci is the context, including environment information, back-
151   ground, and collaboration history. Similarly, agent can also vote on proposals with voting function
152   vij = fv (ui , Ci , S|pi ∈ S ⊆ X), i, j ∈ I, where S represents a candidate list of proposals to vote on.
153   With all proposals and agents’ votes, a social choice method (function) F decides one group decision
154   x∗ = F (pi , vij |i, j ∈ I). For example, when F = M ajority, p1 = Apple, p2 = Banana, p3 =
155   Carrot, v1,j , v2,j = (Y es, N o, N o), v3,j = (N o, Y es, N o), then x∗ = p1 = Apple.
156

178   3.2   S OCIAL C HOICE M ETHODS
179
      To investigate the impact of different social choice methods on group decision-making in multi-agent
180
      collaboration, we compare the following 6 mechanisms:
181
182    • Unanimous Voting(Arrow, 2012): The proposal that receives votes from all agents will be se-
183      lected.
184
       • Majority Voting(Arrow, 2012): The proposal that receives votes from more than half of all agents
185
         will be selected.
186
187
       • Plurality Voting(Arrow, 2012): The proposal that receives the most votes will be selected.
188    • Rated Voting(Baujard et al., 2018): Each agent assigns ratings on a 5-point Likert scale to all
189      candidate proposals, with 1 being the lowest and 5 being the highest. The proposal with the
190      highest total score will be selected.
191    • Ranked Voting(Arrow, 2012): Each agent ranks all candidate proposals from the most preferred
192      to the least preferred. Social Choice will assign 1, 1/2, 1/3... points to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd...
193      candidates on each ballot. The proposal with the highest total points will be selected.
194    • Cumulative Voting(Black et al., 1958): For |I| candidate proposals, each agent is given |I| points
195      to distribute among the proposals as they see fit. The proposal with the highest total points will
196      be selected.
197
198   Unanimous, majority and plurality voting are one-vote mechanisms, where agents only choose the
199
      best candidate from the list; rated, ranked and cumulative voting are score-based mechanisms, which
      ask a nuanced, gradient preference over candidates.

      4.2   C OMPLEX E NVIRONMENT - R ECOMMENDATION S YSTEM
236
237   Introduction For the complex environment, we focus on a recommendation system. It reveals
238   unique collaborative behaviors absent in exchange economies. First, strong information asymmetry
239   exists, as no single piece of information can accurately predict ratings. Second, group decision-
240   making fosters diverse problem-solving approaches, with agents both consuming and contributing
241   information in a feedback loop that enhances recommendations.
242   The task is to predict user ratings for specific items by analyzing historical interactions and identify-
243   ing data patterns. This task is challenging for a single-agent system due to the overwhelming amount
244   of scattered and limited essential information. In our setting, we divide the background information
245   into several parts and assign each to different agents. The agents must then collaborate by exploring,
246   analyzing, exchanging, and synthesizing their information to reach a comprehensive conclusion.
247
248   Dataset We evaluate MAS performance using the MovieLens-100k dataset (Harper & Konstan,
249   2015). The dataset consists of three main tables: user, which contains demographic information
250   of all users; item, which includes details of all movies, such as title, release date, and genre; and
251
      data, which holds 100,000 ratings from 943 users on 1,682 movies. Due to resource constraints, we
      randomly selected 100 examples from the u1.test split for evaluation. To simplify the data structure,
252
      we pre-processed it into three tables: basicInfo, userHistory, and movieHistory. Each table was
253
      assigned to a different agent, creating a 3-agent collaboration system. The definition and example
254   of each table can be found in Appendix C.4.
255
256   Metrics We use Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) to represent
257   utility Ur , assessing the accuracy of predictions compared to the gold rating at round r. To evaluate
258   MAS performance in the recommendation task, we compare it against two baselines: a simple
259   Always Guess 4, which predicts the median regardless of input, and a strong State of the Art (SoTA)
260   model from Behera & Nain (2023), which employs collaborative filtering with temporal features.

291   4.5   E ARLY S TOPPING IN MAS
292
      In the previous experiments, we enforced 10 rounds of collaboration to compare agent behaviors
293   and identify patterns. However, effective early stopping can prevent redundancy, avoid stagnation,
294   and optimize decision-making by reducing unnecessary iterations. In this experiment, we evaluate
295   the following early stopping methods:
296
297    • @10 is our baseline, the final performance after 10 rounds without early stopping.
298    • First Agreement stops collaboration whenever a proposal passes the social choice criterion.
299
       • Consecutive Agreements is when no one make additional proposal after a proposal has been
300      accepted in the previous round.
301
       • Validation Checkpoint is the average number of rounds that produced the best outcomes in the
302
         train set, used as an early stopping criterion for all test sets.
303
304    • Information Difference is the average embedding distance captured at the round that produced
305
         the best outcomes in the train set, and stops collaborations in the test set when the distance
         became lower than the threshold. This idea is supported by the observation in Section 5.3.1.
306
307    • Dialogue Act method utilizes pairs of dialogue acts, linking one from the previous round to
308      another from the current round. We perform ordinary least squares (OLS) regression on all such
309
         pairs in relation to the performance. The regression coefficients indicate the most impactful pairs
         that act as stop signals. Further details of the algorithm are provided in Appendix C.7.
310
311   We use 5-fold cross validation to compare the methods with Oracle: the oracle performance for
312   early stopping, which reflects the best outcomes from each test simulation.

475
476   6.1    I MPACT OF S OCIAL C HOICE M ETHODS ON C OLLABORATIVE B EHAVIOR
477
478   The study demonstrates that social choice methods significantly impact multi-agent collaboration,
479   with score-based mechanisms achieving higher performance and efficiency, especially in early
480   rounds, by allowing agents to express nuanced preferences. In contrast, one-vote mechanisms strug-
481   gle with rigidity, leading to lower initial performance. The strictness of social choice methods, such
482
      as unanimous voting, reduces collaboration performance due to its inability to accommodate dissent.
      Score-based mechanisms, which support expressing preference gradients, foster dynamic exchanges
483
      and improved decision quality, making them more effective in negotiation-heavy tasks.
484
485   One-vote mechanisms also have unique advantages. First, the format of voting is simpler than score-
      based mechanisms, which led to fewer format errors during experiments from LLMs. Furthermore,


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      unanimous voting showed the highest fairness and non-decreasing outcome over rounds when utility
487   is quantifiable. These strengths suggest that social choice methods in decentralized MAS should be
488   thoughtfully chosen based on the specific task, or the primary metric being prioritized. Mixture of
489   different methods in different stages of collaboration may also bring effectiveness, which we leave
490   for future studies.
491
492   6.2   L INGUISTIC I NDICATORS OF C OLLABORATION
493
494   Linguistic analysis of agent conversations reveals several key indicators of effective collaboration.
495   The length and complexity of messages increase progressively across rounds, suggesting that deeper
496   engagement and richer exchanges occur as the collaboration advanced. This is particularly evident
497   in the recommendation system environment, where tasks require greater information exchange and
498   negotiation. Complex tasks inherently demand more intricate language, as agents need to convey
499
      not just factual information but also interpret, analyze, and synthesize inputs from others.
500   Dialogue acts provide another layer of insight into collaborative behavior. Inform, Propose and
501   Request acts dominate conversations, highlighting that agents were frequently sharing new infor-
502   mation and seeking clarifications, which are essential for progressing towards a solution. Confirm
503   act became more prevalent in later stages, particularly in environments where validation was cru-
504   cial. Interestingly, Propose and Evaluate acts were more frequent in negotiation-oriented tasks, like
505
      exchange economies, where agents needed to offer and assess potential solutions regularly. This
      linguistic pattern underscores that successful collaboration hinges on the flow of information, clar-
506
      ification, and ongoing evaluation of proposals. These indicators align with patterns seen in human
507
      collaboration, where information exchange and continuous feedback loops drive cooperative suc-
508   cess.
509
510
      6.3   E ARLY S TOPPING IN M ULTI -AGENT C OLLABORATION
511
512   Early stopping is essential in multi-agent collaboration because it prevents diminishing returns and
513   inefficiency in decision-making. Our experiments shows that while the initial rounds of collabo-
514   ration led to significant improvements in group utility and decision quality, continuing beyond a
515   certain point often results in stagnant or even declining performance. This highlights the need for
516   mechanisms that can intelligently terminate the collaboration process once optimal solutions have
517   been reached, avoiding unnecessary iterations.
518   Key insights into effective early stopping methods emerge from analyzing conversational patterns.
519   Information difference suggests that as the rounds progress, the novelty of information decreases,
520   with fewer new ideas being introduced. Transition graphs of dialogue acts reveal common col-
521   laborative patterns. These signals lead to linguistic feature-based stopping methods, which halt
522   collaboration when no meaningful new information is detected, or when dialogue act patterns indi-
523   cate the need for termination. Our findings demonstrate that implementing early stopping methods
524   based on linguistic cues can enhance the efficiency and effectiveness of decentralized multi-agent
525
      collaboration by preventing unnecessary prolonged discussions.
526
527   7     C ONCLUSION
528
529   This paper investigates the dynamics of cross-agent communication and decentralized decision-
530   making in MAS, exploring how agent conversation and social choice methods impact collaboration
531   and collective intelligence. We show that only the correct selection of a social choice method for
532   a given environment can promote the success of a MAS, linguistic features of agent conversations
533   serve as indicators of effective collaboration, and these cues provide valuable information for the
534   effective termination of MAS.
535   LLM generation and prediction are unstable, with single agents often making quasi-random actions
536   based on observations. Decentralized MAS improve performance by guiding agents through con-
537   versations to make more targeted proposals and enabling group decision-making to select the best
538   option. Our study provides insights into how group communication and decentralized decision-
539   making enhance multi-agent collaboration, offering important implications for designing more effi-
      cient MAS environments.

      A.1   ROUND TABLE P LATFORM D ESIGN
672
673   In this section, we explain the details of the design in RoundTable by breaking it down to phases.
674
675
      Input and Initialization defines each agent for collaboration. Each agent will be given a specific Ci
      and ui , which include the same input user query, information of RoundTable, and the social choice
676
      method F . However, they may have different background, information access, or utility function.
677
      While Ci and ui can be defined automatically by agent recruiting or profiling (Chen et al., 2023),
678   we manually define them for simplicity.
679
680   Message Phase is the initial stage of the iterative collaboration process, where all agents participate
681
      in a simultaneous, open chat conversation. During this phase, agents can freely choose recipients
      based on their context to send messages. They can respond to others’ questions, share insights, or
682
      ask their own questions. There are no restrictions on whom to communicate with or what to say.
683
      Once generated, all messages are updated to Ci for everyone, regardless of the original sender or
684   intended recipients. This process guarantees that all messages are sent simultaneously, makes it has
685   no specific order of communication. Since only one message can be sent per iteration, back-and-
686   forth conversations can take place across multiple rounds.
687
      Proposal Phase allows agents to present a proposal pi , offering a potential solution or response to
688
      the user query from their perspective, aiming to fulfil their own goal or maximize the utility. Before
689
      generating a proposal, agents will be asked to do a step by step reasoning, which is by default
690   invisible to other agents. Reasoning is designed to give agents a chance to summarize and analyze
691   information in Ci . The most recent proposal from each agent will be considered as a candidate
692   for voting in the next phase. Like the Message Phase, proposals are made simultaneously and will
693   be updated for Ci once everyone has generated a response. Agents also have the option to skip
694   submitting a new proposal; in such cases, their previous proposal will automatically be included
695   among the candidates.
696   Voting Phase collects one latest proposal pi from each agent, additionally include the latest in-
697   termediate group decision x∗ from previous rounds to form a candidate proposal list. Agents in
698   this round are asked to express preferences toward candidates. The preferences can be in various
699   formats, including voting, rating and ranking, depends on what types of social choice method F
700   is used. Similar with the proposal phase, agents have option to not vote (give up). With all the
701   collected proposals and preferences, social choice method selects one proposal as the intermediate
      group decision. Finally, voting details and the result are updated to all agents’ context Ci .

845   C.1     S IMPLE AND C OMPLEX E NVIRONMENTS
846
      Simulated environments are simplified, abstract representations of the world. They include eco-
847
      nomic experiments, such as those based on game theory, and games like chess. The primary advan-
848
      tages of simulated environments are: 1. They allow for the emphasis on specific agent behaviors
849   within a controlled setting by modifying the environmental design; 2. The utility function can be
850   mathematically expressed, and the quality of each proposal can be quantified numerically.
851
852
      Complex environments are real-world applications that are more intricate and challenging, offering
      a closer reflection of scenarios where agents must handle numerous variables and unpredictability.
853
      These include tasks like collaborative problem-solving, negotiations, and real-time strategy games.
854
      While evaluation in these settings can be subjective, they provide deeper insights into how agents
855   adapt, cooperate, and make decisions in dynamic, less controlled conditions, highlighting their re-
856   silience and versatility.

914

      based on the final recommendation but also on the process of reaching that recommendation. The
