009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            In this paper, we introduce a novel 3D-aware image generation framework that
013
               ensures high-quality and view-consistent image generation. Our core idea is to
               leverage the semantic latent space of a pre-trained 2D GAN for 3D view-consistent
014
               image generation, eliminating need for large-scale dataset use and prior knowledge
015
               of camera poses. To achieve this, we propose a latent refiner with multi-view
016            and geometry-preserving capabilities, enabled by self-calibrated depth and pose
017            estimation. Thanks to the advances of diffusion models, our refiner allows for
018            view-consistent latent manipulation in GANs and can be trained using a self-
019            supervised fashion. Our method optimizes the latent codes of a pre-trained 2D
020            GAN across a wide range of pose angles. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our
021            method through evaluations and comparisons with existing baselines on benchmark
022            datasets. Experimental results show the superiority of our method over existing
023            works in both the quality and view-consistency of generated images.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Automatic 3D asset generation has witnessed rapid developments in recent years. Such progress has
057   empowered the generation ability of neural networks in a wide range of data generation-related tasks
058   such as VR/AR applications and interactive games. Recently, with the advancement of generative
059   models and 3D representation, 3D-aware GANs (Chan et al., 2022; Skorokhodov et al., 2022; Schwarz
060   et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022) and conditional 3D diffusion models using text and
061   images (Liu et al., 2024; 2023b; Long et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024) have made significant progress
062   with potential applications across various industries adopting visual computing methods.
063   Among these generative models, we are inspired by 3D-aware GANs (Chan et al., 2022) that extends
064   a 2D GAN by incorporating neural implicit representations and rendering, resulting in improved
065   multi-view consistency. However, 3D-aware GANs require extensive training data to be able to
066   maintain the quality of generated images. These methods necessitate significant computational
067   resources, particularly during rendering and training phases, leading to longer training time and need
068   for high-performance GPUs. Computational constraints often mandate lower-resolution rendering,
069   followed by 2D upscaling, which can compromise view consistency. GMPI (Zhao et al., 2022) and
070   Ray-conditioning (Chen et al., 2023) suggest another approach to reduce the computational issues.
071
      Specifically, GMPI modifies a 2D GAN to generate multi-plane images, ensuring view consistency
      and 3D awareness with minimal structural changes. Ray-conditioning (Chen et al., 2023) enhances
072
      photo-realism over photo-consistency, conditioning 2D GANs with light-field priors to produce
073
      high-resolution images without using comprehensive 3D models. However, they still require pose
074   distribution and large-scale data sources. Additionally, their ability to maintain 3D consistency may
075   diminish at wider viewing angles.
076
077
      In this paper, we opt to investigate pre-trained 2D GANs and adapt them for 3D-aware image synthesis.
      Our motivation for this choice is to avoid any dependency on use of large-scale multi-view or 3D
078
      data in training as these are usually difficult and expensive to be obtained. We also choose GANs
079
      instead of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) as, compared with diffusion models, by design, GANs’
080   latent spaces are more interpretable and can be computed in much faster speed. Furthermore, latent
081   manipulation in a 2D GAN (Shen et al., 2020; Shen & Zhou, 2021; Härkönen et al., 2020; Wu et al.,
082   2021; Zhu et al., 2023; Tewari et al., 2020) has demonstrated an ability to learn 3D-aware features,
083   resulting in generated images with diverse object poses. Inspired by the ability to learn implicit 3D
084   knowledge in 2D GANs, our goal is to generate multi-view consistent images by refining the latent
085   space of a pre-trained 2D GAN with a tailor-designed latent refiner, enabling cross-view and identity
086   consistency (see Fig. 1). Note that our method neither requires 3D data nor prior knowledge on
087   camera pose distribution during training. Additionally, it utilizes pre-trained models available online
088
      for 3D-aware image synthesis, thus further enhances its flexibility and practicality.
089   Key to our method is the integration of image inverse warping loss and consistency regularization
090   strategies, which is shown to enhance the quality of generated images via latent refinement. We
091   address the challenge of maintaining multi-view consistency by incorporating self-calibrated depth
092   and pose estimation, supplementing 3D information to training of our latent refiner. We realize our
093   latent refiner with a diffusion model, which is capable of maintaining high consistency through a
094
      close form of forward-backward consistency regulation. The model is trained to learn geometric
      consistency in a multi-view setting, guided by a photometric loss and pose matrices. Such a training
095
      process ensures the fidelity of generated data across a wide range of views without relying on
096
      extensive datasets and heavy computational burden.
097
098   We evaluate our method on the FFHQ dataset (Karras et al., 2019) and SDIP Elephants
099   dataset (Mokady et al., 2022). Experimental results show that our method outperforms state-of-the-art
100
      3D-aware GANs in wide-angle conditions in terms of both multi-view consistency and image quality.

102   2   R ELATED W ORK
103
104   GAN-based image manipulation. Several studies (Shen et al., 2020; Shen & Zhou, 2021; Härkönen
105   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021; Collins et al., 2020; Ling et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2020; Tewari et al.,
106   2020; Zhu et al., 2023) have found that the latent space of the StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019; 2020b;a;
107   2021) includes remarkably disentangling attributes and rich semantic information, showcasing the
      capability to alter images produced by pre-trained GANs via arithmetic manipulations of their latent


                                                         2

108
      vectors. These alterations keep generated images within their original distribution while infusing
109   them with new attributes. By applying pose disentanglement arithmetic manipulations to pre-trained
110   models, one can obtain a variety of multi-view face generators. This inspires us to explore the
111   conversion of a 2D GAN into a 3D-aware GAN. However, manipulations of high-dimensional vectors
112   in pose generation make other face attributions (e.g., geometry) inconsistent across viewpoints. Also,
113   latent semantic information is sensitive, and thus different samples may lead to different generation
114   results. Several works (Tewari et al., 2020) require additional labeling, pre-trained attributes classifier,
115   or 3D reconstruction to obtain accurate 3D parameters. DragGAN (Pan et al., 2023) learned latent
116   codes through a loss function that enforces the movement of user-selected image locations towards
117
      target ones. Unlike previous methods, our method learns view-consistent latent codes using pose
      information through self-calibrated learning.
118
119   3D-aware GANs. The success of neural radiance fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) in multi-view
120   rendering has opened a new research direction in 3D-aware GANs (Chan et al., 2022; Skorokhodov
121   et al., 2022; Schwarz et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022). In particular,
122   EG3D (Chan et al., 2022) learned a tri-plane representation with upsampling layers. GRAM (Deng
123
      et al., 2022) learned a surface manifold representation with implicit isosurfaces, like the multi-plane
      representation used in GMPI (Zhao et al., 2022). EpiGRAF (Skorokhodov et al., 2022) conditioned
124
      a discriminator with camera pose data to reduce the complexity of its architecture, improving the
125
      training time. Compared with methods that manipulate the latent space, 3D-aware GANs achieve
126   better view consistency. However, existing 3D-aware GANs require large-scale datasets to maintain
127   the generation quality (e.g., high resolutions). Moreover, it is hard to reuse pre-trained models to build
128   3D-aware GANs. Recently, Ray-conditioning (Chen et al., 2023) utilized a 2D GAN conditioned by a
129   light field prior for multi-view rendering. Various inversion and editing techniques can be created by
130   applying StyleGAN, without the use of a geometry 3D prior. However, directly use of the latent space
131   of the StyleGAN may not preserve the identity of objects across views. Our method can maintain
132   the identity of objects in multiple views due to the aid of depth and pose information, which are
133   learned efficiently with self-supervision. In addition to the methods we have discussed, there is an
134
      ongoing research trend adapting these methods for handling large-scale, complex data (Sargent et al.,
      2023; Skorokhodov et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2022), as well as exploring their integration with diffusion
135
      models (Xiang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2023) and text-based techniques (Seo et al.,
136
      2023; Rombach et al., 2022; Qian et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; 2024; Long et al., 2023). However,
137   such an exploration is beyond the scope of our work.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
143   We aim to design a method that can generate high-quality and multi-view consistent images of a given
144   subject, e.g., a human face. This objective could be achieved by using image-to-image translation
145   techniques (Isola et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2017). However, training of a conditional image-to-image
146   translation model is known to be computationally expensive while generation results are limited in
147   both image resolution and viewpoints. In this paper, instead of working directly on the image domain,
148   we propose to manipulate the latent space of a GAN model. Specifically, we adopt a pre-trained
149   2D GAN (e.g., StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020b)), denoted as G, to generate images of a subject
150   defined by a latent code w in different viewpoints. To do so, a straightforward approach is to generate
151
      latent vectors ŵ by sampling the latent space W (e.g., the GANSpace (Härkönen et al., 2020)), i.e.,
      ŵ ∼ pw , w ∈ W . However, we observed that such an approach may result in view-inconsistent
152
      images (see Fig. 1). To address this issue, we develop a latent refiner that revises sampled codes
153
      ŵ into view-consistent latent codes ŵ+ by taking into account the geometric information (depth
154   and pose) of the input subject across views. We realize our latent refiner by a guided diffusion
155   model (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). We learn the depth and pose information in a self-supervised
156   learning fashion. The pipeline of our method is illustrated in fig. 2, where each step is described in a
157   respective subsection below.

198   3.3    P SEUDO DEPTH AND POSE ESTIMATION
199
      Given the sampled latent codes w and ŵ, and their corresponding images G(w) and G(ŵ), we train
200
      a depth and a pose estimator. Specifically, we train from scratch an unsupervised depth estimation
201
      model based on Monodepth2 (Godard et al., 2019) on our generated data, i.e., G(w) and G(ŵ).
202   The depth estimation model receives inputs as an image G(w) and its subsequent image G(ŵ), and
203   estimates a pseudo depth map dw for the subject captured by the image pair (G(w), G(ŵ)). Fig. 3
204   illustrates several pseudo depth estimation results. We noticed disruptive effects at the subject’s
205   boundaries. This is because the depth estimator is trained with pseudo data. However, the pseudo
206   depth maps are sufficient to carry out the overall 3D information for further processing in the pipeline.
207
      Similarly, we customize Monodepth2 (Godard et al., 2019) to make it our pose estimator. We train the
208   pose estimator to predict a pose transformation Tw→ŵ (a 3 × 3 matrix) that transforms the pose from
209   G(w) to that in G(ŵ). Note that, as shown in Fig. 3, the training data for both the depth estimator and
210   pose estimator is created at uniform intervals from the pseudo-multi-view images G(w) and G(ŵ).

300   For quantitative evaluation, we employed the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017)
301   and Kernel Inception Distance (KID) (Bińkowski et al., 2018) as performance metrics. These metrics
302   are widely used for assessment of the perceptual quality of generated images against real images
303   (i.e., photo realism). However, unlike previous research, we first evaluated our method for multi-view
304   consistent image generation by using a pose estimator model to obtain the overall distribution of
305   poses. We divided this distribution into several zones and compared real and generated samples within
306   each zone. We then generated and selected samples from each zone, resulting in a total of 6,000
307
      images for evaluation. By comparing images of the same instances from multiple viewpoints within
      these zones, we assessed the multi-view consistency and image quality of generated images. This
308
      experimental design, grounded in the dataset’s angle distribution, enables a precise and substantial
309
      evaluation of our method’s ability to produce high-quality and view-consistent images.
310
311   We also compared our method with existing ones using the Identity Consistency (IC) metric (Tov
312   et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2019) and Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004). IC
313
      and SSIM reflect the consistency of a generated image against its original input. Specifically, we
      measured IC by utilizing face recognition features (Tov et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2019) for faces and
314
      CLIP features (Radford et al., 2021) for elephants.

518   4.5   L IMITATIONS
519
520   Our method is not without limitations. In particular, the performance of our method relies on the
521   initial set of multi-view latent codes used to construct the latent space for subsequent processing.
522   Our method thus requires the latent manipulations to be able to generate reasonable results. Hence,
523   it is challenging to construct a meaningful latent space if pose disentanglement is poorly executed,
524   preventing the creation of initial multi-view images with reasonable quality.
525
526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   We propose a novel method for multi-view image generation. Our method first generates pseudo
529   latent-image samples from a latent space using a pre-trained 2D GAN with latent manipulation ability.
530   The generated pseudo latent-image samples are used to train a depth estimator and a pose estimator,
531   which are then employed to condition a latent refinement process governed by a diffusion model. By
532   introducing new warping and regularization losses, our method can be trained using self-supervised
533   approach. We demonstrated the robustness of our method via extensive experiments on benchmark
534   datasets. Experimental results show that our method achieves the best geometric and semantic feature
535   consistency in a wide rotation margin.
536   The creation of multi-view representations used to initialize a latent space in our method relies on the
537   effectiveness of a 2D latent manipulation method, such as the one offered by GANspace Härkönen
538   et al. (2020). This means the necessity for further advancements to improve the capacity of latent
539   manipulation. Extending the proposed method to incorporate other generative models, such as
      diffusion models, would also be a potential research direction.

      A.1    L OSS FUNCTIONS STUDY
720
721   In this section, we study the effect of the loss functions used in our method. In particular, we remove
722   the warping loss and some parts of the regularization losses, including the feature loss and the latent
723   loss, and re-train the corresponding models for comparison. We use the Frechet inception distance
724   (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), Kernel inception distance (KID) (Bińkowski et al., 2018), IC, and SSIM
725   as performance metrics for the quality and consistency of multi-view image synthesis.
726
      We report the results of these experiments in Table 5. The results clearly illustrate the significant
727   influence of the warping loss, particularly regarding the image quality and view-consistency of
728   generated images. The integration of feature and latent losses plays a pivotal role in refining latent
729   codes, making them aligned with near-source view latent codes. We visualize several generation
730   results by the loss functions in Fig. 8. As shown, without feature and latent regularizations, although
731   still appearing similar to the source view, there are differences in the fine details of the generation
732   results such as the extent to which the hair covers the ears or smile strength.

734   A.2    C OMPARISON TO DIFFUSION - BASED NOVEL VIEW SYNTHESIS
735
736   We included a comparison of our method with diffusion-based methods in 9. In this comparison,
737   we used the center images from one of our samples as the input for image-to-3D conversion. Some
738
      existing methods predict multi-view images at specific angles, so we could not match the angles
      exactly. However, this does not affect image quality and multi-view consistency comparison. The
739
      results show that although diffusion-based methods can cover broader angles and a variety of objects,
740
      they often fall short in photorealism and identity consistency with the input image and across different
741   views. In contrast, our method was preserved well. This demonstrates that our approach is useful for
742   efficiently obtaining 3D-aware images, even for specific datasets that are difficult to generalize.
743
744
      Our method operates without the need for 3D data or camera pose information, whereas diffusion-
      based methods require abundant 3D data for fine-tuning. Despite this significant difference, our
745
      method performs comparably by utilizing a self-calibration mechanism that estimates and adjusts
746
      conditions based on limited initial information. Additionally, our approach employs a lightweight
747   training strategy, as it does not require fine-tuning of a GAN. Despite these fundamental differences,
748   our method demonstrates strong performance in generating high-quality and consistent multi-view
749   images, as seen in our comparisons with diffusion-based novel view synthesis.
