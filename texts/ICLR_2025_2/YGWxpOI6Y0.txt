                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012
               Building on the advances of language models, Large Multimodal Models (LMMs)
               have contributed significant improvements in video understanding. While the
013
               current video LMMs utilize advanced Large Language Models (LLMs), they rely
014
               on either image or video encoders to process visual inputs, each of which has its
015            own limitations. Image encoders excel at capturing rich spatial details from frame
016            sequences but lack explicit temporal context, which can be important in videos with
017            intricate action sequences. On the other hand, video encoders provide temporal
018            context but are often limited by computational constraints that lead to processing
019            only sparse frames at lower resolutions, resulting in reduced contextual and spatial
020            understanding. To this end, we introduce VideoGPT+, which combines the com-
021            plementary benefits of the image encoder (for detailed spatial understanding) and
022            the video encoder (for global temporal context modeling). The model processes
023
               videos by dividing them into smaller segments and applies an adaptive pooling
               strategy on features extracted by both image and video encoders. Our architecture
024
               showcases improved performance across multiple video benchmarks, including
025
               VCGBench, MVBench and Zero-shot question-answering. Further, we develop
026            112K video-instruction set using a novel semi-automatic annotation pipeline which
027            further improves the model performance. Additionally, to comprehensively eval-
028            uate video LMMs, we present VCGBench-Diverse, covering 18 broad video
029            categories such as lifestyle, sports, science, gaming, and surveillance videos. This
030            benchmark with 4,354 question-answer pairs evaluates the generalization of ex-
031            isting LMMs on dense video captioning, spatial and temporal understanding, and
032            complex reasoning, ensuring comprehensive assessment across diverse video types
033            and dynamics. Our code, dataset, and pre-trained models will be publicly released.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Existing methods for video understanding often rely solely on either image encoders or video
039
      encoders (Maaz et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c). Most works focus on image encoders,
      which encode multiple frames and either fuse the information or concatenate the embeddings before
040
      passing them to the LLM. When fusing the information, spatial or temporal pooling is typically
041
      used (Maaz et al., 2024). Spatial pooling has shown minimal effectiveness in capturing video
042   information, whereas temporal pooling retains some spatial information but lacks explicit temporal
043   context. On the other hand, concatenating embeddings without pooling (Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
044   2024c; Zhang et al., 2024b) can rapidly increase computational complexity due to the extended
045   context length required by the LLM, limiting the number of frames that can be processed. While this
046   approach provides better spatial representation, the overall context is still limited to few frames. The
047   limited context results in a poor understanding of the video, especially if a uniform sampling strategy
048   is employed, as it only captures small segments of the video, missing important temporal dynamics.
049   In order to address these challenges, we propose VideoGPT+ which effectively combines the
050   merits of both image and video encoders (see Fig. 2). By leveraging an image encoder for rich
051   spatial details and a video encoder for global temporal context, our model achieves improved video
052   understanding. To model finegrained temporal dynamics in VideoGPT+ , we use a segment-wise
053   sampling strategy. Unlike uniform sampling used in existing video LMMs (Maaz et al., 2024), which
      may miss important temporal dynamics, our approach divides the video into smaller segments and

      Our work has three main contributions:
084
      • We present VideoGPT+, the first video-conversation model that benefits from a dual-encoding
085     scheme based on both image and video features. These complimentary sets of features offer rich
086     spatiotemporal details for improved video understanding (Sec. 3).
087   • Addressing the limitations of existing VideoInstruct100K dataset (Maaz et al., 2024), we develop
088     VCG+ 112K with a novel semi-automatic annotation pipeline, offering dense video captions along
089     with spatial understanding and reasoning-based QA pairs, improving model performance (Sec. 4).
090   • Recognizing the lack of diverse benchmarks for video-conversation task, we propose
091     VCGBench-Diverse, which provides 4,354 human annotated QA pairs across 18 video cate-
092     gories to extensively evaluate the performance of a video-conversation model (Sec. 5).

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
095
096   Building on advances in language models, LLMs offer a flexible interface for various multimodal
097   applications. Early efforts in image-based conversation models such as BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023b),
098   MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024) and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023c;b) project image features into the language
099   space through a learnable module and perform instruction tuning for visual conversations capabilities.
100   Other efforts extend these models to visual grounding tasks (Peng et al., 2023; Rasheed et al., 2024;
101   You et al., 2023), exploring the potential of LLMs in complex vision tasks.
102   Video Conversation Models: Initial works like Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) and Video-
103   LLaMA (Zhang et al., 2023) extend image-based models to the video domain by introducing
104   components to encode temporal features, where frame-level visual features are fed to the LLM.
105   However, this is computationally expensive and quickly fills its context window. To address this issue,
106   Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) employs spatial and temporal pooling. LLaMA-Vid (Li et al.,
107   2023d) proposes representing a single image with two tokens, context and content. IG-VLM (Kim
      et al., 2024) treats a video as a grid of images, while LITA (Huang et al., 2024b) employs slow-fast


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      token pooling to reduce the number of visual features. Chat-UniVi (Jin et al., 2024) uses clustering
109   in both spatial and temporal dimensions to merge tokens, and VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c) uses
110   Q-Former (Li et al., 2023b) to learn a fixed number of queries by cross-attending to the visual features.
111   MobileVLM (Chu et al., 2023; 2024) utilize a lightweight CNN to reduce the spatial dimensions.
112   Other notable methods include (Liu et al., 2024b; Lin et al., 2023; Munasinghe et al., 2023; Song
113   et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a).
114
      Alternatively, methods such as VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024) use pretrained video encoders. Although
115   video encoders provide temporal context, they are limited by computational constraints, operating
116   with limited frames at lower resolutions, restricting temporal context and spatial understanding.
117   Our VideoGPT+ model addresses these issues by using segment-wise sampling and effectively
118   combining image and video encoders to capture rich spatial and temporal details (see Fig. 2).
119
      Video Instruction Tuning Datasets: VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c) builds a video-instruction tuning
120
      dataset consisting of 7K instructions using videos from WebVid-10M (Bain et al., 2021). Video-
121   ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) introduces a semi-automatic annotation pipeline to generate VideoIn-
122   struct100K using videos from ActivityNet (Fabian Caba Heilbron & Niebles, 2015). VideoChat2 (Li
123   et al., 2024) combines multiple existing image and video datasets to develop a 1.9M joint image-video
124   instruction tuning dataset. In our experiments, we use VideoInstruct100K and a subset of the dataset
125   from VideoChat2. Additionally, addressing the limitations of the VideoInstruct100K dataset (Maaz
126   et al., 2024), we develop VCG+ 112K through a novel semi-automatic annotation pipeline, which
127   provides dense video captions along with 112K QA pairs targeting reasoning, spatial and temporal
128   understanding, which further improves model’s understanding of video content (see Fig. 3).
129   Video Conversation Benchmarks: Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) introduces VCGBench and
130   zero-shot QA benchmarks, where VCGBench includes 500 videos with 3000 QA pairs, evaluated
131   using GPT-3.5 across various metrics. Despite its comprehensive evaluation, it only contains videos
132   from the ActivityNet dataset. The Zero-shot evaluation covers MSVD-QA (Xu et al., 2017), MSR-
133   VTT-QA (Xu et al., 2017), TGIF-QA (Jang et al., 2019), and ActivityNet-QA (Fabian Caba Heilbron
134   & Niebles, 2015). MVBench (Li et al., 2024) consists of 4K QA pairs evaluating 20 temporal
135
      tasks, though it mostly includes short videos averaging 5-40 seconds. Another recent benchmark,
      Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024), addresses the issue of diversity by incorporating a wide range of videos.
136
      However, both MVBench and Video-MME are limited to MCQs, which, while straightforward for
137
      evaluation, restrict the range of questions that can be asked and reduce the depth of understanding the
138   model can demonstrate. By confining to predefined choices, MCQs introduce bias and fail to capture
139   the model’s true understanding. Considering the limitation of existing benchmarks, which often lack
140   focus on generalization and diversity, we propose VCGBench-Diverse, featuring 4,354 QA pairs
141   from 877 videos across 18 domains, evaluated using open-ended questions (see Fig. 4).

      For effective video understanding, combining detailed spatial information with explicit temporal
146
      context is crucial. To achieve this, we propose VideoGPT+, which features a dual encoder design
147   that leverages the complementary strengths of an image encoder and a video encoder.
148
149   Overall Architecture: The overall architecture consists of (i) segment-wise sampling, (ii) dual visual
150
      encoder, (iii) vision-language adapters that project vision features to the language domain and (iv)
      a large language model. Frames selected through a segment-wise sampling strategy are encoded
151
      through a dual encoder consisting of an image and a video encoder. Both sets of features are projected
152
      to language space using vision-language (V-L) adapters, and the resulting tokens are pooled through
153   adaptive token pooling and concatenated before being fed to the LLM (see Fig. 2).
154
155   Segment-wise Sampling: To extract fine-grained temporal cues, we use a segment-wise frame
156
      sampling strategy. Given an input video V ∈ RT ×H×W ×C , we divide it into K segments, where

      Dual Vision Encoder: Our design leverages the complementary strengths of an image encoder that
189   captures detailed spatial features and a video encoder that provides explicit temporal context. The
190   image encoder g, processes T frames, g(X) ∈ RT ×Hg ×Wg ×Dg , producing local features that provide
191   frame-level context. Meanwhile, the video encoder h, operates on low-resolution video segments Vk ,
192   yielding global features that provide segment-wise context, h(Vk ) ∈ Rn×hh ×wh ×Dh .
193
      The primary goal of VideoGPT+ is to leverage the capabilities of a pre-trained LLM alongside
194
      visual modalities from both a pre-trained image encoder and a pre-trained video encoder. Specifically,
195   we utilize the pre-trained CLIP model, ViT-L/14 (336 × 336) (Radford et al., 2021) as the image
196   encoder, and InternVideo-v2 (224 × 224) (Wang et al., 2024) as the video encoder. These models
197   are selected for their robust performance and their ability to complement each other in capturing
198   both spatial and temporal information. Both encoders are pre-trained on large-scale datasets in a
199   multimodal setting using contrastive loss, facilitating their integration within our architecture.
200
      Visual Adapter: The output embeddings from the second last layer of both image and video encoders
201   are passed through separate V-L projection layers, Wg and Wh , respectively. These Multi-Layer
202   perceptrons (MLPs) project the visual features into the language space. The projection layers are
203   trainable, while the visual encoders remain frozen, preserving the rich, pre-trained representations.
204   The projected embeddings are reshaped back into their grid forms and subjected to a 2 × 2 adaptive
205   token pooling, which operates on the spatial dimensions of the local and global features. This pooling
206   reduces the token length by a factor of 4, thereby allowing to fit in larger visual context within the same
207   LLM context window. The pooled embeddings from the local features form Eimg ∈ RT ×hg ×wg ×Dt ,
208   while the pooled embeddings from the global features of each segment form Evid ∈ Rn×hh ×wh ×Dt .
209   Large Language Model: We obtain the final representation by concatenating the embeddings Eimg
210   with K segment-wise embeddings Evid , such that we have detailed spatial representation across
211   all segments followed by their global temporal context. We then concatenate the text embeddings
212   Etext ∈ RL×Dt of the user text query with the visual embeddings,

      4    DATASET
242
243
      Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) introduces the VideoInstruct100K dataset, which employs a
244
      semi-automatic annotation pipeline to generate 75K instruction-tuning QA pairs. To address the
245
      limitations of this annotation process, we present VCG+ 112K dataset developed through an improved
246   annotation pipeline. Our approach improves the accuracy and quality of instruction tuning pairs
247   by improving keyframe extraction, leveraging SoTA large multimodal models (LMMs) for detailed
248   descriptions, and refining the instruction generation strategy.
249
      Keyframe Extraction: VideoInstruct100K uses a fixed number of video keyframes, regardless of
250
      video length or dynamics, to generate frame-level dense captions. This often results in both insufficient
251
      and redundant information. We address this by first extracting scenes from videos (Castellano, 2022),
252   and then selecting one keyframe/scene. Consequently, we obtain detailed information for videos with
253   rich content and reduce redundancy for videos with less content. It provides better visual context by
254   extracting more stable keyframes, thus offering a more accurate video representation.
255
      Frame-Level Descriptions: After extracting keyframes, we use a SoTA image LMM, LLaVA-
256
      v1.6 (Liu et al., 2024a), to generate dense descriptions for each keyframe. These descriptions
257
      encompass comprehensive visual details, including spatial attributes, scene context, and object
258   characteristics, which are often absent in concise ground truth captions. While ground truth captions
259   are precise, they lack the granularity to capture intricate visual and spatial information. To address
260   this, we augment them captions with detailed but noisy information from the frame-level descriptions,
261   thus enhancing the quality and accuracy of the subsequent video descriptions.
262
      Detailed Video Descriptions: VideoInstruct100K (Maaz et al., 2024) prompts GPT-3.5 directly
263
      with frame-level descriptions and concise ground truth captions to generate QA pairs, imposing
264   a significant cognitive load on the model to verify frame-level descriptions with the ground truth.
265   We improve this process by first creating a coherent and detailed video description. We prompt
266   GPT-4 to integrate the detailed frame-level descriptions with the ground truth captions by comparing
267   information and removing any inconsistencies. The resulting detailed descriptions include a timeline
268   of events, actions, object attributes, and scene settings, providing a thorough representation of the
269   video content. This structured input simplifies the task for LLM, thereby enhancing the generated
      QA pairs quality.

288   Improved Instruction Tuning Data: Using the ground truth captions and detailed video descriptions,
289   we generate two types of high-quality QA pairs using GPT-3.5: descriptive and concise. For
290
      descriptive instruction pairs, we focus on three categories: (i) dense captioning, which provides

291

296   relationships between events; and (iii) short temporal questions, addressing specific moments or
297   sequences, such as what happened at the beginning or end.

      5    P ROPOSED B ENCHMARK
301
302   Recognizing the limited diversity in existing video conversation benchmarks, we introduce
303
      VCGBench-Diverse to comprehensively evaluate generalization ability of video LMMs. While
      VCG-Bench (Maaz et al., 2024) provides an extensive evaluation protocol, it is limited to videos from
304
      the ActivityNet200 (Fabian Caba Heilbron & Niebles, 2015) dataset. Our benchmark comprises a
305
      total of 877 videos, 18 broad video categories and 4,354 QA pairs, ensuring a robust evaluation frame-
306   work. The detailed breakdown of VCGBench-Diverse is illustrated in Fig. 4, showcasing the
307   distribution of videos across content domains, video capturing methods, and reasoning complexities.
308
309
      We collect videos from 18 distinct domains, including lifestyle, how-to, science and technology,
      news, travel, entertainment, film, sports, comedy, activism, gaming, education, surveillance, pets,
310
      cooking, music, automobile, and traffic These categories encompass a broad spectrum of real-world
311
      scenarios, ensuring that models are evaluated on a diverse set of challenges. In addition to content
312   diversity, VCGBench-Diverse includes a variety of video capture methods, which ensures a
313   comprehensive assessment of robustness to different filming techniques, camera movements, quality
314   levels and lighting. The benchmark covers five video capture methods including static and controlled
315   settings, dynamic and unpredictable settings, fixed camera perspectives, professional and high-quality
316   videos, and uncontrolled and variable quality. Further, the benchmark evaluates models across six
317   reasoning complexities, including sequential understanding, complex action and predictive reasoning,
318   contextual and world knowledge reasoning, causal reasoning, narrative and emotional reasoning, and
319   analytical and critical reasoning, which is crucial for understanding diverse video content.
320   The videos in VCGBench-Diverse are sourced from HDVILA (Xue et al., 2022), MPII (Andriluka
321   et al., 2014), YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2018), UCF Crime (Sultani et al., 2018), and STUD Traffic (Xu
322   et al., 2021). The video durations range from 29 sec to 471 sec, with an average of 217 sec. Human
323   annotators are tasked with writing detailed descriptions based on their understanding of both audio
      and visual elements of the videos. This comprehensive annotation process involves a set of annotators

      who are provided with an initial set of ten videos each. These annotations undergo a meta-review
325   stage where feedback is provided, and necessary corrections are made to meet the required standards.
326   Following this, annotators receive additional batches, with random samples being selected for quality
327   checks by the meta-reviewer. The final human annotations are utilized to generate QA pairs using
328   GPT-3.5, based on prompts detailed in Fig. 10.
329
      Following VCG-Bench (Maaz et al., 2024), the evaluation is computed over five different aspects:
330
      (i) correctness of information (ii) detail orientation (iii) contextual understanding (iv) temporal
331   understanding and (v) consistency. Additionally, VCGBench-Diverse provides a breakdown
332   of performance across three key aspects: (i) dense video captioning, which assesses the ability to
333   generate detailed and accurate descriptions of the video content, (ii) spatial understanding, which
334   evaluates the capability to understand and describe the spatial relationships and settings within the
335   video, and (iii) reasoning, which tests the adeptness in inferring and explaining causal relationships
336   and actions within the video.

339
340
      We perform quantitative evaluation of VideoGPT+ on five standard benchmarks: i) VCG-
341
      Bench (Maaz et al., 2024), ii) VCGBench-Diverse, iii) MVBench (Li et al., 2024), iv) Video-
342
      MME (Fu et al., 2024) and v) Zero-shot QA.
343
344   Implementation Details: We use CLIP-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) as our image encoder,
345   InternVideo-v2 (Wang et al., 2024) stage-2 1B model as our video encoder in conjunction with
346
      Phi-3-Mini-3.8B (Abdin et al., 2024) based LLM with 4K context window in our experiments. The
      image encoder operates at 336 × 336, while the video encoder operates at 224 × 224 resolution.
347
      Our training consists of two pretraining stages and one instruction-tuning stage. In the pretraining
348
      stage, we train with only the image encoder and only the video encoder on the CC-595K dataset (Liu
349   et al., 2023a), with only the visual adapters being learned while the rest of the model is kept frozen.
350   During the instruction-tuning stage, we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) with r = 64 for LLM, while
351   visual adapters are fully trained and vision encoders are kept frozen. The LR is set to 1e−3 during
352   pretraining and 2e−4 during instruction tuning.
353
      For experiments on VCGBench, VCGBench-Diverse and Zero-shot QA, we sample 16 frames
354
      from videos, while for MVBench which consists of relatively shorter videos, we sample 8 frames.
355   We keep the same sampling strategy during inference. For VCGBench and VCGBench-Diverse,
356   the model is trained on VideoInstruct100K (Maaz et al., 2024), VCG+ 112K , conversation and
357   caption data from VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c) and VQA dataset from WebVid (Bain et al., 2021),
358   that combines to approximately 260K single turn conversations. For MVBench, the model is trained
359   on Kinetics-710 (Kay et al., 2017), Something-Something-v2 (Goyal et al., 2017), conversations
360   from VideoChat (Li et al., 2023c), CLEVRER (Yi et al., 2019), VQA dataset from WebVid (Bain
361   et al., 2021) and NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021) datasets, which combines to approximately 330K
362   single turn conversations. We run all trainings for one epoch. Following previous approaches (Maaz
363
      et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c), we employ GPT-3.5-Turbo-0613 for VCGBench and
      Zero-shot QA evaluation. However, for our proposed VCGBench-Diverse, we employ the latest

      GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125 for evaluation.

531
      In this work, we introduce VideoGPT+, a novel video conversation model that leverages the
532
      complementary benefits of image and video encoders to achieve enhanced video understanding.
533
      VideoGPT+ demonstrates better performance across multiple video benchmarks, owing to its dual-
534   encoder design, lightweight visual adapters that map image/video features to a common space and
535   a segment-wise sampling strategy that retains fine-grained temporal information. We also develop
536   VCG+ 112K, a 112K video-instruction set using a resource-efficient semi-automated annotation
537   pipeline that delivers further gains. Lastly, we propose VCGBench-Diverse, a diverse benchmark
538   covering 18 video categories, to comprehensively evaluate video LMMs. Despite reported improve-
539   ments, video LMMs still find challenges in precise action localization, understanding very long
      videos, and navigating long paths; areas where major improvements can unlock new applications.
