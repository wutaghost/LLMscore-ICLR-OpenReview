                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011
                  Pairwise preferences over model responses are widely collected to evaluate and
012               provide feedback to large language models (LLMs). Given two alternative model
013               responses to the same input, a human or AI annotator selects the “better” re-
014               sponse. This approach can provide feedback for domains where other hard-coded
015               metrics are difficult to obtain (e.g., quality of a chat interactions), thereby helping
016               measure model progress or model fine-tuning (e.g., via reinforcement learning
017               from human feedback, RLHF). However, for some domains it can be tricky to
018               obtain such pairwise comparisons in high quality - from AI and humans. For ex-
019               ample, for responses with many factual statements or complex code, annotators
020
                  may overly focus on simpler features such as writing quality rather the underly-
                  ing facts or technical details. In this work, we explore augmenting standard AI
021
                  annotator systems with additional tools to improve performance on three chal-
022
                  lenging response domains: long-form factual, math and code tasks. We propose
023               a tool-using agentic system to provide higher quality feedback on these domains.
024               Our system uses web-search and code execution to ground itself based on ex-
025               ternal validation, independent of the LLM’s internal knowledge and biases. We
026               provide extensive experimental results evaluating our method across the three tar-
027               geted response domains as well as general annotation tasks, using RewardBench
028               data (incl. AlpacaEval and LLMBar), as well as three new datasets for areas where
029               pre-existing datasets are saturated. Our results indicate that external tools can in-
030               deed improve AI annotator performance in many, but not all, cases. More gener-
031
                  ally, our experiments highlight the high variability of AI annotator performance
                  with respect to simple parameters (e.g., prompt) and the need for improved (non-
032
                  saturated) annotator benchmarks. We share our data and code publicly.1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
078
079   Pairwise feedback is widely used to understand LLM performance on complex tasks that more tra-
080
      ditional benchmarks fail to measure well. Given a prompt and two possible responses, the annotator
      decides which response is ”better”. This pairwise judgement can be used for evaluation (e.g., Chat-
081
      bot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024)) or to provide feedback for training (e.g., via RLHF (Stiennon et al.,
082
      2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) or DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023)). Both human and AI annotators (also
083   referred to as LLM-as-a-Judge) are used to collect such feedback. Human annotators are often con-
084   sidered higher quality but more expensive.
085
086
      Both human and AI annotations have notable limitations: AI annotators have been observed to
      be susceptible to a number of biases, including changing preference based on superficial features
087
      like response order Zheng et al. (2023) or response length Dubois et al. (2024)). Whilst possibly
088
      providing higher quality annotations than AI annotators, human annotators also have known issues.
089   For example, human annotators have been observed to let their assessment of truthfulness be affected
090   by the assertiveness of responses (Hosking et al., 2024).
091
092
      In certain domains it is particularly challenging to obtain high-quality annotations: for responses
      containing long-form factual, advanced coding and math content both AI and (many) human anno-
093
      tators struggle to provide reliable annotations (Zheng et al., 2023). Annotating responses in these
094
      domains requires expertise and careful deliberation, challenging to achieve for human annotators
095   in a limited amount of time. AI annotators may be less ”time-constrained” but nevertheless due
096   to known reliability issues (e.g, hallucinations, limited basic arithmetic) often fail to provide high
097   quality annotations in these domains (Yang et al., 2023).
098
      In this work, we aim to explore improving the annotation quality of widely used AI annotators
099
      on these challenging domains by augmenting the annotators with tools that can externally validate
100
      answers. We enable responses to be fact-checked using web-search, or verified using code execution.
101   Our setup is illustrated in Figure 2. In particular, we make the following contributions:
102
103   1. Extensible framework for using tools with existing AI annotators. We introduce a new
104      framework that enables the integration of new tools on top of existing AI annotators to improve
105      annotation quality in certain domains using external validation. Our framework is agentic in the
106      sense that an LLM assesses the response domain and plans the optimal tool usage accordingly.
107      We provide a number of initial tool implementations: (1) a long-form fact checking tool based
         on the Search Augmented Fact Evaluation (SAFE) method by Wei et al. (2024); (2) a code check

109       execution. We open-source the corresponding code2

113

122   2       P ROBLEM : PAIRWISE F EEDBACK ON C OMPLEX TASKS
123
124   For many task domains, pairwise feedback can be easier to obtain than absolute metrics. Never-
125   theless, for some domains even a relative pairwise judgement can be difficult to collect — from
126   both human and AI annotators. In this work, we consider three particularly challenging response
127   domains: tasks that require model responses with (1) long-form factual, (2) advanced coding or (3)
128   math content. For such tasks, even a relative judgement requires robust understanding of the task
129   domain, and, for human annotators, careful deliberation. For example, judging code without under-
130
      standing the relevant syntax may force an annotator (AI or human) to revert to higher level features
      such as style – that may not fully correlate with ground-truth preferences. Similarly, when com-
131
      paring responses with a large number of factual statements, an annotator may easily miss a single
132
      incorrect factual statement — instead possibly again relying on writing style to make a judgement.
133   At the same time, annotators only judging according to factual or functional correctness may miss
134   other response traits (e.g., readability) distinguishing a merely correct from an excellent response.
135
136
      In the pairwise setting, annotators are typically evaluated based on their agreement3 with ground-
      truth annotations on datasets, where such annotations are either available by construction or created
137
      by human annotators (Lambert et al., 2024). This agreement is equivalent to the accuracy of the
138
      binary classification task of predicting the correct ranking for each response pair. In this setting, the
139   goal of pairwise feedback annotation is to maximise the agreement with ground-truth annotations.
140
141   In general, for many response pairs there is ambiguity regarding which response is better — espe-
142
      cially for domains with known disagreements such as political preferences (Kirk et al., 2024). To
      improve the reliability of our evaluation, we attempted to primarily test on response pairs where
143
      experts agree on the preference and avoided more contentious topics.

146   3       M ETHOD : AI A NNOTATORS WITH T OOLS FOR E XTERNAL VALIDATION
147
148   We introduce a new framework for augmenting existing AI annotators with tools – grounding their
149   annotations in the real world with external validation. The general functioning of our framework
150   is illustrated in Figure 2. Our goal is to improve the performance of AI annotators on a specific
151   set of target domains: responses containing long-form factual, advanced coding and math content.
152   To achieve this annotation quality improvement, we leverage external validation via tools built on
153   web search and code execution. At the same time, we want to avoid reducing performance on other
154   non-target domains. We use an agentic setup to determine when each tool gets used, letting an
155
      underlying LLM assess the domain of the response considered and thereby which tool would be
      most useful. To avoid regression on non-target domains, our agentic framework reverts back to a
156
      baseline annotator whenever the responses are assessed to be outside the domain of all available

193   As is done in many annotator approaches (e.g., certain AlpacaEval configurations (Dubois et al.,
194   2023)), we build on structured output throughout our pipeline to create a reliable method with low
195   parsing error rate. Instead of plain text responses, structured output forces the model to return
196   JSON-formatted outputs. With this approach, each LLM call is not only configured by a single
197   prompt message but also by the JSON format and description of the requested output.
198   Our approach consists of three distinct parts: (1) initial domain assessment, determining which tools
199   to use (if any); (2) tools, running the selected tools for each response; and (3) final decision, creat-
200   ing a final preference judgement based on all outputs. If the first step (initial domain assessment)
201   determines that no tools would be helpful, our approach alternatively skips steps (2) and (3). In-
202   stead, we revert to the (4) baseline annotator. In the following subsections, we describe each step
203   in more detail. For full reproducibility, we further share the prompts in Appendix E and make the
204   corresponding code publicly available.4
205
206   3.1      S TEP 1: I NITIAL DOMAIN ASSESSMENT
207
208
      The initial domain assessment ensures that each tool is only run if the model responses are within a
      domain where the tool is known to be likely helpful. For example, for the code execution tool, the
209
      domain assessment ensures that there is code present in the response. This assessment helps avoid
210
      running tools in scenarios where they are unlikely to help. For each tool, we created a number of
211   questions about a response (e.g. ”Whether text might benefit from running code.”). For each re-
212   sponse, an LLM is prompted with these questions. The LLM answers are then parsed and determine
213   whether a tool is deemed useful and run – or not. If not a single tool is deemed useful, the agent
214   reverts back to a baseline evaluator. With this setup, our method aims to reduce unnecessary infer-
215

      ence costs and to avoid regressing on domains where the tools are not useful. Further, by allowing
217   clearly separated tool domains, the setup allows integrating a large number of domain-specific tools
218   with limited adverse performance effects on other domains.
219
220   3.2   S TEP 2: T OOL USAGE
221
222   If the initial assessment deems one or more tools useful, the respective tools are run. We initially
223   implemented three different tools as part of our extensible framework, chosen to specifically tackle
224   the limitations of LLM-as-a-Judge systems discussed in Section 2:
225   Fact-checking. We build on the Search Augmented Factuality Evaluator (SAFE) by Wei et al.
226   (2024) to create a fact-checking tool for the pairwise setting. Our fact-checking tool follows similar
227   steps as the original SAFE algorithm: (1) separating atomic facts, (2) making atomic facts self-
228   contained, and (3) checking whether self-contained facts are supported by web-search. Our tool
229   omits the relevance check in the original SAFE algorithm. In a pairwise preference setting we
230   consider the truthfulness of all facts relevant – even if they do not directly relate to the task or
231   prompt. It is ultimately up to the final assessment to decide which factual statements, and their
232   truthfulness, is most important.
233   Code execution. Taken into account existing works that show that compiler/runtime output is a
234   useful signal, we build on top of OpenAI’s code interpreter API to create a code-execution tool.
235   For both proposed answers to a prompt, the code-execution tool will verify its correctness using
236   execution feedback. Internally, OpenAI’s code interpreter API can create additional unit tests, run
237   multiple execution steps and draw a conclusion. Only the last conclusion is used in the agent’s final
238   assessment to determine which response is better.
239   Math checker. Noting that autoregressive language models are not reliable arithmetic engines (Yang
240   et al., 2023), we prompt-constrain our code-execution tool to perform math (and in particular arith-
241   metic) validation on each of the model outputs. As in the case of general code execution, multiple
242   checks may be executed per model output, and the final assessment uses the outcome of these checks
243   to inform its overall decision. We created a separate math checker after preliminary tests indicated
244   a standard code interpreter tool does not transfer well to math annotation settings.
245
246   3.3   S TEP 3: F INAL ASSESSMENT
247
      In the final assessment step, we combine the results of all tools per response alongside the original
248
      prompt and response, to prompt an LLM to make an informed preference judgement based on all
249   collected information. Critically, this step allows the LLM to access the external validation results
250   when making a decision. The LLM response to this step provides the final preference judgement
251   (e.g., ”Text A is preferred.”) as well as a chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning for the judgement (e.g.
252   ”Text A is preferred because [...]”).

      4.1   DATASETS
257
258   Existing datasets. A number of benchmarks aim to evaluate AI annotator capabilities, notable
259   examples include (subsets of) AlpacaEval5 (Dubois et al., 2023), MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023),
260   LLMBar (Zeng et al., 2024) and RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). We use the latter, Reward-
261   Bench, for our evaluation, as it represents a superset including the other tasks. This benchmark
262   provides a broad coverage of response domains, including mathematical reasoning, code generation
263   and general chatbot conversation. We find that some subsets of the benchmark are highly saturated:
264   state-of-the-art LLM-as-a-judge systems already achieve close to 100% agreement with the ground-
265
      truth annotations. For example, we find that a simple GPT-4o-based baseline AI annotator achieves
      above 97% across all HumanEval-based coding subsets (Chen et al., 2021) in RewardBench (each
266
      subset has at most 5 datapoints6 to improve on). Similarly, the same baseline achieves over 90% on

279   1. Long-form fact checking: LongFact pairwise. We create a dataset of response pairs, where
280      responses vary in long-form factual correctness, using the LongFact prompt dataset by Wei et al.
281      (2024). We use OpenAI’s gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18 model to generate two long-form factual
282      responses for each prompt. We then manually introduce factual errors into one of the responses.
283      We further collect human preference annotations from 3 annotators over the entire new dataset,
284      and these annotators, on average, agree with 76.83% of those ground-truth annotations when not
285
         selecting a tie. 18% of the average human annotations are ties. Full details on the data generation

288      et al., 2021), we create a pairwise response dataset to evaluate the ability to determine code
289

         truth solutions for most problems. We take the “competition” subset, arguing it is these harder
290
         problem/solution combinations that are tricky to evaluate correctly. We only keep samples that
291
         contain a ground-truth solution, leaving us with 310 items. We then use GPT-4-0613 to generate
292      solutions to the problems, until we have failing solutions for all 310 items.
293
      3. Challenging maths: GSM8k hard pairwise. We select a “hard” subset of the GSM8k (Cobbe
294
         et al., 2021b) dataset by keeping the 116 examples that GPT-4o is unable to solve. For each of
295
         these examples we generate pairwise responses by keeping both the ground-truth answer and the
296      incorrect answer that GPT-4o provided.
297
298   We additionally create a pairwise response dataset where responses vary in short-form factual cor-
299   rectness using the TruthfulQA dataset7 by Lin et al. (2022). Unlike the previous three datasets,
300   baseline annotators are able to achieve high (saturated) performance on this dataset and we thus
301   primarily use this dataset for our regression tests. Further, unlike the long-form responses in our
302   LongFact pairwise dataset, responses in this dataset are typically between a single word and single
303   sentence long, relating to a single fact. See Appendix C for full data generation details.

      We compare our method to two popular AI annotator configurations that are widely used in academic

      5   R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Pairwise AI annotators. As human annotations are costly and time-intensive, extensive work has
489   been done to explore the use of AI annotators as an alternative. Works such as LLM-as-a-judge
490   (Zheng et al., 2023), AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2023) and G-Eval (Liu et al., 2023) popularized
491   AI annotators in the context of evaluation. The ArenaHard annotator is another popular choice (Li
492   et al., 2024b). Various efforts have also explored the use of AI annotators for generating training
493   data, such as constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022). This line of work is also known as reinforcement
494   learning from AI feedback (RLAIF) (Lee et al., 2024).
495   AI annotator problems. A number of biases have been observed in AI annotators, for example
496   (1) length bias (Zheng et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024), where annotators prefer more verbose
497   outputs (even when not corresponding to human preference); (2) position bias (Zheng et al., 2023),
498   where the model’s annotation affected by order in which they are shared with the model; and (3)
499   self-enhancement bias (Panickssery et al., 2024; Stureborg et al., 2024), where annotators prefer
500   responses that are high probability under judging model’s distribution.
501   Augmented AI evaluators. Given the known limitations of basic AI annotators, various augmenta-
502   tions of such annotators have been explored. Li et al. (2024a) explore the use of external validation
503   tools to improve the performance of a reward model (RM), in a framework named Themis. Sim-
504   ilar to our work, the tools considered include code interpreter and web search tools. However,
505   Themis requires a language model with customized architecture and fine-tuning—preventing the
506   use of Themis with the latest state-of-the-art closed-source models. Dubois et al. (2024) propose
507   augmenting AI annotators to be length-controlled using a generalized linear model to address the
508   widely observed length bias. Others explore using multiple AI annotators simultaneously to improve
509
      performance (Verga et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2023).
510   Outside of the pairwise setting, the Search Augmented Factuality Evaluator (SAFE) by Wei et al.
511   (2024), and prior work FActScore (Min et al., 2023), RARR (Gao et al., 2023), Factcheck-Bench
512   (Wang et al., 2024), all aimed at improving the capability of verifying fact within text – including
513   model responses. Gou et al. (2023) explore the use of external validation tools to improve genera-
514   tive performance, demonstrating improvements for question answering, programming and toxicity
515
      reduction tasks.
516
517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work we have presented a novel framework for augmenting AI annotators with tools to exter-
520   nally validate outputs and address existing limitations with AI and human annotations. We compare
521   our method to state-of-the-art and widely used AI annotators, including the AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois
522   et al., 2023) and ArenaHard annotator (Li et al., 2024b). To challenge our method on annotation
523   tasks where the existing datasets appear saturated (coding, math) or little pairwise data exists (long-
524
      form factual responses), we created new pairwise datasets, building on LongFact (Wei et al., 2024),
      GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021a), and APPS (Hendrycks et al., 2021). We evaluate our method’s ef-
525
      fectiveness across both these new datasets as well as the aggregate RewardBench dataset (Lambert
526
      et al., 2024). We observe that our external validation-based method often improves baseline annota-
527   tor performance, with strongest effectiveness when annotating advanced coding responses but also
528   in the context of long-form factual responses, with more mixed results in advanced math responses.
529
530
      We conclude that, whilst external validation tools can improve annotation quality of AI annotator
      (or LLM-as-a-Judge) for certain scenarios, such tools represent a trade-off in terms of complexity
531
      and cost, and may not always be the right fit for every use-case. More broadly, our results highlight
532
      the strong effect that simple configuration parameters, such as prompt and parsing method, can
533   have on annotator performance — even if the same underlying LLM is used. When considering
534   more technically involved augmentations like our external validation tools, we recommend to also
535   carefully evaluate simpler configurations as an alternative across a wide range of scenarios, as we
536   have done. A robust AI annotator testing pipeline can be critical to determine the right annotator.
537   RewardBench represents an important first step into this direction, as do our own new pairwise
538   datasets, we hope. We would welcome future work that develops further datasets to improve the
539   reliability and comprehensiveness of AI annotator evaluation. We publicly release the code for our
      framework and experiments.

823   centage of datapoints where the annotators agree (Agreed) and disagree (Disagree) with the original
824   annotations, and the percentage of datapoints where the annotators do not provide responses that can
825   be correctly parsed (Not avail.). Both results for the standard API with tools (e.g. “ArenaHard base-
826   line (GPT-4o + code-interpreter + search)”) and without tools (e.g. “ArenaHard baseline (GPT-4o)”)
827   are shown.

829   performance across any of the tested datasets and LLM-as-a-Judge configurations. Unlike with
830   our framework, we do not see notable improvements of the tool-enabled over the non-tool baselines.
831   Across all datasets, the tool-enabled baselines are either roughly equivalent or worse than the non-
832   tool baselines. This observation aligns with our own prior experience during the development of our
833   framework: we observed that GPT-4o requires notable scaffolding guidance to effectively make use
834   of tools in our annotation settings.
835   Observation B: Adding tools reduces the output reliability of GPT-4o-based ArenaHard base-

841   ablation study. Overall, this observation highlights the sensitivity of LLM-as-a-Judge annotators to
842
      changes in model and configuration parameters.

844   vides, GPT-4o struggles to make effective use of tools in the annotations tasks considered as part of

      C    A DDITIONAL DATA GENERATION DETAILS
919
920
      Long-form fact checking: LongFact pairwise. We create a dataset of response pairs, where re-
921   sponses vary in long-form factual correctness, using the LongFact prompt dataset by Wei et al.
922   (2024). In particular, we use OpenAI’s gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18 model to generate two responses
923   at temperature 0.1 for 100 randomly sampled prompts from LongFact-object prompt subset used in
924   the experiments by Wei et al. (2024). We use the same postamble as the original work, asking the
925   model to respond to the prompt in 8 or 5 sentences, generating 20 and 80 samples for each setting
926   respectively. Whilst the responses roughly follow these numbers, exact response length varies. For
927   each resulting response pair, we manually introduce between 1-3 factual errors (e.g., wrong num-
928   bers, names, or dates) into one of the two responses. We only change factual information, trying to
929
      avoid applying any stylistic changes that could affect model preferences. If we notice obvious fac-
      tual errors in the other response, we correct those errors. Using this procedure, we create a dataset
930
      of pairwise long-form factual responses, where we know one response to be (likely) less factually
931
      correct than the other. Further, as they are generated by the same model, but with a non-zero tem-
932   perature, the responses are similar in style and quality but, in most cases, not exactly identical. This
933   setting makes the task more challenging as the (incorrect) adapted facts are often not necessarily ob-
934   vious to detect. We further collect human preference annotations from 3 annotators over the entire
935   new dataset, and these annotators, on average, agree with 76.83% of those ground-truth annotations
936   when not selecting a tie. 18% of the average human annotations are ties.
937
      Short-form fact checking: TruthfulQA pairwise. We additionally create a pairwise response
938   dataset where responses vary in short-form factual correctness using the TruthfulQA dataset11 by
939   Lin et al. (2022). Unlike the previous three datasets, baseline annotators are able to achieve high
940   (saturated) performance on this dataset and we thus primarily use this dataset for our regression
941   tests. For each prompt included in a random subsample of 400 datapoints from TruthfulQA, we
942   pair up the value in the ”Best Answer” column and a randomly selected answer from the ”Incorrect
943   Answers” column. We randomly shuffle the order of the pairs, with our ground-truth preference

945   mark specifically focuses on question prompts that may be answered incorrectly by humans due to

      misconceptions or misunderstandings. Unlike the long-form responses in our LongFact pairwise
