                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Federated Instruction Tuning (FIT) has shown the ability to enable model instruc-
012            tion tuning among massive data owners without exposing privacy. However, it still
013            faces two key challenges, i.e., data and resource heterogeneity. Due to the vary-
014            ing data distribution and preferences among data owners, FIT cannot adapt to the
015            personalized data of individual owners. Moreover, clients with superior computa-
016            tional abilities have to compromise to maintain the same fine-tuning architecture
017            as the weaker clients. Such a constraint prevents the powerful clients from having
018            more trainable parameters for better fine-tuning performances. To address these
019            issues uniformly, we propose a novel Personalized Federated Instruction Tuning
020
               (PerFIT) framework based on architecture search. Specifically, PerFIT allows
               each client to search for a personalized architecture by expanding the trainable
021
               parameter space of the global model, pruning them, and obtaining personalized
022
               sparse patterns. We further propose personalized parameter-wise aggregation to
023            facilitate flexible aggregation among clients with diverse sparse patterns. This
024            procedure allows personalized instruction fine-tuning within the expanded param-
025            eter spaces, concurrently preserving the same number of trainable parameters as
026            the vanilla state, thus introducing no extra resource burden. The evaluations with
027            multiple LLMs on various instruction-following datasets demonstrate that our ap-
028            proach can achieve up to a 23% decrease in personalized perplexity compared to
029            the state-of-the-art FIT methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      The emergent abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) (23) have presented the powerful capa-
034
      bility of solving various language-related tasks, including reasoning, text generation, and question-
035
      answering. To obtain better-aligned LLMs that can precisely follow the instructions of humans,
036   Instruction Tuning (IT) (26; 25) has been proposed and demonstrated essential effectiveness in en-
037   hancing the generalizability of the foundation LLMs to downstream tasks. Compared to the conven-
038   tional Fine Tuning (FT) methods, IT incorporates the vanilla text with specific instructions paired
039   with corresponding answers, thereby unlocking the existing abilities of LLMs.
040
      Although IT is superior to traditional FT, the success of IT greatly relies on the variety, quality, and
041
      quantity of the training data. In addition, the increasing concerns about data privacy (7) and the
042   expensive expenses of data collecting and cleaning jointly impede the obtaining of large amounts of
043   valuable data. Worse still, the heterogeneity of private data fails to reflect the meaningful statistical
044   property of the domain, resulting in the implantation of inevitable bias during IT. To overcome the
045   aforementioned issues, Federated Instruction Tuning (FIT) (32; 29) was proposed as the explorations
046   of the instruction-based optimization framework in Federated Learning (FL). The two frameworks
047   seamlessly integrated Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (9; 14), enhancing the fea-
048   sibility of lightweight local fine-tuning processes. Moreover, they showed that FIT can leverage
049   instruction-following data with guarantees of privacy and improve the performance of LLMs.
050   Despite the fact the privacy-guaranteed FIT framework based on PEFT methods can alleviate data
051   heterogeneity and allow collaborative training, the preference for local data is not taken into consid-
052   eration. Existing FIT method ignores resource heterogeneity since every client has to share the same
053   structure of fine-tuning modules, potentially causing the waste of resources on clients with more
      powerful capabilities given that more trainable parameters offer better fine-tuning performance (1).

      To address the challenges of handling local data and resource heterogeneity (11), we propose an
055   adaptive personalized federated instruction tuning method to enable local clients to fully use their
056   data and resources. Our method is motivated by the intrinsic connection between data heterogeneity
057   and architecture heterogeneity, thereby authorizing each client to search for a personal IT architec-
058   ture. Specifically, we adopt the efficient foresight pruning method based on the Taylor expansion
059   of the loss to simplify the expensive Neural Architecture Search (NAS) (16) process. Benefiting
060   from the data-guided pruning, each client has a personal sparse structure of the IT modules that fit
061   the personalized local data. Furthermore, we propose a personalized aggregation mechanism that
062   achieves parameter-wise aggregation across clients to enhance the information interactions. Our
063
      contributions are summarized as follows:
064          • We develop a novel personalized federated instruction tuning method by exploring diverse
065            local fine-tuning architectures based on heterogeneous local data. Our approach can simul-
066            taneously enable collaborative learning among clients with heterogeneous resources.
067          • We propose a personalized parameter-wise aggregation strategy for the fine-tuned modules
068            to promote information interaction across local clients with various architectures.
069
             • We conduct comprehensive experiments on three well-known LLMs and four instruction-
               following datasets in both resource heterogeneity and homogeneity scenarios, which ade-
070
               quately show the effectiveness of our method.

073   2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Federated Instruction Tuning of Large Language Models. Existing LLMs have demonstrated
076   substantial performance in deriving task-relevant answers by simply decorating the vanilla input
077   with instructions. However, the fine-tuning process is still a promising option to achieve better re-
078   sults when confronting unexplored tasks (18). To preserve the advantages of instruction data and
079   fine-tuning, instruction tuning was proposed as an essential approach to optimize the performance
080   of LLMs. This method improves the efficacy of LLMs in handling diverse and complex tasks by
081   fine-tuning them with human instructions and aligning them with real-world tasks (28). Previous
082
      work in this area focuses on two ways to generate instructions: i) prompts manually created by
      humans (27) and ii) instruction-following data auto-generated by machines (25). Despite the fact
083
      that the first method is expensive, the quality of instruction data manufactured with human effects
084
      is elevated due to the precise human annotation. The latter utilizes a self-instruct method based on
085   open-sourced LLMs to auto-generate instruction data. Specifically, a powerful LLM is deployed to
086   generate massive task-specific instruction data, which is subsequently leveraged to boost the align-
087   ment ability of another trainable LLM. However, due to the high value of collecting instruction
088   data for various tasks, the owners of specific data are unlikely to share it with other competitors
089   (29). Thus, the data cross-silo scenarios still exist. In addition, the heavy burdens brought by full-
090   parameter fine-tuning weaken the feasibility of conducting fine-tuning on local clients. To tackle
091   these problems, the FIT frameworks proposed by (32; 29) provide a lightweight solution based on
092   the Low-Rank Adapter (LoRA) (9) to overcome the challenge brought by heterogeneous data, but
093
      the personalization aspects of local clients including data and resource heterogeneity (e.g., number
      of trainable parameters that clients can afford) are not taken into consideration. Therefore, we delve
094
      into the LoRA-based fine-tuning method and propose a personalized FIT method to address both
095
      challenges simultaneously.
096
097   Personalized Federated Learning. Personalized Federated Learning (PFL) focuses on training a
098   client-specific model to achieve better performance on each local dataset instead of a global model
099
      to accommodate all client data uniformly. Specifically, the personalization of clients includes two
      major aspects: i) data heterogeneity (17) and ii) resource heterogeneity (12). The former indicates
100
      the differences in local data distributions and the latter shows the diversity in terms of memory con-
101
      sumption, computation abilities, communication overhead, etc. To address the data heterogeneity
102   challenges, existing methods including (20) introduced regularization terms to guide the local objec-
103   tives. To tackle the challenge of resource heterogeneity, (19) proposed to distinguish personalized
104   models from a global model through a hypernetwork. (30) derives Federated Neural Network Search
105   (FL-NAS) to obtain personalized architectures based on data and resource heterogeneity. FedSelect
106   (21) iteratively grows subnetworks of local personalized with decreasing sparsity values. While the
107   previously mentioned methods are effective from certain viewpoints, most focus on a singular as-
      pect of personalization. Worse still, none of them are tailored for PFL on LLMs. To address the two

109   a fine-grained LoRA architecture search based on local data, aiming to meet the resource and data

      Computation and Time Complexity Analysis. Similar to the work in (6) and (3), we applied 8-
286
      bit quantization on the frozen model and gradient-checkpoint methods to relieve the GPU memory
287
      burden when using the Adam optimizer to conduct local fine-tuning. Note that the computational
288   resources required by NAS are less than those for fine-tuning. This is because the computational
289   cost of NAS is as low as fine-tuning with the SGD optimizer, which is less complex than the Adam
290   optimizer. Moreover, for each client in PerFIT, the pruning is performed only once at the first round
291   of local training. As a result, the computational overhead of pruning in PerFIT is negligible in
292   practice. For each client, we maintain a bitmap data structure to represent the mapping between
293   client model parameters and their counterparts in the expanded space. Our aggregation operation
294   described in Algorithm 3 has a time complexity of O(M K). Here, M is the number of LoRA
295   modules, and K is the number of selected clients in each FL communication round.

360   Dataset. We conducted our experiments on four datasets: Databricks-dolly-15k (5), MedAlpaca
361   (8), CodeAlpaca (2), and MathInstruct (31). Databricks-dolly-15k is a general instruction-following
362   dataset, including creative writing, brainstorming, classification, closed QA, generation, information
363
      extraction, open QA, and summarization. MedAlpaca, Code-Alpaca, and MathInstruct are domain-
      specific instruction-following dataset. We performed two types of splitting methods to emulate the
364
      heterogeneous data distributed to local clients. The first is the pathological non-IID setup where
365
      each client is randomly assigned the same number of data points. For Databricks-dolly-15k, we ran-
366   domly assigned 2 classes among 8 total classes to each client. For other domain-specific datasets,
367   we randomly assigned 200 data to each client. The second non-IID setup follows the Dichilet dis-
368   tribution, which is parameterized by a coefficient β, denoted as Dir(β). β determines the degree of
369   data heterogeneity. The smaller the β is, the more heterogeneous the data distributions will be. We
370   set the β as 0.5 throughout the experiments. Since the Databricks-dolly-15k is the only dataset that
371   has labels, we only apply the Dichilet method on it.
372   Models and Baselines. To showcase the effectiveness of our method on various LLMs, we uti-
373   lized three open-source large language models: Alpaca-7B (22), Vicuna-7B-v1.5 (4) and LLaMA-
374   2-7B (23). The first two LLMs have been fine-tuned based on the LLaMA-1-7B (24) to enhance
375   their abilities to understand and respond to human inputs effectively while the LLaMA-2-7b model
376   has not been. We used the official tokenizers that correspond to the model weights. We devel-
377   oped our method based on two federated instruction tuning frameworks: Federatedgpt (32) and
      OpenFedLLM(29). Note that the mentioned two methods focus on obtaining a global fine-tuned


                                                        7
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
       model. They also show that federated instruction tuning is better than only using local data to fine-
379    tune. Since our method is the first solution to focus on the personalized federated instruction tuning
380    problem, we only compare it against the global model.
381

       Configurations. For all experiments, we set the number of total clients as 100. The backbones of
       the LLMs are frozen during pruning and local fine-tuning to save the memory cost. We add LoRA
383
       to three attention modules for every layer, i.e., Query, Key, and Value matrices. For homogeneous
384
       resource baselines, we set the basic rank r of LoRA as 8. The coefficient alpha remains the same

519    experiments using the model Alpaca-7B and the dataset Databricks-dolly-15k with the pathological
520    non-IID distribution. We initialized the Alpaca-7B model using either the uniform or the normal
521    distribution. We conducted FL based on standard FIT (with a rank of 8) and our PerFIT (with a
522    rank of 12), respectively. For FIT, the final perplexity of the case with uniform initialization is
523    0.35 smaller than that of the case with normal initialization, where the two cases have the same
524
       convergence rates. For PerFIT, the case with uniform initialization achieves a perplexity of 2.96,
       while the case with normal initialization achieves a perplexity of 3.93. We can observe that our
525
       PerFIT method consistently outperforms the FIT method under different initialization methods. Note
526
       that the two PerFIT cases have better convergence rates than their FIT counterparts. Therefore, our
527    proposed method consistently outperforms FIT under different initialization strategies.

530    6                                C ONCLUSION
531
532    While federated instruction tuning has demonstrated the ability to improve global model perfor-
533
       mance without revealing private instruction-following data, this approach fails to address the issues
       of personalized data and varying client resources. In this paper, we introduced a novel personalized
534
       federated instruction tuning named PerFIT. By enabling local clients to search for personalized fine-
535
       tuning architectures in an expanded LoRA space, we effectively mitigate the difficulties posed by
536    heterogeneous data and resource distributions We analyzed the convergence property of our method,
537    showing that our method tailored for LLMs exhibits a similar convergence rate to the sparse fed-
538    erated training method. Comprehensive experimental results on representative LLMs under two
539    non-IID scenarios demonstrated the effectiveness of our proposed method.
