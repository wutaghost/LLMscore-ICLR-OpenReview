                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012            With the growing prominence of the Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture in
013
               developing large-scale foundation models, we investigate the Hierarchical Mixture
               of Experts (HMoE), a specialized variant of MoE that excels in handling complex
014
               inputs and improving performance on targeted tasks. Our investigation highlights
015
               the advantages of using varied gating functions, moving beyond softmax gating
016            within HMoE frameworks. We theoretically demonstrate that applying tailored
017            gating functions to each expert group allows HMoE to achieve robust results,
018            even when optimal gating functions are applied only at select hierarchical levels.
019            Empirical validation across diverse scenarios supports these theoretical claims.
020            This includes large-scale multimodal tasks, image classification, and latent domain
021            discovery and prediction tasks, where our modified HMoE models show great
022            performance improvements.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent years, the integration of mixture-of-experts (MoE) within large-scale foundation models has
028
      markedly advanced the machine learning field (Jiang et al., 2024; Fedus et al., 2022; Riquelme et al.,
      2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Mustafa et al., 2022). MoE architectures, known for their ability to efficiently
029
      handle diverse and complex datasets, have facilitated significant improvements in model performance
030
      without a proportional increase in computational demand. They address bottlenecks associated with
031   traditional deep learning architectures by dynamically allocating resources to parts of the model for
032   which they are most relevant (Yuksel et al., 2012; Shazeer et al., 2017). The Hierarchical Mixture of
033   Experts (HMoE) model (Fritsch et al., 1996) is a special type of MoE architecture that is characterized
034   by a layered structure of decision modules and expert networks that operate in tandem to refine
035   decision-making at each level, optimizing the allocation of computational resources and enhancing
036   specialization for complex tasks. Unlike the standard MoE, which typically involves a single gating
037   network directing inputs to various expert networks, HMoE introduces multiple layers of gating
038   mechanisms and experts. This hierarchical design divides the problem space recursively, allowing
039
      different experts to specialize in subspaces of the input space, leading to enhanced flexibility and
      model generalization (Jiang & Tanner, 1999; Azran & Meir, 2004).
040
041   Figure 1 compares HMoE and standard MoE in processing multimodal input data. The hierarchical
042   structure of HMoE makes it particularly effective at handling complex inputs, such as data that can
043   be divided into semantically meaningful subgroups. This recursive partitioning enables HMoE to
044   select features and specialize in various segments of the input space more effectively, especially in
045
      high-dimensional data scenarios (Peralta & Soto, 2014). The unique capability of HMoE to handle
      complex datasets makes it particularly valuable across a range of applications. Historically, HMoE
046
      has been applied on image classification (Irsoy & AlpaydДұn, 2021), speech recognition (Peng et al.,
047
      1996; Zhao et al., 1994), and complex decision-making tasks (Jeremiah et al., 2013; Moges et al.,
048   2016). However, there is a notable lack of recent studies on HMoE in the literature, partly due to its
049   more complex structure as compared to standard MoE. For instance, while standard MoE requires
050   the selection of a single gating function, HMoE necessitates the choice of multiple gating functions,
051   introducing additional hyperparameters and therefore greater complication in model specification.
052   Given the increasing complexity of input data in the modern era, such as multiple modalities or
053   subgroups defined by ambiguous latent domains, there is a growing demand for models that can
      deliver accurate and individualized predictions for each subgroup. Therefore, it is worthwhile to study

073   In this paper, we investigate distinct selections of gating functions within HMoE and their impact
074   on overall performance. This is a critical issue and will lay the groundwork for future research in
075   this relatively unstudied domain. It is important to note that expert specialization, as discussed in

077   specialized in specific tasks or aspects of the data. To address this, we conduct a comprehensive
078   analysis of the convergence behavior of experts within two-level HMoE models, using three different
079   combinations of the conventional softmax gating (Jordan & Jacobs, 1994) and the Laplace gating
080
      as suggested in Han et al. (2024). Our theoretical analysis reveals that employing Laplace gating
      at both levels of the HMoE framework accelerates expert convergence and significantly improves
081
      performance relative to baseline. We further validate this through extensive empirical evaluations
082
      across diverse scenarios, demonstrating HMoEвҖҷs effectiveness on complex datasets, such as those
083   with inherent hierarchies or clustered data that can be partitioned into subgroups. By incorporating the
084   three aforementioned combinations of gating functions, our experiments confirm that using Laplace
085   gating at both levels consistently improves performance across multiple downstream tasks compared
086   to the standard softmax gating baseline. Additionally, we observe that different combinations of
087   Laplace and softmax gating can also noticeably enhance results, leading to better and more robust
088   performance by offering a broader selection of gating function combinations. These findings highlight
089   the practical benefits of selecting appropriate gating functions to enhance HMoEвҖҷs capabilities.

      HMoE Implementation. We implement the two-level HMoE module, inspired by Lepikhin et al.
390   (2020). Algorithm 1 in Appendix outlines the procedure, which employs a recursive computation
391   strategy to process inputs in a coarse-to-fine manner. The inputs are first partitioned by the outer
392   dispatcher, followed by the inner dispatcher, into subgroups, which are then sent to specialized groups
393   and experts for independent processing. The outputs from the experts are recursively combined using
394   inner and outer combination tensors to produce the final output. Gating losses from both levels are
395   integrated and scaled to regularize training, promoting balanced expert utilization.
396
397   3.1   P RIMARY R ESULTS
398   HMoE Improves Multimodal Fusion. We first evaluate the effectiveness of HMoE on the MIMIC-
399   IV dataset, a comprehensive database containing records from nearly 300k patients admitted to a
400   medical center between 2008 and 2019, focusing on a subset of 73,181 ICU stays. We integrated
401   diverse patient modalities, including vital signs (time series), clinical notes, and CXR (chest X-ray
402   images). Our tasks of interest in the MIMIC dataset include 48-hour in-hospital mortality prediction
403   (48-IHM), 25-type phenotype classification (25-PHE), and length-of-stay (LOS) prediction. The
404   baselines include: (1) the Multimodal Transformer (MulT), which models modality interactions
405   (Tsai et al., 2019); (2) the Multimodal Adaptation Gate (MAG), which addresses consistency and
406
      differences across modalities (Rahman et al., 2020); (3) the early fusion method Tensor Fusion

407
      designed for integrating multimodal data from MIMIC-IV; (5) MISTS, a cross-attention approach
408
      combined with irregular sequence modeling (Zhang et al., 2023); and (6) multimodal fusion using
409   MoE (Han et al., 2024). The data is first processed by modality-specific encoders, with the obtained
410   modality embeddings then fed into 12 stacked HMoE modules with residual connections to produce
411   the outcome. Details of the building blocks are provided in the appendix. Table 2 presents the
412   outcomes of integrating time series, clinical notes, and CXR data into various prediction tasks. The
413   HMoE (Laplace-Laplace) outperforms the baselines in most scenarios, often by a significant margin.
414   While the MoE-based fusion method (Han et al., 2024) has proven effective in multimodal fusion, the
415   inherent hierarchical structure of the HMoE module further enhances its ability to process multimodal
416   inputs, allowing for more specialized expert assignment and improved performance.
417   HMoE Enhances Clinical Latent Domain Discovery. Many datasets in high-stakes applications
418   can be categorized into different latent domains. For instance, in clinical prediction tasks, patients can
419   be grouped based on latent domains such as age, medical history, treatment, and symptoms. Training
420   a generic model on heterogeneous patient data is often less effective than using a domain-specific

422   a fixed classifier to each domain without considering the interactions between them. Moreover, it
423
      relies heavily on a separate hierarchical clustering process, which is separated from model training

424
      of patient modalities. We extend this framework by evaluating HMoE for latent domain modeling
425

532
                               In this work, we explored diverse gating function combinations beyond Softmax in a two-level
533
                               hierarchical mixture of experts (HMoE). Our theoretical analysis demonstrated that using Laplace
534                            gating in HMoE improves convergence behavior, and employing Laplace gating at both levels
535                            significantly optimizes performance. We validated this theoretical finding on multiple real-world tasks,
536                            while also showcasing the effectiveness of HMoE in handling complex inputs, such as multimodal
537                            and multidomain data. However, the enhanced ability to process complex inputs comes with increased
538                            computational demands, which is a key limitation of HMoE. For future work, we plan to explore
539                            techniques like pruning to reduce computational costs in large-scale multimodal tasks and to identify
                               more suitable downstream applications for HMoE.

767   A     R ELATED W ORKS
768
769   MoE (Jacobs et al., 1991; Xu et al., 1994) has gained significant popularity for managing complex
770   tasks since its introduction three decades ago. Unlike traditional models that reuse the same parameters
771
      for all inputs, MoE selects distinct parameters for each specific input. This results in a sparsely
      activated layer, enabling a substantial scaling of model capacity without a corresponding increase
772
      in computational cost. Recent studies (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022; Mustafa et al.,
773
      2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024) have demonstrated the effectiveness
774   of integrating MoE with cutting-edge models across a diverse range of tasks. Nie et al. (2021);
775   Zhou et al. (2022); Puigcerver et al. (2023) have also tackled key challenges such as accuracy and
776   training instability. With the growing prevalence of MoE, the HMoE architecture has also been
777   utilized to enhance model generalization performance in complex data structures. For instance, Ng &
778   McLachlan (2007) leveraged HMoE to more effectively manage hierarchical data, thereby improving
779   classification accuracy in medical datasets. Similarly, Peralta & Soto (2014) introduced regularized
780   HMoE models with embedded local feature selection, which enhanced model performance in high-
781   dimensional scenarios. Due to its ability to assign input partitions to specialized experts, HMoE
782
      is particularly well-suited for multi-modal or multi-domain applications (Zhao et al., 2021). Prior
      research has demonstrated that HMoE can ensure robust generalization capabilities (Azran & Meir,
783
      2004). However, existing studies have primarily assessed HMoE in small-scale experiments and have
784
      not shown its effectiveness in large-scale real-world settings.
785
786   While MoE has been widely employed to scale up large models, its theoretical foundations have
787   remained relatively underdeveloped. First of all, Mendes & Jiang (2011) studied the maximum
788
      likelihood estimator for parameters of the MoE with each expert being a polynomial regression
      model. In particular, they investigated the convergence rate of the estimated density to the true
789
      density under the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and gave some insights on how many experts
790
      should be chosen. Next, Ho et al. (2022) conducted a similar convergence analysis for input-free
791   gating Gaussian MoE but using the Hellinger distance for the density estimation problem instead
792   of the KL divergence. Additionally, they utilized the generalized Wasserstein distance to capture
793   the parameter estimation rates which were negatively affected by the algebraic interactions among
794   parameters. Nguyen et al. (2023) then generalized these results to a more popular setting known as
795   softmax gating Gaussian MoE. Rather than leveraging the generalized Wasserstein distance for the
796   parameter estimation problem, they proposed novel Voronoi-based loss functions which were shown
797   to characterize the parameter estimation rates more accurately. Recently, Han et al. (2024) advocated
798   using a new Laplace gating function which induced faster convergence rates than the softmax gating
799
      functions due to a reduced number of parameter interactions. However, to the best of our knowledge,
      a comprehensive convergence analysis for HMoE has remained elusive in the literature.

      Tasks of Interest. We design an in-hospital mortality prediction task (referred to as 48-IHM) to
811   assess our methodвҖҷs capability in forecasting short-term patient deterioration. Additionally, accurately
812   predicting patient discharge times is vital for improving patient outcomes and managing hospital
813   resources efficiently Bertsimas et al. (2022), leading us to implement the length-of-stay (LOS) task.
814   Both the 48-IHM and LOS tasks are framed as binary classification problems, utilizing a 48-hour
815   observation window (for patients staying at least 48 hours in the ICU) to predict in-hospital mortality
816   (48-IHM) and patient discharge (without death) within the subsequent 48 hours (LOS). Moreover,
817   recognizing the presence of specific acute care conditions in patient records is key for several clinical
818   goals, such as forming cohorts for studies and identifying comorbidities Agarwal et al. (2016).
819
      Traditional approaches, which often rely on manual chart reviews or billing codes, are increasingly
      being complemented by machine learning models Harutyunyan et al. (2019). Automating this process
820
      demands high-accuracy classifications, which drives the development of our 25-type phenotype
821
      classification (25-PHE) task. This multilabel classification problem involves predicting one of 25
822   acute care conditions using data from the entire ICU stay. We summarize the details of these tasks
823   below:
824
825          вҖў 48-IHM: This is a binary classification task where we aim to predict in-hospital mortality
826            based on data collected during the first 48 hours of ICU admission, applicable only to
827            patients who remained in the ICU for at least 48 hours.
828          вҖў LOS: The length-of-stay task is structured similarly to 48-IHM. For patients who stayed
829            in the ICU for a minimum of 48 hours, the objective is to predict whether they will be
830            discharged (without death) within the next 48 hours.
831
             вҖў 25-PHE: This multilabel classification task involves predicting one of 25 acute care condi-
832            tions Elixhauser (2009); Lovaasen & Schwerdtfeger (2012), such as congestive heart failure,
833            pneumonia, or shock, at the conclusion of each patientвҖҷs ICU stay. Since the original task
834            was developed for diagnoses based on ICD-9 codes, and MIMIC-IV includes both ICD-9
835            and ICD-10 codes, we convert diagnoses coded in ICD-10 using the conversion database
836            from Butler (2007).
837
838   Evaluation. We concentrated on patients with complete data across all modalities, which yielded
839
      a dataset of 8,770 ICU stays for the 48-IHM and LOS tasks, and 14,541 stays for the 25-PHE task.
      To assess the performance of the single-label tasks, 48-IHM and LOS, we utilize the F1-score and
840
      AUROC as our evaluation metrics. For the 25-PHE task, following prior research (Zhang et al., 2023;
841
      Lin et al., 2019; Arbabi et al., 2019), we rely on macro-averaged F1-score and AUROC as the primary
842   measures of evaluation. For the multimodal fusion task, we allocated 70% data for training, while
843   the remaining 30% was evenly divided between validation and testing. For clinical latent domain
844   discovery, similar to Wu et al. (2024), we segment the dataset into four temporal groups: 2008-2010,
845   2011-2013, 2014-2016, and 2017-2019. Each group is then divided into training, validation, and
846   testing sets, following a 70%, 10%, and 20% split, respectively. Patients admitted after 2014 are
847   treated as the target test data, while all earlier patients are used as the source training data.

851   The eICU dataset (Pollard et al., 2018) includes over 200,000 visits from 139,000 patients admitted
852   to ICUs in 208 hospitals across the United States. The data was gathered between 2014 and 2015.
853   The 208 hospitals are categorized into four regions based on their geographic location: Midwest,
854
      Northeast, West, and South. We define our cohorts by excluding visits from patients younger than 18
      or older than 89, as well as visits exceeding 10 days in length or containing fewer than 3 or more than

861   Evaluation. The eICU dataset is divided into four regional groups: Midwest, Northeast, West, and
862   South. Each region is further split into 70% for training, 10% for validation, and 20% for testing. To
863   assess the performance gap between regions, we compare the backbone modelвҖҷs performance when
      trained on data from the same region versus data from other regions, as proposed by Wu et al. (2024).
