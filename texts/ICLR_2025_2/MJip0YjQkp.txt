011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Spike cameras, bio-inspired vision sensors, asynchronously fire spikes by accu-
014            mulating light intensities at each pixel, offering ultra-high energy efficiency and
015
               exceptional temporal resolution. Unlike event cameras, which record changes in
               light intensity to capture motion, spike cameras provide even finer spatiotempo-
016
               ral resolution and a more precise representation of continuous changes. In this
017
               paper, we introduce the first video action recognition (VAR) dataset using spike
018            camera, alongside synchronized RGB and thermal modalities, to enable compre-
019            hensive benchmarking for Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs). By preserving the
020            inherent sparsity and temporal precision of spiking data, our three datasets offer
021            a unique platform for exploring multimodal video understanding and serve as a
022            valuable resource for directly comparing spiking, thermal, and RGB modalities.
023            This work contributes a novel dataset that will drive research in energy-efficient,
024            ultra-low-power video understanding, specifically for action recognition tasks us-
025            ing spike-based data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Video Action Recognition (VAR) is a key task in computer vision that focuses on detecting and
030   classifying actions or activities from video sequences automatically (Wani & Faridi, 2022). Unlike
031   image classification, which can be seen as analysis of an individual frame, VAR captures both spatial
032   and temporal dynamics, adding complexity such as heavy information redundancy introduced by the
033   temporal dimension, motion variations, changes in lighting or camera angles (Yu et al., 2024), just
034   to name a few. These complexities make VAR highly valuable for real-world applications such as
035   surveillance, healthcare, industrial automation, and sports analysis (Poppe, 2010), where rapid and
036   accurate processing is essential.
037   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), inspired by biological neurons, offer a promising alternative to
038   traditional Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) for tasks involving temporal dynamics, such as VAR
039   (Guo et al., 2023). SNNs operate on sparse, discrete spikes, enabling event-driven computation,
040   which significantly reduces energy consumption compared to ANNs (Roy et al., 2019). This makes
041   SNNs particularly suited for energy-constrained environments like autonomous robots and edge
042   devices (Liu et al., 2024). However, despite their energy efficiency, video understanding with SNNs
043   remains limited. Current research on VAR using SNNs typically relies on converting conventional
044
      RGB video data into spike trains, leading to information loss and constraining the full potential of
      SNNs (Yu et al., 2024).
045
046   A key limitation in existing research is the scarcity of spiking datasets that capture the rich temporal
047   dynamics of real-world video sequences. While event cameras capture data based on brightness
048   changes, spike cameras operate by generating spikes when the photon accumulation at each pixel
049   surpasses a threshold. This enables spike cameras to capture absolute brightness at ultra-high sam-
050
      pling frequencies (up to 20,000 Hz), providing textural spatiotemporal details than event cameras
      (Amir et al., 2021). Despite these advantages, existing datasets, such as DVS128 (see Section 2.1),
051
      are based on event cameras and lack the richness needed for complex action recognition tasks.
052
053   In the context of SNNs, video data presents unique challenges. Videos inherently involve temporal
      sequences, which align naturally with the temporal nature of SNNs. Any temporally structured data


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      can be treated as a video, provided that each time step has a corresponding visual representation.
055   When processing temporal input with an SNN, two distinct approaches arise: (1) aligning the tem-
056   poral dimension of the input with the native temporal axis of the SNN model, or (2) encoding the
057   entire video input separately from the SNN’s native temporal axis. In the latter approach, the video
058   is encoded as spike train as a whole, hence another temporal dimension is added which coincides
059   with the SNN’s temporal axis.
060
      Motivated by the lack of a complex spiking dataset suitable to challenge and push forward the
061   SNNs capacity to address VAR task, we introduce a novel dataset specifically designed for human
062   action recognition using spike cameras. In addition, we provide spiking data with synchronized
063   RGB and thermal modalities to enhance motion capture in low-light conditioning and provide a
064   comprehensive multimodal representation of human actions. By combining these modalities, we
065   provide a framework to explore the complementary information across different sensor types, with
066   the aim of improving the robustness and diversity of SNN models for action recognition tasks.
067   Our datasets are collected from 44 participants, representing a wide range of demographic factors
068   such as age, height, weight, sex, and ethnicity. Each participant performed 18 distinct daily ac-
069   tions (Figure 1), captured over two sessions, resulting in a total dataset duration of 264 minutes. To
070   establish a baseline for comparison, we utilized state-of-the-art (SOTA) architecture based on convo-
071   lutional neural networks (CNNs) and transformer blocks to provide a baseline model for our datasets
072   in both ANN and SNN through ANN-SNN conversion. To summarize, our key contributions are as
073   follows:
074
                • We introduce SPACT18, the first VAR dataset captured using spike camera, setting a new
075
                  benchmark for SNN-based models, and extend it with synchronized RGB and thermal
076
                  modalities for comprehensive multimodal benchmarking.
077
078
                • We propose a compression algorithm for the spiking dataset, yielding new spiking datasets
                  with lower native latency, while preserving critical temporal information, providing a
079
                  framework for preprocessing and compression for the research community.
080
081             • We evaluate our dataset across modalities using SOTA lightweight ANN models and SNN
082
                  baseline obtained through ANN-SNN conversion, and direct training, highlighting critical
                  challenges in spiking video recognition, such as the high latency in ANN-SNN and low
083
                  accuracy in direct training, and providing novel research areas for optimizing SNNs.

      2.2   SPIKE CAMERA DATASETS

124
      Spike cameras have garnered attention for capturing high-speed dynamic scenes, spurring the cre-
125
      ation of datasets to improve tasks like motion estimation, depth estimation, and image reconstruc-
126   tion. Zhu et al. (2020) introduced a dataset for reconstructing visual textures using a retina-inspired
127   sampling method, demonstrating the utility of spike cameras for tasks like object tracking but lim-
128   iting their scope to low-level applications. Similarly, datasets like Spk2ImgNet and PKU-Spike-
129   Stereo by (Zhao et al., 2021) and (Wang et al., 2022b) focus on dynamic scene reconstruction and
130   stereo depth estimation, respectively, without addressing high-level tasks such as video classifica-
131   tion. Similarly, Hu et al. (2022) ’s SCFlow dataset serves as a benchmark for optical flow estima-
132   tion, and Zhao et al. (2020) developed a motion estimation dataset leveraging spike intervals for
133   high-speed motion recovery, both confined to low-level vision tasks.
134   Despite these advancements, a significant gap remains in developing spike camera datasets for high-
135   level tasks like video action recognition (Zhu et al., 2019; Xiang et al., 2023). Current datasets are
136   predominantly designed for tasks such as optical flow (Zhao et al., 2022), motion estimation (Zheng
137   et al., 2023), depth estimation (Zhang et al., 2022) and image reconstruction, limiting the exploration
138   of spike camera data in vision understanding applications, such as event recognition and semantic
139   understanding in dynamic environments. This gap underscores the need for future research on high-
140
      level spike-based vision tasks. Moving toward acceleration research in spiking video understanding,
      we introduce the first multimodal VAR dataset using a spike camera synchronized with RGB and
141
      thermal cameras, enhancing the understanding of VAR through complementary modalities.

144   2.3   V IDEO ACTION R ECOGNITION A RCHITECTURES
145
146   Video understanding architectures have evolved from traditional CNNs to more sophisticated mod-
147
      els. Early approaches like 3D CNNs, such as X3D Feichtenhofer (2020), extended 2D CNNs to
      capture both spatial and temporal aspects of videos Carreira & Zisserman (2017a); Tran et al. (2015;
148
      2018a). Two-Stream Simonyan & Zisserman (2014), TSN Wang et al. (2016), and SlowFast Fe-
149
      ichtenhofer et al. (2019b) further improved action recognition by incorporating spatial-temporal
150   streams, sparse sampling, and parallel networks. Attention-based models, including TimeSformer
151   Bertasius et al. (2021) and ViViT Arnab et al. (2021), enhanced temporal understanding by captur-
152   ing long-range dependencies. Recent models, like VideoSwin Liu et al. (2022b) and UniFormer Li
153   et al. (2022; 2023), combine convolution and self-attention for performance optimization.
154
      On the other side, research on SNN-based video classification remains relatively limited. For in-
155
      stance, a reservoir recurrent SNN with 300-time-step spike sequences from UCF101 was proposed
156   by Panda & Srinivasa (2018), while a heterogeneous recurrent SNN Chakraborty & Mukhopadhyay
157   (2023) showed strong performance on datasets like UCF101, KTH, and DVS-Gesture. Wang et al.
158   (2019) introduced the two-stream hybrid network (TSRNN), combining CNN, RNN, and a spiking
159   module to enhance RNN memory. Another approach, a two-stream deep recurrent SNN, utilized
160   ANN-to-SNN conversion, integrating channel-wise normalization and tandem learning Zhang et al.
161   (2023a). To address conversion errors, You et al. (2024) developed a dual threshold mapping frame-
      work, reducing latency in SNNs using the SlowFast backbone. SVFormer, a spiking transformer

375
376   To evaluate SPACT18, we adopted a systematic approach utilizing SOTA lightweight models.
377   Given spiking data’s energy efficiency and computational advantages, the results were benchmarked
      against highly efficient models to ensure fairness and relevance in future comparisons. We selected


                                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      two prominent lightweight architectures, X3D and UniFormer, known for their balance between
379   computational efficiency and competitive accuracy on benchmark datasets.
380
381   X3D is a highly optimized CNN for video understanding Feichtenhofer (2020), balancing accu-
382
      racy and efficiency by expanding input’s temporal and spatial dimensions, reducing computational
      demands without sacrificing performance. This makes X3D particularly suitable for low-resource
383
      environments without compromising on accuracy. In contrast, UniFormer adopts a hybrid architec-
384
      ture that fuses convolutional operations with self-attention mechanisms Li et al. (2022). This allows
385   the model to capture local and global dependencies in video data, making it versatile across differ-
386   ent modalities. UniFormer’s ability to effectively model spatiotemporal relationships with moderate
387   computational cost ensures its competitive standing, even on challenging datasets.
388
      We employed both small and medium variants for each model, which provide different trade-offs
389
      between model complexity and performance. The models were tested across all data modalities:
390
      thermal and RGB data (in both RGB and grayscale channels). Even though compressed 10k and
391   1k datasets significantly reduce the latency of the spiking dataset, training ANN models on them
392   turned out to be infeasible. In order to provide baselines for them, we performed ”rate” encoding
393   of the datasets, which consists in the following. Instead of using the original binary 10k (resp. 1k)
394   time steps for training the ANN, we reshaped the temporal dimension of spike trains into 100 × 100
395   (resp. 10×100) for compressed 10k (resp. 1k) dataset. We then computed the average along the first
396   dimension to convert the binary spikes into rate-encoded values, effectively reducing the temporal
397   complexity while preserving essential spike information for ANN training.
398   ANN-SNN conversion transforms a pre-trained ANN into a spike-based SNN (Diehl et al., 2015;
399   Cao et al., 2015). For video models, this process is challenging due to the widespread use of non-
400   ReLU activations and the depth of these models exacerbates the propagation of errors across layers.
401   We selected MC3 to provide an SNN baseline for SPACT18. After training MC3, we recorded
402   the maximum ReLU activations channel-wise using training dataset. This channel-wise approach
403   ensures a more precise threshold for each neuron, improving the conversion accuracy. Then, each
404   ReLU was replaced with a Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neuron, using the recorded maximum ac-
405
      tivation as the neuron’s threshold, while the initial membrane potential is set to half of the threshold.
      For inference, video inputs were encoded using a constant-encoding scheme.
406
407   The MC3 model is a variant of 3D CNNs for video action recognition, using a ResNet-18 backbone

409   hybrid approach with 2D spatial and 1D temporal convolutions to reduce complexity while capturing
410   motion information. Notably, MC3 utilizes ReLU activations, making it well-suited for ANN-SNN
411
      conversion and balancing accuracy and efficiency.

413   input size is defined as (Frame rate × Image dimension), and all experiments were conducted using
414   a single RTX A6000 GPU. The dataset was then divided into 80% for training, 10% for validation,
415   and 10% for testing. This split was performed subject-wise, ensuring that each subject and all
416   corresponding activities remained within a single set, thus enabling a rigorous evaluation of the
417
      model’s generalization capacity. Train, validation and test subjects are split at random and kept the
      same for all experiments for fair comparisons and consistency.

477
478   6.1    V IDEO ACTION R ECOGNITION C LASSIFICATION
479
480   We believe that SPACT18 can be exploited by the SNN’s research community as benchmark to
481   accelerate video understanding tasks, for efficient deployment on realworld applications.
482   Direct SNN Training: Our dataset offers rich temporal information, with raw spiking data con-
483   taining 100k time steps per sample. Direct training on such large-scale data is computationally
484   intensive, particularly for SNNs, as their training computational complexity scales with the num-
485   ber of time steps. This makes SPACT18 (and its compressed versions) a challenge for developing
      specialized algorithms tailored for direct SNN training and evaluation.

501
      Multimodal Action classification: Multimodal research in SNNs is still in its early stages, fac-
      ing significant challenges compared to ANNs, particularly in integrating data like RGB and thermal
502
      camera inputs with event-driven spikes from sensors such as spike cameras (Dai et al., 2024; Rathi
503
      & Roy, 2021). While most SNN research has focused on single-modality event-driven data, the syn-
504   chronization and fusion of diverse modalities, along with the development of learning algorithms to
505   handle such multimodal inputs, remain key obstacles (Bjorndahl et al., 2024). The SNN community
506   can benefit from multimodal datasets that include RGB, thermal, and spike camera data, enabling the
507   creation of energy-efficient models for real-time, low-power applications like surveillance, robotics,
508   and autonomous systems (Safa et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). These datasets allow researchers
509   to explore fusion, enhance action recognition, improve cross-modal learning, and benchmark neu-
510   romorphic hardware, ultimately advancing the use of SNNs in dynamic environments.
511
512   6.2   L OW L EVEL V ISION A PPLICATIONS
513
514   In addition to the main task of video action classification, our dataset can be used in other tasks as:
515   Reconstruction: Recently, significant research has focused on spike camera reconstruction for
516   high-speed moving objects, with most datasets presented specifically to the reconstruction methods
517   being developed (Zhao et al., 2020; 2021; 2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2022). Addition-
518   ally, spike cameras typically require sunlight for optimal performance; however, our dataset was
519   collected indoors under artificial lighting, presenting a new challenge. This unique setting also pro-
520   vides an opportunity to benchmark reconstruction algorithms under less ideal lighting conditions.
521   Compression: We have proposed a new compression algorithm to reduce the temporal resolution
522   of raw spiking data, enabling more efficient training on smaller datasets. However, more efficient
523   algorithms could be devloped to extract features from the original raw data and compress it into a
524   smaller spiking representation with minimal loss of the rich temporal information inherent in the
525   original dataset. Furthermore, our dataset can also be used to evaluate compression algorithms for
526   efficient storage and transmission of spike camera data (Feng et al., 2023).

531
      This paper introduces SPACT18, the first spiking VAR benchmark dataset using spike cameras syn-
      chronized with RGB and thermal modalities, advancing spiking video understanding. Key findings
532
      show spiking data achieves competitive performance with compressed 10k (rate), while compressed
533
      1k with degraded accuracy highlights the trade-offs of extreme compression. Thermal data outper-
534   formed other modalities, achieving 85.7% accuracy with UniFormer, demonstrating the potential of
535   multimodal fusion. In contrast, direct spiking training and ANN-SNN conversion remain challeng-
536   ing due to low accuracy, high latency and computational complexity. Although, Hybrid approaches
537   like Respike excel in cross-modal integration underscoring the trade-off between accuracy and en-
538   ergy efficiency. SPACT18 lays a foundation for energy-efficient models, with future work focusing
539   on optimized SNN training, improved ANN-SNN conversion, and multimodal integration for prac-
      tical applications.

883   The literature extensively uses multi-modalities for video action recognition, including RGB, ther-
884   mal, depth, and IMU data. Additionally, some datasets have employed event cameras. A key differ-

      entiator of our work is including spiking data, which adds significant value.
886   Table 6 highlights the key features of widely used action recognition datasets and emphasizes the
887   unique contributions of SPACT18. Unlike other datasets, SPACT18 integrates spiking data with
888   RGB and thermal modalities, providing a comprehensive multi-modal framework for video under-
889   standing. The inclusion of a thermal camera is particularly important as it captures motion effec-
890   tively under low lighting and low frame rates, complementing RGB and spike data. Synchronizing
891
      these modalities enhances the dataset’s diversity and robustness, allowing for broader applications
      and meaningful performance comparisons. Furthermore, SPACT18 provides raw spiking data from
892
      native spike camera recordings, offering researchers the opportunity to optimize SNNs specifically
893
      for video understanding tasks. With its high-resolution data, diverse set of 18 activity classes, and
894   multi-modal design, SPACT18 represents a notable advancement in action recognition and spiking
895   research, driving innovation in energy-efficient video processing technologies.
896
897
      The table 7 compares several action recognition datasets, highlighting features like data sources,
      modalities, resolutions, and class/sample counts. The SPACT18 dataset offers diverse modalities
898
      (Spike, RGB, Thermal), unlike traditional datasets (e.g., HMDB-51, UCF-101, Kinetics-400) that
899
      use only RGB data. SPACT18’s higher-resolution RGB and Thermal videos enable detailed feature
900   extraction, and its balanced and high samples per class (approximately 176) support robust training.
901   Overall, SPACT18 bridges the gap between conventional video recognition datasets and multi-modal
902   action recognition, enhancing understanding of complex human activities.
