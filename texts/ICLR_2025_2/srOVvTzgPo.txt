009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Compositionality has long been considered a fundamental aspect of human cog-
012            nition - enabling the learning, manipulation, and generation of natural language.
013            Understanding how this concept applies to Large Language Models (LLMs) and
014            how it can be effectively evaluated remains a key challenge. In this work, we
015            explore the potential of formalizing cognitive notions from theory, such as com-
016            positionality, to develop more nuanced evaluation frameworks for LLMs. Using
017            a sheaf-theoretic approach, we define compositionality through four distinct con-
018
               ditions that capture its multifaceted nature. This formalization offers a structured
               perspective on evaluating LLMs, moving beyond surface-level assessments to un-
019
               cover deeper insights into their behavior. Our findings suggest that theoretical
020
               frameworks like this one can play a crucial role in advancing the understanding
021            and evaluation of LLMs, providing a foundation for more comprehensive and pre-
022            cise performance analyses.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Compositionality has long been a key focus in the study of human cognition. Early work by Fodor
028   & Pylyshyn (1988) challenged the capability of non-symbolic neural network models to be com-
029   positional due to lack of symbolic representations but Smolensky (1987),Van Gelder (1990), and
030   Chalmers (1993) were instrumental in challenging the prevailing scepticism by asserting that the
031   networks’ intricate connection weights and activation patterns can lead to functional composition-
032   ality. However, as Aizawa & Aizawa (2003) points out, neither the symbolic nor the functional
033
      view of compositionality succeeds in building compositionality as a core tenet of the theory that
      can necessitate the development of compositional behaviour of a system without relying on ad-hoc
034
      assumptions. Moreover, neither the symbolic nor functional theories provide any elucidation on
035
      the processes involved in being compositional beyond a primarily concatenative lexicalist view of
036   combining tokens or lexemes.
037
038   Such issues become more pronounced when we talk of compositionality for systems like LLMs
039
      where compositionality is not a core design feature but can emerge through the process of learning
      and manipulating representations. Also, LLMs today are highly performant connectionist systems
040
      and are increasingly seen as possible models of human language (Mahowald et al., 2024; Hu et al.,
041
      2024) or cognition (Kauf et al., 2023; Hardy et al., 2023; Marjieh et al., 2023; Lamprinidis, 2023)
042   which makes it imperative for us to try and answer two important questions with respect to LLMs
043   and their compositional abilities:
044
045         • How do we define compositionality for LLMs?
046
            • How do LLMs perform in compositionality tasks, i.e., can these tasks help us better under-
047           stand the capabilities of these models and provide insights into their overall performance?
048
049   To address the first question, we thus defer to a sheaf theoretic definition of compositionality for
050   LLMs that uses elements of categorical compositionality (Phillips & Wilson, 2010; 2016b) and
051   sheaf theoretic topology (Phillips, 2018; 2020) to define and delineate different aspects of com-
052   positionality. Such a way of defining compositionality has two distinct advantages: It allows us to
053   model compositionality as a learning process that goes beyond first-order systematicity (understand-
      ing relations between entities) to the development of second-order systematicity (understanding the


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      structure of such relations themselves) (Phillips & Wilson, 2016a; Davis et al., 2020). Moreover, it
055   also enables us to address compositionality not merely in terms of symbols – which neural networks
056   do not explicitly possess due to polysemanticity (Huben et al., 2023; Lecomte et al.) – or through the
057   direct composition of vectors – which is challenging due to non-linearity (Mikolov et al., 2013) – but
058   rather in terms of patterns governing the structure of form-meaning mappings that models must learn
059   and represent. Specifically, we model compositionality as a sheaf-theoretic phenomenon where sys-
060   tematic generalization capabilities arise from sheaving constructions performed on presheaves via
061   sheaf morphisms.
062   Using our definition of compositionality, we formalize the possible structure of tasks needed for
063   evaluating the different processes and aspects linked to developing compositionality. We also evalu-
064   ate a wide range of LLMs on our tasks and try to determine whether performance on compositional
065   tasks is capable of illuminating pitfalls and overall performance trends of different LLMs. Our
066   findings reveal that the tasks are capable of reaffirming some well-known performance trends, e.g.,
067   larger models are usually better, and detecting lesser known ones, e.g., instruction-tuned models can
068   be quite inconsistent across benchmarks. This suggests that the connection between composition-
069
      ality and model performance might not be coincidental. Just as compositionality underpins human
      cognition, it most likely is also a fundamental characteristic of LLMs.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
073
074
075   The investigation of compositional abilities of LLMs is not a new area of work but one of the main
076
      issues has been that most works do not adhere to a common notion of compositionality. Earlier
      works focused on analyzing compositional abilities in trained artificial neural networks like Lake
077
      & Baroni (2018) and Kim & Linzen (2020a) where compositionality is considered a process of
078
      uncovering the underlying syntactical structure of phrases to generalize correctly. Hupkes et al.
079   (2020) proposes that compositionality is more than simple syntactic structure and breaks down the
080   notion of compositionality into four aspects (systematicity, productivity, substitutivity, localism and
081   overgeneralisation)- while this was the first work to address the complex nature of compositionality,
082   the primary assumptions still centred around syntactic structure recovery. Moreover, these works
083   focus on networks trained specifically for the task at hand and were before the rise of current LLMs
084   which are highlighted by their pretraining and finetuning regimes.
085   For LLMs, the question of defining compositionality becomes more complex- given pretraining
086   on a different tasks these models generalize extremely well on new tasks but how can we define
087   or understand this compositional generalization ability in such models? Most works that investi-
088   gate compositionality in LLMs adhere to the general notion of compositionality as building up of
089   complex expressions from simple ones (Lake & Baroni, 2018; Kim & Linzen, 2020b; Hupkes et al.,
090   2020; Lepori et al., 2023; Drozdov et al., 2022; SHAO et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023), none of which
091   provide us with a formalization of the notion of compositionality and give any insights into what
092   models need to learn to become compositional. Some recent works have considered compositional-
093
      ity as the ability to perform multi-hop reasoning (Dziri et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Okawa et al.,
      2024) which is somewhat misleading as this notion of combining solutions to subproblems is far
094
      removed from the concept of compositional generalization as discussed in language and cognition
095
      sciences. Moreover, such notions of compositionality are overly symbolic and do not consider the
096   proclivities of neural networks which are capable of a different manifestation of functional composi-
097   tional abilities Smolensky (1987); Van Gelder (1990); Chalmers (1993). Defining compositionality
098   in a symbolic or functional framework is not only limiting in terms of understanding and defining the
099   processes that lead to compositionality, but it also restricts our interpretation of the term to learning
100   lower order relations as opposed to higher order relations and morphisms that enable generalization
101   in language.
102   In cognitive sciences, however, there has been some work in attempting a more formal understand-
103   ing of compositionality that goes beyond the typical symbolic notion of compositionality Rappe
104   (2022); Montemayor & Balci (2007) and focuses on LLM-like connectionist architectures Martin
105   & Doumas (2020); Elmoznino et al. (2024). The most significant of such work for our purposes
106   is the characterization of compositionality in terms of uncovering the underlying structure of data
107   by learning the mathematical structures that characterize the data Phillips & Wilson (2010; 2016b);
      Phillips (2018; 2020)- such a notion of compositionality is not dependent on symbolic notions of

      combining symbols to build up complex expressions and also highlights what kinds of structures
109   models need to develop for compositional generalization, which makes this approach suitable to
110   analyzing systems like LLMs which are not symbolic in principle.

      3   D EFINING C OMPOSITIONALITY
114
115
      We adopt a sheaf-theoretic approach to compositionality for LLMs, incorporating elements of cate-
      gorial compositionality and sheaf-theoretic topology to define various aspects of it. This approach
116
      offers two key benefits: it models compositionality as a learning process extending beyond first-order
117
      systematicity (relations between entities) to second-order systematicity (relations between relations)
118   (Phillips & Wilson, 2016b;a). Additionally, it frames compositionality not merely in terms of sym-
119   bols or vector composition, but as patterns in form-meaning mappings that models must learn, using
120   sheaving constructions and morphisms to achieve systematic generalization (Phillips, 2018).
121
      In general, a sheaf is defined in the following manner: Let X be a topological space. A sheaf F
122
      on X is a functor from the category of open sets Open(X) to the category of sets, satisfying the
123
      following conditions:
124
125         1. For every open set U ⊆ X, there is a set F(U ), called the section of F over U .
126
            2. If V ⊆ U , then there is a restriction map ρU,V : F(U ) → F(V ).
127
128         3. Gluing condition: If {Ui } is an open cover of U and sections si ∈ F(Ui ) agree on the
129            overlaps (i.e., si |Ui ∩Uj = sj |Ui ∩Uj ), then there exists a unique section s ∈ F(U ) such that
130
               s|Ui = si for all i.
131         4. Locality condition: If s, t ∈ F(U ) are sections such that for each i ∈ I, s|Ui = t|Ui ,
132            then s = t.
133
134
      Another concept from sheaf theory that facilitates the preservation of local-to-global information, is
      a natural transformation.
135
136   Natural Transformation: If F, G are sheaves on a topological space X, viewed as functors from
137   the category of open sets of X (denoted by Open(X)) to the category of sets (or other suitable
138   categories), then a natural transformation between two sheaves F and G is a family of maps:
139
140                             ηU : F(U ) → G(U ) for each open set U ⊆ X,
141
142   such that for every inclusion of open sets V ⊆ U , the following diagram commutes:
143
144                                                    resF
                                                          U,V
145                                           F(U ) −−−−→        F(V )
146
                                              ↓ ηU               ↓ ηV
                                                       resG
                                                          U,V
147                                           G(U )    −−−−→     G(V )
148
149   where resU,V denotes the restriction maps of the sheaves F and G.
150
      In the linguistic topological space, the property of compositional generalization can thus be under-
151   stood as the structuring of sheaves from presheaves where gluing and locality conditions ensure
152   that the local data (meanings, transformations) are consistent when combined globally, which paral-
153   lels systematic compositionality in language – ensuring that local rules generalize across contexts.
154   Moreover, being compositional in a way as to appropriately arrive at global information from local
155   requires learning appropriate natural transformations, with commutating restrictions, for the pur-
156   poses of preserving the local-global structures in an appropriate manner. Thus, for a model to be
157   compositional, it must learn the following:
158
159
            1. R ESTRICTION M APS : The ability to define proper restriction maps which ensures that data
               assigned to larger sets can be consistently related to smaller sets across sections.
160
161         2. G LUING C ONDITIONS : The ability to avoid violations of the gluing conditions i.e. dis-
               cover appropriate overlaps while discovering global sections.


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
            3. L OCALITY C ONDITIONS : The ability to avoid violations of the locality conditions i.e.
163            determine when the local sections of data come from a global section and when they do
164            not.
165
            4. L EARNING NATURAL T RANSFORMATIONS :The ability to discover natural transforma-
166
               tions that preserve the coherence of sheaves.
167
168   Now for each of the four aspects of being compositional, we define formalization of a task that can
169   test these properties and also come up with concrete language processing tasks or datasets which we
170   use to evaluate large language models.

172   3.1   E VALUATING R ESTRICTION M APS
173
174   Let X be a topological space, and let F be a sheaf over X. For any open set U ⊂ X, the sheaf
175   assigns a set of sections F (U ) to U , representing data or objects over U .
176   For open sets V ⊆ U , there is a restriction map:
177
                                               resU,V : F (U ) → F (V ),
178
179   which maps sections over U to sections over V , ensuring consistency. For a section s ∈ F (U ), the
180   restriction map ensures that:
181                                    resU,V (s) = sV      where sV ∈ F (V ).
182
183   This maintains the consistency of data from larger sets to smaller sets. A violation occurs when the
184   section on U does not restrict consistently to V :
185                                                 resU,V (s) ̸= sV ,
186
      indicating that global data is inconsistent with local data. Consider open sets U1 , U2 ⊂ U with
187
      U1 ∩ U2 ̸= ∅. Sections s1 ∈ F (U1 ) and s2 ∈ F (U2 ) must agree on their overlap:
188
189                                     resU1 ∩U2 ,U1 (s1 ) = resU1 ∩U2 ,U2 (s2 ).
190   Failure to satisfy this gives:
191
192                     resU1 ∩U2 ,U1 (s1 ) ̸= resU1 ∩U2 ,U2 (s2 )     =⇒       s ∈ F (U1 ∪ U2 ).
193
194
      For U ⊂ X covered by open sets U1 , U2 , . . . , Un , restriction maps ensure that sections si ∈ F (Ui )
      agree on overlaps:
195
                                      resUi ∩Uj ,Ui (si ) = resUi ∩Uj ,Uj (sj ),
196
197   so that we can glue these sections to form a global section over U . A violation occurs when:
198                                      resUi ∩Uj ,Ui (si ) ̸= resUi ∩Uj ,Uj (sj ),
199
      which prevents forming a consistent global section. The restriction map ensures that local and global
200
      data are consistent. Failure of the restriction map prevents gluing local sections into a global section,
201
      violating the sheaf’s core properties.
202
203   The SCAN dataset Lake & Baroni (2018) provides an appropriate task to test the understanding
204   of the formation of restriction maps in LLMs. It involves simple commands (”jump twice”) paired
205
      with corresponding action sequences (”JUMP JUMP”). The model is expected to ensure that the
      mappings for complex instructions can be restricted consistently to simpler components. For in-
206
      stance, ”jump twice” should be restricted to ”jump” in a way that aligns with the learned mapping
207
      for ”jump.” If the model fails to consistently apply the restriction, it violates the restriction map
208   property, indicating it cannot generalize compositionally across instructions. For more details on
209   the suitability of this dataset for this task, please refer to A.1.
210
211   3.2   E VALUATING G LUING C ONDITIONS
212
213   Let X be a topological space and {Ui }i∈I be an open cover of X. For each open set Ui , a sheaf F
214   assigns sections (data) si ∈ F (Ui ). A ∈ F (U1 ) is a section defined over an open set U1 ⊂ X and
215   CA ∈ F (U2 ) is a section defined over another open set U2 ⊂ X, where CA represents a compound
      form of A. Let the sets U1 and U2 overlap, i.e., U1 ∩ U2 ̸= ∅.


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      If the relation between A and CA is not properly determined, leading to:
217
218
219                                      s1 (A)|U1 ∩U2 ̸= s2 (CA)|U1 ∩U2 ,
220   then there is no unique global section s ∈ F (U1 ∪ U2 ) that can satisfy both:
221
222                                  s|U1 = s1 (A) and s|U2 = s2 (CA).
223
224   Thus, the failure to determine the relation between A and CA constitutes a violation of the gluing
225   condition. can be expressed as:
226
                             s1 (A)|U1 ∩U2 ̸= s2 (CA)|U1 ∩U2 =⇒ s ∈ F (U1 ∪ U2 ).
227
228
229
      LLMs should be able to understand the violations of gluing condition where present. To test this
      in LLMs, we use our version of the AddOne Task Pavlick & Callison-Burch (2016) with the mini
230
      Antails Dataset. For a given sentence with a noun (N) like The runner set a record, we
231
      substitute N with an adjective – noun combination like The runner set a new record and test the
232   model to see whether it can understand the entailment pattern. The model here has to maintain its
233   understanding of entailment patterns with adjective substitution. Please refer to A.2 for more details
234   on the suitability of this task for testing this condition in LLMs.

      3.3   E VALUATING L OCALITY C ONDITIONS
237
238   Let U ⊆ X be a topological space and F be a sheaf on U , assigning sections si ∈ F (Ui ) to open
239   sets Ui ⊂ U . Consider a task where we are given a triple (a, b, c), where a and b are semantically
240   related, but a and c are not. sab is the section over an open set U1 ⊂ U , capturing the semantic
241   relationship between a and b, sac is the section over an open set U2 ⊂ U , capturing the semantic
242   relationship between a and c. U1 ∩ U2 ̸= ∅ represents the overlap between the regions covered by
243   sab and sac .
244   If the sections sab and sac were to satisfy the locality condition, we would require:
245
246
247
                                              sab |U1 ∩U2 = sac |U1 ∩U2
248
      However, since a and c are not semantically related, the sections sab and sac should differ in the
249
      overlap U1 ∩ U2 . If the model fails to distinguish between sab and sac , this would violate the
250
      locality condition because it would incorrectly equate the unrelated pair (a, c) with the related pair
251   (a, b), implying:
252
253
254                      sab |U1 ∩U2 = sac |U1 ∩U2   (incorrect, as a and c are not related)
255
256   This failure results in: sab = sac which is a contradiction, since:
257
258                  sab ̸= sac    (as a and b are semantically related, but a and c are not).
259
260   Thus, this failure to distinguish between (a, b) and (a, c) constitutes a violation of the locality con-
261   dition in sheaf theory.
262
      To evaluate LLMs on their ability to respect locality conditions, we propose the
263
      COMPCOMB dataset- a new task type using a handcrafted toy dataset which is a novel contri-
264   bution of this work (more details on suitability of dataset for this task in A.3). Each data point
265   consists of a triple – a noun, an adjective that goes with the noun, and an exocentric compound
266   which contains the noun. For example, (coat, trenchcoat and turncoat) – when we take the word
267   “coat”, we know that “trenchcoat” ( a special type of coat) is closely related to it but the exocentric
268   compound “turncoat” (a betrayer) is not since it is semantically different. This tests the LLM’s
269   ability to distinguish between genuine compounds and combinations by avoiding generalization on
      the basis of surface forms.

      3.4    L EARNING U NIVERSAL T RANSFORMATIONS
271
272   Let FA , FB , and FC be sheaves over a topological space X. We are given the following mappings:
273
                                                             ϕA,B : FA → FB ,
274
275                                                           ϕA,C : FA → FC .
276
277
      The task is to find a mapping:ϕA,BC : FA → FBC where FBC represents a combined sheaf con-
      structed from FB and FC . The sheaf FBC combines the data from FB and FC in a way that respects
278
      both the mappings ϕA,B and ϕA,C . A natural transformation η must respect the restriction maps of
279
      the sheaves. If the task of finding ϕA,BC : FA → FBC fails, this indicates that we cannot construct
280   a natural transformation between the sheaves FA and FBC . Specifically, the failure occurs if the
281   mappings ϕA,B and ϕA,C are inconsistent with the desired mapping ϕA,BC . This would result in
282   the failure of the following commutative diagram:
283                                                                       ϕA,BC
284                                                          FA           −−−−→         FBC
285                                                        ↓ ϕA,B                        ↓
286                                                          FB                         FC
287
288
      If ϕA,B and ϕA,C do not align in a way that allows the construction of ϕA,BC , then there is no natural
      transformation between FA and FBC , indicating a failure to establish the relationship between A,
289
      B, and C. This indicates that the failure to relate FA → FBC stems from the inconsistency between
290
      ϕA,B and ϕA,C , violating the conditions required for a natural transformation between the sheaves.
291
292   An LLM must be able to distinguish appropriately when the diagram commutes and when it doesn’t
293   i.e. between situations when the natural transformation exists and when it doesn’t. To test this in
294
      LLMs, we use the PLANE Dataset Bertolini et al. (2022) that tests adjective – noun entailment
      in a situation where the entailment pattern for an AN – N and AN – H (where AN is the adjective –
295
      noun combination, N is the noun and H is a hypernym of N) combination is already given and the
296
      model is tested on entailment of AN – AH combination. Please refer to A.4 for more details on the
297   suitability of this task for testing this condition in LLMs.

      Each of these behavioural datasets is evaluated with a comparative log probability setup. The evalu-
366   ation involves computing the model’s log probabilities for two possible completions: one being the
367   correct option and the other the control (incorrect option). The model’s preference between the two
368   completions is determined by comparing their log probabilities and the setup focuses on the model’s
369   probabilistic confidence in its outputs. The completion with the higher log probability is consid-
370   ered indicative of the model’s judgement and we conduct experiments with two prompts to ensure
371   robustness for our results. For both the Antails Dataset and the PLANE Dataset, which
372   involve binary classification tasks, the two completions correspond to entailment and non-entailment
373   outcomes.
374   The prompt completions used in our evaluation are simple prompts. We choose not to use advanced
375   prompts like few-shot Wei et al. (2021) and chain of thought Wei et al. (2022c) to avoid giving
376   undue advantages to the instruct models since they are typically trained to show the best perfor-
377   mance with advanced instruction prompts Longpre et al. (2023). Moreover, we also choose the log
      probability evaluation instead of prompt-output evaluation due to problems with prompted output

      evaluation. Recent research indicates that prompt outputs of LLMs are often misleading (Sclar et al.,
379   2024; Turpin et al., 2024; McCoy et al., 2023) with log-likelihood comparisons being better for un-

381   similar uncertainties and high variation across very similar prompts in prompting output evaluations

383   evaluations.

      Representational Analysis: This dataset type evaluates the model based on its internal representa-
385   tions, rather than its input – output behaviour. The Compcomb Dataset is specifically designed
386   to examine how well the model’s internal representations encode the relationships between related
387   and unrelated adjective – noun and exocentric compound pairs. It is a dataset with 50 samples.

      To evaluate the model’s representations, we extract data from two key layers of the model:
389
390          • The embedding layer: This layer captures the model’s initial word representations before
391            any processing from the deeper layers.
392          • The final hidden layer: This layer captures the model’s most complex and abstracted repre-
393            sentations, which reflect its deep understanding of the input after all layers have processed
394            it.

      sidered to be accurate if its representations for noun and adjective – noun combinations are closer

      representations in these two layers, we can gain insights into how well the model captures seman-
399
      tic relationships and distinctions between input items (such as distinguishing between a noun and
400   its related and unrelated compounds). This setup allows for an analysis of the model’s ability to
401   differentiate semantically related pairs from unrelated ones based purely on internal representation
402   quality.

422   4.3   R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
423
      Our experiments evaluated compositionality in terms of learning different aspects of creating a sheaf
424
      that leads to complete compositional generalization in a model. In 1 and 2 we compare the perfor-
425   mances of each model type (base, instruction following checkpoint, and larger model) where each
426   subplot indicates the results for a dataset/condition and in 1 we provide the actual accuracies of
427   model performance across each dataset.
428
      Across the four model families tested, we present a brief overview of how they perform on each
429
      aspect of compositionality:
430
431   Restriction Condition: For the SCAN Dataset, which tests the restriction conditions, we observe
      in that while none of the models perfectly satisfy the restriction condition, within each model family


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the largest models get the highest accuracies showing an improved understanding in this aspect of
433   compositionality. This aligns with most LLM evaluation studies on the impacts of scaling (Wei
434   et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024). However, more surprisingly, we see that
435   instruction tuned models perform the worst for Llama2, Codellama, and Mistral – indicating
436   that instruction tuning likely leads to a loss in the development of restriction maps which could be
437   explained by the fact that while the model retains it’s most important generalizations, it loses some
438   local information to accommodate instruction tuning, leading to loss of restriction mapping. This
439   also echoes more recent research that investigates the negative impacts and knowledge degradation
440   of instruction tuned or aligned models (Ghosh et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).
441   Gluing Condition: The evaluation of the gluing condition with the Antails Dataset shows
442   a more variable pattern of behaviour across model families – while larger models are better for the
443   majority of model families, instruction tuning leads to better performance in Falcon and Llama2
444   while it leads to worse performance in the acquisition of gluing condition for both Codellama and
445   Mistral models. Such a variance across model types and families might be indicative of a higher
446   level of difficulty in acquiring the gluing conditions of compositionality, making it very specific to
447
      different model training data and procedures.
448   Locality Condition: We evaluate the locality condition with our Compcomb Dataset and
449   observe more stable trends across all families of models (Falcon, Llama2,Codellama, and
450   Mistral) showing that instruction tuned models do better than base models while scaled models
451   still perform the best. This indicates that instruction tuning and scaling both contribute to improved
452   learning of the locality conditions and the learning process might be more stable across models,
453
      as compared to the gluing condition. Compared with the restriction condition, we see that while
      instruction tuning leads to loss of information on local sections of the topology and the ability to
454
      distinguish when the global sections can be reconstructed and when they cannot, it still systemati-
455
      cally retains information on the presence of a unique global section.
456
457   Natural Transformation: The PLANE Dataset is targeted at analysing the ability of models to
458   find the appropriate conditions for natural transformations between sheaves. The performance trends
459
      here are more stable across model families where the larger models show uniform improvements in
      their abilities to realize natural transformations inherent in the data. Also, models in the Llama2,
460
      Codellama and Mistral family show similar patterns of learning as the restriction condition
461
      where instruction tuned models show worsening abilities in recognizing the correct natural transfor-
462   mation. Another interesting pattern emerges here- exactly the same model families where instruc-
463   tion tuning harmed learning of the gluing condition also shows inverse scaling (Wei et al., 2022b;
464   Michaelov & Bergen, 2022; McKenzie et al., 2023; Gupta, 2023) for learning of natural transfor-
465   mations. This might be indicative of a subtly stronger interplay between learning restrictions and
466   finding natural transformations that gets reflected in the compositional abilities of the model.

      5   D ISCUSSION
470
      Our work focuses on the development of a sheaf-theoretic interpretation of compositionality that
471
      portrays compositional generalization as emerging from the ability to construct sheaves and natural
472
      transformations between sheaves. Such an interpretation is not only advantageous from a cognitive
473   point of view, where it has been found to be relevant for understanding reasoning processes and
474   pitfalls in humans (Phillips, 2018) but also from the point of view of understanding and evaluating
475   capabilities of models of language like LLMs.
476
477          • Systematic Understanding of Compositionality: By breaking down the complex phe-
478            nomenon of compositionality into four testable conditions related to constructing proper
479            sheaves and morphisms, our approach allows for precise evaluation of this phenomenon in
480            models. These conditions provide the foundation for targeted understanding of specific as-
481            pects of compositionality, enabling a more structured and systematic evaluation framework
482
               for LLMs. It allows us to break down the complex phenomenon of compositionality into
               four aspects of building a proper sheaf/sheaf morphism.
483
484          • Nuanced Task-based Evaluation: We provide a suitable task paired with four different
485            conditions, which makes it easier to evaluate the compositional abilities of language mod-
               els and analyse their performance in terms of each aspect. Our testable conditions allow


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
               us to identify four tasks that map to each condition and the focus here is to show that for-
487            malization should lead to testable conditions not to estbalish that the tasks we show are the
488            only or optimal tests of compositionality.
489
             • Potential Downstream Applications: Compositionality has been considered a core fea-
490
               ture of human language abilities which leads to their superior performance in tasks like
491            reasoning, generalization and quick learning from limited data. As models of language,
492            we can also expect that compositionality might be a core feature driving downstream per-
493            formance of models. The performance of LLMs in this small set of tasks already reveals
494            different behavioural trends that have been observed from different tasks and benchmarks-
495            both scaling and inverse scaling but also both improvement and worsening performance
496            of fine tuned models. This indicates that the aspects of compositionality delineated here
497            might have a causal impact on general reasoning capabilities in models and might even be
498            indicative of their overall performance trends.
499          • Dynamic View of Compositionality:The view of compositionality as a dynamic process
500            (instead of an ideal static arrangement of discrete symbols) is more amenable to inter-
501            pretability. By focusing on how local connections and transformations aggregate to form
502            global representations, we can analyse the development of different aspects of composi-
503
               tionality in different model components to gain a clearer insight into the inner workings of
               models, allowing us to identify how individual parts contribute to the whole. This, in turn,
504
               can facilitate the debugging, refining, and optimizing of models by targeting specific local
505
               processes that influence overall performance and consistency in such models.
506
507   In summary, our approach to compositionality offers a comprehensive framework that enriches both
508   cognitive and computational understanding of how complex structures are formed from simpler
509   components and enables a more structured evaluation of their reasoning abilities. This work is not
510   aimed at finding the best definition of compositionality or the ideal set of tasks to measure composi-
511   tionality in LLMs, but rather it aims to highlight that our current understanding of compositionality-
512   especially for connectionist systems like LLMs- is quite limited and that ultimately, this perspective
513   not only advances the theoretical understanding of compositionality but can also provide practical
514   tools for evaluating and improving the performance of complex systems like language models.
515
516   6   L IMITATIONS & F UTURE W ORK
517
518   Our work is aimed at attempting a formal definition of compositionality, influenced by theories
519   from human cognition, and providing possible tasks that could be used to test LLMs under such
520   formal frameworks- however, we do not claim that our framework is the only one or even that the
521   tasks we choose to assess compositionality are the best- merely that compositionality is a complex
522   phenomenon that deserves a more nuanced formal definition in case of LLMs and that such formal-
523   ization can also help us choose tasks for better insightful evaluation in such models. We leave it up
524
      to future works to develop similar formal notions of compositionality and develop more nuanced
      evaluations for the same.
525
526   In terms of datasets and models, our collection is small i.e we use small dataset samples and few
527   models due to compute limitations. Moreover, some of our datasets are limited in size and they may
528   not be the perfect ones to capture each facet of compositionality and further research should focus
529   on large scale evaluation with larger datasets and developing even better datasets suited to testing
530
      each condition in the framework.
531   The link between compositionality and overall model performance is suggested but not fully estab-
532   lished. It remains uncertain to what extent compositionality directly impacts general model capabil-
533   ities or whether other factors like model size or training data play a larger role.
534   An area of future work is the generalization and application of this framework to a wider range of
535   models. Currently, our work focuses on specific LLM types such as instruction tuned and scaled
536   models due to current compute limitations. However, it could be used to evaluate models with a
537   wider range of sizes and training or finetuning methods to explore how different processes of learn-
538   ing can impact compositionality in models. Moreover, the framework is general enough to allow
539   potential generalization to test composition and reasoning abilities in different types of emerging
      language model architectures (Fu et al., 2023; Hasani et al., 2023).

      A.1    SCAN FOR R ESTRICTION C ONDITION
759
760   In sheaf theory, for a topological space X and an open set U ⊂ X, a sheaf F assigns to U a set
761   of sections F (U ), representing data or mappings over U . If V ⊂ U , the restriction map resU,V :
762   F (U ) → F (V ) ensures that the data on V is the restriction of the data on U .
763   For a section s ∈ F (U ), the restriction to the subset V is:
764
765                                                resU,V (s) = s|V ,
766   which guarantees that the local data F (V ) is consistent with the global data F (U ).
767
768
      The SCAN task consists of simple instructions (“turn left twice”) paired with target out-
      puts(“LTURN LTURN”). Let X represent the set of all possible instructions, and let F be a sheaf
769
      that assigns to each open set U ⊂ X the corresponding action mappings for the instructions in U .
770
      For instance:
771                          F (Usimple ) = {action mappings for simple instructions},
772
                           F (Ucomplex ) = {action mappings for complex instructions}.
773
774   For a complex instruction Ucomplex and a subset Usubcomplex ⊂ Ucomplex , the restriction condition
775
      requires that the action mapping for the complex instruction scomplex ∈ F (Ucomplex ) restricts consis-
      tently to the simpler instruction in Usubcomplex . This is expressed as:
776
777                                  resUcomplex ,Usubcomplex (scomplex ) = ssubcomplex .
778
779   A violation occurs when the learned mapping for the complex instruction does not restrict consis-
780   tently to its subcomponents. Mathematically, this violation can be represented as:
781                                  resUcomplex ,Usubcomplex (scomplex ) ̸= ssubcomplex .
782
783
      This failure indicates that the model’s mapping for the complex instruction does not align with its
      simpler parts, which would violate the **restriction map** property in sheaf theory. Let us look at
784
      a specific example:
785
786   Let Ujump represent the instruction ”jump” and Ujump twice represent the instruction ”jump twice.” The
787   restriction condition requires that the mapping for the complex instruction ”jump twice” reduces to
788   the simpler instruction ”jump”:
789                                     resUjump twice ,Ujump (sjump twice ) = sjump .
790
791
      A failure occurs when:
                                       resUjump twice ,Ujump (sjump twice ) ̸= sjump ,
792
793   indicating that the model fails to restrict the mapping for the complex instruction correctly to the
794
      simpler one. For any instruction α composed of subinstructions β and γ, the restriction conditions
      require:
795
                               resUα ,Uβ (sα ) = sβ , and resUα ,Uγ (sα ) = sγ .
796
797   A violation occurs when:
798                               resUα ,Uβ (sα ) ̸= sβ      or    resUα ,Uγ (sα ) ̸= sγ .
799
      This shows that the model’s understanding of the complex instruction α does not correctly restrict
800
      to its components β or γ, violating the sheaf’s restriction requirement. Thus, the SCAN task tests
801
      the restriction map property in sheaf theory.

      A.2    A NTAILS FOR G LUING C ONDITION
804
805   The gluing condition ensures that if sections over different open sets agree on their overlaps, they can
806   be combined to form a global section over the union of those sets. In the context of LLMs, under-
807   standing how well the model glues together local information to form a correct global interpretation
808   is crucial. The Antails task naturally emerges as an ideal test for this, as it evaluates whether the
809   model can combine information from local contexts (substituting a noun with an adjective-noun
      combination) into a global sentence-level entailment. For a given sentence with a noun (N) like


                                                             15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      “The runner set a record”, we substitute N with an adjective – noun combination like “The runner
811   set a new record” and test the model to see whether it can understand the entailment pattern. The
812   model here has to maintain it’s understanding of entailment patterns with adjective substitution.
813
814
      It tests whether a model can identify violations of the gluing condition by evaluating its ability to
      combine local modifications in a sentence into a globally consistent interpretation. Specifically, the
815
      task examines whether the model can recognize whether the entailment patterns between a sentence
816
      and its modified version remain consistent after a substitution.
817
818   Let X be a topological space representing the set of all sentences. Consider two open sets U1 ⊂ X
819   and U2 ⊂ X corresponding to two different forms of the same sentence: - U1 contains the original
820
      sentence with a noun N , - U2 contains the sentence with an adjective-noun compound CA replacing
      N.
821
822   Let:
823                                    A ∈ F (U1 ) and       CA ∈ F (U2 )
824   represent the sections (data) corresponding to the original sentence A and the modified sentence
825   CA, respectively.
826
827
      The gluing condition requires that if the sections A and CA agree on the overlap U1 ∩ U2 , i.e.,
828                                     s1 (A)|U1 ∩U2 = s2 (CA)|U1 ∩U2 ,
829
830   then there exists a global section s ∈ F (U1 ∪ U2 ) such that:
831                                  s|U1 = s1 (A) and s|U2 = s2 (CA).
832
833   The task examines whether the model can combine the local information from A and CA into a
834   globally consistent interpretation. Specifically, the model is tasked with determining whether the
835   global entailment pattern is preserved after the substitution of N with CA.
836
      For example: Let A correspond to the sentence:A : The runner set a record. and let CA correspond
837
      to the sentence: CA : The runner set a new record. The model must determine whether the global
838
      entailment of A and CA remains consistent. If the model can correctly identify that the entailment
839   patterns agree, it satisfies the gluing condition. Otherwise, a failure to recognize the correct global
840   entailment pattern indicates a violation of the gluing condition.
841
842
      Mathematically, if the model fails to glue the local information, we observe:
843                                     s1 (A)|U1 ∩U2 ̸= s2 (CA)|U1 ∩U2 ,
844
845   which implies that:
846                  s ∈ F (U1 ∪ U2 ) such that s|U1 = s1 (A) and           s|U2 = s2 (CA).
847
848
      Thus, the task serves as a direct test of the gluing condition, by evaluating whether the model can
849   combine local changes (substituting N with CA) into a coherent global interpretation of the sen-
850   tence’s entailment pattern.

      A.3    C OMPCOMB FOR L OCALITY C ONDITION
853
854   In sheaf theory, the locality condition ensures that if local sections (data) agree on overlapping
855   regions, they must arise from the same global section. The Compcomb Dataset is designed to
856   test whether a model can distinguish between semantically related pairs (coat and trenchcoat) and
857   unrelated pairs (coat and turncoat) , ensuring that the model does not overgeneralize by incorrectly
858   equating unrelated elements. This naturally aligns with the locality condition, as the task tests
859   whether the model can correctly handle cases where local sections should differ based on semantic
860
      distinctions.
861   Let U ⊆ X be a topological space, and let F be a sheaf on U , assigning sections si ∈ F (Ui ) to open
862   sets Ui ⊂ U . Consider a task where we are given a triple (a, b, c), where a and b are semantically
863   related, but a and c are not. sab ∈ F (U1 ) captures the semantic relationship between a and b, while
      sac ∈ F (U2 ) captures the semantic relationship between a and c, where U1 ∩ U2 ̸= ∅.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      The locality condition requires that if sections agree on overlaps, they come from the same global
865   section:
866                                          sab |U1 ∩U2 = sac |U1 ∩U2 .
867
      However, since a and c are not semantically related, the sections sab and sac should differ on U1 ∩U2 .
868
869   If the model fails to distinguish between sab and sac , this results in:
870
                                       sab |U1 ∩U2 = sac |U1 ∩U2    (incorrect),
871
872   which violates the locality condition, implying:
873
874
                     sab = sac    (contradictory, as a and b are related, but a and c are not).
875
876
      The Compcomb dataset is designed to evaluate whether models can respect the locality condition by
      avoiding overgeneralization. For each data point, we define a noun a (e.g., ”coat”), an adjective-noun
877
      combination b that is semantically related to a (e.g., ”trenchcoat”), and an exocentric compound c
878
      that contains a but is semantically unrelated (e.g., ”turncoat”). Let sab ∈ F (U1 ) represent the
879   section capturing the semantic relationship between a and b, and let sac ∈ F (U2 ) represent the
880   section capturing the relationship between a and c, where U1 ∩ U2 ̸= ∅. The model should be able
881   to distinguish between these sections, satisfying:
882
883
                                                     sab ̸= sac .
884   The model is tested on whether it can differentiate between these semantically related and unrelated
885   pairs. A model failure occurs if it incorrectly generalizes the relationship between a and c based on
886   surface forms, treating it as semantically similar to the relationship between a and b. This can be
887   formalized as:
888                                          sab |U1 ∩U2 = sac |U1 ∩U2 .
889   Such an equation would imply that the model overgeneralizes by equating the unrelated pair (a, c)
890   with the related pair (a, b), thereby violating the locality condition. The correct behavior, respecting
891   the locality condition, requires:
892                                           sab |U1 ∩U2 ̸= sac |U1 ∩U2 .
893   Thus, the failure to distinguish between (a, b) and (a, c) constitutes a violation of the locality condi-
894   tion, where the model wrongly generalizes the semantic relation between unrelated elements based
895   on surface similarity.

897   A.4    PLANE FOR NATURAL T RANSFORMATIONS
898
899   In sheaf theory, a natural transformation between two sheaves ensures that mappings between ob-
900   jects are consistent across different spaces, respecting the relationships between the mappings. The
901   PLANE dataset tests this ability by requiring the model to combine mappings for adjective – noun
902   (AN – Noun) and adjective – hypernym (AN – Hypernym) pairs into a consistent, global mapping
903   for AN – AH (adjective – hypernym combinations). If the model fails to maintain the consistency
904
      required for a natural transformation, it indicates an inability to generalize the relationships between
      these mappings, which the PLANE dataset is specifically designed to detect.
905
906   The PLANE Dataset evaluates whether models can construct the correct natural transformation when
907   combining adjective – noun (AN) entailments with their hypernyms. Specifically: ϕA,B corresponds
908   to the entailment mapping for the AN –Noun combination, while ϕA,C corresponds to the entailment
909   mapping for the AN – Hypernym combination. The task is to find ϕA,BC , which corresponds to
910
      the combined entailment mapping for the AN – Hypernym combination (AN – AH). For example,
      AN phrases containing intersective (I) adjectives (e.g., red, dead, and Finnish) describe a subset
911
      of entities subsumed by the noun itself and also a subset of entities which all have that adjective
912
      as a property. For example, a red car is both a car and a red thing. Thus, AN phrases containing
913   intersective adjectives satisfy all forms of inference types (IT):
914
915         red car |= car   (IT 1),    red car |= vehicle (IT 2),       red car |= red vehicle (IT 3).
916
917   Subsective adjectives (small, intelligent, strong etc) only satisfy IT1 and IT2 while intensional ad-
      jectives (fake, former, possible etc) only satisfy IT3.


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      The dataset requires the model to: 1. Understand the relationship between ϕA,B (AN – Noun) and
919   ϕA,C (AN – Hypernym). 2. Combine these two mappings systematically to form ϕA,BC (AN –
920   AH), which must respect both the AN – N and AN – H mappings.
921
922
      If the model fails to construct ϕA,BC correctly, it demonstrates that the model cannot construct a nat-
      ural transformation between these entailments. The dataset requires that the commutative diagram
923
      holds:

938   A failure occurs when:

948   entailment pattern from the AN – Noun and AN – Hypernym combinations to the AN – AH combi-

      nation.
950   It is ideal for testing the model’s ability to construct natural transformations. It requires the model to
951   combine multiple mappings (AN – N and AN – H entailments) and ensure consistency when moving
952   to the combined entailment pattern (AN – AH). If the model cannot ensure the commutative diagram
953   holds or fails to combine the mappings, it indicates a failure in learning the natural transformation
954   between these entailment patterns.
955   The Plane dataset was created by Bertolini et al. (2022) to test compositionality in language models
956   and inference with phrase-level adjective-noun entailment. There are three different adjective classes
957   in this dataset: intersective (I), subsective (S), and intensional (O).
958
      The intersective adjectives (I) describe entities that can be categorized both by the noun and the
959   adjective. For example, a ”red car” is both a car and a red object. This satisfies all forms
960   of inference. For example, Redcar |= car and Redcar |= vehicle (hypernym of ”car”) and
961   Redcar |= redvehicle.
962
      The subsective adjectives (S) describe entities that are part of the noun’s category but do not neces-
963
      sarily share the property of the adjective. For example, a ”small elephant” is an elephant but not nec-
964
      essarily a small entity in general. (e.g., smallelephant |= elephant; smallelephant |= animal),
965   but not (smallelephant ̸|= smallanimal).
966
967   The intensional adjectives (O) negate core properties of the noun. For example, a ”fake gun” is
968
      not a real gun, so the first two types of inferences do not hold (f akegun ̸|= gun; f akegun ̸|=
      weapon). However, the third inference holds (e.g., f akeGlock |= f akegun |= f akeweapon), as
969
      the modification leads to a new subset of entities described by the hypernym of the noun.
