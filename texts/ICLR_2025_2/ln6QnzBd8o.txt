011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Many combinatorial optimization problems (COPs) in routing, scheduling, and
014            assignment involve parameters such as price or travel time that must be predicted
015            from data; so-called predict-then-optimize (PtO) problems. Decision-focused
016            learning (DFL) is a family of successful end-to-end techniques for PtO that trains
017            machine learning models to minimize the error of the downstream optimization
018
               problems. This requires solving the COP for each training instance with the pre-
               dicted parameters and computing the derivative of the solution with respect to the
019
               predicted parameters—tasks that become computationally prohibitive for large
020
               COPs. When the COP is an integer linear program (ILP), a recent work, DYS-Net,
021            applies Davis-Yin splitting (DYS) to solve and differentiate through quadratically
022            regularized ILP. While this fully neural approach significantly accelerates training,
023            it has only been evaluated on datasets where true cost parameters are unobserved,
024            limiting its comparability to state-of-the-art techniques. In this work, we experi-
025            mentally demonstrate that minimizing empirical regret using DYS-Net results in
026            suboptimal regret on test data compared to state-of-the-art DFL methods across
027            three different COPs. We attribute this to the plateau effect: regret remains constant
028            over regions of the parameter space, with sharp changes occurring only at transi-
029
               tion points resulting in low gradient values over much of the space when regret
               is minimized. We illustrate how minimizing a noise contrastive surrogate loss
030
               avoids this problem. Through extensive experiments, we show that minimizing this
031
               surrogate loss allows DYS-Net to achieve test regret levels that are comparable to
032            or lower than the state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, by achieving state-of-the-art
033            regret levels with significantly reduced training times, our approach represents
034            a substantial advance in DFL research, particularly in improving its scalability
035            towards large-scale PtO problems.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Many decision-making problems in real-world can be cast as optimization problems. Some parameters
041
      of these optimization problems are often unknown due to uncertainty or the anticipation of future
042   events. As prediction of these parameters is crucial for making high-quality decisions, leveraging
043   contextual information is important at prediction time. The availability of historical data, combined
044   with the rapid growth of predictive machine learning (ML), has fueled increasing interest in data-
045   driven contextual optimization (Sadana et al., 2025).
046
      When the goal is to predict parameters (such as cost or travel time) of an optimization problem,
047
      such problems can be viewed as “predict-then-optimize”(PtO) problems, including two key steps—
048   the prediction of the unknown parameters and the subsequent optimization using those predicted
049   parameters. Prediction-focused learning is the approach to tackle PtO problems by treating the
050   prediction step independent of the optimization step, based on the assumption that increasing accuracy
051   of predictions would lead to good quality decisions. However, in practice, ML models fail to achieve
052   100% accuracy, and in the presence of prediction errors, such a prediction-focused approach fails
053   to consider how the error in predictions impacts the solution to the optimization problem. This fact
      motivates the research in decision-focused learning (DFL), as surveyed by Mandi et al. (2024).


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      DFL trains ML models to predict the uncertain parameters by directly minimizing the task loss,
055   which reflects the quality of the solutions made using the predicted parameters. Gradient-based DFL
056   entails computing the derivative of the optimization problem’s solution with respect to the predicted
057   parameters. However, for combinatorial optimization problems (COPs), this derivative is almost
058   always zero because slight parameter changes typically do not alter the solution, except at certain
059   transition points where the derivative does not exist.
060
      In this paper, we focus on predicting parameters of COPs, where the predicted parameters appear
061   linearly in the objective function. Previous works in DFL use two broad categories of approaches
062   for such problems: (a) turning the COP into a differentiable mapping by smoothing the optimization
063   to a convex optimization problem (Wilder et al., 2019; Mandi & Guns, 2020), and then minimizing
064   the task loss, and (b) using surrogate loss functions (Elmachtoub & Grigas, 2022; Mulamba et al.,
065   2021; Mandi et al., 2022), for which gradients or subgradients exist. Both approaches require solving
066   the (smoothed) COP for each training instance with the predicted cost parameter and computing
067   the derivative of the COP solution with respect to the predicted parameter. This poses significant
068   scalability challenges, especially for large-scale COPs.
069   To improve the scalability of DFL, McKenzie et al. (2024) recently developed a fast, fully differ-
070   entiable neural optimization layer, DYS-Net, for integer linear programs (ILPs). DYS-Net can be
071   viewed within the first category of DFL approaches, as it incorporates neural smoothing of the LP.
072   As DYS-Net can be implemented entirely as a neural network, it significantly accelerates training.
073   However, McKenzie et al. (2024) consider datasets where true cost parameters are unobserved,
074   whereas in most PtO benchmark problems, as in (Tang & Khalil, 2023), it is assumed that the true
075
      parameters are observed. Consequently, DYS-Net has not been compared to state-of-the-art DFL
      techniques.
076
077   In this work, we consider the task of minimizing the empirical regret of the training set using DYS-Net
078   in datasets, where the true cost parameters are observed. We argue that this would result in low
079   gradient values over much of the space as regret remains constant over regions of the parameter space,
080   with sharp changes occurring only at transition points. To address this, we propose to minimize the
081
      surrogate losses, even though it is possible to minimize regret directly by differentiating through the
      smoothed optimization problem. We justify the advantage of using a surrogate loss by comparing the
082
      pattern of the gradient landscape with respect to regret and the surrogate loss. In this way, this paper
083
      combines the two approaches of DFL. Minimizing the surrogate losses using DYS-Net allows us to
084   accelerate DFL with regret lower than or equal to state-of-the-art DFL techniques.
085
086   In summary, this paper makes the following contributions:
087
             • We show that although ‘smoothing’ makes the optimization problem differentiable, the
088            gradient remains zero over most of the parameter space due to the plateau effect.
089
             • To address the plateau effect which occurs even after smoothing, we combine the two
090
               families of DFL approaches by minimizing a surrogate loss post-smoothing.
091
092
             • We empirically demonstrate that for smoothing approaches, minimizing surrogate losses
               results in lower regret on test data than minimizing the regret.
093
094          • We show that by minimizing the surrogate loss using DYS-Net achieves regret comparable
095            to existing state-of-the-art methods while reducing training time by up to five-fold.

099   In PtO problems, decisions are made by solving COPs. In this work, we focus on COPs with linear
100   objectives and the prediction of objective function parameters. These COPs can be formulated as
101   LPs or integer LPs (ILPs), both of which have extensive practical applications. Any LP can be
102   transformed in the following standard LP form:

106   y ∈ RK . ILPs differ from LPs in that the decision variables v are restricted to integer values. For
107   brevity, we use F to denote the feasible space. So, for the standard LP formulation, F = {v ∈
      RK |Av = b ; v ≥ 0}. Unless it is explicitly stated otherwise, v ⋆ will denote v ⋆ (y).

      To account for uncertainty in the decision-making, PtO problems comprise two steps—the prediction
109   of the unknown parameters and solving the optimization problem using the predicted parameters.
110   We consider PtO formulation, where the vector of cost parameters y is not known prior to solving.
111   Instead, a list of contextual information ϕ, correlated with y is available for predicting y. In PtO
112   problems, an ML model Mω (with trainable parameters ω) is trained to map ϕ → y using past
113   observation pairs {(ϕi , yi )}N
                                    i=1 . Given their success in predictive tasks, neural networks have become
114   the preferred choice for the predictive modeling task in PtO problems.
115   A straightforward approach to the PtO problem is to train Mω to generate accurate parameter
116   predictions ŷ = Mω (ϕ) by minimizing the prediction errors with respect to ground-truth y. Previous
117   works (Wilder et al., 2019; Elmachtoub & Grigas, 2022; Mandi et al., 2020) justify why such a
118   prediction-focused approach produces suboptimal performance. By contrast, in decision-focused
119   learning (DFL), the ML model is directly trained to optimize the task loss, the quality of the resulting
120   decisions. When only the parameters in the objective function are predicted, the task loss of interest is
121   typically regret, which measures the suboptimality of a decision resulting from prediction errors. The
122   regret for making the decisions v under the true realization y can be expressed in the following form:
123
124
                                          Regret(v, y) = y ⊤ v − y ⊤ v ⋆ (y)                               (2)
125
      In PtO problems, one can consider other task losses (Appendix H), such as squared decision errors
126
      (SqDE) between v ⋆ (y) and v ⋆ (ŷ), i.e., SqDE = ||v ⋆ (y) − v ⋆ (ŷ)||2 .

      The DFL approach trains Mω to directly minimize N1 i=1 Regret(v ∗ (Mω (ϕi )), yi ), the empirical
132   risk minimization counterpart of E[Regret(v ∗ (Mω (ϕ)), y)] since the true distribution is unknown.
133   This minimization of regret in gradient descent-based learning requires backpropagation through the
                                                                                                     ⋆
134
      COP, which involves computing the derivative of v ⋆ (ŷ) with respect to ŷ = Mω (ϕ). While dvdŷ(ŷ)
135
      can be computed for convex optimization problems through implicit differentiation (Agrawal et al.,
136   2019; Amos & Kolter, 2017), it is more challenging when the optimization problem is combinatorial.
137   This is because when the parameters of a COP change, the solution either remains unchanged or
138   shifts abruptly, meaning the derivatives are almost always zero and undefined at abrupt changes.
139
      Broadly there are two approaches of implementing DFL for COPs: (a) smoothing the COP to a
140
      smooth convex optimization problem and (b) using a surrogate loss that is differentiable. For a
141
      detailed discussion on how existing DFL techniques tackle this challenge, we refer readers to the
142   survey paper by Mandi et al. (2024).

      3.3   A DDRESSING THE S CALABILITY OF DFL
229
230   Implementing DFL entails a significant computational burden, as it requires solving and differentiating
231   the COP or the ‘smoothed’ COP for each training instance in every epoch using predicted parameters.
232   Mulamba et al. (2021) address the scalability issue by using solution caching instead of repeatedly
233   solving the optimization problem. Tang & Khalil (2024) avoid solving the COP during training using
234   an approach, called CaVE, by minimizing the angle between the predicted cost vector and the ‘normal
235   cone’ of the true optimal solution, While such approaches aim to avoid solving the COP to improve
236   scalability, a faster and more scalable implementation of the optimization problem is another research
237
      direction, which has been receiving increasing attention recently. Research in this area is tangential
      to the learning-to-optimize paradigm (Bengio et al., 2021; Kotary et al., 2021), which trains an ML
238
      model to output COP solutions directly from the parameters.
239
240   In order to find a heuristic solution to a COP using ML, graph neural networks emerge as a key
241   building block (Khalil et al., 2022; Cappart et al., 2023). However, for quadratically regularized
242   LPs, a recent work by McKenzie et al. (2024) demonstrate that a simple feedforward neural network
243
      can be used to obtain a heuristic solution. Their technique, called DYS-Net, is based on a three-
      operator splitting technique (Davis & Yin, 2017) to compute the solution. Next, we will provide a
244
      brief overview of DYS-Net, as we aim to train by minimizing a surrogate loss using DYS-Net for
245
      accelerating DFL.
246
247
      DYS-Net for LPs. The principle of DYS-Net emerges from projected gradient descent (Duchi
248
      et al., 2008). Projected gradient descent differs from standard gradient descent in that after each
249   iteration, the current predictions are projected into the feasible space if they are not in the feasible
250   space. However, projecting into the feasible space of a combinatorial optimization problem is itself
251   an expensive operation. If we consider standard form LPs, the feasible space can be expressed as:
252                                                   .                     .
253
                            F ≡ F1 ∩ F2 where F1 = {Av = b} and F2 = {v ≥ 0}.
254   Although projecting an infeasible solution v directly into F is not a trivial operation, projecting into
255   F1 and F2 separately are much simpler tasks. Projecting into F1 takes the following form:
256                                                .
257                                       PF1 (v) = v − A† (Av − b)
258   where A† is the pseudo inverse of A. Projecting into F2 takes the following form:
259
                                                     .
260                                         PF2 (v) = max{0, v}

      where max operates element-wise. Cristian et al. (2023) propose an iterative approach, continuously
262

265                     vk+1 = vk − PF2 (vk ) + PF1 (2 − αµ)PF2 (vk ) − vk − αy                     (DYS)

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we experiment with the recently proposed DYS-Net, a fast neural solver for LPs. By
529   minimizing Regret or SqDE , DYS-Net cannot attain regret as low as existing DFL techniques, such
530   as SPO and PFY. So, we challenge the conventional DFL approach of directly minimizing empirical
531   regret when a smoothing operation is applied to make the optimization problem differentiable. Instead,
532   we recommend minimizing a surrogate loss, such as LSCE and justify this by comparing the pattern
533   of the gradient landscape concerning regret and the surrogate loss. By doing so, we effectively merge
534   the two families of approaches in DFL. Our experimental evaluations show that for most problems
535   minimizing LSCE using DYS-Net produces regret as low as the state-of-the-art SPO method, with a
536
      clear advantage in runtime up to five-fold.
537   Future work includes applying this approach to real-world large-scale applications with full GPU
538   training. Furthermore, new fully neural smoothing approaches or better surrogate losses can also
539   benefit from this joint approach. While used here for linear objective functions, future work can
      investigate the joint applicability of both smoothing and surrogates for non-linear optimization too.
