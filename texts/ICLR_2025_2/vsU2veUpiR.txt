011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Methods for knowledge editing and unlearning in large language models seek
014            to edit or remove undesirable knowledge or capabilities without compromising
015            general language modeling performance. This work investigates how mechanis-
016
               tic interpretability—which, in part, aims to identify model components (circuits)
               associated to specific interpretable mechanisms that make up a model capability—
017
               can improve the precision and effectiveness of editing and unlearning. We find a
018
               stark difference in unlearning and edit robustness when training components local-
019            ized by different methods. We highlight an important distinction between meth-
020            ods that localize components based primarily on preserving outputs, and those
021            finding high level mechanisms with predictable intermediate states. In particular,
022            localizing edits/unlearning to components associated with the lookup-table mech-
023            anism for factual recall 1) leads to more robust edits/unlearning across different
024            input/output formats, and 2) resists attempts to relearn the unwanted informa-
025            tion, while also reducing unintended side effects compared to baselines, on both a
026            sports facts dataset and the CounterFact dataset across multiple models. We also
027
               find that certain localized edits disrupt the latent knowledge in the model more
               than any other baselines, making unlearning more robust to various attacks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Large language models (LLMs) often learn to encode undesirable knowledge. The possibility of se-
033
      lectively editing or unlearning this type of knowledge is viewed as paramount for ensuring accuracy,
034
      fairness, and control of AI. Yet, editing and unlearning of knowledge from these models remains
035   challenging.
036
037   Common editing and unlearning methods often come at the cost of affecting other general or tangen-
038
      tial knowledge or capabilities within the model. Moreover, the edits achieved through these methods
      may not be robust – e.g., slight variations in the prompt formulation can often still elicit the original
039
      fact or capability, or the original answers are still present/extractable given white-box access.
040
041   Some recent work has explored editing or unlearning techniques that rely on mechanistic inter-
042   pretability methods attempting to trace which components of a network store specific facts (Meng
043   et al., 2023). These methods, such as causal tracing or attribution patching, focus on measuring how
044
      output or task accuracy is affected when clean/corrupted input is patched into specific components.
045   We coin a new term to categorize localizations which measure causal effects of components on only
046   the output: Output-Tracing localizations. The effectiveness of output-tracing (OT) techniques like
047   Causal Tracing for editing has been questioned by Hase et al. (2023). Our research confirms these
048   doubts, finding that localized editing and unlearning of facts based on several existing OT methods
049   often perform equal to or worse than simply updating the entire model. This is particularly evident
050
      when evaluating the robustness of edits against prompt variations and relearning, and when probing
      for remaining latent knowledge.
051
052   Another style of interpretability techniques first breaks down computations into high-level mecha-
053   nisms with predictable intermediate states. Based on such work by Nanda et al. (2023); Geva et al.
      (2023), we link certain MLP layers to a fact lookup (FLU) mechanism for facts used in our analysis,

079   Summary of Contributions
080
081          • We perform a rigorous evaluation of several standard editing approaches on factual recall
082            tasks, and we identify mechanisms for factual lookup and attribute extraction on Gemma-
083            7B, Gemma-2-9B, and Llama-3-8b. We demonstrate that gradient-based editing localized
084            on the factual lookup mechanism is more robust than OT localizations and baselines across
085            multiple datasets, models, and evaluations.
086          • We demonstrate that it is more difficult to elicit the forgotten ground truth answers using
087            alternative prompting with FLU localizations. We also demonstrate slower or no relearning
088            of the ground truth answers, retraining edited models on half of the edited set and evaluating
089            them on the other half of the edited set.
090          • We analyze intermediate representations using probing, and provide further evidence that
091            editing with FLU localization modifies the internal latent information to reflect the desired
092            edited answer more than other localizations and baselines. We also analyze the weights
093            that are modified for each localization, and find that OT techniques and baselines modify
094
               the attribute extraction mechanisms more than the fact lookup mechanism.
095          • We show that editing and unlearning localized on these mechanisms is more parameter
096            efficient, by controlling for the sizes of edits made to the model with weight masking.

098   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
099
100   Mechanistic Interpretability is a subfield of AI interpretability, aiming to understand the internal
101   processes of AI models by attributing them to subnetworks (called circuits) within the model (Olah
102   et al., 2020). We focus on the factual recall interpretability literature (Nanda et al., 2023; Geva et al.,
103   2023; Chughtai et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023), which studies methods that aim to discover mechanisms
104
      for the retrieval and formatted extraction of factual information.
105   Output tracing methods aim to automatically find causally important subnetworks of components
106   for a task. Causal Tracing (Meng et al., 2023) and Automated circuit discovery (ACDC) (Conmy
107   et al., 2023) utilize repeated activation patching to attempt to find the subnetworks that are most crit-
      ical for the model’s output on that task. Efficient methods such as attribution patching (Nanda, 2023)


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and edge attribution patching (Syed et al., 2023) are linear approximations of activation patching for
109   discovering important components quickly.
110
      Fact Editing and Machine Unlearning seek to modify pre-trained models to eliminate or alter
111
      learned knowledge such as capabilities or facts. Some prior approaches focus on identifying and
112   removing specific individual training data points, aiming to obtain a model that is “similar” to one
113   that had never trained on these data points (Cao & Yang, 2015; Xu et al., 2023). One formalization
114   of unlearning to match a retrained-from-scratch model is due to Ginart et al. (2019), and is closely
115   inspired by differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2014).
116
      Fact editing focuses on overwriting factual information while preserving overall language generation
117
      ability. Meng et al. (2023) attempts to identify MLP modules that are most responsible for factual
118   predictions via Causal Tracing and then applies a rank-one transformation upon these modules to
119   replace factual associations.
120
121
      In the context of LLMs and safety, techniques such as Helpful-Harmless RLHF (Bai et al., 2022) and
      Representation Misdirection for Unlearning (Li et al., 2024b) aim to suppress dangerous knowledge
122
      or harmful tendencies in LLMs. Li et al. (2024a); Zou et al. (2023; 2024) approach unlearning
123
      and dangerous knowledge suppression from a top-down feature view, reading or suppressing linear
124   features related to memorized, harmful, and undesired concepts. A related line of work on safety
125   proposes methods making it difficult to modify open models for use on harmful domains (Tamirisa
126   et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024; Henderson et al., 2023), including through adversarial relearning.
127
128   Failures of Unlearning and Editing have been shown for both localized and nonlocalized meth-
129   ods. Patil et al. (2023) extract correct answers to edited facts from the intermediate residual stream
130   and through prompt rephrasing. Yong et al. (2024) show that low-resource languages jailbreak
131   models output unsafe content, and Lo et al. (2024); Lermen et al. (2023); Deeb & Roger (2024)
132   demonstrate that relearning with a small amount of compute/data causes models to regain unde-
133   sirable knowledge/tendencies. Even without explicit finetuning, Xhonneux et al. (2024) show that
134
      in-context learning alone suffices to reintroduce undesirable knowledge despite the model being
      designed to refuse to output such knowledge. Lee et al. (2024) shows that even after alignment
135
      techniques are applied to make models nontoxic, toxicity representations are still present, just not
136
      triggered - they argue that this is a reason that models lack robustness and can still be jailbroken to
137   trigger this unwanted behavior.
138
139   Hong et al. (2024) evaluates unlearning by measuring residual knowledge left in internal activations,
140
      and demonstrate that current approaches fail to remove this residual knowledge and thus can be
      exploited. They attempt unlearning by targeting the MLPs these residual knowledge traces reside
141
      in, but fail to find a non-oracle unlearning approach that successfully removes residual information.

144   2     M ETHODS
145
146   Our experiments are designed to test the effectiveness of localization for editing of facts. In this
147   section we describe the tasks used and the localization and editing methods evaluated.
148
149   2.1   E DITING TASKS
150
151
      We focus on editing subsets of two datasets: (1) Sports Facts dataset from Nanda et al. (2023),
      which contains subject-sport relations across three sports categories for 1567 athletes, and (2) the
152
      CounterFact dataset from Meng et al. (2023).
153
154
      Sports Facts: Sports-Athlete-Editing, Full-Sports-Editing, and Sports-Unlearning tasks In
155
      the Sports Facts dataset, we edit two general groups of factual associations. For the first editing task,
156   we edit factual associations for a constant set of randomly selected athletes belonging to any of the
157   three sport categories. We test editing these sets of associations by replacing their correct sports with
158   one of the other two incorrect sports (with equal probability). To increase the comprehensiveness
159   of our evaluation, we run experiments with different forget set sizes: 16 athletes and 64 athletes.
160   We refer to this task as Sports-Athlete-Editing. These chosen forget sets are constant between all
161   localizations. For the second editing task, we unlearn all athlete-sport associations for athletes who
      play one sport. In this case, we establish a forget set consisting of all the athletes who play one sport


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (basketball, baseball, or football), and we edit the association by replacing the athlete’s correct sport
163   with golf. For comprehensiveness, we vary the sport that the forget set is constructed from. We refer
164   to this task as Full-Sports-Editing. Finally, we also design an unlearning task, Sports-Unlearning,
165   where the goal is to unlearn factual associations for all athletes in one of the sports.
166
167   CounterFact-Editing and Sequential-CounterFact-Editing task In the CounterFact dataset,
168   following Geva et al. (2023), we first filter the dataset for facts which our models assign higher
169   than 50% probability to the right answer, which varies per model. The goal of our CounterFact-
170   Editing task is to edit a constant set of facts, replacing the correct answers with an alternative false
171   target, with the retain set being the rest of the non-forget facts. We vary forget set sizes to be of 16
172
      and 64 facts. In Sequential-CounterFact-Editing task, we edit a total of 64 facts by sequentially
      editing four randomly selected subsets of 16 facts. We test sequential editing here because facts from
173
      CounterFact can reside in different parts of the model, so we wish to test if we can exploit different
174
      localizations for different facts. These chosen forget sets are constant between all localizations.
175
176
      Models We implement editing on the Gemma-7B LLM, the Gemma-2-9B LLM, and the Llama-
177   3-8b LLM. We don’t use the Pythia-2.8B (Biderman et al., 2023) and GPT-2 models tested in the
178   previous fact interpretability literature because our larger models have stronger general capabilities
179   which we can measure for side effects, and also because our larger models can provide factual
180   knowledge in more input/output formats for more robustness evaluations.
181
182   2.2   L OCALIZATION M ETHODS AND BASELINES
183
184   Given a model M : X 7→ L mapping sequence of tokens X to logits L ∈ R|V | over vocabulary V ,
185   we consider M to be a directed acyclic graph (C, E) with C being a set of model components and
186
      E being edges between components. Adopting notation from Elhage et al. (2021), we consider the
      query, key, value, and output weights of each head along with the input, gate, and output projection
187
      weights of each MLP as components.
188
189   We are interested in finding S : C → − R, a mapping of components to their importance in a given
190   task. A localization is a set of components Cτ := {c : c ∈ C, |S(c)| > τ }, where τ is a threshold.
191   In practice, we fix τ such that Cτ contains the same number of parameters in OT, FLU, and random
192
      localizations. We use these efficient localization methods for finding these mappings:
193
      Output Tracing (OT) localization: Causal Tracing and Attribution Patching First, we test
194
      Causal Tracing, a method for finding components with high direct causal importance for factual
195
      associations (Meng et al., 2023). We also use Attribution Patching (Nanda, 2023) as a fast and
196   acceptably accurate approximation of causal tracing to automatically localize over components with
197   high direct and indirect importance. We additionally consider the versions of these localizations
198   with only MLPs (Causal Tracing MLPs and Attribution Patching MLPs).
199
      We hypothesize that these output-based techniques will prioritize the shared extraction components
200
      and other mechanisms for reformatting predictions over the more diffuse FLU components, and thus
201
      appear more precise yet leave the underlying latent information present in the model. This might de-
202   crease robustness under alternative extraction methods, thus motivating non-OT-based localization,
203   described next. We discuss the precise components/mechanisms highlighted by OT localizations in
204   Appendix A.3.
205
206   Fact Lookup (FLU) localization: Next, we use manually derived localizations for MLP layers.
207   For Sports Facts, our localization is inspired by Nanda et al. (2023), who discovered components in
208   Pythia 2.8B responsible for token concatenation, fact lookup, and attribute extraction. They, along
209   with Geva et al. (2023), find that the fact-lookup stage enriches the latent stream with information
210   about the subject (athlete) at the subject’s token position, and the attribute extraction stage extracts
211   the latent sport information and formats it in the final token position. We replicate a key result of
212
      their work in our three models by using a linear probing technique and attention ablations to discover
      which MLPs are sufficient for developing accurate representations of which of the three sports each
213
      athlete plays.
214
215   For CounterFact, we replicate findings from Geva et al. (2023) and do further causal analyses to
      identify particular MLPs. We first use path patching from Goldowsky-Dill et al. (2023) to isolate


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      causally important attribute extraction mechanisms, both attention heads and MLPs, in later layers
217   of the model. Then, we use the same technique to patch between MLPs and the attribution extraction
218   mechanism to find which MLPs enrich the latent stream with information to be extracted. We pick
219   the MLPs that have the largest effect on the logit difference as mediated through this extraction
220   mechanism. More details about the manual analysis for both datasets is outlined in Appendix A.2.1.
221
      Importantly, FLU differs from OT techniques because we consider the causal effects of ablations
222
      upon intermediate representations used by the factual recall mechanism, not just the effects on the
223   output. We hypothesize that the optimal location for robust editing is in the fact lookup stage rather
224   than in the attribute extraction stage, because adversaries can develop alternative methods for ex-
225   tracting knowledge from the latent stream through alternative prompts or white-box methods so we
226   want to prevent the knowledge from ever being added to the latent stream. Thus, we exclusively
227   modify the fact lookup MLPs.
228
229   Baselines: Random-MLPs, Random, All-MLPs, and Nonlocalized We additionally consider
230   four baselines: one corresponding to Cτ = C (i.e., no localization, optimizing all the components
231   of the model), another that randomly chooses components, another that trains all MLP components,
232   and another trains a random selection of MLP components. We test the last MLP baselines to deter-
233   mine if our mechanistically localized MLPs are uniquely important - we want to know if the same
234
      unlearning performance can be achieved with just the heuristic that training only MLPs improves
      robustness, or if mechanistic understanding of the role of the component is crucial.
235
236   In Appendix A.5, we analyze the proportions of each mechanism (the extraction heads, extraction
237   MLPs, and fact lookup MLPs, by parameter count) that are present in each localization.
238   The main text focuses on comparing FLU, Causal Tracing, and Nonlocalized, while the appendix
239   has the same figures with all above localizations included, with the same conclusions in every case.
240

      2.3   PARAMETER U PDATE M ETHOD
242
243   Once we have a localization Cτ , we run one of the unlearning or editing methods, restricting weight
244   updates to only components in Cτ . We update weights using gradient descent on a combination of
245   loss functions.
246
247   Localized Fine-Tuning Following work by Lee et al. (2023) and Panigrahi et al. (2023), we fine-
248   tune the parameters within the localized components. For editing, we use a loss function L =
249   λ1 Linjection +λ2 Lretain +λ3 LSFT , where Linjection is a cross-entropy loss on the forget facts maximizing
250   the probability of the alternative new false target. Lretain is a cross-entropy loss on a train split of the
251   remaining facts, and LSFT is a cross-entropy loss on the Pile dataset (Gao et al., 2020). We sweep
252
      over learning rates and injection loss λs for three representative localizations in Appendix A.6 .

520   4   D ISCUSSION
521
522   Recent work by Hase et al. (2023) argued that localization is not useful for model editing. Our
523   findings demonstrate that the relationship between localization and fact editing/unlearning is more
524   nuanced, and reveals that not all localization techniques are equal.
525   Our work evaluates the efficacy of different localization methods for modifying factual associations.
526   We demonstrate clear benefits of localization for editing robustness through localized fine-tuning
527   combined with FLU interpretability.
528
      In Sections 3.3 and 3.4 and Appendix A.5, we provide evidence that OT and baseline approaches
529
      fail to be robust because they target easily-localizable and high direct logit importance extraction
530   components that transform existing latent factual knowledge to the desired output format. This can
531   fail to generalize to different input and output formats and does not target the source of knowledge in
532   the model: other input/output formats can allow alternative mechanisms to extract this knowledge,
533   and relearning can repair the original mechanisms to recover accuracy. In contrast, FLU mechanistic
534   understanding allows us to target editing at the sites where knowledge is sourced, which robustly
535   prevents that information from entering the latent stream in any format.
536   Our work also suggests unlearning/editing as a potential testbed for different interpretability meth-
537   ods, which might sidestep the inherent lack of ground truth in interpretability (Templeton et al.,
538   2024). We hope our work provides a framework for evaluating localizations and explanations.
