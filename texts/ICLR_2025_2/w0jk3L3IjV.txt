008                                                A BSTRACT
009
               The detection and generalization of out-of-distribution (OOD) data are critical in
010
               numerous real-world applications. While OOD detection enhances model reliabil-
011            ity against outliers, generalization enables adaptability to unforeseen variations.
012            Despite their importance, the intrinsic relationship between OOD detection and
013            generalization remains underexplored, posing risks in practical deployments. This
014            study identifies the Detection-Generalization Paradox on OOD data, where optimiz-
015            ing one objective can degrade the other. We investigate this paradox by analyzing
016            the behaviors of models trained under different paradigms, focusing on represen-
017            tation, logits, and loss across in-distribution, covariate-shift, and semantic-shift
018            data. Based on our findings, we propose Distribution-Robust Sharpness-Aware
019
               Minimization (DR-SAM), an optimization framework that balances OOD detection
               and generalization. DR-SAM employs both in-distribution and semantic-shift data
020
               during training, using data augmentation on in-distribution data to simulate poten-
021
               tial covariate-shift scenarios and computing perturbations on model parameters to
022            enhance OOD generalization. By determining the worst-case gradient direction,
023            the model’s decision boundary is adjusted to better encompass covariate-shift sam-
024            ples. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that DR-SAM improves the detection of
025            semantic-shift samples and enhances generalization for covariate-shift samples.

      4.1   C OMPARING DR-SAM WITH THE MOST- RELATED WORKS
331
332   Several previous works also study the model’s detection-generalization problem. The pioneer
333   work SCONE (Bai et al., 2023) proposes to enhance the model’s OOD-D and OOD-G ability
334   simultaneously when training the model with wild data. In contrast, DR-SAM follows the common
335   practice in OOD-D. Unlike SCONE, DR-SAM employs training distribution containing only labeled
336   in-distribution samples and unlabeled auxiliary outliers. In addition, DR-SAM focuses on alleviating
337   the generalization trade-off due to the employment of auxiliary outliers. Whereas SCONE (Bai et al.,
338   2023) pays more attention to handling samples from mixing types of distributions. Averly & Chao
339   (2023) proposes a novel evaluation framework that focuses on detecting and rejecting samples beyond
340
      the model’s classification capability, which aims to evaluate the existing model’s robustness with
      samples from mixing distribution types. Conversely, DR-SAM simultaneously enhances the model’s
341
      generalization and detection ability.
342
343   The most related work DUL (Zhang et al., 2024) propose a novel and theoretical guarantee opti-
344   mization framework to enhance the model’s OOD-D ability while maintaining the original OOD-G
345   capability. DUL incorporates distributional uncertainty in the Bayesian framework to bridge the
346
      detection and generalization learning target. Specifically, DUL encourages the exposed outlier to
      have high uncertainty while maintaining a non-increased overall uncertainty to ensure generalization
347
      capability. Instead of focusing on decoupling the uncertainty under the Bayesian framework, we
348
      propose DR-SAM to minimize the model’s loss of sharpness under the framework of SAM. We
349   provide a detailed discussion in Appendix G.3.

524   achieves higher sharpness than SAM and DR-SAM, but it does not explicitly optimize the loss’s
525   sharpness. DR-SAM, on the other hand, achieves lower sharpness within the specific radii but high at
526   the outer region. This allows the model to be robust to the covariate-shifted samples while sensitive
527   to the semantic-shifted ones.
528
529   6         C ONCLUSION
530   In this paper, we identify the Detection-Generalization Paradox on out-of-distribution (OOD) data,
531   where optimizing for detection can degrade generalization and vice versa. We analyze this paradox by
532   examining models trained under various paradigms, focusing on representation, logits, and loss across
533   different data shifts. To address this, we propose Distribution-Robust Sharpness-Aware Minimization
534   (DR-SAM), an optimization framework that balances OOD detection and generalization. DR-SAM
535   uses data augmentation and perturbations on model parameters to simulate covariate-shift scenarios
536   and enhance OOD generalization. Empirical results show that DR-SAM improves the detection of
537
      semantic-shift samples and boosts generalization for covariate-shift samples.

1046
       CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) is one of the widely used color image datasets in machine
1047   learning, containing real objects in the real world. It has a total of 10 classes which are airplane,
1048   automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, and truck. There are 6000 images for each category,
1049   of which 5000 images are used for training and 1000 images are used for testing.
1050
       CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) is similar to the CIFAR-10 data in that it has color images
1051
       of real objects. It contains a total of 100 categories, divided into 20 superclasses. Each class has 600
1052
       images, including 500 for training and 100 for testing.
1053
1054   CIFAR-10/100-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) is obtained by corrupting the original CIFAR test
1055   set. It has applied a total of 15 corruptions which are Gaussian Noise, Shot Noise, Impulse Noise,
1056
       Defocus Blur, Frosted Glass Blur, Motion Blur, Zoom Blur, Snow, Frost, Fog, Brightness, Contrast,
       Elastic, Pixelate, JPEG.
1057
1058   Place365 (Zhou et al., 2018) is a scene recognition dataset with a total of 434 scene categories. There
1059   are two versions of the dataset which are Places365-Standard and Places365-Challenge-2016. The
1060   Place365 has a total of 10 million images, with between 5,000 and 30,000 training images per class.
1061   MNIST (Deng, 2012) is a dataset of handwritten number images with 10 classes, each representing
1062   a number between 0 and 9. The MNIST dataset has a total of 70,000, 28×28 greyscale images, of
1063   which 60,000 are in the training set and 10,000 are in the test set.
1064
       SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011) is a image datasets with real-world numbers. The numbers in it are
1065
       captured from various scenes, such as door numbers and historical buildings. It divided into 10
1066   categories and each number in the image is belonged to one class. It contains 73257 digits for training
1067   and 26032 digits for testing.
1068
1069
       Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014) a real world surface texture dataset. The images are collected from
       wood, blankets, cloth, leather, etc. There are 64 categories and a total of 8674 images. The Texture is
1070
       mainly used to evaluate the capabilities of the model or as a pre-training dataset.
1071
1072   TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015) contains 100000, 64×64 coloured images with 200 classes. Each
1073   class has 500 training images, 50 validation images, and 50 test images.
1074   TIN-597 (Zhang et al., 2023b) obtained from ImageNet-1K that is not overlapped with TIN dataset.
1075   It has 597 classes and is cleared of CIFAR-10/100 related categories.
1076
       ImageNet-200 is the subset from the ImageNet-1K that has same 200 classes as ImageNet-R. In
1077
       comparison to ImageNet-1K, it contains identical OOD datasets.
1078
1079   SSB-hard (Vaze et al., 2021) is the hard split of SSB dataset which has 980 classes and contains 49K
       images. It used to explore semantic shift tasks and obtained based on fine-grained datasets.
