                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Image quality assessment (IQA) serves as the golden standard for all models’
012
               performance in nearly all computer vision fields. However, it still suffers from poor
013            out-of-distribution generalization ability and expensive training costs. To address
014            these problems, we propose Dog-IQA, a stanDard-guided zero-shot mix-grained
015            IQA method, which is training-free and utilizes the exceptional prior knowledge
016            of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). To obtain accurate IQA scores,
017            namely scores consistent with humans, we design an MLLM-based inference
018            pipeline that imitates human experts. In detail, Dog-IQA applies two techniques.
019            First, Dog-IQA objectively scores with specific standards that utilize MLLM’s
020            behavior pattern and minimize the influence of subjective factors. Second, Dog-
021
               IQA comprehensively takes local semantic objects and the whole image as input
               and aggregates their scores, leveraging local and global information. Our proposed
022
               Dog-IQA achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance compared with training-
023
               free methods, and competitive performance compared with training-based methods
024            in cross-dataset scenarios. Our code will be released soon.

074   Our approach is inspired by the human evaluators’ scoring process and the MLLMs’ behavior
075   pattern (Yin et al., 2023). Thus, we design an inference pipeline mimicking human image scoring
076   which is shown in Figure 2. Our key designs stem from the following observations. First, when
077   human evaluators score images, they are typically provided with a clear standard for each quality
078   level (Wu et al., 2024b). Without such a standard, discrepancies arise—for example, one person
079   may interpret a score of 60 as merely passing, while another views 50 as average. By providing a
080   consistent scoring standard, evaluators are more likely to agree on quality assessments. Second, when
081
      humans assess image quality, they consider both global and local quality (Navon, 1977; Gerlach &

082

083

084   scores, such as 86.5, which would require generating multiple tokens.

086   Building on these observations, we propose two novel techniques. First, we develop a standard-
087
      guided scoring system that aims to establish a clear mapping between quality levels and scores and
      restrict the MLLM to scoring within a predefined range {1, 2, . . . , K}. The mapping and restriction
088
      ensure the model’s understanding of the quality scale. Second, we utilize segmentation models to
089

090   using an area-weighted average approach. Our key contributions can be summarized as follows:
091

093            any task-specific training or fine-tuning. Dog-IQA fully leverages the inherent capabilities of
094            pre-trained MLLM and segmentation model to provide accurate IQA scores. Our Dog-IQA
095            serves as a new paradigm for training-free approaches in IQA tasks.
096          • We design two key mechanisms to enhance IQA performance. The standard-guided scoring
097            mechanism ensures consistent and objective quality evaluation by aligning scores with
098            predefined standards. The mix-grained aggregation mechanism refines the final quality score
099            by aggregating global and object-centered sub-image quality scores.
100          • We conduct extensive experiments and compare Dog-IQA against SOTA IQA methods
101            across multiple datasets. The main experiments show that our proposed Dog-IQA achieves
102            SOTA performance compared with training-free methods, and competitive performance
103            compared with training-based methods in cross-dataset scenarios.

148   3 M ETHODOLOGY
149   We provide a comprehensive explanation of our proposed Dog-IQA method. The overall pipeline of
150   our proposed Dog-IQA is shown in Figure 3. The image to be assessed is segmented into multiple
151
      sub-images with the segmentation process pipeline. Given a detailed standard, the MLLM rates the
      whole image and sub-images with scores in {1, 2, . . . , 7}. These scores will be finally aggregated
152
      to form the final number. Specifically, we first propose the standard-guided scoring mechanism,
153
      which effectively leverages its prior knowledge and its behavior pattern. Second, we discuss the
154   mix-grained aggregation mechanism, which consists of the process of obtaining suitable sub-images
155   and the aggregation of scores. The rationale behind using sub-images as inputs is also included.
156
157   3.1 S TANDARD - GUIDED S CORING M ECHANISM
158   The ultimate goal of image quality assessment (IQA) is to evaluate images in a manner that closely
159   mirrors human judgment. Thanks to their extensive training data and vast prior knowledge, MLLMs
160   are capable of perceiving images in a way that aligns with human perception (Wu et al., 2023), giving
161   them an inherent advantage for IQA tasks. However, expecting an MLLM to output precise quality
      scores, such as 87.5, is impractical. This is because a score like 87.5 is not represented by a single


                                                                      3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      token, but by four separate tokens: 8, 7, dot, and 5 respectively. Typically, MLLMs can hardly grasp
163   the internal relationship between these tokens, making it difficult for them to associate these values
164   with image quality. These observations and analyses motivate us to insight 1:
165
166
      It is more effective to represent image quality using one single token to achieve an accurate score.
167   Additionally, relying solely on numeric outputs may not be the most optimal approach for two key
168   reasons. First, numbers constitute only a small fraction of the data within the training set compared
169   to textual information. However, using only text is also not feasible, as we still need to extract a
170   quantitative score. Second, human interpretation of numeric scores can vary. For instance, some may
171   consider a score of 60 to be just passing, while others may view 50 as an average score. Therefore,
172
      when human evaluators score image quality, they are often provided with clear standards for each
      level of quality (Wu et al., 2024b). This observation brings us to insight 2:
173
174   A combination of text and numbers is a more effective prompt format for MLLM IQA.
175
      In our proposed method, we integrate these two insights and design the prompt as follows:

      3.2     M IX - GRAINED AGGREGATION M ECHANISM
202
203   The mix-grained aggregation mechanism can be divided into two parts. The first part introduces the
204   segmentation pipeline, while the second part presents the aggregation of multiple scores.
205   Segmentation Process Pipeline. When humans recognize an image, they start from the global
206   structure and gradually dive into the local parts. (Navon, 1977; Förster, 2012; Gerlach & Poirel, 2018)
207   This hierarchical process also applies when assessing image quality. Therefore, under the assumption
208   that MLLMs share a similar perception process, it is essential to deliberately leverage meaningful
209   sub-images. Specifically, ‘meaningful’ means that these sub-images should not be obtained through
210   random cropping but through instance or semantic segmentation techniques.
211   The segmentation model is an excellent choice as it tends to segment the semantic objects out. The
212   object segmented by the segmentation model is padded with zeros around. While this padding
213   has minimal impact on human perception, as humans can easily recognize the black padding as
214   meaningless and mentally disregard it, this is not the case for MLLMs. The visual encoder within
215   the MLLM processes the padding as part of the actual image, leading the model to misinterpret the
      black regions as the real background. This misunderstanding can result in distinct errors, such as the

239   Assessment Score Aggregation. For a given image Xi , after obtaining its global score sglobal ,
240   segmented masks Mi , and their corresponding scores si , we proceed to compute the final predicted
241
      score. A simple approach of averaging s to determine the final score for Xi yields suboptimal
      performance. This is because some blurred objects, although too small to be perceptible to humans,
242
      may be disproportionately penalized by MLLMs. Even worse, these blurred objects account for a
243
      large proportion of most images, leading to an unfairly low score.
244
245   To address this, we propose using a weighted average of the scores, where the area of the correspond-
                                                                                         (i)
246   ing masks determines the weights. Mathematically, this can be expressed as slocal =< si , ai >,
247   where < ·, · > is inner product and ai is the vector representing the areas of the masks in Mi . This
248   approach aligns more closely with human perception, as the dominant object in an image typically
249   occupies the largest region, and its quality represents the image’s quality.
250   Furthermore, it is well-established that image quality can significantly impact the performance of
251   neural networks in tasks such as classification, segmentation, and detection. In the context of mask
252   segmentation, the number of masks in an image can serve as an indicator of image quality. For
253   high-quality, sharp images, the clearer structure enables the model to segment more detailed masks.
254   Based on this observation, we introduce a segmentation score as an additional component of the final
255   score. This segmentation score is defined as sseg = cK/cmax , where c is the number of masks, and
256
      cmax is the maximum number of masks observed across the entire dataset. The normalization by
      K/cmax ensures that sseg remains in the same range as the MLLM scores. Consequently, the final
257                                                                     (i)      (i)        (i)        (i)
258   predicted score in our proposed Dog-IQA framework is given by sDog = (sglobal + slocal )/2 + sseg .

300   4.2   C OMPARISON WITH S TATE - OF - THE -A RT M ETHODS
301   We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the performance of our proposed Dog-IQA model. The
302   comparisons with SOTA methods are divided into two categories: training-free methods, shown in
303   Table 2, and training-based methods, as presented in Table 3.
304
      Training-free methods can be broadly categorized into two types. The first category includes CLIP-
305
      IQA, which leverages the prior knowledge of CLIP and generates scores based on the similarity
306   between text and image embeddings. The second category consists of models such as BIQI, BLINDS-
307   II, BRISQUE, and NIQE, which rely on hand-crafted features. As shown in Table 2, the traditional
308   hand-crafted features often fail to score accurately due to the complex nature of human opinions on
309   image quality. CLIP-IQA benefits from its prior knowledge and demonstrates higher accuracy than
310   hand-crafted feature-based methods. Our Dog-IQA model consistently achieves superior performance
311   across all metrics and datasets, significantly outperforming existing training-free methods.
312   Table 3 summarizes the performance of various training-based methods in cross-dataset evaluations.
313   These experiments test the out-of-distribution generalization ability of the models, which is a crucial
314   aspect of IQA. For these comparisons, we select KonIQ and SPAQ as training sets due to their
315   large size and in-the-wild characteristics. Notably, our Dog-IQA method requires no training or
316   fine-tuning on these datasets, making its strong performance even more remarkable.

407   4.4       A BLATION S TUDY
408   The ablation studies provided in Tables 4, 5, and 6 highlight the significance of various components
409   in our proposed Dog-IQA model. By systematically altering key aspects of the model, the experiment
410   evaluates how each component affects performance on two datasets: SPAQ and AGIQA-3k. We
411   examine components including 1) the standard given to MLLM, 2) the selection of the mask and
412   bounding box, 3) the aggregation of local scores, 4) the effectiveness of sseg , 5) the influence of
413   global and local quality, 6) the number of words, and 7) MLLM selection. The experiment results are
414   shown in Tables 4, 5, and 6. Next, we will analyze the impact of each component in detail.
415   Standard. Standard-guided scoring is a critical aspect of our model. We compare three forms of
416   standards, namely number, word, and sentence. The number-based approach involves asking the
417   MLLM to score image quality directly in the range {1, 2, . . . , K}. The word-based approach adds
418   descriptive adjectives, such as excellent, fair, and bad, to each score. The sentence-based approach
419   assigns a sentence describing quality for each score level, such as 4: Fair! The overall quality of the
420   image is fair. There are certain merits but also some deficiencies.
421   As shown in experiments 1, 2, and 7 in Table 4, the word-based standard yields the best performance
422   as it provides an accurate mapping between number and quality. While sentences offer more detailed
423   context than numbers, they can introduce abstract terms (e.g., some, certain) that may distract the
424   model, resulting in slightly lower performance. Numbers, on the other hand, perform poorly because
425   the MLLM struggles to understand their relationship to image quality without additional context. In
426   conclusion, associating a word with each score effectively enhances the MLLM’s scoring accuracy.
427   Mask and Bounding Box. When scoring sub-images, we test three input formats: masks (semantic
428   object coverings), bounding boxes (enclosing the masks), and the entire image. As shown in
429   experiments 4, 5, and 7 in Table 4, using masks significantly degrades performance. This is mainly
430   because the constant padding applied to masked areas is still interpreted by the MLLM’s visual
431   encoder, negatively influencing the score. Conversely, using the entire image as input provides
      moderate results, though still inferior to bounding boxes. Bounding boxes improve performance

502   5   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
503   In this section, we will discuss the limitations of our proposed Dog-IQA. First, the impressive
504   performance of Dog-IQA can be attributed not only to our novel design but also to the capabilities
505   of the underlying MLLM. Ultimately, it is the MLLM that generates the quality scores, while our
506   design better exploits its extensive prior knowledge. However, as shown in Table 6, the performance
507   of MLLM increases significantly with version updation which will finally promote the development
508   of IQA. Consequently, the selection of MLLM matters and Dog-IQA’s performance may decline
509
      when utilizing MLLMs with poor image understanding ability.
510   Second, as an important part of the overall pipeline, the segmentation process can significantly impact
511   the accuracy of Dog-IQA. If we switch to a segmentation model with subpar performance, mix-
512   grained segmentation may fail, resulting in a direct score for the entire image instead. Additionally, if
513   the segmentation model primarily outputs bounding boxes that lack a clear main object—such as only
514   capturing the edges of an object or half of a human face—this can lead to MLLM’s misjudgment and
515
      inaccurate scores, further degrade our Dog-IQA’s performance. Thus, the choice of segmentation
      model and the segmentation granularity are critical factors influencing Dog-IQA’s performance.
516
517   Third, because the MLLM must evaluate the quality of each mask, the inference speed of Dog-IQA
518   is relatively slow compared to models that require only a single inference. On average, Dog-IQA
519   processes 7.22 masks and the entire image, resulting in 7× longer inference time. After testing on a
520   single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU, our proposed Dog-IQA can segment the whole SPAQ dataset in
521
      50 minutes and score each mask and the total data within 6 hours. This process can be performed
      with data parallel, which means it takes around 1.5 hours to obtain the final result when running on 4
522
      GPUs. While the text embeddings can be pre-calculated and reused, allowing for the omission of the
523
      text encoder, the total inference time remains longer than single forward inference. In conclusion,
524   Dog-IQA may suffer from low processing speed if the segmentation granularity is too fine.
525
526   6   C ONCLUSION
527   In this work, we propose Dog-IQA, a standard-guided zero-shot mix-grained IQA method, which is
528   training-free and utilizes the exceptional prior knowledge of MLLMs. With the combination of SAM2
529   and mPLUG-Owl3, we propose two key mechanisms to enhance IQA performance. The standard-
530   guided scoring mechanism ensures consistent and objective quality evaluation by aligning scores
531   with predefined standards. The mix-grained aggregation mechanism refines the final quality score by
532   aggregating global and object-centered sub-image quality scores. We conduct extensive experiments
533   across a variety of datasets, benchmarking our proposed Dog-IQA against SOTA methods. The
534   results demonstrate that Dog-IQA outperforms all previous training-free approaches and achieves
535
      competitive performance relative to training-based methods, which strongly supports the novelty and
      robustness of our proposed mechanisms. We also systematically conduct ablation studies, which
536
      further confirm the effectiveness of the novel mechanisms. This work highlights the exceptional
537
      image understanding capabilities of MLLMs and confirms the feasibility of attaining remarkable
538   outcomes using solely pre-trained models. Future research will aim to reduce the computational costs
539   associated with multiple inferences and enhance pixel-level quality assessments.
