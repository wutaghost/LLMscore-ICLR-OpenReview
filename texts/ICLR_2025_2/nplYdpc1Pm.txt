                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Research on multi-modal contrastive learning strategies for audio and text has
015
               rapidly gained interest. Contrastively trained Audio-Language Models (ALMs),
016
               such as CLAP, which establish a unified representation across audio and language
017            modalities, have enhanced the efficacy in various subsequent tasks by providing
018            good text aligned audio encoders and vice versa. These improvements are evi-
019            dent in areas like zero-shot audio classification and audio retrieval, among others.
020            However, the ability of these models to understand natural language and temporal
021            relations is still a largely unexplored and open field for research. In this paper,
022            we propose to equip the multi-modal ALMs with temporal understanding without
023            loosing their inherent prior capabilities of audio-language tasks with a temporal
024            instillation method TeminAL. We implement a two-stage training scheme Temi-
025
               nAL A & B, where the model first learns to differentiate between multiple sounds
               in TeminAL A, followed by a phase that instills a sense of time, thereby enhanc-
026
               ing its temporal understanding in TeminAL B. This approach results in an average
027
               performance gain of 5.28% in temporal understanding on the benchmark ESC-50
028            dataset, while the model remains competitive in zero-shot retrieval and classifi-
029            cation tasks on the AudioCap/Clotho datasets. We also note the lack of proper
030            evaluation techniques for contrastive ALMs and propose a strategy for evaluating
031            ALMs in zero-shot settings. The general-purpose Zero-Shot Temporal Evaluation
032            (ZSTE) strategy , is used to evaluate various prior models. ZSTE demonstrates
033            a general strategy to evaluate all ZS contrastive models. The model trained with
034            TeminAL successfully outperforms current models on most downstream tasks.

040

041

042   sourced data (Radford et al., 2021; Liang et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2020). Contrastive learning-based
043   models have demonstrated exceptional adaptability across a range of related tasks, such as image
044   classification (Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020a), natural language processing (Gao et al., 2021) and
045   speech processing (Ravanelli et al., 2020), making them a crucial area of research in multi-modal
046   machine learning. One notable early model in this domain is CLIP, developed by Radford et al.
047   (2021). CLIP learns the relationship between text and images, aligning them in a common latent
048   domain. It stands out as a groundbreaking vision-language model, enabling tasks such as generating
049   images from text (Rombach et al., 2022) and formulating image captions (Mokady et al., 2021).

051   language (Xu et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023;

053   2022; Wu et al., 2023b; Deshmukh et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a). Contrastive models generally ex-

146   2.1   F OUNDATION MODELS AND M ULTI - MODAL TEXT- AUDIO LEARNING
147
148   The expansion of Pretrained Foundation Models (PFMs) now includes auditory (Baevski et al.,
149   2020), visual (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), text-image (Ramesh et al., 2021; Radford et al., 2021), and
150   multi-modal data (Lu et al., 2019; Akbari et al., 2021), driving multi-modal integration. Recent
151   work uses audio-visual contrasts for sound localization (Chen et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022a), cross-
152   modal retrieval (Surı́s et al., 2022), and zero-shot classification (Wu et al., 2022b; Guzhov et al.,
153
      2022). Audio-text models are gaining traction, including those in the DCASE competition for audio
      retrieval with language (Xie et al., 2022), and PFMs have been applied in music tagging (Manco
154
      et al., 2022), environmental sound identification (Zhao et al., 2021; Lou et al., 2022; Mei et al., 2022;
155
      Koepke et al., 2022), and zero-shot tasks (Zhao et al., 2021; Lou et al., 2022; Mei et al., 2022; Koepke
156   et al., 2022; Elizalde et al., 2023). Open-ended models (Kong et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
157   2024; Deshmukh et al., 2023) enable QA capabilities, but our focus is on contrastive learning for
158   audio encoders. The trend is towards integrating language into auditory systems, with applications in
159   text-to-audio (Ghosal et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2023), music generation from text
160   (Agostinelli et al., 2023), and sound source separation (Liu et al., 2023b). Frameworks like CLAP
161   and Compa (Elizalde et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2023) unify auditory-linguistic domains, offering
      strong zero-shot performance in multimodal tasks.

      2.2   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING AND P OST-T RAINING
163
164   Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) has revolutionized machine learning, especially in NLP and com-
165   puter vision (He et al., 2020b; Bao et al., 2021). SSL involves training models to predict parts of
166   their input using other parts, leveraging the data’s inherent structure for supervision. A prominent
167   SSL method, Contrastive Learning, learns representations by contrasting positive and negative ex-
168   amples, effectively distinguishing similar and dissimilar data samples (Radford et al., 2021; Liang
169   et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020a). This approach has significantly
170
      advanced representation learning, achieving state-of-the-art results across various domains (Chen
      et al., 2020; He et al., 2020a).
171
172   Post-training introduces an additional self-supervised phase to existing models using a limited set
173   of data before downstream task evaluation, reducing the costs of initial large-scale training (Luo
174   et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2022). Luo et al. (2022) employs static mean-pooling, whereas Xue et al.
175   (2022) aligns image captions with video subtitles. In this unsupervised setting, post-training usually
176
      fine-tunes few parameters, maintaining the core strengths of the parent model.
177
178   2.3   Z ERO - SHOT I NFERENCE : L IMITATIONS OF C LASSICAL Z ERO -S HOT R ETRIEVAL
179
      Zero-shot inference enables models to recognize unseen classes without relying on labeled data
180   from each target class, unlike traditional supervised learning (Xian et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020b).
181   While zero-shot learning facilitates generalization to unseen classes, conventional audio-retrieval
182   benchmarks often lack compositional complexity, typically involving single acoustic events without
183   proper word order (Radford et al., 2021; Baevski et al., 2020; Gemmeke et al., 2017). In traditional
184   audio classification, models are trained on specific classes like musical genres or environmental
185   sounds, but zero-shot audio classification requires identifying audio samples from previously un-
186   seen classes. For example, a model trained on animal and vehicle sounds should also classify new
187   categories like “machinery” or “insects” (Wang et al., 2020a). As illustrated in fig. 8, zero-shot clas-
188
      sification involves encoding audio and text prompts through respective encoders and using cosine
      similarity to predict classes (Harwath & Glass, 2015; Kim & Pardo, 2018). Zero-shot audio retrieval
189
      extends this concept by finding relevant audio clips from unseen classes based on queries, such as
190
      retrieving ”birdsong” or ”ocean waves” when trained only on spoken words and ambient sounds
191   (Fonseca et al., 2021). As shown in fig. 9, the process involves encoding prompts and audio clips,
192   with cosine similarity determining the most relevant match (Chang & Yang, 2019). This approach
193   leverages class information to understand semantic relationships.

297   We propose a multi-stage training approach, outlined in fig. 1, with two stages: TeminAL A and
298
      TeminAL B. In TeminAL A, the model is trained to distinguish between single and multiple sounds,
      while in TeminAL B, the model learns to differentiate temporally distinct sounds along with their
299
      corresponding text labels. Both stages use contrastive learning with a modified infoNCE loss func-
300
      tion (Oord et al., 2018), detailed further in this section and appendix B.3, the difference in training
301   being the training data and contrastive objective. We have already elaborated on the different train-
302   ing dataset and it’s formulation in section 3.2, while the detailed loss function for both stages has
303   been discussed in this section. Context (text) and audio encodings are processed through their re-
304   spective encoders, producing embeddings Ce and Ae as shown in fig. 3. These embeddings are used
305   to form a (batch × batch) matrix to identify positive and negative pairs (see Figures 4). Similar-
306   ity scores are calculated from these embeddings and used to compute the modified infoNCE loss
307   function, as described latter in the section. Logits derived from similarity scores are transformed
308   using a Softmax function to generate probabilities (equation 4 and equation 5), which are evaluated
309
      with cross-entropy against true labels. The loss function is computed as the sum of text loss (Lc )
      and audio loss (La ), which sum up to form LB = LcB + β(LaB ) which stands for the TeminAL
310
      B loss. The text loss LcB optimizes the selection of texts from n possible options generated by
311
      Ce (equation 3), while audio loss LaB does the same for the audio embeddings (equation 7). This
312   dual-component loss ensures balanced training of both context (Ce ) and audio (Ae ) encoders. The
313   overall methodology is schematically depicted in Figure 3.
314
315

317

318   audio-text pairs. For a time-aligned audio-text pair (a, c), following section 3.1, we design a loss
319   function that maintains chronological order within the pair, differing only in the loss components.

405   3.5   D ETAILS ON HYPER - PARAMETERS OF THE L OSS FORMULATION
406
407   The loss function formulation introduces hyper-parameters that affect the temporal sensitivity of the
408
      encoders. The choice of {αso , αco , αst , αct } and {βA , β}, which can be either 0 or 1, significantly
      impacts the model’s behavior. For example, setting αst = 1 increases sensitivity to time-reversed
409
      sound samples by adding the term αst exp(za · zT(c) ) to the expression Ctr denominator of equa-
410
      tion 6. This adjustment forces the encoders to ensure the sum of terms equals unity, which reduces
411   the encoding values of non-similar pairs, enhancing sensitivity to time-reversed samples and guiding
412   the encoders to assign lower values to dissimilar batch samples.
413
414
      These coefficients also help adapt the model to different datasets. In fig. 4, these parameters ex-
      tend the contrastive loss function over time: the top three sub-squares represent TNCE(za , zc ),
415
      the middle sub-squares represent TNCE(za , zT(c) ), and the last three sub-squares represent
416
      TNCE(za , zO(c) ). The top-left quadrant shows contrastive loss on stitched pairs, with positive
417
      (green) and negative (red) diagonal terms crucial for temporal understanding.
418
419   The key role of β is to increase the number of training samples, while the α coefficients enhance
420   sensitivity to time-reversal and overlapping sounds. When α = 0, sensitivity is nullified, but higher
421
      values compel the encoders to refine recognition of temporal variations by minimizing the denom-
      inator. Without these hyper-parameters, the loss converges similarly, but encoders learn different
422
      relationships. Their inclusion ensures distinct sample treatment, enhancing temporal sensitivity.

      4.2    ZSTE AND D OWNSTREAM TASKS
433
434   We construct comprehensive evaluation of our proposed method in order to satisfy our objectives
435   of temporal instillation. ZSTE (Zero Shot Temporal Evaluation) is our evaluation framework for
436   assessing contrastive models in zero-shot tasks. The implementation is discussed in algorithm 2 and
437   algorithm 3. ZSTE begins with basic classification on unseen classes in Task 1 (fig. 10), progressing
438   to complex scenarios involving overlapping audio features and novel composite text classes in Task
439   2. The subtasks 2A and 2B involve the model being able to distinguish both classes and at–least
440
      one class respectively, model which correctly understands both sounds performs well on subtask
      A. The subtask 2C and 2D are similar tasks as 2A and 2B but on overlapping sounds rather than
441
      concatenated sounds. Thus given the overlapping text-audio pair, we need compute the accuracy
442
      of the model to detect both the audio classes in 2C and at-least one of the classes in 2D (fig. 11).
443   Models which are able to distinguish multiple overlapping sounds, perform better in 2C.
444
445   ZSTE Task 3 evaluates temporal comprehension by testing sequences of interchanged acoustic
446
      events, building on the configuration from Task 2 but now using temporal texts (fig. 12). A model
      capable of understanding temporal relationships in text will excel in this task. Task 4 (fig. 13) as-
447
      sesses the model’s ability to maintain focus amid irrelevant class labels, which act as noise to the
448
      actual audio embeddings. Task 5 (fig. 14) examines the model’s generalization to out-of-distribution
449   prompts, reflecting real-world complexities. Each of these three tasks includes subtasks A and B:
450   Subtask A requires detecting all audio classes, while Subtask B involves identifying at least one
451   class. These tasks test the model’s grasp of temporality and general language attribution. Models
452   with a robust understanding of both temporality and language generalisability will perform better.
453
      This comprehensive approach ensures robust evaluation of the model’s zero-shot learning capabil-
454
      ities. The primary aim is to foster model improvement rather than solely benchmark performance.
455   Further details on ZSTE is shown in appendix B.4.

557   6   C ONCLUSION
558
559   This research introduces the Temporal Instillation in Audio-Language Models, a post-training tech-
560   nique that enhances temporal and language understanding in Audio-Language Models (ALMs). Our
561
      approach employs sequential inversion and temporal augmentations, effectively improving sequen-
      tial discernment in ALMs. The hierarchical training strategy proves crucial, as seen in the perfor-
562
      mance comparison between TeminAL B and TeminAL AB, highlighting the need for structured
563
      training in complex tasks like time instillation. Our findings also demonstrate that modifying the
564   infoNCE loss improves model sensitivity, as shown in our parametric study (table 2). Zero Shot
565   Temporal Evaluation (ZSTE) results (section 5) confirm T–CLAP’s strength in zero-shot classifi-
566   cation and retrieval tasks, offering new evaluation insights for contrastive learning models. While
567   T–CLAP shows a slight decrease in traditional audio classification accuracy, it consistently outper-
568   forms baseline models in scenarios involving temporal relationships, demonstrating enhanced se-
569   quential information processing. This study opens new research directions, particularly in refining
570   contrastive loss for broader task optimization, paving the way for ALMs that excel in both retrieval
571   and complex temporal-linguistic tasks.

      B.1     P ROOF OF P ROPOSITIONS
870
      B.1.1    P ROPOSITION 1 :
871
872   Contrastive models, when used for audio-text matching, do not comprehend the semantic relation-
873   ship between the audio and text, but rather operate by matching similar audios to similar texts based
874   on superficial features.
875   Let faudio : A → Rd and ftext : T → Rd be the functions mapping audio A and text T into a
876   d-dimensional embedding space, respectively. The similarity score between an audio sample a and
877   a text sample t is given by s(a, t) = faudio (a) · ftext (t).
878
879         1. Contrastive models can yield high similarity scores for pairs of audio and text samples that
880            share similar superficial features but lack semantic congruence.
881         2. Contrastive models, as defined, cannot inherently discern semantic relationships between
882            audio and text but rely on the co-occurrence of similar features in their respective embed-
883            dings.
884
885   Assume a pair of audio samples a1 , a2 and text samples t1 , t2 such that a1 and t1 , a2 and t2 are
886   semantically congruent but share similar superficial features with a2 and t1 respectively.
887
888   According to the model, s(a1 , t1 ) and s(a2 , t2 ) should be high. However, due to the shared
889   superficial features, s(a1 , t2 ) and s(a2 , t1 ) may also be high, indicating a false positive match.
890
891
      This contradiction shows that the model’s high similarity score does not necessarily correspond to a
892   true semantic match, supporting the hypothesis.

      B.2     DATASET SELECTION AND CREATION
895
896   For dataset selection and creation process we chose ESC–50 dataset. Due to its high audio quality,
897   adequate pre–processing, suitable length and number of samples, and its inherent robustness. Im-
898   portantly, we excluded datasets generated through crowd–sourcing to reduce labeling inaccuracies.
899   ESC–50’s assortment of 50 classes encompasses a variety of real-world sounds from natural, animal,
900   and human sources, making it versatile for different applications and particularly effective for zero-
901   shot classification tasks, which require identifying items from previously unseen categories. From
902   the ESC–50 dataset we get 50 pairs of Audio, Label data, these pairs are then processed according
903
      to algorithm 2 to make a training dataset. We select two distinct sounds from the possible 50 sounds
      giving us a total of 2450 pairs (ai , aj ) and (ti , tj ) of sounds. For each pair we have 3 possible
904
      configurations using keywords ‘before’ , ‘after’ and ’while’ as suggested in section 3.1. Thus our
905
      total dataset thus becomes 7350 data pairs. For teminAL A, we only use 2450 pairs of data while
906   selecting either one of the audio and text from this pair. The prompt used for concatenating the texts
907   are ‘single sound of ti ’ and ‘combined sound of ti and tj ’.
908
909
      Our Sequential Inversion Approach challenges traditional contrastive learning methods, which typ-
      ically align audio segments with matching text while contrasting them against unrelated pairs. This
910
      practice, akin to a bag-of-words model, often fails to capture sequential nuances as it emphasizes dis-
911
      tinguishing features over temporal understanding. To foster a deeper comprehension of sequences,
912   we introduced a novel technique for generating negative samples that share thematic elements, com-
913   pelling the model to focus on the order of events. This method, depicted in fig. 2, utilizes two types
914   of temporal augmentations “before” and “while” to enhance the model’s ability to discern sequential
915   information. The transformation aims to capture the dynamic interplay between the two arguments,
916   allowing the model to discern the original audio-transcript pair (a, c) from its transformed versions
917   (a, O(c)) and (O(a), c). It is applicable to concatenated audio or transcription pairs, effectuating a
      temporal reordering of the components.

                 class in the ESC dataset. We then measured accuracy by assessing how often the model
1297             correctly identified the label associated with a given audio input (refer to Figure 10).

1299

1300
                 the validation set. The task configuration paralleled that of Task 1, with the key difference
1301             being that the accuracy assessment was conducted on audio clips featuring either concate-
1302             nated or overlapping sounds (refer to Figure 11). We measured two accuracy metrics: one
1303             based on the model correctly identifying the two highest probabilities corresponding to the
1304             correct labels and another based on the model selecting at least one correct class.
1305           • Task 3 : In contrast to the preceding task, which disregarded temporality, this new exper-
1306             iment focuses on assessing T–CLAP’s capability to accurately discern classes with their
1307             respective temporal relationships. For this task, we presented the model with three prompts
1308             following the same format as those encountered during training: “[class label 1] before
1309             [class label 2]”, “[class label 2] before [class label 1]”, and “[class label 1] while [class la-
1310             bel 2]” (see Figure 12) while picking the 2 classes similar to Task 2. By exposing the model
1311             to an audio featuring one of these three temporal combinations, we gauged its accuracy in
1312             correctly identifying the corresponding temporal relationship within each prompt.
1313           • Task 4 : This task represents a more challenging iteration of Task 3. Here, our objec-
1314             tive is to challenge the model by introducing prompts that include additional class labels
1315             not present in the audio (Figure 13), aiming to create confusion for the model during the
1316
                 evaluation process.
1317           • Task 5 : In our final task, we aimed to push the model’s boundaries by presenting it with a
1318             temporal prompt it had not encountered during training, assessing its ability to generalize to
1319             novel temporal inputs. Our hypothesis was rooted in the nature of the text encoder, T5; if T–
1320
                 CLAP had truly grasped the temporal nuances embedded in “before” and “while” prompts,

1321
                 testing its comprehension of the “before” temporal aspect, we provided the model with four
1322
                 prompts structured as follows: “In this concatenated sound” followed by“The first sound is
1323             [class label 1]”, “The second sound is [class label 1]”, “The first sound is [class label 2]”

1325             two correct prompts, and we evaluated the model based on its ability to correctly identify
1326             the combination of two prompts out of the six possible options. The model received a score
1327             of 1 if it correctly identified both prompts and 0.5 if it identified only one.

1330             was to select the two correct prompts, considering the two correct classes in both possible

1406   In evaluating retrieval tasks, specifically text-to-audio and audio-to-text, we assess CompA-CLAP

1418   aiming to forge robust audio representations by marrying audio data with corresponding natural lan-
1419   guage descriptions. Their model considers various audio and text encoders and enhances the model

1436   B.7   L IMITATIONS OF THE C URRENT M ODEL
1437
             1. General-Purpose ALM: Our proposed model is not a general-purpose Audio-Language
1438
                Model (ALM) capable of performing all downstream applications across all datasets. The
1439            current implementation is specifically designed and validated on the ESC-50 dataset as
1440            a proof-of-concept and to achieve our defined objectives. Consequently, generalization
1441            remains a limitation, although addressing this was beyond the scope of our work.
1442
             2. Temporality Beyond the Dataset: The model does not provide a general understanding
1443            of temporality beyond the ESC-50 dataset. Results from the ZSTE Task 4 and 5 confirm
1444            that neither does the model propose general temporality, nor does it achieve it. Notably, we
1445            emphasize that achieving general-purpose temporality would require larger, more compre-
1446            hensive pre-trained text encoders. Specifically, open-ended text encoders (e.g., encoders
1447            from encoder-decoder models) would be more suitable than encoders trained on closed
1448            masked language modeling techniques, such as BERT.
1449         3. Zero-Shot Evaluation Scheme: While our zero-shot evaluation scheme is designed to be
1450            general-purpose, it is inherently limited to contrastive models. Furthermore, the evaluation
1451            has not been tested on domains beyond the ESC-50 dataset. The reported ZSTE results are
1452            restricted to this dataset because it aligns with the training data used in our model and those
1453            of prior works. To mitigate data leakage, we ensure that our evaluation dataset is separate
1454            from the training data. However, it is important to note that the primary objective of this
1455
                evaluation is to validate the proof-of-concept rather than to set new benchmarks.
1456         4. Evaluation Dataset Overlap: A broader limitation, relevant to most models in this do-
1457            main, is the overlap between evaluation datasets across various benchmarks. These over-
                laps can occur in terms of sound events or contextual similarities. Therefore, we cau-


                                                          27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
                tion against uncritical comparisons of model performance on classical benchmarks without
1459            careful consideration of dataset overlap.
1460
1461   By addressing these limitations, we hope to guide future researchers in expanding and improving
1462   upon the current work to achieve broader applicability and more generalized performance.
