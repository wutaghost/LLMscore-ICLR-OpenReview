009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The expansion of Transformers and the collection of high-quality multimodal
012            datasets have propelled deep neural networks to achieve unprecedented perfor-
013            mance in vision and language tasks. However, applying these advances is non-
014            trivial in real-world applications. The extensive number of parameters compli-
015            cates model updates, and real-world data often features a long-tailed distribution
016
               along with noisy labels. To address the above issues, we propose to explore the
               internal structure of the neural network for learning with sample relationships,
017
               rather than just increasing the number of model parameters. Specifically, we
018
               introduce RetFormer, a model enhanced with a multimodal knowledge base for
019            storing world knowledge, and a retrieval cross-fusion module designed to es-
020            tablish robust multimodal sample relationships by leveraging content from the
021            knowledge base. RetFormer establishes a robust relationship between image and
022            text modalities by integrating information from external knowledge bases into the
023            model’s decision-making process, thus overcoming the limitations of traditional
024            approaches on model size and datasets. Our experiments demonstrate the benefits
025            of integrating large-scale image-text datasets into vision tasks and exemplify the
026            importance of modeling the relationship between image and text modalities. We
027
               have evaluated our approach on the task of long-tailed recognition and learning
               with noisy labels and have shown that it achieves state-of-the-art accuracies.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The conjunction of large-scale Transformers with extensive pre-trained datasets has met with sig-
033   nificant success in both vision and NLP domains. Large language models such as the PaLM se-
034   ries(Chowdhery et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023), GPT series(Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
035   2022), and LLaMA series(Touvron et al., 2023a;b), along with multimodal large language mod-
036   els like Gemini(Team et al., 2023), GPT-4(Achiam et al., 2023), and Claude 3(Anthropic, 2024),
037   have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance across various downstream tasks, owing to their ro-
038   bust capabilities in understanding, reasoning, and generation. According to the scaling law(Kaplan
039
      et al., 2020), extensive multimodal and multilingual datasets such as WIT(Srinivasan et al., 2021),
      LAION(Schuhmann et al., 2022), and DataComp(Gadre et al., 2024) have the same importance as
040
      large Transformers. Nevertheless, this approach is critically dependent on the collection of large-
041
      scale training data samples and the increase of model parameters, which turns out to be non-trivial
042   in real-world applications.
043
044   In the training of large-scale Transformers, world knowledge is implicitly encoded within the
045
      vast number of model parameters, which can exacerbate certain challenges inherent in the cur-
      rent machine-learning paradigm. These challenges include difficulties in model updating, limited
046
      interpretability, and scalability issues. Furthermore, real-world datasets invariably encounter two
047
      principal problems: (a) noisy labels resulting from the ambiguity of the data itself and annotator
048   errors, (b) class imbalance arising from natural phenomena. These issues often occur simultane-
049   ously, complicating the estimation of the true distribution of the dataset. The quest for robust deep
050   representation learning through the exploration of sample relationships in scenarios of data scarcity
051   or interference from noisy labels has garnered significant interest from the research community, par-
052   ticularly for tasks where there is a lack of high-quality training data to ensure generalization, such as
053   learning with noisy labels and long-tailed recognition. While previous research(Zhang et al., 2023;
      Wei et al., 2021) efforts aimed at addressing these challenges have made significant contributions,

079   As illustrated in Figure 1 (c)(d)(e), we search for images and the corresponding description text
080
      related to the query image. When we search for samples of the same class, the image modality
      presents concrete low-level invariant features (e.g., shape, color, texture), and the textual modality
081
      usually contains much high-level and abstractly relevant information. When we search for different
082
      classes of samples, there may be shared knowledge transfer in the image and text modalities with
083   the query image. There may also be optional semantic information in the textual modality when
084   we search for noise labels. In addition, textual descriptions are a priori knowledge that can be
085   summarized by experts, which may be useful when there are not enough images to learn a general
086   class representation for recognition.
087
      To address the aforementioned issues, we propose an alternative perspective based on the above
088
      insight. As shown in Figure 1 (b), instead of statically compiling world knowledge into model
089   weights, we construct an external image-text pair knowledge base for storing world knowledge
090   and then utilize a retrieval module to identify and obtain relevant knowledge from a predefined
091   knowledge base, modeling the effective relationships between image and text modalities, which
092   is then used to enhance the model’s predictive power. We overcome the limitations of traditional
093   methods on model size and training datasets by seamlessly integrating information from external
094   databases into the model’s decision-making process. This semi-parametric approach enables the
095   model to incorporate external knowledge to improve its understanding and predictive capabilities.
096   To evaluate our approach, we focus on long-tailed recognition and learning with noisy labels, which
097   are challenging and meaningful tasks. We conduct extensive experiments on three datasets: CIFAR-
098   100-LT(Cao et al., 2019b), ImageNet-LT(Liu et al., 2019b), and a real-world noisy dataset WebVi-
099   sion(Li et al., 2017).
100
      In conclusion, our primary contributions are fourfold:
101
102   (1) We analyze and highlight the interplay and distinctions between image and text modalities within
103   the context of retrieval augmentation in vision, noting that the text modality can complement re-
104
      trieval content, which aids in long-tailed recognition and learning with noisy labels.
105   (2) We introduce a new image-text retrieval-augmented framework called RetFormer, which uti-
106   lizes a small external knowledge base to model the relationship between text and image knowledge,
107   thereby enhancing the performance of the model without incurring significant computational over-
      head.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116
117   Data Scarcity Learning. Learning with imperfect training data has proven to be a very challenging
118
      task and has been explored in a variety of data-scarce tasks. Long-tailed recognition is one of
      the key problems. Most of the solutions are variants of the core idea of “adjustment” (e.g., re-
119
      sampling(Chawla et al., 2002; Han et al., 2005; He & Garcia, 2009), re-weighting(Cui et al., 2019;
120
      Zhong et al., 2021)). Learning with noisy labels is another important task. The main methods
121   revolve around filtering noisy labels, including but not limited to correcting wrong labels(Liu et al.,
122   2022), reweighting examples(Ren et al., 2018), and selecting confident examples(Patel & Sastry,
123   2023; Wang et al., 2022). Some methods combine multiple techniques, e.g., DivideMix(Li et al.,
124   2020) and Sel-CL+(Li et al., 2022).
125
      Sample Relationship. There are diverse and tight relationships between different samples, and these
126
      relationships are widely used through various types of strategies.Mixup(Zhang et al., 2017) intro-
127   duces prior knowledge to the model by performing simple linear mixing between training samples.
128   Some approaches via investigating sample/class relationships to conduct transductive inference, e.g.,
129   transductive few-shot classification(Liu et al., 2018), and meta embedding(Liu et al., 2019a; Zhu &
130   Yang, 2020). BatchFormer(Hou et al., 2022) applied a vanilla Transformer encoder into the batch
131   dimension of each mini-batch to implicitly explore sample relationships during training.
132
      Retrieval augmented in computer vision Recent approaches in computer vision perform various
133   tasks by retrieving from external memory. Nakata et al. (2022) store feature maps from the training
134   set in the memory, and perform k-NN for classification. RDM(Blattmann et al., 2022) retrieves
135   nearest neighbors from a memory for generative vision models. RAC(Long et al., 2022) uses only
136   the training dataset itself as an external source of information and extracts embeddings of relevant
137   text segments with the pre-trained CLIP model. MAM(Iscen et al., 2023) considers texts to be of
138   different importance from each other and introduces an additional dataset.
139   Our work is different in that we consider that both image and text modalities possess exploitable
140   knowledge. Therefore, we propose to make the neural network itself capable of learning multimodal
141   sample relationships based on the maximum inner product search algorithm. Experiments show
142   better robustness of our novel framework across multiple tasks.

255
      Downstream dataset. Building a knowledge base directly using the downstream dataset is the
256
      most straightforward option. This guarantees that for each query image, there will exist at least one
257   instance of the same class. The disadvantage of this choice is that most downstream datasets are not
258   rich enough in textual descriptions and may contain only the labels of the images.
259
260
      DataComp. DataComp(Gadre et al., 2024) is a testbed for dataset experiments centered around a

262
      of DataComp and comprised of 1.4B samples.

283   3.2.4    R ETRIEVAL COMPLEXITY
284
285   Time complexity. We use the HNSW(Malkov & Yashunin, 2018) algorithm from the Faiss(Johnson
286   et al., 2019) library to perform approximate k-NN searches in our experiments. It has sub-linear
287   complexity, which means that for an in-memory dataset of N elements, it takes O(logN ). In fact, the
288   time consumed for querying in a 1B-element knowledge base is on the order of milliseconds. Since
289
      the encoder parameters are frozen, the results of one precomputation can be reused in subsequent
      training and validation, which also saves a lot of computation.
290
291   Spatial complexity. We use a fixed vision encoder in our experiments, so we pre-calculated and
292   saved the results for all k-NN, thus saving storage space. For example, ImageNet-LT contains
293   115.8K images from 1000 categories. In our experiments, you need about 30GB of extra space
294   for training.

298
      ships, we also provide an intuitive explanation from the perspective of gradient propagation. Intu-

301   samples in the retrieval module, as shown in Figure 3. Specifically, given a sample x0 with a re-
302   trieval subset DN N = {(I2i−1 , T2i )}ki=1 of size k and corresponding losses L0 , L1 , Li , ..., L2×k ,
303   we have:

307   That is, the retrieval module brings in new gradient terms ∂L    ∂x0 . From a perspective of gradi-
                                                                         i

308   ent optimization, Li also optimizes the network according to samples (I2i−1 , T2i )}ki=1 , which
309   is significantly different compared to the model without the retrieval module. In other words,
310   (I2i−1 , T2i )}ki=1 can be thought of as virtual samples of yi , where yi is the label of xi . We be-

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we introduce RetFormer, a new multimodal retrieval-augmented vision language
534   framework for long-tailed recognition and learning with noisy labels. We emphasize that the image
535   and text modality of retrieved instances have implicit intrinsic relationships, and propose to enable
536   deep neural networks themselves with the ability to explore these sample relationships. We propose
537   a simple, but effective, retrieval cross-fusion module that learns multimodal sample relationships
538   and computes their contributions. Extensive experiments on a variety of long-tailed recognition and
539   learning with noisy labels benchmarks validate that our approach works better than well-designed
      vision-based methods.
