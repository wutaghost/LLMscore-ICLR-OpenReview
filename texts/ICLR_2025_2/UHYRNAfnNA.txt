009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning (RL) agents can exploit unintended strategies to achieve
012            high rewards without fulfilling the desired objectives, a phenomenon known as
013            reward hacking. In this work, we examine reward hacking through the lens of Gen-
014            eral Utility RL, which generalizes RL by considering utility functions over entire
015            trajectories rather than state-based rewards. From this perspective, many instances
016            of reward hacking can be seen as inconsistencies between current and updated util-
017            ity functions, where the behavior optimized for an updated utility function is poorly
018
               evaluated by the original one. Our main contribution is Modification-Considering
               Value Learning (MC-VL), a novel algorithm designed to address this inconsistency
019
               during learning. Starting with a coarse yet value-aligned initial utility function,
020
               the MC-VL agent iteratively refines this function based on past observations while
021            considering the potential consequences of updates. This approach enables the
022            agent to anticipate and reject modifications that may lead to undesired behavior.
023            To validate our approach, we implement MC-VL agents based on the Double
024            Deep Q-Network (DDQN) and Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradients
025            (TD3), demonstrating their effectiveness in preventing reward hacking in diverse
026            environments, including those from AI Safety Gridworlds and the MuJoCo gym.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   From mastering video games (Mnih et al., 2015) to optimizing robotic control (Levine et al., 2016),
032   reinforcement learning (RL) agents have solved a wide range of tasks by learning to maximize
033
      cumulative rewards. However, this reward-maximization paradigm has a significant flaw: agents
      may exploit poorly defined or incomplete reward functions, leading to a behavior known as reward
034
      hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), where the agent maximizes the reward signal but fails to meet the
035
      designer’s true objectives.
036
037   For instance, an RL agent tasked with stacking blocks instead flipped them, exploiting a reward based
038   on the height of the bottom face of a block (Popov et al., 2017). Similarly, a robot arm manipulated
039
      objects in arbitrary ways that exploited a classifier-based reward system, tricking it into labeling
      incorrect actions as successful due to insufficient negative examples (Singh et al., 2019). Ibarz et al.
040
      (2018) describe reward model exploitation in Atari games, where agents exploit flaws in reward
041
      functions learned from human preferences and demonstrations. These incidents underscore that while
042   RL agents may maximize rewards, their learned behaviors often diverge from the goals intended by
043   the reward designers.
044
045
      As RL systems scale to more complex, safety-critical applications like autonomous driving (Kiran
      et al., 2021) and medical diagnostics (Ghesu et al., 2017), ensuring reliable and safe agent behavior
046
      becomes increasingly important. Pan et al. (2022) showed that reward hacking becomes more
047
      common as models grow in complexity. Moreover, Denison et al. (2024) demonstrated that agents
048   based on large language models, trained with outcome-based rewards, can generalize to changing the
049   code of their own reward functions. Reward hacking also becomes more prominent with increased
050   reasoning capabilities. For example, during testing of the o1-preview (pre-mitigation) language
051   model on a Capture the Flag (CTF) challenge, the model encountered a bug that prevented the target
052   container from starting. Rather than solving the challenge as intended, the model used nmap to scan
053   the network, discovered a misconfigured Docker daemon API, and exploited it to start the container
      and read the flag via the Docker API, bypassing the original task altogether (OpenAI, 2024).

      In this paper, we frame reward hacking within the General Utility RL (GU-RL) formalism (Zahavy
055   et al., 2021; Geist et al., 2022). We describe an agent that optimizes a learned utility function, which
056   assigns value to trajectories based on past observed rewards. Many instances of reward hacking,
057   such as manipulating the reward provision process (Everitt et al., 2021) and tampering with the
058   sensors (Ring & Orseau, 2011), can be viewed as inconsistent updates to the utility function. We
059   define an update as inconsistent when the trajectories produced by a policy optimized for the updated
060   utility function would be evaluated poorly by the prior utility function. To address this issue, we
061   introduce Modification-Considering Value Learning (MC-VL). In MC-VL, the agent updates its
062   utility function based on the observed rewards, similar to value-based RL, but it also predicts the
063
      long-term consequences of potential updates and can reject them. In our formulation, avoiding
      inconsistent utility updates is an optimal behavior.
064
065   For example, consider a robot trained to grasp objects using human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017).
066   A standard RL agent, if rewarded for positioning its manipulator between the object and the camera
067   in the middle of the training, can exploit this reward by learning to repeat that behavior (OpenAI,
068   2017). In contrast, an MC-VL agent would first forecast the consequences of updating its utility
069
      function based on this new reward. Drawing from prior experiences where positive rewards were
      given only for positioning the manipulator near the object, the MC-VL agent might predict low utility
070
      for positioning the manipulator in front of the camera. As a result, the agent would reject the update,
071
      staying focused on the intended grasping task.
072
073   Several prior works have discussed the theoretical possibility of mitigating reward or sensor tampering
074   using current utility optimization, where an agent evaluates potential changes to its utility function
075
      using its current utility function (Orseau & Ring, 2011; Hibbard, 2012; Everitt et al., 2016; 2021).
      Dewey (2011) suggested learning the utility function from past observations. However, to the best
076
      of our knowledge, no prior work has formalized this within the GU-RL framework, applied this
077
      idea to standard RL environments, or implemented such an agent. In this work, we provide an
078   algorithm to learn the utility function, estimate future policies, and compare them using the current
079   utility function. Additionally, we introduce a learning setup where the initial utility function is
080   learned in a Safe sandbox version of the environment before transitioning to the Full version. Our
081   experiments, conducted across various environments, including benchmarks adapted from the AI
082   Safety Gridworlds (Leike et al., 2017), are, to the best of our knowledge, the first to demonstrate the
083   ability to prevent reward hacking in these environments. Furthermore, our results provide insights
084   into the key parameters influencing MC-VL performance, laying the groundwork for further research
085   on preventing reward hacking in RL.

089
      The problem of agents learning unintended behaviors by exploiting misspecified training signals
090   has been extensively discussed in the literature as reward hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), reward
091   gaming (Leike et al., 2018), or specification gaming (Krakovna et al., 2020). Krakovna et al. (2020)
092   provide a comprehensive overview of these behaviors across RL and other domains. The theoretical
093   foundations for understanding reward hacking are explored by Skalse et al. (2022), who argue that
094   preventing reward hacking requires either limiting the agent’s policy space or carefully controlling
095   the optimization process.
096   Laidlaw et al. (2023) propose addressing reward hacking by regularizing the divergence between the
097   occupancy measures of the learned policy and a known safe policy. Unlike their approach, which
098   may overly restrict the agent’s ability to learn effective policies, our method does not require the
099   final policy to remain close to any predefined safe policy. Eisenstein et al. (2024) investigate whether
100   ensembles of reward models trained from human feedback can mitigate reward hacking, showing
101   that while ensembles reduce the problem, they do not completely eliminate it. To avoid additional
102   computational overhead, we do not use ensembles in this work, but they could complement our
103   method by improving the robustness of the learned utility function.
104   A specific form of reward hacking, where an agent manipulates the mechanism by which it receives
105   rewards, is known as wireheading (Amodei et al., 2016; Taylor et al., 2016; Everitt & Hutter, 2016;
106   Majha et al., 2019) or reward tampering (Kumar et al., 2020; Everitt et al., 2021). Related phenomena,
107   where an agent manipulates its sensory inputs to deceive the reward system, are discussed as delusion-
      boxing (Ring & Orseau, 2011), measurement tampering (Roger et al., 2023), and reward-input

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tampering (Everitt et al., 2021). Several studies have hypothesized that current utility optimization
109   could mitigate reward or sensor tampering (Yudkowsky, 2011; Yampolskiy, 2014; Hibbard, 2012).
110   One of the earliest discussions of this issue is in by Schmidhuber (2003), who developed the concept
111   of Gödel-machine agents, capable of modifying their own source code, including the utility function.
112   They suggested that such modifications should only occur if the new values are provably better
113   according to the old ones. However, none of these works addressed learning the utility function or
114   described the optimization process in full detail.
115   Dewey (2011) introduced the concept of Value-Learning Agents, which learn and optimize a utility
116   function based on past observations as a potential solution to reward tampering. Everitt & Hutter
117   (2016) considered a setting where the agent learns a posterior given a prior over manually specified
118   utility functions, proposing an agent that is not incentivized to tamper with its reward signal by
119   selecting actions that do not alter its beliefs about the posterior. More recently, Everitt et al. (2021)
120   formalized conditions under which an agent optimizing its current reward function would lack the
121   incentive to tamper with the reward signal. Our work suggests an implementation of value learning in
122   standard RL environments, where the utility function is learned from the past rewards. Additionally,
123
      our method is applicable to other instances of reward hacking beyond reward tampering. Moreover, it
      aims to prevent reward hacking, rather than simply removing the incentive for it.

126   3    BACKGROUND
127
128   We consider the usual Reinforcement Learning (RL) setup, where an agent learns to make decisions
129   by interacting with an environment and receiving feedback in the form of rewards (Sutton & Barto,
130   2018). This interaction is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman, 2014) defined by
131   the tuple (S, A, P, R, ρ, γ), where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, P : S × A × S → R is
132   the transition kernel, R : S × A → R is the reward function, ρ is the initial state distribution, and γ is
133
      the discount factor. The objective in a standard RL is to learn a policy P∞ π : S → A that maximizes the
      expected return, defined as the cumulative discounted reward Eπ [ t=0 γ t R(st , at )]. The expected
134
      return from taking action a in state s and subsequently following policy π is called state-action value
135
      function and denoted as Qπ (s, a).
136
137
      Deep Q-Networks (DQN) and Double DQN (DDQN) DQN (Mnih et al., 2013) and DDQN (van
138
      Hasselt et al., 2016) are RL algorithms that approximate the state-action value function Q(s, a; θ)
139   using neural networks, where θ are the network parameters. Both algorithms store past experiences in
140   a replay buffer and update network parameters by minimizing a loss L(θ) on the temporal-difference
141   error based on the Bellman equation:
142
143                 L(θ) = ||Q(st , at ; θ) − sg[rt + γQ(st+1 , arg max Q(st+1 , a; θ̂); θ− )]||,             (1)

145   where sg denotes stop gradient, (st , at , rt , st+1 ) represents a transition sampled from the buffer, and
146   θ− refers to parameters of a target network, which stabilizes learning by being a slower updating
147   version of the current Q-network. DQN uses θ̂ equal to θ− , while DDQN proposed to use θ instead
148   to reduce the overestimation bias. The policy π(s) is obtained by arg maxa Q(s, a; θ).

150   General-Utility RL (GU-RL) In this work, we focus on an agent that optimizes its current utility
151   function. This problem is naturally framed within the General-Utility Reinforcement Learning
152   (GU-RL) (Geist et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020; Zahavy et al., 2021), which generalizes standard
153
      RL to maximization of utility function F . Unlike traditional RL, where rewards are assigned to
      individual transitions, F intuitively assigns value to entire trajectories. GU-RL offers a more general
154
      framework that encompasses tasks like risk-sensitive RL (Mihatsch & Neuneier, 2002), apprenticeship
155
      learning (Abbeel & Ng, 2004), and pure exploration (Hazan et al., 2019).

180   We aim to address reward hacking in RL by introducing Modification-Considering Value Learning
181   (MC-VL). The MC-VL agent continuously updates its utility function based on observed rewards
182   while avoiding inconsistent utility modifications that could lead to suboptimal behavior under the
183   current utility function. This is achieved by comparing policies induced by the current and updated
184   utility functions. To compare the policies, we compare the trajectories they produce.

237   Modification-Considering VL (MC-VL) The distinction between VL agents and standard RL
238   agents becomes apparent when the agent is modification-considering, meaning it evaluates the
239   consequences of modifying its utility function. For the agents optimizing URL , it is always optimal to
240   learn from new transitions, as they provide information about the utility being optimized. However,
241   for VL agents optimizing UVLt at time step t, it may be optimal to avoid learning from certain
242   transitions. Specifically, the agent may predict its future behavior after updating its utility to UVLt+1
243   and compare it to the predicted behavior under its current utility UVLt . If the updated behavior has
244   lower utility according to UVLt , it is optimal to avoid such an update since the agent is currently
245
      optimizing UVLt .
246   To formalize this decision-making process, we introduce an additional boolean action that determines
247   whether to modify the utility function after an interaction with the environment. The modified action
248   space is Am = A × {0, 1}, where each action am    i = (ai , modifyi ) includes a decision to modify or
249   to keep the current utility. The policy is adjusted to take the full transition as input, rather than just
250   the environment state. After each interaction, the agent explicitly decides whether to update its utility
251
      function based on the new experience. Algorithm 2 presents the modified version of GU-GPI for such
      an agent. We refer to the transitions where the optimal choice is modify = 0 as utility-inconsistent,
252
      and to the process of selecting modify as utility inconsistency detection.

278   Initial Utility Function An MC-VL agent described in Algorithm 2 requires some initial utility
279   function as input. In this work, we propose to learn this initial utility function in a Safe sandbox
280   version of the environment, where unintended behaviors cannot be discovered by the exploratory
281   policy. Examples of Safe environments include simulations or closely monitored lab settings where
282   the experiment can be stopped and restarted without consequences if undesired behaviors are detected.
283   To differentiate from the Safe version, we refer to the broader environment as the Full environment.
284   This Full environment may include the Safe one, for example, if the agent’s operational scope is
285
      expanded beyond a restricted lab setting. Alternatively, the Safe and Full environments may be
      distinct, such as when transitioning from simulation to real-world deployment. For the proposed
286
      approach to perform effectively, however, the Safe and Full environments must be sufficiently similar
287
      to allow for successful generalization of the learned utility function.

340   Box Moving Environment The environment consists of two parts: the left part represents an
341   external world with a box that can be moved up and down, while the right part is a room where the
342   agent can move. When the box reaches the top-most or bottom-most cell, the agent receives a reward
343
      of +1 or -1, respectively, and the box teleports back to the central cell. The agent can move up and
      down, and stepping on arrows on the floor moves the box in the corresponding direction.
344
345   In the Safe version, the top and bottom of the room are empty, and the optimal strategy is to repeatedly
346   press the up arrow. This setting allows the agent to learn an initial utility function that favors moving
347   the box upward. In the Full version, the agent has more interaction opportunities. The top cell is
348   replaced by an up arrow, allowing the agent to move the box up twice as fast by alternating between
349
      two up arrows. This setup shows that the Full environment can offer more efficient ways to achieve
      outcomes that were learned to be good in the Safe version. The bottom empty cell is replaced by a
350
      down arrow. However, by stepping into this cell, the agent can trick the reward system to give itself a
351
      +5 reward. While this results in higher observed rewards, it also moves the box downward, which
352   was learned to be undesirable in the Safe version.
353
354   The No Inconsistency version modifies the top cell to provide +5 reward instead. The agent can
355
      collect this reward without disrupting upward box movement, demonstrating that not all changes in
      reward structure result in utility inconsistency.

358   Absent Supervisor In this environment, the agent can alter its behavior based on whether it detects
359   the presence of a supervisor. The goal is to reach a target cell for +50 reward, but a punishment
360   cell along the path gives -30 reward if stepped on while a supervisor is present. Each movement
361   costs -1 reward, encouraging the agent to take the shortest path. In the Safe version, the supervisor
362
      is always present, leading the initial utility function to learn that going through the punishment cell
      is undesirable. In the Full version, the supervisor is present only half the time. The performance
363
      metric penalizes the agent for stepping on the punishment cell, even in the supervisor’s absence. We
364
      expect that when the agent steps on the punishment cell without supervision, it will experience utility
365   inconsistency, as learning from this transition leads to the policy that visits the punishment cell, which
366   contradicts the low utility assigned to this action previously.
367

369
      Tomato Watering This environment models tampering with observations. The agent is rewarded
      based on the number of watered tomatoes, which dry out randomly. Stepping on the bucket cell
370
      replaces all cells with watered tomatoes, representing a delusion. In the Safe version, the bucket is
371
      removed from the environment. The performance metric tracks the true number of watered tomatoes.
372   In the Full version, we expect utility inconsistency when the agent tries to delude itself using the
373   bucket if it compares policies based on a true environment transition model where stepping on the
374   bucket does not affect the tomatoes. In this case, its new behavior, standing on the bucket cell, should
375   have lower utility than watering the tomatoes. However, if a delusional transition model is used, a
376   room full of watered tomatoes should still appear to have high utility. Without access to the labels we
377   assigned to cells, the agent cannot distinguish between deluding itself by putting a bucket on its head
      and the real change, i.e. efficient watering of all tomatoes at once.

469   5.4   A BLATIONS AND SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
470
471
      We tested several alternative schemes for utility inconsistency detection and mitigation. As shown
      in Figure 4a, checking all transitions for utility inconsistency yields similar results to checking
472
      only those where the predicted reward significantly differs from the observed reward. However,
473
      discarding all such transitions prevents the algorithm from learning an optimal non-hacking policy.
474   Comparing policies before and after each gradient step without forecasting future policies also fails
475   to prevent reward hacking. Replacing the reward of inconsistent transitions with large negative values
476   is less effective at preventing reward hacking than not adding them to the replay buffer. Having such
477   transitions in the replay buffer prevents the algorithm from forecasting the correct future policy when
478   checking for inconsistency, and over time the replay buffer gets populated with both transitions with
479   positive and negative rewards, destabilizing training.
480   Figure 4b illustrates the performance with varying amounts of initial utility function training in the
481   Safe version. Remarkably, one run avoided reward hacking after just 100 steps of such training.
482   After 300 steps, all seeds converged to the optimal non-hacking policy, even though most had not
483   discovered the optimal policy within the Safe version by that point. This result suggests that future
484   systems might avoid reward hacking with only moderate training in a Safe environment. Additionally,
485   this experiment shows that without any training in Safe environment (0 steps) our algorithm behaves
      identical to the baseline. Additional experiments are reported in Appendix B.

      6    L IMITATIONS
487
488
      While our method effectively mitigates reward hacking in several environments, it comes with
489   computational costs, which are detailed in Appendix D. Checking for utility inconsistency requires
490   forecasting two future policies by training the corresponding action-value functions until convergence.
491   In the worst case, where each transition is checked for potential utility inconsistency, this process
492   can lead to a runtime slowdown proportional to the number of iterations used to update the action-
493   value functions. A potential optimization discussed in this paper involves only checking transitions
494   where the predicted reward significantly deviates from the observed reward. However, this approach
495   introduces an additional hyperparameter for the threshold of predicted reward deviation. Balancing
496   computational efficiency with effectiveness is a key area for future research. Promising avenues
497
      include leveraging Meta-RL (Schmidhuber, 1987) to accelerate policy forecasting. A particularly
      promising direction is in-context RL (Laskin et al., 2022) which can learn new behaviors in-context
498
      during inference, quickly and without costly training (Bauer et al., 2023).
499
500   Another limitation is that our approach addresses only a subset of reward hacking scenarios. Specifi-
501   cally, it depends on the reward model and value function generalizing correctly to novel trajectories.
502   This approach may not address reward hacking issues caused by incorrect reward shaping, like in
503
      the CoastRunners problem (OpenAI, 2023). In this case, if the agent already learned about a small
      positive reward (e.g., knocking over a target), the agent’s current utility function may assign high
504
      utility to behaviors that exploit this reward, even if they fail to achieve the final goal (completing the
505
      loop). Alternative methods, such as potential-based reward shaping (Ng et al., 1999), may be more
506   appropriate for addressing such issues.
507
508   Finally, our current implementation assumes access to rollouts from the true environment transition
509
      model, while only the reward model is learned. Extending our approach to work with learned latent
      transition models represents a promising direction for future research. Additionally, using a learned
510
      world model to predict utility-inconsistent transitions before they occur could further enhance the
511
      method’s applicability and efficiency. Improvements to computational efficiency and the integration
512   of learned transition models would also enable testing our method in more complex environments,
513   which is an important direction of future work.

516   7    C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we introduced Modification-Considering Value Learning, an algorithm that allows an
519
      agent to optimize its current utility function, learned from observed transitions, while considering
      the future consequences of utility updates. Using the General Utility RL framework, we formalized
520
      the concept of current utility optimization. Our implementations, MC-DDQN and MC-TD3, demon-
521
      strated the ability to avoid reward hacking in several previously unsolved environments. Furthermore,
522   we experimentally showed that our algorithm can improve the policy performance while remaining
523   aligned with the initial objectives.
524
525
      To the best of our knowledge, this is the first implementation of an agent that optimizes its utility
      function while considering the potential consequences of modifying it. We believe that studying such
526
      agents is an important direction for future research in AI safety, especially as AI systems become
527
      more general and aware of their environments and training processes (Berglund et al., 2023; Denison
528   et al., 2024). One of the key contributions of this work is providing tools to model such agents using
529   contemporary RL algorithms.
530
531
      Our empirical results also identify best practices for modeling these agents, including the importance
      of forecasting future policies and excluding utility-inconsistent transitions from the training process.
532
      Additionally, we introduced a set of modified environments designed for evaluating reward hacking,
533
      where agents first learn what to value in Safe environments before continuing their training in Full
534   environments. We believe this evaluation protocol offers a valuable framework for studying reward
535   hacking and scaling solutions to real-world applications.

802   C        Q UALITATIVE O BSERVATIONS
803
804   During our preliminary experiments, we encountered several instances where our algorithm failed
805   to detect utility inconsistencies, leading to reward hacking behaviors. Here, we describe these
806   occurrences and how we addressed them.
807
808   Utility Inconsistency Check Rollout Steps When using smaller inconsistency check rollout steps
809   h, we noticed that during the evaluation of future trajectories, the non-hacking policy sometimes
      needed to traverse several states with low rewards to reach a high reward region. In such cases,


                                                                    15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      the reward hacking policy, which remained stationary, had a higher estimated utility. Increasing h
811   resolved this issue.
812
813
814   Predicting Policy Changes without Additional Data Initially, we forecasted only one future
815   policy by training with the checked transition added to each mini-batch, and compared the resulting
816   policy to the current one. We didn’t train a second policy without adding anything to mini-batches.
817   However, in some cases, this led to situations where the copy learned better non-hacking behaviors
818   than the current policy because it was trained for longer, and thus performed better in evaluations on
819   average even if in some cases it expressed the reward-hacking behavior. The solution was to forecast
820   two policies, one with the checked transition added to each mini-batch and one without.
821
822
823
      Sensitivity to Stochasticity Evaluations in stochastic environments were found to be noisy. To
      mitigate this noise, we compared the two policies starting from the same set of states and using the
824
      same random seeds of the environment transition model. We also ensured consistency of the updates
825
      by keeping the random seeds the same while sampling mini-batches.
826
827
828   Handling Inconsistent Transitions We observed that if a utility-inconsistent transition was re-
829   moved from the replay buffer and another utility-inconsistent transition occurred in the same episode,
830   the algorithm sometimes failed to detect the inconsistency the second time because there was no
831   set of transitions in the replay buffer connecting this second utility-inconsistent transition to the
832   starting state. To resolve this issue, we reset the environment every time the agent detected utility
833   inconsistency. In practical applications, it would be reasonable to assume that after detecting potential
834   reward hacking, the agent would be returned to a safe state instead of continuing exploration.
835
836
837
      Irreversible Changes In the Rocks and Diamonds environment, when comparing policies starting
      from the current state after the rock was pushed into the goal area, the comparison results were always
838
      the same, as it was impossible to move the rock out of the goal area. We addressed this by evaluating
839
      not only from the current state but also from the initial state of the environment. This underscores
840   the importance of future research into detecting utility inconsistency before it occurs or avoiding
841   irreversible changes in general.
842
843
844   Small Inconsistent Rewards In the Rocks and Diamonds environment, the agent sometimes failed
845   to detect the utility inconsistency if the switch changed the reward of the stone to +1. Qualitative
846   analysis revealed that during inconsistency checks, our agent didn’t always learn the optimal policy
847   of pushing both the diamond and the rock into the goal area since the reward model didn’t learn
848   to add together those rewards. With just a rock in a goal area, and switch on, the reward was not
849   higher than just pushing the diamond in, so the agent didn’t learn to do that either. Thus, both the
850
      policy learned with inconsistent transition and the policy learned without it behaved identically and
      the inconsistency was not detected. After updating from such a transition, the agent’s current utility
851
      no longer assigned negative utility to trajectories pushing the rock when the lever was pressed. We
852
      sidestepped this issue by changing the reward for the rock to +10. This issue would also be resolved
853   if the reward model generalized better to add the rewards from different sources.

893   Our algorithm introduces several additional hyperparameters beyond those typically used by standard
894   RL algorithms:
895
896   Reward Model Architecture and Learning Rate Hyperparameters specify the architecture and
897   learning rate of the reward model Ṙ. Since learning a reward model is a supervised learning task,
898   these hyperparameters can be tuned on a dataset of transitions collected by any policy, using standard
899   methods such as cross-validation. The reward model architecture may be chosen to match the
900   Q-function Q̇.
901
902   Inconsistency check training steps l This parameter describes the number of updates to the
903   Q-function needed to predict the future policy based on a new transition. As shown in Figure 5a,
904   this value must be sufficiently large to update the learned values and corresponding policy. It can be
905   selected by artificially adding a transition that alters the optimal policy and observing the number of
906
      training steps required to learn the new policy.
907
      Inconsistency check rollout steps h This parameter controls the length of the trajectories used
908
      to compare two predicted policies. The trajectory length must be adequate to reveal behavioral
909
      differences between the policies. In this paper, we used a fixed, sufficiently large number. In episodic
910   tasks, a safe choice is the maximum episode length; in continuing tasks, a truncation horizon typically
911   used in training may be suitable. Computational costs can be reduced by choosing a smaller value
912   based on domain knowledge.
913
914   Number of inconsistency check rollouts k This parameter specifies the number of trajectories
915   obtained by rolling out each predicted policy for comparison. The required number depends on the
916   stochasticity of the environment and policies. If both the policy and environment are deterministic, k
917   can be set to 1. Otherwise, k can be selected using domain knowledge or replaced by employing a
      statistical significance test.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      From mastering video games (Mnih et al., 2015) to optimizing robotic control (Levine et al., 2016),
      reinforcement learning (RL) agents have solved a wide range of tasks by learning to maximize
034
      cumulative rewards. However, this reward-maximization paradigm has a significant flaw: agents
035
      may exploit poorly defined or incomplete reward functions, leading to a behavior known as reward
036   hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), where the agent maximizes the reward signal but fails to meet the
037   designer’s true objectives.
038
039
      For instance, an RL agent tasked with stacking blocks instead flipped them, exploiting a reward based
      on the height of the bottom face of a block (Popov et al., 2017). Similarly, a robot arm manipulated
040
      objects in arbitrary ways that exploited a classifier-based reward system, tricking it into labeling
041
      incorrect actions as successful due to insufficient negative examples (Singh et al., 2019). Ibarz et al.
042   (2018) describe reward model exploitation in Atari games, where agents exploit flaws in reward
043   functions learned from human preferences and demonstrations. These incidents underscore that while
044   RL agents may maximize rewards, their learned behaviors often diverge from the goals intended by
045   the reward designers.
046
      As RL systems scale to more complex, safety-critical applications like autonomous driving (Kiran
047
      et al., 2021) and medical diagnostics (Ghesu et al., 2017), ensuring reliable and safe agent behavior
048   becomes increasingly important. Pan et al. (2022) showed that reward hacking becomes more
049   common as models grow in complexity. Moreover, Denison et al. (2024) demonstrated that agents
050   based on large language models, trained with outcome-based rewards, can generalize to changing the
051   code of their own reward functions. Reward hacking also becomes more prominent with increased
052   reasoning capabilities. For example, during testing of the o1-preview (pre-mitigation) language
053   model on a Capture the Flag (CTF) challenge, the model encountered a bug that prevented the target
      container from starting. Rather than solving the challenge as intended, the model used nmap to scan


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the network, discovered a misconfigured Docker daemon API, and exploited it to start the container
055   and read the flag via the Docker API, bypassing the original task altogether (OpenAI, 2024).
056
057   In this paper, we frame reward hacking within the General Utility RL (GU-RL) formalism (Zahavy
058
      et al., 2021; Geist et al., 2022). We describe an agent that optimizes a learned utility function, which
      assigns value to trajectories based on past observed rewards. Many instances of reward hacking,
059
      such as manipulating the reward provision process (Everitt et al., 2021) and tampering with the
060
      sensors (Ring & Orseau, 2011), can be viewed as inconsistent updates to the utility function. We
061   define an update as inconsistent when the trajectories produced by a policy optimized for the updated
062   utility function would be evaluated poorly by the prior utility function. To address this issue, we
063   introduce Modification-Considering Value Learning (MC-VL). In MC-VL, the agent updates its
064   utility function based on the observed rewards, similar to value-based RL, but it also predicts the
065   long-term consequences of potential updates and can reject them. In our formulation, avoiding
066   inconsistent utility updates is an optimal behavior.
067   For example, consider a robot trained to grasp objects using human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017).
068   A standard RL agent, if rewarded for positioning its manipulator between the object and the camera
069   in the middle of the training, can exploit this reward by learning to repeat that behavior (OpenAI,
070   2017). In contrast, an MC-VL agent would first forecast the consequences of updating its utility
071   function based on this new reward. Drawing from prior experiences where positive rewards were
072   given only for positioning the manipulator near the object, the MC-VL agent might predict low utility
073   for positioning the manipulator in front of the camera. As a result, the agent would reject the update,
074   staying focused on the intended grasping task.
075   Several prior works have discussed the theoretical possibility of mitigating reward or sensor tampering
076   using current utility optimization, where an agent evaluates potential changes to its utility function
077   using its current utility function (Orseau & Ring, 2011; Hibbard, 2012; Everitt et al., 2016; 2021).
078   Dewey (2011) suggested learning the utility function from past observations. However, to the best
079   of our knowledge, no prior work has formalized this within the GU-RL framework, applied this
080   idea to standard RL environments, or implemented such an agent. In this work, we provide an
081
      algorithm to learn the utility function, estimate future policies, and compare them using the current
      utility function. Additionally, we introduce a learning setup where the initial utility function is
082
      learned in a Safe sandbox version of the environment before transitioning to the Full version. Our
083
      experiments, conducted across various environments, including benchmarks adapted from the AI
084   Safety Gridworlds (Leike et al., 2017), are, to the best of our knowledge, the first to demonstrate the
085   ability to prevent reward hacking in these environments. Furthermore, our results provide insights
086   into the key parameters influencing MC-VL performance, laying the groundwork for further research
087   on preventing reward hacking in RL.
088
089
090   2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   The problem of agents learning unintended behaviors by exploiting misspecified training signals
093   has been extensively discussed in the literature as reward hacking (Skalse et al., 2022), reward
094   gaming (Leike et al., 2018), or specification gaming (Krakovna et al., 2020). Krakovna et al. (2020)
095   provide a comprehensive overview of these behaviors across RL and other domains. The theoretical
096   foundations for understanding reward hacking are explored by Skalse et al. (2022), who argue that
097   preventing reward hacking requires either limiting the agent’s policy space or carefully controlling
098
      the optimization process.
099   Laidlaw et al. (2023) propose addressing reward hacking by regularizing the divergence between the
100   occupancy measures of the learned policy and a known safe policy. Unlike their approach, which
101   may overly restrict the agent’s ability to learn effective policies, our method does not require the
102   final policy to remain close to any predefined safe policy. Eisenstein et al. (2024) investigate whether
103   ensembles of reward models trained from human feedback can mitigate reward hacking, showing
104
      that while ensembles reduce the problem, they do not completely eliminate it. To avoid additional
      computational overhead, we do not use ensembles in this work, but they could complement our
105
      method by improving the robustness of the learned utility function.
106
107   A specific form of reward hacking, where an agent manipulates the mechanism by which it receives
      rewards, is known as wireheading (Amodei et al., 2016; Taylor et al., 2016; Everitt & Hutter, 2016;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Majha et al., 2019) or reward tampering (Kumar et al., 2020; Everitt et al., 2021). Related phenomena,
109   where an agent manipulates its sensory inputs to deceive the reward system, are discussed as delusion-
110   boxing (Ring & Orseau, 2011), measurement tampering (Roger et al., 2023), and reward-input
111   tampering (Everitt et al., 2021). Several studies have hypothesized that current utility optimization
112   could mitigate reward or sensor tampering (Yudkowsky, 2011; Yampolskiy, 2014; Hibbard, 2012).
113   One of the earliest discussions of this issue is in by Schmidhuber (2003), who developed the concept
114   of Gödel-machine agents, capable of modifying their own source code, including the utility function.
115   They suggested that such modifications should only occur if the new values are provably better
116   according to the old ones. However, none of these works addressed learning the utility function or
117
      described the optimization process in full detail.
118   Dewey (2011) introduced the concept of Value-Learning Agents, which learn and optimize a utility
119   function based on past observations as a potential solution to reward tampering. Everitt & Hutter
120   (2016) considered a setting where the agent learns a posterior given a prior over manually specified
121   utility functions, proposing an agent that is not incentivized to tamper with its reward signal by
122   selecting actions that do not alter its beliefs about the posterior. More recently, Everitt et al. (2021)
123
      formalized conditions under which an agent optimizing its current reward function would lack the
      incentive to tamper with the reward signal. Our work suggests an implementation of value learning in
124
      standard RL environments, where the utility function is learned from the past rewards. Additionally,
125
      our method is applicable to other instances of reward hacking beyond reward tampering. Moreover, it
126   aims to prevent reward hacking, rather than simply removing the incentive for it.

129   3    BACKGROUND
130
131   We consider the usual Reinforcement Learning (RL) setup, where an agent learns to make decisions
132   by interacting with an environment and receiving feedback in the form of rewards (Sutton & Barto,
133   2018). This interaction is modeled as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman, 2014) defined by
134   the tuple (S, A, P, R, ρ, γ), where S is the set of states, A is the set of actions, P : S × A × S → R is
135   the transition kernel, R : S × A → R is the reward function, ρ is the initial state distribution, and γ is
136   the discount factor. The objective in a standard RL is to learn a policy P∞ π : S → A that maximizes the
137   expected return, defined as the cumulative discounted reward Eπ [ t=0 γ t R(st , at )]. The expected
138
      return from taking action a in state s and subsequently following policy π is called state-action value
      function and denoted as Qπ (s, a).

143
      using neural networks, where θ are the network parameters. Both algorithms store past experiences in
      a replay buffer and update network parameters by minimizing a loss L(θ) on the temporal-difference
144
      error based on the Bellman equation:

154   General-Utility RL (GU-RL) In this work, we focus on an agent that optimizes its current utility

      framework that encompasses tasks like risk-sensitive RL (Mihatsch & Neuneier, 2002), apprenticeship

      Initial Utility Function An MC-VL agent described in Algorithm 2 requires some initial utility
      function as input. In this work, we propose to learn this initial utility function in a Safe sandbox
293
      version of the environment, where unintended behaviors cannot be discovered by the exploratory
294
      policy. Examples of Safe environments include simulations or closely monitored lab settings where
295   the experiment can be stopped and restarted without consequences if undesired behaviors are detected.
296   To differentiate from the Safe version, we refer to the broader environment as the Full environment.
297   This Full environment may include the Safe one, for example, if the agent’s operational scope is
298   expanded beyond a restricted lab setting. Alternatively, the Safe and Full environments may be
299   distinct, such as when transitioning from simulation to real-world deployment. For the proposed
300   approach to perform effectively, however, the Safe and Full environments must be sufficiently similar
301   to allow for successful generalization of the learned utility function.

      5.4   A BLATIONS AND SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS
515
      We tested several alternative schemes for utility inconsistency detection and mitigation. As shown
516   in Figure 4a, checking all transitions for utility inconsistency yields similar results to checking
517   only those where the predicted reward significantly differs from the observed reward. However,
518   discarding all such transitions prevents the algorithm from learning an optimal non-hacking policy.
519   Comparing policies before and after each gradient step without forecasting future policies also fails
520   to prevent reward hacking. Replacing the reward of inconsistent transitions with large negative values
521   is less effective at preventing reward hacking than not adding them to the replay buffer. Having such
522   transitions in the replay buffer prevents the algorithm from forecasting the correct future policy when
523   checking for inconsistency, and over time the replay buffer gets populated with both transitions with
524
      positive and negative rewards, destabilizing training.
525   Figure 4b illustrates the performance with varying amounts of initial utility function training in the
526   Safe version. Remarkably, one run avoided reward hacking after just 100 steps of such training.
527   After 300 steps, all seeds converged to the optimal non-hacking policy, even though most had not
528   discovered the optimal policy within the Safe version by that point. This result suggests that future
529   systems might avoid reward hacking with only moderate training in a Safe environment. Additionally,
530
      this experiment shows that without any training in Safe environment (0 steps) our algorithm behaves
      like a regular DDQNidentical   to the baseline. Additional experiments are reported in Appendix B.

533   6     L IMITATIONS
534
535   While our method effectively mitigates reward hacking in several environments, it comes with compu-
536   tational costs, which are detailed in Appendix D. Checking for utility inconsistency requires forecast-
537   ing two future policies by training the corresponding action-value functions until convergence. In the
538   worst case, where each transition is checked for potential utility inconsistency, this process can lead to
539   a runtime slowdown proportional to the number of iterations used to update the action-value functions.
      A potential optimization discussed in this paper involves only checking transitions where the pre-


                                                               10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      dicted reward significantly deviates from the observed reward. However, this approach introduces an
541   additional hyperparameter for the threshold of predicted reward deviation. Balancing computational
542   efficiency with effectiveness is a key area for future research. Promising avenues include leveraging
543   Meta-RL (Schmidhuber, 1987) to accelerate policy forecastingor employing techniques like zero-shot
544   prompting (Kojima et al., 2022) of foundational vision-language-action models (Baker et al., 2022)
545   to estimate future behaviors without the need for extensive training.:::A:::::::::::
                                                                                      particularly ::::::::
                                                                                                   promising
546   direction is in-context  RL  (Laskin  et al., 2022) which   can  learn new behaviors
      :::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
                                                                                            in-context during
547   inference, quickly  and without  costly training (Bauer
      ::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::::
                                                               et al., 2023).
548   Another limitation is that our approach addresses only a subset of reward hacking scenarios. Specifi-
549   cally, it depends on the reward model and value function generalizing correctly to novel trajectories.
550   This approach may not address reward hacking issues caused by incorrect reward shaping, like in
551   the CoastRunners problem (OpenAI, 2023). In this case, if the agent already learned about a small
552   positive reward (e.g., knocking over a target), the agent’s current utility function may assign high
553   utility to behaviors that exploit this reward, even if they fail to achieve the final goal (completing the
554   loop). Alternative methods, such as potential-based reward shaping (Ng et al., 1999), may be more
555
      appropriate for addressing such issues.
556   Finally, our current implementation assumes access to rollouts from the true environment transition
557   model, while only the reward model is learned. Extending our approach to work with learned latent
558   transition models represents a promising direction for future research. Additionally, using a learned
559   world model to predict utility-inconsistent transitions before they occur could further enhance the
560   method’s applicability and efficiency. Improvements to computational efficiency and the integration
561
      of learned transition models would also enable testing our method in more complex environments,
      which is an important direction of future work.

565   7    C ONCLUSION
566
567
568   In this work, we introduced Modification-Considering Value Learning, an algorithm that allows an
569   agent to optimize its current utility function, learned from observed transitions, while considering
570   the future consequences of utility updates. Using the General Utility RL framework, we formalized
571   the concept of current utility optimization. Our implementationimplementations,
                                                                             :::::::::::::
                                                                                                MC-DDQN ,
572   demonstrated its:::
                       and:::::::::
                           MC-TD3, :::::::::::
                                      demonstrated :::
                                                     the ability to avoid reward hacking in several previously
573
      unsolved environments. Furthermore, we experimentally showed that our algorithm can improve the
      policy performance while remaining aligned with the initial objectives.
574
575   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first implementation of an agent that optimizes its utility
576   function while considering the potential consequences of modifying it. We believe that studying such
577   agents is an important direction for future research in AI safety, especially as AI systems become
578   more general and aware of their environments and training processes (Berglund et al., 2023; Denison
579
      et al., 2024). One of the key contributions of this work is providing tools to model such agents using
      contemporary RL algorithms.
580
581   Our empirical results also identify best practices for modeling these agents, including the importance
582   of forecasting future policies and excluding utility-inconsistent transitions from the training process.
583   Additionally, we introduced a set of modified environments designed for evaluating reward hacking,
584   where agents first learn what to value in Safe environments before continuing their training in Full
585
      environments. We believe this evaluation protocol offers a valuable framework for studying reward
      hacking and scaling solutions to real-world applications.

845   supervisors. In Figure 5c, it can be observed that increasing the number of supervisors from 1 to 10
846   leads to unstable utility inconsistency detection, despite the change being purely visual. Qualitative
847
      analysis revealed that our neural networks struggled to adapt to this distribution shift, resulting in
      predicted rewards deviating significantly from the ground truth.
848
849   Furthermore, we explored the impact of removing two walls from the Absent Supervisor environment
850   after training in the Safe version. Without these two walls, a shorter path to the goal is available
851   that bypasses the Punishment cell, although going through the Punishment cell remains faster. In
852   Figure 5d, it is evident that while our algorithm can learn a better policy that avoids the Punishment
853
      cell, the inconsistency detection becomes unreliable. This decline in reliability is attributed to the
      increased distribution shift between the Safe and Full versions of the environment.

856   C        Q UALITATIVE O BSERVATIONS
857
858   During our preliminary experiments, we encountered several instances where our algorithm failed
859   to detect utility inconsistencies, leading to reward hacking behaviors. Here, we describe these
860   occurrences and how we addressed them.
861
862   Utility Inconsistency Check Rollout Steps When using smaller inconsistency check rollout steps
863   h, we noticed that during the evaluation of future trajectories, the non-hacking policy sometimes
      needed to traverse several states with low rewards to reach a high reward region. In such cases,


                                                                    16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      the reward hacking policy, which remained stationary, had a higher estimated utility. Increasing h
865   resolved this issue.
866
867
868   Predicting Policy Changes without Additional Data Initially, we forecasted only one future
869   policy by training with the checked transition added to each mini-batch, and compared the resulting
870   policy to the current one. We didn’t train a second policy without adding anything to mini-batches.
871   However, in some cases, this led to situations where the copy learned better non-hacking behaviors
872   than the current policy because it was trained for longer, and thus performed better in evaluations on
873   average even if in some cases it expressed the reward-hacking behavior. The solution was to forecast
874   two policies, one with the checked transition added to each mini-batch and one without.
875
876
877
      Sensitivity to Stochasticity Evaluations in stochastic environments were found to be noisy. To
      mitigate this noise, we compared the two policies starting from the same set of states and using the
878
      same random seeds of the environment transition model. We also ensured consistency of the updates
879
      by keeping the random seeds the same while sampling mini-batches.
880
881
882   Handling Inconsistent Transitions We observed that if a utility-inconsistent transition was re-
883   moved from the replay buffer and another utility-inconsistent transition occurred in the same episode,
884   the algorithm sometimes failed to detect the inconsistency the second time because there was no
885   set of transitions in the replay buffer connecting this second utility-inconsistent transition to the
886   starting state. To resolve this issue, we reset the environment every time the agent detected utility
887   inconsistency. In practical applications, it would be reasonable to assume that after detecting potential
888   reward hacking, the agent would be returned to a safe state instead of continuing exploration.
889
890
891
      Irreversible Changes In the Rocks and Diamonds environment, when comparing policies starting
      from the current state after the rock was pushed into the goal area, the comparison results were always
892
      the same, as it was impossible to move the rock out of the goal area. We addressed this by evaluating
893
      not only from the current state but also from the initial state of the environment. This underscores
894   the importance of future research into detecting utility inconsistency before it occurs or avoiding
895   irreversible changes in general.
896
897
898   Small Inconsistent Rewards In the Rocks and Diamonds environment, the agent sometimes failed
899   to detect the utility inconsistency if the switch changed the reward of the stone to +1. Qualitative
900   analysis revealed that during inconsistency checks, our agent didn’t always learn the optimal policy
901   of pushing both the diamond and the rock into the goal area since the reward model didn’t learn
902   to add together those rewards. With just a rock in a goal area, and switch on, the reward was not
903   higher than just pushing the diamond in, so the agent didn’t learn to do that either. Thus, both the
904
      policy learned with inconsistent transition and the policy learned without it behaved identically and
      the inconsistency was not detected. After updating from such a transition, the agent’s current utility
905
      no longer assigned negative utility to trajectories pushing the rock when the lever was pressed. We
906
      sidestepped this issue by changing the reward for the rock to +10. This issue would also be resolved
907   if the reward model generalized better to add the rewards from different sources.
