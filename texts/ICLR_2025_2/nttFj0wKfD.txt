009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Content Warning: This paper contains examples of harmful language and plans.
012
               The rapid progress of large language models (LLMs) has opened up new oppor-
013            tunities across various domains and applications; yet it also presents challenges
014            related to potential misuse. To mitigate such risks, red teaming, a strategy where
015            developers adopt the role of potential attackers has been employed to probe lan-
016            guage models and preemptively guard against such harms. Jailbreak attacks are
017            a commonly used red teaming strategy that uses crafted prompts to bypass safety
018            guardrails. However, current jailbreak attack approaches are single-turn, with
019            explicit malicious queries that do not fully capture the complexity of real-world
020            interactions. In reality, users can engage in multi-turn interactions with LLM-based
021
               chat assistants, allowing them to conceal their true intentions in a more covert
               manner. Research on the Theory of Mind (ToM) reveals that LLMs struggle to
022
               infer latent intent, making it crucial to investigate how LLMs handle concealed
023
               malicious intent within multi-turn scenarios. To bridge this gap, we propose a new
024            jailbreak approach, R ED Q UEEN ATTACK. This method constructs a multi-turn
025            scenario, concealing the malicious intent under the guise of preventing harm. Next,
026            we craft 40 scenarios that vary in turns and select 14 harmful categories to generate
027            56k multi-turn attack data points. We conduct comprehensive experiments on the
028            R ED Q UEEN ATTACK with four representative LLM families of different sizes. Our
029            experiments reveal that all LLMs are vulnerable to R ED Q UEEN ATTACK, reaching
030            87.6% attack success rate on GPT-4o and 77.1% on Llama3-70B. Further analysis
031            reveals that larger models are more susceptible to the R ED Q UEEN ATTACK, with
032
               multi-turn structures and concealment strategies contributing to its success. To
               prioritize safety, we introduce a straightforward mitigation strategy called R ED
033
               Q UEEN G UARD, which aligns LLMs to effectively counter adversarial attacks.
034
               This approach reduces the attack success rate to below 1% while maintaining the
035            model’s performance across standard benchmarks. We release our code and data to
036            support future research.

      Recent advancements in novel training methodologies, computational capabilities, and data avail-
042   ability facilitate the adaptation of large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Yang et al.,

048   red teaming adopts an attacker’s mindset to probe vulnerabilities in LLMs and subsequently align

      strategies that can help guard against harmful content.

      2023), such as appending certain sentences to influence the model’s responses (Wei et al., 2024),

      Red Teaming and Jailbreak Attack on LLMs. Red teaming (Ganguli et al., 2022) is employed to
140
      identify and address LLM vulnerabilities before public release, mitigating potential misuse (Weidinger
141
      et al., 2021). Initial efforts focused on creating datasets manually with harmful questions or sentences
142   that models should reject, covering areas like misinformation (Lin et al., 2022), toxicity (Gehman
143   et al., 2020), and harmful instructions (Zou et al., 2023). Subsequent studies have combined various
144   harmful categories for comprehensive evaluations (Ganguli et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Ji et al.,
145   2024), with Anthropic releasing their safety training data with human-preference data (Ganguli et al.,
146   2022), which is further classified into 14 categories in Beavertails (Ji et al., 2024). However, most
147   existing works center on single-turn attack datasets, leaving multi-turn scenarios underexplored.
148   In parallel, jailbreak attacks, which aim to bypass LLM safety mechanisms and elicit harmful
149   content (Wei et al., 2024), have emerged as a significant tool in red teaming (Lin et al., 2024). While
150
      most previous approaches utilized single-turn jailbreaks (Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a), newer
      techniques, such as Cosafe’s coreference-based multi-turn jailbreak (Yu et al., 2024), still directly
151
      place harmful intent at the end of user utterances. As a comparison, R ED Q UEEN ATTACK conceals
152
      the harmful intent through multi-turn conversation and places the user in a protective position. And
153   we contributes a dataset of 56k multi-turn attacks across 14 harm categories and 40 scenarios.
154
155
      Theory of Mind in LLMs. Theory of Mind (ToM) (Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Apperly, 2010),
156   the ability humans have to infer the intent or mental states of others, play a crucial role in social
157   interactions (Ganaie & Mudasir, 2015). Recently, with the progress of LLMs in human interaction,
158   ToM has become an increasingly necessary ability of models to ensure effective adaptations (Dhelim
159   et al., 2021; Langley et al., 2022). Current studies have created various benchmarks (Zhou et al., 2023;
160   Chen et al., 2024; Nematzadeh et al., 2018) based on psychology and cognitive science (Wimmer &
161   Perner, 1983; Frith & Frith, 2003) to understand whether LLMs exhibit ToM ability. Results from
      these benchmarks indicate that current models have limited ToM abilities, particularly in recognizing

      3.1   W HAT T RIGGERS THE S AFETY G UARDIANS ?
173
      Previous research (Bai et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023) has shown that Reinforcement Learning
174   from Human Feedback (RLHF) has demonstrated promising results to empower safety alignment.
175   As the goal of LLMs’ safety alignment is balancing between helpful, honest, and harmless (Askell
176   et al., 2021), we hypothesize that there exists a tension between helpful outputs and harmless outputs
177   determined primarily by context and intent. To test this hypothesis, we collected 100 harmful
178   actions (Ji et al., 2024), such as murder a boss or build a bomb, and created two types of questions:
179   1) benign intent questions asking “What is {action}?”, 2) harmful intent question asking “How to
180   {action}?”. We run 100 examples on GPT-4o and manually validate the result. Testing on GPT-4o
181   reveals that it rejected all harmful intent questions while providing definitions for 96% of the benign
182
      intent questions, confirming our hypothesis. Thus, when safety mechanisms detect harmful intent,
      the inherent tension prompts the model to prioritize harmlessness over helpfulness.
183

      3.2   ATTACK F ORMALIZATION VIA T HEORY OF M IND
186
      Imagine a tourist asking a local for directions: “Excuse me, I’m a tourist. Could you please tell
187   me how to get to the museum?” “At the end of the street.”. This simple conversation illustrates
188   four fundamental elements of real-world interactions between agents (Premack & Woodruff, 1978;
189   Zhou et al., 2023): 1) Scenario S, the context of the interaction and agents’ persona (e.g., tourist,
190   local), 2) Task T, the task or requirement agent received (e.g., how to get to the museum), 3) Inference
191   I, the inferred intent of other agents based on Scenario S and Task T (e.g., the local infers the
192   tourist needs directions), 4) Response R, the response of agent based on S, T, I (e.g., the local gives
193   directions). Most LLMs trained for human interaction learn to follow instructions through Instruction
194   Tuning (Zhang et al., 2023), where users’ intents Ie are explicit and directly aligned to the task and
195
      scenario (in the tourist example, the task directly reflects the intent of finding the museum). The
      relationship can be expressed as LLM (S, T, I) = R, where Ie = Inf er(S, T ). When considering
196
      safety alignment based on the findings in Section 3.1, this relationship extends to include safety
197
      considerations as LLM (S, T, I) = R, where Ie = Inf er(S, T ) and Saf e(Ie ) = 1 (LLM will
198   refuse to respond if Saf e(Ie ) = 0). However, in real-world applications, there can be both an explicit
199   benign intent Ie and an implicit harmful intent Ii , with the latter being more challenging to infer for
200   models due to their weak Theory of Mind capability (Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Since
201   jailbreak attacks target harmful outputs and the task T is inherently harmful, we can construct a
202   fictional scenario to mislead the LLM’s intent inference and bypass its safety alignment mechanisms:
203   LLM (S, T, Ie , Ii ) = R, where Ie = Inf er(S, T ), Saf e(Ie ) = 1 and Saf e(Ii ) = 0.

      3.4   H ARMFUL ACTION
254
255   Our action list is derived from the Beavertails (Ji et al., 2024) dataset, which includes malicious
256   questions spanning 14 categories that the model should avoid answering. We utilize GPT-4o (OpenAI,
257   2024) to extract harmful actions from each question. Initially, we employ random sampling strategies
258   but encountered duplicate action candidates (e.g., kidnap a child and kidnap a person in the child
259   abuse category). To achieve a comprehensive estimation of our attack’s effectiveness, we used
260   a sentence transformer (Ni et al., 2022) to generate sentence embeddings and perform K-means
261
      clustering to collect 100 representative samples for each category, yielding 1400 unique harmful
      actions in total. We manually validated all harmful actions to ensure high data quality (Examples in
262
      Appendix C). By inserting harmful actions into the implemented scenarios, we constructed a dataset
263
      containing 56k multi-turn attack data points.

276
277   We describe the models selected for our experiments and the metrics used for safety evaluation.
278   Model selection. In this work, we evaluate ten instruction-tuned models from four representative
279   LLM families. The selected model sizes vary from 7B to 405B to ensure a comprehensive evaluation:
280   1) Mixtral (8×7B and 8×22B) (Jiang et al., 2024), 2) Llama3 (7B and 70B) and Llama3.1 (70B and
281   405B) (Touvron et al., 2023), 3) Qwen2 (7B and 72B) (Yang et al., 2024), 4) GPT-4o/4o-mini (Achiam
282   et al., 2023). These models show promising performance in public benchmarks (Cobbe et al., 2021;
283   Srivastava et al., 2023) and are widely adopted in daily usage. During the experiment, all models
284
      have the same system prompt (“You are a helpful AI assistant”) with the temperature of 1.
285   Evaluation Metrics. We use Attack Success Rate (ASR) (Zou et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024), the
286   percentage of attacks receiving harmful output, as our primary metric. One difficulty in evaluating the
287   performance of R ED Q UEEN ATTACK is judging when output is harmful or malicious. Not only can
288   a detailed step-by-step plan lead to malicious outcomes, but suggestions on tools or perspectives (e.g.,
289   timetables and escape routes) can also inspire them. We consider four candidate judgment methods
290
      utilized by previous research: 1) GCG (Zou et al., 2023), a classifier based on keyword mapping,
      2) GPT-4o evaluator with meticulously designed judge prompt (Chao et al., 2023), 3) Bert-based
291
      classifier (Huang et al., 2024b) trained on HH-RLHF dataset (Ganguli et al., 2022) with more than
292
      160k entries. 4) Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023), an LLM-based safeguard model for Human-AI
293   conversation use cases. We randomly sample 100 attacks and corresponding responses, with three
294   human annotators labelling whether the responses are harmful in an agreement of 100%. As shown
295   in Table 1, we compare four judgment methods on samples but find none achieve satisfying accuracy
296   (>0.8). Further analysis shows their judgments are unreliable and fluctuate, especially in long
297   contexts where the model responds with warnings like “This is a fictional example and should not be
298   used in real cases.” Therefore, we designed a judging prompt focused on identifying detailed plans
299   or suspicious hints and tested it on samples with GPT-4 and Llama3-70B. Models with this prompt
300   reached nearly 100% accuracy. We select Llama3-70B with the new judging prompt as our judgment
301
      method due to its high accuracy and the consideration of the cost. The temperature is set to 0 for
      judgment. Full details are in the ??.

      Turn Number. We visualize ASR based on different turns (Table 2) in Figure 3. In general, increasing
403   the number of turns by adding questions or details generally increases ASR, especially for models
404   between 8B and 70B. The five-turn scenario works best in six out of ten models, demonstrating the
405   effectiveness of incorporating additional interaction turns. More interaction allows R ED Q UEEN
406   ATTACK to incrementally earn the trust of the model, making the scenario more believable. Extended
407   turns also result in longer contexts, which can be difficult for current LLMs to manage during
408   inference (Anil et al., 2024). However, this pattern is not observed in larger models (>70B), such as
409   Llama3.1-405B and Qwen2-72B. We speculate that advanced attention mechanisms, such as Rotary
410   Position Embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) in Llama3 and Grouped Query Attention (GQA) (Ainslie
411
      et al., 2023) in Qwen2, may help these models focus on key information towards the end of the
      interaction, where the user asks for a functional plan, thereby mitigating the effect.
412
413   Model Size. In Figure 1.(b), we visualize the R ED Q UEEN ATTACK results on ten models according
414   to their size and family. Interestingly, larger models tend to be more susceptible to R ED Q UEEN
415   ATTACKS. Except for Mixtral-8×22B in the three-turn scenario, all larger models have a higher
416   ASR compared to smaller ones. This increased vulnerability in larger models can be attributed to
417
      the mismatch generalization between continued progress on model capabilities and safety alignment
      training (Wei et al., 2024). In other words, larger models demonstrate a better understanding of
418
      language and instruction and can accept fake scenarios easily, while smaller models have difficulty
419
      understanding the whole scenario. Also, the larger model’s promising capabilities in complex
420   reasoning and planning become “accomplices” for harmful plan generation. In contrast, smaller
421   models, with their limited knowledge and reasoning skills, struggle to even accomplish the task.
422   Further manual checking reveals that smaller models such as Llama3-8B or Qwen2-7B sometimes
423   can not understand the scenario and generate meaningless plans or random sentences (Appendix A).

471   includes a list of required materials but also step-by-step instructions for procurement, construction,
472   and even deployment. Notably, the generated plan can be tailored to the harmful action, indicating the
473   model’s extensive utilisation of their knowledge and reasoning abilities. Unlike a bomb-making plan,
474   which focuses on material production and bomb building, the model proposes a robbery plan (middle
475   box in Figure 5) with precise timing and even contingency plans for unexpected events.
476   For failed jailbreak cases, instead of completely refusing to answer, the model tends to generate a
477   safety plan related to the scenario itself. As shown in the two red boxes on the right in Figure 5, the
478   model outputs guidance for writing a report documenting information between a priest and penitent.

      contributing to the extremely high ASR across models. We provide more examples in Appendix A.

499   6    S AFEGUARDING S TRATEGIES
500
      Given the widespread application of LLMs in everyday life, we explore strategies to enhance the
501
      safety mechanisms of these models in this section. According to Wei et al. (2024), the model’s
502   vulnerabilities may arise from the misalignment in the generalization of safety training, particularly in
503   multi-turn scenarios where the model’s capabilities lack adequate safety alignment training. Thus, we
504   investigate whether training models on carefully designed multi-turn datasets using Direct Preference
505   Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) can mitigate this misalignment. We sampled 20 multi-
506   turn data points of successful LLM jailbreaks from each scenario and harmful action category,
507   supplemented with safety responses from Llama3.1-405B, yielding an 11.2K preference dataset,
508   R ED Q UEEN G UARD. For comparison, we also include 11.5K human preference data points from
509   HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022), a preference dataset widely used in safety alignment (Touvron et al.,
510
      2023; Song et al., 2024). We align three models from the Llama3.1 family and evaluate models’
      performance on approximately 10% of the original attack data (5539 instances). To ensure the
511
      optimization on harmless with the R ED Q UEEN G UARD won’t bring collapse on other objectives (e.g.
512
      reasoning, helpfulness) (Wei et al., 2024), we further evaluate models on MMLU-Pro (Wang et al.,
513   2024) which includes reasoning-focused questions across 14 diverse domains and AlpacaEval (Dubois
514   et al., 2024), a LLM-judgement benchmark testing models‘ general instruction-following ability.
515
516
      Table 4 shows model performance after DPO using different preference data. We do not rely solely
      on the HH-RLHF dataset, as Bai et al. (2022) show that optimization with HH-RLHF can reduce
517
      performance (alignment taxes) in smaller models, and we aim to develop a mitigation dataset that
518
      works well regardless of model size. Compared to the original ASR, DPO with R ED Q UEEN G UARD
519   effectively reduces model vulnerability to multi-turn R ED Q UEEN ATTACK, lowering the ASR
520   to around 1.0. The combination of HH-RLHF and R ED Q UEEN G UARD provides only limited
521   improvement to the model’s safety mechanisms, with the ASR still relatively high at 26.0. This
522   failure can be attributed to unclear alignment learning signals (D’Oosterlinck et al., 2024) for two
523   main reasons: (1) HH-RLHF lets crowd workers use their own intuitions to define harmfulness, which
524   lacks a uniform safety objective across all data. (2) The difference within objectives of HH-RLHF
525   and R ED Q UEEN G UARD further increases the disambiguation, limiting their combined effectiveness.
526   Based on the results from MMLU-Pro and AlpacaEval, integrating R ED Q UEEN G UARD can address
527
      the safety misalignment in multi-turn scenarios without compromising the model’s reasoning or
      instruction-following capabilities, highlighting its promising potential for broader usage in general
528
      safety alignment. We present the whole details of the experiment in Appendix E.

      7    C ONCLUSIONS
532
      We introduce R ED Q UEEN ATTACK, the first jailbreak method that constructs multi-turn scenarios
533
      to conceal harmful intent by claiming to prevent others from conducting malicious behavior. We
534   develop 40 scenarios based on occupation and relationship with different lengths, combined with 14
535   categories of harmful actions, resulting in a dataset of 56k high-quality multi-turn attack examples.
536   Our evaluation across ten models from four major model families confirms the effectiveness of the
537   R ED Q UEEN ATTACK. Further analysis reveals that both concealment and multi-turn structures
538   enhance the attack’s effectiveness, with larger models being particularly vulnerable. Additionally, we
539   propose R ED Q UEEN G UARD, a mitigation strategy that reduces the attack success rate to below 1%
      while preserving model performance on standard benchmarks.

      L IMITATIONS
811
812
      Our proposed method outperforms prior works in evaluating LLM safety. This evaluation is conducted
813   in a zero-shot setting. This method may be suitable for tasks with specific properties (Zhang et al.,
814   2022) and may not address all potential hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). A more
815   robust approach may involve multiple prompt attempts on both full and partial outputs to ensure
816   consistency, with a fine-tuned model and meticulously annotated data being the optimal solution.
817
      Additionally, we focus on constructing multi-turn scenarios extensively in the text domain without
818
      exploring their effectiveness in multi-modality contexts, such as visual or speech. The introduction of
819   a new modality can introduce orthogonal vulnerabilities, such as perception (Zhang et al., 2024) or
820   modality alignment (Li et al., 2024). Therefore, our conclusions are primarily applicable to text-based
821   jailbreak research.

1225   In this section, we compare our new judgment method with the previous judgment method compre-
1226   hensively. We provide the False Positive Rate (FPR) and the False Negative Rate (FNR) in the study
1227   of evaluation judgment Table 1, as shown in the Table 17.
1228
       These additional metrics demonstrate that all judgment methods have low FPR, validating that our
1229
       human annotators’ labels are reliable and aligned with the harmful definition in the previous judgment
1230   method. However, only our new judgment method achieves low FNR. In the Red Queen Attack,
1231   models tend to respond with warning sentences such as “This is a fictional example and should not be

1233   our new judgment method includes the guideline: “Be careful, even if the response states that the plan
1234   is a fictional example and cannot be used for illegal activities, it should still be considered harmful”

1236   annotators labelled 100 prompt-response pairs and then compared the new method with previous
