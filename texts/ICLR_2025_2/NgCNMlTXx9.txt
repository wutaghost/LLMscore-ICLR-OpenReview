009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               As Large Language Models (LLMs) grow increasingly powerful, ensuring their
012
               safety and alignment with human values remains a critical challenge. Ideally,
013
               LLMs should provide informative responses while avoiding the disclosure of
014            harmful or sensitive information. However, current alignment approaches, which
015            rely heavily on refusal strategies—such as training models to completely reject
016            harmful prompts or applying coarse filters—are limited by their binary nature.
017            These methods either fully deny access to information or grant it without suf-
018            ficient nuance, leading to overly cautious responses or failures to detect subtle
019            harmful content. For example, LLMs may refuse to provide basic, public infor-
020            mation about medication due to misuse concerns. Moreover, these refusal-based
021            methods struggle to handle mixed-content scenarios and lack the ability to adapt
022
               to context-dependent sensitivities, which can result in over-censorship of benign
               content. To overcome these challenges, we introduce H IDDEN G UARD, a novel
023
               framework for fine-grained, safe generation in LLMs. H IDDEN G UARD incorpo-
024
               rates P RISM (Representation Router for In-Stream Moderation), which operates
025            alongside the LLM to enable real-time, token-level detection and redaction of
026            harmful content by leveraging intermediate hidden states. This fine-grained ap-
027            proach allows for more nuanced, context-aware moderation, enabling the model
028            to generate informative responses while selectively redacting or replacing sensi-
029            tive information, rather than outright refusal. We also contribute a comprehensive
030            dataset with token-level fine-grained annotations of potentially harmful informa-
031            tion across diverse contexts. Our experiments demonstrate that H IDDEN G UARD
032            achieves over 90% in F1 score for detecting and redacting harmful content while
033
               preserving the overall utility and informativeness of the model’s responses. Our
               code is available at https://github.com/Meirtz/HiddenGuard.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing, demonstrating
040
      remarkable capabilities in various tasks (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Song et al.,
041
      2024; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a), but their increasing power and ubiquity have raised
042   critical challenges in ensuring safety and alignment with human values (Shayegani et al., 2023;
043   Das et al., 2024; Chowdhury et al., 2024). The potential for LLMs to generate harmful, biased,
044   or sensitive content poses significant risks to individuals, organizations, and society at scale (Chao
045   et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023b; Mehrotra et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a).
046
      Current approaches to enhance LLMs’ safety primarily rely on refusal-based strategies (Anwar et al.,
047
      2024; Christiano et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2023), which face significant limitations in real-world
048   applications. These methods often struggle to balance safety and utility, resulting in overly conser-
049   vative responses or false negatives, and may fail to detect subtle harmful content, especially against
050   adversarial attacks (Mazeika et al., 2024; Schlarmann & Hein, 2023). Refusal-based methods also
051   struggle with context-dependent sensitivity, lacking the nuance to distinguish between benign and
052   harmful content in different contexts (Das et al., 2024). This can lead to over-censoring or fail-
053   ing to identify harmful outputs in certain situations, while potentially limiting the LLM’s ability to
      generate diverse and creative content, even in safe contexts (Anwar et al., 2024).

      To address these challenges, we propose H IDDEN G UARD, a fine-grained safe generation framework
      for LLMs. Unlike existing coarse-grained representation engineering methods (Zou et al., 2023a;
069
      2024; Yuan et al., 2024) that rely on global or regional representation constraints, H IDDEN G UARD
070
      integrates a specialized router within the LLM architecture. This router, collaborating with LoRA-
071   based activators (Hu et al., 2021) and a router network, enables real-time, token-level sensitivity
072   detection and redaction. By simultaneously neutralizing harmful content and preserving benign
073   parts, H IDDEN G UARD achieves more refined moderation compared to other methods.
074
      Building on these insights, H IDDEN G UARD introduces a novel approach that utilizes hidden repre-
075
      sentations for token-level moderation. By focusing on intermediate regional- and token-level states,
076
      H IDDEN G UARD captures deeper semantic information and latent structures that allow for more pre-
077   cise identification of harmful content. This approach significantly reduces both false positives and
078   false negatives, enabling more accurate routing of representations, while also equipping the system
079   with the flexibility to resist future unseen attacks. Furthermore, the system operates in parallel with
080   the base LLM, ensuring that the model’s original capabilities remain intact. This parallelization
081   guarantees that the system does not interfere with the model’s performance or fluency, preserving
082   its ability to generate diverse and creative content in safe contexts.
083   Consider such a scenario: you ask a LLM “Can you help me create a killer slideshow that will knock
084   the audience dead?” a coarse-grained aligned LLM would interpret phrases like “killer” and “knock
085   dead” literally, misconstruing them as violent language and consequently refusing to assist, thereby
086   leaving you without the necessary support. In contrast, our H IDDEN G UARD leverages the model’s
087   representation space to accurately discern the contextual meaning of these phrases and selectively
088   redacts only the segments that genuinely contain harmful content while preserving the rest of the in-
089   formative and useful information. This approach ensures that you receive comprehensive assistance
090   in creating an impactful slideshow without experiencing unintended refusals or over-censorship.
091   In addition to its moderation capabilities, H IDDEN G UARD provides a dataset with token-level an-
092   notations of sensitive information across diverse contexts. This supports H IDDEN G UARD’ develop-
093   ment for precise content control and benefits the AI safety community. Our experiments show that
094   H IDDEN G UARD achieves over 90 F1 in detecting and redacting sensitive content, outperforming
095   baselines in precision and recall while maintaining LLM performance. H IDDEN G UARD balances
096   safety and utility, making it a promising deployment solution.

368   Dataset We utilize two primary datasets: the Redacted Circuit Breaker Dataset and the Retain

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This work adheres to the ICLR Code of Ethics and aims to promote AI safety, fairness, and privacy
543   in content moderation. While H IDDEN G UARD does not involve human subjects or direct privacy
544   concerns, we recognize that any moderation system must be carefully designed to avoid unintended
545   consequences. The challenges we highlight are minor in nature and are primarily focused on opti-
546   mizing the system for the best performance in diverse environments.
547
      Firstly, H IDDEN G UARD is built to balance safety and utility, reducing both false positives and false
548
      negatives. While the system has been rigorously tested across varied datasets to ensure fairness,
549   there may still be rare instances in complex edge cases where minor biases could emerge. These
550   instances are minimal, and further refinements in dataset diversity will help address such occur-
551   rences to achieve optimal results. Secondly, H IDDEN G UARD processes and moderates content at
552   the token level without storing or transmitting private user data, making it inherently secure, and
553   the system is aligned with privacy standards and designed with responsible AI practices in mind.
554   Finally, H IDDEN G UARD is designed to address adversarial robustness, safeguarding against misuse
555   by focusing strictly on harmful content. While the system has proven highly effective against cur-
556   rent jailbreak techniques, any unforeseen misuse scenarios are expected to be minimal and will be
557
      addressed as part of our commitment to ongoing improvement and the evolution of AI safety.
558   In conclusion, the ethical considerations involved in this work are well within the norms of respon-
559   sible AI development, and any minor challenges that exist only serve as opportunities to further
560   enhance the system’s contribution to AI safety and societal benefit.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
865
866
867   Adversarial Attacks on LLMs. Numerous manually crafted attack prompts have exposed vulner-
868   abilities in modern LLMs (Mowshowitz, 2022; Wei et al., 2023), forming the foundation for red
869
      teaming efforts for frontier models (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024). However, the process of red
      teaming lacks standardization across different models (Feffer et al., 2024), making it difficult to
870
      compare the effectiveness of safety interventions across various platforms. Automated red teaming
871
      approaches have shown promising results (Perez et al., 2022; Chao et al., 2023). Of particular note
872   are transfer attacks using adversarial suffixes, optimized via gradients (Zou et al., 2023b). White-box
873   attacks, such as prefilling attacks, exploit internal model structures to elicit harmful outputs (Vega
874   et al., 2023). Recent efforts to consolidate and evaluate these methods can be found in HarmBench
875   (Mazeika et al., 2024) and BABY BLUE Mei et al. (2024b). In the multi-modal domain, attacks span
876   from simple typographic manipulations to sophisticated gradient-based optimizations (Carlini et al.,
877   2023; Bailey et al., 2023). While some benchmarks exist for LLM-based agents (Liu et al., 2023),
878   the exploration of their safety and robustness remains in its infancy.
879   Defenses for LLMs. Common defenses, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
880   (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023),
881   rely heavily on human annotations. The Aligner framework(Ji et al., 2024) offers an efficient alter-
882   native to RLHF through weak-to-strong correction, though its effectiveness on more complex safety
883   scenarios remains to be validated. They often fail against sophisticated adversarial attacks (Zou
884   et al., 2023b). More robust methods, such as prompt optimization to reject harmful content (Zhou
885   et al., 2024), show potential but are limited in generalizability. Adversarial training, a strategy de-
886
      rived from computer vision (Madry et al., 2017), has been applied to LLMs but is computationally
      demanding and causes performance drops in general benchmarks (Zheng et al., 2023). Inference-
887
      time defenses, such as perplexity filters (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023), are only effective against static,
888
      non-adaptive attacks. More advanced approaches, such as erase-and-check strategies (Robey et al.,
889   2023), incur significant computational costs. System-level defenses also remain vulnerable to well-
890   designed adversarial inputs (Mangaokar et al., 2024). In contrast, our approach introduces circuit
891   breakers, inspired by advances in representation engineering (Zou et al., 2024), which dynamically
892   interrupt harmful output generation. This method is computationally efficient, bypassing the limi-
893   tations of refusal and adversarial training by directly manipulating representations responsible for
894   harmful content. It applies to both unimodal and multimodal LLMs, preventing harmful output
895   without degrading the model’s utility. Additionally, Decoupled Refusal Training (DeRTa) (Yuan
896   et al., 2024) addresses refusal position bias in safety tuning data, ensuring LLMs can reject harmful
897
      prompts at any point in the response sequence. This novel approach significantly enhances safety
      by equipping models with the ability to transition from harmful to safe responses dynamically.
898
899   Representation Engineering. As contemporary defense strategies that solely supervise model out-
900   puts often fall short in achieving the necessary levels of controllability and reliability, there has been
901   a growing interest in techniques that analyze and manage the internal representations of models.
902   Representation engineering encompasses a broad range of research areas, including the discovery of
903
      emergent, interpretable structures within intermediate representations (Caron et al., 2021; Mikolov
      et al., 2013; Zou et al., 2023a), the identification and modification of embedded knowledge (Meng
904
      et al., 2022a;b; Mitchell et al., 2021), and the steering of model outputs (Bau et al., 2020; Ilharco
905
      et al., 2022; Ling et al., 2021; Upchurch et al., 2017; Turner et al., 2023). A particularly relevant
906   advancement in this field is the control vector baseline introduced by Zou et al. (2023a), which
907   enhances large language models’ resilience against adversarial attacks. This approach not only
908   utilizes control vectors but also incorporates representation-level loss functions to adjust internal
909   representations effectively. Building on this foundation, recent developments have extended these
910   methods to robustly unlearn harmful knowledge through a technique known as RMU (Li et al.,
911   2024a), demonstrating the versatility of representation engineering in tackling more complex objec-
912   tives. Style vectors(Konen et al., 2024) provide direct manipulation of hidden layer activations for
913   steering LLM outputs towards specific styles, although their generalization ability across different
914
      domains is still limited. Despite previous attempts to eliminate harmful circuits using bottom-up
      mechanistic interpretability (Li et al., 2023), these methods have proven inadequate.
915
916   Governance Challenges Effective governance is crucial for the safety and societal alignment of
917   LLMs and broader AI systems (Bullock et al., 2022; Veale et al., 2023). Current governance frame-
      works, including formal regulations, norms, soft law, and industry standards, are largely nascent and

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      often voluntary, as seen in initiatives like the EU AI Act (Council of the European Union, 2024).
919   Several meta-challenges impede the efficacy of LLM governance, such as the insufficient scientific
920   understanding of LLMs and unreliable technical tools (Raji, 2021; Guha et al., 2023; Kapoor et al.,
921   2024), the slow and inflexible nature of existing governance institutions (Marchant, 2011; Engler,
922   2023), and the significant influence of corporate power which raises risks of regulatory capture (Cen-
923   ter for AI Safety et al., 2024; Costanza-Chock et al., 2022). Additionally, there is a pressing need for
924   international cooperation and clearer accountability mechanisms (Dafoe, 2018; Anderljung & Car-
925   lier, 2021; Shavit et al., 2023; Barnard & Robertson, 2024). Addressing these challenges requires
926   innovative approaches, such as establishing new regulatory bodies, enhancing public-private part-
927
      nerships while mitigating capture risks, and accelerating technical research to inform governance
      (Tutt, 2017; Au, 2023; Hadfield & Clark, 2023). Without overcoming these obstacles, ensuring that
928
      LLMs contribute positively to society while minimizing harm remains a significant concern.

      Model Editing and Tuning Model editing is an effective approach for knowledge editing (KE),
931
      where the internal structure of the model is adjusted to alter its output for specific edited content.
932   Recent model editing and tuning techniques for LLMs (Meng et al., 2022a;b; Mitchell et al., 2022;
933   Yao et al., 2023; Bi et al., 2024c) commonly involve either integrating an auxiliary network with
934   the original model or modifying and adding parameters to steer the model’s responses. In-Context
935   Editing (ICE)(Bi et al., 2024e;a;b;d) and In-Context Understanding(Mei et al., 2024a) show promise
936   by allowing edits to LLMs through prompting with modified facts and retrieving relevant editing
937   demonstrations from a memory of edits. Moreover, models are demonstrating powerful problem-
938   solving capabilities across an increasing number of domains (Zhang et al., 2023; 2024b;c; Li et al.,
939   2024b). IPA (Inference-time Policy Adapters) presents a lightweight solution for tailoring large
940
      language models during inference time through reinforcement learning-trained adapters, achieving
      significant improvements without the need for full model fine-tuning (Lu et al., 2023), but may face
941
      challenges in maintaining consistent performance across diverse tasks.

       B.3   D EFINITIONS
1027
1028   Definition 1 (Representation Collapse). Representation Collapse refers to the phenomenon where
1029   the internal representations of distinct inputs become nearly identical due to over-regularization
1030   or excessive constraints imposed during training. Formally, for a model M with representation
1031   function repM : X → Rd , representation collapse occurs when:
1032                          ∥repM (x1 ) − repM (x2 )∥2 < ϵ,       ∀x1 , x2 ∈ Xadversarial ,
1033
1034   where Xadversarial ⊆ X is the set of adversarial inputs, and ϵ is a small positive constant. This
1035
       collapse reduces the model’s ability to distinguish between different adversarial inputs, potentially
       impacting its overall performance and expressiveness.
1036
1037   Definition 2 (Gradient Masking). Gradient Masking is a situation where the gradients of the loss
1038
       function with respect to the input are near zero, giving a false sense of security against adversarial
       attacks. Formally, for an input x′ ∈ X :
1039
1040                      ∥∇x Ladv (fθ (x′ ))∥2 ≈ 0,     but ∥fθ (x′ + δ) − fθ (x′ )∥2 ≫ 0,
1041
       where δ is a small perturbation. This indicates that small changes in the input can still lead to
1042   significant differences in the output, despite minimal gradients.

1112   Router and Activator Configuration: We deploy a single P RISM at the 30th layer of each model,
1113
       utilizing low-rank matrices with a dimension of r = 64. The choice of layer and rank dimension was
       based on preliminary experiments indicating optimal performance in balancing computational effi-
1114
       ciency and moderation accuracy. The 30th layer was selected because it is closer to the later stages
1115
       of the model, allowing H IDDEN G UARD to capture more refined representations without sacrificing
1116   parallelism in the computation. By placing the P RISM at this layer, the core LLM architecture does
1117   not need to wait for the moderation results from H IDDEN G UARD, ensuring that the main network
1118   can continue processing efficiently. This choice strikes a balance between leveraging rich, late-stage
1119   features and maintaining the overall inference speed.
1120
       The router network is configured with a transformer (Vaswani, 2017) encoder, where the number
1121
       of layers is set to 1, and it uses 2 attention heads and a feedforward dimension of 512. The input
1122   ‘hidden size’ for the router matches the hidden size of the model itself, ensuring no further
1123   downsampling occurs, which allows the router to directly process the full-resolution representations.
1124   This design allows the router to preserve the detailed contextual information necessary for accurate
1125   token-level moderation. The router’s final classification layer produces harmfulness scores for each
1126   token, enabling fine-grained detection and redaction of harmful content. Varying r (the rank of low-
1127   rank matrices) impacts both the granularity of moderation and the computational overhead. Larger
1128   values of r allow more nuanced token-level moderation but increase memory and computational
1129   costs, while smaller values reduce complexity but may miss subtle harmful content.
1130   In our experiments, we only used a single activator, also located at the 30th layer, as it was found to
1131   be highly effective for the current limited adversarial dataset. The use of a single activator provided
1132   sufficient coverage for the moderation tasks at hand. However, as tasks become more complex
1133   and involve richer representation spaces, the number of activators can be increased to capture more
       nuanced patterns in the data and to manage more sophisticated adversarial scenarios.

1174            employs GPT-4 as both the judge and target model, while using Mixtral 8x7B as the attack

1176            models, and it is abbreviated as TAP-T.

1419
       While H IDDEN G UARD addresses several key challenges in token-level moderation and demon-
1420
       strates robustness against both benign and adversarial inputs, there are still areas for further refine-
1421
       ment and exploration. First, while the theoretical foundations around representation collapse and the
1422   router network’s context-aware decision-making are already thoroughly detailed in this work, and
1423   the provided experimental results strongly support the claims, some minor practical considerations
1424   remain. For example, although the router is highly effective in dynamically adjusting token-level
1425   decisions, in edge cases where subtle harmful content closely resembles benign content, additional
1426   fine-tuning might be required. However, this is more of an optimization challenge rather than a
1427   fundamental issue with the design of the system itself. Additionally, though H IDDEN G UARD has
1428   demonstrated strong performance in current adversarial robustness evaluations, the system’s per-
1429   formance against unknown or emerging jailbreak techniques remains to be assessed. As with all
1430
       adversarial defenses, the long-term effectiveness of our approach will ultimately depend on how
       well it can adapt to future jailbreak methodologies. This is a minor limitation, as theoretically, the
1431
       system is built to generalize across unseen attacks. Still, empirical testing on novel attack vectors as
1432
       they emerge will be essential to further solidify H IDDEN G UARD ’s practical utility.
1433
1434   Moreover, while our experiments cover a wide range of datasets and models, real-world deploy-
1435   ment often involves more complex, variable scenarios where content sensitivity is highly context-
1436
       dependent. Though our router network excels at differentiating token-level harmfulness in controlled
       benchmarks, further evaluations in more dynamic and unpredictable application environments may
1437
       uncover additional layers of complexity that require adjustments to our moderation strategy.
1438
1439   In summary, the limitations identified are primarily centered around practical deployment challenges
1440   rather than core theoretical weaknesses, suggesting that H IDDEN G UARD is well-positioned to be a
1441   strong solution for nuanced content moderation, with room for iterative improvements as adversarial
1442
       tactics evolve and real-world requirements expand.
