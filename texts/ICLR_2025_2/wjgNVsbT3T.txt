009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            As the application of Large Language Models (LLMs) expands, the demand for
012            reliable evaluations increases. Existing LLM evaluation benchmarks primarily
013            rely on static datasets, making it challenging to assess model performance in
014            dynamic interactions with users. Moreover, these benchmarks often depend on
015            specific background knowledge, complicating the measurement of a model’s log-
016            ical reasoning capabilities. Other dynamic evaluation methods based on strong
017
               models or manual efforts may introduce biases and incur high costs and time
               demands, hindering large-scale application. To address these issues, we pro-
018
               pose TurtleBench. TurtleBench collects real user guesses from our online Tur-
019
               tle Soup Puzzle1 platform that we developed. This approach allows for the rel-
020            atively dynamic generation of evaluation datasets, mitigating the risk of model
021            cheating while aligning assessments more closely with genuine user needs for
022            reasoning capabilities, thus enhancing the reliability of evaluations. TurtleBench
023            includes 1,532 user guesses along with the correctness of guesses after annota-
024            tion. Using this dataset, we thoroughly evaluated nine of the most advanced
025            LLMs available today. Notably, the OpenAI o1 series models did not achieve
026            leading results in these evaluations. We propose several hypotheses for further re-
027            search, such as “the latent reasoning of o1 utilizes trivial Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
028
               techniques” and “increasing CoT length not only provides reasoning benefits but
               also incurs noise costs.” The TurtleBench data and evaluation code are available
029
               at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TurtleBench-D52E.
030
               Note: The dataset mentioned in this paper may contain some elements of horror;
031
               please view selectively.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   As the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) continue to improve, they are increasingly
037   being applied across various scenarios such as e-commerce, healthcare, and daily conversations (Li
038   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). In these real-world contexts, LLMs must address a
039   wide range of user inquiries and provide logically coherent responses. However, the unpredictabil-
040   ity of user questions complicates the scenarios that models face, raising the bar for the reasoning
041   capabilities of LLMs. Thus, evaluating these models’ reasoning abilities is of significant impor-
042   tance (Liang et al., 2024a).
043   However, current model evaluation practices are plagued by issues such as fraud and data contam-
044   ination (Zhou et al., 2023). On one hand, we call for integrity and fairness in evaluation efforts;
045   on the other hand, the inherent limitations of many existing benchmarks cannot be overlooked. For
046   instance, benchmarks like MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and ARC (Clark et al., 2018), which
047   consist of single-turn static questions based on common sense and academic knowledge, contain
048   many memorization-based items. This evaluation method primarily assesses the model’s memory
049
      capacity, making it difficult to accurately measure its language comprehension and logical reasoning
      skills. Furthermore, since the test sets in these benchmarks are static, they may become contami-
050
      nated, compromising the reliability of the evaluation results. In contrast, MT-Bench (Zheng et al.,
051
      2023) is a multi-turn dialogue evaluation benchmark where models must respond to preset questions
052

080   and answer follow-up inquiries. However, the open-dialogue approach introduces new challenges,
081   as it does not provide clear standard answers, making the quality of model responses reliant on eval-
082   uations from strong models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023). Using GPT-4 as a judge may introduce
083   bias, with lower scores for certain models while being more lenient towards ChatGPT. Moreover,
084
      this evaluation method has its limitations, as it cannot assess models stronger than GPT-4. A bet-
      ter alternative is the Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024), which selects better-performing models
085
      through votes from real users. This method is straightforward and has a higher credibility. How-
086
      ever, for a new model to obtain reliable scores, it must undergo extensive public testing to gather
087   substantial user feedback, making its scores credible.
088
089   To address these limitations, we propose TurtleBench, a reliable benchmark for assessing LLM rea-
090
      soning capabilities. We designed and launched an online Turtle Soup game. As shown in the left
      half of Fig. 1, we present the surface and bottom story to the model, allowing it to determine the cor-
091
      rectness of user guesses. This Turtle Soup game encapsulates nearly all the information needed for
092
      reasoning, enabling the LLM to make judgments based on contextual information (the surface and
093   bottom story). This design ensures that the evaluation focuses primarily on reasoning capabilities
094   rather than knowledge recall, thereby enhancing the reliability of LLM evaluations. By collecting
095   user guesses inputted during the Turtle Soup game and conducting detailed multi-turn manual anno-
096   tations, we constructed a bilingual dataset in Chinese and English. Compared to existing benchmarks
097   for evaluating LLM reasoning capabilities, TurtleBench has three main advantages:
098
099          • No additional background knowledge required. All information needed for reasoning
100            evaluation in TurtleBench is contained within the task itself, limiting the assessment to the
101
               model’s reasoning capabilities without relying on external knowledge bases, thus avoiding
               unfair evaluations arising from differences in knowledge bases among models.
102
103          • Objective and quantifiable results. In the assessment of multi-turn dialogue benchmarks,
104            the output of the model is a piece of text, making it challenging to quantify model per-
105
               formance. TurtleBench quantifies the model’s reasoning ability through clear ground truth
               (Correct/Incorrect), eliminating interference from subjective factors.
106
107          • Dynamic data reduces the risk of cheating. Existing static benchmark datasets may be
               manipulated by some models during training to boost scores, whereas TurtleBench ensures

                   dynamic updates of evaluation data through continuously collected new guesses from users,
109                reducing the likelihood of models gaming fixed datasets for score inflation.

      Additionally, we systematically evaluated the performance of nine LLMs on TurtleBench. When
      assessing the OpenAI o1 models, we identified several directions for future enhancements in large
113
      reasoning models (Valmeekam et al., 2024), including the incorporation of more complex reasoning
114
      topologies in latent Chain-of-Thought (CoT) processes and dynamically selecting reasoning needs
115   for questions to mitigate the influence of noise tokens in reasoning.

      2       T URTLE B ENCH
119
120
121   In this section, we describe the details of collecting real user guess data for TurtleBench, including
122   data preprocessing and annotation, and present summary statistics of the dataset. The process of
123   dataset creation is illustrated in Fig. 1.
124
125
      2.1       DATA C OLLECTION
126
127
      We designed and launched a Turtle Soup Puzzle game2 specifically to collect user guesses for
128
      TurtleBench. Specifically, we first gathered 1,500 common Turtle Soup stories from the internet
129
      and filtered them down to 32 ethical and logically challenging stories to serve as the source for the
130   Turtle Soup Puzzle platform. Users are assigned a story during the game and make guesses based
131   solely on the available surface story. We used Claude-3.5-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024) as the judge to
132   determine whether players’ guesses are Correct, Incorrect, or Unknown. Users have eight opportuni-
133   ties to guess, and the answer is revealed immediately upon a correct guess or exhaustion of attempts.
134   As participation in the game increased, we noted a significant piece of user feedback: “LLM Judge-
135   ment Is Not Reasonable!” This negatively impacted the gaming experience, highlighting the need
136   for TurtleBench.
137   Within two weeks of the platform’s launch, over 4,000 users posed more than 26,000 guesses, which
138   we parsed from logs and saved as our raw dataset.
139
140
141   2.2       DATA P RE - PROCESSING
142
143   During the data preprocessing stage, we first removed duplicates from the 26,000 collected entries;
144   for example, “Is the Turtle Soup poisonous?” and “Is the soup he drank poisonous?” essentially
145   pose the same question. Next, we eliminated questions that could not be answered with Correct,
146   Incorrect, or Unknown, such as, “How old is the man this year?” Finally, we excluded ambiguous
147
      questions. For example, in the story “The Best Friend” (refer to Fig. 10), the guess “Did he do
      something to his wife’s best friend” contains the word “something,” which could refer to anything,
148
      making it ambiguous. Through these preprocessing steps, we could initially enhance the quality of
149
      the dataset.
150
151   In the annotation phase, we initially categorized entries into three classes: Correct, Incorrect, and
152   Unknown. However, during the annotation process, we found it challenging to distinguish between
153
      the labels for ”Incorrect” and ”Unknown” in many cases. For instance, in the story “The Turtle
      Soup” (see Fig. 9), both responses to the guess “The turtle is kept by a man” could be reasonable.
154
      To ensure evaluation stability, we categorized “Unknown” responses as “Incorrect,” resulting in a
155
      final classification of two categories: Correct and Incorrect. Ultimately, from the original 26,000 en-
156   tries, we annotated 4,448 guesses. We conducted preliminary tests across all LLMs and filtered out
157   simple questions that all models answered correctly. On the remaining 1,699 entries, we performed
158   a secondary confirmation of annotations. We ultimately obtained a dataset of 1,532 accurately an-
159   notated entries.

      3.1                     S ETUP
189
190   Models We evaluated nine top LLMs on the TurtleBench, covering both open-source and closed-
191   source models, as shown in Table 1. The o1 series models (o1-preview and o1-mini) (OpenAI,
192   2024b) and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a) represent OpenAI’s current state-of-the-art models. The
193   Claude series models developed by Anthropic were assessed, specifically the advanced Claude-
194   3.5-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024). Llama models (Dubey et al., 2024) are open-sourced by Meta, and
195   we conducted experiments on Llama-3.1-405B and Llama-3.1-70B. Additionally, we evaluated pop-
196   ular models recently developed by Chinese institutions, including Moonshot-v1-8k (MoonShot-AI,
197
      2024), DeepSeek-v2.5 (DeepSeek-AI, 2024), and Qwen-2-72B (Qwen-Team, 2024). For all closed-
      source models, we used their official APIs; for all open-source models, we utilized the Model-as-a-
198
      Service Provider, SiliconFlow’s API 3 .
199
200
      Settings When evaluating LLMs on the TurtleBench dataset, we ensured parameter settings were
201   consistent whenever possible. We set the temperature of all LLMs to 0 and top p to 0.9. Fur-
202   thermore, we uniformly employed two prompt templates: 0-shot and 2-shot templates. Complete
203   prompt templates can be found in Appendix A. It is important to note that OpenAI’s o1 series mod-
204   els (o1-preview and o1-mini) currently do not support custom parameters 4 , so we maintained the
205   default settings. Additionally, to save on API costs, we only evaluated the o1 series models in the
206   0-shot setting; for a related cost analysis, see Appendix E.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
387
388
      In real-world scenarios, the language understanding and reasoning capabilities of LLMs face in-
389   creasingly complex and diverse challenges, making reliable evaluations of LLMs a critical issue.
390   Although many benchmarks have been proposed, the reliability of existing evaluation methods still
391   faces several challenges (Yu et al., 2024).
392
      For example, benchmarks that evaluate models’ commonsense reasoning abilities often use static
393
      datasets and multiple-choice questions. These include MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021), ARC (Clark
394
      et al., 2018), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), AGIEval (Zhong et al., 2023), PIQA (Bisk et al.,
395   2020), and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). However, static datasets pose a risk of data leakage, where
396   models can overfit the test data to improve evaluation results (Zhou et al., 2023). Furthermore,
397   these benchmarks often heavily rely on background knowledge, making it difficult to disentangle
398   the model’s logical reasoning capabilities from the evaluation.
399
      At the same time, some studies have proposed multi-turn dynamic interactive evaluation bench-
400
      marks, such as MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), BotChat (Duan et al., 2024), and AgentBench (Liu
401   et al., 2023). These benchmarks typically do not have definitive correct answers, often relying on
402   powerful models like GPT-4 as judges. However, this evaluation method can lead to instability and
403   unreliability due to biases in the judge models, and it also tends to incur high costs.
404
      To address the issues in these benchmarks, some studies have proposed real-time benchmarks based
405
      on human interaction, such as Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024) and FlagEval (BAAI, 2024).
406
      While these methods are more credible, new models often require longer test periods to obtain
407   reliable scores, leading to high time costs.
408
409   We believe that a reliable evaluation benchmark should align with the real-world application needs
410
      and focus on the performance and practicality of LLMs in real scenarios (Han et al., 2024). More-
      over, the evaluation dataset should be capable of real-time updates to prevent models from cheating
411
      by memorizing test data. Therefore, this paper proposes TurtleBench, an evaluation benchmark with
412
      a continuously updating dataset, offering concise and easily quantifiable evaluation results, ensuring
413   reliability while meeting the real-world needs of users.
414
415
416   5   C ONCLUSION
417
418   We propose a new reliable evaluation benchmark, TurtleBench, specifically designed to assess
419
      LLMs’ reasoning and understanding abilities in real-world scenarios. Our evaluation framework col-
      lects 32 turtle soup stories and releases a turtle soup game in which LLMs serve as judges. Through
420
      this game, we can collect user query data in real-time and dynamically update the evaluation dataset,
421
      thereby avoiding distortion in evaluation results caused by data contamination and enhancing the
422   credibility of the assessment. We evaluated nine of the currently most popular top LLMs, and the
423   results show that closed-source models represented by GPT-4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet still exhibit
424   state-of-the-art overall performance, while the latent reasoning techniques of o1 still have room for
425   improvement. In future research, we will continue to explore methods to enhance the reliability of
426   LLM evaluations to obtain more authentic assessment results, facilitating the application of LLMs
427   in real-world scenarios.
