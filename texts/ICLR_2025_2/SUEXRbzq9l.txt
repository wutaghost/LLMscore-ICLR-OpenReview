009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the problem of computing the statistical or total variation (TV)
012            similarity between distributions P and Q, which is defined as sTV (P, Q) :=
013            1 − dTV (P, Q), where dTV is the total variation distance between P and Q. Statis-
014            tical similarity is a basic measure of similarity between distributions with several
015
               natural interpretations. We focus on the case when P and Q are products of
               Bernoulli trials. Recent work has established, somewhat surprisingly, that even for
016
               this simple class of distributions exactly computing the TV distance (and hence
017
               statistical similarity) is #P-hard. This motivates the question of designing mul-
018            tiplicative approximation algorithms for these computational tasks. It is known
019            that the TV distance computation admits a fully polynomial-time deterministic
020            approximation scheme (FPTAS). It remained an open question whether efficient
021            approximation schemes exist for estimating the statistical similarity between two
022            product distributions. In this work, we affirmatively answer this question by de-
023            signing an FPTAS for estimating the statistical similarity between two product
024            distributions. To obtain our result, we introduce a new variant of the knapsack
025            problem, which we call multidimensional Masked Knapsack problem, and design
026
               an FPTAS to estimate the number of solutions to this problem. This result might
               be of independent interest.

034   TV similarity or statistical similarity. In particular, the statistical similarity between distributions P
035   and Q is defined as sTV (P, Q) := 1 − dTV (P, Q).
036   The statistical (distance) similarity is a fundamental measure for quantifying the (dis)similarity
037   between probability distributions. It has the following intuitive interpretation: If sTV (P, Q) ≥ 1 − ε,
038   then for any event E, its probability with respect to P and Q differs by at most ε. Moreover, the
039   fundamental fact that any process, deterministic or randomized, cannot decrease statistical similarity
040   between two random variables is very useful in module analysis of large systems. Because of these
041   reasons, the notions of statistical distance or similarity between probability distributions has been
042   used in many areas, including probabilistic algorithms Mitzenmacher & Upfal (2005), machine
043   learning Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David (2014), and information theory Cover & Thomas (2006).
044   To further motivate the notion, we elaborate on some natural interpretations and equivalent charac-
045   terizations of statistical similarity between distributions. Interestingly, sTV can be interpreted by
046   using the notion of a coupling between probability distributions. A coupling between probability
047   distributions P and Q is a random variable (X, Y ) where X ∼ P , Y ∼ Q. An optimal coupling
048   O = (X, Y ) is a coupling for which PrO [X = Y ] is maximized. It is well known that in optimal
049   case, sTV (P, Q) = PrO [X = Y ]. Thus, by computing sTV between P and Q we can compute the
050
      probability that X equals Y under the optimal coupling O. Couplings, introduced by Doeblin (1938),
      have been fundamental in the realms of computer science and mathematics, and have underpinned
051
      some of the most seminal results (Lindvall, 2002; Levin et al., 2006; Meyn & Tweedie, 2012).
052
053   Moreover, it is known that the minimal total error in hypothesis testing equals the statistical similarity
      between the underlying distributions (Lehmann & Romano, 2008; Nielsen, 2014). In a similar

074
                 Is there an efficient multiplicative approximation algorithm for estimating the
075                            statistical similarity between product distributions?
076
077   Recent works of (Bhattacharyya et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; 2024) showed that efficient multi-
078   plicative approximation algorithms exist for estimating total variation distance between product
079   distributions. Since sTV = 1−dTV , it might appear that sTV can be estimated using a dTV estimation
080   algorithm. However for a multiplicative approximation, this is not the case. That is, it is not possible
081   in general to use an efficient multiplicative approximation algorithm for a function f in order to
082
      design an efficient multiplicative approximation algorithm for 1 − f .
083   For instance, let f be a function that takes as input a Boolean DNF formula ϕ and outputs the
084   probability that a random assignment satisfies ϕ. It is known that there is a randomized multiplicative
085   approximation algorithm for estimating f Karp et al. (1989). However, a multiplicative approximation
086   algorithm for estimating 1 − f implies that all NP-complete problems have efficient randomized
087   algorithms (RP = NP). This is because the complement of a DNF formula is a CNF formula
088
      and there is no efficient randomized multiplicative approximation for estimating the acceptance
      probability of CNF formulas unless RP = NP.

131   1.2   R ELATED W ORK
132
      The computational aspects of TV distance have attracted attention from a complexity theoretic
133
      viewpoint, where it has been shown that additive approximations of TV distance between distributions
134
      belong to various zero-knowledge classes (Goldreich et al., 1999; Sahai & Vadhan, 2003; Malka,
135   2008; Dixon et al., 2020; Bouland et al., 2017). In all of these works, the class of distributions
136   considered are distributions samplable by polynomial-size circuits. The work of Sahai & Vadhan
137   (2003) established that additively approximating the TV distance between two distributions that are
138   samplable by Boolean circuits is hard for the complexity class SZK (Statistical Zero Knowledge).
139   Since complexity of additive approximations for dTV and sTV are equivalent, the above result holds
140   also for statistical similarity. Goldreich et al. (1999) showed that the problem of deciding whether a
141   distribution samplable by a Boolean circuit is close or far from the uniform distribution is complete
142   for NISZK (Non-Interactive Statistical Zero Knowledge). A recent work of Bhattacharyya et al.
143
      (2023) considered much simpler class of distributions. They showed that (a) exactly computing the
      TV distance between product distributions is #P-complete, and (b) multiplicatively approximating
144
      the TV distance between Bayes nets is NP-hard.
145
146   Regarding algorithmic aspects, Bhattacharyya et al. (2020) designed efficient algorithms to additively
147   approximate the TV distance between distributions that are efficiently samplable and efficiently
148   computable. Feng et al. (2023) designed a fully polynomial-time randomized approximation scheme
149
      (FPRAS) for estimating the TV distance between two arbitrary product distributions. Interestingly,
      their work used couplings to devise the algorithm. More recently, Feng et al. (2024) gave an FPTAS
150
      for the same task.

359   5.1     BACKGROUND ON B RANCHING P ROGRAMS
360
361   We first fix some notation and terminology. A (W, n)-branching program is a branching program of
362   width W over n Boolean input variables. A read-once branching program (ROBP) is a branching
363   program whereby each input variable is accessed only once. A monotone (W, n)-ROBP is a (W, n)-
364   ROBP such that in each of its layers L, the nodes of L are totally ordered under some relation ≺, and
365
      whenever u ≺ v for some nodes u and v it is the case that the set of partial accepting paths that start
      at u are a subset of the set of partial accepting paths that start at v.
366
367   Given a branching program M and a string z, the notation M (z) denotes the output (“accept”/“reject”)
368   of M on input z. An implicit description of a monotone ROBP is a description according to which
369   one can efficiently check the relative order of two nodes under ≺ (within any layer), and given a node
370   u one can efficiently compute its neighbors.
371   The following notion of small-space sources was introduced by Kamp et al. (2011).
372
      Definition 11 (Kamp et al. (2011)). A width-w small-space source is described by a (w, n)-branching
373   program D with an additional probability distribution pv on the outgoing edges associated with
374   vertices v ∈ D. Samples from the source are generated by taking a random walk on D according to
375   the pv ’s and outputting the labels of the edges traversed.
376
377   We require the following useful claims by Gopalan et al. (2010). Claim 12 is an application of
      dynamic programming.

379   of width at most S, it is the case that Prx∼D [M (x) = 1] can be computed exactly in time O(nSW ).
380
      Claim 13 (Gopalan et al. (2010)). Given a (W, n)-ROBP M , the uniform distribution over M ’s

      7   C ONCLUSION
512
513
      We have given a simple FPTAS for estimating the statistical similarity between product distributions.
514
      We reiterate that the known FPTAS for TV distance Feng et al. (2024) does not in general yield
515   an FPTAS for statistical similarity. In fact, we use different techniques than that of Feng et al.
516   (2024) to design the FPTAS for statistical similarity. Our algorithm is based on a reduction to a
517   new knapsack counting problem which we call (multidimensional) #M ASKED K NAPSACK which
518   might be of independent interest. Extending our results to more general classes of distributions
519   and establishing lower bounds is a promising and significant research direction. Finally, we believe
520   that a complexity-theoretic study of functions f in #P with range in [0, 1], for which there are
521   approximation schemes for both f and 1 − f , is significant.
