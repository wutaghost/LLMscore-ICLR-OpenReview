011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Meeting the growing data demands of modern AI applications requires efficient,
014            high-speed communication links. We propose an edge inference framework that
015            dynamically optimizes non-uniform quantization levels in programmable ADC
016
               receivers. While integer linear programming (ILP) offers high-quality solutions,
               its significant computational cost (120 seconds per instance on high-performance
017
               CPUs) and hardware requirements make it unsuitable for on-chip use. On-chip
018
               solutions are essential for fast, periodic adjustments to track time-varying effects
019            such as temperature drift and ensure reliable communication. To address this, we
020            train a convolutional neural network (CNN) using ILP-generated labels, achieving
021            a 24,000x speedup with inference on a RISC-V microcontroller. The CNN lever-
022            ages a custom loss function tied to system-level metrics, reducing area metric
023            errors from 29% to less than 2%. Unlike prior works embedding neural networks
024            in the signal path, our framework adapts periodically to channel variations without
025            disrupting communication. This enables improved error rates, energy efficiency,
026            and a scalable pathway for on-chip edge intelligence in next-generation systems.

      2.1   TARGET L INKS AND ADC L EVELS
136
137   In modern long-reach SERDES designs, dedicated ADC blocks typically employ fixed, uniform
138   quantization levels, followed by extensive DSP blocks such as feedforward and decision feedback
139   equalizers (FFE/DFE) and maximum likelihood sequence detectors (MLSD) to recover the signal.
140   In contrast, our work focuses on shorter-reach interfaces, such as memory links (LPDDR, GDDR,
141   DDR) and chip-to-chip interconnects over PCB or module substrates. These interfaces often utilize
142   simpler receivers with lower effective number of bits (ENOB ≤ log2 (k)) ADCs, or in some cases,
143   no explicit ADC circuits at all.
144   While evaluating higher ENOB ADCs (EN OB > 3) is a valuable research direction, our focus on
145   shorter-reach links is motivated by their distinct tradeoffs and their prevalence in modern computing
146   platforms. By targeting systems with lower ENOB (EN OB < 3, k < 8), we aim to reduce
147   power consumption and hardware complexity. This is achieved by optimizing non-uniform ADC
148   levels and employing LUT-based signal detection to improve receiver efficiency. Further details on
149   receiver design trade-offs and architectural differences are provided in Section A.1.

646   Our CNN is trained to approximate the solution of an ILP problem by learning from optimal ILP
647   solutions generated offline. While we demonstrated that the CNN achieves good performance, a
      more formal proof of the error bounds between the ILP and CNN solutions has not yet been derived.


                                                      12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      Under certain conditions, the optimization problem solved by the ILP guarantees optimality. How-
649   ever, the CNN-based solution, introduces approximation errors due to the following factors:
650
651           • Neural Network Generalization Error: The CNN learns a mapping from inputs to op-
652             timal ILP solutions, but the approximation may deviate from optimality, specifically for
653             unseen test cases. We did shows results on the unseen test case, but we make the general
654             observation here.
655           • Universal Function Approximators: While neural networks are universal approximators,
656             the limited capacity of the network (depth and neuron count) and training data limitations
657             may introduce errors.
658
659   Formally, let SILP represent the optimal solution derived from the ILP, and let SCN N be the
660
      solution predicted by the CNN. We are interested in deriving a bound on the approximation error
      ∥SILP − SCN N ∥ under some relevant metric factoring in the multi-task solution nature.
661
662   While a closed-form bound on ∥SILP − SCN N ∥ has not been established, the approximation
663   error can be tied to the CNN’s ability to minimize the custom loss function during training. We
664   hypothesize that error bounds could be formulated based on the following factors:
665
              • Network Capacity: Larger CNN architectures may provide tighter approximations of the
666
                ILP solutions. Of course, in our practical implementation, we have the additional constraint
667
                of fitting within a microcontroller.
668
669
              • Training Data: A more extensive dataset can improve the network’s generalization, po-
                tentially reducing the error. We mentioned this in the paper for the k = 4 case.
670
671           • Loss Function Behavior: The custom loss function, which mimics the ILP objective, plays
672             a big role in the approximation error (along with the more conventional losses). A formal
673             analysis of the custom loss function including the affine translation and Gumbel-Softmax
674
                may help to bound this error.
