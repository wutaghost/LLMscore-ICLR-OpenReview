009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce the framework of "social learning" in the context of large language
012            models (LLMs), whereby models share knowledge with each other in a privacy-
013            aware manner using natural language. We present and evaluate two approaches
014            for knowledge transfer between LLMs. In the first scenario, we allow the model
015            to generate abstract prompts aiming to teach the task. In our second approach,
016            models transfer knowledge by generating synthetic examples. We evaluate these
017            methods across diverse datasets and quantify memorization as a proxy for privacy
018
               loss. These techniques inspired by social learning yield promising results with low
               memorization of the original data. In particular, we show that performance using
019
               these methods is comparable to results with the use of original labels and prompts.
020
               Our work demonstrates the viability of social learning for LLMs, establishes
021            baseline approaches and highlights several unexplored areas for future work.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Increasingly, large language models are considered a crucial building block for agents that can
027   reason [27], use tools [21] and adapt to environmental cues [20, 42] for many real-world tasks. As
028
      such, personal assistants are now commonly powered by such models [29] while larger entities, e.g.
      companies, can also have their own agents. When considering networks of personal agents, the ability
029
      to transfer information and foster collaboration is highly desirable. For instance, a spam detector can
030
      be collaboratively maintained by sharing newly detected spam templates.
031
032   Collaboration among language models to solve complex problems involves various research areas [37],
033
      for example task planning [15], information retrieval [11, 43] and information exchange [19]. LLMs
      have shown impressive capabilities at performing novel tasks by following natural language instruc-
034
      tions or using a limited number of examples [5, 38]. This suggests that natural language might become
035
      a viable means of knowledge transfer for personal agents. However, a critical concern is how to ensure
036   the privacy of users is upheld by preventing the leakage of sensitive information between agents.
037
038
      In this work, we introduce the paradigm of privacy-aware "social learning" to transfer knowledge
      between LLMs. We take inspiration from the theory of social learning as defined by Bandura
039
      & Walters [4] which proposes that new behaviors can be acquired by observing and imitating
040
      others. Indeed, mechanisms of social learning have proven highly effective in persistent multi-agent
041   systems by allowing agents to benefit from the accumulated learning of others [2, 25]. The resulting
042   framework enables agents to generate examples and instructions tailored for task-specific information
043   transfer with an emphasis on safeguarding the privacy of shared examples and knowledge. We posit
044   that this framework is advantageous as it provides knowledge transference between models in a
045   human-interpretable way without sharing private data.
046   The key contributions of our work are (1) proposing and formalizing the concept of social learning
047   for LLM-driven agents; (2) suggesting baseline implementations of social learning and benchmarking
048   them across a diverse set of tasks and (3) establishing metrics to measure private data leakage, and
049   using them to demonstrate the benefits of social learning whilst preserving privacy.

085   2.1   S OCIAL L EARNING P ROTOCOL
086
      We provide a canonical definition of social learning in this section by considering m agents
087   T1 , . . . , Tm , called teachers that teach a task (e.g. yes/no question answering) to another agent
088   S, called the student. Each teacher has access to its own silo of data DTi which contains a distinct
089   subset of examples for the task. Meanwhile, the student does not have access to any training data. A
090   user queries the student at inference time to solve new, unseen instances of the task. As such, the
091   goal is to transfer the knowledge of the teachers to the student so that it can successfully respond to a
092   query.
093   Similar to standard machine learning models, we consider two operation modes for this environment:
094   training and inference. During training the agents collaborate without any input to transfer task-related
095   knowledge whereas at inference time the student relies on this transferred knowledge to answer the
096   specific instance of the task. Therefore, the student can augment its knowledge (stored in DS ) by
097   communicating with teachers during training and subsequently relies on the accumulated knowledge
098   to answer queries at inference time.
099   At training time, part of the role of the student is that of an aggregator where it must select a subset
100   of the information provided to it by the teachers. In this work, we only consider the most basic
101   version of the student at inference which replies to a user input by appending the input to a prompt,
102   querying its language model, and returning the continuation. The whole process is illustrated in
103   Figure 1.
104   A solution to the problem of how to teach the student can be to send all the data accessible by the
105   teachers to the student and have it concatenate all of these data points to create the final prompt.
106   In this case, the student receives all the knowledge and the task is reduced to generating a good
107   response based on the available data. However, it is important to consider cases where this is not
      possible, for example because of privacy constraints. In particular, we consider the scenario where

      the original examples accessible by the teachers contains private data that should not be shared with
109   other parties. Therefore, the goal is to teach the student without sharing such private information
110   which automatically excludes the possibility of sharing the original examples. Similarly, we focus on
111   the cases where the user’s query to the student contains private data and therefore can not be directly
112   shared with the teachers. In our evaluations, we consider directly sharing the original examples of the
113   teachers as a baseline to compare our methods against.

      2.2     M ETHODS
115
116   The mechanisms of knowledge transfer in our work are inspired by social learning theory [4]. The
117   theory outlines models of observational learning amongst humans and we use two of these as the
118   basic models of communication in our framework. While a combination of these basic models is
119   most likely more effective, in this work we only look at the performance when they are employed
120
      separately. These models are simple enough that they satisfy additional constraints which allows us
      to avoid the need for abilities in language models that are yet to be perfected. We refer the interested
121
      reader to Appendix A where we provide an overview of these constriants and their motivations.

125   2.2.1    V ERBAL S OCIAL L EARNING : S HARING I NSTRUCTIONS
126
127
      In the verbal instruction model from social learning theory, a behavior is described in detail and a
128
      participant is instructed in how to engage in the behavior.
129
130   Conversely, LLMs are able to perform new tasks based on short, textual instructions describing the
131   tasks in question [24]. Previous work has also shown that these instructions can be generated by
132
      prompting an instruction-tuned LLM with examples and then asking it to complete the instruction for
      them [14].
133
134   Similar to the verbal instruction model, we can thus ask teacher agents to generate instructions based
135   on their silo of private data. These instructions are then shared with the student who integrates the
136   instructions in its prompt. In this work, when using this model, we focus on the scenario where there
137
      is only a single teacher. We apply this simplification to avoid the need for an aggregation mechanism
      that merges multiple instructions and leave developing such mechanisms for future work.
138

140
141   2.2.2    L IVE MODELS : S HARING E XAMPLES
142
143
144
      In the live models method from social learning theory, an individual demonstrates the desired
      behavior and the learner imitates.
145
146   Conversely, a technique used that allows LLMs to perform well on a new task is including examples
147   of that task in the prompt [5], a technique called few-shot learning. Even including a few examples
148   can greatly improve the downstream performance.
149   One option for teaching using this learning model is sharing examples from the teacher’s private
150   dataset. However, this method compromises privacy which is why we only consider it as a baseline.
151   Instead, we consider sharing artificial examples that are generated based on the real data.
152   To let teachers generate artificial examples, we make use of their language models. In particular,
153   given the capability of language models to follow the format of the input and replicate it [34], the
154   continuation of a few-shot prompt can be expected to contain new examples. Hence, to generate a
155   new artificial example, each teacher selects ngen examples from its private set and generates artificial
156   examples by providing them as the few-shot prompt to its language model, using the model to
157   generate a continuation without any additional instructions.
158   The continuations are generated by querying the model with temperature sampling with temperature τ
159   and selecting the top scoring (based on perplexity) k continuations. Some of these continuations might
160   be discarded due to concerns such as privacy or faulty generation while the rest are sent to the aggre-
161   gator, the component responsible for generating the final prompt for the student. The aggregator then
      picks from the at most ngen · k generated examples, and adds the selected ones to the student’s prompt.

164   3.1   LLM S AND AGENTS
165
166
      Zero-shot or few-shot prompting has been shown to be highly effective for transfer learning, notably
      by Brown et al. [5]. In such approaches, a large pre-trained language model is zero-shot or few-shot
167
      prompted by being shown examples of the desired behaviour, without training, to perform a new task.
168
      Variations on these methods such as chain-of-thought prompting [39] have shown that even simple
169   prompt modifications can have a substantial impact on target task performance [39, 7] and enable
170   new capabilities.
171
      There is a large pre-existing body of work focused on multi-agent based communication via dialogue
172
      to solve complex tasks [10, 32]. The motivation is that by cross-agent interaction, LLMs can
173
      collectively exhibit enhanced performance by aggregating their strengths. Multiple works have
174   focused on debate between LLMs to improve output of models. For instance, [12] allow multiple
175   language model instances to propose and debate their responses and reasoning processes. Their
176   findings indicate that this approach significantly enhances mathematical and strategic reasoning
177   across a number of tasks. Perez et al. [28] also propose a debate procedure to verify the accuracy
178   and safety of generated content. However, in these scenarios, the concept of agents having access to
179   separate datasets is not considered.
180   Most similar to our work, Zeng et al. [44] introduce a modular framework that allows multimodal
181   models to exchange information with each other and capture new capabilities using zero-shot transfer.
182   Their approach does not require fine tuning and aims to capitalize on the different types of knowledge
183   contained by models capturing different modalities.
184
      3.2   F EDERATED L EARNING
185
186   Federated learning [18, 22, 17] is a technique for training models on decentralized data without
187   collecting any of this data in a central place. Instead, a central server coordinates the fleet of
188   participants during the training process. In each round of training, a subset of participants is sampled.
189   Each participant receives the current weights of the model, uses their local data to update them, and
190
      then sends back the gradients. The server combines all the model updates across participants and
      uses them to update the model of the next iteration.
191
192   Social learning is similar to federated learning in that no raw data is meant to be transmitted and that
193   the participants aim to jointly learn to perform a task. However, in contrast to federated learning, social
194   learning does not update any model weights and instead works solely by exchanging information
195
      expressed in natural language. This has a few advantages:
196
       1. All components are agnostic to the specific models used. Teachers and students can be based on
197       different model sizes, architectures and weights. All they need to be able to do is to input and
198       output natural language.
199
200
       2. Text is more compact than gradients. In federated learning today, it would be prohibitively
          expensive to send full updates for the largest foundation models. With social learning, everything
201
          is expressed in text fitting a prompt, which can easily be transmitted across networks.
202
203    3. Text is much more interpretable than gradients. One can read what teachers produce and analyze
204       it.
205
      While social learning is distinctively different from federated learning, some of its concepts can be
206
      transferred across to the social learning setting. In our privacy analysis in Section 5 for example, we
207
      adapt Secret Sharer [6], a technique that is also popular in federated learning.

259   4.1   L IVE MODELS : S HARING E XAMPLES
260   We follow the process outlined in Section 2.2.2 with m = 8 teachers and compare the performance
261   of a prompt with n generated examples for different values of n against several baselines. The
262   dataset is distributed between teachers randomly so all teachers will have the same data distribution.
263   The zero-shot performance of the model on the task institutes a low bar baseline. As a high bar,
264   we consider the performance of doing few-shot learning with n private examples from one of the
265   teachers, equivalent to asking that teacher to directly solve the task. Note that this is not feasible in
266   practice and thus is a high bar since sending private examples of a teacher, or querying one teacher
267
      with inputs given to the student violates their privacy. Therefore, we do not aim to outperform this
      baseline but to show that we can perform comparably using the generated examples.
268
269   In most of our experiments we use a basic aggregation mechanism where the aggregator picks one of
      the artificially generated candidates at random. We call this aggregator the random aggregator.

      We start by considering the scenario where the student’s language model is the same as the teachers’.
271   Since the only difference between the teachers and the student in this case is the set of examples they
272   can access, we can compare the effect of using generated examples instead of real ones more clearly.
273   The results are shown in Table 1 and highlight various patterns that give insight on effectiveness of
274   generating artificial examples. We now discuss several of these patterns in detail.
275
      For the majority of tasks, we observe no significant difference between using original private examples
276   and the generated ones, especially when the number of examples is high enough, e.g. n = 16. This is
277   especially interesting since we observe that these generated examples are sufficiently different from
278   the real ones. We confirm this in Appendix D where we report a high average normalized distance
279   between each generated example and the prompt used for generating it. We note that this investigation
280   is different from measuring the amount of data leakage which we investigate in Section 5 as the
281   examples can be different and yet still contain sensitive information.
282   The main exception where a difference can be observed between generated and real examples is the
283   spam detection task. Based on our observations, we conjecture that one of the underlying reasons
284   that makes generating artificial examples for this task more challenging is that the language model
285   favors not spam examples over spam examples. Boolean Questions is another task where the model
286   struggles when given generated examples, though the gap closes when the number of examples is
287   large enough. In this task we also observe that the language model seems to strongly favor questions
288   with a yes answer, suggesting that the favor of one class is a re-occurring challenge in generating
289
      examples for classification tasks. For Boolean Questions we also observe another challenge that the
      language model tends to generate questions that do not have a yes or no answer. We provide some
290
      qualitative examples of both good and bad generations in Appendix J.
291
292   Finally, we observe that generating factual examples is not essential for transferring knowledge. For
293   example, we observe that some of the generated examples and provided solutions in the GSM8K task
294
      can be wrong without hurting performance. As shown in prior work [23], the demonstrations are not
      only useful to show the mapping between the input and the label but are also important to clarify the
295
      format and the input and label distributions. We conjecture that in these cases the model mainly relies
296
      on its own intrinsic ability to map the input to the label while using the demonstrations to learn the
297   other aforementioned aspects of the task. We highlight that these aspects are sometimes essential to a
298   good performance on the task. Indeed, on the GSM8K task, thinking step by step is part of the format
299   learned from the examples which significantly improves performance [39].

302
303
      We additionally investigated two extensions to the above setup which we only briefly describe here
      with details described in the appendix.
304
305   Teaching to a larger student This ability is natural to social learning since teachers only share text,
306   enabling knowledge to be transferred between different models of different sizes and architectures.
307   On the other hand, typical gradient-based federated learning methods such as FedAvg [22] and
308   FedOpt [33] require the same model size and architecture to be used everywhere. Given that the
309   largest of language models currently can be only executed on data centers, it would be especially
310   useful to be able to transfer knowledge back to such models. In our experiments, we find this to be
311   generally feasible in social learning, with a small drop in performance compared to teachers and
312   student being of the same size, as is expected to be in this more difficult setting. Details and results
313
      of this setup is provided in Appendix G.
314
      Voting aggregator As an example of a more sophisticated aggregator, we evaluated an aggregator
315   where teachers vote on their preferred examples. To be able to do this, teachers keep a hold-out
316   dataset that is used during the voting process. After teachers generated examples using their training
317   dataset, the aggregator sends back all received examples to the teachers to let them vote. The most
318   popular examples are then used by the student during evaluation. We find this protocol to improve
319   results for intermediate values of n, the total number of examples picked by the aggregator. We refer
320   interested readers to Appendix H for more details and results.

      5      M EMORIZATION
373
374   In the previous sections of the paper, we discussed how well teachers can teach students in social
375   learning in terms of model quality. In this section, we investigate whether the instructions and exam-
376   ples transferred to students indeed help reduce private data leakage or not. To this end, we propose and
377   evaluate metrics to measure how much social learning can memorize sensitive information included
      in the private examples.


                                                                7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      As a first step, we first investigate how often teachers copy over one of their private examples verbatim.
379   This can happen when the teacher repeats one of the examples given in its prompts. On all datasets
380   we found this to be the case in fewer than 0.1% of cases, meaning the exact data point is rarely leaked.
381   As shown in Table 5 in the Appendix, the Levenshtein distances between original and generated
382   examples are also generally high. However, that does not necessarily mean that no sensitive parts of
383   the original example are memorized, either verbatim or in more subtle ways.
384   To investigate this further, we adapt the existing Secret Sharer [6] technique for social learning. Secret
385   Sharer is an established technique for measuring how much a given training process leads a model
386   to memorize some of its training data. It has been used in federated learning [36, 13], making it an
387   interesting technique to adapt to social learning.
388
      Secret Sharer works by inserting artificial secret data points, called canaries, into the training data
389   set. Injection of canaries provides access to a known set of secrets that should not be shared, making
390   it measurable how much the secrets present in the data are memorized. To implement this, one canary
391   is randomly sampled from a list containing NSS potential canaries, while the other NSS − 1 data
392   points that were not sampled serve as comparison elements. In our experiments, we generate canaries
393   containing secret codes and names. This is done by using random four-digit numbers for the codes
394   and by taking names from a dataset of the most common names given to newborns in the US in 2020
395   [16]. The codes or names are inserted into patterns shown in Table 13 in the appendix.
396   After performing training using the data containing the canary, the score assigned by the model
397   to the canary included in the data is compared with the scores of the comparison data points that
398   were not included in the training data. This metric, called rank, counts the number of comparison
399   examples that get assigned a higher score than the canary that was actually trained on. Secret Sharer
400   assumes a scoring function based on the model that assigns a higher score to examples that the model
401   memorized. Since the rank is a random variable, the average of the rank across TSS runs is computed
402
      and used for making deductions. For example, if the model has not memorized the canary, the rank’s
403
      distribution would be uniform, leading to an average rank of N2SS . In the case of perfect memorization,
      the rank would be 0.
404
405   To illustrate the method further, consider the example of adding the canary
406   The secret code is 1234 to the training set. After training, we can check how
407   high the model’s score is in that particular example as opposed to the same string with different codes.
408
      A model that only learned a high-level pattern, would not assign a significantly higher score to the
      string containing the particular code it was trained on whereas a model that memorized the concrete
409
      data point would.
410
411   In standard gradient descent training, the model’s loss for the example can be used as the score. In
412   social learning, we do not optimize any numerical loss and do not update any weights. Instead, the
413
      social learning process produces a string in the form of new examples or an instruction which can
      be added to the model’s prompt. Therefore, we use the following mechanism to compute the score:
414
      Given the final prompt from social learning process and a canary, the likelihood of that canary as
415
      a continuation of the learned prompt is determined by the model. This value is normalized by the
416   number of tokens in the canary to make it comparable to the score of other canaries. This normalized
417   value is used as the score. We call this scoring function the example reconstruction likelihood. An
418   example of this can be seen in Figure 3.
419
      Putting everything together, a Secret Sharer experiment in social learning then works as follows:
420
421
        1. A canary element and NSS − 1 comparison elements are sampled.
422
423     2. The canary element is inserted into the training dataset of all teachers.
424
        3. The social learning process is executed, which results in examples or an instruction generated
425
           by the teacher.
426
427     4. The example reconstruction likelihood is computed on (a) The canary element used in training.
428        and (b) The NSS − 1 comparison elements not used in training.
429     5. The rank is computed by counting how many of the comparison elements have a higher likelihood
430        than the one we trained on.
431
        6. The above process is repeated TSS times and the average rank is returned.

      6    F UTURE W ORK
463
      Improving Teaching Process Both for sharing examples and sharing instructions, our results show
464   there is room for improvement. Future work could explore other aggregators, ways of introducing
465   learning loops, or other techniques for generating instructions or examples.
466
467   Generalized Settings and Other Modalities Future work could also consider more generalized
468   settings, such as cases where teachers are allowed to communicate with each other or are available
469   during inference. Instead of text-based examples and communication, future work can investigate
470
      social learning based on other modalities, such as image or audio data. These settings introduce
      other challenges and require capabilities from the models that are yet to be perfected.
471
472   Alternative Privacy Metrics and Differential Privacy While the privacy experiments using Se-
473   cret Sharer provide some information about privacy in social learning, we do not consider them to be
474   exhaustive. Future work could look into different ways of measuring data leakage in social learning.
475   Furthermore, differential privacy guarantees could be added to social learning by making teachers
476   use recently proposed mechanisms for differentially-private in-context learning [35, 40].

478   7    C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this work, we introduced the social learning framework which allows language models with access
481   to private data to transfer knowledge through textual communication while maintaining the privacy
482
      of that data. In this framework, we identified sharing examples and sharing instructions as basic
      models and evaluated them on multiple tasks. Furthermore, we adapted the Secret Sharer metric to
483
      our framework, proposing a metric for measuring data leakage. The paper evaluates these methods
484
      on several datasets, reports results, and outlines directions for future work.

      A    A DDITIONAL S IMPLIFYING C ONSTRAINTS
703
704   We impose the following constraints on the communications between the teachers and the student in
705   our social learning methods:
706
            1. Teachers do not directly communicate with each other: teachers are not able to send text
707
               messages to each other either directly or via the student. This constraint removes the effect
708
               of planning and debate capabilities of the language models.
709
710
            2. The query to all teachers is the same: the student always sends the same message to all the
               teachers. This constraint removes the need for the student to analyze teacher’s knowledge of
711
               the task and react based on it.
712
713
            3. The conversation flow is fixed: the tasks requested from the teachers are fixed in advance
               and do not depend on the conversation. For example, teachers might initially be asked to
714
               describe the task and then be prompted with a description from multiple teachers to produce
715
               a consolidated version. However, the student will not ask for clarifications about a specific
716            part of the description that is vague. This constraint removes the requirement of models to
717            generate instructions during learning as the prompts can be manually fixed.
718
719   To define a social learning method, we have to define the response functions of teachers and the
720   student:
721         • Teachers’ Response: We need to define Ti (M ) which is the message sent to the student
722           in response to the message M received from the student. For example if M is a question,
723           Ti (M ) can be the answer based on a teacher’s private data.
724
            • Student’s Response: Since the student sends the same message to all the teachers, we can
725           assume that it replies only after receiving the update from all teachers. The student responds
726           to the message by possibly sending a new message to the teachers and creating an updated
727           prompt PSNew . As such, to define the response function of the student we need to specify
728           RG (MT1 , MT2 , . . . , MTm , PScurrent ) as a pair (PSnew , Mnext ).
729
730
      The training starts by querying the student to generate the first message to the teachers. Afterwards,
      the teachers and student alternate responding to each other’s messages. Once the training is completed,
731
      the final prompt can be used by the student during inference.

796   baselines. In particular, to test the significance of the difference observed in the accuracy of a certain
797   method in comparison to a given baseline, we first combine all the example and output pairs generated
798   by either the baseline or the considered method. We randomly permute the aforementioned pile and
799
      break it into a pile with the same number of pairs as the baseline and another pile with the same
      number of pairs as the considered method. We compute the accuracy of each pile and measure the
800
      difference. Repeating this process 104 times allows us to obtain an approximate distribution of the
801
      observed difference if the baseline and the considered method’s output are not significantly different.
802   We use this distribution to compute the probability of the real difference in accuracy between the
803   baseline and the considered method and report that as the p-value. When discussing results, if the
804   p-value is below the threshold 0.05 we say the result is significant and state that we could not observe
805   a significant difference otherwise.
