009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The AI community has been exploring a pathway to artificial general intelligence
012            (AGI) by developing “language agents”, which are complex large language models
013            (LLMs) workflows involving both prompting techniques and tool usage methods.
014            While language agents have demonstrated impressive capabilities for many real-
015            world tasks, a fundamental limitation of current language agents research is that
016            they are model-centric, or engineering-centric. That’s to say, the progress on
017            prompts, tools, and workflows of language agents requires substantial manual
018            engineering efforts from human experts rather than automatically learning from
019
               data. We believe the transition from model-centric, or engineering-centric, to
               data-centric, i.e., the ability of language agents to autonomously learn and evolve
020
               in environments, is the key for them to possibly achieve AGI.
021
               In this work, we introduce agent symbolic learning, a systematic framework that
022
               enables language agents to optimize themselves on their own in a data-centric way
023
               using symbolic optimizers. Specifically, we consider agents as symbolic networks
024            where learnable weights are defined by prompts, tools, and the way they are stacked
025            together. Agent symbolic learning is designed to optimize the symbolic network
026            within language agents in a data-centric way by mimicking two fundamental algo-
027            rithms in connectionist learning: back-propagation and gradient descent. Instead
028            of dealing with numeric weights, agent symbolic learning works with text-based
029            weights, loss, and gradients. We conduct proof-of-concept experiments on both
030            standard benchmarks and complex real-world tasks and show substantial improve-
031            ments over static agent frameworks and simple prompt/tool optimization methods.
032
               In addition, agent symbolic learning enables language agents to update themselves
               after being created and deployed in the wild, resulting in “self-evolving agents”.
033
               We will open-source the agent symbolic learning framework to facilitate future
034
               research on data-centric agent learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Recent advances in large language models (Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang
040
      et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) open the possibility of building language agents
041
      that can autonomously solve complex tasks. The common practice for developing AI agents is to
042   decompose complex tasks into LLM workflows where prompts and tools are stacked together (Park
043   et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023b; Xie et al., 2023). In a sense,
044   language agents can be viewed as AI systems that connect connectionism AI (i.e., the LLM backbone
045   of agents) and symbolism AI (i.e., the workflow of prompts and tools), which partially explains their
046   effectiveness in real-world problem-solving scenarios.
047
      However, the current state of language agent development is limited by the extensive engineering
048   effort required to build and customize language agent systems for a specific task. Specifically,
049   researchers and developers have to manually decompose complex tasks into subtasks, which we
050   refer to as nodes, that are more tractable for LLMs and then carefully design prompts and tools,
051   including API functions, knowledge bases, memories, etc., for specific nodes. The complexity of this
052   process makes the current landscape of language agent research model-centric, or engineering-centric.
053   This means it is almost impossible for researchers to manually tune or optimize language agents
      on datasets on which we can train neural nets in a data-centric way. This limits the robustness and

      language agents to new tasks or data distributions. We believe the transition from engineering-centric

078
079   To this end, a number of recent efforts have been made on automatic optimization of language agents.
080   For example, DSpy (Khattab et al., 2023) introduces a framework for algorithmically optimizing
081   LLM prompts via bootstrapping or random searching in a combinatory space of different prompt
082
      components and GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al., 2024) further proposes to tackle the combinatorial
      optimization challenge raised in DSPy via an iterative optimization process. Agent-pro (Zhang
083
      et al., 2024b) proposes a framework to optimize the components of the prompts corresponding to the
084
      agents’ internal policy in competitive environments. AgentOptimizer (Zhang et al., 2024a) proposes
085   a framework to optimize functions with carefully engineered prompts. While effective in some
086   scenarios, these approaches only optimize separate modules in an agent system such as a prompt
087   for a specific node. As a result, these optimization methods are prone to local optimum of isolated
088   prompts, tools, and nodes that lead to compromised performance for the entire agent system. This
089   resembles the early practice in training neural nets (Hinton and Salakhutdinov, 2006) where layers
090   are separately optimized and it now seems trivial that optimizing neural nets as a whole leads to better
091   performance. We believe that this is also the case in agent optimization and joint optimization of all
092   symbolic components within an agent is the key for optimizing agents.
093   In this work, we introduce a agent symbolic learning framework for training language agents. The
094   agent symbolic learning framework is inspired by the connectionist learning procedure (Hinton, 1990)
095   used for training neural nets. To be specific, we make an analogy between language agents and neural
096   nets: the agent workflow of an agent corresponds to the computational graph of a neural net, a node
097   in the agent workflow corresponds to a layer in the neural net, and the prompts and tools for a node
098   correspond to the weights of a layer. In this way, we are able to implement the main components of
099
      connectionist learning, i.e., backward propagation and gradient-based weight update, in the context
      of agent training using language-based loss, gradients, and weights. We implement the loss function,
100
      back-propagation, and weight optimizers in the context of agent training with carefully designed
101
      LLM workflows. Specifically, for a training example, our framework first conducts the “forward pass”
102   (agent execution) and stores the input, output, prompts, and tool usage in each node in a “trajectory”.
103   We then use an LLM-based loss function to evaluate the outcome following recent LLM-as-a-judge
104   framework (Zheng et al., 2023), resulting in a text-based loss. Then we back-propagate the text-based
105   loss from the last to the first node along the trajectory, resulting in natural language analysis and
106   reflection for the symbolic components within each node including the prompts and tool descriptions.
107   We refer to these reflections and analyses as “language gradients” since they carry the same role as
      conventional gradients in the training of neural nets: guide the direction to which optimizers should


                                                         2
108
      change the weights so that the overall loss is minimized. Finally, we update all symbolic components
109   in each node, as well as the computational graph consisting of the nodes and their connections,
110   according to the language gradients using LLMs with carefully designed prompts and workflows. Our
111   approach also naturally supports optimizing multi-agent systems by considering nodes as different
112   agents or allowing multiple agents to take actions in one node.
113
      The agent symbolic learning framework is an agent learning framework that mimics the standard
114
      connectionist learning procedure. In contrast to existing methods that either optimize single prompt
115   or tool in a separate manner, the agent symbolic learning framework jointly optimizes all symbolic
116   components within an agent system, including prompts, tools, and the workflow that stacks them
117   into an agent system. This top-down optimization scheme also enables the agent symbolic learning
118   framework to optimize the agent system “holistically”, avoiding local optimum for each separated
119   component. This makes it possible for language agents targeting complex real-world problems to
120   effectively learn from data, opening up the possibility to transform the current state of language agent
121   research from engineering-centric to data-centric.
122   In sum, by learning from LLM-generated critics (language-based loss) and reflections (language-
123   based gradients), the agent symbolic learning framework has the following advantages compared to
124   conventional frameworks for language agents in which the prompts, tools, and workflows are static
125   and require human expert efforts for optimization: first, agent symbolic learning enables the agent
126   system to learn from failure or unstable cases and update the prompts by adding few-shot examples
127   or principles; second, it enables the system to include new nodes (subtasks) and adjust the workflow
128   to improve the overall performance or handle some common failure patterns; third, our approach
129
      enables the agent system to update the tool descriptions and implementation or implement new tools
      for improved performance.
130
131   Moreover, since the language-based loss function does not require ground-truth when generating the
132   language loss and the optimization framework only requires calling of LLM APIs instead of tons of
133   GPUs, our framework enables language agents to learn from experience and actively update all their
134   symbolic components after being created and deployed in the wild, enabling “self-evolving agents”1 .
135
      We believe this could be very helpful in the pursuit of artificial general intelligence.
136   As a proof-of-concept, we conduct a series of experiments on both standard LLM benchmarks and
137   complex agentic tasks. Our results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed agent symbolic
138   learning framework on optimizing and designing prompts and tools, as well as updating the overall
139   agent workflow, by data-centric learning. We will open-source all codes and prompts in the agent
140   symbolic learning framework to facilitate future research on data-centric agent learning.

      2.1   L ANGUAGE M ODELS , P ROMPTS , AND L ANGUAGE AGENTS
146
      Language model is a family of machine learning model that is trained to evaluate the probability
147
      of sequences of words or tokens. Large language models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2018; 2019;
148   Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) often refer to
149   language models that adopt the autoregressive probability factorization scheme, parametrized by
150   the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), consists of a large amount of parameters, and
151   trained on large-scale corpus. With scaling of model size, training data, and computation, LLMs have
152   demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating human-like texts and understanding context.
153
      Prompts, on the other hand, is the key for unleashing the capabilites of LLMs. Prompts are critical
154   components in controlling the behavior and output of LLMs and serve as the interface between human
155   and LLMs. The design of prompts significantly impacts the performance of language models and a
156   number of progress have been made on prompt engineering, including in-context learning (Brown
157   et al., 2020), chain-of-thought prompting (Nye et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022), ReAct (Yao et al., 2022),
158   self-refine (Madaan et al., 2023), self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023), recurrent prompting (Zhou
159   et al., 2023a), etc.
160

162
      Language agents further extend the functionality of language models beyond simple prompting by
163   allowing LLMs to use tools (Schick et al., 2023) and integrating LLMs into broader systems capable
164   of executing multi-step tasks (Park et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b; Chen et al.,
165   2023b; Xie et al., 2023). By stacking prompts and tools into carefully designed workflows, agents are
166   versatile in various applications, from customer service automation to advanced data analysis.

      2.2   F ROM AUTOMATED P ROMPT E NGINEERING TO AGENT O PTIMIZATION
169
170
      With the increasing popularity of prompt engineering in both academic and industry, a number of
171
      recent work investigated methods to automate the prompt engineering process. For example, Pryzant
172   et al. (2020) and Yang et al. (2024) uses carefully designed prompts to unleash LLMs’ ability to do
173   prompt engineering for themselves. On the other hand, Prasad et al. (2023) and Guo et al. (2024)
174   employs different search algorithms such as genetic algorithms for prompt optimization.
175
      Since prompts are critical components of agents, the success of automated prompt engineering
176
      opens up the possibility of automated agent optimization. Similar to the case in automated prompt
177
      engineering, methods for agent optimization can also be categorized into two categories: prompt-
178   based and search-based. For example, Agent-pro (Zhang et al., 2024b) and AgentOptimizer (Zhang
179   et al., 2024a) leverage carefully designed prompts to optimize either the prompts or the tools in a node
180   of the agent workflow. These methods work on isolated components within an agent. Another line of
181   research explored search-based agent optimization algorithms. Sordoni et al. (2023) uses variational
182   inference to optimize stacked LLMs. DSpy (Khattab et al., 2023) uses search algorithms to find the
183   best prompts or nodes in a combinatory space. GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al., 2024) further improved
184   the search algorithm for the combinatory optimization problem. These approaches have a few major
185   limitations. First, the search algorithm mainly works when the metric can be defined numerically
186
      with equations that can be coded. However, most agentic tasks are real-world complex problems of
      which the success can not be defined by some equations, such as software development or creative
187
      writing. Second, these approaches update each component separately and therefore suffer from the
188
      local optimum of each node or component. These approaches also lack the functionality of adding
189   nodes in the workflow or implementing new tools. Our proposed agent symbolic learning framework,
190   on the other hand, is the first agent learning method that optimize the agent system “holistically” and
191   is able to optimize prompts, tools, nodes, as well as the way they are stacked into agents.
192
      Furthermore, a number of recent efforts have been done on synthesizing data to fine-tune the LLM
193
      backbone of an agent (Chen et al., 2023a; Qiao et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024). This line of research
194
      is orthogonal to our work and we believe they can be complementary to each other. ICE (Qian et al.,
195   2024) is also a related work investigating inter-task transfer learning for language agents, which can
196   be complementary with our method for building self-evolving agents.

302   The key is the design for the prompt template, which is expected to holistically evaluate how the agent
303   performs with respect to the input, environment, and task requirements. To this end, we carefully
304   design a prompt template for language loss computation consisting of the following components: task
305   description, input, trajectory, few-shot demonstrations, principles, and output format control. Among
306   them, task description, input, and trajectory are data-dependent while the few-shot demonstrations,
307   principles, and output format control are fixed for all tasks and training examples. The language loss
308
      consists of both natural language comments and a numerical score (also generated via prompting).
      We can optionally feed the ground-truth label for the input when generating the language loss. We
309
      call this scenario supervised agent learning. It can also generate language loss without ground-truth
310
      by evaluating the output and trajectory according to the task description. In this case, we can say that
311   the agent is doing unsupervised agent learning, which enables language agents to self-evolving. We
312   present the detailed implementation of this prompt template in the Appendix.
313
314   Back-propagation of Language Gradients In standard connectionist learning, the goal of gradient
315   back-propagation is to calculate the impact of the weights with respect to the overall loss so that
316   the optimizers can update the weights accordingly. Similarly, in our framework, we also design a
317   “back-propagation” algorithm for language gradients. Specifically, we iterate from the last node to the
318   first node and compute the gradient for each node with LLMs using a carefully designed prompt:
319
                               ∇nlang = LLM(Pgradient (∇n+1
                                                        lang , In , On , Pn , Tn , Llang ))                                 (2)
320
321   The prompt template Pgradient is designed to instruct the LLM to generate language gradients that
322   are analyses and reflections for the symbolic components within the node. Inspired by the idea of
323   back-propagation, we give the language gradients of the node executed after the current node, as well
      as the information on the execution of the current node, which is stored in the trajectory. That’s to


                                                            6
324
      say, when doing analysis and reflection, the LLM not only needs to consider how the prompts and
325   tools suit the subgoal of the current node but also has to consider how they affect the accomplishment
326   of the subgoal of the next node. By chaining from top to bottom, the language gradients for all nodes
327   are relevant and responsible for the overall success of the agent. This method effectively reduces the
328   risk of optimizing toward the local optimum for each isolated prompt and tool, leading to the overall
329   performance of agent systems.
330
331
332   Language Gradient-based Update The final step in the framework is to update the prompts
333   and tools in each node and optimize the overall agent workflow with the help of language gradi-
334
      ents. This is accomplished via “symbolic optimizers”. Symbolic optimizers are carefully designed
      prompt workflows that can optimize the symbolic weights of an agent. We create three types of
335
      symbolic optimizers: PromptOptimizer, ToolOptimizer, and WorkflowOptimizer. We present detailed
336
      implementation of these prompts in the Appendix.
337
338
339   PromptOptimizer: To facilitate prompt optimization, we split prompts into different components,
340   including task description, few-shot examples, principles, and output format control. We then design
341   separate prompts tailored for the optimization of each prompt component. All prompts share a
342   detailed explanation and demonstration of how the LLM should focus on the language gradients
343   when reasoning about how to edit the original prompt components.
344
345
346   ToolOptimizer: The ToolOptimizer is a workflow of prompts that first instructs the LLM to
347   decide the kind of operation it should use: whether the tools should be improved (by editing the
348
      tool description used for function calling), deleted, or new tools need to implement. Then the
      ToolOptimizer calls different prompts specifically designed for tool editing, deletion, and creation.
349
350
351
      WorkflowOptimizer: The goal of the WorkflowOptimizer is to optimizer the agent workflow
352   consisting of nodes and their connections. The prompt is designed to first introduce the agent
353   programming language used to define the agent workflow (we use the agent programming language
354   introduced in Zhou et al. (2023b)). Then the prompt describes the definition of a few atomic operations
355   that the LLM can use to update the workflow, including adding, deleting, and moving the nodes. It
356   then instructs the LLM to first analyze how the workflow could be improved and then implement the
357   update using the atomic operations. Detailed descriptions of the agent programming language and
358   the atomic operations used to update the agent workflow are available in the Appendix.
359   Since all aforementioned optimizers operate in natural language space and some optimization
360   operations need to be done in code space, we use a simple strategy that retries any illegal update
361   up to three times and discards the update if the error persists. We also use a rollback strategy that
362   re-runs the current example after optimization and rolls back to the original agent if the performance
363   evaluated using the language-based loss function drops. Furthermore, we also include a “learning
364   rate” component for each prompts in the optimizers which controls how aggressive the LLM should
365   be when optimizing prompts, tools, and agent workflows.
366
367
      Batched Training The aforementioned optimization scheme works with one training example at a
368
      time, which resembles stochastic gradient descent. Inspired by the fact that mini-batch stochastic
369
      gradient descent works better, or more stably, in practice, we also devise a batched training variant for
370   symbolic optimizers. Specifically, we conduct forward pass, loss computation, and back-propagation
371   for each example separately. Then we feed a batch of language gradients for the same node, and
372   prompt the LLM to holistically consider all these language gradients when updating the agent.
373
374
375   Cost and Efficiency Compared to conventional static agent frameworks, agent symbolic learning
376   does not involve additional compute or API costs during inference time. As for training time, for each
377   training example, the agent symbolic learning framework requires roughly 3 to 5 times the API costs
      (in terms of the number of input and output tokens) compared to that required for inference time.

408   agentic tasks. For the creative writing task, we follow Yao et al. (2023) and give 4 random sentences

413   executable software given a simple product requirement document (PRD). We evaluate the compared
414   agents according to the executability of the generated software, which is quantified by numerical
415
      scores ranging from 1 to 4, corresponding to increasing levels of execution capability. Specifically, a
      score of 1 signifies execution failure, 2 denotes successful code execution, 3 represents conformance
416
      to the anticipated workflow, and 4 indicates flawless alignment with expectations.

507   5    C ONCLUSION
508
509   This paper introduces agent symbolic learning, a framework for agent learning that jointly optimizes
510   all symbolic components within an agent system. The agent symbolic learning framework draws
511   inspiration from standard connectionist learning procedure to do symbolic learning. It uses language-
512   based loss, gradients, and optimizers to optimize prompts, tools, and the agent workflow with respect
513   to the overall performance of the agent system. The proposed framework is among the first attempts
514   to optimize agents that can solve complex real-world tasks using sophisticated workflows. Our
515   frameworks enables language agents to “learn from data” and perform “self-evolve” after being
516   created and deployed in the wild. We conduct several proof-of-concept experiments and show that the
517
      agent symbolic learning framework can effectively optimize agents across different task complexity.
      We believe this transition from model-centric to data-centric agent research is a meaningful step
518
      towards approaching artificial general intelligence and open-source the codes and prompts for the
519
      agent symbolic learning framework to accelerate this transition.

523
524   The scope of experiments in this paper is not super large enough to cover most agentic tasks in the real
525
      world. They are rather proof-of-concept experiments showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed
      method. We believe the community on agent research should work on a standard evaluation procedure
526
      to facilitate future research. Another limitation is that the experiments are done with text-only models
527
      and tasks, while experiments with multi-modal agents and tasks would be very interesting.

530   the wild poses certain safety risks. We believe it is important to reveal these potential risks to the
531
      agent research & development community and we need to discuss methods for effective regulation.
