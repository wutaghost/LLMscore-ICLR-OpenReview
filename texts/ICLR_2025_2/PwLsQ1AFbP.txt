010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Diffusion model (DM) based adversarial purification (AP) has proven to be a
013            powerful defense method that can remove adversarial perturbations and generate
014            a purified example without threats. In principle, the pre-trained DMs can only
015
               ensure that purified examples conform to the same distribution of the training data,
               but it may inadvertently compromise the semantic information of input examples,
016
               leading to misclassification of purified examples. Recent advancements introduce
017
               guided diffusion techniques to preserve semantic information while removing the
018            perturbations. However, these guidances often rely on distance measures between
019            purified examples and diffused examples, which can also preserve perturbations
020            in purified examples. To further unleash the robustness power of DM-based
021            AP, we propose an adversarial guided diffusion model (AGDM) by introducing
022            a novel adversarial guidance that contains sufficient semantic information but
023            does not explicitly involve adversarial perturbations. The guidance is modeled
024            by an auxiliary neural network obtained with adversarial training, considering
025            the distance in the latent representations rather than at the pixel-level values.
026
               Extensive experiments are conducted on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and ImageNet to
               demonstrate that our method is effective for simultaneously maintaining semantic
027
               information and removing the adversarial perturbations. In addition, comprehensive
028
               comparisons show that our method significantly enhances the robustness of existing
029            DM-based AP, with an average robust accuracy improved by up to 7.30% on
030            CIFAR-10. The code will be available upon acceptance.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Deep neural networks (DNNs) have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial examples (Szegedy
036   et al., 2014), leading to disastrous implications. Since then, numerous methods have been proposed
037   to defend against adversarial examples. Notably, adversarial training (AT, Goodfellow et al., 2015;
038   Madry et al., 2018a) typically aims to retrain DNNs by using adversarial examples, achieving
039
      robustness over seen types of adversarial attacks. However, the model trained by AT is almost
      incapable of defending unseen types of adversarial attacks (Laidlaw et al., 2021; Dolatabadi et al.,
040
      2022). Another class of defense methods is adversarial purification (AP, Yoon et al., 2021) typically
041
      based on pre-trained generative models, aiming to eliminate potential adversarial perturbations for
042   both clean or adversarial examples before feeding them into the classifier. Unlike AT technique, AP
043   operates as a pre-processing step that can effectively defend against unseen types of attacks and does
044   not require retraining classifiers. Hence, AP has emerged as a promising defense method and proven
045   to be a powerful alternative to AT (Shi et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2022).
046
      Recently, diffusion models (DMs, Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have gained significant attention
047
      for their ability to generate high-quality images through diffusing images with Gaussian noises in
048   a forward process and then denoise images in a reverse process. Motivated by the great success of
049   DMs, Yoon et al. (2021); Nie et al. (2022) has shown that the pre-trained DM can be leveraged for
050   adversarial purification as well as the theoretical analysis (Xiao et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023;
051   Bai et al., 2024), which tries to purify either clean examples or adversarial examples by firstly
052   adding Gaussian noises through the forward process with a number of timestep and then removing
053   noises including adversarial perturbations to restore purified examples. Although DM-based AP can
      achieve remarkable robust performance and generalization ability to unseen attacks, some studies

071   (Wu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) have shown that pre-trained DMs can restore the clean examples
072   by removing adversarial perturbations, but they fail to ensure purified examples retain the same
073   semantic information as original images. The reason is that adversarial perturbations can be gradually
074   destroyed by Gaussian noises, but there is a risk that the semantic information of image might also be
075   lost under too many timesteps in the forward process, leading to the purified examples being totally
076
      different from the expected clean examples. In principle, these DMs can only ensure that purified
      examples conform to the same distribution of the training data, but it may inadvertently compromise
077
      the semantic information of input examples, leading to misclassification of purified examples.
078
079   To address the above issue, one solution is to fine-tune the DMs using adversarial examples and
080   their groundtruth labels by AToP (Lin et al., 2024), but this is computationally expensive. Another
081   solution is to directly impose a guidance in the reverse process without fine-tuning the DMs. For
082
      instance, several guided diffusion techniques Wu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2024)
      are introduced to preserve semantic information while removing the perturbations. The idea is to
083
      leverage the guidance to control the distribution of purified examples towards the distribution of
084
      input examples. However, these works often utilize the specific distance measures between purified
085   examples and diffused examples as their guidance, yet the diffused examples inherently contain
086   adversarial perturbation information. As a result, the adversarial perturbations cannot be removed
087   completely, i.e., the perturbations are also preserved in the purified example, and thus the robustness
088   performance is still unsatisfied (Kang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Consequently, the existing
089   DM-based AP methods are still confronted with the formidable challenge of the trade-off between
090   preserving semantic information and removing perturbations. This raises a critical question: how
091   to advance the robustness of DM-based AP against adversarial attacks while effectively removing
092   perturbations and preserving semantic information?
093   To further unleash the robustness power of DM-based AP, we propose an adversarial guided diffusion
094   model (AGDM) by introducing a novel adversarial guidance during the reverse process, as illustrated
095   in Figure 1. Unlike other guided diffusion models, we train an auxiliary neural network by adversarial
096   training to model the probabilities of adversarial guidance that contains sufficient semantic information
097   but does not explicitly involve adversarial perturbations. Furthermore, unlike AT optimizing the
098   classifier and AToP optimizing the purifier, AGDM optimizes the guidance to better guide the
099
      diffusion-based purifier for adversarial purification, avoiding the huge computational burden issue of
      AToP. Finally, we heuristically create a conceptual diagram to review the whole process of DM-based
100
      AP and explain why AGDM can effectively remove the perturbations without sacrificing the semantic
101
      information, as shown in Figure 2. To demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, we empirically
102   evaluate the performance by comparing with the latest AT and AP methods across various attacks,
103   including AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020), StAdv (Xiao et al., 2018), PGD (Madry et al., 2018b;
104   Lee & Kim, 2023) and EOT (Athalye et al., 2018), on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and ImageNet datasets
105   under multiple classifier models. The results show that our method is effective for simultaneously
106   maintaining semantic information and removing the adversarial perturbations, and exhibits robust
107   generalization against unseen attacks. Specifically, on CIFAR-10, our method improves robust
      accuracy against AutoAttack by up to 8.26% compared to vanilla DMs. Furthermore, our results on

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL T RAINING AND A DVERSARIAL P URIFICATION
125
126   Given a classifier fγ with input x and output y, the adversarial attacks aim to find the adversarial
127   examples x′ that can fool the classifier model fγ . The adversarial examples can be obtained by
128
                                  x′ = x + δ,    δ = arg max L(fγ (x + δ), y),
129                                                    ∥δ∥≤ε
130
      where δ is an imperceptible adversarial perturbation and ε is the maximum scale of perturbation. To
131
      defend against adversarial attacks, the most popular technique is adversarial training (AT, Goodfellow
132
      et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018a), which requires the classifier fγ trained with adversarial examples
133   by solving the min-max optimization problem, i.e., minγ Epdata (x,y) [max∥δ∥≤ε L(fγ (x + δ), y)].
134
135   Another technique is adversarial purification (AP, Yang et al., 2019), which aims to utilize a model gθ
136
      that can purify adversarial examples before feeding them into the classifier fγ , resulting in the same
      classification output with the clean example x, i.e., fγ (gθ (x + δ)) = fγ (x). It should be noted that
137
      the purifier model gθ is not necessary to satisfy gθ (x + δ) = x. As a plug-and-play module, gθ is
138
      thus often achieved by a pre-trained generative model and can be integrated with any classifiers.

      2.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS
141
142   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have proven to be a potent class of generative
143   model capable of generating high-quality images through two distinct processes: a forward process
144   transforming an image entirely into noise by gradually adding Gaussian noise, and a reverse process
145   transforming noise into the generated image by gradually denoising image.
146   As described in DDPM (Ho et al., 2020), given a data distribution x0 ∼ q(x0 ), the forward pro-
147   cess involves T steps and any step t can be rewritten as one direct sample from q(xt | x0 ) =

177   3.1    M OTIVATION TO I NTRODUCE A DVERSARIAL G UIDANCE
178
179   Based on Wang et al. (2022), we heuristically create a conceptual diagram as shown in Figure 2. The
180   movement of the red star throughout the diagram illustrates the whole process. Initially, adversarial
181   attacks shift (=⇒) the red star to a different data distribution, causing misclassification. Then, the data
182
      distribution is diffused (−→) with the continuous addition of Gaussian noise. Finally, both Gaussian
      noise and adversarial perturbations are removed step by step during the reverse process, allowing the
183
      red star to gradually move back (L99) to the clean data distribution. We heuristically argue that the
184
      limitations of existing DM-based AP methods stem from the lack of guidance or improper guidance,
185   which may lead the red star into the clean data distribution but with the wrong category.
186
187   In Wang et al. (2022), a guidance of minimizing the distance (mean squared error) between adversarial
188
      examples and purified examples was introduced under the assumption that the distributions of
      adversarial examples and clean examples are close. This guidance can effectively prevent DM from
189
      generating a totally different image when diffused step T is larger. However, it is also possible that
190
      purified examples will be too close to adversarial examples such that even adversarial perturbation is
191   preserved. In Bai et al. (2024), an improved guidance was introduced, which uses contrastive loss
192   for encouraging purified examples xt−1 to be similar to xt . However, this guidance also considered
193   the distance between purified examples and diffused adversarial examples in terms of pixel values,
194   thus the perturbations can be partially preserved. Our conjecture is that the distribution of purified
195   examples should not be similar to that of adversarial examples, and the guidance should not use
196   adversarial perturbations explicitly. To this end, we consider that the guidance can be designed over
197   robust representations of purified examples and adversarial examples. Even if the distribution of their
198   representation is similar, the purified examples can be ensured not to contain adversarial perturbations
199
      explicitly while also keeping the semantic information similar.

      4     R ELATED W ORKS
282
      Adversarial robustness: To defend against adversarial attacks, researchers have developed various
283
      techniques aimed at enhancing the robustness of DNNs. Specifically, Zhang et al. (2019) propose
284
      TRADES that incorporates classification loss and discrepancy loss into adversarial training to enhance
285   the robustness of classifiers. Lin et al. (2024) propose AToP that fine-tunes the generator-based purifier
286   with adversarial training and makes it more suitable for robust classification tasks. Unlike Zhang
287   et al. (2019) optimizing the classifier and Lin et al. (2024) optimizing the purifier, our method utilizes
288   TRADES loss to train an auxiliary neural network to better guide diffusion model for adversarial
289   purification, avoiding the substantial computational cost of adversarial training on DMs and effectively
290   defending against unseen attacks.
291   Diffusion model based adversarial purification: Motivated by the great success of DMs, Yoon et al.
292   (2021); Nie et al. (2022) utilize a pre-trained DM for adversarial purification and achieve remarkable
293   performance in robust classification. In subsequent research, Wu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022) aim
294   to further preserve semantic information by minimizing the distance between adversarial examples
295   and purified examples. Bai et al. (2024) propose an improved guidance, which uses contrastive loss
296   to encourage the purified examples from adjacent steps to be similar. However, both guidances utilize
297   the distance measures in terms of pixel values, thus the perturbations can be partially preserved.
298   Distinguishing with these methods, we leverage distance measures within latent representations from
299
      an auxiliary neural network rather than relying on pixel-level differences, avoiding the recovery
      of perturbation information. Additionally, Zhang et al. (2024) propose classifier guidance, which
300
      preserves semantic information by directly using the confidence score from the downstream classifier
301
      trained on clean examples. However, if attackers gain access to the classifier information used in
302   the guidance, it may lead to incorrect confidence under adversarial attacks. In contrast, we train the
303   auxiliary neural network using adversarial training to provide more robust guidance.

      6    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose an adversarial guided diffusion model (AGDM) for adversarial purification,
489
      which can enhance the robustness power of DM-based AP by introducing adversarial guidance during
490   the reverse process. We conduct extensive experiments to empirically demonstrate that AGDM
491   is effective for simultaneously maintaining semantic information and removing the adversarial
492   perturbations, and exhibits robust generalization against unseen attacks.
493
      Limitations and discussion: Similar to previous studies (Nie et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang
494
      et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024), our proposed AGDM also features a time-consuming reverse process.
495
      Additionally, this paper adopts a heuristic perspective, we aim to use theoretical analysis in the future
496   to more comprehensively demonstrate the effectiveness of AGDM. In summary, we leave the study
497   of utilizing our adversarial guidance in more reliable and fast sampling strategies for future research.
