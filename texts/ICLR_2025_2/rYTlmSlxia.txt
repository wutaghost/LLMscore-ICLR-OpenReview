009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) offers in-depth subterranean insights, yet sub-
012            surface anomaly detection in GPR data remains challenging due to limited train-
013            ing data, typically confined to some normal data samples free from any subsurface
014            structures or anomalies, and the variability of subsurface conditions. In response,
015            this paper introduces practical and accurate subsurface anomaly detection within
016            the Cubic Decay Reservoir Network (CuDeRes) model space. Our approach em-
017            ploys commonly available normal GPR data, segmented into blocks. Each data
018            block is independently fitted using the introduced CuDeRes, which incorporates
019
               three reservoirs with spatial decay to adequately capture the data-inherent multi-
               directional dynamics, resulting in a compact fitted readout model. Representing
020
               each data block with the fitted model, together with the distance measurement
021
               between models, the original GPR data blocks are mapped into the CuDeRes
022            model space, and the fitted models are collected into a “Model Depot”. For subse-
023            quent anomaly detection in newly collected GPR data, the same segmentation and
024            CuDeRes fitting approaches are applied, where the data blocks are represented
025            by fitted models for comparative assessment against the model depot. Anomalies
026            are detected through model dissimilarities, and subsequently clustered within the
027            CuDeRes model space, allowing us to accurately identify the data blocks with
028            potential subsurface anomalies and ascertain their anomaly types. Experiments
029            on real-world GPR data demonstrate the practical effectiveness of our approach,
030
               notably using only limited normal data.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR), employing Electromagnetic (EM) waves, serves as a vital tool for
036   detecting subsurface structures or anomalies beneath urban roadways (Chen & Cohn, 2011). This
037   technology operates by transmitting high-frequency waves and analyzing the signals reflected back,
038
      which reveal the diverse properties and compositions under the surface (Zhou et al., 2018). The
      integration of multi-channel technology in GPR systems facilitates the simultaneous collection of
039
      EM waves across multiple antennas, enabling the generation of 3-dimensional GPR (3D GPR) data1 ,
040
      essential for assessing subsurface conditions (Goodman et al., 2013).
041
042   Detecting underground anomalies in GPR data typically involves segmenting data along the detect-
043   ing direction, and further identifying sections with potential subterranean issues (Zhou et al., 2023),
044
      a labor-intensive and time-consuming process when performed manually. Algorithms for image or
      signal feature extraction and classification aid in categorizing GPR data, but struggle due to the vari-
045
      able characteristics of subsurface anomalies, affected by their composition, size, and surrounding
046
      environment. Recent advances in Deep Learning (DL), particularly Convolutional Neural Networks
047   (CNNs), have also been applied to object and anomaly detection in GPR data (Liu et al., 2021; Liang
048   et al., 2022b). Despite their potential, DL methods face significant considerations: 1) The scarcity of
049   GPR data, particularly in a targeted detection area, often leads to limited training datasets predom-
050   inantly composed of normal samples; 2) The variability of underground environments undermines
051   the generalization capabilities of DL models, restricting their adaptability to various or unfamiliar

      Given the challenges associated with GPR data processing, the framework of Learning in the Model
077   Space (LMS) offers a viable alternative (Chen et al., 2013). LMS transitions data from data space to
078   model space by fitting the data with appropriate models that capture and describe the dynamics (i.e.,
079   changing information) within the data. Consequently, the fitted models serve as more stable and
080   parsimonious representations of the data, enabling effective implementation of learning algorithms
081   on the models rather than the original data. Successfully applied to diagnosing the Barcelona water
082   network (Quevedo et al., 2014) and the Tennessee Eastman Process (Chen et al., 2014), along with
083   diverse time-series classification tasks (Gong et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2022), LMS has proven its
084   efficacy in various tasks using the Echo-State-Network-based (ESN-based) methods for sequential
085
      data fitting and representation. Notably, LMS focuses on the data-intrinsic dynamics, which allows
      for reduced reliance on training data and diminishes the computational demands compared to many
086
      DL methodologies, particularly when optimally configuring the model (Ma et al., 2020).
087
088   While efforts have been made to apply LMS in GPR data processing, the following considerations
089   persist: 1) LMS typically fits data uni-directionally to capture inherent dynamics, primarily de-
090   signed for sequential data with contextual relationships. However, GPR data exhibits both vertical
091
      variations and horizontal correlations, stemming from the continuity of the subsurface medium and
      EM waves. Effectively capturing these multi-directional dynamics is essential for accurate fitting,
092
      representation, and classification of GPR data. 2) According to the spacing between GPR antenna
093
      channels, the sampling frequency along the detecting direction, and the dielectric constant of the
094   underground medium, the distances between adjacent points vary across different directions within
095   GPR data, leading to differing correlations along distinct spatial orientations, with some directions
096   correlating stronger than others. Such variation necessitates a precise capture of the unique dynam-
097   ics within each direction. Despite attempts to optimize dynamic capture using accurately labeled
098   multi-type data, acquiring a sufficiently diverse training dataset often remains impractical.
099

100   eRes) model space for subsurface anomaly detection in GPR data, illustrated in Figure 1. Our
101   approach only requires some normal GPR data, commonly available from the detecting area, to
102   support anomaly detection in subsequently collected data. We segment the normal GPR data into
103   blocks, with data segments in each block, referred to as GPR data blocks, being fitted by CuDeRes2 .
104   Given GPR data’s vertical continuity along EM waves and horizontal correlations due to subsurface
105   medium consistency, each point in GPR data is correlated with its surroundings in multiple direc-

210   2.3   A B RIEF I NTRODUCTION OF E CHO S TATE N ETWORK
211
212   Echo State Networks (ESNs) represent a subclass of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), known
213   for simplicity and efficiency in processing sequential data. As depicted in Figure 3, an ESN’s ar-
214   chitecture consists of three primary components: an input layer, a reservoir within the hidden layer,
215   and an output layer. A notable feature of ESNs is the use of randomized and fixed weights for both
      the input and the reservoir weights, also essential to maintain the Echo State Property (ESP).


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      In fitting sequential data, ESN first computes the hidden state for each point in the input sequence,
217   and the output layer maps this onto the target sequence, using ridge regression to determine output
218   weights. Although effective in fitting data along the processed direction, ESNs fail to adequately
219   capture dynamics in multi-dimensional data, missing changes in other directions.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
223
      As illustrated in Figure 1, our approach consists of two stages:
224
225           • Training Stage: Normal GPR data, free from subsurface anomaly and easily available
226             in the detection area, is segmented along the detecting direction into same-size blocks,
227             each independently fitted by CuDeRes to capture its multi-directional dynamics. The fitted
228             models, representing their respective data blocks, are collected into a “Model Depot”.
229           • Anomaly Detection: The same segmentation and fitting procedures are employed on
230             newly collected GPR data, with the resulting models compared to those in the model depot.
231             Models that deviate from the norm are identified and clustered, enabling the identification
232             of data blocks that contain subsurface anomalies and the determination of their types.
233
234
      In our approach, fitting GPR data and capturing its multi-directional dynamics via CuDeRes are
      crucial for data representation and subsequent anomaly detection on the fitted models. Therefore, we
235
      start with a comprehensive introduction to CuDeRes and its fitting process, followed by descriptions
236
      of the training stage and anomaly detection in the mode space.

357   3.3      A NOMALY D ETECTION IN C U D E R ES M ODEL S PACE
358
359   Given the “Model Depot”, anomaly detection on the newly collected GPR data is performed through
360
      the following three steps: 1) Data Segmentation and CuDeRes Fitting: Apply the same segmen-
      tation and fitting procedures used during training, obtaining the fitted CuDeRes model for each data
361
      block. 2) Model Discrimination: For each model, find the closest normal model in the model depot
362
      through the distance measurement given in Equation 9, examining its anomaly score, with overly
363   high scores indicating anomalies. 3) Model Clustering: Cluster the abnormal models identified in
364   the previous step, where each cluster signifies a type of anomaly.
365
366
      The first step, previously described and the same as the training stage, thus would not be repeated
      here. Details for the subsequent steps are provided as follows.

      3.3.1      D ISTANCE - BASED M ODEL D ISCRIMINATION
369
370   For anomaly detection with newly collected GPR data, the segmentation and CuDeRes fitting pro-

529
530      This study introduces learning in the Cubic Decay Reservoir Network (CuDeRes) for anomaly de-
531
         tection within 3D GPR data, specifically: 1) We introduce a novel CuDeRes, featuring three reser-
         voirs and spatial decay in each direction, designed to adequately and accurately capture the multi-
532
         directional dynamics within GPR data, resulting in a compact fitted readout model; 2) Representing
533
         GPR data using the fitted CuDeRes model, along with a computable distance measurement between
534      models, enables effective anomaly detection within the category-discriminative CuDeRes model
535      space; models fitted from different anomalies show noticeable differences owing to the distinct dy-
536      namics captured, while same-type data blocks derive similar fitted models due to their consistent
537      inherent dynamics; 3) Our approach concentrates on the inherent dynamics present in GPR data,
538      and leverages only limited normal GPR data, easily obtainable in the detecting area, to support
539      the subsequent anomaly detection, enabling its practical usability. Our future work will focus on

      In our experiments, three major subsurface diseases appear: cavities, looseness, and cracks

756
             • “Cavity” beneath experimental roads refers to a space predominantly filled with air. These
757            cavities can vary in shape and may have either smooth or rough boundaries. They are a pri-
758            mary factor leading to the subsidence observed in urban roadways. Due to the significantly
759            lower dielectric constant of air compared to surrounding materials, EM waves encountering
760            these cavities produce a pronounced reflection. Furthermore, these EM waves can undergo
761            multiple reflections within the cavity, occasionally accompanied by diffraction effects.
762
             • “Looseness” describes a soil condition characterized by increased porosity and reduced
763            density, especially when compared to adjacent soil with comparable water content. This
764            state leads to diminished particle cohesion, rendering the soil structure less compact. Such
765            a scenario can potentially lead to soil collapse and subsequent formation of underground
766            cavities. Essentially, loose soil can be conceptualized as a mixture of soil and air. The
767            heterogeneity in soil properties within areas of looseness results in erratic reflected waves
768            in GPR data, as waves traverse through these zones.
769          • “Crack” in road infrastructures refers to horizontal gaps within the previously dense un-
770            derground layers, typically air-filled. These gaps create a distinct scenario where wave
771            reflections are observed as the waves encounter the delamination. This interaction results
772            in notable alterations and sharp discontinuities in the waveform. When detecting such hori-
773            zontal delaminations, the collected waves exhibit changes nearly simultaneously, indicative
774            of this specific underground anomaly.

791   A.3   M ANIPULATIONS ON THE R AW GPR DATA
792
793   We have executed specific manipulations on the 3D GPR data to eliminate noise and accentuate sub-
794   surface objects. This process involves three key tasks: removing undesired surface echoes, reducing
795
      noise, and offsetting propagation losses. Initially, reflectance from the ground surface is eliminated.
      Subsequently, a standard median filter is employed on the data to diminish electromagnetic noise
796
      and interference. The final step involves applying a time-variant gain adjustment to counteract the
797
      propagation losses due to medium attenuation and the dispersion of signal energy radially. Our
798   approach to gain manipulation involves two main stages:
799
800          • Considering that targets like manhole covers can lead to data oversaturation, we isolate
801            these sections by setting a threshold for wave intensity significantly above the average.
802            Post this, we apply gain adjustments to the rest of the data.
803
             • The time-variant gain technique is utilized to progressively increase the gain exponent rel-
804            ative to the depth of detection, thus amplifying variations without causing oversaturation.
805            In instances where oversaturation is observed in data collected from roads, we modify the
806            gain for all corresponding data, ensuring the preservation of variable information. This
807            fine-tuning process requires manual intervention.
808
809   These manipulations are designed to maintain and highlight the dynamic information in the data
      while avoiding oversaturation. All adjustments are conducted using the Matgpr Matlab package,

      specifically tailored for GPR data analysis. It’s crucial to note that these modifications are consis-
811   tently applied across data from similar underground environments to ensure uniform treatment of all
812   data gathered from the same road.
