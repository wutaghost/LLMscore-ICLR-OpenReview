011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Motivated by applications in online bidding and sleeping bandits, we examine the
014
               problem of contextual bandits with cross learning, where the learner observes the
               loss associated with the action across all possible contexts, not just the current
015
               round’s context. Our focus is on a setting where losses are chosen adversarially,
016
               and contexts are sampled i.i.d. from a specific distribution. This problem was
017            first studied by Balseiro et al. (2019), who proposed an algorithm that achieves
018            near-optimal regret under the assumption that the context distribution is known
019            in advance. However, this assumption is often unrealistic. To address this issue,
020            Schneider & Zimmert (2023) recently proposed a new algorithm that achieves
021            nearly optimal expected regret. It is well-known that expected regret can be sig-
022            nificantly weaker than high-probability bounds. In this paper, we present a novel,
023            in-depth analysis of their algorithm and demonstrate that it actually achieves near-
024            optimal regret with high probability. There are steps in the original analysis by
025
               Schneider & Zimmert (2023) that lead only to an expected bound by nature. In
               our analysis, we introduce several new insights. Specifically, we make extensive
026
               use of the weak dependency structure between different epochs, which was over-
027
               looked in previous analyses. Additionally, standard martingale inequalities are not
028            directly applicable, so we refine martingale inequalities to complete our analysis.

031   1   INTRODUCTION
032
033   In the contextual bandits problem, a learner repeatedly observes a context, chooses an action, and
034   incurs a loss specific to that action. The goal of the learner is to minimize the cumulative loss over
035   the time horizon. The contextual bandits problem is a fundamental problem in online learning hav-
036
      ing broad applications in fields like online advertising, personalized recommendations, and clinical
      trials (Li et al., 2010; Kale et al., 2010; Villar et al., 2015).
037
038   We consider the cross-learning contextual bandits problem. In this setting, the learner not only
039   observes the loss for the current action under the current context, but also observes the loss for the
040   current action under all other contexts. This problem models many interesting scenarios. One such
041   example is the problem of learning to bid in first-price auctions. In this problem the context is the
042
      bidder’s private value for the item, while the action is the bid. The cross-learning structure comes
      from the fact that the bidder can deduce the utility of the bid under all contexts (i.e., the utility
043
      of the bid under different private valuations for the item). Other examples include multi-armed
044
      bandits with exogenous costs, dynamic pricing with variable costs, and learning to play in Bayesian
045   games (Balseiro et al., 2019).
046
047   Technically, the most interesting setting for the cross-learning contextual bandits problem is when
048
      the losses are chosen adversarially but the contexts are i.i.d. samples from an unknown distribution
                                                                                                   √
049   ν. Recently, Schneider & Zimmert (2023) gave an algorithm achieving nearly optimal O(     e KT )
050
      expected regret in this scenario.
051   Schneider & Zimmert (2023) designed a sophisticated algorithm that operates over multiple epochs
052   to achieve near-optimal regret. A key technique in their analysis is to sidestep high-probability
053   bounds and instead focus on bounding the expected summation to improve their results. As a con-
      sequence, their analysis only provides a bound that holds in expectation. It is not immediately clear

065   1.1   T ECHNICAL OVERVIEW
066
067   Our theorem is built on a new and more in-depth analysis of the algorithm in Schneider & Zimmert
068   (2023). This new analysis introduces several new insights. In particular, we exploit the weak depen-
069
      dency structure between different epochs, which was overlooked in previous work. One difficulty of
      doing so is that standard martingale inequalities are not directly applicable, so we refine martingale
070
      inequalities to complete our analysis.
071

      An important application of the cross-learning contextual bandits problem, which is also the primary
128
      motivation for proposing this problem in Balseiro et al. (2019), is to solve the problem of learning to
129   bid in first-price auctions. In this problem the context is the bidder’s private value for the item, while
130   the action is the bid. The cross learning structure comes from the fact that the bidder can deduce the
131   utility of the bid under all contexts (i.e., the utility of the bid under different private valuations for
132   the item).
133
      Balseiro et al. (2019) used the cross-learning contextual bandits problem to model the bidding prob-
134
      lem and obtained an O(T 3/4 ) regret bound for bidders with an unknown value distribution partic-
135
      ipating in adversarial first-price auctions, where the only feedback is whether the bidder wins the
136   auction. Later, many works studied different settings of the bidding in first price auctions problem.
137   For example, Han et al. (2020b) considered the problem with censored feedback, where each bidder
138   observes the winning bid. Han et al. (2020a) considered the scenario when the value is also adver-
139   sarial. Ai et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023) considered the problem under budget constraints. In all
140   these scenarios, the cross learning structure between different values is an essential component of
141   the analysis.
142   Another interesting application of the cross-learning contextual bandits problem is the sleeping ban-
143   dits problem (Kleinberg et al., 2010; Neu & Valko, 2014; Kale et al., 2016; Saha et al., 2020). In
144   this problem, a certain set of arms is unavailable in each round. The sleeping bandits problem is mo-
145   tivated by instances like some items might go out of stock in retail stores or on a certain day some
146   websites could be down. When losses are adversarial and availabilities are stochastic, previous work
147   either requires exponential computing time (Kleinberg et al., 2010; Neu & Valko, 2014) or results in
148   suboptimal regret (Kale√et al., 2016; Saha et al., 2020). The first computationally efficient algorithm
149   with optimal regret O(
                           e KT ) is proposed in Schneider & Zimmert (2023) by modeling the problem
150   as a cross-learning contextual bandit.
151   We also note that handling unknown context distributions is a common and challenging problem
152   across various contextual bandit problems. For example, in the adversarial linear contextual bandits
153   problem (Neu & Olkhovskaya, 2020), the linear MDP problem (Dai et al., 2023), and the oracle-
154   based adversarial contextual bandits problem (Syrgkanis et al., 2016), existing algorithms often
155   rely on knowledge of the context distribution. Removing the reliance on knowledge of the context
156   distribution is typically non-trivial (Liu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023).
157   Recently, Hanna et al. (2023) proposed a method for stochastic linear contextual bandits that maps
158   a multi-context problem to a single-context problem. Unfortunately, their approach cannot be di-
159   rectly applied to our problem for two reasons. First, their method is designed for stochastic bandits,
160   whereas we deal with adversarial bandits. Second, their approach is limited to linear contextual
161   bandits. Whether it can be adapted, with certain modifications, to address our problem remains an
      intriguing question.

444   5   C ONCLUSIONS
445
446   We reanalyze the algorithm proposed by Schneider & Zimmert (2023) and show that it actually
447   achieves near-optimal regret with high probability for the cross-learning contextual bandits problem
448   when the losses are chosen adversarially but the contexts are i.i.d. sampled from an unknown dis-
449   tribution. Our key technique is utilizing the weak dependency structure between different epochs
450   for an algorithm executing over multiple epochs. It is of interest to investigate that whether this
451   techniques is applicable for deriving high probability bounds for algorithms executing over multiple
452   epochs in other problems.
