009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Formal verification has emerged as a promising method to ensure the safety and
012            reliability of neural networks. Naively verifying a safety property amounts to
013            ensuring the safety of a neural network for the whole input space irrespective of
014            any training or test set. However, this also implies that the safety of the neural
015            network is checked even for inputs that do not occur in the real-world and have
016
               no meaning at all, often resulting in spurious errors. To tackle this shortcoming,
               we propose the VeriFlow architecture as a flow based density model tailored to
017
               allow any verification approach to restrict its search to the some data distribution of
018
               interest. We argue that our architecture is particularly well suited for this purpose
019            because of two major properties. First, we show that the transformation that is
020            defined by our model is piecewise affine. Therefore, the model allows the usage
021            of verifiers based on constraint solving with linear arithmetic. Second, upper
022            density level sets (UDL) of the data distribution take the shape of an Lp -ball in
023            the latent space. As a consequence, representations of UDLs specified by a given
024            probability are effectively computable in the latent space. This property allows for
025            effective verification with a fine-grained, probabilistically interpretable control of
026            how (a-)typical the inputs subject to verification are.

028   1     I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The outstanding performance of neural networks in tasks such as object detection (Zhao et al.,
031   2019) image classification (Rawat & Wang, 2017), anomaly detection (Pang et al., 2021) and
032   natural language processing (Goldberg, 2016) made them a popular solution for many real-world-
033   applications, including safety-critical ones. With the increasing popularity of neural networks, defects
034   and limitations of these systems have been witnessed by the general public. The AI incident database1
035   keeps track of harms and near-harms caused by AI-Systems in the real world.
036   Ideally, safety and fairness properties of such inherently opaque neural networks should be formally
037   guaranteed when used in safety-critical applications. As a solution, formal verification can be used to
038   check whether a neural network satisfies a given safety property for the entire input space, or whether
039   there exists some (synthetic) input for which the desired property is violated. This is in contrast to the
040   statistical testing methods classically employed in Machine learning, where the output of the neural
041   network is checked for a finite set of samples, usually from a held-out test set.
042   However, state-of-the-art formal verification methods only allow for verifying either global or local
043   properties. Global properties ensure a specific behaviour of the neural network on the whole input
044   space. As an example, fairness properties require the neural network to predict the same output for any
045   two inputs that only differ in some sensitive attribute. Local properties, on the other hand, ensure a
046   specific behaviour of the neural network only in some part of the input space that is usually restricted
047   using the training set. One well-studied example for a local property is adversarial robustness which
048   requires the neural network to classify any point from the data set as the same class as any minor
049
      perturbation of that point. However, both global and local properties have shortcomings limiting their
      applicability. Global properties refer to the entire input space, but we may not want or need to verify
050
      the property for noise-inputs or on regions of the input space for which there were no training samples
051
      available (epistemic uncertainty). Local properties, on the other hand, suffer from the same problem
052   as statistical testing, i.e., they rely on a high-quality data set that the verification property is based on.

      To overcome these problems, we design a flow model tailored towards the application in neural
055   network verification and leverage it to restrict the input space of the neural network under verification
056   to the underlying data distribution. In contrast to generative adversarial networks (GANs) and
057   variational autoencoders, flow models do not only allow for efficient sampling but also provide
058   probabilistic interpretability via tractable likelihoods (Papamakarios et al., 2021; Goodfellow et al.,
059   2014; Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Dinh et al., 2015; Tabak & Vanden-Eijnden, 2010). This feature
060   is important to facilitate fine-grained probabilistic control when restricting the input space to typical
061   inputs when specifying the verification property. This approach makes the verification property less
062   reliant on the dataset, while still keeping the input space focused on meaningful data.
063   To illustrate this idea, consider a neural network trained to classify images of handwritten digits.
064   Assume we want the classifier to always be confident about it’s classification of a certain digit. More
065   precisely, our verification property is that the neural network’s confidence is high if it classifies
066   an image as 7 or 8, respectively. If the neural network does not satisfy the verification property,
067   the verification tool will return an image that is classified as 7 or 8 with low confidence. Such
068   counterexamples are illustrated in Figure 2. The two counterexamples on the left side in Figure 2
069
      were found with a traditional (constraint-based) verifier without leveraging our flow model. These
      counterexample are noise images without any meaning and come from a region of the input space
070
      with high epistemic uncertainty. However, the counterexamples on the right side of Figure 2 were
071
      obtained by leveraging our flow model to restrict the input space of the verification property to typical
072   inputs. In the context of Figure 2, the right side restricts the input space to a UDL of given probability
073   and therefore, the counterexamples come from within the data distribution and provide better insights
074   into the classifiers’ weakness when used in a real-world scenario. We refer to Section 4 for more
075   details on this experiment.
076
      We briefly list our main theoretical results. We design a novel flow model with L1 -radially monotonic
077
      base distributions that provides the following theoretical properties crucial for the verification domain
078   while outperforming its normalizing counterparts in nearly all benchmarks. Specifically, we design a
079   flow model that:
080
081         1. Maps upper/lower (log-)density level sets of the target distribution to upper/lower
082            (log-)density level sets of the base distribution allowing for fine-grained probabilistic control
083            during sampling.
084         2. Allows the definition of the pre-image of a density level set in the latent space via linear
085            constraints.
086
087   These properties enable us to restrict the verification to a probabilistically meaningful subset of the
088   input space and differentiate VeriFlow from generic flow architectures. Indeed, density level sets of
089   flow models generally do not have tractable pre-images in latent space.
090   We identify sufficient conditions for a flow layer to have the aforementioned properties, survey the
091   literature, and present a collection of layers - with its most representative members being additive
092   coupling layers and bijective affine layers - that can be arbitrarily combined to yield a flow with the
093   desired properties.

      2    BACKGROUND AND O RGANIZATION
096
097
      Verifying neural networks involves checking if a network f satisfies a semantic property P , often
098
      expressed as ϕ(x) =⇒ ψ(f (x)), where ϕ and ψ are pre- and postconditions. In this paper, we
099   use two conceptually different verification approaches: constraint-based verification and abstract
100   interpretation. We briefly explain the core idea of each verification approach and refer to Albarghouthi
101   (2021) for an in-depth explanation.
102
103   Constraint-based Verification This approach translates f and P into a logical formula Ψf,P ,
104   whose validity implies f satisfies P . Efficient SMT solvers like Marabou 2 (Wu et al., 2024) handle
105   these formulas for networks with linear components such as ReLU activations. Verifying Ψf,P
106   involves checking the unsatisfiability of ¬Ψf,P . If ¬Ψf,P is satisfiable, a counterexample exists.
107   These methods are complete and provide counterexamples when the property is violated, though their
      runtime can be high for positive proofs.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



108
      Abstract Interpretation Abstract interpretation symbolically executes a neural network using
109   geometric abstract domains like zonotopes, which over-approximate input sets. By propagating these
110   through the network, the procedure over-approximates possible outputs. Verification succeeds if the
111   output lies entirely within the "safe space" defined by the semantic property ψ. If outputs are outside,
112   the property fails; partial overlap leaves the result inconclusive due to over-approximation.
113
114   Organization On a high level, we propose a flow model that piecewise linear and thus, can be
115   encoded into the semantic property P and used as part of the specification for the downstream
116   verification task. In the next section, we present our flow model architecture. To the best of our
117   knowledge, all propositions in Section 3 are novel contributions. In the consequent Section 4, we
118   show our experimental results and showcase, how the flow model can be leveraged for enhancing the
119
      verification of a global property.

123   Flow models provide an elegant way to represent a density estimator and a generative model by a
124   single network. More precisely, we train a flow to transform a simple base (or latent) distribution B
125   into the data (or target) distribution D using a continuous invertible map with continuous inverse, i.e.
126   a diffeomorphism 2 . The map F is implemented by an invertible neural network. We obtain a density
127   estimator and a generative model by applying the flow in both directions:

      While most neural networks are intrinsically differentiable, they do not represent bijections in general.
135
      One needs to design specific architectures that restrict the hypothesis space to diffeomorphisms. Note
136   that the existence of an inverse does not necessarily imply that the inverse can be easily computed.
137   There are flow architectures that allow only one of the above operations to be efficiently performed
138   (Kobyzev et al., 2020; Papamakarios et al., 2021). However, it is also not uncommon that both
139   operation have the same complexity (Dinh et al., 2015). The major goal of this work is to design a
140   flow architecture that does not only allow to perform both operations efficiently, but it also allows
141   an efficient analysis of the flow in a verification context. For the latter, it is often required to not
142   only sample individually, but verify on a space of sampled objects. We define the spaces containing
143   high-density and low-density samples as upper- and lower density level sets.
144
145   Definition 1. Given the density of the input distribution pD : Rd → R+ . The set of points whose
146   density exceeds a given threshold t is called the upper density level set (UDL) and is defined as
147   L↑D (t) := {x ∈ Rd | pD (x) > t}. Respectively, the lower density level set (LDL) contains the set of
148   points deceeding the density threshold: L↓D (t) := {x ∈ Rd | pD (x) ≤ t} = Rd \ L↑D . If for q ∈ [0, 1]
149   there is a unique UDL of D with probability q, then we denote this set by UDLD (q).
150
151   Upper density level sets naturally capture the center of the distribution while LDLs capture the
152   tail. Note that the existence and uniqueness of the UDL with given probability is guaranteed if
153   PD ({x | p(x)RD = t}) = 0 for all t > 0, where PD denotes the probability induced by pD , i.e.
154   P (x ∈ S) := S pD (x)dx.

247   Interestingly, it turns out that restricting the popular coupling layers to piecewise affine operations
248
      naturally leads to uniformly scaling flows. We shortly summarize our findings in the remaining of

251   We survey the literature to identify learnable components that satisfy our needs. Most prominently,
252   additive conditioning layers, like additive coupling, turn out to yield exactly the networks that we
253   envision. This encompasses the seminal NICE architecture (Dinh et al., 2015), additive auto-regressive
254
      layers (Kingma et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2018; Papamakarios et al., 2017), and masked additive
      convolutions (Ma et al., 2019).
255
256   Additionally, bijective affine transformations represented by LU-decomposed affine layers (LUNets
257   by Chan et al. (2023)) turn out to be equally well suitable. These layers also constitute a powerful
258   replacement for the permutations or masks that are usually employed before a coupling-like layer.
259   Although this addition turned out to be very beneficial, we note that the number of parameters scales
260
      quadratically with the dimension, which can be a performance bottleneck on high dimensional data.
261   Finally, we summarize the good properties of flows build from these layers in the following proposi-
262   tion, which also defines our proposed architecture. A more in-depth treatment of the layer architectures
263   can be found in the supplementary material.
264
265
266
267   Proposition 4 (VeriFlow). Let F be a network that is purely built from the layer types (masked)
268   additive coupling, additive autoregression, masked additive convolution, LU layers, component-wise
269   scaling, and permutation of input dimensions. If the first three layer types only use piecewise affine
      conditioning networks, then F is a uniformly scaling piecewise affine flow.

278   4.1   V ERIFICATION E XPERIMENTS
279
280   We conduct our verification experiments on a downscaled version of the MNIST dataset, where the
281   original 28 × 28 pixel images have been reduced to 10 × 10 pixels. This downsizing was necessitated
282   by the limitations of the constraint-based verifier Marabou, which struggles to scale to large networks.
283   It is essential to note that this constraint is specific to the verifier used and not an inherent restriction
284   of our approach. In fact, our subsequent results demonstrate that by leveraging abstract interpretation,
285   our methodology successfully scales to larger networks. Nonetheless, to maintain comparability
286
      across our results, we also used the downscaled MNIST dataset for our scalability experiments.
287   We trained a total of 10 flow models independently from each other. Each flow model is trained on
288   a specific MNIST class representing a digit. We denote a flow model for digit i on our downscaled
289   MNIST dataset (MNIST10×10 i     ), which we denote by g0 , . . . , g9 . Each flow model in our experiments
290   has 3 additive coupling layers with 100 neurons and ReLu activation functions. Furthermore, we
291   trained classifiers with varying depth fℓ , where ℓ corresponds to the number of layers 1 ≤ ℓ ≤ 15,
292
      with each layer consisting of a matrix multiplication, addition and ReLU activation, in the network.
      We trained the networks with Adam-optimizer obtaining an accuracy score of around 90% for all
293
      classifiers. We did not aim at making the classifiers particularly safe or unsafe w.r.t. the verification
294
      tasks at hand. The final classification then corresponds to the respective digit with the highest score.
295
296   As verification tools, we use the Python interface of the C++ implemented verification framework
297   Marabou 2 (Wu et al., 2024) for deductive verification and ERAN for abstract interpretation (ERAN).
298
      Since both verifiers only allow for one network to be parsed, we merge both networks together by
      piping the output of the flow model gτ into the classifier fℓ , resulting in one neural network computing
299
      the composition fℓ ◦ gτ : R10×10 → R10 . We run all our verification-experiments on a Ubuntu 22.04

      machine with an i7-1365U CPU at 1.80 GHz, 32 GB RAM and Intel Iris Xe Graphics.

335   Scalability In this experiment, we assess the scalability of our flow model in the verification domain.
336   To this end, we consider both the deductive verification tool Marabou 2 (Wu et al., 2024) and the
337   abstract interpretation tool ERAN (ERAN). In order to assess the scalability reliably, we focus on
338   verifying properties that are satisfied by the neural network. Otherwise, the deductive verifier could
339   terminate early after finding a counterexample while barely touching the search space. We verify for
340   a classifier fℓ that for a subspace of the 1% UDL, the network fℓ classifies the whole output space of
341
      the flow model g0 restricted by the UDL as the digit 0. We show this property only for a subspace of
      the UDL because the full UDL does not constitute a zonotope as required when using the deepzono
342
      domain (Singh et al., 2018). One could eliminate this problem by either choosing an ∞-radial base
343
      distribution for the flow or by using abstract interpretation algorithms that can handle more general

464   5    R ELATED W ORK
465
466   We first provide an overview of verification tools that can be leveraged for verification and could
467   take our flow model as part of the specification. Generally speaking Polytopes (Chen et al., 2008),
468   Zonotopes or even simple boxes are sufficient for representing the UDL of our flow model in
469   the latent space precisely. This enables the use of more recent developments based on these do-
470
      mains that enhance precision or scalability for verification such as Deepzono (Singh et al., 2018),
      DeepPoly (Singh et al., 2019a), GPUPoly (Serre et al., 2021), RefineZono (Singh et al., 2019b),
471
      multi-neuron abstraction (Müller et al., 2023) and DiffPoly (Banerjee et al., 2024).
472
473   Besides of Marabou and ERAN, another verification framework that achieves promising results in the
474   VNNComp competition is α, β−crown (abcrown). It consists of numerous verification algorithms
475   and combines abstract interpretation methods with branch-and-bound methods. In particular, GCP-
476
      CROWN (Zhang et al., 2022) recently became part of the α, β−crown framework and enables the use
      of general cutting plane methods in combination with GPU accelerated bound propagation methods.
477
      Similarly, the verifier MN-BAB (Ferrari et al., 2022) is utilizes both branch-and-bound and convex
478
      relaxation but still provides completeness of the verification result.
479
480   Other verifiers that participate in the VNNComp are Cora Althoff (2015), PyRAT (Lemesle et al.,
481   2024), nnenum (Bak, 2021a) and NNV (Lopez et al., 2023). We refer to the VMNComp (Brix et al.,
482
      2023) competition for a comprehensive overview. Note that while the aforementioned verifiers are
      conceivable for use as downstream verifiers with VeriFlow, they are generally incomplete and do not
483
      provide counterexamples when the neural network is unsafe.
484
485   However, the research in the verification context often focuses on the verification of local robustness
      properties (Balunovic et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2023; Banerjee & Singh, 2024). These works also


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



486
      uncover that neural networks are highly non-robust even for small perturbations and even when
487   trained with robust training algorithms (Gowal et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019b;a). Verification of
488   global properties as tackled in our work is arguably harder as it is naturally subject a greater input
489   space. In this context, our flow model enables relaxing the global property by restricting the input
490   space to the high-density region of the data distribution. This removes the necessity of the neural
491   network to ensure the global property on the whole input space, which includes meaningless noise
492   data. In other words, VeriFlow aims to push the challenging verification of global properties more
493   towards local properties by restricting the input space to the data distribution.
494   Optimization-based approaches have recently emerged as a more scalable and higher-performing alter-
495   native to constraint-based and simple zonotope-based methods for verifying neural networks (Toledo
496   et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Mangal et al., 2020; Müller et al., 2023; Koller et al., 2024). While our
497   flow model is, in principle, compatible with these optimization-based approaches and our restriction
498   of the search space to an upper density sets can be formulated as a constrained optimization, we leave
499   a thorough evaluation of their integration for future work.
500   Paralleling our efforts to design a flow model amenable to verification using existing infrastructure,
501   recent studies have explored automated preprocessing techniques for neural networks, including
502   pruning (Guidotti et al., 2020) and regularization methods (Leofante et al., 2023; Böing & Müller,
503   2022). The ultimate goal of this direction is to render them more suitable for verification with
504   state-of-the-art verifiers.
505   Flow models are on the forefront of modern density estimation techniques and have received signif-
506   icant attention over the last decade (Papamakarios et al., 2019). A constant Jacobian determinant
507   is usually observed at the time of the introduction of the respective layer in the context of the like-
508   lihood computation (Dinh et al., 2015; Ma et al., 2019; Kingma et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2018;
509   Papamakarios et al., 2017), although typically without further investigation of the induced properties.
510   The role of the Jacobian determinant in general has been investigated in the context of the exploding
511   determinant phenomenon (Kim et al., 2020; Liao & He, 2021; Lyu et al., 2022). There is also a
512   notable application of flow architectures with constant Jacobian determinant for anomaly detection.
513
      OneFlow uses a constant Jacobian determinant to compute and minimize the volume the image of a
      unit hyper sphere around the origin in the latent space, drawing a connection between flows and deep
514
      one-class SVMs (Maziarka et al., 2022).

517   6    C ONCLUSION
518
519   We have presented the VeriFlow architecture, a flow-based density model that enables effective
520   verification of neural networks within a specified data distribution and fits in any existing verification
521   infrastructure. By using a novel approach of restricting the search space to probabilistically meaningful
522
      subsets of the input space, VeriFlow mitigates spurious errors and provides fine-grained, interpretable
      control over the input space. Independent of the verification domain, VeriFlow outperforms the NICE
523
      architecture, taken as baseline for uniformly scaling flow architectures, by a large margin.
524
525   Our flow-based verification approach shares a limitation inherent to neuro-symbolic frameworks: the
526   quality of the verification results is contingent upon the quality of the specification networks, in this
527   case, the flow model. However, as noted by Xie et al. (2022), these networks are generally smaller
528
      and more manageable than the networks under verification, allowing for additional training efforts
      and quality assurance measures, such as adversarial training. Moreover, the creation and improvement
529
      of flow models could be facilitated through public competitions, community-driven initiatives, or
530
      even future regulatory oversight, ultimately ensuring their accuracy and reliability.
531
532   We envision three promising avenues for future research. Firstly, we believe that verification tools
533   should be enhanced to provide more comprehensive support for the capabilities of popular deep
534
      learning frameworks like PyTorch, particularly in scenarios involving multiple networks. Secondly,
      a deeper theoretical and practical understanding of the expressivity of piecewise affine uniformly
535
      scaling flows is needed. To the best of our knowledge there is no known universal approximation
536
      theorem that applies to our architectures. Lastly, well-calibrated density level sets are essential for
537   producing interpretable verification results. Therefore, improving the consistency of density level sets
538   in deep learning models poses an important challenge that warrants further investigation.

      LUN ETS
928
929   Recently, bijective fully-connected layers have been proposed by Chan et al. (2023) as a so-called
930   LUNet. The idea is to ensure that that both, the affine transformation of a fully connected layer and
931   the non-linearity are bijections. Bijectivity is ensured by representing the linear transform of the layer
932   by an LU-factorization A = LU with lower/upper triangular Matrices L and U . Bijectivity is ensured
933   by adding the constraints that the diagonal of L contains only ones and diagonal of U is always
934   non-zero. In this case, Propositions 2 and 5 will still hold if we replace the layer architecture and use
935
      leaky ReLU instead of ReLU, but Proposition 3 will in general not hold anymore as the determinant
      of the layer Jacobean is not constant anymore.
936
937   LUNet is a very different approach to guaranteeing the bijectivity of the transformation compared
938   to additive coupling. It has the advantage that the entire input can be transformed by a single layer.
939   The restriction that the affine transform needs to be bijective, however, fixes the capacity of the
940   transformation to d2 parameters where d is the input dimension. This can be problematic, especially
941
      when working with high-dimensional data.

      B IJECTIVE A FFINE L AYERS
944   The bijective affine transform T (x) = (LU )x + b at the heart of an LU-layer deserves special
945   attention. Note that the determinant of the Jacobean is is constant for T . Since computing the inverse
946   of an affine transform also has the complexity of computing the affine transform it follows that we
947   can add bijective affine layers to the list of layers in Proposition 6 without loosing the validity of the
948   statement. Bijective affine layers can be an interesting alternative to the intermediate permutation
949   layers of the NICE architecture. Using an affine bijection instead of a simple fixed permutation of
950   the dimensions allows the architecture to correlate the components of I1 and I2 in the subsequent
951
      coupling layer in a learnable fashion. As an example, consider the extreme case where all components
      of the target distribution are independent. In this case, the components I1 and I2 will be independent,
952
      no matter which permutation of the components we have applied beforehand. An affine bijection,
953
      however, can be capable of combining the variables in a way such that the components I1 and I2
954   become correlated.

      Following the description given by Chan et al. (2023), we regularize the parameters of the LU
964
      layers. Without any form regularization, we observed exploding determinants on many tasks when
965
      working with LU layers. Additionally, we adopt the technique of soft training (Kim et al., 2020).
966   During training, We sample a noise scale σ from a prior distribution P for each training sample and
967   perturb the sample with noise sampled from from the base distribution (Gaussian or Laplacian) with
968   covariance σI. For the noise scale prior we use a Laplacian with small standard deviation. We fit a
969   conditional flow on the perturbed data where the conditioning variable is the noise scale σ. During
970   inference with unperturbed data, the noise scale is set to 0. We observed significant improvements
971   through soft training, in terms of test likelihoods but also in terms of subjective visual quality as
      evaluated by the authors.

       C.2    E PISTEMIC U NCERTAINTY V ERIFICATION
973
974    For the far tail of the data distribution there are usually no samples available. Hence, any model
975    trained purely from data has never gotten information about these areas (epistemic uncertainty). In
976    that context, we can verify that a classifier was trained with a vanishing inductive bias by moving away
977    from the training data. In this case the uncertainty estimates given by a classifier should converge
978    towards a prior distribution, e.g., uniform, as we move further outwards in the tail (Kendall & Gal,
979    2017; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021). However, it is known that many deep neural network training
980    methods produce badly calibrated networks with overconfident predictions, especially in areas of high
981
       epistemic uncertainty (Guo et al., 2017; Minderer et al., 2021). In that context, we want to verify that
       our classifier is not overconfident in the far tail of the data distribution. More precisely, we leverage
982
       our flow model to restrict the input to the 9% tail of the data distribution, trimming the last 1% to
983
       avoid an unbounded input space and verify that the classifier has a low confidence for all atypical
984    inputs.
