009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Developing robots capable of generalized skills remains an exceedingly challeng-
012            ing task. Drawing from psychology, the concept of affordance has emerged as
013            a promising intermediate representation to guide robot manipulation. However,
014            prior work has primarily focused on 2D affordances from video, neglecting crit-
015            ical spatial information such as camera positioning, absolute position, depth and
016
               geometry. In this paper, we present a novel training-free method that constructs
               3D affordances from egocentric demonstration videos. To address the challenge of
017
               insufficient static, high-quality frames for 3D reconstruction in egocentric videos,
018
               we employ the 3D foundational model DUST3R Wang et al. (2024), which recon-
019            structs scenes from sparse images without requiring COLMAP Schönberger &
020            Frahm (2016). We analyze videos using hand detection to identify contact times
021            and 2D contact points, reconstruct these interactions using DUST3R, and project
022            the 2D contact points into 3D space using gaussian heatmaps. Finally, we derive
023            hand trajectories through 3D hand pose estimation and process them using linear
024            regression to integrate the spatiotemporal dynamics of human-object interactions.
025            We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method on the ego4d-exo dataset Grau-
026            man et al. (2024) for seven real-world hand-object manipulation tasks in cooking
027
               scenes.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Teaching a home robot to generalize across daily tasks is highly challenging, particularly even
032   straightforward manipulation tasks can rapidly escalate in complexity with variations in the envi-
033   ronment. Although end-to-end reinforcement learning has seen significant advancements, achieving
034   robust generalization remains challenging. In the domain of autonomous driving, intermediate rep-
035   resentations such as monocular depth estimation, 3D object detection, and semantic segmentation
036   have been developed to enhance system performance. Similarly, in the context of robot manip-
037   ulation, various intermediate representations have been proposed to address these challenges and
038
      enhance task generalization.
039   The term ”affordance,” borrowed from psychology, was defined by James Gibson as ’an action
040   possibility available in the environment to an individual, irrespective of their ability to perceive it.’
041   In robotics, affordance typically refers to the ways objects can be utilized, and this concept is widely
042   applied in the perception community to build representations that assist robots in learning how to
043   interact with functional objects. These representations often take the form of heatmaps, sometimes
044
      complemented by usage trajectories, indicating how objects should be used.
045   Moreover, within the field of machine learning, there has been a predominant focus on extracting
046   information from large-scale image and text datasets. However, in applications that involve inter-
047   acting with the environment, such as robotics, there is a critical need for information beyond mere
048   pixels or text. Effective robotic functioning requires spatial-temporal data. As such, we assert the
049   importance of extracting information from videos, which provide rich content encompassing both
050
      spatial and temporal dimensions, as essential for advancing robotic capabilities.
051   While previous methods, as defined Bahl et al. (2023), view affordance primarily in terms of its
052   contact point and the target object’s post-contact trajectory in 2D pixel space from video, this ap-
053   proach introduces several problems in robotic learning. Firstly, depth perception remains ambiguous
      in pixel space, making it difficult for robots to ascertain the distance to objects. Furthermore, the

      2.1   AFFORDANCE
103
104   The concept of affordance, originally introduced by James J. Gibson in psychology, describes the
105   potential actions enabled by the relationship between an agent and its environment. This abstract
106   concept has been interpreted variously across different studies. One of the most recognized defini-
107   tions within the field identifies affordances with contact heatmaps that highlight interaction contact
      points. Key research efforts have explored this concept across various dimensions.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Cross-view-AG Luo et al. (2022) introduces a method to learn and apply affordance grounding from
109   diverse human-object interactions to egocentric views, using the new AGD20K dataset. Then, Af-
110   former Chen et al. (2023) extends affordance from image to video by grounding human affordances
111   from demonstration videos to target images through a transformer model. VRB Bahl et al. (2023)
112   presents a method to learn visual affordances from human interaction videos for deployment across
113   various robotic platforms to perform real-world tasks effectively. MIRA Lin Yen-Chen (2022) uses
114   mental imagery and affordance prediction to optimize robot actions in complex scenarios through
115   novel view synthesis. Finally, Affordance Diffusion Ye et al. (2023) develops a method to synthesize
116   hand-object interactions from RGB images, employing a two-step generative approach to enhance
117
      affordance prediction capabilities.
118   Another point worthy of mention is that there is extensive research related to affordance, it often
119   lacks task-specific datasets that bridge to real world scenario. Typically, studies create datasets on a
120   task-by-task basis or borrow from video understanding datasets, which frequently results in a lack
121   of quantitative measures and universal criteria. To address these challenges, SceneFun Delitzas
122   et al. (2024) introduces a fine-grained 3D affordance dataset. Specifically, it offers comprehensive
123
      annotations including functional interactive elements, affordance categories, natural language task
      descriptions, and 3D motion estimation parameters, enabling more rigorous and nuanced analyses.
124
125
126   2.2   OBJECT REPRESENTATION FOR ROBOT MANIPULATION
127
128
      In robot manipulation, point clouds have been effectively utilized in PartManip Geng et al. (2023),
      leveraging data directly from active sensors for intuitive interactions. Recent advancements, such as
129
      those in Weng et al. (2023), employ neural implicit representations like signed distance fields, which
130
      are crucial for accurately computing distances to objects, thereby facilitating key robotic tasks. Ad-
131   ditionally, Neural Descriptor Fields (NDFs) introduced in Simeonov et al. (2022) enable robots to
132   learn and generalize object manipulation tasks with translational invariance, using self-supervised,
133   SE(3)-equivariant mappings that convert 3D spatial coordinates into meaningful descriptors for in-
134   teraction.
135
      Further advancing these representations, innovative research, exemplified by F3RM Shen et al.
136
      (2023) and LERF-TOGO Rashid et al. (2023), integrates vision-language model embeddings with
137   physical object interactions, allowing robots to determine object positions precisely via language
138   prompts, a pivotal skill for effective manipulation and grasping. Furthermore, the concept of affor-
139   dances, often visualized as heatmaps on objects, as demonstrated in studies like VRB Bahl et al.
140   (2023), significantly enhances a robot’s ability to comprehend an object’s functional uses.
141
142
      2.3   ROBOT LEARNING FROM VIDEO
143
144   Learning from demonstration has long been an effective strategy for training robots. This approach
145   is exemplified by works such as Liu et al. (2018). However, prior to the development of extensive
146   pretraining techniques, the challenge of training robots to generalize from video demonstrations
147   prompted researchers to pivot towards reinforcement learning. Numerous strategies have been devel-
148   oped to utilize video as a foundational resource. One notable method is imitation from observation
149   Liu et al. (2018), which enables robots to learn basic tasks like sweeping and pushing by emulating
150   human actions from videos, employing context translation and deep reinforcement learning. Addi-
151
      tionally, DexMV Qin et al. (2021) offers a robust platform and pipeline for dexterous manipulation
      using human videos, allowing robots to master complex manipulation tasks by transforming human
152
      video demonstrations into robot-executable demonstrations via an innovative translation technique.
153
      Lastly, SWIM Mendonca et al. (2023) harnesses large-scale internet data to train world models for
154   robotics, utilizing structured action spaces derived from human videos.

157   3     METHOD
158
159   Follow from the work of VRB Bahl et al. (2023), we define an affordance as the contact point and
160   the post-contact trajectory. Our objective is to extend this concept from 2D to 3D. To achieve this,
161   we aim to reconstruct the scene, bind 2D heatmap on to the 3D mesh, and model the post-contact
      trajectories in 3D using video and camera pose ground truth as inputs.

181   3.1   C ONTACT D ETECTION AND H AND S EGMENTATION
182
183   To address the motion blur and poor image quality inherent in egocentric videos, we first employ
184
      NAFNet Chen et al. (2022) to enhance the quality of the video frames, thereby improving the effec-
      tiveness of our subsequent model.
185
186   Follow from VRB Bahl et al. (2023), we use off-the-shelf hand contact detector Shan et al. (2020)
187   to detect hand contact of the object in the scene. Additionally, we utilize LangSAM Kirillov et al.
188   (2023) for hand segmentation. For the final step in our process, we use LaMa Suvorov et al. (2021)
189   to remove the hand from images, thereby preparing them for reconstruction.
190
191   3.2   S INGLE I MAGE R ECONSTRUCTION
192
      To address the challenges of video demonstrations that lack sufficient imagery of the scene and the
193
      contact object, we consider the limitations of traditional 3D reconstruction methods like COLMAP.
194
      These methods are often slow and susceptible to failure, typically due to poor image quality, inade-
195   quate correspondence between images, or simply not having enough images.
196
197   We adopt the 3D reconstruction foundation model, DUST3R Wang et al. (2024). Dust3R is capable
198
      of generating point maps and meshes from sparse, low-quality images and does not require detailed
      camera parameters, making it ideal for scenarios involving sub-optimal image data.
199
200   For our process, we input the hand-removed contact image into Dust3R for single-image reconstruc-
201   tion.
202
203   3.3   3 D CONTACT POINT
204
205
      We project the 2D contact point, obtained from the contact detection Shan et al. (2020) output, onto
      the 3D mesh using the camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters and the depth data from Dust3r
206
      Wang et al. (2024) output. To accurately locate the nearest point on the mesh, we employ a KD tree
207
      search to get the contact position. Finally, we bind a heatmap to this 3D contact point to determine
208   the 3D affordance.
209
210
      3.4   R EGRESS P OST C ONTACT T RAJECTORY
211
212   To determine the post-contact trajectory, we initially employ the HaMeR 3D pose estimation model
213   Pavlakos et al. (2024). To correct inaccuracies in the 3D hand pose predictions, we project these
214   poses back into 2D and refine them using the hand mask from LangSAM Kirillov et al. (2023).
215   These refined poses are then converted back into the world coordinate system using the ground truth
      camera pose. Finally, we use linear regression to establish the post-contact trajectory based on all

      the 3D contact points, after which we calculate the vector and extend the trajectory from the 3D
217   contact point.

222   4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP AND DATASET
223
      In our research, we utilize the Ego4D-exo dataset Grauman et al. (2024). This dataset encompasses
224
      a range of actions including cooking, basketball shooting, rock climbing, and piano playing, among
225   others. Predominantly, we have concentrated on the cooking segment for our analysis of 3D af-
226   fordances. This segment is particularly rich in functional interactive elements such as drawers and
227   refrigerators, as well as tools like knives and bottles that are essential for daily kitchen tasks. These
228   elements provide a comprehensive context for studying and modeling human-object interactions
229   within a 3D space.
230
      In the cooking scene, although numerous scenes appear to be present in ego4d-exo, many of them
231   are duplicative. We identified and categorized the activities into seven principal types: picking up
232   objects, using a knife, peeling vegetables, opening the refrigerator, opening drawers, opening cabi-
233   nets, and handling a tea bottle. To ensure a thorough evaluation, we manually labeled 2D heatmaps
234   and the ground truth trajectories.

344
345   4.4.1     P RE - PROCESS VIDEO
346
      Before inputting the video frames into the model, we first calibrate the fisheye egocentric video to a
347
      standard 512*512 video. Subsequently, we manually eliminate any irrelevant video segments.
348
349
      4.4.2     D EALING L EFT, RIGHT HAND PROBLEM
350
351   Our current model often struggles in scenarios involving dual-hand interactions, primarily due to
352   limitations in the hand contact detection model Shan et al. (2020) and the 3D hand pose model
353   Pavlakos et al. (2024). Additionally, most state-of-the-art large models face challenges in accurately
354   distinguishing between the left and right hands, often failing to capture such fine-grained details. To
355   address this issue, considering that our task and video predominantly focus on the right hand, we
356
      need to use LangSAM Kirillov et al. (2023) and Lama Suvorov et al. (2021) to remove the left hand
      initially. This approach will prevent incorrect predictions that could lead to overall result failure.
357
358
359
      4.4.3     I NCREASE ERROR TOLERANCE IN 3D HAND POSE
360   We have removed outlier detection in 3D hand pose and applied the LangSAM hand detection mask
361   to filter out points. However, to increase the fault tolerance for outlier points, we have repeatedly
362   dilated the hand masks. This ensures that fewer points are left outside. Particularly, when the hand
363   is viewed from the side, the 3D hand pose model Pavlakos et al. (2024) often predicts poses that are
364   slightly out of bounds.
365

410   5.1     FUTURE DIRECTION
411
412
      5.1.1    MODEL MORE COMPLEX POST CONTACT TRAJECTORY

413   Our model currently only uses simple linear regression to model the post-contact trajectory. To
414   address the complex needs of human daily tasks, more advanced models are required to represent
415   features such as curved shapes, circles, or even repetitive actions.
416
417   5.1.2    HAND OBJECT CONTACT DETECTION
418
419   During our work, we found that hand-object contact detection is prone to failure due to the versatility
420
      of the objects, contact angles, and lighting conditions. Furthermore, the contact point often cannot
      be accurately determined through overlap, object detection, or segmentation alone. Additional rea-
421
      soning is needed to ground accurate location.
422
423
      5.1.3    L EVERAGE DIFFUSION MODEL FOR MORE COMPLETE 3 D SCENE
424
425   Due to the lack of information in the video, Dust3r Wang et al. (2024) only regresses point-to-point
426   in the 3D point map. Hence, the mesh will lack completeness. Leveraging large generative models
427   for a strong scene prior might be beneficial for completing the 3D scene.
