011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) poses significant memory challenges,
014            as the back-propagation process demands extensive resources, especially with
015            growing model sizes. Recent work, MeZO, addresses this issue using a zeroth-
016
               order (ZO) optimization method, which reduces memory consumption by matching
               the usage to the inference phase. However, MeZO experiences slow convergence
017
               due to varying curvatures across model parameters. To overcome this limitation,
018
               we introduce HELENE, a novel scalable and memory-efficient optimizer that
019            integrates annealed A-GNB gradients with a diagonal Hessian estimation and layer-
020            wise clipping, serving as a second-order pre-conditioner. This combination allows
021            for faster and more stable convergence. Our theoretical analysis demonstrates
022            that HELENE improves convergence rates, particularly for models with hetero-
023            geneous layer dimensions, by reducing the dependency on the total parameter
024            space dimension. Instead, the method scales with the largest layer dimension,
025            making it highly suitable for modern LLM architectures. Experimental results on
026            RoBERTa-large and OPT-1.3B across multiple tasks show that HELENE achieves
027
               up to a 20× speedup compared to MeZO, with average accuracy improvements of
               1.5%. Furthermore, HELENE remains compatible with both full parameter tuning
028
               and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), outperforming several state-of-the-art
029
               optimizers. The codes will be released after reviewing.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      LLMs have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various downstream tasks. Fine-tuning these
035
      models has become the standard approach for improving task-specific performance, in which the first-
036   order optimizers like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951), Adam (Diederik,
037   2014) and AdamW (Hutter & Loshchilov, 2017) are widely used. While effective, however, these
038   methods demand significant memory resources primarily due to the backpropagation process, which
039   makes fine-tuning challenging, especially for large-scale models. To overcome this limitation, Malladi
040   et al. (2023) proposed a memory-efficient zeroth-order optimizer (MeZO) that estimates gradients
041   using only two forward passes per training step, contributing to considerable memory savings.
042   However, recent studies show that loss functions in deep learning often exhibit heterogeneous
043   curvatures across different model parameters and different model layers (Sagun et al., 2016; Ghorbani
044   et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2020), which poses challenges to zeroth-order (ZO)
045   optimization. This variation in curvature can overall hinder training efficiency and lead to the sub-
046   optimal solution. To address this issue, more advanced techniques are required, such as incorporating
047   second-order information to better account for curvature differences (Liu et al., 2023; Tran &
048   Cutkosky, 2022; Jahani et al., 2021). However, in ZO optimization, directly computing the Hessian
049   from first-order derivatives is nearly impossible, and partial Hessian evaluations are computationally
050
      intensive, leading to slower convergence. Moreover, we also observe that these methods like Naive
      Newton’s method and Sophia (Liu et al., 2023) fail in fine-tuning LLMs in practice as illustrated in
051
      Figure 1 and Figure 2.
052
053   To overcome the aforementioned challenges, we propose HELENE, a novel optimizer designed to
      estimate second-order curvature information efficiently in the context of ZO optimization. Originating

      2.1   Z EROTH -O RDER G RADIENT E STIMATORS AND M E ZO

121   Zeroth-order (ZO) optimization has long been studied in the context of convex and non-convex
122   objectives. One of the typical ZO gradient estimators is the simultaneous perturbation stochastic
123   approximation (SPSA) (Spall, 1992; Maryak & Chin, 2001). Given a model with parameters θ ∈ Rd
124   and loss function L, SPSA estimates the gradient on a minibatch B as:
125                             L(θ + ϵz; B) − L(θ − ϵz; B)
126                       gϵ (θ) =                               z ≈ zz ⊤ ∇L(θ; B)                      (1)
                                                 2ϵ
127
      where z ∈ Rd with z ∼ N (0, Id ) and ϵ is the perturbation scale.
128
129   Building on the basic principles of ZO optimization, MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) introduces a
130   memory-efficient implementation of ZO-SGD. This approach reduces memory requirements, allowing
131   optimization to proceed with the same memory usage as the inference phase of a model. The key
132
      innovation in MeZO lies in its use of a consistent random seed s to sample the random vector z,
      ensuring the same perturbation z at each step.
133
134
      2.2   D IAGONAL H ESSIAN A PPROXIMATION
135
136   While zeroth-order methods like MeZO provide valuable tools for gradient estimation, optimization
137   can be significantly enhanced by incorporating second-order information, such as curvature. However,
138   directly computing and applying the full Hessian matrix is computationally expensive, particularly in
139   high-dimensional parameter spaces. Specifically, directly applying the Hessian pre-conditioner by
140   calculating the inverse Hessian and multiplying it with the gradient vector at each iteration H −1 g
141   is particularly computationally expensive. To address this challenge, inexact Newton methods have
142   been developed, where approximations of the Hessian are used instead of the full matrix (Dembo
143   et al., 1982; Bollapragada et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2020).
144   A simple yet effective alternative is to approximate the Hessian by its diagonal elements, which
145   reduces computational complexity while retaining useful curvature information. In this approach, a
146   general descent direction can be written as follows:
147                                           ∆θ ≈ diag(H)−1 g,
148
      where diag(H) represents the diagonal elements of the Hessian matrix. This method enhances opti-
149
      mization by enabling efficient inverse Hessian application and supporting inexact Newton methods,
150
      providing improved convergence in complex problems.

158   3.1   M OTIVATION
159
160   Highly variable curvature across different layers and parameters. Fine-tuning large language
161   models (LLMs) has become essential for achieving state-of-the-art performance on various down-
      stream tasks. Commonly employed first-order optimizers such as Stochastic Gradient Descent

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (SGD)(Robbins & Monro, 1951), Adam(Diederik, 2014), and AdamW (Hutter & Loshchilov, 2017)
163   have proven effective in this regard. However, these methods require substantial memory, making
164   them difficult to apply to large models in memory-constrained environments. To mitigate this,
165   zeroth-order (ZO) optimizers, such as MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023), have been introduced, offer-
166   ing memory-efficient solutions by approximating gradients through forward passes. Nevertheless,
167   even with memory savings, existing ZO methods encounter significant challenges when dealing
168   with heterogeneous curvatures in LLMs, which can lead to inefficient convergence and sub-optimal
169   solutions. One key challenge is the inability of optimizers to adapt to the highly variable curvature
170   across different layers and parameters in large models (Sagun et al., 2016; Ghorbani et al., 2019;
171
      Zhang et al., 2022). While techniques that estimate second-order information—such as curvature-
      aware methods—have shown promise in improving optimization efficiency (Liu et al., 2023; Tran &
172
      Cutkosky, 2022; Jahani et al., 2021), they are challenging to integrate into ZO optimizers due to the
173
      noise from label-sampling and the difficulty of computing or approximating the Hessian efficiently in
174   high-dimensional spaces.
175
176   Limitation of EMA to balance short-term gradient noise and long-term convergence. A com-
177
      monly used technique to manage these curvature variations is the Exponential Moving Average
      (EMA), which smooths the gradient updates over iterations. However, EMA alone can be insufficient
178
      for highly non-convex loss landscapes, especially when it lacks mechanisms to adaptively adjust the
179
      weights between the past momentum and the current gradient in the presence of strong noise in gradi-
180   ent estimation. Without annealing, EMA risks accumulating excessive bias over time, particularly
181   when ZO gradient estimation is noisy, leading to suboptimal convergence. This issue is compounded
182   when the optimizer needs to balance short-term gradient noise and long-term convergence, calling for
183   more ZO-specific strategies to mitigate these effects.
184
      Challenge in managing extreme curvature values using Universal clipping. Furthermore, clipping
185
      the Hessian to manage extreme curvature values is another widely adopted strategy. Sophia (Liu
186   et al., 2023), for example, performs global clipping with value 1 of Hessian-based updates to ensure
187   numerical stability, which essentially can slow down the convergence. While effective at curbing
188   extreme updates, applying a universal clipping threshold across all parameters is inherently suboptimal
189   for models with heterogeneous curvatures. A universal clip might suppress meaningful gradient
190   information in some layers while insufficiently addressing extreme Hessian values in others, thus
191   limiting the optimizer’s ability to adaptively handle the diverse learning dynamics across layers (Tran
192   & Cutkosky, 2022). This approach may result in slower convergence or failure to escape saddle points
193   and local maxima, where more flexible, curvature-aware updates are required (Yao et al., 2020).
194   To overcome these limitations, HELENE addresses both the limitation of EMA and the issue of global
195   Hessian clipping. We instantiate MeZO-Gradient Descent, MeZO-Adam, MeZO-Newton’s method,
196   MeZO-Sophia, and HELENE on a simplified 2D problem to illustrate the advantages HELENE,
197   as shown in Figure 1. A visual comparison of the methods reveals that while MeZO-Adam and
198   MeZO-Gradient Descent struggle to converge effectively, Newton’s method and Sophia find it hard
199   to maintain stability when facing heterogeneous curvature, whereas HELENE succeeds. Refer to
200
      Section 5 for a more comprehensive empirical analysis, including up to 20× faster convergence and
      improved accuracy across various tasks and datasets.

203   3.2   HELENE: H ESSIAN L AYER - WISE C LIPPING AND G RADIENT A NNEALING
204
205   In HELENE, we introduce an annealing mechanism to mitigate bias in SPSA-estimated gradients,
206   combined with a clipped diagonal Hessian pre-conditioner that adjusts parameter update step sizes
207   based on layer-wise curvature. First, the gradient is calculated using the SPSA, while the diagonal
208   Hessian is efficiently estimated by the proposed new A-GNB method, to eliminate the noise incured
209   in sampling labels from the model output used in GNB and Sophia. At each iteration, SPSA provides
210   an estimate gt using two forward passes with random perturbations, and A-GNB returns ht , the
211   diagonal Hessian of the mini-batch loss.
212   We apply an exponential moving average (EMA) to both the gradient and diagonal Hessian estimates
213   to reduce noise and improve stability. To further enhance convergence, we apply layer-wise magnitude-
214   based clipping to the diagonal Hessian, ensuring extreme values do not disproportionately affect
215   parameter updates. We provide our pseudo code in Algorithm 1 and each module description in the
      following section in details.

251   training process and results in an increase in loss during the later stages. To mitigate these issues, we

254   later stages. We observe that, unlike first-order methods such as SGD, Zero-order methods require
255

257
      weight of the gradient in the momentum update. The increase in noise in SPSA is likely due to

259   noise magnitude from sampling perturbations to exceed that of the true gradient signal. Notably, our
260   annealing approach is simple to implement, requiring the tuning of only a single hyperparameter.

262

      3.5   L AYWERWISE C LIPPED D IAGONAL H ESSIAN TO HELP N EWTON ’ S METHOD
317
318   As discussed in the motivating examples, fine-tuning LLMs and optimizing non-convex functions
319   pose challenges for Newton’s method, which uses the Hessian as a pre-conditioner. The method
320   may converge to local maxima rather than local minima. Moreover, the inaccuracy of Hessian
321   estimates and changes in the Hessian along the optimization trajectory can render second-order
322   information unreliable. To address these issues, we draw inspiration from Sophia. While Sophia
323   performs clipping on the Newton update H −1 g, we propose a more robust approach by applying
      layer-wise clipping directly to the Hessian matrix H.using a universal clipping threshold for the

327   extreme Hessian values more effectively preserves essential second-order information.

329

519   6     C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we present a novel optimizer, HELENE, which is designed to address the challenges of
522   fine-tuning LLMs. HELENE integrates a new asymptotic Gauss-Newton-Bartlett (A-GNB) estimator
523
      for diagonal Hessian estimation, and a novel layer-wise clipping with the annealing module. The A-
      GNB estimator eliminates the need for label sampling, providing an unbiased Hessian approximation
524
      and improving the precision of curvature-aware updates. Furthermore, our layer-wise clipping
525
      mechanism provably ensures more adaptive Hessian updates based on the curvature of each layer,
526   enhancing stability and scalability. Theoretical analysis shows that HELENE reduces convergence
527   steps from O(d) to O(maxi di ), making it highly scalable for modern architectures with many layers.
528   Experimental results on models like RoBERTa-large and OPT-1.3B demonstrate that HELENE
529   achieves up to a 20× speedup compared to MeZO and improves performance by 1.5% on average.
530   Compatible with both full parameter tuning and parameter-efficient fine-tuning, HELENE outperforms
531   many state-of-the-art optimizers across diverse tasks and datasets.

      A     R ELATED WORK
757
758
759   A.1   Z ERO - ORDER O PTIMIZATION
760
761
762
      Zeroth-order (ZO) optimization, which only relies on the forward passes of neural networks, offers
      significant memory savings during the training process. Recently, MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) adapted
763
      the traditional zeroth-order SGD optimization method for fine-tuning LMs, achieving performance
764
      comparable to full-parameter fine-tuning while significantly reducing memory usage. Thus, zeroth-
765   order optimization is regarded as a promising approach for memory-efficient fine-tuning of LLMs.
766   Several studies have aimed to improve the MeZO algorithm. For instance, Gautam et al. (2024)
767   introduced a zeroth-order optimization algorithm that integrates both full-batch and mini-batch
768   information to produce asymptotically unbiased, low-variance gradient estimations. However, the
769   convergence rate of their approach still leaves room for improvement. In pursuit of better gradient
770   estimation, Jiang et al. (2024) proposed an innovative perturbation sampling technique inspired by
771   the Adam optimizer. Other methods, such as SPSA (Spall, 1992; Maryak & Chin, 2001), have shown
772   to be effective in non-convex multi-agent optimization (Tang et al., 2020; Hajinezhad & Zavlanos,
773
      2018) and in generating black-box adversarial examples (Chen et al., 2017; Cai et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
      2019; Ye et al., 2018).
774
775
776
777   A.2   S ECOND - ORDER I NFORMATION FOR F INE - TUNING LLM S
778
779
      Classic second-order optimization algorithms pre-condition the gradient with curvature informa-
780
      tion (BROYDEN, 1970; Nesterov & Polyak, 2006; Conn et al., 2000). Over the years, people have
781   developed numerous ways to adapt these methods to deep learning. To the best of our knowledge,
782   BECKER (1988) was the first to use diagonal Hessian as the pre-conditioner. Martens et al. (2010)
783   approximated the Hessian with conjugate gradient. Schaul et al. (2013) automatically tuned the
784   learning rate of SGD by considering diagonal Hessian. Pascanu (2013) considered Gaussian Newton’s
785   approximation of Hessian and Fisher information matrix. Martens & Grosse (2015) and follow-up
786   works (Ba et al., 2017; George et al., 2018; Martens et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022) proposed to
787   approximate the Hessian based on the structure of neural networks. Despite these progress on deep
788   learning applications, for decoder-only LLMs, Adam still appears to be the most popular optimizer.
789
      The authors of this paper suspect that many previous second-order optimizers face the challenge that
      the computational / memory overhead due to frequent Hessian computation hinders improvements in
790
      wall-clock time (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Gupta et al., 2018). Some of them also depend on specific
791
      model architecture or hardware structures, e.g., Anil et al. (2020) offloads hessian computation to
792   CPUs, and George et al. (2018) needs ResNet and very large batch size to approximate the Fisher
793   information matrix. To the best of our knowledge, there was no previous report that second-order
794   optimizers can achieve a speed-up on LLMs in total compute.
795
      There is also a concurrent work HiZOO (Zhao et al., 2024) that utilizes Hessian information to
796
      enhance zeroth-order optimization for fine-tuning LLMs. A major focus of HiZOO is to introduce one
797
      more forward pass to handle heterogeneous curvatures across parameter dimensions. However, our
798   work focus on incorporating layer-wise clipping to exclude extreme Hessian values and Exponential
799   Moving Average (EMA) to improve generalization.
800
801
802
803   A.3   G RADIENT C LIPPING
804
805   Global gradient clipping has been a widely adopted practice in fine-tuning LLMs (Chen et al., 2020;
806   Zhang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022). This technique stabilizes training by mitigating the impact of
807   rare examples and large gradient noise. In addition to gradient clipping, HELENE is the first method
808   to clip the Hessian matrix in second-order optimization techniques. This approach addresses the issue
809   of the Hessian matrix fluctuating along the optimization trajectory and reduces the errors in Hessian
      approximations.
