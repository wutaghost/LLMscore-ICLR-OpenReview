009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) have demonstrated their effectiveness in a
012
               variety of graph-based tasks. However, their performance heavily depends on the
013
               availability of a sufficient amount of labeled data, which is often costly to acquire
014            in real-world applications. To address this challenge, GNN-based Active Learning
015            (AL) methods have been proposed to improve labeling efficiency by selecting the
016            most informative nodes in a graph for labeling. The existing graph active learning
017            methods employ different heuristic approaches, while efficiency sometimes, they
018            fail to explicitly explore the influence of labeled data on unlabeled data, thus
019            limiting the generalizability of graph models to various types of graph data. In this
020            paper, we propose an Attention-based Graph Coreset Labeling framework (AGCL).
021            AGCL can, with limited budgets, gradually discover core data to be labeled from
022
               a global view so as to obtain a training dataset that can efficiently depict the
               whole graph space and maximize the performance of GNNs. Specifically, we
023
               explicitly explore and exploit the correlations between nodes in the unlabeled pool
024
               and those in the labeled pool using an attention architecture and directly connect
025            the correlations with the prediction performance on unlabeled set. By leveraging
026            influence (attention) scores, AGCL identifies and labels data with the maximum
027            representation difference from the existing labeled pool, thereby enhancing sample
028            complexity. We theoretically demonstrate the superiority of the attention-based
029            data selection strategy in reducing the covering radius bound, thereby improving
030            the expected prediction performance on unlabeled data. Our experimental results
031            show that the labeled coreset significantly enhances the generalizability of various
032            graph models across different graph datasets, as well as CNN models in image
033
               classification tasks.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038
039
      Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Duvenaud et al. (2015); Kipf & Welling (2017); Hamilton et al.
      (2017)) have emerged as powerful approaches for learning representations of graph-structured data.
040
      It has been noted (Zhang et al. (2022b)) that success of GNNs in various graph-based learning tasks
041
      (Xu et al. (2018); Klicpera et al. (2019); Zhang & Chen (2018)) requires plenty of labeled data.
042   However, sufficient informative training data is often not available, as human annotation is expensive
043   and time-consuming, particularly for biological graphs that contain specialized structures requiring
044   expert labeling.
045
      Active learning (AL) provides solutions by selecting and annotating a few highly informative and
046
      representative points that can depict a large portion of the data space, especially uncertain regions.
047
      While various active learning methods have been proposed and applied for CNN models (Sener &
048   Savarese (2018); Caramalau et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2016); Yoo & Kweon (2019)) on independent
049   and identically distributed (i.i.d) data, these methods fail to capture both the graph structure and node
050   features, leading to suboptimal performance when applied to GNNs (Gao et al. (2018a); Madhawa
051   & Murata (2020)). Additionally, the interconnected and interdependent nature of nodes in a graph
052   means that the choice of labeled data partitions has a significant impact on the performance of GNN
053   models (Shchur et al. (2018); Fu et al. (2024)). Therefore, it is not directly applicable to apply active
      learning methods from CNNs to GNNs.

054
      To select more representative data on graphs, GNN-based active learning methods (Gao et al. (2018b);
055   Cai et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2019)) incorporate graph structural information into query heuristics
056   (uncertainty, diversity, or density). Several recent works (Zhang et al. (2022b; 2021e;a;c)) considered
057   the characteristic of influence propagation in the graph and proposed a series of graph active learning
058   methods aiming to identify nodes with maximum influence for the rest. For example, the Grain method
059   (Zhang et al. (2021e)) connects labeled data selection in GNNs with social influence, maximizing
060   the number of unlabeled nodes influenced by labeled ones. The intuition behind these methods is to
061   exploit the assumption that nodes that are close in feature space and graph structure are likely to have
062   the same label, i.e., they focus on the local structure of the graph.
063   While efficient, these strategies 1) lack a direct correlation with the expected prediction performance
064   on unlabeled nodes in the final task, and 2) mainly focus on the local graph structure, failing to
065   comprehensively explore the influence between labeled and unlabeled data across the entire graph
066   space. However, in real-world applications, graphs can be complex; for instance, in heterophilic
067   graphs, connected nodes may have different labels. These challenges raise a critical question for
068   graph annotation: Given a fixed labeling budget, how can we develop a general framework that
069
      efficiently and effectively identifies core data in the graph by considering both the graph structure
      (local and global) and features, ultimately improving model performance?
070
071   In this paper, we propose a general graph active learning framework called Attention-based Graph
072   Coreset Labeling (AGCL). We address the graph annotation problem as an unlabeled coreset selection
073   problem for GNNs, focusing on selecting data that maximizes coverage of the remaining data in the
074   graph representation space. The challenge of graph coreset selection lies in designing an effective
075
      measure that evaluates the correlations between labeled and unlabeled data, while considering the
      complex graph structure and features, which directly links to the expected predictions on unlabeled
076
      data. In AGCL, we explicitly connect the labeled and unlabeled pools beyond the original graph
077
      connections, construct the influence between them using an attention-aggregation strategy in the
078   embedding space, iteratively select core data from a global perspective. We theoretically demonstrate
079   that selecting unlabeled data with the maximum representation difference from the labeled pool,
080   based on an attention-based metric, helps reduce the bound radius δ, thereby decreasing the total
081   loss of the graph data. Empirically, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across
082   various GNN architectures and different types of graph data (homophily and heterophily) as well as
083   different data scales (same-scale and large-scale). Additionally, we illustrate how AGCL can serve as
084   a general active learning framework, extending its applicability to image classification. In summary,
085   our main contributions are:
086
             • We propose an attention-based active learning framework for graph models, which iteratively
087            selects and annotate data in a graph by addressing the coreset selection problem for non-i.i.d.
088            graph data.
089
             • We theoretically prove the superiority of the attention-based selection strategy: selecting
090
               unlabeled data with the maximum representation difference from the current labeled pool
091            can help reduce the bound in graph coreset selection and directly enhance the performance
092            of the graph model.
093
             • Our proposed AGCL is a general active learning framework that can be applied to both
094
               graph data and image tasks. We conduct extensive experiments on both types of data to
095            demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method for various classification tasks.

      2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
101
      In recent years, graph neural networks (GNNs) have attracted increasing attention due to their
102   superiority in the processing of graph-structured data (Henaff et al. (2015); Kipf & Welling (2017);
103   Gilmer et al. (2017); Bronstein et al. (2017); Velickovic et al. (2018)). To improve the expressive
104   power of GNNs, different message-passing schemes have been developed to propagate and aggregate
105   neighborhood information (Kipf & Welling (2017); Velickovic et al. (2018); Feng et al. (2020)).
106   Recently, some studies tried to understand the generalization ability of GNNs from the perspective of
107   training data. Zhu et al. (2021) explored the influence of training data and presented Shift-Robust
      GNN (SR-GNN), designed to account for distributional differences between biased training data and


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a graph’s true inference distribution. Ma et al. (2021) extended PAC-Bayesian analysis for graph data
109   to analyze the generalization performance of GNNs, and demonstrated that the distance between a
110   test subgroup and the training set can be a key factor affecting the GNN performance. Su et al. proved
111   that the distance of the training set to the rest of the vertexes in the graph is negatively correlated to
112   the learning outcome of GNNs.
113
114   2.2   ACTIVE L EARNING ON G RAPHS
115
116   In practice, obtaining sufficient informative training data is challenging, as human annotation is
117   expensive and time-consuming. Active learning and semi-supervised representation learning with few
118   labels are both designed to address the scarcity of labeled data, but from different perspectives. While
119   few-labeled semi-supervised learning focuses on comprehensively leveraging the small amount of
120   labeled data and the large amount of unlabeled data to achieve better performance, active learning
121
      focuses on selecting and labeling the most informative nodes to maximize model performance with
      minimal cost.
122
123   Generally, active learning is an iterative labeling process in which a model is learned at every
124   iteration, and a set of data points is chosen to be labeled from a pool of unlabelled points to maximize
125   model performance. Based on the query strategy, the majority of work can be divided into three
126   categories (Settles (2009)): theoretically-motivated methods (MacKay (1992)), ensemble approaches
127
      (McCallum et al. (1998); Freund et al. (1997)) and uncertainty based (Tong & Koller (2001); Li &
      Guo (2013); Settles & Craven (2008)). Demir et al. (2010) used a heuristic to first filter the pool
128
      based on uncertainty and then choose the points to label using diversity. Sener & Savarese (2018)
129
      proposed an effective batch active learning method for deep CNNs. In this method, the active learning
130   problem is defined as coreset selection; however, it is only for nonstructural data. Several attempts
131   have been made for applying AL on graph-structured data (Bilgic et al. (2010); Gu et al. (2013);
132   Kuwadekar & Neville (2011)) based on a graph signal processing framework. Subsequently, a series
133   of GNN-based AL methods (Cai et al. (2017); Gao et al. (2018a)) have been studied using different
134   metrics, including uncertainty, information density, and graph centrality to evaluate training data.
135   However, simply combining these metrics may not select informative data. Recently, several works
136   (Cui et al. (2022); Ma et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022a); Fuchsgruber et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2019);
137   Li et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021c)) were proposed to further consider the graph information.
138
      To maximize the coverage of the labeled data, a new node selection metric is proposed in ALG
      (Zhang et al. (2021a)) to maximize the effective reception field. Grain (Zhang et al. (2021e)) further
139
      generalizes the reception field to the number of activated nodes in social influence maximization.
140
      Reinforcement learning (Hu et al. (2020a)) and LLM (Chen et al. (2023)) are also used to improve
141   active learning on graphs. While most existing approaches are based on some query heuristics to
142   implicitly encode the relationships between labeled and unlabeled data, they often struggle to identify
143   truly informative data points in the face of complicated graph structures.
144
145   2.3   C ORESET S ELECTION
146
147   Coresets are defined as small and informative weighted data subsets, ensuring that models fitted
148   to the coreset also provide a good fit for the original data. Several works, such as those by Wei
149   et al. (2015), Mirzasoleiman et al. (2020), and Killamsetty et al. (2021a), have studied the efficient
150   training of deep learning models using selected coresets. Mirzasoleiman et al. (2020) focused on
151   selecting representative coresets of the training data that closely estimate the full training gradient.
152   Killamsetty et al. (2021b) treated coreset selection as an optimization problem for the validation set
153
      loss, aiming for efficient learning with a focus on generalization. Killamsetty et al. (2021a) proposed
      GRAD-MATCH, which selects subsets approximating the full training loss or validation loss gradient
154
      using orthogonal matching pursuit. Meanwhile, coreset selection methods (Sener & Savarese (2018);
155
      Ash et al. (2019)) were also used for active learning scenarios, where a subset of data instances from
156   the unlabeled set is selected to be labeled.

      4     ATTENTION - BASED G RAPH C ORESET L ABELING (AGCL)

      In this section, we present AGCL, a general graph active learning framework using a labeling coreset
      from the graph data to maximize the generalization ability of various GNN models. We define graph
197
      annotation as a coreset selection problem without labels on GNNs in subsection 4.1. To address this
198
      problem, we theoretically showed that labeling the unlabeled data that have maximum representation
199   difference compared to the existing labeled pool obtains a smaller bound radius δ and reduce the
200   total prediction loss in subsection 4.2. Hence we construct an attention-based model to explicitly
201   evaluate the influence of each unlabeled sample u for each labeled sample v. While capturing the
202   correlations between labeled and unlabeled data from a global perspective, AGCL selects and labels
203   data according to attention scores at each batch of data labeling, enhancing the information complexity
204   in the labeled pool. The above process is repeated until the labeling budget B runs out. We introduce
205   each component of AGCL in subsection 4.3.

392   We compare our proposed method with other active learning approaches for graphs: Random,
393   Standard, FeatProp (Wu et al. (2019)), AGE (Cai et al. (2017)), GRAIN (Zhang et al. (2021e)), RIM
394   (Zhang et al. (2021d)), ALG (Zhang et al. (2021b)), GraphPart (Ma et al. (2022)), and NC-ALG

      Random sampling, CoreSet (Sener & Savarese (2018)), VAAL (Sinha et al. (2019)), and (CoreGCN
      Caramalau et al. (2021)). In general active learning, the initial pool is usually uniformly randomly
397
      selected from the whole data. For i.i.d. data, this initial data selection method is reasonable. However,
398
      for non-i.i.d. graphs in which nodes are connected by edges, it is of great importance to utilize
399   initial knowledge of the graph. Thus, except for the random selection, we propose a structure and
400   feature-based initial pool selection method.
401
402
      Considering both the features and graph structure, we propagate features among nodes with the
      layer-wise propagation rule:

407   layer with H (0) = X. After k iterations of aggregation, the representation of a node hki captures the
408   structural information within its k-hop neighborhood. Then, we select k nodes into the initial pool
409   using the k-medoids method.

523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In conclusion, we have presented an Attention-based Graph Coreset Labeling (AGCL) framework for
526   graph labeling. Our approach addresses the limitations of existing graph active learning methods in
527   capturing comprehensive graph structural information by connecting labeled and unlabeled data using
528   an attention architecture. Through AGCL, we effectively identify the most informative unlabeled
529   sample for the labeled pool, gradually expanding the labeled dataset to cover the entire graph
530   representation space. This results in improved sample complexity and diversity, leading to enhanced
531
      performance of GNNs across various types of graph data. In theoretical analysis, we clear show that
      selecting unlabeled data with maximum representation difference from the labeled pool helps reduce
532
      the bound radius δ, thereby increasing coverage in the representation space. Empirical evaluations
533
      across different graph datasets and image classification tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of AGCL
534   in improving the generalization ability of graph models. While the proposed AGCL can be applied
535   to large-scale datasets by operating on subgraphs or subsets, we aim to further explore alternative
536   methods to address efficiency challenges in large-scale datasets. In the paper, we assume a practical
537   scenario where the dataset does not contain a significant amount of outlier data. If there are many
538   anomalies in the dataset, the proposed method may select points that have different representations
539   from the majority of the data. Therefore, it is necessary to consider excluding these anomalies. In the
      future, we will further consider this case and to achieve class-balanced coreset.
