009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Effectively utilizing extensive unlabeled high-density EEG data to improve per-
012            formance in scenarios with limited labeled low-density EEG data presents a sig-
013            nificant challenge. In this paper, we address this by framing it as a graph transfer
014            learning and knowledge distillation problem. We propose a Unified Pre-trained
015            Graph Contrastive Masked Autoencoder Distiller, named EEG-DisGCMAE, to
016
               bridge the gap between unlabeled/labeled and high/low-density EEG data. To
               fully leverage the abundant unlabeled EEG data, we introduce a novel unified
017
               graph self-supervised pre-training paradigm, which seamlessly integrates Graph
018
               Contrastive Pre-training and Graph Masked Autoencoder Pre-training. This
019            approach synergistically combines contrastive and generative pre-training tech-
020            niques by reconstructing contrastive samples and contrasting the reconstructions.
021            For knowledge distillation from high-density to low-density EEG data, we propose
022            a Graph Topology Distillation loss function, allowing a lightweight student model
023            trained on low-density data to learn from a teacher model trained on high-density
024            data, effectively handling missing electrodes through contrastive distillation. To
025            integrate transfer learning and distillation, we jointly pre-train the teacher and stu-
026            dent models by contrasting their queries and keys during pre-training, enabling
027
               robust distillers for downstream tasks. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our
               method on four classification tasks across two clinical EEG datasets with abun-
028
               dant unlabeled data and limited labeled data. The experimental results show that
029
               our approach significantly outperforms contemporary methods in both efficiency
030            and accuracy.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Electroencephalography (EEG) is a pivotal tool for elucidating neural dysfunctions, making it indis-
036   pensable for the clinical diagnosis of brain disorders (Sanei & Chambers, 2013). Manual analysis of
037   resting-state EEG (rs-EEG) signals often suffers from low accuracy due to their inherent complex-
038   ity. In contrast, computer-aided diagnostic methods offer substantial improvements in diagnostic
039
      performance. Traditional methods typically involve extracting temporal and spatial features from
      EEG signals and applying machine learning techniques to develop effective classifiers (Trivedi et al.,
040
      2016). Recent advances have seen deep graph learning revolutionize EEG signal analysis. Instead of
041
      treating EEG data as conventional numerical inputs, researchers now represent it as non-Euclidean
042   graph data. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016) are employed to capture the
043   intricate features and topological structures inherent in these graphs. This innovative approach has
044   markedly enhanced the accuracy and reliability of EEG-based diagnostics, showcasing the potential
045   of GNNs in advancing applications (Song et al., 2018).
046
      Despite these advancements, several critical issues remain unresolved. Firstly, acquiring a substan-
047
      tial amount of accurately labeled clinical rs-EEG data for supervised training on a specific task is
048   challenging due to the complexities involved in data collection (Siuly et al., 2016). Models trained
049   on these limited labeled datasets often exhibit poor accuracy and generalization (Lashgari et al.,
050   2020). Thus, a significant but underexplored research problem is how to effectively utilize this vast
051   amount of unlabeled data to enhance model performance and robustness (Tang et al., 2021). Sec-
052   ondly, the performance of EEG devices varies markedly with the precision of the data they capture.
053   High-density (HD) EEG devices, with their extensive array of electrodes, record high-resolution
      brain signals, greatly improving the accuracy of diagnostic tasks (Stoyell et al., 2021). However,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      these devices are often prohibitively expensive and cumbersome, limiting their practical deploy-
055   ment. Conversely, low-density (LD) EEG devices, which are more affordable and easier to deploy
056   (Justesen et al., 2019), capture lower-resolution signals, thus reducing diagnostic accuracy (Cataldo
057   et al., 2022). Addressing how to leverage rich information from HD EEG to enhance diagnostic per-
058   formance with LD EEG, which is more portable, is crucial for making LD EEG-based diagnostics
059   more accessible and practical (Kuang et al., 2021).
060
      In this paper, we address these challenges through a series of innovative methods. We construct
061   graphs from EEG data and apply GNNs to extract topological features and train the model effec-
062   tively. To leverage unlabeled EEG data to enhance performance on limited labeled data, we frame
063   this as a Graph Transfer Learning (GTL) problem. We propose a graph self-supervised pre-training
064   (PT) approach (Xie et al., 2022) on a large volume of heterogeneous unlabeled EEG graphs. This
065   pre-trained model is subsequently fine-tuned (FT) on the scarce labeled data, allowing knowledge
066   acquired from the extensive unlabeled dataset to improve performance on the labeled data. We in-
067   troduce a novel unified graph self-supervised pre-training paradigm, GCMAE-PT, which combines
068   Graph Contrastive Pre-training (GCL-PT) (Qiu et al., 2020) with Graph Masked Autoencoder Pre-
069
      training (GMAE-PT) (Hou et al., 2022). This approach integrates contrastive and generative pre-
      training by reconstructing contrastive samples and contrasting the reconstructed samples, enabling
070
      them to jointly supervise and optimize each other, thereby enhancing overall model performance.
071
      To improve model performance with HD EEG data when training on LD EEG data, we address this
072   as a Graph Knowledge Distillation (GKD) problem (Yang et al., 2020) and design a Graph Topology
073   Distillation (GTD) loss function. This allows a student model trained on LD EEG to learn from a
074   teacher model with HD EEG by accounting for missing electrodes through contrastive distillation,
075   while simultaneously compressing model parameters. Moreover, to ensure that models pre-trained
076   with GTL excel as distillers in downstream GKD tasks, we integrate GTL and GKD by contrasting
077   the queries and keys of the teacher and student models during the GTL pre-training process. This
078   integration demonstrates that our unified pre-trained graph contrastive masked autoencoders serve
079   as effective distillers, providing a robust solution for EEG analysis.

      2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS FOR EEG M ODELING
085
086
      Recent advancements in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have demonstrated their potential in en-
087   hancing the modeling and interpretation of EEG data. Notably, the Dynamical Graph Convolutional
088   Neural Network (DGCNN) (Song et al., 2018) was introduced to improve emotion recognition by
089   dynamically learning the interrelationships among EEG channels. Similarly, the Regularized Graph
090   Neural Network (RGNN) (Zhong et al., 2020) applied a regularization strategy to advance emotion
091   recognition from EEG data. Liu et al. (Liu et al., 2023) tackled a similar problem by developing
092   a novel method for emotion recognition from few-channel EEG signals, integrating deep feature
093   aggregation with transfer learning. For medical EEG field, Tang et al. (Tang et al., 2021) em-
094   ployed self-supervised GNNs to advance seizure detection and classification, achieving significant
095
      improvements in identifying rare seizure types.
096
097
      2.2   S ELF -S UPERVISED G RAPH P RE -T RAINING
098
099
      Self-supervised learning (SSL) pre-training (Zhang et al., 2022a) has proven effective in harness-
100
      ing extensive unlabeled datasets. Two predominant SSL methods are contrastive learning-based
101
      (CL-PT) pre-training, originating from computer vision, and generative-based masked autoencoders
102   (MAE-PT) pre-training, adapted from natural language processing (NLP). These pre-training tech-
103   niques have been extended to graph models. For instance, GCC (Qiu et al., 2020) and GraphCL
104   (You et al., 2020) were among the pioneers in applying contrastive learning to graphs by leveraging
105   graph augmentation to generate sample pairs and construct contrastive losses. Concurrently, GMAE
106   (Hou et al., 2022) and GPT-GNN (Hu et al., 2020) adapted the generative masked pre-training ap-
107   proach from NLP (Devlin et al., 2018) to graphs. These methods involve masking nodes and edges,
      followed by reconstruction, enabling graphs to capture and refine local topological features.

      2.3                G RAPH K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
109
110   Graph Knowledge Distillation focuses on transferring knowledge from a complex, large-scale model
111   (teacher) to a more streamlined and efficient model (student), thus preserving performance while
112   reducing computational demands. G-CRD (Joshi et al., 2022) introduced a distillation loss function
113   for GNN-to-GNN transfer, employing a contrastive learning strategy to enhance similarity among
114   nodes of the same class and increase separation between different classes. MSKD (Zhang et al.,
115   2022b) proposed a multi-teacher distillation approach, integrating various teacher GNN models of
116
      different scales into a single student GNN model. Approaches such as Graph-MLP (Hu et al., 2021),
      and VQGraph (Yang et al., 2024) focused on transferring knowledge from structure-aware teacher
117
      GNNs to structure-agnostic student MLPs.

143   3.1                EEG G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION
144
145   An EEG graph can be formally represented as G = (V, A), where V = {v1 , . . . , vn } denotes the set
146
      of nodes in the graph G. The matrix X ∈ Rn×d represents the node features, with n indicating the
      number of nodes (or electrodes) and d specifying the dimensionality of the feature vector associated
147
      with each node. The adjacency matrix of the EEG graph G is denoted by A ∈ Rn×n . The EEG
148
      graph G is derived from the original EEG time series signals S = {s1 , . . . , sn } ∈ Rn×t recorded by
149   EEG caps, where n represents the number of channels or electrodes, and t denotes the length of the
150   time series for each channel.
151                                 X = P SD(F ilter(S))         A = Corr(X )                            (1)
152
      To convert resting-state EEG (rs-EEG) time series into graph representations, we first apply band-
153
      pass filtering F ilter(·) to extract EEG signals within the following frequency bands: θ (4-8 Hz), α
154
      (8-14 Hz), β (14-30 Hz), and γ (30-50 Hz). Subsequently, we compute the power spectral density
155   (PSD) features P SD(·) for each band, selecting the α band for this study. These PSD features are
156   utilized as node features for the EEG graph. The Pearson correlation Corr(·) is then computed
157   between nodes to construct the adjacency matrix A, which represents the edge connectivity.

159   3.2                U NIFIED G RAPH P RE -T RAINING FOR D ISTILLATION
160
161   To fully leverage the extensive amount of unlabeled EEG data, we propose a graph self-supervised
      pre-training approach to pre-train EEG models from a graph-based perspective. Our motivation


                                                                                                                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      stems from the observation that prior research has predominantly focused on either contrastive-
163   based or generative-based pre-training methods for EEG time series, with limited studies addressing
164   these techniques within the context of EEG graph models. To address this gap, we introduce a
165   unified graph self-supervised pre-training paradigm, termed GCMAE-PT, based on the following
166   assumptions:
167
      Assumption 1: (Combining GCL and GMAE for Enhanced Distillation) Hybridizing contrastive-
168
      based and generative-based pre-training by simultaneously reconstructing contrastive pairs and
169   contrasting the reconstructed samples provides a more robust distiller, rather than applying these
170   methods separately or in sequence.
171
172
      Assumption 2: (Joint Pre-Training of Teacher and Student Models) Both the teacher and student
      models benefit from joint pre-training through the contrasting of each other’s positive and negative
173
      pairs, leading to improved distillation performance.

      4.1     EEG DATASETS AND D OWNSTREAM TASKS
327
328   We evaluated our EEG-DisGCMAE framework on two clinical datasets with rs-EEG time series:
329   the Establishing Moderators and Biosignatures of Antidepressant Response in Clinical Care (EM-
330   BARC) (Trivedi et al., 2016) and the Healthy Brain Network (HBN) (Alexander et al., 2017). The
331   EMBARC dataset comprises EEG data from 308 eye-open and 308 eye-closed samples, while the
332   HBN dataset includes 1,594 eye-open and 1,745 eye-closed samples. Detailed dataset preprocessing
333   information is provided in the appendices. For EMBARC, we performed binary classification tasks:
334
      sex classification in Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) patients (Male vs Female) and depression
      severity classification based on the Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAM D17 ) (Williams, 1988)
335
      (Mild vs Severe Depression) (Boessen et al., 2013). For HBN, we conducted binary classifications
336
      for MDD (Healthy vs MDD) and Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) (Healthy vs ASD). Additional
337   details can be found in the appendices. We tested three EEG electrode density levels: high-density
338   (HD), medium-density (MD), and low-density (LD). In EMBARC, these densities correspond to the
339   10-20 EEG system electrode distributions of 64 (HD), 32 (MD), and 16 (LD) electrodes, respec-
340   tively. For HBN, the densities correspond to 128 (HD), 64 (MD), and 32 (LD) electrodes.
341
342   4.2     C OMPARATIVE E XPERIMENT A NALYSIS
343
344   We compared the proposed EEG-DisGCMAE against five categories of methods: Traditional
345   Machine Learning Methods (SVM, MLP, LSTM), GNN-based Models (GCN, GFormer, Hyper-
346   GCN (Feng et al., 2019)), EEG-specific Models (EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018), DGCNN, EEG-
347
      Conformer (Song et al., 2022), RGNN), Graph Contrastive Pre-training Models (GCC, GraphCL,
      GRACE, AutoGCL (Yin et al., 2022)), and Graph Generative Pre-training Models (GraphMAE,
348
      GPT-GNN, GraphMAE2, S2GAE (Tan et al., 2023)). As demonstrated in Table 1, our model outper-
349
      forms all other state-of-the-art methods. Notably, pre-training-based models, including those based
350   on GCL-PT (GCC, GraphCL, GRACE, AutoGCL (Yin et al., 2022)) and GMAE-PT (GraphMAE,
351   GPT-GNN, GraphMAE2, S2GAE (Tan et al., 2023)), utilize large Graph Transformers as their back-
352   bone in this study. In contrast, our method can be suitable to both spatial-based Graph Transofrmer
353   and spectral-based vanilla GCNs (DGCNN) as the backbone. We evaluated both tiny and large
354   model sizes. As illustrated in Fig. 2 (a), our tiny model, with only 1.3M parameters, performs
355   comparably to pre-training-based methods with larger models (5.7M parameters). Moreover, our
356   large-tiny model, despite having a similar parameter size to others, significantly outperforms them
357   by about 5% in both AUROC and accuracy. This demonstrates that our approach achieves a superior
358
      balance between performance and efficiency, delivering high performance with a more compact pa-
      rameter set. As illustrated in Fig. 2 (b), we investigated the relationship between model parameters
359
      and performance across three factors: model size, model type, and varying input EEG densities. It
360
      is evident that when the model type and input EEG density are fixed, the large-size model outper-
361   forms the tiny-size model. For a given model, reducing the input density (i.e., using LD data) leads
362   to a decline in performance compared to using HD data. However, after pre-training and distilla-
363   tion, the performance of the initially less effective tiny-size model improves significantly, reaching
364   a level comparable to that of the large-size teacher model using HD data without pre-training. This
365   demonstrates that our proposed GCMAE-PT and GTD loss can enhance model performance while
366   maintaining a lightweight parameter set without compromising efficiency.

527
      In this paper, we present an innovative framework for EEG pre-training and distillation, which effec-
      tively integrates contrastive-based and generative-based graph pre-training paradigms. Furthermore,
528
      our framework incorporates a specifically designed EEG graph topology distillation loss function,
529
      tailored for the distillation process from high-density to low-density EEG data. Our method demon-
530   strates substantial and efficient improvements over contemporary approaches, significantly enhanc-
531   ing the accuracy of EEG-based disease diagnosis while facilitating seamless deployment across
532   diverse medical devices. Moreover, our method is readily extendable to a range of EEG application
533   scenarios, including emotion recognition, brain-computer interfacing, and epilepsy detection.

738   C.1   GTL FOR UNLABELED / LABELED EEG
739
740   Many existing methods primarily focus on training models with limited labeled EEG data, overlook-
741
      ing the potential of abundant unlabeled data. These methods emphasize novel GNN architectures
      but fail to fully leverage the available data. Additionally, they do not exploit high-density (HD) EEG
742
      data to improve models for low-density (LD) scenarios. This underscores the need for strategies that
743
      integrate both labeled and unlabeled data, and use HD data to enhance performance in LD contexts.
744
745   Moreover, most pre-training methods are directly applied to EEG time series, with very few ad-
746   dressing the issue from the perspective of large-scale graph pre-training. In contrast, our approach
747
      proposes pre-training EEG graph models using a graph-based pre-training perspective. This not only
      aims to transfer knowledge from unlabeled EEG data to tasks on labeled EEG data but also benefits
748
      HD-to-LD distillation. This is based on the following observation:
749
750   Observation: An LD EEG graph can be viewed as an HD EEG graph with specific nodes removed.
751   In graph contrastive self-supervised pre-training, contrastive views are obtained by graph augmen-
752   tation, such as removing nodes and edges. Another graph pre-training method, graph masked au-
753
      toencoders pre-training, operates by masking node features and then reconstructing them. The rela-
      tionships between these methods are formulated as follows:
754
755
                         Density Decrease ⇐⇒ Node Dropping ⇐⇒ Node Masking

                            Electrodes Loss       GCL Augmentation       GMAE Masking

757   called GCMAE-PT. This approach intricately combines Graph Contrastive Pre-training (Qiu et al.,
758   2020; You et al., 2020) with Graph Masked Autoencoders Pre-training (Hou et al., 2022), allowing
759   us to model and capture the relationships among the three entities described in Eq. 20.

769

       M     A NALYSIS OF S HARED K EY P OOL Q UEUE
1110
1111
       The reason for implementing the proposed teacher-student shared key pool queue is that we have
1112
       two types of original input data: HD and LD EEG graphs. Through the key pool queue, we allow
1113   high-density and low-density EEG key samples to share the same gradient update process within a
1114   batch. This approach also enables both the teacher and student models to simultaneously capture
1115   shared patterns between these two types of data.
1116
1117
1118
       N     C HALLENGES , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORKS
1119
       EEG graph self-supervised pre-training offers a promising avenue for leveraging extensive EEG
1120
       data, paving the way for large-scale graph-based EEG models. Our proposed GCMAE-PT method
1121
       is well-suited as a pre-training approach for large-scale EEG foundation model. However, a key
1122   challenge is unifying data with varying electrode configurations across different EEG systems to
1123   address data heterogeneity.
1124
1125
       In our study, while constructing a unified EEG pre-training dataset from multiple sources, we faced
       the constraint of all datasets being from the same EEG system (10-20 system). To standardize the
1126
       data, we reduced the number of electrodes in datasets with more electrodes to match those with
1127
       fewer electrodes, creating a unified pre-training dataset. This approach, however, leads to a loss
1128   of information from removed electrodes and restricts the use of datasets with fewer electrodes for
1129   pre-training on datasets with more electrodes.
1130
1131
       Addressing the challenge of integrating EEG data with differing electrode counts from various sys-
       tems, while preserving electrode precision, is crucial for developing a comprehensive pre-training
1132
       dataset. Successfully overcoming this issue could enable large-scale graph pre-training and establish
1133
       a robust EEG graph foundation model, representing a significant advancement in the field.
