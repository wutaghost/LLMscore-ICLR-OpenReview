                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Most protein language models (PLMs), which produce high-quality protein rep-
012            resentations, use only protein sequences during training. However, the known
013            protein structure is crucial in many protein property prediction tasks, so there is
014            a growing interest in incorporating the knowledge about the protein structure into
015            a PLM. Currently, structure-aware PLMs are trained from scratch or introduce a
016            huge parameter overhead for the structure encoder. In this study, we propose MU-
017            LAN, a MULtimodal PLM for both sequence and ANgle-based structure encod-
018            ing. MULAN has a pre-trained sequence encoder and an introduced parameter-
019            efficient Structure Adapter, which are then fused and trained together. According
020
               to the evaluation on 9 downstream tasks, MULAN models of various sizes show
               quality improvement compared to both sequence-only ESM2 and structure-aware
021
               SaProt as well as comparable performance to Ankh, ESM3, ProstT5, and other
022
               PLMs considered in the study. Importantly, unlike other models, MULAN offers
023            a cheap increase in the structural awareness of the protein representations due to
024            finetuning of existing PLMs instead of training from scratch. We perform a de-
025            tailed analysis of the proposed model and demonstrate its awareness of the protein
026            structure.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Proteins, as unbranched heteropolymers, play a pivotal role in nearly all biological functions (Finkel-
032   stein & Ptitsyn, 2016). Comprising 20 distinct amino acids, the specific sequence of these amino
033
      acids determines the complex three-dimensional (3D) structure of the protein (Anfinsen et al., 1961).
      Subsequently, this 3D configuration governs the protein’s function (Finkelstein & Ptitsyn, 2016).
034
      Advances in genome sequencing initiated the growth of the number of publicly available protein
035
      data, unveiling a vast resource for understanding the molecular basis of life. The application of
036   modern machine learning techniques for a better understanding of protein sequences can boost the
037   development of diverse fields such as drug discovery, protein design, and biotechnology.
038
039
      The abundance of protein sequences and their text-like nature made it possible to apply top-
      performing approaches from natural language processing to proteins. It is tempting to expect that
040
      protein sequence information alone would be sufficient for large protein language models (PLMs)
041
      to infer protein structure and function. Recently, large PLMs, such as ProtTrans (Elnaggar et al.,
042   2021), ESM2 (Lin et al., 2022), and Ankh (Elnaggar et al., 2023) have made remarkable progress
043   in protein representation learning, surpassing previous approaches across various downstream tasks.
044   However, it appears that the representation abilities of sequence-only PLMs are limited and some
045   kind of structural information should be encoded directly into PLM. This limitation is represented
046   by a significantly better performance (Lin et al., 2023) of structure-infused Alphafold (Jumper et al.,
047   2021) compared to sequence-only ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023).
048   At the same time, structural information about a huge amount of proteins has also become easily
049   available with the revolutionary method AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021). Recently, several struc-
050   tural protein language models (SPLMs) were proposed. For example, SaProt (Su et al., 2023) and
051   ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024) have successfully added some knowledge about the protein structure into
052   the model, showing a better performance compared to sequence-only PLMs. Additionally, existing
053   SPLMs require training from scratch (Wang et al., 2023; Heinzinger et al., 2023; Hayes et al., 2024;
      Li et al., 2024). Furthermore, hybrid sequence-structure models add a large structure encoder with

055   training and inference (Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2022;

      In our study we present a simple yet effective structure-aware PLM, that is computationally and
084
      parameter-efficient and does not require training from scratch. Our main contributions are:
085
086          • We introduce MULAN, a novel MULtimodal PLM for both sequence and ANgle-based
087            structure processing. We propose the Structure Adapter, a lightweight MULAN module
088            that uses residue torsion angles to represent the protein structure. Our model can work on
089            top of existing PLMs through PLM finetuning, so it offers a cheap increase of structural
090            awareness due to avoiding training from scratch.
091          • We evaluate the obtained structure-aware protein representations on a wide range of down-
092            stream tasks and compare MULAN to relevant baselines. We show that adding MULAN to
093            both ESM2 and SaProt of various sizes is beneficial for the quality of the downstream tasks.
094            The main improvements are shown for protein-protein interaction prediction (up to 0.1 in
095            AUROC for MULAN-ESM2 L), and for the protein GO annotation (0.023 for medium and
096            0.012 for large MULAN-ESM2 in Fmax for GO CC).
097          • We perform an extensive ablation study to highlight the effectiveness and demonstrate the
098            structural awareness of MULAN embeddings (see Section 4.2 and Figure 1).

166   3     E XPERIMENTS
167
168   3.1   T RAINING DETAILS
169
170   We use protein structures for Swiss-Prot proteins from AlphaFold Protein Structure Database (Al-
171
      phaFold DB) (Varadi et al., 2022) for the pre-training stage (503, 724 structures). We filter out
      proteins with a length of less than 30 amino acids. The final set comprises 501, 348 proteins. Fol-
172
      lowing Rives et al. (2021), we randomly selected the validation set of 5000 proteins.
173
174   The small MULAN model (MULAN-ESM2 S 9M) was initialized from the ESM2 8M checkpoint.
175   For the medium-sized models (MULAN M 37M), we use 35M ESM2 or SaProt, while for large
176   models (MULAN L 652M) we take 650M ESM2 or SaProt checkpoints.
177   During the pre-training stage, we randomly crop proteins that are longer than 1022 residues to the
178   length of 1022. We follow Rives et al. (2021) and use dynamic batch size without the concatenation
179   of proteins along the sequence dimension during training. Also, to fully utilize GPU and minimize
180   the amount of padding, we use sorted batching with dynamic batch size as in (Gonzalez et al., 2023):
181   we keep a fixed number of tokens in the batch and form the batch from proteins with similar lengths.
182   The extended training details and hyperparameters are detailed in Appendix A.
183
184   3.2   D OWNSTREAM TASKS
185
186   We tested our model on 10 downstream tasks, of which the first 9 are main tasks and the last one,
187
      Secondary structure prediction, served as a sanity check to verify the MULAN’s structure aware-
      ness. We summarize the information about all these datasets in Appendix B.1 and Table 5. For the
188
      prediction of the Localization (both binary and 10-class), Thermostability, Metal Ion Binding, Gene
189
      Ontology (GO), and Human Protein-Protein Interaction (HumanPPI) we follow the setup of Su et al.
190   (2023). Fluorescence prediction was taken from Ankh (Elnaggar et al., 2023) setup. We used the
191   Secondary structure prediction task from TAPE (Rao et al., 2019) benchmark.
192
193
      AlphaFold structures for proteins from the datasets were retrieved from AlphaFold DB by Uniprot
      accession number if available. PDB IDs were mapped to UniProt accession numbers and retrieved
194
      from AlphaFold DB. If no protein identifier was available or there was no AlphaFold model for the
195
      UniProt accession number in the database, the protein was modeled by the standalone version of
196   AlphaFold. For all described datasets we keep the original data splits. For the Secondary structure
197   prediction, we use original experimental PDB structures for evaluation. The description of datasets
198   and processing steps is detailed in Appendix B.1.
199
      To evaluate all binary classification tasks we use the area under the ROC curve (AUC); for multi-
200
      class classification we measure accuracy; for multilabel GO annotation task, we follow (Gligorijević
201
      et al., 2021) and use Fmax score; and for the regression tasks we measure Spearman’s correlation
202   coefficient (SCC).

234   4     R ESULTS AND DISCUSSION
235
236
      4.1   R ESULTS
237
238   As baseline models for comparison we take sequence-only PLMs (ProteinBert (Brandes et al.,
239   2022), ESM2 (Lin et al., 2022) and Ankh (Elnaggar et al., 2023)); structure-aware SPLMs
240   (ProstT5 (Heinzinger et al., 2023), SaProt (Su et al., 2023) and ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024)); hy-
241   brid sequence-structure models (S-PLM (Wang et al., 2023) and PST (Chen et al., 2024)), which are
242   top-performing to the best of our knowledge. We do not compare to ESM-GearNet (Zhang et al.,
243   2023a), because SaProt has reported to be better (Su et al., 2023), and LM-GVP (Wang et al., 2022)
244   and GearNet (Zhang et al., 2023b) were surpassed by ESM-GearNet (Zhang et al., 2023a). Another
245
      hybrid model DeProt (Li et al., 2024) does not have implementation available. We train MULAN
      on top of two model families: ESM2 and SaProt AF of various sizes. The quality of the protein rep-
246
      resentations on the considered downstream tasks is reported in Table 2. Note that we report results
247
      for models of different sizes in separate parts of the table and highlight the best results in bold also
248   separately according to the model size. Also, we do not report error bars for the results due to the
249   computationally expensive process of re-training PLMs. Our main findings are discussed below.
250
251
      Adding MULAN is beneficial for different PLMs First of all, we aim to highlight the down-
252   stream performance gains introduced by MULAN when applied to both ESM2 and SaProt models.
253   These improvements are shown in Table 1: we report gains of MULAN models compared to the
254   base PLMs used for MULAN initialization. For example, MULAN-ESM2 S refers to the gains
255   of this MULAN model compared to the small ESM-2 8M model. Positive gains are in bold. The
256   results demonstrate that MULAN is effective on both small, medium, and large models. We show
257   that the performance of both considered PLMs was improved in most cases by adding the proposed
258   Structure Adapter.
259   MULAN demonstrates the most impressive results when applied to the small ESM2 model, signifi-
260   cantly increasing the quality of Metal Ion Binding prediction (.070 increase in AUROC) as well as
261   protein function prediction: GO MF (.059 in SCC) and Fluorescence (.022 in SCC). This fact in-
262   dicates that both protein property prediction and interaction prediction tasks benefit from the struc-
263   tural input. Overall, MULAN shows the best results in HumanPPI (for all base models there is a
264   significant improvement, up to 0.1 in AUROC for MULAN-ESM2 L). Also, MULAN increases
265   downstream quality in GO CC and GO BP tasks.
266   Furthermore, we show that MULAN-ESM generally offers higher performance gains compared to
267   MULAN-SaProt. We suppose that it is because SaProt is already a structure-aware model, while
268   ESM2 is sequence-only. Moreover, the results show that MULAN is a general-purpose PLM that
269   can produce high-quality results for protein downstream tasks of different nature. We show that this
      simple strategy can enhance PLM performance for all considered models. We expect that MULAN

      5     R ELATED WORK
433
434
      5.1   S EQUENCE - BASED MODELS
435
436   Protein sequences are similar to human language: like letters are assembled into words that, in turn,
437   form sentences, amino acids are chained into protein sequences that encode protein 3D structure
438   which determines function. This resemblance makes it promising to apply best practices from the
439   natural language processing field for solving protein-related tasks. Most PLMs are pre-trained with
440   a masked language modeling (MLM) objective (Devlin et al., 2018): a part of the input sequence’s
441   residues is randomly masked or replaced with other residues, and then the model aims to predict
442
      these corrupted tokens using the remaining sequence context. Models from the Transformer fam-
      ily Heinzinger et al. (2019); Brandes et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2021); Rives et al. (2021); Lin et al.
443
      (2023); Elnaggar et al. (2021; 2023) have made huge progress in the protein representation learning,
444
      among which ESM2 (Lin et al., 2023) and Ankh (Elnaggar et al., 2023) are currently showing the
445   best results. They show high performance in various downstream tasks, for example, the prediction
446   of protein secondary structure, residue contacts, sub-cellular localization, and the effect of mutation.
447
448
      5.2   S TRUCTURE - INFORMED MODELS
449
450   The amino acid sequence solely defines the protein structure (Anfinsen et al., 1961), which, in turn,
451   defines all protein properties, including its function. However, the sequence alone is not sufficient
452   enough for PLMs to infer all information about the protein (Lin et al., 2023). Thus, attempts to
453   infuse PLMs with structural context were made. To improve PLM’s capabilities, Zhang et al. (2024)
454   proposed to finetune ESM2 on the remote homology detection task, which seems to implicitly in-
455   corporate protein structure-based features into the model. Indeed, the protein structure is more con-
456   served than the protein sequence (Chothia & Lesk, 1986), and homology search, which is a more
457
      sensitive task than finding similar protein sequences, gives the model more structural knowledge.
458   Recently, the idea of using protein 3D structure directly during the model pre-training has been
459   given a lot of attention from the research community. ProstT5 (Heinzinger et al., 2023), the first
460   structure-augmented PLM, uses Foldseek, a special structural alphabet (van Kempen et al., 2023)
461   (3Di) that describes the tertiary structure. As a result, each protein can be represented with either
462   an amino acid sequence or a string of Foldseek letters of the same length that carries informa-
463
      tion about tertiary interactions. Incorporating that information into PLM was done by finetuning
      a sequence-only ProtT5 model to translate between the amino acid sequence and 3Di sequence to
464
      obtain structure-aware protein representations. Another structure-informed PLM, SaProt (Su et al.,
465
      2023), uses the 3Di alphabet to encode the structure similarly to ProstT5. It represents each residue
466   as a combination of amino acid and 3Di letters and is trained with MLM from scratch. This approach
467   gives SaProt an improvement over ESM2 on various downstream tasks. Tan et al. (2024) suggest an
468   adapter-based approach (SES-Adapter) that works with Foldseek sequences and residue secondary
469   structure annotation. However, it is an approach for structure-aware downstream task tuning rather
470   than a general-purpose PLM. It needs to be applied and trained for each downstream task separately
471   and does not provide protein structure-aware embeddings for general use. As a result, SES-Adapter
472   cannot be compared to MULAN. Recently, ESM3 (Hayes et al., 2024) has incorporated sequence,
473   structure, and function modalities for the protein representation learning task as well as protein gen-
474
      eration. Similarly to MULAN, they embed and fuse different modalities of the protein, but train a
      large PLM from scratch.
475
476
477   5.3   H YBRID MODELS
478
      The graph-like tertiary structure of proteins gives ideas of infusing PLMs with structure information
479
      via Graph Neural Networks. In this setup, pre-training is left as MLM only (Mansoor et al., 2021;
480   Zheng et al., 2023) or is augmented by Masked Structure Modeling task where not only parts of the
481   sequence are masked but parts of the structure too (LM-GVP (Wang et al., 2022), GearNet (Zhang
482   et al., 2023b), MIF (Yang et al., 2023). In ESM-GearNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) it was proposed to
483   fuse the protein sequence and structure information from state-of-the-art PLMs with graph structure
484   encoders (GearNet). The authors used various pre-training strategies including diffusion-based,
485   and reported a performance boost compared to ESM2 and GearNet on several downstream tasks.
      DeProt (Li et al., 2024) works similarly to ESM-GearNet. DeProt uses local protein structure around


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the residue to get the residue-level structure encoding. Li et al. (2024) proposed DeProt, another way
487   of Chen et al. (2024) presented PST, an approach to combine a graph encoder and a PLM and to
488   jointly train them to obtain structure-aware protein representations. PST modifies the self-attention
489   mechanism of the underlying PLM and trains the whole model jointly. The graph encoder has as
490   many parameters as the base PLM used. PST shows better results compared to relevant baselines, eg.
491   ESM2 and ESM-GearNet. Wang et al. (2023) presents S-PLM, a contrastive learning approach to
492   jointly train protein sequence and structure encoders. A structure encoder is a Swin-Transformer that
493   works on residue-residue contact matrices and is trained from scratch. S-PLM does not take protein
494   structure explicitly during inference, relying only on the learned structure-aware representations.
495
496   6   C ONCLUSION
497
498   In this paper, we propose MULAN, a novel multimodal 3D structure-aware protein language model
499   for both sequence and structure processing. MULAN works on top of a pre-trained PLM and has
500   the introduced lightweight Structure Adapter that processes residue dihedral angles. Our model fine-
501   tunes the pre-trained PLM model offering a cheap incorporation of the knowledge about the protein
502
      structure into the model. Also, unlike other hybrid structure-aware models, the Structure Adapter
      of MULAN adds minor parameter overhead to the base PLM: 0.3% for large MULAN. We train
503
      MULAN models of various sizes and evaluate their protein representations on 9 downstream tasks.
504
      For most of the downstream tasks, our model demonstrates an increase in performance compared to
505   ESM2 and SaProt models, which were used for MULAN initialization. The best results were shown
506   for the protein-protein interaction and the protein GO annotation prediction. Additionally, MULAN
507   demonstrates comparable performance to ESM3, ProstT5, and other PLMs considered in the study,
508   while having a faster training or inference pipeline. Finally, we demonstrate the structural awareness
509   of our model in multiple experiments.

750
751   Downstream task prediction is done using the model with the Light Attention architecture (Stärk
752   et al., 2021), which was designed to work with protein embeddings and shows better results than
753   an MLP. The only difference is that we extend it by adding two extra intermediate layers L1 and
754   L2 : Li = Dropout(ReLU(BatchNorm(Linear))) : Rhi−1 → Rhi , where h1 and h2 are the model
755   hyperparameters, and h0 is the initial embedding dimension. They are added before the output
      Linear layer, which projects embeddings of size h2 into the downstream task target dimension.

      C.3   L EARNING RATE STRATEGIES
908
909
      We used the same learning rate for all parameters of MULAN during training. However, we initial-
910
      ize the whole protein encoder from the ESM2 pre-trained checkpoint, while the Structure Adapter
911
      is newly initialized. This fact suggests the possibility of using a smaller learning rate for ESM2
912   modules compared to the Structure Adapter in order not to harm the pre-trained weights a lot. This
913   idea was suggested and shown its effectiveness in Zhang et al. (2023a), where they have a similar
914   combination of randomly initialized and pre-trained modules. We follow the suggested setup and
915   decrease the learning rate for ESM2 modules by a factor of 10. According to the results, for MU-
916   LAN there is no clear benefit of using the reduced learning rate for ESM2 modules (see Table 9), so
917   we decided to keep the learning rate constant for all MULAN modules to reduce the number of used
      hyperparameters.

943   C.4   A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTS
944
945
      Addition vs concatenation of embeddings MULAN uses the structure bias from the Structure
      Adapter in a manner of positional embeddings: these structural embeddings are added to the main
946
      amino acid embeddings from the ESM2 model. However, one may concatenate these embeddings
947
      instead of summing up. This approach leads to different objectives used for amino acid embeddings
948   (MLM) and structure embeddings (MLM for angle restoration). Also, concatenation leads to an
949   increase in the length of the content passed to the Transformer model, causing significant memory
950   and time overheads. The results of the experiment with the concatenation of embeddings did not
951   show any benefit compared to the base setup.
952
953
954
      Importance of angle masking In our pre-training strategy, both amino acids and corresponding
      angle vectors are masked together. However, there are two other options that we have tested: inde-
955
      pendent masking of angles and residue letters and no angle masking at all. These experiments have
956
      shown worse results than the base approach. We explain it with the fact that residue letters and their
957   angle vectors are connected. For example, if the letter is masked, and the angle vector has no side
958   chain torsion angles defined, then the range of possible outcomes decreases from all 20 amino acids
959   to only two: Glycine and Alanine. The opposite also holds: the known residue letter helps to restore
960   the corresponding residue angle vector or at least the number of residue angles. Thus, we keep the
961   joint masking strategy to force MULAN to learn as much information as possible.

964   D     S TRUCTURAL AWARENESS OF MULAN
965
966   According to the results from Table 10, all MULAN models demonstrate the awareness of the protein
967   secondary structure. They surpass similar-sized ESM2 models by a large margin. Moreover, even a
968   small MULAN utilizes the knowledge about the protein structure better than structure-aware ProstT5
969   and PST with more than a billion parameters. We do understand that the correct information about
970   the secondary structure can be derived from angle inputs as well as from the Foldseek tokens used
971   by SaProt or direct secondary structure types used by ESM3. Hence, this experiment is done only
      to demonstrate that MULAN actively uses the 3D structure.
