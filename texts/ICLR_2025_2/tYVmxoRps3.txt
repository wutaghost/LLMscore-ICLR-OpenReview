009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large pre-trained language models have demonstrated impressive capabilities, but
012
               there is still much to learn about how they operate. In this study, we conduct a in-
013
               vestigation of the autoregressive transformer’s ability to perform basic addition
014            operations. Specifically, by using causal analysis we found that a few different
015            attention heads in the middle layers control the addition carry, with each head pro-
016            cessing carries of different lengths. Due to the lack of globality in these attention
017            heads, the model struggles to handle long-sequence addition tasks. By perform-
018            ing inference intervention on mistral-7B, partial task performance can be restored,
019            with the accuracy on 20-digit long-sequence additions from 2% to 38%. Through
020            fine-tuning, a new mechanism branches out for handling more complex cases, yet
021            it still faces challenges with length generalization. Our research reveals how the
022
               model performs addition, and further provides insights into the debate on whether
               these models are merely statistical.

026   1   INTRODUCTION
027
028
      As large pre-trained language models increase in scale, they demonstrate increasingly powerful
029
      performance on an increasing number of tasks (Brown et al., 2020). But their working principle is
030   still a black box. As the application of large models expands, we have to start to care about safety
031   and ethical issues (Weidinger et al., 2021). On the one hand, some studies believe that the model is
032   just a model that relies on statistics (Bender & Koller, 2020; Merrill et al., 2021). On the other hand,
033   some studies have found that the language model internally encodes other basic world concepts
034   (Abdou et al., 2021; Patel & Pavlick, 2021).
035
      Addition and subtraction, despite being the simplest arithmetic operations, are still challenging tasks
036   for current large language models (Nogueira et al., 2021). Understanding how these models perform
037   such operations internally is highly beneficial for improving their transparency and interpretability.
038
039
      We focus on the current mainstream pre-trained models and investigate their behavior on integer
      addition tasks. It is worth noting that our research can be validated on larger models. For ex-
040
      ample, when giving the following input to ChatGPT 4o: “answer directly without programming:
041
      633331+266667=”, the model is highly likely to respond with 900,000 or another incorrect answer
042   starting with 9 (the correct answer is 899,998). In this paper, we will investigate why such errors
043   occur and implement mitigation measures.
044
045
      We conducted experiments on pre-trained models, including Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023) and
      LLaMA-7B (Touvron et al., 2023). While these models demonstrate a baseline accuracy in per-
046
      forming integer addition, they are far from achieving precise results. Through causal analysis, we
047
      identified a subset of attention heads, primarily in the middle layers, that are responsible for en-
048   coding digit information relevant to bitwise addition. Visualizing the associated attention patterns
049   showed a high degree of interpretability. Ablation studies further highlighted the critical role of
050   these heads in determining the output, governing whether the model performs simple modular addi-
051   tion or full addition with carry. However, as the length of the carry chain increases, the information
052   encoded in the attention head gradually loses its significance, accompanied by the rapid decline of
053   interpretability of the attention pattern, resulting in a decrease in the accuracy of the model (See Fig
      1).

      2   RELATED WORK
086
087
      Studying arithmetic on language models has become popular with the continuous improvement of
088
      model capabilities. Zhou et al. (2024) studies how the Transformer processes modular addition
089
      from the perspective of Fourier transforms, that the MLP layer mainly approximates the size of
090   the results through low-frequency features, while the attention layer performs modular operations
091   through high-frequency features. Quirke et al. (2023) shows how a one-layer model decomposes
092   the task into parallel digit-specific computation streams and applies different algorithms to each
093   digit by analyzing the training loss curve. Another similar work is Stolfo et al. (2023), which uses a
094   causal mediation analysis framework to reveal the internal information flow in large language models
095   during arithmetic reasoning tasks. Our research aims to go a step deeper and broader, providing a
096   general framework to explain any pre-trained transformer-based autoregressive models’ real internal
097   operation process, the findings in our work could be work on a wide scale.
098   Lee et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2022); Liu & Low (2023) focus on improving the performance of
099   language models in arithmetic tasks. Their core idea is to extend the reasoning steps of the model,
100   thereby reducing the complexity of serial calculations. This is done by employing training strate-
101   gies like the ”scratchpad” approach, where intermediate reasoning steps are made explicit, or by
102   providing the model with hint prompts that guide it through mathematical expressions.
103   Broader interpretability research has recently focused on mechanistic interpretability (Geiger et al.,
104   2021; Conmy et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022). Mechanical interpretation methods, which treat the
105   model as a computational graph composed of attention and MLP components, seek to locate the sub-
106   graph responsible for the actual task computation within the entire computational graph. Hanna et al.
107   (2024) explores the mechanistic interpretability of how GPT-2 performs mathematical comparison
      (greater-than) tasks through internal circuits without explicit training.

      Different from the way humans calculate, the autoregressive model needs to output the answer from
141   front to back, which is more challenging. Consider the process needed for the model output to be
142   the correct zi , there are three cases. Case 1: When xi+1 + yi+1 < 9, zi = (xi + yi ) mod 10. Case
143   2: When xi+1 + yi+1 > 9, then zi = (xi + yi ) mod 10 + 1. Case 3: When xi+1 + yi+1 = 9, we
144   need to check if there is a carry from subsequent digits. If xi+2 + yi+2 > 9, then zi = (xi + yi )
145   mod 10 + 1; if xi+2 + yi+2 < 9, then zi = (xi + yi ) mod 10; if xi+2 + yi+2 = 9, continue
146   checking further.
147   For simplicity, we refer to an equation with a carry chain of length d as CCd (Carry Chain). For
148   example, 44 + 28 is CC1, 356 + 247 is CC2, and 35556 + 24447 is CC4. On the other hand,

150   example, 345 + 252 is OC2, and 46612 + 33385 is OC4.

333   For the CC1 task with high accuracy, there is a strong correlation between attention and model
334   output (see Table 1). Ablating the top two attention heads has an immediate and dramatic effect.
335   Notably, after ablating on Gemma, the accuracy of the CC1 task drops to just 1.47%. The ablation
336   removes the carry and reverts the output of the first digit to z1′ − 1 (See Fig 6). As d increases, the
337   impact of ablating attention heads diminishes, and accuracy declines accordingly.
338   When re-weighting weights to match CC1 level, we observed varying degrees of improvement
339   across different CCd tasks. However, this improvement was still limited and did not restore accuracy
340   to the CC1 level. Additionally, as d increases, the effect of re-weighting continues to diminish.
341   This suggests that the model’s difficulty in handling carries is not solely due to insufficient attention
342   weight allocation, but rather a deeper issue in the model’s mechanism for processing such operations.
343   Interestingly, the three models exhibit distinct patterns in handling tasks, and when uncertain about
344   the presence of a carry, they tend to default to one of these patterns. Mistral-7B achieves higher
345   accuracy on OC tasks compared to CC tasks, while Gemma shows the opposite, with better per-
346   formance on CC tasks than OC tasks. Llama2 falls between these two models. The relatively
347   low accuracy of Llama2 can be attributed to its inherently lower performance in modular addition
348   compared to the other two models (see Appendix C)..
349   To summarize the experimental findings in this section, pre-trained autoregressive models rely on the
350   staircase attention patterns to transmit carry-encoded value tokens to the final token for prediction.
351   When this information transmission is disrupted, the model defaults to modular addition. As the
352   carry length increases, the model loses its ability to transmit this information, leading to disordered
353   attention patterns and a loss of carry-related information in the value vectors.

356   4     I NFERENCE INTERVENTION
357
358   Based on the findings in section 3, we performed a full intervention enhancement experiment on
359   the model’s addition task. Many of the model’s errors stemmed from incorrect handling of car-
360   ries—either missing a carry or introducing an unnecessary one. In contrast, errors related to mod-
361   ular addition were relatively minor (See Appendix B). The primary goal of this experiment was
362   to explore the potential for improving the model’s performance without additional training, by at-
363
      tempting to restore its internal mechanisms, rather than transforming the model into a fully accurate
      calculator.

366   4.1   EXPERIMENTS
367
368   Our experiment follows the following steps: when the model processes X + Y , it dynamically
369   intervenes in internal activation. When the model generates zi , we determine whether there is a
370   carry from the following sequence. We melt the model’s attention weights for xi+1 and yi+1 if there
371   is no carry and let the model do modular addition (xi+1 + yi+1 ) mod 10. If there is a carry from
372   a carry chain of length m, the attention weight an,i+m is re-weighted. Here, the attention heads
373   are selected from the top 2 attention heads that cause the maximum T E when d = m. For a more
374
      detailed algorithm explanation please refer to Appendix C.
375   In the experiment, the number pairs corresponding to each length n were randomly sampled from
376   10n−1 to 10n , and each length contained 9000 questions. The model used greedy sampling. Due to
377   the need for n times of inference interferences for numbers with a length of n, this computationally
      intensive experiment ends at a length of 20.

      The result (See Fig 7) shows improvement in most cases, with longer sequences experiencing more
      significant improvements. The lack of improvement for sequences shorter than length 5 is that the
409
      proportion of these randomly sampled short sequences that contain CC2 or above is relatively low.
410
      Specifically, for sequences of length 3, only about 17% of the questions contain CCd where d ̸= 1,
411   this proportion increases to 26% at length 5, 56% at length 10, and 86% at length 20. Table 1 shows
412   that the improvement brought by re-weighting is mainly in more difficult cases, but harms the case
413   of CC1, where the model already performs sufficiently well.
414
      Additionally, this improvement has an upper limit, as accuracy remains close to zero for sequences
415
      of length 60 (shown in Appendix C). This is partly due to the limitations of attention discussed in
416
      Section 3, where simply adjusting weights cannot fully compensate for performance decline caused
417   by the information loss. Another critical factor is that while intervention improves the model’s
418   handling of carries, it does not enhance the model’s accuracy in performing basic modular addition.
419   Additionally, many errors arise from number misalignment, where the model incorrectly adds xi
420   with yj where i ̸= j (See Appendix B). Many studies (McLeish et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023) have
421   that positional encoding is a primary cause of this issue.

      5.1   GENERALIZATION
471
472
      Related studies (Sabbaghi et al., 2024; McLeish et al., 2024; Kazemnejad et al., 2024) have high-
473
      lighted the critical role of positional encoding in enabling length generalization for arithmetic tasks.
474   Since arithmetic relies fundamentally on the alignment of digits, positional encoding that effectively
475   captures this structural information greatly enhances generalization (Sabbaghi et al., 2024; McLeish
476   et al., 2024). However, handling long carry chains remains a significant challenge for models (Sab-
477   baghi et al., 2024), as it requires not only precise digit alignment but also conditional reasoning
478   based on the sum of individual digits. While Gemma2-2B employs RoPE, study (Kazemnejad et al.,
479   2024) indicates that it still faces limitations in achieving length generalization.
480   Figure 8 shows the fine-tuning performance of Gemma2-2B. The model seems difficult to achieve
481   noticeable generalization. The accuracy drops sharply after surpassing the maximum training length.
482   We attempt to explain this phenomenon from the perspective of attention head formation by mea-
483   suring the maximum T E that the attention heads can cause. The results (8c) indicate that beyond
484   the training length, model performance declines in tandem with maximum T E, suggesting a lack
485   of attention heads capable of effectively transferring carry digit information. The detailed causal
      analysis heat maps are visualized in Appendix D.

      5.2   E MERGENCE OF NEW PATTERN
487
488   We perform a causal analysis on the fine-tuned model with a maximum training length d = 10.
489   Pre-trained models handle cases like OC1 and CC1 by allocating attention to the digit immediately
490   following the current calculation digit. After fine-tuning, the model retains and refines the original
491   mechanism (see Appendix D) while also developing a new strategy (see Figure 9) to address more
492   complex cases (d > 2). The original mechanism continues to handle simple cases(d ≤ 2) in parallel
493   with the new strategy. Notably, the model introduces a specialized attention pattern, referred to as the
494
      Target Head, which focuses directly on the actual carry digit (a centralized attention weight on digit
      “7” in Figure 9). Simultaneously, another attention mechanism, named the Detection Head, emerges.
495
      This head becomes active even before the “=” token appears, transmitting the corresponding xi
496
      information to the current token yi token when it appears.
497
498   A guess is that the detection head obtains the information of xi and yi to determine whether xi + yi
499   is greater than 9. The carry chain length-related information is then passed to the Target Head,
500
      enabling it to focus on the actual carry token and execute the carry operation. To validate this,
      we performed zero ablation on the Detection Head and observed a disruption in the Target Head’s
501
      attention patterns, resulting in a significant accuracy decrease for complex cases, while simpler
502
      cases remained unaffected (see ablation studies in Appendix D). Intuitively, the differentiation of
503   attention functions is more reasonable, since it involves the concept of sum judgment, but it is still
504   unclear why the model does not continue to use the original mechanism and how the information is
505   delivered. Addressing this question will require further studies.

526   6     CONCLUSIONS
527
528   In this study, we investigated how pre-trained autoregressive language models perform addition
529   operations.
530   We found that the model relies on localized attention distribution for handling carry operations,
531   which makes it challenging for the model to process inputs with long sequences. We attempted to
532   restore the model’s task performance by intervening in attention during inference without additional
533   training. While some task performance was recovered, the model’s inherent limitations remained.
534   Finally, fine-tuning on specialized addition tasks revealed that the model develops a new and more
535   efficient mechanism to handle complex cases, while retaining the original mechanism for simpler
536
      situations, yet it continues to struggle with length generalization. This loss of generalization ability
      is related to the loss of ability to form functional attention heads.
537
538   Our findings offer valuable insights into how language models process arithmetic tasks and serve as
539   a reference point for evaluating whether current language models rely solely on statistical patterns
      rather than deeper reasoning mechanisms.

       The dataset includes questions of CCd, OCd, and randomly generated number pairs. To ensure

1068   and 30% consists of OCd tasks. The dataset includes 106 samples for each length d, which means
1069   4 ∗ 105 randomly sampled numbers pairs, 3 ∗ 105 CCd samples, and 3 ∗ 105 OCd samples. For the

1072   process has musked the prompt, ‘X + Y =” sequence, and only predicts for Z.
