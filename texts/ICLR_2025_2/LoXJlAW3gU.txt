009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Protein design necessitates a profound understanding of the intricate nature of the
012
               protein universe. While approaches based on discrete diffusion and autoregression
013            are actively developing in the field of protein sequence generation, continuous
014            diffusion remains underappreciated and underexplored. To address this gap, this
015            research introduces DiMA, a latent diffusion model that leverages Gaussian dif-
016            fusion on representations derived from protein language models,such as ESM-2
017            and CHEAP, to generate amino acid sequences. We quantitatively investigate the
018            impact of various components of the latent diffusion model and protein encoders,
019            revealing their contributions to enhanced protein generation performance. Addition-
020            ally, we conduct an extensive evaluation of existing methods alongside DiMA using
021
               multiple metrics across two protein modalities, covering quality, novelty, diversity,
               and distribution matching of generated proteins. Our findings demonstrate that
022
               DiMA consistently produces novel, high-quality, and diverse protein sequences
023
               that accurately reflect the inherent structural and functional diversity of the protein
024            space. Furthermore, we show that the proposed model can be easily adapted to
025            address conditional tasks, such as protein family generation and inpainting. This
026            work advances the field of protein design by providing a robust framework for
027            latent diffusion on various protein representations, facilitating high-quality protein
028            sequence generation.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Generative modeling of proteins is gaining traction as a key area in academic research, potentially
034   reshaping bioinformatics, synthetic biology, and protein-based therapeutics (Wu et al., 2021; Ovchin-
035   nikov & Huang, 2021). A key part of this research area is the focus on the generation of protein
036   sequences or 3D models. Despite the increasing emphasis on conditional generation and family-
037   specific fine-tuning (Madani et al., 2023; Sevgen et al., 2023), the foundational step of unconditional
038   generation remains a challenging yet vital aspect. The reason is simple: proficiency in unconditional
039
      generation provides a solid groundwork for more specialized and nuanced conditional generation,
      followed by subsequent fine-tuning.
040
041   Recent advancements in generative modeling across various domains, including text, images, and
042   video, have begun to significantly influence the field of protein generation, leading to the development
043   of innovative approaches and methodologies. In particular, many autoregressive models have been
044   introduced for the generation of amino acid sequences (Madani et al., 2023; Ferruz et al., 2022; Shin
045
      et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2024), demonstrating their effectiveness in capturing the complex dependencies
      inherent in protein sequences. In addition to autoregressive models, diffusion models have also been
046
      successfully applied to protein generation tasks. Notably, several studies (Alamdari et al., 2023;
047
      Wang et al., 2024) have adapted categorical diffusion (Austin et al., 2021) for amino acid sequence
048   generation, effectively generalizing the ESM-2 encoder to a generative task. While significant
049   progress has been achieved in both discrete and three-dimensional diffusion models (Watson et al.,
050   2023; Wu et al., 2022; Lin & AlQuraishi, 2023; Fu et al., 2024), developing a Gaussian diffusion
051   model based on continuous protein representations remains a challenging task. Some studies (Lee
052   et al., 2023) utilize specific image-like representations of protein structures to adapt Gaussian diffusion
053   for discrete proteins limiting their usability for other protein representations, while others (Zhang
      et al., 2023a) primarily focus on conditional tasks, leaving unconditional generation underexplored.

054
      Existing studies (Lee et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) indicates that Gaussian diffusion, which has
055   gained popularity in the realm of image processing, has yet to yield satisfactory results in the context of
056   unconditional protein generation. This observation highlights the pressing need for more specialized
057   approaches that are tailored to the unique characteristics and complexities of protein sequences. As
058   the field continues to evolve, it is crucial to explore methodologies that can effectively address these
059   challenges, ultimately enhancing our understanding of protein structure and function. Furthermore,
060   Gaussian latent diffusion presents two notable advantages over discrete diffusion methods. First, the
061   continuous nature of the latent space enables direct application of established score-based techniques
062   like classifier and classifier-free guidance without requiring discrete approximations. This creates
063
      opportunities for more controlled and directed protein generation. Second, recently developed
      CHEAP (Lu et al., 2024) encoder, that produces a compact protein representation of both sequential
064
      and three-dimensional protein information, enables the generation of latent that produces both protein
065
      sequence and structure.
066
067   In this study, we explore Gaussian latent diffusion for protein generation and propose DiMA, a latent
068   diffusion model based on protein language model (pLM) encodings. We investigate the use of ESM-2
069
      (Lin et al., 2023a) and CHEAP (Lu et al., 2024) pLMs as encoders to obtain sequences of continuous
      encodings, upon which we train a denoising diffusion model. During inference, iterative refinement
070
      is performed, and the resulting encoding is decoded to amino acid sequence. We investigate several
071
      model components in detail: proteins encoding and decoding, diffusion model architecture, noise
072   schedule, self-conditioning, and length sampling. Additionally, we conduct an evaluation of existing
073   methods alongside DiMA using multiple metrics across two protein modalities, covering quality,
074   novelty, diversity, and distribution matching of generated proteins. Furthermore, we showcase the
075   conditional generation capabilities of our method through family specific generation and inpainting.
076
      The main contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
077
078          • We introduce DiMA, a diffusion-based generative model for protein sequence design. DiMA
079            uses a latent Gaussian diffusion approach through the encodings of a protein language
080            model.
081
             • We investigate components of latent diffusion model for protein generation and reveal the
               impact of our architectural design choices and implemented techniques for effective training
082
               and sampling.
083
             • We conduct an evaluation of existing methods alongside DiMA using multiple metrics across
084            two protein modalities, covering quality, novelty, diversity, and distribution matching of
085            generated proteins.
086          • We demonstrate that DiMA consistently produces novel, high-quality, and diverse protein
087            sequences that accurately reflect the inherent structural and functional diversity of the protein
088            space.

092   2    R ELATED W ORK
093
      Diffusion generative models, introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015), have gained attention for
094
      their remarkable results in image (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b;a), and speech generation
095
      (Chen et al., 2020; Popov et al., 2021). Due to their impressive generative quality, some studies
096   have extended the application of diffusion models to the text domain. Hoogeboom et al. (2021)
097   and Austin et al. (2021) proposed multinomial diffusion for discrete data corruption. Subsequently,
098   other works (Li et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023b; Gulrajani & Hashimoto, 2023; Han et al., 2022;
099   Strudel et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2022) adapted Gaussian diffusion to sequence learning by embedding
100   discrete data into continuous space. Yuan et al. (2022) extended the text diffusion model to the
101   sequence-to-sequence setting. Ye et al. (2023) conducted a study on the discrepancy of the text
102   embedding space, demonstrating that the diffusion task at small noise scales is trivial. Zhang et al.
103   (2023b) implemented latent text diffusion inside a VAE with an autoregressive decoder. Lovelace
104
      et al. (2022) utilized diffusion models to generate a fixed-length latent representation, mapped into
      a high-dimensional space with the reconstruction network before being fed into an autoregressive
105
      decoder to generate text.
106
107   In protein science, deep learning has emerged as a transformative tool. Pre-trained on extensive
      protein sequence datasets, it provides representations widely employed in various tasks (Elnaggar


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2024). Generative models for protein sequences, exemplified
109   by recent advancements, enhance predictions of proteins with improved properties and functions (Wu
110   et al., 2021; Ovchinnikov & Huang, 2021). Simultaneously, progress in the sequence-to-structure
111   domain, as seen in models like AlphaFold (Jumper et al., 2021) and ESMFold (Lin et al., 2023a),
112   enables the prediction of 3D protein conformation from amino acid sequences. Models such as
113   ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022) or ESM-IF1 (Hsu et al., 2022) predict an amino acid sequence
114   given a specific 3D structure, effectively reverse engineering the process.
115   In the realm of protein generation, a diverse array of autoregressive models has been developed,
116   establishing a sophisticated baseline for subsequent model classes (Madani et al., 2023; Ferruz
117   et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2024; Hesslow et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2021). Beyond
118   autoregressive approaches, both categorical and continuous diffusion methods have emerged as
119   promising techniques for sequence generation (Alamdari et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Lee et al.,
120   2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). Additionally, three-dimensional diffusion models have been successfully
121   utilized for the generation of protein structures (Watson et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022; Lin & AlQuraishi,
122   2023; Fu et al., 2024). Notably, there are models that facilitate the simultaneous generation of both
123
      sequence and structure, providing a more integrated approach to protein design (Campbell et al.,
      2024; Ingraham et al., 2023). Furthermore, the field has seen the introduction of energy-based models
124
      (Frey et al., 2023) and generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Repecka et al., 2021), which offer
125
      alternative frameworks for protein generation.

      The proposed method comprises three parts. The first part is a pre-trained single-sequence encoder
      (E) that learns a meaningful latent space corresponding to the original protein space. The second part
132
      is a diffusion model (F) that generates vectors of protein latent space from a Gaussian noise. The
133
      third part is a decoder (D) that maps generated latent into the sequence of amino acids.
134
      Encodings. We utilize a pre-trained transformer-based pLM as an encoder with ESM-2 (Lin et al.,
135
      2023a) being the default choice unless otherwise specified. The encoder maps the sequence of discrete
136
      amino acids y = [y1 , ..., ys ] of length s to the latent vectors x = [x1 , ..., xs ] ∈ Rs×d , x = E(y).
137   Then, we employ dimension normalization to encourage each component of a single vector in the
138   sequence x to have zero mean and unit variance z0 = N ormalize(x). This transformation allows us
139   to adapt the discrete protein input to a standard Gaussian diffusion framework.
140   Noise schedule. We have found that the linear and cosine noise schedulers widely employed in the
141   image domain (Song et al., 2020b; Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) are sub-optimal for
142   the protein domain. We conjecture that this happens due to the sequential and discrete nature of the
143   protein representations.

      This training procedure also allows sampling with zero self-condition, which is used at the first
174
      iteration of generation. Unlike the approach presented in (Chen et al., 2022) we do not concatenate

      ẑ0,t to zt . Instead, we apply a linear transformation to ẑ0,t and incorporate it into the input of each
176   transformer block. This modification is designed to enhance the integration of information from
177   the denoised encodings into the transformer network, thereby improving the quality of generation.
178   Further details regarding the architecture can be found in Appendix E.1 and Figure 14.
179   Decoder. The proposed architecture allows us to use the decoder of ESM-2 pre-trained simultane-
180   ously with the encoder on masked language modeling objectives. However, we found that additional
181   finetuning of the decoder on a task of amino-acid reconstruction results in a more precise generation
182   of amino acid sequences from the latents x during inference. The decoder architecture comprises a
183   single linear layer.
184   Length sampling. An important aspect of the inference phase involves determining the length of the
185   generated sequence. There are two common approaches in this topic: padding generation and defining
186   the sequence length prior to sampling. While many diffusion models for discrete data generate padding
187   tokens concurrently with semantic tokens, our research indicates that using an attention mask during
188   training is crucial for optimal performance. We conjecture that the encodings for special tokens often
189
      contain information that is meaningless from the diffusion model’s perspective. Minimizing the
      reconstruction loss for these encodings can hinder the training process. By incorporating an attention
190
      mask, we can effectively focus the model’s attention on the relevant semantic tokens, leading to more
191
      accurate and efficient generation.
192
193   During inference, the length of the generated sequence is sampled from the length distribution of
194   the training dataset. This approach ensures that the generated sequences maintain a realistic length
195
      distribution, aligning with the characteristics of the training data (refer to Appendix E.3 for further
      details). Once the sequence length is determined, a random Gaussian vector is sampled. Using a
196
      fixed number of steps T , we iteratively generate the final ẑ0 . Following this generation process, the
197
      denormalized latent is mapped back to the corresponding amino acid sequence using the decoder.
198   This final step completes the generation process, yielding the desired protein sequence.
199
      Model architecture. We use the 12-layer Transformer model with 16 attention heads and a hidden
200
      size of 320 as a backbone for our diffusion model. We modify the model to ensure the effective
201   operation of denoising diffusion within the specific context of protein-related data (see Appendix
202   E.1 for more details). One noteworthy modification involves incorporating the time embedding into
203   each transformer block. To achieve this, we use a linear projection before summation prior to each
204   transformer block. Our experiments consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach for
205   time conditioning (refer to Section 4.2 and Table 1). An additional modification involves incorporating
206   long skip connections (Bao et al., 2023) into the transformer model. Our practical experiments have
207   demonstrated that this modification significantly accelerates the convergence of the model, leading to
208   more efficient training.

227   4.1   E VALUATION METRICS
228
      We conduct an evaluation of the generated sequences, employing a diverse set of metrics that
229   collectively assess the quality, diversity, distributional matching, and novelty of the generated proteins
230   across two modalities: sequence and structure.
231
232
      To assess the quality of generated proteins, we employ sequence- and 3D structure-based metrics:
      pLDDT, pseudo-perplexity, scPerplexity, TM-score, and BLAST identity score. No single metric is
233
      sufficient for evaluating protein sequence quality; therefore, we utilize a diverse suite of complemen-
234
      tary metrics. A key limitation of perplexity is its tendency to assign low (and thus better) values to
235   low-information, repetitive sequences. However, such sequences typically perform poorly on our
236   structural metrics (pLDDT, TM-score, scPerplexity), as repetitive sequences generally do not fold
237   into stable structures. Conversely, structure-based metrics may mislead when evaluating intrinsically
238   disordered proteins (IDPs) that lack stable 3D structures. In this context, sequence-based metrics like
239   perplexity provide valuable complementary information. Detailed information on the computation of
240   these quality-related metrics is available in Appendix B.1.
241   To further evaluate the diversity of the generated proteins, we employ a two-pronged approach that
242   considers both sequence-level and cluster-level metrics. We assess the diversity of the generated
243   amino acid sequences by quantifying the internal diversity of the amino acid sequences. Specifically,
244   we calculate the Rep metric to penalize models with a tendency to repeatedly generate popular or
245   commonly observed subsequences of amino acids. While such behavior may potentially inflate
246   quality metrics, such as perplexity, it is undesirable for a robust protein generation model, as it may
247   indicate overfitting or a lack of generative capability. In addition to sequence-level diversity, we
248   also evaluate the cluster diversity of the generated protein samples. These metrics aim to capture
249
      the model’s ability to generate a diverse set of protein clusters and a diverse range of their members,
      rather than concentrating the output on the most popular clusters or their prototypical representatives.
250
      The CD0.5 metric reflects the diversity of the generated protein clusters, while the CD0.95 metric
251
      provides insights into the diversity of the cluster representatives. By encouraging structural cluster
252   diversity, we mitigate the risk of mode collapse. Additional details regarding the diversity metrics
253   computation can be found in the Appendix B.2.
254
255
      To examine the distributional similarity of the generated proteins and the test datasets, we calculate
      Fréchet distance (FD), maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), and 1-Wasserstein optimal transport
256
      (OT) on ProtT5 sequence representations and ProteinMPNN structure representations. These metrics
257
      are widely used for accessing in generative modeling, as they simultaneously reflect both the quality
258   and diversity of the generated sequences. We utilize sample sizes of 2,048 sequences and compute
259   these distributional metrics against an independent test set. Additional details on the computation of
260   these distributional similarity metrics can be found in Appendix B.3.
261
      To assess the novelty of the generated proteins, we compute the distance between each generated
262
      sequence and its nearest neighbor in the training dataset. The mean of these distances across generated
263
      sequences-Novelty aims to ensure that the generated proteins are distinct from the training proteins
264   set. The specific details of the novelty metric computation and the distance measure employed can be
265   found in Appendix B.4.

339   We analyze the latent spaces of all ESM-2 encoders: ESM-8M, ESM-35M, ESM-150M, ESM-
340   650M, and ESM-3B. For this experiment, we train smaller version of DiMA, utilizing a transformer

      architecture with 6 layers, 16 heads, a hidden size of 320, and 8M parameters. To adapt diffusion
      architecture to the varying embedding dimensions of different ESM-2 encoders, we explore two
342
      approaches:
343          • Dimensionality Reduction: We attempt to compress the latent spaces of the encoders to the
344            target dimension of 320 using two reconstruction tasks. The first task involves training a
345            separate model to reconstruct the original encodings from the compressed representations
346            (comp ESM [enc]). The second task involves training a separate model to reconstruct the
347            original amino acid sequences from the compressed representations (comp ESM [seq]).
348            After compression, the diffusion model is trained to reconstruct the compressed space of the
349            encoder.
350          • Projectors Addition: Alternatively, we add three linear layers to our diffusion model,
351
               leaving other parameters untouched. The first linear layer projects the input zt to the
               dimension of 320, the second one projects self-condition ẑ0,t , and last one projects the
352
               output back to the initial dimension of the encoder. In this case, the diffusion model is
353
               trained to reconstruct the initial encoder space.
354
355   These two approaches provide distinct strategies for adapting the diffusion model to varying encoder
356   dimensions, enabling us to explore the impact of different pLM representations on the performance
357
      of protein sequence generation.
358   Table 2 presents a comparison of the latent spaces induced by different ESM-2 encoders, highlighting
359   the impact of the choice of protein language model (pLM) on protein generation. The results
360   demonstrate a clear advantage for latent spaces derived from larger encoders. While the diffusion
361   model consistently generates higher-quality proteins when training with ESM-3B, as indicated by
362   quality metrics, the model with ESM-650M exhibits a stronger ability to approximate the distribution
363
      of amino acid sequences in the training data. The observed behavior reflects a fundamental trade-
      off between quality and diversity in our current setup and reveals the interplay between model
364
      capacity and latent space complexity. DiMA with ESM-2 3B encoder shows improved quality metrics
365
      compared to 650M version (pLDDT increased from 71.5 to 74.6, ppl improved from 9.53 to 8.52),
366   but this comes with decreased diversity (CD0.5 drops from 0.748 to 0.660). This suggests that 8M
367   parameter diffusion model we use for these scaling experiments is reaching a capacity limit where
368   improvements in one aspect come at the cost of another. Given limited capacity, the model optimizes
369   for generation quality in a smaller region of the latent space (leading to better quality metrics) rather
370   than attempting broader but lower-fidelity coverage (yielding better distribution and diversity metrics).
371   These findings suggest that to fully leverage larger encoders like ESM-2 3B, we likely need to scale
372   up the diffusion model accordingly. The current results represent a capacity-constrained optimization
373   where the model must balance quality and coverage.
374   The model that uses compressed representations of ESM-2 150M (Table 2, bottom) through the
375   proposed dimensionality reduction techniques struggles to effectively learn how to generate proteins,
376   resulting in significantly degraded performance across all evaluated metrics. These results prove this
377   straightforward compressing technique with training additional decoder model a non-viable option
      for adapting a high-dimensional encoder.

      These findings highlight the importance of selecting an appropriate latent space for training the
379   diffusion model. When sufficient resources are available, we recommend using the largest available
380   encoder to maximize the potential for generating high-quality protein sequences. This approach offers
381   a significant advantage by allowing the utilization of the rich latent space of a large protein language
382   model during training. However, during inference, the encoder model can be discarded, resulting in a

      We conduct a series of experiments using CHEAP encoder instead of ESM-2. We aim to test the possi-
388   bility to apply the optimal hyperparameters discovered through our ablation studies directly to another
389   latent space. We tested two variants: CHEAP shorten 1 dim 64 and CHEAP shorten 2 dim 64.
390   Both encoders compress one dimension to 64, but CHEAP shorten 2 dim 64 additionally reduces
391   the sequence length dimension by half. For these experiments, we only replaced ESM-2 with CHEAP

397
      diffusion for protein generation generalize well across different embedding spaces. This opens up
398
      exciting possibilities for developing new protein design models based on continuous latent diffusion.

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we introduce DiMA, a continuous diffusion-based model for protein sequence gen-
533
      eration that operates within the space of protein model representations. A comprehensive ablation
534   study quantitatively verifies the impact of DiMA’s architectural features and design choices on its
535   performance. Through extensive experiments, we evaluate the quality, diversity, distribution sim-
536   ilarity, and biological relevance of the generated sequences. The results demonstrate that DiMA
537   achieves comparable protein generation quality with multibillion models while utilizing a hundred
538   times fewer parameters. Overall, this findings suggest that DiMA models are capable of generating
539   diverse variants of natural-like proteins. The framework presented in this study provides a foundation
      for future research in protein generation.

858   three-dimensional structure of the given protein sequence. For each amino acid within the predicted
859   structure, ESMfold provides a pLDDT score, which represents the confidence of the model in the
860   predicted positions of amino acids in the 3D structure. We average these pLDDT scores for all
861   amino acids in the sequence to gauge the overall confidence in the predicted protein structure. It is
862   worth noting that, while higher average pLDDT scores indicate a reliable structure prediction, lower
863   scores may not necessarily denote poor prediction. In some cases, they can also signify the presence
      of intrinsically disordered regions in the protein, segments that are inherently flexible and do not

920   used in structure generation (Yim et al., 2023). We employ MMseqs2 (Steinegger & Söding, 2017)

922   mode = 1.). While clustering at a moderate threshold (50%) reveals the model’s ability to generate
923   diverse proteins, individual clusters may still contain nearly identical sequences—an undesirable
924   characteristic for generative models. Therefore, we complement our analysis with CD0.95 , which
925   specifically identifies near-duplicate sequences. This dual-threshold approach provides a more

1009   1-Wasserstein optimal transport (OT). The BLAST identity metric effectively evaluates the simi-
1010   larity between generated and natural sequences. However, its limitation lies in assessing the model’s
1011   capability to produce diverse sequences, as it may identify the same dataset sequence as the closest
1012   match for every generated sequence. To overcome this limitation, we employ transportation theory to
1013   establish optimal pairs between generated sequences and the dataset.
1014   Optimal transport theory, initially devised for solving economic problems, has found applications in
1015   various fields, including physics, biology, and tomography. To implement our approach, we calculate
1016   pairwise Levenshtein distances and use them as transportation costs. Subsequently, we determine
1017   optimal sequence pairs using the Earth Mover Distance (EMD) solver with a uniform distribution of
1018   the samples. We use the average distance between these optimal pairs, measuring both the diversity
1019   and proximity of generated samples to the dataset.

1328   C.7    I NPAINTING
1329
1330   To demonstrate that solid performance in regime of unconditional generation enables effective condi-
1331   tional generation we conduct a proof-of-concept experiment on sequence inpainting, a challenging
1332   conditional generation task that requires generating novel sequences that maintain structural and
1333   functional coherence.
1334   We evaluate DiMA’s conditional generation capabilities using 180 sequences from our SwissProt
1335   test set, specifically selecting sequences with at most 50% identity to their nearest neighbors in the
1336   training set to prevent memorization effects. For each sequence, we mask a random region of variable
1337   length (8-50 amino acids) and assess generation quality using multiple stringent criteria: the complete
1338   sequence must achieve pLDDT ≥ 80, the inpainted region must maintain pLDDT ≥ 80, and the
1339   unmasked regions must preserve their structure (RMSD ≤ 1Å compared to the reference structure).
1340
       We want to point out, that these criteria are extremely tough, considering that we essentially use
       language models with no use of 3D-structure data.
1341
1342   To enable conditional generation, we augment DiMA with a lightweight adapter consisting of three
1343   transformer blocks, whose outputs are added to all diffusion transformer blocks. This adapter is
1344   trained on our unconditional training set with random region masking for 10,000 steps. To account
1345   for generation stochasticity, we perform 10 generation attempts per sequence and consider generation
1346
       successful if any attempt satisfied all quality criteria.
1347   The results demonstrate DiMA’s strong performance in conditional generation. DiMA achieves a
1348   42.2% success rate, outperforming both DPLM (40.0%) and random baseline (21.1%). Notably,
1349   DiMA generates inpainted regions with substantially higher average quality (pLDDT 66.9) compared
       to DPLM (59.3) and random baseline (50.9). Furthermore, the generated sequences show significant
