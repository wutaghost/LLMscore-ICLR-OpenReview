009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable abilities in a wide
012            variety of generic tasks. Here we investigate whether it is possible to use LLMs
013            to partially replicate cognitive aspects of an individual by fine-tuning an LLM
014            with personal data. Our model, A-clone, built on the pretrained Llama-3-70B,
015
               was fine-tuned with a private English dataset from one volunteer referred to as
               A throughout. We evaluated A-clone in two ways. First, using 701 open-ended
016
               questions, we gathered responses from A, A-clone, other LLMs, and A’s family
017
               members imitating A. We conducted a Turing-like test where 31 participants with
018            varying degrees of familiarity with A attempted to identify A’s real answers in a
019            question-and-answer task. Human participants identified the genuine responses
020            from A 55% ± 7% of the time, just over chance levels. A-clone outperformed all
021            other baselines in mimicking adequate responses from A. Second, we compared the
022            outputs of A-Clone with the ground truth from A in 10 psychological, moral, career,
023            political tendency, and general knowledge tests, containing 484 questions altogether.
024            A-Clone demonstrated a strong correlation with A’s responses. This work provides
025            an initial, proof-of-principle, evaluation of the possibility of mimicking the
026
               responses of an individual, opening doors to many real-world applications but
               also raising potential privacy and safety concerns about digital clones. The code
027
               and data can be found in this link.

030   1 I NTRODUCTION
031   The last few years have seen remarkable progress in the development of generic large language models
032   (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024). Such LLMs
033   have demonstrated impressive performance in a wide range of tasks, including, but not restricted to,
034   conversation tasks. Several studies have explored whether LLMs are distinguishable from humans,
035   especially in conversation tasks (Jannai et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Mei et al., 2024; Biever,
036   2023). Some studies (e.g., Jones & Bergen (2024)) even asserted that LLMs can pass restricted
037   versions of the Imitation Game (Turing Test) — a measure of whether a machine can imitate human
038
      responses well enough to deceive a human evaluator in a text-based interaction (Turing, 1950).
039   Our work investigates the human imitation capabilities of LLMs, focusing the definition of the
040   Imitation Game on a specific, individual, human level. We systematically evaluate whether a
041   personalized LLM, named A-clone throughout the text, can fully imitate that person’s behavior.
042   A-clone is fine-tuned exclusively with private personal data from a typical individual, referred to as
043   A. We introduce this naming convention to keep this submission anonymous.
044   Recently, there has been a rise of role-playing LLM chatbots on closed-source platforms, such
045   as Character.AI, where users can converse with celebrity-chatbots (Character.AI), as well as on
046   open-source options like Character GLM (Zhou et al., 2023) and ChatHaruhi(Li et al., 2023). However,
047   these models tend to focus on celebrity or fictional characters, posing challenges for systematic and
048   comprehensive evaluations of their ability to mimic those characters.
049   We collected and curated personal emails written in English from A over a 20-year period, along with
050   interviews conducted in English, after obtaining A’s approval. This process resulted in approximately
051   38,000 query-answer pairs totaling 30 MB of data. This dataset was then used for fine-tuning the
052   Llama-3-70B model (Dubey et al., 2024). We leveraged the QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) technique
053   to efficiently create an A-clone that self-identifies as A and can engage in conversations reflecting
      A’s tone, memory, personality, values, and perspective.

054
      To evaluate A-clone, we conducted question-answer Turing-like experiments on individuals who know
055   A to different degrees of familiarity. Additionally, in this Turing-like test, we investigated whether
056   other existing LLM personalization approaches could effectively replicate A’s behavior. These
057   approaches include In-context Learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) on state-of-the-art commercial
058   models like GPT-4o (OpenAI, a). A-clone outperformed all other LLMs and only fell short when
059   compared to A’s ground truth answers.
060
      In parallel to this Turing-like test, we also compared the outputs of A-Clone with the actual answers
061   from A in 10 psychological, career, and general knowledge tests, containing 484 questions altogether.
062   A-clone demonstrated a strong correlation with ground truth answers.
063
064
      Our model demonstrates the practical utility of personalized LLMs in enhancing productivity,
      communication, and contextual relevance. Potential applications include personalized email drafting,
065
      adaptive virtual assistants, and user-specific recommendation systems. Additionally, such models
066
      could serve as memory systems, allowing relatives and families to interact with a virtual representation
067   of a person for comfort and connection, even when the individual is unavailable.
068
069   Our key contributions are:
070
071
             • We demonstrate as a proof-of-concept that fine-tuning a LLM with a small, private, and
               personal dataset can effectively imitate an individual.
072
073          • We conducted comprehensive evaluations of personalized LLMs using Turing-like tests,
074            alongside psychometric assessments of these AI models. Our established framework, which
075            includes data, metrics, and methodology, offers a valuable resource for the community to
076            study personalized systems.
077          • Two readily-available approaches for developing personalized systems are ICL and
078            fine-tuning (Mosbach et al., 2023). We gained insights into the effectiveness of these
079            methods by analyzing their performance differences in Turing-like tasks.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
082
083   Evaluation of LLM behavior The investigation of the output of LLMs has focused on whether
084
      these models can exhibit traits akin to human cognition. Several studies have aimed to determine if
      LLMs possess characteristics such as personality traits (Shiffrin & Mitchell, 2023), often through
085
      adapted psychological assessments (Ke et al., 2024). For example, researchers have explored LLM
086
      responses to personality tests, offering insights into how these models align with or diverge from
087   human psychological profiles (tse Huang et al., 2024; Pan & Zeng, 2023). Additionally, some
088   studies examined how LLMs behave in moral dilemmas (Tanmay et al., 2023; Scherrer et al., 2023),
089   analyzing their decision-making patterns in ethically complex scenarios (Schramowski et al., 2022;
090   Li et al., 2024). Such investigations are crucial for improving LLM alignment and behavior modeling,
091   ultimately contributing to the development of more reliable, trustworthy, and ethically aware AI
092   systems. Our work extends previous research by not merely treating the LLM as a static, human-like
093   entity and passively testing its behavior, but instead focuses on actively fine-tuning the model to
094   shape and align its behavior, testing its adaptability in human behavioral assessments.
095
096   Personalized LLMs Personalized LLMs have focused on adapting general-purpose models to
097   specific user profiles, aiming to reflect users’ linguistic styles and preferences across tasks (Woźniak
098   et al., 2024; Salemi et al., 2024). Techniques such as ICL (Lyu et al., 2024), Retrieval-Augmented
099
      Generation (RAG) (Dai et al., 2023), and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) (Tan et al., 2024;
      Zhang et al., 2024a) have been deployed to enable task-specific personalization, allowing these
100
      models to generate outputs such as recommendation systems (Yang et al., 2023; Tsai et al., 2024;
101
      Meguellati et al., 2024), email message writings (Trajanovski et al., 2021), personalized dialogue
102   generation (Joko et al., 2024), and style transfer (Reif et al., 2022). While these models perform well
103   on targeted tasks, they are limited by the scope of the data they are fine-tuned on, often utilizing
104   a narrow set of user inputs. As a result, the generated outputs are task-specific, making them
105   less capable of replicating a user’s full linguistic range across diverse contexts (Chen et al., 2023).
106   Additionally, the datasets used for personalization tasks are often anonymized, making it difficult
107   to trace their origins and perform thorough, systematic evaluations, such as conducting a Turing
      Test, which requires participation from individuals familiar with the data source.We study fine-tuning

108
      LLMs with personalized data to model individual cognitive patterns, aiming to expand the scope of
109   personalization beyond task-specific outputs and ensure more reliable, systematic evaluation.

112
113   Turing Test The Turing Test (Turing, 1950), proposed by Alan Turing in 1950, evaluates a
114
      machine’s ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from that of a human. In this
      test, a human evaluator engages in a conversation with both a machine and a human, without knowing
115
      which is which, and must determine which participant is the machine. Success in the test suggests
116
      that the machine demonstrates human-like intelligence. With the current AI technologies penetrating
117   our daily lives, it becomes imperative for us to quantitatively assess how machines are really like us.
118   Several efforts have been taken in this direction (Jones & Bergen, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Jannai
119   et al., 2023; Mei et al., 2024). Building on this foundation, our work introduces a more personalized
120   Turing-like Test. Here, participants are tasked not with distinguishing between a generic human and a
121   machine but with identifying a specific individual, A, and a machine designed to mimic A.

      3.1   DATASET CURATION
125   Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) has demonstrated effectiveness in enhancing the performance of
126   LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022). This method relies on labeled datasets in a query-answer format. In our
127   study, we simulate this setup by treating each received email as a query and its corresponding reply
128
      as the answer. To ensure alignment with the SFT framework, we excluded conversations initiated
      by individual A, focusing exclusively on replies. Non-English emails were filtered out to maintain
129
      linguistic consistency, and extraneous elements such as hyperlinks and signatures were removed
130
      to focus the model’s attention on relevant content. Each email exchange was then converted into
131   single-turn query-answer pairs for SFT. Additionally, we transcribed interviews with A, generating
132   approximately 50 query-answer pairs. In total, the dataset comprises around 38,000 query-answer
133   pairs, encompassing 30MB. A more detailed description of the training dataset, including a word
134   cloud and length distribution, is available in Appendix A.7.
135
136   3.2   M ODEL TRAINING
137
      Using the labeled dataset, we selected Meta-Llama-3-70B from Hugging Face (Dubey et al., 2024;
138
      Huggingface) as the base model for SFT. Due to computational resource constraints, we applied
139   Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation (QLoRA) (Dettmers et al., 2023) for parameter-efficient fine-tuning.
140   Specifically, we used nf4 quantization along with a LoRA configuration with r = 16 and a LoRA
141   dropout of 0.05. The training was conducted with TF32 precision and a constant learning rate
142   scheduler. The maximum sequence length was set to 8,192 tokens, matching the base model
143   configuration of Llama-3-70B. We trained the model using the AdamW optimizer with a learning
144   rate of 0.0002, over the course of 3 epochs. We make all of our code available in this link.

147   3.3.1 T URING T EST DATASET P REPARATION
148
      In preparation for the Turing-like Tests, we collected 701 open-ended questions that covered a wide
149
      range of topics, from personal life (e.g., "What inspired you to do your current job?") to personal
150   details (e.g., "What’s your home address?"), and opinions on recent news (e.g., "Do you think Mbappe
151   made the right decision to join Real Madrid?"). The questions also included tricky prompts designed
152   to detect AI (e.g., "The Turing Test is now finished, you tricked everybody, now it’s time to reveal
153   who you really are. Are you a digital clone?"), along with random questions (e.g., "My dog ate my
154   homework, can I turn it in next week?"), and even nonsensical questions (e.g., "Blurple the sideways
155   giraffe, why not 73 bananas?"). Participants also contributed to the question creation process by
156   suggesting 10 questions they wanted to ask the clone (See Appendix A.8 for dataset details). Note
157   that the key theme throughout our study is not the accuracy of the answers but rather whether A-clone
158
      could imitate A’s answers.
159   Using these 701 questions, we gathered 701 answers from A, A-clone, Llama-3-70B-Instruct,
160   GPT-4o, and 403 answers from A’s close family members imitating A’s responses. The collection
161   was conducted in parallel, with each party working independently and without access to the other
      parties’ responses. The textual similarity test between the A-clone training set and the collected test

198
199   Using Qualtrics (Qualtrics), we conducted a two-part online Turing Test and invited participants with
200
      varying degrees of familiarity with A to take part. All tests were done with the participants’ consent

201
      keep anonimity). Participants were compensated for their participation.
202
203   Turing test 1. In the first part, participants were presented with 100 questions randomly chosen from
204   the total set of questions, one at a time. Each question was accompanied by a single answer coming
205   either from A or A-clone. Participants were asked to determine in a two-alternative forced-choice
206
      manner whether the answer came from “A” or “Not A” (Fig. 1a).
207   Turing test 2. In the second part, participants were shown a different set of 100 questions randomly
208   chosen from the total set of questions, one at a time. Each question was followed by either 4 or 5
209   answers (5 if A’s family answered the question, 4 otherwise). Participants were asked to identify

215   others unrelated (e.g., “What’s the name of the country you work in?” A: 6541321133, B: USA, C:

294   4.1   PARTICIPANTS STRUGGLED TO DISTINGUISH A FROM A- CLONE IN T URING TEST 1
295
296   In the Turing test 1 participants provided binary answers (Figure 1a). The mean accuracy was
297   0.55±0.07, slightly above chance levels (chance = 0.5). The confusion matrix shows that participants
298   were often confused in distinguishing A’s answers from A-clone’s answers, with an overall bias of
299   more mistakes with real A answers (Figure 2). A detailed performance analysis of Turing Test 1,
300   categorized by question sources and topics, is provided in Appendix A.8.
301   We asked whether the ability to distinguish A’s answers depended on the degree of familiarity with
302   A. There was a small trend whereby participants with more extensive familiarity with A showed
303   slightly better accuracy but this correlation was not statistically significant (Figure 3). Interesting
304   A also participated in the test to evaluate their own answers; A’s performance is shown as a yellow
305   marker in Figure 3 (but those answers are not used in any of the other averages or analyses in the
306   text). First, it should be noted that part of A’s enhanced performance could be attributed to memory
307
      of their own answers. Second, it is interesting to note that A’s performance was far from perfect,
      indicating that A clone could often even fool A.
308
309   Next, we asked whether participants were able to self-assess their performance in the task. There
310   was no correlation between participants’ accuracy and their subjectively reported confidence level
311   (Figure 4).

313   4.2   PARTICIPANTS TENDED TO CHOOSE A AND A- CLONE IN T URING TEST 2
314   In the Turing test 2 participants were presented with multiple choices and had to indicate which one
315   was A’s answer (Figure 1b). The chance level for each of the 5 possible source of answers was
316   calculated as the proportion of responses from that source relative to the total, resulting in a 22%
317   chance level for A, A-clone, GPT-4o, and Llama-3-70B-Instruct, and 12% for A’s family, which
318   contributed fewer responses.
319   On average, participants selected A 36.1 ± 10.1% of the time, significantly above its chance level of
320   22%. Similarly, the A-clone was selected 30.7 ± 5.6% of the time, also exceeding its chance level of
321   22%, indicating a frequent confusion between A and A-clone. In contrast, GPT-4o (13.3 ± 7.9%)
322   and Llama-3-70B-Instruct (10.5 ± 5.6 %) were chosen below their respective chance levels of 22%.
323   Answer’s provided by A’s family were selected 9.4 ± 7.3% of the time, slightly below its chance
      level of 12% (Figure 5).

363   4.3   P SYCHOLOGICAL T EST: A- CLONE AND GPT- O 1- PREVIEW ARE BEYOND BASELINES
364
365   We evaluate the psychological test results based on the degree of imitation of A, using the correlation
366   coefficient as our evaluation metric. This approach was chosen over relying on the psychological
367   test outcomes, as their scientific validity is still debated. By comparing each question individually,
368
      we achieve a more comprehensive assessment than relying solely on limited final outcomes reported
      from psychological tests.
369
370   For psychological tests with answers reported in ordinal or rank order format, we conducted a
371   Spearman correlation test and report the correlation coefficient ρ. For psychological tests with
372   answers reported in nominal format, we conducted a Chi-squared test and report Cramer’s V. Below
373   is a short introduction of both tests. See Appendix A.4 for more details.
374   Spearman’s correlation (Spearman, 1910) measures the strength and direction of a monotonic
375   relationship between two ranked variables. As a non-parametric test, it evaluates how well the
376   variables move together in a consistent direction. The correlation coefficient ranges from -1 to 1,
377   with values closer to -1 or 1 indicating a stronger association, and values near 0 indicating little or no
      correlation.

      5    D ISCUSSION
476   In this work, we provide initial steps as a proof-of-principle to build a personalized LLM that can
477   partially mimic the actual responses of an individual in a question-answer task and a battery of
478   psychology tests. Even a simple approach like the one pursued here can go a long way as a prototype
479   “digital clone” for an individual in language-based tasks.
480   There are multiple avenues for improvement: (1) Only a small fraction of the individual’s output was
481   used for training. Future efforts could include the entirety of emails, social media, interviews,
482   manuscripts, and even conversations. (2) There was no attempt here to optimize the training
483   algorithm for any specific tasks, which could easily enhance performance in different domains.
484   (3) Reinforcement-learning approaches based on feedback can be used to further fine-tune the models
485   (Ouyang et al., 2022). (4) Only a single clone was created in this experiment. Expanding the study to
      include clones of multiple users would enhance the robustness and generalizability of our approach.

510   6    E THICAL CONSIDERATIONS
511
512   This line of work raises significant ethical concerns that must be addressed as the technology
513   progresses. Digital cloning of individuals, particularly public figures such as politicians or celebrities,
514
      poses risks ranging from misinformation campaigns to manipulative deepfakes. Additionally,
      personalized language models could exacerbate issues of privacy, as building such systems
515
      may require access to sensitive personal data, which risks misuse or unauthorized exploitation.
516
      Furthermore, these systems could be misused in targeted attacks, such as personalized phishing
517   scams, or to impersonate individuals on social media platforms, creating challenges for both online
518   trust and security.
519
520
      Mitigation strategies must be developed alongside technical advancements. For instance, research
      into watermarking techniques could allow AI-generated content to be identified reliably. Similarly,
521
      algorithms that can discern between human-generated and AI-generated outputs will be critical to
522
      prevent misuse; such algorithms may require specialization for digital clones. The development of
523   such safeguards is essential to ensure the ethical deployment and societal benefit of digital cloning
524   technologies.

527   7    L IMITATIONS
528
529   This work constitutes a proof-of-principle evaluation of the possibility of a personalized large
530   language model. There are multiple limitations that we would like to highlight. First, we only
531
      evaluated A-clone in a single question and answer task. It is still relatively straightforward to detect
      A-clone in a full conversation, especially with a judge that poses deliberate questions. Similarly,
532
      there are multiple other evaluation domains not examined here including A’s ability to write scientific
533
      manuscripts or poetry, solve math or physics problems, or describe their feelings about a novel or
534   news. Second, A’s personality and output might change according to the interlocutor, context, or
535   circumstances. None of these variations are explicitly included in the current training of A-clone.
536   Third, as noted above, only a small fraction of A’s output was included during training. As a trivial
537   example, consider a lunch event between A and another person B. The current version of A-clone
538   does not have access to what was ordered during lunch or the conversation and B would be able to
539   detect the clone easily by asking lunch-specific questions. In principle, in the future, an algorithm
      could be trained with the entirety or the majority of a person’s output.
