011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Accurate and efficient simulation of wave equations is crucial in computational
014            physics, especially for wave imaging applications like ultrasound computed to-
015            mography (USCT), which reconstructs tissue properties from scattered waves.
016            Traditional numerical solvers for wave equations are computationally intensive
017            and often unstable, limiting their practical applications for quasi-real-time imaging.
018
               Neural operators offer an innovative approach by accelerating PDE solving using
               neural networks; however, their effectiveness in realistic imaging is constrained
019
               by existing datasets that oversimplify real-world complexity. In this paper, we
020
               present OpenWaves, a large-scale wave equation dataset designed to bridge the
021            gap between theoretical equations and practical imaging applications. OpenWaves
022            provides over 16 million frequency-domain wave simulations using real USCT
023            configurations, featuring anatomically realistic human breast phantoms across four
024            categories. It enables comprehensive benchmarking of popular neural operators
025            for both forward simulation and inverse imaging tasks, allowing analysis of their
026            performance, scalability, and generalization capabilities. By offering a realistic
027            and extensive dataset, OpenWaves not only serves as a platform for developing
028            innovative neural PDE solvers but also facilitates their deployment in real-world
029
               medical imaging problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Imaging technology decodes wave-matter interactions and plays a critical role in scientific discoveries
034
      and biomedical diagnosis. In recent years, Ultrasound Computed Tomography (USCT) has emerged
035
      as an innovative, radiation-free method with exceptional potential for high-resolution imaging of
036   human tissues.(Guasch et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022) As illustrated in Fig. 1(a), USCT employs a
037   specialized transducer arrayвЂ”annular, cylindrical, or hemisphericalвЂ”for data acquisition. Unlike
038   conventional B-mode ultrasound, which requires manual operation and relies solely on reflected
039   signals, USCT is fully automatic. It sequentially emits waves from each transducer and measures
040   signals with the remaining ones, collecting both transmitted and reflected signals from tissues.(Cueto
041   et al., 2022) This method enables USCT to reconstruct detailed 2D and 3D tissue structures similar to
042   those produced by X-ray computed tomography (CT)(Wu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023).
043   Wave scattering within tissues is significant in USCT because ultrasonic wavelengths are comparable
044   to human tissue structures. To account for this, USCT employs partial differential equations (PDEs) to
045   model wave propagation and solves a nonlinear PDE-constrained inverse problem to reconstruct tissue
046   properties such as attenuation and sound speed.(Bernard et al., 2017; PГ©rez-Liva et al., 2017) This
047   process is known as full waveform inversion (FWI). The computationally intensity and numerically
048   instability of traditional wave equation solvers makes FWI a bottleneck for quasi-real-time USCT
049   imaging, limiting its widespread clinical applications (Ali et al., 2024).
050   Neural operators have recently revolutionized PDE-based simulations and inverse problems due
051   to their powerful approximation capabilities and fast computational speed. By leveraging neural
052   networks to map between PDE parameter spaces and physical fields, neural operators have shown
053   remarkable potential across diverse scientific applications, such as turbulent flow modeling, weather
      forecasting, and material design.(Lu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; 2021; Lu et al., 2021) High-quality

      PDE datasets, like PDEBench(Takamoto et al., 2022) and OpenFWI(Deng et al., 2022), have been
      instrumental in advancing neural operators. However, although these datasets cover various PDEs,
074
      they often simulate oversimplified scenariosвЂ”such as small regions of interest (ROIs), simple
075
      geometric boundaries, or unrealistic random porous media. These simplified settings limit the
076   applicability to real-world problems, where complexity is much greater. To promote the practical
077   deployment of neural operators, an application-driven, realistic, and large-scale dataset is desired.
078
079
      In this paper, we introduce OpenWaves, a large-scale USCT dataset designed for benchmarking wave
      simulation and imaging using neural operators. OpenWaves connects theoretical wave equations
080
      with a practical medical imaging application, offering over 16 million frequency-domain wave
081
      simulations based on the Helmholtz equation (wave frequencies Г— source locations Г— scattering media
082   в†’ wavefields; 8 Г— 256 Г— 8,000 в†’ 16,384,000). The dataset features anatomically realistic human
083   breast phantoms across four categories (Fig. 1(b)), and the source locations and frequencies mimic
084   the settings of a real annular USCT system. With its quasi-realistic setup, OpenWaves serves both as
085   a resource for theoretical studies in deep learning and as a platform for training neural operators that
086   can be deployed in real medical imaging systems.
087
      We also implement multiple popular ML surrogates for both forward simulation and direct inverse
088   imaging tasks. Both cases are evaluated using standard metrics, such as relative root mean square
089   error (RRMSE) for forward simulation and structural similarity index measure (SSIM) and peak
090   signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) for imaging. Our experiments demonstrate that well-designed datasets
091   like OpenWaves enable neural PDE solvers to enhance real-world wave simulation and imaging tasks.
092
      In the remainder of this paper, we review related datasets and baselines (Section 2), introduce the
093
      OpenWaves dataset (Section 3), present and discuss benchmarking results (Section 4), and conclude
094   by summarizing the datasetвЂ™s contributions and limitations.

099   2.1    N EURAL O PERATORS
100
      Neural operators are machine learning models designed to learn mappings between infinite-
101
      dimensional function spaces, enabling data-driven solutions to partial differential equations (PDEs).
102   They are versatile tools for both forward simulations, predicting PDE solutions given parameters, and
103   inverse problems, inferring underlying parameters from observations. For forward simulations, base-
104   line neural operator frameworks include UNet(Ronneberger et al., 2015), which utilizes convolutional
105   neural networks with encoder-decoder architectures; the Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)(Li et al.,
106   2021; 2020) and its variantsвЂ”UNet FNO (UFNO)(Wen et al., 2022), Born FNO (BFNO)(Zhao et al.,
107   2023), Adaptive FNO (AFNO)(Guibas et al., 2022)вЂ”which leverage Fourier modes to capture global
      information efficiently; and the Multigrid Neural Operator (MgNO)(He et al., 2023), combining


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      multigrid methods with neural networks to handle multi-scale problems. In inverse problem, frame-
109   works like InversionNet(Zeng et al., 2022), which uses a convolutional neural network to directly
110   model the inversion operator, have been developed. Deep Operator Network (DeepONet)(Lu et al.,
111   2019; 2021; Cai et al., 2021; Di Leoni et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2021) introduces a вЂњbranch and trunkвЂќ
112   architecture to efficiently separate input functions from evaluation locations for operator learning.
113   Fourier-DeepONet(Zhu et al., 2023) and the Neural Inverse Operator (NIO)(Molinaro et al., 2023)
114   extend this approach by integrating DeepONet with FNO, combining local and global representations
115   to improve accuracy and efficiency in mapping observations to PDE parameters.
116
117   2.2    PDE DATASETS
118
      High-quality datasets are crucial for advancing deep learning approaches to PDEs, as they provide
119
      benchmarks for training and evaluating neural operator models.(Lu et al., 2022; de Hoop et al., 2022;
120
      Benitez et al., 2023) PDEBench(Takamoto et al., 2022) is a widely used benchmark dataset that
121   covers various forms of PDEs primarily in fluid mechanics, such as Darcy flow, advection, diffusion,
122   and Navier-Stokes equations, but it lacks wave propagation PDEs. OpenFWI(Deng et al., 2022)
123   specifically targets wave equations for geophysical problems, benchmarking neural networks for
124   direct inversion from partial seismic wavefield observations. Recently, WaveBench(Liu et al., 2024)
125   has been introduced to benchmark neural operators for forward simulations using extensive datasets
126   of time-harmonic and time-varying wave simulations.
127   Despite their contributions, both OpenFWI and WaveBench assume oversimplified scattering media
128   or sourcesвЂ”OpenFWI uses layered structures, and WaveBench employs Gaussian random fields and
129   MNIST(LeCun et al., 1998) with fixed source locationsвЂ”and limit simulations to small ROIs (fewer
130   than 40 wavenumbers). These simplifications may lead to overly optimistic evaluations that fail to
131   accurately assess neural operator performance in realistic applications, such as biomedical imaging
132   scenarios where physical properties vary more complexly and ROIs exceed 100 wavenumbers. This
133   underscores the need for a dataset that captures the complexities of real-world wave phenomena,
134   motivating us to create OpenWaves, a more accurate benchmark for neural operator models in
135
      practical biomedical imaging settings. To enable consistent model comparisons, we also provide a
      unified PyTorch environment for benchmarking various models for forward and inverse tasks.

199   3.2     OVERVIEW OF THE DATASET
200
      3.2.1    P HYSICAL S ETTINGS AND S TATISTICS
201
202   OpenWaves includes 8,000 breast phantoms designed to represent the distribution of diverse human
203   breast types in the population. As shown in Fig. 1(b), the dataset is divided into four groups, each
204   corresponding to a specific breast density type: heterogeneous (HET), fibroglandular (FIB), all
205   fatty (FAT), and extremely dense (EXD). The wavefields are simulated using parameters from a real
206   annular USCT system, which consists of 256 transducers arranged in a 220 mm diameter ring. The
207   system operates at frequencies (П‰/2ПЂ) ranging from 300 kHz to 1500 kHz, corresponding to acoustic
208
      wavelengths between 1 mm and 5 mm. In our simulations, we focus on 8 frequencies between 300
      kHz and 650 kHz, sampled at 50 kHz intervals, resulting in ROIs with approximately 50 to 100
209
      wavenumbers. For each breast phantom, wavefields are simulated by activating each transducer at all
210
      frequencies, generating a total of 8, 000 Г— 256 Г— 8 = 16, 384, 000 data entries. Detailed statistics
211   and physical settings of the dataset are summarized in Table 1.
212
213   3.2.2    DATA G ENERATION
214
215   The dataset generation involves two key steps: 1) generating anatomically accurate breast phantoms,
      and 2) simulating the corresponding wavefields using real USCT system parameters.

      Phantom Generation The breast phantoms are generated using a medical simulation tool developed
217   by the Virtual Imaging Clinical Trial for Regulatory Evaluation (VICTRE) project at the US Food
218   and Drug Administration (FDA).(Li et al., 2022) This tool produces 3D models of various breast
219   anatomies, categorized into the four density types mentioned earlier. These models are sliced into 2D
220   tissue maps, and then scaled by a random factor to simulate breasts of varying sizes. To replicate real
221   experimental conditions, the area surrounding the breast models is filled with water.
222
      Wavefield Simulation After generating the breast phantoms, we simulate the wavefields using
223   numerical solvers based on the USCT systemвЂ™s source locations and frequencies. We employ the
224   Convergent Born Series (CBS) algorithm(Osnabrugge et al., 2016), an iterative solver for simulating
225   the Helmholtz equation. Unlike the standard Born series, CBS incorporates a preconditioner to ensure
226   convergence, making it reliable for simulating complex media with strong scattering properties.

417   4.3.1    DATA C OMPLEXITIES

419
      Breast Types Different breast categories have distinct internal structures, leading to significant
420   variations in sound speed distribution and wave scattering effects within the tissue. As observed in
421   Figures 1, the heterogeneous and extremely-dense breasts exhibit the most complex tissue structures
422   and the strongest scattering because of their higher densities, while the fibroglandular and fatty breasts
423   show the weakest scattering. This is further validated by the prediction accuracy of the learned neural
424   operators for both forward and inverse problems as shown in Appendix A.3 (Figures 8 and 9), where
425   heterogeneous and extremely-dense breasts reveal higher errors.
426   Frequencies All baseline neural operators experience performance degradation when learning
427   wavefields at higher frequencies, as shown in Fig. 4 (a). This indicates that higher frequency wave
428   equations define a more challenging operator learning task with greater complexity (Engquist & Zhao,
429   2018). Among all baselines, the UNet degrades the fastest, while the FNO and MgNO show less
430   pronounced error increases. This suggests that incorporating global, local, and multiscale features is
431   crucial for achieving high-accuracy approximations in operator learning across different frequency
      levels.

      5     C ONCLUSION
521
522   We introduced OpenWaves, a large-scale, anatomically realistic USCT dataset designed to bridge the
523   gap between numerical studies of wave equations and practical imaging applications. OpenWaves
524   provides over 16 million frequency-domain wave simulations based on a real USCT system, featuring
525
      anatomically accurate human breast phantoms across four density categories. We benchmarked
      several baseline methods for both forward wave simulation and inverse imaging tasks, comparing
526
      their performance. Our results highlight the strengths and limitations of existing neural operator
527
      architectures, providing insights into their generalization capabilities and scalability. OpenWaves
528   offers a valuable platform for developing and benchmarking neural wave equation solvers, enabling
529   their application in real-world imaging tasks involving complex wave phenomena.
530
531
      Limitations While OpenWaves represents a significant step toward realistic benchmarking of neural
      wave equation solvers, it has certain limitations. The dataset is currently limited to breast phantoms;
532
      including other organs like limbs or brains would enhance its applicability. Simulations are restricted
533
      to 2D due to computational constraints; incorporating 3D data would provide a more accurate
534   representation of real-world scenarios. The dataset primarily varies sound speed as the tissue
535   property; incorporating other properties like attenuation and anisotropy could further enhance realism.
536   Additionally, our study focuses on neural operator architectures without extensively exploring the
537   influence of their hyperparameters such as the number of FNO layers or other network parameters.
538   Future work will address these limitations by expanding the datasetвЂ™s scope and conducting more
539   comprehensive analyses, aiming to provide even more valuable resources for the development of
      robust neural wave equation solvers.
