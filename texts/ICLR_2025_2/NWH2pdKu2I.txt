009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) has become a promising solution for Parameter-
012            Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) of pre-trained Vision Transformer (ViT) models
013            on downstream vision tasks. VPT partially fine-tunes a set of learnable tokens
014            while keeping the majority of the model parameters frozen. Recent research
015            has explored modifying the connection structures of the prompts. However, the
016            fundamental correlation and distribution between the prompts and image tokens
017            remain unexplored. In this paper, we leverage metric learning techniques to
018
               investigate how the distribution of prompts affects fine-tuning and transfer learning
               performance. Specifically, we propose a novel framework, Distribution Aware
019
               Visual Prompt Tuning (DA-VPT), to guide the distributions of the prompts by
020
               learning the distance metric from their class-related semantic data. Our method
021            demonstrates that the prompts can serve as an effective bridge to share semantic
022            information between image patches and the class token. We extensively evaluated
023            our approach on popular benchmarks in both recognition and segmentation tasks.
024            The results show the effectiveness of our proposed method and offer a new direction
025            for PEFT optimization in vision transformers. We demonstrate that our approach
026            enables more effective and efficient fine-tuning of ViT models by leveraging
027            semantic information to guide the learning of the prompts, leading to improved
028            performance on various downstream vision tasks. The code will be released.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the rapid development of scaling up in the size of models and training datasets (Deng et al., 2009;
      Sun et al., 2017; Mahajan et al., 2018), a significant number of vision foundation models have been
034
      proposed in recent years. In particular, pre-trained models based on the Vision Transformer (ViT)
035
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) backbone demonstrate remarkable performance across various computer
036   vision tasks (He et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021b; He et al., 2022a). Following the powerful abilities
037   of pre-trained vision models, fine-tuning these foundation models for downstream tasks, such as
038   visual recognition (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and semantic segmentation (Kirillov et al., 2023), has
039   become a popular strategy.
040
      However, the conventional full fine-tuning strategy, which involves updating all parameters for
041
      downstream tasks, is often criticized for its high training costs, overfitting, and the risk of catastrophic
042   forgetting due to mismatches between the scale and distribution of the pre-training and local datasets
043   (Kornblith et al., 2019). These weaknesses particularly affect performance when the scale of the
044   model and data becomes larger (Toneva et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2019). To address these challenges,
045   a group of promising research works proposes Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), where the
046   majority of model parameters are frozen, and only a small subset of learnable parameters is updated
047   (Kornblith et al., 2019; Houlsby et al., 2019; Pfeiffer et al., 2020; Zaken et al., 2021; Li & Liang,
048   2021; Chen et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2022).
049   Starting from the NLP area, Houlsby et al. (2019) and subsequent works (Pfeiffer et al., 2020;
050   Hu et al., 2021) propose updating a minimal number of parameters while achieving performance
051   comparable to full fine-tuning. Extending these techniques to vision tasks, Chen et al. (2022)
052   introduce parallel residual networks alongside the ViT backbone. To further improve the performance
053   of PEFT strategies, Jia et al. (2022) first propose Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) to prepend a set of
      learnable tokens, namely prompts, to the input data in each ViT layer. This adaptation simplifies

086   However, existing VPT approaches mainly focus on manipulating the connection or structure of the
087
      prompts, while the intrinsic connection between the prompts and the data representations is often
      ignored, which causes difficulty in optimizing parameter values in the prompts. The existing VPT
088
      work (Jia et al., 2022) and its followers (Yoo et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Pei et al., 2024) propose
089
      to randomly initialize the prompts, then update them from a single objective of the downstream tasks.
090   We still lack a clear understanding of how the prompts support the model in transfer learning. In this
091   paper, we investigate the correlation between the prompts and the pre-trained model by answering an
092   interesting question: Can the prompts be guided to deliver information between the image and
093   the class tokens to improve the representation capability?
094
      To this end, we introduce a novel framework that guides the VPT optimization process by leveraging
095
      the semantic connection between the learnable prompts, the visual image patches, and the class
096   tokens. Specifically, we propose connecting the prompts and the visual data by constructing and
097   learning a semantic metric between them in the deep layers of the ViT. For every prompt in the deep
098   layer, we establish a semantic mapping between the prompt and its closest image class. As illustrated
099   in Figure 1 (a), we construct a semantic metric in the latent space by comparing the prompt with the
100   corresponding image patches of the same or different class. Additionally, we construct a semantic
101   metric from the class token to the prompts.
102   Our key intuition is to improve the similarity between the prompt and the image patches labeled
103   with the same semantic classes while reducing the similarity of those with different classes. By
104   constructing the semantic metric in the feature and prompt space, we demonstrate that semantic
105   information can be easily transferred from image patches to the class tokens through the related
106   prompt in the corresponding class. In essence, our framework employs the related prompt as a bridge
107   to connect the class tokens and the semantic information of image patches by effectively guiding their
      attention maps, as depicted in Figure 1 (b).

      Our extensive experiments on 24 popular visual recognition tasks in both Fine-Grained Visual
109   Classification (FGVC) (Jia et al., 2022) and Visual Task Adaptation Benchmark (VTAB-1k) (Zhai
110   et al., 2019) demonstrate substantial improvements over the standard VPT, highlighting the efficacy
111   of our method on both supervised and self-supervised pre-trained models, such as MAE. We also
112   extensively evaluate our method on the ADE20k segmentation task. We demonstrate that our
113   proposal significantly improves the learning efficiency of the prompts with improved performance
114   in downstream tasks while requiring fewer prompts and learnable parameters compared to both the
115   baseline VPT and the other state-of-the-art PEFT methods.
116   In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
117
118   • We propose Distribution Aware Visual Prompt Tuning (DA-VPT), a novel and efficient framework
119     to improve the learning performance of prompts by constructing semantic metrics between the
120     prompts and the corresponding image feature patches in the deep layers of ViT.
121   • We reveal the importance of guiding the learning process of the prompts and demonstrate that the
122     prompts can be an effective bridge to connect the semantic information between image patches and
123     class tokens via the attention mechanism.
124   • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method on 24 popular downstream visual recognition tasks
125     and 2 segmentation tasks showing significant improvement compared to the vanilla VPT on both
126     supervised and self-supervised pre-trained vision models.

132   The Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) is a fundamental model architecture that
133   applies the original Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017b) to computer vision tasks. Given an
134   input image I, ViT divides it into a sequence of N flattened 2D patches, which are then linearly
135   projected into a D-dimensional embedding space. A learnable [CLS] (Class) token xcls ∈ RD
136   is prepended to the patch embeddings, serving as a global representation for classification tasks.
137   The resulting sequence of embeddings x ∈ R(N +1)×D is then passed through L Transformer block
138   layers, each consisting of a Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) mechanism which is defined as
139
      MHSA(xl ) = Concat(H1 , · · · , Hh ) for layer l ∈ L, where each head H computes a scaled dot-

                                       d
141   projected from input embedding xl−1 in the previous layer. The final output of the ViT is the [CLS]
142   token xLcls , which is used for downstream classification tasks.
143
      Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) (Jia et al., 2022) present a promising PEFT technique for ViT that
144   adapts the pre-trained model to downstream tasks by introducing a small set of learnable parameters,
145   namely prompts. In a specific ViT block layer, a sequence of M learnable prompt tokens p =
146   {p1 , . . . , pM } ∈ RM ×D is concatenated with the patch embeddings x = {x1 , . . . , xN } ∈ RN ×D .
147   Jia et al. (2022) propose two VPT settings: VPT-Shallow where the prompts are only inserted into
148   the first ViT layer, and VPT-Deep where the prompts are appended into every ViT layer. We follow
149   the VPT-Deep setting since it has a higher capacity and aligns with our proposed method, where the
150   metric learning objective guides the prompts in the deep layer. The resulting sequence of embeddings
151   [xcls , p, x] ∈ R(M +N +1)×D is then processed by the next ViT encoder layers. Specifically for
152   image-embedded patches xl in the lth layer, the output of the l + 1 layer can be described as follows:

242   2.3   P ROJECTION AND S ALIENCY PATCH S ELECTION
243
      To ensure that the selected prompts effectively focus on critical information in an image sample
244
      and filter out false positive image patches, we propose selecting saliency information from the
245   image patches as positive and negative samples for comparison with the prompts in the loss function
246   LML (x, p). A straightforward approach is to extract the saliency patches from the attention map
247   queried by the prompts. However, in practice, generating the attention map can be resource-intensive,
248   particularly under optimized attention mechanisms such as Flash Attention. To mitigate this, we
249   propose an alternative approach: instead of relying on the attention map, we use the output represen-
250   tation immediately following the attention layer to compare with the prompts. As shown in Figure 2,
251   the output representation xl = MHSA(xl ) is a concatenation of the representations from each head,
252   which also serves as a saliency aggregation of the image patches.
253
254   2.4   DYNAMICALLY M APPING C LASSES AND P ROMPTS
255
      We set M learnable prompts in each layer of the ViT block, where M ≪ C (total number of classes).
256
      This is to avoid optimization difficulties and unequal training opportunities. Thus, we develop a
257   semantic mapping strategy to map C classes to the M prompts. Before training, we run an additional
258   epoch to pick samples and perform mean pooling to [CLS] token for each class to obtain the set of
259   class representations s ∈ RC×D . We generate these class representations by running the samples
260   through the pre-trained ViT. We then use k-means clustering to group these representations into M
261   clusters, assigning classes to prompts based on the clusters, as illustrated in Figure 2.
262
      To maintain the semantic mapping, we update the dynamic mapping after each epoch. During
263   the training of each epoch, we collect and calculate the mean of class representations s. We then
264   update the k-means with the centroids from the previous epoch and adjust the class-prompt mapping
265   accordingly.

348
      We evaluated our proposed method on two widely-used visual transfer learning benchmarks: Fine-
349
      Grained Visual Classification (FGVC) (Jia et al., 2022) that contains 5 datasets for general visual
350   recognition, and the Visual Task Adaptation Benchmark (VTAB-1K) (Zhai et al., 2019) with 19
351   datasets for few-shot transfer learning. Additionally, we tested our approach on ADE20K (Zhou
352   et al., 2019) and PASCAL Context (Mottaghi et al., 2014) for dense prediction tasks.
353
      We use the plain Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) as the pretrained backbone.
354
      To evaluate the generalization of our method, we initialize the backbone using either supervised
355
      pre-training on ImageNet-21K (Deng et al., 2009) or self-supervised pre-training on ImageNet-1K
356   without labels, using methods such as MoCo v3 (Chen et al., 2021) and MAE (He et al., 2022a). For
357   architecture, we adopt the base-size model ViT-B (12 layers) for visual classification tasks and the
358   large-size model ViT-L (24 layers) for semantic segmentation, consistent with existing works. For
359   more experimental details and data set settings, see Section B and Table 5 in the Appendix.
360
      For all of our experiments, we follow two branches of settings: DA-VPT, our principal proposed
361
      method, which builds on the conventional VPT-Deep Jia et al. (2022) architecture while incorporating
362   our proposed metric learning losses LML (x, p) and LML (p, xcls ), as introduced in Section 2.2. DA-
363   VPT+ is an advanced version of DA-VPT, where we further apply efficient bias tuning to enhance
364   learning capability, as detailed in Section 2.5.

         5                    C ONCLUSION
521
522
         In this paper, we introduce Distribution-Aware Visual Prompt Tuning (DA-VPT), a novel and efficient
523
         framework for improving the learning performance of visual prompts in Vision Transformer (ViT)
524      models. By constructing semantic metrics between the prompts and the corresponding image feature
525      patches in the deep layers of ViT, our method effectively guides the learning process of the prompts,
526      enabling them to serve as a bridge connecting the semantic information between image patches
527      and class tokens via the attention mechanism. Through extensive experiments on 24 popular visual
528      recognition tasks and 2 segmentation tasks in different domains, we demonstrated that DA-VPT
529      significantly improves the performance of downstream tasks compared to vanilla VPT, while requiring
530      fewer prompts and learnable parameters. Our results highlight the importance of considering the
531      intrinsic connection between visual prompts and data samples and showcase the potential of our
532
         approach to enhance the transfer learning capabilities of pretrained vision models. We believe that
         our findings can inspire further research on parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategies and contribute
533
         to the development of more effective and efficient vision foundation models.

818   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) Initially introduced by Vaswani et al. (2017a), Trans-
819   formers have been pre-trained across various domains, including natural language processing (e.g.,
820
      LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), GPT (Brown et al., 2020)) and computer vision (e.g., MAE (He et al.,
      2022b), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021a), ViT-22b (Dehghani et al., 2023)). Recent advancements in
821
      PEFT focus on freezing most parameters while selectively fine-tuning others to improve efficiency
822
      for downstream tasks. Kornblith et al. (2019) suggested training only the classification head, whereas
823   Zaken et al. (2021) achieved significant gains by tuning only the bias terms. Further refinements by
824   Lian et al. (2022) and Xie et al. (2023) involve adjusting additional shifting and scaling factors. A
825   subset of PEFT strategies, such as those by Houlsby et al. (2019), involves tuning lightweight models
826   known as adapters alongside Transformer backbones. These approaches were further extended by
827   Pfeiffer et al. (2020) with bidirectional projection networks, and by Chen et al. (2022), who adapted
828   them for computer vision tasks, significantly improving performance. Most recently, Zhang et al.
829   (2024) introduced a combination of sparse adapters, further boosting their efficacy.
830   Visual Prompt Tuning (VPT) This area of research uses learnable vectors, or prompts, to add
831   task-specific information to input data (Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Lester et al., 2021; Liu
832   et al., 2021). It also uses vision-language models that can be tuned to help with vision tasks (Radford
833   et al., 2021b; Zhou et al., 2022; Ge et al., 2023). Jia et al. (2022) first demonstrated the effective
834   application of prompts in Vision Transformers (ViT), introducing variations like VPT-Shallow at the
835   input layer and VPT-Deep across all Transformer layers. Gao et al. (2022) used visual prompts for
836   test-time domain adaptation, and Gao et al. (2023a) expanded prompt tuning to video recognition.
837
      Studies by Tsai et al. (2024) and Ren et al. (2024b) further explored robust visual perception and
      deep metric learning applications of visual prompts.
838
839   Following the seminal work of Jia et al. (2022), later studies such as those by Han et al. (2023) and
840   Yoo et al. (2023) developed dynamic mechanisms for selecting the number and placement of visual
841   prompts. Tu et al. (2023) enhanced VPT by linking intermediate layers directly with task-specific
842   heads, and Pei et al. (2024) introduced a spatial selection mechanism to better coordinate attention
843
      between image patches and visual prompts. More recently, Han et al. (2024) analyzed the underlying
      conditions contributing to the success of VPT. In contrast to existing research that mostly trains visual
844
      prompts with a single downstream goal, our study aims to improve prompt effectiveness by revealing
845
      the connections and distributions between the prompts and image patches using novel metric learning
846   guidance.
847
848   Metirc Learning (ML) This field focuses on learning representations and metrics that distinguish
849
      the separation between two data samples by arranging similar data points closer together in the
      representation space. Initial approaches employed contrastive loss to differentiate samples from
850
      distinct classes (Chopra et al., 2005; Hadsell et al., 2006). Subsequent methods introduced an anchor
851
      point, utilizing triplet loss with a specified margin to compare both positive and negative samples
852   (Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Cheng et al., 2016; Hermans et al., 2017). Further advancements in Metric
853   Learning have leveraged Neighbourhood Components Analysis (NCA), inspired by the semantic
854   relationships between different classes, to explore data distributions and class relationships more
855   deeply (Roweis et al., 2004; Movshovitz-Attias et al., 2017; Teh et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2020;
856   Venkataramanan et al., 2022; Roth et al., 2022). Recent studies have shown the effectiveness and
857   robustness of NCA-based metric learning, particularly when applied to Vision Transformer (ViT)
858   backbones (Ermolov et al., 2022; Patel et al., 2022; Kotovenko et al., 2023). These studies highlight
859   the critical role of data distributions in learning discriminating representations (Wang et al., 2017;
860
      Laradji & Babanezhad, 2020; Ren et al., 2019; 2021; 2024a).
861   In this paper, we extend these principles by examining the distributions and interactions between
862   the prompts and image patches within ViTs. We propose a novel learning framework that utilizes a
863   metric learning objective to guide the configuration and optimization of visual prompts, aiming to
      enhance their performance in discriminative tasks.

867   Benchmarks. FGVC consists of five fine-grained visual classification datasets: CUB-200-2011
868   (Wah et al., 2011), NABirds (Van Horn et al., 2015), Oxford Flowers (Nilsback & Zisserman,
869   2008), Stanford Dogs (Khosla et al., 2011), and Stanford Cars (Gebru et al., 2017). Following the
870   protocol outlined in VPT (Jia et al., 2022), we split each dataset into train (90%) and val (10%)
871   subsets. VTAB-1K comprises 19 diverse visual tasks, categorized into three groups: (i) Natural,
872   which includes natural images captured by standard cameras; (ii) Specialized, consisting of images
873   captured by specialized equipment; and (iii) Structured, which involves tasks requiring structural
874
      understanding. ADE20K is a popular semantic segmentation benchmark containing 150 fine-grained
      semantic concepts, while PASCAL Context provides pixel-wised labels of objects from 60 classes.
875
876   Implementation Details for Classification Tasks For the FGVC datasets, we apply data augmenta-
877   tion by randomly resizing and cropping the images to a resolution of 224 × 224 pixels and applying
878   random horizontal flips. For VTAB-1K, following the protocol in (Zhai et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2022),
879   we directly resize the images to 224 × 224 pixels without applying any additional data augmentation
880
      techniques. We optimize the model using the AdamW optimizer with a mini-batch size of 32 for
      a total of 100 epochs. We employ a linear warm-up strategy for the first 10 epochs and a cosine
881
      learning rate schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016), which gradually decays the learning rate from its
882
      initial value to 1e-8 over the course of training. The initial learning rate and other hyperparameters
883   are determined through cross-validation on the val set for each dataset. For all experiments, we report
884   the average accuracy score on the test set over three runs with different random seeds, following the
885   existing works (Jia et al., 2022; Lian et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023b).

1019
1020     As a work that follows the Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) paradigm, our method has certain
1021     limitations. One challenge is the potential difficulty in searching for the optimal hyperparameters, as
1022     our method introduces additional regularization terms, namely the metric learning losses, which work
1023     in conjunction with the original cross-entropy loss for the downstream tasks. The effectiveness of
1024     these regularization losses is sensitive to the weight ratios β and λ, and their optimal values may vary
1025     depending on the backbone model and the specific downstream task. This sensitivity necessitates a
         careful and potentially time-consuming hyperparameter search to achieve the best performance.
