009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            How a neural network (NN) generalizes to novel situations depends on whether
012            it has learned to select actions heuristically or via a planning process. Guez et al.
013            (2019, “An investigation of model-free planning”) found that a recurrent NN (RNN)
014            trained to play Sokoban appears to plan, with extra computation steps improving
015            the RNN’s success rate. We replicate and expand on their behavioral analysis,
016            finding the RNN learns to give itself extra computation steps in complex situations
017            by “pacing” in cycles. Moreover, we train linear probes that predict the future
018
               actions taken by the network and find that intervening on the hidden state using
               these probes controls the agent’s subsequent actions. Leveraging these insights,
019
               we perform model surgery, enabling the convolutional NN to generalize beyond
020
               its 10 × 10 architectural limit to arbitrarily sized inputs. The resulting model
021            solves challenging, highly off-distribution levels. We open-source our model and
022            code, and believe the neural network’s small size (1.29M parameters) makes it an
023            excellent model organism to deepen our understanding of learned planning.

026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In many tasks, the performance of both humans and some neural networks (NNs) improves with more
029   reasoning: whether by giving a human time to think before making a chess move, or by prompting or
030   training a large language model (LLM) to reason step by step (Kojima et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024).
031
      Among other reasoning capabilities, goal-oriented reasoning is particularly relevant to AI alignment.
032   So-called “mesa-optimizers” – AIs that have learned to pursue goals through internal reasoning
033   (Hubinger et al., 2019) – may internalize goals different from the training objective, leading to
034   harmful misgeneralization (Di Langosco et al., 2022; Shah et al., 2022). Understanding how popular
035   NNs without a special inductive bias learn to plan and represent the planning objective could be key
036   to detect, prevent or correct goal misgeneralization.
037
      Within goal-oriented reasoning, we distinguish between plans and search algorithms. A plan is an
038   internal representation of a sequence of the actions which an agent will take. A search algorithm is an
039   algorithm that considers many plans, evaluates them according to their predicted outcomes, and picks
040   the plan evaluated as best. Search algorithms perform better the more plans they can evaluate and,
041   with enough compute, generalize very well to novel problem instances (Russell & Norvig, 2009).
042
      In this work, we investigate search and planning in a Deep Recurrent ConvLSTM (DRC) that plays
043
      Sokoban, a challenging puzzle game that remains a benchmark for planning algorithms (Peters et al.,
044   2023) and reinforcement learning (Chung et al., 2024). Guez et al. (2019) formulated the DRC, an
045   architecture without any special inductive bias towards planning. Despite that, they argue that it
046   performs search internally, because 1) the DRC performs unusually well in procedural environments,
047   2) it generalizes well from a small variety of levels, and 3) it solves about 5% more levels with extra
048   compute at test time.
049
050   1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
051
052   The main contribution of this work is providing conclusive evidence that the DRC internally represents
053   a plan. Firstly, we find where the plan is represented in the activations using linear probes
      (classifiers trained on NN activations) like concurrent work (Anonymous, 2025). Secondly, we show


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that intervening on the probe logits to change the plan causes the agent to execute the new plan. In
055   other words, the plan representation is causal.
056
057   We provide evidence that the DRC improves its plan with more computation, and that it has learned
058
      to use this capability when it needs to, and not just when forced by no-ops. Firstly, we observe that the
      length of the plan and its accuracy at predicting future actions both increase over time. Secondly, we
059
      observe that the DRC sometimes takes actions which return to previous states of the environment, in
060
      effect wasting time. This overwhelmingly happens at the beginning of episodes and can be substituted
061   by no-ops. Additionally, the average per-step increase in plan length and improvement in plan
062   F1-score is larger during cycles than during non-cycles, even controlling for cycles being at the
063   beginning of episodes. Altogether this suggests that utilizing additional computation to improve the
064   plan (e.g. by creating state cycles) is part of the network’s behavior and not an accident.
065
      Thirdly, we find several pieces of evidence which add weight to the hypothesis that the DRC agent is
066
      performing a search algorithm of some kind. However, we cannot rule out the DRC using heuristics
067   that produce plans and take multiple steps to compute. We find behaviors that are more likely if the
068   DRC is doing search: Giving the RNN time to think with no-ops (Guez et al., 2019) disproportionately
069   helps with levels that are hard (fig. 1, bottom-left) and have longer solutions (fig. 5, right), or that
070   require waiting many steps before the first reward (fig. 5, left). We can use the representations in
071   the DRC’s convolutions to generalize beyond the 10 × 10 grids in the training dataset to larger,
072   more challenging levels (fig. 2, right). This illustrates that the DRC’s representations can generalize
073   out-of-distribution very well and, as Guez et al. (2019) argued, search generalizes very well in
074   procedural environments.
075   Finally, we fully open-source all training and interpretability data, tools, and trained models, unlike
076   previous work. We believe the DRC is an excellent model organism for understanding planning in
077   sophisticated neural networks. Its behavior is complex enough to be interesting and its size (1.29M
078   parameters) is small enough to be tractable to reverse engineer. We hope that future work can use
079   these open-source resources to find conclusive evidence of search and understand how NNs learn to
080   implement it.

      DRC(D, N ) architecture. Most of this paper focuses on the behavior and representations of a
136
      DRC(3, 3) neural network (Guez et al., 2019). The core of this network is a D-layer ConvLSTM
137   (Shi et al., 2015), which is repeatedly applied N times per environment step (fig. 2, left). The output
138   of the last ConvLSTM layer (the Dth layer) is fed to the input at the next tick, effectively giving the
139   network D · N layers of sequential computation to output the next action. We take D = 3, N = 3.
140
      A linear combination of the mean- and max-pooled activations of the ConvLSTM is also fed to the
141
      next step, thus letting it communicate quickly across the receptive field (known as pool-and-inject).
142
      An encoder block consisting of two 4 × 4 convolutions processes the input, which is also fed to each
143   ConvLSTM layer. At the end, an MLP with 256 hidden units transforms the flattened ConvLSTM
144   outputs into the policy (actor) and value function (critic) heads.

147   player’s goal is to push all boxes onto target tiles while navigating walls. We use the Boxoban

150   levels into train, validation and test sets, with three difficulty levels: unfiltered, medium, and hard.
151   Guez et al. (2019) generated these sets by filtering levels that cannot be solved by progressively
152   better-trained DRC networks, so easier sets occasionally contain difficult levels. In this paper, we use
153
      the unfiltered-train (900k levels) set to train networks. To evaluate them, we use the unfiltered-test
      (1k levels)2 , medium-validation (50k levels), and hard (∼3.4k levels) sets, which do not overlap. To
154
      test DRC(3, 3) generalization to different sizes, we use the levels collected by Þorsteinsson (2009)
155
      (see appendix B).

      3     H YPOTHESES AND T OOLS
168
      3.1   T OOLS
169
170   Probes. In the interpretability literature, a probe is a simple (usually linear) model which is trained
171   to predict some label (often called the ‘concept’) based on intermediate activations of a NN (Alain &
172   Bengio, 2016; Belinkov, 2016). If a simple model is able to predict a relatively complex concept, it
173   must mean that those activations represent this concept in a very accessible way. The accuracy of a
174   probe on a dataset is exactly the conditional V-entropy in the theory of usable information (Xu et al.,
175   2020), where V is the model class of the probe.
176   In this paper we train two kinds of probes (fig. 2, left), both in the model class of logistic regression:
177   grid-wise probes (the input are channels in a single location, one probe output per grid position) and
178   global probes (the input is the entire layer’s activations). See section 4.1.
179
180   Checking probe causality. Probes can tell you whether a model is computing some representation,
181   but not whether it is used for further processing. Probes often predict labels which are related to
182   the representations the model uses to compute further steps, but which are not the representations
183   themselves. This is more of a problem the more complex the model class V is.
184   Li et al. (2023) propose a solution to check whether their nonlinear probes are capturing what the
185   model uses in its computation. During a forward pass of the NN, set the activations to a value that
186   would make the output of the probe something different, and check whether the NN behavior changes
187   accordingly.
188
189   3.2   H YPOTHESES : DEFINING PLANNING AND SEARCH
190
191
      Guez et al. (2019) presented behavioral evidence that the DRC internally executes a planning
      algorithm, but they did not make precise what this means exactly. To be concrete, we distinguish
192
      between two types of (related) planning-like algorithms, and give their informal definitions here.
193
194
      Definition 1 (Plan). A plan of a NN is a collection of future actions {at for all t > t0 }, that is
      causally represented in the NN activations zt0 at time t0 .
195
196
      Definition 2 (Causally represented). Given a simple model classes for plans and whether they are
      selected Vp , Vs , a plan is causally represented if
197
198         1. The plan {at }t>t0 can be extracted from the current NN state zt0 using a model v ∈ V.
199
200         2. If this plan is selected, its sequence of actions {at }t>t0 predicts what the NN actually does
201            with high precision and recall.
202         3. Modifying zt0 changes the realized actions of the NN (future at when running the policy) in
203            a manner consistent with the predictive model v.
204   Definition 3 (Search). A search algorithm is an algorithm that, for at least some time steps, includes
205   the following steps: 1) generate several possible plans, 2) evaluate the value of each plan according
206   to an approximation of its consequences (with a model of the world or otherwise), and 3) pick the
207   plan with the best value.
208
209
      An algorithm does not need to evaluate every single intermediate state to be search, nor does its
      evaluation need to be perfect. Many conceivable ways of evaluating plans only use parts of a model
210
      of the world, or use heuristics to evaluate the plans. The important characteristic of search here is the
211
      explicit representation and choice between several possible plans.
212
213   Using these definitions, we can write hypotheses that make increasingly concrete claims.
214   Hypothesis 1 (The DRC(3, 3) has a plan). The actions of the DRC(3, 3) are determined by a plan
215   that is always selected (Vp = logistic regression, Vs = {1}). This plan predicts and causes the
      actual sequence of actions the DRC takes.


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Hypothesis 2 (Plan improves with computation). On average, each iteration of the DRC(3, 3) brings
217   the plan closer to the actual sequence of actions the DRC executes.
218
      Hypothesis 3 (Pacing to improve plans). Sometimes, the DRC(3, 3) takes more steps than would be
219
      optimal to push a box. When that happens, it is usually so that the DRC has computation to come up
220
      with a plan that solves the level, rather than because it is an incompetent agent.
221
222   We generated hypothesis 3 from anecdotal observations of DRC behavior. It makes sense to think
223   before box pushes because they are potentially irreversible, and even if reversible one box push takes
224   4 steps to correct (3 to go around the box and 1 to push it back).
225
      This last hypothesis we do not test for directly. Instead, we provide circumstantial evidence for it in
226
      the same manner as Guez et al. (2019).
227
228
      Hypothesis 4. The NN is doing search: it has several plans, and selects among them based on their
      value.

231   4     T HE DRC(3, 3) CAUSALLY REPRESENTS ITS PLAN
232
233   This section tests hypothesis 1. We present probes which predict the future actions of the DRC(3, 3)
234   and other various future features of the environment. Of these, the one which predicts the future
235   direction boxes will move in has a strong causal effect in the actions of the DRC. Following the
236   standard of evidence in Li et al. (2023), we declare this conclusive evidence that the DRC(3, 3)
237   represents and uses plans. The spatial structure of the probes and some of the targets are due to
238
      Anonymous (2025).
239   By intervening on the probes, we are able to lock the DRC into a plan when there are two options of
240   equal value. However in most cases, if we stop intervening on the RNN activations with a suboptimal
241   plan, the DRC comes up with a better plan online and follows it. We have some evidence for the
242   DRC(3, 3) considering multiple plans: for example, in the level in fig. 1 (right) the box probe first
243   considers moving box 4 down and right (shown in red), but then rethinks that and ends up taking
244
      different actions. However, we have been unable to find multiple simultaneous representations of
      plans, or anything that would decide between them such as the value of the plans. In all, this section
245
      provides very little evidence in favor of search, but conclusive evidence for a causal plan.

      4.1   P ROBE METHODOLOGY: TRAINING , INTERVENTION AND TARGETS
248
249   Training. We train logistic or linear regression probes with L1 decay to predict various features of
250   the environment from the agent’s activations. We train on two types of inputs. 1) Grid-wise inputs
251   (Anonymous, 2025): each square in the 10 × 10 grid is a different data point, potentially with a
252   different label depending on location and timestep. The input is the 64-dimensional LSTM state
253   (h, c) at a square, usually concatenated for the 3 layers. 2) Global inputs: Each time step produces
254   only one data point. The probes take as input the 64 channels of all of the squares in the 10x10
255   grid, resulting in a 6400-dimensional input for each layer. In both cases, the per-layer activations are
256   concatenated for probes that are trained on multiple layers.
257   The dataset consists of states collected by evaluating the DRC(3, 3) on the hard Boxoban levels,
258   excluding the first 5 steps of each episode because the plan is still forming. For multi-class probes,
259   training and F1 scores are computed as one-vs-all: the presence of absence of a particular class is the
260   probe label. We search the best learning rate and L1 decay with grid-search by evaluating the F1 on a
261   validation split of 20% of timesteps from the hard levels. We take the first 1000 medium-validation
262   levels as our test set to report the results (table 1).
263
264   Causal intervention. Given a probe that is predictive of future behavior, we edit the agent’s
265   activations such that the probe predicts the behavior we desire to induce. To the extent that the agent
266
      carries out the edited behavior, we can conclude that it executes a plan encoded in the linear direction
      of the probe.
267
268   For a linear probe on the activations h with parameter vector p, we intervene in order to increase the
269   logits for a particular behavior, given by p · h (we omit a scalar intercept for brevity). Unlike previous
      work in steering vectors for LLMs (Turner et al., 2023; Rimsky et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), we found


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      that simply adding a perturbation equal to a fixed multiple of p caused the policy to degenerate and
271   choose random actions. We instead add an adaptively-scaled multiple of p to the activations, chosen
272   to minimize the change in the activations while still causing the probe’s logit to be greater than a
273   desired amount α. More precisely, we find arg minh′ ∥h′ − h∥22 , with the constraint that h′ · p > α.
274   The solution to this is h′ = h + p̂ max(0, α − (h · p)), where p̂ = p/∥p∥22 .
275
276   Targets. We choose targets that plausibly encode the steps of a plan. Most of these labels are for
277   grid-wise probes, but the pacing and value probes are global. The direction probes are very similar to
278   Anonymous (2025).

                  predicts the corresponding direction that the agent takes from that square. For squares that

363    5         P LAN IMPROVEMENT AND PURPOSEFUL EXTRA COMPUTATION
364
365   This section tests hypotheses 2 and 3: whether the plan improves with computation and whether the
366   DRC purposefully spends time before irreversible actions to improve it.
367
368   Plan improvement. If hypothesis 2 is true, then the ability of probe-extracted plans to predict
369   DRC actions should increase when it is given extra computation with no-ops. Figure 3 (right) shows
370   exactly this effect: the plan starts out very incorrect, and quickly becomes much more reliable.
371   Figure 3 (middle) shows that plans also get more complex: the length of the plans also increases over
372   time, at least until 6 which the probe predicts a direction grows over time.
373
374    5.1        AGENT “ PACES ” TO GET MORE COMPUTATION
375
376   On occasion, the DRC exhibits a curious behavior: the agent “paces” in a cycle, returning to the same
377   location, without touching any box. The per-step penalty makes this behavior naively sub-optimal,
      because it does not advance the puzzle state.


                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Since the DRC(3, 3)’s performance improves with thinking steps, could the agent be using pacing
379   to gain computation time to improve its plan and so avoid irreversible actions which may block the
380   solution? The following evidence suggests the agent has developed this meta-strategy:
381
382   The agent paces mostly at the start of episodes. In some levels, a single sub-optimal step can lock
383   the level, which makes calculating a valid plan in early steps beneficial. Figure 4 (left) shows most
384   cycles start in the first 5 steps of the episode, and (middle) forcing the model to think for six steps
385   eliminates about 75% of these early cycles.
386
387   Thinking steps almost entirely substitute pacing We run the DRC(3, 3) on medium-validation
388   levels and record when and where cycles occur. We merge all cycles which overlap with other cycles,
389   for a total of 13 702 cycles.
390   We then make the DRC take N thinking steps just before it would start a cycle of length N . As shown
391   in fig. 4 (right), these thinking steps replace the cycles: comparing the cycle and the substitution, the
392   DRC follows the same trajectory for at least 60% of the levels and for a minimum of 30 steps4 .
393
      If our hypothesis that cycles are utilized for thinking holds true, we would expect to see uninterrupted
394
      periods of action after each cycle. In the N steps after an N -step cycle concludes, 98.8% of
395   trajectories have no cycles. When substituting them for NOOPs, this becomes 82.4%, lower but still
396   much higher than 0%; indicating that thinking steps largely remove the NN’s perceived need for
397   cycles.
398
399   Plan quality over time. If the DRC uses cycles as a way to improve its plan when needed, we
400   would expect the F1 score of plans to be lower at the beginning of cycles than during non-cycles.
401
      We have seen that there are large effects on the F1 score of the plan from the timestep. We correct for
402   this by pairing each cycle sample with a non-cycle sample as follows: if a cycle starts at time t0 , we
403   sample another level uniformly at random, conditional on t0 not being the start of a cycle. We use the
404   hard level set. Using this protocol, the F1 score at the start of cycles is 51.42%, whereas at the start
405   of non-cycles it is 57.80% (error bars pending but small).
406
      We would also expect the increase in F1 score for every step to be larger in the cycles case. And it is:
407
      the F1 score increases 0.93% ± 0.13% per step in the case of cycles, and 0.45% ± 0.11% in the case
408   of non-cycles. Figure 16 contains the histogram, and we can see that the distributions mostly match
409   except the per-step improvement of cycles is right-skewed. This is some indication that ’cycles’ are a
410   bad proxy for when the DRC is in thinking mode, which also explains the failure of the pacing probe
411   in section 4.1.
412
      Is the higher F1 score for cycles because they are closer to the end-of-episode and have fewer things
413
      to predict? No: we can compare how many squares the plan grows by for every step of cycles and
414   non-cycles. On average, the plan grows by 2.03 squares in the case of cycles, and 1.37 for non-cycles.
415
416   Every test that we conduct here has a small but unmistakable effect in favor of hypothesis 3.

483
484   This section goes beyond the capabilities of DRC(3, 3) as a black-box unit. By looking for inter-
485   pretable features in the activations of the ConvLSTM core, we found that layer 3, at the last tick, has
      four channels which represent the next action to take. Since the network up until the ConvLSTM


                                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      core is completely convolutional, we can evaluate it on inputs of any size – unlike DRC(3, 3) as a
487   whole, which only works on 10 × 10 inputs, and is trained only on levels with four boxes.
488
489
      Spatial aggregation. We start with the Layer 3 grid-wise binary next-action probes for each action
490
      from table 10. For each spatial location, these predict the action the DRC(3, 3) will take in this
491   step. We aggregate the spatial grid-wise predictions with three different methods: mean-pooling,
492   max-pooling, and the proportion of locations with positive probe readouts for a particular action. We
493   learn their relative weights and a bias for each action by optimizing the cross-entropy loss against
494   the actions predicted by the MLP block. We use the Adam optimizer with learning rate ℓ = 10−3 ,
495   annealed linearly to 0 for 10000 steps. Table 5 lists the learned parameters, which place most weight
496   on the mean-pooling aggregation rule. The training set consists of 3000 levels, 1000 from each of the
497   Boxoban unfiltered, medium, and hard training sets.
498
499   Results. The aggregated features obtain 83% and 77.9% accuracy at predicting the MLP’s action
500   output on the training set and the medium-difficulty validation levels, respectively. This is less than
501   expected given the 90%+ F1 scores of individual action probes in table 10.
502   The adjusted ConvLSTM is able to solve many levels that are out-of-distribution: larger than 10 × 10
503   in both dimensions, and with more than four boxes, e.g. those in fig. 2 (right) and fig. 8. Out of
504   our test set collected from Þorsteinsson (2009), it can solve 59/480 levels with both dimensions
505   > 10, 30/203 levels with only one dimension > 10 and 180/482 levels with both dimensions ≤ 10.
506   Appendix B has a breakdown by level collection.

      7   R ELATED WORK
509
510
      Interpreting agents and planning. Several works have attempted to find the mechanism by which
511   a simple neural network does planning in mazes (Mini et al., 2023; Knutson et al., 2024; Brinkmann
512   et al., 2024), gridworlds (Bloom & Colognese, 2023), and graph search (Ivanitskiy et al., 2023). Men
513   et al. (2024) investigates LLM reasoning in a simple block-stacking task. We believe the DRC we
514   present is a clearer example of an agent than what these works focus on, and should be similarly
515   possible to interpret.
516
      Concurrent work (Anonymous, 2025) interprets another DRC Sokoban agent and can also predict
517   future actions. That team invented grid-wise probes and the Agent-Directions and Box-Directions
518   targets.
519
520
      Other works interpret superhuman game-playing agents. Jenner et al. (2024) find evidence of
      lookahead in Leela Chess Zero (Team, 2018), without predicting future actions. McGrath et al.
521
      (2021); Schut et al. (2023) find interpretable concepts in AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2017).
522
523
524
      Probing for world models. Planning usually requires a model of the world. Li et al. (2023); Nanda
      et al. (2023b); Karvonen (2024) examine agents trained to predict possible moves in games, and are
525
      able to probe for the board state. Wijmans et al. (2023) also probe the state of navigation neural
526
      networks. Gurnee & Tegmark (2023) find a representation of positions in Earth for location-associated
527   tokens in an LLM.
528
529
      Goal misgeneralization and mesa-optimizers for alignment. From the alignment perspective, AIs
530   optimizing monomaniacally for a goal have long been a concern (e.g. Yudkowsky, 2006; Omohundro,
531   2008; see the preface of Russell, 2019). In a machine learning paradigm (Hubinger et al., 2019), the
532   goal of the training system is not necessarily optimized; instead, the NN may optimize for a related
533   or different goal (Di Langosco et al., 2022; Shah et al., 2022) or for no goal at all.
534
535   Neural network architectures that reason. Many papers try to enhance NN thinking by altering
536   the training setup or architecture. Schwarzschild et al. (2021b;a); Bansal et al. (2022) improve and
537   evaluate a specific kind of RNN training that produces algorithmic thinking, though Knutson et al.
538   (2024) argue that they do not generalize enough and thus do not implement the correct algorithm.
539   Other works endow RNNs with variable amounts of computation per step, with (Chung et al., 2024)
      or without (Graves, 2016) an explicit world model.

540
      Systematic Generalization. Previous work has identified certain conditions like diverse datapoints
541   and egocentric environments, under which neural networks generalize systematically (Lake & Baroni,
542   2023; Hill et al., 2020; Mutti et al., 2022). Similar interpretability work as ours can be done across
543   many such neural networks to find common planning mechanisms and the conditions in which they
544   emerge.

      8   C ONCLUSION
547
548
      We replicated a small RL-based Sokoban agent, whose performance benefits from increased compute
549   in the form of externally imposed no-op steps (Guez et al., 2019). We find that the agent learns to
550   explicitly exploit this capability by pacing, which substitutes for the external no-op thinking steps.
551   Extra computation helps with longer levels and those that require non-myopic thinking.
552
      We show that the agent causally represents plans (sequences of actions to take). The most fundamental
553
      plan representation we find is the box-direction probes, which can predict and control where the agent
554
      pushes a box. By building up a chain of box probe steps over time, the agent is able to figure out
555   how to place boxes to solve the level. Secondarily, we find agent-direction probes that take lower
556   precedence than the box-direction probes, but can control the agent moves in isolated setups. We
557   additionally find that the box probe chains get longer and longer with more thinking steps (fig. 3).
558
      Finally, we find that extracting a decision from the plan takes place almost entirely within the
559
      ConvLSTM, with the MLP doing little more than decoding it. To demonstrate this, we show that the
560
      agent can generalize to levels bigger than what it had ever seen during training by manually replacing
561   its MLP decoder based on our mechanistic understanding.
562
563   We hope the model organism we trained and open-source will catalyze research into NNs that learn
564
      to plan, and to understand their inner objectives.

805   A    T RAINING THE TEST SUBJECT
806
807   DRC(D, N ) architecture. Guez et al. (2019) introduced the Deep Repeating ConvLSTM (DRC),
808   whose core consists of D convolutional LSTM layers with 32 channels and 3 × 3 filters, each applied
809   N times per time step. Our DRC(3, 3) – or just DRC for brevity – has 1.29M parameters. Before the
      LSTM core, two convolutional layers (without nonlinearity) encode the observation with 4 × 4 filters.


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      The LSTM core uses 3 × 3 convolutional filters, and a nonstandard tanh on the output gate
811   (Jozefowicz et al., 2015). Unlike the original ConvLSTM (Shi et al., 2015), the input to each layer of
812   a DRC consists of several concatenated components:
813
814          • The encoded observation is fed into each layer.
815
             • To allow spatial information to travel fast in the ConvLSTM layers, we apply pool-and-inject
816            by max- and mean-pooling the previous step’s hidden state. We linearly combine these
817            values channel-wise before feeding them as input to the next step.
818
             • To avoid convolution edge effects from disrupting the LSTM dynamics, we feed in a 12 × 12
819
               channel with zeros on the inside and ones on the boundary. Unlike the other inputs, this one
820
               is not zero-padded, maintaining the output size.
821
822
      ResNet architecture. This is a convolutional residual neural network, also from Guez et al. (2019).
823
      It serves as a non-recurrent baseline that can only think during the forward pass (no ability to think
824
      for extra steps) but is nevertheless good at the game. The ResNet consists of 9 blocks, each with
825   4 × 4 convolutional filters. The first two blocks have 32 channels, and the others have 64. Each block
826   consists of a convolution, followed by two (relu, conv) sub-blocks, each of which splits off and is
827   added back to the trunk. The ResNet has 3.07M parameters.
828
829   Value and policy heads. After the convolutions, an affine layer projects the flattened spatial output
830   into 256 hidden units. We then apply a ReLU and two different affine layers: one for the actor (policy)
831   and one for the critic (value function).
832
833   RL training. We train each network for 2.003 billion environment steps5 using IMPALA (Espeholt
834   et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2023). For each training iteration, we collect 20 transitions on 256 actors
835   using the network parameters from the previous iteration, and simultaneously take a gradient step.
836   We use a discount rate of γ = 0.97 and V-trace λ = 0.5. The value and entropy loss coefficients are
837   0.25 and 0.01. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 4 · 10−4 , which linearly anneals to
838   4 · 10−6 at the end of training. We clip the gradient norm to 2.5 · 10−4 . Our hyperparameters are
839
      mostly the same as Guez et al. (2019); see appendix A.1.
840
841   A* solver. We used the A* search algorithm to obtain optimal solutions to each Sokoban puzzle.
842
      The heuristic was the sum of the Manhattan distances of each box to its nearest target. Solving a
      single level on one CPU takes anywhere from a few seconds to 15 minutes.6
843

845   A.1    T RAINING HYPERPARAMETERS
846
      All networks were trained with the same hyperparameters, which were tuned on a combination of the
847
      ResNet and the DRC(3, 3). These are almost exactly the same as Guez et al. (2019), allowing for
848
      taking the mean of the per-step loss instead of the sum.
849
850
      Time limits. During training, we want to prevent strong time correlations between the returns, so
851
      the gradient steps are not correlated over time. For this reason, the time limit for each episode is
852   uniformly random between 91 and 120 time steps.
853
854
      Loss. The value and entropy coefficients are 0.25 and 0.01 respectively. It is very important to not
855
      normalize the advantages for the policy gradient step.
856
857
      Gradient clipping and epsilon The original IMPALA implementation, as well as Huang et al.
858
      (2023), sum the per-step losses. We instead average them for more predictability across batch sizes,
859   so we had to scale down some parameters by a factor of 1/640: Adam ϵ, gradient norm for clipping,
860   and L2 regularization).
861
         5
862      A rounding error caused this to exceed 2B (appendix A.2).
         6

      co/datasets/AlignmentResearch/boxoban-astar-solutions/.

864
      Weight initialization. We initialize the network with the Flax (Heek et al., 2023)   p default: normal
865
      weights truncated at 2 standard deviations and scaled to have standard deviation 1/fan_in. Biases
866
      are initialized to 0. The forget gate of LSTMs has 1 added to it (Jozefowicz et al., 2015). We initialize
867   the value and policy head weights with orthogonal vectors of norm 1. Surprisingly, this makes the
868   variance of these unnormalized residual networks decently close to 1.
869
870   Adam optimizer. As our batch size is medium-sized, we pick β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.99. The
871   denominator epsilon is ϵ = 1.5625 · 10−7 . Learning rate anneals from 4 · 10−4 at the beginning to
872   4 · 10−6 at 2,002,944,000 steps.
873
874   L2 regularization. In the training loss, we regularize the policy logits with L2 regularization with
875   coefficient 1.5625 × 10−6 . We regularize the actor and critic heads’ weights with L2 at coefficient
876   1.5625 × 10−8 . We believe this has essentially no effect, but we left it in to more closely match Guez
877
      et al. (2019).
878
879
      Software. We base our IMPALA implementation on Cleanba (Huang et al., 2023). We implemented
      Sokoban in C++ using Envpool (Weng et al., 2022) for faster training, based on gym-sokoban

      (Schrader, 2018).

      A.2    N UMBER OF TRAINING STEPS
883
884   In the body of the paper we state the networks train for 2.003B steps. The exact number is
885   2 002 944 000 steps. Our code and hyperparameters require that the number of environment steps be
886   divisible by 5 120 = 256 environments × 20 steps collected, because that is the number of steps in
887   one iteration of data collection.
888
      However, 2B is divisible by 5 120, so there is no need to add a remainder. We noticed this mistake
889   once the networks already have trained. It is not worth retraining the networks from scratch to fix this
890   mistake.
891
892
      At some point in development, we settled on 80 025 600 to approximate 80M while being divisible
      by 256 × 20 and 192 × 20. Perhaps due to error, this mutated into 1 001 472 000 as an approximation
893
      to 1B, which directly leads to the number we used.

1128   Probe architecture We train multiple different linear probes on the hidden states h and cell states
1129   c activations of the network. The probes are trained either on the states of the individual layers or
1130   by concatenating the states of all the three layers. We collect the activations of the model by letting
1131   it play through the hard levels. The training set for the probes are constructed by taking a random
1132   sample of cached levels and including all the timesteps in the level excluding the first five timesteps.
1133   We exclude these intial steps as we believe they can be noisy as the network comes up with the stategy
       to solve the levels.

1176   work with as it directly sets the L0 norm of the SAE activations. The hyperparameter search space
1177   for training the SAE is provided in Table 6. We train separate SAEs for each layer with the specified
1178   hyperparameters and pick the one that achieves greater than 90% explained variance while having
