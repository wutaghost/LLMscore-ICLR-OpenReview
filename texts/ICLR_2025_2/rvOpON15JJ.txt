009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Human motion generation is a long-standing problem, and scene-aware motion
012            synthesis has been widely researched recently due to its numerous applications.
013            Prevailing methods rely heavily on paired motion-scene data whose quantity is
014            limited. Meanwhile, it is difficult to generalize to diverse scenes when trained
015            only on a few specific ones. Thus, we propose a unified framework, termed
016
               Diffusion Implicit Policy (DIP), for scene-aware motion synthesis, where paired
               motion-scene data are no longer necessary. In this framework, we disentangle
017
               human-scene interaction from motion synthesis during training and then introduce
018
               an interaction-based implicit policy into motion diffusion during inference. Syn-
019            thesized motion can be derived through iterative diffusion denoising and implicit
020            policy optimization, thus motion naturalness and interaction plausibility can be
021            maintained simultaneously. The proposed implicit policy optimizes the interme-
022            diate noised motion in a GAN Inversion manner to maintain motion continuity and
023            control keyframe poses though the ControlNet branch and motion inpainting. For
024            long-term motion synthesis, we introduce motion blending for stable transitions
025            between multiple sub-tasks, where motions are fused in rotation power space and
026            translation linear space. The proposed method is evaluated on synthesized scenes
027
               with ShapeNet furniture, and real scenes from PROX and Replica. Results show
               that our framework presents better motion naturalness and interaction plausibility
028
               than cutting-edge methods. This also indicates the feasibility of utilizing the DIP
029
               for motion synthesis in more general tasks and versatile scenes.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Synthesizing human motion in real 3D scenes has attracted significant attention in recent years Cao
035   et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021; 2022a); Zhao et al. (2023), due to its wide applications in scene
036   simulation, digital human animation, and virtual/augmented reality.
037   Thanks to learning-based 3D perception Qi et al. (2017); Zhao et al. (2021), pioneers Cao et al.
038   (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Zhao et al. (2023) have attempted to synthesize motion in scenes with
039   feasible human-scene interaction. However, in almost all previous works, paired motion-scene data
040   are required to learn scene-aware motion policies. The majority of prevailing methods Starke et al.
041   (2019); Zhao et al. (2023) learn Explicit Policies to directly predict the desired motion based on
042   current states and goals (Fig. 1 (a)). Some of them Wang et al. (2021; 2022a) utilized a second-stage
043   Implicit Policy optimization but sacrifice the motion naturalness for interaction plausibility (Fig. 1
044
      (b)). Recent works Huang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) utilized conditional Diffusion Policies to
      achieve better performance (Fig. 1 (c)), where massive paired motion-scene data is also necessary.
045
046   In fact, captured human motion data Mahmood et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2023) is far more abundant
047   than paired motion-scene data Hassan et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2022b). Motion synthesis that relies
048   heavily on paired data will inevitably suffer from limited diversity. Meanwhile, the generalization
049   ability is hard to guarantee when trained on limited scenes and applied to various other scenes.
050   Based on this observation, we propose a unified framework, termed Diffusion Implicit Policy (DIP)
051   (Fig. 1 (d)), which disentangles human-scene interaction from motion synthesis during training and
052   then integrate motion denoising with implicit policy optimization during inference. In this way,
053   paired motion-scene data is no longer necessary for training, and motion naturalness and interaction
      plausiblity can be ensured simultaneously for scene-aware motion synthesis.

      To synthesize reasonable motion in 3D scenes, we first train a motion diffusion model conditioned
081
      on actions and keyframe joints, which can be derived from motion itself. Furthermore, we design
082
      various reward functions to score motion naturalness and interaction plausibility. These rewards
083   will optimize the sample distribution during motion denoising. We choose to adjust the centroid
084   of the distribution in a GAN inversion manner, applying these reward functions to the outputs of
085   the diffusion model at the centroid rather than directly to the centroid itself. In this way, the pro-
086   posed method can identify a better intermediate noised motion with higher motion naturalness and
087   interaction plausibility in the final synthesized motions.
088
      In addition, for long-term motion synthesis involving multiple tasks, we need to take historical
089   motion as constrain when synthesizing future motion. To maintain continuity between historical and
090   future motions, we employ a time-variant motion blending, where we interpolate the rotation matrix
091   in the power space and the translation in standard linear space. Thus far, the proposed framework
092   can synthesize long-term motion in general scenes without any training on paired motion-scene data.
093
      For performance evaluation, we use scenes cluttered with furniture from ShapeNet Chang et al.
094
      (2015) to assess the ability on human-object interaction. We also take PROX Hassan et al. (2019)
095   and Replica Straub et al. (2019) to demonstrate the generalization ability in scene-aware motion syn-
096   thesis. We compared the proposed method with prevailing works based on physical and perceptual
097   scores. Comprehensive experiments support our claims and indicate that the synthesized motion
098   produced by the proposed method demonstrates better performance.
099
      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We propose a brand-new framework,
100
      termed Diffusion Implicit Policy, for scene-aware motion synthesis. In this framework, we disen-
101   tangle human-scene interaction from motion synthesis during training and transform scene-aware
102   motion synthesis into a joint optimization problem, where motion naturalness and interaction plau-
103   sibility are ensured by iterative diffusion denoising and implicit policy optimization. (2) We propose
104   to adjust the centroid of the sampling distribution during denoising process in a GAN Inversion
105   manner for higher interaction plausibility, where the motion representation is designed to be fully
106   differentiable with respect to the human mesh and joints. (3) We design to generate new motion
107   based on historical constrains via inpainting and blend the motion in the power space of the rotation
      matrix using time-variant coefficients to synthesize long-term motion for multiple subsequent tasks.

111   Human Scene Interaction. Generating realistic and plausible human-scene interactions has been
112
      widely explored in the artificial intelligence and computer graphics communities Savva et al. (2014;
      2016); Zhang et al. (2020a;b); Zhao et al. (2022). PLACE Zhang et al. (2020a) modeled the prox-
113
      imity based on the distance between the human body and the 3D scene to synthesize reasonable
114
      interactions. An optimization step was taken to adjust pose for plausible interactions under geo-
115   metric constraints. PSI Zhang et al. (2020b) generated human bodies in 3D scenes conditioned on
116   scene semantics and a depth map. Wang et al . Wang et al. (2021) generated a human body with
117   pre-defined translation and orientation based on the scene point cloud. POSA Hassan et al. (2021b)
118   designed a contact feature map for the human body, indicating the contact and semantic information
119   for each vertex in the human mesh. COINS Zhao et al. (2022) utilized a Transformer-based genera-
120   tive network to encode the human body and 3D objects into a shared feature space and synthesizes
121   diverse compositional interactions. Narrator Xuan et al. (2023) exploited the relationship between
122   the 3D scene and the textual description based on a scene graph for interaction generation.
123   Inspired by the optimization stage in static human-scene interaction, we design interaction-based
124   reward functions as an implicit policy for scene-aware motion synthesis.
125
      Motion Synthesis. Motion synthesis is a long-standing problem that has been studied for a sig-
126
      nificant period Clavet et al. (2016); Holden et al. (2017); Starke et al. (2019). This topic has been
127   researched conditioned on various signals, including motion prefixes Mao et al. (2019), actions Guo
128   et al. (2020); Petrovich et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2023), music Gong et al. (2023); Tseng et al. (2022),
129   and text Petrovich et al. (2022); Guo et al. (2022); Tevet et al. (2023). Action2Motion Guo et al.
130   (2020) employed a recurrent conditional VAE for motion creation, where historical data was uti-
131   lized to predict the subsequent pose. ACTOR Petrovich et al. (2021) encoded the entire motion
132   sequence into a latent feature space, significantly reducing the accumulative error in recurrent meth-
133   ods. TEMOS Petrovich et al. (2022) utilized a VAE to learn a shared latent space for motion and
134   textual description. The motion distribution and text distribution were well aligned by minimizing
135
      the KL divergence. T2M Guo et al. (2022) further trained a text-to-length estimator, enabling the
      network to automatically predict the length of the generated motion. MDM Tevet et al. (2023) and
136
      MotionDiffuse Zhang et al. (2024) are the pioneers to leverage diffusion model for human motion
137
      synthesis. Subsequent works Chen et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b); Dai et al.
138   (2024); Zhang et al. (2023c); Karunratanakul et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2024) further improved the
139   controllability and quality of the generated results through database retrieval, spatial control, fine-
140   grained captioning.
141
      Thanks to the advancements in motion synthesis, we follow the MDM Tevet et al. (2023) and extend
142
      it to scene-aware motion synthesis, using interaction-based reward functions as an implicit policy.
143
144   Scene-Aware Motion Synthesis. Synthesizing realistic human motion in various scenes has gar-
145   nered much attention in recent years Zhang et al. (2022); Hassan et al. (2023); Mir et al. (2024).
146   Wang et al . Wang et al. (2021) utilized the PointNet Qi et al. (2017) to extract scene feature and
147
      optimized the entire motion based on the scene after generation. Wang et al . Wang et al. (2022a)
      brought more diversity into scene-aware motion synthesis by introducing three levels of diversity.
148
      SAMP Hassan et al. (2021a) utilized a mixture of expert networks to first predict the action state
149
      and then generate the motion. GAMMA Zhang & Tang (2022) modeled human pose using body
150   markers and learned a latent space for plausible motion, where a policy network was later trained
151   later to give appropriate motion under specific conditions. DIMOS Zhao et al. (2023) further in-
152   troduced human-scene interaction and used PPO Schulman et al. (2017) to learn a policy network
153   over a latent motion space. PAAK Mullen et al. (2023) placed human motion in scenes according
154   to keyframe interactions. SceneDiffuser Huang et al. (2023) proposed a diffusion-based framework
155   where a scene-conditioned diffuser is accompanied by a learning-based optimizer and planner to
156   achieve the goal. LAMA Lee & Joo (2023) introduced a test-time optimization stage for controller
157   network via reinforcement learning to predict the action cues for motion matching Clavet et al.
158
      (2016) and motion modification. AMDM Wang et al. (2024) designed a two-stage framework with
      a scene affordance map as an intermediate representation for final human motion synthesis.
159
160   Compared with these methods, we propose to disentangle scene-aware motion synthesis into mo-
161   tion prior learning via diffusion model and implicit policy learning via interaction-based reward
      functions, and integrate them in a unified framework, termed Diffusion Implicit Policy.

      3.2    OVERVIEW
192   In this paper, we attempt to synthesize human motion in 3D scenes given a sequence of interaction
193   sub-tasks (Fig. 2 (a)).
194
195
      We can first decompose the whole command into a list of sub-task (interaction behavior and object
      pair) to be finished via current LLMs Achiam et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023). For each sub-task,
196
      the human may go to some place or interact with the objects in the scene (e.g. sitting on the chair).
197
198   Given a sub-task, we will first locate the goal position as COINS Zhao et al. (2022). Then, we will
199   judge current action, and fetch feasible human-scene interaction reward functions accordingly.
200   We train a diffusion model conditioned on motion action and keyframe joints to synthesize natural
201   motion with easy control, where body meshes and joints are fully differentiable for Diffusion Im-
202   plicit Policy (Sec. 3.3). Later, we model the interaction-based reward functions (Sec. 3.4) and take
203   them to optimize the sampling distribution of denoised motion at each denoising step (Sec. 3.5). The
204   motion prior from diffusion model and implicit policy from reward function are integrated together
205   to synthesize scene-aware motion with desired interactions (as shown in Fig. 2 (b)).
206   Given historical motion, the synthesized future motion should be in consistent with it. Thus, we
207   design to derive the long-term motion via a time-variant blending (Sec. 3.6) where translation are
208   interpolated linearly and rotation are blended in the matrix power space for motion transition. By
209   now, we can synthesize long-term motion in 3D scenes when all sub-tasks are finished.

363
364   As for the command for multi-task motion synthesis, such as “The person first sits on the bed, then
365   goes to the corner of the room, and finally sits on the chair.”, we need to infer future motion “Sit on
366   the chair” while maintaining continuity with previously synthesized motions “The person first sits
367   on the bed and then goes to the corner of the room”.
368
      For any previous synthesized motion P̃1:S̃ , we will select the latest H = min(S̃, Hmax ) frames as
369   external historical constrains for future motion synthesis. Explicitly, we extract those pelvis joints
370   {J˜s,pelvis }s=S̃−H+1:S̃ as trajectory hints for conditional motion diffusion. In addition, we take the
371
      body skeleton joints from the historical frames to form J = {J˜s }s=S̃−H+1:S̃ , and use them for pose
372
      constraints in the implicit policy optimization.
373
374   After a new round of motion synthesis, we obtain the generated motion {P̂s }s=1:S . For more natural
375   motion transition in the overlapping H frames, we take a time-variant motion blending. Different
376   from direct linear interpolation in the pose representation like priorMDM Shafir et al. (2024). We
377   only utilize linear interpolation for the human translation τ̌1:H . As for human orientation and joints’
      rotations, we take the axis-angle representation θ̄rot,s = θ̃S̃−H+s and θ̂rot,s and convert them to the

      rotation matrix M̄rot,s and M̂rot,s . The blending occur in the power space of rotation matrix which
379   can be formulated as:
380                                                          −1 γ

      where γ = (1 : H)/(H + 1), and θ̌1:H is converted from M̌rot,1:H . Thus, the blended pose take the

      5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   Conclusion. In this paper, we propose a unified framework, termed Diffusion Implicit Policy, for
529   long-term motion synthesis in 3D scenes. In this framework, interaction is disentangled from motion
530   learning during training, and motion prior from diffusion model and implicit policy from interaction-
531
      based reward are integrated together to iteratively optimize the motion from random noise, pursuing
      motion naturalness, diversity and interaction plausibility simultaneously. We utilize joint hints and
532
      inpainting to ensure that keyframe poses remain consistent with the historical motion. We adjust the
533
      sample distribution centroid in a GAN Inversion manner achieve better interaction plausibility while
534   maintaining motion continuity. We introduce motion blending in the power space of the rotation
535   matrix and the linear space of translation with time-variant coefficients to ensure smooth transitions
536   between multiple tasks for long-term motion synthesis. Comprehensive experiments on generated
537   scenes with ShapeNet furniture, and scenes from PROX and Replica indicate the effectiveness and
538   generalization capability. This paper provides a promising solution for synthesizing scene-aware
539   motion without the need for paired motion-scene data during training. It also encourages future
      works to learn from unpaired motion and scene data.

      A.4     D ISCUSSION
852
853   According to the experiments in this paper, we could see entangling motion diffusion model and
854   interaction-based implicit policy makes full utilization of the stochastic procedure in motion denois-
855   ing, and can outperform current explicit policy method even without paired motion-scene data for
856   training. This also indicates the proposed Diffusion Implicit Policy can generalize to diverse scenes
857   as no specific scenes are required for training.
858   Even though, there are still some limitations in current method. As the implicit policy is introduced
859   into motion denoising with limited gradient scale, there is still occasional collision between hu-
860   man and scene. Meanwhile, the motion style cannot be controlled currently, where action may be
861   replaced by command in future work for easier style control.
