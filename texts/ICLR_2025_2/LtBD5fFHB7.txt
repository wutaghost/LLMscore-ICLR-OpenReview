                                                                A BSTRACT
042
043                           In this work, we present a novel approach for motion customization in video gen-
044                           eration, addressing the widespread gap in the exploration of motion representation
045
                              within video generative models. Recognizing the unique challenges posed by the
                              spatiotemporal nature of video, our method introduces Motion Embeddings, a set
046
                              of explicit, temporally coherent embeddings derived from a given video. These
047
                              embeddings are designed to integrate seamlessly with the temporal transformer
048                           modules of video diffusion models, modulating self-attention computations across
049                           frames without compromising spatial integrity. Our approach provides a compact
050                           and efficient solution to motion representation, utilizing two types of embeddings:
051                           a Motion Query-Key Embedding to modulate the temporal attention map and a
052                           Motion Value Embedding to modulate the attention values. Additionally, we in-
053                           troduce an inference strategy that excludes spatial dimensions from the Motion
                              Query-Key Embedding and applies a debias operation to the Motion Value Em-

054
               bedding, both designed to debias appearance and ensure the embeddings focus
055            solely on motion. Our contributions include the introduction of a tailored motion
056            embedding for customization tasks and a demonstration of the practical advan-
057            tages and effectiveness of our method through extensive experiments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
062
063   In recent years, generative models have rapidly evolved, achieving remarkable results across various
064
      domains such as image (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Betker
      et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022) and video (He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2023;
065
      Wang et al., 2023). Within the realm of imagery, customization is a popular topic, empowering
066
      models to learn specific visual concepts from user-provided images at both the object and style
067   levels. These concepts are combined with the model’s extensive prior knowledge to produce diverse
068   and customized outcomes. The success of image customization has led to high expectations for
069   extending such capabilities to video generation models, which are developing rapidly and drawing
070   significant attention.
071
      However, extending these techniques to Text-to-Video (T2V) generation introduces new challenges
072
      due to the spatiotemporal nature of video. Unlike images, videos contain motion in addition to
073   appearance, making it essential to account for both. Current customization methods (Hu et al.,
074   2021; Mou et al., 2023; Sohn et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Zhang & Agrawala, 2023; Gal et al.,
075   2022; Ruiz et al., 2023) primarily focus on appearance customization, neglecting motion, which is
076   critical in video. Motion customization deals with adapting specific movements or animations to
077   different objects or characters, a task complicated by the diverse shapes and dynamic changes over
078   time (Siarohin et al., 2019a;b; Yatim et al., 2023; Jeong et al., 2023). These methods, however, fail to
079   capture the dynamics of motion. For instance, textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022) learns embeddings
080   from images but lacks the ability to capture temporal correlations, which are essential for video
081
      dynamics. Similarly, fine-tuning approaches like DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) and LoRA (Hu
      et al., 2021) struggle to disentangle motion from appearance.
082
083   In this paper, we address the challenge of motion customization, focusing on the critical issue of
084   motion representation. The current state-of-the-art methods face several limitations: 1) Some ap-
085   proaches lack a clear representation of motion, as seen in Yatim et al. (2023), where motion is only
086   indirectly injected through loss construction and optimization at test time, leading to additional com-
087
      putational overhead. 2) Some other methods (Jeong et al., 2023) attempt to parameterize motion as
      a learnable representation, yet they fail to separate these parameters from the generative model. This
088
      coupling compromises the generative model’s diversity after learning. 3) While there are also some
089
      methods that attempt to separate motion representation from the generative model using techniques
090   like low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), such as in Motion Director (Zhao et al., 2023),
091   they lack a well-defined temporal design, limiting their effectiveness in capturing motion dynamics,
092   as evidenced by our experiments.
093
      To address the aforementioned issues, we propose a novel framework for motion customization.
094
      Our method learns explicit, temporally coherent embeddings, termed Motion Embeddings, from
095
      a reference video. These embeddings are integrated into the temporal transformer modules of the
096   video diffusion model, modulating the self-attention across frames.
097
098   We introduce two types of motion embeddings: Motion Query-Key Embedding, which captures
099
      global relationships between frames by influencing the temporal attention map (QK), and Motion
      Value Embedding, which captures spatially varying movements across frames by modulating the
100
      attention value (V). The Motion Query-Key Embedding excludes spatial dimensions (H and W )
101
      to avoid capturing appearance information, as the temporal attention map inherently carries spatial
102   details of objects, which could entangle appearance information of the reference video and thus hin-
103   der motion transfer. While the Spatial-2D Motion Value Embedding may still risk capturing static
104   appearance information, we address this by introducing a debiasing strategy that models frame-to-
105   frame changes, ensuring that the embeddings primarily represent motion dynamics. This approach
106   is conceptually similar to techniques like optical flow, where motion is isolated by tracking changes
107   between frames, helping to prevent overfitting to specific appearance details and improving gener-
      alization to novel content.

112          • We design two approaches to debias appearance for this motion representation: a 1D Mo-

      2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121
      Text-to-Video Generation. Text-to-Video (T2V) generation task aims to synthesize high-quality
122
      video from user-provided text prompts, which are composed of the expected appearances and mo-
123   tions. Previously, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Vondrick et al., 2016; Saito et al.,
124   2017; Pan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; Tian et al., 2021) and Autoregressive Transformers (Yan
125   et al., 2021; Le Moing et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2022) have been widely explored
126   in this area. On the other hand, diffusion models have demonstrated powerful generation capa-
127   bilities in the field of Text-to-Image (T2I) generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021;
128   Ramesh et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022) and have begun to extend to video
129   generation (He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023; He et al., 2022). Recently, sev-
130   eral works have tried to transfer the pretrained T2I diffusion models to T2V generation models
131
      by inserting temporal layers into the image generation networks such as AnimateDiff (Guo et al.,
      2023), and Make-a-Video (Singer et al., 2022). These Text-to-Video (T2V) models approach frame
132
      generation as a series of image creations, integrating a temporal transformer to bolster inter-frame
133
      relationships—a notable deviation from Text-to-Image (T2I) models (He et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
134   2023a; Wang et al., 2023; Singer et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; 2024; Chen et al., 2024; cerspense,
135   2023). Additionally, certain approaches incorporate an extra 3D convolutional layer to enhance tem-
136   poral consistency (cerspense, 2023; Wang et al., 2023). These T2V models are designed for video
137   generation through text inputs and may encounter difficulties when needed to produce videos with
138   customized motions.
139
140   Video Editing. Video editing generates video that adheres to the target prompt as well as preserves
141   the spatial layout and motion of the input video. As the basis of video editing, image editing has
142
      achieved significant progress by manipulating the internal feature representation of prominent T2I
      diffusion models (Cao et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023b; Tu-
143
      manyan et al., 2023; Patashnik et al., 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2022; Qi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023).
144
      MagicEdit (Liew et al., 2023) takes use of SDEdit(Meng et al., 2021) for each video frame to con-
145   duct high-fidelity editing. Tune-A-Video (Wu et al., 2023) finetunes a T2I model on the source video
146   and stylizes the video or replaces object categories via the fine-tuned model. Control-A-Video (Chen
147   et al., 2023b) presents Video-ControlNet, a T2V diffusion model that generates high-quality, consis-
148   tent videos with fine-grained control by incorporating spatial-temporal attention and novel noise ini-
149   tialization for motion coherence. TokenFlow(Geyer et al., 2023) performs frame-consistent editing
150   by the feature replacement between the nearest neighbor of target frames and keyframes. However,
151   these methods fall short as they just duplicate the original motion almost at pixel-level, resulting in
152   failures when being require significant structural deviation from the original video.
153
      Video Motion Customization. Motion customization involves generating a video that maintains
154
      the motion traits from a source video, such as direction, speed, and pose, while transforming the
155
      dynamic object to match a text prompt’s specified visual characteristics. This process is distinct
156   from video editing (Bar-Tal et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2023; Geyer et al., 2023; Liew
157   et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023), which typically transfers motion between similar videos within the same
158   object category. In contrast, motion customization requires transferring motion across diverse object
159   categories, often involving significant shape and deformation changes over time, necessitating a deep
160   understanding of object appearance, dynamics, and scene interaction (Yatim et al., 2023; Jeong
161   et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Ling et al., 2024; Jeong et al., 2024). Diffusion Motion Transfer
      (DMT) (Yatim et al., 2023) injects the motion of the reference video through the guidance of a


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      handcrafted loss during inference, bringing additional computation costs that could not be ignored.
163   Video Motion Customization (VMC) (Jeong et al., 2023) encodes the motion into the parameters of
164   a T2V model. However, finetuning the original T2V model could seriously limit the diversity of the
165   generation model after learning the motion. Motion Director(Zhao et al., 2023) adopts LoRA(Hu
166   et al., 2021) to embed the motion outside the T2V model. Nevertheless, the structure of LoRA
167   limits the scalability and interpretability, as we could not easily integrate several reference motions
168   by these methods. Other works that represent motion using parameterization (Wang et al., 2024; He
169   et al., 2024) or trajectories (Ma et al., 2023a; Yin et al., 2023), but these approaches fall outside the
170   scope of our discussion on reference video-based methods.

172   3     M ETHODOLOGY
173
174   3.1   T EXT- TO -V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODEL
175
176   In video diffusion models, the evolution from Text-to-Image (T2I) to Text-to-Video (T2V) models
177   is marked by the introduction of the temporal transformer module to the basic block. While T2V
178   models utilize spatial convolutional layers and spatial transformers in basic block for integrating
179   image features and textual information (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al.,
180
      2022; Betker et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022) , T2V models build on this by adding the temporal
      transformer (He et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). This module
181
      is key for video generation, enabling the treatment of videos as sequences of batched images. It
182
      specifically handles the inter-frame correlations through a frame-level self-attention mechanism,
183   ensuring the temporal continuity vital for dynamic video content.
184
185   In this module, an input spatiotemporal feature tensor is provided, initially shaped as X ∈
186
      R1×C×N ×H×W , where C, N, H, W represents channels, number of frames, height, and width re-
      spectively. Batch size equals to one, and we omit the batch size dimension in our later notation
187
      for simplicity. This tensor is subsequently transformed into a feature tensor F, with dimensions
188
      F ∈ R(H×W )×N ×C . The temporal attention mechanism (TA) within this module specifically tar-
189
      gets the N dimension, corresponding to frames.
190
191   To facilitate this operation, F is projected through three distinct linear layers to generate the Query
192   (Q = Wq F), Key (K = Wk F), and Value (V = Wv F) matrices, respectively. This setup enables
193
      the execution of self-attention across the frame sequence, encapsulated by the formula:
194

197   where Q, K, and V are the query, key, and value matrices obtained from F, and dk represents the

199   the softmax function. This temporal attention mechanism allows each frame’s updated feature to
200   gather information from other frames, enhancing the inter-frame relationships and capturing the
201   temporal continuity essential for video generation.

282   Figure 3: Debiasing appearance from Motion Embeddings. Left: For the Motion Query-Key
283   Embedding, which influences the attention map, we exclude the spatial dimensions. Including them
284   would cause the attention map between frames to capture the object’s shape (e.g., the shape of
285   the tank in the original video is visible in the attention map). Right: Following the concept of
286   optical flow, we apply a debias operation to the Spatial-2D Motion Value Embedding, removing
287   static appearance and preserving dynamic motion.
288
289   Motion Query-Key Embedding (MQK ) The Motion Query-Key Embedding MQK is designed
290   to influence the attention map within the temporal transformer modules by adjusting the query and
291   key components. By adding MQK to F before the projection to queries and keys via Equation 3,
292   we effectively modify the computation of the attention weights. These attention weights determine
293   how frames attend to each other across time, which are critical in modeling the motion of the target
294   video.
295   Additionally, the shape of mQK i   ∈ R1×N ×C is designed to exclude spatial dimensions (H and W ),
296   which is crucial for removing appearance information. The rationale behind this is that the temporal
297   attention map models the relationships between spatial regions across frames, inherently carrying the
298   appearance information of objects. The temporal attention map has a shape of (H ×W )×N ×N . By
299   examining any one of the attention maps, which has the shape H × W , the object’s shape becomes
300   apparent, as illustrated in Figure 3. If mQK
                                                i  included spatial dimensions, appearance details would
301   be captured in the embedding, limiting the ability to transfer motion to new content.
302
303
      Motion Value Embedding (MV ) As MQK excludes spatial dimensions, it is better suited for
304   representing global motion (e.g., camera motion) but is less effective at capturing local motion (e.g.,
305   instance motion). To address this, we incorporate the Motion Value Embedding MV in our repre-
306   sentation. Specifically, mVi ∈ R(H×W )×N ×C includes spatial dimensions, allowing the embedding
307   to represent motion at each spatial location across time frames. This fine-grained representation
308   is essential for modeling local object movements and detailed motion information, enhancing the
309   realism and coherence of the generated videos.
310
      However, MV may capture static appearance information, leading to overfitting and limiting gen-
311   eralization. To address this, we apply the differencing operation from Equation 5, which isolates
312   dynamic motion by subtracting the motion value embedding of the previous frame from the current
313   one, removing static appearance. This approach, similar to optical flow, ensures MV focuses on
314   motion dynamics, improving generalization to novel text prompts.

      4.4       L IMITATIONS
524
      Our performance relies on the generative priors acquired by the T2V model. Consequently, the
525
      interplay between a specific target object and the motion in the input video may occasionally fall
526
      outside the T2V model’s training distribution. On the other hand, our method may encounter chal-
527   lenges when the input video contains interfering motions from multiple objects, as this can affect the
528   quality of our motion embedding. This is because we learn the overall motion from the entire video
529   rather than focusing on the motion of a specific instance. Addressing this limitation by isolating
530   instance-level motion is a potential area for future improvement.

533   5         C ONCLUSION
534
535   In conclusion, we presented a novel approach to motion customization in video generation, address-
536   ing the challenge of motion representation in generative models. Our Motion Embeddings efficiently
537   capture both global and spatial motion while preserving temporal coherence. Additionally, our infer-
538   ence strategy ensures motion-focused customization by removing appearance influences. Extensive
539   experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, providing a strong foundation for future
      advancements in instance-level motion learning.
