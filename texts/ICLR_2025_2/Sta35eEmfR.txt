009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made significant strides by
012            integrating visual and textual modalities. A critical factor in training MLLMs
013            is the quality of image-text pairs within multimodal pretraining datasets. How-
014            ever, de facto filter-based data quality enhancement paradigms often discard a
015            substantial portion of high-quality image data due to inadequate semantic align-
016            ment between images and texts, leading to inefficiencies in data utilization and
017            scalability. In this paper, we propose the Adaptive Image-Text Quality Enhancer
018
               (AITQE), a model that dynamically assesses and enhances the quality of image-
               text pairs. AITQE employs a text rewriting mechanism for low-quality pairs and
019
               incorporates a negative sample learning strategy to improve evaluative capabilities
020
               by integrating deliberately selected low-quality samples during training. Unlike
021            prior approaches that significantly alter text distributions, our method minimally
022            adjusts text to preserve data volume while enhancing quality. Experimental re-
023            sults demonstrate that AITQE surpasses existing methods on various benchmark,
024            effectively leveraging raw data and scaling efficiently with increasing data vol-
025            umes. We hope our work will inspire future works. The code and the model are
026            available at https://github.com/iclr2025-No9242/AITQE.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in multimodal large language models (MLLMs), which integrate visual and
032   textual modalities, have significantly expanded the capabilities of artificial intelligence (Achiam
033
      et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Lu et al., 2024; Laurençon et al., 2024b;a; McKinzie
      et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024c;b; Zhang et al.,
034
      2023; 2024). These models are typically constructed by combining large language models (LLMs)
035
      with visual encoders like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), DFN (Fang et al., 2023), and SigLIP (Zhai
036   et al., 2023). They leverage multimodal pretraining datasets including image-text pairs, interleaved
037   image-text sequences, detailed captions, and optical character recognition (OCR) data.
038
039
      A crucial component of these datasets is high-quality image-text pairs, where each image is paired
      with a corresponding textual description. Effective semantic alignment between image and text
040
      significantly enhances the model’s ability to integrate multiple modalities. Similar to textual data
041
      collection, raw image-text pairs are typically sourced from the Internet (e.g., SBU (Ordonez et al.,
042   2011), CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018), CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021), LAION (Schuhmann et al.,
043   2021; 2022b)). These raw pairs undergo quality enhancement processes to curate high-quality data,
044   during which a significant proportion of image-text pairs are discarded. For instance, in Qwen-
045   VL (Bai et al., 2023), over 70% of the original image-text pairs are removed. Notably, many high-
046   quality images are excluded solely because their corresponding text lacks semantical alignment (see
047   Figure 1). This filtering approach leads to two drawbacks: (1) the unnecessary loss of a considerable
048   amount of high-quality images, and (2) increased difficulty in scaling pretraining data, as a larger
049   volume of raw data must be collected to achieve the desired quantity of high-quality data. For
050
      example, training on 10 million data pairs may require collecting at least 30 million raw data pairs.
051   Recent studies, like MLM-filter (Wang et al., 2024b), have attempted to filter image-text data by
052   leveraging multimodal language model scoring rather than relying solely on CLIPScore (Radford
053   et al., 2021). However, despite these efforts, existing approaches continue to suffer from the signif-
      icant drawback of discarding a substantial amount of high-quality images. This limitation raises a

      critical question: How can we improve data quality more effectively, ensuring high standards while

069   In this work, we attempt to address this challenge. We demonstrate that current filter-based quality
070   enhancement techniques inherently discard high-quality images due to low-quality or semantically

073
      the loss of valuable images. Conversely, lowering this threshold to include pairs with high-quality
      images but low-quality text can degrade model performance due to the inclusion of semantically
074
      misaligned data. This presents a dilemma in balancing data quality and quantity.
075
076   To address this limitation, we propose the Adaptive Image-Text Quality Enhancer (AITQE), a
077   model designed to dynamically assess and enhance the quality of input image-text pairs. Specifi-
078   cally, for pairs exhibiting low quality-such as low semantic similarity between modalities or subpar
079
      linguistic quality, AITQE performs text rewriting, generating high-quality text based on the input
      image and the raw low-quality text. Additionally, we introduce a contrastive sample learning strat-
080
      egy that deliberately incorporates low-quality image-text pairs during model training to strengthen
081
      the model’s evaluative capabilities. Unlike existing approaches such as ShareGPT4V (Chen et al.,
082   2023), which focus on generating highly detailed captions that significantly alter the text distribu-
083   tion, our rewriting method makes minimal adjustments to improve low-quality or semantically un-
084   related text. This strategy maximizes data volume preservation while enhancing overall data quality.
085   Moreover, our approach facilitates a more efficient exploration of scaling-laws in pretraining data.
086
      Our contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We introduce an image-text pair quality enhancement method that adaptively improves data
089            quality. The proposed Adaptive Image-Text Quality Enhancer (AITQE) is developed and
090            trained to automatically filter or rewrite image-text pairs based on their assessed quality,
091            thereby producing high-quality data pairs.
092          • We propose a contrastive sample learning strategy to strengthen the model’s evaluative
093            capabilities. By incorporating deliberately selected low-quality samples during training,
094            this strategy enhances the model’s ability to discern and effectively improve low-quality
095            image-text pairs.
096          • We demonstrate that our quality enhancement method surpasses existing approaches on
097            benchmark datasets using the same raw data. This shows that AITQE maximally leverages
098            the information inherent in the raw data and validates that our method scales effectively
099            with increasing volumes of raw data.

103   2.1   I MAGE -T EXT DATA F ILTERING
104
105   Image-text data filtering is essential for creating high-quality image-text datasets. Traditional filter-
106   ing techniques predominantly rely on predefined rules, such as language type, length and content
107   of text, image size and content, and the removal of duplicate entries (Byeon et al., 2022; Yu et al.,
      2023a; Schuhmann et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2024a). Some approaches employ single-modality


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      filtering strategies. For example, Xu et al. (2024) analyze CLIP’s data curation process by extracting
109   textual entries to filter the dataset. Similarly, DataComp (Gadre et al., 2023) utilizes ImageNet class
110   names for text filtering and leverages class clustering to select data based on visual content overlap
111   with these classes.
112
      In addition to these methods, CLIP-Score (Radford et al., 2021) has become integral to large-scale
113
      data construction. LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2021) incorporated CLIP to calculate image-text
114
      similarity scores and filter high-quality image-text pairs. Subsequently, many datasets (Schuhmann
115   et al., 2022b) have adopted this approach. DataComp offers a data pool for evaluating various data
116   filtering methods, where several CLIP-based approaches have shown promising performance (Maini
117   et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c). Recently, DFN (Fang et al., 2023) trains a CLIP-like filter using
118   high-quality image-text pairs to enhance filtering quality.
119
      Recent studies, such as MLM-Filter (Wang et al., 2024b), employ instruction-tuned MLLM-based
120
      filters. This method utilizes four distinct scoring metrics generated by the model to filter data in
121   DataComp. In contrast, our approach enables simultaneous output of all metrics, providing a com-
122   prehensive overall evaluation. Furthermore, AITQE does not emphasize filtering data but focuses
123   on enhancing data quality for MLLM training.
124
125   2.2   C APTION - BASED I MAGE -T EXT E NHANCEMENT
126
127   Caption-based image-text enhancement has emerged as a crucial technique for improving the quality
128   of image-text datasets. LaCLIP (Fan et al., 2023) utilizes LLMs to generate new captions; however,
129   it does not incorporate visual information from the images themselves. To overcome this limita-
130   tion, Nguyen et al. (2023) employ captioning models to generate new captions for images, using
131   multimodal models such as BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) and CoCa (Yu et al., 2022). These regenerated
132
      captions are then used to train CLIP models more effectively.
133   Furthermore, many MLLMs possess inherent caption generation capabilities and integrate re-
134   captioning in their training processes. For instance, ShareGPT4V (Chen et al., 2023) utilizes GPT-
135   4V to generate highly detailed captions and subsequently trains a model to produce high-quality
136   captions solely based on image input. However, such models uniformly rewrite all captions without
137   assessing their original quality, which may introduce biases from the detailed captions and signifi-
138
      cantly alter the text distribution. In contrast, our approach automatically evaluates the quality of each
      image-text pair and adaptively decides whether to rewrite the text. AITQE performs minimal adjust-
139
      ments, enhancing low-quality or semantically misaligned captions only when necessary, rather than
140
      indiscriminately rewriting all captions. This strategy preserves the diversity of the original data and
141   mitigates potential biases introduced by extensive rewriting. Additionally, by incorporating both the
142   image and the original caption, our method addresses both visual and textual dimensions, ensuring
143   a more balanced and effective enhancement of the dataset.
144
      Regarding the integration of original and synthetic captions, VeCLIP (Lai et al., 2024) and CapsFu-
145
      sion (Yu et al., 2023b) utilize LLMs to merge raw captions with synthetic ones, producing refined
146
      captions. However, these methods require a two-stage process: first generating captions and then in-
147   tegrating them with the original ones. This approach not only involves multiple stages but also risks
148   being influenced by low-quality original captions. In contrast, our method adopts an end-to-end
149   framework that evaluates the quality of original captions and performs adaptive rewriting, making
150   minimal adjustments. This strategy reduces the potential adverse effects of poor-quality original
151   captions and provides a more streamlined and effective solution for enhancing image-text datasets.

223   3.2   D ETAILS OF AITQE MODEL
224
225   AITQE model structure The AITQE model is composed of SigLIP as the vision encoder, Qwen-
226   2-7B as the language model, and an MLP to connect the visual input with the language model.
227
      We employ a two-stage training method. In the first stage, the training data consists of a mix of
      558k randomly selected samples from LAION-400M and approximately 690k samples from Caul-
228
      dron (Laurençon et al., 2024), and we train all model parameters. In the second stage, the training
229
      data includes the constructed 520k supervised fine-tuning (SFT) samples mixed with 1.8M samples
230   from Cauldron, and we again train all model parameters.
231
232   Enhance image-text data quality We place the rewritten caption (if available) and the overall
233
      score at the beginning of target output in the training data, followed by an “⟨overall⟩” indicator
      after the overall score. This setup facilitates early stopping during the generation phase, reducing
234
      the required computing resource and time. If a complete set of scores is needed, the model can
235
      output all of them. Thus, when using the model, inputting the image, caption and corresponding
236   instructions will yield the scores, and if the score is low, a rewritten caption will also be provided.

337   in a 2.02 point improvement in the average score of the trained MLLM. This improvement can be
338   primarily attributed to the increased volume of training data. Interestingly, while AITQE-filtering
339
      shows improvements over random selection, this gain (+1.82) is less pronounced compared to the
      256K scenario (+7.16). And it’s important to note that the average score of AITQE under 558k
340
      setting is less than that in 256k (−3.32). We posit that this downgrade is due to that the selection
341
      process prioritizing higher-scoring samples, the 558K dataset inevitably included the highest-score
342   256K samples along with additional lower-score data. Consequently, the overall average quality
343   of the 558K dataset decreased. Compared with MLM-Filter, the average performance of AITQE
344   (61.89) is comparable to the average of MLM-Filter (61.84). Compared with LLaVA-558K, AITQE
345   is also slightly better on average (+0.8). This indicates that AITQE provides a consistent and ef-
346   fective filtering approach. These results indicate that increasing data volume through filtering a
347   fixed pool inevitably incorporates lower-quality samples, potentially offsetting the benefits of larger
348   datasets. This highlights the crucial balance between data quantity and quality in MLLM training,
349   especially with limited data resources.

520   5     L IMITATIONS
521
522   While our study demonstrates the effectiveness of AITQE, two primary limitations warrant consid-
523   eration. First, our model does not incorporate a Chain of Thought (CoT) approach in its evaluation
524
      process (first explanation then score), which could potentially enhance assessment accuracy but at
      the cost of increased computational resources and time in order to get the final score. This presents
525
      a trade-off between efficiency and potential accuracy that merits further investigation. Second, our
526
      reliance on a single data source, although beneficial for controlling experimental variables, may
527   limit the generalizability of our findings. Expanding to diverse data sources could provide a more
528   comprehensive evaluation of AITQE’s performance and improve its robustness across varied do-
529   mains. These limitations offer valuable directions for future research, including exploring efficient
530   CoT integration and extending the study to encompass a wider range of multimodal datasets.

533
534   In conclusion, this work introduces the Adaptive Image-Text Quality Enhancer (AITQE), an ap-

536   By overcoming the limitations of traditional filter-based methods, AITQE maximizes the utiliza-
537   tion of valuable visual information. Our experimental results demonstrate AITQE’s superiority over
538   existing approaches, showcasing its ability to scale effectively with increasing data volumes. This
539   research provides a practical solution to enhance multimodal datasets, potentially leading to more
      capable MLLM in the future.

      A    Q UALITATIVE A NALYSIS ON T EXTCAPS O UTPUTS
757
758
      In Sec. 4.3, we compare AITQE with ShareCaptioner, and MLLM trained with AITQE-enhanced
759
      data shows better results on 6 out of 8 benchmarks except SEED2 and MMEH . In Textcaps, the
760   performance gap is huge, and we managed to find out the reason. Here we present a qualitative
761   analysis of the outputs on Textcaps from MLLMs trained with data produced by ShareCaptioner
762   (denoted as MS ) and AITQE (denoted as MA ).
763
      First, the outputs from MS show clear patterns like starting with ”the image captures...”, ”the image
764
      presents...”, ”the image showcases...”, ”in the center of the image...”, ”in the image...”, followed by
765
      detailed descriptions of the content. These patterns are introduced by the training data generated
766   by ShareCaptioner and are not present in the reference captions in Textcaps’s tests. The outputs
767   from MA do not have such patterns, and it produces more concise captions. Second, the reference
768   captions in Textcaps’s tests are relatively much shorter than those from MS and similar to those
769   from MA . For these reasons, the Textcaps score is low for MS .
770
      It’s important to note that without Textcaps, the average result of MA is 64.06 and that of MS is
771
      63.63, still suggesting a slightly better or at least competitive performance of AITQE compared to
772   ShareCaptioner. This shows the effectiveness of AITQE as an image-text data-quality enhancer.
