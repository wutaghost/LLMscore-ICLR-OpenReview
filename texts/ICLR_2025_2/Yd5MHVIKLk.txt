009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing text-to-image models still struggle to generate images of multiple ob-
012            jects, especially in handling their spatial positions, relative sizes, overlapping, and
013            attribute bindings. To efficiently address these challenges, we develop a training-
014            free Multimodal-LLM agent (MuLan), as a human painter, that can progressively
015            generate multi-object with intricate planning and feedback control. MuLan har-
016            nesses a large language model (LLM) to decompose a prompt to a sequence of
017            sub-tasks, each generating only one object by stable diffusion, conditioned on
018            previously generated objects. Unlike existing LLM-grounded methods, MuLan
019
               only produces a high-level plan at the beginning while the exact size and loca-
               tion of each object are determined upon each sub-task by an LLM and attention
020
               guidance. Moreover, MuLan adopts a vision-language model (VLM) to provide
021
               feedback to the image generated in each sub-task and control the diffusion model
022            to re-generate the image if it violates the original prompt. Hence, each model in
023            every step of MuLan only needs to address an easy sub-task it is specialized for.
024            The multi-step process also allows human users to monitor the generation process
025            and make preferred changes at any intermediate step via text prompts, thereby
026            improving the human-AI collaboration experience. We collect 200 prompts con-
027            taining multi-objects with spatial relationships and attribute bindings from dif-
028            ferent benchmarks to evaluate MuLan. The results demonstrate the superiority
029            of MuLan in generating multiple objects over baselines and its creativity when
030
               collaborating with human users.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have shown
036   growing potential in generative AI tasks, especially in creating diverse and high-quality images
037   with text prompts (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022). However, current state-of-the-art
038   text-to-image (T2I) models such as Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) and DALL-E 3 (Betker
039
      et al., 2023) still struggle to deal with complicated prompts involving multiple objects and lack
      precise control of their spatial relations, potential occlusions, relative sizes, etc. As shown in
040
      Figure 2, to generate a sketch of “The orange pumpkin is on the right side of the black door”, even
041
      the SOTA open-source T2I model, Stable Diffusion XL (Podell et al., 2023), still generates wrong
042   attribute-binding as well as incorrect spatial positions of several objects.
043
044   Among works that aim to improve the controllability of T2I models on complicated prompts, a
045
      recent promising line of research seeks to utilize large language models (LLMs), e.g., ChatGPT,
      GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), to guide the generation process (Lian et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023).
046
      Specifically, an LLM is prompted to generate a layout for the given prompt, i.e., a bounding box
047
      for each object in the image, given detailed instructions or demonstrations if necessary. However,
048   due to the limited spatial reasoning capability of LLMs as well as their lack of alignment with the
049   diffusion models, it is still challenging for LLMs to directly generate a complete and precise layout
050   for multiple objects. Without a feedback loop interacting with the generative process, the layout’s
051   possible mistakes cannot be effectively detected and corrected. Moreover, the layout is often
052   applied as an extra condition in addition to the original prompt (e.g., bounding boxes combined
053   with GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023)), so the diffusion models may still generate an incorrect image due
      to its misunderstanding of the complicated prompt.

      To address the limitations and challenges of previous methods, we develop a training-free and con-
083
      trollable T2I generation paradigm that does not require demonstrations but mainly focuses on im-
084
      proving the tool usage of existing models. Our paradigm is built upon a progressive multi-object
085   generation by a Multimodal-LLM agent (MuLan), which generates only one object per stage, con-
086   ditioned on generated objects in the image and attention masks of the most plausible positions to
087   place the new object. Unlike previous methods that add conditions to each model and make the task
088   even more challenging, MuLan uses an LLM as a planner decomposing the original T2I task into a
089   sequence of easier subtasks. Each subtask generates one single object, which can be easily handled
090   by diffusion models. To be noted, the LLM applied at the beginning of MuLan only focuses on
091   high-level planning rather than a precise layout of bounding boxes, while the exact size and position
092   of each object are determined later in each stage by LLM and attention guidance based on the gen-
093
      erated objects in the image. Hence, we can avoid mistakes in the planning stage and find a better
      placement for each object adaptive to the generated content and adhering to the original prompt. In
094
      addition, MuLan builds a feedback loop monitoring the generation process, which assesses the gen-
095
      erated image per stage using a vision-language model (VLM). When the generated image violates
096   the prompt, the VLM will adjust the diffusion model to re-generate the image so any mistake can be
097   corrected before moving to the next stage. Furthermore, we develop a strategy applied in each stage
098   to handle the overlapping between objects, which is commonly ignored by previous work (Lian
099   et al., 2023).
100
      Therefore, MuLan obtains better controllability of the multi-object composition. An illustration
101
      of the progressive generation process is shown in Figure 1. Note that there is a concurrent work
102   called RPG (Yang et al., 2024) sharing a similar high-level idea (i.e., decomposing the prompt into
103   sub-tasks) with MuLan. However, there still exist substantial differences between ours and RPG.
104   MuLan generates each object conditioned on previously generated objects while RPG generates
105   all objects independently. MuLan does not require any manually designed demonstrations for
106   in-context learning. In addition, as shown in Section 4.1, MuLan can be directly applied to
107   human-agent interaction during generation, which greatly boosts the flexibility and effectiveness of
      the generation. To evaluate MuLan, we curate a dataset of intricate and challenging prompts from

142   2   R ELATED W ORK
143
144   Diffusion models As a new family of generative models, diffusion models have attracting more
145   and more attention due to its powerful creative capability. Text-to-image generation, which aims to
146   generate the high-quality image aligning with given text prompts, is one of the most popular appli-
147   cations (Nichol et al., 2021; Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023). Among
148   different powerful diffusion models, the latent diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2022) has shown
149   amazing capability and has been widely used in practice due to the efficiency and superior perfor-
150
      mance, which is also the backbone of the current SOTA stable diffusion models. Different from
      the typical diffusion models which directly perform the diffusion and denoising process in the pixel
151
      space, the latent diffusion model perform the whole process in the encoded latent space (Rombach
152
      et al., 2022), which can greatly reduce the training and inference time. Recently, empowered by a
153   significantly expanded model capacity, Stable Diffusion XL has demonstrated performance levels
154   approaching commercial application standards (Podell et al., 2023). Detailed background on the
155   procedure of diffusion models is provided in Appendix G.
156
157   Composed generation in diffusion models Although Stable Diffusion model has shown unprece-
158   dented performance on the T2I generation task, it still struggles with text prompts with multi-object,
159   especially when there are several spatial relationships and attribute bindings in the prompts. To
160   achieve more controllable and accurate image compositions, many compositional generation meth-
161   ods have been proposed. StructureDiffusion (Feng et al., 2022) proposed a training-free method to
      parse the input prompt and combine it with the cross-attention to achieve better control over attribute


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      bindings and compositional generation. On the other hand, Promptist (Hao et al., 2022) aimed to
163   train a language model with the objective of optimizing input prompts, rendering them more com-
164   prehensible and facilitative for diffusion models. Recently, Ranni (Feng et al., 2024) finetunes an
165   LLM to generate bounding boxes and colors. Then they use these as conditions to finetune text-
166   to-image models for image generation. In addition, AnyDoor (Chen et al., 2024b) also requires
167   finetuning of diffusion models for better generation. Several works utilize the large language model
168   to directly generate the whole layout for the input prompt with in-context learning, and then generate
169   the image conditioned on the layout (Lian et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). While
170   all the previous take the whole input prompt, we propose to turn the original complicated task into
171
      several easier sub-tasks. A training-free multimodal-LLM agent is utilized to progressively generate
      objects with feedback control so that the whole generation process would be better controlled. Very
172
      recently, a concurrent work RPG (Yang et al., 2024) also proposed to utilize LLM agent to de-
173
      compose the prompt into different subtasks. However, MuLan generates each object step by
174   step and correct mistakes after each step rather than treating all subtasks independently and
175   does not need a well-designed in-context learning demonstrations. We defer a more thorough
176   discussion with RPG (Yang et al., 2024) in Appendix B.

      3     M ULTIMODAL -LLM AGENT (M U L AN )
179
180
      Existing diffusion models often struggle with complicated prompts but can handle simpler ones.
181
      Recent approaches train a model or apply in-context learning given similar examples to produce a
182   detailed layout for the prompt in advance and the diffusion model can generate each part of the layout
183   with a simpler prompt separately. Rather than generating all objects at once or in parallel, MuLan
184   is inspired by many human painters, who start by making a high-level plan, painting objects one
185   after another as planned, and correcting mistakes after each step if needed. Thereby, the constraints
186   between objects can be naturally taken into account.
187
188   3.1   OVERVIEW
189
190   MuLan begins by strategically planning and decomposing an intricate input prompt into a man-
191
      ageable sequence of sub-prompts, each focusing on an easier sub-task generating one single ob-
      ject. MuLan then adopts a progressive strategy that generates one object in each stage conditioned
192
      on previously generated objects using a diffusion model. Simultaneously, a VLM offers insightful
193
      feedback and adaptively adjusts the generation process to guarantee precision in accomplishing each
194   subtask. Compared to previous methods, MuLan is entirely training-free and does not require any
195   in-context examples. As illustrated in Fig. 1, MuLan is composed of three components:
196
197   • Prompt decomposition by LLM planning, which produces a sequence of sub-prompts, each
198     focusing on generating one object in the prompt.
199   • Conditional single-object diffusion with LLM planning and attention guidance, which gener-
200     ates a new object conditioned on the previous step’s image using a stable diffusion model. While
201     a sub-prompt from LLM planning provides text guidance, the object’s size and position are con-
202
        trolled by an attention mask, which guides the object to be correctly positioned and generated.
203   • Feedback control by interacting with VLM, which inspects the image generated per stage and
204     adjusts hyperparameters and attention guidance to re-generate the image if it violates the original
205     prompt.
206
207   3.2   P ROMPT D ECOMPOSITION BY LLM P LANNING
208
      Given a complex prompt p, MuLan first uses an LLM to automatically decompose p into N object-
209
      wise sub-prompts p1:N . During decompostion, MuLan specifically asks the LLM to produce a
210   sequence of objects that will be created in the default order from left to right and bottom to top in
211   the image. The LLM can easily finish this task by leveraging its prior knowledge to fill all objects
212   of p to an empty list of the pre-defined order without in-context learning which requires manually
213   designed examples. Let objs = {obj1 , · · · , objn , · · · , objN } be the LLM-planned N objects
214   extracted from p. For the first object, the sub-prompt is simply p1 =“{obj1 }”. For object-n with
215   n > 1, the subtask is to generate object-n conditioned on previous objects and the textual sub-
      prompt is defined as pn =“{objn } and {objn−1 }”. MuLan conducts the above global planning

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      by an LLM at the very beginning before generating any image. The detailed prompts and template
217   for LLM planning can be found in Appendix I.
218
219   When generating each object in Section 3.3, we will use the LLM again as a local planner of the
220
      object’s position and size, i.e., by generating a mask in the image and coordinating its overlap with
      previous objects. Then a diffusion model is used to generate the object under the attention guidance
221
      of the mask. These will be further elaborated in Section 3.3.

      3.4   I NTERACTION WITH VLM AND H UMAN U SERS DURING G ENERATION
300
301   To correct the possible mistakes made in the sequential generation process, MuLan builds an adap-
302   tive feedback-loop control by interacting with a vision-language model (VLM). After each gener-
303   ation stage, MuLan queries the VLM to inspect the generated object(s) and its consistency with
304   the input prompt. If they do not align well, MuLan will adjust the backward guidance of the cur-
305   rent stage to re-generate the object. More specifically, MuLan will modify the hyperparameters of
306   backward guidance to control the strength of the guidance. We empirically found that the errors
307   are typically the size or the position of the generated object. For example, the object may be too
308   large and outside the rough mask. Hence the guidance strength needs to be larger to make the ob-
309
      ject smaller. In the whole generation process, if MuLan needs to regenerate an object, it will try
      different guidance strength, i.e., the weight of the gradient of the energy function (Eq. 1), and the
310
      loss threshold that is used for stopping criteria of guidance. In cases with incorrect positions, it
311
      will also re-plan the spatial location and regenerate the object. Such a close-loop control involves
312   LLM, diffusion, and VLM and significantly automates the T2I generation for complicated prompts,
313   leading to a more accurate generation in practice.
314
315
      In addition, the multi-step process naturally allows human-agent interaction/collaboration during
      generation in practice. Users can timely monitor the generation process. In this way, the interaction
316
      enables users to make preferred changes and adjustments to the generated images easily and effec-
317
      tively by providing adjusting prompts to MuLan at any intermediate step, such as attribute adjust-
318   ment, object adjustment, and spatial relationship adjustment. With the adjusting prompts, MuLan
319   will utilize the LLM to modify the original prompt accordingly and change the generation process to
320   the preferred one. An illustration for different changes or adjustments during generation is shown in
321   Figure 4, which indicates MuLan can achieve both simple and composed complex adjustments with
322   interaction. In contrast, for other existing generation and editing methods, users have to wait until
323   the whole generation process is finished. Therefore, the proposed framework is more user-friendly
      and flexible in terms of human-agent interaction and collaboration.

346   4   E XPERIMENTS
347
348   Dataset To evaluate our framework, we construct a prompt dataset from different benchmarks.
349
      Specifically, since our focus is to achieve better generation for complex prompts containing multi-
      objects with both spatial relationships and attribute bindings, we first collect all complex spatial
350
      prompts from T2I-CompBench (Huang et al., 2023). To make the experiments more comprehen-
351
      sive, we let ChatGPT generate about 400 prompts with different objects, spatial relationships, and
352   attribute bindings so that the prompt sets consists of about 600 prompts. To further evaluate the
353   capability of our framework on extremely complex and hard prompts, we manually add prompts
354   that SDXL fails to generate, leading to a hard prompt dataset containing 200 prompts. Similar to
355   the complex spatial prompts in T2I-CompBench (Huang et al., 2023), each prompt in our curated
356   dataset typically contains two objects with various spatial relationships, with each object containing
357   attribute bindings randomly selected from {color,shape,texture}.
358
359   Models & Baseline As a training-free framework, MuLan can be incorporated into any existing
360   diffusion models. We evaluate two stable diffusion models with our framework, Stable Diffusion
361   v1.4 (Rombach et al., 2022) and the SOTA Stable Diffusion XL (Podell et al., 2023). To verify the
362   superiority of MuLan, we compare it with previous controllable generation methods and general
363
      T2I generation methods. Specifically, we evaluate Structure Diffusion (Feng et al., 2022), Promp-
      tist (Hao et al., 2022), the original Stable Diffusion v1.4, the original SDXL, and the recent SOTA
364
      diffusion model PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2023).
365
366
      Implementation Details MuLan use GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) as the LLM planner, and
367
      LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023) as the VLM checker to provide the feedback. We also conducted
368
      an ablation study to show the importance of the feedback control provided by the VLM and the
369   effect of different VLMs. Moreover, we found the attention blocks utilized during the attention
370   guidance are vital, which can be classified as near-input blocks, near-middle blocks, and near-output
371   blocks. We utilize the near-middle blocks in our main experiments and also show the ablation re-
372   sults of different block. Our codes (including the prompt dataset) are available in the supplementary
373   material. All the experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU.

494   5   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
495
496   In this paper, we propose a training-free multimodal-LLM agent (MuLan) to progressively gener-
497   ate objects contained in the complicated input prompt with closed-loop feedback control, achieving
498
      better and more precise control on the whole generation process. By first decomposing the compli-
      cated prompt into easier sub-tasks, our method takes turns to deal with each object, conditioned on
499
      the previous one. The VLM checker further provides a guarantee with feedback control and adap-
500
      tive adjustment for correct generation at each stage. Moreover, the application to the human-agent
501   interaction further enhances the significance of MuLan, making the generation more flexible and ef-
502   fective to align with the preferences of users. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of
503   MuLan over previous methods, showing the potential of MuLan as a new paradigm of controllable
504   diffusion generation. However, there are still limitations to be further addressed in the future work.
505   Since the whole generation contains multiple stages, depending on the number of objects, it will
506   take a longer time than a one-stage generation approach. On the other hand, MuLan may also fail to
507   generate correct objects in some non-common corner cases of image composition. We defer more
508   detailed discussion and illustrations of the limitations to Appendix N.

662   B    D IFFERENCES BETWEEN M U L AN AND THE CONCURRENT WORK RPG
663
664   As stated in Introduction and Related work, although we acknowledge that our proposed framework
665   shares a similar high-level idea with RPG, we would like to emphasize that there are still substantial
666   differences between ours and RPG.
667   Firstly, our proposed MuLan aims to progressively generate each object given each subprompt. At
668   the same time, the objects are generated conditioned on previously generated objects. In RPG, on the
669   other hand, all objects are generated independently. In addition, different from RPG which requires
670   manually designed in-context examples for the CoT reasoning, ours does not have such requirement.
671   We directly utilize LLMs for the planning during generation, which is an easier task and can be done
672   by LLMs without in-context learning. What’s more, MuLan can adaptively control and correct the
673   generation results using feedback by the VLMs while RPG does not have the feedback for the
674   generation. Also, for the common overlapping problem between objects, we propose a strategy to
675
      generate several candidates to deal with it. In contrast, in RPG, the overlapping parts are treated as
      a whole for generation.
676
677   More importantly, as we show in Section 4.1, our proposed framework can be directly applied to
678   human-agent interaction during generation to facilitate flexible and effective changes/adjustments of
679   the process while RPG cannot achieve the interaction. To summarize, the main differences between
680   MuLan and RPG are as follows:
681
             • Our proposed MuLan generates each object conditioned on previously generated objects
682
               while RPG generates all objects in parallel independently.
683
684
             • MuLan does not require any in-context learning during the whole generation; in RPG,
               specifically designed in-context examples are needed for Chain-of-Thought reasoning.
685
686          • MuLan utilizes the VLM-based feedback control to ensure each object can be generated
687            correctly while RPG does NOT have such a feedback mechanism.
688          • We propose a strategy to deal with overlapping/interaction between objects whereas RPG
689            directly treats overlapping objects as a whole part to generate.
690          • MuLan can be directly applied to human-agent interaction during generation for flexible
691            and various adjustments of the generation process while RPG cannot achieve it.

841
842   Consisting of the diffusion process and the reverse process, diffusion models have shown impressive
843   capability for high-quality image generation by iteratively adding noise and denoising (Ho et al.,
844   2020). Let x0 ∼ q(x0 ) be the true data distribution. Starting from x0 , the diffusion process adds
845   different levels of noise pre-defined by the schedule {βt }T1 , producing x1 , · · · , xT . As T → ∞,
846   xT will become the standard Gaussian distribution N (0, I). Accordingly, the reverse process aims
847   to reverse the above process and reconstruct the true data distribution from p(xT ) = N (0, I) by a
848   parameterized noise model ϵθ (·). With ϵ ∼ N (0, I), the training loss of the model can be simplified
849   as
850                                                     √         √
                               L(θ) = Et,x0 ,ϵ ∥ϵ − ϵθ ( ᾱt x0 + 1 − ᾱt ϵ, t)∥2 .                      (3)

854
      their efficiency and superior performance. Instead of performing diffusion and its reverse process in
      the pixel space, they add noise and denoise in a latent space of z encoded by a pre-trained encoder
855
      E. Thereby, the diffusion process starts from z0 = E(x0 ) and subsequently produces latent states
856
      z1 , · · · , zt , · · · , zT . Accordingly, the training loss becomes

1078   Inference time of MuLan Since MuLan generates objects in a progressive manner, it will take
1079   longer time than one-stage methods. However, there is a tradeoff between accuracy and efficiency.
       Most existing one-stage methods generally fail on the complex prompts we focus on. We aim to

       accurately and precisely control the generation process by the proposed progressive pipeline. To
1081   show the tradeoff more clearly, we conducted experimental comparisons on how the image-prompt
1082   alignment and inference time would vary with the increasing number of objects. As shown in the
1083   visualization results of Figure 13, although the inference time of MuLan increases with more objects,
1084   the image-prompt alignment can be maintained. In one stage methods (e.g., SDXL (Podell et al.,
1085   2023), PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2023)), however, the alignment with prompt becomes worse and worse
1086   with more objects.
