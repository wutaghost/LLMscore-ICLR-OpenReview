009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Large Language Models (LLMs) based on Transformers excel at text processing,
012                but their reliance on prompts for specialized behavior introduces computational
013                overhead. We propose a modification to a Transformer architecture that elimi-
014                nates the need for explicit prompts by learning to encode context into the model’s
015                weights. Our Contextually Guided Transformer (CGT) model maintains a con-
016                textual summary at each sequence position, allowing it to update the weights on
017                the fly based on the preceding context. This approach enables the model to self-
018                specialize, effectively creating a tailored model for processing information follow-
019
                   ing a given prefix. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method on synthetic
                   in-context learning tasks and language modeling benchmarks. Furthermore, we
020
                   introduce techniques for enhancing the interpretability of the learned contextual
021
                   representations, drawing connections to Variational Autoencoders and promoting
022                smoother, more consistent context encoding. This work offers a novel direction
023                for efficient and adaptable language modeling by integrating context directly into
024                the model’s architecture.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
      Transformer models, laying at the foundation of many modern Large Language Models (LLMs), are
029
      exceptionally powerful at understanding and generating text. One of the efficient ways for guiding
030
      and specializing their behavior is through the use of prompts – instructions or examples provided at
031   the beginning of an input sequence. These prompts steer model’s attention guiding its output towards
032   a desired specialized behavior. However, there’s a trade-off. Prompts, especially lengthy or complex
033   ones, increase the amount of data the model has to process during inference running with the same
034   prompt again and again. This additional processing translates into higher computational costs and
035   larger latencies thus motivating an exploration of alternative approaches. An active research direc-
036   tion (Phang et al., 2023) is to adjust the model’s internal parameters θ → θ + δθ(ρ) to replicate
037   the outcome of applying a specific prompt ρ. This approach effectively incorporates the desired
038   behavior directly into the model. Consequently, the prompt becomes unnecessary during inference,
039
      resulting in faster and more resource-efficient computations. Existing methods for transforming
      prompts into weight updates (Phang et al., 2023) have a couple of common characteristics. First,
040
      they tend to treat the prompt as distinct from the main input, handling it separately. Second, they
041
      often employ a secondary independently-trained model specifically for interpreting the prompt and
042   calculating the appropriate weight update for the primary model responsible for core language tasks.
043   This separation allows for specialized handling of prompts, but can introduce additional complexity.
044
045
      In this paper, we propose a novel Transformer design that we call a Contextually Guided Transformer
      (CGT) that combines both of these components1 into a single model. For any given Transformer
046
      layer `, our model simultaneously performs two key functions: (a) parses the the input sequence,
047
      producing for each token an embedding vector y ` that reflects the contextual information (computed
048   at layer `), and (b) uses this context summary y ` to modulate the computation of subsequent layers
049   (beyond layer `) by effectively generating the weights for these layers. Unlike other approaches,
050   CGT model maintains a summary of the preceding context at each position within the sequence.
051   We train the model to isolate a sequence prefix of any length and compute its corresponding context
052   embedding y ` . We then freeze this embedding y ` , along with the generated weights of layers beyond

      We believe that CGT models can be particularly useful for scenarios where adapting the model’s
076   behavior based on an initial context is crucial for effective processing of the subsequent information.
077   We empirically study this technique in three setups: (a) linear regression setup, (b) synthetic in-
078   context learning setup, where the model is presented with multiple demonstrations of an arithmetic
079   task with hidden parameters and (c) text datasets including c4 and wikipedia. In all of these
080   examples, we show that the CGT model maps any given prefix into a specialized model that performs
081   well on the remainder of the sequence. For example, in the in-context learning task, we can convert
082   multiple examples of a task presented in-context into a specialized model capable of solving the
083   corresponding task for new inputs.
084   Our second contribution is a set of techniques for improving interpretability of the context summary
085   y ` . Studying y ` empirically, we observe that it contains information about the prefix it summarizes,
086   but the context summary encoding is not changing gradually from one token to the next, which
087   makes it difficult to interpret it as a consistent context representation. This motivated us to propose a
088   number of techniques that are aimed at improving the properties of y ` endowing it with the desired
089   smoothness prior. Starting with a more grounded approach based on interpreting a Transformer
090
      as a Variational Autoencoder, we then derive a simpler regularization scheme that improves the
      properties of the learned representation y ` while also having a positive impact on the performance
091
      of the underlying model. We believe that these developed techniques could be useful in a more
092
      general setting of sequence representation learning. Equipped with the knowledge of the properties
093   of the underlying semantic representation (such as it’s characteristic time scale), one could use our
094   proposed VAE model to discover representations adhering to this “smoothness prior”.

      2 R ELATED W ORK
098   Learning representations with various scales. Publications (Xu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2022;
099   Rao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023) have explored techniques for assessing the significance of indi-
100
      vidual tokens with varying levels of detail, aiming to reduce computational overhead. Specifically,
      (Xu et al., 2022) shares a conceptual similarity with our approach, which involves applying distinct
101
      update mechanisms to tokens based on their importance. While their approach distinguishes be-
102
      tween informative and placeholder tokens, ours divides embedding dimensions into two segments,
103   each tasked with capturing either local or global context. Also, while (Schmidhuber, 1992) employs
104   a dual-network architecture to address temporal dependencies in sequential data, where the first net-
105   work dynamically adjusts weights for the second to adapt to temporal patterns, and (Mujika et al.,
106   2017) proposes a recurrent neural network (RNN) with heterogeneous cell types to capture both
107   long-term and short-term dependencies, neither approach facilitates on-the-fly weight updates based
      on contextual information.

      Transformer + VAE. Integrating Transformer and Variational Autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma &
109   Welling, 2014) has been a subject of numerous endeavors. (Casale et al., 2018) employs Gaussian
110   processes as priors for the latent space, enabling the model to capture intricate data dependencies.
111   Addressing the issue of controllability in narrative generation, (Wang & Wan, 2019; Fang et al.,
112   2021) develop a conditional VAE framework. (Henderson & Fehr, 2023) introduces a model that
113   incorporates nonparametric variational methods to enhance the information bottleneck in Trans-
114   formers, leading to better capture of latent representations and improved efficiency across various
115   natural language processing tasks. Similarly to the previous work, our approach proposes a VAE-
116   based method with a meticulously designed regularizer, enabling more flexible control over the
117
      representations, ensuring they evolve slowly.
118
      In-context learning. In-context learning has garnered significant attention among researchers,
119
      particularly with the rise of large language models, owing to its adaptability to unforeseen tasks
120   (Brown et al., 2020). Several studies (Von Oswald et al., 2023; von Oswald et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
121   2022; Min et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022) have examined the mechanics of in-context learning to
122   grasp its functionality and rationale. Especially, (Hendel et al., 2023) argues that LLMs learn to
123   represent tasks as vectors in their activation space. When presented with in-context examples, the
124   model constructs a task vector that guides its predictions for new inputs. This work highlights the
125   role of representation learning in ICL and suggests that LLMs can effectively capture task-specific
126   information from few-shot examples. However, it is limited to relatively simple tasks like word map-
127   pings and further does not adjust the weights, limiting its adaptability to complex tasks. In contrast,
128
      our proposed CGT effectively extracts global context from prompts and generates task-specialized
      models through weight modulations.

132   In this section, we detail two core components of our method: (a) our model that simultaneously
133   summarizes the context at layer ` and uses it to generate weights for layers above ` and (b) techniques
134   for enforcing a smoothness prior on the context representation. The outline can be summarized as
135   follows:
136
            1. In Section 3.1, we describe the CGT model architecture that simultaneously computes the
137
               context representation y ` and uses it to modulate local computation above layer `.

            2. In Section 3.2, we detail the auxiliary loss Laux that we use for making it possible to freeze
               y ` at any point in the sequence effectively generating a model specialized for the remainder
140
               of that sequence.

      In the following, we consider a modified autoregressive causal Transformer model with L layers,
147
      where each layer is represented by embeddings z ν with ν = 1, . . . , L. The model input is a sequence
148   of n tokens t := {t1 , . . . , tn } with each token taking value in a finite set of all possible tokens T .

150   3.1 M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
151   A central principle of our architecture shown in Figure 1(a) is a carefully designed separation of
152   information flows, enabling efficient computation of the entire model for a given value of y ` (which
153   can be either sequence-dependent or fixed). The model is divided into two stages.
154
      The first stage processes the input sequence and generates a contextual representation y ` , which
155
      summarizes information from all preceding tokens at each position. At each layer ν ≤ ` our model
156   maintains two independent sets of activations xν and y ν produced by two sets of Transformer heads.
157   Crucially, the self-attention and MLP operations within these layers are structured to ensure distinct
158   information pathways: x components are processed based on prior x components alone, while y
159   has a full visibility of both x and y. This separation ensures that x remains independent of y at
160   each layer before `, while the final y ` at layer ` aggregates information from both pathways across
161   all preceding layers. Importantly, this also allows the the value of y ` to be provided externally by
      simply bypassing the y ` pathway, thus reducing computational cost.

163   quent layers ν > `. For each layer ν > `, y ` dynamically generates the weights of linear operators
164   Tκ , where κ = 1, . . . , 2(L − `). These operators are applied before each MLP and self-attention

231   3.3 L EARNING S LOW F EATURES
232
      The interpretation of y ` as the context summary intuitively suggests that y ` should not change dras-
233   tically between consecutive tokens. However, in our experiments with the proposed model and the
234   auxiliary loss, we only witnessed the emergence of smooth y ` in strongly regularized models (see
235   Section 4). More generally, y ` was seen to contain additional irrelevant information and exhibit
236   a non-trivial dependence on the sequence position. Here we propose a set of techniques for en-
237   forcing a “slowness prior” on the evolution of y ` allowing us to obtain more interpretable context
238   representations. Here we assume that y ` viewed as a random variable is a Gaussian process, or in
239   a discretized form, a multivariate Gaussian distribution p0 (y1` , . . . , yn` ) with the mean hys` i = ms
240   and covariance h(ys` − ms )(yt` − mt )i being given by a known kernel Ks,t . If Ks,t decays towards
241   zero with growing |s − t|, it ensures that computed at two nearby points in time, the y ` activations
242
      maintain a certain degree of coherence, but this correlation disappears over time.
243   In the following, we choose an unbiased prior with ms = 0, but the choice of Ks,t depends on
244   the nature of the generative process. For example, if y ` is expected to capture a slowly changing
245   context information with a characteristic temporal scale λ, the covariance Ks,t would only depend
246   on |s − t| and could scale roughly as exp(−|s − t|2 /2λ2 ). However, in in-context learning tasks, y `
247   would be expected to change more rapidly at the beginning of the sequence and saturate after enough
248   examples of the task are presented to the model. The corresponding Ks,t would not generally vanish
249   for large enough s and t. In Appendix C we consider a trivial example of the sequence mean α
250   estimation with a prior α ∼ N (0, 1) given a sequence of observations α +  βs with βs sampled iid
251
      from N (0, 1). The covariance matrix for this problem is given by:
252                                                       2
                                             Ks,t = 1 +     min(s, t).                                 (1)
253                                                      st
254   The important characteristic of this covariance matrix is that Ks,t → 1 when both s and t go to
255   infinity. The constant value of 1 reflects information of the original prior on α and 1/t dependence
256   is due to the estimation error disappearing as more and more examples are shown. A proper choice
257   of the prior kernel Ks,t is thus problem-dependent.

      5                                     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      Learning slow features that carry information about the global context in a sequence is important for
533
      understanding and interpreting data. Here we propose an approach for incentivizing a Transformer
534   model to discover such slow representation within its inner activations. We then modify the model
535   architecture to parameterize local computation by these learned slow features, showing that it is then
536   possible to generate models that are uniquely specialized to a particular local context and no longer
537   need to have direct access to it. While we only consider several simple examples in our experiments
538   (a synthetic few-shot in-context learning task, linear regression and a mixture of texts), we believe
539   that this approach can prove useful for representation learning, model interpretability and generation
      of specialized models.

1284   The effect of varying β in our VAE experiments with the in-context few-shot learning dataset are
1285   shown in Figure 17. We trained multiple models with different values of β and observed that the
1286   model with β = 0.01 and hence virtually non-existent KL divergence term exhibited strong peri-
1287   odicity (on task boundaries), but as we increased β, model activations y ` became smoother (see
1288   Fig. 17(a)). Also, while for smaller β, the model tended to encode some task information in rapidly
1289   changing activation components, this behavior almost vanished at higher values of β and model
1290   activations became a good predictor of the task multipliers a and b. The effect of β on model ac-
1291   curacy was also unsurprising in that strong regularization with higher values of β appeared to hurt
1292
       model performance (see Fig. 17(b)) suggesting that there might be a minor conflict between learn-
       ing maximally useful representations y ` and these representations adhering perfectly to our desired
1293
       prior.
