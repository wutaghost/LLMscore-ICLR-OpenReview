011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series forecasting (TSF) is critical in numerous applications; however, un-
014            like other AI domains where benchmark datasets are meticulously standardized,
015            TSF datasets often suffer from data inconsistencies, missing values, and improper
016            temporal splits. These issues have an impact on model performance and eval-
017            uation. This paper addresses these challenges by proposing inconsistency-free
018            versions of three well-known TSF datasets. Our methodology involves identify-
019
               ing and correcting data inconsistencies using a combination of linear interpola-
               tion and context-aware imputation strategies. Additionally, we introduce a novel
020
               cycle-inclusive data splitting method, which respects the longest cycle in each
021
               dataset, ensuring that models are evaluated over meaningful temporal patterns.
022            Through extensive testing of multiple transformer-based models, we demonstrate
023            that our revised datasets and cycle-inclusive splitting lead to more accurate and
024            interpretable forecasting results, as well as fairer comparison of TSF models. Fi-
025            nally, our findings highlight the need for proper dataset refinement and tailored
026            data splitting strategies in TSF tasks, and pave the way for future work in the
027            development of more robust forecasting benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      Time series forecasting (TSF) is essential for numerous applications across diverse domains, includ-
      ing weather prediction, energy management, and traffic forecasting (Lim & Zohren, 2021). Bench-
034
      mark datasets play a crucial role for evaluating machine learning (ML) models on specific tasks.
035
      Their accuracy heavily depends on the task(s) for which the dataset was designed (Koch et al.,
036   2021) and its quality over both training and evaluation sets. However, time series data, typically
037   collected through monitoring devices or by human input, are prone to errors such as missing values
038   and faulty sensor readings. These issues can significantly distort model performance evaluations,
039   leading to unreliable comparisons across different models.
040
      In other ML fields, significant efforts have been put into designing standardized and cleaned datasets,
041
      exempt of biases (Dixon et al., 2018). These datasets provide a controlled environment where differ-
042   ences in model performance are solely attributed to the models themselves, rather than inconsisten-
043   cies in the data. This practice has been crucial for advancing the field, enabling researchers to make
044   fair comparisons and draw meaningful conclusions (Sim et al., 2003). Unfortunately, the TSF field
045   lacks such standardized datasets, which leads to fragmented and often misleading evaluations of
046   forecasting models. Furthermore, as Liu & Wang (2024) emphasize, the distribution shift between
047   training and testing sets presents additional challenges in TSF.
048   We hypothesize that there is an urgent need for cleaner and standardized TSF datasets to ensure fair
049   benchmarking of models. Errors inherent in current TSF datasets—-such as incorrect temperature
050   conversions, arbitrary imputation of missing data, and improper temporal splits—-obscure the true
051   capabilities of forecasting models. These inconsistencies not only hinder accurate performance
052   assessments but also stall progress in the field. Our research confirms that such errors impact model
053   evaluation, particularly when comparing results (1) before and after data corrections, and (2) without
      or with data splitting strategy revision.

089   2.1   I MPORTANCE OF DATASET IN B ENCHMARKING TASK
090
091   Standardized datasets offer several key advantages: (i) Controlled Environment: Cleaned datasets
092   enable researchers to assess models in a controlled configuration where the effects of preprocessing
093
      are minimized. This is crucial for understanding the inherent strengths and weaknesses of differ-
      ent models. It allows differences in performance to be attributed to the models themselves rather
094
      than data inconsistencies, ensuring consistency across studies and enabling fair comparisons be-
095
      tween different models and approaches (Sun et al., 2022). (ii) Progressive Evaluation: Starting
096   with clean data establishes a reliable baseline for model performance. Researchers can then in-
097   troduce controlled errors or inconsistencies in a structured manner to evaluate model robustness
098   comprehensively. (iii) Real-World Applicability: While real-world data are rarely perfect, insights
099   gained from benchmarking on cleaned datasets can guide the development of more robust mod-
100   els. Moreover, techniques used to clean these datasets can be applied to real-world data, improving
101   their quality and facilitating model performance. (iv) Community Standards: The practice of us-
102   ing standardized datasets promotes reproducibility, transparency, and collaboration within the ML
103   community, advancing the field as a whole (Sim et al., 2003).
104   For example, in computer vision (CV), benchmark datasets are often curated to ensure consistency:
105   samples are free of corrupted or missing pixels, and all images are resized to a fixed dimension (Xiao
106   et al., 2017). Although real-world data may contain imperfections, such as missing pixels or varying
107   image sizes, these cleaned datasets serve an important purpose: establishing a baseline allowing
      researchers to isolate the effects of model design without interference from data inconsistencies.

      Once this baseline is established, controlled errors can be introduced to assess model robustness to
109   missing data, as shown in studies like (Collier et al., 2020).
110
111   Standardized datasets provide a clear foundation for understanding model behavior, comparing re-
112
      sults across studies, and ensuring the robustness of models in real-world applications. While they are
      critical for benchmarking, the focus of research papers remains on model performance, neglecting
113
      potential issues within the datasets themselves. Such issues can significantly influence outcomes,
114
      either positively or negatively (Sambasivan et al., 2021). TSF is no exception. Although initiatives
115   like GluonTS (Alexandrov et al., 2020) or TFB (Qiu et al., 2024) aim to promote fairer benchmark-
116   ing by standardizing experimental settings and pipelines, their effectiveness can be compromised if
117   the underlying datasets contain errors or inconsistencies.

      2.2     M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES DATASETS
121
      MTSF has gained significant attention following the introduction of attention mechanisms (Vaswani
122
      et al., 2017) and their successful applications in both CV and natural language processing (NLP).
123
      Consequently, in this study, we focus on the most cited papers addressing MTSF from 2019 to 2023
124   to identify the most commonly used datasets in the domain.
125
126   LogTrans (Li et al., 2019) was among the first model to leverage the Transformer principle to ad-
127
      dress the computational cost of capturing long-term dependencies in MTSF. However, the model
      that arguably had the greatest impact on the community was Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), which
128
      introduced the probspare attention mechanism, significantly advancing MTSF. The number of pub-
129
      lications addressing MTSF has then increased exponentially, leading to several variants: time se-
130   ries decomposition (Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021)), usage of frequency domain (FEDformer (Zhou
131   et al., 2022)), reversible instance normalization (RevIn (Kim et al., 2022)), cross-dimension de-
132   pendency (Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023)), patch attention and channel-independent structure
133   (PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023)), and recently, inverse architecture (iTransformer Liu et al. (2024)).
134
      The supremacy of Transformer-based model was questioned in 2022 when Linear-based models
135
      demonstrated comparable, and sometimes superior, performance. Zeng et al. (2023) introduced
136   both normalized (NLinear) and time series decomposition (DLinear) versions. Following this, Li
137   et al. (2024) proposed reversible models (RLinear, RMLP, etc.).
138
139
      All these models were benchmarked on well-known and publicly available datasets, which are not
      only used in MTSF tasks but in numerous other studies such as (Liu et al., 2022) and (Wu et al.,
140
      2023). Table 1 in the supplementary material lists the datasets found in these cited papers. However,
141
      despite their widespread use, to the best of our knowledge, these datasets have not been analyzed
142   in depth or described in terms of the different variations that might exist. Detailed and fine-grained
143   analysis of these datasets should enable researchers to better understand their characteristics and
144   design models that are more tailored to the data, ultimately leading to improved MTSF performance.

      2.3     E XISTING P RACTICES
148
      2.3.1    P RE - PROCESSING
149
150   In the reviewed literature, datasets are standardized using StandardScaler(), which removes the
151   mean and scales each feature to unit variance based on the training set. While widely adopted,
152   this practice can lead to problems when datasets include values that significantly deviate from the
153   rest of the data–particularly if these outliers are unintended anomalies. Such deviations can dis-
154   tort the standardization process, resulting in skewed data representation that may negatively affect
155   model performance, especially the process is reversed. Although recent efforts (Alexandrov et al.,
156   2020; Qiu et al., 2024) have provided TSF datasets from various domains, they do not address the
157   inconsistencies inherent in these datasets.
158   Another issue arises when datasets contain excessive missing time steps that are filled with a fixed
159   value within the range of real data. For instance, Alexandrov et al. (2020) in GluonTS describe a
160   method for identifying missing time steps, and suggest filling them with a “desired value”. However,
161   this practice can distort the natural distribution of the dataset, further impacting model accuracy
      during training and evaluation.

      In addition, if there is a substantial distribution shift between the training, validation, and evaluation
163   sets, standardization can exacerbate these discrepancies, leading to unreliable model performance.

      2.3.2    DATA S PLITTING
166
167   In most studies, datasets are commonly split chronologically, either using a fixed ratio (e.g., 7:1:2
168   for training, validation, and evaluation) (Wu et al., 2021) or based on temporal periods (e.g., 15/3/4
169   months) (Zhou et al., 2021). However, such splitting methods can lead to suboptimal results, par-
170   ticularly when datasets exhibit cyclic patterns. Training models on a subset that does not fully
171   represent the entire temporal cycle can compromise their ability to generalize effectively across the
172   full cycle (Botache et al., 2023).
173   Such non-cycle-based splitting approaches may result in models being evaluated on different parts
174   of the cycle, resulting in unfair comparisons and reducing their relevance for real-world forecasting
175   tasks, where capturing the entire temporal cycle is essential for long-term accuracy. While frame-
176   works like GluonTS (Alexandrov et al., 2020) offer flexible options for defining splitting strategies,
177   the impact of these methods on model performance remains underexplored in the literature.

182   3.1     I NCONSISTENCIES IN TIME SERIES FORECASTING DATASETS
183
      Time series data are prone to errors due to various factors such as device failures, recording issues, or
184
      human errors, resulting in inconsistencies like missing values, irregular observations, or data gaps.
185
      While some research has developed models that are robust to such inconsistencies (Chen et al.,
186   2024; Niu et al., 2023; Barazani & Tolpin, 2022), these approaches typically rely on datasets where
187   errors are clearly identified, allowing for accurate assessments of model performance under impaired
188   conditions. Similarly, in research focused on imputing missing data, a ground truth dataset free of
189   errors or inconsistencies is essential for benchmarking the effectiveness of imputation methods.
190
      In TSF, models are not just tasked with predicting future values–they also need to capture complex
191
      patterns such as auto-correlation, trends, and seasonality. As such, datasets with unidentified in-
192   consistencies cannot be used as-is for fair and accurate model evaluations. This is especially true
193   for MTSF, where models must analyze multiple channels and determine interdependencies between
194   them, adding to the complexity of the forecasting task. As a consequence, addressing these incon-
195   sistencies is crucial to ensure that models are evaluated on datasets that accurately represent the
196   underlying phenomena. Proper pre-processing is essential for evaluating models fairly and prevent-
197   ing distortions that can mislead conclusions about their performance.
198
199   3.2     DATA S PLITTING I SSUES IN TIME SERIES FORECASTING
200
201   Time series data often exhibit periodic fluctuations driven by natural cycles–such as seasonal
202   changes due to the Earth’s revolution–or human-made cycles like weekly and monthly patterns.
203   These cyclical variations are fundamental to the data and must be appropriately accounted for in the
204   design of forecasting models and the splitting of datasets.
205   For artificial intelligence (AI) models that follow the typical training-validation-evaluation
206   paradigm, model weights are updated using the training data while the score on the validation set
207   guides hyperparameter optimization. For data exhibiting cyclical patterns, however, if the validation
208   set does not represent all cycles present in the dataset, the model risks being over-optimized for the
209   specific period covered by the validation set. Similarly, if the evaluation set does not encompass all
210   cycles, the reported performance metrics will only reflect the model’s capabilities for the considered
211   period, potentially resulting in misleading conclusions about its overall performance. Compounding
212
      this issue, MTSF studies often use ratio-based chronological splits. This approach exacerbates the
      risk of misrepresenting model performance by disregarding the cyclical nature of the data.
213
214   As a result, cycle-inclusive data-splitting method, ensuring that each split captures the longest
215   present cycle, is needed to ensure that models are evaluated on data that reflects the full range
      of temporal patterns, improving the reliability and generalizability of performance metrics.

      4.1   I NCONSISTENCIES D ETECTION
219
220   Given the diverse nature of datasets used in MTSF–spanning domains such as electricity consump-
221   tion, traffic flow, finance, and health–detecting inconsistencies is a complex and domain-specific
222   challenge. A universal approach to identifying errors across all datasets requires careful design, as
223   certain values may carry different meanings depending on the context. For example, the value “0” is
224   frequently used to replace missing data, but its interpretation varies significantly between datasets.
225   In weather datasets, a humidity value of 0 is physically impossible on Earth and therefore could
226
      likely indicate an error. However, in solar energy datasets, a value of 0 indicates a lack of energy
      generation, typically occurring at night or during severe weather conditions. Such a value should not
227
      immediately be flagged as an error without further investigation. For instance, encountering a zero
228
      value during a period of otherwise high energy generation could indicate a potential error, as sudden
229   changes in weather conditions are uncommon. In such cases, cross-referencing the observation with
230   weather data for that specific time and location, or consulting other external sources, is necessary to
231   validate the observation’s validity.
232
      To address these challenges, we adopted a combination of domain knowledge and visual inspection
233
      to detect inconsistencies. First, we plotted the time series data to visually examine any anomalies,
234
      irregular patterns, or outliers. Additionally, where domain-specific knowledge is readily available,
235   we leveraged it to cross-check known relationships and ensure the recorded data adhered to expected
236   patterns. While this approach relies heavily on expert knowledge and is resource-intensive, it effec-
237   tively demonstrates the existence of inconsistencies in these datasets. The results underscore the
238   need for more sophisticated, and potentially automated, methods in future analyses to ensure that
239   datasets used in MTSF are accurate and reliable for model training and evaluation.
240
241   4.2   DATA C ORRECTION AND I MPUTATION
242
243   To correct the inconsistencies identified in the datasets, we implemented a systematic approach.
244   First, for each type of inconsistency discovered, we added a new column to the dataset to mark
245   time steps with such inconsistencies, before replacing the corresponding values with “NaN”. We
246
      then categorized the inconsistencies into two scenarios: (i) in isolated time steps (i.e., preceding
      and following time steps are consistent) and (ii) in consecutive time steps. For each scenario, we
247
      observed two cases: (a) inconsistencies affecting a subset of the dataset channels and (b) inconsis-
248
      tencies affecting all channels (e.g., missing time steps). In the first scenario (i), we employed linear
249   interpolation to fill in the gaps, this solution is suitable for both cases (a) and (b).
250
251   In the second scenario (ii), we treated case (a) by first using context-aware imputation. For that,
252
      we created a new dataset consisting of all time steps without marked inconsistencies, referred to
      as the “correct data”. For each time step with inconsistencies, we filtered the “correct data” to
253
      include only entries that shared the same time, month and values contained in the channels without
254
      inconsistencies. We then replaced the inconsistent values of the target channels with the average of
255   these filtered values. If no similar data points were available, we resorted to linear interpolation to
256   fill in the remaining inconsistencies. For case (b) of the second scenario (ii), we again used linear
257   interpolation to avoid introducing out-of-context information. Although these correction strategies
258   may not be optimal, they provide a foundational approach to assess the impact of a corrected
259   version of the datasets. Future research can build upon this work by proposing more sophisticated
260   methods for data correction.
261
262   4.3   C YCLE -I NCLUSIVE DATA S PLITTING
263
264   To ensure unbiased model evaluation, the length of the sets after splitting must be at least equal to the
265   cycle length most represented in the dataset, considering all channels. We define the overall longest
266
      cycle (OLC) as a cycle that is shared by a substantial number of the channels under consideration.
      Specifically, if the longest cycle is only observed in one or fewer than ten channels, we do not
267
      classify it as the OLC.
268
269   To determine the OLC, we propose focusing on the frequency analysis of the dataset. By applying
      the fast Fourier transform (FFT), we can transpose the data into the frequency domain. In this


                                                         5

270
      representation, we identify the fundamental frequency for each channel and subsequently determine
271   the corresponding fundamental period. By combining this frequency analysis outputs with domain
272   knowledge, as well as distribution and correlation analyses, when available, we can define the OLC
273   and establish more appropriate lengths for the validation and evaluation sets.
274
      This cycle-inclusive splitting method ensures that models are tested over the most representative and
275
      longest cycle present in the data.

      5.1   W EATHER FROM I NFORMER
287
288   This dataset is a subset of the local climatological data (LCD) dataset, originally comprising weather
289   observations across 20 indicators from various global stations. Specifically, the version referenced
290   by (Zhou et al., 2021) focuses on 12 weather indicators from a single station in the U.S. spanning
291   over a four-year period. Unfortunately, the authors did not disclose the name or ID of this weather
292   station, complicating the assessment of data processing methods applied to generate this dataset.
293
      Inconsistencies: Our detailed analysis (see supplementary material) identified several critical in-
294   consistencies within this dataset. For instance, indicators provided in both Celsius and Fahrenheit
295   should exhibit a linear relationship, yet discrepancies were noted in the wet bulb temperature data.
296   Specifically, for a Fahrenheit value of 32◦ F , the corresponding Celsius values ranged from −9.5◦ C
297   to 9.9◦ C, which is inconsistent. Moreover, our plots of Celsius against Fahrenheit for all tempera-
298   ture indicators revealed instances where both values were equal to zero, suggesting that missing data
299   had been incorrectly replaced with zeros. These errors, present in both the validation and evaluation
300   sets, can hinder the learning process and accurate evaluation of models.
301   Revised versions: To create a more appropriate version for MTSF, we addressed the identified
302   errors. Conversion discrepancies were corrected using the formula TC = (TF − 32) ∗ 5/9, where
303   TC and TF denote temperatures in Celsius and Fahrenheit, respectively. Any time step where both
304   Celsius and Fahrenheit values were zero was considered corrupted. For these instances, any other
305   channels for which the observation was also equal to zero were treated as invalid. The replacement
306   process followed the methodology outlined in Section 4. Our first corrected revision, referred to
307   as “LCDWf 1H 4Y USUNK”, is the same dataset with inconsistencies corrected. However, we
308
      noticed that WetBulbCelsius, the target channel for univariate prediction, is the only temperature
      indicator in this dataset represented as a “float”; the others are integers. Consequently, we propose a
309
      second version, “LCDWi 1H 4Y USUNK”, where the target channel data are rounded to integers.
310
311   Overall longest cycle: Our frequency analysis (detailed in the supplementary material) reveals that,
312   aside from Relative Humidity, Wind Speed and Direction– which exhibit a fundamental period of 24
313   hours–most channels display a fundamental frequency corresponding to 365.25 days. Furthermore,
314
      our data distribution study indicates that the yearly cycle significantly influences the value ranges
      across channels. Specifically, channels with a fundamental period of 365.25 days demonstrate sub-
315
      stantial seasonal fluctuations in data distribution. Additionally, examining the data distribution per
316
      set, based on ratio splitting, further highlights significant distribution shifts. On the contrary, our
317   cycle-inclusive split significantly mitigate this shift between sets.
318
319   These findings are supported by our correlation analysis, which illustrates that correlations between
320
      channels vary markedly by season, particularly between Winter and Summer. Collectively, these
      analyses affirm that the minimum length for validation and evaluation sets should be of one year to
321
      ensure robust model evaluation.
322
323   Experiment setting: We conducted performance tests on both our corrected versions and the origi-
      nal Informer dataset. These tests also used two distinct splitting strategies: the conventional chrono-

367   5.3   ELECTRICITY LOAD DIAGRAMS
368
369   The UCI ELD dataset is one of the most “confusing” for researchers tackling MTSF. Several variants
370   of the dataset have been used in the literature, creating challenges for reproducibility and model
371   comparison. For instance, Li et al. (2019) appear to have used the full dataset available on UCI,
372   named electricity-f, though they did not describe their splitting strategy. Zhou et al. (2021), on
373   the other hand, used a trimmed version of the dataset, both in terms of channels and the covered
374
      period, which they named ECL. In the Informer paper, the dataset was supposedly two years long
      with a 15/3/4 month split, but a closer inspection of their code revealed that they used three years
375
      (2012-2014) with a 7/1/2 ratio split.
376
377   In addition, ECL excluded clients with continuous zero consumption at the beginning of the se-
      lected period, resulting in a dataset with 321 channels. This decision likely aimed to prevent models


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      from misinterpreting prolonged zeros, which likely reflect unrecorded data rather than actual zero
379   consumption. However, this assumption is inferred from our observations, as no explanation was
380   provided by the authors. Wu et al. (2021) also used this reduced version of the dataset with a 7/1/2
381   ratio split, but they shifted the timestamps by more than four years and modified client IDs for
382   unknown reasons, to finally name it Electricity. These uncertainties complicate the task of com-
383   paring models and ensuring the reproducibility of published results, underscoring the need for a
384   standardized benchmark dataset.
385   Inconsistencies: Our analysis of the ECL dataset identified several inconsistencies. Some clients
386   show anomalies or have extended periods of missing data. For instance, “MT 106” shows no
387   consumption between March 18th and May 8th , 2012, while “MT 298” experiences several pro-
388   longed zero-consumption periods and, when active, exhibits nearly constant consumption. Besides,
389   “MT 182” ceases consumption after September 23rd , 2012. These inconsistencies, present in the
390   validation and evaluation sets, could distort model accuracy and introduce bias during MTSF tasks.
391   Revised version: Building on the revisions made to obtain ECL, we propose further refining the
392   dataset by removing clients with prolonged zero-consumption periods and irregular consumption
393   patterns (detailed in the supplementary material). To forecast effectively, models would need exter-
394   nal information about these unusual patterns to differentiate them from normal behavior–information
395   not provided in the dataset. Our revised version, “PELD 1H 3Y 308”, consists of hourly observa-
396   tions over three years, with 308 clients remaining after filtering.
397   Overall longest cycle: Given the large number of channels in this dataset, we conducted a frequency
398   analysis across all channels and studied the data distribution of a random sample. The frequency
399   analysis shows that most channel have a fundamental period of 1 day, very few of them exhibit
400   a fundamental period larger than one year but the other majority have a fundamental period of
401   365.33 days. In addition, the distribution analysis revealed significant seasonal variations in some
402   channels, indicating a yearly dependency. Similarly to the weather datasets, the conventional ratio
403   splitting also reflects this strong seasonality, as evidenced by notable shifts in the evaluation set’s
404   data distribution, while our cycle-inclusive split significantly mitigate data distribution differences
405
      between sets. As a result, we concluded that the minimum duration for validation and evaluation
      sets should be at least one year.
406
407   Experiment setting: We evaluated model performance on both the ECL and our revised PELD
408   dataset using two splitting strategies: the conventional chronological ratio split of 7:1:2, commonly
409   found in the literature, and a chronological cycle-inclusive split of 1/1/1 year. We believe the latter
410   provides a more suitable framework for fair and comprehensive evaluation of MTSF models.

      7   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
502
503   These results highlight the crucial importance of cleaning datasets used for TSF, as inconsistencies
504
      and improper data splitting can hinder both the learning process and the evaluation of models. This
      study contributes to a better understanding of how model performance varies with the nature of
505
      multivariate time series (MTS) datasets, though analysis of other TSF datasets is needed to confirm
506
      these findings. This study opens up to several directions for future research, such as training models
507   to identify and label inconsistencies or paving the way for more sophisticated imputation methods,
508   potentially using large language models (LLMs).
509
510
      However, this study has limitations that need to be addressed. Firstly, we posit that some model ar-
      chitectures may be better suited to specific types of datasets. For instance, Informer excels with MTS
511
      datasets having clear inter-variable relationships but struggles with spatiotemporal MTS datasets.
512
      This observation suggests that Informer, which focuses on temporal tokens, may not be ideal for
513   spatiotemporal datasets, unlike inverse architectures such as iTransformer, which focus on variate
514   tokens. Further investigation could involve creating a cleaned four-year version of ELD (increas-
515   ing training samples but significantly reducing the number of clients) to compare transformer-based
516   models with their inverse versions using different data splitting. Moreover, the imputation quality
517   remains uncertain without ground truth. Future work should include different metrics: 1. for overall
518   performance, 2. for evaluating the performance for prediction horizons involving replaced values,
519   and 3. for evaluating those with only original values. Finally, rather than relying on generic temporal
520   embeddings, future models should be tailored to capture the most important temporal cycles present
521
      in the data. This could significantly improve the performance of models on datasets with strong
      seasonalities or long-term cycles.
522
523
524   8   C ONCLUSION
525
526
      This study underscores the critical importance of redefining and refining multivariate time series
527
      forecasting (MTSF) datasets to ensure accurate and reliable model performance. By systematically
528   addressing inconsistencies and implementing cycle-aware splitting methods, our revised datasets
529   provide a more robust foundation for fair comparison of MTSF models, paving the way for more
530   reliable forecasting outcomes. While our work provides significant insights, it also opens avenues
531   for further research. Expanding the analysis to other datasets will be essential to verify the gener-
532   alizability of our findings. Additionally, the development of more advanced imputation techniques,
533   possibly leveraging the power of large language models (LLMs), offers substantial potential to fur-
534   ther enhance dataset accuracy and quality.
535   Though challenges remain–such as the need for ground truth validation and refinement of temporal
536   embeddings–this work lays a solid foundation for future advances in MTSF. By addressing these
537   challenges head-on, we can significantly improve the reliability of forecasting models, enabling
538   better decision-making across a wide range of industries, from energy management to finance and
539   beyond. Our efforts contribute to establishing stronger benchmarks for TSF tasks and highlight the
      importance of data integrity in advancing the field.
