009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Schema matching – the task of finding matches between attributes across disparate
012            data sources with different tables and hierarchies – is critical for creating interoper-
013            able machine learning (ML)-ready data. Addressing this fundamental data-centric
014            problem has wide implications, especially in domains like healthcare, finance and
015            e-commerce — but also has the potential to benefit ML models more generally, by
016
               increasing the data available for ML model training. However, schema matching is
               a challenging ML task due to structural/hierarchical and semantic heterogeneity
017
               between different schemas. Previous ML approaches to automate schema matching
018
               have either required significant labeled data for model training, which is often
019            unrealistic or suffer from poor zero-shot performance. To this end, we propose
020            Matchmaker - a compositional language model program for schema matching, com-
021            prised of candidate generation, refinement and confidence scoring. Matchmaker
022            also self-improves in a zero-shot manner without the need for labeled demonstra-
023            tions via a novel optimization approach, which constructs synthetic in-context
024            demonstrations to guide the language model’s reasoning process. Empirically, we
025            demonstrate on real-world medical schema matching benchmarks that Matchmaker
026            outperforms previous ML-based approaches, highlighting its potential to accelerate
027
               data integration and interoperability of ML-ready data.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The success of machine learning (ML) models hinges on a critical yet often overlooked challenge:
032   access to large, integrated and interoperable datasets (Jain et al., 2020; Gupta et al., 2021; Renggli
033   et al., 2021; Sambasivan et al., 2021). Although well-structured and uniform datasets like those on
034   Kaggle are commonly assumed as the norm, such data is a rare luxury in practice. In real-world
035   scenarios, tabular data often exists in heterogeneous and disparate databases with diverse formats,
036   schemas, and terminologies, requiring harmonization to make the data "ML-ready" and interoperable.
037   The heterogeneity of databases presents three critical issues for ML: (1) data harmonization and
038   integration is an arduous task. Hence, researchers often limit the features/covariates used for model
039
      training to a smaller, often common, set of features (Avati et al., 2021; Si et al., 2021; Rajkomar et al.,
      2018), thereby limiting the potential performance of their ML models; (2) even if all the features are
040
      used, the lack of data interoperability means limited external validation of ML models (Balch et al.,
041
      2023; Lehne et al., 2019; Williams et al., 2022; Tiwari et al., 2020; Colubri et al., 2019), which can
042   undermine the credibility and utility of the ML models; and (3) missed opportunities for insights
043   on larger harmonized datasets (e.g., larger patient populations), which may not be apparent when
044   analyzing data sources independently.
045
      Schema matching is a critical first step in data harmonization, aiming to establish correspondences
046
      between attributes (i.e., features/covariates) measured across different data sources. Once matched,
047
      these correspondences can help harmonize data from disparate sources into a cohesive, ML-ready
048   format. To understand the concept of schema matching, let us unpack the components of a schema. A
049   schema defines how data is organized in a database, comprising different tables (collections of related
050   data entries) and columns (also known as "attributes" or "features") that represent specific data fields.
051   Importantly, schemas go beyond simple tabular data commonly found in CSV files, as they capture
052   the hierarchical structure and relationships between different tables and their attributes. For example,
053   in healthcare, schemas from different hospitals may have varying tables and attributes representing
      patient information, lab measurements, diagnoses and treatments, with complex relationships and

      semantics and relationships. It goes beyond simple one-to-one column matching, considering not

079
      The importance and value of schema matching cannot be overstated, as integrating data from various
      data sources such as different regions, organizations or applications is vital in healthcare but also in
080
      finance and e-commerce (Sheetrit et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2021; El Haddadi et al., 2024). Schema
081
      matching is also generally valuable to anyone working on ML, as a step toward increasing the training
082   and validation data available to the ML community for model training. e.g, in healthcare, integrating
083   data from multiple hospitals can lead to more comprehensive datasets to train more performant
084   ML prognostic models. Similarly, in e-commerce, combining diverse customer data from various
085   platforms can enable more accurate ML models built on customer data.
086
      Unfortunately, prior ML approaches for "automated" schema matching often require extensive labeled
087
      data (Li et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021), which is often costly and time consuming to acquire, making
088   these methods impractical for real-world use. Although LLM-based methods (Narayan et al., 2022;
089   Mirchandani et al., 2023) have attempted to address this, they have poor zero-shot performance
090   and poor scalability in terms of the number of LLM calls. These limitations have hindered the
091   adoption of ML for schema matching, meaning schema matching is still a largely manual and time-
092   consuming task. To highlight the need for automated and better performing ML schema matching, in
093   the healthcare domain, it took 500 hours for two experts to map the schemas between the MIMIC
094   database and the OMOP common data model (Paris et al., 2021), demonstrating the substantial and
095   non-trivial effort required.
096   Despite the need, schema matching is a challenging ML task, as shown in Fig. 1, as without access to
097   the raw data, schema matching methods must rely only on the attribute names and other metadata
098   to infer correspondences between attributes across schemas. This requires reasoning about various
099   challenges, namely: ▶ Semantic heterogeneity: ambiguous potential mappings, where attributes
100   across schemas might have the same name but different meanings, or different names but the same
101   meaning. ▶ Structural heterogeneity: schemas that have varied architectures, hierarchies, and
102
      representational granularity. ▶ Database heterogeneity: differences in the number and organization
      of tables across schemas. e.g. source schema table information may be represented across multiple
103
      target schema tables. Hence, it is non-trivial to identify the appropriate table for an attribute. ▶
104
      Information mismatch: Information may be contained in one schema, but not in another schema.
105   Hence, reasoning about "no possible match" is as important as reasoning about a possible match.

131   This work engages with literature on schema matching (see Fig. 3) and contributes to data-centric AI.

      Schema matching. Previous ML-based schema matching approaches have shown promise, but suffer
133   from limitations that hinder their practical applicability. Early works (Mudgal et al., 2018; Shraga
134   et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020) computed similarity scores between schemas (Do & Rahm, 2002; Gal,
135   2011), but focused on the simpler entity matching task (matching items within columns) rather than
136   the more complex schema matching problem. Recent methods like SMAT (Zhang et al., 2021)
137   applied deep learning (i.e. attention) to tackle full schema matching. However, they require extensive
138   labeled matches (over 50%), rendering them impractical for real-world environments where labeled
139   data is scarce or expensive to obtain, often requiring domain experts to annotate.
140   To reduce the need for labels, LLMs have been applied to schema matching (Zhang et al., 2023a;
141   Narayan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b). However, methods like LLM-DP using pre-trained
142   LLMs (Zhang et al., 2023a; Narayan et al., 2022) have demonstrated poor zero-shot performance
143   (see Sec. 5). Performance improvements were obtained with human-labeled examples of ±500
144   examples, from which in-context examples are selected. However, reliance on human labeling is often
145   unrealistic, limiting applicability. Interestingly, even LLMs such as Jellyfish (Zhang et al., 2023b),
146   which are fine-tuned for schema matching on task datasets, have shown poor matching performance.
147
      Beyond matching performance, both LLM and supervised methods (e.g. SMAT (Zhang et al., 2021)),
      formulate schema matching as a binary classification task over the full Cartesian product of source
148
      and target schema attributes. e.g. for each pair of source-target attributes, the LLM is prompted to
149
      provide a label of Yes/No for the match (i.e. Is attribute A related to Attribute B? yes/no). The result
150   is poor scalability which is computationally expensive for large schemas and costly due to the large
151   number of LLM calls, hindering real-world applicability. We compare LLM calls in Appendix D.1.
152
153
      The closest work to ours is ReMatch (Sheetrit et al., 2024), which uses retrieval to find semantically
      similar candidate matches, thus reducing the search space. It then prompts an LLM to match a
154
      source schema attribute with retrieved target schema candidates. However, ReMatch relies solely
155
      on semantic matching, which we empirically demonstrate in Sec. 5 does not suffice for real-world
156   schemas. Our approach Matchmaker diverges from ReMatch along three dimensions (see Table
157   1): (1) System: ReMatch uses a single LLM call, while Matchmaker decomposes the task into a
158   multi-stage compositional LLM program with iterative reasoning steps. (2) Candidate generation:
159   ReMatch relies solely on semantic retrieval, while Matchmaker incorporates diverse candidate gener-
160   ation sources, including retrieval for semantic candidates and an LLM-driven contextual reasoning
161   candidates. (3) Optimization: ReMatch has a fixed/static LLM prompt template, while Matchmaker
      is an LLM program where we dynamically optimize the prompts via synthetic in-context examples.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES .
182   Consider the schema matching task, where the goal is to map attributes from a source schema (Ss )
183   to a target schema (St ). Each schema S is defined as a collection of tables T = {T1 , T2 , . . . , Tm }.
184   Each table Ti contains a set of attributes Ai = {Ai1 , Ai2 , . . . , Aik }. Additionally, each table Ti is
185   associated with metadata mi describing the purpose and content of the table. Similarly, each attribute
186   Aij is associated with a description dij , which includes information describing the attribute, its data
187   type and relational context. These descriptions and data types offer key contextual information to aid
188
      in the matching process.
189   The schema matching task, defined below, aims to find matches between attributes across different
190   schemas, accounting for their structural hierarchies, interrelationships and constraints.. Recall that
191   schema matching operates solely on schema-level information (attributes and metadata), without
192   having access to the raw data. This adds to the complexity, as matching must be performed without
193   the benefit of analyzing the actual data values.
194   Definition 1 (Schema Matching). The goal of schema matching is to find a mapping function
195   f : As → At ∪ {∅} that correctly assigns each attribute of the source schema Ss to a corresponding
196   attribute in the target schema St or to the empty set ∅, indicating no possible match.
197
198   3.2   S CHEMA MATCHING AS INFORMATION RETRIEVAL .
199
200   As outlined in Sec. 2, schema matching is often formulated as a supervised binary classification
201
      problem (match/no match) over the entire Cartesian product of source and target schema attributes.
      Beyond the computational side, this formulation has several drawbacks: ▶ Labeling Cost: It requires
202
      manual annotation of attribute pairs by domain experts, which is time-consuming and costly. ▶ Class
203
      Imbalance: The prevalence of non-matching attribute pairs significantly outnumbers matching pairs,
204   resulting in severe class imbalance. ▶ Lack of Ranking: It does not yield a ranked list of candidate
205   matches, which is critical for human review if multiple possible matches exist.
206
207
      ▶ 1. Candidate generation: For each source query attribute Asi ∈ As from the source schema Ss ,
      we generate a set of potential matches from the target schema St . Let Ci ⊆ At be the set of candidate
208
      target matches for query attribute Asi . The candidate generation process is defined as a function
209
      g : As × At → P(At ), where P(At ) denotes the power set of At , such that Ci = g(Asi , At ).
210
211   ▶ 2. Ranking: We rank the candidates based on their relevance to the query attribute. We define
212   a ranking function r : (As × Ds ) × (At × Dt ) → R, where Ds and Dt represent the contextual
213
      information associated with attributes in As and At , respectively. For each source attribute Asi ∈ As
      and its associated contextual information dsi ∈ Ds , the ranking function r assigns a relevance score
214
      to each candidate attribute Atj ∈ Ci ⊆ At and its associated contextual information dtj ∈ Dt :

      4    M ATCHMAKER : LLM- BASED S CHEMA M ATCHING
250
251   We propose Matchmaker, a self-improving compositional language model (LM) program for schema
252   matching (see Fig. 3), defined as a three-step LM program. For further details see Appendix A.2.
253
254
      1. Multi-vector documents (Sec. 4.1): Creation of multi-vector documents from the target schema
      to facilitate semantic candidate retrieval of potential target attribute matches.
255
      2. Candidate generation (Sec. 4.2): Employing two types of candidate generation: semantic retrieval
256
      and reasoning-based. The candidates are then refined into a smaller candidate set to evaluate.
257   3. Confidence scoring (Sec. 4.3): match confidence of a candidate target attribute to a query attribute.
258
259     Steps 1-3 define the unoptimized Matchmaker program. Finally, a key aspect of Matchmaker is
260   our zero-shot optimization via synthetic in-context examples to improve performance (Sect. 4.4).
261
      Why LLMs for schema matching? Large Language Models (LLMs) form the foundation of
262
      Matchmaker, serving as key components within a compositional program comprised of multiple
263
      language model calls. Specifically, LLMs exhibit several appealing properties and capabilities for
264   schema matching: ▶ Contextual understanding: LLMs have been pretrained on vast corpora of
265   information, equipping them with extensive prior knowledge spanning different contexts and settings
266   (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Singhal et al., 2023). This contextual understanding enables LLMs to
267   effectively reason about schema hierarchies and identify potential matches. ▶ Hypothesis proposers:
268   LLMs have been shown to be “phenomenal hypothesis proposers” (Qiu et al., 2023), making them
269   particularly useful for candidate generation tasks. ▶ Capable rankers: LLMs have been shown to
      be highly capable at relevance ranking; assessing the suitability of candidates given a query and a set

      of options (Zhuang et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2024), especially “when ranking candidates retrieved by
271   multiple candidate generators” (Hou et al., 2024).

273   Defining a compositional LM program. A compositional language model program, denoted as
274
      L, is a multi-stage pipeline consisting of multiple LLM calls, i.e., L = {l1 , l2 , . . . , ln }, where
      li : (s, ks ) → Y represents a specific LLM call taking as input a prompt string s and in-context
275
      examples ks (which could be ∅). In the following sections (Secs. 4.1-4.3), we define the different
276
      components of L specific to Matchmaker. Finally, we describe our optimization process (Sec. 4.4).

278   4.1   M ULTI - VECTOR DOCUMENTS (S TEP 1)
279
      To efficiently retrieve semantically similar candidates from the target schema, we build a vector
280
      database that encodes target schema attributes. We begin by representing the target schema as a
281   collection of structured documents. Specifically, for each table T in the target schema St , we create a
282   document for each table consisting of the attribute names and append the attribute’s textual description
283   and data type, providing contextual information about each attribute. The metadata of each document
284   includes the description of the table itself.
285
      Unlike conventional approaches that encode each document as a single high-dimensional vector,
286
      Matchmaker utilizes multi-vector representations. Specifically, we use ColBERT-v2 (Santhanam
287   et al., 2022) to encode the document chunks, producing an embedding per token (i.e., token-level
288   dense vector), capturing token-level interactions — as it has been shown to provide improvements in
289   expressivity (Thakur et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2024) and out-of-domain performance (Santhanam et al.,
290   2022). The following section explains how semantically similar candidates are retrieved using this
291   multi-vector representation.
292
293   4.2   D IVERSE CANDIDATE GENERATION (S TEP 2)
294   To narrow down the search space, Matchmaker identifies a subset of candidate attributes from the
295   target schema that are likely matches for a query attribute qi ∈ As from the source schema. We draw
296   inspiration from (Hou et al., 2024), which demonstrates that LLM ranking performance improves
297   “‘when ranking candidates are retrieved by multiple candidate generators.” Hence, while semantic
298   candidates are commonly used, Matchmaker goes beyond and employs two distinct types of candidate
299   generation: (i) Semantic retrieval candidates retrieved from the vector database, and (ii) Reasoning-
300   based candidates using a language model. This is then followed by a candidate refinement step. We
301
      outline each type of candidate generation applicable to a given query attribute qi ∈ As .
302   (i) Semantic retrieval candidates. Given query qi , we encode it using ColBERT-V2, producing a
303   multi-vector query embedding. Matchmaker then uses this query embedding to retrieve the top-k
304   matching target schema attributes in the vector database. The top-k semantically similar candidates
305   are denoted as Cs . Similarity is computed using a late-interaction approach (Khattab & Zaharia,
306   2020), though a Maxsim operator which identifies the highest similarity scores for the query tokens,
307
      and these scores are aggregated to generate a relevance score for that document. All documents are
      then ranked based on their overall relevance scores. The top-k documents, which contain the most
308
      semantically similar attributes to the query, are retrieved as matches.
309
310   (ii) Reasoning-based candidates. To complement semantic matches, Matchmaker generates
311   reasoning-based candidates using a candidate reasoner LLM denoted as lc : (qi , At ) → CR , where
312   qi is the i-th query, At is the set of all target attributes and CR is a reasoning-based candidate set.
313
      Matchmaker employs Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) to reason about the target
      attributes At given the context of the schema hierarchy, descriptions and data types — generating the
314
      most likely and relevant target schema candidate matches for each query qi . This metadata allows the
315
      LLM to reason about the schema structure and relationships beyond just attribute names.
316
317   Refinement. At this stage, the set of candidates is C = CR ∪ Cs . Matchmaker then refines this set
318   by selecting the most relevant candidates for each query attribute, resulting in a smaller, prioritized
319
      candidate set C ∗ to score and rank. Candidate refinement is achieved with a refiner LLM using CoT,
      denoted as lr : s → C ∗ , where s = (C, qi ) and qi is the i-th source query.
320

      4.3   C ONFIDENCE SCORING (S TEP 3)
322
323   The refined set of candidates, C ∗ remains unordered. Hence, this step aims to obtain confidence
      scores to rank the candidates but also gauge the certainty of each match, recognizing that sometimes


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      no suitable source-to-target attribute match exists, which requires the system to abstain from making
325   a match. While language models may not be well-calibrated at the sequence level, recent research
326   has shown that they exhibit better calibration at the token level (Ren et al., 2023), a feature notably
327   beneficial in multiple-choice question (MCQ) tasks (Kadavath et al., 2022). Leveraging this insight,
328   Matchmaker structures the candidate scoring task as an MCQ format, labeling each candidate in C ∗
329   for query qi as options (A), (B), (C), etc. Additionally, to account for none of the target candidates
330   being a good match or there might be no possible match in the target schema, Matchmaker includes
331   an abstain option by adding "NONE of the above" as a choice. This ensures that the LLM is not
332   forced to select a candidate when there is no suitable match (Ren et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023).
333   Matchmaker finally performs candidate ranking, where it is common to evaluate each candidate
334   individually (Hu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2023). Confidence scores are obtained
335   by prompting the LLM to reason about the relevance of each candidate ci ∈ C ∗ to the given query qi .
336   Furthermore, prior work has shown that LLMs can provide good uncertainty at token-level (Kadavath
337   et al., 2022) like in our MCQ, which is achievable via prompting (Tian et al., 2023). Consequently,
338   Matchmaker elicits a confidence score by prompting the LLM to provide a value between 0 and 100,
339
      indicating the relevance of a match. The confidence scores are used to rerank candidates or, if "None
      of the above" receives the highest score, return an empty list (i.e. no suitable matches for the query).
340

      4.4   S ELF - IMPROVEMENT: Z ERO - SHOT OPTIMIZATION W / SYNTHETIC IN - CONTEXT EXAMPLES
342
343   Matchmaker optimizes the language model program L by leveraging the few-shot learning capabilities
344   of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2022). This is achieved by selecting
345   input-output demonstrations (i.e. in-context examples). In Sec. 5, we contrast this with an alternative
346   self-improvement method via self-reflection. However, selecting in-context examples is non-trivial
347
      for schema matching for two reasons.
348   (i) Lack of labeled demonstrations: We do not have access to labeled input-output demonstrations
349   from which to select in-context examples. To overcome this challenge, we use the unlabeled schemas
350   to create a "evaluation" set Deval = {e1 , e2 , . . . , em }, made up of different types of source queries.
351   Specifically, we identify "easy queries" where the top-n (n=5) target schema semantic matches have a
352   similarity score > 0.95, and "challenging queries" with the lowest semantic matches.
353   (ii) Lack of an evaluator: To assess Matchmaker’s capabilities on the evaluation set and guide the
354   optimization process, we need a validation metric. Since no validator is readily available, we propose
355   to use an evaluator LLM, E : (ei , L(ei )) → R, that employs chain of thought (Wei et al., 2022) to
356   score the relevance (from 0-5) of matches obtained from L when evaluated on examples from Deval .
357
358   Algorithm 1 Optimize LM program L

369   Zero-shot optimization w/ synthetic in-context examples. To optimize our multi-stage language
370   model program, we aim to select in-context examples for each component in L. However, in-context
371   demonstrations for the intermediate stages are typically unavailable.
372   To address this, we simulate traces by running L on the evaluation examples ei ∈ Deval . A trace
373   captures the intermediate input-output pairs of each component in L during the execution of L on
374   a given example. The evaluator E then scores the final output, assessing Matchmaker’s (L) overall
375   performance on each example. We then adopt a bootstrapping process (Khattab et al., 2023) that
376   selects the intermediate input-output pairs from the traces that produced the highest evaluation scores
377   as synthetic in-context examples for each component of L. In other words, we use the input-output
      pairs generated by Matchmaker itself (which resulted in good evaluation performance) as synthetic

      in-context examples to guide the LLM reasoning. This allows us to improve the program in a zero-
379   shot manner, without relying on actual labeled data. Algorithm 3 provides an overview of the process.
380   We refer to L with the systematically selected in-context examples as Matchmaker (Optimized).

492   5.4   M ATCHMAKER IN PRACTICE : H UMAN - IN - THE - LOOP DEFERRAL AND REMEDIAL ACTION .
493
494   How might we use Matchmaker in practice for schema matching? Let us examine two cases.
495
      (1) Matchmaker with human-in-the-loop deferral: We evaluate the effectiveness of integrating
496   Matchmaker with a human-in-the-loop approach by deferring uncertain matches to human experts (i.e.,
497   an oracle) for correction. High-uncertainty cases are identified using the entropy of Matchmaker’s
498   confidence scores, with the most challenging matches (those with the highest entropy) deferred to
499   the oracle. We evaluate different deferral percentages p ∈ {0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50} and observe that
500   entropy-based deferral consistently yields greater performance gains compared to random deferral, as
501   shown in Fig. 4(a). This finding highlights the practical value of Matchmaker in real-world settings,
502   where based on entropy, one could strategically seek human oversight for challenging matches
503   and improve overall schema matching performance. The appropriate deferral percentage, however,
504
      depends on context-specific factors such as human bandwidth and expert availability.
505   (2) Evaluating ease of remedial action based on the similarity between incorrect predictions
506   and true target attributes: Not all errors in source-target matching are equal; some might be
507   easier to rectify than others. We hypothesize that errors involving semantically similar attributes
508   are easier to correct compared to those involving completely dissimilar attributes. We analyze
509   the cosine similarity between incorrectly predicted attributes and their true target attributes using
510
      Pubmed-Bert embeddings. To simulate post-hoc remedial action, we assess the performance gains
      achieved by correcting erroneous predictions that exceed different similarity thresholds. Figure 4(b)
511
      shows substantial improvements in accuracy@1 when "fixing" errors, with high semantic similarity
512
      between the erroneous prediction and true attribute (e.g., cosine similarity ≥ 0.8). These results
513   suggest that Matchmaker’s incorrect predictions are often semantically close to the true attributes
514   (i.e. our errors are not far off), making them more amenable to post-hoc remedial actions. This
515   demonstrates the viability of post-hoc remedial actions to improve schema matching performance.
516   Further error analysis can be found in Appendix D.

      6     D ISCUSSION
530   Matchmaker introduces a novel approach to schema matching, using a self-improving composi-
531   tional program using LLMs. Matchmaker’s superior performance compared to existing ML-based
532   approaches, underlines its potential to accelerate data integration for ML-ready data. Matchmaker’s
533   zero-shot self-improvement mechanism, using synthetic in-context examples, showcases the potential
534   of using LLMs to handle complex reasoning tasks without relying on labeled data.
535   Limitations and opportunities. (1) Matchmaker, while effective in schema matching, represents
536   just one component of the broader data harmonization process and needs to be integrated with other
537   tasks to generate ML-ready data. (2) Despite its advantages over alternative ML-based approaches,
538   Matchmaker is not a panacea and does not achieve perfect automation. It is best used with a human-
539   in-the-loop (Sec. 5.4) to ensure reliability in real-world settings. (3)Future work should address
      many-many schema matches or explore the viability of non-English schemas.

      A.1   M ATCHMAKER WITHIN THE CONTEXT OF LLM TABLE REASONING .
867
868   There has recently been works on LLMs for table reasoning. We contrast them to Matchmaker along
869   a variety of dimensions below.
870
      Task/Goal: The table reasoning papers tackle a variety of tasks centered around understanding
871   and interacting with tabular data. Some examples include: TabSQLify (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024) and
872   OPENTAB (Kong et al., 2023) focus on table question answering and fact verification, aiming to
873   extract relevant information from tables to answer questions or verify statements. Chain-of-Table
874   (Wang et al., 2023b) and "Large Language Models are Few-Shot Table Reasoners" (Chen, 2023)
875   explore LLMs’ capabilities in reasoning over tables for question answering and fact verification
876   tasks. The survey paper "Large Language Model for Table Processing" (Lu et al., 2024) covers a
877   broader range of tasks, including table manipulation, table augmentation, and text-to-SQL conversion,
878   showcasing LLMs’ potential in interpreting and manipulating tabular data. In contrast, Matchmaker
879
      addresses the task of schema matching, which aims to find correspondences between attributes across
      different schemas or tables. The goal is to enable data integration by mapping attributes from a source
880
      schema to a target schema, considering the structural and semantic differences between them. This
881
      task is crucial for creating ML-ready datasets by harmonizing data from diverse sources.
882
883   Approach: Table reasoning approaches span prompting LLMs for direct answers (Chen, 2023),
884   program synthesis to generate SQL/code (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Kong et al., 2023), iterative table
885
      transformation (Wang et al., 2023b), instruction tuning (Lu et al., 2024), and agent-based methods (Lu
      et al., 2024). Matchmaker proposes a novel self-improving compositional language model program. It
886
      leverages LLM reasoning via a pipeline with multiple LLM calls for candidate generation, refinement
887
      and confidence scoring. It also self-improves without labeled data via synthetic in-context examples.
888
889   Inputs: The table reasoning papers mostly focus on single tables as input along with a question/query.
890   Matchmaker takes as input two tables/schemas (source and target) that need to be matched. It operates
891
      solely on schema-level information (attribute names, metadata) without access to raw data in the
      tables. This is also a key difference compared to the table reasoning papers, which often rely on the
892
      actual data values for answering questions or verifying facts.
893
894   Outputs: Table reasoning papers aim to output answers to questions, binary fact verification labels,
895   updated tables after manipulation, generated SQL/code, etc. In contrast, Matchmaker outputs a
896   mapping between the source and target schema attributes, or indicates no match is possible for certain
897
      attributes. The set of attribute pairs representing the schema matching results, can be used to guide
      data integration processes.
898
899   Use of the LLM: Table reasoning employs LLMs for direct answer generation (Chen, 2023), program
900   synthesis (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024; Kong et al., 2023), iterative prompting (Wang et al., 2023b), or as
901   part of an agent system (Lu et al., 2024). Matchmaker uses LLMs for reasoning within a compositional
902   program, generating candidates, refining them, and scoring confidence.
903   Optimization/Training: Table reasoning works explore fine-tuning (Nahid & Rafiei, 2024), instruc-
904   tion tuning (Lu et al., 2024), and in-context few-shot learning (Chen, 2023). Matchmaker introduces
905   a novel optimization process to select synthetic in-context examples for self-improvement without
906   labeled data or fine-tuning.
907   Key differences: In summary, while the table reasoning papers focus on tasks like question answering,
908   fact verification, and table manipulation on single tables, Matchmaker addresses the distinct task of
909   schema matching across table pairs. Its novel approach of a self-improving compositional language
910   model program operating on schema-level information contrasts with general table reasoning which
911   mostly use LLMs for direct table QA or program synthesis.

      23: end for

       A.3   S CHEMA MATCHING CHALLENGES .
973
974          • Database Heterogeneity: The number of tables in each schema may differ, i.e., |Ts | =
                                                                                                   ̸ |Tt |,
975            making it challenging to establish correspondences between attributes across schemas.
976          • Structural Heterogeneity: Schemas may have different architectures, hierarchies, and
977            representational granularity. If we define a hierarchy function h(Ti ) that describes the level
978            of nesting within tables, differences in h(Tsj ) and h(Ttk ) for any j, k can lead to significant
979            challenges in aligning attributes Asj and Atk .
980          • Semantic Heterogeneity: Attributes in different schemas may have the same name but
981            different meanings, or different names but the same meaning. Let Ni = {nij |Aij ∈ Ai }
982            be the set of attribute names for schema Si . Semantic heterogeneity occurs when ∃Asj ∈
983            As , Atk ∈ At : f (Asj ) = Atk ∧ nsj ̸= ntk or when ∃Asj ∈ As , Atk ∈ At : f (Asj ) ̸=
984            Atk ∧ nsj = ntk .
985          • Data Type Heterogeneity: Attributes in different schemas may have different data types,
986            even if they refer to the same concept. Let dij be the data type of attribute Aij . Data type
987            heterogeneity occurs when ∃Asj ∈ As , Atk ∈ At : f (Asj ) = Atk ∧ dsj ̸= dtk .
988          • Information Mismatch: Some attributes in one schema may lack a corresponding match in
989            the other schema. This necessitates reasoning about "no possible match" cases, which is as
990            important as reasoning about possible matches.
991
             • Unsupervised Nature: Schema matching is unsupervised, where no labeled data pairs
992            (Asj , Atk ) are available to train or validate the mappings. This necessitates reliance on the
993            intrinsic structure and semantic information encoded in Ai , making the development of an
994            effective mapping function f challenging without external supervision.

       A.5   F URTHER DETAILS ON SCHEMA MATCHING FORMALISM
1081
1082
       In this appendix, we provide further details on the formulation of schema matching. We look at
1083   properties that a schema matching algorithm or function should possess, as well as, detailing how
1084   Matchmaker satisfies these properties.
1085
1086
       Properties necessary. In practice, correctness in schema matching is evaluated against expert-
       validated ground truth mappings between the datasets (e.g. MIMIC to OMOP and Synthea to OMOP).
1087
       However, this begs the question what properties would be useful ti improve emprical performance.
1088
1089   These lie along the following dimensions:
1090
1091          • Semantic Equivalence/Consistency: f (AS ) = At implies A and At represent the same
1092            real-world concept (i.e. the mapped attributes serve equivalent purposes)
1093
              • Type Compatibility: Mapped attributes must have compatible data types
1094
1095
              • Structural Consistency: Mappings must respect schema hierarchies
1096
1097          • Coverage: f identifies all valid matches while avoiding incorrect mappings through absten-
1098            tion. i.e. coverage is maximized by improved accuracy@k
1099
1100   We can then practically assess if a function f (such as Matchmaker) satisfies these criteria based on
1101   its performance against expert-validated ground truth mappings in real-world benchmark datasets as
1102   has been done in the paper.
1103   How does Matchmaker satisfy these properties?
1104
1105   While we have empirically shown Matchmaker satisfies the properties needed of a schema matching
1106
       function f , based on its strong performance on real-world schema matching tasks where it signifi-
       cantly outperforms existing approaches on standard benchmarks. In particular, the strong empirical
1107
       performance outperforming the baselines implies that Matchmaker better satisfies the properties
1108
       compared to the baseline schema matching algorithms.
1109

1122          • Structural consistency is maintained by incorporating table metadata and hierarchical infor-
1123            mation in document creation (Section 4.1), using reasoning-based candidate generation that

                considers schema structure (Section 4.2), and including table context in confidence scoring.

              • Coverage is optimized through our MCQ format with a "None of the above" option enabling
1126
                abstention when no good match exists, while confidence scoring helps identify and rank
1127            high-quality matches. Our empirical results validate that these properties translate to superior

1155

1157

1158

1159           program L is then run on each evaluation example ei ∈ Deval . We capture full execution
1160           traces including intermediate reasoning steps, candidate generation and refinement decisions,
1161           and final confidence scores and matches. The synthetic in-context examples refer to the
1162           intermediate input-output pairs generated by the LLM for the intermediate steps of the
1163           compositional LLM program. This deals with the challenge of a lack of labeled examples
1164           (i.e. zero-shot).
1165         • Addressing (C2): To handle the lack of an evaluator (validation metric), we use an evaluator
1166           LLM E (i.e. an LLM-as-a-judge) to assess match quality through chain-of-thought reasoning,
1167           producing scores from 0-5 based on match relevance. Finally, the top-n traces are selected
1168           based on these evaluation scores. This systematic approach, detailed in Algorithm 1, enables
1169           principled selection of in-context examples based on traces that lead to good performance.
1170           We then use these as in-context examples for the different parts of the LLM program (as they
1171           led to good performance) — in order to guide the reasoning. As shown in the main paper
1172
               our novel approach to self-improve outperforms random selection of in-context examples
               and self-reflection confirming that our systematic selection of in-context samples is the key
1173
               driver of performance gains, rather than the mere inclusion of any in-context examples.

       A.7   E XTENDED RELATED WORK
1176
1177   Classical Schema Matching approaches. Classical approaches to schema matching, as thoroughly
1178   reviewed by Rahm & Bernstein (2001), use a range of strategies, including heuristic-driven linguistic
1179   matching, constraint-based methods, and structural analysis. These methods have historically focused
1180   on simple relational schemas, matching elements between individual tables or flat structures. In
1181   particular, the primary focus is matching between individual tables or simple schemas (such as
1182   purchase orders).
1183   Key Weaknesses of Classical Approaches and How Matchmaker Addresses Them:
1184
1185         • Single-Table and Flat Structure Focus: Classical methods typically perform schema matching
1186           at the element level, treating tables as isolated entities and matching attributes based on
1187           direct comparisons of names, data types, or simple structural cues. In particular, often a
               focus was simple relational schemas, where the goal was to map elements between single


                                                        22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
                tables. However, this approach fails to handle the complexity of modern data systems, where
1189            schemas are often multi-table, hierarchical, or require cross-table reasoning. Contrast:
1190            Matchmaker, in contrast, uses LLM-based reasoning to connect attributes across multi-table
1191            and hierarchical schemas, understanding how data relationships span multiple tables. This
1192            makes our approach significantly more capable of handling complex and interrelated schema
1193            structures.
1194          • Dependency on Heuristics and Limited Semantic Understanding: Classical methods rely
1195            on heuristic-driven matching based on linguistic similarities (e.g., name matching using
1196            synonyms, hypernyms, or edit distance) and structural constraints like key relationships.
1197            While these heuristics work in well-defined contexts, they are insufficient for domains
1198            where semantic meaning is implicit, such as in healthcare and as we show in Fig 1 — only
1199            semantic matching is in fact insufficient. Contrast: Matchmaker employs chain-of-thought
1200            prompting and advanced LLMs to perform reasoning, allowing it to capture relationships
1201
                that are not explicitly defined in the schema structure or names. This enables Matchmaker
                to handle complex mappings that classical methods cannot infer.
1202
1203          • Manual Effort and Lack of Adaptability: Classical techniques require significant manual
1204            effort for tuning and adaptation, making them less suitable for rapidly evolving or heteroge-
1205
                neous environments. Constraint-based approaches, in particular, are difficult to scale across
                different domains without manual intervention. Alternatively, they might also rely on labeled
1206
                data for effective matching. This makes these classical approaches impractical in real-world
1207
                environments. Contrast: Matchmaker’s zero-shot and self-optimization capabilities mean it
1208            can adapt autonomously to new schemas using synthetic in-context examples, significantly
1209            reducing the need for manual tuning and making it more practical for dynamic, real-world
1210            data integration tasks.
1211
1212   Key Weaknesses of SMAT and how Matchmaker improves: We also compared Matchmaker to
1213   state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods like SMAT Zhang et al. (2021), which applies attention mechanisms
1214   for schema matching. While SMAT represents an important advancement over classical methods, it
1215
       has several limitations that Matchmaker overcomes:
1216
              • High Dependency on Labeled Data: SMAT requires extensive labeled data (over 50%
1217            labeled matches) for training, which is often impractical in real-world schema matching.
1218            Contrast: Matchmaker’s zero-shot matching capability allows it to perform well without
1219            any labeled training data, using LLMs to generate and refine matches autonomously.
1220
              • Binary formulation: SMAT formulates the problem as binary classification task over the
1221            full Cartesian product of source and target schema attributes. e.g. for each pair of source-
1222            target attributes. This leads to a large amount of comparisons. Contrast: Matchmakers
1223            formulation as information retrieval reduces the number of comparisons and leads to greater
1224            efficiency — in addition to the better performance.

1226   A.8   M ETRICS : ACCURACY, PRECISION , RECALL , F1-S CORE
1227
1228   In our m:1 schema matching setup, accuracy@1, precision, recall, and F1-score are equivalent due to
1229   the specific constraints of the task and the prediction mechanism employed. Below, we provide a
1230   detailed explanation of this equivalence:
1231   2. Task Constraints: The schema matching task is constrained such that each source attribute can
1232   match to at most one target attribute (m:1 constraint). This ensures that the number of predictions
1233   equals the number of source attributes.
1234
       Equivalence of Metrics Given the above setup, the following equivalences hold:
1235
1236   Precision:
                                                      True Positives (TP)
1237                         Precision =
                                           True Positives (TP) + False Positives (FP)
1238
1239   In our setup, every prediction corresponds to exactly one target attribute, and there are no extraneous
1240
       or unassigned predictions. Therefore:
1241                                           Correct Matches
                                 Precision =                     = Accuracy@1.
                                               Total Predictions

                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       Recall:
1243                                                   True Positives (TP)
1244
                               Recall =
                                          True Positives (TP) + False Negatives (FN)
1245
       Since every source attribute must be matched to a target attribute, there are no unassigned predictions
1246   in our setup. However, incorrect matches can occur, leading to both false positives (FP) and false
1247   negatives (FN). In our m:1 schema matching setup, a prediction is either correct (a true positive,
1248   TP) or incorrect. An incorrect match to the wrong target attribute results in a false positive (FP)
1249   for the predicted target and a corresponding false negative (FN) for the true target. Consequently,
1250   the number of FP and FN are always equal, as they reflect the same prediction errors. In this setup,
1251   precision, recall, and accuracy@1 are equivalent because they all measure the proportion of correct
1252   matches (TPs) relative to the total predictions, with incorrect matches impacting all metrics identically.
1253   This equivalence holds when correctness is measured against the ground truth annotations from the
1254
       benchmark datasets. Thus:
1255                                           Correct Matches
                                    Recall =                      = Accuracy@1.
1256                                           Total Predictions
1257
1258   F1-Score:
                                                        Precision · Recall
1259                                      F1-Score = 2 ·
                                                        Precision + Recall
1260
       As both precision and recall are equal to accuracy@1 in this setup, the F1-score simplifies to:
1261
1262                                          F1-Score = Accuracy@1.
1263
1264   In summary, due to the constraints of our m:1 schema matching task and the argmax prediction
1265   mechanism, accuracy@1, precision, recall, and F1-score are mathematically equivalent. We report
1266   accuracy@1 in the main results, but the corresponding precision, recall, and F1-scores are identical
1267   and can be directly interpreted from the accuracy@1 values. We note this equivalence does not hold
1268   for one-to-many mappings

       D.1   N UMBER OF LLM CALLS
1947
1948   Goal. To compare the number of LLM calls required by Matchmaker and other baseline methods for
1949   schema matching on the MIMIC-OMOP and SYNTHEA-OMOP datasets.
1950
       Experiment. We count the number of LLM calls made by each method during the schema matching
1951   process on both the MIMIC-OMOP and SYNTHEA-OMOP datasets. For methods that do not rely
1952   on LLMs (e.g., SMAT), we consider the number of forward passes through the neural network as
1953   equivalent to an LLM call for comparison purposes.
1954
       Results. Table 6 presents the number of LLM calls required by each method on the two datasets.
1955
1956
                                          Table 6: Number of LLM calls
1957
1958                          Method          MIMIC-OMOP         SYNTHEA-OMOP
1959                          Matchmaker         1340                 890
1960                          ReMatch             268                 178
1961                          Jellyfish-13b      24771               29637
1962                          Jellyfish-7b       24771               29637
1963                          LLM-DP             24771               29637
1964                          SMAT               24771               29637
1965
1966   Discussion. The results in Table 6 highlight the efficiency of Matchmaker and ReMatch in terms of
1967   the number of LLM calls required for schema matching.
1968   Both Matchmaker and ReMatch formulate schema matching as an information retrieval problem,
1969   which significantly reduces the search space compared to the binary classification formulation used
1970   by Jellyfish-13b, Jellyfish-7b, LLM-DP, and SMAT.
1971
       The high number of LLM calls required by Jellyfish-13b, Jellyfish-7b, LLM-DP, and SMAT can
1972   be attributed to their formulation of schema matching as a binary classification problem over the
1973   Cartesian product of source and target attributes. In this formulation, the LLM is prompted to provide
1974   a label of Yes/No for each pair of source-target attributes, resulting in a large number of LLM calls
1975   that scales (O(n2 )). Consequently, these methods are computationally expensive and less scalable
1976   compared to Matchmaker and ReMatch, which employ a more efficient approach.
1977
       The fewer number of LLM calls used by Matchmaker and ReMatch has practical implications in terms
1978   of computational cost and runtime efficiency. By reducing the number of LLM calls, these methods
1979   can perform schema matching more quickly and with lower computational overhead compared to
1980   methods that rely on a large number of calls. This is particularly important when dealing with
1981   large-scale schemas or when schema matching needs to be performed frequently in real-world
1982   applications.

       E    B ROADER I MPACT
2161
2162
       Schema matching is a critical step in data integration, enabling the creation of large, harmonized
2163   datasets that can be used to train machine learning models. The proposed Matchmaker approach,
2164   with its self-improving compositional language model program, has the potential to significantly
2165   accelerate and automate the schema matching process, thus facilitating the development of more
2166   accurate and robust ML models across various domains.
2167
       The importance and value of schema matching cannot be overstated, as integrating data from various
2168
       sources such as different regions, organizations or applications is vital in many fields, including
2169   healthcare, finance, and e-commerce. By enabling the integration of data from disparate sources,
2170   schema matching plays a critical role in creating comprehensive, harmonized datasets that can provide
2171   a more complete picture of the domain under study. For example, in healthcare, integrating data
2172   from multiple hospitals can lead to more representative and diverse datasets, allowing researchers to
2173   identify patterns and insights that may not be apparent when analyzing data from a single institution.
2174
       Moreover, schema matching is not only valuable for specific domains but also for the machine
2175   learning community as a whole. By increasing the pool of available data (larger and more diverse) for
2176   training and validation, schema matching can contribute to the development of more accurate, robust,
2177   and generalizable ML models. Furthermore, having access to a larger pool of data can enable more
2178   rigorous validation and testing of ML models, allowing researchers to assess their performance across
2179   different subpopulations, time periods, and data sources. This, in turn, can lead to more reliable and
2180   trustworthy ML models that can be confidently applied in real-world settings.
2181   Below we describe some positive implications that could be unlocked as schema matching approaches
2182   such as Matchmaker are used in practice. We also show some drawbacks with mitigation strategies.
2183
2184
       Positive Impacts:
2185
              • Improved data integration: Matchmaker can help overcome the challenges of integrating
2186            data from heterogeneous sources, leading to the creation of larger, more comprehensive
2187            datasets. This can enable the development of more powerful and generalizable ML models.
2188
              • Accelerated research and discovery: By reducing the time and effort required for data
2189
                integration, Matchmaker can accelerate research and discovery in fields, where data often
2190            resides in disparate databases with diverse schemas.
2191
              • Enhanced decision-making: The ability to train ML models on larger, more diverse datasets
2192
                enabled by Matchmaker can lead to more accurate and reliable predictions, supporting better
2193
                decision-making in various applications.
2194
2195   Potential Drawbacks and Mitigation Strategies:
2196
2197          • Overreliance on automated schema matching: While Matchmaker can significantly auto-
2198            mate the schema matching process, it is not perfect and may make errors. Overreliance
2199            on automated methods without human oversight could lead to incorrect data integration.
2200
                Mitigation: Matchmaker should be used as a tool to assist human experts in the schema
                matching process, rather than as a complete replacement. The paper demonstrates how
2201
                Matchmaker can be effectively used with a human-in-the-loop approach, leveraging the
2202
                strengths of both human expertise and automated methods.
2203
2204
              • Propagation of errors: If Matchmaker introduces errors during the schema matching process,
                these errors can propagate downstream and affect the quality of the resulting integrated
2205
                datasets and ML models. Mitigation: It is essential to implement rigorous validation and
2206
                quality control measures to detect and correct errors introduced by Matchmaker. This
2207            can include manual spot-checks, automated consistency checks, and the use of domain-
2208            specific validation rules. Establishing a feedback loop to continuously monitor and improve
2209            Matchmaker’s performance based on real-world usage can also help mitigate the propagation
2210            of errors.
