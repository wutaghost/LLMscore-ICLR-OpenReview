009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Data free quantization of neural networks is a practical necessity as access to
012
               training data in many situations is restricted due to privacy, proprietary concerns,
013
               or memory issues. We introduce a data free weight rounding algorithm for Deep
014            Neural Networks (DNNs) that does not require any training data, synthetic data
015            generation, fine-tuning, or even batch norm statistics. Instead, our approach fo-
016            cuses on preserving the direction of weight vectors during quantization. We
017            demonstrate that traditional weight rounding techniques, that round weights to
018            the nearest quantized level, can result in large angles between the full-precision
019            weight vectors and the quantized weight vectors, particularly under coarse quan-
020            tization regimes. For a large class of high-dimensional weight vectors in DNNs,
021            this angle error can approach 90 degrees. By minimizing this angle error, we
022
               significantly improve top-1 accuracy in quantized DNNs. We analytically derive
               the angle-minimizing rounding boundaries for ternary quantization under the as-
023
               sumption of Gaussian weights. Then, leaving the Gaussian assumption behind,
024
               we propose a greedy data-free quantization method based on the cosine similarity
025            between the full-precision weight vectors and the quantized weight vectors. Our
026            approach consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art data-free quantization
027            techniques and, in several cases, surpasses even data-dependent methods on well-
028            established models such as ResNet-18, VGG-16, and AlexNet with aggressive
029            quantization levels of 3 to 6 bits on the ImageNet dataset. Code will be made
030            available at time of publication.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) excel at many computer vision tasks. However, deploying these
037   models on resource-constrained devices poses significant challenges due to their computational and
038   storage requirements. Quantization is one promising approach to tackle these challenges. There
039
      are two common approaches to quantization, quantization aware training (QAT) and post training
      quantization (PTQ). QAT trains the model from scratch using the quantized weights and activation.
040
      Many works have shown the effectiveness of QAT (Hubara et al., (2016), Esser et al., (2019), Cour-
041
      bariaux et al., (2015), Rastegari et al.,(2016), Choi et al., (2015), Judd et al., (2015)). While these
042   approaches hold promise, they are not always feasible in practical application areas. Accessing the
043   original training data may not always be possible due a number or reasons including its size, privacy
044   concerns, proprietary nature of the data, etc.
045
      Post training quantization (PTQ) is particularly important as it allows users to deploy models in
046
      memory constrained environments without access to the entire original training data set. Yet many
047
      post training quantization methods are still dependent on a small subset of training data for calibra-
048   tion. Numerous papers have shown great results using quantization methods that rely on a small
049   amount of training data for calibration (Nagal et al.,(2020), Hubara et al., (2021), Choukroun et
050   al., (2019), Migacz et al., (2017), Lin et al., (2016), Han et al., (2015)). Yet there are still many
051   situations where even accessing small amounts of data is impossible. For this reason industry has
052   largely focused on model quantization schemes that do not require access to the training data for
053   fine-tuning ( Nagel et al., (2019), Zhao et al., (2019)). Data Free quantization methods are therefore
      very important.

054
      Recently, a new method of data free quantization was introduced- synthetic data generation. This
055   allows quantization aware training without any training data. Several papers have shown impressive
056   results in Qzero, DSG, ect (Cai et al., 2020, Qin et all 2023). However as noted in papers publishing
057   new methods without data generation such as Squant and UDFC (Guo et al., 2022, Bai et al., 2023)
058   generating the synthetic samples introduces extra computational costs, is complex, and depends on
059   the availability of BN layers. The trade offs between these varied methods at times makes fair com-
060   parison difficult. The authors of DFQ (Nagel et al., 2018) previously proposed 4 levels of practical
061   quantization applicability. Level 1, no training data and no back-propagation required. Level 2,
062   requires data but no back-propagation. Level 3, requires data and back-propagation and works for
063
      any model. Level 4, requires data and back-propagation but only works for specific models. Now
      we propose an additional quantization level 0 that does not require any training of synthetic data nor
064
      back-propagation. In this paper we present a method that meets the level 0 quantization standard.
065
066   With the exception of Zhang et al., (2019) there is a dearth of data-free methods that consider
067   quantization based angle errors. Data free weight quantization techniques have generally tried to
068   minimize the quantization error by taking the MSE optimization approach which leads to a round-
069
      to-nearest quantization scheme. In this work we propose an alternative view. We show that at
      low bit implementations, quantization causes a large angle between the full precision weight vector
070
      associated with a single neuron and its rounded counterpart. This angular error changes the decision
071
      boundary and associated input space for which the Relu nonlinearity turns ON/OFF. Therefore, it
072   has a large effect on the accuracy of the neural network. We propose a greedy algorithm to greatly
073   reduce the angle error associated with course weight quantization. Below the contributions of this
074   paper are summarized.
075
076          • We show that for a large class of weight vectors common in DNNs, round to nearest quan-
077            tization can lead to very large angle errors, i.e. the angle between the full precision and the
078            quantized weight vectors. High dimensional weight vectors, with the majority of weights
079            near zero, can generate angle errors close to 90 degrees. In particular, we show that in
080            the limit, the angle error can tend to 90 degrees under certain conditions. We will pro-
081            vide conditions under which large angles can occur under conventional round to nearest
082
               quantization and provide some illustrative examples from popular DNNs.
083          • We analytically derive the optimum rounding threshold for minimizing the angle error for
084            ternary weight quantization (weights ∈ {−1, 0, 1} ), assuming the weights have a Gaussian
085            distribution.‘ We show that the rounding threshold for minimizing the angle error is much
086            smaller than the round to nearest threshold (0.5) and depends on the distribution of the
087            weights.
088          • We introduce a data free greedy algorithm for weight rounding that drastically reduces
089            the angular error associated with weight quantization. This algorithm works for any word
090            length implementation and makes no assumptions on the underlying distribution of the
091            weights.
092
             • Using our proposed rounding algorithm we show significant top-1 accuracy boosts on two
093            well benchmarked models AlexNet, VGG16, and Resnet-18 on the imagenet dataset. These
094            results highlight the importance of the angular error and its effect on model accuracy.

097   2   R ELATED W ORK
098
099   The near endless applications of effective edge AI have motivated a plethora of works in the DNN
100   model quantization area. The problem of model compression for deployment in resource constrained
101   environments is well known. Post-training-quantization (PTQ) methods had remarkable success in
102   using fine-tuning with a small amount of training data to aggressively quantized pre-trained models.
103   In this section we will discuss previous works that are relevant to our proposed method. We focus
104
      on works that present results on very course quantization of weights to below 8 bits.
105   Lybrand Saab (2021) presented a Greedy Path-Following Quantization (GPFQ) that employed a
106   deterministic quantization of the model layers in an iterative fashion without requiring a complex
107   re-training. Zhang et al.,(2023) later improved and generalized the GPFQ method. This method
      showed near full precision accuracy results for weight quantization as low as 3 bits on the VGG16


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and Alexnet models on Imagenet. While their results are very impressive they still rely on a small
109   calibration set of training images to implement their quantization.
110
111   Recently, there has been a renewed focus on data free quantization techniques for Deep Neural
112
      networks (DNNs) that do not require any access to the original data set. These techniques are
      important when the underlying training data is private, proprietary, or difficult to process. This
113
      is an additional step beyond post training quantization (PTQ), as the latter sometimes still uses a
114
      small calibration subset of the original dataset to fine-tune the quantized model. Banner et al.,(2018)
115   introduced a novel technique that exploited per channel quantization to achieve 4 bit quantization
116   of DNNs with only small top-1 accuracy loss. This method was further notable as it did not require
117   training data to fine-tune the quantized model weights. Although it uses some training data to
118   determine clipping values for the activation, it a data-free method in terms of weight quantization.
119   Nagel et al., (2019) proposed a data free quantization method that equalized the weight ranges across
120   in a DNN to reduce quantization error bias. This method improved top-1 accuracy of quantized
121   methods on well known DNNs without relying on a calibration subset of the original training data.
122   While each of the above mentioned data-free quantization methods have unique contributions (per
123
      channel quantization, clipping, weight equalization, ect.) one commonality is that many employ
      a round to the nearest integer, or signed integer, quantization. These methods do not consider the
124
      angular error that quantization creates between a weight vector and its quantized counter part.
125
126   There are to the best of our knowledge relatively few data-free PTQ methods that consider the an-
127   gle error. In the area of QAT binary quantization, Anderson et al. (2017) showed that the angle
128   between a random vector (from a standard normal distribution) and its binarized counter part con-
129
      verges to 37 degrees as the dimension of the vector goes to infinity. Zhang et al., (2019) introduced
      a data-free PTQ method called Target None Re-training Ternary (TNT). The algorithm was of com-
130
      plexity O(N log N ) and experiments were performed for a uniform and a gaussian distribution of the
131
      weights in a high dimensional vector. The experiments showed that for very long vectors with gaus-
132   sian distribution cosine similarity was approximately 0.9, while there was significant uncertainty in
133   the range of cosine similarity for shorter vectors. Using simulation experiments, the authors also
134   investigated the optimal number of non-zero entries in the vector in order to obtain this maximum
135   cosine similarity and concluded that the distribution and the number of non-zero elements have a
136   large impact on the achievable cosine similarity. In the subsection on ternary quantization, our paper
137   will revisit these topics in an analytical manner where cosine similarity is analyzed using the quan-
138   tization boundary rather than the number of nonzero vector entries. Further this work provides an
139   angle aware data free quantization (Angle-DFQ) method that extends beyond the ternary case and
140
      can be implemented for any bit width without any assumption on the underlying distribution of the
      weights.

145   In this section we motivate the need to reduce quantization angle errors and offer a detailed explana-
146   tion of our method. We show how quantization induced angle errors affect DDNs at the neuron level
147   in section 3.1. In section 3.2 we show that significant quantization induced angle errors can occur for
148   a large class of vectors commonly found in popular DNNs. In section 3.3 we present the illustrative
149   special case of tertiary weight rounding (weights ∈ {−1, 0, 1}), where the rounding boundary for
150   minimizing the decision plane angle error is much smaller than the round to the nearest value of
151   0.5. In section 3.4 we present our data free greedy weight rounding method that greatly reduces the
152
      angle error associated with course weight quantization.

      5   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This paper presents an in-depth analysis of quantization effects on the angle of the weight vector
526
      and equivalently the angle of the decision hyperplane of a neuron. It is shown that under certain
527
      conditions the angle between the full precision and the quantized weight vector can approach 90◦ ,
528   which corresponds to almost half of the input space being classified incorrectly by the ReLU non-
529   linearity. It is also shown that the error angle minimizing quantization boundary is not n + 12 as is
530   the default method when minimizing the 2-norm of the error vector under the round to the nearest
531   method. Using the case of ternary quantization, it is shown that the optimal quantization boundary
532   depends on the distribution of weights and can be close to a quantization point, i.e., zero in the case
533   of tertiary quantization.
534   Armed with this theoretical foundation, we introduced Angle-DFQ; a data-free quantization method
535   that greatly boosts quantized model accuracy without the need for data or fine-tuning. While the
536   Angle-DFQ algorithm is not guaranteed to always find the optimal quantized weight vector, it shows
537   low complexity and exhibits accuracy improvements for weight quantization in Resnet-18, AlexNet
538   and VGG-16. The simplicity and straight forward nature of the Angle-DFQ method is a further
539   advantage for adaptation of this work in industry. The Angle-DFQ technique is well fitted for de-
      ploying models in the memory constrained environments required in many edge AI applications.
