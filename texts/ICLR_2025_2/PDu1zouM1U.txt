009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we explore the composition of word embeddings to create richer,
012            more meaningful representations of multi-word units. Existing methods, such as
013            averaging word embeddings, provide simple and efficient approaches. However,
014            they often fail to capture the complexity of multi-word interactions. To address
015            this, we employ the Pyramidal Recursive learning (PyRv) method, which recur-
016            sively combines word embeddings into unified representations. Originally de-
017            veloped for constructing representations hierarchically from subwords to phrases,
018            PyRv is well-suited for progressively merging individual word vectors into phrase
019            vectors. We evaluate the effectiveness of PyRv for embedding composition using
020
               fastText embeddings on the dependency relation labeling task. Using a single fast-
               Text word embedding yields an accuracy of 71%. Averaging five fastText word
021
               embeddings (the middle word and its four neighboring words) results in a signif-
022
               icant drop in accuracy to 34%. In contrast, by composing five word embeddings
023            with PyRv, we achieve an accuracy of 77%, demonstrating the superior ability
024            of PyRv to integrate multiple word embeddings into more expressive representa-
025            tions. These findings highlight the potential of PyRv as a lightweight yet powerful
026            technique for word embedding composition.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Word embeddings are foundational to many natural language processing (NLP) tasks, providing a
033
      way to map words into continuous vector spaces that capture semantic relationships between them.
      By converting words into numerical representations, word embeddings allow machines to process
034
      and understand text in a way that retains important linguistic properties. Popular word embedding
035
      methods, such as Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013), GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014), and FastText
036   (Bojanowski et al., 2017), have demonstrated the utility of these representations by positioning
037   semantically similar words closer in the vector space. The effectiveness of NLP models often hinges
038   on the quality of these embeddings, as richer and more informative representations can lead to
039   improved performance across various downstream tasks.
040
      In many real-world applications, however, understanding text at the word level alone is insufficient.
041
      The need to represent larger linguistic units, such as phrases or sentences, necessitates techniques
042   for combining word embeddings into more complex structures. Word composition, which involves
043   aggregating individual word embeddings to represent multi-word expressions or entire sentences,
044   serves this purpose. By integrating information from multiple word vectors, compositional methods
045   aim to capture both the meanings of individual words and the syntactic and semantic interactions
046   between them, particularly in morphologically complex languages, such as Croatian, which was
047   used for evaluation in this paper.
048   There are several established methods for combining word embeddings. Simple techniques include
049   element-wise operations such as addition, averaging, or multiplication, which produce a composite
050   vector by leveraging individual word vectors (such as in Joulin et al. (2016) and Arora et al. (2017)).
051   These methods, while computationally efficient, may fail to fully capture the complexity of phrase
052   or sentence meaning. More sophisticated approaches employ weighted combinations, context-aware
053   methods, or syntactic structures to improve the expressiveness of the resultant embeddings (such as
      in Socher et al. (2013) and Bahdanau (2014)).

054
      While transformer-based models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) and GPT (Brown et al., 2020),
055   offer robust pre-trained sentence embeddings by learning deep contextual representations, they are
056   often computationally expensive and may not always align with the specific needs of certain tasks.
057   Word composition methods provide a more lightweight and flexible alternative, especially in cases
058   where transparency and control over the aggregation process are crucial. Additionally, word-level
059   composition techniques can better retain the granularity of individual word meanings, which is
060   sometimes diluted in sentence-level embeddings produced by transformer models.
061   Word composition provides a valuable approach for constructing meaningful representations of
062   multi-word units, balancing computational efficiency and interpretability. These methods remain
063   relevant, particularly in domains where sentence embedding techniques may obscure important de-
064   tails or where domain-specific customization of embedding composition is required.

107   representations at each level of abstraction. Initially developed for constructing representations from
      tokens (subwords) up to phrases (as illustrated in Figure 1), PyRv is well-suited for combining word

111   One of the key properties of the PyRv method is representation compositionality, which enables the
112
      composition of multiple embeddings into a coherent, semantically rich representation. This property
      aligns with the objective of this paper, where the focus is on effectively combining word embeddings
113
      to capture more complex linguistic structures. By recursively merging embeddings, PyRv maintains
114
      the semantic integrity of each word while constructing increasingly abstract representations at higher
115   levels of the hierarchy.
116
117   The primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of a method for composing multi-word
118
      units into unified representations using Pyramidal Recursive learning (PyRv). To assess the effec-

120

131   2   R ELATED WORK
132
133
134
      Composing word embeddings to generate meaningful representations of larger text units remains a
      critical area of study in natural language processing. Approaches to this problem span from simple
135
      aggregation techniques that compose word embeddings to more complex neural architectures that
136
      embed entire sentences, each offering unique advantages in different contexts.
137
138   A common approach is to average word embeddings to generate a single vector representing a phrase
139   or sentence. Joulin et al. (2016) introduced this idea in the context of fastText, where word embed-
140
      dings are averaged and subsequently used for efficient text classification. This technique, inspired
      by the continuous bag of words (CBOW) model (Mikolov et al., 2013), offers a computationally
141
      lightweight solution that performs competitively with deeper models in various NLP tasks.
142
143   Building upon this, Arora et al. (2017) proposed an enhanced version where word embeddings are
144   combined using weighted averages, followed by post-processing through PCA/SVD. The weighting
145   scheme they propose significantly improves performance on textual similarity tasks. This method
146
      demonstrates that simple compositional techniques can rival more complex architectures, especially
      in unsupervised settings.
147
148   Wieting et al. (2015) conducted a comparative study that highlighted the trade-offs between simple
149   word averaging and more complex models like LSTMs for sentence embedding. Their findings
150   showed that while LSTMs perform well on in-domain data, simple word averaging techniques tend
151   to outperform LSTMs in out-of-domain tasks. This suggests that straightforward compositional
152
      methods, despite their simplicity, are robust and generalizable across diverse datasets.
153   Recursive models have also been explored for word compositionality. Socher et al. (2013) proposed
154   a recursive neural network based on syntactic parse trees to generate phrase and sentence repre-
155   sentations, useful in tasks like sentiment analysis. Zhao et al. (2015) introduced a Self-Adaptive
156   Hierarchical Sentence Model, using recursive structures without relying on syntax, showing that
157   supervised learning can effectively capture compositional semantics in a non-syntactic hierarchy.
158   Transformer (Vaswani, 2017) architectures use attention mechanism to compose embeddings. Bah-
159   danau (2014) introduced the attention mechanism in neural machine translation, allowing models
160   to dynamically focus on different parts of the input sequence during decoding. The introduction
161   of attention helped relieve the encoder from compressing all information into a single fixed-length
      vector, thus enabling more flexible and effective composition of representations over sequential data.

162
      In this work, we build on these approaches by applying the Pyramidal Recursive learning (PyRv)
163   method (Babić & Meštrović, 2024) to recursively combine word embeddings into more abstract
164   representations. Unlike the averaging techniques of Joulin et al. (2016) and Arora et al. (2017), PyRv
165   enables hierarchical composition, capturing both word-level and higher-level semantic structures in
166   a more structured way. Unlike most other recursive models, such as the one introduced by Socher
167   et al. (2013), which rely on syntactic trees, PyRv operates without requiring explicit parse structures,
168   and unlike the model introduced by Zhao et al. (2015), it is fully unsupervised. Additionally, by
169   recursively merging embeddings, our approach offers a simpler alternative to attention mechanisms
170   for constructing rich representations of text.

172   3     E MBEDDING METHOD
173
174   In this section, we introduce word embeddings and common composition techniques, followed by a
175   detailed explanation of how the PyRv method is applied to compose word embeddings.
176
177   3.1   W ORD EMBEDDING
178
179   Word embeddings, such as those produced by fastText (Bojanowski et al., 2017), are commonly
180   used to represent individual words. To represent multiple words, basic techniques like averaging or
181   concatenation can be applied. However, both approaches come with notable limitations.
182   When averaging embeddings (e.g., taking the mean of multiple word vectors), important informa-
183   tion, particularly word order, is lost. Concatenation preserves all information but presents two sig-
184   nificant challenges.
185
      First, concatenated embeddings vary in dimensionality depending on the number of words, which
186   complicates their use as input for models that require a fixed input size. Second, the dimensionality
187   of concatenated representations can grow excessively large. For instance, a 5-word phrase embedded
188   with 300-dimensional fastText results in a 1500-dimensional vector (5x300).
189
      In our evaluation, we use averaging to compose Croatian fastText embeddings (Grave et al., 2018)
190
      to maintain a consistent dimensionality across all methods, ensuring that the results are comparable.
191
192
      3.2   P Y RV WITH FAST T EXT WORD EMBEDDINGS
193
194   Pyramidal Recursive learning (PyRv) is a method designed to construct hierarchical representations
195   of text, moving progressively from low-level units such as characters or subwords to higher-level
196   representations such as words, phrases, sentences, and even paragraphs. PyRv combines represen-
197   tations recursively, forming increasingly abstract and semantically rich embeddings at each level of
198   the hierarchy.
199
      To address the limitations of averaging and concatenation, we explore the use of PyRv for composing
200   multiple word embeddings into a single, unified representation. Unlike previous work on PyRv
201   (Babić & Meštrović, 2024), where the recursion starts from subwords or tokens, in this study we
202   begin with word embeddings produced by fastText. This hybrid approach is referred to as PyRv+FT.
203
      For this paper, we use pre-trained Croatian fastText word vectors (Grave et al., 2018) to embed
204
      words, which are then recursively combined into phrase embeddings via the PyRvNN model. The
205
      PyRv+FT embeddings are trained on Croatian Wikipedia texts (Wikimedia public dump, January
206   11, 2020) for 10 epochs.
207
208   We evaluate PyRv+FT on two downstream tasks, described in detail in the next section. Through
209
      this process, we investigate how PyRv improves the compositionality of word embeddings, while
      maintaining manageable dimensionality and enhancing representational quality.

213
214   In this section, we describe the evaluation of fastText and PyRv+FT embeddings on two key NLP
215   tasks: Universal Part-of-Speech tagging (UPOS) and Universal Dependency Relation labeling (DE-
      PREL). UPOS tags represent core grammatical categories such as nouns, verbs, and adjectives, while

      DEPREL captures the syntactic relationships between words in a sentence, indicating dependencies
217   like subjects, objects, and modifiers.
218
219   For this evaluation, we use the hr500k 2.0 dataset (Ljubešić et al., 2016), a Croatian corpus with
220
      labeled data for both UPOS and DEPREL tasks (amongst others). This dataset contains 901 texts,
      24,763 sentences, and a total of 499,635 tokens.
221
222   Additionally, we perform a qualitative analysis of PyRv+FT embeddings by visualizing the repre-
223   sentation space to gain deeper insights into its structure and characteristics.
224
225   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
226
227
      To assess the performance of different embedding methods on the downstream tasks of UPOS and
      DEPREL, we use a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) model with one hidden layer. The hidden layer
228
      consists of 1,000 neurons and uses the ReLU activation function. The input to the model is a 300-
229
      dimensional vector (the size of both fastText and PyRv+FT embeddings). The output layer, with
230   softmax activation, adjusts to the number of classes in each task: 17 classes for UPOS and 37
231   classes for DEPREL. Each evaluation is conducted by training the MLP for one epoch.
232
233
      The embedding procedure remains consistent across both UPOS and DEPREL tasks, differing only
      in the MLP output labels.
234
235   The method of embedding a word from a sentence depends on the embedding strategy employed:
236
237         • fastText 1 word: Embeds only the target word, ignoring its surrounding context.
238         • mean fastText N words: Represents the target word by embedding all N words (the target
239           word and its N-1 neighboring context words) separately and averaging the embeddings to
240           obtain a final representation.
241         • PyRv+FT N words: Embeds each word using fastText, but instead of averaging the N
242           embeddings, it recursively combines them using the PyRvNN model to generate a single,
243           unified embedding.

323   Punctuation (punct) refers to punctuation marks such as ”.”, ”?”, ”!”, and ”,”. Since punctuation
      is straightforward to classify without context, a single fastText embedding for the target word alone

      achieves a perfect F1 score of 1. Averaging five fastText embeddings (the target word and its four
325   neighboring words) significantly degrades performance, reducing the F1 score to 0.61. However,
326   using PyRv to compose these embeddings preserves the high performance, maintaining an F1 score

328
      Conjunct (conj) denotes a relation between elements connected by coordinating conjunctions like
329
      ”and,” ”or,” or ”,”. In coordinate structures, the first element is conventionally treated as the head,
330
      with subsequent elements connected through the conj relation. For example, in the sentence ”Bill is
331   big and honest,” the word ”honest” is labeled as conj (connected to ”big”). Similarly, in ”He came
332   home, took a shower and immediately went to bed,” the words ”took” and ”went” are both labeled
333   as conj (connected to ”came”). Classifying conjuncts accurately requires contextual information. A
334   single fastText word embedding, without any context, yields a poor F1 score of 0.03. Averaging the
335   embeddings of the target word and its four neighbors improves performance significantly, achieving
336   an F1 score of 0.32 (a 10.8x improvement). Composing these embeddings using PyRv further boosts
337   performance, reaching an F1 score of 0.64 (a 21.85x improvement over the single word embedding).
338   Adnominal clause (acl) refers to finite or non-finite clauses that modify a nominal. For instance,
339   in ”the issues as he sees them,” the word ”sees” is labeled as acl (connected to ”issues”). In ”There
340   are many online sites offering booking facilities,” the word ”offering” is labeled as acl (connected to
341   ”sites”). Using a single fastText word embedding results in an F1 score of 0.14. Averaging the target
342   word’s embedding with its four neighbors actually degrades performance slightly, reducing the F1
343   score to 0.11. In contrast, composing these word embeddings with PyRv substantially improves
344   performance, raising the F1 score to 0.53 (a 3.84x improvement over the single word embedding).

346   4.3   Q UALITATIVE ANALYSIS
347
348   To gain qualitative insights into the structure of PyRv+FT embeddings, we visualize the represen-
349   tation space. A portion of this space is shown in Figures 4 and 5 (in the Appendix). In these
350   visualizations, each node represents a phrase consisting of two or three words. A two-word phrase
351   is connected to a three-word phrase if the shorter phrase is part of the longer one.
352
      We highlight specific clusters within the visualized space, with detailed examples of phrases from
353   these clusters (translated to English) provided in Tables 3 and 4 (Tables with original phrases in
354   Croatian are in Appendix: 8 and 9). The areas circled in the figures contain phrases built around
355   the prepositions ”u” (Croatian for ”in”) and ”na” (Croatian for ”on”). For example, Area C contains
356   two-word phrases like ”primjena na” (eng. ”application on”), while Area A includes phrases such
357   as ”na svijet” (eng. ”on the world”). Similarly, Area B features three-word phrases like ”primjena
358   na svijet” (eng. ”application on the world”), and Area D includes phrases like ”na svijet oko” (eng.
359   ”on the world around”). Same holds for the preposition ”u”.
360   The organization of phrases in the representation space is not random: phrases with similar syntactic
361   structures (e.g., where the preposition appears at the beginning, middle, or end of the phrase) tend
362   to cluster together. Furthermore, within these broader areas, smaller sub-clusters form based on the
363   specific preposition (”u” or ”na”) present in the phrase.

367
368   In this paper, we explored the use of Pyramidal Recursive learning (PyRv) for the composition of
369   word embeddings and evaluated its ability to generate meaningful representations of multi-word
370   units. Our findings show that PyRv outperforms simple averaging methods in embedding composi-

377   34%. In contrast, composing context words with PyRv attains a significantly higher accuracy of
