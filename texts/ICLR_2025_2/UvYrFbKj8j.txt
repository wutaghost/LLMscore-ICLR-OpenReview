009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in generating
012            coherent and contextually relevant text. However, their large parameters and
013            high memory requirements limit their efficiency and adoption in industry and
014            academia. Recent studies have shown that dynamically adjusting inference op-
015            erations can improve model performance without significantly increasing size. In
016            this paper, we introduce the stutter mechanism, a novel method that enhances
017            transformer models by selectively applying additional layers to more challenging
018
               tokens. This approach mimics a human speaker’s stutter, allocating more compu-
               tational effort where needed, thus improving language capabilities without gener-
019
               ating excessive tokens. Our experiments with various Pythia models demonstrate
020
               that the stutter mechanism consistently enhances performance across benchmark
021            datasets. Specifically, the Pythia-410M model, enhanced by our method, outper-
022            forms the larger Pythia-1B model on WinoGrande and WSC. Additionally, our
023            method is data-efficient, requiring only less than 1% of the pretraining data for
024            the additional training. These results highlight the stutter mechanism’s potential
025            to enhance LLMs’ efficiency and performance in real-world applications.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Decoder-only transformers (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020) have become the standard
031   for large language models. These models, such as GPT-3 and its successors, have demonstrated
032   remarkable capabilities in generating coherent and contextually relevant text across a wide range of
033
      applications, from natural language understanding to creative writing. The architecture’s simplicity,
      combined with its ability to scale effectively with increased data and computational resources, has
034
      made it the go-to choice for developing state-of-the-art language models. However, despite their
035
      success, there remains significant room for improvement, particularly in efficiency and adaptability
036   to varying input complexities.
037
038   Typically, a transformer processes all inputs with the same procedure. This uniform approach, while
039
      straightforward, does not account for the varying difficulty levels of different inputs or the specific
      quality requirements of the output. There have been a number of attempts aiming to dynamically
040
      adapt the operation flow of a transformer to the difficulty level of the input or the requirements on the
041
      quality of the output (Snell et al., 2024). These methods include techniques such as adaptive com-
042   putation time, where the model decides how many layers to apply based on the input’s complexity,
043   and dynamic layer skipping, which allows the model to bypass certain layers when they are deemed
044   unnecessary. Such approaches aim to make the model more efficient by allocating computational
045   resources more judiciously, thereby improving both speed and performance.
046
      Inspired by recent upscaling studies (Kim et al., 2024; Chowdhery et al., 2023), which have shown
047
      that larger models tend to perform better across a variety of tasks, we sought to explore ways to
048   enhance the language capabilities of existing transformer models without significantly increasing
049   their size. Upscaling studies have demonstrated that increasing the number of parameters and layers
050   in a model can lead to substantial improvements in performance. However, this comes at the cost
051   of increased memory requirements and training time. Our goal was to find a method that could
052   leverage the benefits of upscaling while mitigating its drawbacks, particularly in terms of efficiency
053   and memory consumption. While using pause tokens (Goyal et al., 2024) is an intuitive method
      that allows encoding the prefix in parallel, they require substantial computational resources and time


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      for pretraining on the entire C4 dataset. Similarly, the work ALBERT (Lan et al., 2020) focuses
055   on reducing memory usage by factorizing embedding parameters and sharing parameters across
056   layers. Our approach, however, aims to improve performance through the stutter mechanism, which
057   enhances the model’s ability to utilize prior information effectively.
058
      In this paper, we propose the stutter mechanism, a minimally intrusive method to dynamically raise
059
      the language ability of an existing transformer when needed. Our approach is based on the hypoth-
060
      esis that not all tokens are equally easy to generate; for at least some of them, a transformer can
061   do better by ”giving more thought” to an in-flight token by ”transforming” it with more operations.
062   It works as if a human speaker stutters when encountering a key diverging point in a speech. This
063   analogy captures the essence of our method: by selectively applying additional layers to more chal-
064   lenging tokens, the model can allocate more computational effort where it is most needed, thereby
065   improving overall performance without a significant increase in resource usage.
066
      This paper is about, once identified, how to apply more layers. It is compatible with any methods that
067   determine the tokens that deserve to be given more thoughts. This method is minimally intrusive,
068   requiring only minor modifications to the existing transformer architecture, and is highly effective
069   in enhancing the model’s language capabilities.
070
      We implemented our method on Pythia-160M, Pythia-410M, and Pythia-1B. Our experiment results
071
      show that the proposed methods effectively raised the accuracies of the Pythia models on the LAM-
072
      BADA (OpenAI), PIQA, WinoGrande, WSC, ARC-e, ARC-c, SciQ and LogiQA benchmarks. With
073   the help of the stutter mechanism, a smaller model can even outperform a much larger model.
074
075   Our contributions are threefold:
076          • Innovative Mechanism for Enhanced Language Capability: We introduce the stutter
077            mechanism, a novel and minimally intrusive method that dynamically allocates additional
078            computational resources to more challenging tokens. By leveraging specific transformer
079            layers to serve as a silent thinking process, our approach improves the model’s language
080            capabilities without significantly increasing resource usage. This mechanism is compatible
081            with existing methods for identifying tokens that require more computational effort, making
082            it a versatile addition to current transformer architectures.
083          • Performance Improvements on Various Benchmarks: We demonstrate that the stut-
084            ter mechanism significantly enhances the performance of transformer models on various
085            benchmarks. Specifically, our experiments show that the Pythia-410M model, enhanced by
086            the stutter mechanism, outperforms the larger Pythia-1B model on WinoGrande and WSC.
087
               These results highlight the practical effectiveness of our approach in real-world applica-
               tions.
088
089          • Data and Computational Efficiency: We show that only one billion tokens (less than 1%
090            of the pretraining data) are sufficient to train the stutter mechanism, reducing the computa-
091
               tion time and cost by a significant amount. Therefore, our method is not only effective but
               also practical for large-scale deployment.

095
096   In this section, an overview of key concepts and techniques relevant to the development of trans-
097   former models is provided. We discuss the architecture and scaling trends of decoder-only trans-
098
      formers, methods for upscaling and pruning, and approaches to improve computational efficiency.
      Additionally, we explore the loss functions used in training and the confidence levels of transformers
099
      in token prediction.

      2.1   D ECODER - ONLY TRANSFORMERS
102
103   The Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) series by OpenAI showcases the power of decoder-
104   only transformer architectures (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). GPT-2, released in 2019
105   with 1.5 billion parameters, demonstrated impressive text generation capabilities. GPT-3, introduced
106   in 2020, expanded to 175 billion parameters, significantly enhancing performance and enabling more
107   complex and accurate text generation. This progression highlights the trend that increasing model
      parameters leads to substantial performance improvements (Hoffmann et al., 2022).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      As the number of parameters increases, the depth of the model also tends to increase. For example,
109   GPT-2 has 48 layers, while GPT-3 scales up to 96 layers. This trend is also observed in various large
110   language models where more layers are added to accommodate the growing number of parameters,
111   thereby enhancing the model’s capacity to learn complex patterns and dependencies in the data
112   (Zhao et al., 2023). This scaling law is further supported by studies showing that larger models
113   continue to improve performance with increased size (Kaplan et al., 2020).
114
115   2.2   U PSCALING
116
117   While increasing the number of parameters and layers can enhance model performance, it also in-
118   troduces significant computational challenges. To address these challenges, upscaling methods are
119   employed to increase the parameter count and the depth of a transformer. These methods can be
120
      broadly categorized into training-free attempts and upscale-and-train attempts. Training-free up-
      scaling involves techniques such as parameter sharing and repeating layers without additional train-
121
      ing. Recently, merged LLMs have shown success in improving performance without re-training. An
122
      evolutionary algorithm is proposed in (Akiba et al., 2024) to search for a better merge combination
123   which is costly and limits the number of repetitions.
124
125   On the other hand, upscale-and-train methods involve increasing the model size and then training it
126
      on large datasets to achieve better performance. For instance, the SOLAR 10.7B model demon-
      strates effective depth upscaling techniques that significantly enhance model performance (Kim
127
      et al., 2024). Additionally, the authors in (Chowdhery et al., 2023) discuss how scaling pathways
128
      can be used to efficiently upscale models.
129
130
      2.3   L AYERS SKIPPING AND PRUNING
131
132   Despite the benefits of upscaling, the increased model size can lead to inefficiencies during infer-
133   ence. To decrease the runtime computational requirements of a transformer, various methods such
134   as layer skipping and pruning are employed. Layer skipping involves dynamically skipping certain
135   layers during inference based on the input data, thereby reducing the computational load. Pruning,
136   on the other hand, involves removing less important weights or neurons from the model, which can
137   significantly reduce the model size and inference time while conceding some performance. The
138   authors in (Fan et al., 2024) explore these techniques in detail, showing how selective layer usage
139   can maintain performance while reducing computational costs. Another approach proposed in (Liu
140
      et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022) demonstrates that layer sparsity can be contextualized, suggesting that
      not all layers are necessary for processing simpler input tokens. In addition, observations from (Ha-
141
      lawi et al., 2023) show that early-exiting in critical layers (around layer 28 in GPT2-XL) improves
142
      the model performance.
143
144
      2.4   H OW CONFIDENT IS A TRANSFORMER ON A GIVEN TOKEN
145
146   Understanding the training and inference processes is essential (Lieberum et al., 2024), but it is
147   equally important to evaluate the model’s confidence in its predictions. The confidence of a trans-
148   former on a given token can be measured by the probability distribution it outputs for the next token
149   prediction. Studies have shown that transformers can generate high-confidence predictions for cer-
150   tain tokens, which can be used to gauge the model’s certainty in its predictions. While there are
151   extensive studies on the overall performance of transformers in generating sequences, there is on-
152   going research to understand the confidence levels at the token level. For example, authors in (Sun
153   et al., 2024; Lad et al., 2024) discuss the confidence and interpretability of transformer layers in
154
      generating specific tokens. Additionally, the study delves into how models process and generate
      tokens with varying levels of confidence (Halawi et al., 2023).

205
206   The stutter mechanism is designed to enhance the model’s ability to process and understand a spe-
207   cific token n by performing the inference for that token twice. This approach allows the model to
208
      ”think again” about the token, leveraging additional semantic information gathered during the first
      pass.
209
210   As illustrated in Figure 1, in the first pass, the model processes the token n by doing standard
211   self-attention with token 0 : n − 1 and stores the hidden state hnl∗ , which captures the semantic
212   information of the token. Research indicates that the last layer of a transformer model behaves
213   differently from other layers (Lad et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023), often filtering out a lot of information

256   3.3     T RAINING
257
258   To train the proposed architecture, we start with an existing transformer and freeze all its weights
259   except those in the token-retrospect map. Our primary objective is to demonstrate the effectiveness
260
      of the stutter mechanism, so the selection of specific tokens to stutter is beyond the scope of this
      paper. Therefore, during training, we stutter every token exactly once.
261
262   For the training set preparation, we perform a pass of the training sequence X through the inherited
263   transformer to capture h0:N
                                l∗ . Following this, we train the stutter transformer by stuttering at every
264   token. In the new training model, each layer is augmented by attending to the additional input.
265   Specifically, additional parameters in the token-retrospect map are introduced for this purpose. Dur-
266
      ing the training process, only the additional attention parameters are trained, while the rest of the
      network remains frozen.
267
268   The number of additional parameters is the same as the pretrained self-attention parameters, which
269   constitute only 10% of the entire model. Since the number of parameters does not increase sig-
      nificantly, it doesn’t require a large amount of data for training. In our experiment, performance

282   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
283
284   We utilized ”The Pile (Gao et al., 2020)” as our training dataset, a large-scale and diverse text cor-
285   pus originally segmented into 30 compressed files, each containing approximately 7 million sam-
286   ples. Note that all existing weights of the transformers are frozen, and only weights of the newly
287   introduced token-retrospect map are trainable. Since the token-retrospect map has significantly
288   fewer parameters than the original model, we randomly selected a subset of ”The Pile” for training.
289
      Specifically, we used 1 billion tokens to train our token-retrospect map. Following the Pythia (Bi-
      derman et al., 2023) model’s approach, we employed a parallel training setting where hidden states,
290
      MLP outputs, and attention outputs are combined. In line with this setting, we also integrated our
291
      token-retrospect outputs to further enhance the model’s performance.
292
293   For our experiments, we utilized 4 NVIDIA A6000 GPUs to train the stutter mechanism. The
294   training dataset consisted of 1 billion tokens, which were trained for 1 epoch. We employed a
295
      learning rate of 5e-5, using a cosine scheduler with a warmup ratio of 0.01. The optimizer used was
      Adam, and we set the gradient accumulation steps to 8.
296
297   In the initial pass of the model with L layers, we store the hidden states of the (L − 1)-th layer for
298   each token. The token-retrospect map was initialized using Gaussian initialization with a mean of 0
299   and a standard deviation of 1e-5 before the stuttering process. We store our checkpoint models every
300   5000 steps and evaluate them on the LAMBADA (OpenAI) dataset as the in-training evaluation.
301
      Stuttering is enabled for all tokens during inference and each token is only allowed to repeat once.

472

473
      insights of the transformer and to evaluate the impact of different layers on the model’s performance.
474
475   As shown by our results in Figure 3, our findings suggest that attending at specific intermediate
476   positions can indeed boost performance. While attending to layer 10 and layer 11 yields similar
477
      performance, layer 12 generally results in lower improvements across tasks except for LogiQA. As
      indicated by previous work (Lad et al., 2024), the last layer filters out some semantic information
478

479

480   benchmarks, is improved significantly by attending the last layer. One explanation is domain knowl-
481   edge is stored in a specific layer. Another explanation is excluding some of the information is helpful
482   in tasks like LogiQA, where all the options are quite similar and confusing. Notably, We observe
483   consistent declines in performance in the ARC-C benchmark regardless of the attended layer. That
484   is because this dataset contains many lengthy options. While the stutter mechanism performs well
485   in providing short answers, the additional stutter mechanism might weaken the logits contributed by
      the attention mechanism, resulting in inferior performance for long-context benchmarks.

      5                             C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
503
      We propose the stutter mechanism that effectively enhances the performance of LLMs by facilitating
504
      an extended thinking process. This approach not only addresses the limitations of increasing model
505   sizes but also optimizes computational efficiency by tailoring the processing requirements to the
506   complexities of different tasks. Our extensive experiments with various Pythia models demonstrate
507   that the stutter mechanism consistently improves performance across benchmark datasets. With the
508   help of the stutter mechanism, a smaller model can even outperform a much larger model. While our
509   proposed method has shown promising results in enhancing the language capabilities of transformer
510   models, there are several avenues for future research and development that could further improve
511   and extend our approach. Here, we outline some potential directions for future work:
512
                                     • Efficient Repeating Mechanism: Future work could optimize the repeating mechanism by
513
                                       dynamically determining the exact number of layers each token needs, rather than applying
514
                                       the entire network. This real-time assessment would minimize unnecessary computations,
515                                    enhancing efficiency and performance by adapting more precisely to input complexities.
516
                                     • Different Ways of Heuristic: Refining heuristics for the ”stuttering” mechanism is another
517
                                       key area. Using fine-tuning or Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF),
518                                    we can develop smarter heuristics to decide when to stutter, how many times, and when to
519                                    stop, making the model more adaptive and effective in handling reasoning tasks.
520
                                     • Interpreting Reasoning Mechanism: Understanding how LLMs reason is crucial for
521
                                       building trust and transparency in AI systems. By analyzing attention distributions, we can
522                                    identify which attention heads contribute most to reasoning ability. This insight can help us
523                                    understand the internal mechanisms of LLMs and how they process information to arrive
524                                    at conclusions. Future work could focus on developing methods to visualize and interpret
525                                    these attention patterns, potentially guiding further improvements in model design.

630   A.1    T IME C OMPLEXITY OF S TUTTER M ECHANISM
631
632
      Training: In our current setting in training, we stutter at every token. Our method employs a two-
      pass process. For a given sample X = x1 , x2 , . . . , xn :
633
634          1. First Pass: We store the hidden states h1l∗ , h2l∗ , . . . , hnl∗ for each token in the sequence.
635
             2. Second Pass (Stutter Phase): During this phase, we utilize the stored hidden states by
636
                passing both the stored hidden states and the current token to the model. For instance,
637             to generate the final prediction for the next token of x3 , we inputx2 and h2l∗ to the stutter
638             model. The original Feed-Forward (FF) and Attention (Attn) mechanisms perform the same
639             as in the base model, where x2 will attend on x1 . Our token retrospect mechanism performs
640             linear attention using x2 and h2l∗ to extract information from the first pass to generate the
641             next token.
642
643   If the base model has time complexity O(n), the time complexity of the stutter model is 2 × O(n)
644
      since we run each sample twice, remaining the same time complexity as the base model.
645   Inference: In the benchmark dataset we use for inference, the only token we use for stutter is the
646   choice of the samples. In the Lambada-openai dataset, it only predicts the last token. In the multiple-
647   choice dataset, the only tokens we stutter are the choice tokens. Two examples from each type of
      dataset is given below:

733
      When fine-tuning large language models, it is common practice to use around 1 billion tokens. In
734   our approach, we freeze the original model weights and only train the token-retrospect part, which
735   consists of about 10% of the original parameters. This strategy allows us to efficiently fine-tune the
736   model without the need for extensive computational resources.
737
      To further investigate the impact of training on larger datasets, we conducted a series of experiments
738
      where we continually trained the token-retrospect map on increasing amounts of data. The training
739
      remained stable over extended periods and larger datasets, but the efficiency of additional training
740   diminished. This indicates that our method is robust and does not require excessive amounts of data
741   to achieve optimal performance.
742
743
      The results of these experiments are summarized in Table 7, which shows the performance metrics
      for models trained on 1 billion and 2 billion tokens. As shown, the improvements from training on 2
744
      billion tokens are minimal, reinforcing the idea that our approach achieves a good balance between
745
      efficiency and performance with 1 billion tokens.
746
747   In conclusion, while the stutter mechanism can benefit from additional training data, the marginal
748   gains observed beyond 1 billion tokens suggest that our method is efficient and effective with a
749
      relatively modest amount of training data. This efficiency makes our approach particularly suitable
      for scenarios where computational resources are limited.
