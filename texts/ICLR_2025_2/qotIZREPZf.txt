009                                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011            Line-search methods are commonly used to solve optimization problems. The
012            simplest line search method is the steepest descent where we always move in
013            the direction of the negative gradient. Newton’s method on the other hand is a
014            second-order method that uses the curvature information in the Hessian to pick
015            the descent direction. In this work, we propose a new line-search method called
016            Constrained Gradient Descent (CGD) that implicitly changes the landscape of the
017
               objective function for efficient optimization. CGD is formulated as a solution to
               the constrained version of the original problem where the constraint is on a func-
018
               tion of the gradient. We optimize the corresponding Lagrangian function thereby
019
               favourably changing the landscape of the objective function. This results in a line
020            search procedure where the Lagrangian penalty acts as a control over the descent
021            direction and can therefore be used to iterate over points that have smaller gradient
022            values, compared to iterates of vanilla steepest descent. We reinterpret and draw
023            parallels with the Explicit Gradient Regularization (EGR) method, discussing its
024            drawbacks and potential enhancements. Numerical experiments are conducted on
025            synthetic test functions to illustrate the performance of CGD and its variants.

048   Deep Neural Networks have been successful at a variety of tasks primarily due to their over-
049   parameterization. However, with such over-parameterization comes the problem of an uneven loss
050   landscape that contains many local maxima and minima with some providing better performance
051   than the others (Neyshabur et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018). As a result, optimizing over the param-
052   eter space has become incredibly relevant in the current times. To tackle this problem, one of the
053   approach that is used is to modify the loss/objective function in such a way that the new function
      is easier to optimize. This is typically done by adding a regularization term that will make the

054
      loss landscape more smooth, and which would in turn reduce the chances of the optimizer reaching
055   sub-optimal minima, thereby improving generalizability. Considerable research in deep learning is
056   now focused on designing loss functions that have smoother landscapes and therefore are easier to
057   optimize (Du et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2022; Barrett & Dherin, 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Karakida
058   et al., 2023).
059
      In this work, we focus on this idea of modifying the objective function for effective numerical
060
      optimization and propose an algorithmic paradigm to achieve it. To illustrate the key idea, consider
061   minimizing a loss function f1 (·) over R2 as shown in Figure 1 (Left). Additionally consider a steeper
062   loss function f2 (·) such that for every x ∈ R2 we have f1 (x) ≤ f2 (x). Note that the functions are
063   such that their minima coincides. Now perform gradient descent (GD) with a fixed step size on both
064   these functions. Their respective trajectories are plotted in Figure 1 (Right) on the contours of f1 . It
065   is clear from the figure that the iterates of GD on f2 are much closer to the minima as compared to
066   iterates of GD on f1 . We make this observation a focal point of this work and investigate if one can
067   achieve the latter iterates (iterates from GD on f2 ) on the former function (f1 ).
068   Towards this, we propose Constraint gradient descent (CGD), a variant of the GD algorithm which
069   achieves this by constraining the gradient norm to be appropriately small. Instead of solving this
070   constrained optimization problem, we consider the Lagrangian of this function and perform GD
071   on it. The Lagrangian parameter λ controls the penalty on gradient norm and thereby controls the
072   steepness of the modified function. It is on this modified function that we seek to apply GD to
073   possibly attain iterates that are much closer to the local minima as compared to GD iterates. From
074   Figure 1 (Right) we additionally see that compared to GD, the CGD iterates (which is nothing but
075
      GD iterates on f2 ) have a lower gradient norm. Since the path to minima is over points with lower
      gradient norm, this we believe is an attractive feature and even amounts to better generalization
076
      properties for high dimensional functions such as loss landscapes of neural networks.
077
078   The idea of flat minima and their attractiveness goes long back to Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997):
079   An optimal minimum is considered “flat” if the test error changes less in its neighbourhood. Keskar
080   et al. (2017) and Chaudhari et al. (2017) observe better generalization results for neural networks
081
      at flat minima. Gradient regularization on the other hand has only been recently studied from a
      perspective of deep neural networks (DNNs) (Zhao et al., 2022; Karakida et al., 2023; Barrett &
082
      Dherin, 2022; Smith et al., 2021) where the square of the L2 norm is penalized.
083
084   While the above methods only penaliize the squared L2 -norm of the gradient, we propose a more
085   general approach where the penalty could be on any positive function of the gradient. This opens
086   doors to investigating potential applications where penalty functions beyond squared L2 norm might
087
      prove more beneficial. A key aim of this work is to understand this gradient regularization from the
      perspective of a numerical optimization algorithm and identify properties and features that may not
088
      have been obvious earlier. We believe CGD and its variants discussed in this work have the ability
089
      to find flatter minimas which may prove to be useful for training of neural networks.
090
091   We summarize our contributions below:
092
093          • We propose a new line-search procedure called Constraint Gradient Descent (CGD) that
094
               performs gradient descent on a gradient regularized loss function.
095          • While CGD requires the Hessian information, we also propose a first-order variant of CGD
096            using finite-difference approximation of the Hessian called CGD-FD. We define appropri-
097            ate stopping criteria in CGD-FD for settings where gradient computation can be expensive.
098
099          • We conduct experiments over synthetic test functions to compare the performance of CGD
100
               and its variants compared to standard line-search procedures.
101          • Our work also provides new insights to gradient regularization based methods. In fact, we
102            re-interpret and identify pitfalls in Explicit Gradient Regularization (EGR) Methods using
103            our formulation.
104
105   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In the next section, we recall some preliminaries on
106   line-search methods. We then discuss the CGD algorithm and propose its variants. We then illustrate
107   the performance of our algorithm on several test functions and conclude with a discussion on future
      directions.

123   i.e., we wish to find x∗ ∈ D such that
124

      We focus on the situation where x∗ is obtained using an iterative line-search procedure (for details
127
      refer Nocedal & Wright (2006, Ch. 3)). The steepest descent (gradient descent) method, Newton’s
128   method, and quasi-Newton’s method are some examples of line-search methods. In an iterative
129   algorithm, the key idea is to begin with an initial guess x0 for the minimizer and generate a sequence
130   of vectors x0 , x1 , . . . until convergence (or until desired level of accuracy is achieved). In such
131   algorithms, xk+1 is obtained using xk using a pre-defined update rule. For line-search methods, the
132   update rule can be written in a general form as follows,
133
                                                xk+1 = xk + αpk                                            (2)

135   where α ∈ R+ is the step size (or learning rate) and pk ∈ Rn corresponds to the direction in the k-th

137   For example, for steepest gradient descent method we have Pk = −In and for Newton’s method
138
      Pk = −Hk−1 . For pk to be a descent direction, the following condition must hold:
139                                                 ∇fkT pk < 0.                                           (3)

      In this section, we propose an iterative line-search method to find a minimizer x∗ of the given
      function f (x) (see Equation 1). We shall refer to our approach as Constrained Gradient Descent
145
      (CGD) method. It is known that if x∗ is a local minimizer of the function f (x) then the gradient
146
      at point x∗ is equal to zero (Nocedal & Wright, 2006, Ch. 2). The key idea in our approach is to
147   focus on the set of x ∈ D such that ∇f (x) is close to zero. For this consider a general constrained
148   optimization problem:
149                                         x∗ = arg min f (x)                                          (4)
150                                                     x∈D

      where the constraint h(·) is defined on the gradient and ϵ is a small positive real number. The uncon-
      strained optimization problem corresponding to Equation 4 is obtained by penalizing the gradient
153
      constraint with a Lagrange multiplier λ > 0:
154                                                  h                       i
155                                   x⋆ = arg min f (x) + λ h (∇f (x))                                  (5)
156                                           x∈D
157   Note that ϵ doesn’t affect the optimization and hence has been removed from the objective. Observe
158   that such a formulation provides us with a modified loss function to optimize over. Choosing a non-
159   negative constraint h(·) is preferred so that we do not introduce artificial minima points. In this case,
160   the modified loss function will become steeper and hence easier to descent on than the original loss
161   function. This can also be referred to by penalization or using a gradient penalty since the objective
      is penalized at points which are not local minima depending on the gradients at these points.

      EGR has the drawbacks of having fictitious minima identified in Lemma 1. Since, the loss landscape
455   of neural networks is highly uneven (Li et al., 2018), it’s likely that artificial stationary points are
456   introduced and the optimizer might converge at local-maxima points since their nature changes
457   with regularization. For example, in Figure 4 (Right) we observe artificial stationary points being
458   introduced between local-minima and local-maxima of the original loss function while some local-
459   maxima also turn into local-minima of the penalized loss function. Therefore, one needs to ensure
460   that there are suitable fixes for these scenarios for better performance. Specifically, the direction
461   along of the penalized loss function, −∇g(·) should be a descent direction and that we shouldn’t
462   stop at points where ∇g(·) = 0 but ∇f (·) ̸= 0 as discussed in Lemma 1.
463   Another downfall with EGR is that it requires hessian evaluation for each mini-batch while training.
464   This might become a costly operation for bigger models and hence some approximation of the
465   hessian should be used. Another thing we observe from the experimental results for CGD-FD, is
466   that the improvment through optimizing the regularized function is mostly only during the initial
467   steps. Hence, one should only use EGR for some initial steps to reach a good-enough starting point
468   while not exhausting the budget for gradient evaluations.

      In this work, we considered a new line swarch method that penalizes the norm of the gradient and
      provides iterates that have lower gradient norm compared to vanilla gradient descent. We identify
474
      properties of this algorithm, provide a varient that does not require Hessian and illustrate connections
475
      to the widely popular explicit gradient regularization literature.

477   There are several future directions arising from this work. We would like to investigate in greater

      optimization. We hope this work sputters more discussions on gradient regularization helping in
