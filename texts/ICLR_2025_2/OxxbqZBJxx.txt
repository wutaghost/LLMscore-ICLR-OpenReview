009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The surging interest in learning from preference data has resulted in an elaborate
012            landscape of methods and evaluations. This work offers a framework to simplify
013            this landscape, starting from the underlying sampling distribution for preference
014            data. First, we show that the only evaluation of a generative model that is grounded
015            in the preference data sampling distribution is win rate. Given that win rate is all
016
               that can matter from preference data alone, we relate common preference learning
               algorithms to direct win rate optimization (DWRO). We outline the theoretical ben-
017
               efits of RLHF as a variant of DWRO; explain why checkpointing is difficult with
018
               DPO as a non-DWRO objective; and characterize the limits of SFT on preferred
019            samples with regard to the extent of win rate improvement possible. Furthermore,
020            we provide closed-form expressions for the expected win rate improvement of the
021            above objectives, formalizing the role of a model’s starting point in the win rate im-
022            provement possible. Finally, we conduct an empirical analysis of existing methods
023            and alternative DWRO objectives which suggests that optimization improvements
024            are likely key to advancing preference learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Learning from preference data, often referred to as human feedback, has emerged as a key step in
029
      training large language models, particularly given the success of reinforcement learning from human
030   feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017) on state-of-the-art and high-profile language models
031   such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2024). The goal of learning from preference data is to finetune powerful
032   base language models to output generations more in line with human preferences (Stiennon et al.,
033   2020; Ouyang et al., 2022), motivated by the fact that pretraining on internet-scale data has enabled
034   large language models to exhibit fluent generations of text (Minaee et al., 2024) but not necessarily
035   responses aligned with what humans prefer to see.
036   In recent years, the space of algorithms and evaluations for preference learning has grown substantially
037   (Kaufmann et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024), resulting in a complex landscape of methods and analyses.
038   Consequently, it can be difficult to pinpoint a clear, overarching framework to compare disparate
039   works and to guide progress. How can we understand preference learning from the ground up?
040
      A common approach to describe preference learning is to focus on the historical development of
041
      methods in the space. Such a description typically starts with reinforcement learning from human
042   feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017), which involves learning a reward model from preference
043   data and optimizing the language model policy to maximize the learned rewards. Follow-up works can
044   generally be described as efforts to improve upon RLHF, among which direct preference optimization
045   (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) was developed to estimate the same solution but via a single step
046   of direct finetuning rather than a two-step procedure of RLHF. In other words, the landscape of
047   preference learning can often be described by how methods relate to each other, typically connecting
048   back to RLHF as a focal point. In this work, in contrast, we ask: is there another way to understand
049   preference learning that does not center around the earliest or most popular methods?
050   We address this question by developing a framework for understanding the endeavor of learning from
051   preferences starting from the sampling distribution implied by pairwise preference data. We first show
052   that the only evaluation of a generative model rooted in the preference data sampling distribution
053   itself is win rate; any other notions of good are a function of outside assumptions (Section 3).
      From this insight, we introduce a win rate-centric framework for understanding preference learning

054
      (Section 4, Section 5). Given win rate is the only relevant evaluation without additional assumptions,
055   we relate common preference learning algorithms (RLHF, DPO, SFT) to directly optimizing for win
056   rate, outlining benefits of RLHF for being a variant of direct win rate optimization (DWRO) and
057   limitations of DPO and SFT for not. Namely, as a variant of DWRO, RLHF confers benefits from
058   (1) optimizing train loss corresponds to optimizing for the test evaluation we care about (up to noise
059   and overfitting), which is not true of DPO and related variants; to (2) its solution can achieve the
060   maximum win rate possible over a competitor as regularization strength goes to zero, unlike SFT.
061   These insights are relevant not just to the three methods discussed, but also for other methods in
062   the same family (e.g., DWRO variants other than RLHF, Direct Alignment Algorithms other than
063
      DPO). We then conduct an empirical comparison of different methods which offers complementary
      insights to the theoretical analysis (Section 6). Namely, we show that despite their theoretical benefits,
064
      direct win rate optimization methods underperform relative to expectations due to difficulties in
065
      optimization. We conclude by discussing the takeaways from our combined theoretical and empirical
066   analysis (Section 7). These include explanations to ground specific strategies or current practices in
067   preference learning, as well as guidance for future research.

      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
070         1. We prove that the only evaluation of a generative model grounded in the preference data
071            distribution is win rate. This result justifies using win rate as a singular focal point to
072            understand the landscape of preference learning.
073

074            combined theoretical and empirical analysis under this framework, we:
075
                (a) present theoretical benefits of methods which directly optimize for win rate (e.g.,
076
                     RLHF) and limitations of methods which do not (e.g., DPO, SFT);
077
                (b) experimentally demonstrate the challenges of optimizing for win rate and the central
078
                     role of optimization success for the performance of different methods;
079
                (c) discuss implications for the current practice of choosing methods;
080
                (d) bring attention to connections between preference learning and probabilistic inference

118
119   Here, we outline the setup for learning from pairwise preferences. We first describe the underlying
120   sampling distribution of the data used in preference learning (Section 3.1). Then we show why based
121   on the sampling distribution alone, the only evaluation which respects the underlying preference
122   environment and sampling prevalences is win rate (Section 3.2).
123
124   3.1   T HE S AMPLING D ISTRIBUTION
125
      The goal of preference learning is to learn a generative model that performs well in a given context.
126
      However, whereas typical maximum likelihood training employs samples from the distribution of
127
      interest, the setup of preference learning does not: only samples from generation competitors and
128   their relative preference under the query-preference environment are available.
129
130   The sampling distribution for preferences involves input x, candidate outputs y0 and y1 , and a label
131
      ℓ ∈ {0, 1} denoting which of y0 or y1 is preferred. Let ℓ = 1 denote that y1 is preferred, and ℓ = 0
      denote that y0 is preferred. Then, the overall sampling distribution can be defined as follows:
132
133   Definition 1. A sampling distribution for (pairwise) preference learning is a distribution over input
134
      x ∈ X , candidate outputs y0 , y1 ∈ Y, and preference label ℓ ∈ {0, 1} defined by:
135         1. Query distribution: p(x)
136
137
            2. Generation competitor 0: p(y0 | x)
138         3. Generation competitor 1: p(y1 | x)
139
            4. Preference classifier: p(ℓ | x, y0 , y1 ).
140
141   1, 2, and 3 are user-specified distributions; 1 denotes the inputs of interest, and 2 and 3 are the
142   candidate competitors one chooses to evaluate. 4 is only distribution that cannot be directly specified,
143   rather it is defined by the environment in which the user choose to collect the preferences.
144
      Generation competitor 0 and 1 can be the same distribution and often are in existing open-source
145
      preference datasets (Lambert et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2022).

394
395   Here, we compare RLHF, DPO, and SFT, in order to complement our above theoretical analysis
396   with an empirical one. In particular, rather than compare the conventional two-stage SFT + RLHF or
397   DPO, we compare all three methods on the same footing by how much they are able to improve win
398   rate over the starting model. Moreover, as RLHF is just one version of a KL-regularized direct win
399   rate optimization objective, we also compare different variants of DWRO-KL. We vary h (identity,
400   log, and logit), β (1, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001), and the estimation of the preference classifier (perfect and
401   estimated with and without the Bradley Terry assumption).
402
403   6.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
404
      We employ Pythia-2.8b (Biderman et al., 2023) as our base model and the OpenAssistant (OASST)
405
      (Kopf et al., 2023) and Anthropic Helpfulness and Harmlessness (HH) (Bai et al., 2022) datasets
406
      for preference annotations. We train the base models on the data outputs and use these finetuned
407   models as our initial models. To simulate a preference environment, we train an oracle judge model
408   per dataset to estimate p(ℓ = 1 | x, y0 , y1 ) and relabel the preference annotations in the dataset using
409   this judge model. We use this same oracle to evaluate win rate after training. See Appendix I for
410   further details on the judge model. We additionally train 1. a reward model on the oracle-labeled
411   judge annotations (accuracy is 82.8 for OASST and 81.36 for HH) and 2. an imperfect judge model
412   (accuracy is 80.47 for OASST and 85.16 for HH). As expected, the BT assumption is helpful for
413   preference classifier estimation in OASST but not in HH, as the OASST directly abides by this
414   assumption (outputs are globally ranked) whereas HH does not explicitly. For optimization, we use
415
      the PPO algorithm implemented in the TRL library (von Werra et al., 2020). See Appendix J for
      additional training and evaluation details.
416
417   Implementing DWRO-KL. Different variants of DWRO-KL can be implemented as different
418   choices for the function used to score outputs. Namely, each can be written in the form
419   maxθ Ep(x) Epθ (y | x) [ψ(x, y) − β log pθ (y | x) + β log pref (y | x)] for different choices of ψ. All ex-
420   pectations over generator distributions use a single-sample Monte-Carlo estimate. Concretely, for
421
      given query we sample one response each from the current and original model to compute the score,
      which is either a function of 1. the preference probability under the judge model which takes in
422
      the pair as input, or 2. the BT preference probability under the reward model which takes each
423
      sequence in as input separately. The one exception is DWRO-KL-logit-BT (i.e., RLHF), where we
424   drop Ep(y0 |x) [r(x, y0 )] and only optimize r(x, y).

      7    D ISCUSSION
511
512
513   Amidst the increasingly complex landscape around aligning language models to human preferences,
514
      this work provides a simple insight: win rate is all that can matter from preference data alone.
      We first illustrate that the only evaluation grounded in preference data alone is win rate under the
515
      preference classifier dictated by the data, which means that the only goal for preference learning that
516
      is based in the preference data distribution itself is win rate. This insight prompts us to explain how
517   common procedures in preference learning relate to win rate optimization. We see that, based on
518   their relationship to DWRO, RLHF should be preferred over DPO and SFT. In experiments, however,
519   we see that ease of optimization plays an important role in a method’s success. In this regard, SFT is
520   easiest to optimize, then DPO and finally RLHF.
521
522   What’s the value of so many preference learning algorithms? Our analysis may seem to suggest
523   that there is little benefit in developing many different preference learning methods; after all, directly
524   optimizing for win rate seems to be the theoretical ideal, and different choices for DWRO objectives
525   do not seem to currently play a significant role empirically. However, the combined overall theoretical
526   and empirical analysis paint a different picture: notably, no existing method is optimal with respect to
527   both the objective being optimized and the ease of optimization, suggesting why it might be useful to
528
      combine methods (i.e., to take advantage of different strengths) as well as develop new ones (i.e., to
      strike a better overall balance).
529
530
531
      What’s next for preference learning? This work offers several takeaways for future work. First
      and foremost, the analysis suggests that the most important improvements in preference learning will
532
      likely fall under the umbrella of moving closer directly optimizing for win rate, either in the objective
533
      itself or the practical optimization of it. How might we be able to make progress on either front?
534   For one, the connection between RLHF / DWRO-KL objectives and variational inference suggests
535   a rich field of inquiry to draw upon from the latter (variational inference, probabilistic inference)
536   to improve the former (preference learning), from variance reduction techniques (Mohamed et al.,
537   2020b) to alternative optimization objectives and algorithms altogether (e.g. (Naesseth et al., 2020)).
538   There are likely many other promising directions for future inquiry; as long as future work focuses on
539   connecting to the central goal of (better) win rate optimization, there is amply opportunity to advance
      the endeavor of learning from preference data.

1001
       The model is trained with a language modeling loss on the output, which is either ‘A‘ or ‘B‘. For
1002

1003

1006   is trained with a maximum sequence length of 512 and a maximum input length of 511. On the
