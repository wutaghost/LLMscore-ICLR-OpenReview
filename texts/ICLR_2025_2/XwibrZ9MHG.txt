024                                                   A BSTRACT
025
026                Data-driven methods have shown great potential in solving challenging manipula-
027                tion tasks, however, their application in the domain of deformable objects has been
028                constrained, in part, by the lack of data. To address this, we propose PokeFlex,
029                a dataset featuring real-world paired and annotated multimodal data that includes
030
                   3D textured meshes, point clouds, RGB images, and depth maps. Such data can
                   be leveraged for several downstream tasks such as online 3D mesh reconstruction,
031
                   and it can potentially enable underexplored applications such as the real-world
032
                   deployment of traditional control methods based on mesh simulations. To deal
033                with the challenges posed by real-world 3D mesh reconstruction, we leverage a
034                professional volumetric capture system that allows complete 360° reconstruction.
035                PokeFlex consists of 17 deformable objects with varying stiffness and shapes. De-
036                formations are generated by dropping objects onto a flat surface or by poking the
037                objects with a robot arm. Interaction forces and torques are also reported for the
038                latter case. Using different data modalities, we demonstrated a use case for our
039                dataset in online 3D mesh reconstruction. We refer the reader to our website1 or
040                the password protected supplementary material2 for further demos and examples.

050   loop execution of control methods based on mesh simulations (Duenser et al., 2018). In light of

083   Current state-of-the-art simulation methods can be an attractive alternative to collect such datasets as
084   they provide easy access to privileged information such as deformed mesh configurations and contact
085   forces (Tripicchio et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2022; Macklin, 2022; Qiao et al., 2021; Todorov et al.,
086   2012; Faure et al., 2012). However, such simulators require careful system identification and fine-
087
      tuning to address the sim-to-real gap, which ultimately requires real-world data. Static scans rotating
      around the scene (Pai et al., 2001; Garcia-Camacho et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2024) or custom multi-
088
      camera systems (Chen et al., 2022) can be used to collect real-world 3D models. The former can
089
      be excessively time-consuming and is unsuitable to capture temporal dynamics. The latter requires
090   careful synchronization and data curation, especially when using noisy lower-cost sensors.
091
092   To address these challenges, we leverage a professional multi-view volumetric capture system
093
      (MVS) that allows capturing detailed 360° mesh reconstructions of deformable objects over time
      (Collet et al., 2015), which we use as ground-truth meshes. We integrate a robotic manipulator with
094
      joint-torque sensing capabilities into the MVS, enabling contact force estimation and facilitating
095
      automated data collection. Moreover, to enhance reproducibility and to expand the diversity of data
096   modalities, we also integrate and synchronize lower-cost Azure Kinect and Intel RealSense D405
097   RGB-D sensors into the MVS.

099
      Our work proposes the PokeFlex dataset (Figure 1), featuring the real-world behavior of 17 de-
      formable objects, including everyday and 3D-printed objects. Deformations are generated via con-
100
      trolled poking and dropping protocols. An overview of the paired, synchronized, and annotated
101
      data is presented in Table 1, and illustrated in Figure 2. We demonstrated a use case of the Poke-
102   Flex dataset, proposing baseline models capable of ingesting PokeFlex multimodal data. We present
103   evaluation criteria for benchmarking the results. Specifically, we train neural network models for
104   deformed mesh reconstructions based on template meshes and various input data modalities, includ-
105   ing images, point clouds, end-effector poses and forces. The proposed architectures are suitable for
106   online applications, reconstructing 3D meshes at a range from 106 Hz to 215 Hz depending on the
107   input data modality, on a desktop PC with an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. The pretrained models will
      be available with the PokeFlex dataset.

120   2     R ELATED WORK
121
122   Deformable object datasets. Depending on the use of synthetic or real-world data, deformable
123   object datasets can be roughly categorized into two major groups. Huang et al. (2022), for in-
124   stance, evaluates multiple grasping poses for deformable objects on a large-scale synthetic dataset.
125   Qualitative sim-to-real experiments for such dataset, show that their simulator captures the general
126
      deformation behavior of objects during grasping. Similarly, Lu et al. (2024) introduces a simulation
      environment and benchmark for deformable object and garment manipulation, incorporating static
127
      scans of real-world objects to generate simulation models. Notably, they also scan 3 plush toys in
128
      static configurations. However, careful system identification and parameter tuning are necessary to
129   achieve higher sim-to-real fidelity for synthetic datasets.
130
131   On the other hand, real-world data collection opens up the door to better capture the complex behav-
132
      ior of deformable objects. Current real-world datasets focus mostly on RGB images. HMDO (Xie
      et al., 2023) also provides real-world 3D meshes for objects undergoing deformation due to hand
133
      manipulation. However, they fell short of providing point cloud or force contact information. Chen
134
      et al. (2022) provides points clouds and force contact information but it does not perform 3D mesh
135   reconstruction and the deformations are only globally produced using an airstream. Li et al. (2024)
136   offer a large number of frames, however, the object diversity in their dataset is limited. Zhang et al.
137   (2024) presents a pilot dataset with only one type of deformable object under quasi-static deforma-
138   tion, limited camera views, and no reported interaction forces.
139
      In a departure from other datasets, PokeFlex offers a more comprehensive list of features including;
140
      3D meshes, point clouds, contact forces, higher diversity of objects, and multiple types of defor-
141   mations as detailed in Table 2. For simplicity, we report only the effective number of paired time
142   frames in our table, in contrast to what is reported by Xie et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2024), where
143   the total number of samples is computed as the number of time frames times the number of cameras.
144
      Data-driven mesh reconstruction methods vary widely in terms of the input data modalities they
145
      employ. Previous approaches that rely on point clouds to predict deformations are typically trained
146
      on synthetic data (Amin Mansour et al., 2024; Lei & Daniilidis, 2022; Niemeyer et al., 2019). While
147   synthetic training data offers controlled and dense point cloud representations, it often leads to a
148   sim-to-real gap as real-world point cloud measurements tend to be noisy and sparse, especially
149   in dynamic and unstructured environments. In contrast, methods using single images as input have
150   gained attention for their real-world reconstruction capability without the need for depth information
151   (Wang et al., 2021; Jack et al., 2019; Kanazawa et al., 2018). However, many of these image-
152   based approaches are not optimized for online inference, making them unsuitable for downstream
153   applications in robotics, where online feedback is essential. For instance, Xu et al. (2024) proposes
154   an instant image-to-3D framework to generate high-quality 3D assets, but requires up to 10 seconds
155
      per frame, limiting its practicality for scenarios demanding real-time processing.

202

203
      signal. In addition to the MVS control system, we use two desktop PCs to read the additional
204   data streams: a Robot PC that reads the robot data and the streams of the two RealSense D405
205   cameras and a dedicated Kinect PC that reads the streams of the two Azure Kinect devices. The
206   robot PC is synchronized with the capture system by reading the same LTC signal provided by the
207   Atomos Ultrasync device. The Kinect cameras are hardware-synchronized with each other. Their
208   synchronization with the capture system is achieved retrospectively by comparing the current time-
209   code displayed on a screen in the camera frames of the Kinect and the camera frames of the capture
210   system. An overview of the architecture is illustrated on Figure 4 (Right).
211   We utilize a system similar to that described by Collet et al. (2015) to reconstruct the meshes and tex-
212   tures of the objects under deformation. When recording at 30 fps, the MVS generates approximately
213   27 GB of raw data per second. This data is then processed using commercial software provided by
214   Acturus Studio on 10x On-Prem Nodes servers, achieving an output rate of approximately one 3D
215   frame per minute. The authors curate the reconstructed meshes and textures to ensure that only the
      deformable objects are retained in the scene.

      3.2    L EARNING - BASED M ESH R ECONSTRUCTION
233
234
235   We leverage PokeFlex to train models for template-based mesh reconstruction, where we infer the
236
      deformation of the rest-state mesh of an object using various combinations of input data modalities:
      sequences of images, point clouds, and/or robot data. Figure 5 illustrates the building blocks that we
237
      used to generate different architectures depending on the input modalities.
238
239   At a high level, we use three main common components for all models: an encoder for extracting
240   features from an input modality, an attention mechanism for exploiting temporal information from
241   the sequences, and a conditional Real-NVP (Amin Mansour et al., 2024) for predicting the offsets of
242
      template vertices, yielding the predicted deformed mesh. Real-NVP utilizes a series of conditional
      coupling blocks, each defined as a continuous bijective function. This continuous bijective operation
243
      ensures that the model is homeomorphic, which allows stable deformation of a template mesh while
244
      preserving its topology.
245
246   Image input: For pipelines using images as input, we use a DinoV2 vision transformer to extract
247   embeddings of each image frame. In particular, we use a DinoV2-small model, pretrained via distil-
248
      lation from the largest DinoV2 transformer presented in Oquab et al. (2023) (LVD-142M dataset).
      The embedding dimension is later reduced using a 1D convolutional layer and a subsequent fully
249
      connected layer (Feature Dim. Reduction block in Figure 5).
250
251   Point cloud input: When using point clouds, we leverage a FoldingNet encoder (Yang et al., 2018)
252   for representation learning, which is trained end-to-end together with the attention mechanism and
253   the conditional-NVP.
254   Robot data input: To fuse the robot data, we concatenate the measured end-effector forces and the
255   position of the interaction point. The concatenated data is later fed into a single fully connected
256   layer, to match the dimensionality of the embeddings used for the attention mechanisms.
257
      A self-attention mechanism is employed for variations of the architecture in Figure 5 that use a single
258   data modality as input. In contrast, a cross-attention mechanism is applied when handling multiple
259   data modalities simultaneously. For the experiments presented in the results section, we use cross-
260   attention to handle a mixture of image sequences and robot data sequences as input. However, other
261   combinations of input data are also possible.
262
      All architectures are end-to-end trained using the same loss. We include the weights of the DinoV2
263
      transformer during backpropagation for finetuning. The main point face distance (PFD) criterion
264   LPFD accounts for the global deformation of the objects, which computes the average squared dis-
265   tance d(p, f ) from the set of sampled points pi ∈ P on the predicted mesh to the nearest faces in
266   the set of triangular faces fi ∈ F of the ground truth mesh and vice versa (eq. (1)). Moreover, to
267   deal with the local deformations generated in the poking region, we add a region-of-interest (ROI)
268   loss LROI (eq. (2)) that computes the unidirectional chamfer distance from the points pi in the ROI
269   to the set of sampled points qi ∈ Q of the ground truth mesh. The ROI is defined using the indicator
      function I(C(pi )), which evaluates to 1 if point pi is close enough to the contact point t according

308   4.1      DATASET
309
310   The PokeFlex dataset comprises 17 deformable objects (Figure 6), including 13 everyday items as
311   well as 4 objects that are 3D printed with a soft thermoplastic polyurethane filament. Even though
312   the everyday objects in our dataset can be purchased from global vendors, their availability is not
313   guaranteed worldwide. Therefore, to enhance the usability of our dataset we include deformable
314
      3D printed objects, providing print files and detailed specifications for reproducibility. The 3D
      printed objects include the Stanford bunny (Turk, 1994), a cylinder, a heart (Noor et al., 2019), and
315
      a pyramid. Further details about the 3D printing can be found in Appendix A.1.
316
317   The dimensions and the weights of the PokeFlex objects range from 7 cm to 58 cm and from 22 g to
318   1 kg, respectively. Furthermore, using Hooke’s law and applying RANSAC for linear regression to
319   avoid outliers, we estimated the objects’ stiffnesses to be in the range of 148–3,879 N/m.
320   For the poking protocol, we recorded 4-8 sequences with a duration of 5-6 seconds at 30 fps for
321   each object. Similarly, for the dropping protocol, we recorded 3 sequences of 1 second at 60 fps
322   for each object. Figure 7 shows two reconstructed sequences for poking and dropping. In the case
323   of the poking sequences, each frame includes synchronized and paired data from all modalities, as
      illustrated in Figure 2.

354   Overview of training data. In the following experiments, we exclusively used poking sequences
355   from the dataset because of the higher diversity of input data modalities available. The input se-
356   quence length was set to 5, chosen heuristically for better performance. The train-validation split
357   was generated by randomly choosing one recording sequence per object as the validation set.
358
      Metrics. During training, we reposition and re-scale all meshes into a cube of unit size ([−0.5, 0.5]3 )
359
      to maintain a consistent scale across all objects. The losses LPFD and LROI are computed in this
360   normalized scale. Additionally, we calculate the relative point-to-face distance (RPFD) by dividing
361   LPFD by the average point-to-face distance between the template mesh MT and the ground truth
362   mesh MGT . An RPFD value below 1 indicates that the predicted deformed mesh MP is closer to
363   the ground truth than the undeformed template, with smaller values indicating better accuracy.
364

365

366   tance CDUL1 (eq. (4)) and the volumetric Jaccard Index J (eq. (5)), which we defined in terms of
367   the volume operator V . The two metrics provide insights into the L1 Norm surface distance and the
368   volume overlap ratio, respectively.

      6              C ONCLUSION
519
520
      This paper introduced PokeFlex, a new dataset that captures the behavior of 17 deformable volumet-
521
      ric objects during poking and dropping. The focus is on volumetric objects, while thin clothing items
522   or thin cables are not considered in the dataset. Compared to previously existing datasets, we provide
523   a wider range of paired and annotated data modalities, which are supplemented with data streams
524   from lower-cost camera sensors. In an effort to enhance reproducibility, the objects included in our
525   dataset can be either purchased from global providers or 3D printed with our open-source models.
526   The 3D printed objects also allow for finer control over their expected behavior through knowledge
527   of their material properties and internal structures, especially useful for sim-to-real transfer.
528   Using different combinations of the data modalities provided in PokeFlex, we train and benchmark
529   a list of baseline models for the task of multi-object template-based mesh reconstruction. In doing
530   so, we present a list of suitable criteria for evaluating PokeFlex.
531
      We are excited about the potential of PokeFlex to inspire new research directions in deformable ob-
532
      ject manipulation and to serve as a foundational resource for the robotics community. With its rich,
533
      multimodal data and its focus on reproducibility, we believe that PokeFlex will drive innovation in
534   both simulation-based and real-world applications of deformable object manipulation. This includes
535   better material parameter identification to fine-tune simulators, viewpoint-agnostic online 3D mesh
536   reconstruction methods, and policy learning for manipulation tasks. As we continue to expand the
537   dataset and explore new possibilities, we anticipate that PokeFlex will become an invaluable tool for
538   researchers developing next-generation techniques. We look forward to sharing this dataset with the
539   community and fostering collaborations that push the boundaries of robotics research.
