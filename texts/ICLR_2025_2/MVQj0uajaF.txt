031                                              A BSTRACT
032
033            Recent developments in large-scale pre-trained text-to-image diffusion models have
034            significantly improved the generation of high-fidelity images, particularly with
035            the emergence of diffusion transformer models (DiTs). Among diffusion models,
036            diffusion transformers have demonstrated superior image generation capabilities,
037            boosting lower FID scores and higher scalability. However, deploying large-scale
038            DiT models can be expensive due to their excessive parameter numbers. Although
039            existing research has explored efficient deployment techniques for diffusion models
040
               such as model quantization, there is still little work concerning DiT-based models.
               To tackle this research gap, in this paper, we propose TerDiT, a quantization-aware
041
               training (QAT) and efficient deployment scheme for ternary diffusion transformer
042
               models. We focus on the ternarization of DiT networks, with model sizes rang-
043            ing from 600M to 4.2B, and image resolution from 256×256 to 512×512. Our
044            work contributes to the exploration of efficient deployment of large-scale DiT
045            models, demonstrating the feasibility of training extremely low-bit DiT models
046            from scratch while maintaining competitive image generation capacities compared
047            to full-precision models. Code has been uploaded in the supplemental materials.

050   1   I NTRODUCTION
051
052   The advancements in large-scale pre-trained text-to-image diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; 2022a;
053   Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) have led to the successful generation
      of images characterized by both complexity and high fidelity to the input conditions. Notably, the


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      emergence of diffusion transformer models (DiTs) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) represents a significant
055   stride in this research direction. Compared with other diffusion models, diffusion transformers have
056   demonstrated the capability to achieve lower FID scores but with higher computation GFLOPS (Pee-
057   bles & Xie, 2023). Recent research highlights the remarkable image and video generation capabilities
058   of diffusion transformer architectures, as demonstrated in methods like Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser
059   et al., 2024) and Sora (Brooks et al., 2024).
060
      Given the impressive performance of diffusion transformer models, researchers are now training
061   larger and larger DiTs (Liu et al., 2024). For instance, Stable Diffusion 3 trained DiT models ranging
062   from 800 million to 8 billion parameters. Additionally, there is speculation among researchers that
063   Sora might boast around 3 billion parameters. Given the enormous parameter numbers, deploying
064   these DiT models will be costly, especially on certain end devices (e.g., mobile phones).
065
      To deal with the deployment dilemma, there were recent works on the efficient deployment of
066
      diffusion models (Li et al., 2023; 2024b; He et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), most of which focus on
067   model quantization. However, as far as we are concerned, there are still two main shortcomings in
068   current research. Firstly, the exploration of quantization methods for transformer-based diffusion
069   models remains quite limited (Chen et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024) compared to quantizing U-
070   Net-based diffusion models. Secondly, most prevailing quantization approaches heavily rely on
071   post-training quantization (PTQ) (Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Shang et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
072   2023; Wang et al., 2023a), which leads to unacceptable performance degradation, particularly with
073   extremely low bit width (e.g., 2-bit and 1-bit). For example, the 2-bit weight quantization results
074   of Q-DiT (Chen et al., 2024) and Q-Diffusion (Li et al., 2023) are shown in Fig. 8. The extremely
075
      low-bit quantization of neural networks is important as it can significantly reduce the computation
      resources for deployment (Sze et al., 2017), especially for huge models. During our research, we find
076
      that there is no work considering the extremely low-bit quantization of DiT models.
077
078   To tackle these shortcomings, we propose to leverage the quantization-aware training (QAT) technique
079   for the extremely low-bit quantization of large-scale DiT models. Low-bit QAT methods for large-
080   scale models have been discussed in the LLM domain. Recent works observed that training large
081
      language models with extremely low-bit parameters (e.g., binary and ternary) from scratch can also
      lead to competitive performance (Wang et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024b) compared with their full-
082
      precision counterparts. This indicates that significant precision redundancy still exists in large-scale
083
      models, and it is feasible to conduct QAT for large-scale DiT models.
084
085   In this paper, we focus primarily on ternary weight networks (Li et al., 2016) and provide TerDiT,
086   the first quantization scheme for extremely low-bit DiTs to our best knowledge. Our method achieves
087
      quantization-aware training (weight-only) and efficient deployment for ternary diffusion transformer
      models. Different from the naive quantization of linear layers in LLMs and CNNs (Ma et al., 2024b;
088
      Li et al., 2016), we find that the direct weight ternarization of the adaLN module (Perez et al., 2017)
089
      in DiT blocks (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024a) leads to large dimension-wise scale and shift
090   values in the normalization layer compared with full-precision models (due to weight quantization,
091   gradient approximation), which results in slower convergence speed and poor model performance.
092   Consequently, we propose a variant of adaLN by applying an RMS Norm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019)
093   after the ternary linear layers of the adaLN module to mitigate this training issue effectively.
094
      With this modification, we scale the ternary DiT model from 600M (size of DiT-XL/2 (Peebles & Xie,
095
      2023)) to 4.2B (size of Large-DiT-4.2B (Gao et al., 2024)), with image resolution from 256×256 to
096   512×512. We further deploy the trained ternary DiT models with 2-bit CUDA kernels, resulting in
097   over 10× reduction in checkpoint size and about 8× reduction in inference memory consumption,
098   while achieving competitive (or even better) generation quality compared with full-precision models.
099
      The contributions of our work are summarized as follows:

      2    R ELATED W ORKS

      Diffusion Models. Diffusion models have gained significant attention in recent years due to their
122   ability to generate high-quality images and their potential for various applications. The concept
123   of diffusion models was first introduced by (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), proposing a generative
124   model that learns to reverse a diffusion process. This work laid the foundation for subsequent
125   research in the field. (Ho et al., 2020) further extended the idea by introducing denoising diffusion
126   probabilistic models (DDPMs), which have become a popular choice for image generation tasks.
127   DDPMs have been applied to a wide range of domains, including unconditional image generation (Ho
128   et al., 2020), image inpainting (Song et al., 2020), and image super-resolution (Saharia et al., 2021).
129   Additionally, diffusion models have been used for text-to-image synthesis, as demonstrated by the
130
      DALL-E model (Ramesh et al., 2021) and the Imagen model (Saharia et al., 2022). These models
      showcase the ability of diffusion models to generate highly realistic and diverse images from textual
131
      descriptions. Furthermore, diffusion models have been extended to other modalities, such as audio
132
      synthesis (Chen et al., 2020) and video generation (Ho et al., 2022b), demonstrating their versatility
133   and potential for multimodal applications.
134
135   Quantization of Diffusion Models. The quantization of diffusion models has been studied in recent
136
      years to improve the efficiency of diffusion models. Post-training quantization (PTQ) methods, such
      as those presented in (Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2024; Shang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Wang
137
      et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024), offer advantages in terms of quantization time
138
      and data usage. However, these methods often result in suboptimal performance when applied to
139   low-bit settings. To address this issue, (He et al., 2023) proposes combining quantization-aware
140   low-rank adapters (QALoRA) with PTQ methods, leading to improved evaluation results. As an
141   alternative to PTQ, quantization-aware training (QAT) methods have been introduced specifically for
142   low-bit diffusion model quantization (Wang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Chang
143   et al., 2023). Despite their effectiveness, these QAT methods are currently only limited to small-sized
144   U-Net-based diffusion models, revealing a research gap in applying QAT to large-scale DiT models.
145   Further exploration of QAT techniques for large DiT models with extremely low bit width could
146   potentially unlock even greater efficiency gains and enable the effective deployment of diffusion
147
      models in resource-constrained environments.
148   Ternary Weight Networks. Ternary weight networks (Li et al., 2016) have emerged as a memory-
149   efficient and computation-efficient network structure, offering the potential for significant reductions
150   in inference memory usage. When supported by specialized hardware, ternary weight networks can
151   also deliver substantial computational acceleration. Among quantization methods, ternary weight
152   networks have garnered notable attention, with two primary approaches being explored: weight-only
153
      quantization and weight-activation quantization. In weight-only quantization, as discussed in (Zhu
      et al., 2016), solely the weights are quantized to ternary values. On the other hand, weight-activation
154
      quantization, as presented in (Alemdar et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2018), involves quantizing both
155
      the weights and activations to ternary values. Recent research has demonstrated the applicability
156   of ternary weight networks to the training of large language models (Ma et al., 2024b), achieving
157   results comparable to their full-precision counterparts. Building upon these advancements, our
158   work introduces, for the first time, quantization-aware training and efficient deployment schemes
159   specifically designed for ternary DiT models. By leveraging the benefits of ternary quantization in
160   the context of DiT models, we aim to push the boundaries of efficiency and enable the deployment of
161   powerful diffusion models in resource-constrained environments, opening up new possibilities for
      practical applications.

      3     T ER D I T
180
      In this section, we introduce TerDiT, a framework designed to conduct weight-only quantization-
181
      aware training and efficient deployment of large-scale ternary DiT models. We first give a brief review
182
      of diffusion transformer (DiT) models in Sec. 3.1. Then building upon the previous open-sourced
183   Large-DiT (Gao et al., 2024), we illustrate the quantization function and quantization-aware training
184   scheme in Sec. 3.2, conduct QAT-specific model structure improvement for better network training in
185   Sec. 3.3, and introduce ternary deployment scheme in Sec. 3.4.
186
187   3.1    P RELIMINARY: D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER M ODELS
188
189   Diffusion Transformer. Diffusion transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023) (DiT) is an architecture that
190   replaces the commonly used U-Net backbone in the diffusion models with a transformer that operates
191   on latent patches. Similar to the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture shown in Fig. 2 (C), DiT
192   first pachifies the spatial inputs into a sequence of tokens, then the denoising process is carried out
193
      through a series of transformer blocks (Fig. 2 (B)). To deal with additional conditional information
      (e.g., noise timesteps t, class labels l, natural language inputs), DiT leverages adaptive normalization
194
      modules (Karras et al., 2019) (adaLN-Zero) to insert these extra conditional inputs to the transformer
195
      blocks. After the final transformer block, a standard linear decoder is applied to predict the final noise
196   and covariance. The DiT models can be trained in the same way as U-Net-based diffusion models.
197
198   AdaLN Module in DiT. The main difference between DiT and traditional ViT is the need to
199
      inject conditional information for image generation. DiT employs a zero-initialized adaptive layer
      normalization (adaLN-Zero) module in each transformer block, as shown in the red part of Fig. 2 (B),
200
      which calculates the dimension-wise scale and shift values from the input condition c:
201
202                                                        adaLN(c) = MLP(SiLU(c)).                                                                       (1)
203
204
      AdaLN is an important component in the DiT model (Peebles & Xie, 2023) and has been proven
      more effective than cross-attention and in-context conditioning methods. Within the DiT architecture,
205
      the adaLN module integrates an MLP layer with a substantial number of parameters, constituting
206
      approximately 10% to 20% of the model’s total parameters. Throughout the training of TerDiT,
207   we observe that the direct weight-ternarization of this module yields undesirable training results
208   (analyzed in Sec. 3.3).

      RMS Normalized AdaLN Module. We analyze the DiT model for QAT-specific model structure
287
      improvement based on the above insights. In a standard ViT transformer block, the layer norm
288
      is applied for every self-attention layer and feedforward layer. This is also the case with the self-
289   attention layers and feedforward layers in the DiT block, which can help to properly scale the range
290   of activations. However, the DiT block differs from traditional transformer blocks due to the presence
291   of the AdaLN module, as introduced in Sec. 3.1. Notably, there is no layer normalization applied
292   to this module. In the context of full-precision training, the absence of layer normalization does
293   not have a significant impact. However, for ternary DiT networks, its absence can result in large
294   dimension-wise scale and shift values in the adaLN (normalization) module, posing bad influences
295   on model training. To mitigate this issue, we introduce an RMS Norm after the MLP layer of the
296   adaLN module in each ternary DiT block:

300   in faster convergence speed and lower training loss, leading to better quantitative and qualitative
301   evaluation results. To better showcase the effect, the actual activation distribution with/without the
302   RMS Norm after model training is analyzed in Sec. A.1.
303   Remark: what are the differences between TerDiT and classic low-bit quantization approaches
304   like BitNet (Wang et al., 2023b; Ma et al., 2024b)?
305
      In BitNet, a BitLinear layer is designed, which applies a layer norm to the input activation of the
306
      Linear layer. BitNet then replaces the Linear layers in LLaMA with BitLinear layers and removes
307   the RMS Norm before the attention and SwiGLU layers. Similar approaches can be found in earlier
308   works like Xnor-net (Rastegari et al., 2016), and Bi-real net (Liu et al., 2018).
309
310
      Different from applying layer norm to all the BitLinear layers of the model, TerDiT focuses on simple
      yet effective modifications to the model structure by simply adding an RMS Norm within the adaLN
311
      module of each DiT block. With fewer norms added, our method leads to faster training speed and
312
      better evaluation scores. Comparative experiments are introduced in Sec. 4.2.

      3.4   D EPLOYMENT S CHEME
316
      After training the DiT model, we find that there are currently no effective open-source deployment
317
      solutions for ternary networks (Ma et al., 2024b). In this case, we deploy the trained networks with a 2-
318   bit implementation. To be specific, we pack the ternary linear weights to int8 values (4 ternary numbers
319   into one int8 number) with the pack_2bit_u8() function provided by (Badri & Shaji, 2023).
320   During the inference process of the DiT model, we call the complementary unpack_2bit_u8()
321   function on the fly to recover packed 2-bit numbers to floating-point values, then perform subsequent
322   calculations. The addition of the unpacking operation will slow down the inference process, but
323   we believe that with further research into model ternarization, more hardware support for inference
      speedup will become available.

513    5           D ISCUSSIONS AND F UTURE W ORKS
514
515
      In this paper, we propose quantization-aware training (QAT) and efficient deployment methods for
516   large-scale ternary DiT models. Competitive evaluation results on the ImageNet generation task
517   (256×256 and 512×512) compared with full-precision models and baseline quantization methods
518   demonstrate the feasibility of training a large ternary DiT from scratch while achieving promising
519   generation results. To our best knowledge, this work is also the first study concerning the extremely
520   low-bit quantization of DiT models. In this section, we give more explanations and discussions.
521
       Firstly, training ternary DiT models is less stable and more time-consuming than training full-
522    precision networks. Although we discuss methods to enhance training stability by adding norms, it
523    still requires more time than training full-precision networks, leading to increased carbon dioxide
524    emissions during model training in a broader context. We plan to further explore hardware-software
525    co-development to increase training speed in the future.
526
       Secondly, in our paper, we do not make a complete comparison between ternary quantization and
527
       INT8/FP16 quantization, as INT8 and FP16 do not fall under the category of extremely low-bit
528    quantization, which is outside the comparison scope of our paper. On the one hand, hardware and
529    software support for INT8 and FP16 is mature, with many hardware chips and software libraries
530    readily available (Micikevicius et al., 2017; Frantar et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2022; Lin et al.,
531    2023). In contrast, research on extremely low-bit quantization for LLMs or Stable Diffusion remains
532    in its early stages. On the other hand, while FP16 or INT8 can achieve strong performance, they
533    reduce memory usage by only up to 75%. In theory, ternary quantization can reduce memory usage
534    by up to 16×. Therefore, it is undeniable that ternary quantization holds greater potential compared
535    to FP16 or INT8. In fact, hardware and software accelerations for ternary (binary) CNNs have already
536
       been implemented on FPGA (Zhu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2017; Rutishauser et al., 2024). We will
       continue to explore the hardware acceleration implementation of TerDiT in future work.
537
538   We hope our work can serve as a pipeline for the extremely low-bit quantization for diffusion
539   transformers, and inspire the community to engage in this area, fostering broader advancements (e.g.,
      software and hardware co-design) in this field of study.
