008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            Mitigating bias in automated decision-making systems, specifically deep learning
011            models, is a critical challenge in achieving fairness. This complexity stems from
012            factors such as nuanced definitions of fairness, unique biases in each dataset, and
013            the trade-off between fairness and model accuracy. To address such issues, we
014            introduce FairVIC, an innovative approach designed to enhance fairness in neural
015            networks by addressing inherent biases at the training stage. Unlike other methods
016
               that require a user-defined declaration of what it means to be fair, FairVIC inte-
               grates an abstract concept of fairness through variance, invariance and covariance
017
               terms into the loss function. These terms aim to minimise the model’s dependency
018
               on protected characteristics for making predictions, thus promoting fairness. Our
019            experimentation consists of evaluating FairVIC against other comparable bias mit-
020            igation techniques, on a number of datasets known for their biases. Additionally,
021            we conduct an ablation study to examine the accuracy-fairness trade-off. We also
022            extend FairVIC by offering multi-objective lambda recommendations, allowing
023            users to train a fairer model with a set of weights that are tuned best for their ap-
024            plication. Through our implementation of FairVIC, we observed a significant im-
025            provement in fairness across all metrics tested, without compromising the model’s
026            accuracy. Our findings suggest that FairVIC presents a straightforward, out-of-
027
               the-box solution for the development of fairer deep learning models, thereby of-
               fering a generalisable solution applicable across many tasks and datasets.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the ever-increasing utilisation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in everyday applications, Neural
034
      Networks (NNs) have emerged as pivotal tools for Automated Decision Making (ADM) systems in
035
      industries such as healthcare (Esteva et al., 2017), finance (Dixon et al., 2017), and recruitment (Var-
036   darlier & Zafer, 2020). However, the inherent bias embedded in datasets and subsequently learned
037   by these models poses significant challenges to fairness. Such a bias can lead to adverse decisions
038   affecting real lives. For instance, several studies have shown how bias in facial recognition tech-
039   nologies disproportionately misidentifies individuals of certain ethnic backgrounds (Birhane, 2022;
040   Cavazos et al., 2020), leading to potential discrimination in law enforcement and hiring practices.
041
      Real-world consequences exemplify the urgent need to address these challenges at the core of AI
042   development. Ensuring fairness in deep learning models presents complex challenges, primarily due
043   to the black-box nature of these models, which often complicates understanding and interpreting
044   decisions. Moreover, the dynamic and high-dimensional nature of the data involved, combined
045   with nuances in fairness definitions, further complicates the detection and correction of bias. This
046   complexity necessitates the development of more sophisticated, inherently fair algorithms.
047
      Previous mitigation strategies dealing with algorithmic bias – whether through pre-processing, in-
048   processing, or post-processing – have significant limitations. Pre-processing techniques, which at-
049   tempt to cleanse biased data, are labour-intensive, dependent on expert intervention (Salimi et al.,
050   2019), and often insufficient to eliminate all biases. Current in-processing methods frequently lead
051   to unstable models and often rely heavily on ad-hoc fairness metrics. Post-processing techniques,
052   which adjust model predictions directly, ignore deeper issues without addressing the underlying bi-
053   ases in the data and model. These approaches lack stability, generalisability, and the ability to ensure
      fairness across multiple metrics (Berk et al., 2017).

      In this paper, we introduce FairVIC (Fairness through Variance, Invariance, and Covariance), a
055   novel approach that embeds fairness directly into neural networks by optimising a custom loss func-
056   tion. This function is designed to minimise the correlation between decisions and protected charac-
057   teristics while maximising overall prediction performance. FairVIC integrates fairness through the
058   concepts of variance, invariance, and covariance during the training process, making it more princi-
059   pled and intuitive, and universally applicable to diverse datasets. Unlike previous methods, FairVIC
060   offers a robust, out-of-the-box solution that introduces a more abstract concept of fairness to sig-
061   nificantly reduce bias. Our experimental evaluations demonstrate FairVIC’s ability to significantly
062   improve performance in all fairness metrics tested without compromising prediction accuracy. We
063
      compare our proposed method against comparable in-processing bias mitigation techniques, such
      as adversarial and constraint approaches, and highlight the improved, robust performance of the
064
      FairVIC model.
065
066   Our contributions in this paper are multi-fold:
067          • A novel, out-of-the-box in-processing bias mitigation technique for neural networks.
068          • An extension to dynamically tune the lambda weights during training, alleviating hyperpa-
069            rameter selection and fine-tuning from the user.
070          • A comprehensive experimental evaluation, using a multitude of comparable methods on a
071            variety of metrics across several datasets, including different modalities such as tabular and
072            text.
073          • An extended analysis of our proposed method to examine its robustness.
074   This paper is structured as follows: Section 2 discusses current approaches to mitigating bias
075   throughout each processing stage. Section 3 describes any preliminary details for this work, includ-
076   ing the fairness metrics used in the evaluation. Section 4 outlines our method and its lambda-tuning
077   extension, including how each term in our loss function is calculated and an algorithm detailing
078   how these terms are applied. Section 5 describes the experiments carried out, Section 6 outlines the
079   results with discussion, and Section 7 concludes this work. Any extra information, including the
080   dataset metadata and any extra experiments, is to be found in the Appendix.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084
085   There exist three broad categories of mitigation strategies for algorithmic bias: pre-processing, in-
086
      processing, and post-processing. Each aims to increase fairness differently by acting upon either the
      training data, the training step, or the predictions outputted by the model, respectively.
087
088   Pre-processing methods aim to fix the data before training, recognising that bias is primarily an
089   issue with the data itself (Caton & Haas, 2020). In practice, this can be done a number of different
090   ways, such as representative sampling, or re-sampling the data to reflect the full population (Shekhar
091   et al., 2021; Ustun et al., 2019), reweighing the data such that different groups influence the model in
092   a representative way (Calders & Žliobaitė, 2013; Kamiran & Calders, 2012), or generating synthetic
093
      data to balance out the representation of each group (Jang et al., 2021). Another set of approaches
      utilises causal methods to delineate relationships between sensitive attributes and the target variables
094
      within the data (Chiappa & Isaac, 2019; Kusner et al., 2017; Russell et al., 2017). Such techniques
095
      as these are labour-intensive and do not generalise well, requiring an expert with knowledge of the
096   data to manually process each case of a new dataset (Salimi et al., 2019). They also cannot provide
097   assurances that all bias has been removed – a model may draw upon relationships between features
098   that lead to bias, which are hard for the expert to spot.
099
      In-processing methods aim to train models to make fairer predictions, even upon biased data. There
100
      are a plethora of ways in which this has been done. For example, Celis et al. (2019) and Agarwal
101
      et al. (2018) utilise a chosen fairness metric and perform constraint optimisation during training.
102   This has the effect that a single fairness metric needs to be chosen, introducing human bias, and this
103   metric must perfectly capture the bias within the data to effectively mitigate it. Therefore, fairness
104   cannot be achieved across multiple definitions in this way. Another approach involves incorporating
105   an adversarial component during model training that penalises the model if protected characteris-
106   tics can be predicted from its outputs (Zhang et al., 2018; Wadsworth et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019).
107   These methods are often effective but their main shortcoming is seen in their instability. Finally,
      the most relevant comparisons from previous work to our proposed method are regularisation-based


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      techniques that incorporate fairness constraints or penalties directly into the model’s loss function
109   during training. There are a number of ways that this can be done, such as through data augmentation
110   strategies to promote less sensitive decision boundaries (Chuang & Mroueh, 2021) or by incorpo-
111   rating fairness adjustments into the boosting process (Cruz et al., 2023). The performance of these
112   models differs from approach to approach, and those that work by constraining the model by a fair-
113   ness metric directly suffer from the issue of human bias and misrepresenting the bias within the
114   data/model.
115   Post-processing techniques involve adjusting model predictions or decision rules after training to
116   ensure fair outcomes. In practice, decision thresholds have been adjusted for different groups to
117   achieve equal outcomes in a particular metric (Hardt et al., 2016). Alternatively, labels near the
118   decision boundary can be altered to favour less biased outcomes (Kamiran et al., 2012; 2018). Cali-
119   bration (Kim et al., 2018; Noriega-Campero et al., 2019) adjusts the predictions of the model directly
120   so that the proportion of positive instances is equal across each sub-group. This category of methods
121   can oversimplify fairness, and they do not fix the underlying issue within the model. For those tech-
122   niques that require the specification of a single fairness metric, the same issue applies surrounding
123
      this choice as before.
124   There lies a number of issues which have not yet been solved in parallel within one technique. These
125   are: stability, generalisability, equal improvements to fairness across metrics (Berk et al., 2017), and
126   built without requirements for user-induced definitions of fairness. In this paper, we solve all these
127   requirements for an effective, out-of-the-box approach to mitigate bias through FairVIC.

      3.1   VICR EG
132
133
      Variance-Invariance-Covariance Regularization (VICReg) (Bardes et al., 2021) has previously been
134   used in self-supervised learning to tackle feature collapse and redundancy. It maximises variance
135   across features to ensure the model produces diverse outputs for different inputs, minimises invari-
136   ance between augmented representations of the same input to enhance stability, and reduces covari-
137   ance among features to capture a broader range of information. VICReg is confined to this specific
138   context and objective, and the application of these principles outside of self-supervised methods
139   remains largely unexplored. In contrast, FairVIC extends these principles to supervised learning
140   for bias mitigation. This adaptation addresses the challenges of fairness in decision-making sys-
141   tems, expanding the application of VIC principles beyond their original scope and offering a novel,
142
      generalisable solution to fairness in supervised learning models.

166   3.3   I NDIVIDUAL FAIRNESS
167
168   While FairVIC aims to increase group fairness, the invariance term promotes direct improvements
169   in individual fairness. This can be observed in our evaluations through counterfactual fairness.
170
      Counterfactual fairness ensures that decisions made by an algorithm are fair even when considering
      hypothetical (counterfactual) scenarios. For each individual, the sensitive attribute is switched to
171
      assess the model’s ability to perform equally in both the original and counterfactual scenarios.
172
173   Formally, if u denotes the unprivileged group, p the privileged group and Ŷ is the decision outcome,
174   then the model is considered counterfactually fair if Ŷu = Ŷp for different groups u and p of the
175   sensitive attribute while all non-sensitive features remain the same.

      4     A PPROACH
178
179
      We propose FairVIC (Fairness through Variance, Invariance, and Covariance), a novel loss function
180   that enables a model’s ability to learn fairness in a robust manner. FairVIC is comprised of three
181   terms: variance, invariance, and covariance. Optimising for these three terms encourages the model
182   to be stable and consistent across protected characteristics, thereby reducing bias during training.
183   By adopting this broad, generalised approach to defining bias, FairVIC significantly improves per-
184   formance across a range of fairness metrics. This makes it an effective strategy for reducing bias
185   across various applications, ensuring more equitable outcomes in diverse settings.
186

194   encouraging the model to be fair by minimising these variations.

235   During the training of a deep learning model, the model iterates over epochs E. Data is shuffled into
236   batches, upon which the model predicts to produce a set of predictions Ŷ . Typically, the true labels Y
237   and predictions Ŷ are then passed into a suitable accuracy loss function (e.g., binary cross-entropy,
238   hinge loss, Huber loss, etc.) and the resulting loss attempts to be minimised by an optimiser.
239   In the case of FairVIC, in addition to computing a suitable accuracy loss Lacc , we also calculate
240   our three novel terms Lvar , Linv , and Lcov using Equations 1, 2, and 3 respectively. Each of these
241   individual loss terms is then multiplied by its respective weighting factor λ and summed to form
242   the total loss Ltotal . Subsequently, gradients are computed, and the optimiser adjusts the model
243   parameters with respect to this combined loss. Further details are provided in Algorithm 1.
244   The multipliers λ enable users to balance the trade-off between fairness and predictive perfor-
245   mance, which is typical in bias mitigation techniques. Assigning a higher weight to λacc directs
246   the model to prioritise accuracy while increasing the weights of (λvar , λinv , λcov ) shifts the focus
247   towards enhancing fairness in the model’s predictions. In our implementation, the lambda co-
248   efficients (λacc , λvar , λinv , λcov ) are constrained such that their sum equals one. In other words,
249   λacc = 1 − λvar − λinv − λcov . This normalisation ensures the optimisation will not produce multiple
250   solutions in the form {k.λacc , k.λvar , k.λinv , k.λcov }, k ∈ R.

300   5.1   DATASETS
301
302   We evaluate FairVIC on four datasets that are used as benchmarks in bias mitigation evaluation
303   due to their known biases towards certain subgroups of people within their sample population.
304   These datasets allow for highlighting the generalisable capabilities of FairVIC across different de-
305   mographic disparities.
306   Tabular datasets. The main body of evaluation is done using three tabular datasets: Adult In-
307   come (Becker & Kohavi, 1996), COMPAS (Angwin et al., 2022), and German Credit (Hofmann,
308   1994), all of which are binary classification tasks. Adult Income aims to predict whether an indi-
309   vidual’s income is > $50K or ≤ $50K. It is known for its gender and racial biases in economic
310   disparity. The Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions (COMPAS)
311   dataset is frequently used for evaluating debiasing techniques. It has a classification goal of predict-
312
      ing recidivism risks and is infamous for its racial biases. Finally, the German Credit dataset was
      used to assess creditworthiness by classifying individuals as either good or bad credit risks, with
313
      known biases related to age and gender (Kamiran & Calders, 2009).
314
      Language dataset. To show the ability of FairVIC to work for different data modalities, we also
315   utilise CivilComments-WILDS – a natural language dataset. We take a sample of 50K comments
316   from the dataset, which is comprised of a collection of comments on the Civil Comments platform.
317   The binary classification goal is to label comments as toxic or non-toxic. Our sample is stratified
318   to retain the same proportion of toxic comments as in the original dataset. We take ethnicity as the
319   protected characteristic where comments are marked as white or non-white (Koh et al., 2021).

      5.2   C OMPARABLE T ECHNIQUES
325
326   To highlight the performance of FairVIC, we evaluate against five comparable in-processing bias
327   mitigation methods. These are:
328
      Adversarial Debiasing. This method leverages an adversarial network that aims to predict protected
329
      characteristics based on the predictions of the main model. The primary model seeks to maximise its
330   own prediction accuracy while minimising the adversary’s prediction accuracy (Zhang et al., 2018).
331   Exponentiated Gradient Reduction. This technique reduces fair classification to a sequence of
332   cost-sensitive classification problems, returning a randomised classifier with the lowest empirical
333   error subject to a chosen fairness constraint (Agarwal et al., 2018).
334   Meta Fair Classifier. This classifier allows a fairness metric as an input and optimises the model
335   with respect to regular performance and the chosen fairness metric (Celis et al., 2019).
336   Fair MixUp. This technique generates synthetic samples by linearly interpolating between pairs of
337   training data points by protected attribute to smooth decision boundaries. The loss function is then
338
      further constrained by a fairness metric (Chuang & Mroueh, 2021).
      FairGBM. This method uses a gradient-boosting decision tree model that integrates fairness con-
339
      straints directly into the boosting process by adjusting the loss function to account for fairness met-
340
      rics (Cruz et al., 2023).
341
342   Alongside this, a baseline-biased model was established that was a neural network with binary cross-
343   entropy loss only. Details on the neural network architecture/ hyperparameters used for both the
344
      baseline model and the FairVIC model can be found in Appendix A.2.

      7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
531
532
      In this paper, we introduced FairVIC, an in-processing bias mitigation technique that introduces
533
      three new terms into the loss function of a neural network- variance, invariance, and covariance.
534   Across our experimental evaluation, FairVIC significantly improves scores for all fairness metrics
535   compared to previous comparable methods which typically aim to improve only upon a single met-
536   ric. This balance showcases FairVIC’s strength in providing a robust and generalisable solution
537   applicable across various tasks and datasets. We also presented an extension to automatically tune
538   the weights of each term during training and conducted a full ablation study to analyse the effect
539   of each term in FairVIC. Future work would look to extend FairVIC to consider multiple protected
      characteristics simultaneously and expand its utility to image datasets.
