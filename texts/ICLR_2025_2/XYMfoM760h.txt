009                                                        A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite the unprecedented success of text-to-image diffusion models, controlling
012            the number of depicted objects using text is surprisingly hard. This is important for
013            various applications from technical documents, to children’s books to illustrating
014            cooking recipes. Generating object-correct counts is fundamentally challenging
015            because the generative model needs to keep a sense of separate identity for every
016
               instance of the object, even if several objects look identical or overlap, and then
               carry out a global computation implicitly during generation. It is still unknown if
017
               such representations exist. To address count-correct generation, we first identify
018
               features within the diffusion model that can carry the object identity information.
019            We then use them to separate and count instances of objects during the denoising
020            process and detect over-generation and under-generation. We fix the latter by
021            training a model that predicts both the shape and location of a missing object, based
022            on the layout of existing ones, and show how it can be used to guide denoising
023            with correct object count. Our approach, CountGen, does not depend on external
024            source to determine object layout, but rather uses the prior from the diffusion
025            model itself, creating prompt-dependent and seed-dependent layouts. Evaluated on
026            two benchmark datasets, we find that CountGen strongly outperforms the count-
027
               accuracy of existing baselines.

048   1    I NTRODUCTION
049
050   Text-to-image diffusion models provide an accessible way to control the generation of visual content.
051   A major failure mode is their inability to count, that is, they often fail to generate the correct number
052   of items in response to text prompts. For instance, when asked to generate an image of Goldilocks
053   and the three bears, models may generate only two bears (Figure 1). Counting failures are particularly
      frustrating: The accuracy is surprisingly low, and mistakes are often obvious for people to detect.


                                                                   1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To illustrate the difficulty of the problem, consider some naive attempts to work around it. First,
055   one can manually design layouts per count, to determine the spatial organization and the number
056   of objects, then provide it as a conditioning signal to a generative model (Dahary et al., 2024b).
057   This approach would fail to generate prompt-dependent layouts, which is highly desirable. One
058   could also try asking large vision-language models to propose layouts (e.g., Chen et al. (2023); Feng
059   et al. (2023)), but these approaches do not use the visual prior information that text-to-image models
060   already collected, and, as we show below, their performance is rather poor for the counting task.
061   Why is it so hard for diffusion models to count while they generate? First, counting objects requires
062   that models capture “objectness" – the high-level coherent representation of something being a
063   separate entity, even if surrounded by other similar entities. Capturing objectness is by itself a hard
064   task in image understanding (Alexe et al., 2012; Kuo et al., 2015), and long studied in cognitive
065   psychology (Spelke, 1990). It is currently not known to what extent diffusion models represent
066   objectness of entities they generate. A second main challenge is that text-to-image diffusion models
067   struggle with controlling spatial layout just from text. Producing a correct number of objects requires
068   obeying a global and complex spatial relation between entities in an image (Chefer et al., 2023;
069
      Dahary et al., 2024a).
070   To address the problem of accurate count generation we describe several new contributions, which
071   together form our method CountGen. First, we analyze the representations of the self-attention layers
072   in SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), and identify features that capture objectness and instance identity.
073   We then use these features to develop ways to detect instances of objects during the denoising
074   process, find their spatial layout and count them. Specifically, we localize the features that correspond
075
      to objects using the cross-attention maps and cluster them to form object instance segmentation.
      Importantly, we do not have to wait for an image to be fully generated, and we can accurately count
076
      the number of objects already at an intermediate step of the denoising process.
077
078   Given this new capability to count the number of objects being generated during the denoising process,
079   we further develop methods to correct generation when the count does not match the prompt. First,
080   we train a layout-modification network we call ReLayout. It takes a spatial layout of k objects and
081
      generates a similar spatial layout with one more instance of an object added in a natural location for
      the input layout. For example, given a row of five kittens sitting on a branch, it learned to add a sixth
082
      kitten to the same row. This model is trained using image-pair samples generated by the diffusion
083
      model itself. Finally, we show how to use the new layouts in a new test-time-optimization procedure.
084
085   We evaluate CountGen on text prompts from the T2I-CompBench (Huang et al., 2023) which includes
086   prompts with numbers. CountGen greatly improves accuracy, as evident by human evaluation
087
      experiments, from 29% accuracy for SDXL to 48% by our method. It also improves over all other
      baseline methods including large commercial models like the recent DALL-E 3 (Betker et al., 2023).
088
      To support future work in this field, we design and release a dataset that can be evaluated automatically.
089
      Specifically, we release CoCoCount, a set of prompts based on COCO classes(Lin et al., 2014),
090   which can easily be evaluated using COCO-trained object detectors, like YOLO (Wang et al., 2024).
091   CountGen also significantly improves over all baseline methods on CoCoCount, importantly from
092   26% accuracy for SDXL to 52% by our method.
093
      In summary, this paper makes the following new contributions (1) We identify novel features that
094
      represent objectness and instance identity in SDXL (Podell et al., 2023). (2) We design an inference-
095
      time optimization to guide SDXL to generate an accurate number of instances for an object. (3) We
096   describe a learning approach to automatically modify layouts to add a new instance of an object
097   while preserving the structure of the scene. (4) We achieve state-of-the-art results in count-accurate
098   generation.

101   2    R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Generating images with accurate object count. Numerous papers noted that text-to-image diffusion
104   models often fail to produce images that accurately match text prompts, especially when these prompts
105   specify an exact number of objects (Kang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Paiss et al., 2023; Wen
106   et al., 2024; Battash et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023;
107   Rassin et al., 2022; Dahary et al., 2024a; Rassin et al., 2024; Chefer et al., 2023). Various efforts
      were made to improve the accuracy of these models. Most relevant to our work, Kang et al. (2023)

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      proposed a classifier-guidance approach to improve object count accuracy. The method “counts”
109   instances at each diffusion step using a pretrained counting network and adjusts the denoising process
110   using gradient guidance. However, it requires using an additional U-Net in every denoising step.
111
112
      An important line of work suggests breaking the generation process into two steps: (1) Text-to-layout -
      setting a spatial location for every object instance; and (2) Layout-to-image - generating an image with
113
      the correct object count using the given layout. Text-to-Layout: Several studies used large language
114
      models (LLMs) to propose spatial layouts (Chen et al., 2023; Phung et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023;
115   Gani et al., 2024). LayoutGPT (Feng et al., 2023) injects visual commonsense into the LLM prompt
116   which enables it to generate desirable layouts. Gani et al. (2024) suggest decomposing complex
117   prompts into smaller prompts before injecting them into the LLM. Layout-to-image: Providing a
118   predefined layout with the exact number of subjects helps ensure that the generated images reflect
119   the intended count (Chen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Bounded Attention (Dahary et al., 2024a)
120   addresses this challenge by channeling attention to bounding boxes corresponding to object instances.
121   However, this approach requires users to manually provide the bounding boxes for all the instances
122   of each object. In contrast to these separate-step approaches, CountGen, addresses the two steps of
123
      count-accurate generation. It first corrects the layout that emerges during generation so it contains
      the correct number of instances. It then uses a novel test-time optimization method to generate a
124
      count-accurate image.
125
126   Controlling text-to-image models through attention-based loss. To address the issue of object
127   neglect—when objects mentioned in a text prompt fail to appear in the generated image— Chefer
128   et al. (2023) developed a novel loss function that ensures all objects in the prompt are reflected in
129
      the cross-attention maps used during image generation. Rassin et al. (2024) tackled the challenge
      of incorrect attribute association by designing a loss function that binds the cross-attention maps of
130
      subjects and their attributes more effectively. Inspired by these advancements, CountGen includes
131
      a novel cross-attention maps loss function designed to ensure the generation adheres closely to the
132   input layout.

184   3     O UR APPROACH : C OUNT G EN
185
186   Our method, CountGen, aims to enhance text-conditioned image generators to accurately produce the
187   intended number of objects for complex input prompts. Our methodology involves a two-step process:
188   initially, we generate a natural layout that specifies where and how objects should appear in the
189   image Section 3.2. That layout is based on a layout that emerges naturally from the text-conditioned
190   generation (Section 3.1). At the second step (Section 3.3), we use this layout as a blueprint to generate
191
      the final image.
192
193   3.1   D ISCOVER OBJECT- INSTANCE L AYOUT DURING EARLY GENERATION
194
      To count object instances during generation, one must first find an internal representation that captures
195
      the separate identity of different object instances. It is not known if this representation exists in
196   diffusion models like SDXL. We now discuss this representation and then show how we can detect
197   the layout of object instances during early generation.
198
199    An emerging instance-identity representation in SDXL. We begin by exploring the notion of
200   ‘objectness’ in SDXL. While previous work (Chefer et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2023; Tewel et al., 2023)
201    utilized the cross-attention mechanism to localize objects of a given class in generated images, little
202    research has been conducted on whether the model encodes information about object instances and
203    how to distinguish between different instances of an object. We tackle this problem by exploring a
204   variety of features across different layers and timesteps of the diffusion process to determine if and
205
      where the model encodes instance-level information. Figure 3 illustrates this analysis using PCA
      visualization of self-attention features from various layers across SDXL at timestep t = 500, which
206
       shows the most robust instance representation (See appendix Figure 10). While most layers do not
207                                                                      up
       exhibit separability at the instance level, we notice that layer l52 tends to generate different features
208    for different instances of the same object, with each instance having its distinct color. Based on
209                                                                      up
       this finding, we select the self-attention features from layer l52   at timestep t = 500 to serve as our
210    instance-level features.
211
212   Identifying object instances. Building on the findings of Hertz et al. (2023), which show that
213   cross-attention maps can pinpoint a token’s position in a generated image, we create a foreground
214   mask for each object described in the prompt. By contrasting these foreground masks, derived from
215   the cross-attention, with the self-attention features, we effectively segregate pixels associated with
      objects from those belonging to the background. Subsequently, we cluster the object-associated

217   refine our object representations and enhance the accuracy of the generated layouts.

231   3.2     R E L AYOUT : C ORRECTING THE N UMBER OF O BJECTS IN THE M ASK
232
233   We now introduce our layout-correction component, ReLayout, which preserves the overall scene
234   composition while correcting the number of objects. For example, Figure 2 depicts an image
235
      generated using the prompt “a photo of six cats”, but only four cats were generated. Our ReLayout
      generates a new layout with the correct number of instances while keeping the overall composition of
236
      the kittens sitting in a row. More examples are shown in Figure 4.
237
238   The input to the ReLayout is an object-layout described in Section 3.1, from which we initially infer
239   the number of generated instances. Next, our ReLayout component takes one of two corrective actions
240   based on the discrepancy between the generated and expected counts. In cases of over-generation,
241
      where more instances were generated than requested, ReLayout deterministically removes the smallest
      instances to achieve the desired cluster count. We find that this simple strategy produces appealing
242
      results. In cases of under-generation, a more intricate challenge arises: the ReLayout must insert
243
      new instances to the scene in a way that preserves the original scene structure. This process involves
244   a sophisticated understanding of different object layouts—like the stark contrast between linearly
245   arrayed bottles and the clustered arrangement of elephants—to seamlessly augment the layout. In
246   Section 3.2.1, we detail our approach for handling under-generation. In cases where the number of
247   instances is correct, the ReLayout maintains the initial layout.
248
249   3.2.1    H ANDLING U NDER - GENERATION
250
251   To address under-generation issues, we train a U-Net model to predict a new layout, represented as a
252
      multi-channel mask, from an existing layout. In practice, each forward pass of the U-Net generates
      a mask with an additional instance. This process is applied in iterations until the mask reflects the
253
      correct number of instances. In what follows we provide detailed information on the architecture and
254
      training of our U-Net model.
255
256
      Creating a training dataset. To train our ReLayout U-Net, we need a dataset of layout pairs with k
257
      and k + 1 objects, that maintain the same scene composition. We begin with the empirical observation
258   that slight variations in the object count specified in the prompt—while keeping the starting noise
259   and the rest of the prompt consistent—typically results in images with similar layouts, as shown
260   in appendix Figure 9. This consistency is crucial as it allows us to generate a training dataset of
261   layout pairs where each pair has a similar object composition, differing by only one object, thereby
262   preserving the overall scene structure.
263
      Following this observation, we generate a set of ~10K pairs of images of Ik and Ik+1 , where each pair
264   consists of images that differ by only one in the number of objects depicted. Each pair is generated
265   with random fixed seeds and prompts that fit the same template, such as "a photo of two cats" versus
266   "a photo of three cats”. To confirm that each image pair accurately represents an k and k + 1 object
267   scenario, we extract object masks Mk and Mk+1 as described in Section 3.1, and verify the object
268   count in one image is exactly one more than in its paired image. Overall, the final dataset for training
269   consists of pairs of binary masks (Mk , Mk+1 ), representing the U-Net task of learning to generate a
      mask with k + 1 objects from a mask with k objects.

      generation in the background.

      4   E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      Compared methods. We compare CountGen against seven baseline methods: (1) SDXL (Podell
327
      et al., 2023); (2) Repeated Object: SDXL, with a modified prompt, where an object is repeated in
328   the prompt the number of times it is required to generate, as in replacing “three cats” with “a cat and
329   a cat and a cat”. This is a naive approach that parallels prompts like “A cat and a dog”. (3) Reason
330   Out Your Layout: (Chen et al., 2023) uses GPT-3.5 (Brown et al., 2020) to generate layouts then
331   trained an adapter to integrate it to SD-1.4 (Rombach et al., 2022); (4) DALL-E 3 (Betker et al.,
332   2023); (5) Random masks + BoundedAttn : generate a layout with the correct amount of clusters
333   placed randomly in the image and apply a layout-guidance generation method on top; (6) Counting
334   Guidance (Kang et al., 2023) : boost generation of SD with a counting network; (7) RPG (Yang
335   et al., 2024): generates the layout using GPT-4 and then uses SDXL.
336   Full details on how we used these baselines are given in Appendix B.3. We also compared our
337   layout-to-image phase, CountGen-Image, described in section Section 3.3 with Bounded Attention
338   (Dahary et al., 2024a).
339
340   Datasets. We evaluate our method and the baselines using two datasets. (1) T2I-Compbench-
341   Count. A subset of T2I-Compbench (Huang et al., 2023), which is a benchmark for open-world
342   compositional text-to-image generation. This subset specifically includes 218 prompts that specify a
343   single object and its number (between 2 to 10). (2) CoCoCount (ours). We generate a dataset with
344   automatic evaluation in mind. Specifically, we sample classes from COCO, which are more favorable
345   to accurate and automatic detection by methods, like YOLOv9 (Wang et al., 2024). We design simple
346   prompts around these classes, with a number between 2 and 10. In total, there are 200 prompts with
347
      various classes, numbers and scenes. See full details in Appendix C.4.
348
349   Count accuracy evaluation. We evaluate the results of CountGen and the baselines using human
350   and automatic evaluation method, which is standardized and reproducible. In both settings, we seek
351
      to identify if the number of instances generated by the object matches the request in the prompt.
352   Human evaluation. We quantified the count-accuracy of our method and baselines using human raters.
353   Raters were asked for every image: (1) Is the object in the image?; (2) Are its instances well-formed?;
354   (3) How many instances of the object are in the image? If the answer to question (1) or (2) is “no”,
355   then we do not ask question (3). We provide details on the platform, rater selection and pay, and
356   screenshots of the task in Appendix D.1.
357   Automatic evaluation. For automatic evaluation, we use the YOLOv9 model (Wang et al., 2024)
358   with its default settings, as it represents the current state-of-the-art in the YOLO object detection
359   benchmarks. To extract the number of objects in the image, we simply count the number of detected
360   bounding-boxes corresponding to the target object.
361
362   Image quality evaluation. Forcing the diffusion model to obey the count in the text prompt is
363   inevitably expected to reduce the naturalness and visual appeal of generated images, simply because
364   more constraints are added. This effect has been observed in other studies using test-time optimization
365   (Rassin et al., 2024; Chefer et al., 2023). We evaluate the image quality of CountGen by presenting
366   human raters with two images, by CountGen and SDXL, and asking them to select whether one
367
      image is more natural and well-formed than the other or to indicate that both images are equally good.

523   8   C ONCLUSIONS
524
525   The task of generating images that depict the number of requested objects correctly is a hard task. It
526   requires models to capture “objectness", and obey global spatial constraints, at the same time they
527   generate a well-formed natural image. Current text-to-image diffusion models perform poorly in this
528   task (Table 1), especially when asked to generate more than three objects (Figure 7).
529
      Our CountGen approach took three steps to address this task. First, we identified a notion of
530
      objectness from the self-attention layers of the diffusion model. Then, we trained a U-Net model that
531   learned to correct the number of instances of an object in a given layout, whether it is removing or
532   adding instances of an object such that the structure of the layout is preserved. Third, we developed a
533   layout-guidance optimization method method to generate images from the corrected layout.
534
      Together, this approach almost doubled the counting accuracy from 26% in standard SDXL to 52%
535
      using our method applied to SDXL. We expect the lessons learned from this method, specifically the
536
      features that represent objectness and the process of learning to automatically fix a layout, to become
537   useful in other problems of structured generation like spatial constraints in text-to-image models or
538   spatio-temporal constraints in video generation.

716   To accurately extract count numbers from the textual prompts, we employ spaCy’s dependency graph
717   parser (Honnibal & Montani, 2017) to identify and isolate indices of related subjects and numeric
718
      modifiers. This methodology is inspired by the approach detailed in "Linguistic Binding in Diffusion
      Models" by Rassin et al. (2024), which demonstrates the automated extraction of subjects and their
719
      attribute modifiers. We have adapted this technique to specifically recognize numeric modifiers, both
720
      spelled out (e.g., "five dogs") and in numeral form (e.g., "5 dogs"). This adaptation ensures that each
721   numeric modifier is correctly associated with its corresponding noun, thereby facilitating accurate
722   cross attention in our model’s processing pipeline.

726   Layout guided generation. In our implementation, the self-attention masking is applied at
727   timesteps t ∈ [1000, 900], in the decoder layers of the U-Net. The object layout loss is applied at
728   timesteps t ∈ [1000, 500], in all layers of the U-Net. Our pipeline used the Attend-and-Excite (Chefer
729   et al., 2023) code base as a starting point.

731   ReLayout. The ReLayout U-Net was built upon the U-Net Implementation of (Buda et al., 2019).
732   We trained the U-Net with a learning-rate of 8e-6, a batch-size of size 32 and the Adam optimizer. The
733   intersection penalty is set to 0.25 and the Dice penalty is set to 1. During training we apply a horizontal
734   flip augmentation across all masks, and shuffle augmentation where we randomly re-arrange the input
735   channels.
