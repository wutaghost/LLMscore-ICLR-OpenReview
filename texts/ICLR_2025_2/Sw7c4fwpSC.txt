034                                                    A BSTRACT
035
036
              Recent advances in diffusion models have significantly enhanced image genera-
037
              tion capabilities. However, customizing these models with new classes often leads
038           to unintended consequences that compromise their reliability. We introduce the
039           concept of open-world forgetting to emphasize the vast scope of these unintended
040           alterations, contrasting it with the well-studied closed-world forgetting, which is
041           measurable by evaluating performance on a limited set of classes or skills. Our
042           research presents the first comprehensive investigation into open-world forgetting
043           in diffusion models, focusing on semantic and appearance drift of representations.
044           We utilize zero-shot classification to analyze semantic drift, revealing that even
045           minor model adaptations lead to unpredictable shifts affecting areas far beyond
046
              newly introduced concepts, with dramatic drops in zero-shot classification of up
              to 60%. Additionally, we observe significant changes in texture and color of gen-
047
              erated content when analyzing appearance drift. To address these issues, we pro-
048
              pose a mitigation strategy based on functional regularization, designed to preserve
049           original capabilities while accommodating new concepts. Our study aims to raise
050           awareness of unintended changes due to model customization and advocates for
051           the analysis of open-world forgetting in future research on model customization
052           and finetuning methods. Furthermore, we provide insights for developing more
053           robust adaptation methodologies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Recent advancements in image generation have led to the development of remarkably powerful
058   foundational models capable of synthesizing highly realistic and diverse visual content. Techniques
059   such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and more recently
060   autoregressive models (Yu et al., 2022), Rectified Flows (Liu et al., 2023), and Denoising Diffusion
061   Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020), have each contributed to significant progress in the
062   field. These methods offer unique strengths in sample quality, diversity, and controllability. Among
063   them, diffusion models have gained particular prominence due to their recent successes and growing
064
      influence, especially in enabling text-based image generation (Shonenkov et al., 2023; Ramesh et al.,
      2022) and complementary multimodal conditioning (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023; Mou et al., 2023),
065
      making them a key focus in current research and applications.
066
067   Given the high-quality image generation capabilities of these models, a major focus of research has
068   been on how to efficiently incorporate new content and adapt them to new tasks and domains. To ad-
069   dress this challenge, various state-of-the-art transfer learning methods have been introduced. These
070
      include finetuning approaches such as DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023b) and CustomDiffusion (Ku-
      mari et al., 2023), which allow models to learn new concepts effectively. Conditioning-based meth-
071
      ods like ControlNet (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023) and IP-Adapters (Ye et al., 2023) enable precise
072
      control over generated images by incorporating additional guidance signals. Prompt methods like
073   Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2023) and Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2023) enable semantic image
074   editing and learning new concepts without modifying the base model. Parameter-efficient techniques
075   such as Low-Rank Adaptations (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) have shown great promise, allowing for
076   rapid adaptation with minimal computational overhead (Blattmann et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023b).
077   These techniques enable models to learn new concepts effectively, even with few examples.
078
      These methods for adapting diffusion models (Ruiz et al., 2023b; Kumari et al., 2023) mainly rely
079   on transfer learning and primarily focus on finetuning model weights to accommodate newly in-
080   troduced data. However, they lead to unforeseen changes in the model’s behavior, which can have
081   significant implications, altering the model’s existing knowledge, and skills, or the alignment be-
082   tween language and visual content within the network. The field of continual learning has long
083   studied the issue of catastrophic forgetting in neural networks when these aim to adapt to new data
084   (often referred to as new tasks) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; De Lange et al., 2021). Traditionally, this
085   field has focused on what we term closed-world forgetting, where evaluation is limited to a fixed
086   set of classes encountered in previously learned tasks or skills. This setting assumes a clear, prede-
087
      fined set of concepts to evaluate against. In contrast, modern foundation models introduce what we
      term open-world forgetting: degradation of the model’s capabilities across its vast, unconstrained
088
      knowledge space. Unlike closed-world settings, open-world forgetting is particularly challenging
089
      to measure since the model’s prior knowledge spans countless concepts, making it impossible to
090   exhaustively evaluate what has been forgotten or altered during the adaptation process.
091
092   In this paper, we focus on two popular personalization methods, namely Dreambooth (Ruiz et al.,
093
      2023b) and CustomDiffusion (Kumari et al., 2023), for a case study of open-world forgetting. These
      techniques are especially relevant, as they only add very little new knowledge to the network: a sin-
094
      gle new concept represented by a small set of typically 3-5 images. Although one might expect that
095
      finetuning the model with such limited data would have minimal impact on the vast knowledge of
096   the foundation model (e.g., Stable Diffusion), our analysis reveals that even these small updates can
097   lead to highly detrimental consequences. As Figure 1 illustrates, finetuning can drastically alter the
098   image representation of concepts seemingly unrelated to the training images. The complexity of the
099   forgetting underscores the need for a better understanding of how and where it occurs. Without this
100   understanding, finetuned models risk becoming less reliable, less robust, and ultimately less trust-
101   worthy, particularly in safety-critical applications where precision and predictability are paramount.
102   We propose to analyze open-world forgetting from several perspectives. First, we examine semantic
103   drift using the recent observation that diffusion models can function as zero-shot classifiers; we
104   propose to compare zero-shot capacity of models before and after adaptation on a set of image
105   classification data sets. Second, we analyze appearance drift by evaluating changes in color and
106   perceptual measurements before and after adaptation. Lastly, we assess the extent of forgetting in
107   closely related concepts (local drift) versus unrelated concepts. To address these three aspects of
      drift, we explore a straightforward, yet effective mitigation strategy by introducing a regularization

      technique during the training of new concepts. In conclusion, the main contributions of this work
109   are:
110
111   • We are the first to systematically analyze open-world forgetting in diffusion models due to model
112     adaptation. Results show that even when adapting to very small domains, the consequences can
113     be highly detrimental.
114   • We propose two approaches to analyze open-world forgetting, which are designed to assess se-
115     mantic and appearance drift caused by the adaptation. We leverage the zero-shot classification
116     capabilities of diffusion models to measure the semantic drift, and observe drastic performance
117     drops (of over 60% for some classes). Appearance drift analysis confirms that customization leads
118     to considerable changes in intra-class representation, color, and texture.
119   • We introduce a method to mitigate open-world forgetting, addressing the challenges of observed

121     while allowing for effective customization. Experiments confirm that it greatly reduces both the
122     semantic and appearance drift caused by open-world forgetting.

124   2   R ELATED WORK
125
126   Text-to-image diffusion model adaptation. Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion model adaptation is
127   also referred to T2I personalization or subject-driven image generation. This aims to adapt a given
128   model to a new concept by providing a few images and binding the new concept to a unique token.
129   As a result, the adaptation model can generate various renditions of the new concept guided by
130   text prompts. Depending on whether the adaptation method is finetuning the T2I model, they are
131   categorized into two main streams. One of the most representative methods focuses on learning new
132   concept tokens while freezing the T2I generative backbones. Textual Inversion (TI) (Gal et al., 2023)
133   is a pioneering work focusing on finding new pseudo-words by performing personalization in the text
134
      embedding space. The following works (Dong et al., 2022; Daras & Dimakis, 2022; Voynov et al.,
      2023; Han et al., 2023a) continue to improve this technique stream. Another stream is finetuning the
135
      T2I generative models while updating the modifier tokens. One of the most representative methods is
136
      DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023a), where the pre-trained T2I model learns to bind a modified unique
137   identifier to a specific subject given 3∼5 images, while it also updates the T2I model parameters.
138   HyperDreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2024) extends this approach for face domain by training per-subject
139   LoRAs to inform a HyperNetwork that can rapidly adapt to new subjects. CAFE (Zhou et al., 2024)
140   takes a different approach by leveraging instruction-based personalization through extensive datasets
141   of image-instruction pairs. Custom Diffusion (Kumari et al., 2023) and other approaches (Han et al.,
142   2023b; Chen et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023a) follow this pipeline and further improve the quality of
143   the generation.
144   Finetuning methods often achieve state-of-the-art performance but introduce forgetting in large T2I
145   models. While research focuses on improving new concept generation, it overlooks continuous
146   model updating and forgetting mitigation. Recent works (Sun et al., 2024; Smith et al., 2023) ad-
147   dress token forgetting but neglect other impacts of finetuning, such as semantic drifting in color,
148   appearance, and visual recognition, which this paper explores.
149
150   Assessing forgetting. The main challenge of continual learning is to learn incrementally and ac-
151   cumulate knowledge of new data while preventing catastrophic forgetting, which is defined as a
152   sudden drop in performance on previously acquired knowledge (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Mc-
153
      Clelland et al., 1995). The vast majority of studies on continual learning focus on, what we here
      call, closed-world forgetting, where the knowledge of the network can be represented by its per-
154
      formance on a limited set of classes (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; De Lange et al., 2021; Masana
155
      et al., 2022). However, as argued in the introduction, the growing importance of starting from large
156   pretrained models (also known as foundation models), which have a vast prior knowledge, requires
157   new techniques to assess forgetting. The forgetting of large language models (LLMs) during con-
158   tinual finetuning has received some attention in recent years, showing the importance of pretraining
159   to mitigate forgetting (Cossu et al., 2024), however, they mainly evaluate on down-stream-task per-
160   formance Scialom et al. (2022). To the best of our knowledge, open-world forgetting has not yet
161   been systematically analyzed for text-based image generation models which multi-modal nature can
      further worsen the impact of forgetting due to misalignment of the modalities.

189   3.2   C USTOMIZATION APPROACHES
190
191   Diffusion models often require finetuning for specific domains or user needs. This involves intro-
192

193

195   with a small set of images. It reuses an infrequent token of the vocabulary to represent a unique
196   subject, allowing the model to generate images of the subject in varied contexts or styles. This
197   approach induces language drift and reduced output diversity in the model, which is mitigated by
198
      replaying class-specific instances alongside the subject training, called prior preservation loss. The
      final training objective reads

      3.3   D RIFT C ORRECTION
217
218   The two studied approaches, Dreambooth (Ruiz et al., 2023b) and Custom Diffusion (Kumari et al.,
219   2023), apply finetuning to adapt to the new data: they mainly focus on how good the learned model
220   is on the target data, and do not study the possible detrimental effects for other classes. The Dream-
221   booth method includes a method called prior regularization, which by replaying general instances
222   of the concept being learned (see Eq. 3), helps to prevent the model from overfitting to the new data
223   and ensures that the representation of the superclass remains stable. This same mitigation strategy
224
      is also applied in custom diffusion (Kumari et al., 2023).
225   In this paper, we propose another regularization technique that can be applied during new concept
226   learning. The method is remarkably simple and is motivated from continual learning literature.
227   This field has proposed a variety of methods to counter forgetting during the learning of new con-
228   cepts (De Lange et al., 2021). Regularization methods aim to regularize the learning of new concepts
229   in such a way that it does not change weights which were found relevant for previous tasks. The
230
      field differentiates between parameter regularization methods, like EWC (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017)
      which directly learn an importance weight for all the network parameters, or functional (or data)
231
      regularization, like Learning-without-Forgetting (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Pan et al., 2020) which regu-
232
      larizes the weights indirectly by imposing a penalty on changes between the (intermediate) outputs
233   of a previous and current model.
234
235   We propose to apply a functional regularization loss to the network during the training of new
236
      concepts. Our loss, called drift correction loss, constrains the difference between the outputs of the
      pre-trained and fine-tuned models when the new concept is not present in the prompt. It has the
237
      following form:
238
239
                     Eϵ,x,c,t [wt ∥ϵ − ϵθ (xt , c, t)∥ + λwt′ ∥ϵθ∗ (xpr    pr ′          pr pr ′
                                                                     t′ , c , t ) − ϵθ (xt′ , c , t )∥],  (4)
240   where the second term is the distillation loss, λ is a relative weighting parameter and ϵθ∗ is the
241
      base model. This loss helps to maintain consistency in the model’s internal representations while
      allowing it to learn new information effectively. For the training process, we choose instances from
242
      the same class as the concept being learned, similar to those used by prior regularization. The change
243
      between our proposed drift correction method (Eq. 4) and the existing prior regularization (Eq. 3) is
244   that we do not require the finetuned network to estimate the true forward noise, but instead we want
245   it to estimate the same noise as the original starting network. We will see that this small change
246   significantly improves stability and mitigates forgetting.

      for DreamBooth with Drift Correction without the prior regularization (DB-DC\pr). Similarly, we

253   4     O PEN -W ORLD F ORGETTING IN G ENERATIVE M ODEL A DAPTATION

      In this section, we explore the effects of finetuning on foundational image generation models, partic-
      ularly how even slight modifications can significantly impair the model’s ability to retain previously
256
      acquired knowledge. We hypothesize that this degradation affects not only the model’s performance
257
      on newly introduced tasks, but also its capacity to accurately reproduce or classify previously learned
258   concepts. Given the broad scope of knowledge encompassed by the pretrained model, we refer to
259   this phenomenon as open-world forgetting.
260
261
      As an initial experiment, to assess open-world forgetting, we evaluate both the original unaltered
      model (called base model from now on) and the Customized Model Set on 10,000 user prompts
262
      from DiffusionDB (Wang et al., 2023) dataset (prompt examples are provided in Appendix B.1).
263
      Specifically, we measure the change of the resulting images using the cosine distance between CLIP-
264   I encodings (Radford et al., 2021) when generating images with the same prompt and seed. Distances
265   in the CLIP-I embedding are related to semantic similarity between images, with smaller distances
266   indicating more similar visual content and larger distances suggesting more significant differences
267   in the generated images. The distribution is plotted in Figure 2. For a detailed description of our
268   experimental setup, please refer to Appendix B.1. It is important to note that a personalization
269   method that does not alter the model would yield identical image outputs, resulting in a plot density
      concentrated at 1.

297   To better assess the impact of open-world forgetting, we propose to categorize the effects into two
298   distinct types: semantic and appearance drift. Semantic drift implies a change at the class or object
299
      level, where one concept is effectively misencoded as another. Appearance drift, on the other hand,
      refers to shifts in the appearance of a concept that do not necessarily imply a change in recognition
300
      (e.g., alterations in color, texture, or scene composition). It is important to note that these two
301
      categories are highly correlated, and changes in either of them impact the other.
302

      4.1   S EMANTIC D RIFT
304
305   Semantic drift refers to alterations in a model’s representation that cause the generation of semanti-
306   cally divergent content following customization. In the experiment depicted in Figure 2a, almost all
307   prompts exhibit some level of drift, with a notable long tail of highly dissimilar generations. Many
308   of these pronounced deviations have resulted in the generation of content that semantically no longer
309   align with the input prompt.
310

406
      Appearance drift manifests through alterations in visual attributes at varying degrees of intensity.
407
      Finetuning can cause the model to reinterpret these visual features, leading to inconsistencies be-
408
      tween original and newly generated outputs. Although initially subtle, appearance drift can substan-
409   tially impact customized models. For example, when attempting to learn and generate a set of new
410   concepts within the same context (e.g., for synthetic dataset creation or advertising purposes), each
411   customization of the base model may result in color and content changes. This variability makes it
412   challenging to achieve consistent results across multiple iterations. Moreover, as the customization
413   process alters the model’s manifold, the resulting model becomes less reliable in domains outside
414   the scope of the customization training images. This limitation highlights the importance of under-
415   standing and mitigating appearance drift in applications of fine-tuned text-to-image models.
416
417   How to measure appearance drift? Quantifying appearance drift presents unique challenges due
418   to the inherent variability in text-to-image model outputs. Traditional metrics like LPIPS (Zhang
419   et al., 2018) and DIST (Ding et al., 2020) are designed for image pair comparisons. However, the
420   inherent variability in T2I model outputs means that images generated from the same prompt can
421   vary significantly due to changes in seed, model weights, and prompt interpretation. Comparing
422   just two images fails to capture the full range of possible outputs and does not adequately represent
423   the model’s capabilities or biases. Consequently, conclusions drawn from such limited comparisons
424
      may lack statistical significance.
425   To address this variability, the research community has employed metrics that measure distances
426   between probability distributions of real-world observations and generated data2 . For example,
427   FID (Heusel et al., 2017) assumes Gaussian distributions and compute its distance. KID (Bińkowski
428   et al., 2018) is similar but uses a kernel-based approach that is more reliable. In addition to these
429   metrics, we also propose a new metric that directly measures the color drift between image sets.

      Color Drift Index. With the aim to introduce a clearly interpretable difference measure for the
452
      color content of the generated images, we propose a novel approach that measures specific properties
453   in pixel space. Our method focuses on color assessment, as traditional image generation metrics
454   tend to be more sensitive to intra-class and texture variations. Inspired by natural image statistics,
455   we introduce the Color Drift Index (CDI), which estimates the color distribution associated with a
456   specific concept by analyzing a large number of generated images.
457
      We utilize the CIE chromaticity diagram3 , where each pixel color is projected onto a lobe-shaped
458
      space representing all visible colors. Given a set of images I = {xi } and their density distribution in
459   the CIE chromaticity diagram pCIE (I), we calculate the CDI as the Wasserstein distance (Panaretos
460   & Zemel, 2019) between the color distributions of two sets of images:
461
                                                   CDI(Ia , Ib ) = Wp (pCIE (Ia ), pCIE (Ib )).                                                                                              (6)

463   We evaluate the appearance drift using the CDI together with KID (FID results are presented in
464   Appendix B.3). We conducted a comprehensive experiment adopting the carefully curated selection
465   of common concepts from the dataset of Torralba & Oliva (2003). For each prompt, we generated
466   1,000 images using both original and the ten models from the Customized Model Set. Figure 4
467   presents the mean values of the metrics across several adaptations, providing a visual representation
468   of the differences captured by each measure. For a detailed overview of the results, including in-
469
      dividual model performances, refer to Table 8 in the appendix. To help interpret the KID and CDI
      scores, we provide a control setting (blue line). In this configuration, we measure CDI and KID
470
      between images generated with the base model but using different seeds (functioning as a lower
471
      bound). If we are sampling from the same distribution, the base model should yield lower distances
472   (approaching zero as the number of samples grows) than the customized models (DB and CD).
473
474   The results in Figure 4 reveal two key insights. First, the base model consistently produces smaller
475
      distance values compared to DB, confirming that the distribution of each concept is indeed changing
      due to appearance drift. Second, each concept is affected differently by the drift, attributable to the
476
      fact that each concept relates to different parts of the model’s manifold. Furthermore, as demon-
477
      strated in Appendix C, the magnitude and nature of the drift vary as a function of the content in
478   the replay buffer and training images. Also, importantly, Figure 4 shows that our proposed method
479   (DC) significantly reduces the impact of the appearance drift introduced by customization methods.
480   Especially, the drift measure in KID is considerably reduced.
481
      These findings underscore the complexity and pervasiveness of appearance drift in fine-tuned text-
482
      to-image models. They highlight the need for robust mitigation strategies and careful consideration
483
      when deploying customized models in real-world applications, emphasizing the importance of on-
484   going research in this area to ensure the reliability and consistency of generated outputs.

502   4.3   L OCAL D RIFT
503
      In this paper, we have focused on drift throughout the whole diffusion model manifold. Previous
504
      works, especially those in the machine unlearning community (Gandikota et al., 2023), have con-
505
      centrated on local drift. When removing a concept from a model, it is believed to mainly impact the
506   representation of closely related concepts (hence the name local drift). Our findings suggest that the
507   effects of finetuning are more pervasive than previously thought, potentially influencing the model’s
508   understanding and representation of far-away categories as well as close-by (local) categories.
509
      Here, we repeated our experiments from Section 4.1 and 4.2 to measure the local semantic and local
510
      appearance drift. For this setup, we generated 1,000 samples of the closest concepts (superclasses)
511
      to each trained model (see Appendix B.1 for the details) and evaluated the CLIP-I, CDI, FID, and
512   KID metrics. As Figure 5a shows, the semantic drift is showing a significant shift towards the
513   left, indicating that local drift is more pronounced. For appearance drift, Figure 5c depicts a more
514   uniform color and KID shift over all the models; this shows that related concepts are affected with
515   a similar magnitude by appearance drift. Again the application of our proposed Drift Correction
516   method greatly reduces both the local semantic drift (as measured in Figure 5b and it almost removes
517   the local appearance drift as measured by KID, even though some color drift remains (see Figure 5c).
518
519   5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
520
521   Our investigation into unintended consequences of generative model adaptation reveals several key
522   findings. First, we demonstrate that finetuning foundational generative models leads to substantial
523   open-world forgetting, manifesting as both semantic and appearance drift. Our results show that
524   even minor adaptations can cause significant deterioration in the model’s ability to maintain its orig-
525   inal capabilities across a broad spectrum of concepts and visual attributes. To quantify these effects,
526   we introduced novel evaluation approaches: measuring semantic drift through zero-shot classifica-
527   tion performance across diverse image classification tasks, and assessing appearance drift through
528   our proposed Color Drift Index combined with traditional metrics like KID. These methods provide
529
      a framework for understanding and measuring the impact of model adaptation on both semantic un-
      derstanding and visual representation. Additionally, we propose a technique to mitigate open-world
530
      forgetting using functional regularization. Our experiments demonstrate that this method effectively
531
      preserves foundational knowledge while allowing for successful customization, offering a promising
532   direction for developing more robust adaptation techniques.
533
534   The increasing proliferation of foundation models and their widespread adaptation across various
535
      domains underscores the importance of understanding and addressing open-world forgetting. While
      our study provides valuable insights and measurement techniques, the vast knowledge space of
536
      foundation models makes comprehensive evaluation challenging. Future research directions might
537
      explore active optimization methods to identify the most affected areas of model knowledge during
538   adaptation. Furthermore, extending our methodology to other forms of model adaptation, such as
539   unlearning techniques, remains an important area for future work.

       C.1      D OES FINETUNING LEAD TO LOSS OF DIVERSITY ?
1083
1084   Finetuning large foundational models on a limited set of images (typically around 5) of a specific
1085   subject can lead to overfitting, a phenomenon observed in previous studies such as DreamBooth.
1086   This overfitting often results in a loss of diversity in generated images and a noticeable shift towards
1087   the characteristics of the training subject. While prior regularization techniques have been employed
1088   to mitigate this shift, they have not fully resolved the issue, as our analysis demonstrates.
1089   To assess the impact of finetuning on diversity, we adapt the metric introduced in the DreamBooth
1090   study. This metric quantifies diversity by calculating the average Learned Perceptual Image Patch
1091   Similarity (LPIPS) between generated images of the same subject using identical prompts. A higher
1092   LPIPS score indicates greater diversity among the generated images.
1093   Our proposed method not only improves the mitigation of subject shifting, as evidenced in Figures 4
1094   and 5, but also maintains the diversity of the original model. To validate this, we conducted an
1095   extensive evaluation using 100 different prompts, each generating 100 images. These prompts were
1096   sourced from the DiffusionDB subset, as detailed in Appendix B.1.
1097
       Figure 12 presents the results of our diversity analysis. The data demonstrates that our method
1098
       preserves diversity at a level comparable to, or even exceeding, previous approaches. This finding
1099   is particularly significant as it indicates that our technique not only addresses the shifting problem
1100   more effectively but does so without compromising the model’s ability to generate diverse outputs.
1101
1102
       The preservation of diversity while improving subject fidelity represents a crucial advancement in
       finetuning methodologies for large generative models. It ensures that the fine-tuned model retains
1103
       its creative capacity and versatility across a wide range of prompts and subjects, even as it gains
1104
       enhanced capabilities in representing specific training subjects. This balance between specificity
1105   and diversity is essential for the practical application of fine-tuned models in various creative and
1106   technical domains.
