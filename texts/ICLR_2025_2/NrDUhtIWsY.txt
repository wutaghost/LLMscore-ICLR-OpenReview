011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge distillation (KD) has become a fundamental technique for model com-
014            pression in object detection tasks. The data noise and training randomness may
015
               cause the knowledge of the teacher model to be unreliable, referred to as knowl-
               edge uncertainty. Existing methods only transfer this knowledge and could limit
016
               the student’s ability to capture and understand the potential “dark knowledge”.
017
               In this work, we introduce a new strategy that explicitly incorporates knowl-
018            edge uncertainty, named Uncertainty-Driven Knowledge Extraction and Transfer
019            (UET). Given that the knowledge distribution is unknown and high-dimensional in
020            practice, we introduce a simple yet effective sampling method with Monte Carlo
021            dropout (MC dropout) to estimate the teacher’s knowledge uncertainty. Lever-
022            aging information theory, we integrate knowledge uncertainty into the conven-
023            tional KD process, allowing the student model to benefit from knowledge diver-
024            sity. UET is a plug-and-play method that integrates seamlessly with existing distil-
025            lation techniques. We validate our approach through comprehensive experiments
026
               across various distillation strategies, detectors, and backbones. Specifically, UET
               achieves state-of-the-art results, with a ResNet50-based GFL detector obtaining
027
               44.1% mAP on the COCO dataset—surpassing baseline performance by 3.9%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep learning-based object detectors have demonstrated strong performance in fundamental tasks
033   LeCun et al. (2015); Girshick et al. (2014), but their large-scale parameters limit deployment in
034   resource-constrained, real-world applications. Knowledge distillation (KD) Hinton et al. (2015)
035   offers an effective model compression strategy, leveraging well-trained teacher model’s dark knowl-
036   edge to guide student models. In object detection, aligning the intermediate representations between
037   teacher and student models is a common practice Cao et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2022c); Zhang &
038   Ma (2023); Kang et al. (2021), often following the “Knowledge Extraction-Transfer (ET)” paradigm.
039
      However, a key question arises: Is the teacher’s knowledge always reliable for distillation?
040   The answer might be negative. In real-world scenarios, uncertainty is intrinsic to knowledge and
041   critically affects knowledge transfer Szulanski (2000). In object detection, uncertainty arises from
042   noisy training data and the inherent randomness of model training. Despite careful annotation,
043   imprecise bounding boxes introduce aleatoric uncertainty Kendall & Gal (2017), particularly in
044   complex scenarios like occlusions. Additionally, model design and training randomness contribute
045
      to epistemic uncertainty Kendall & Gal (2017). Together, these uncertainties shape the teacher’s
      knowledge, which is often overlooked in traditional distillation paradigms, potentially limiting the
046
      student model’s ability to learn latent knowledge. To explore this further, we conducted repeated
047
      training on the COCO 2017 dataset Lin et al. (2014) using the same teacher detector Li et al. (2020),
048   resulting in two distinct teacher models, A and B (Fig. 1 (a)). Both achieved nearly identical perfor-
049   mance (mAP 44.9, Fig. 1 (c)). However, when distilling knowledge from these teachers to students
050   C and D using FGD Yang et al. (2022c), although their performance was similar, their feature rep-
051   resentations varied significantly (Fig. 1 (b)). This suggests that: (1) their feature representations
052   differ due to aleatoric and epistemic uncertainties, which we refer to as knowledge uncertainty in
053   this work, and (2) both teachers A and B contribute valuable knowledge. It is well known that
      knowledge distillation relying on multiple teachers’ knowledge can offer diverse insights and has

070   Traditional ET paradigms align only the deterministic knowledge from one teacher, treating it as a
071   “hard label” providing valuable knowledge, which also limits the student model’s ability to learn
072   potential “dark knowledge”. While uncertainty quantification has proven beneficial in fields like
073   segmentation Holder & Shafique (2021) and localization Yang et al. (2022b), the high-dimensional
074   and unknown distribution of teacher knowledge makes direct quantification challenging. Ensemble
075   models can capture uncertainty but are computationally expensive and impractical in many cases.
076   In this work, we propose a novel distillation paradigm, Uncertainty-driven Knowledge Extraction
077   and Transfer (UET). Unlike the conventional ET approach, UET integrates both deterministic and
078   uncertain knowledge based on information theory, allowing the student model to benefit from both
079   the precision of deterministic knowledge and the diversity of uncertain knowledge. To estimate the
080   teacher’s knowledge uncertainty, we employ Monte Carlo dropout (MC dropout) Gal & Ghahramani
081   (2016), offering a simple yet effective way to capture and integrate uncertainty into the distillation
082   process. Importantly, UET is a plug-and-play method that integrates seamlessly with existing distil-
083
      lation techniques. We validate the effectiveness of UET through extensive experiments on the MS
      COCO dataset, applying it across various distillation strategies, detectors, and backbone architec-
084
      tures. When using FGD as the baseline, ResNet50-based detectors such as GFL Li et al. (2020),
085
      Faster R-CNN Ren et al. (2015), RetinaNet Lin et al. (2017b), and FCOS Tian et al. (2020) achieve
086   mAP scores of 44.1%, 40.8%, 39.9%, and 42.9% respectively, outperforming prior state-of-the-art
087   KD methods. In summary, the contributions of this paper can be summarized:
088
089                        • We propose a new paradigm of “Uncertainty-Driven Knowledge Extraction and Transfer”,
090                          for the feature-based distillation methods. This approach integrates deterministic knowl-
091                          edge and uncertain knowledge based on the information theory, which can effectively guide
092                          the student detector’s training.
093
                           • We propose a simple yet effective method for uncertainty estimation by combining Monte
094
                             Carlo dropout, enabling the capture of the teacher model’s uncertainty during the knowl-
095                          edge distillation process. This method seamlessly integrates with existing distillation meth-
096                          ods, delivering improvements without any additional complexity.
097
098                        • We conduct extensive experiments on the COCO dataset to verify the effectiveness of the
099                          proposed paradigm across various distillation strategies, detectors, and backbone architec-
100                          tures, achieving SoTA performance.

104   2.1              O BJECT DETECTION WITH KNOWLEDGE DISTILLATION
105
106   Object detection is one of the most fundamental tasks in image processing. Due to the advancements
107   in deep learning, detectors based on convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have achieved remark-
      able results Li et al. (2020); Lin et al. (2017b); Tian et al. (2020); Ren et al. (2015). Additionally,


                                                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      recent developments have introduced anchor-free methods (like FCOS Tian et al. (2020)) to reduce
109   the detector’s reliance on anchors. In this work, we explore the effectiveness of the proposed method
110   across different types of detectors.
111
112
      Knowledge distillation aims to transfer knowledge from a well-trained but cumbersome teacher
      detector to a lightweight student detector. Currently, KD in object detection can be categorized
113
      into feature-based distillation Cao et al. (2022); Yang et al. (2022c); Zhang & Ma (2023); Kang
114
      et al. (2021); Huang et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2022d); Zhu et al. (2023); De Rijk et al. (2022)
115   and logit-based distillation Zheng et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Zhao et al.
116   (2022). Due to its simpler and more uniform form, feature-based knowledge distillation is popular
117   in object detection. Presently, feature-based knowledge distillation follows the paradigm of “ET”.
118   Most works focus on the design of discriminative knowledge extraction modules (such as scale-
119   aware knowledge Zhu et al. (2023), relationship-related knowledge Tian et al. (2020), etc.) and
120   transfer methods (Pearson Correlation Coefficient Cao et al. (2022), SSIM De Rijk et al. (2022),
121   etc.). In contrast to other works, we consider the uncertainty of knowledge in the teacher model and
122   propose a novel UET paradigm for feature-based distillation. We aim to improve the existing feature
123
      distillation procedure by following UET to help the student model explore potential knowledge.
124
125   2.2   U NCERTAINTY ESTIMATION
126
127
      Uncertainty often arises from insufficient knowledge and data during model training, prompting
      the need for robust uncertainty estimation methods to quantify prediction reliability Gal & Ghahra-
128
      mani (2016); Hüllermeier & Waegeman (2021); Kendall & Gal (2017); Kononenko (1989); Jalonen
129
      (2012); Duncan et al. (2017); Sun et al. (2017); Milanés-Hermosilla et al. (2021); Ledda et al. (2023).
130   Recent research has witnessed a surge in exploring uncertainty in the knowledge distillation task.
131   For instance, the UNIX Xu et al. (2023a) proposed to reduce computation costs by combining un-
132   certainty sampling and adaptive mixup to prioritize informative samples. Uncertainty distillation
133   method Holder & Shafique (2021), on the other hand, aimed to quantify prediction uncertainty by
134   training a compact model to mimic the output distribution of a large ensemble of models, enabling
135   efficient and reliable uncertainty estimation. Similarly, the Uncertainty-aware Contrastive Distilla-
136   tion (UCD) Yang et al. (2022a) method attempts to alleviate catastrophic forgetting in incremental
137   semantic segmentation by contrasting features between new and frozen models. PAD Zhang et al.
138
      (2020) proposed prime-aware adaptive distillation, which incorporates uncertainty learning to iden-
      tify prime samples in distillation and adaptively emphasize their impact. Similarly, AKD Zhang
139
      et al. (2023) strived to generate inference ensemble models from a teacher model and adaptively
140
      adjust their contribution to knowledge transfer using an uncertainty-aware factor, refining the pre-
141   vious distillation methods for dense prediction tasks. Despite these advances, existing uncertainty
142   estimation methods often require fine-grained modeling, and there has been limited exploration in
143   the object detection domain. To address this limitation, we introduce knowledge uncertainty into the
144   KD framework for object detection, guiding students to learn more potential knowledge to enhance
145   its detection performance.

      3.3   J OINT KNOWLEDGE DISTILLATION OF DETERMINISTIC AND UNCERTAINTY
217
218   After formulating the feature distillation with knowledge uncertainty, we can make use of the
219   teacher’s knowledge uncertainty in the training process of the student model. However, the con-
220   ditional distribution of the teacher’s knowledge is unknown, which results in sampling from this dis-
221   tribution is also challenging. Modeling the uncertainty of the deep learning model has been a topic
222   of considerable attention Hüllermeier & Waegeman (2021); Kendall & Gal (2017). Especially, Gal
223   & Ghahramani (2016) establishes a theoretical connection between dropout training in deep neural
224
      networks and Bayesian inference in deep Gaussian processes. More importantly, they introduced a
      Monte Carlo estimate method based on the dropout, referred to as MC dropout, which can realize the
225
      estimating of uncertainty. There has been a lot of work Zhang et al. (2023); Yelleni et al. (2024); Xu
226
      et al. (2023b) estimating uncertainty through MC dropout and achieving outstanding performance
227   in specific fields. Inspired by this, we utilize MC dropout to estimate the uncertain knowledge of the
228   teacher models. More specifically, we perform M -times the teacher’s forward process with dropout
229   and denote the teacher’s knowledge of the j-th run as DropoutTj (x). We also discuss the impact of
230   the MC dropout on training time in Section A.5. The set {DropoutTj (x)|j = 1, . . . , M } is used as

      a sample of the uncertain knowledge FT as in Equation 2. Then, we have

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this paper, we investigate the significance of introducing knowledge uncertainty from teacher
533
      models in object detection distillation and its impact on the learning process of student detectors.
534   Building upon this, we propose a novel UET general distillation paradigm, aimed at facilitating the
535   acquisition of latent knowledge by student models, while easily being adaptable to other distilla-
536   tion methods. Additionally, we present a simple yet effective uncertainty estimation approach by
537   integrating MC dropout, which seamlessly introduces uncertainty knowledge at minimal computa-
538   tional cost. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of following the UET paradigm across
539   various types of KDs, detectors, and backbones. In summary, we demonstrate that utilizing teacher
      knowledge uncertainty enhances the learning capabilities of student models in the KD process.

760   Expanding to lightweight detectors presents additional challenges in KD, as the knowledge gap
761   between teachers and students tends to be larger. Furthermore, due to the limitations in learning
762   capacity, student models may not fully comprehend the knowledge of the teacher model. This im-
763   plies that knowledge incomprehensible to these student models may affect their learning process as
764   noise. Therefore, introducing knowledge uncertainty in the teacher model may be more crucial in
765
      the KD process for lightweight detectors. To further explore this, we conduct experiments using the
      GFL framework, and the results are presented in Table 10. Compared with the setting of Section
766
      4.1, we only alternate the ResNet50 with a lightweight backbone in the student detector. As hypoth-
767
      esized, introducing knowledge uncertainty is crucial in lightweight detectors, leading to significant
768   improvements. When the student model with ResNet34 as the backbone, introducing knowledge un-
769   certainty results in a detector achieving 41.9% mAP, which is 2.2% higher than the 39.7% obtained
770   with pure FGD.
