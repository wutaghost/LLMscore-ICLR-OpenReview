009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011               This paper introduces a new challenge for image similarity search in the context of
012               fashion, addressing the inherent ambiguity in this domain stemming from complex
013               images. We present Referred Visual Search (RVS), a task allowing users to define
014               more precisely the desired similarity, following recent interest in the industry. We
015               release a new large public dataset, LRVS-Fashion, consisting of 272k fashion
016               products with 842k images extracted from fashion catalogs, designed explicitly
017
                  for this task. However, unlike traditional visual search methods in the industry,
                  we demonstrate that superior performance can be achieved by bypassing explicit
018
                  object detection and adopting weakly-supervised conditional contrastive learning
019
                  on image tuples. Our method is lightweight and demonstrates robustness, reaching
020               Recall at one superior to strong detection-based baselines against 2M distractors.1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038
      Image embeddings generated by deep neural networks play a crucial role in a wide range of computer
039   vision tasks. Image retrieval has gained substantial prominence, leading to the development of
040   dedicated vector database systems (Johnson et al., 2019). These systems facilitate efficient retrieval
041   by comparing embedding values and identifying the most similar images within the database.
042
      Image similarity search in the context of fashion presents a unique challenge due to the inherently
043
      ill-founded nature of the problem. The primary issue arises from the fact that two images can be
044
      considered similar in various ways, leading to ambiguity in defining a single similarity metric. For
045   instance, two images of clothing items may be deemed similar based on their color, pattern, style, or
046   even the model pictured. This multifaceted nature of similarity in fashion images complicates the
047   task of developing a universally applicable similarity search algorithm, as it must account for the
048   various ways in which images can be related.
049
      An intuitive approach is to request users furnish supplementary information delineating their interests,
050
      such as providing an image of an individual and denoting interest in the hat (see Fig. 1). Numerous
051   industry leaders including Google, Amazon, and Pinterest have adopted this tactic, however academic
052   discourse on potential alternative methodologies for this task remains scarce as the domain lacks

      dedicated datasets. For convenience, we propose terming this task Referred Visual Search (RVS),
055   as it is likely to garner attention from the computer vision community due to the utility for product
056   search in extensive catalogs.
057
058
      In practice, object selection in complex scenes is classically tackled using object detection and crops
      (Jing et al., 2015; Hu et al., 2018; Ge et al., 2019; Shiau et al., 2020). Some recent approaches use
059
      categorical attributes (Dong et al., 2021) or text instead (Das et al., 2022), and automatically crop
060
      the image based on learned attention to input attributes. It is also possible to ask the user to perform
061   the crop himself, yet in all the situations the performance of the retrieval will be sensitive to this
062   extraction step making it costly to build a generic retrieval tool. Recently, Jiao et al. (2023) went
063   a step further, incorporating prior knowledge about the taxonomy of fashion attributes and classes
064   without using crops. They use a multi-granularity loss and two sub-networks to learn attribute and
065   class-specific representations, resulting in improved robustness for fashion retrieval, yet without
066   providing any code.
067   In this work, we seek to support these efforts by providing a dataset dedicated to RVS. We extracted
068   a subset of LAION 5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022) focused on pairs of images sharing a labeled
069   similarity in the domain of fashion, and propose a method to eliminate the need for explicit detection
070   or segmentation, while still producing similarities in the embedding space specific to the conditioning.
071   We think that such end-to-end approach has the potential to be more generalizable and robust, whereas
072   localization-dependent approaches hinge on multi-stage processing heuristics specific to the dataset.
073   This paper presents two contributions to the emerging field of Referred Visual Search, aiming at
074   defining image similarity based on conditioning information.
075
076    X The introduction of a new dataset, referred to as LRVS-Fashion, which is derived from the
077      LAION-5B dataset and comprises 272k fashion products with nearly 842k images. This dataset
078      features a test set with an addition of more than 2M distractors, enabling the evaluation of method
079      robustness in relation to gallery size. The dataset’s pairs and additional metadata are designed to
080
         necessitate the extraction of particular features from complex images.
081    X An efficient method for learning to extract referred embeddings using weakly-supervised training.
082      Our approach demonstrates superior accuracy against a strong detection-based baseline and
083
         existing published work. Furthermore, our method exhibits robustness against a large number of
         distractors, maintaining high R@1 even when increasing the number of distractors to 2M.

086   2    R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Retrieval Datasets. Standard datasets in metric learning literature consider that the images are
089   object-centric, and focus on single salient objects (Wah et al., 2011; Krause et al., 2013; Song et al.,
090   2016). In the fashion domain there exist multiple datasets dedicated to product retrieval, with paired
091
      images depicting the same product and additional labeled attributes. A recurrent focus of such
      datasets is cross-domain retrieval, where the goal is to retrieve images of a given product taken in
092
      different situations, for example consumer-to-shop (Liu et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2016; Liu et al.,
093
      2016; Ge et al., 2019), or studio-to-shop (Liu et al., 2016; Lasserre et al., 2018). The domain gap is in
094   itself a challenge, with issues stemming from irregular lighting, occlusions, viewpoints, or distracting
095   backgrounds. However, the query domain (consumer images for example) often contains scenes with
096   multiple objects, making queries ambiguous. This issue has been circumvented with the use of object
097   detectors and landmarks detectors (Kiapour et al., 2015; Huang et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016; Ge et al.,
098   2019). Some are not accessible anymore (Kiapour et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2016).
099
      With more than 272k distinct training product identities captured in multi-instance scenes, our new
100   dataset proposes an exact matching task similar to the private Zalando dataset (Lasserre et al., 2018),
101   while being larger than existing fashion retrieval datasets and publicly available. We also create an
102   opportunity for new multi-modal approaches, with captions referring to the product of interest in
103   each complex image, and for robustness to gallery size with 2M added distractors at test time.
104
105   Instance Retrieval. In the last decade, content-based image retrieval has changed because of the
106   arrival of deep learning, which replaced many handcrafted heuristics (keypoint extraction, descriptors,
107   geometric matching, re-ranking. . . ) (Dubey, 2022). In the industry this technology has been of
      interest to retail companies and search engines to develop visual search solutions, with new challenges

108
      stemming from the large scale of such databases. Initially using generic pretrained backbones to
109   extract embeddings with minimal retraining (Yang et al., 2017), methods have evolved toward domain-
110   specific embeddings supervised by semantic labels, and then multi-task domain-specific embeddings,
111   leveraging additional product information (Zhai et al., 2019; Bell et al., 2020; Tran et al., 2019). The
112   latest developments in the field incorporate multi-modal features for text-image matching (Zhan et al.,
113   2021; Yu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023), with specific vision-language pretext tasks.
114
      However, these methods often consider that the query image is unambiguous, and often rely on a
115   region proposal system to crop the initial image (Jing et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2018;
116   Shiau et al., 2020; Bell et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022). In our work, we bypass this step and propose an
117   end-to-end framework, leveraging the Transformer architecture to implicitly perform this detection
118   step conditionally to the referring information.
119
120   Referring Tasks. Referring tasks are popular in vision-language processing, in particular Referring
121   Expression Comprehension and Segmentation where a sentence designates an object in a scene, that
122   the network has to localize. For the comprehension task (similar to open-vocabulary object detection)
123   the goal is to output a bounding box (Luo et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2022a;b; Liu et al., 2023). The
124   segmentation task aims at producing an instance mask for images (Zhang et al., 2017; Luo et al.,
125   2020; Huang et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023) and recently videos (Wu et al., 2022;
126
      Botach et al., 2022). In this paper, we propose a referring expression task, where the goal is to embed
      the designated object of interest into a representation that can be used for retrieval. We explore the
127
      use of Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2023) and Segment Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023) to create a
128
      strong baseline on our task.
129
130
      Conditional Similarity. Conditional similarity search has been studied through the retrieval process
131
      and the embedding process. On one hand, for the retrieval process, Hamilton et al. (2021) propose to
132
      use a dynamically pruned random projection tree. On the other hand, first steps in conditional visual
133   similarity learning focused on attribute-specific retrieval, explicitly defining different similarity spaces
134   depending on labeled attributes, either with a mask (Veit et al., 2017) or different projection heads
135   (Mu and Guttag, 2022). In the fashion domain. ASEN (Ma et al., 2020) uses spatial and channel
136   attention to an attribute embedding to extract specific features in a global branch. Dong et al. (2021)
137   and Das et al. (2022) build upon this model and add a local branch working on an attention-based
138   crop. Recently, Jiao et al. (2023) incorporated prior knowledge about fashion taxonomy in this
139   process to create class-conditional embeddings based on known fine-grained attributes, using multiple
140   attribute-conditional attention modules. In our work, we use Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al.,
141
      2021) to implicitly pool features depending on the conditioning information, without relying on
      explicit ROI cropping or labeled fine-grained attributes.
142
143   Recently, Vaze et al. (2023) proposed a zero-shot benchmark to study general Conditional Image Sim-
144   ilarity (CIS) through four evaluation datasets, each exploring one combination of "Focus"/"Change"
145   an "Attribute"/"Object". Their unnamed "Focus an Object" task closely aligns with our proposed
146   RVS, yet it is evaluated with a gallery size of only 15 images whereas our benchmark scales to a
147
      significantly larger and more realistic setup. Conversely, "Change an Attribute" or "Change an Ob-
      ject", corresponds to Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) (Vo et al., 2019) another conditional retrieval
148
      task popular in fashion, that uses a text prompt as an image editing instruction. A common approach
149
      involves using a combiner network after embedding both the image and the modifying text (Lee et al.,
150   2021; Chen et al., 2022; Baldrati et al., 2023b). More recent work leverages textual inversion and text
151   encoders to address zero-shot CIR (Baldrati et al., 2023a; Gu et al., 2024; Agnolucci et al., 2024)
152
153
      In a different domain, Asai et al. (2022) tackle a conditional document retrieval task, where the user
      intent is made explicit by concatenating instructions to the query documents.

      3.1       C ONSTRUCTION
197
198   Image Collection. The URLs in LRVS-F are a subset of LAION-5B, curated from content delivery
199   networks of fashion brands and retailers. By analyzing the URL structures we identified product
200   identifiers, which we extracted with regular expressions to recreate groups of images depicting the
201   same product. URLs without distinct identifiers or group membership were retained as distractors.
202
203
      Annotations. We generated synthetic labels for the image complexity, the category of the product,
204   and added new captions to replace the noisy LAION alt-texts. For the complexity labels, we
205   employed active learning to incrementally train a classifier to discern between isolated objects on
206   neutral backdrops and photoshoot scenes. The product categories were formed by aggregating various
207   fine-grained apparel items into 10 coarse groupings. This categorization followed the same active
208   learning protocol. Furthermore, the original LAION captions exhibited excessive noise, including
209   partial translations or raw product identifiers. Therefore, we utilized BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023) to
210   generate new, more descriptive captions.
211
212   Dataset Split. We grouped together images associated to the same product identifier and dropped
213   the groups that did not have at least a simple and a complex image. We manually selected 400 of
214   them for the validation set, and 2,000 for the test set. The distractors are all the images downloaded
215   previously that were labeled as "simple" but not used in product groups. This mostly includes images
      for which it was impossible to extract any product identifier.

      Dataset Cleaning. In order to mitigate false negatives in our results, we utilized Locality Sensitive
217   Hashing and OpenCLIP ViT-B/16 embeddings to eliminate duplicates. Specifically, we removed
218   duplicates between the test targets and test distractors, as well as between the validation targets and
219   validation distractors. Throughout our experiments, we did not observe any false negatives in the
220   results. However, there remains a small quantity of near-duplicates among the distractor images.

      3.2   C OMPOSITION
223
224
      In total, we extracted 272,451 products for training, represented in 841,718 images. This represents
225
      581,526 potential simple/complex positive pairs. We additionally extracted 400 products (800 images)
226   to create a validation set, and 2,000 products (4,000 images) for a test set. We added 99,541 simple
227   images in the validation gallery as distractors, and 2,000,014 in the test gallery.
228
229
      We randomly sampled images and manually verified the quality of the labels. For the complexity
      labels, we measured an empirical error rate of 1/1000 on the training set and 3/1000 for the distractors.
230
      For the product categories, we measured a global empirical error rate of 1%, with confusions mostly
231
      arising from semantically similar categories and images where object scale was ambiguous in isolated
232   settings (e.g. long shirt vs. short dress, wristband vs. hairband). The BLIP2 captions we provided
233   exhibit good quality, increasing the mean CLIP similarity with the image by +7.4%. However, as
234   synthetic captions, they are not perfect and may contain occasional hallucinations.

239   3.3   B ENCHMARK
240
241   We define a benchmark on LRVS-F to evaluate different methods on a held-out test set with a large
242   number of distractors. The test set contains 2,000 unseen products, and up to 2M distractors. Each
243
      product in the set is represented by a pair of images - a simple one and a complex one. The objective
      of the retrieval task is to retrieve the simple image of each product from among a vast number of
244
      distractors and other simple test images, given the complex image and conditioning information.
245
246   For this dataset, we choose to frame the benchmark as an asymmetric task : the representation of
247   simple images (the gallery) should not be computed conditionally. This choice is motivated by three
248   reasons. First, when using precise free-form conditioning (such as LAION texts, which contain
249
      hashed product identifiers and product names) a symmetric encoding would enable a retrieval based
      solely on this information, completely disregarding the image query. Second, for discrete (categorical)
250
      conditioning it allows the presence of items of unknown category in the gallery, which is a situation
251
      that may occur in distractors. Third, these images only depict a single object, thus making referring
252   information unnecessary. A similar setting is used by Asai et al. (2022).
253
254   The main metric is Recall at 1 (R@1) with 2M added distractors, as it is the most challenging and
255
      realistic setting. We report R@1 for other gallery sizes to evaluate the robustness of the methods,
      along with categorical accuracy at 1 (Cat@1) as a sanity check. These metrics can also be evaluated
256
      with respect to the object category or its visibility. We propose such analysis in Appendix B.4.
257
      Additionally, we provide a list of subsets sampled with replacement to be used for boostrapped
258   estimation of confidence intervals on the metrics. We created 10 subsets of 1000 test products, and
259   10 subsets of 10K, 100K and 1M distractors. We also propose a validation set of 400 products with
260   nearly 100K other distractors to monitor the training and for hyperparameter search.

303   et al., 2021). The conditioning is an additional input token with an associated learnable positional
304   encoding, concatenated to the sequence of image patches. The content of this token can either be
305   learned directly (e.g. for discrete categorical conditioning), or be generated by another network (e.g.

330   5.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
331
332   All our models take as input images of size 224 × 224, and output an embedding vector of 512
333
      dimensions. We use CLIP weights as initialization, and then train our models for 30 epochs with
      AdamW (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2019) and a maximum learning rate of 10−5 determined by a
334
      learning rate range test (Smith, 2017). To avoid distorting pretrained features (Kumar et al., 2022),
335
      we start by only training the final projection and new input embeddings (conditioning and positional)
336   for a single epoch, with a linear warm-up schedule. We then train all parameters for the rest of the
337   epochs with a cosine schedule.
338
339
      We pad the images to a square with white pixels, before resizing the largest side to 224 pixels. During
      training, we apply random horizontal flip, and random resized crops covering at least 80% of the
340
      image area. We evaluate the recall of the model on the validation set at each epoch, and report test
341
      metrics (recall and categorical accuracy) for the best performing validation checkpoint.
342
343   We used mixed precision and sharded loss to run our experiments on multiple GPUs. B/32 models
344   were trained for 6 hours on 2 V100 GPUs, with a total batch size of 360. B/16 were trained for 9
345
      hours on 12 V100, with a batch size of 420. Batch sizes were chosen to maximize GPU memory use.

347   5.2   R ESULTS
348
349   Zero-shot Models We evaluate pretrained models on LRVS-F without fine-tuning to assess the
350   zero-shot performance of existing methods. A CLIP ViT-B/16 yields 4.8%R@1 with 2M distractors,
351
      which estimates the proportion of queries that are visually similar to the target item. We note that
      the Cat@1 is already of 63.3%. MagicLens (Zhang et al., 2024) is a generalist CIS method trained
352
      on 36.7M triplets, achieving SOTA zero-shot results on the GeneCIS benchmark (Vaze et al., 2023).
353
      It reaches 52.2%R@1 with 10K distractors but drops to 7.5%R@1 with 2M distractors, highlighting
354   the significant challenge posed by our large gallery. We experiment with using a large multimodal
355   model (LMM), MiniCPM-V (Yao et al., 2024). We experiment with using the hidden state of the last
356   token or a global average pooling as the embedding, for multiple layers. The best result, 9.2%R@1, is
357   obtained using global average pooling on the last layer, underscoring the need for further research
358   into optimizing LMM embeddings for challenging retrieval tasks. Finally, we evaluate FAME-ViL
359   (Han et al., 2023), a model pretrained on various fashion-related tasks, including CIR. It achieves
360   9.3%R@1 confirming that while CIR and RVS are closely related, they remain distinct tasks. Please
361   refer to Table 2 for the complete results.
362
363   Detection-based Baseline We leverage the recent Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2023) and Segment
364   Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023) to create a baseline approach based on object detection and segmenta-
365   tion. In this setting, we feed the model the query image and conditioning information, which can be
366   either the name of the category or a caption. Subsequently, we use the output crops or masks to train
367   a ViT following the aforementioned procedure. Please refer to Tab. 1 for the results.
368   Initial experiments conducted with pretrained CLIP features showed a slight preference toward
369   segmenting the object. However, training the image encoder revealed that superior performances
370   can be attained by training the network on crops. Our supposition is that segmentation errors lead to
371   definitive loss of information, whereas the network’s capacity is sufficient for it to learn to disregard
372   irrelevant information and recover from a badly cropped image.
373   Overall, using Grounding DINO makes for a strong baseline. However, it is worth highlighting that
374   the inherent imprecision of category names frequently results in overly large bounding boxes, which
375   in turn limits the performances of the models. Indeed, adding more information into the dataset such
376   as bounding boxes with precise categories would help, yet this would compromise the scalability
377   of the model as such data is costly to obtain. Conversely, the more precise boxes produced by the
      caption-based model reach 67.8%R@1 against 2M distractors.

415   Categorical Conditioning We compare our method with categorical detection-based approaches,
416   and unconditional ViTs finetuned on our dataset. To account for the extra conditioning information
417   used in our method, we evaluated the latter on filtered indexes, with only products belonging to the
418   correct category. We did not try to predict the item of interest from the input picture, and instead
419   consider it as a part of the query. We also report unfiltered metrics for reference. Results are in Tab. 2.
420   Training the ViTs on our dataset greatly improves their performances, both in terms of R@1 and
421   categorical accuracy. Filtering the gallery brings a modest mean gain of 2 − 4%R@1 across all
422   quantities of distractors (Tab. 2), reaching 62.4%R@1 for 2M distractors with a ViT-B/16 architecture.
423   In practice, this approach is impractical as it necessitates computing and storing an index for each
424   category to guarantee a consistent quantity of retrieved items. Moreover, a qualitative evaluation of
425   the filtered results reveals undesirable behaviors. When filtering on a category divergent from the
426   network’s intrinsic focus, we observe the results displaying colors and textures associated with the
427   automatically focused object rather than the requested one.
428   We also compare with ASEN (Dong et al., 2021) trained on our dataset using the authors’ released
429   code. This conditional architecture uses a global and a local branch with conditional spatial attention
430   modules, respectively based on ResNet50 and ResNet34 backbones, with explicit ROI cropping.
431   However in our experiments the performances decrease with the addition of the local branch in the
      second training stage, even after tuning the hyperparameters. We report results for the global branch.

      We train our CondViT using the categories provided in our dataset, learning an embedding vector
472   for each of the 10 clothing categories. For the i-th product in the batch, we randomly select in the
473   associated data a simple image xs and its category cs , and a complex image xc . We then compute
474   their embeddings ziA = φ(xc , cs ), ziB = φ(xs ). We also experimented with symmetric conditioning,
475   using a learned token for the gallery side (see Appendix B.1).
476
      Our categorical CondViT-B/16, with 68.4%R@1 against 2M distractors significantly outperforms
477
      all other category-based approaches (see Fig. 5, left) and maintains a higher categorical accuracy.
478   Furthermore, it performs similarly to the detection-based method conditioned on richer captions,
479   while requiring easy-to-acquire coarse categories. It does so without making any assumption on the
480   semantic nature of these categories, and adding only a few embedding weights (7.7K parameters) to
481   the network, against 233M parameters for Grounding DINO-B. We confirm in Appendix B.3 that its
482   attention is localized on different objects depending on the conditioning.

494   We compare these models with CIR methods: CoSMo (Lee et al., 2021) and CLIP4CIR (Baldrati et al.,
495   2023b). Both use a compositor network to fuse features extracted from the image and accompanying
496
      text. CoSMo reaches performances similar to an unconditional ViT-B/32, while CLIP4CIR performs
      similarly to our textual CondViT-B/32. We hypothesize that for our conditional feature extraction
497
      task, early conditioning is more effective than modifying embeddings through a compositor at the
498
      network’s end. Our CondViT-B/16 model significantly outperforms all other models and achieves
499   results comparable to our caption-based approach using Grounding DINO-B (see Fig. 5, right). As the
500   RVS task differs from CIR, despite both utilizing identical inputs, this was anticipated. Importantly,
501   CondViT-B/16 accomplishes this without the need for explicit detection steps or dataset-specific
502   preprocessing. Notably, we observe that our models achieve a categorical accuracy of 98% against
503   2M distractors, surpassing the accuracy of the best corresponding detection-based model, which
504   stands at 94.3%.

      6   C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
508
509   We studied an approach to image similarity in fashion called Referred Visual Search (RVS), which
510
      introduces two significant contributions. Firstly, we introduced the LAION-RVS-Fashion dataset,
      comprising 272K fashion products and 842K images. Secondly, we proposed a simple weakly-
511
      supervised learning method for extracting referred embeddings. Our approach outperforms strong
512
      detection-based baselines. These contributions offer valuable resources and techniques for advancing
513   image retrieval systems in the fashion industry and beyond.
514
515   However, one limitation of our approach is that modifying the text description to refer to something
516
      not present or not easily identifiable in the image, mimicking CIR, does not work effectively. For
      instance, if the image shows a person carrying a green handbag, a refined search with "red handbag" as
517
      a condition would only retrieve a green handbag. Examples of such failures are illustrated in Appendix
518
      B.2. The system may also ignore the conditioning if the desired item is small (see Appendix B.4 for
519   an analysis). Additionally, extending the approach to more verticals would be relevant.

522   7   E THICS AND R EPRODUCIBILITY S TATEMENTS
523
524   Harmful and Private Content. Our dataset is a subset of the publicly released LAION 5B dataset,
525   enriched with synthetic metadatas (categories, captions, product identifiers). However, our process
526   began by curating a subset of domains, focusing exclusively on domains affiliated with well-known
527   fashion retailers and URLs containing product identifiers. As such, these images come from large
528   commercial fashion catalogs. Our dataset contains images that appear in online fashion catalogs and
529   does not contain harmful or disturbing images. Most of the images are pictures of isolated attire on
530
      neutral backgrounds. Images depicting people are all extracted from professional photoshoots, with
      all the ethical and legal considerations that are common practices in the fashion retail industry.
531
532   We release our dataset only for research purposes as a benchmark to study Referred Visual Search
533   where no public data exists, which is a problem for reproducibility. This is an object-centric instance
534   retrieval task that aims to control more precisely the content of image embeddings. On this dataset,
535   to optimize the performances, embeddings should only contain information regarding the referred
536
      garment, rather than the model wearing it.
537
538   Dataset Biases. Our dataset lacks metadata for a comprehensive exploration of bias across gender
539   and ethnicity. However, based on an inspection of a random sample of 1000 images, we estimate that
      roughly 2/3 of the individuals manifest discernible feminine physical attributes or attire.

      A.2       C ONSTRUCTION
865
866
      Image Collection: The raw data of LRVS-F are collected from a list of fashion brands and retailers
867
      whose content delivery network domains were found in LAION 5B. We used the automatically
868   translated versions of LAION 2B MULTI and LAION 1B NOLANG to get english captions for all
869   the products. This represents around 8M initial images.
870
871
      We analyzed the format of the URLs for each domain, and extracted image and product identifiers
      using regular expressions when possible. We removed duplicates at this step using these identifiers,
872
      and put aside images without clear identifiers to be filtered and used as distractors later.
873
874
875   Image Annotation: The additional metadata that we provide were generated using deep learning
876   models. We generated indicators of the image complexity, classified the products in 11 categories,
877   and added new image captions.
878
      First, we used a model to classify the complexity of the images, trained with active learning. We
879
      started by automatically labeling a pool of images using information found in the URLs, before
880
      manually filtering the initial data, and splitting between training and validation. Then, we computed
881   and stored the pre-projection representations extracted by OpenCLIP B16 for each image, and trained
882   a 2-layers MLP to predict the category. After training, we randomly sampled 1000 unlabeled images
883   and annotated the 100 with the highest prediction entropy, before splitting them between training and
884   validation data. We repeated these 2 steps until reaching over 99% accuracy and labeled the entire
885   dataset using this model.
886
      We used a second model to automatically assign categories to the simple images. LAION captions
887   are noisy, so instead of using them we used BLIP2 FlanT5-XL (Li et al., 2023) to answer the question
888   "In one word, what is this object?". We gathered all the nouns from the answers, using POS tagging
889   when the generated answer was longer, and grouped them in 11 categories (10 for clothing, 1 for
890   non-clothing). We automatically created an initial pool of labeled data, which we manually filtered,
891   before applying the same active learning process as above. We then annotated all the simple images
892   with this model. Please refer to Appendix A.3 for the list of categories and their composition.
893   Finally, we automatically added new descriptions to the simple images, because the quality of some
894   LAION texts was low. For example, we found partially translated sentences, or product identifiers.
895   We generated 10 captions for each image using BLIP2 FlanT5-XL with nucleus sampling, and kept
896   the two with largest CLIP similarity.
897
898
899   Dataset Split: We grouped together images associated to the same product identifier and dropped
900   the groups that did not have at least a simple and a complex image. We manually selected 400 of
901   them for the validation set, and 2,000 for the test set. The distractors are all the images downloaded
902
      previously that were labeled as "simple" but not used in product groups. This mostly includes images
      for which it was impossible to extract any product identifier.
903
904   Finally, we used Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH) with perceptual hash, and OpenCLIP B16
905   embeddings to remove duplicates. We created FAISS indexes based respectively on hamming
906   distance and cosine similarity, automatically removing samples with extremely high similarity. We
907   manually inspected samples near the threshold. We used this process on complex images from the
908
      training set to remove products duplicates, on train and test sets to reduce evaluation bias, and on
      gallery images and distractors for both the validation and test sets.

954   B.1   A BLATION S TUDIES
955
      Insertion Depth. We study the impact of the insertion depth of our additional conditioning token
956
      by training a series of CondViT-B/32, concatenating the conditioning token before different encoder
957
      blocks for each one of them.
958
959   Fig. 9 indicates that early concatenation of the conditioning token is preferable, as we observed a
960   decrease in recall for deep insertion (specifically, layers 10-12). However, there was no statistically
961
      significant difference in performance between layers 1-8. Consequently, we decided to concatenate
      the token at the very beginning of the model. We hypothesize that the presence of residual connections
962
      in our network enables it to disregard the conditioning token until it reaches the optimal layer. The
963
      choice of this layer may depend on factors such as the size of the ViT model and the characteristics
964   of the dataset being used.
965
966
967   Asymetric Conditioning. We experiment with using conditioning for the simple images too, using
968
      a single learned "empty" token for all the simple images. We denote this token c∅ . Then for each
      simple image xs we compute its embedding as φ(xs , c∅ ).
969
970   Results in Tab. 5 show that there is no really significant difference between both approaches, even
971   though CondViT-B/16 results are better without this additional token for large amounts of distractors
      (≥ 100K). We choose to keep an asymmetric embedding process.
