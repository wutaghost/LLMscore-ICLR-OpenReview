                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
                 Graph neural networks (GNNs) are a valuable tool for extracting meaningful rep-
010
                 resentations from graph-structured data. Graphs, like relational databases, rep-
011
                 resent relationships between entities. Recent research has explored the poten-
012              tial of using GNNs for downstream tasks on relational data, such as entity res-
013              olution and missing value imputation. However, applying GNNs to relational
014              databases presents two challenges. The first challenge is data conversion: rela-
015              tional databases, organized as tables connected by key / foreign key constraints,
016              must be transformed into graphs without losing essential information. The sec-
017              ond challenge is ensuring that the embedding technique can adapt to the dynamic
018              nature of databases. When a database is updated, the embeddings of the result-
019              ing database should be recomputable efficiently. This requires that previously
020
                 computed embeddings remain stable despite changes to the data. Motivated by
                 using GNNs for relational databases, we study stability, i.e., how much the em-
021
                 beddings generated by a GNN change when the input graph undergoes modifica-
022
                 tions. Building upon the work of Gama et al. (2020), which established a limit for
023              the distance between embeddings of similar graphs, we focus on node-level sta-
024              bility for GNN embeddings, particularly when the graphs originate from relations.
025              We propose several techniques for transforming relational databases into graphs.
026              To assess the effectiveness of these methods, we conduct experiments using the
027              TPC-E database benchmark and analyze their stability.

030   1       I NTRODUCTION AND M OTIVATION
031
      Graphs offer a powerful representation of real-world data, capturing intricate relationships and de-
032   pendencies. However, many modern databases still adhere to the relational data model, popularized
033   by Codd decades ago; according to one source,1 a whopping 72% of the world’s database manage-
034   ment systems are relational as of June 2022, while by contrast, a mere 1.8% are graph DBMS. Given
035   the predominance of enterprise and other operational data in the form of relational databases, many
036   predictive tasks must operate directly on this relational data.
037
      Recent advances in deep learning, particularly Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), have demonstrated
038   their effectiveness in learning from tabular, relational data. Our research focuses on “in-database”
039   learning, where GNNs are applied directly to relational data within operational databases, after
040   transforming the relational data into a graph representation. This approach is motivated by the
041   ubiquity of relational databases and by the dynamic nature of operational data, which raises unique
042   challenges compared to static data like images or text.
043
      Several recent studies have explored graph representations of relational databases. By transforming
044   relational data into graphs, these studies have successfully applied GNNs to address downstream
045   tasks like entity resolution and missing value imputation within the original relational data. How-
046   ever, the existing studies often overlook the stability of graph representations of relational data and
047   of the corresponding GNN models. In general, small changes in the underlying data can lead to
048   significant shifts in the GNN-learned representations, potentially resulting in drastically different
049   predictions and thus different outcomes in downstream tasks. Therefore, techniques to understand
050   and mitigate or bound these effects are essential.
051   Inspired by the successful application of GNNs to relational databases, we investigate GNN stability.
052   This involves understanding how changes to the graph representation of a relational database affect

      the generated embeddings. Building upon the work of Gama et al. (2020), we revisit the influence
055   of graph topology on GNN outcomes and establish a boundary for the distance between embeddings
056   of similar graphs. Gama et al. (2020)’s study primarily focuses on small graph perturbations, over-
057   looking the potential impact of more significant changes often seen in relational databases, such as
058   modifying attribute values or adding /removing tuples. To address this issue, we modify the specific
059   type of GNN considered in Gama et al. (2020) and derive a global stability theorem based on their
060   local stability results.
061   Our first contribution is hence to extend the stability results to take into account more significant
062   graph perturbations, and to provide bounds that do not directly depend on the size of the input graph
063   (Section 4); both of these properties are desirable in the context of relational databases.
064
      Our second contribution is a comprehensive analysis of existing heterogeneous (having different
065
      types of nodes) database-to-graph constructions. We identify one major drawback, their tendency
066
      to be overly heterophilic (with dissimilar nodes grouped together), a characteristic that can hinder
067   the effectiveness of GNNs Zhu et al. (2021),He et al. (2022), Wang et al. (2022b). To address
068   it, we propose an alternative construction method (detailed in Section 5). Our experiments on the
069   Cora and TPC-E datasets demonstrate that our approach is up to 10 times more stable than existing
070   state-of-the-art methods (Section 6).

      2   R ELATED WORK
073
074   Graph Neural Networks GNNs are a robust and versatile tool for prediction tasks related to
075   graph-structured data. Inspired by Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), GNNs compute infor-
076   mation for each node by aggregating data from neighboring nodes. Many GNN architectures have
077   been proposed; these include GNNs with attention mechanisms Veličković et al. (2017), simple
078
      convolution Kipf & Welling (2016), spectral convolution Defferrard et al. (2016), inductive learning
      Hamilton et al. (2017), or message passing approaches Gilmer et al. (2020), Battaglia et al. (2016).
079
080
      Stability of GNNs When working with GNNs, it is essential to consider how their output is in-
081
      fluenced by the structure of the input graph. A desirable property is isomorphism invariance: when
082   two graphs are identical in structure (isomorphic), a GNN should produce the same embeddings.
083
084   The concepts of invariant and equivariant GNNs have been investigated by Maron et al. (2018), fo-
085
      cusing on ensuring that the network’s output remains unchanged when nodes are permuted. Keriven
      & Peyré (2019) proposed universal GNNs, which achieve permutation invariance by using message-
086
      passing frameworks, guaranteeing that the network’s behavior is not affected by the order of nodes.
087
088   While permutation equivariance is a valuable property for GNNs, it does not fully explain how em-
089   beddings change when nodes or edges are added or removed. Diffusion-based GNNs, introduced
090   by Gama et al. (2018), focus on ensuring stability under local graph perturbations in diffusion pro-
091
      cesses. Subsequent work by Gama et al. (2020) derived stability guarantees for GNNs, focusing
      on how small perturbations in graph structure affect GNN performance, ensuring robust feature ex-
092
      traction from locally perturbed graphs. The work of Wang et al. (2022a) has further explored GNN
093
      stability, including the use of positional encoding. Zou & Lerman (2020) introduced graph wavelet
094   filter banks to achieve robustness against structural changes; they ensure stability by filtering graph
095   signals and capturing multi-scale information without the need for supervised learning. Finally,
096   Böker et al. (2024) extended the Weisfeiler-Lehman test to graphons, giving a more general frame-
097   work for analyzing stability in large graphs. Another point of view of stability, when the graphs are
098   unchanged, but instead the features applied to the GNN vary, is studied in Jia & Zhang (2023).
099
      Another line of research focuses on GNN robustness against adversarial attacks. Such attacks in-
100   volve making subtle changes to the input data with the intention of misleading the GNN into making
101   incorrect predictions or classifications. To defend against such attacks, Zügner et al. (2018) proposed
102   robust training techniques that enhance the resilience of GNNs. Ennadir et al. (2024) explored the
103   use of noise injection to mitigate the impact of adversarial perturbations. Nikolentzos et al. (2024)
104   discussed the vulnerability of GNNs to attacks targeting both the graph structure and node attributes,
105   highlighting the need for robust defenses.
106
107   Generating embeddings for relational databases Several approaches have been developed to
      create embeddings for relational databases using language models. Fey et al. (2023) introduced a

108
      modular neural message-passing system that can operate directly on relational databases, aligning
109   with the formal relational model and enabling deep learning directly in database systems, without
110   the need for costly data pre-processing stages.
111
112
      Other methods focus on constructing heterogeneous or n-partite graphs to generate relevant embed-
      dings. Zahradnı́k et al. (2023) proposed Relational Deep Learning (RDL), which treats relational
113
      databases as heterogeneous graphs and employs Message Passing Graph Neural Networks (MPNNs)
114
      to learn from interconnected data. Similarly, Cappuzzo et al. (2024) proposes an approach for miss-
115   ing value imputation using GNNs on heterogeneous graphs, based on techniques designed for het-
116   erogenous graphs such as those in Schlichtkrull et al. (2018).
117
118
      Some methods utilize homogeneous graphs. Toenshoff et al. (2023) generated a bipartite graph and
      employed random walk methods to create embeddings, verifying their stability by the accuracy of
119
      downstream tasks. Cappuzzo et al. (2020) proposed a tripartite graph representation of relational
120
      databases, while Niepert (2016) used a Gaifman graph for generating embeddings.
121
122   Many existing homogeneous graph representations of databases exhibit a heterophilic property,
123   where nodes of different types are more likely to be connected, which can hinder the generation
124
      of meaningful embeddings Abu-El-Haija et al. (2019), Zhu et al. (2020), Zhu et al. (2021), Chien
      et al. (2020). We present in this paper graph constructions that alleviate this problem.

      While this result is interesting and has been a model for our theoretical work, we face several limita-
217   tions when trying to apply it. Firstly, the result is local, i.e., only relevant when the amplitude of the
218
      perturbation ε = ||S − Ŝ|| goes to 0. Yet the perturbations we are interested in – in the context of
219   relational databases – can produce modification of graphs that cannot be considered as infinitesimal.
220   For example, a tuple being removed would result in the deletion of several edges in the graph rep-
221   resentation of the database; the perturbation, measured as ||S − Ŝ||op , would typically be roughly
222   equal to the number of deleted edges. Moreover, the assumption on the filters (|H(λ)| ≤ 1) may not
223   always hold, since the parameters of the filters are the result of the SPGNN training and therefore
224   are hard to control. Finally, a key limitation of Gama et al. (2020) is the dependence on the graph
225   size (N ), making it difficult to establish results on asymptotic stability, particularly when dealing
226   with large graphs and ensuring that embeddings remain reasonably stable as the data grows.
227   In the next section, we establish a result (Theorem 2) that addresses these issues by using a degree 1
228   assumption on the polynomial defining the filters. We also show that this assumption can generalize.

352   5   A PPLICATION : STABILITY OF EMBEDDINGS FOR RELATIONAL DATABASES
353
354   One application scenario where stability is important are databases. This is because databases have
355   frequent updates and even may contain missing values. To ensure that embeddings results from
356   graph constructions from databases can be used even under perturbation, they must be stable.
357
358   Transforming Relational Databases to Graphs Consider a database D = (∆, R) with a set of
359
      tuples ∆, a set of relations R, and the set of its attribute names Ω. The active domain of the database
      is denoted adom(D), to which we add the NULL value, denoted ⊥.
360
361   There are multiple ways to encode relational databases as graphs. One approach from the state-of-
362   the-art is EmBDi Cappuzzo et al. (2020), which constructs a tripartite graph having three types of
363   nodes corresponding to tuples in ∆, values in the database, and attributes in Ω respectively. Edges
364   exist between values and tuples, and between values and attributes As this graph has three types of
365
      node and is tripartite, EmBDi constructs a heterogeneous and heterophilic graph. Another recent
      construction is FoRWaRD Toenshoff et al. (2023), a bipartite graph linking attribute value nodes to
366
      the corresponding tuple nodes of the same relation. A value node from a relation is be linked to a
367
      tuple node of another relation only if there are foreign key constraints between the two relations. For
368   multiple relations, an edge is present if there are foreign key constraints between them. FoRWard
369   is a heterogeneous and heterophilic graph, containing two types of nodes (hence a bipartite graph).
370   A limitation of these two graph constructions is their heterophilic nature and their inability to fully
371   capture the underlying structure of the database.
372
      To address these limitations, we propose 5-DB, a novel graph encoding approach for relational
373
      databases, designed to more comprehensively capture the underlying database structure.
374
      Definition 5.1 (5-DBconstruction). Let D = (∆, R) be a database, and Ω its set of attributes. We
375
      create a graph as follows.
376
377   (Nodes). For each relation R ∈ R, we create a node vR with label R. For each non-null element
      a ∈ adom(D) \ {⊥}, we create a node va with label a. For each tuple t in a relation R ∈ R, we


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      create a node vt with label t. For each attribute name A ∈ Ω, we create a node vA with label A. For
379   each relation R ∈ R, each attribute A ∈ Ω(R), and each tuple t ∈ R, we create a node vA,t with
380   label (A, t).
381
382
      (Edges). For each relation R ∈ R and each tuple t ∈ R, we create an edge (vR , vt ). For each tuple
      t in a relation R ∈ R and attribute name A of the relation R, we create (vt , vA,t ). For each relation
383
      R ∈ R, each tuple t ∈ R, and each attribute A ∈ Ω(R), we create (vA,t , vA ). For each non-null
384
      value a of attribute A in tuple t, we create (vA,t , va ). If the value is ⊥, no edge is created.
385
386   5-DB has five types of nodes which are never connected to other nodes of the same type. This
387   graph is hence both heterophilic and heterogeneous. We also distinguish a variant of 5-DB, one
388   in which attribute nodes are removed, denoted 5-DB-Att. Additionally, we define another variant,
389   5-DB Gaif, which is similar to 5-DB-Att but with an added constraint: values belonging to the
390   same tuple form a clique. 5-DB Gaif’s rationale is to increase the homophily of the graph. Finally,
391   Tuple+Clique is similar to FoRWaRD, where the tuples of the same relation form a clique. The
392   goal of this construction is to investigate whether imposing density can enhance the stability of the
393
      generated embeddings. All the above graphs are heterogeneous.
394
395
      Perturbations Our primary motivation for studying GNN embeddings is their application to rela-
      tional databases, which are often subject to frequent updates (perturbations). Two common types of
396
      perturbations in databases are:
397
398          • Tuple Removal: Some tuples present in the original database may be deleted in the up-
399            dated version. In the corresponding graph representation, this translates to removing nodes
400            associated with these tuples.
401
             • Value Removal: Tuples in the updated database might have missing values. This can be
402            represented in the graph by removing edges between attribute nodes and value nodes.
403
404
             • Tuple/Value Addition Since adding tuples/values to a database D to obtain a database D is
405            the exact opposite of removing tuples/values in D̂ to obtain D, and given that our bounds
406            are symmetric in the matrices S and Ŝ derived from the databases, the case of tuple/value
407
               addition is exactly the same as the case of tuple/value removal. Therefore, in the following,
               we only consider the case of removal.
408
409
      From the perspective of this study, adding tuples is essentially the opposite of removing tuples, and
410   adding values is the reverse of removing values. Therefore, to analyze the impact of these operations,
411   we can simply switch the corresponding Graph Shift Operators (GSOs) in our stability formulas.
412
413
      When a database is modified, its size changes. If we consider directly the corresponding graphs, their
      GSOs have different sizes, making them incomparable. To circumvent the size mismatch problem,
414
      in the case of deletion, we do not truly delete the nodes targeted by the deletion, but we remove
415
      all their edges instead. This yields a graph Ĝ ′ with exactly the same node set as G, but with fewer
416
      edges. We can therefore directly compute ||S − Ŝ ′ ||op . In the case of a tuple addition, we add a row
417
      / column of 0s in the matrix S in the place of the row / column that represents the tuple in the matrix
418
      Ŝ. This is one illustration the removal / addition symmetry mentioned before.
419
420   Putting it all together, by transforming a relational database into a graph using one of the methods
421   discussed earlier and applying the dimension matching procedure above, we can directly leverage
422   the results of Corollary 2.1 to analyze the stability of tuple embeddings within a database.

      First, to demonstrate the effectiveness of order-one SPGNNs, we compare them with other GNN
454   architectures. Our results indicate that despite their apparent simplicity, order-one SPGNNs can
455   achieve performance comparable to more complex GNN models. For simplicity and computational
456   efficiency, we use the order-one filter H(S) = H1 S + H0 . We compare SPGNNs with GCNs Kipf
457   & Welling (2016) and GATs Veličković et al. (2017) on the Cora dataset Yang et al. (2016), a graph
458   having 2,708 nodes, 10,556 edges, and 7 node classes. We use the same architecture for all models:
459   a 4-layer GNN followed by a 2-layer MLP for node classification. Each layer of the GNN contains
460   10-dimensional features. We remove a proportion of nodes from the graph, ranging from 0 to 90%
461   and we report the classification accuracy of the models on the reduced graphs. All GNNs are trained
462
      once and then remain fixed; this is because our main objective is to evaluate the stability of the
      embeddings with respect to perturbations.
463
464   The results are shown in Figure 1. Fig. 1(a) shows the accuracy when the classification MLP is
465   re-trained on each incomplete graph. Interestingly, it shows that the accuracy of the SPGNN is more
466   stable than the one of the GCN and GAT. This suggests that the SPGNN embeddings are more robust
467   to perturbations. On the other hand, when the MLP is not re-trained, the three variants are indistin-
468
      guishable in terms of accuracy, as shown in Fig. 1(b). This suggests that the SPGNN embeddings
      are not necessarily more informative, in a geometric sense, than those generated by GCN and GAT.
469
      The results in Figure 1(c) indicate that the stability of SPGNN embeddings is influenced to some
470
      extent by the degree of nodes in the graph. However, this influence is relatively minor, suggesting
471   that SPGNNs can be applied effectively to graphs with varying node degree distributions.

533
534   Motivated by the dynamic nature of databases, this research investigates the stability properties
535   of embeddings generated by an important class of GNNs, Signal Processing GNNs (SPGNNs). We
536   refine existing global stability bounds by eliminating the dependence on graph size. Additionally, we
537   extend our analysis to the node level. When applied to graphs generated from relational databases,
538   we demonstrate that considering the heterogeneous and heterophilic nature of these graphs can lead
539   to more stable embeddings. To achieve this, we propose improved database-to-graph constructions
      that outperform existing methods in terms of embedding stability.
