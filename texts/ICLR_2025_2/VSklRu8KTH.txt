                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               The expressivity of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) can be described via ap-
               propriate fragments of the first-order logic. In this context, uniform expressivity
011
               guarantees that a GNN can express a logical query without the parameters depend-
012
               ing on the size of the input graphs. It has been established that the two-variable
013            guarded fragment with counting (GC2) can be expressed uniformly via Rectified
014            Linear Unit (ReLU) GNNs Barceló et al. (2020). Moreover, GC2 is the frag-
015            ment that can be expressed at most by a GNN with any activation function. In
016            this article, we prove that, on the contrary of ReLU GNNs, there are GC2 queries
017            that cannot be uniformly expressed via any GNN with rational activations. As a
018            consequence, non-polynomial activation functions do not grant GNNs GC2 uni-
019            form expressivity in general, answering an open question formulated by Grohe
020            (2021). We then present a strict subfragment of GC2 (RGC2), and prove that ra-
021
               tional GNNs can express RGC2 queries uniformly over all graphs. Our numerical
               experiments illustrates that despite this theoretical disadvantage, rational GNNs
022
               are still able to learn some GC2 queries if some level of error is allowed.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are deep learning architectures for input data that incorporates some
      relational structure represented as a graph, and have proven to be very performant and efficient for
029
      various types of learning problems ranging from chemistry Reiser et al. (2022), social network anal-
030
      ysis Zhang et al. (2022), bioinformatics and protein-ligand interation Khalife et al. (2021); Knutson
031   et al. (2022), autonomous driving Singh & Srivastava (2022); Gammelli et al. (2021); Chen et al.
032   (2021), and techniques to enhance optimization algorithms Khalil et al. (2017; 2022) to name a few.
033   Understanding the ability of GNNs to compute, approximate or express functions over graphs, and
034   the dependence of their capacity on the activation function is beneficial for every aspect of learning:
035   from the understanding of the target class, the design of a GNN for a given task, to the algorithmic
036   training. For example, certain activation functions may endow GNNs with more expressivity than
037   others, or require less parameters to express the same functions.
038   In this context, several approaches have been conducted in order to describe and characterize the
039   expressive power of GNNs. The first approach consists in comparing GNNs to other standard com-
040   putation models on graphs such as the color refinement or the Wesfeiler-Leman algorithms. These
041   comparisons stand to reason, because the computational models of GNNs, Weisfeiler-Leman, and
042   color refinement algorithms are closely related. They all operate under the paradigm of inferring
043   global graph structure from local neighborhood computations. In that regard, it has been proven
044   Morris et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2018) that the color refinement algorithm precisely captures the ex-
045
      pressivity of GNNs. More precisely, there is a GNN distinguishing two nodes of a graph if and
      only if color refinement assigns different colors to these nodes. This results holds if one supposes
046
      that the size of the underlying neural networks are allowed to grow with the size of the input graph.
047
      Hence, in his survey, Grohe (2021) emphasizes the fact that this equivalence has been established
048   only for unbounded GNN, and asks: Can GNNs with bounded size simulate color refinement? In
049   Aamand et al. (2022), the authors answer by the negative if the underlying neural network are sup-
050   posed to have Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation functions. In Khalife & Basu (2023) the
051   authors provide a generalization of this result, for GNNs with piecewise polynomial activation func-
052   tions. Furthermore, explicit lower bounds on the neural network size to simulate the color refinement
053   can be derived for piecewise-polynomial activation functions given upper bounds on the number of
      regions of a neural network with piecewise-polynomial activation.

054
      The second line of research to study the expressive power of GNNs is to characterize the types of
055   boolean queries that a GNN can simulate. For example, can a GNN express if a vertex of a graph
056   is part of a clique of given size? Furthermore, can we characterize the set of queries that can be
057   expressed by GNNs? If the number of parameters of the GNN does not depend on the size of
058   the input graph, the GNN is said to express the query uniformly. Therefore, uniform expressivity
059   guarantees that the number of parameters remains only dependent on the complexity of the target
060   query1 . This becomes relevant from a practical standpoint as it captures the expressivity of GNNs
061   with a fixed number of parameters with respect to the number of vertices in the input graphs. The
062   suitable logic over labelled graphs for GNNs is a two variable fragment of graded model logic,
063
      referred to as GC2. Any GNN expresses a query of this logic, and conversely, any query of this logic
      can be expressed by a GNN whose size and iterations only depends on the depth of the query Barceló
064
      et al. (2020); Grohe (2021). For specific activation functions such as ReLUs, the size of a GNN
065
      required to express a given query of GC2 does not depend on the size of the input graph, but only
066   on depth of the query. In recent results Grohe (2023), the author provides a non-uniform description
067   of the logic expressible by GNNs with rational piecewise-linear activations (or equivalently, rational
068   ReLUs). The non-uniform results are presented for GNNs with general arbitrary real weights and
069   activation functions. Additionally, Rosenbluth et al. (2023) compared the impact of the aggregation
070   function on the expressivity of GNNs, showing that GNNs with Max or Mean aggregation functions
071   have distinct expressivity from the Sum aggregation GNNs. In this article, we focus on uniform
072   expressivity and consider the following question: What is the impact of the activation on the logic
073   uniformly expressed by GNNs?
074   A natural start for such investigation are polynomial activations. They have demonstrated clear
075   limitations for feedforward Neural Networks (NNs) as exposed with the celebrated theorem of ap-
076   proximation Leshno et al. (1993), stating that polynomial activations are the only ones leading to
077   NNs being unable to approximate any continuous function on a compact set. For example, in the
078   case of NNs, rational activations (i.e. fractions of polynomials) do not share this limitation. Beyond
079   this ability, rational activations yield efficient approximation power of continous functions Boullé
080
      et al. (2020), if one is allowed to consider different rational activation functions (of bounded degree)
      in the neural networks. In the same spirit, this article exposes a comparison of the power of the
081
      activation function in the case of GNNs. In particular, we will compare rational activations with
082
      those of piecewise linear activations whose expressive power is known.
083
084   Main contributions. In this work we present an additional step towards a complete understanding of
085   the impact of the activation function on the logical expressivity of GNNs. We show that the class of
086
      GNNs with rational activations (i.e. all activation functions that are fractions of polynomials2 ) have
      weaker expressivity than piecewise linear activations, or ReLUs. We prove that GNNs with rational
087
      activations cannot express all GC2 queries uniformly over all graphs, while they can with ReLU
088
      GNNs. Our approach demonstrates that this limitation is inherent to rational activations, as our
089   findings remain valid even when the following contidions are allowed: i) the weights of the rational
090   GNNs are arbitrary real numbers with infinite precision, and ii) the weights of the ReLU GNNs
091   are restricted to integers (also, the underlying neural networks are supposed to have finitely many
092   linear pieces). This result holds for sum-aggregation function and can be extended to aggregation
093   functions that are rational with bounded-degree3 . This shows how the power of GNNs can change
094   immensely if one changes the activation function of the neural networks. These results also seem
095   to suggest that ReLU GNNs possess special ability for uniform expressivity over rational GNNs, a
096   property in contrast with the efficient approximation power of rational NNs Boullé et al. (2020). In
097
      addition, we describe a strict subfragment of GC2, called RGC2, that rational GNNs can express
      uniformly.
098
099   We would like to point out we focus our study solely on the ability of classes of GNNs to express
100   given queries (i.e. can we even find a GNN in the class that does the job?). In particular, we do not
101   address the question of how the learning process impacts expressivity in this work, but we briefly
102   touch upon those interactions with some numerical experiments.

189   from a alphabet containing:

192           • the quantifiers ∀ and ∃

218   Free variables and assignments. Since GNNs can output values for every vertex of a graph, the
219   formulas we are interested in order to describe them from a logical standpoint must also take as “in-
220   put” some vertex variable. Therefore, we need to add some component to the S-structure described
221   as above, resulting in what we call an interpretation.
222
      First we need the concept of bound and free variable of a formula. A bound variable of a formula
223
      is a variable for which the scope of a quantifier applies. In comparison, a free variable is not bound
224   to a quantifier in that formula. Previous Formulas 1 and 2 contain no free variable. In contrast,
225   the formula ϕ(x) := ∃y∃z(¬(y = z) ∧ E(x, y) ∧ E(x, z)), which expresses that vertex v has two
226   out-neighbors, has a single free variable x. To interpret formulas with one (resp. k) free variable,
227   we need an assignment that maps the set of free variables in the logic, to the universe A (resp. Ak ).
228
      Now, an interpretation of FO(S) is a pair (U, β) where U is an S-structure and β is an assignment. To
229
      interpret a formula with free variables, every graph is associated to an S-structure and an assignment;
230   both are usually implicit in practice. Any formula in FO(S) (with and without free variables) can
231   now be thought of as a 0/1 function on the class of all interpretations of FO(S):
232
233         1. If ϕ is a formula without a free variable (in this case, the formula is said to be a sentence),
234            then any graph G is an S-structure and is mapped to 0 or 1, depending on whether G
235            satisfies ϕ or not.
236         2. If ϕ(x) is a formula with a single free variable x, then any pair (G, v), where G = (V, E)
237            is a graph and v ∈ V , is an interpretation with G as the S-structure and the assignment β
238            maps x to v. Thus, every pair (G, v) is mapped to 0 or 1, depending on whether (G, β)
239            satisfies ϕ or not. This example can be extended to handle formulas with multiple free
240            variables, where we may want to model 0/1 functions on subsets of vertices.

514   7   D ISCUSSION AND OPEN PROBLEMS
515
516   Analyzing rigorously how the expressivity of GNNs is altered with the choice of activation function
517   is valuable as it can help in selecting an appropriate architecture for a given learning problem. In this
518   regard, the universal approximation properties of neural networks, and in particular the efficiency of
519
      rational neural networks Boullé et al. (2020) for that purpose, may lead to believe that rational GNNs
      have maximal expressivity from a formal logical standpoint. Our results show that it is not the case,
520
      and rational GNNs have strictly weaker expressivity than ReLU GNNs. However, it is unclear if
521
      such limitation carries over for other (non rational) aggregation functions such as max. Furthermore,
522   the proof of our positive result does not show that negation is never allowed in subformulas. We
523   conjecture it is not the case and that RGC2 is close to the logic expressed by rational GNNs. An
524   interesting path for future research is to understand whether this desirable property of ReLU GNNs
525   confer them a significant advantage over rational GNNs on graphs of bounded order, which may be
526   more relevant for practical applications.
527
      It is also essential to note that expressivity is just one facet of practical use of GNNs and the related
528   machine learning algorithms. This article does not delve into other crucial aspects such as the
529   GNN’s ability to generalize from provided data, and the computational efficiency of learning and
530   inference. In particular, we have not investigated the ability of a GNN to learn a logical query from
531   examples and how the numerical optmization part used for training may impact expressivity. Our
532   numerical experiments suggest that these factors may be at play, including the architecture chosen
533   from learning that may differ from the ones that allow to express the uniform queries. We also wish
534   to convey that theoretical investigations on the expressivity of GNNs and logical expressivity can
535   suggest potential avenues to integrate logic-based and statistical reasoning in GNN architectures.

736

738

            5. If ϕ is a formula and x is a variable, then ∀xϕ and ∃xϕ are formulas.

            3. free(¬ϕ) = free(ϕ)

749         4. free(ϕ1 ⋆ ϕ2 ) = var(ϕ1 ) ∪ var(ϕ2 ), where ⋆ ∈ {∨, ∧, →, ↔}

      Definition 13. Given a first order logic F O(S), an S-structure is a pair U = (A, I) where A is a
757   nonempty set, called the domain/universe of the structure, and I is a map defined on S such that
758
759         1. I(c) is an element of A for every constant symbol c.
760
            2. I(f ) is a function from An to A for every n-ary function symbol f .
761
762         3. I(R) is a function from An to {0, 1} (or equivalently, a subset of An ) for every n-ary
763            relation symbol R.

766   the logic to the domain A. An interpretation of F O(S) is a pair (U, β), where U is an S-structure
767
      and β is an assignment.

771   Definition 14. The depth of a formula ϕ is defined recursively as follows. If ϕ is of the form in
772   points 1. or 2. in Definition 11 , then its depth is 1. If ϕ = ¬ϕ′ or ϕ = ∀xϕ′ or ϕ = ∃xϕ′ , then the
773   depth of ϕ is the depth of ϕ′ plus 1. If ϕ = ϕ1 ⋆ ϕ2 with ⋆ ∈ {∨, ∧, →, ↔}, then the depth of ϕ is
774   one plus the maximum of the depths of ϕ1 and ϕ2 .
775
      This is equivalent to the depth of the tree representing the formula, based on the inductive definition.
776
      The length/size of the formula is the total number nodes in this tree. Up to constants, this is the
777   number of leaves in the tree, which are called the atoms of the formula.
