009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Large language models (LLMs) with billions of parameters have achieved remark-
012           able success across various applications, but they require substantial computa-
013           tional resources and large datasets. While parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods
014           like LoRA and QLoRA have significantly reduced computational costs and mem-
015           ory usage, robustly training LLMs for individual clients with datasets distributed
016
              on isolated devices remains challenging. To address this, recent work has explored
              the use of federated learning (FL) to collaboratively train LLM adapters on dis-
017
              tributed private data, thereby avoiding the high computational and communication
018
              costs. In these approaches, the LLMs are frozen, and the adapters are collabora-
019           tively trained through adapter-sharing and aggregation methods. However, in this
020           paper, we identify a significant issue: these approaches may suffer from quantiza-
021           tion bias when clients operate with different levels of quantization on LLMs. To
022           resolve this, we propose a novel framework called Federated Quantization-Aware
023           LoRA (FedQLoRA), which estimates the quantization error and separates it from
024           the LoRA adapter trained on local data via a quantization-aware adapter. Addi-
025           tionally, we address the heterogeneity bias problem that arises from severe data
026           heterogeneity among clients, such as in non-IID settings. We propose an itera-
027
              tive version of the framework that improves both the dynamic quantization-aware
              adapter and the LoRA adapter alternately within the FL framework. We conduct
028
              extensive experiments to validate the performance of our proposed framework.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across various applications, pri-
035   marily due to their impressive performance. However, with billions of parameters, fine-tuning these
036   models demands substantial computational resources and large datasets. Parameter-efficient fine-
037   tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021), address this
038   issue by freezing most of the pre-trained LLM parameters and updating only a small subset. To
039
      further minimize memory usage, learnable quantization methods like QLoRA Dettmers et al. (2024)
      employ a high-precision quantization approach on pre-trained models while incorporating a small
040
      set of learnable low-rank adapter weights. These methods significantly reduce computational costs
041
      and memory usage, making LLMs more efficient and adaptable for heterogeneous devices.
042
043   Despite their success, PEFT methods like QLoRA/LoRA still require substantial training data to
044   achieve significant improvements over the base model, which may be spread across distributed de-
045
      vices. Collaboratively training LLMs on this distributed data while ensuring privacy protection
      presents a significant challenge. Recently, many studies have explored applying federated learn-
046
      ing (FL) to train LLMs on isolated data across distributed devices. Unlike traditional FL methods,
047
      which share all local model parameters with a central server, the massive number of parameters in
048   LLMs (billions) makes this type of communication highly inefficient. To address this, most recent
049   approaches freeze the LLMs and instead train only the adapters, which consist of a much smaller set
050   of parameters. These adapters are trained locally on each client and then aggregated at the server,
051   significantly reducing communication overhead. For example, Cho et al. (2023) proposed a method
052   that aggregates heterogeneous LoRA modules using zero-padding and redistributes them hetero-
053   geneously through truncation. Similarly, Sun et al. (2024) addressed synchronization issues with
      LoRA in FL by introducing FFA-LoRA, which freezes the non-zero-initialized low-rank matrices

055   clients, these methods significantly reduce both computational and communication costs.

087

088

089   The LoRA adapter is then retrained on the local quantized LLM with quantization-aware adapter
090   using local data. This process allows the LoRA adapter to effectively separate quantization errors
091   while capturing unbiased information from local data.
092
      Although a locally quantized LLM paired with a LoRA adapter provides a reasonable approximation
093
      of the unquantized model, it may still suffer from heterogeneity bias when faced with significant
094
      data heterogeneity among clients, such as in non-IID settings. In these cases, each local client might
095   use different sampling methods from the global distribution to generate their heterogeneous local
096   datasets, leading to discrepancies between the models trained on them. To address this, instead of
097   using local data directly, we propose utilizing a LoRA adapter trained on global data to estimate
098   the unquantized LLMs better. We introduce an iterative version of FedQLoRA, incorporating a
099   dynamic quantization-aware adapter. Specifically, we first train LoRA adapters based on local data
100   and then aggregate these at the server to form a global LoRA adapter. This global adapter is then
101   used to update both the local LoRA adapters and the quantization-aware adapters. The updated
102   quantization-aware adapters provide a more accurate estimation of quantization error. With these
103
      improved adapters, we can train the local LoRA adapters using local data while minimizing the
      impact of quantization bias, initiating a new iteration. During this process, the quantization-aware
104
      adapter becomes more accurate through the improved LoRA adapter, and the LoRA adapter, in turn,
105
      is refined by the more precise quantization-aware adapter.

      Low-rank Adapter: Low-rank Adapter (LoRA) fine-tuning(Hu et al. (2021)) reduces memory re-
134
      quirements by introducing a small set of trainable parameters, called adapters, while keeping the full
135   model parameters fixed. The core idea behind LoRA is to reduce the number of trainable parame-

151   Federated learning enables multiple clients to train a global model collaboratively without exposing
152   their private data. Suppose we have N clients, each with locally collected data that is inaccessible to
153
      others. Each client i has its own private dataset Di and a personalized large language model (LLM)
      with parameters Wiqi , which may be quantized to lower precision to reduce memory usage. The
154
      quantized parameters Wiqi for the LLMs may have different levels of quantization denoted by qi and
155
      originate from different unquantized LLMs, but all share the same model structure. This ensures that

159   In this work, we keep the quantized LLMs fixed and focus on the adapters, which consist of a small
160   set of parameters. Recent FL research has established a consensus that the knowledge acquired by
161   individual clients consists of both general knowledge and client-specific knowledge. In this paper,
      we adopt a personalized federated learning framework, where we consider a shared component σ

      dataset Lang (1995) and multi-class news classification using the NC (News Classification) dataset
      Lewis et al. (2019) from XGLUE Liang et al. (2020). Accuracy and micro-F1 score are used as the
369
      evaluation metric for this multi-class classification task. Both datasets are randomly partitioned for
370
      training and evaluation, with each client working on a subset of the categories, ensuring a balanced
371   distribution of data across the clients.

      ”LoRA” with local adapter training based on local data, FFA-LoRA Sun et al. (2024) with aggrega-
      tion of non-zero LoRA adapter, H-LoRA Cho et al. (2023) with padding and truncation over LLMs
375
      and the H-LoRA-T with truncation of LLMs into lowest precision based on H-LoRA.

      chitecture Sanh (2019) as the LLM backbone. This model is a distilled version of BERT, retaining

379   cally to the q lin and v lin layers within the self-attention mechanism. Two levels of quantization

      5   R ELATED W ORK
492
493   Parameter efficient fine-tuning: Houlsby et al. (2019) proposed Adapters, which inserts small,
494   learnable layers into pre-trained models. This method allows fine-tuning with fewer parameters,
495   reducing computational demands while maintaining task performance. Lester et al. (2021) demon-
496   strated that prompt tuning scales well with large language models, offering a competitive alternative
497   to full fine-tuning by optimizing continuous prompts with fewer parameters. Li & Liang (2021)
498   introduced Prefix-Tuning, which adds learnable prefix vectors to Transformer layers, allowing effi-
499
      cient fine-tuning of generative models by updating only a small set of parameters. Similarly, Zaken
      et al. (2021) proposed BitFit, a method that fine-tunes only the bias terms of Transformer models,
500
      further minimizing the number of trainable parameters. Hu et al. (2021) introduced LoRA (Low-
501
      Rank Adaptation), which modifies model weights with low-rank matrices during fine-tuning. Ex-
502   panding on adapter methods, Pfeiffer et al. (2020) proposed AdapterFusion, which improves model
503   generalization by combining task-specific adapters during inference. To further reduce parame-
504   ter updates, Liu et al. (2022) developed IA3, which scales activations in self-attention modules,
505   allowing efficient fine-tuning without modifying the core model weights. Dettmers et al. (2024) in-
506   troduced QLoRA, which combines LoRA with quantization, reducing memory and computational
507   costs for fine-tuning large models on resource-limited devices. Additionally, Li et al. (2023) pro-
508   posed LoftQ, a novel quantization method that optimizes LoRA and quantized backbone weights
509   together, improving LoRA fine-tuning in low-bit quantization scenarios.
510   Federated efficient fine-tuning: Recently, several preliminary papers have emerged on federated
511   learning for large language models (LLMs), focusing on updating and sharing adapters in parameter-
512   efficient fine-tuning (PEFT). FederatedScope-LLM(Kuang et al. (2024)) explored the overall fed-
513   erated architecture in LLMs using various PEFT methods, while OpenFedLLM(Ye et al. (2024))
514   built an integrated framework/codebase that supports federated instruction tuning. Among all PEFT
515   methods, the LoRA adapter significantly reduces communication costs while demonstrating excel-
516
      lent performance in federated learning. Some studies have specifically examined federated LoRA
      adapters in the context of data heterogeneity. For instance, FFA-LoRA(Sun et al. (2024)) addressed
517
      synchronization issues in federated learning by freezing the non-zero-initialized low-rank matrices
518
      and updating only the zero-initialized ones. Other works have focused on model heterogeneity. One
519   study(Cho et al. (2023)) proposed a method that aggregates heterogeneous LoRA modules using
520   zero-padding and redistributes them heterogeneously through truncation. Additionally, FDLoRA(Qi
521   et al. (2024)) introduced a variant of personalized federated learning that utilizes dual LoRA tun-
522   ing. Another framework, pFedLoRA(Yi et al. (2024)), was designed for model-heterogeneous per-
523   sonalized federated learning based on LoRA tuning and included an iterative training method that
524   alternates between training homogeneous small adapters and heterogeneous LLMs. Our proposed
525   methods focus on federated learning across clients with large language models (LLMs) that have
526   varying levels of quantization, and it is the first work to identify and address quantization bias.
527
528
529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we explored federated fine-tuning with adapters for clients using heterogeneous LLMs
532
      with varying levels of quantization. We are the first to identify the issue of quantization bias when
533
      applying the adapter-sharing and aggregation method in FL, as adopted by most recent work. To
534   address this, we proposed a novel framework called FedQLoRA, which estimates and separates
535   quantization error from the LoRA adapter trained on local data through the use of a quantization-
536   aware adapter. Additionally, we tackled the issue of heterogeneity bias that arises from significant
537   data heterogeneity among clients, such as in non-IID settings. We introduced an iterative version
538   of the framework that alternately improves both the dynamic quantization-aware adapter and the
539   LoRA adapter within the FL framework. We would explore the application of FedQLoRA to more
      real-world industrial scenarios, such as recommendation systems, in our future work.
