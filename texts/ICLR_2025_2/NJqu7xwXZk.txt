009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Black-Box Discrete Prompt Learning (BDPL) is a prompt-tuning method that op-
012            timizes discrete prompts without accessing model parameters or gradients, mak-
013            ing the prompt tuning on a cloud-based Large Language Model (LLM) feasible.
014            Adapting Federated Learning (FL) to BDPL could further enhance prompt tuning
015            performance by leveraging data from diverse sources. However, all previous re-
016            search on federated black-box prompt tuning had neglected the substantial query
017            cost associated with the cloud-based LLM service. To address this gap, we con-
018
               ducted a theoretical analysis of query efficiency within the context of federated
               black-box prompt tuning. Our findings revealed that degrading FedAvg to activate
019
               only one client per round, a strategy we called FedOne, enabled optimal query
020
               efficiency in federated black-box prompt learning. Building on this insight, we
021            proposed the FedOne framework, a federated black-box discrete prompt learning
022            method designed to maximize query efficiency when interacting with cloud-based
023            LLMs. We conducted numerical experiments on various aspects of our frame-
024            work, demonstrating a significant improvement in query efficiency, which aligns
025            with our theoretical results.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Prompt tuning has emerged as a vital technique for adapting large language models (LLMs) (Liu
031   et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020) to specific tasks without retraining the entire model. Tradition-
032   ally, many tuning methods require access to the model’s intermediate representations (Brown et al.,
033
      2020; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021), categorizing them as white-box ap-
      proaches. However, when such access is unavailable, black-box prompt tuning becomes essential.
034
      This approach focuses on tuning the input prompts without access to the internal processes of the
035
      model (Sun et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2022; Deng et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023).
036
037   Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2020; 2021; Karimireddy et al., 2020;
038   Mishchenko et al., 2022) has emerged as a promising approach for leveraging decentralized data
039
      from multiple clients while preserving privacy. Applying federated learning to prompt tuning offers
      a valuable opportunity to improve client capabilities and enhance model performance. Most feder-
040
      ated prompt tuning methods to date have focused on white-box scenarios where clients have access
041
      to model parameters (Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). In those approaches, only the trainable
042   parameters (prompts) are trained by the client and shared with the server, significantly reducing both
043   the number of trainable parameters and the communication costs compared to fine-tuning baselines.
044
045
      However, several practical limitations hinder the applicability of federated prompt tuning that relies
      on white-box access. First, white-box prompt learning is not applicable to closed-source LLM that
046
      are accessed via APIs, as these models are not openly shared. In such scenarios, users are restricted
047
      to interacting with the model through the API endpoints without access to the internal structure or
048   weights of the LLM. This limitation prevents the application of white-box prompt learning tech-
049   niques. Second, FL is typically applied in scenarios involving thousands of edge devices, each with
050   limited computational resources. However, white-box prompt learning demands substantial compu-
051   tational power, as it requires devices to perform computation on the entire LLM. These operations
052   are computationally intensive, rendering them impractical for edge devices with constrained capa-
053   bilities. This presents a significant challenge when trying to implement white-box prompt learning
      in FL environments, as it can lead to excessive resource demands on the participating devices.

070   In contrast, applying black-box discrete prompt learning to federated learning offers several distinct
071   benefits (Zhao et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Che et al., 2023), enhancing both the
072   practicality and effectiveness. First, this approach preserves the privacy of closed-source LLMs by
073   not requiring access to their internal model weights or architecture. For example, Diao et al. (2022);
074
      Lin et al. (2023) introduces a black-box prompt learning method that uses only discrete prompts as
      inputs and relies solely on the model’s output loss to train the prompts. Besides, black-box prompt
075
      tuning reduces the computational burden on the client, as it eliminates the need for computation
076
      on the entire model, thereby enabling participation from edge devices with limited computational
077   resources. Moreover, the communication costs associated with discrete prompts are lower compared
078   to white-box prompt tuning methods, as the white-box prompt tuning require transmitting large
079   matrices of numerical values.
080
      Despite these advantages, the application of BDPL in FL still faces two significant unresolved chal-
081
      lenges, tempering its overall promise. First, previous research (Lin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023) on
082
      federated black-box prompt tuning has neglected the substantial cost associated with queries to the
083   LLM cloud service (Figure 1, left). Second, a convergence analysis for Federated BDPL aimed at
084   optimizing discrete prompts has not yet been provided.
085
086
      Targeting the above problems, we introduce a novel federated learning framework, FedOne, de-
      signed to optimize query efficiency in Federated BDPL. We offer the first convergence analysis of
087
      Fed-BDPL in this context and further extend the analysis of the query efficiency towards the cloud-
088
      based LLM server. The results demonstrate that by limiting activation to a single client per round,
089   FedOne achieves optimal query efficiency (Figure 1, right). Our approach is particularly well-suited
090   for scenarios involving limited computational resources, such as mobile devices or IoT systems,
091   where local training of LLMs is impractical.
092
      Formally, this paper makes the following key contributions:
093
094   • We identify that existing federated black-box prompt tuning methods overlook the significant
095     costs associated with querying cloud-based LLM services.
096   • To address this gap, we present the first theoretical analysis of Federated BDPL, with a focus on
097     understanding and evaluating the query efficiency when interacting with cloud-based LLMs.
098   • Based on our analysis, we introduce the FedOne framework, a novel approach designed to opti-
099     mize query efficiency in Federated Black-box Prompt Learning by activating only one client per
100     round when querying cloud-based LLMs.

299   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
300
301
      For our experiment, we utilized the GLUE benchmark (Wang et al., 2018), which includes a wide
      range of tasks including MNLI (Williams et al., 2018), QQP (Iyer et al., 2017), SST-2 (Socher et al.,
302
      2013), MRPC (Dolan & Brockett, 2005), CoLA (Warstadt et al., 2019), QNLI (Wang et al., 2018),
303
      and RTE (Dagan et al., 2005; Haim et al., 2006; Giampiccolo et al., 2007; Bentivogli et al., 2009).
304
305   In the baseline experiment within the federated learning framework, we employed 100 clients. In the
306   FedOne framework, there was only one client activated per round for training and aggregation. We
307
      adopted the k-shot framework from Perez et al. (2021), adapting it to a federated learning context.
      Each client received a k-shot dataset comprising k samples per class.
308
309   The model architecture employed is RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019). The trainable prompts were
310   placed at different positions in the model depending on the algorithm of the baselines. For the
311   training procedure, we conducted a hyperparameter tuning phase using a grid search approach to
312   explore learning rates of [3e−4, 1e−4, 3e−5, 1e−5]. The batch size was set at 32, the prompt length
313
      was set at 20, and the optimization algorithm employed was AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017).
      Further details about the dataset and evaluation metrics of them are available in Appendix B.1.

      5      R ELATED WORKS
460
461
      5.1     W HITE - BOX AND BLACK - BOX PROMPT TUNING
462
463   Prompt tuning is a technique for adapting LLM to downstream tasks. It tailors the model’s responses
464   to specific tasks or styles without requiring the retraining of the entire model. In white-box prompt
465   tuning, the learner is granted full access to the LLM, allowing them to modify and access inter-
466   mediate results of the model and acquire the gradient. Li & Liang (2021) and Lester et al. (2021)
467   proposed a lightweight and modular alternative to full model fine-tuning for natural language gen-
468   eration tasks, which optimizes a sequence of continuous soft prompts, prepend in the embedding
469   layers of the LLM. Liu et al. (2021) propose the P-tuning v2. Instead of only applying the prompt
470   in the input layer in Li & Liang (2021), they adapt trainable parameters on all layers’ inputs, which
471
      can effectively match the performance of fine-tuning across a wide range of models.
472   In situations where the learner cannot access the intermediate result of the LLM model, the learner
473   has to use a black-box prompt tuning method. In the black-box prompt tuning, the learner can only
474   query the output of the LLM with the input of the model. Most of the research assumes the input
475   is at the soft prompt layer of the LLM (Sun et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Others research use the
476   discrete prompt which is concrete with the input text, which is more portable, and is usable for any
477
      cloud-based LLM API (Diao et al., 2022). Xiao et al. (2023) presents a privacy-preserving, efficient
      transfer learning method that adapts large foundation models to specific tasks. This method does
478
      not require access to the full model or compromise data privacy. It utilizes a lightweight adapter
479
      and a compressed emulator for model tuning. Chen et al. (2023) introduces an efficient method
480   for optimizing instructions for black-box LLMs using Bayesian optimization of soft prompts. This
481   approach significantly improves LLM performance across a variety of tasks without requiring direct
482   access to the model’s internals. Deng et al. (2022) proposes an efficient method to optimize discrete
483   text prompts using reinforcement learning, demonstrating superior performance compared to other
484   prompt optimization techniques across a variety of tasks. Sun et al. (2022) proposed black-box tun-
485   ing (BBT), a method that optimizes continuous prompts by optimizing a lower-dimensional vector
      and projecting them to the prompt searching space. They use the Covariance Matrix Adaptation


                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) for optimizing the vector. Diao et al. (2022) introduced BDPL which
487   optimizes the discrete prompt with the policy gradient method.
488
489   The black-box prompt tuning method is versatile and applicable to various tasks and models without
490
      model-specific modifications. However, its major drawback is computational inefficiency, requiring
      multiple forward passes through the model, which leads to high costs and extended training times.
491
      Additionally, the convergence of the derivation-free optimization method is often slow.
492
493
494   5.2   F EDERATED LEARNING
495
496   Federated learning (McMahan et al., 2017; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; 2021; Marfoq
497   et al., 2022; Mishchenko et al., 2022), first introduced by McMahan et al. (2017), is a paradigm that
498   enables devices to collaboratively train a shared predictive model by locally aggregating updates. In
499   this framework, each client maintains a copy of the model for local training, and the server selects
500   a subset of clients in each round for aggregation. Since the introduction of FedAvg, it has lacked
501
      formal theoretical convergence guarantees. As a result, researchers have made significant efforts to
      establish and demonstrate its convergence (Zhou & Cong, 2017; Stich, 2018).
502
503   Partial client activation is a key area of research in FL and has gained significant attention due to
504   its impact on improving convergence rates and system efficiency. Stich (2018) shows that in the
505   convex case, increasing the number of activated clients significantly improves convergence rates in
506   Federated Learning with independent and identically distributed (IID) data, achieving a linear speed-
507
      up. Li et al. (2019) extended the understanding of federated learning by analyzing the convergence
      of the FedAvg algorithm, under the convex case. They demonstrate that under the non-IID setting,
508
      the convergence rate has a weak dependence on the number of activated clients, which implies that
509
      the FedAvg is not able to achieve linear speedup under this case, therefore the participation ratio can
510   be set smaller to alleviate the straggler effect without affecting the convergence rate.
511
512
513   5.3   F EDERATED PROMPT TUNING
514
515   Applying federated learning to prompt tuning can enhance the model by incorporating additional
516   data. This approach leverages distributed datasets to improve model performance while adhering
517
      to data privacy. To apply white-box prompt tuning in FL, each client maintains the entire model
      but trains only the prompt parameters. These parameters are then shared and aggregated across
518
      clients (Zhao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Che et al., 2023). However, this method assumes
519
      white-box access to LLM, which is impractical for closed-source LLMs. Consequently, black-box
520   prompt learning has been adapted for FL to address these limitations (Lin et al., 2023; Sun et al.,
521   2023). Lin et al. (2023), applied the black-box prompt tuning method Diao et al. (2022) to the
522   Federated Learning, where the client can train the probability matrix for the discrete prompt via
523   querying the cloud-based LLM API. Sun et al. (2023) applied the BBT to FL. In this approach,
524   clients train only low-dimensional vectors using CMA-ES via querying the cloud-based LLM API.
525
      For federated prompt tuning, most existing research has focused on conventional issues in FL, such
526   as data heterogeneity (Zhao et al., 2023), privacy (Zhang et al., 2023), security (Zhao et al., 2023),
527   and client computation-communication efficiency (Lin et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). However,
528   no studies have yet addressed the query efficiency of Federated Black-box Prompt Tuning, a novel
529   challenge introduced by the deployment of Black-box Prompt Tuning through cloud-based APIs.
530
531
532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   We identified that previous research on federated black-box prompt tuning had overlooked the sig-
535   nificant query costs associated with cloud-based LLM services. To address this issue, we conducted
536   a theoretical analysis of query efficiency in the context of federated black-box prompt tuning, re-
537   vealing the relationship between query efficiency and the number of activated clients. Based on
538   our findings, we propose the FedOne framework, which achieves optimal query efficiency with re-
539   spect to the number of activated clients. We performed numerical experiments on various aspects of
      FedOne, further validating its performance through real-world experiments on GPT-3.5.

       Remark 4. We carefully analyze the optimal K∗ by balancing convergence complexity and query
1611
       time, aiming to achieve the fastest convergence with the fewest queries. Specifically, we fix the
1612
       convergence accuracy, denoted as ϵ, and determine the number of iterations, Tϵ , required to achieve
1613   this accuracy. We then analyze the total number of queries, given by Tϵ K∗ ∝ K∗ . This analysis
1614   shows that increasing K∗ for K∗ = 1, 2, . . . , K, the number of activated clients can accelerate
1615   convergence by leveraging more data; however, it also increases query overhead, leading to higher
1616   communication and computational costs. We rigorously demonstrate that the number of queries
1617   required for Fed-BDPL to achieve an ϵ-solution is proportional to K∗ . This result emphasizes that
1618   optimal query efficiency is achieved when only a single client (K∗ = 1) is activated per round.
