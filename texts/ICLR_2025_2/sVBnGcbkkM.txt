                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Reinforcement Learning has achieved significant success in generating complex
010
               behavior but often requires extensive reward function engineering. Adversarial vari-
011            ants of Imitation Learning and Inverse Reinforcement Learning offer an alternative
012            by learning policies from expert demonstrations via a discriminator. However, these
013            methods struggle in complex tasks where randomly sampling expert-like behaviors
014            is challenging. This limitation stems from their reliance on policy-agnostic dis-
015            criminators, which provide insufficient guidance for agent improvement, especially
016            as task complexity increases and expert behavior becomes more distinct. We intro-
017            duce RILe (Reinforced Imitation Learning environment), a novel trainer-student
018            system that learns a dynamic reward function based on the student’s performance
019
               and alignment with expert demonstrations. In RILe, the student learns an action
               policy while the trainer, using reinforcement learning, continuously updates itself
020
               via the discriminator’s feedback to optimize the alignment between the student
021
               and the expert. The trainer optimizes for long-term cumulative rewards from the
022            discriminator, enabling it to provide nuanced feedback that accounts for the com-
023            plexity of the task and the student’s current capabilities. This approach allows for
024            greater exploration of agent actions by providing graduated feedback rather than bi-
025            nary expert/non-expert classifications. By reducing dependence on policy-agnostic
026            discriminators, RILe enables better performance in complex settings where tradi-
027            tional methods falter, outperforming existing methods by 2x in complex simulated
028            robot-locomotion tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful framework for teaching agents to perform
      complex tasks. In recent years, deep reinforcement learning has demonstrated remarkable success in
034
      replicating sophisticated behaviors, including playing Atari games, chess, and Go (Mnih et al., 2013;
035
      Silver et al., 2018). However, these achievements often come at a cost: the tedious and challenging
036   process of designing reward functions, as predicting the policy outcome from a manually crafted
037   reward function remains notoriously difficult.
038
039
      To overcome the reward engineering problem, Imitation Learning (IL) leverages expert demonstra-
      tions to learn a policy. Since vast amounts of expert data are required to accurately learn expert
040
      behaviors, Adversarial Imitation Learning (AIL) approaches, such as GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016),
041
      have been proposed as data-efficient alternatives. AIL employs a discriminator to measure similarity
042   between learned behavior and expert behavior, rewarding the agent accordingly. While computa-
043   tionally efficient, AIL methods suffer from a critical limitation: the policy-agnostic nature of their
044   discriminators. The discriminator lacks any inherent incentive to guide the agent towards expert-
045   like behavior, in contrast to engineered reward functions in RL. Consequently, AIL methods face
046   challenges in complex tasks requiring extensive exploration to find optimal actions. For instance, in
047   digital locomotion tasks, AIL methods often struggle to consistently replicate expert performance
048   (Peng et al., 2018).
049   Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) is another approach to alleviate reward engineering. Unlike
050   IL, which directly learns expert behavior, IRL seeks to infer the underlying reward function that
051   motivates the agent to acquire expert behaviors. The reward function and the agent are trained
052   iteratively, with updates to the reward function based on the agent’s behavior. This iterative process
053   renders IRL computationally expensive (Zheng et al., 2022). Adversarial Inverse Reinforcement
      Learning (AIRL) (Fu et al., 2018) attempts to address this inefficiency by introducing a discriminator

054
      that enables simultaneous learning of the policy and reward function. However, in AIRL, the reward
055   function is tightly coupled to the discriminator, potentially limiting its ability to capture complex task
056   structures or long-term dependencies and inheriting the limitations of a policy-agnostic discriminators.
057   This highlights the need for a method that can learn a more flexible reward function without the
058   computational overhead of traditional IRL methods.
059
      To overcome these challenges and effectively learn behaviors in complex settings, we propose
060
      Reinforced Imitation Learning (RILe) (Fig. 1-(d)). RILe aims to combine the ability to learn a reward
061   function that actively guides the agent to imitate expert behavior with the computational efficiency of
062   adversarial frameworks. At the core of RILe is a novel trainer-student system designed to address the
063   shortcomings of existing methods:
064
             • A student agent that learns to replicate the expert’s policy via RL in the environment
065
             • A trainer agent that learns a reward function via RL and guides the student agent during
066
               training
067
068   By integrating the trainer-student dynamic, RILe decouples reward learning from policy learning and
069   the discriminator, allowing each component to specialize and thereby overcome the limitations of
070
      policy-agnostic discriminators. While RILe utilizes a discriminator similar to those in adversarial
      frameworks, its role is fundamentally redefined. In RILe, the discriminator’s primary function is
071
      to provide feedback to the trainer agent by distinguishing expert data from student roll-outs. This
072
      feedback serves as the reward signal for the trainer, not directly influencing the student agent. The
073   trainer leverages the discriminator’s feedback to learn a reward function that effectively guides the
074   student agent. This approach enables more nuanced reward shaping, particularly beneficial in tasks
075   requiring complex decision-making and extensive exploration.
076
      Our contributions are two-fold:
077
078         1. Decoupled Reward-function Learning: We introduce a novel approach where the trainer
079            agent learns the reward function independently from both the student agent and the discrim-
080            inator. Unlike existing methods that derive rewards directly from discriminator outputs,
081            our trainer agent uses reinforcement learning to optimize the reward function based on
082
               the feedback from the discriminator. By focusing on long-term reward maximization, RL
               enables the trainer to distill inconsistent feedback from the discriminator into meaningful
083
               rewards, leading to better student performance.
084
            2. Dynamic Reward Customization: Our trainer agent dynamically adjusts rewards based on
085            the student agent’s progress, facilitating a better learning experience and enabling accurate
086            imitation of expert behavior in complex settings. This adaptive approach allows for more
087            gradual learning, particularly in tasks where the optimal behavior may change depending on
088            the agent’s current capabilities.
089
      We evaluate RILe against state-of-the-art methods in AIL, and AIRL, specifically GAIL Ho & Ermon
090
      (2016) AIRL Fu et al. (2018), GAIfO Torabi et al. (2018b), BCO Torabi et al. (2018a), IQ-Learn
091   Garg et al. (2021) and DRAIL Lai et al. (2024). Our experiments span three scenarios: (1) Tailoring a
092   reward function dynamically in a discrete maze task, (2) Investigating the impact of expert data on the
093   trainer-student dynamics in a humanoid locomotion task, and (3) Imitating expert data in continuous
094   control tasks. The results demonstrate RILe’s superior performance, especially in complex tasks, and
095   its ability to learn an effective dynamic reward function where baseline methods fail.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
098
099
      We review literature on learning from expert demonstrations, focusing on Imitation Learning (IL)
100   and Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL), which form the conceptual foundation of RILe.
101
102   Imitation Learning Early work introduced Behavioral Cloning (BC) (Bain & Sammut, 1995),
103   which learns a policy congruent with expert demonstrations through supervised learning. DAgger
104   (Ross et al., 2011) introduces data aggregation. GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) introduces adversarial
105   methods, where a discriminator aims to discriminate expert demonstrations, while a generator tries
106   to fool the discriminator. BCO (Torabi et al., 2018a) extends BC and GAIfO (Torabi et al., 2018b)
107   extends GAIL to state-only observation scenarios. DQfD (Hester et al., 2018) proposes two-stage
      approach with pre-training, and ValueDice (Kostrikov et al., 2020) uses a distribution-matching


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      objective between policy and expert. DRAIL (Lai et al., 2024) enhances adversarial imitation learning
109   via a diffusion-based discriminator, which improves learning efficiency. Despite progress, IL faces
110   challenges in efficacy and generalization (Zheng et al., 2022; Toyer et al., 2020). RILe addresses
111   these by introducing an adaptive teacher agent to guide the student beyond expert demonstrations.
112
113   Inverse Reinforcement Learning IRL, introduced by Ng & Russell (2000), learns the expert’s
114   intrinsic reward function. Key developments include Apprenticeship Learning (Abbeel & Ng, 2004),
115   Maximum Entropy IRL (Ziebart et al., 2008), and adversarial approaches like AIRL (Fu et al.,
116   2018). IQ-Learn (Garg et al., 2021) reformulates IRL integrates inverse learning of the reward
117   function into Q-learning. Recent work explores handling unstructured data (Chen et al., 2021) and
118
      cross-embodiment scenarios (Zakka et al., 2022). Despite advancements, IRL faces challenges in
      computational efficiency and scalability (Arora & Doshi, 2021). RILe addresses these by jointly
119
      learning policy and reward function in a single process.

      A standard Markov Decision Process (MDP) is defined by (S, A, R, T, K, γ). S is the state space
126
      consisting of all possible environment states s, and A is action space containing all possible envi-
127   ronment actions a. R = R(s, a) : S × A → R is the reward function. T = {P (·|s, a)} is the
128   transition dynamics where P (·|s, a) is an unknown state state transition probability function upon
129   taking action a ∈ A in state s ∈ S. K(s) is the initial state distribution, i.e., s0 ∼ K(s) and γ is
130   the discount factor. The policy π = π(a|s) : S → A is a mapping from states to actions. In this
131   work, we consider γ-discounted infinite horizon settings. Following Ho & Ermon (2016), expectation
132   with respect to the policy π ∈ Π refers to the expectation when actions are sampled from π(s):
                          P∞

196   Imitation Learning (IL) aims to directly approximate the expert policy from given expert trajectory
197   samples τE . It can be formulated as IL(τE ) = arg minπ E(s,a)∼τE [L(π(·|s), a)], where L is a loss
198   function, that captures the difference between policy and expert data.
199   GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016) introduces an adversarial imitation learning setting by quantifying the
200   difference between the agent and the expert with a discriminator Dϕ (s, a), parameterized by ϕ
201   (Fig. 1-(c)). The discriminator distinguishes between between expert-generated state-action pairs
202   (s, a) ∼ τE and non-expert ones (s, a) ∈/ τE . The goal of GAIL is to find the optimal policy that
203   fools the discriminator while maximizing an entropy constraint. The optimization is formulated as a
204   zero-sum game between the discriminator Dϕ (s, a) and the policy π:
205
                       min max Eπ [log Dϕ (s, a)] + EτE [log (1 − Dϕ (s, a))] − λH(π).                    (3)
206                      π     ϕ

208   expressed as R(s, a) = −log (Dϕ (s, a)).

      function decoupled from environment dynamics (Fig. 1-(c)). AIRL structures the discriminator as:

      4    RIL E : R EINFORCED I MITATION L EARNING
222
223
      We propose Reinforced Imitation Learning (RILe) to learn the reward function and acquire a policy
      that emulates expert-like behavior simultaneously in one learning process. Our RILe framework
224
      introduces a novel trainer-student dynamic to overcome limitations in existing imitation learning
225
      methods. Figure 2 illustrates our approach.
226
227   In RILe, the student agent learns an action policy by interacting with the environment, while the
228   trainer agent learns a reward function that effectively guides the student toward expert-like behavior.
229
      Both agents are trained simultaneously via reinforcement learning, with assistance from an adversarial
      discriminator.
230
231   Unlike traditional AIL, where the discriminator directly influences the student, RILe decouples this
232   process by introducing the trainer agent. The discriminator provides immediate feedback solely to the
233   trainer agent. This decoupling allows the trainer to adjust the reward function on-the-fly considering
234   the current stage of the student’s learning process, and guiding the student without waiting for its
235
      policy to converge, a significant efficiency improvement over traditional IRL.
236   In our framework, the trainer agent takes the key role. Trained via RL, the trainer learns to pro-
237   vide tailored feedback to the student by maximizing the cumulative rewards it receives from the
238   discriminator. This approach equips RILe with three key advantages that set it apart from existing
239   AIL frameworks: (1) the trainer associates its reward signals to future improvements in the student’s
240   behavior, even if these improvements occur after many steps, (2) the trainer encourages the student to
241
      explore actions that steer it in the right direction, even when immediate expert-like behavior isn’t
      achieved yet, and (3) the trainer adjusts its reward function based on the student’s current policy,
242
      creating a learning path that gradually guides the student toward expert behavior.
243
244   This approach enables RILe to overcome limitations of previous methods, particularly in complex
245   tasks requiring extensive exploration, by promoting the discovery of expert-like strategies even when
246   the student’s initial policy significantly diverges from expert behavior.
247   In the following, we define the components of RILe and explain how they can efficiently learn
248   behavior from imperfect data.
249

252   state sS ∈ S. However, rather than from a hand-crafted reward function, the student agent receives its
253   reward from the policy of the trainer agent πT . Therefore, the reward function is represented by the
254
      trainer policy. Thus, the student agent is guided by the actions of the trainer agent, i.e., the action of
      the trainer is the reward of the student: rS = πT ((sS , aS )). The optimization problem of the student
255
      agent is then defined as

332
333   We evaluate the performance of RILe by addressing three key questions:
334
335         1. How does RILe’s adaptive reward function evolve compared to baseline methods and how
336            does this evolution enhance the learning process?
337         2. How dynamic is RILe’s adaptive reward function, and how does this adaptability benefit the
338            student agent compared to the policy-agnostic discriminator in AIL?
339         3. Is RILe efficient and scalable to high-dimensional continuous control tasks?
340
            4. Can RILe use expert-data explicitly to imitate expert behavior?
341

343   where we demonstrate how the trainer agent modifies the reward function to guide the student during
344   training. For the second question, we evaluate the dynamics of the learned reward function and
345   analyze the correlation between these changes and improvements in the student’s performance. For
346   the third question, we evaluate RILe’s effectiveness in imitating motion-capture data within robotic
347   control tasks, using LocoMujoco (Al-Hafez et al., 2023), and imitating expert demonstrations in
348
      standard tasks, using (Brockman et al., 2016; Todorov et al., 2012). To answer the last question, we

      RILe’s performance when expert data is explicitly used by the agents. Additional experimental results

      are provided in the Appendix, where we evaluate the robustness of the learned reward function and

      6     D ISCUSSION
520
521   As our experiments demonstrate, RILe consistently outperforms baseline models across various
522   settings thanks to its adaptive learning approach, where the trainer agent dynamically adjusts the
523   reward function based on the student’s current learning stage.
524   Our Maze experiments exemplify how the trainer agent adapts rewards based on the student’s current
525   training stage. The trainer encourages the student to take actions that are suboptimal in terms
526   of immediate imitation but optimal for long-term learning. This adaptive strategy enables RILe
527   to achieve better performance compared to baselines in our continuous control experiments. In
528   contrast, as shown in Section 5.2, the policy-agnostic discriminators of AIL methods fail to provide
529   constructive guidance in complex settings, limiting the student’s improvement, limiting the student’s
530
      ability to improve. Meanwhile, RILe’s trainer continues to offer informative rewards, highlighting
      the importance of adaptive reward mechanisms.
531
532   However, RILe faces challenges in maintaining policy stability with a changing reward function.
533   Freezing the trainer is effective but limits further adaptation, and the discriminator tends to overfit
534   quickly. Future work could focus on exploring methods from fully cooperative multi-agent reinforce-
535   ment learning to allow continuous adaptation, establishing bounds for trainer updates, and exploring
536
      discriminator-less approaches.
537   Despite these challenges, RILe demonstrates significant advantages in adaptability, robustness, and
538   generalization. By providing dynamic and tailored rewards, it effectively guides the student through
539   complex learning processes, making it a promising direction for future research in imitation learning
      and opening up new possibilities for dynamic and responsive learning frameworks.

      C     T RAINING S TRATEGIES
757
758
      The introduction of the trainer agent into the AIL framework introduces instabilities that can hinder
759
      the learning process. To address these challenges, we employ three strategies.
760
761   Freezing the Trainer Agent Midway: Continuing to train the trainer agent throughout the entire
762   process can lead to overfitting on minor fluctuations in the student’s behavior. This overfitting causes
763
      the trainer to assign inappropriate negative rewards, which diverts the student away from expert
      behavior—especially since the student agent may fail to interpret these subtle nuances correctly in
764
      the later stages of training. To prevent this, we freeze the trainer agent once its critic network within
765
      the actor-critic framework converges during the training process.
766
767   We consider the trainer’s critic network to have converged when the change in the exponential moving
768   average (with a smoothing factor of 0.99) of the critic output and its variance over a window of
769
      50000 training iterations fall below a certain threshold. In all our experiments, this threshold is set to
      0.1, which we found empirically after our hyperparameter search (see Appendix H). This threshold
770
      works for all settings where the reward is bounded between −1 and 1, which is the case for all our
771
      experiments.
772
773   Reducing the Trainer’s Target Network Update Frequency: We decrease the target network
774   update frequency of the trainer agent to half that of the student agent. After our hyperparameter
775
      sweeps (see Appendix H), we empirically found that updating at half the student agent’s frequency
      works best. This adjustment aims to prevent overestimation bias in the trainer’s value function and to
776
      slow down its learning pace. By updating less frequently, the trainer provides more consistent and
777
      reliable reward signals. This steadier guidance helps the student agent better understand and adapt to
778   the trainer’s rewards, facilitating more stable learning.
779
780   Increasing the Student Agent’s Exploration: We increase the exploration rate of the student agent
781
      compared to standard AIL methods. We implement an epsilon-greedy strategy within the actor-critic
      framework, allowing the student to occasionally take random actions. This increased exploration
782
      enables the student to visit a wider range of state-action pairs. Consequently, the trainer agent receives
783
      diverse input, helping it learn a more effective reward function. This diversity is crucial for the trainer
784   to observe the outcomes of various actions and to guide the student more effectively toward expert
785   behavior.

797
798   In this experiment, we select the student agent’s hyperparameters to be identical to those used
799   in GAIL, ensuring that the only difference between the agents is the reward function. Therefore,
800
      we use the best hyperparameters identified for GAIL, applied to both GAIL and RILe, from our
      hyperparameter sweeps in Appendix H.
801
802   RFDC: We calculate the Wasserstein distance between reward distributions over consecutive 10,000-
803   step training intervals, denoted as times t and t + 10, 000. This metric quantifies how much the
804   overall reward distribution shifts over time. Changes in reward distributions depend both on the
805   reward function and the student policy updates. Since we use the same student agent with the
806
      same hyperparameters, higher RFDC values still indicate that the reward function is adapting more
      dynamically in response to the student’s learning progress.
807
808   FS-RFDC: We compute the mean absolute deviation of rewards between consecutive 10,000-step
809   training intervals for a fixed set of states derived from expert data. As the fixed set, we use all the
      states in the expert data. Since the states used for calculating rewards are fixed, changes in this value

810
      purely depend on the reward function updates. This metric assesses how the reward values for specific
811   states change over time.
812
813   CPR: We evaluate how changes in the reward function correlate with improvements in student
814
      performance. We store rewards from both the learned reward function and the environment-defined
      rewards in separate buffers. In other words, we collect samples from two reward functions: the
815
      learned reward function and the environment-defined reward function. The environment rewards
816
      consider the agent’s velocity and stability. Every 10,000 steps, we calculate the Pearson correlation
817   between these rewards and empty the buffers. This metric evaluates whether increases in the learned
818   rewards relate to performance enhancements.

822   During training, we use 8 different random seeds and 8 distinct initial positions for the robot. The
823   validation setting mirrors the training conditions: we sample initial positions from the same set
824   of 8 possibilities and use the same random seeds. In this setting, the student agent selects actions

826   For the test setting, we evaluate the policy’s ability to generalize to new, unseen scenarios. We modify
827   the initial positions of the robot by randomly initializing it in stable configurations not included in the
828   fixed set used during training. Additionally, we use different random seeds from those in training,
829   introducing new random variations that affect the environment’s dynamics during state transitions.

1048   H           H YPERPARAMETERS
1049
1050   We present hyperparameters in Table 8. For DRAIL, we replaced the discriminators with the
1051   implementation provided by DRAIL and adopted their hyperparameters for the HandRotate task.
1052
       Our experiments revealed that RILe’s performance is particularly sensitive to certain hyperparameters.
1053
       We highlight three key observations:
1054
1055               • RILe is more sensitive to the hyperparameters of the discriminator compared to other
1056                 methods. Specifically, increasing the discriminator’s capacity or training speed, by using
1057                 a larger network architecture or increasing the number of updates per iteration, adversely
1058                 affects RILe’s performance. A powerful discriminator tends to overfit quickly to the expert
1059                 data, resulting in high confidence when distinguishing between expert and student behaviors.
1060                 This poses challenges for the trainer agent, as the discriminator’s feedback becomes less
1061
                     informative.
1062               • The update frequency of the trainer agent’s target network influences the stability of the
1063                 RILe framework. Lower update frequencies lead to improved stability. A slower-updating
1064                 trainer provides more consistent reward signals, allowing the student agent to better adapt
1065
                     to the rewards. However, a lower update frequency slows down the learning process, as
                     the trainer adapts more slowly to changes in the student’s behavior. Therefore, there is a
1066
                     trade-off between stability and learning speed that needs to be balanced.
1067
1068
                   • Enhancing the exploration rate of the student agent benefits RILe more than it does baseline
                     methods. By encouraging the student to explore more, through strategies like higher entropy
1069
                     regularization or implementing an epsilon-greedy policy, the student visits a broader range of
1070
                     state-action pairs. This increased diversity provides the trainer agent with more varied data,
1071                 enabling it to learn a more effective and robust reward function. The additional exploration
1072                 helps the trainer to better capture the effects of different actions.
