                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
010            We study the performance of transformers as a function of the number of rep-
011
               etitions of training examples with algorithmically generated datasets. On three
               problems of mathematics: the greatest common divisor, modular multiplication,
012
               and matrix eigenvalues, we show that for a fixed number of training steps, models
013
               trained on smaller sets of repeated examples outperform models trained on larger
014            sets of single-use examples. We also demonstrate that two-set training - repeated
015            use of a small random subset of examples, along normal sampling on the rest of
016            the training set - provides for faster learning and better performance. This high-
017            lights that the benefits of repetition can outweigh those of data diversity. These
018            datasets and problems provide a controlled setting to shed light on the still poorly
019            understood interplay between generalization and memorization in deep learning.
020

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      When training neural networks, it has become customary to use the largest and most diverse datasets
025   available, and to limit example reuse as much as possible. This tendency is manifest in large lan-
026   guage models. GPT (Radford & Narasimhan, 2018) was trained for 100 epochs (each example was
027   seen 100 times on average), BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) on 40 and GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) on
028   20. In recent models, most examples in the pre-training corpus are seen only once, a few specialized
029   datasets are iterated 2 or 3 times, and fine-tuning examples are seen once or twice. Meanwhile, data
030   budgets are on the increase: GPT-2 was trained on less than 10 billion tokens, GPT-3 (Brown et al.,
031   2020) was pre-trained on 300 billion, Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022) and Llama (Touvron et al.,
032   2023) on 1.4 trillion, Llama2 (Touvron & et al., 2023) on 2 trillion, and Llama3 (Dubey & et al.,
033
      2024) on 15.6 trillion. Whereas the use of large train sets is grounded in theory (Vapnik & Kotz,
      2006), the practice of not repeating training examples is less motivated. It reflects the belief that,
034
      when availability permits fresh data is superior to repeated use of a corpus (Komatsuzaki, 2019;
035
      Raviv et al., 2022; Hernandez et al., 2022; Muennighoff et al., 2023). This belief is grounded in the
036   idea that memorization of repeated examples hinders generalization (Zhang et al., 2017). From a
037   human learner point of view, this is counter-intuitive. When faced with a situation we never experi-
038   enced, we recall similar instances (Proust, 1919), and use them as anchors to navigate the unknown.
039   If memorization benefits human learners (Ambridge et al., 2015), why should it hinder machines?
040
      In this paper we challenge the view that the repetition of training examples is undesirable, and that
041
      for a given training budget (TB, the total number of training examples), one should maximize the
042   data budget (DB, the number of distinct training examples). We explore the impact of repeated
043   samples in three controlled settings using generated data: computing the greatest common divisor
044   (GCD) of two integers (Charton, 2024), modular multiplication of two integers, and calculating the
045   eigenvalues of symmetric real matrices (Charton, 2022). These settings allow for perfect control over
046   the distribution of repeated examples, unlike natural datasets (e.g. text from the web) which may
047   feature unintended duplication and redundancy. Our experiments uncover two striking phenomena:
048   1. Repetition Helps: For fixed training budgets (300M to 1B examples), models trained from small
049      data budgets (25 to 50M examples) outperform models trained on large DB. This sometimes
050      gives rise to “emergent” phenomena: properties only learned by models trained on small DB.
051   2. Two-Set Training: For fixed data budgets, learning speed and performance are significantly
052      enhanced by randomly selecting a subset of training examples, and repeating them more often
053      during training. The “two-set effect” is all the more surprising as the repeated examples are not
         curated, and only differ from the rest of the training data by their frequency of use.

077   In ablation experiments, we show that the performance of two-set training cannot be improved by
078   curating the set of repeated examples, or refreshing it as training proceeds. This sets us apart from
079   curriculum learning, and strengthens the observation that repetition of a few random examples is
080   really all we need. We also show that mixing repeated and non-repeated examples in the same mini-
081   batches is required for two-set training to work. Finally, we propose a smooth extension of two-set
082   training, by introducing a probability distribution on the training set.
083   Our work isolates an interesting phenomenon in a clean setting. The three tasks we study each
084   exhibit idiosyncratic structure that allows to test a variety of hypotheses. For instance, the GCD
085   dataset exhibits an inverse polynomial distribution of results, reminiscent of Zipf’s law in natural
086   language (Zipf, 1935). This allows us to test whether amplification of the tail of the distribution
087   can benefit learning, by incorporating it into two-set training (while an attractive hypothesis, our
088   ablations show that this seems not to be the case). In contrast, the results of modular multiplication
089
      are almost uniformly distributed, indicating that our conclusions do not depend on the existence of
      a power-law. Finally, the eigenvalue problem features non-linear, approximate calculations on reals.
090
091   In all three cases, the benefits of repetition are significant, but come in different flavors, from improv-
092   ing performance and accelerating learning (GCD), to allowing a new task to be learned (multiplica-
093   tion), or to be accessible to smaller models (eigenvalues). Alternatively, small random subsets of the
094   data repeated at high frequency can elicit similar effects. These findings have profound implications
095
      and should lead to a paradigm shift where the training set size becomes a mere hyper-parameter, not
      solely governed by the availability of data and the belief that more is always better.
096

098   et al., 2023). We use training budget to distinguish it from the compute cost arising from model size.

      2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
102
103   In this paper, we focus on relatively small transformer models performing mathematical tasks, plac-
104
      ing it into a long established corpus of works that study interesting phenomena in a controlled setting,
      and advance our understanding of the underlying mechanisms in larger models in the wild, see e.g.
105
      Power et al. (2022); Garg et al. (2022); Charton (2024); Dohmatob et al. (2024).
106
107   One such example is the study of “grokking”, first observed with modular arithmetic - a phe-
      nomenon where models generalize long after achieving 100% accuracy on their (small) training


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      set (Power et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022b; 2023). On the surface, grokking shares similarities with
109   our work: a small training dataset is iterated for many epochs, the phenomenon is isolated in clean
110   experiments on synthetic data, and it contradicts traditional wisdom regarding overfitting (Mohri
111   et al., 2018). But there are important differences: in grokking, delayed learning occurs, we observe
112   no such delay; grokking occurs for “tiny” training samples (hundreds or thousands of examples), our
113   models use millions (even for modular multiplication); grokking is very sensitive to the optimizer
114   used, our findings are robust across optimizers (Appendix D.5), and, of course, no two-set approach
115   is documented in the grokking setting.
116   Another related setting is “benign overfitting” (Bartlett et al., 2020; Belkin, 2021; Bartlett et al.,
117   2021), where an over-parametrized model perfectly fits noisy data, without harming prediction ac-
118   curacy. One could argue that our work presents a quantitative manifestation of benign overfitting,
119   inasmuch as decreasing the data budget increases model over-parametrization. However, this would
120   not account for the decrease in performance once the data budget falls below a certain number (one
121   could argue that overfitting is no longer benign, then), nor for the possibility of two-set training.
122   Prior works have studied the role of data reuse in language models. Hernandez et al. (2022) study
123   data repetition in models with up to 800M parameters with training budgets of 100M tokens to
124   exhibit detrimental impact of repetition of a subset of the training data. In the context of scarcity of
125   training data, Muennighoff et al. (2023) find for LLMs of contemporary size (up to 9B) that with
126   constrained data for a fixed training budget, training with up to 4 epochs of repeated data yields
127   negligible changes to loss compared to having unique data; any further repetition decreases the
128   value of additional training. A limitation of these works is the lack of control over repetition in the
129
      training set: partial copies, of sentences, paragraphs sometimes whole documents, abound in pre-
      training corpora. Allen-Zhu & Li (2024) undertake a controlled study on synthetic language data
130
      in the context of knowledge retrieval and find that knowledge augmentation - repeated inclusion of
131
      reformulated variants - of a small subset of the data leads to performance improvement; an effect
132   somewhat akin to what we observe in two-set training.
133
134   Our work is related to, but different from, curriculum learning (CL) (Bengio et al., 2009; Wang
135
      et al., 2022), where training data is presented in a meaningful order, usually from “easy” to “hard”
      samples. Two-set training differs from curriculum learning in at least two important ways: in CL,
136
      datasets are curated, our subsets are completely random; in CL, the training distribution shifts over
137
      time, while our subsets are static. Our ablations show that curating the repeated set, or changing it
138   over time, as in CL, brings no improvement in performance (and may even have an adverse effect).
139
140   Lastly, our work touches upon the expansive area of out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization (Gul-
141
      rajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021; Lopez-Paz, 2025), which studies generalization when train and test dis-
      tributions differ. Curiously, while our two-set approach increases the frequency of some training
142
      examples, because the repeated set is chosen at random, the training set remains distributionally
143
      equivalent to the test set. Thus, our study falls outside the usual framework of OOD studies. For
144   additional discussion of our setting and related work, see Appendix A.

147   3   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS AND BASELINES
148
149   We focus on three problems of mathematics: computing the greatest common divisor, multiplication
150   modulo 67, and computing the eigenvalues of real symmetric matrices. The GCD and eigenvalues
151   were studied in prior work (Charton, 2022; 2024; Dohmatob et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024).
152   Greatest common divisor. The model is tasked to predict the GCD of two integers uniformly
153   distributed between 1 and 1 million, encoded in base 1000. Following Charton (2024), who observes
154   that throughout training almost all pairs of integers with the same GCD are predicted the same, we
155   evaluate model performance by the number of GCD below 100 predicted correctly, measured on a
156   random test sample of 100, 000 pairs: 1000 pairs for each GCD from 1 to 100. Charton reports a
157   best performance of 22 correct GCD for a model trained on uniformly distributed inputs.
158
      Note. We prefer this test metric over a more standard accuracy on random input pairs, because the
      GCD are distributed according to an inverse square law. In particular the probability that a GCD is
159
      1 is about 62%. As a result, the accuracy metric results in overly optimistic model performances.
160
161   Modular multiplication. Modular arithmetic plays an important role in many public key cryptog-
      raphy algorithms (Diffie & Hellman, 1976; Regev, 2005), and is known to be a hard problem for


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      neural networks (Palamas, 2017). Modular addition was studied in several previous works, in the
163   context of grokking (Power et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a) and mechanistic interpretability (Zhong
164   et al., 2023)1 . While modular multiplication over Z/pZ× is mathematically is equivalent to modu-
165   lar addition mod p − 1, these problems differ computationally, due to the hardness of the discrete
166   logarithm (Diffie & Hellman, 1976). In most previous works on arithmetic modulo p, model inputs
167   are sampled from integers between 0 and p, which results in a very small problem space for small p.
168   In this work, we study the multiplication modulo 67 of two integers from 1 to 1 million. This allows
169   for a much larger problem space, and training sets. Model accuracy is evaluated by the percentage
170   of correct predictions of a × b mod 67, on a test set of 10, 000 examples (a new test set is generated
171
      at every evaluation). In this problem, all outcomes from 1 to 66 are uniformly distributed, while 0
      appears nearly twice as often.
172
173   Eigenvalue calculation. This problem was introduced to deep learning by Charton (2022), who
174   showed that transformers can learn to predict the eigenvalues of real symmetric matrices with in-
175   dependent and identically distributed entries, rounded to three significant digits. The eigenvalue
176   problem is arguably a harder problem than the previous two, non-linear and typically solved by iter-
177
      ative algorithms. Nonetheless the eigenvalue problem seems an easier task for transformers, as even
      smaller transformers are able to solve this task for matrices of size up to 8x8 (Charton, 2022). We
178
      include this problem to show that our conclusions extend beyond arithmetic problems on integers.
179
      Note also that because matrix entries and eigenvalues are rounded, this problem features noisy in-
180   puts and outputs. Model accuracy is evaluated as the percentage of model predictions that predict the
181   correct eigenvalues of a test matrix with less than 5% relative error (in ℓ1 distance). It is measured
182   on a test set of 10, 000 samples, generated afresh at every evaluation.
183
      Models and tokenizers. In all experiments, we use sequence-to-sequence transformers (Vaswani
184
      et al., 2017) with 4 layers in the encoder and decoder (4-layers encoders and 1-layer decoder for
185
      eigenvalues), an embedding dimension of 512, and 8 attention heads. Models have 35 million pa-
186   rameters for GCD and modular multiplication, and 22 million for eigenvalues. They are trained to
187   minimize a cross-entropy loss, using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), with a learning rate
188   of 10−5 , over batches of 64. The integer inputs and outputs of the GCD and multiplication problems
189   are tokenized as sequences of digits in base 1000, preceded by a separator token. The real num-
190   bers in the eigenvalue problem are encoded as floating point numbers, rounded to three significant
191   digits, and tokenized as the triplet (s, m, e) – sign, (base 1000) mantissa, (base 10) exponent – i.e.
192   f = s · m · 10e (P1000 encoding from Charton (2022)). All experiments are run on one NVIDIA
193   V100 GPU with 32 GB of memory.

      Finally, on the eigenvalue problem, Charton (2022) trained models with unlimited data budgets
271   (DB≈TB) and observed that whereas 4-layer transformers can learn to compute the eigenvalues of
272   5 × 5 matrices, deeper models are required for larger problems: 6-layers for 8 × 8 matrices, 8 for

275
      In our experiments, we wanted to study whether smaller DB could induce small models to learn
276
      large problems. We trained 4-layer transformers to predict the eigenvalues of 10 × 10 matrices. We
277   trained 5 models for each data budget of 1, 5, 10, 25, 50 and 100M, and 5 for an unlimited DB (one
278   pass over the training data), with TB up to 500 million. As expected, none of the models trained
279   on unlimited DB did learn: all test accuracy remained close to 0. However, 4 of the 30 models
280   trained on smaller DB achieved 99% accuracy: 3 models trained on 50 million examples (repeated
281   10 times), and one model trained on 10 million (repeated 50 times). Scaling even further, to 12 × 12
282   matrices, still using 4-layer transformers, with a TB of 420 millions, 2 models (out of 35) begin
283   learning: a 10M model achieved 21% accuracy, and a 5M 3.5%. As in previous experiments, for a
284   given training budget, smaller data budgets and repeated training examples prove beneficial, but on
285
      this task, small datasets improve model scaling. With small DB, problems that required 8-layer or
      12-layer transformers can be learned by 4-layer models.
286
287   This first series of experiments clearly indicates that repetition helps learning. On three different
288   tasks, for a fixed training budget, models trained on a small data budget, i.e. fewer distinct examples
289   repeated several times, achieve much better performance than models trained from examples used
290   only once or repeated a small number of times, as is customary in most recent works on language
291
      models (Muennighoff et al., 2023).
292   This phenomenon applies in different ways for different problems. On the GCD task, small DB
293   allow for faster learning and higher accuracy. For modular multiplication, we observe emergence:
294   a task inaccessible to models trained with large or unlimited DB is learned with small DB. Finally,
295   for eigenvalues, small DB allow for better model scaling: tasks that normally require 8 or 12-layer
296   transformers are learned by 4-layer models. But in all cases, the repetition achieved by small DB
297
      prove beneficial: smaller data budgets with repetition can elicit “emergent learning”.

299   5   T WO - SET TRAINING
300
301   The previous experiments demonstrate that for a fixed training budget, the optimal data budget is
302   not the largest possible, as commonly practiced. On all three tasks, training from a set of distinct
303   examples an order of magnitude smaller than the training budget, repeated many times, improves
304   performance. We now turn to a different but related problem: how to best use a given data budget?
305
      As we have seen, repeated examples help the model learn. Training from a small subset of the
306   available data should therefore be beneficial, since it would increase repetition. However, models
307   trained from very small datasets will eventually overfit their data, causing their accuracy to saturate.
308   Yet, this can be prevented by increasing the size of the training set. To address these contradictory
309   requirements – a small train set to increase repetition vs a large train set to avoid overfitting – we
310   propose two-set training. We randomly split the training sample into a small set of examples that
311   will be repeated many times during training, and a large set of examples that will be seen a few times
312   only. By doing so, we hope that the small set fosters learning, while the large set prevents overfit.
313   Specifically, for a data budget of N distinct examples, we randomly select S < N examples that
314   will form the repeated set – in practice, we shuffle the training set, and assign the S first examples to
315   the repeated set. During training, examples are selected from the repeated set with probability p, and
316   from the N − S others with probability (1 − p). As a result, a model trained with a training budget
317   of T will see pT examples from the repeated set, repeated pT /S times on average, while the N − S
318   remaining examples will be repeated (1 − p)T /(N − S) times on average. The repetition levels in
319   both samples can be adjusted by choosing the values of S and p. Note that the limiting cases p = 0
320
      and p = 1 correspond to one-set training, with a data budget of N − S and S examples respectively.

322   and a training budget of 600 million, predict 27 GCD on average (Figure 2 (Left)). Experimenting
323   with two-set training for different values of S and p, we observe that models trained on a repeated
      set of 250, 000 examples or less, with a probability p of 0.25 or 0.5, predict more than 62 GCD on

451   In this section, we discuss possible improvements to two-set training. Detailed ablation results can
452   be found in Appendix D.
453
454   Curating the repeated sample. In two-set training, repeated examples are randomly sampled
455   from the available training data. We now experiment with a possible improvement: selecting the
456   repeated examples. Perhaps what really matters is the repetition of a particular class of “informa-
457   tive” examples, as in curriculum learning. The GCD problem is particularly well suited for this type
458   of investigation. Charton (2024) showed that increasing the proportion of small integers, or over-
459
      sampling the tails of the distribution of GCD in the training set (Prob(GCD = k) ∼ k12 ), greatly
      improved model performance.

464   four “good choices” of S and p (Table 4), a data budget of 100M and training budget of 600M.

      Batching matters. All models in this paper are trained on mini-batches of 64 examples. In two-set
      training, batches mix examples from the repeated and the large set. We experimented with batches
491
      that only use samples from one set at a time. For instance, when training with p = 0.25, 25% of
492
      batches would use repeated examples only. For both GCD and modular multiplication, we observe
493   that models trained on batches from one sample only fail to learn. This indicates that mixing repeated
494   and non-repeated examples is required for two-set training to happen (see also Appendix D.2).

      From two to many-set training. Two-set training effectively makes the training sample non
      identically-distributed: examples from the repeated sample occur with a larger probability. We
497
      can generalize this method by introducing a probability distribution P on the training examples,
498
      such that for any i ≤ N , P (i) is the probability that the i-th example is selected during training.

505   wider class of methods, that use non-uniform sampling over a randomly ordered training set.

513   7      D ISCUSSION
514
515   Our findings indicate that repetition, and possibly memorization, fosters learning. They suggest that
516   models should be trained on datasets of repeated, but not necessarily curated examples, and that
517   amplifying a randomly chosen subset of the training data may bring additional learning benefits.
518   Two-set training is easy to implement, and applicable to a large variety of situations. Its extension
519
      to smooth distributions allows for finer control over repetition levels in the training sets. One fea-
      ture of our tasks - which is the case in most reasoning tasks in AI4Math settings - is that they are
520
      deterministic : there is only one correct solution. For this community, our insights are of imme-
521
      diate relevance, as they give prescriptive advice on how to utilize training data (iterate rather than
522   one-pass, use two-set training).
523
524   We can contemplate how our observations carry over to large language models (LLM) trained on
525
      natural data. An important factor is the presence of repetition in the training data. We believe
      that pre-training corpora – text scraped from the internet, public code repositories – feature many
526
      repeated examples (quotes, copied passages, duplicated functions), and that the phenomena we de-
527
      scribe are already at work in LLMs during the pre-training stage. Fine-tuning corpora, on the other
528   hand, are often curated and feature less repetition. We believe two-set training, and associated meth-
529   ods, may prove beneficial for fine-tuning LLMs.
530
531
      Our observations on two-set training are thought-provoking and deserve further study. The fact
      that the repeated set can be chosen at random, and that curating repeated examples bring little to
532
      no improvement in performance suggest that what matters, here, is seeing the exact same example
533
      several times. The particulars of the example, its informational value, interest, whether it is typical
534   or exceptional, seem to have little impact. This is all the more curious as, even in the two-set setting,
535   repetition occurs at a very low frequency. In the two-set GCD experiments, repeated examples were
536   seen 3000 times over a training budget of 600 million: once every 200,000 examples on average. The
537   frequency is even lower for modular multiplication. Besides, the repeated examples are mixed with
538   non-repeated examples into mini-batches, and our experiments indicate that this mixing is required
539   for the two-set effect to appear. Still, this very infrequent repetition, and mini-batch mixing, brings
      a significant boost in model performance.

705   A    A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION
706
707   Our empirical work studies the role of repetition in learning. In our two-set setting, a select subset of
708   the data is presented to the learner at increased frequency. This is reminiscent of continual learning,
709   where, to avoid catastrophic forgetting, certain examples from a previous task are repeated again.
710
      However, it is important to point out that continual learning refers to a setting with distribution
      shift: the learner is supposed to adjust to new distributions in the input - and to avoid catastrophic
711
      forgetting of earlier tasks. This is an extremely interesting research area - but it is not what we study.
712
      In our setting the distribution is always the same, both for repeated examples (Section 4) and for the
713   new phenomenon of two-set training (Section 5). Both the more frequently sampled and the less
714   frequent larger set come from exactly the same underlying distribution - which is what makes this
715   effect so surprising.
716
      Our work also resembles curriculum learning, where training data is presented in a particular order
717
      (usually from easy to hard). Our work is different from curriculum learning: We show that randomly
718
      selecting a small subset of the training data, and repeating them more often can significantly enhance
719   performance or even overcome learning bottlenecks. We discover a synergistic effect: neither train-
720   ing on the small set alone, nor training with unlimited data budget in one epoch would allow any
721   learning at all - it is the combination of both that makes two-set training powerful! The fact that the
722   repeated set can be chosen at random, and that curating repeated examples brings no improvement
723   in performance sets it aside from curriculum learning and suggest that what matters, here, is seeing
724   the exact same example several times.
725   Can the two-step procedure be performed by showing only the repeated samples in a first learning
726   phase, and then moving on to the more diverse samples in a second phase - thus placing it icloser
727   to the realm of curriculum learning? We have performed variants of this experiment (see Appendix
728   D.6) to provide a negative answer to this question.
729
      Very few theoretical works have tried to study repeated examples in the learning process. Indeed,
730
      the fact that our observed two-set effect disappears when data is presented in mono-batches (either
731   from the frequent set or the larger set), means that the mechanisms at play must involve optimization.
732   Noteworthy are two recent works from Dandi et al. (2024) and Arnaboldi et al. (2024) in the special
733   case of the the multi-index model for two-layer nets. There, it is shown that batches need to be seen
734   more than once for beneficial symmetry breaking - and ultimately learning - to happen. Our mono-
735   batch ablation shows that this cannot explain what we observe in our case, however. First, in our
736   case, no two batches are the same, as data gets reshuffled. However, mono-batches are more likely
737   to resemble each other when they come from the smaller, frequent set, compared to the multi-batch
738   case where by design most examples in each batch will never be seen again (in the case of unlimited
739
      data at least). This means that our mono-batch case should resemble the scenario in Arnaboldi et al.
      (2024) more - yet it fails to show the observed effect.
740
741   This raises several tantalizing questions: how does the transformer “figure” that a given example, lost
742   in a mini-batch, has been seen, several hundred thousand examples before? Our research suggests
743   that there exists a qualitative difference between “déjà vu” and “jamais vu” examples – data points
744   the model has already seen, or never seen. How do transformers, and perhaps other architectures,
745
      identify, and then process, “déjà vu” examples? To our knowledge, this aspect was overlooked in
      many prior works on model interpretation. We believe our findings point to a number of interesting
746
      questions about memorization in language models. This is an intriguing subject for further study.

1008   Two-set training with a small randomly selected subset S amounts to assigning different probabilities
1009   to elements in the training set. For a randomly shuffled training set of size N , two-set training
1010   amounts to selecting the first S elements with probability p/S (with replacement) and the N −S last
1011

1012

1013

1016   partition.
