009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Dark matter makes up approximately 85% of total matter in our universe, yet it
012            has never been directly observed in any laboratory on Earth. The origin of dark
013            matter is one of the most important questions in contemporary physics, and a
014            convincing detection of dark matter would be a Nobel-Prize-level breakthrough
015            in fundamental science. The ABRACADABRA experiment was specifically de-
016
               signed to search for dark matter. Although it has not yet made a discovery, ABRA-
               CADABRA has produced several dark matter search results widely endorsed by
017
               the physics community. The experiment generates ultra-long time-series data at a
018
               rate of 10 million samples per second, where the dark matter signal would mani-
019            fest itself as a sinusoidal oscillation mode within the ultra-long time series. In this
020            paper, we present the TIDMAD — a comprehensive data release from the ABRA-
021            CADABRA experiment including three key components: an ultra-long time series
022            dataset divided into training, validation, and science subsets; a carefully-designed
023            denoising score for direct model benchmarking; and a complete analysis frame-
024            work which produces a community-standard dark matter search result suitable for
025            publication as a physics paper. This data release enables core AI algorithms to
026            extract the signal and produce real physics results thereby advancing fundamen-
027
               tal science. The data downloading and associated analysis scripts are available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TIDMAD.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The quest to uncover the nature of dark matter is one of the biggest challenges in contemporary
033   physics. Several key observations in astrophysics and cosmology have confirmed the existence of
034   dark matter, which constitutes approximately 85% of all mass in the universe (Rubin & Ford, 1970;
035   Tyson et al., 1998; Tegmark & et. al., 2004; Aghanim & et. al., 2020; Adams & et. al., 2023).
036   However, dark matter has never been detected by any detector on Earth. Because the composition
037   of dark matter is unknown, theoretical physicists propose various dark matter candidates — hypo-
038   thetical particles that can be characterized by their physical parameters. Experimental physicists
039
      then design experiments to search for these candidates. A convincing detection of any dark matter
      candidate would be a Nobel-Prize-level breakthrough in fundamental science, but even if nothing
040
      is detected, the null results still play a significant role in advancing our understanding of physics
041
      by setting limits within the physical parameter space. This means that a particular experiment has
042   eliminated the existence of a dark matter candidates within these limits and does not have sufficient
043   sensitivity to test outside these limits. These limits can be reciprocally used by theoretical physicists
044   to propose better dark matter candidates, thereby improving our understanding of this mysterious
045   constituent of our universe.
046
      Attributable to its extremely rare interactions with normal matter, the signal of dark matter is often
047
      submerged in a sea of noise from various sources internal and external to the experimental appara-
048   tus. While the signal shape is extremely well parameterized, modeling backgrounds with traditional
049   methods is intractable given they are composed of both transient and persistent noise sources, fre-
050   quency dependant and independent sources, phase coherent and incoherent noise, and amplifier
051   dependant and independent noise. Machine learning (ML) offers a promising means to reduce this
052   noise. Advancements in denoising techniques using ML algorithms have the potential to signifi-
053   cantly improve dark matter analyses Saleem et al. (2023). These techniques enable the detection
      of weaker dark matter signals, or in the case of no observation, the setting of stronger limits. In

081   The training data include time series data where a dark matter-like signal is injected by hardware.

087

092   denoising algorithms are presented in Section 3 and Appendix C, where the resulting denoised data
093   is benchmarked against the raw, un-denoised detector data.

095   1.1   A XION DARK MATTER AND ABRACADABRA
096
097   In recent years, the axion has emerged as one of the leading dark matter candidates as a result
098   of its theoretical elegance. Axions interact with normal matter via electromagnetism, which can
099   be characterized by a physics parameter gaγγ . Arising from its small mass ma < 1eV (10−6
100   times smaller than electron), axions acts as a classical field oscillating at a frequency fa = ma /2π.
101   Astrophysical measurements determine that the Earth exists in a bath of dark matter with a known
102   local density of ρDM de Salas & Widmark (2021).

106   we are immersed in a bath of a feebly electromagnetically-interacting, oscillating dark matter field
107   to detect this elusive particle. Specifically, in the presences of a static magnetic field B0 , the axion,
      henceforth referred to as dark matter, induces an oscillating magnetic field Ba . Thus, to detect dark

      1.2                    TIDMAD CONSTRUCTION
123   In the classical analysis, we use a calibration procedure to determine the end-to-end response of
124   our system for different signal frequencies. As shown in Figure 1, the ABRA detector contains a
125   toroidal magnet equipped with both a pickup loop and a calibration loop. During calibration, we
126   first inject a fake dark matter signal into the calibration loop at a specific frequency. This generates
127   a sine wave with a known amplitude and frequency, creating a dark matter-like flux with our pickup
128   loop. Finally, this flux is detected by the SQUID sensor for detector calibration.
129   The dark matter signal injected into the calibration loop by the signal generator follows the form
130   prescribed by axion theory, as shown in Equation 1 and derived in Appendix A. We specifically
131   choose to inject sine waves with frequencies from 1.1 kHz to 4.9 MHz, corresponding to axion
132   masses ma = [0.005, 17] neV, to target the mass range that our experimental hardware is designed
133   to detect. The injected signals were all set to an amplitude of 50 mV to ensure a reasonable signal-
134   to-noise ratio. A total of 309 different frequencies were sequentially stepped through, from 1.1 kHz
135   to 4.9 MHz, simulating 309 distinct axion masses in our detector hardware.

168   The data presented in this paper were acquired using the ABRACADABRA detector. The overall
169   schematics of the data is shown in Figure 3. All data are saved as a series at 10 MS/s (Megasample

171   converted into a physics units of mV (millivolts) with a scaling factor of 40/128. The procured
172   datasets are stored at Open Science Data Federation (OSDF) Weitzel et al. (2017) in .hdf5 format
173   and can be accessed via the download data.py script in the github repository provided.

192   Training dataset: The training dataset contains 80.23 Gigasamples of time series data, corre-
193   sponding to roughly two hours of data collection. For training purposes, we injected a fake dark
194   matter signal into the hardware as discussed in Section 1.2. The injected signal scans through
195   dark matter frequencies from 1100 Hz to 5 MHz at two different amplitudes: 50 mV (standard)
196

197
      download data.py script, providing a more challenging scenario for signal recovery. All train-
198
      ing data are partitioned into 20 files. Each file contains two channels: the injected time series is
199   saved in the second channel (CH2), while the SQUID time series is saved in the first channel (CH1).
200   As discussed in Section 1.2, the training task is to recover the CH2 ground truth time series using
201   the CH1 noisy time series as input.
202
203
      Validation dataset: The validation dataset, consisting of 80.23 Gigasamples, has the same format
204   as the training dataset. The only difference is that the validation dataset was independently col-
205   lected at a different time using the same detector apparatus, making it an out-of-sample dataset with
206   slightly altered noise conditions. After training, users can perform model inference by running the
207   inference.py script to denoise the CH1 time series while preserving the injected signal. The
208   denoised SQUID time series in CH1 and the injected time series in CH2 are then processed through
209   the benchmark.py script. To determine the efficacy of the model’s denoising, a benchmarking
210   score called ‘denoising score’ is calculated, which will be discussed in detail in Section 4.1.
211
212   Science dataset: The science dataset comprises 833.82 Gigasamples of time series data collected
213   over a 24-hour period. This data is distributed across 208 .hdf5 files. Unlike the training and
214   validation datasets, there are no injected signals, meaning that only the CH1 time series is saved per
215   file. The inference task for ML models is to denoise the SQUID time series in CH1. The denoised
      science data is then analyzed to obtain a dark matter limit, which will be discussed in Section 4.2.

      3   E XPERIMENTS
217
218
219
      We experimented five different denoising algorithms including two traditional algorithms and three
      deep learning models. The traditional algorithm can be directly applied to the validation dataset
220
      using inference.py, while deep learning models need to be trained first on the training dataset
221
      with train.py. The five algorithms are listed below:
222
223          • Moving average: a simple moving average with a window size of 100, implemented using
224            the numpy convolve function.
225
226          • Savitzky-Golay filter: with a window size of 100 and a polynomial order of 11.
227
             • FC net: an autoencoder architecture designed for transforming input data. This model
228
               consists of an encoder and a decoder. The encoder encodes the input data into a low-
229            dimensional representation, while the decoder reconstructs the original data from this
230            encoded representation. Both the encoder and decoder are composed of multiple fully-
231            connected layers and activation layers. FC-Net outputs a single float point number at each
232            time step, and the training is conducted by minimizing the mean square error between this
233            float point number and corresponding ground truth time series at every sample.
234
             • PU net: a deep learning architecture based on the UNet architecture Ronneberger et al.
235
               (2015). U-Net uses convolution layers as encoder and deconvolution layers as decoder,
236
               with contracting paths established between each pair of convolutional and deconvolution
237            layers at the same level. This allows information at different encoding levels to flow to the
238            decoding part. Positional encoding layers are introduced at all encoder layers to enhance
239            the model’s ability to understand positions in the time series. Since every sample of the
240            ground truth time series has to be 8-bit integers ranging from -128 to 127, we require the
241            model to output a 256-class classification decision at every time step, where each class
242            corresponds to one possible output value. This effectively redefines the denoising task into
243            a semantic segmentation task.
244
             • Transformer: the transformer utilizes a self-attention mechanism to capture long-distance
245
               dependencies in sequences Vaswani et al. (2017). After processing by the multi-layer
246            Transformer encoder, the model effectively extracts features and represents the input se-
247            quence. Finally, the encoded sequence is mapped to the output dimension through a linear
248            layer for the same 256-class classification decision as PU-Net. Positional encoding is also
249            added before the time series is fed into Transformer layers. Both PU-Net and Transformer
250            are trained using Focal Loss to handle class-imbalanced segmentation labels Lin et al.
251            (2017).
252
253   The benchmarking results of these models are discussed in Section 4. There are two additional
254   constraints for the deep learning models. First, because of memory constraints, we segment the
255
      ultra-long SQUID and injected time series into smaller segments before feeding them into each
      model. The exact segment sizes are outlined in Table 1. Secondly, as a result of the large range
256
      of injected frequencies, training multiple versions of the same model to handle different frequency
257
      ranges is necessary. Both limitations and additional details of the deep learning models are discussed
258   in Appendix B.

      We developed two benchmarking criteria to evaluate the performance of denoising algorithms.
264   Benchmark 1: Denoising Score provides a quantitative measure of denoising performance based
265   on the signal-to-noise ratio. This score is designed to be linear with respect to the noise level and
266   equal to one when no denoising is applied. While Benchmark 1 offers a quick, straightforward as-
267   sessment of model performance, it lacks direct relevance to fundamental science. To bridge this gap,
268   we developed Benchmark 2: Dark Matter Limit, which directly links AI algorithms to community-
269   standard physics result by automating the entire dark matter analysis on the science dataset. This
      benchmark allows AI algorithms to directly improve the physics reach of dark matter detectors.

415   5   L IMITATIONS AND APPLICATIONS
416
417   Hardware and datataking period: As illustrated in Figure 5, the baseline models fail to surpass
418   the results of ABRA Run 3. This is attributed to hardware limitations and changes since the last
419   data run, including (1) replacing the dark matter pickup cylinder with a pickup loop, consequently
420   reducing the geometric coupling to the dark matter signal, and (2) reducing the data taking period
421
      to 24 hours from three months. The decision to implement these changes was driven by the aim to
      enable ABRA to simultaneously search for dark matter and gravitational waves, thereby enhancing
422
      the scientific scope of the experiment. The shortened data taking duration was necessitated by
423
      operational constraints of the dilution refrigerator. Because the signal-to-noise ratio scales as the
424   fourth root of integration time, we can increase ABRA’s sensitivity to dark matter by increasing the
425   data taking period Ouellet & Bogorad (2019). Another more efficacious way to increase this signal-
426   to-noise ratio is improving our denoising with ML; a doubling of our noise reduction represents a 16x
427   speed up our data taking time revealing the out-sized return on investment in denoising techniques
428   when compared to increased detector run time.
429

469   We present TIDMAD, the first dataset and benchmark designed to yield a community-standard dark
470   matter search result. TIDMAD includes all necessary inputs and processing to train time series
471   denoising algorithms and produce a science-level dark matter limit. Through a series of experiments,
472   we developed three ultra-long time series deep learning algorithms, benchmarked their ability to
473   recover hardware-injected signals, and set dark matter limits. Clear performance improvements
474   were demonstrated on both benchmarks. Our future work will focus on enhancing the denoising
475
      algorithm to achieve better dark matter limits, expanding to other nuclear and particle experiments,
      and embedding these algorithms onto FPGA chips for real-time denoising during data taking.

480   the AI/ML community to make direct scientific advancements. This transparency aims to foster

      greater collaboration between the ML and particle physics communities, benefiting both fields.

      A    DARK M ATTER S IGNAL AND S IGNAL I NJECTION
649
650
651
652   Axion dark matter appears in our detector as a time oscillating current with two free model pa-
653   rameters (ma , gaγγ ) described by Equation 1. This signal can be fully derived from axion theory
654   and is extremely well-defined. While a full derivation of this equation is presented in Reference
655   Ouellet & Bogorad (2019), schematically, this theoretical prediction for the dark matter signal in
656   our detection comes from four phenomena – the omnipresent axion field, axion’s interactions with
657
      electromagnetism, the specific geometry of our detector, and the velocity distribution of the dark
      matter.
658
659   Under certain conditions, axions are created in the early universe via the misalignment mechanism
660   Dine & Fischler (1983); Preskill et al. (1983). When axions are created they produce a time oscil-
661   lating omnipresent axion field with an oscillation frequency equal to ma and phase coherence. If
662   axions are dark matter then the abundance of axions and the strength of this axion field are set by
663
      our astrophysical observations of dark matter density de Salas & Widmark (2021).
664   The axion field can interact with other physical forces including electromagnetism. If an axion field
665   collides with an electromagnetic field, some of the axions will be transformed into photons. These
666   photons produce a secondary electromagnetic field – an axion-induced electromagnetic field. In the
667   geometry of our detector, the axion-induced electromagnetic field is read out with a pickup wire
668   which samples the field like a typical radio antenna. This means the time oscillating axion field
669
      turns into a time oscillating current in our detector (i.e. our dark matter signal).
670   If earth were stationary, sitting in a uniform bath of dark matter, then the dark matter signal frequency
671   would be exactly the axion field oscillation frequency (i.e. the axion mass) and completely coherent.
672   However, the earth is moving within the Milky Way galaxy and thus the axion field gains a velocity
673   with respect to earth at about vDM ∼ 220km/s. Doppler shifting spreads this frequency such
                                                                             2
674   that it is distribution around the original field frequency ∆f = vDM       f Ouellet & Bogorad (2019).
675
      This frequency distribution is six orders of magnitude smaller than the signal frequency, therefore
      can be treated a coherent, single frequency sine-wave to good approximation. For signal injection,
676
      this single frequency approximation is used while for the dark matter analysis, we model the full
677
      frequency distribution.
678
679   Thus, we have established that the dark matter signal in the ABRA detector is approximately sine-
680   wave current. The frequency of this oscillating signal is a model parameter meaning if we knew
681
      the axion mass, this frequency would be set. However, theoretical models point to a range of pos-
      sible axion masses, not a singular value. Ideally we would create a dark matter detector that could
682
      search the entire range of valid dark matter masses, but experimental constraints such detector size,
683
      configuration, and readout electronics preclude this possibility. Instead, experiments must be tai-
684   lored to search for smaller areas of the axion mass parameter space with ABRA the detector being
685   specifically designed to target ma = 0.4 − 10 neV Ouellet & et. al. (2019a).
686
687
      The second free signal parameter is the sine wave amplitude which is proportional to gaγγ , the
      strength of the axion’s interactions with electromagnetism. Theoretical calculations constrain this
688
      parameter to gaγγ = Cma where C = [−0.39, 0.22] depending on the theory Shifman et al. (1980);
689
      Dine et al. (1981). Theoretically motivated axion couplings can be seen in Figure 5 as the gold band
690   in the ma , gaγγ parameter space. Experimentally, the goal is to detect ever smaller signal amplitudes
691   to reach lower values of gaγγ .
692
693
      To inject a fake signal into the hardware, we replicate the signal current, given by Equation 1, with
      a signal generator. The signal generator is connected to a calibration loop depicted in Figure 1 de-
694
      signed to mimic an axion field incident on the detector. We specifically choose to inject sine waves
695
      with frequencies from 1.1 kHz to 4.9 MHz, ma = [0.005, 17] neV, to contain the masses our experi-
696   mental hardware was built to target. The injected fake signals we used all have amplitudes of 50 mV
697   to achieve a reasonable signal-to-noise ratio. While injecting smaller fake signal amplitudes would
698   effectively simulate dark matter candidates with smaller electromagnetic couplings, fake signals
699   smaller than 50 mV are difficult to detect with traditional techniques. Though smaller couplings
700   provide an interesting ML task, our denoising score benchmark is predicated on finding injected
701   signals with traditional techniques and subsequently we did not use smaller fake signal amplitudes.
      However, we did take data injected with signals ranging from 1.1 kHz to 4.9 MHz at a signal am-

703   publicly available (see Datasheet) for an extra challenge.

705   To summarize, our signal injection scheme involves exciting hardware with a sine wave from a
706
      signal generator. We sequentially step through 309 different frequencies from 1.1 kHz to 4.9 MHz

710   B    D ETAILS AND LIMITATIONS OF DEEP LEARNING MODEL
711
712   There are two special treatments we took to train the three deep learning models:
713
714   Training segmentation: To feed the time series data into limited GPU memories, the training time
715
      series are segmented into 4 milliseconds. This imposes a fundamental lower limit on the frequencies
      that the models can detect. While this lower limit, approximately 250 Hz given the sampling fre-
716
      quency of 10 MS/s, is relatively small, it does establish a foundational lower threshold for frequency
717
      resolution in the model. The transformer model requires additional memory, therefore we have to
718   further reduce the segment to 2 millisecond or 500 Hz. Both of these limits are well below the dark
719   matter search range: 0.4 neV (100 kHz) to 8 neV (2 MHz).
720
721   Frequency splitting: Since the injected dark matter signal spans two orders of magnitude in fre-
722   quency, the observed features in the injected time series significantly vary. During training, we no-
723   ticed that a single deep learning model to denoise the entire dataset would fail to generalize across
724   the different injected frequency ranges. To address this issue, we trained four deep learning models
725   per architecture, each focusing on a specific frequency range: the first covering the low-frequency
726   regime (training/validation files 0-3), the second covering the mid-low regime (training/validation
727   files 4-9), the third covering the mid-high regime (training/validation files 10-14), and the fourth
728
      covering the high-frequency regime (training/validation files 15-19). During the benchmark 1 in-
      ference, we selected the input validation data corresponding to the frequency range for which each
729
      model was trained and averaged the results of the four models. For the benchmark 2 inference, we
730
      ran all four models on the science data and selected the highest-performing model, as represented
731   in Figure 5. In future work, we aim to develop a single denoising model generalizable to wide
732   frequency ranges.

791
792
793   The detailed analysis flow to produce the dark matter limit is depicted in Figure 7. The first step in-
794   volves performing a fast Fourier transform on the time series data in 10-second segments to produce
795
      power spectral densities (PSDs). These PSDs are then averaged across the full dataset to generate
      our average PSD, reflecting the power in the pickup loop as a function of frequency. Since one of the
796
      physics parameters, dark matter mass (ma ), , is directly proportional to the frequency, the analysis
797
      script conducts 11.1 million independent searches for dark matter with varying mass points from 0.4
798   neV (100 kHz) to 8 neV (2 MHz).
799
800   At each mass point, the dark matter limit is obtained using a frequentist log-likelihood ratio test
801
      statistic (TS) Foster et al. (2018). Given the local velocity distribution and density of dark matter
      from astrophysical measurements, as well as our choice of dark matter mass, we create a dark matter
802
      signal template for each mass point. These templates are compared to a chunked frequency subset
803
      of the average PSD, constructed using a sliding window whose width scales as δf /f ≈ 5.5 ×
804   10−6 . We use equation 1.1 and calibration data to produce the other physics parameter gaγγ given
805   detector geometry. By floating the template signal amplitude and allowing the mean background
806   level of noise to vary within each sliding window, we fit the signal template to the data to construct
807   a likelihood as a function of gaγγ . We then use the TS to determine the 95% one-sided upper limits
808   on gaγγ for every mass point Salemi & et. al. (2021); Foster et al. (2018). The resulting limits on
809   gaγγ as a function of ma (black line) as well as the 1/2σ containment (green, yellow) can be seen
      on the dark matter sensitivity plot, i.e. ”Brazil band” in Figure 7.

      In Figure 5, the TIDMAD dark matter limits, denoised and raw, are presented alongside ABRA’s
836   previous Run 3 limits and various state-of-the-art axion dark matter experiments and observations.
837   In gold, the theoretically motivated couplings for dark matter candidates are highlighted as discussed
838   in Appendix A. In this appendix, a brief summary of the origins of these limits will be provided.
839
840   Light-shining-through-walls: In this class of experiments, a high-intensity laser is directed to-
841   wards a solid barrier. While conventional light cannot traverse the barrier, the interaction with the
842   wall may cause a fraction of the light to convert into axions. As a result of their weak interaction
843   with matter, these axions could pass through the wall. On the opposite side, detectors are placed to
844   identify any reconverted light, which would indicate the presence of axions. Light-shining-through-
845   walls experiments must both create axions from photons and detect these axions by converting them
846
      back into photons, whereas ABRA only needs to detect axions, not create them.
847
848   Cavity Haloscopes: This class of experiments generate a strong magnetic field to stimulate ax-
849
      ions to convert into microwave photons within a resonant cavity-enclosed space. The resonant cavity
      is finely tuned to amplify specific frequencies of electromagnetic radiation. Sensitive radio receivers
850
      then measure the power within the cavity to identify any potential photon signals indicative of ax-
851
      ions. While the conversion mechanism is identical to ABRA, the resonant cavity only amplifies
852   targeted frequencies, while ABRA’s readout chain has broadband amplification of axion induced
853   signals. ABRA, Cavity Haloscopes, and SHAFT are all examples of Haloscopes – experiments that
854   search for dark matter axions in our galaxy’s dark matter halo.
855
856   SHAFT: The Search for Halo Axions with Ferromagnetic Toroids (SHAFT) experiment is an ax-
857   ion haloscope with a broadband readout, similar to ABRA in both detection and readout mechanism.
858   There are two main differences between SHAFT and ABRA (1) SHAFT uses toroidal magnets with
859   ferromagnetic material in the core to convert the axions (2) SHAFT contains a pairs of stacked
860   ferromagnetic toroids each of which has a separate pickup coil and SQUID readout.
861
862   Astrophysics:      There are numerous astrophysical processes that would be altered if the axion
863   exists. Broadly, this class of exclusions takes astrophysical observations, calculates how these pro-
      cesses would change if axions exits, and sets limits on possible axion couplings. These limits in-


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      clude the following astrophysical processes. Stellar Cooling. Axions produced in hot astrophysical
865   plasma can transport energy out of stars. This transport of energy critically affects stellar lifetimes,
866   thus observations of stellar energy-loss rates can set limits on axion’s couplings to matter. Pho-
867   ton Flux Large photon fluxes from astrophysical objects like Supernova 1987A traverse the galaxy
868   before being detected terrestrially. Within the galactic magnetic field, some of these supernova pho-
869   tons could be converted into axions. By observing the gamma-ray signals from such events, strong
870   bounds on axions couplings to photons can be derived. Black Hole Superradiance. Light particles,
871   like axions, affect the gravitational waves emitted by black holes through the superradiance mecha-
872   nism in which axion fields extract energy and angular momentum from the black hole. Observations
873
      of stellar black hole spin measurements can therefore constrain allowable axion couplings.
874
875
      CAST: The CERN Axion Solar Telescope (CAST) experiment is a prominent axion helioscope.
      In contrast to haloscopes which search for axions created in the early universe within the dark matter
876
      halo surrounding our galaxy, helioscopes search for axions created in our Sun’s heliosphere. CAST
877
      uses a strong, movable superconducting magnet to convert axions produced in the core of our Sun
878   into into X-ray photons when aligned with the Sun. CAST is equipped with highly sensitive X-ray
879   detectors at both ends of the magnet, designed to capture these photons. By tracking the Sun and
880   searching for excess X-rays that correlate with solar axions, CAST aims to detect solar axions.
