009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Flow matching is a simulation-free approach that scalably generates an Ordinary
012            Differential Equation (ODE), in which its path traverses between two different dis-
013            tributions. However, conventional flow matching relies on the training pairs drawn
014            independently, inducing high variance that might slow down training process and
015            degrade the performance upon training. To mitigate this, we propose Augmented
016            Flow Matching (AFM), a simple yet efficient framework that can be ubiquitously
017            applied to flow matching with slight modification to the models. We first find
018
               that when some auxiliary variables are correlated to the training data, then they
               contribute to variance reduction of the flow matching loss estimation, when used
019
               together with the training data pair. With this observation, we construct auxiliary
020
               variables that are correlated to the training pair, which is obtained by simple and
021            effective linear operation from the input data. Finally, we show that with this sim-
022            ple modification on the training phase, we achieve the improved model flexibility
023            and performance when the ODE is applied on the learned model.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep generative modeling is considered as the problem of approximating the probability density and
029   sampling from the distribution. As the deep learning framework emerged, normalizing flows (Papa-
030   makarios et al., 2021; Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Papamakarios et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2019),
031   a likelihood-based model developed from the variational autoencoder (Kingma & Welling, 2014),
032   constructed an invertible mapping between a tractable distribution that is easy to be sampled to a
033
      complex distribution which is generally intractable. In spite of their successes, these models require
      invertible modules to work, which prevents them from catching up with the state-of-the-art perfor-
034
      mances that are achieved with Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) in
035
      generating high-quality examples. The continuous normalizing flow (Chen et al., 2018; Grathwohl
036   et al., 2019) lifted those restrictions on invertible modules using neural ordinary differential equa-
037   tion (NODE), enabling the evaluation of free-form Jacobian on continuous-time ODE, in a memory-
038   efficient way. However, they also have scalability issues, as they use the adjoint sensitivity method
039   which runs a full simulation within the ODE trajectory in the training phase.
040
      As a counterpart, diffusion models (Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015)
041
      first process the forward stochastic processes which move data to noise, then trained the reverse
042   process by learning the score function of the data distribution. And Huang et al. (2021); Kingma et al.
043   (2021); Song et al. (2021) showed that the forward and reverse stochastic process, represented by
044   the Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE), has its equivalent ODE that induces the same evolution of
045   probability density function, according to the Fokker-Planck equation. Inspired by this observation,
046   flow matching Lipman et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023) is a simulation-free method to learn the ODE
047   drift in a similar manner to diffusion models. By doing this, one can not only generate data from
048   noise, but also transfer the source distribution to target distributions.
049   However, training the ODE with flow matching suffers from high variance by randomized pairing
050   between the source and target dataset. Pooladian et al. (2023); Tong et al. (2024); Song et al. (2023)
051   generated the pair between those two distributions by constructing the Optimal Transport (OT) map-
052   ping between source and target data (Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024) or canonicalization
053   via the rotation matrix (Song et al., 2023). However, the optimal transport approach is costly to be
      applied with larger minibatch size, as the complexity of matching algorithm, such as Hungarian al-

071   To resolve this issue, we first observed that the training variance of flow matching can be effec-

073   honored line of works in the variance reduction on Monte Carlo estimation. For instance, Rao-

      sufficient statistic always has reduced variance compared to the original estimator. Inspired by this,
075
      we first clarify that correlating the source and target distribution with augmented random variables
076
      effectively reduces the training variance. Then, we propose an simple and effective method, Aug-
077   mented Flow Matching (AFM), that designs the random variables that augment a small number of
078   dimensions in both source and target. Finally, we show that the flow matching with augmented ran-
079   dom variables outperforms that of the naı̈ve flow matching in a variety of datasets, both qualitatively
080   and quantitatively. This approach can be plugged-in to other existing training methods that enhances
081   efficiency, such as optimal transport or curvature-minimizing approach (Lee et al., 2023).
082
      To summarize, our contributions are the following:
083
084         • Inspired by the statistical perspective, we first propose the variance reduction theorem in flow
085           matching, such that the variance of the flow estimator reduces by introducing some auxiliary
086           random variables as conditions, which allows for more effective training.
087         • Then, we propose a simple and powerful method that entangles the training data pair, by
088           introducing the auxiliary variables. As this auxiliary variable is accessible in the training
089           phase, we can successfully take advantage of the variance reduction which happens in the
090           presence of those auxiliary variables. While a variety of choices are available for choosing
091           the auxiliary variable, we chose the simplest form for universality: the linear multiplication
092           between source and target data. These auxiliary variables are used together with the training
093
              data for training, augmenting the data dimension of the model.
094         • Finally, we verify that by training with these auxiliary variables, we achieve improved perfor-
095           mance in various datasets, both in qualitative and quantitative ways, with sparse modifications
096           on the baseline networks.

      Variance reduction in Machine Learning Methods Our method, AFM, lies on the line of vari-
358
      ance reduction of the statistical estimates. Rao (1992); Blackwell (1947) showed that the variance of
359   the estimator can be reduced by conditioning to sufficient statistics. With this, Doucet et al. (2000)
360   used Rao-blackwellization for efficient particle filtering. Variance reduction techniques are also ap-
361   plied to improving diffusion models. For instance, Xu et al. (2023b); Niedoba et al. (2024) performed
362   multiple Monte Carlo estimates of the conditional score function for single loss evaluation to reduce
363   the training variance.

      6     C ONCLUSION
520
      In this paper, we analyzed some of the limitations of conditional flow matching, which is caused
521
      by the high variance of independent conditional flow matching methods. To mitigate this issue,
522
      we introduced a simple and effective method, Augmented Flow Matching (AFM), which correlates
523   the training pair by concatenating auxiliary variables to them and enables variance reduction in
524   the flow matching problem, making the training process more efficient and widening the class of
525   ODEs to have more flexibility. Finally, with our experiment, we demonstrated the improvement
526   in the sampling performance of the ODE constructed with the augmented variables, especially in
527   low-NFE regimes.
528
529   Limitations. Although our method is able to increase the sampling efficiency of the flow-matching
530   method, the use of the auxiliary variables result in additional parameters for the flow estimator. As
531   shown in the experiments, the effect of the variance reduction saturates as the dimension of the
532
      auxiliary variables grows. Hence it is crucial to balance the gain from the auxiliary variables and the
      cost from the additional parameters.

886   2    """
887    3   Input
888    4     x0: data drawn from source distribution.
       5     x1: data drawn from target distribution.
889

890    7     afm_fn: AFM function.

892    9   Return
893   10     y0: augmented source data. Should be easily sampled.
      11     y1: augmented target data. Function output of y0, x0, x1.
894

896   14   y0 = jax.random.normal(step_rng, shape=x0.shape[:-1] + (aug_dim,))

898   16   y0 = jnp.concatenate([x0, y0], axis=-1)
      17   y1 = jnp.concatenate([x1, y1], axis=-1)
