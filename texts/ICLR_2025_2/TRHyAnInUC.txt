                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011             Causal discovery (CD) involves inferring cause-and-effect relationships as di-
012             rected acyclic graphs (DAGs). In this work, we assume that the data is generated
013             by an additive noise model (ANM). Recent work has formulated the problem as
014             a continuous optimization problem, which consists of solving an inverse problem
015             and satisfying an acyclicity constraint. However, solving the inverse problem in
016             CD is often unstable, i.e. high sensitivity of the effects to perturbations in the
017             causes. To address this instability, we formulate the inverse problem as a regular-
018             ized optimization scheme and propose a novel variation-negotiation regularizer.
019
                Compared to traditional regularization techniques for the continuous optimization
                problem, e.g. ω1 penalty on graphs, the proposed regularizer exploits the variation
020
                variable in ANMs to stabilize the solutions (i.e. DAGs). This regularizer is advan-
021
                tageous as it does not rely on any hypotheses, such as graph sparsity, about true
022             DAGs. The variation-negotiation regularizer regulates the DAG purely based on
023             observed data.
024             Building on the proposed regularizer, a series of improvements to the regularized
025             optimization scheme reveal the connections between solving the regularized op-
026             timization problem and learning a diffusion model, as they share comparable ob-
027             jective functions. This insight leads us to develop an equivalent diffusion model
028             called DAG-invariant Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model. Extensive em-
029             pirical experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate that the proposed
030             diffusion model achieves outstanding performance on all datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
      Identifying cause-and-effect relationships among variables is a challenging problem in various sci-
035
      entific fields such as economics (Hoover, 2017), biology (Sachs et al., 2005), and climate science
036
      (Zhang et al., 2011). Cause-and-effect relations can be represented as directed acyclic graphs
037   (DAGs), where nodes are variables, and directed edges indicate direct causal effects. The objec-
038   tive of causal discovery (CD) is to recover DAGs from observed data. In this work, we assume the
039   observational data follow additive noise models (ANMs), meaning each variable is defined as a func-
040   tion over a subset of the remaining variables, which are represented by a DAG, plus an unexplained
041   variation variable1 (Hoyer et al., 2008).
042
      Traditional methods search the DAG space by testing conditional independence between variables
043   (Spirtes et al., 2001) or by optimizing some goodness of fit measure (Chickering, 2002). A main
044   challenge of these methods is that searching for true DAGs is extremely time-consuming (Chick-
045   ering, 1996). To address it, Zheng et al. (2018) formulates the DAG search as a continuous opti-
046   mization over the space of all graph adjacency matrices. The optimization objective comprises two
047   parts: solving an inverse problem, where, given observational data, an adjacency matrix is solved ac-
048   cording to ANMs, and satisfying an acyclicity constraint on the matrix. However, while promising,
049   continuous optimization-based approaches struggle to combat instability in solving the inverse prob-
050   lem. The instability of an inverse problem refers to the high sensitivity of the effects to perturbations
051
      in the causes (Calvetti & Somersalo, 2018).

053   introduce other noises later. To eliminate the ambiguity, following the naming system (Manzour et al., 2021),
      we use the notion of unexplained variation.

054
      In this paper, we investigate how to trade the unstable inverse problem in CD with a relatively stable
055   one using a regularization technique. We start by formulating the inverse problem as a regularized
056   optimization problem that consists of a data consistency (recovery) term and a regularization term.
057   Then, a novel variation-negotiation regularizer is proposed as the regularization term. Differing
058   from previous regularization techniques for CD that explore the characteristics of DAGs, e.g. ω1
059   penalty on graphs (Zheng et al., 2018; Nazaret et al., 2024), the proposed regularizer alternatively
060   exploits the unexplained variation variable in ANMs. This variation variable can be represented in
061   terms of DAGs according to ANMs, so estimating the variation variable is equivalent to regularizing
062   the solution (i.e., DAGs). We then use denoising techniques (Vincent et al., 2008) to estimate the
063
      value of the variation variable through a negotiation strategy. Regularizing DAGs through the varia-
      tion variable has two main benefits. Firstly, the regularizer does not depend on any general hypothe-
064
      sis about true DAGs, such as the belief that real-life causal graphs are sparse. Instead, the variation-
065
      negotiation regularizer solely regulates DAGs based on observed data. Secondly, it paves the way
066   for the connection between CD and diffusion models. With the proposed variation-negotiation reg-
067   ularizer, the regularized optimization objective can be reinterpreted as a single variation consistency
068   (recovery) term without any regularization term. To probe the variation from diverse observations,
069   we extend the single variation consistency term to multiple variation consistency terms by imposing
070   diversified noise. With this extension, we find that solving the proposed regularized optimization
071   problem and training a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) share comparable
072   objective functions.
073   The discovery motivates us to study diffusion models, such as DDPMs, which have recently emerged
074   as powerful generative models (Cao et al., 2024). They use a sequence of probabilistic distributions
075   to corrupt data in the forward process and learn a sequence of probabilistic models to reverse the
076   forward process (Song et al., 2021). Although DDPMs achieve breakthrough performance in data
077   generation, to our knowledge, only one work has studied applying DDPMs in CD tasks (Sanchez
078   et al., 2023), where a diffusion model is used as a parameterized density estimator to replace a
079   kernel-based estimation model in a CD algorithm (Rolland et al., 2022). Unlike this simple applica-
080
      tion of diffusion models, our work aims to explore the intrinsic relation between CD and diffusion
      models. By posing the notion of DAG-invariance, where true DAGs remain invariant with
081
      any alteration to their corresponding observational data, we propose a diffusion model called
082
      DAG-invariant Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (D 3 P M ), whose training objective
083   is shown to be equivalent to the proposed regularized optimization objective. In other words,
084   D 3 P M s are coined to respond to the duty call from continuous optimization-based CD approaches
085   which suffer from instability.
086
      We conducted a series of empirical studies to evaluate the performance of D 3 P M on 1040 syn-
087
      thetic datasets with up to 5000 variables and real-world datasets. The results demonstrate the
088
      superiority of D 3 P M over all baselines with reasonable training costs. The code is publicly avail-
089   able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/D-3PM-07D1.

144

      3.1     C ONTINUOUS P ROGRAM WITH VARIATION - NEGOTIATION R EGULARIZER
148
149   Continuous optimization-based approaches for CD involve modeling a continuous program (Zheng
150   et al., 2018):
151
152                         A→ , ω → = arg min D(fω (XA), X),               s.t. A is a DAG,                   (6)
                                              A,ω

154   where D is a similarity measure, and fω is a parameterized function used to approximate f in Eq.
155   (1). A high-quality solution to the continuous program is expected to satisfy two conditions: the
156   minimization problem is solved and the DAG-ness constraint is satisfied. The focus of our work
157   is on improving the solution to the minimization problem, which can be formulated as an inverse
158
      problem: given X, A needs to be solved. Unfortunately, inverse problems always suffer from
      instability, where small variations in the space of X can correspond to very large variances in the
159
      matching parameters (Kasim et al., 2019; Calvetti & Somersalo, 2018).
160
161   To address the instability, we aim to reformulate the problem in a way that limits its instability and
      makes it possible to recover reasonably good solutions, a process known as regularization (Calvetti

172   regularizer focuses on exploiting the variation variable Z. The regularization effect of regulating Z
173   on A can be found in the formula Z = X ↔ f (XA), which is derived from Eq. (1). Therefore,
174   Z directly influences the measure of the data consistency term in Eq. (7). Without making general
175
      hypotheses about true DAGs, such as graph sparsity, the variation-negotiation regularizer aims to
      estimate the variation Z accurately. The estimation would consequently have a regularization effect
176

177

182

183

187   employed to predict Z + N. As a result, it holds that ZN := gε (X + N) ↔ N ≃ (Z + N) ↔ N = Z.

277   3.2.1    DAG- INVARIANCE
278
279   We introduce the concept of DAG-invariance for D 3 P M . Let A be the DAG of given tabular
280   data, then A remains invariant during the noising process on tabular data X. This means that all
281
      immediately noised data of X share an identical DAG. For each step t, the immediately generated
      Xt can be expressed as Xt = f (X0 A) + Z + Nt , where Nt represents the t-th noise, and the
282
      DAG A remains constant for every t. An opposite notion which might facilitate the understanding
283
      of DAG-invariance is DAG-variance: Xt is suggested to be represented as Xt = f (X0 At ) + Zt ,
284   where At (we assume DAG At always exists for each Xt ) and Zt are different from A and Z,
285   respectively, at least at one time. The notion of DAG-invariance can be extended to the forward and
286   reverse diffusion processes of DDPMs as follows: Xt = c1 (t)(f (X0 A) + Z) + c2 (t)Nt , where
287   c1 (t) and c2 (t) are certain time-dependent coefficients involved in diffusion process.
288
      The concept of DAG-invariance offers two main benefits. Firstly, it makes the technique of vari-
289
      able substitution X0 = f (X0 A) + Z feasible throughout the diffusion process, allowing A to be
290   explicitly involved in the forward and reverse processes of D 3 P M . Additionally, as A correspond-
291   ing to the given data is shared across all noisy data generated in all timesteps, we can treat A as a
292   trainable matrix (parameters), paving the way for modelling the optimization problem. Lastly, the
293   fundamental mechanism of DDPMs remains unaffected. Specifically, with DAG-invariance, the for-
294   ward transition kernel of D 3 P M , q(Xt |Xt↑1 ), remains consistent with the one in Eq. (2), which
295   follows a Gaussian distribution. It also ensures that the reverse transition kernel q(Xt↑1 |Xt ) is also
296   a Gaussian distribution (Feller, 1949).
297   There might be a concern about DAG-invariance: whether optimizing A would be negatively influ-
298   enced as the imposed noise is extremely large. It should be reassured, since, for D 3 P M , coefficients
299   cdata (t) and cnoise (t) are designed to scale the weights of optimizing A for varying noise magni-
300   tudes, as mentioned for Eq. 10.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
379
380
      The approaches for CD can be categorized into four branches as defined in (Hasan et al., 2023).
381
      The first category is the constraint-based approaches, such as PC (Kalisch & Bühlmann, 2007;
382
      Spirtes et al., 2001), FCI, and CD-NOD (Colombo et al., 2012; Zhang, 2008). These approaches
383   detect causal relationships in observational data through conditional independence tests and then
384   infer whether the data satisfies a DAG. They offer strong interpretability and the ability to incorpo-
385   rate domain knowledge but heavily rely on the quantity and quality of data. The second category
386   is Functional Causal Model (FCM) based approaches, such as ANM (Hoyer et al., 2008), CAM
387   (Bühlmann et al., 2014), PNL (Zhang et al., 2015), IGIC (Janzing et al., 2012), FOM (Cai et al.,
388   2020), SCORE (Rolland et al., 2022), SAM (Kalainathan et al., 2022), and DiffAN (Sanchez et al.,
389   2023). These approaches distinguish among different DAGs in the same equivalence class by im-
390   posing additional assumptions on the data distributions and/or function classes. They exhibit strong
391
      applicability and are adept at handling nonlinear relationships but come with strong assumptions
      imposed by the model.
392
393   The third category is Score-based algorithms, such as GES (Chickering, 2002; Hauser & Bühlmann,
394   2012), fGES (Ramsey et al., 2017), RL-BIC (Zhu et al., 2020), and CORL (Wang et al., 2021). These
395   algorithms search over the space of all possible DAGs to find the graph that best explains the data.
396   They increase the potential for searching for the correct causal graph while preserving sufficient
397
      interpretability but significantly reduce the efficiency of the model due to searching through all
      possible DAG spaces. The fourth category is the continuous optimization-based approaches such
398
      as NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018), which transforms the originally discrete and challenging-to-
399
      optimize DAG search space into a continuous and optimizable constraint space (Chen et al., 2023;
400   Hasan et al., 2023). These methods leverage the powerful learning capabilities of deep learning
401   to learn accurate causal graphs and improve optimization capabilities (Hasan et al., 2023), coupled
402   with reduced computation time when utilizing GPUs.
403
      Concurrent work The proposed method is closest to continuous optimization-based approaches. It
404
      explores the similarity between the proposed regularized continuous program for CD and diffusion
405
      models. Transitioning the resemblance to equivalence, D 3 P M is accordingly designed. A related
406   work is DiffAN, which studies the link between CD and diffusion models. However, the difference
407   between our work and DiffAN is vast. Firstly, DiffAN belongs to the category of FCM-based CD
408   methods, which deviates from continuous optimization-based approaches. Secondly, DiffAN heav-
409   ily relies on SCORE, which takes advantage of the Hessian of the data log-likelihood for topological
410   ordering. For estimating the Hessian, SCORE utilizes a second-order Stein gradient estimator over a
411   radial basis function kernel, while DiffAN replaces the kernel-based estimation with diffusion mod-
412   els. By contrast, our work focuses on revealing the inseparable connection between diffusion models
413   and CD rather than treating diffusion models as a plug-and-play density estimation approach.

514   6     C ONCLUSION
515
516   To address the instability encountered by continuous optimization-based CD approaches, we pro-
517   pose the variation-negotiation regularizer, which eliminates any general hypotheses about true
518
      DAGs. Based on this regularizer, we identify a similarity between the regularized optimization
      problem and the training objective of diffusion models. This leads to the development of a novel
519
      diffusion model, called D 3 P M , whose training objective is equivalent to the regularized optimiza-
520
      tion problem. We demonstrate its superiority over various baselines with different dataset settings.
521
522   In terms of future work, it would be valuable to extend the assumption of data generation beyond
523   ANMs. Additionally, exploring the adaptation of the variation-negotiation regularizer and D 3 P M
524
      to observational time-series data represents a promising research direction.
