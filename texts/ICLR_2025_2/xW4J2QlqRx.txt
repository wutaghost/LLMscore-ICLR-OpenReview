009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series forecasts are often influenced by exogenous contextual features in ad-
012            dition to their corresponding history. For example, in financial settings, it is hard to
013            accurately predict a stock price without considering public sentiments and policy
014            decisions in the form of news articles, tweets, etc. Though this is common knowl-
015            edge, the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) forecasting models fail to incorporate
016            such contextual information, owing to its heterogeneity and multimodal nature. To
017            address this, we introduce ContextFormer, a novel plug-and-play method to sur-
018            gically integrate multimodal contextual information into existing pre-trained fore-
019
               casting models. ContextFormer effectively distills forecast-specific information
               from rich multimodal contexts, including categorical, continuous, time-varying,
020
               and even textual information, to significantly enhance the performance of existing
021
               base forecasters. ContextFormer outperforms SOTA forecasting models by up to
022            30% on a range of real-world datasets spanning energy, traffic, environmental, and
023            financial domains.

      Classical methods. These methods predict future values for time series using statistical techniques.
100
      Established approaches include ARMA (AutoRegressive Moving Average), which captures tempo-
101
      ral dependencies through autoregression and moving averages, and exponential smoothing methods
102   like Holt-Winters and STL (Seasonal-trend decomposition using LOESS), which account for trends
103   and seasonality. The Box-Jenkins methodology is also used for building models to handle non-
104   stationary data (Shumway & Stoffer, 2017; Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, 2018). These techniques
105   have long been the foundation of time series forecasting, providing reliable ways to analyze past
106   trends and make accurate predictions. Prophet (Taylor & Letham, 2017), developed by Facebook,
107   builds upon the traditional techniques by incorporating additional features such as holiday effects
      and non-linear trends, thus providing greater flexibility and accuracy. Prophet is capable of manag-

109   data and outliers. Classical time series forecasters, despite their capability to integrate covariates,
110   often struggle against deep learning models because they lack the adaptability to fully utilize large
111   datasets and automatically learn intricate temporal dependencies.
112
      Deep-learning methods. These methods have been the go-to method to learn the time series fea-
113
      tures perform forecasting, utilizing the recent advancements in neural network architectures, such as
114
      RNNs (Sherstinsky, 2020) and transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017). Notable RNN-based approaches
115   include DeepAR (Salinas et al., 2019) and LSTNet (Lai et al., 2018). SOTA transformer-based
116   approaches include iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024), which applies the attention and feed-forward
117   network on the inverted dimensions and PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), a channel-independent trans-
118   former which takes time series segmented into subseries-level patches as input tokens. Other promi-
119   nent approaches include Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), FEDformer
120   (Zhou et al., 2022) and TimesNet (Wu et al., 2022). Recent works have focused on foundation mod-
121   els, which are deep models pre-trained on large amounts of data, enabling them to learn extensive
122   information and a variety of patterns. They can be fine-tuned or adapted to specific tasks with rela-
123
      tively small amounts of task-specific data, showcasing remarkable flexibility and efficiency. Popular
      foundation models include Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024), Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024), Lag-Llama
124
      (Rasul et al., 2024), and TimesFM (Das et al., 2024). Existing deep learning forecasters fail against
125
      context-aware models because they lack the ability to incorporate external factors and dynamic con-
126   textual information into predictions.
127
128   Forecasting with covariates. One of the earliest approaches to conditional forecasting was pro-
129
      posed by Borovykh et al. (2018), which employed a CNN-based model with dilated convolutions
      to capture extensive historical data for improved forecasting. Recent advancements include models
130
      such as TFT (Lim et al., 2021), NBEATSx (Olivares et al., 2023), TiDE (Das et al., 2023), TSMixer
131
      (Chen et al., 2023), and TimeXer (Wang et al., 2024b), which integrate covariates in various ways.
132   For instance, TiDE uses dense MLPs to encode past time series data and decode it with future covari-
133   ates, while TimeXer employs transformer-based architectures to incorporate metadata. LLM-based
134   models like Ploutos (Tong et al., 2024) embed metadata as part of textual queries. Some pre-trained
135   models, such as TimesFM (Das et al., 2024), have extended fine-tuning capabilities to handle co-
136   variates through exogenous linear models (see Appendix B), though they require access to future
137   covariate values. Among the other time-series models, TimeWeaver (Narasimhan et al., 2024),
138   a diffusion-based framework for conditional synthesis, integrates metadata using attention-based
139   encoders. Acknowledging the results shown by the aforementioned models, we propose a novel

      approach to build such context-aware forecasting models from the existing context-agnostic archi-
      tectures. Our technique enables these models to effectively incorporate contextual information while
141
      preserving and utilizing the time-series features acquired by the base models during pre-training.

      In this section, we formally define the context-aware time series forecasting problem. We are given
146
      a multivariate time series Xhist = x1 , x2 , . . . , xL , with xi ∈ RF . Here, L denotes the history
147
      time series length, while F represents the number of channels. Each sample xi is associated with
148
      contextual metadata ci , comprising both categorical features cicat ∈ NKcat and continuous features
149
      cicont ∈ RKcont . The symbols Kcat and Kcont represent the number of categorical and continuous
150   metadata features, respectively. Together, these features form a vector ci = cicat ⊕ cicont , where
151   ⊕ denotes vector concatenation. Note that ci can include both time-varying and time-invariant

152   metadata features. Now, we can define a metadata sequence as Chist = c1 , c2 , . . . , cL , having the
153   same number of timesteps as Xhist .
154
      To understand this better, let us take the example of the Beijing AQ dataset, which contains the
155
      time series data of six air pollutant concentrations: CO, N O2 , SO2 , O3 , P M 2.5, and P M 10
156   concentration (F = 6), sampled on an hourly basis for four days (L = 96). The metadata here
157   includes information on the location, air pressure, amount of rainfall, temperature, dew point, wind
158   speed, and wind direction for each time series sample. In this dataset, location (12 unique labels)
159   and wind direction (17 unique labels) are categorical values (Kcat = 2), while the other features
160   are continuous (Kcont = 5). All metadata except for location is time-varying. Therefore, given
161   such historical time series data and its
                                              paired metadata, the task is to predict the future data samples

      5.1        M ODEL S TRUCTURE
295
      The ContextFormer architecture, illustrated in Fig. 5, consists of a metadata embedding module, a
296
      temporal embedding module, and multiple cross-attention blocks. These components complement
297   the base model architecture. The working of these components and the required pre-processing steps
298   for time series forecasting are described herein:
299
300
      Base Model. The base model for the ContextFormer can be any forecasting model capable of
      processing input time series and generating a hidden state representation. Neural network-based
301
      forecasters usually have an input layer, some hidden layers, and a final projection layer. The input
302
      layer processes the time series to generate embeddings, which are passed through the hidden layers.
303
      The projection layer maps the final hidden state to the dimensionality of the output X̂future .
304
305   Metadata Embedding. The metadata embedding block processes the paired metadata for a given
306   time series sample xi ∈ RF . As described in Sec. 3, the paired metadata ci comprises both cate-
307   gorical and continuous features denoted by cicat and cicont , respectively. For ease of processing, we
308
      represent the categorical features through one-hot encoding. These categorical and continuous fea-
      tures are initially passed through separate dense encoders tailored to their respective types, producing
309
      individual embeddings. The resulting embeddings are then concatenated and fed into a transformer
310
      network (Vaswani et al., 2017), enabling the model to effectively capture and leverage correlations
311   across categorical and continuous domains while respecting their distinct characteristics.
312
313   Temporal Embedding. Similar to the metadata embedding block, this module generates temporal
314
      embeddings from the timestamps of a given sample. Timestamps are first decomposed into com-
      ponents such as year, month, day, hour, and minute, depending on the dataset’s granularity. These
315
      components are processed through a transformer network to extract temporal embeddings in a way
316
      similar to metadata processing. These embeddings help the model capture long-range correlations
317   and periodic patterns within the time series.
318
319   Cross-attention Layers. The cross-attention layers are transformer blocks that use the hidden state
320
      representations of the historical time series, along with either the temporal or metadata embeddings,
      to extract relevant contextual information for forecasting.
321
322   Further details on the architectural implementation of the base models and the ContextFormer ad-
323   ditions are provided in the Appendix D.2, with the information on design parameters, including all
      the time-series and metadata embedding dimensions given in Table 8 and Table 9, respectively.

      5.2   T RAINING
325
326   The ContextFormer architecture can either be fully trained from scratch along with the base model
327   using paired contextual metadata or can be used to fine-tune a pre-trained base model. One potential
328   fine-tuning strategy involves a plug-and-play approach, where the context-aware model builds on a
329   pre-trained forecaster that has already been trained on historical time-series data. In this approach,
330   the pre-trained base model (except for the final layer) is frozen, and the ContextFormer components
331   are added with their weights initialized to zero. The zero-initialization approach is motivated by the
332
      AR example in Sec. 4.2, where the model with zero-initialized parameters performs identically to
      the context-agnostic model. Subsequently, the temporal and metadata embedding modules, along
333
      with the cross-attention blocks and final projection layers, are trained for a specified number of
334
      epochs.
335
336   Why do we opt for fine-tuning rather than training the context-aware model from scratch?
337   The advantages of using the plug-and-play fine-tuning setup with a pre-trained forecaster, compared
338
      to training a context-aware model from scratch, are as follows:
339   1. The fine-tuned model is guaranteed to perform at least as well as the context-agnostic base model,
340      provided the test distribution matches the training distribution. However, this guarantee does not
341      apply to a context-aware model trained from scratch.
342   2. For datasets with irrelevant metadata, training a context-aware model from scratch can be unsta-
343      ble, potentially hindering the model from effectively learning the time series’ trend and season-
344      ality. In contrast, fine-tuning can utilize a pre-trained model, which has already captured time
345
         series dependencies, allowing it to focus solely on learning the contextual information.
      3. If a time series dataset includes metadata for only some data points, training a context-aware
346
         model from scratch would either require ignoring data points without metadata or augmenting
347
         new metadata, both of which are undesirable. With our fine-tuning approach, the model can be
348      pre-trained on the entire dataset and then fine-tuned using only the data points that have metadata.
349   4. The plug-and-play design of our model allows the creation of multiple context-aware models

      Can the ContextFormer-enhanced models outperform SOTA context-aware forecasters ?
487   Over the recent years, numerous context-aware models have been developed that incorporate co-
488   variates as inputs while claiming superior performance compared to the transformer-based context-
489   agnostic architectures used in our study (Wang et al., 2024b; Das et al., 2024). This claim is sup-
490   ported by our results in Table 2, where both TiDE and TimeXer outperform the context-agnostic
491   models in 75% of these experiments based on the MSE metric. On the other hand, this result gets
492   flipped when we compare these models with the new ContextFormer-enhanced architectures; In 9
493   out of 12 experiments, our best-performing ContextFormer-enhanced model surpasses the perfor-

495
      in building new context-aware models from pre-trained forecasters. Additionally, note that the per-
      formance of our ContextFormer-enhanced models is limited by the constraints of base architectures;
496
      thus, with the availability of more accurate forecasters in the future, this technique could outperform

      any existing models that natively support covariates.

524   7    C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduced ContextFormer, a novel technique for integrating contextual informa-
527
      tion into existing forecasting models. Through comprehensive evaluations on diverse real-world
      datasets, we demonstrated ContextFormer’s ability to effectively handle complex, multimodal meta-
528
      data while consistently outperforming baseline models and even forecasting foundation models. In
529
      addition, we proposed a resource-efficient plug-and-play fine-tuning framework that offers signifi-
530   cant improvements to forecasting accuracy over training context-aware models from scratch.
531
532   In future work, we aim to test our approach on other contextual modalities such as images, videos,
533
      etc. An interesting future work is to analyze the effects of forecasting metadata first; thereby, we
      propose a two-step forecasting pipeline where we first forecast metadata. We hypothesize that the
534
      forecasted metadata can be used to obtain better forecasts. Our key intuition is that metadata is more
535
      human-interpretable, and therefore forecasting metadata could be an easier task to solve.
536
537   Reproducibility. The implementation and hyperparameter details have been provided to help re-
538   produce the results reported in the paper. The source code will be released post publication.

712   maximizes the mutual information.

724   minimizing the MSE is equivalent to maximizing the log-likelihood.

1000   TiDE (Das et al., 2023), introduced by Google Research in 2023, is a straightforward MLP-based
1001   encoder-decoder architecture designed for long-term time series forecasting. It effectively handles
1002   non-linear dependencies and dynamic covariates, presenting itself as a parameter-efficient model.
1003   Unlike transformer-based solutions, TiDE avoids self-attention, recurrent, or convolutional mech-
1004
       anisms, achieving linear computational scaling with respect to context and horizon lengths. The
       model encodes the historical time-series data along with covariates using dense MLPs and decodes
1005
       the series alongside future covariates, also leveraging dense MLPs. To align with our problem for-
1006
       mulation outlined in Section 3, we adapted TiDE by masking future covariates during both training
1007   and inference. The embedding dimension was set to 16, as given in the TimeXer GitHub repository.
1008
1009   TimeXer (Wang et al., 2024b), introduced in 2024, is an innovative transformer-based architecture
1010
       designed for time series forecasting that incorporates exogenous variables through a cross-attention
       mechanism. It utilizes patch-level representations for endogenous variables and variate-level repre-
1011
       sentations for exogenous variables, linked via an endogenous global token. This architecture aims to
1012
       jointly capture intra-endogenous temporal dependencies and exogenous-to-endogenous correlations.
1013   To ensure a fair comparison with our models, we maintained the design parameters as specified in
1014   Table 8. Since neither of the benchmark forecasters includes a separate processing pipeline for
1015   discrete and continuous metadata, we concatenated these metadata types and passed them jointly
1016   through the models for all benchmarking experiments. Training parameters were kept consistent
1017   with those outlined in Appendix D.2.3, while the remaining parameters were adopted directly from
1018   the official TimeXer GitHub repository.

1121   E.4   E FFECT OF T EMPORAL I NFORMATION VS F ULL C ONTEXT
1122
1123
       Throughout this text, we have used the term contextual information to encompass all static or time-
       varying, continuous, or discrete data associated with the time series in question. This includes easily
1124
       accessible temporal information such as timestamps (e.g., month or day), which can significantly
1125
       impact forecasting accuracy, especially for datasets with long-range periodicity. While frameworks
1126   like GLAFF (Wang et al., 2024a) demonstrate the potential to enhance existing forecasters using
1127   learned timestamp encodings, our approach primarily focuses on integrating paired metadata with
1128   temporal information for improved forecasting accuracy. Our architecture uses separate embeddings
1129   and cross-attention layers for temporal and non-temporal data. In Table 13, we present results com-
1130   paring the effects of using only temporal information (commonly available in time series) versus
1131   utilizing the full contextual information, which includes both timestamps and associated metadata.
