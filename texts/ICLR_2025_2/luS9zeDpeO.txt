011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We investigate the decentralized safe multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
014            problem based on homogeneous multi-agent systems, where agents aim to maxi-
015            mize the team-average return and the joint policy’s entropy, while satisfying safety
016            constraints associated to the cumulative team-average cost. A mathematical model
017            referred to as a homogeneous constrained Markov game is formally character-
018
               ized, based on which policy sharing provably preserves the optimality of our safe
               MARL problem. An on-policy decentralized primal-dual actor-critic algorithm
019
               is then proposed, where agents utilize both local gradient updates and consensus
020
               updates to learn local policies, without the requirement for a centralized trainer.
021            Asymptotic convergence is proven using multi-timescale stochastic approxima-
022            tion theory under standard assumptions. Thereafter, a practical off-policy version
023            of the proposed algorithm is developed based on the deep reinforcement learning
024            training architecture. The effectiveness of our practical algorithm is demonstrated
025            through comparisons with solid baselines on three safety-aware multi-robot coor-
026            dination tasks in continuous action spaces.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) involves multiple agents operating within
032   a shared environment, which learn to make sequential decisions that optimize a common objective.
033   Over the past few years, numerous efficient cooperative MARL algorithms have been developed fol-
034   lowing the centralized-training (CT) paradigm (Kuba et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). These algorithms
035   address the non-stationarity issue by training centralized critics, which also maintain scalability in
036   coordination through the use of decentralized policies. Moreover, the policy sharing mechanism is
037   widely used in CT-based algorithms for homogeneous agents, since it empirically enhances learning
038   scalability and efficiency (Gupta et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019) and has been proven to preserve the
039
      optimality of cooperative MARL problems (Chen et al., 2022). Nevertheless, in many real-world
      scenarios, it requires centralized or all-to-all communication to achieve centralized training, which
040
      makes CT-based algorithms impractical when communication resources are limited.
041
042   Decentralized MARL algorithms aim to solve the cooperative MARL problem under mild commu-
043   nication conditions (Zhang et al., 2019). Under the decentralized training setting, there only exists a
044   possibly time-varying and sparse communication network among agents. Each agent learns its pol-
045
      icy based on local experiences and necessary information shared by its neighbors, such as network
      parameters (Zhang et al., 2018) and local state-action pairs (Qu et al., 2022). Early works in this
046
      field mainly focus on designing convergent decentralized algorithms under standard assumptions.
047
      Based on the theoretical results, recent works further showcase the potential of applying decentral-
048   ized algorithms to challenging MARL tasks involving many decision-makers, such as multi-robot
049   coordination (Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024) and traffic control (Du et al., 2022; Ma et al.,
050   2024). Realizing that agents in the real world can be safety-critical systems, researchers have paid
051   increasing attention to the decentralized safe MARL problem, where the learned policies should
052   meet specific safety constraints. Despite that existing approaches have demonstrated promising per-
053   formance on safe MARL tasks with discrete action spaces (Lu et al., 2021; Ying et al., 2023b), it still
      remains a challenge to design efficient decentralized algorithms for continuous safe MARL tasks.

      Contributions. We develop decentralized safe MARL methods for networked homogeneous multi-
055   agent systems. A decentralized safe MARL problem is formulated, which uses the entropy regular-
056   ization mechanism to address sample efficiency issues in high-dimensional spaces. The optimality
057   of our problem under the policy sharing setting is studied, based on which decentralized safe MARL
058   algorithms are proposed. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
059
060   • A subclass of the constrained Markov game (MG) (Gu et al., 2023) is characterized, which extends
061     the model proposed in Chen et al. (2022) under the safe MARL setting. Based on this model, a
062     decentralized safe MARL problem is formulated employing an entropy regularizer in the objective
063
        function design, where policy sharing is proven to preserve optimality and safety.
064   • An on-policy decentralized primal-dual actor-critic algorithm is proposed, where a novel decen-
065     tralized dual variable update step is designed to deal with the centralized constraint. Asymptotic
066     convergence of the proposed algorithm is established based on multi-timescale stochastic approx-
067     imation theory under standard assumptions.
068   • Compared with existing works on decentralized safe MARL (Lu et al., 2021; Ying et al., 2023b),
069     a practical off-policy decentralized algorithm is further proposed based on the deep reinforce-
070     ment learning (DRL) training architecture, which can effectively deal with continuous spaces.
071     Simulation results on safety-aware continuous multi-robot coordination tasks demonstrate the ef-
072     fectiveness of our practical decentralized algorithm.
073
074   Related Work. Decentralized MARL algorithms solve the cooperative MARL problem based on
075   a possibly time-varying and sparse communication network. Based on the use of the communica-
076
      tion network, existing decentralized algorithms can be divided into two categories: communication
      for parameter information and communication for state-action information. In the former category,
077
      decentralized algorithms usually assume the availability of the global state due to the coupled state
078
      transition function, where agents locally exchange parameter information over the communication
079   network to estimate global value functions (Zhang et al., 2018; Suttle et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2024).
080   These algorithms obtain theoretical convergence under practical communication conditions. Note
081   that some practical decentralized MARL algorithms have been proposed by combining theoretical
082   decentralized algorithms with DRL (Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024), achieving comparable per-
083   formance to solid CT-based MARL baselines. In the latter category, decentralized algorithms rely
084   on some spatial correlation decay assumptions of weakly coupled Markov decision processes (Qu
085   et al., 2022; Ying et al., 2023a). In these algorithms, each agent replaces the global state and action
086   with local state-action pairs received from its k-hop neighbors in its critic network during training.
087
      Our algorithm is more pertinent to the former, where we assume that the global state and action
      information is available to each agent. It is worth pointing out that the communication mechanism
088
      employed in the latter can be effectively incorporated into our algorithm under the local observation
089
      setting (see Appendix J.5 for more details).
090
091   Safe MARL has seen a surge of interest in recent years. Cai et al. (2021) combine the well-known
092   CT-based algorithm MADDPG (Lowe et al., 2017) with decentralized control barrier function (CBF)
093
      shields to achieve safe exploration, which requires accurate system model of agents when designing
      CBF conditions. Gu et al. (2023) develop a model-free safe multi-agent policy iteration algorithm
094
      based on the multi-agent trust region learning theory (Kuba et al., 2022), which attains monotonic
095
      improvement in reward and satisfaction of safety constraints at every iteration. Note that central-
096   ized units are required by these algorithms during the training process. Lu et al. (2021) propose
097   the first decentralized safe MARL algorithm for networked multi-agent systems, which employs a
098   primal-dual framework to search for the saddle point associated to reward and cost. However, each
099   agent must maintain and share a copy of the global policy, which may not be preferred in privacy-
100   sensitive applications and could result in scalability issues. Moreover, the learning performance of
101   this algorithm can be severely limited by the vanilla policy gradient method in high-dimensional
102   spaces (Lillicrap et al., 2016). Following the similar decentralized setting as Qu et al. (2022), Ying
103   et al. (2023b) propose a convergent primal-dual actor-critic algorithm for safe MARL, which utilizes
104
      shadow rewards to deal with the general utility setting. Nevertheless, this algorithm inevitably faces
      challenges in estimating local state-action occupancy measures in continuous spaces.

389   6       E XPERIMENTS
390
391   Environments. We build three safety-aware swarm robotic tasks1 to evaluate the proposed algo-
392
      rithm, which are Aggregation, Swapping and Formation. All the tasks are implemented in the classic
      Multi-Agent Particle Environment (Lowe et al., 2017), where all agents follow a discrete second-
393
      order dynamics model and move within a shared 2D space. Each task contains 10 agents, and the
394
      action space for each agent is continuous. Note that the tasks are visualized in Appendix J.1, which
395   are also briefly described as follows. Aggregation: Each agent aims to aggregate at the origin. It
396   receives a higher reward as its position gets closer to the origin, and incurs a penalty if it collides
397   with other agents. There exists a hazardous area in the environment that covers the origin. Each
398   agent i incurs a cost of ci,t+1 = 1 if it enters this area, and ci,t+1 = 0 otherwise. Swapping (Hu
399   et al., 2023): Each agent aims to move to the initial position of the agent located in the diagonal area.
400   It receives a higher reward as it approaches the target position, and incurs a penalty if it collides with
401   other agents. There exists velocity saturation constraints on the agents. Each agent i incurs a cost
402   of ci,t+1 = 1 if the 2-norm of its velocity exceeds a given threshold, and ci,t+1 = 0 otherwise.
403
      Formation (Agarwal et al., 2020): All agents aim to evenly distribute themselves along a circumfer-
      ence centered at their mean position. They share a team reward which increases as the total distance
404
      between their positions and the ideal formation positions decreases. There exists a landmark in the
405
      environment. The team cost function is defined as the distance between the agents’ mean position
406   and the landmark’s position, which is shared by all agents.
407
408   Baselines. In our experiments, two CT-based off-policy MARL algorithms MASAC and MASAC-
409
      Lagrangian (MASAC-Lag) are chosen as baselines. MASAC (Willemsen et al., 2021) solely aims
      to maximize the total reward, without considering safety. MASAC-Lag extends SAC-Lagrangian
410
      (Ray et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2021) under the MARL setting, which also employs the primal-dual
411
      method for learning safe policies. In these CT-based algorithms, only one actor NN is built due to the
412   homogeneity of agents, which is trained using the mean reward and cost directly. In addition, two
413   decentralized MARL baselines named DPDAC and DAC-ER are incorporated. DPDAC is a variant
414   of our algorithm, which doesn’t employ the entropy regularizer, such that α = 0. DAC-ER (Hu et al.,
415   2024) reported state-of-the-art learning performance on multi-robot coordination tasks considering
416   continuous action spaces. Similar to MASAC, this algorithm doesn’t consider constraint satisfac-
417   tion. In Appendix J.2, the differences between DPDAC-ER and the baselines are summarized, and
418   the implementation details of these algorithms are introduced.
419   Results. The learning performance of all algorithms is evaluated over five independent trials across
420   three tasks, where the smoothed learning curves are shown in Fig. 1. Note that the policies learned
421   by DAC-ER and MASAC obtain the largest returns in all the tasks, but they are unsafe for largely
422   violating the constraints. Our proposed algorithm DPDAC-ER demonstrates similar learning per-
423   formance to MASAC-Lag in terms of both reward and cost, which also exhibits excellent learning
424   stability across all the trials. Both the algorithms can converge to safe policies across all the tasks
425   at the cost of relative lower returns. Note that DPDAC-ER outperforms MASAC-Lag in terms of
426
      reward in the Formation task. This result could be explained by that the agents in DPDAC-ER have
      different policies at the early stage of the training process, which helps to sample richer experiences
427
      for policy learning. It is worth pointing out that DPDAC has the worst learning stability among the
428   algorithms, which fails to learn safe policies in the Formation task. This result can be attributed to
429   the poor exploration capability of the vanilla policy gradient method in continuous spaces, which
430   highlights the importance of incorporating the entropy regularization mechanism in our algorithm.

482   7   C ONCLUSION
483
484   In this paper, a decentralized safe MARL problem for networked multi-agent systems has been
485   investigated under the entropy-regularized setting. A subclass of constrained MGs considering ho-
      mogeneous agents has been characterized, where policy sharing provably preserves both optimality


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and safety. An on-policy decentralized primal-dual actor-critic algorithm has been proposed, which
487   is asymptotically convergent under the linear critic assumption. For practical applications, a decen-
488   tralized off-policy version of the proposed algorithm has been developed based on the DRL training
489   architecture. Simulation results on three safety-aware continuous multi-robot tasks demonstrate the
490   effectiveness of the proposed decentralized algorithm. Our future work aims to develop practical
491   decentralized safe MARL algorithms under the local observation setting.

666   positions are denoted by ptj and pok , respectively, where j, k ∈ {1, 2}. Under the full observation
667   setting, the local states at time t are s1,t = (pL,t , pt1 , pt2 , po1 , po2 ) and s2,t = (pR,t , pt1 , pt2 , po1 , po2 ),
668   and it is apparent that S1 = S2 . The action of each agent is the feasible velocity command sent to
669   its robot, such that A1 = A2 due to the homogeneity of robots. The reward for each agent is the
670   negative of the distance between the robot it controls and its corresponding target, such that we have
671   R1 (st , at ) = −kpL,t − pt2 k and R2 (st , at ) = −kpR,t − pt1 k. The cost for each agent is an indicator
672   function, which becomes 1 the robot it controls collides with other landmarks or agents. From Fig.
673   2, we have C1 (st , at ) = 1 and C2 (st , at ) = 0. Each agent’s local observation includes the absolute
674   position of the robot it controls and a list of the relative positions between the robot and landmarks,
675
      sorted by distance. Here, we have o1 (st ) = (pL,t , pL,t − pt1 , pL,t − pt2 , pL,t − po1 , pL,t − po2 ) and
      o2 (st ) = (pR,t , pR,t − pt2 , pR,t − pt1 , pR,t − po2 , pR,t − po1 ). After the permutation M = [2, 1],
676
      we have M (s1,t , s2,t ) = (s2,t , s1,t ) and M (a1,t , a2,t ) = (a2,t , a1,t ) based on the definition of
677
      the permutation M , i.e., agent 1 (2) now controls the robot on the right (left) with the action a2,t
678   (a1,t ). Due to that the robots are homogeneous, the state transition function is permutation invariant.
679   We can obtain that R1 (M st , M at ) = R2 (st , at ) and R2 (M st , M at ) = R1 (st , at ) based on the
680   definition of the reward. Thus, the permutation preserving property holds for the reward. Note that
681   this property also holds for the cost due to that C(M st , M at ) = (0, 1) = M C(st , at ). Finally,
682   we have o1 (M st ) = o2 (st ) and o2 (M st ) = o1 (st ), which means that the permutation preserving
683   property holds for the observation. As a result, this safe position swapping task is an example of the
684   homogeneous constrained MG.

687   A.2     P RACTICAL EXAMPLES
688
689   Apart from the illustrative example, we now show that practical multi-agent coordination tasks can
690   also be examples of the homogeneous constrained MG, which are shown as follows:
691   Leader-following flocking. In this task, each follower agent must maintain specific distances from a
692   moving leader and other agents. The local state of an individual agent is defined as the concatenation
693   of its own system state and the leader’s system state. The agent’s observation includes its own
694   system state and the relative system state information of both the other agents and the leader. An
695   agent receives a higher reward when its distance to the leader approaches a predefined value, while
696   incurring costs if its distances from other agents are either too large or too small.
697   Data collection with UAVs. In this task, a team of homogeneous UAVs operates in an urban en-
698   vironment to collect as much data as possible from several Internet of Things (IoT) devices. Each
699   UAV is rewarded based on the amount of data collected within a given time interval and incurs a
700   cost if it collides with other UAVs or enters no-fly zones. Each UAV’s observation includes its ab-
701   solute position, the relative positions of other UAVs, IoT devices, and no-fly zones, as well as the
      remaining data at each IoT device.

735   C     D ISCUSSIONS ABOUT ASSUMPTIONS
736
737   Assumption 1. This assumption is standard in early works on actor-critic algorithms with function
738   approximation (Bhatnagar et al., 2009; Suttle et al., 2019). Note that the first part of this assumption
739   is reasonable due to that entropy regularization penalizes overly deterministic policies.
740   Assumption 2. The conditions on the weight matrices {Wt }t≥0 in this assumption are widely con-
741   sidered in existing works on decentralized MARL (Zhang et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al.,
742   2024). In condition (i), the row stochasticity of Wt requires each agent to make the weights assigned
743   to the updates coming from its neighbors summing to one. The column stochasticity of Wt is only
744   required to hold on average. This allows us to incorporate various gossip types of communication
745   schemes, such as the broadcast gossip scheme and the pairwise gossip scheme, for the networked
746
      multi-agent system (Bianchi & Jakubowicz, 2013). Condition (ii) is related to the connectivity of
      the communication topology, which holds for the random gossip schemes mentioned above if and
747
      only if the underlying communication graph is connected (Bianchi & Jakubowicz, 2013). Condition
748
      (iii) means that Wt , ri,t+1 and ci,t+1 are independent conditioned on the past. This is common for
749   practical multi-agent systems, since the random communication link failures and the gossip schemes
750   are usually independent of the past and irrelevant to the rewards as well as the costs received by the
751   agents (Zhang et al., 2018).
752
      Assumption 3. This assumption can be easily satisfied as the reward and cost functions are typically
753
      designed manually and can be bounded within limited state and action spaces.
754
755   Assumption 4. This assumption can also be naturally satisfied if we properly select the features for
      the linear critics.

      Assumption 5. This assumption has been used in existing safe reinforcement learning algorithms
757   which enjoy convergence based on multi-timescale stochastic approximation theory (Borkar, 2005;
758   Bhatnagar, 2010). Note that the condition in the last sentence of this assumption will be employed
759   to analyze parameter consensus (Zhang et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024).
760
      Assumption 6. In the first part of this assumption, the stability requirement on the critic parameters
761     z
      ωi,t , ∀z ∈ {r, c} can be relaxed if the lower boundedness of the nonzero elements in Wt can be
762
      ensured (Zhang et al., 2018), and this assumption can also be satisfied empirically through using the
763   clip trick which can constrain the parameters of NNs within certain ranges. The second part of the
764   assumption is commonly used in decentralized MARL works (Zhang & Zavlanos, 2019; Chen et al.,
765   2022; Hu et al., 2024), which can be satisfied in practice when the policy parameter space is large.
