009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Therapeutic peptides show promise in targeting previously undruggable binding
012            sites, with recent advancements in deep generative models enabling full-atom pep-
013            tide co-design for specific protein receptors. However, the critical role of molec-
014            ular surfaces in protein-protein interactions (PPIs) has been underexplored. To
015            bridge this gap, we propose an omni-design peptides generation paradigm, called
016
               SurfFlow, a novel surface-based generative algorithm that enables comprehensive
               co-design of sequence, structure, and surface for peptides. SurfFlow employs
017
               a multi-modality conditional flow matching (CFM) architecture to learn distribu-
018
               tions of surface geometries and biochemical properties, enhancing peptide binding
019            accuracy. Evaluated on the comprehensive PepMerge benchmark, SurfFlow con-
020            sistently outperforms full-atom baselines across all metrics. These results high-
021            light the advantages of considering molecular surfaces in de novo peptide dis-
022            covery and demonstrate the potential of integrating multiple protein modalities
023            for more effective therapeutic peptide discovery. Anonymous codes are available
024            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SurfFlow-880B/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
      Peptides, short-chain proteins composed of roughly 2 to
029
      50 amino acids linked by peptide bonds, play critical roles
030
      in various biological processes, including cell signaling,
031   enzymatic catalysis, and immune responses (Wang et al.,
032   2022). They are essential mediators in pharmacology due
033   to their ability to bind cell surface receptors with high
034   affinity and specificity, inducing intracellular effects with
035   low toxicity, minimal immunogenicity, and ease of de-
036   livery, as well as being readily synthesized and modi-
037   fied. (Muttenthaler et al., 2021). Conventional simulation
038   or searching methods of peptide discovery rely on fre-
039
      quent calculations of physical energy functions, a process
      hindered by the vast design space of peptides (Bhardwaj
040
      et al., 2016). This has spurred a growing demand for com-
041
      putational approaches that facilitate in silico peptide de-
042   sign and analysis.
043
044   Recent progress in diffusion probabilistic (Ho et al., Figure 1: Comparison of full-atom pep-
045
      2020) and flow-based (Lipman et al., 2022) models tide design with and without the surface
      have illuminated tremendous promise in molecular de- constraint.
046
      sign (Guan et al., 2023), antibody engineering (Luo et al.,
047
      2022; Martinkus et al., 2024), de novo protein design (Yim et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) as well
048   as peptide discovery (Ramasubramanian et al., 2024). Peptides, when unbound, often exist in high-
049   energy, high-entropy states with unstable conformations, only becoming functional upon binding to
050   target receptors. Therefore, peptide design must be explicitly conditioned on binding pockets Vanhee
051   et al. (2011). Besides, as residues interact with each other through non-covalent forces formed by
052   side-chain groups, increasing efforts have been made to capture protein-peptide interactions through

                                                          1
054
      Despite advances, growing attention is given to molecular surfaces in protein-protein interactions
055   (PPIs), as these interactions are largely dictated by how complementarily the surfaces of interacting
056   proteins fit together (Kastritis & Bonvin, 2013; Song et al., 2024). The shape, electrostatic potential,
057   and hydrophobicity of molecular surfaces are key determinants of the interaction’s strength and
058   specificity (Jones & Thornton, 1996; Lee et al., 2023; Wu & Li, 2024b). The surface geometry
059   such as protrusions, grooves, and clefts enables lock-and-key or induced-fit mechanisms essential
060   for specific binding. The molecular surfaces act as a fundamental interface that dictates how proteins
061   recognize and bind to each other, making it as essential as side chains in PPIs. For these reasons,
062   it is vital to simultaneously consider all molecular modalities – sequence, structure, and surface –
063
      during peptide generation (see Fig. 1), enhancing the consistency across aspects in what we term
      omni-design.
064
065   Toward this goal, we propose SurfFlow, a surface-based generative algorithm built upon a multi-
066   modality Conditional Flow Matching (CFM) architecture (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-
067   Eijnden, 2022; Albergo et al., 2023). In SurfFlow, CFM is applied not only to internal geometries
068   such as backbone and side-chain angles but also to the molecular surface, represented by surface
069
      point positions and unit norm vectors as a rigid frame in SE(3). Additionally, omni-design requires
      incorporating biochemical property constraints, as complementary surface geometries alone do not
070
      guarantee successful binding-accurate placement of charges, polarity, or hydrophobicity at the bind-
071
      ing interface is also necessary (Gainza et al., 2023). To achieve this, SurfFlow learns the trans-
072   formation from prior distributions to the expected biochemical property distributions. Since surface
073   features such as hydrophobicity are categorical, we apply the Discrete Flow Models (DFMs) (Camp-
074   bell et al., 2024) to discrete data space using Continuous-Time Markov Chains (CTMC). Finally,
075   recognizing that key peptide characteristics like cyclicity and disulfide bonds influence stability and
076   binding affinity(Buckton et al., 2021), we include these factors as additional conditions to enhance
077   the capacity and generalization of SurfFlow. We evaluate SurfFlow on the comprehensive peptide
078   design benchmark PepMerge (Li et al., 2024), and experiments demonstrate that it consistently out-
079   performs full-atom baselines across all metrics, highlighting the advantages of considering surfaces
080
      for de novo peptide design.

      Proteins and Molecular Surfaces. A protein is a biomolecule consisting of multiple amino acid
086   residues, each defined by its type, backbone frame, and side-chain torsion angles (Fisher, 2001).
087   The type of the i-th residue, denoted by ai ∈ {1 . . . 20}, is determined by its side-chain R group.
088   The rigid frame of each residue is constructed from the coordinates of four backbone heavy atoms
089   N, Cα, C, and O, with Cα positioned at the origin. This frame is represented by a position vector
090   xi ∈ R3 and a rotation matrix Oi ∈ SO(3) (Jumper et al., 2021). Unlike the backbone, the side-
091   chain conformation is more flexible, involving up to four rotatable torsion angles between side-chain
092   atoms, denoted by χi ∈ [0, 2π)4 . Additionally, the backbone torsion angle φi ∈ [0, 2π) affects the
093   position of the oxygen atom.
094   We further consider the molecular surface, which is computed by moving a probe of a certain radius
095   (approximately 1 Å) along the protein to calculate the Solvent Accessible Surface (SAS) and Solvent
096   Excluded Surface (SES). The probe’s coordinates define the surface as an oriented point cloud Q =
097   {qi : 1 ≤ i ≤ m}. Each surface point qi has associated attributes (xsi , nsi , τis , Υsi ), where xsi ∈
098   R3 represents its 3D coordinates, and nsi ∈ R3 is the corresponding unit normal vector. τis ∈
099   Rψτ and Υsi ∈ RψΥ capture its continuous and categorical physicochemical properties, such as
100   hydrophobicity, hydrogen bonding, and charge (Gainza et al., 2020; Song et al., 2024; Wu & Li,
101
      2024b).
102   This work focuses on designing peptides based on target proteins. Formally, given a peptide C pep
103   with npep residues and a target protein C rec with nrec residues, we aim to model the conditional
104   joint distribution p (C pep | C rec ). The receptor can be sufficiently and succinctly parameterized as
                                    nrec
105   C rec = {(ai , Oi , xi , χi )}i=1  , where χi [0] = φi and χi ∈ [0, 2π)5 . As for the ligand peptide, the
                                                                           npep                              m
106   surface is also portrayed, resulting in Cpep = {(aj , Oj , xj , χj )}j=1   ∪ {(xsi , nsi , τis , Υsi )}i=1 with
107   m ≫ npep . Practically, software like PyMol (DeLano et al., 2002) or MSMS (Robinson et al., 2014)
      can be utilized to compute the raw molecular surface of a protein.

359   Baselines. Two lines of state-of-the-art protein design approaches are chosen as baselines. The

361   ProteinGen (Lisanza et al., 2023). RFDiffusion produces protein backbones and sequences are later
362   forecast by ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022). ProteinGen improves RFDiffusionby jointly sam-

364   generation, including Diffusion (Luo et al., 2022) and PepFlow (Li et al., 2024).

367   Designed peptides should closely resemble native sequences and structures. We use the amino
368   acid recovery rate (AAR) to quantify sequence identity between generated peptides and ground
369
      truth. Structural similarity is assessed through the root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) of Cα atoms

370

371

373   Affinity is defined as the percentage of generated peptides with higher binding affinities (lower
374   binding energies) than the native peptide, while Stability indicates the proportion of complexes with
375   lower total energy than the native state. Energy calculations are performed using Rosetta (Alford
376   et al., 2017). (3) Design: Designability reflects the consistency between designed sequences and
377   structures. It is measured by the fraction of sequences that can fold into structures similar to their
      corresponding generated forms, with Cα RMSD < 2 Å as the threshold. We use ESMFold (Lin

      4.3   S IDE - CHAIN PACKING
455
456   Side-chain packing is a critical task in protein structure modeling, focusing on the prediction of
457   peptide side-chain angles. Our approach generates 64 distinct side-chain conformations for each
458   peptide using multiple models, employing a partial sampling strategy to efficiently recover the most
459   probable side-chain angles. This method allows us to navigate the conformational space effectively
460   while reducing computational overhead and maintaining high accuracy.
461
462   Baselines. We compare SurfFlow against several established approaches. These include energy-
463   based methods such as RosettaPacker (Leman et al., 2020) and SCWRL4 (Krivov et al., 2009),
464   which rely on physical energy minimization techniques for side-chain positioning. Additionally, we
465
      evaluate against learning-based models including DLPacker (Misiura et al., 2022), AttnPacker (Mc-
      Partlon & Xu, 2023), and DiffPack (Zhang et al., 2024), which leverage various DL strategies like
466
      attention mechanisms and diffusion to predict side-chain configurations based on learned represen-
467
      tations of protein structure.
468
469
      Metrics. Two primary metrics are employed for evaluation. First, we calculate the Mean Absolute
470   Error (MAE) of four key torsion angles: χ1 , χ2 , χ3 , and χ4 . Given the inherent flexibility of side
471   chains and the importance of small deviations in structural biology, we also report the proportion of
472   predictions that fall within a 20◦ deviation from the ground truth. This additional metric captures
473   the practical accuracy of side-chain prediction, emphasizing how well the models perform within
474   biologically relevant error margins.
475
476   Results. Table 3 reports the results, it can be observed that the incorporation of molecular surfaces
477   contributes to more accurate predictions of all four side-chain angles compared to full-atom models
478   and other baselines. SurfFlow attains the highest correct ratio of 63.02%. The enhanced accuracy
479
      suggests that surface information provides crucial context for predicting side-chain configurations,
      likely by better representing the local environment and potential interactions that influence side-
480
      chain positioning.

483   5     R ELATED W ORKS
484
485   Protein Design with Generative DL. Generative models have made significant strides in protein
      design, particularly in applications like engineering enzyme active sites (Yeh et al., 2023). These


                                                                  9
486
      methods generally fall into three categories: sequence design, structure design, and co-design. In
487   sequence design, protein sequences are crafted through techniques such as oracle-guided directed
488   evolution (Jain et al., 2022) or by leveraging protein language models (Madani et al., 2020; Verkuil
489   et al., 2022). Another common strategy, called fix-backbone sequence design, involves generating
490   sequences that fit a predefined backbone structure (Ingraham et al., 2019; Jing et al., 2020; Hsu et al.,
491   2022; Gao et al., 2022b;a; Zheng et al., 2023b). Given the importance of 3D structural information
492   in proteins, some approaches focus on first generating protein backbone structures (Anand & Achim,
493   2022), which are then paired with sequence prediction models like ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al.,
494   2022) to determine the matching sequence. Co-design methods, which generate sequence-structure
495
      pairs simultaneously, are especially useful for antibody design (Jin et al., 2021; Kong et al., 2022;
      Wu & Li, 2024a). Recent studies also emphasize side-chain interactions in conditional protein
496
      generation, enabling full atomic detail (Martinkus et al., 2024; Krishna et al., 2024). However, none
497
      of these methods have addressed the simultaneous generation of protein sequence, structure, and
498   surfaces.
499
500   From a technical standpoint, diffusion and flow-based models have become popular for designing
501
      novel and diverse proteins (Ingraham et al., 2023; Lin & AlQuraishi, 2023). Some studies focus on
      sequence generation alone using discrete diffusion models (Alamdari et al., 2023; Frey et al., 2023;
502
      Gruver et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024). These models are also applied to generate protein structures
503
      in 3D or SE(3) space (Trippe et al., 2022; Anand & Achim, 2022; Bose et al., 2023; Yim et al.,
504   2023; Wu et al., 2024). Among them, RFDiffusion (Watson et al., 2023) has seen notable success,
505   with wet-lab validation of generated proteins. However, these methods often require a separate
506   model for sequence generation. In co-design, earlier efforts include ProteinGenerator (Lisanza et al.,
507   2023), which uses Euclidean diffusion over one-hot encoded amino acids while predicting structure
508   at each step using RosettaFold (Baek et al., 2021). Protpardelle (Chu et al., 2024) applies Euclidean
509   diffusion to structure while iteratively predicting the sequence. Multiflow (Campbell et al., 2024)
510   introduces a DFM model over protein sequences, offering flexible conditioning during inference.
511   Early co-design methods by (Luo et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022) focused on designing CDR loops
512
      in antibodies. Co-design for peptides is also gaining traction, with models like PepFlow (Li et al.,
      2024) and PPIFlow (Lin et al., 2024) excelling in full-atom peptide design using multi-modality
513
      FM. Finally, PepGLAD (Kong et al., 2024) explored peptide structure and sequence diffusion, but
514
      no code has been made available.
515
516
517   Molecular Surface Modeling. The properties of a protein’s molecular surface are crucial in de-
518   termining the nature and strength of its interactions with other molecules. This surface is shaped
519   by van der Waals (vdW) radii (Connolly, 1983) and is often represented as meshes created from
520   signed distance functions. MaSIF (Gainza et al., 2020) was a pioneering effort in applying mesh-
521
      based geometric deep learning to abstract internal protein folds and study protein interactions. Later,
      Sverrisson et al. (2021) simplified the process by representing molecular surfaces as point clouds,
522
      assigning atom types to each point to reduce pre-computation costs. Other key works have inte-
523
      grated protein surface data with structural information in a multimodal approach (Somnath et al.,
524   2021), using advanced pretraining techniques (Wu & Li, 2024b) and implicit neural representations
525   (INRs)(Park et al., 2019) for self-supervised learningLee et al. (2023) and dynamic structure mod-
526   eling Sun et al. (2024). Despite these developments, protein design based on surface characteristics
527   remains relatively unexplored. However, recent progress, such as Gainza et al. (2023)’s extension
528   of MaSIF for de novo binder design, and SurfPro (Song et al., 2024), which eliminates the need for
529   handcrafted feature extraction, has begun to address this gap by generating functional proteins di-
530   rectly from surface data. SurfFlow stands out as the first approach to generate all protein modalities
531   simultaneously using flow-based algorithms.
532
533
534   6   C ONCLUSION
535
536   This work presents SurfFlow, a novel deep generative model that produces all protein modalities –
537   sequence, structure, and surface – concurrently. We apply SurfFlow to solving a specific peptide
538   design challenge and integrate some key characteristics like cyclicity and disulfide bonds into the
539   generation process. Empirical results prove the reasonability and promise of considering molecular
      surfaces for protein discovery. Limitation and future work is elucidated in Appendix E.

821   A    PARAMETERIZATION WITH N ETWORKS
822
823   In order to model the joint distribution of the peptide based on its target protein p (C pep | C rec ),
824   we adopt an encoder-decoder framework to generate peptides. The encoder extracts the geometric
825   and biochemical features of the receptor C rec as the condition of the generation process, while the
826
      decoder regresses the vector fields of our multi-modality flow matching architecture.
827
828
      Encoder. We first utilize a time-independent equivariant geometric encoder to capture the context
      information of the receptor. Specifically, it takes the sequence and structure of the target protein Crec
829
      and computes the hidden residue representations hrec ∈ Rnrec ×ψrec and the residue-pair embeddings
830
      zrec ∈ Rnrec ×ψpair .
831
832
      Decoder. The decoder receives (hrec , zrec ) and is time-dependent. It consists of two geometric
833   networks: one is the 6-layer Invariant Point Attention (IPA) module (Jumper et al., 2021) to regress
834                                                                          npep
      the vector fields of the internal structures {(aj , Oj , xj , χj )}j=1      , and the other is an variant of
835   equivariant graph neural networks (EGNN) (Satorras et al., 2021) to regress the vector fields of
                                                     m
836   the surface geometry {(xsi , nsi , τis , Υsi )}i=1 . To be specifica, we first sample a random timestep
837   t ∼ U(0, 1) to construct the time-dependent vector fields for every modality of the peptide Cpep ,
838   containing sequence, structure, and surface. Both IPA and EGNN take the timestamp t, the interplant
839   state of peptide’s internal structure (at , Ot , xt , χt ) as well as receptor’s information (hrec , zrec ) as
840   input, and the interplant state of peptide’s surface (xst , nst , τts , Υst ) is  also forwarded  into EGNN.
841   Subsequently, IPA recovers the internal structure of original peptide â1 , Ô1 , x̂1 , χ̂1 , while the
842
843
      3-layer EGNN recovers the surface x̂s1 , n̂s1 , τ̂1s , Υ̂s1 . Moreover, two additional losses containing
844   the backbone position loss Lbb (θ) and the torsion angle loss Ltor (θ) (Li et al., 2024) is imposed for
845   extra constraint.
846
847   Equivariance. The joint distribution p (C pep | C rec ) must satisfy the roto-translational equivari-
848   ance to ensure the generalization. That is, for any translation vector ϵ ∈ R3 and for any orthogonal
849
      matrix O ∈ R3×3 , it should satisfy:
850                                         p (OC pep + ϵ | OC rec + ϵ) ,                                      (18)

      where OC pep + ϵ = {(aj , Oj , Oxj + ϵ, χj )}j=1  ∪ {(Oxsi + ϵ, nsi , τis , Υsi )}i=1 .      Following the
852
853
      standard operation called zero-mass-center (Yim et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024), we substract the mass
      center of the receptor from all inputs’ coordinates to achieve the invariance to translation, which
854
      also improves the training stability. Moreover, it can be proven that when the prior distributions
855
      p(a0 ), p(O0 ), p(x0 ), p(χ0 ), p(xs0 ), p(ns0 ), p(τ0s ), and p(Υs0 ) are SE(3)-invariant, while the vector

857   T(3)-invariant, and the vector field v pos (·) is SE(3)-equivariant, then the density p (C pep | C rec )
858   generated by the ODE sampling process is SE(3)-equivariant. Notably, the choice of IPA and EGNN
859   guarantees the equivariance and invariance requirement of those vector fields.

894   B.2    P HYSICHEMICAL S URFACE F EATURES
895
896   Three types of surface biochemical properties are leveraged in the experiments. To be specific,
897   free electrons and potential hydrogen bond donors (FEPH) is a categorical variable, while electro-
898   statics and hydropathy are continuous variables. Therefore, ψτ = 1 and ψΥ = 2. We resort to
899   MaSIF (Gainza et al., 2020)’s scripts to acquire these surface features.
900
901   Free electrons and proton donors. The location of FEPH in the molecular surface was computed
902   using a hydrogen bond potential as a reference. Vertices in the molecular surface whose closest
903   atom is a polar hydrogen, a nitrogen, or an oxygen were considered potential donors or acceptors in
904   hydrogen bonds. Then, a value from a Gaussian distribution was assigned to each vertex depending
905   on the orientation between the heavy atoms. These initial values range from -1 (optimal position for
906   a hydrogen bond acceptor) to +1 (optimal position for a hydrogen bond donor). Then the point is
907   determined as an acceptor or a donor (a binary label) by whether FEPH is negative or positive.
908
909   Hydropathy. Each vertex was assigned a hydropathy scalar value according to the Kyte and
910   Doolittle scale of the amino acid identity of the atom closest to the vertex. These values, in the
911   original scale, ranged between -4.5 (hydrophilic) to +4.5 (most hydrophobic) and were then normal-
912   ized to be between -1 and 1.
913
914   Poisson-Boltzmann continuum electrostatics. PDB2PQR was used to prepare protein files for
915   electrostatic calculations and APBS (v.1.5) was used to compute Poisson-Boltzmann electrostatics
916   for each protein. The corresponding charge at each vertex of the meshed surface was assigned using
917   Multivalue, provided within the APBS suite. Charge values above +30 and below -30 were capped
      at those values and then values were normalized between -1 and 1.
