011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In the field of weather forecasting, traditional models often grapple with dis-
014            cretization errors and time-dependent source discrepancies, which limit their
015            predictive performance. In this paper, we present WeatherODE, a novel one-
016
               stage, physics-driven ordinary differential equation (ODE) model designed to
               enhance weather forecasting accuracy. By leveraging wave equation theory
017
               and integrating a time-dependent source model, WeatherODE effectively ad-
018
               dresses the challenges associated with time-discretization error and dynamic at-
019            mospheric processes. Moreover, we design a CNN-ViT-CNN sandwich struc-
020            ture, facilitating efficient learning dynamics tailored for distinct yet interre-
021            lated tasks with varying optimization biases in advection equation estimation.
022            Through rigorous experiments, WeatherODE demonstrates superior performance
023            in both global and regional weather forecasting tasks, outperforming recent
024            state-of-the-art approaches by significant margins of over 40.0% and 31.8% in
025            root mean square error (RMSE), respectively. The source code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/WeatherODE-5C13/.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Weather forecasting is a cornerstone of modern society, affecting key industries like agriculture,
032
      transportation, and disaster management (Coiffier, 2011). Accurate predictions help mitigate the
      effects of extreme weather and optimize economic operations. Recent advancements in high-
033
      performance computing have significantly boosted the accuracy and speed of numerical weather
034
      forecasting (NWP) (Bauer et al., 2015; Lorenc, 1986; Kimura, 2002).
035
036   The swift advancement of deep learning has opened up a promising avenue for weather forecast-
037   ing (Weyn et al., 2019; Scher & Messori, 2019; Rasp et al., 2020a; Weyn et al., 2021; Bi et al.,
038
      2023; Pathak et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023). However, the existing weather forecasting models based
      on deep learning often fail to fully account for the key physical mechanisms governing small-scale,
039
      complex nonlinear atmospheric phenomena, such as turbulence, convection, and airflow. These dy-
040
      namic processes are crucial to the formation and evolution of weather systems, but most models
041   focus on learning statistical correlations from historical data instead of explicitly extracting or inte-
042   grating these physical dynamics. Furthermore, these models typically rely on fixed time intervals
043   (e.g., every 6 hours) for predictions, limiting their applicability to varying temporal scales. Con-
044   sequently, separate models are often required for different forecast periods (Bi et al., 2023), which
045   constrains flexibility and reduces generalization.
046
      Another line of research utilizes neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018) that incorporate partial differen-
047   tial equations to guide the physical dynamics of weather forecasting. Among these methods, the
048   advection continuity equation stands out as a key equation governing many weather indicators:

115   2     R ELATED W ORKS
116
117   The most advanced weather forecasting techniques predominantly rely on Numerical Weather Pre-
118   diction (NWP) (Lorenc, 1986; Kimura, 2002), which employs a set of equations solved on super-
119
      computers to model and predict the atmosphere. While NWP has achieved promising results, it
      is resource-intensive, requiring significant computational power and domain expertise to define the
120
      appropriate physical equations.
121
122   Deep learning-based weather forecasting adopts a data-driven approach to learning the spatio-
123   temporal relationships between atmospheric variables. These methods can be broadly classified into
124   Graph Neural Networks (GNN) and Transformer-based methods. GNN-based methods (Lam et al.,
125
      2022; Keisler, 2022) treat the Earth as a graph and use graph neural networks to predict weather
      patterns. Transformer-based approaches have shown significant success in weather forecasting due
126
      to their scalability (Chen et al., 2023b;a; Han et al., 2024; Vaswani, 2017). For example, Pangu (Bi
127
      et al., 2023) employs a 3D Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) and an autoregressive model to
128   accelerate inference. Fengwu (Chen et al., 2023a) models atmospheric variables as separate modal-
129   ities and uses a replay buffer for optimization, with Fengwu-GHR (Han et al., 2024) subsequently
130   extending the approach to higher-resolution data. Additionally, ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023) and
131   Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024) introduce a pretraining-finetuning framework, where models are first
132   pretrained on physics-simulated data and then finetuned on real-world data. However, these models
133   frequently neglect the fundamental physical dynamics of the atmosphere and are limited to providing
134   fixed lead time for each prediction.
135   Physics-driven methods, which integrate physical constraints in the form of partial differential
136   equations (PDEs) (Evans, 2022) into neural networks, have gained increasing attention in recent
137   years (Cai et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024b). In weather forecasting, DeepPhysiNet (Li et al., 2024a)
138   incorporates physical laws into the loss function, marking an initial attempt to combine neural net-
139   works with PDEs. ClimODE (Verma et al., 2024) advances further by leveraging the continuity
140   equation to express the weather forecasting process as a full PDE system modeled using neural
141   ODEs (Chen et al., 2018). NeuralGCM (Kochkov et al., 2024) incorporates more physical con-
142
      straints and designs neural networks to function as a dynamic core. However, it is complex and
      difficult to modify, as it operates with over a dozen ODE functions similar to the NWP method.
143
      In contrast, our proposed WeatherODE offers a more straightforward and efficient foundation for
144
      ongoing improvements.

247   3.3   A DVECTION ODE
248
249   In the discretized ODE system in Equation 3, the term u̇(tn ) can be computed from the current
250   values of u(tn ) and v(tn ) using the advection equation. For v̇(tn ), we design an advection model:
251                               v̇(tn ) = fθ (u(tn ), ∇u(tn ), v(tn ), (ϕs , ϕt )),
252
253   where (ϕs , ϕt ) represent the spatial-temporal embeddings and details can be found in Appendix C.2.
254   The design of the advection model fθ is crucial for ensuring the stability of the numerical solution, as
255   it takes the output from the velocity model as input. We argue that fθ should converge more slowly
256   than the CNN-based velocity model, because the initial estimates of v(t0 ) from the velocity model
257   are likely to be inaccurate. If fθ converges too quickly based on early, imprecise values, it could
258   cause the numerical solution to become unstable, potentially leading to failure during optimization.
259
      To address this, fθ is designed with a Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as the
260   primary network, complemented by a linear term. The ViT, with its inherently slower convergence
261   relative to CNNs, provides strong global modeling capabilities, while the linear term contributes to
262   stabilizing the training process by promoting smoother convergence (Linot et al., 2023). A detailed
263   analysis of how different architectural choices impact training stability is available in Section 5.3.

281   3.5   S ANDW ICH S TRUCTURE D ESIGN FOR SOLVING ADVECTION EQUATION
282
283   The hybrid CNN-ViT-CNN architecture optimally combines local feature extraction and global con-
284   text modeling, enabling efficient learning dynamics suited for distinct yet interconnected tasks in the
285   advection equation estimation.
286   The sandwich design of our neural ODE model, comprising a CNN for fast-converging tasks (veloc-
287   ity estimation and source term modeling) and a ViT for slower-converging tasks (advection equation
288   modeling), leverages the strengths of different architectures tailored to specific learning tasks. CNNs
289   excel at local feature extraction and are particularly suited for tasks requiring rapid convergence,
290   such as deriving initial conditions and identifying impacts from source terms with high spatial cor-
291   relation. In contrast, Vision Transformers (ViTs) utilize attention mechanisms that capture global
292   context and relationships, making them better suited for tasks with more complex interactions, such
293   as solving the advection equation, where the dynamics often involve long-range dependencies. From
294
      a theoretical standpoint, the effectiveness of this hybrid architecture can be framed through the lens
      of inductive biases: the CNN’s ability to model locality and translation invariance complements
295
      the ViT’s ability to model global interactions and dependencies, resulting in a more robust solution
296
      strategy for the coupled problem. Moreover, such sandwich design choice is also related to the
297   robustness of training as we discuss in Section 5.3.

530   6      C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we tackle several challenges faced by neural ODE-based weather forecasting mod-
533   els, specifically addressing time-discretization errors, global-local biases across individual tasks in
534   solving the advection equation, and discrepancies in time-dependent sources that compromise pre-
535   dictive accuracy. To address these issues, we present WeatherODE—a novel sandwich neural ODE
536
      model that integrates wave equation theory with a dynamic source model. This approach effectively
      reduces errors and promotes synergy between neural networks and neural ODEs. Our in-depth anal-
537
      ysis of WeatherODE’s architecture and optimization establishes a strong foundation for advancing
538
      hybrid modeling in meteorology. Looking forward, our work opens avenues for further exploration
539   of hybrid models that blend traditional physics-driven and modern machine-learning techniques.

      C.1     DATA F LOW
705
706   We normalized all inputs by computing the mean and standard deviation for each variable at each
707   pressure level (for atmospheric variables) to achieve zero mean and unit variance. After normaliza-
708   tion, the input u(t0 ) ∈ RK×H×W with its spatial derivative ∇u(t0 ) ∈ R2K×H×W are processed by
709   the velocity model fv (·) to estimate the initial velocity v0 ∈ R2K×H×W . Both u(t0 ) and v(t0 ) are
710   then fed into the ODE system, where the u̇(tn ) is calculated by advection equation and the advection
711   model fθ (·) uses u(tn ), ∇u(tn ), v(tn ) and (ϕs , ϕt ) to model v̇(tn ). The ODE system outputs the
712
      predicted future state {u(tn )}N
                                     n=1 , where N represents the lead time.
713   The predicted {u(tn )}N n=1 , along with u(t0 ), and v(t0 ), are then passed into the source model fs (·)
714   to estimate the source term {s(tn )}Nn=1 . The final prediction for the lead time and each intermediate
715   time point is obtained by adding s(tn ) to u(tn ) and then applying the inverse normalization. For
716   training and evaluation, we selected five key variables from the K input variables: z500, t850, t2m,
717   u10, and v10.

735   C.3     O PTIMIZATION
736
      All experiments are conducted with a batch size of 8, running on 4 NVIDIA A800-SXM4-80GB
737
      GPUs for 50 epochs. We use the AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999. The learning
738
      rate is set to 1e-4 for the ODE model components and 5e-4 for the rest. A weight decay of 1e-5 is
739   applied to all parameters except for the positional embeddings. The learning rate follows a linear
740   warmup schedule starting from 1e-8 for the first 10, 000 steps (approximately 1 epoch), transitioning
741   to a cosine-annealing schedule for the remaining 90, 000 steps (approximately 9 epochs), with a
742   minimum value of 1e-8.
