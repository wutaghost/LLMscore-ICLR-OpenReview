009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As machine learning models become increasingly complex, concerns about their
012            robustness and trustworthiness have become more pressing. A critical vulnerabil-
013            ity of these models is data poisoning attacks, where adversaries deliberately alter
014            training data to degrade model performance. One particularly stealthy form of
015            these attacks is subpopulation poisoning, which targets distinct subgroups within
016            a dataset while leaving overall performance largely intact. The ability of these
017            attacks to generalize within subpopulations poses a significant risk in real-world
018
               settings, as they can be exploited to harm marginalized or underrepresented groups
               within the dataset. In this work, we investigate how model complexity influ-
019
               ences susceptibility to subpopulation poisoning attacks. We introduce a theoretical
020
               framework that explains how models with locally dependent learning behavior—a
021            characteristic exhibited by overparameterized models—can misclassify arbitrary
022            subpopulations. To validate our theory, we conduct extensive experiments on
023            large-scale image and text datasets using popular model architectures. Our re-
024            sults show a clear trend: models with more parameters are significantly more
025            vulnerable to subpopulation poisoning. Moreover, we find that attacks on smaller,
026            human-interpretable subgroups often go undetected by these models. These re-
027            sults highlight the need to develop defenses that specifically address subpopula-
028            tion vulnerabilities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Machine Learning (ML) models are increasingly adopted across a wide range of domains and in-
      dustries. This progress can be attributed to the availability of large-scale representative datasets
034
      and the increasing complexity and scale of ML models (Pugliese et al.). As ML transitions from re-
035
      search to real-world applications, it raises significant concerns regarding privacy, accountability, and
036   trustworthiness. Despite recent advancements, these models remain vulnerable to real-world issues,
037   including biases and insufficient safety measures (Hendrycks et al. (2021); Amodei et al. (2016)).
038
039
      In addition to the inherent brittleness of ML models, their adoption in critical applications has made
      them a target of adversarial attacks. One prominent such attack is data poisoning, where adversaries
040
      deliberately manipulate the training data to degrade model performance (Kumar et al. (2020); Biggio
041
      et al. (2012)). Data poisoning attacks can be classified as either untargeted, aiming to impact the
042   model’s overall functionality (Luo et al. (2023); Mallah et al. (2023)), or targeted, where specific
043   malicious patterns are introduced to influence only a particular subset of the data (Geiping et al.
044   (2021); Shafahi et al. (2018); Huang et al. (2020)). The expansive and frequently unverified sources
045   of datasets used in modern ML systems offer a realistic attack surface. Traditional defenses, such as
046   data cleaning, are often inadequate, particularly when adversarial alterations are subtle or or when
047   the data is contributed in a concealed or encrypted way for privacy reasons (Lycklama et al. (2023;
048   2024)).
049   Subpopulation poisoning attacks present a particularly concerning attack vector in ML settings in-
050   volving large, diverse datasets. In these attacks, adversaries manipulate data to degrade perfor-
051   mance on specific subpopulations, while maintaining the model’s overall accuracy on the rest of the
052   dataset (Jagielski et al. (2021)). Unlike conventional targeted attacks, subpopulation attacks do not
053   require access to specific test instances (Shafahi et al. (2018); Geiping et al. (2021)), making them
      an attractive option in real-world scenarios. Moreover, the ability of these attacks to generalize to an


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      entire subpopulation poses a significant risk in real-world settings, as these can be exploited to harm
055   marginalized or underrepresented groups within the dataset.
056
057   Previous studies have shown that ML models often treat subpopulations differently, amplifying the
058
      risk that certain groups may be more susceptible to adversarial attacks. This discrepancy in model
      behavior arises from the nature of the structure of modern datasets, which frequently follow long-
059
      tailed distributions, with underrepresented subpopulations comprising the tail (Feldman (2020)).
060
      Modern overparameterized deep learning models, with their high capacity, are inherently capable
061   of memorizing rare data samples from the tail of these distributions (Zhang et al. (2021)). The
062   requirement for ML algorithms to memorize in order to perform well on common deep learning
063   tasks has largely been studied in the context of privacy and fairness (Hooker et al. (2020b;a); Carlini
064   et al. (2019b)). However, it may also have significant implications for robustness. As model capac-
065   ity increases, so does the tendency to memorize subpopulations, which might lead to inconsistent
066   handling and greater vulnerability to exploitation. In this work, we shed light on how variations in
067   model capacity – and by extension, model complexity – affect the susceptibility of subpopulations
068   to poisoning attacks.
069   Contributions. As we continue to pursue better performance, the shift toward more complex
070   models becomes inevitable. It is, therefore, crucial to understand the trade-offs that come with
071   this added complexity. In this work, we examine the relationship between model capacity and
072   subpopulation poisoning attacks to better understand how robustness is affected as models become
073   more complex. Our goal is to identify which subpopulations are most vulnerable in this context,
074   paving the way for the development of future targeted defenses.
075   Specifically, we make the following contributions:
076
077         1. We highlight the vulnerability of locally-dependent mixture learners to subpopulation poi-
078            soning attacks in a theoretical framework, building on existing work regarding the memo-
079            rization of long-tailed data distributions. We show why defending against these attacks can
080            be particularly challenging.
081         2. We demonstrate that complex models experience greater shifts in their decision boundaries
082            when subjected to subpopulation poisoning attacks.
083
            3. We empirically investigate the vulnerability of realistic, overparameterized models to sub-
084            population poisoning attacks across real-world image and text datasets. Through 1626 in-
085            dividual poisoning experiments across different subpopulations, datasets, and models, we
086            demonstrate that larger, more complex models are significantly more prone to such attacks.

089   2   R ELATED W ORK
090
091   In this section, we first provide an overview of related research into the analysis of subpopulations
092   in ML. We then review existing work on subpopulation poisoning attacks. Finally, we discuss recent
093
      work investigating the connection between model capacity and poisoning attacks.
094   Subpopulation Analysis. The treatment of subpopulations in machine learning has been ex-
095   plored across various domains, including fairness (Ganesh et al. (2023)), privacy (Bagdasaryan &
096   Shmatikov (2019)), learning dynamics (Mangalam & Prabhu (2019)), and adversarial robustness,
097   particularly in test-time adversarial attacks (Raina & Gales (2023)). For instance, Carlini et al.
098   (2019a) characterize data distributions in terms of prototypical versus rare samples across five scor-
099
      ing metrics related to adversarial robustness, privacy and difficulty of learning. Their analysis shows
      a strong correlation between these metrics for the majority of training data, suggesting the presence
100
      of a broader concept of “well-representedness” that encompasses various dimensions of data char-
101
      acterization. Several works have specifically examined how model capacity affects the treatment
102   of long-tail samples. Hooker et al. (2020a) demonstrate that model pruning techniques dispropor-
103   tionately impact outlier data points while preserving overall model accuracy. Their results indi-
104   cate that the degree of disproportionate impact increases with more aggressive pruning. Similarly,
105   Hooker et al. (2020b) and Hooker et al. (2020a) confirm that quantized models exhibit a similar pat-
106   tern of disparate treatment toward rare samples. While these studies primarily focus on individual
107   sample-level characterizations, they strongly suggest that model capacity plays a critical role in the
      classification performance on long-tail distributions.

108
      Subpopulation Poisoning. Jagielski et al. (2021) first formalized the notion of a “subpopulation
109   data poisoning attack”, and proposed two ways to define subpopulations, one based on data annota-
110   tions and another using clustering techniques. They show that different subpopulations experience
111   varying levels of accuracy degradation when subjected to poisoning. However, the work did not ex-
112   plore potential causes for this disparity among subpopulations or investigate the influence of model
113   characteristics, such as size or complexity. Building on this foundation, Rose et al. (2023) analyze
114   subpopulation susceptibility by visualizing decision boundaries, with a particular focus on the Adult
115   dataset. Notably, they found no correlation between subpopulation size and susceptibility to attack,
116   highlighting the difficulty in generalizing semantically meaningful properties that could predict sub-
117
      group vulnerability. While this study provides a deeper understanding, it is limited to linear SVM
      models and does not extend to more complex architectures or non-convex learning objectives. As
118
      a result, the potential role of overparameterization in subpopulation poisoning attacks, which could
119
      be significant, remains unexplored.
120
121   Model Capacity and Poisoning. More recently, several works have investigated the impact of
122   model capacity on backdoor poisoning attacks, which involve hidden model behaviors triggered by
123
      specific inputs, such as an (artificial) image pattern or text phrase. For instance, Wan et al. (2023)
      explored the feasibility of poisoning foundational language models during instruction tuning. In
124
      their analysis, they found that larger models tend to be more susceptible to such poisoning attempts.
125
      Bowen et al. (2024) conducted a more detailed study in the direction of model capacity, examining
126   the vulnerability of various model architectures with varying capacities across three distinct poison-
127   ing scenarios. Their results showed a general trend: larger models are more vulnerable to backdoor
128   poisoning across all settings, with the notable exception of the Gemma-2 family of models. While
129   these studies offer valuable insights into the relationship between model size and vulnerability to
130   backdoor attacks, they focus specifically on single-trigger backdoors. These findings do not neces-
131   sarily extend to subpopulation poisoning, which targets entire subgroups of data rather than specific
132   triggers. Understanding how subpopulations, which may encompass broader and more diverse seg-
133   ments of data, interact with model architecture remains a critical and largely unexplored area.

      3     BACKGROUND
136
137
      We first outline relevant theory to model long-tailed data distributions and then discuss background
138
      on data poisoning attacks.
139
140   Preliminaries. We consider binary classification. Let X be the feature space and Y = {0, 1}
141   be the label space. The goal is to learn a hypothesis f : X → Y that minimizes the error on some
142
      (unknown) distribution D over X × Y. The model trainer samples an ordered n-tuple of i.i.d.
      samples D = ((x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )) from D. Given a dataset D and a hypothesis space H, a
143
      learning algorithm A produces a hypothesis h ← A(D). Typically, the learner A chooses h via
144
      empirical loss minimization under a cross-entropy loss.
145
146   For a distribution D over a domain Z, we use z ∼ D to denote sampling z from D. For a function
147   F with domain X , we denote by Dx∼D [F (x)] the probability distribution of F (x), when x ∼ D.
148
149   3.1   L ONG -TAILED D ISTRIBUTIONS
150
151   Informally, “long-tailed” distributions are characterized by a large number of rare, atypical obser-
152   vations forming the “long tail”. For instance, image datasets might contain objects captured from
153   unusual angles, and word frequencies in natural language often follow Zipf’s law. Learning from
154
      such distributions poses challenges for fairness and privacy, because models might produce biased or
      inaccurate predictions on underrepresented groups, and rare events can be used to uniquely identify
155
      individuals.
156
157   Feldman (2020) formalizes this idea by modeling the data distribution as a mixture of distinct sub-
158   populations D1 , . . . , Dk . The marginal distribution D(x) is composed of a weighted sum of compo-
                          Pk
159   nent distributions i=1 γi Di (x) over the subpopulations. The mixture weights {γi }ki=1 determine
160   the frequency of each subpopulation in the dataset and define the long-tail explicitly. Jagielski et al.
161   (2021) provide a definition of such a mixture distribution based on a simplification of the model
      presented by Feldman.

172   Building on this theoretical insight, Feldman & Zhang (2020) empirically observe this memorization
173   behavior in real-world datasets. By identifying closely related pairs of individual training and test
174   samples, the work highlights how models learn specific behavior of these rare instances to accurately
175   predict rare subpopulations.
176
177   3.2   DATA P OISONING
178
179   In a data poisoning attack, the adversary modifies the training dataset with the goal of influencing
180   the model’s behavior. Poisoning attacks can be categorized based on their objectives and the impact
181
      on the compromised model. Availability attacks aim to reduce the overall accuracy of the model
      across the data distribution (Biggio et al. (2012); Jagielski et al. (2018); Xiao et al. (2015); Koh
182
      et al. (2022)). In contrast, targeted attacks seek to misclassify a specific set of points, Dtarget , while
183
      maintaining high accuracy on the rest of the data distribution to avoid detection (Geiping et al.
184   (2021), Huang et al. (2020), Koh & Liang (2017), Shafahi et al. (2018)). A prominent example of
185   targeted attacks is backdoor attacks (Gu et al. (2019)), which induce misclassification by embedding
186   an attacker-chosen trigger into test data points.
187
      Subpopulation poisoning attacks interpolate between availability and targeted attacks (Jagielski et al.
188
      (2021)). The adversary seeks to manipulate the model’s performance over an entire subpopulation,
189
      rather than specific, known instances. Unlike targeted attacks, where the adversary knows the pre-
190   cise set of target samples, Dtarget , subpopulation poisoning attacks are broader in scope, where the
191   adversary targets a distribution, Dp , instead of isolated points. The adversary’s goal is to minimize
192   the model’s accuracy on the subpopulation without affecting predictions on points outside the sub-
193   population. In this work, we focus on adversaries restricted to modifying (i.e., “flipping”) the labels
194   of a subset of points within the training dataset.

204   Jagielski et al propose two methods for defining the filter function F that identifies the target sub-
205   population. The first method clusters the data based on the samples’ latent space representations,
206   leveraging the model’s internal structure to define the subpopulation. This approach has been shown
207   to achieve higher attack effectiveness, as it exploits the model’s learned feature space to define
208   closeness of samples. The second method is based on predefined semantic annotations associated
209   with the samples in the dataset. Although more challenging to poison, semantic subpopulations are
210
      better aligned with real-world attacker objectives, which are based on meaningful domain-specific
      properties of the data.

215   We hypothesize that increasing model complexity exacerbates vulnerability to subpopulation poi-
      soning attacks. Specifically, as models grow larger, their ability to memorize increases, resulting

221   4.1   P OISONING M IXTURE L EARNERS
222
223   Our goal in this section is to highlight how models with local dependence on subpopulations become
224   increasingly susceptible to such attacks as their complexity grows. ML models exhibit varying
225   degrees of local dependence on the subpopulations in their training data. That is, their predictions
226   for any point x are closely tied to how they generalize across the subpopulation Dx of x. We capture
227
      this dependence in a mixture learner that learns a classifier f based on a dataset D sampled from
      a mixture distribution. Let D be a noisy k-subpopulation mixture distribution D over X × Y as
228
      in Definition 1 consisting of k subpopulations D1 , . . . , Dk . A mixture learner is an algorithm A
229
      that takes as input a dataset D sampled from D and returns a classifier f : X → {0, 1}. We
230   assume that for every point x in a dataset D, the distribution over predictions f (x) for a random
231   predictor output by A(D) is close to the distribution over predictions that A produces over the
232   entire subpopulation to which x belongs. This assumption of local dependence – where the learner
233   makes similar predictions for all points in a subpopulation – forms the foundation for understanding
234   why such models are susceptible to poisoning attacks.

249   Completely locally dependent learners produce classifiers that are highly vulnerable to

260   The proof of Theorem 1 is based on the intuition that for δ = 0, the learner that minimizes the
261   empirical loss must choose the most common label for each subpopulation. As a result, when

285   Implications for Overparameterized Models. Previous work has demonstrated that local depen-
286   dence is present in a variety of learning algorithms, including overparameterized linear models, k-

290   complex, realistic datasets (Feldman & Zhang (2020)). This memorization effect becomes more
291   pronounced as model size increases, particularly for uncommon or atypical samples in the training
292   set (Carlini et al. (2019b)). These findings suggest that larger models are more prone to local de-
293
      pendence on subpopulations, which in turn suggests that these are more vulnerable to subpopulation

      4.2        M ETHODOLOGY
297   In this section, we describe the experimental framework used to measure the susceptibility of models
298   to subpopulation poisoning attacks, detailing the subpopulations, the assumptions on the adversary,
299   and the attack strategy. Finally, we present a toy experiment with a synthetic dataset to demonstrate
300   the core ideas of the theoretical model and the methodology.
301
      Subpopulations. We define subpopulations in our experiments using manual annotations that
302
      capture semantic information about the dataset samples, such as demographic attributes or visual
303   characteristics in image data. These annotations, already present in the datasets we employ, repre-
304   sent real-world subpopulations and are commonly used in other benchmarks, such as in the context
305   of fairness. This approach allows us to reflect realistic scenarios in which an adversary targets se-
306   mantically meaningful groups based on these attributes.
307
      We implement this concretely by defining each subpopulation through specific combinations of
308
      annotated features. Let A = {a1 , a2 , . . . , ak } be the ordered tuple of binary annotation features of
309   the dataset, and c1 , . . . , cn denote an ordered n-tuple of length-k binary vectors, one corresponding
310   to each sample in the dataset D = {xi , yi }ni=1 . For each ci , the j-th entry in the binary vector
311   corresponds to the presence or absence of annotation feature aj . We select a subset of m annotation
312   features A′ ⊆ A and define the subpopulations as the cartesian product of values of A′ . Formally,
313   if A′ consists of m features, then for each possible binary combination vk ∈ {0, 1}m , we define the
314   corresponding subpopulation Dk as

356
357
      We conduct experiments on three datasets: Adult, CivilComments, and CelebA. For each dataset,
      we use models of varying complexity and apply poisoning attacks to subpopulations defined by the
358
      datasets’ annotations. We experiment with multiple poisoning ratios α ∈ {0, 1, 2} for Adult and
359
      CivilComments, and α ∈ {0, 1, 2, 4} for CelebA.

      5.1                            S UBPOPULATION A NALYSIS
414
415   We now focus on the results for individual subpopulations in CelebA and CivilComments. As
416   opposed to the models for the Adult dataset, the models used in these datasets have a high number
417   of parameters relative to the dataset size, making them prone to overfitting on the training data. As
418   discussed in Section 4.1, this likely has implications for their subpopulation-local behavior.
419
      We explore the difference in accuracy for the clean and poisoned model for subgroups of different
420   sizes. Overall, we observe a positive correlation between subgroup size and poisonability in Fig-
421   ures 4a and 5a. For example in CivilComments, the subgroup with size 314 has an average target
422   damage of 48% for poisoning with α = 2.0, whereas the subgroup with size 5541 has 79% tar-
423   get damage. In more detail, the results reveal a hinge-like relationship between subgroup size and
424   target damage that is consistent across datasets. Thus, we group results across three categories of
425   subgroups: small, mid-sized, and large, and discuss conclusions for each in more detail.
426   Small Subgroups are Difficult to Poison. There appears to be a threshold for subgroup size
427   below which the poisoning attack does not achieve significant target damage. This behavior holds
428   across all models and poisoning rates. Figures 4b and 5b highlight that the smallest subgroups
429   in CivilComments and CelebA exhibit insignificant target damage (i.e., 0.04 and 0.09 on average
430   respectively) even at higher poisoning rates. As a result, it appears that subpopulation poisoning
431   attacks on small subgroups defined by semantic annotations is infeasible, as the model does not
      generalize the behavior across the entire subpopulation.

466

       In CivilComments at α = 2.0, the difference in target damage across models is only 2%, while in

470    process. Consequently, even smaller models experience substantial shifts in their decision bound-
471    aries, despite them being more rigid, reducing the impact of model size on attack susceptibility.
472

473
       additionally reveal a notable range of medium-sized subgroups where model complexity signifi-
474    cantly impacts target damage. In CivilComments, for example, BERT Tiny exhibits only 2% target

477    subpopulations within this size range in CivilComments. For CelebA (cf. 5a), although this ordering

480    investigation with a broader set of subpopulations may shed further light on this conclusion.
481    Finally, across both datasets, we observe a substantial variation in subgroup susceptibility to poi-
482    soning across models, independent of subgroup size. For example, the subgroup with size 3111
483    (subgroup “toxic”, “black”) in CivilComments exhibits at most 22% difference in target damage
484    between models, which is much smaller than for subgroup 314 (80%). This suggests that some
485    subpopulations are more prone to being memorized by models with higher capacity than others, and

       6          C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
519
520
       This work sheds light on the intricate relationship between model complexity and the vulnerability of
521
       machine learning systems to subpopulation poisoning attacks. Our analysis highlights that as models
522    grow in complexity, their susceptibility to such attacks increases, particularly in cases involving
523    underrepresented subpopulations. This vulnerability is exacerbated by the long-tailed nature of
524    modern datasets, where subpopulations may be more exposed to adversarial manipulations.
525
       Future Directions. Our findings underscore the importance of considering subpopulation-specific
526
       risks in the design of defenses against poisoning attacks. The effect of learning subpopulations-
527
       specific behavior has largely been studied in the context of privacy and fairness. However, as we
528    show in this work, it also has significant implications for robustness. Consequently, techniques
529    to improve robustness inevitably result in tradeoffs with respect to accuracy, privacy, fairness, and
530    robustness, and future defenses must carefully balance these objectives to achieve meaningful im-
531    provements. Moreover, our results show that attack success depends heavily on the choice of target
532    subpopulation, highlighting the necessity of evaluating defenses across diverse subpopulations in
533    benchmarking datasets. Finally, identifying vulnerable subpopulations can guide the development
534    of robust training pipelines using techniques from fairness and privacy research, such as balanced
535    sampling or re-weighting Navarro et al. (2024); Richards et al. (2023).
536    Our work surfaces a characteristic of long tail subgroups; future work could measure the agreement
537    of this characteristic with similar metrics in the domains of fairness or privacy, such as group or
538    sample level memorization (Carlini et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2020)). This could help determine
539    whether the observed behavior is a general property of these subpopulations or specific to poisoning,
       offering insights into model behavior on long-tail distributions.

729
730   We provide a detailed description of the models, datasets and subpopulations used in our experi-
731   ments in this appendix.
732   Gaussian Dataset. We generate a synthetic 2D dataset using Gaussian distributions. The dataset
733   has two classes, each composed of multiple subgroups (clusters). The distance between the class
734   centers is given by the class separation parameter and 25 subgroup centers are then scattered around
735   the class centers using a specified standard deviation. For each subgroup, a random number of points
736   is generated from a normal distribution around its center, and the points are assigned the correspond-
737   ing class label. Finally, a fraction of labels is randomly flipped to introduce noise, simulating label
738   errors in the dataset.
739   Adult. UCI Adult is a tabular dataset with 32561 training samples, with the task of predicting
740   whether a person’s income is above $50K a year using the other demographic attributes. Here we
741   form the subgroups using the features Ethnicity, Gender and Education. We filter out subgroups
742   smaller than 100 training points, yielding 63 subgroups.
743
      CelebA. The CelebFaces Attributes Dataset (Liu et al. (2015)) is an image dataset of human faces
744   accompanied by 40 attribute annotations per image. The training set has 162,770 samples. We
745   use the attribute ’Male/Female’ as the task. We train the ResNet model family on this dataset,
746   specifically ResNet18, ResNet50 and ResNet101, with 11 million, 26 million and 45 million train-
747   able parameters respectively (He et al. (2016)). We select the attributes “Dark Hair”, “Young”,
748   “Pale Skin”, “Beard”, “Chubby” to divide the data into subpopulations, and then filter such that the
749   subgroups have at least 100 training samples and at least 10 test samples, resulting in 9 subgroups.
750   CivilComments. The CivilComments Dataset (Borkan et al. (2019)) is a text dataset where each
751   entry has 19 binary annotations, such as toxic, male, Christian, etc. The task is to identify whether a
752   given comment is toxic, while the other annotations are used to form the subgrouping together with
753   toxicity (e.g., nontoxic male). This yields 36 subgroups in total, though four of them are too small
754   to consider (see Table 2). In total, there are 269037 training samples and 133781 test samples. We
755   use pre-trained versions of BERT: bert-tiny, bert-small, and bert-medium (Turc et al. (2019)) as well
      as DistilBERT (Sanh et al. (2019)) and added and trained a classification layer.
