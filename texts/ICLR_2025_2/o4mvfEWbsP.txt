009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Band selection is crucial in spectral imaging, as it involves choosing the most
012            relevant bands from large hyperspectral datasets to retain essential information
013            while reducing the burden of data transmission and analysis. Addressing this need,
014            we introduce a novel method for band selection that utilizes an Expectation Maxi-
015            mization algorithm to facilitate selection through the sparsification of spectral band
016            importance. Our method enhances sparsity effects and effectively delineates the
017            relationships between spectral bands during the sparsification process. Supported
018
               by thorough theoretical analysis and experimental validation on public datasets,
               our approach has proven to be both robust and practical. Compared to other
019
               sparsification methods, it not only excels in achieving significant sparsity effects
020
               but also demonstrates marked advantages in illustrating inter-band relationships.
021            Our method delivers outstanding performance in band selection tasks and holds
022            potential for broader applications in other sparsity-oriented contexts in the future.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Hyperspectral imaging technologies, extensively employed in areas like remote sensing and agricul-
028   ture, provide detailed spectral information but encounter challenges such as high operational costs
029   and complex data processing Mahdianpari et al. (2018); Toker et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022); Xiao
030   & Wei (2023). A fundamental aspect in this domain is the efficient selection of the most informative
031   spectral bands from the extensive data available. This selection is not only crucial for enhancing
032   classification performance, as demonstrated by the Hughes phenomenon, but also for reducing data
033
      transmission stress, thereby making the technology more adaptable for various applications.
034   Recent advancements in band selection, particularly those utilizing deep learning techniques Zhou
035   et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2021), mark a significant shift away from traditional methods Tang et al.
036   (2021); Huang & He (2005); Zhang et al. (2007); Fauvel et al. (2015). These modern approaches,
037   which include reconstruction-based Cai et al. (2019) and classification-based methods Feng et al.
038   (2020); Jia et al. (2023), are primarily focused on learning the importance of spectral bands. The
039
      central challenge lies in designing mechanisms that effectively represent band importance in alignment
      with these specific tasks. The importance extends beyond the contribution of individual bands,
040
      encompassing the significance of various band combinations. For instance, NDVI and NDWI are
041
      commonly used empirical formulas in spectroscopy that assess material properties based on the
042   interactions among different spectral bands.
043
044   However, it has been noted that many existing methods fall short in effectively capturing the im-
045
      portance of relationships between spectral bands. The two most prevalent techniques, clustering
      and ranking, both necessitate post-processing, namely the selection of bands based on their relative
046
      importance. These approaches are problematic because the assigned importance is not always precise
047
      and overlooks the interplay between bands, potentially excluding crucial band combinations. To
048   overcome these limitations, we have adopted a method based on the sparsity of band importance,
049   leveraging the training process of the network to inherently select bands. This method aims to
050   enable the network to independently discover the most representative bands, thereby circumventing
051   the conventional need for post-processing. However, this approach introduces new challenges: 1)
052   Existing methods of imposing sparsity, such as L1 and L2 losses, do not consistently yield stable
053   sparsity effects; 2) Current sparsity techniques also fail to depict the inter-band relationships during
      the sparsification process.

054
      To address these challenges, we have developed innovative solutions within our framework. We
055   introduce a Sparsity Loss based on the Expectation Maximization (EM) algorithm Dempster et al.
056   (1977), meticulously designed to enhance sparsity in the levels of importance. This method not only
057   improves sparsity effects but also theoretically facilitates the exploration of relationships between
058   spectral bands, thereby enabling more stable and reliable band selection. To our knowledge, our
059   approach is the first to implement a sparsity representation method based on the EM algorithm. We
060   have conducted comprehensive theoretical analysis and experimental validation to confirm the sparsity
061   effects and the capability of our proposed method to accurately depict inter-band relationships. Our
062   approach has undergone rigorous analysis and has been thoroughly validated.
063   In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 1) We have developed a band selection method
064   for deep learning that utilizes sparse importance representation, suitable for both supervised and
065   unsupervised tasks. The integration of Sparsity Loss effectively alleviates the challenge of depicting
066   the importance among spectral bands. 2) Our methodology introduces an innovative and unique
067   sparsification technique based on the Expectation-Maximization algorithm, marking a significant
068   advancement in the field of sparse representation. 3) This paper supports our proposed method with
069
      comprehensive theoretical proofs and analyses, alongside extensive experimental evidence to validate
      the efficacy and benefits of our approach. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance in band
070
      selection methods and, in significance tests, significantly outperforms other sparsity methods.

073   2     R ELATED W ORK
074
075   The classification of traditional band selection methods for hyperspectral images, as outlined by Sun
076   et al. Sun & Du (2019), is organized into six categories: Ranking methods (Chang et al. (1999);
077   Huang & He (2005)) assess band importance based on predefined criteria but ignore correlations
078   among bands. In contrast, search-based methods (Zhang et al. (2007); Fauvel et al. (2015)) optimize
079   subsets while considering these correlations, albeit at a higher computational cost. Clustering methods
080   (Qian et al. (2009); Imbiriba et al. (2015)) reduce redundancy by selecting representative bands from
081
      clusters, though their effectiveness can depend on the chosen algorithm. Sparsity methods (Sun
      et al. (2015; 2017)) utilize sparse representation for band selection, facing challenges in parameter
082
      optimization. Embedding learning (Zhang & Ma (2009); Zhan et al. (2017)) combines selection with
083
      classifiers for end-to-end optimization but may lack interpretability. Lastly, hybrid schemes (Datta
084   et al. (2015); Jiang & Li (2015)) blend various approaches to capitalize on their strengths, resulting in
085   enhanced effectiveness but increased complexity.
086
087
      With the rapid development of deep learning Shone et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2021); Pramanik
      et al. (2021), band selection methods based on deep learning Ribalta Lorenzo et al. (2020); Feng
088
      et al. (2020); Pestel-Schiller et al. (2021); Yu et al. (2022) have garnered significant attention in the
089
      academic community. These methods are recognized for their ability to automatically learn complex
090   features from hyperspectral data and effectively handle high-dimensional, highly correlated, and
091   noisy data, adapting to various application scenarios. For instance, Cai et al. (2019) introduced
092   BS-Nets, which use attention mechanisms to assess band importance, while Zhao et al. (2020)
093   enhanced classification performance through CNN interpretability using 1D GradCAM to visualize
094   band contributions. Wu & Yan (2021) developed HyperDesc for joint optimization of band selection
095   and feature extraction via a non-local spectral-spatial attention network. Most recently, Zhou et al.
096   (2023) proposed IGAEBS, an unsupervised hyperspectral band selection method that uses GCNs
097   to extract structural features and iteratively refines a band relation graph. While Yao et al. (2024)
098
      designed a novel BS module and cascaded band-specific spatial attention blocks for supervised band
      selection. However, these methods do not fundamentally address the issue of spectral band confidence
099
      representation. To tackle these challenges, we introduce a novel sparse learning strategy that achieves
100
      band selection in one step while implicitly representing the relationships between spectral bands.

107   In response to the challenge of band selection, we propose a robust and efficient sparse framework.
      Our model adopts a task-driven approach to training band selection. The selection process utilizes

      one does not encounter any local maxima, implying that the initial values of ci will most likely

      3.6                     A DVANTAGES IN D EPICTING R ELATIONSHIPS B ETWEEN S PECTRAL BANDS
249
250   Within our framework, it is possible to describe the relationships between spectral bands. Firstly,
251   unlike other sparsification methods, our approach involves competition among selections π, which
252   inherently represents the multivariate relationships among spectral bands. Secondly, from the
253   perspective of individual spectral bands, our method can theoretically describe the relationships
254   between bands, specifically, P (bj = 1|bi = 1, S(k,B) , c). This probability indicates the likelihood
255   that band j is selected given that the sparse event S(k,B) occurs and band i is selected:
256
                                                                               P (bj = 1, bi = 1, S(k,B) |c)
257                                          P (bj = 1|bi = 1, S(k,B) , c) =                                 .               (13)
                                                                                   P (bi = 1, S(k,B) |c)
258
259   Here, P (bj = 1, bi = 1, S(k,B) |c) denotes the sum of probabilities for all selections that select k
260   spectral bands passing through nodes bj = 1 and bi = 1; P (bi = 1, S|c) represents the sum of
261   probabilities for all selections that select k spectral bands passing through node bi = 1 (as shown in
262   Figure 1(b) and 1(c)). Similarly, the expression for P (bj = 1 | bi = 1, bk = 1, bm = 0, . . . , S(k,B) , c)
263   is the same.
264   Therefore, during the sparsification process, as the importance of a particular band increases, it
265   influences the probability of selection for all spectral bands globally. This means that bands with
266   significant local importance may no longer be decisive and could be influenced by the global
267   distribution of bands, tending towards a relatively optimal global outcome. Our experiments also
268   demonstrated a sequentially sparsified series of attributes, leading us to believe that our method can.
269   describe the multivariate relationships between spectral bands. Our future work will continue to focus
      on addressing this issue, as we believe our theoretical framework provides a viable solution.

      3.7   A NALYSIS OF p(π|c)
271
272   The physical meaning of p(π|c) is that, prior to selecting spectral bands, we assume that all bands or
273   band selections are equivalent. This assumption is commonly made in the design and comparison of
274   spectral band selection algorithms. Importantly, our method is also applicable to specific scenarios or
275   applications with additional requirements for the selected bands. It does not necessitate a globally
276   uniform distribution, rather, a piecewise locally uniform distribution can also be effectively utilized.
277   Variants of the method for such cases will be briefly introduced in the appendix.

289   3.9   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
290
291   The computation of loss and gradient involves both forward and backward dynamic programming
292   processes. The theoretical computational complexity of these operations is quantified as 2 × O(B ×
293
      (2 × b + 1)). For the band selection task, our computational complexity is minimal, even lower than
      the number of floating-point operations required for a 1 × 1 convolution. In the future, we plan to
294
      apply this method to larger-scale sparse tasks, such as model compression. For models with hundreds
295
      of millions of parameters, we aim to explore alternative approximate methods, such as Monte Carlo
296   sampling or hybrid sparsification techniques, to enhance computational efficiency and broaden the
297   applicability of our approach.

463   relationships among these bands. The goal was to maximize the sum of binary weights by selecting
464
      5 and 10 bands (|cA ∗ c|1 ). To achieve this, we applied four different methods: L1, L2, Gumbel,

465
      maximized the weight sum. By comparing the results generated by these methods, we utilized
466
      statistical tests (T-tests) to assess and demonstrate the superiority of the EM method in mining and
467   utilizing the relationships between spectral bands. The experiment results indicated that the EM
468   method showed a statistically significant advantage (p < 0.05) in depicting the relationships between
469   bands compared to the L1, L2, and Gumbel methods, thereby proving its potential and effectiveness

1018
1019   In certain specific application scenarios, the assumption of a uniform distribution may not be
1020   applicable. Practical needs may require selecting a specific number of bands within different spectral
1021   ranges. For instance, for color imaging, we might need to choose several bands in the red, green,
1022
       and blue spectral regions, while for nighttime imaging, bands in the near-infrared region are more
       relevant. These practical considerations are important and must be addressed.
1023

1025   above, a specific spectral range, such as the near-infrared region, follows a uniform distribution within
       that region. Each local region can use dynamic programming to compute the path probability sum
