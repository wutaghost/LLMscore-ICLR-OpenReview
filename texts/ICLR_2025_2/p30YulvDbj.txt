009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Major depressive disorder (MDD) or depression is a chronic mental illness that
012            significantly impacts individuals’ well-being and is often diagnosed at advanced
013            stages, increasing the risk of suicide. Current diagnostic practices, which rely
014            heavily on subjective assessments and patient self-reports, are often hindered by
015            challenges such as under-reporting and the failure to detect early, subtle symp-
016
               toms. Early detection of MDD is crucial and requires monitoring vital signs in
               everyday living conditions. Electroencephalogram (EEG) is a valuable tool for
017
               monitoring brain activity, offering critical insights into MDD and its underlying
018
               neurological mechanisms. While traditional EEG systems typically involve multi-
019            ple channels for recording, making them impractical for home-based monitoring,
020            wearable sensors can effectively capture single-channel EEG data. However, gen-
021            erating meaningful features from this data poses challenges due to the need for
022            specialized domain knowledge and significant computational power, which can
023            hinder real-time processing. To address these issues, our study focuses on devel-
024            oping a deep learning model for the binary classification of MDD using single-
025            channel EEG data. We focused on specific channels from various brain regions,
026            including central (C3), frontal (Fp1), occipital (O1), temporal (T4), and parietal
027
               (P3). Our study found that the channels Fp1, C3, and O1 achieved an impressive
               accuracy of 88% when analyzed using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
028
               with leave-one-subject-out cross-validation. Our study highlights the potential of
029
               utilizing single-channel EEG data for reliable MDD diagnosis, providing a less
030            intrusive and more convenient wearable solution for mental health assessment.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Major depressive disorder (MDD), a widespread condition affecting individuals globally is charac-
036   terized by persistent feelings of sadness, hopelessness, and a lack of interest or pleasure in daily
037   activities [Marx et al. (2023)], often accompanied by suicidal thoughts or attempts [American Psy-
038   chiatric Association et al. (2013); Tiller (2013); Australian Bureau of Statistics (2007); Obuobi-
039
      Donkor et al. (2021)]. MDD is particularly prevalent among individuals aged 18-29 and affects an
      estimated 350 million people worldwide [Organization et al. (2008)]. Notably, there have been re-
040
      ported increases in MDD cases during the COVID-19 pandemic [Lawrence et al. (2015); Lakhan
041
      et al. (2020)]. This highlights the urgent need for effective screening and intervention strategies in
042   this age group. To enable timely intervention and effective treatment, there is a pressing need for
043   accurate and objective diagnostic tools for MDD. This could be achieved through the monitoring of
044   specific physiological signals in real-world settings using wearable devices.
045
      Diagnosing MDD primarily relies on clinician-administered and self-rated scales, which are sub-
046
      jective and unsuitable for continuous monitoring [American Psychiatric Association et al. (2013)].
047
      Experience-based diagnoses can be inaccurate due to variability in individual experiences. MDD
048   affects various regions of the brain [Pandya et al. (2012)], with key areas such as the dorsal and
049   medial prefrontal cortex, dorsal and ventral anterior cingulate cortex, orbital frontal cortex, hip-
050   pocampus, insula, and amygdala playing crucial roles [Drevets (2000; 1999a); Buchsbaum et al.
051   (1997)]. Electroencephalogram (EEG) which measures the brain’s electrical activity [Carney et al.
052   (2001)] stands out as a superior biomarker for MDD detection as it directly measures brain activ-
053   ity and connectivity, offering insights into mood regulation and cognition [Olbrich & Arns (2013);
      Mumtaz et al. (2015)]. Using more electrodes in EEG recordings enhances coverage of brain activ-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ity across different regions, but it also increases complexity and can reduce patient comfort due to
055   the need for conductive gel and hair preparation [Montoya-Martı́nez et al. (2021)]. While clinical
056   settings can handle the use of multiple electrodes, wearable devices prioritize simplicity, often using
057   fewer channels, which can compromise data quality and accuracy. The challenge lies in selecting
058   the most effective channels, requiring thorough research to ensure fast and reliable data collection
059   [Alotaiby et al. (2015)]. A promising solution is the development of machine/deep learning models
060   that can accurately diagnose the medical condition using data from fewer electrodes, making the
061   process more efficient and practical.
062   Some studies have investigated the use of deep learning in MDD diagnosis using EEG data, but the
063   channel reduction remains less explored. For example, Acharya et al. (2018) employed a 13-layer
064   Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), while Ay et al. (2019) combined CNN with Long Short-Term
065   Memory (LSTM) models to capture both spatial and temporal patterns in EEG data. Using ten-
066   fold cross-validation, these models demonstrated impressive accuracy, with Acharya et al. (2018)
067   achieving 96% accuracy and Ay et al. (2019) reaching 99.12% for right hemisphere EEG data. The
068   performance improvement in Ay et al. (2019) could be attributed to the inclusion of LSTM, which
069
      is designed for sequential learning and is well-suited for handling the temporal dynamics inherent
      in EEG signals. Contrary to this trend, Mumtaz & Qayyum (2019) compared the performance of a
070
      11-layer 1D CNN and a 14-layer 1D CNN-LSTM model on the same dataset, achieving 98.32% and
071
      95.97% accuracy, respectively, using ten-fold cross-validation. Interestingly, their results showed
072   a higher accuracy for the CNN-only model. Khan et al. (2021) used partial directed coherence
073   (PDC) to estimate effective brain connectivity for MDD classification. They trained a 3D CNN with
074   PDC matrices, achieving 100% accuracy using 10-fold cross-validation. In a later study Khan et al.
075   (2022) applied wavelet coherence (WCOH) on EEG segments, achieving 100% accuracy with a 2D-
076   CNN model, but noted that further assessments are needed to validate these findings. Saeedi et al.
077   (2021) introduced five deep learning models for MDD detection, focusing on brain connectivity
078   analysis. Their 1DCNN-LSTM model outperformed others, achieving 99.25% accuracy. Dang et al.
079   (2020) developed a frequency-dependent multi-layer brain (FDMB) network paired with a CNN
080
      model to classify MDD from EEG signals and achieved an accuracy of 97.27%. Loh et al. (2022)
      suggested a CNN model for MDD detection using spectrogram images from EEG data. These
081
      images were passed through an eight-layer CNN network, achieving 99.58% accuracy with hold-out
082
      validation. However, they acknowledged that 2D-CNN models can be computationally expensive,
083   making clinical implementation challenging. The only deep learning study where it talked about
084   channel reduction is by Rafiei et al. (2022), but again the reduced number of channels is 10, which is
085   again a multichannel EEG. The study introduces a customized InceptionTime model for automated
086   detection of MDD using raw EEG signals, achieving 91.67% accuracy with 19 channels and 87.5%
087   with 10 channels and with the first minute of EEG recordings.
088
      The only single channel detection found is in the classical machine learning technique where there
089   is a requirement of feature domain knowledge. For example, Bachmann et al. (2018) identified the
090   EEG channel Pz as the most relevant, achieving 92% accuracy with 13 subjects in each class. Deep
091   learning, unlike classical machine learning, excels at automatically learning complex representations
092   from raw data without the need for manual feature engineering [LeCun et al. (2015)]. In classical
093   machine learning, feature engineering is often labor-intensive and requires domain expertise, which
094   can limit model performance by relying on predefined features [Schmidhuber (2015)]. Deep learn-
095   ing’s ability to discover intricate patterns directly from data has led to breakthroughs in fields such as
096   medical diagnostics, where it outperforms traditional methods by capturing subtle, high-dimensional
097
      relationships [Goodfellow (2016)]. The aim of our study is to investigate the applicability of single
      channel EEG data for MDD classification using deep learning.
098
099   The primary contribution of this study lies in the detection of MDD using a single EEG channel
100   by leveraging CNN model. Unlike traditional approaches that rely on multiple channels, this study
101   demonstrates the feasibility of achieving accurate diagnosis with minimal electrode usage, which is
102   crucial for wearable applications. Our code is available online in the GitHub (link will be shared
103
      after review).

      2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
111
112   The classification of MDD from EEG signals is handled as a binary classification problem, where a
113   CNN model is trained to distinguish between MDD (labeled as 1) and non-MDD (labeled as 0) based
114   on EEG inputs of a specified duration. Once the model is trained, it produces binary predictions for
115   each input signal. An EEG recording is classified as MDD if the proportion of segments predicting
116   MDD surpasses a threshold of x% compared to the total segment predictions for that subject. As
117
      depicted in Figure 1, the process starts with EEG signal acquisition, followed by pre-processing to
      remove artifacts and segment the data. The deep learning model is then used to classify the signals
118
      as originating from either MDD or non-MDD individuals.

      2.3   S EGMENTATION
175
176   In our study, the 5-minute EEG recordings were segmented into non-overlapping 10-second seg-
177   ments. Using shorter segments, such as 10 seconds, is effective for capturing the temporal variability
178   and transient features associated with MDD [Li et al. (2016)]. Although some subjects’ recordings
179   slightly exceed the 5-minute duration, all data were included in the analysis.
180
181   2.4   N ORMALIZATION
182
183   For each channel, the extracted features were normalized using the standard scaler method. This
184   method transforms the features to have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1, ensuring that all
185
      features are on a uniform scale and reducing the impact of outliers. The standard scaler is computed
      using the formula:
186
                                                 z = (x − m)/s                                         (1)
187
188
      where as x is the feature, m is the mean of the feature and s is the standard deviation.
189
190   2.5   H YPER - PARAMETER TUNING
191
      In this study, we used a deep learning model for binary classification using a sequential architecture
192
      designed to analyze time-series data. In our study, we employ hyper-parameter tuning using the
193   80:20 training/validation split of the total segments. Hyper-parameters were optimized for:
194
195          • kernel size among the lengths 3, 5, 7, 11, 21 and 31,
196          • number of max pooling layers (number of convolution block) with blocks 2, 3, 4 and 5, and
197
             • MDD decision threshold across range 10-100% with 10% step.
198
199   To classify an EEG recording as indicative of MDD, we defined a prediction threshold, denoted as
200   x% for the MDD-predicting segments. If the number of segments classified as MDD (class 1 by the
201   deep learning model) exceeds x% of the total predictions, the EEG recording is labeled as MDD;
202   otherwise, it is classified as non-MDD. The threshold x% can range from 10% to 90%, allowing
203   for adjustments in the model’s sensitivity. A lower threshold (e.g., 10%) increases the likelihood of
204   classifying subjects as MDD, while a higher threshold (e.g., 90%) biases the classification towards
205
      healthy subjects. The decision threshold hyper-parameter was searched for 80:20 subject-wise train-
      ing/validation split. The threshold that yielded the highest accuracy was identified through multiple
206
      validation iterations, and this final value was used for leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) testing to eval-
207
      uate performance.

      2.6   C ONVOLUTIONAL NEURAL NETWORK (CNN) MODEL
210
211   The CNN model used for single-channel EEG based MDD classification is as shown in Figure 3.
212   The model begins with an input layer that accepts single-channel EEG data. The model consists
213   of three convolutional layers (Conv1D) with progressively increasing filter sizes of 64, 128, and
214   256, each utilizing a kernel size of 21 and a ReLU activation function to capture intricate features
215   from the input signals. After each convolutional layer, a MaxPooling1D layer with a pool size of
      2 is applied to reduce the dimensionality of the feature maps, enhancing computational efficiency

      while preserving essential information. Following the convolutional and pooling layers, the output
217   is flattened to prepare for the final dense layer. The model culminates in a single dense output layer
218   with a sigmoid activation function, which is designed for binary classification.

237   2.7   M ODEL T RAINING AND T ESTING
238
      In our study, we employed a leave-one-subject-out cross-validation approach using a CNN model.
239
      In LOSO cross-validation, N − 1 subjects are utilized for training, while one subject is set aside
240
      for testing. This method ensures that each subject’s data is used exclusively for testing exactly
241   once, thus preventing any data leakage between the training and testing phases. By adopting LOSO
242   cross-validation, we ensured a more effective model training process and a robust evaluation of the
243   model’s performance.
244
245   2.8   M ETRIC USED
246
247   We utilize accuracy as the primary evaluation metric for our deep learning model due to the balanced
248   nature of our dataset, where the classes (e.g., presence and absence of MDD) are approximately
249   equal in size. In balanced datasets, accuracy provides a reliable measure of model performance be-
250   cause each class contributes equally to the calculation, ensuring the metric is not skewed by class
251
      predominance. Accuracy is straightforward to interpret, offering a clear assessment of overall effec-
      tiveness, which is accessible to clinicians and researchers. High accuracy indicates reliable identi-
252
      fication of both conditions, crucial for clinical decision-making and patient care, making it a robust
253
      and practical choice for evaluating our models in MDD detection using EEG signals.

306   4   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
307
308   The primary finding of this study, based on the deep learning for classifying the MDD and non
309
      MDD include: 1) MDD and non MDD subjects can be classified from single channel EEG signals
      using deep learning models, 2) the single channel classification accuracy shows difference among
310
      the brain regions such as central, frontal, parietal, occipital and temporal, 3) the highest accuracy of
311
      classification is achieved in the frontal, central and occipital with 88% classification accuracy.
312
313   Although EEG has been widely studied in MDD research, the potential to distinguish MDD from
314   non-MDD subjects using single-channel EEG remains under explored, particularly in the context of
315
      early diagnosis via wearable technology. While increasing the number of electrodes enhances the
      granularity of brain activity data, it also introduces challenges in terms of practicality and patient
316
      comfort, especially in clinical environments [Montoya-Martı́nez et al. (2021)]. Wearable devices,
317
      which prioritize minimal channels, underscore the need for research aimed at optimizing channel
318   selection for MDD detection. Deep learning eliminates the need for expert-driven feature extraction,
319   as it can automatically learn relevant patterns directly from raw EEG data. It has also demonstrated
320   that sufficient information retrieval is achievable with fewer EEG channels, as seen in applications
321   like seizure detection [Ullah et al. (2018)] and MDD detection [Acharya et al. (2018); Ay et al.
322   (2019); Mumtaz & Qayyum (2019); Khan et al. (2022)]. While more electrodes in EEG recordings
323   can provide deeper insights into brain activity, this approach can complicate the process and decrease
      patient comfort, especially in clinical settings [Montoya-Martı́nez et al. (2021)]. Wearable devices,


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      which prioritize fewer channels for ease of use, demand further research to identify the optimal
325   channels for effective MDD screening.
326
327   MDD manifests in various brain regions [Drevets (1999b)], facilitating its differentiation through
328
      EEG variations between affected and unaffected individuals. While previous deep learning based
      EEG studies for MDD detection predominantly employ multichannel detection, ranging from 10
329
      [Rafiei et al. (2022)] to 19 channels [Khan et al. (2021; 2022); Dang et al. (2020); Saeedi et al.
330
      (2021); Aydemir et al. (2021); Loh et al. (2022)]. Despite the extensive use of multiple channels
331   spanning different brain regions, there is a lack of comparative analysis regarding region-wise per-
332   formance. In our study, we have used single channel performance from each of the brain regions
333   such as frontal, temporal, occipital, parietal, and central. We observed variations among channels
334   across five distinct regions. Interestingly, selected EEG channels in our study exhibited high classi-
335   fication accuracy, suggesting their potential utility.
336
      Rafiei et al. (2022) have investigated the impact of reducing EEG channels on MDD diagnosis using
337   deep learning and the same dataset that we use in our study. However, the study uses a customized
338   InceptionTime model for automated detection of MDD using 10 channel EEG signals and achieves
339   an accuracy of 87.5% with first minute of EEG recordings. Even though the dataset is 5 minute
340   duration they have used only the first one minute for this analysis. Using single channel EEG, our
341   study yielded 88% accuracy in channel C3, Fp1 and O1 (shown in Figure 5).
342
      The kernel size in a CNN model plays a key role in determining how much of the input data the
343   model processes at a time [Alzubaidi et al. (2021)]. A smaller kernel size captures fine-grained, lo-
344   cal features, which is useful for identifying detailed patterns in the data. In contrast, a larger kernel
345   size can capture more global patterns by considering a broader context of the input. The choice of
346   kernel size directly impacts the model’s ability to recognize features at different scales [Agrawal &
347   Mittal (2020); Phan et al. (2021)], and finding the optimal size is crucial for improving performance,
348   especially in tasks like EEG signal classification where both local and global brain activity patterns
349   may be important. The impacts of kernel size in classification accuracy is as shown in Figure 4
350   and the optimal kernel size is set as 21. The pooling layer in a CNN performs sub-sampling of
351
      feature maps generated by convolution operations, reducing their size while retaining key features
      [Alzubaidi et al. (2021)] and 3 max pooling layer was found optimum. In our analysis, three con-
352
      volution blocks (a block consists of convolution and max pooling layers) were found suitable which
353
      yields a shallow CNN model (number of parameters 9, 39, 265) which favors deploying in resource
354   constrained devices.

381   The study demonstrates that EEG channels from various brain lobes can effectively distinguish
382
      between normal and MDD subjects, highlighting the significance of electrode placement in areas that
      do not require hair removal. This finding is particularly relevant in today’s fast-paced environment,
383
      where mental health concerns are often overlooked. Many individuals may be reluctant to undergo
384
      hair removal from the head, especially if they are unaware of or skeptical about their mental health
385   status. Our study offers a non-invasive method for diagnosing MDD, providing a convenient and
386   accessible way for individuals to assess their mental well-being. Notably, our model achieved an
387   accuracy of 88% for the frontal channel Fp1, which is located near the forehead, making it more
388   suitable for wearable applications. Further research is needed to validate these results across diverse
389   MDD datasets and other channels.
