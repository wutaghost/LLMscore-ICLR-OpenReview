011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Data poisoning attacks pose one of the biggest threats to modern AI systems,
014            necessitating robust defenses. While extensive efforts have been made to develop
015            empirical defenses, attackers continue to evolve, creating sophisticated methods
016            to circumvent these measures. To address this, we must move beyond empirical
017            defenses and establish provable certification methods that guarantee robustness.
018            This paper introduces a novel certification approach using Bilinear Mixed Integer
019            Programming (BMIP) to compute sound, deterministic bounds that provide such
020
               provable robustness. Using BMIP, we compute the reachable set of parameters
               that could result from training with potentially manipulated data. A key insight
021
               to make this computation feasible is relaxing the reachable parameter set to a
022
               convex set between training iterations. At test time, this parameter set allows us
023            to predict all possible outcomes, guaranteeing robustness. Our BMIP approach is
024            more precise than previous methods, which rely solely on interval and polyhedral
025            bounds. Crucially, it overcomes the fundamental limitation of prior approaches
026            where parameter bounds could only grow, often uncontrollably. We show that
027            these tighter bounds eliminate a key source of divergence issues, resulting in more
028            stable training and higher certified accuracy.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Date poisoning attacks are one of the most significant threats to the integrity of machine learning
035   models today. Attackers can potentially exert far-reaching influence by poisoning ubiquitous foun-
036   dation models that are widely used, or targeting systems in critical applications, such as finance,
037   healthcare, or autonomous decision-making. These attacks aim to inject malicious data into the
038   training process, leading the model to make incorrect or harmful predictions during deployment. The
039
      recent trend of training foundation models on massive datasets scraped from all available sources
      has exacerbated this threat, as data curation on this scale is nearly impossible (Wan et al., 2023;
040
      Carlini et al., 2024). Government agencies across the US and Europe have recognized poisoning
041
      as one of the fundamental threats to AI systems and highlight the need for robust defenses in their
042   respective reports (ENISA, 2020; Vassilev et al., 2024) and legislation (European Union, 2024).
043
044   Identifying this threat, many empirical defenses have been developed to mitigate the risks (Cinà
045
      et al., 2023). These approaches typically use a combination of data filtering, robust training, and
      model inspection to detect or prevent the influence of poisoning attacks. However, their empirical
046
      nature means they can detect the presence of specific attacks but cannot guarantee their absence. As
047
      a result, more sophisticated attacks have been developed to circumvent these defenses (Suciu et al.,
048   2018). A recent line of work explores rigorous, provable guarantees that bound the influence that
049   poisoning attacks can have on a model’s predictions (Lorenz et al., 2024; Sosnin et al., 2024). These
050   methods are based on test-time certifiers (Gowal et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018) and use interval and
051   polyhedral constraints to compute upper and lower real-valued bounds for each parameter during
052   training. These parameter intervals bound the influence that data perturbations can have on the
053   trained model. These works are crucial, as they are the first to provide sound, deterministic bounds
      on the model parameters and, consequently, an adversary’s influence on the training.

054
      However, while these types of bounds have been shown to be a good trade-off between preci-
055   sion and computational efficiency for test-time certification (Li et al., 2023), the significant over-
056   approximations of polyhedral constraints limit the method’s scalability for training. Lorenz et al.
057   (2024) have analyzed this limitation and show that interval bounds can cause the training to diverge.
058
      This work addresses the imprecisions of prior methods by proposing a more precise certification
059
      method BiCert. We formulate certified training as an optimization problem with bilinear mixed in-
060
      teger constraints, which can be solved without over-approximations. Since solving an optimization
061   problem across the entire computational graph of training is computationally infeasible even for
062   small models, we strategically cut the computational graph after each parameter update, solving the
063   optimization problem in parameter space. We demonstrate that the bounds achieved by this method
064   remain sound while maintaining computational feasibility, and solve the fundamental limitations of
065   prior methods. An experimental evaluation validates our method, confirming our theoretical analysis
066   and comparing it to prior work. The results show state-of-the-art performance, significantly outper-
067   forming the baselines for larger perturbation radii and demonstrating improved training stability.

069   2   R ELATED W ORK
070
071   There are two major lines of work on certified defenses against training-time attacks: ensemble-
072   based methods and bound-based methods.
073
074   Ensemble-Based Methods. Ensemble-based methods are typically based on either bagging or
075   randomized smoothing. Wang et al. (2020), Rosenfeld et al. (2020), and Weber et al. (2023) extend
076   Randomized Smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019) to training-time attacks. While Wang et al. (2020) and
077   Weber et al. (2023) compute probabilistic guarantees against ℓ0 and ℓ2 -norm adversaries respec-
078
      tively, Cohen et al. (2019) provide these guarantees against label flipping attacks.
079   A similar line of work provides probabilistic guarantees against training-time attacks using bagging.
080   Jia et al. (2021) find that bagging’s data sub-sampling shows intrinsic robustness to poisoning. Wang
081   et al. (2022) enhance the robustness guarantees with advanced sampling strategies, while Levine &
082   Feizi (2021) introduce a deterministic bagging method. Zhang et al. (2022) adapt this approach
083   for backdoor attacks with triggers. Recent studies also explore different threat models, including
084
      temporal aspects Wang & Feizi (2023) and dynamic attacks Bose et al. (2024).
085
      Bound-Based Methods. In contrast to these ensemble-based sampling methods, Lorenz et al.
086
      (2024) and Sosnin et al. (2024) propose to compute sound, deterministic bounds of the model’s pa-
087
      rameters during training. Both methods share the same underlying principle. They define a polytope
088   of allowed perturbations in input space and propagate it through the forward and backward passes
089   during training. By over-approximating the reachable set with polytopes along the way, they com-
090   pute sound, worst-case bounds for the model’s gradients. Using these bounds, the model parameters
091   can be updated with sound upper and lower bounds, guaranteeing that all possible parameters re-
092   sulting from the data perturbations lie within these bounds. Lorenz et al. (2024) use intervals to
093   represent these polytopes and extend the approach to also include test-time perturbations. Sosnin
094   et al. (2024) use a combination of interval and linear bounds and additionally limit the number of
095   data points that can be perturbed. Both approaches’ biggest limitations are the significant over-
096
      approximations caused by using polytopes to represent the reachable sets, which can lead to loose
      bounds and divergence issues.

100
101   The goal of our method is to bound the error that bounded perturbations to the training data can
102   introduce on the final machine learning model. However, precisely computing these bounds is in-
103   feasible even for small model sizes, as it has been shown that this is a ΣP
                                                                                2 -hard problem (Marro &
104
      Lombardi, 2023). The challenge therefore lies in finding a feasible over-approximation, which (i)
      ensures sound bounds, (ii) makes as few over-approximations as possible, and (iii) remains compu-
105
      tationally feasible.

138   There are two leading causes of divergence when training with interval bounds: (i) IBP does not

146   iteration. This cuts the verification graph to a single training iteration while avoiding the divergence

502   6   D ISCUSSION
503
504   BiCert addresses the fundamental limitations of prior certified training approaches, specifically the
505   over-reliance on convex over-approximations that lead to unstable training and diverging parameter
506
      bounds. By introducing Bilinear Mixed Integer Programming (BMIP) for certified training, we pro-
      vide tighter, more precise bounds at each training iteration. Our method ensures that the parameter
507
      bounds can shrink, effectively mitigating the divergence issues faced by previous methods.
508
509   The primary benefit of this approach lies in its improved precision, which leads to significantly
510   higher certified accuracy, especially for larger perturbations. As demonstrated in our experiments,
511   BiCert consistently outperforms existing methods in terms of the certified accuracy of the model.
512
      Additionally, the reduced variance in our results highlights the robustness and reliability of our
      approach.
513
514   This increased precision comes at the cost of a higher computational complexity. Solving piecewise
515   bilinear optimization problems for each parameter update is more computationally expensive than
516   the interval-based methods used in prior work. This trade-off between computational cost and certi-
517   fication precision is crucial when deploying certified defenses in practical settings. While the added
518
      cost may be prohibitive for large-scale models, our method provides a valuable framework for im-
      proving the precision and reliability of certification in smaller models or scenarios where robustness
519
      is critical.
520
521   Looking forward, there are opportunities to explore hybrid approaches that combine the precision
522   of BMIP with the computational efficiency of interval-based methods, potentially achieving a more
523   scalable solution. Intelligently choosing the best constraints to relax while preserving precision
524
      where required could combine the best of both worlds, yielding precise, scalable bounds.
525   Overall, this work provides a significant advancement in certified defenses, demonstrating that more
526   precise certification methods are feasible and necessary for ensuring the robustness of machine learn-
527   ing models against poisoning attacks. Despite the computational cost, our approach offers a sub-
528   stantial improvement in certified accuracy and stability, laying the groundwork for future research
529   in precise, scalable certification techniques.

531   7   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This work addresses fundamental limitations in state-of-the-art certifiers that compute bounds
534   for training-time attacks on neural networks. In particular, their over-reliance on convex over-
535   approximations limits their applicability to larger perturbation sizes, makes training unstable, and
536   can cause models to diverge even for ideal, convex settings. Using Bilinear Mixed Integer Program-
537   ming (BMIP), we can compute exact bounds for each training iteration. This approach significantly
538   improves training stability and certified accuracy of the resulting models, outperforming the state-
539   of-the-art. We believe that this work lays the foundation for effective certified defenses against
      training-time attacks.
