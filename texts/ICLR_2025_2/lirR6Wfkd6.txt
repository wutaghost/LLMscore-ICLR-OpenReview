                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               Quantum neural networks (QNNs) play a pivotal role in addressing complex tasks
               within quantum machine learning, analogous to classical neural networks in deep
013
               learning. Ensuring consistent performance across diverse datasets is crucial for un-
014
               derstanding and optimizing QNNs in both classical and quantum machine learning
015            tasks, but remains a challenge as QNN’s generalization properties have not been
016            fully explored. In this paper, we investigate the generalization properties of QNNs
017            through the lens of learning algorithm stability, circumventing the need to explore
018            the entire hypothesis space and providing insights into how classical optimizers
019            influence QNN performance. By establishing a connection between QNNs and
020            quantum combs, we examine the general behaviors of QNN models from a quantum
021            information theory perspective. Leveraging the uniform stability of the stochastic
022            gradient descent algorithm, we propose a generalization error bound determined
023
               by the number of trainable parameters, data uploading times, dataset dimension,
               and classical optimizer hyperparameters. Numerical experiments validate this
024
               comprehensive understanding of QNNs and align with our theoretical conclusions.
025
               As the first exploration into understanding the generalization capability of QNNs
026            from a unified perspective of design and training, our work offers practical insights
027            for applying QNNs in quantum machine learning.

      Quantum computing leverages the laws of quantum mechanics to solve complex problems more
      efficiently than classical computers, offering notable quantum speedups in areas such as cryptogra-
034
      phy (Shor, 1997), and quantum simulations (Lloyd, 1996; Childs et al., 2018). Recent advancements
035
      in quantum hardware have demonstrated quantum advantages in specific tasks (Arute et al., 2019;
036   Zhong et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021), catalyzing the exploration of quantum computing’s potential
037   in artificial intelligence. This interdisciplinary connection has given rise to quantum machine learn-
038   ing (Biamonte et al., 2017; You et al., 2023; Tang & Yan, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Caro et al., 2022b;
039   Cerezo et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b; Qian et al., 2022a; Yu et al., 2022a; Tian et al., 2023; Li
040   et al., 2019; 2022; Jerbi et al., 2023a; Li & Deng, 2022; Huang et al., 2022a).
041
      One of the leading frameworks in quantum machine learning is the quantum neural network (QNN),
042   which represents the quantum analog of classical artificial neural networks and typically refers
043   to parameterized quantum circuits that are trainable based on quantum measurement results. A
044   well-known architecture within QNNs is the data re-uploading QNN (Pérez-Salinas et al., 2020;
045   Gil Vidal & Theis, 2020), which integrates multiple training and data encoding layers within a single
046   quantum circuit. This approach significantly enhances the expressivity of the models, allowing them
047   to approximate functions more effectively (Pérez-Salinas et al., 2020; Pérez-Salinas et al., 2021; Yu
048   et al., 2022b; Manzano et al., 2023; Jerbi et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023). Such characteristics make the
049   data re-uploading QNN a suitable quantum machine model for supervised learning tasks.
050   Despite these advancements in quantum machine learning, critical challenges remain, particularly in
051   understanding and predicting the performance of models in practical settings. A fundamental criterion
052   for evaluating the performance of any learning algorithm is the generalization gap (Kawaguchi et al.,
053   2022). This gap essentially measures the difference between a model’s accuracy on training data
      and its expected accuracy on unseen data, providing essential theoretical guidance on determining

082   QNNs typically update trainable parameters using classical optimizers, where classical optimizers
083   play a crucial role in the training process by adjusting the parameters based on quantum measurement
084   results. However, the integration of various classical optimizers with quantum processes introduces
085   additional complexity, particularly in ensuring that these updates contribute positively to minimizing
086   the generalization gap. Hence, providing theoretical guidance on the design and training of QNNs
087
      with regard to the strategic use of specific optimizers is of fundamental importance. Among these
      optimizers, stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithm is the most commonly used. Understanding
088
      the effects of SGD is vital as it not only enhances our comprehension of this optimizer but also
089
      informs the uses of its advanced variants in QNN applications.
090
091   To deepen our understanding of QNNs, our work establishes theoretical guarantees on how classical
092   optimizers impact their generalization performance. To summarize, our contributions include:
093
               • We introduce a more general perspective in the study of QNNs by conceptualizing them as a
094
                 special form of trainable quantum combs (cf. Sec. 3.1). This approach allows us to leverage
095              the rich theoretical framework of quantum combs to analyze the properties and dynamics of
096              QNNs.
097
               • We then investigate the stability of QNNs based on the quantum comb perspective to
098              show that QNNs are uniformly stable (cf. Fig.1). We further establish an upper bound
099              on generalization error for data reuploading model (cf. Sec.3.3). This bound not only
100              informs the control of QNN expressivity power through the number of layers and trainable
101              parameters during the designing of QNNs, but also offers new insights into training QNNs
102              with input data dimensions and classical optimizer-dependent parameters. Importantly, the
103              bound introduces a new training guideline suggesting that the learning rate and the number
104              of trainable gates should be designed to be inversely proportional to enable stable training.
105            • To substantiate our theoretical claims, we have conducted extensive numerical experiments
106              focused on assessing changes in the expressivity and stability of QNNs. These experiments
107              underscores the importance of stable learning for the practical training of these networks
                 and hence draw a guideline for future QNN developments.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Currently, the theory of generalization in QNNs mainly focuses on the complexity measures. Du et al.
111   (2022) derives an upper bound for generalization error with the dependence of the number of trainable
112   quantum gates and the operator norm of the observable by leveraging covering number (Vapnik,
113   2013) to quantify the expressivity of VQAs. Later, (Caro et al., 2022a) uses quantum channels
114   to derive more general results and (Du et al., 2023) extends it to multi-class classification tasks.
115   Abbas et al. (2021b) uses the effective dimension (Berezniuk et al., 2020; Abbas et al., 2021a) as
116   another complexity measure for the parameter space of QNN. Gyurik et al. (2023) uses Vapnik-
117
      Chervonenkis dimension (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 2015) that investigates the balance of empirical
      and generalization performance on the dimension of inputs and the Frobenius norm of the observable.
118
      Besides, Caro et al. (2021), Bu et al. (2021; 2022) and Qi et al. (2023) derives an upper bound
119
      based on Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002), where the bound in (Bu et al.,
120   2021; 2022) is based on the circuit depth and the amount of non-stabilizerness in the circuit for both
121   noise and noiseless models, and Qi et al. (2023) mainly investigates the generalization of variational
122   function regression models using tensor-network encodings. Furthermore, the information-theoretic
123   bound (Banchi et al., 2021) based on Rényi mutual information and the generalization behavior of
124   a specific class of QNN (Chen et al., 2021; Kübler et al., 2021; Du et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021;
125   Huang et al., 2021a) are also established.
126   The study of generalization bounds in relation to stability in classical machine learning has laid the
127   substantial groundwork for understanding how small changes in the training set can impact the outputs
128   of learning algorithms (Feldman & Vondrak, 2018; Bousquet et al., 2020; Klochkov & Zhivotovskiy,
129   2021; Yuan & Li, 2024). From seminal contributions by Bousquet and Elisseeff (Bousquet & Elisseeff,
130   2002), stability has been demonstrated to yield dimension-independent generalization bounds for
131   both deterministic learning algorithms (Mukherjee et al., 2006; Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2010) and
132   randomized approaches such as stochastic gradient descent (SGD) (Elisseeff et al., 2005; Hardt
133   et al., 2016; Verma & Zhang, 2019). However, despite these significant advances, the analysis
134
      of generalization guarantees from the perspective of stability remains largely unexplored in the
      context of QNNs. Also, existing generalization bounds for QNNs do not account for the impact of
135
      the classical optimizer, leading to a significant gap in unified designing and training guidance for
136
      effectively implementing powerful QNNs.

186   3     M AIN R ESULT
187
188   In this section, we develop our main result of the generalization bound in quantum machine learning
189   models under the discussion of stability. In short, we make connection between quantum comb
190   and data re-uploading QNNs by expressing the output function via a more general form. Then
191   using the characterization of quantum combs in the output function, we derive an upper bound on
192   the generalization gap for data re-uploading QNN that depends on several QNN parameters and
193   hyperparameters that associated with the classical optimizer. We will first introduce the quantum
194   combs and their connection to data re-uploading QNN in Section. 3.1, then we present that QNNs are
195
      βm -uniform stable in Section. 3.2 and the generalization bound with its implications in Section. 3.3.
196
197   3.1   Q UANTUM C OMBS AND DATA R E - UPLOADING QNN S
198
199   A native quantum classifier initially encodes classical data into the quantum state and utilizes a
200   quantum circuit for classification tasks (Mitarai et al., 2018). However, this naive approach faces
201
      limitations, even with single qubit classifiers, where a single rotation fails to adequately separate
      complex data patterns. To overcome these limitations, the data re-uploading QNN is proposed (Pérez-
202
      Salinas et al., 2020; Gil Vidal & Theis, 2020), drawing inspiration from classical feed-forward
203
      neural networks, in which the classical data is entered and processed in the network several times.
204   The data re-uploading QNN have shown its universality of function approximation (Pérez-Salinas
205   et al., 2020; Pérez-Salinas et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022b; Manzano et al., 2023) and strong learning
206   performances (Jerbi et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023), making it a suitable quantum machine model for
207   supervised learning tasks.
208
      Typically, the model repeatedly encodes classical data into the parameters of quantum gates throughout
209
      the quantum circuit. For simplicity, we can assume that the initial input state is denoted as ρin ,
210   followed by tunable gates U (θ) that are interspersed with data encoding operations U (x). An
211   L-layer data re-uploading QNN repeats this process L times. We find that the data re-uploading
212   QNN in quantum machine learning is naturally a sequential quantum comb (Chiribella et al., 2008).
213   In the following, we show the output of data re-uploading QNNs f (C, x, M) with respect to the
214   measurement operator M in quantum circuits can be well represented by a sequential quantum comb
215   C with the separation of trainable parts and classical data x. Hence, the generalization bound is
      straightforward to analyze and bounded as shown in Section 3.2.

367   Vanishing on the number of samples. Our generalization bound demonstrates a O( √1m ) scaling
368   with the number of training samples m, highlighting that an increase in m directly enhances the
369   generalization performance. It is important to note that for the generalization bound to be meaningful,
370   it must converge to zero as m in the limit m → ∞ and this convergence is contingent upon βm
371   decaying at a rate faster than O( √1m ). Hence, our generalization bound is a non-trivial bound and its
372   scaling in relation to the number of samples aligns with current literature.

375   findings also reveal that the generalization bound demonstrates a linear dependence on both the
376   number of data re-uploading times L, and the dimension of the data D. This linear relationship
377   indicates that as the number of times data L is re-uploaded increases, or as the dimension of the
      data D grows, there is a corresponding increase in the bound, suggesting that more complex data

378
      or more frequent re-uploading could potentially lead to larger errors on unseen data. However, the
379   increase in the number of data re-uploading times L implies a higher expressivity of QNNs (Pérez-
380   Salinas et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022b; Manzano et al., 2023). This directly comes with a trade-off
381   between the expressivity and generalization of data re-uploading QNNs, particularly when dealing
382   with limited training data, which shows the analogy with the bias-variance trade-off in classical
383   neural networks (Geman et al., 1992; Hastie et al., 2009). It is noted that while previous work (Du
384   et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2023) considers the number of parameters as a measure of expressivity, bound
385   which focus on the data re-uploading times L poses a more direct form of expressivity. Below,
386   we demonstrate how the combination of explicit expressivity and optimizer parameters provides
387
      additional insights into understanding the performance of QNN models.
388
389
      Stable training. On the training of QNNs, our generalization bound also have an exponential
      dependence T on the operator norm of the measurement operator ∥M∥∞ , the learning rate η and the
390
      number of parameters K. It is imperative to carefully choose these parameters such that the term is
391
      normalized to be less than 1, i.e. ηK∥M∥∞ < 1 to maintain a low generalization error. In practice,
392   the operator norm of measurement practically is bounded by 1 such that 0 ≤ ∥M ∥∞ ≤ 1 and it is
393   normally chosen as Pauli strings, i.e. ∥X∥∞ = ∥Y ∥∞ = ∥Z∥∞ = 1. Then, balancing the number
394   of parameters and the learning rate is critical. A larger K can be counterbalanced by a smaller η to
395   ensure that each step in the learning process is small enough to prevent instabilities that could arise
396   from complex models. To optimize generalization performance with a fixed number of parameters
397   or learning rate, our bound suggests configuring the learning rate as O(1/K), where K is the fixed
398   number of parameters, or setting the number of parameters as O(1/η), where η is the fixed learning
399   rate. This careful tuning helps to mitigate the risk of overfitting by allowing the model to explore
400
      the parameter space more thoroughly and settle into a stable configuration that generalizes well. By
      incorporating expressivity, our generalization bound unifies the learning and training phases, offering
401
      more practical insights for the design and optimization of QNNs.

516      5                          C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
517
518      In this paper, we have initiated steps towards a deeper theoretical understanding of quantum neural
519      networks by the lens of stability. We have demonstrated that the generalization of data re-uploading
520
         QNNs via stability is contingent upon the number of trainable parameters, data re-uploading times,
         data dimension, and optimizer dependent parameters. While the overall decay of the generalization
521
         error in relation to the sample size aligns with results from previous studies, our result provides a
522
         distinct perspective on the stable training concept, specifically regarding the impact of learning rate
523      η, parameter count K and measurement operator ∥M ∥∞ on training over T iterations, given by the
524      exponential term O(ηK∥M ∥∞ )T . This offers new insights into training QNNs that the learning
525      rate and the number of trainable gates should be chosen to be inversely proportional to ensure stable
526      training and minimize generalization error.
527
         From the technical perspective, we have utilized the quantum information theoretic methods such
528
         as quantum comb architecture and Choi–Jamiołkowski isomorphism to analyze the performance
529      of QNNs. This theoretical framework offers numerous tools for analysing the stability of data
530      re-uploading models. Future research could explore a broader form of the quantum comb and employ
531      semidefinite programming to identify optimal QNNs (Quintino et al., 2019). This approach may offer
532      deeper insights into the fundamental limits that QNNs can achieve in terms of generalization errors.
533      It would also be intriguing to extend our analysis to include other well-known optimizers such as
534      RMSProp and Adam (Ruder, 2016). We believe that our findings deepen the understanding of QNNs’
535      learnability (Anschuetz, 2024) by considering both design and training processes, paving the way for
536      the implementation of powerful QNNs across various machine learning tasks.

       Overall, quantum combs describe a more general kind of transformation by taking several input
       operations and output a new operation. It has shown its advantage for applying quantum comb in
1051
       solving process transformation problems and optimizing the ultimate achievable performance, includ-
1052
       ing transformations of unitary operations such as inversion (Chen et al., 2024; Yoshida et al., 2023),
1053   complex conjugation, control-U analysis (Chiribella & Ebler, 2016), as well as learning tasks (Bisio
1054   et al., 2010; Sedlák et al., 2019). It can also be used for analyzing more general processes (Zhu
1055   et al., 2024) and has also inspired structures like the indefinite causal network (Chiribella et al., 2013;
