009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) have demonstrated significant potential in neu-
012            ral rehabilitation. However, the variability of non-stationary neural signals often
013            leads to instabilities of behavioral decoding, posing critical obstacles to chronic
014            applications. Domain adaptation technique offers a promising solution. Nonethe-
015            less, the existing direct adaptation within latent spaces could result in feature
016            deviations. Therefore, developing a stable and efficient alignment framework is
017            crucial for neural decoders. In this work, we find that dynamical latent features
018            can be extracted from neural dynamics utilizing causal architectures. We also
019            demonstrate that the process of self-consistent alignment can generate more stable
020
               latent features. Based on these insights, we propose a novel hierarchical domain
               adaptation (HDA) method for the alignment of dynamical latent features. Us-
021
               ing Lyapunov theory, we further analytically validate the stability of dynamical
022
               features, which experimentally exhibit significant enhancements across various
023            datasets. Our HDA approach effectively addresses the challenge of non-stationary
024            neural signals, thereby potentially improving the reliability of BCIs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) offer a direct pathway for connecting the brain with external devices,
031   demonstrating great potential in neural rehabilitation for people with paralysis (Collinger et al., 2018;
032   Chaudhary et al., 2016; Willett et al., 2021; Metzger et al., 2023; Willett et al., 2023). Despite recent
033
      advances, one key challenge for BCIs is how to maintain stable performance, considering that the
      non-stationary neural recordings could vary across days (Perge et al., 2013; Wimalasena et al., 2020).
034
      The variability in neural signals could stem from various factors, such as environmental conditions
035
      (Santhanam et al., 2007), device degradation (Woeppel et al., 2021), physiological changes (Athalye
036   et al., 2017) to foreign materials, and behavioral changes (Truccolo et al., 2008). Consequently,
037   frequent recalibration of a BCI system is necessary to maintain its performance, leading to a critical
038   barrier to chronic applications (Pandarinath & Bensmaia, 2022).
039
      To alleviate the burden of recalibration, some studies aimed to develop automatic decoder adjustment
040
      approaches to cope with variability in neural signals(Wimalasena et al., 2020; Degenhart et al., 2020).
041
      One strategy is to align the neural signals across multiple days. These approaches allow neural
042   decoders trained on one day to apply to another day directly. To achieve this, unsupervised domain
043   adaptation (UDA) techniques have been employed to align the distributions of neural signals across
044   different recording sessions. Existing UDA approaches for BCIs can be categorized into two types.
045   The first type performs the distribution alignment in raw neural signal spaces (Farshchian et al., 2018;
046   Ma et al., 2023). The second type aligns on the latent feature spaces and seeks for the spatio-temporal
047   relationships in neural signals (Degenhart et al., 2020; Ju & Guan, 2022; Kobler et al., 2022; Cho
048   et al., 2023; Jude et al., 2022).
049   Unfortunately, unlike conventional data such as images and videos, aligning neural signals is more
050   challenging due to the inherently non-stationary nature of neural activities (Gallego et al., 2020).
051   Directly aligning raw neural signals (Farshchian et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2023) or latent features (Kar-
052   powicz et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023) may result in unstable features for decoding. Therefore, it
053   is crucial to develop a stable and efficient alignment framework, thereby achieving a stable feature
      space for robust neural decoders.


                                                         1
054
      Existing researches have shown that the brain executes various functions by converging towards
055   attractors (Khona & Fiete, 2022), which are linked to dynamical stability in response to neural
056   perturbations. Inspired by these observations, we propose that dynamical latent features can be
057   extracted from neural dynamics utilizing causal architectures (Chen et al., 2024). Furthermore, we
058   show that the process of self-consistent alignment within neural systems promotes the generation of
059   more stable dynamical latent features. Building on these findings, we introduce a novel framework
060   of hierarchical domain adaptation (HDA) that efficiently aligns dynamical latent features. Through
061   validation grounded on Lyapunov theory (Angeli, 2002; Jiang & Wang, 2001), we analytically
062   demonstrate that HDA enhances the dynamical stability of latent features, achieving stable neural
063
      decoders over extended periods. Experimental validation of HDA reveals significant improvements
      across various datasets. Our HDA approach effectively tackles the challenge of non-stationary neural
064
      signals, potentially improving the reliability of BCIs.

085   Unsupervised Domain Adaptation Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) aims to bridge the
086   gap between labeled source domain(s) and unlabeled target domains by matching their distributions.
087
      Some studies have achieved by minimizing discrepancies based on specific metrics (Peng et al.,
      2019a; Sun et al., 2016; Sun & Saenko, 2016), such as the maximum mean discrepancy (Long et al.,
088
      2015; 2017a). Inspired by Generative Adversarial Networks, another line of research utilizes domain
089
      adversarial training to obtain domain-invariant features (Saito et al., 2018; Sankaranarayanan et al.,
090   2018; Chen et al., 2020; Long et al., 2018). For instance, the widely-used Domain Adversarial Neural
091   Network (DANN) (Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015; Ganin et al., 2016) optimizes feature extractors to
092   generate domain-invariant features that confuse the trained domain classifier.
093
      As mentioned in Section 1, for stabilizing BCI decoding over time, UDA-based alignment approaches
094
      have been utilized for unsupervised recalibrations within raw signal and latent feature spaces. Re-
095
      cently, consistent low-dimensional latent dynamics have been leveraged as the latent features for
096   alignment (Karpowicz et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Vermani et al., 2023; Pandarinath et al., 2018;
097   Fang et al., 2023; Safaie et al., 2023). These latent dynamics, situated within the neural manifold
098   (Degenhart et al., 2020; Gallego et al., 2017; Mitchell-Heggs et al., 2023), are assumed to provide a
099   stable underlying representation of behavior.
100
      Nevertheless, some intrinsic features, including low signal-to-noise ratios (Hu et al., 2010), frequently
101
      result in instabilities when attempting to align the high-dimensional raw neural signals (Wang et al.,
102   2023). Meanwhile, alignment in latent spaces typically assumes its stability ensured by neural
103   manifolds (Gallego et al., 2017; Mitchell-Heggs et al., 2023), lacking further consideration for
104   the dynamical stability of latent features. For instance, NoMAD (Karpowicz et al., 2022) and the
105   source-free and unsupervised alignment (Vermani et al., 2023) directly match latent dynamics, which
106   may overlook the potential instability of the source domain‚Äôs extracted latent features. s In contrast,
107   our method proposes a novel hierarchical alignment based on causal architectures in neural dynamics.
      We demonstrate that the iterative process of self-consistent alignment can generate more stable latent


                                                         2
108
      features. This optimization enhances the dynamical stability of latent features, thereby stabilizing the
109   neural manifolds against stochastic perturbations.
110
111   Representation Disentanglement Representation Disentanglement has been used in UDA to learn
112
      domain-invariant features. In the field of computer vision, researchers have successfully applied this
      technique to disentangle semantic latent features for tasks such as cross-domain image classification
113
      (Cai et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2022) and video action recognition (Wei et al., 2023).
114
      Various methods have been explored, including reweighting source features for meta knowledge
115   transfer (Wei et al., 2021) and utilizing deep adversarial autoencoders (Peng et al., 2019b). In the
116   realm of time series analysis, researchers have disentangled semantically meaningful factors to control
117   the shape of ECG signals (Li et al., 2022).
118
      In neural data analysis, researchers have focused on building robust and generalizable representations
119
      using advanced networks such as transformers (Ye & Pandarinath, 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Le & Shliz-
120
      erman, 2022). Recently, unified frameworks have been proposed to enable scalable representations
121   across sessions and subjects (Azabou et al., 2023). To achieve this, representation disentanglement
122   has been employed to understand how different neural populations encode diverse external stimuli
123   and their intrinsic connections with observable behavioral variables. Supervised learning techniques,
124   supported by auxiliary variables (Zhou & Wei, 2020), along with self-supervised learning approaches,
125   such as contrastive learning (Cheng et al., 2020) or swapping (Liu et al., 2021), are used to identify
126   latent variables that are directly related to observable variables.
127   Existing disentanglement approaches for neural data analysis primarily focus on learning domain-
128   invariant representations directly, without performing distribution alignment. Such methods may
129   work well when source domains contain sufficient samples of various sessions and tasks (Wang et al.,
130   2020; Parnami & Lee, 2022). Considering that UDA often requires little data (Ma et al., 2023), they
131   are more practical when less source data is available. In this study, we propose HDA, and leverage
132   disentanglement techniques as a tool to decompose latent spaces for better distribution alignment.
133   The use of UDA with disentanglement techniques to stabilize BCI decoding performance has not
134   been explored.

      We begin by aligning the distribution of target signals with that of the source signals. The characteristic
      of spike signals is their capability to capture neural activities at the neuron level (Buzs√°ki, 2004),
186
      which ensures the sparse dependencies among different channels (Chen et al., 2010; Bighamian
187
      et al., 2019). In comparison to latent feature spaces, where spatio-temporal dependencies are more
188   intricate, the raw neural signal spaces may offer a more direct causal relationship that facilitates
189   the identification of units affected by drifts. This identification may help to align the distribution
190   of the raw signals and forms the basis of our approach to enhance feature stability. Additionally,
191   aligning input raw neural signals prior to optimizing system parameters enables a more statistically
192   consistent input representation for our dynamical system. This approach contrasts with those that
193   apply shared-parameter encoders to derive latent features directly from the original signals across
194   source and target domains (Wei et al., 2023; 2021).
195   Considering the unique properties of biological systems, we found that probability densities based
196   on individual samples may be more accurate to measure distribution discrepancies. This is because
197   sufficient statistics, such as the mean, typically characterize the collective properties of random
198   variables. However, the characteristics of individual samples are often more critical due to the
199   common presence of outliers in biological systems (Gomez-Ramirez & Sanz, 2013). Therefore, we
200   chose the f -divergence, which is based on probability density functions, to measure the discrepancy
201   between distributions. However, since f -divergence is difficult to compute directly, we employed
202
      GANs to implement alignment based on f -divergences in an indirect manner. Given that naive GANs

203
      which is a specific case of f -divergence. The benefits of alignment based on f -divergences are

      demonstrated in Fig. 7(b).

      3.2.3   A DAPTATION IN DYNAMICAL S EMANTIC L ATENT S UBSPACE
241
242   When performing a specific task, the brain processes a wide range of information, including per-
243   ception, decision-making, environmental cues, feedback, and more. For instance, task-irrelevant
244   perceptual information and environmental feedback are also encoded within the latent dynamics.
245   By decomposing these latent spaces to remove irrelevant components, we aim to reduce variability
246   within the latent dynamics, thereby improving alignment of latent spaces. Specifically, inspired
247   by previous studies mentioned in Section 2 and the high parallelism (W√§ssle, 2004) of brains, we
248
      hypothesize that the drifts of dynamical latent features in the target domain primarily stem from latent
      variables that are loosely connected to observable behavioral variables. Based on this hypothesis,
249
      we believe that constructing a semantic subspace, by extracting components of the latent space that
250
      are directly related to behavioral variables, can effectively reduce the drift of neural population
251   dynamics. Furthermore, we have found that, compared to directly aligning latent features, performing
252   alignment only within the semantic subspace can provide the dynamical system with a more efficient
253   self-consistent feedback, guiding it to obtain more stable latent dynamics.

404
405   Baselines We used the following methods as baselines, more details are shown in Appendix B.2:
406   LSTM(Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997): We employed an unaligned LSTM as the decoder to
407   evaluate the efficacy of alignment.
408   CEBRA(Schneider et al., 2023): CEBRA is a machine learning method that compresses time series
409   to uncover hidden structures, demonstrating broad generalizability across various datasets and
410   conditions.
411
      DAF(Jin et al., 2022) DAF leverages an attention mechanism to extract domain-invariant features
      while retaining domain-specific details through a shared module, domain discriminator, and private
412
      modules.
413
      ERDiff(Wang et al., 2023) ERDiff utilizes diffusion models to meticulously reconstruct spatio-
414   temporal structures and seamlessly aligns them with the latent dynamics extracted from VAEs.
415   NoMAD(Karpowicz et al., 2022): NoMAD achieves signal alignment in neural manifolds by
416   capturing the latent dynamics of neural population activities via LFADS (Pandarinath et al., 2018).
417   Cycle-GAN (Ma et al., 2023): This research employs Cycle-GAN to align the distributions of
418   full-dimensional neural recordings at each time step.
419
420   Results We conducted quantitative comparisons using average R2 of target domains. Day 0 corre-
421   sponds to DS , while the other sessions represent DT . Results for CO-M and RT-M are presented
422   in Table 1, with results for the CO-C dataset shown in Table 8. Our method consistently outperforms
423   others across most sessions of the selected datasets. The unaligned LSTM and CEBRA designed
424   for generalizable representations frequently fail over extended periods, demonstrating the need for
425   distribution alignment to stabilize decoding performance. Regarding existing UDA-based alignment
426   methods, HDA achieves over 6.00% and 10.00% higher R2 on average compared to Cycle-GAN for
427   CO-M and RT-M, respectively. Compared to NoMAD, we achieve over 10.00% and 8.00% higher on
428
      average. It can also be seen that HDA achieves much better performance than ERDiff and DAF. In
      addition, we visualized reach trajectories of CO-M integrated from the decoded cursor velocity. As
429
      shown in Appendix C.2, we observe that HDA yields more precise trajectories.
430
431   In addition, HDA consistently demonstrates effective performance across various source days. To
      validate this, we conducted additional experiments across all sessions, with the overall performance

      illustrated in Fig. 6(a). We also conducted experiments using Day 0 as the source session, with source
433   and target training ratios set at 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6. As illustrated in Fig. 6(b), HDA shows effective
434   performance even with a relatively small number of trials.

528   5          C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   In this study, we addressed the challenge of the decoding instability in BCIs caused by the variability
531   of neural signals over time. We present a novel hierarchical domain adaptation (HDA) that focuses
532   on neural dynamics. This framework utilizes causal architecture to extract dynamical latent features,
533   and improves the feature stability based on the self-consistent alignment. The experimental analysis,
534   supported by Lyapunov stability theory, demonstrate that our HDA can effectively improve the
535   stability of dynamical systems, allowing for high-performance behavioral decoding for non-stationary
536
      neural signals. Our work successfully addressed the challenge of non-stationary neural signals,
      thereby potentially advancing the reliability of BCIs in chronic applications.
537
538   The limitations of this study are as follows. For scenarios with abundant data, HDA requires further
539   verification to extract more generalizable features, enabling zero-shot inference. Additionally, the
      extension of HDA to other datasets involving humans needs validation.

844   A.2     LYAPUNOV S TABILITY T HEORY
845
846   A.2.1    R ELATED W ORK
847
848   Lyapunov stability examines the stability of latent state trajectories within a dynamical system when
849
      its initial conditions or external inputs experience perturbations (Jiang & Wang, 2001). This concept,
      introduced by Lyapunov (Lyapunov, 1992), has been widely utilized in the stability analysis of various
850
      dynamical systems, including discrete linear systems (Goh, 1977) and nonlinear non-autonomous
851
      systems (Jiang et al., 1996). With the advent of deep learning, neural networks such as Recurrent
852   Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) are frequently employed
853   to model complex nonlinear dynamical systems, with their hidden variables corresponding to the
854   system‚Äôs latent states. Some studies have integrated deep learning with Lyapunov stability to explore
855   stability during network training (Engelken et al., 2023) and the network robustness (Ribeiro et al.,
856   2020).
857
      In the field of neuroscience, dynamical systems are frequently employed to model cognitive processes
858   (Beer, 2000) and neural activities within the motor cortex (Ijspeert et al., 2013). The stability theory
859   has also been leveraged to analyze these neural activities. For example, studies have identified that
860   discrete attractors in the prefrontal cortex (Inagaki et al., 2019) are related to Lyapunov stability and
861   lay the foundation for the working memory performance of animals undertaking delayed alternation
862   tasks. Inspired by these insights and considering the presence of stable dynamical systems within the
863   brain, we integrated the concept of Lyapunov stability with the process of extracting stable latent
      features from non-stationary neural signals.

      A.2.2    R ATIONALE FOR VALIDATION VIA LYAPUNOV S TABILITY
865
866   In biological systems, similar behaviors often manifest analogous activities within neural populations.
867   However, neural signals from the target domain may deviate from expected similarities with the
868   source domain due to various factors. These stochastic factors can cause drifts in any stochastic
869   dimensions. Here, we argue that Lyapunov stability effectively characterizes the stability of extracted
870
      latent features against random perturbations in original signals. That is to say, the enhancement in
      Lyapunov stability of the dynamical system indicates the stabilization of dynamical latent features.
871
      Therefore, we utilize Lyapunov stability as a tool for a quantitative representation of the system‚Äôs
872
      stability to validate the stability of dynamical latent features.

921   B.2   BASELINE I MPLEMENTATION
922
923   CEBRA. CEBRA is a sophisticated machine-learning technique developed for the analysis and
924   compression of time series data, particularly enhancing the study of behavioral and neural data. This
925   method is capable of uncovering hidden structures within data variability and has been successfully
926   applied to decode activity in the mouse brain‚Äôs visual cortex, even reconstructing what a subject has
927   viewed. The code is available from https://github.com/AdaptiveMotorControlLab/cebra.
928   DAF. The Domain Adaptation Forecaster (DAF) utilizes abundant data from a relevant source domain
929   to enhance performance in a target domain with limited data. DAF employs an attention-based shared
930   module with a domain discriminator and private modules for each domain, promoting the extraction
931   of domain-invariant latent features while simultaneously retraining domain-specific features. Our
932   approach effectively aligns keys from the source and target domains, allowing for effective knowledge
933   transfer despite differing characteristics. Extensive experiments show that DAF outperforms state-of-
934
      the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets, and ablation studies confirm the efficacy
      of our design choices.
935
936   ERDiff. This work proposes leveraging diffusion models to first extract latent dynamic structures in
937   the source domain, then recover them well in the target domain through maximum likelihood align-
938   ment. Empirical evaluation on synthetic and neural recording datasets demonstrates this approach
939   outperforms others by better preserving latent dynamic structures longitudinally and between individ-
940
      uals. We implement this based on the openly available code at https://github.com/yulewang97/ERDiff.
941   NoMAD. NoMAD leverages the latent manifold structure inherent in neural population activity
942   to establish a stable link between brain activity and motor behavior. It demonstrates the ability to
943   provide accurate and highly stable behavioral decoding over extended periods, eliminating the need
944   for supervised recalibration. In this study, we implemented NoMAD using the LFADS code available
945   at https://github.com/arsedler9/lfads-torch/tree/main. As a result, there may be some deviations from
946
      the original implementation.
947   Cycle-GAN. This work proposes utilizing Cycle-GAN to align the distributions of full-dimensional
948   neural recordings and stabilize the original decoding model without requiring recalibration. Through
949   evaluating Cycle-GAN and a related approach (ADAN) on multiple monkey and task datasets,
950   Cycle-GAN demonstrated superior performance for robustly maintaining BCI accuracy longitudinally
951   without additional training. As the study utilizes the same datasets, we directly implement its openly
952
      available code from https://github.com/limblab/adversarial_BCI.
