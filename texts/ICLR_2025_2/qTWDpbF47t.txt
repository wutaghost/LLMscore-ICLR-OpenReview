                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Large-scale Text-to-Video (T2V) diffusion models have recently demonstrated
013            unprecedented capability to transform natural language descriptions into stunning
014
               and photorealistic videos. Despite these promising results, a significant challenge
               remains: these models struggle to fully grasp complex compositional interactions
015
               between multiple concepts and actions. This issue arises when some words dom-
016
               inantly influence the final video, overshadowing other concepts. To tackle this
017            problem, we introduce Vico, a generic framework for compositional video gen-
018            eration that explicitly ensures all concepts are represented properly. At its core,
019            Vico analyzes how input tokens influence the generated video, and adjusts the
020            model to prevent any single concept from dominating. Specifically, Vico extracts
021            attention weights from all layers to build a spatial-temporal attention graph, and
022            then estimates the influence as the max-flow from the source text token to the video
023            target token. Although the direct computation of attention flow in diffusion models
024            is typically infeasible, we devise an efficient approximation based on subgraph
025
               flows and employ a fast and vectorized implementation, which in turn makes the
               flow computation manageable and differentiable. By updating the noisy latent to
026
               balance these flows, Vico captures complex interactions and consequently produces
027
               videos that closely adhere to textual descriptions. We apply our method to multiple
028            diffusion-based video models for compositional T2V and video editing. Empirical
029            results demonstrate that our framework significantly enhances the compositional
030            richness and accuracy of the generated videos.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Humans recognize the world compositionally. That is to say, we perceive and understand the world
037   by identifying parts of objects and assembling them into a whole. This ability to recognize and
038   recombine elements—making “infinite use of finite mean”—is crucial for understanding and modeling
039   our environment. Similarly, in the realm of generative AI, particularly in video generation, it is crucial
040   to replicate this compositional approach.
041
      Despite advancements in generative models, current diffusion models fail to capture the true compo-
042   sitional nature of inputs. Typically, some words disproportionately influence the generative process,
043   leading to visual content that does not reflect the intended composition of elements. While the
044   compositional text-to-image sythesis (Liu et al., 2022; Chefer et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023;
045   Feng et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023) has been more studied, the challenge of compositional video
046   generation has received less attention. This oversight is largely due to the high-dimensional nature of
047   video and the complex interplay between concepts and motion.
048   As an illustration, we highlight some failure cases in Figure 1 (Left), where certain words dominate
049   while others are underrepresented. Common issues include missing subject and spatial confusion,
050   where some concepts do not appear in the video. Even with all concepts present, semantic leakage
051   can occur, causing attributes amplified incorrectly, for example, the prompt of a bird and a cat is
052   misinterpreted as a bird looks like a cat. A challenge specific to T2V is Motion Mixing, where
053   the action intended for one object mistakenly interacts with another, such as generating a flying
      wale instead of flying balloon.

      To address these challenges, we present Vico, a novel framework for compositional video generation
081
      that ensures all concepts are represented equally. Vico operates on the principle that, each textual
082
      token should have an equal opportunity to influence the final video output. At our core, Vico
083   first assesses and then rebalances the influence of these tokens. This is achieved through test-time
084   optimization, where we assess and adjust the impact of each token at every reverse time step of our
085   video diffusion model. As shown in 1, Vico resolves the above questions and provides better results.
086
      One significant challenge is accurately attributing text influence. While cross-attention (Tang et al.,
087
      2023; Mokady et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2023; Rassin et al., 2024) provides faithful attribution in text-
088
      to-image diffusion models, it is not well-suited for video models. It is because such cross-attention is
089   only applied on spatial modules along, treating each frame independently, without directly influencing
090   temporal dynamics.
091
092
      To surmount this, we develop a new attribution method for T2V model, termed Spatial-Temporal
      Attention Flow (ST-flow). ST-flow considers all attention layers of the diffusion model, and views it
093
      as a spatiotemporal flow graph. Using the maximum flow algorithm, it computes the flow values, from
094
      input tokens (sources) to video tokens (target). These values serve as our estimated contributions.
095
096   Unfortunately, this naive attention max-flow computation is, in fact, both computationally expensive
097   and non-differentiable. We thus derive an efficient and differentiable approximation for the ST-Flow.
098
      Rather than computing flow values on the full graph, we instead compute the flow on all subgraphs.
      The ST-Flow is then estimated as the maximum subgraph flow. Additionally, we have develop a
099
      special matrix operation to compute this subgraph flow in a fully vectorized manner, making it
100
      approximately 100× faster than the exact ST-flow.
101
102   Once we obtain these attribution scores, we proceed to optimize the model to balance such contribu-
103   tions. We do this as a min-max optimization, where we update the latent code, in the direct that, the
104
      least represented token should increase its influence.
105   We implement Vico on multiple video applications, including text-to-video generation and video
106   editing. These applications highlight the framework’s flexibility and effectiveness in managing
107   complex prompt compositions, demonstrating significant improvements over traditional methods in
      both the accuracy of generated video. Our contributions can be summarized below:

      With above formulation, our focus is to develop an efficient and precise attribution Ai . Recognizing
202
      issues with cross-attention, we instead calculate Ai as the flow through the entire attention graph.

204   Flawed Cross-Attention in Text-to-Video Models. Cross-attention score has been instrumental
205   in attributing (Tang et al., 2023) and controlling layout and concept composition in text-to-image
206
      models (Hertz et al., 2022; Chefer et al., 2023; Rassin et al., 2024). However, applying it to T2V
      diffusion model introduces new problem.
207
208   This problem arises because T2V models typically employ cross-attention on spatial tokens
209   only (Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). It treats the video as a sequence of
210   independent images, and temporal self-attention mixes tokens across different frames. Consequently,
211   this separation hinders cross-attention’s ability to capture video dynamics, making it challenging to
212
      manage actions across frames.
213   For example, applying the cross-attention-based DAAM attribution (Tang et al., 2023) on
214   VideoCrafterv2 reveals significant issues in visualization. As shown in Figure 3 (Left), cross-
215   attention leads to a flickering pattern in the attention maps, failing to consistently highlight the target
      object across frames.

347   Baselines. We build our method on several open-sourced video diffusion model, including
348
      VideoCrafterv2 (Chen et al., 2024), AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2024) and Zeroscopev2 5 . Since
      no current compositional generation method are specifically designed for video, we re-implement
349
      several methods designed for text-to-image diffusion models and compare with them. These methods
350
      include:
351
352   • Original Model. We directly ask the original base model to produce video based on prompts.
353
354
      • Token Re-weight. We use the compel 6 package to directly up-lift the weight of specific concept
        token, with a fixed weight of 1.5.
355
356   • Compositional Diffusion (Liu et al., 2022). This method directly make multiple noise predictions
357     on different text, and sum the noise prediction as the compositional direction for latent update. In
358     our paper, given a prompt, we first split into short phrases. For example “a dog and a cat”
359     is splitted into “a dog” and “a cat”, make individual denoising, and added up.
360   • Attend-and-Excite (Chefer et al., 2023). A&E refines the noisy latents to excite cross-attention
361     units to attend to all subject tokens in the text prompt.
362
363   Besides those training-free methods, we also includes some recent work that retrain the diffusion
364   model for compositional generation. These includes LVD (Lian et al., 2023) and VideoTetris (Tian
365   et al., 2024).

516
517   In this subsection, we ablate our two key designs: the loss function and the proposed ST-Flow.
518
519
      Loss Function. We modified the loss function from using P      the “min” as a fairness indicator (as
      described in Sec 3.1) to a variance loss, defined as Lfair = − i (Ai − Ā)2 . This aims to minimize
520
      the differences between each Ai and the average attribution value Ā, making it fair. The results is
521
      shown in Table 5, row 3 and 4. We notice while the variance loss ensures uniformity across all tokens,
522
      it overly restricts them, often degrading video quality. Conversely, our original min-loss focuses on
523   the least represented token, enhancing object composition accuracy without significantly affecting
524   overall quality.
525
526
      ST-Flow v.s. Cross-Attention. A major contribution of our work is the development of ST-Flow and
      its efficient computation. We compared it against a model using cross-attention, where cross-attention
527
      maps are extracted and a mean score is computed for each token as Ai . As demotivated in Table 5,
528
      row 2 and 4, using ST-Flow (soft) largely outperform cross-attention. We also provide the running
529   speed analysis in Appendix G, confirming the efficiency of our approach.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we present Vico, a framework designed for compositional video generation. Vico starts
535   by analyzing how input tokens influence the generated video. It then adjusts the model to ensure
536   that no single concept dominates. To implement Vico practically, we calculate each text token’s
537   contribution to the video token using max flow. This computation is made feasible by approximating
538   the subgraph flow with a vectorized implementation. We have applied our method across various
539   diffusion-based video models, which has enhanced both the visual fidelity and semantic accuracy of
      the generated videos.

      A    P ROOF OF T HEOREM 1: S UB - GRAPH F LOW
811
812
      In a network G = (V, E) with a capacity function c : E → R+ , and a subgraph g of G, the maximum
813   flow fg in g is less than or equal to the maximum flow fG in G.
814
815
      P ROOF
816
817        1. Definition of a Subgraph: A subgraph g of G can be defined as g = (V ′ , E ′ ) where
818           V ′ ⊆ V and E ′ ⊆ E. All capacities in g are inherited from G, i.e., c′ (e) = c(e) for all
819           e ∈ E′.
820        2. Flow Conservation: Both G and g must satisfy the flow conservation law at all intermediate
821           nodes. That is, the sum of the flow entering any node must equal the sum of the flow
822           exiting that node, except for the source (where flow is generated) and the sink (where flow
823           is absorbed).
824        3. Reduced Set of Paths: Since E ′ ⊆ E, every path through g is also a path through G, but
825           not every path through G is necessarily a path through g. This reduction in the number of
826           paths (or edges) in g implies that some routes available for flow in G are not available in g.
827        4. Capacity Limitations: For any edge e in E ′ , the capacity in g (i.e., c′ (e)) equals the
828           capacity in G (i.e., c(e)). Therefore, no edge in g can support more flow than it can in G.
829           Additionally, since some edges might be missing in g, the overall capacity of pathways from
830           the source to the sink might be less in g than in G.
831        5. Maximum Flow Reduction: Given the reduction in paths and capacities, any flow that is
832           feasible in g is also feasible in G, but not vice versa. Hence, the maximum flow fg that can
833           be pushed from the source to the sink in g must be less than or equal to the maximum flow
834           fG that can be pushed in G.
835
836   Conclusion: From these points, it follows directly that the maximum flow in a subgraph g cannot
837   exceed the maximum flow in the original graph G. This proves that fg ≤ fG .

883   Video Diffusion Models. Video diffusion models generate video frames by gradually denoising a
884   noisy latent space (Ho et al., 2022b). One of the main challenges with these models is their high
885   computational complexity. Typically, the denoising process is performed in the latent space (Zhou
886   et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b;a). The architectural commonly adopt either a 3D-UNet (Ho et al.,
887   2022b; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Ho et al., 2022a; Harvey et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023a) or diffusion
888   transformer (Gupta et al., 2023; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024). To enhance computational
889   efficiency, these architectures often employ separate self-attention mechanisms for managing spatial
890   and temporal tokens. Conventionally, training these models involves fine-tuning an image-based
891
      model for video data (Wu et al., 2023a; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024). This process
      includes adding a temporal module while striving to preserve the original visual quality.
892
893   Despite their ability to generate photorealistic videos, these models frequently struggle with under-
894   standing the complex interactions between elements in a scene. This shortcoming can result in the
895   generation of nonsensical videos when responding to complex prompts.
896   Compositional Generation. Current generative models often face challenges in creating data from a
897   combination of conditions, with most developments primarily in the image domain. Energy-based
898   models (Du et al., 2020; 2023; Liu et al., 2023), for example, are mathematically inclined to be
899   compositionally friendly, yet they require the conditions to be independent. In practice, many
900   image-based methods utilize cross-attention to effectively manage the composition of concepts (Feng
901   et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b; Rassin et al., 2024). However, when it comes to
902   video, compositional generation introduces additional complexities. Some video-focused approaches
903
      concentrate specific form of composition, including object-motion composition (Wei et al., 2023),
      subject-composition (Wang et al., 2024b), utilize explicit graphs to control content elements (Bar
904
      et al., 2021). Others incorporate multi-modal conditions (Wang et al., 2024a), additional training
905
      data (Tian et al., 2024), or auxiliary modules (Lian et al., 2023). Despite these efforts, a generic
906   solution for accurately generating videos from text descriptions involving multiple concepts is still
907   lacking. We present the first training-free solution for compositional video generation using complex
908   text prompts, an area that remains largely under-explored.
909
      Attribution Methods. Attribution methods clarify how specific input features influence a model’s
910
      decisions. gradient-based methods (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Simonyan et al., 2013; Selvaraju et al.,
911
      2017) identify influential image regions by back-propagating gradients to the input. Attention-based
912   methods (Chefer et al., 2021; Abnar & Zuidema, 2020) that utilize attention scores to emphasize
913   important inputs. Ablation methods(Ramaswamy et al., 2020; Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) modify data
914   parts to assess their impact. Shapley values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017a) distribute the contribution
915   of each feature based on cooperative game theory. In our paper, we extend existing techniques of
916   attention flow to video diffusion models. We develop an efficient approximation to solve the max-flow
917   problem. This improvement helps us more accurately identify and balance the impact of each textual
      elements on synthesized video.
