009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Many real-world multi-agent or multi-task evaluation scenarios can be naturally
012            modelled as normal-form games due to inherent strategic (adversarial, coopera-
013            tive, and mixed motive) interactions. These strategic interactions may be agentic
014            (e.g. players trying to win), fundamental (e.g. cost vs quality), or complimentary
015            (e.g. niche finding and specialization). In such a formulation, it is the strate-
016
               gies (actions, policies, agents, models, tasks, prompts, etc.) that are rated. How-
               ever, the rating problem is complicated by redundancy and complexity of N-player
017
               strategic interactions. Repeated or similar strategies can distort ratings for those
018
               that counter or complement them. Previous work proposed “clone-invariant” rat-
019            ings to handle such redundancies, but this was limited to two-player zero-sum (i.e.
020            strictly competitive) interactions. This work introduces the first N-player general-
021            sum clone-invariant rating, called deviation ratings, based on coarse correlated
022            equilibria. The rating is explored on several domains including LLMs evaluation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Data often captures relationships within a set (e.g., chess match outcomes) or between sets (e.g.,
      film ratings by demographics). These sets can represent anything including human players, machine
028
      learning models, tasks, or features. The interaction data, often scalar (win rates, scores, or other
029
      metrics), may be symmetric, asymmetric or arbitrary. These interactions can be strategic, either in
030   an agentic sense (e.g., players aiming to win) or due to inherent trade-offs (e.g., cost vs quality). This
031   can lead to a game-theoretic interpretation: sets as players, elements as strategies, and interaction
032   statistics as payoffs. This framing is common in analyzing strategic interactions between entities
033   like Premier League teams, chess players (Sanjaya et al., 2022), reinforcement learning agents and
034   tasks (Balduzzi et al., 2018), or even language models (Chiang et al., 2024). More generally, the
035   idea of formulating real-world interactions as normal-form games, empirical game-theoretic analysis
036   (Wellman, 2006), is well explored.
037   The data obtained from such interactions are numerous so it is common to distill the performance of
038   each strategy into a single scalar. Such a process is called a rating method. Many ratings have been
039   proposed including Elo (Elo, 1978), Bradley-Terry (Bradley & Terry, 1952; Zermelo, 1929), Glicko
040   (Glickman, 1995), TrueSkill (Herbrich et al., 2007), Nash averaging (Balduzzi et al., 2018), payoff
041   rating (Marris et al., 2022), α-Rank (Omidshafiei et al., 2019), and some based on social choice
042   theory (Lanctot et al., 2024).
043   Although the real world is a complex multi-agent system, data evaluation rarely utilizes more than
044   two players, nor non-zero-sum interactions. For instance, the leading language model leaderboard,
045   Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024), is most naturally formulated as a three-player general-sum
046   game (model vs. model vs. prompt). Where model players are competing to be best on each prompt,
047   and a prompt player may favour difficult prompts, or prompts that can best distinguish between
048   models. However, due to limitations of Elo, Chatbot Arena is only evaluated as a two-player zero-
049   sum game (model vs. model) which overlooks interesting nuances of the rating problem, such as
050
      specialized models excelling on specific prompt subsets.
051   As well as being N-player general-sum, a rating method should also be scalable, resilient, and direc-
052   tional. Scalability, in the context of rating, is best motivated by Balduzzi et al. (2018). Performance
053   on different tasks may measure identical skills. Over-representation of particular skills introduces
      biases into ratings. Such biases may only be detected post-hoc. Therefore, humans must curate


                                                          1

054
      evaluation datasets to prevent redundant strategies skewing the results. Manual curation does not
055   scale and a scalable rating method should “adjust automatically and gracefully to redundant data”.
056   For example, Chatbot Arena evaluates models on a wide variety of prompts which tests various
057   underlying skills. Without curation or a scalable rating, the evaluation will be influenced by the
058   distribution of prompts collected by users of Chatbot Arena, a population that may not represent
059   the needs of all users. Resilience to manipulation is also important for rating schemes. In Chatbot
060   Arena, any prompt can be submitted by anyone, any number of times. It would be possible to inflate
061   a model’s score by submitting many similar prompts that a model is known to excel at, even if the
062   model is holistically not as strong. Finally a directional rating is one that is useful for driving con-
063
      tinual improvement. Huge investment of resources is driven by companies hill-climbing on LLM
      leaderboards. It would be beneficial if hill-climbing ratings led to maximally improved models.
064
065   The three qualities discussed above can be achieved by a property known as “clone invariance”1 :
066   where copying strategies does not alter the ratings. The property is so important it has been con-
067   tinually rediscovered in multiple fields, notably by Nash averaging (Balduzzi et al., 2018), maximal
068   lotteries (Kreweras, 1960; Fishburn, 1984; Brandt, 2017), and Yao’s Principle (Yao, 1977), as well
069
      as others (Conitzer et al., 2024; Brandl et al., 2016; Laffond et al., 1993; Fisher & Ryan, 1995;
      Felsenthal & Machover, 1992; Rivest & Shen, 2010). Clone invariant methods are scalable because
070
      if one added redundant data it will not affect the rating. This allows evaluators to be maximally in-
071
      clusive: all data can be included and no curation is necessary. Similarly, clone invariant methods are
072   resilient to clone attacks: artificially skewed evaluation distributions will not change the resulting
073   ratings. Clone invariant ratings offer better directional improvement. Hill-climbing on game theo-
074   retic evaluation methods has been shown to give better performance ([citation withheld to maintain
075   anonymity], 2024).
076
      All the clone-invariant methods are game-theoretic and involve computing a Nash equilibrium (NE)
077
      distribution. NE is convex and tractable to compute in two-player zero-sum games. However, in
078   general NE is non-convex and intractable to compute in N-player general-sum games. In particular
079   there are many disjoint equilibria, and it is not clear how to choose one (equilibrium selection prob-
080   lem (Harsanyi & Selten, 1988)). Unfortunately, for this reason, all current clone-invariant methods
081   only work for two-player zero-sum games.
082
      This work circumvents the problems with NE equilibrium selection by focusing on a related solution
083
      concept, coarse correlated equilibrium (CCE) (Hannan, 1957; Moulin & Vial, 1978). The result is
084   the first N-player, general-sum, clone-invariant rating method: deviation rating. Deviation ratings
085   are equilibrium based, and select for the strictest – most stable – equilibrium. Deviation ratings can
086   be computed efficiently with linear programming. They are also unique, always exist, are offset
087   invariant and dominance preserving. This work proves these properties hold for deviation ratings
088   and the rating is explored in a number of contexts including the topical problem of LLM evaluation.

093   Normal-Form Games Normal-form games (NFGs) model single timestep, simultaneous action
094   strategic interactions between any number of N players. Each player, p ∈ [1, N ], selects a strategy
                                        |A |
095   from a set ap ∈ Ap = {a1p , ..., ap p }. A particular strategy is indexed by aip , and ap is a variable

      3       R ATING D ESIDERATA
132
133
      A strategy rating, rp : Ap 7→ R, assigns a scalar to strategies. Similarly, a ranking, rp : Ap 7→ N,
      defines a (partial) ordering over strategies. Rankings can be inferred from ratings. Ratings attempt to
134
      summarize how good a strategy is in relation to the other available strategies, in the strategic context
135
      of an NFG.

      3.1       D ESIDERATA
138
139   There are several desiderata for formulating rating methods including tractability, permutation
140   equivariance, and robustness. This section presents and extends important desiderata that are partic-
141   ularly important in game theoretic rating.
142
143   Dominance Preserving If a strategy dominates another, Gp (ãp , a−p ) ≥ Gp (âp , a−p ) ∀a−p , then a
144         dominance preserving rating should result in ratings, rp (ãp ) ≥ rp (âp ).
145   Clone Invariance Consider adding an additional strategy to a game ãp , which is a copy of existing
146           strategy such that G̃p (ãp , a−p ) = Gp (âp , a−p ) ∀a−p . A clone invariant rating would result
147           in ratings r̃p (ãp ) = r̃p (âp ) = rp (âp ) and r̃p (ap ) = rp (ap ) ∀p, ap (equal and unchanged
148           from original ratings).
149
      Mixture Invariance Consider adding an additional strategy to a game ãp , which is a mixture of the
150
             existing strategies such that G̃p (ãp , a−p ) = ap σ̃(ap )Gp (ap , a−p ). A mixture invariant2
                                                             P
151                                                      P
             rating would result in ratings r̃p (ãp ) = ap σ̃(ap )rp (ap ), and unchanged original ratings.
152
153   Offset Invariance Consider a game Gp ∀p ∈ [1, N ], and another game G̃p ∀p ∈ [1, N ], where
154            G̃p (ap , a−p ) = Gp (a) + bp (a−p ) ∀p ∈ [1, N ], and bp (a−p ) ∈ R ∀a−p ∈ A−p is an
155            arbitrary offset. An offset invariant rating would have ratings rp (ap ) = r̃p (ap ) ∀p ∈
156            [1, N ], ap ∈ Ap .
157
      Generality Some rating strategies are only defined for NFGs with particular structure in the game.

164   Uniform
         1

166   to compute, and is dominance preserving. However, it is not clone invariant nor offset invariant.
167
168   Elo Elo (Elo, 1978) is only defined for symmetric two-player zero-sum games. Elo is popular be-
169   cause one can infer the approximate win probability between two strategies by just comparing their
170   relative ratings. It has a stochastic update rule and is widely using in sports ratings. However, it is not
171   clone invariant nor offset invariant, and has a number of other well-documented drawbacks (Shah &
172   Wainwright, 2018; Balduzzi et al., 2018; Bertrand et al., 2023; Lanctot et al., 2023).

178   to a strategy by Nash averaging is their expected payoff under this maximum entropy Nash equi-

180
      Nash equilibrium, can be extended to other solutions concepts beyond two-player zero-sum games,

181

182
183
      Voting Methods Another way to compare strategies is to rank them rather than rate them; one
184
      way to do so is using social choice theory (i.e. voting mechanisms). Voting-based evaluations
185   have been used for multi-task benchmarks in NLP domains (Rofin et al., 2023) and for general
186   agent evaluation (Lanctot et al., 2023). The main advantage of these methods is that they inherit
187   certain robustness properties, such as clone-invariance (Fishburn, 1984). The main disadvantage is
188   that the quantification of the strength of an assessment (comparison between strategies) is lost by
189   construction due to ordinal outcomes.
190
191   α-Rank One alternative to the payoff rating mentioned above is a mass rating (Marris et al., 2022),
192   which corresponds to the probability mass of a strategy in an equilibrium (i.e. rp (ap ) = σp (ap )).
193   One such mass rating scheme is α-Rank (Omidshafiei et al., 2019). However, instead of using
194   the mass of a Nash equilibrium, α-Rank defines the rating of a strategy as its mass in the stationary
195
      distribution of a dynamical system between sets of pure strategies known as a Markov-Conley chain.

197   4     D EVIATION R ATING
198
199   Typically, the approach for developing game theoretic rating algorithms is to find an equilibrium,
200   and calculate a rating based on that equilibrium. This requires choosing a solution concept and
201   uniquely selecting a single equilibrium from a set. This is not difficult, for example a maximum-
202   entropy coarse correlated equilibrium (MECCE) satisfies these properties. However, if we wish the
203   rating to be clone invariant, the equilibrium selection method needs to somehow be rating-consistent
204   between a game and a larger game containing a clone. This property is hard to achieve for N-player
205
      general-sum games. For example, an MECCE would spread probability mass differently in the
      expanded game resulting in different ratings. An NE based rating, would have consistent ratings,
206
      provided one could reliably select for the same equilibrium each time. Chen et al. (2009) showed
207
      that NE problems do not admit an FPTAS unless PPAD ⊆ P.
208
209   To overcome these problems we side-step selecting a rating-consistent equilibrium, and instead se-
210   lect for unique deviation gains, δpσ (a′p ) (Equation (3a)). We then define ratings from the deviation
211
      gains. We propose a game theoretic rating scheme based on CCEs.

235   4.1      A LGORITHM
236
      This work’s primary innovation is in how we select deviation gains in a way that preserves clone
237
      invariance. The two properties such a selection operator must have are: a) permutation equivariance
238   and, b) clone invariance. The maximum and minimum functions are two functions with this prop-
239   erty4 . Maximizing the deviation gains is counter-intuitive because it does not result in equilibria,
240   and if you limited the procedure to ϵ ≤ 0, it would likely find rpCCE (a′p ) = 0 ∀p, a′p because there
241   are many more degrees of freedom in σ, than there are in the deviation gains. Therefore we opt to
242   minimize the deviation gains (which is equivalent to finding the strictest equilibrium). Concretely,
243   iteratively minimize the maximum deviation gain, freezing active constraints at each iteration (Al-
244   gorithm 1).
245   Each iteration requires solving a linear programming (LP) (Murty, 1983) sub-problem. The inner
246   max operator is implemented using a slack variable and inequality constraints. Each inequality
247   constraint has an associated dual variable. Nonzero dual variables indicate that the constraint is
248   active and can be frozen. There will always be at least one active constraint at optimum, therefore
249   each iteration
              P is guaranteed to freeze at least one more constraint. Therefore the algorithm requires
250   at most p |Ap | outer iterations.
251   This process results in unique ratings and a possibly non-singleton set of CCE equilibria that all
252   evaluate to the same rating. Because the ratings are calculated under an equilibrium, there are no
253   strategies that a player has incentive to deviate to. The recursive procedure used to calculate the
254   deviation ratings select the strictest possible equilibrium. Deviating from such an equilibrium will
255   ensure loosing the maximum amount of payoff, and therefore this equilibrium is the most stable.
256   Strict equilibria tend to have higher payoff, therefore the equilibrium selection criterion is a natural
257   one, where strategies that can give high payoffs in practice are rated highly.

342   5.1   R ATINGS IN C YCLIC AND C OORDINATION E NVIRONMENTS
343
344   Shapley’s game (Shapley, 1964) (Shoham & Leyton-Brown, 2009, p210) is a symmetric general-
345
      sum variant of rock-paper-scissors with losing payoffs for each player if they play the same strategy.
      Therefore it is a cyclic anti-coordination game. In the unbiased form of the game, there is a single
346
      mixed Nash equilibrium, [ 13 , 13 , 13 ]. We consider a biased version of such a game (Table 2a) with a
347                                          87 100 54
      single mixed Nash equilibrium [ 241       , 241 , 241 ].
348
349   A uniform rating of the strategies produces a transitive ranking R ≻ P ≻ N ≻ S (Table 2b).
350   This is because, ignoring strategic interactions, the biases separate the strategies. For example,
351
      rock is particularly effective at defeating scissors, and all possible opponents are considered equally
      when using uniform rating. This is unrealistic because if scissors is vulnerable, one may expect
352
      to encounter that strategy less frequently and therefore perhaps less attention should be placed on
353
      strategies that defeat it. Furthermore, the uniform rating scheme ranks the Nash strategy second last.
354   This is unfortunate because the Nash strategy is the only unexploitable pure strategy in this game,
355   and arguably should be ranked the highest. In contrast, the deviation rating result in equal ratings
356   R = P = S = N. From a game theoretic perspective, this makes intuitive sense: while rock, paper,
357   and scissors all appear in a cycle, and dominate each other, no strategy can be said to be better than
358   another. Similarly, the Nash strategy is a special mixture of the others such that it has the same
359   expected payoff, therefore it should also be rated equally.
360   Now let us sample mixed policies from the biased Shapley game to produce a population of strate-
361   gies, resulting in an expanded symmetric NFG with number of strategies equal to the number of
362   samples. Each strategy is a mixture of the “pure” strategies: R, P, and S. We analyse the ratings of
363   strategies in populations drawn from different distributions to observe how the distribution affects
364   the ratings.
365
      Firstly, consider unbiased sampling (Figure 1a). The uniform rating still rates rock the highest. The
366   other strategies in the population are rated linearly across the space with R ≻ P ≻ S. Deviation
367   ratings continue to rank all strategies equally (due to mixture invariance). Interestingly, equilibrium
368   mass is placed only on the convex hull of the population. Now, consider a biased population where
369   most mixtures play close to paper (Figure 1b). The uniform rating now favours scissors which
370   counters paper: S ≻ R ≻ P. However, deviation rating continues to rate all strategies equally. It is
371   clear that by manipulating the distribution, the uniform rating can be made to rate any of R, P or S
372   the highest. While the deviation rating will always rate them equally.
373   Slightly restricting the domain of the population (Figure 1c), means there is still a cycle, also does
374   not affect the ratings. A population with only minority scissor players (Figure 1d) should favour
375   paper. There is no longer a cycle, and in a world of rock and paper, paper is king. However there is
376   still an anti-coordination aspect to the game which is why both R and P get probability mass under
377   the equilibrium. The uniform rating rates the most mixed strategy the highest because it is best at
      avoiding coordination across the distribution.

      5.2      L ANGUAGE M ODEL R ATING
402
403   There are many leaderboards for evaluating LLMs including Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024),
404   where language models are evaluated on pairwise matchups on a prompt. The model that gives the
405   better response wins. The final ratings are aggregate Elo ratings over many prompts. The ratings are
406   published as a popular and trusted leaderboard of LLMs. However, the Elo ratings depend on the
407   distribution of prompts that are submitted. Therefore popular prompts will drive the ratings. People
408   who submit prompts to Chatbot Arena may not be representative of end users of LLMs nor the tasks
409   they wish to perform with them. Companies developing LLMs may miss important functionalities
410
      if they optimize only for such benchmarks.
411   A more game theoretic approach would be to evaluate the models in the context of a three player
412   game: prompt vs model vs model. The model players’ payoffs are symmetric zero-sum evaluations
413   over every prompt. The prompt player’s payoff is the maximum of the two model players: GP (a) =
414   maximum[GMA (a), GMB (a)] = |GMA (a)| = |GMB (a)|. Therefore the prompt player either has a
415   zero-sum or common-payoff interaction with each model player, depending on who is winning the
416
      prompt, and favours selecting prompts that separate the models.
417   Because Chatbot Arena only has comparison data between two models for each prompt, and we
418   require all models to be evaluated, we instead focus on another benchmark: Livebench (White et al.,
419   2024). Livebench evaluates language models across 18 tasks (curated sets of prompts) resulting in a
420   model vs task dataset. Evaluating models against tasks using the methodology discussed in Balduzzi
421   et al. (2018) is unsatisfying (Lanctot et al., 2023) because models are adversarially evaluated against
422
      the hardest tasks.
423   Our actual objective is to evaluate models relative to other models, in the context of tasks. Therefore,
424   from the model vs task data T (m, t) (Figure 2c) 5 , let us construct a three player model vs model
425   vs task game with payoffs: GA (mA , mB , t) = T (mA , t) − T (mB , t), GB = −GA , GT = |GA | =
426   |GB |. This is similar to the Chatbot Arena game formulation but is derived from only model vs task
427   data.
428   Uniform and Elo in this game result in close to identical ratings (Figure 2a). The deviation
429   ratings place four models equally at the top: claude-3-5-sonnet, gemini-1.5-pro,
430   Llama-3.1-405B and gpt-4o. The grouping property is typical of game theoretic solvers

      6       C ONCLUSION
528
529   This work introduces deviation rating, a novel rating algorithm that produces unique, dominance
530   preserving, clone invariant, mixture invariant, and offset invariant ratings for the most general class
531   of N-player general-sum normal-form games. The method is the first clone-invariant rating algo-
532   rithm for N-player general-sum games. Ratings can be formulated as sequential linear programs,
533   and therefore many off-the-shelf solvers can compute the ratings in polynomial time. Such a rating
534
      scheme allows for scalable, maximally inclusive, clone-attack-proof, data agnostic rating as it natu-
      rally weights strategies according to their relevance in a strategic interaction. Clones and mixtures
535
      do not affect ratings at all. The rating is applicable in general strategic interactions and we highlight
536
      its utility in rating LLMs.

      A     BACKGROUND ON R ATING M ETHODS
811
812
813   A.1   E LO
814
815   Elo (Elo, 1978) is the most well-known rating system for rating players in symmetric two-player
816   zero-sum games, and is most famous for its use in Chess. Its main feature is that the ratings of two
817
      players can be used to predict the win probability. The probability player A beats player B can
      be calculated p̂AB = (1 + 10400rB −400rA )−1 , where 400 and 10 are arbitrary constants that scale
818
      the ratings. The main advantage of Elo is that it can be updated using sparse samples of player
819
      interactions: it does not require knowing the dense win-probabilities between all players.
820
821   If one did know the win probabilities, pij , Elo can be formulated as the solution, minr L, to a logistic
822   regression problem. This form is sometimes known as BayesElo.
823                                P
824                           L=      ij − pij log10 (p̂ij ) − (1 − pij ) log10 (1 − p̂ij )                (7)
825
826
827
      Elo has a number of well-known drawbacks (Shah & Wainwright, 2018; Balduzzi et al., 2018;
      Bertrand et al., 2023; Lanctot et al., 2023). Most importantly, it only works for symmetric two-player
828
      games with probability payoffs, it is not clone-invariant, and can suffer large error in predictions.
829
830   Like most other rating methods, Elo cannot account for intransitive payoffs (e.g., rock-paper-scissor-
831   like cycles), because a player’s strength has to be summarized by a single number. By allowing
832   multiple parameters per-player, it may be possible to capture cycles in the data. Multidimensional
833
      Elo (Balduzzi et al., 2018) is an extension that has this property.
834   Despite its drawbacks Elo remains popular in machine learning. It is used as the rating method in
835   Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024), and is used to measure the performance of game-playing agents
836   (Silver et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2019).
837
838
839   A.2   S OCIAL C HOICE
840
841   Social choice theory focuses study on voting systems. There are a number of desirable criteria
842   voting systems can hold. Two commonly studied criteria are the “independence of clones criterion”
843
      (Tideman, 1987) and “independence of irrelevant alternatives” (IIA) (Shoham & Leyton-Brown,
      2009, p261). Independence of clones is the same as clone-invariance discussed in the main text. It
844
      concerned with the problem of similar candidates splitting the vote and changing election results. If
845
      a voting system has the IIA property, it means that the position of two agents {A, B} in the ranking
846   depends only the relative ordering of {A, B} in the voters’ preferences, not e.g., on some other
847   irrelevant agent C.
848
849
      An impossibility result, Arrow’s impossibility theorem (Arrow, 1950), stated that there is no de-
      terministic rating rule that satisfies both independence of clones and independence of irrelevant
850
      alternatives. However it was shown that one could get around this by casting voting as a two-player
851
      zero-sum game, using probabilistic choices, and finding a minimax solution (von Neumann & Mor-
852   genstern, 1947). The payoffs of the game are integers, Gp (a) ∈ Z ∀a ∈ A, where each element
853   corresponding to a comparison between two alternatives, say A and B. The payoff is defined as the
854   number of voters that prefer a to b minus the number of voters that prefer A to B. The resulting
855   method, maximal lotteries (Kreweras, 1965; Fishburn, 1984; Brandt, 2017), was the first method
856   shown to satisfy both independence of clones and independence of irrelevant alternatives. Maximal
857   lotteries is so fundamental it has been rediscovered multiple times (Conitzer et al., 2024; Brandl
858   et al., 2016; Laffond et al., 1993; Fisher & Ryan, 1995; Felsenthal & Machover, 1992; Rivest &
859   Shen, 2010).
860   Nash averaging (Balduzzi et al., 2018) is similar to maximal lotteries, but Nash averaging allows
861   for arbitrary two-player zero-sum payoffs, Gp (a) ∈ R ∀a ∈ A. For example, in the original paper,
862   a game is derived directly from agent scores in the Atari Learning Environment Bellemare et al.
863   (2013). Solving for a Nash equilibrium is equivalent to a minimax solution in two-player zero-sum
      games.

864
      A.3    E QUILIBRIUM -BASED R ATINGS
865
866   Nash averaging (Balduzzi et al., 2018) rediscovered maximal lotteries but with a notable general-
867   ization: it uses arbitrary two-player zero-sum payoffs. Balduzzi et al. (2018) motivated the rating in
868   the context of evaluating general machine learning models, where models are required to perform
869   well on a variety of different tasks, with each task testing potentially overlapping skills. Although
870   the work focused on reinforcement learning agents, the problem is now also important for language
871   models which are required to respond correctly to a wide variety of prompts.
872   A key property of Nash averaging is that it is clone-invariant. This means that repeated strategies
873   in the game do not affect the ratings of the other strategies, which is important in the context of
874   evaluating agents against tasks which measure overlapping skills. This allows the rating problem to
875   be maximally inclusive with evaluation data, avoids the need for curation, and is therefore scalable.
876   Work to extend Nash averaging, such as payoff ratings (Marris et al., 2022), utilizes equilibria to
877   rate strategies in N-player general-sum games. However, clone-invariance is not guaranteed.
878
879   A.4    E VOLUTIONARY-BASED R ATINGS
880
881   Another game-theoretic rating method, α-Rank (Omidshafiei et al., 2019), is the stationary distri-
882   bution of a dynamical system between sets of pure strategies know as a Markov-Conley chain. The
883   ratings are the resulting mass distributions over strategies. Strategies with more mass are rated
884   higher.
885
886   A.5    OTHER M ETHODS
887
      There are many other ratings methods, including TrueSkill (Herbrich et al., 2007), Glicko (Glick-
888
      man, 1995), and voting-as-evaluation (Lanctot et al., 2023).

891   B     P RACTICAL C OMPUTATION
892
893
      Algorithm
      P           1 sequentially solves linear programs (LPs). In the worst case, deviation ratings require
894      p |Ap | outer iterations (the number of constraints in the deviation gains). The LP inner loop can
      be solved using many algorithms (simplex (Dantzig, 1956), ellipsoid (Khachiyan, 1979)) for which
895
      there are many off-the-shelf solvers (GLOP (Perron & Furnon), Gurobi (Gurobi Optimization, LLC,
896
      2024), ECOS (Domahidi et al., 2013), OSQP (Stellato et al., 2020)) and many frameworks (CVXPY
897   (Diamond & Boyd, 2016; Agrawal et al., 2018)). LPs can be solved in polynomial time (Khachiyan,
898   1979). Therefore deviation ratings can also be solved in polynomial time. Because the algorithm
899   solves a similar problem multiple times it is advantageous to leverage disciplined parameterized
900   programming (DPP) (Agrawal et al., 2019) to eliminate the need to recompile the problem at each
901   outer iteration. Additionally, because the problem is solved repeatedly, care needs to be taken to
902   minimize the accumulation of errors.
903
904   B.1    S YMMETRIES
905
906
      Exploit all symmetries in the problem to improve conditioning, and reduce solve time. There
      are three main symmetries that can be removed: payoff symmetries, joint symmetries, and con-
907
      straint/strategy symmetries. These symmetries are best dealt with by manipulating the constraint
908
      matrix, A, with shape [C, |A1 |, ..., |AN |].
909
910   Payoff Symmetries Frequently, the payoffs may be symmetric across two players by construction
911   (for example in model vs model). Incorporating this information has two benefits. Firstly, it reduces
912   the number of variables to optimize over by half. Secondly, it makes the optimization problem
913   less ill-conditioned. For example, the simplex algorithm may suffer from “small pivots” if payoff
914   symmetries are not removed.
915
      To remove payoff symmetries modify the constraints payoff by averaging over the symmetry per-
916   mutations. For example, in a two player symmetry across players p and q:
917
                  A[c, ..., ap , ..., aq , ...] = 12 (A[c, ..., ap , ..., aq , ...] + A[c, ..., aq , ..., ap , ...]) (8)

      This will result in a constraint matrix, when viewed flat, A[c, a], with repeated columns. These
919   repeated columns can be pruned (see joint symmetries below).
920
921   Doing this preprocessing step will mean that only symmetric equilibria can be found. This is ideal
922
      for our purposes and will not alter any rating values.
923
924   Joint Symmetries Columns in the constraint matrix (which correspond to joint strategies) may be
925
      repeated. This can occur if there are payoff symmetries, repeated strategies, or because of naturally
      occurring structure. Under the objectives we optimize for, probability mass can be arbitrarily mixed
926
      between repeated joint strategies without changing the deviation gains. Therefore we only need
927
      to track one of these joints. Counts should be tracked, to a final full dimensional joint can be
928   reconstructed after a solution has been found.
