009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning systems deployed in real-world applications often encounter data
012            that is different from their in-distribution (ID). A reliable system should ideally
013            abstain from making decisions in this out-of-distribution (OOD) setting. Existing
014            state-of-the-art methods primarily focus on feature distances, such as k-th near-
015            est neighbors and distances to decision boundaries, either overlooking or inef-
016            fectively using in-distribution statistics. In this work, we propose a novel angle-
017            based metric for OOD detection that is computed relative to the in-distribution
018
               structure. We demonstrate that the angles between feature representations and de-
               cision boundaries, viewed from the mean of in-distribution features, serve as an
019
               effective discriminative factor between ID and OOD data. Our method achieves
020
               state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet benchmarks, reducing
021            FPR95 by 0.88% and 7.74% respectively. Our score function is compatible with
022            existing feature space regularization techniques, enhancing performance. Addi-
023            tionally, its scale-invariance property enables creating an ensemble of models for
024            OOD detection via simple score summation.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   A trustworthy deep learning system should not only produce accurate predictions, but also recognize
030   when it is processing an unknown sample. The ability to identify when a sample deviates from the
031   expected distribution, and potentially rejecting it, plays a crucial role especially in safety-critical
032   applications, such as medical diagnosis (Fernando et al., 2021), driverless cars (Bogdoll et al., 2022)
033
      and surveillance systems (Diehl & Hampshire, 2002). The out-of-distribution (OOD) detection
      problem addresses the challenge of distinguishing between in-distribution (ID) and OOD data –
034
      essentially, drawing a line between what the system knows and what it does not.
035
036   Various approaches have been proposed for OOD detection, mainly falling into two categories: (i)
037   methods that suggest model regularization during training (Lee et al., 2018a; Hendrycks et al.;
038   Meinke & Hein), and (ii) post-hoc methods, which leverage a pre-trained model to determine if a
039
      sample is OOD by designing appropriate score functions (Peng et al., 2024; Hendrycks & Gimpel,
      2022; Sun et al., 2022). Post-hoc methods are more advantageous for their efficiency and flexibility,
040
      as they can be applied to arbitrary pre-trained models without retraining. These approaches are often
041
      categorized based on the domain of their score functions, i.e., at which representational abstraction
042   level they assess if a sample is OOD or not. Earlier techniques focus on measuring the model confi-
043   dence in the logits space (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2022; Liu et al., 2020), while the recent works em-
044   ploy distance-based scores (Sun et al., 2022; Sehwag et al., 2021) defined in the model feature space.
045   While logit-based methods suffer from the overconfident predictions of neural networks (Minderer
046   et al., 2021; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2017), the recent success of distance-based
047   techniques highlights that the relationships in the latent space can provide a richer analysis.
048   A natural approach to feature representations is by checking their proximity to the decision bound-
049   aries (Liu & Qin, 2024). Conceptually, this can be related to identifying hard-to-learn examples in
050   data-efficient learning (Joshi et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023). OOD samples can be viewed as hard-
051   to-learn since they do not share the same label distribution as ID data. The success of this approach
052   has been directly showed in fDBD score from Liu & Qin (2024). However, our derivations revealed
053   that the regularization term they use to incorporate ID statistics introduces an additional term that
      does not correlate with ID/OOD separation, ultimately hindering their performance.

054
      In this work, we present Look Around and Find Out (LAFO), a novel approach that exploits the
055   relationship between feature representations and classifier decision boundaries, in the context of the
056   mean statistics of ID features. Unlike the earlier techniques, LAFO introduces a new angle-based
057   measure that calculates the angles between the feature representations and their projection onto the
058   decision boundaries, relative to the the mean statistics of ID features. Changing reference frame to
059   the mean of ID features adds another layer of discriminatory information to the score, as it naturally
060   incorporates the ID statistics to the distance notion, exploiting the disparity between ID and OOD
061   statistics. Moreover, the scale-invariant nature of angle-based representations, as similarly observed
062   in (Moschella et al.), allows us to aggregate the confidence scores from multiple pre-trained models
063
      simply by summing their LAFO scores. This enables to have a score that can be single model based
      or extended to ensemble of models. In summary, our key contributions include:
064
065
             • We present a novel technique for OOD detection, which computes the angles between the
066            feature representation and its projection to the decision boundaries, relative to the mean of
067            ID-features
068
             • Our score is model agnostic, hyperparameter-free and efficient, scaling linearly with the
069
               number of ID-classes. Therefore, it can flexibly be combined with various architectures
070            without the need of additional tuning.
071
             • We demonstrate the state-of-the-art performance of LAFO on widely used CIFAR-10 and
072
               ImageNet OOD benchmarks. Specifically, LAFO achieves a 7.74% reduction in FPR95
073
               score compared to the best previous distance-based method on a large-scale ImageNet OOD
074            benchmark.
075
             • The scale-invariant property of LAFO allows for straightforward aggregation of confidence
076
               scores from multiple pre-trained models, improving ensemble performance. Our experi-
077
               ments show that the ensemble with LAFO reduces the FPR95 by 2.51% on the ImageNet
078            OOD benchmark compared to the best single model performance.

082
083   Previous work in OOD detection falls into two categories: (i) methods that regularize models during
084
      training to produce different outcomes for ID and OOD data, and (ii) post-hoc methods that develop
      scoring mechanisms using pre-trained models on ID data.
085
086
      Model Regularization Early methods addressing the OOD detection problem ?Hendrycks et al.;
087
      Mohseni et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021) utilize additional datasets to represent out-of-distribution
088
      data, training models with both positive and negative samples. This approach assumes a specific na-
089   ture of OOD data, potentially limiting its effectiveness when encountering OOD samples that deviate
090   from this assumption during inference. Malinin & Gales (2018) designed a network architecture to
091   measure distributional uncertainty using Dirichlet Prior Networks. In Geifman & El-Yaniv (2019)’s
092   work, they provide another architecture with an additional reject option to abstain from answer-
093   ing. Their selection model incorporates a hyperparameter, the coverage rate for ID, to control the
094   percentage of ID samples classified. Lee et al. (2018a); Ming et al. (2022); Du et al., focused on
095   synthesizing outliers rather than relying on auxilary datasets to improve the generalizability of the
096   detection method. On the other hand, Meinke & Hein; Van Amersfoort et al. (2020); Wei et al.
097
      (2022) argued that overconfident predictions of the networks on OOD data are the problem to be
      mitigated. For example, Van Amersfoort et al. (2020) puts an additional gradient penalty to limit
098
      the confidence of the network. Whereas, Wei et al. (2022) tackled the same problem by enforc-
099
      ing a constant logit vector norm during training. Although it is natural to impose structures during
100   training for better separability of ID and OOD, these methods face the trade-off between OOD sep-
101   arability and model performance. Moreover, such approaches lack the flexibility of post-hoc score
102   functions, as they necessitate model retraining—a process that can be both time-consuming and
103   computationally expensive.

194   3.2   OOD D ETECTION WITH R ELATIVE A NGLES
195
196   This section presents our OOD score function, which relates the feature representations with the
197   decision boundaries using relative angles in the feature space to discriminate between ID and OOD
198   samples. Figure 1 provides a geometric visualization of our method. Our approach leverages the
199   geometric relationships between three key points in the feature space: (i) the initial representation of
200
      a sample, (ii) its projection onto the decision boundary, and (iii) the mean of in-distribution features.
201   We propose using the relation between feature representations and decision boundaries by deriving
202   closed-form plane equations for the decision boundaries between any two classes. Specifically,
203   we examine the angle formed between the feature representation vector and its projection onto the
204   decision boundary. However, this angle is sensitive to the choice of origin, creating an ambiguity as
205   the geometric relationship between the feature representation and the decision boundary should be
206
      translation-invariant. To address this, we propose to represent features in a reference frame relative
      to the mean of the in-distribution samples. Therefore, we incorporate in-distribution characteristics
207
      by centering around its mean, while ensuring scale and translation invariance.
208
209   We observe that the angle between the centered representation and its projection onto the decision
210   boundary is larger for ID data, indicating them requiring higher cost to change their label which
211   captures the model’s confidence. In contrast, for OOD data, angle is smaller since they are expected
212
      to be more unstable, as they do not contain strong clues about their predicted classes (see Figure 1
      for a conceptual explanation and the ID/OOD histograms in Appendix A.1 for empirical evidence).
213
214   Our framework provides a concise scoring with useful properties such as translation and scale in-
215   variance. These properties enable LAFO to be used in conjunction with existing activation shaping
      algorithms and allow for confidence aggregation across different models through score summation.

      5   C ONCLUSION
493
494
      In this paper, we introduce a novel angle-based OOD detection score function. As a post-hoc mea-
495   sure of model confidence, LAFO offers several key advantages: it is (i) hyperparameter-free, (ii)
496   model-agnostic and (iii) scale-invariant. These features allow LAFO to be applied to arbitrary pre-
497   trained models and used in conjunction with existing activation shaping algorithms, enhancing the
498   performance. Notably, its scale-invariant nature enables simple aggregation of multiple models’
499   confidences through score summation, allowing a creation of an effective model ensemble for OOD
500   detection. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that LAFO achieves state-of-the-art performance,
501   using the relationship between the feature representations and decision boundaries relative to the ID
502   statistics effectively. Despite the state-of-the-art performance achieved by LAFO, one possible lim-
503
      itation might be the use of the mean alone to capture the ID statistics in our score. As a future
      direction, we plan to mitigate this possible limitation by incorporating multiple relative angles to
504
      better capture the ID-statistics beyond the mean, aiming to further improve OOD detection perfor-
505
      mance. We hope that our approach inspires further research into geometric interpretations of model
506   confidence for OOD detection.
