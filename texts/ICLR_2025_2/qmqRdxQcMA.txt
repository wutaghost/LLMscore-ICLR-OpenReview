                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012            This paper introduces a cost-efficient active learning (AL) framework for classi-
013            fication, featuring a novel query design called candidate set query. Unlike tradi-
014
               tional AL queries requiring the oracle to examine all possible classes, our method
               narrows down the set of candidate classes likely to include the ground-truth class,
015
               significantly reducing the search space and labeling cost. Moreover, we leverage
016
               conformal prediction to dynamically generate small yet reliable candidate sets,
017            adapting to model enhancement over successive AL rounds. To this end, we in-
018            troduce an acquisition function designed to prioritize data points that offer high
019            information gain at lower cost. Empirical evaluations on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
020            and ImageNet64x64 demonstrate the effectiveness and scalability of our frame-
021            work. Notably, it reduces labeling cost by 42% on ImageNet64x64.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Deep neural networks owe much of their success to large-scale annotated datasets (Deng et al.,
028   2009b; Kirillov et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Radford et al., 2021). Scaling datasets is crucial for
029   improving both of their performance (Hestness et al., 2017; Zhai et al., 2022) and robustness (Fang
030   et al., 2022). However, the resources demanded for manual annotation pose a significant bottleneck,
031   particularly in fields requiring expert input like medical data. In response to these challenges, cost-
032   efficient methods for dataset collection, such as semi-automatic labeling (Kim et al., 2024; Qu et al.,
033
      2024; Wang et al., 2024), synthetic data generation (Liu et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2019), and active
      learning (AL) (Ash et al., 2020; Kirsch et al., 2019; Sener & Savarese, 2018; Settles, 2009; Sinha
034
      et al., 2019; Wang & Ye, 2015) have been studied.
035
036   This paper investigates AL for classification, where a training algorithm selects informative samples
037   from the data pool and queries annotators for their class labels within a limited budget. We focus
038   on improving the design of annotation queries, emphasizing their critical role. To be specific, we
039
      consider image classification of L classes. In a conventional design of query, an annotator is asked
      to choose a class in the list of L classes. Here, the effort needed to review the entire class list and
040
      identify the correct class increases as the list size L increases; according to an information-theoretic
041
      analysis (Hu et al., 2020), the cost of choosing among L options is log2 L. To address this issue
042   of growing annotation cost, recent studies (Hu et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2024) employ a 1-bit query
043   design asking annotators to check if the top-1 model prediction is correct. While this simplifies and
044   speeds up annotation, it produces weak supervision incompatible with standard classification loss
045   functions, necessitating specialized losses and algorithms like contrastive loss and semi-supervised
046   learning techniques.
047
      We propose candidate set query (CSQ), a novel AL query design that remains cost-efficient with
048   increasing classes and integrates seamlessly with existing loss functions. CSQ presents the annotator
049   with an image and a narrowed set of candidate classes, which is likely to include the ground-truth
050   class. If the ground-truth class is within these candidates, the annotator selects from this smaller
051   group; otherwise, they select from the remaining classes. This query approach can reduce labeling
052   costs by reducing the search space required for annotation, particularly effective in scenarios with
053   a wide range of classes where the search space for the annotator would be extensive. Fig. 1(left)
      compares CSQ with the conventional query in AL for classification to show its efficiency.

      In the CSQ framework, the design of the candidate set is crucial for its effectiveness. Too many
075
      candidates unnecessarily increase the labeling costs. On the other hand, too few candidates are
076
      likely to omit the ground-truth class, requiring additional queries to identify the true class among
077   the remaining classes, which can sometimes be more expensive than the conventional query. To
078   enhance the effectiveness of the CSQ framework, we propose to construct candidate sets guided
079   by prediction uncertainty from a trained model using conformal prediction (Angelopoulos et al.,
080   2023). Conformal prediction aims at constructing a set of predictions including the true class, where
081   each set is properly sized based on the certainty of the model about the input. This strategy enables
082   flexible adjustment of the candidate set for each sample, expanding it for an uncertain sample to
083   include the true label and shrinking it for more certain one to reduce the labeling cost. Furthermore,
084   we optimize the level of certainty in conformal prediction to minimize the labeling cost for each
085
      round. Therefore, this candidate set construction adapts to the increasing accuracy of the model
      over successive AL rounds, refining the candidate set as the model improves.
086
087   Last but not least, we propose a new acquisition function designed to maximize the cost efficiency of
088   CSQ. Conventional acquisition functions in AL are designed to favor samples with high estimated
089   information gain, assuming uniform annotation costs across all samples. On the other hand, in CSQ,
090   the labeling cost for each sample varies according to the size of its candidate set. Thus, we propose
091
      an acquisition function that evaluates samples based on the ratio of estimated information gain to
      labeling cost. Specifically, we combine the conventional acquisition function score, which indicates
092
      the estimated information gain, with the estimated cost derived from the candidate set, favoring
093
      samples that maximize information gain per unit cost. This cost-efficient acquisition function can
094   incorporate with any sample-wise acquisition score, ensuring the selection of both informative and
095   cost-efficient samples.
096
097
      The proposed method achieved state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009),
      CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and ImageNet64x64 (Chrabaszcz et al., 2017). We verify the
098
      effectiveness and robustness of CSQ through extensive experiments with varying datasets, acquisi-
099
      tion functions, and budgets. Notably, CSQ achieves the same performance as the conventional query
100   on ImageNet64x64 at only 42% of the cost, showing its scalability. Ablation studies demonstrate
101   that both our candidate set construction and sampling strategy contribute to the performance. Fur-
102   ther, the necessity of CSQ is demonstrated by a user study involving 40 participants. In short, the
103   main contribution of this paper is four-fold:
104
105     • We propose a novel query design for active learning, where the annotator is presented with an
106       image and a narrowed set of candidate classes that are likely to include the ground-truth class.
107       This approach, termed CSQ, significantly reduces labeling cost by minimizing the search space
          the annotator needs to explore.

118   2     R ELATED W ORK
119
120   Acquisition functions in AL. AL is a well-established problem Settles (2009); Dasgupta (2011);
121
      Hanneke et al. (2014) that focuses on selectively querying the most informative samples for anno-
      tation to maximize model performance within a limited budget. To assess informativeness, vari-
122
      ous acquisition functions have been proposed, considering either the uncertainty of model predic-
123
      tions (Asghar et al., 2017; He et al., 2019; Ostapuk et al., 2019; Fuchsgruber et al., 2024), diversity
124   in feature space (Sener & Savarese, 2018; Sinha et al., 2019; Yehuda et al., 2022), or both (Ash
125   et al., 2020; Hwang et al., 2022; Wang & Ye, 2015; Wang et al., 2019). Disagreement-based AL and
126   its variants are supported by rigorous theoretical learning guarantees (Hanneke et al., 2014; Krish-
127   namurthy et al., 2019). Recent studies have demonstrated that the choice of acquisition functions
128   depends on the budget, with uncertainty being more suitable for a high budget and typicality for a
129   low budget (Hacohen et al., 2022; Hacohen & Weinshall, 2023a). In addition, a look-ahead acqui-
130   sition function that considers nearby samples simultaneously (Kim et al., 2024) and the selection
131   of easily flip-flopped samples (Cho et al., 2024) have also been proposed. However, these methods
132
      assume that all samples require the same cost and select samples based solely on the amount of
      information. We point out that the cost required for each sample can vary and prioritize selecting
133
      samples that offer the most information considering their cost.
134
135   Conformal prediction (CP). CP enables us to quantify uncertainty in predictions with associated
136   confidence levels (Shafer & Vovk, 2008). Recent advances in CP empower classifiers to generate
137   predictive sets that include the true label with a probability chosen by the user (Angelopoulos et al.,
138
      2020). Additionally, in the field of AL, nonconformity measurements from CP are employed in the
      acquisition function to select informative samples (Matiz & Barner, 2020). In contrast, we utilize
139
      CP not only to develop a cost-efficient acquisition function but also to design an efficient candidate
140
      set query reducing the labeling cost.
141
142   Efficient query design. Designing efficient annotation queries reduces the annotation costs of craft-
143   ing datasets. In various computer vision tasks, diverse types of queries have been investigated, in-
144
      cluding conventional classification queries (Hacohen & Weinshall, 2023b) requiring a specific class,
      one-bit queries (Hu et al., 2020) asking for yes or no answers, multi-class queries (Hwang et al.,
145
      2023) identifying all classes within a set of multiple instances, and correction queries (Kim et al.,
146
      2024) utilizing pseudo labels from the model. However, existing queries remain stagnant in their
147   predefined forms regardless of the model’s performance improvement in successive AL rounds. The
148   proposed candidate set query is cost-efficient while provides complete supervision which can be
149   integrated seamlessly with existing loss functions.

180   3.1   C ANDIDATE SET QUERY
181
182   Candidate set query (CSQ) for an instance x is associated with a (non-empty) candidate set Ŷ (x) ⊆
183   Y such that 1 ≤ |Ŷ (x)| ≤ L. CSQ first asks the annotator to choose the ground-truth class in Ŷ (x)
184   (if exists) or to verify the absence of the ground-truth label in Ŷ (x), i.e., the annotator is first asked
185   to pick an option out of (k + 1) choices, where k = |Ŷ (x)|. Only if the absence of the ground-truth
186   class in the candidate set is verified, the annotator is further asked to select the ground-truth class
187   from the remaining ones Y \ Ŷ (x). To analyze the cost of CSQ, following the information-theoretic
188   cost model (Hu et al., 2020) and our empirical study in Table. 1, we assume that the cost of choosing
189   an option out of k many candidates is log2 k. Then, the labeling cost Γ(x, y, Ŷ (x)) of CSQ for input
190   x, ground-truth label y, and candidate set Ŷ (x) can be obtained as:

      Remark 3.2. If we constrain all candidate set sizes k to be fixed, then 1 − α corresponds to the
217   top-k accuracy pk of the model. Therefore, when pk ≥ logL (k + 1), CSQ consistently offers an
218   improvement over the conventional query. For example, in datasets such as CIFAR-10 (L = 10),
219   CIFAR-100 (L = 100), and ImageNet (L = 1000), if the model has a top-1 accuracy (i.e., k = 1)
220   of at least 30.1%, 15.1%, and 10.0% respectively, then CSQ always provides an improvement.
221
222   The above proof and remark demonstrate that under moderate conditions, CSQ is more efficient than
223   the conventional query. As described in Eq. (3), the cost of CSQ decreases as both α and k become
224
      smaller. However, since k and α are inversely related, balancing the trade-off between α and k
      is essential to fully leverage CSQ. Also, fixing candidate set sizes as in Remark 3.2 is suboptimal
225
      because it does not consider the uncertainty of individual samples. In the following section, we
226
      introduce our candidate set construction method, which both reflects the uncertainty of each sample
227   and automatically balances the trade-off between α and k.

      Datasets. Our method is evaluated using three image classification datasets: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky
304
      et al., 2009), CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and ImageNet64x64 (Chrabaszcz et al., 2017).

      CIFAR-10 comprises 50K training and 10K validation images across 10 classes. CIFAR-100 con-
306   tains the same number of images as CIFAR-10, but with 100 classes. ImageNet64x64 is a downsam-
307   pled version of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009a) with a resolution of 64 × 64, which consists of 1.2M
308   training and 50K validation images with 1000 classes. Following previous studies, we evaluate a
309   model using the validation split of each dataset.
310
      Implementation details. For CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, we adopt ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016)
311
      as a classification model. We train it for 200 epochs using AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019)
312   optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1e−3, decreasing by a factor of 0.2 at epochs 60, 120, and
313   160. We apply a weight decay of 5e−4 and a data augmentation consists of random crop, random
314   horizontal flip, and random rotation. For ImageNet64x64, we adopt WRN-36-5 (Zagoruyko, 2016),
315   and train it for 30 epochs using AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of 8e−3. We apply
316   a learning rate warm-up for 10 epochs from 2e−3. After the warm-up, we decay the learning rate
317   by a factor of 0.2 every 10 epochs. We adopt random horizontal flip and random translation as data
318   augmentation. For all the datasets, we use Mix-up (Zhang et al., 2018), where a mixing ratio is
319   sampled from Beta(1, 1). The hyperparameter d in Eq. (8) is set to 1.0, 0.5, and 1.2 for cost-efficient
320
      entropy sampling on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet64x64, respectively. For cost-efficient
      BADGE sampling, d is set to 1.1 for CIFAR-10 and 1.2 for CIFAR-100. Also, we set the size of
321
      calibration dataset ncal to 500 for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100, and 5K for ImageNet64x64.

525   5             C ONCLUSION
526
527
      We propose candidate set query (CSQ), a cost-efficient active learning framework for classification.
528   By narrowing down candidates likely to include the ground-truth class, our approach significantly
529   reduces labeling costs. To manage varying candidate set sizes, we introduce a novel acquisition
530   function that balances performance gain with labeling cost. Experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
531   and ImageNet64x64 show that CSQ significantly reduces labeling costs, demonstrating its potential
532   for efficiently scaling large annotated datasets.
533
      Limitation and Future work. One limitation is that the proposed acquisition function lacks theoret-
534   ical guarantee for label complexity (Dasgupta, 2011; Hanneke et al., 2014) at this point. Establishing
535   a theoretical understanding to quantify the cost required to achieve a target performance remains an
536   interesting direction for future work. Also, although our acquisition function shows improvements
537   over baselines, it relies on hyperparameter d to balance the trade-off between cost and informative-
538   ness. If gscore (x) could measure the true influence (Koh & Liang, 2017) on accuracy, setting d = 1
539   in Eq. (8) would optimize cost per influence, potentially yielding an optimal acquisition function.
      However, improving gscore (x) is beyond the scope of this work.

895   quisition function (Eq. (8)) balances the trade-off between labeling cost and the informativeness of

902
      garding that a larger d prioritizes more uncertain samples, this result aligns with recent observations
      that uncertainty-based selection performs better in scenarios with larger labeling budgets (Hacohen
903
      et al., 2022).

905   Guidelines for selecting proper hyperparameter d. We provide the following guidelines for set-
906   ting d. For datasets with fewer than 100 classes, d values between 0.3 and 1.0 may be effective, as
907   they ensure robustness on simple datasets like CIFAR-10 and reduce labeling costs on more com-
908
      plex datasets like CIFAR-100. For larger datasets closer in scale to ImageNet, exploring d ≥ 1.0
