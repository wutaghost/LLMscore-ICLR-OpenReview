011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In constrained Markov decision processes (CMDPs) with adversarial rewards
014            and constraints, a well-known impossibility result prevents any algorithm from
015            attaining both sublinear regret and sublinear constraint violation, when competing
016            against a best-in-hindsight policy that satisfies constraints on average. In this paper,
017            we show that this negative result can be eased in CMDPs with non-stationary
018            rewards and constraints, by providing algorithms whose performances smoothly
019            degrade
                   √ as non-stationarity increases. Specifically, we propose algorithms attaining
020            Õ( T +C) regret and positive constraint violation under bandit feedback, where C
021            is a corruption value measuring the environment non-stationarity. This can be Θ(T )
022
               in the worst case, coherently with the impossibility result for adversarial CMDPs.
               First, we design an algorithm with the desired guarantees when C is known. Then,
023
               in the case C is unknown, we show how to obtain the same results by embedding
024
               such an algorithm in a general meta-procedure. This is of independent interest, as
025            it can be applied to any non-stationary constrained online learning setting.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018) is concerned with settings where a learner sequentially
033
      interacts with an environment modeled as a Markov decision process (MDP) (Puterman, 2014). Most
      of the works in the field focus on learning policies that maximize learner’s rewards. However, in most
034
      of the real-world applications of interest, the learner also has to meet some additional requirements.
035
      For instance, autonomous vehicles must avoid crashing (Isele et al., 2018; Wen et al., 2020), bidding
036   agents in ad auctions must not deplete their budget (Wu et al., 2018; He et al., 2021), and users
037   of recommender systems must not be exposed to offending content (Singh et al., 2020). These
038   requirements can be captured by constrained MDPs (CMDPs) (Altman, 1999), which generalize
039   MDPs by specifying constraints that the learner has to satisfy while maximizing their rewards.
040
      We study online learning in episodic CMDPs (see, e.g., (Efroni et al., 2020)), where the goal of the
041
      learner is twofold. On the one hand, the learner wants to minimize their regret, which measures how
042   much reward they lost over the episodes compared to what they would have obtained by always using
043   a best-in-hindsight constraint-satisfying policy. On the other hand, the learner wants to ensure that
044   the (cumulative) constraint violation is minimized during the learning process. Ideally, one seeks for
045   algorithms with both regret and constraint violation growing sublinearly in the number of episodes T .
046
      A crucial feature distinguishing online learning problems in CMDPs is whether rewards and con-
047
      straints are selected stochastically or adversarially. Most of the works focus on the case in which
048   constraints are stochastic (see, e.g., (Wei et al., 2018; Zheng & Ratliff, 2020; Efroni et al., 2020; Qiu
049   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2023)), with only one exception addressing settings with
050   adversarial constraints (Stradi et al., 2024b). This is primarily motivated by a well-known impos-
051   sibility result by Mannor et al. (2009), which prevents any learning algorithm from attaining both
052   sublinear regret and sublinear constraint violation, when competing against a best-in-hindsight policy
053   that satisfies the constraints on average. However, dealing with adversarially-selected constraints is
      of paramount importance to cope with real-world environments, which are typically non-stationary.

      1.1   O RIGINAL C ONTRIBUTIONS
055
056   The main contribution of this paper is to show how to ease the negative result of (Mannor et al., 2009),
057   by considering CMDPs with non-stationary rewards and constraints. Specifically, we address CMDPs
058   where rewards and constraints are selected from probability distributions that are allowed to change
059   adversarially from episode to episode. One may think of our setting as bridging the gap between fully-
060   stochastic and fully-adversarial ones. We design algorithms whose performances—in terms of regret
061   and constraint violation—smoothly degrade as a suitable measure of non-stationarity increases. This
062   is called (adversarial) corruption, as it intuitively quantifies how much the distributions of rewards
063   and constraints vary over the episodes with respect to some “fictitious” non-corrupted counterparts.
                                             √
064   We propose algorithms that attain Õ( T + C) regret and constraint violation, where C denotes the
065   corruption of the setting. We remark that C can be Θ(T ) in the worst case, and, thus, our bounds are
066   coherent with the impossibility result by Mannor et al. (2009). Notably, our algorithms work under
067   bandit feedback, namely, by only observing rewards and constraint costs of the state-action pairs
068   visiting during episodes. Moreover, they are able to manage positive constraint violation. This means
069   that they do not allow for a negative violation (i.e., a constraint satisfaction) to cancel out a positive
070   one across different episodes. This is a crucial requirement for most of the practical applications. For
071   instance, in autonomous driving, avoiding a collision does not “repair” a previously-occurred crash.
072   In the first part of the paper, we design an algorithm, called NS-SOPS,   which works assuming that the
                                                                        √
073   value of the corruption C is known. This algorithm achieves Õ( T +C) regret and positive constraint
074   violation by employing a policy search method that is optimistic in both reward maximization and
075   constraint satisfaction. Specifically, the algorithm incorporates C in the confidence bounds of rewards
076   and constraint costs, so as to “boost” its optimism and achieve the desired guarantees.
077   In the second part of the paper, we show how to embed the NS-SOPS algorithm in a meta-procedure
078
                                √
      that allows to achieve Õ( T + C) regret and positive constraint violation when C is unknown. The
079   meta-procedure works by instantiating multiple instances of an algorithm for the case in which C is
080   known, each one taking care of a different “guess” on the value of C. Specifically, the meta-procedure
081   acts as a master by choosing which instance to follow in order to select a policy at each episode. To do
082   so, it employs an adversarial online learning algorithm, which is fed with losses constructed starting
083   from the Lagrangian of the CMDP problem, suitably modified to account for positive constraint
084   violation. Our meta-procedure is of independent interest, as it can be applied in any non-stationary
085   constrained online learning setting, so as to relax the knowledge of C.

      1.2   R ELATED W ORKS
088
089
      Within the literature on CMDPs, settings with stochastic rewards and constraints have been widely
090   investigated. However, their non-stationary counterparts, including adversarial ones in the worst
091   case, are still largely unexplored. In the following, we discuss the works that are most related to ours,
092   while we refer the reader to Appendix A for a comprehensive survey of related works.
093
      Qiu et al. (2020) provide the first primal-dual approach to deal with episodic CMDPs with adversarial
094
      losses and stochastic constraints, achieving, under full feedback, both sublinear regret and sublinear
095
      (non-positive) constraint violation (i.e., allowing for cancellations). Stradi et al. (2024a) are the first to
096   tackle CMDPs with adversarial losses and stochastic constraints under bandit feedback, by proposing
097   an algorithm that achieves sublinear regret and sublinear positive constraint violation. These works
098   do not consider settings where constraints are non-stationary, i.e., they may change over the episodes.
099
      Ding & Lavaei (2023) and Wei et al. (2023) consider the case in which rewards and constraints are
100
      non-stationary, assuming that their variation is bounded. Our work differs from theirs in multiple
101
      aspects. First, we consider positive constraint violation, while they allow for cancellations. As
102   concerns the definition of regret, ours and that used by Ding & Lavaei (2023) and Wei et al. (2023)
103   are not comparable. Indeed, they employ a dynamic regret baseline, which, in general, is harder
104   than the static regret employed in our work. However, they compare learner’s performances against
105   a dynamic policy that satisfies the constraints at every round. Instead, we consider a policy that
106   satisfies the constraints on average, which can perform arbitrarily better than a policy satisfying the
107   constraints at every round. Furthermore, the dependence on T in their regret bound is much worse
      than ours, even when the non-stationarity is small, namely, when it is a constant independent of T


                                                            2

108
      (and, thus, dynamic regret collapses to static regret). Finally, we do not make any assumption on T ,
109   while both regret and constraint violation bounds in (Wei et al., 2023) only hold for large T .
110
111   Finally, Stradi et al. (2024b) are the first to study CMDPs with adversarial constraints. Given the
112
      impossibility result by Mannor et al. (2009), they propose an algorithm that, under full feedback,
      attains sublinear (non-positive) constraint violation (i.e., with cancellations allowed) and a fraction of
113
      the optimal reward, thus resulting in a regret growing linearly in T . We show that sublinear regret
114
      and sublinear constraint violation can indeed be attained simultaneously if one takes into account the
115   corruption C, which can be seen as a measure of how much adversarial the environment is. Moreover,
116   let us remark that our algorithms deal with positive constraint violation under bandit feedback, and,
117   thus, they are much more general than those in (Stradi et al., 2024b).

      2.1    C ONSTRAINED M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESSES
123   We study episodic constrained MDPs (Altman, 1999) (CMDPs), in which a learner interacts with an
124   unknown environment over T episodes, with the goal of maximizing long-term rewards subject to
125   some constraints. X is a finite set of states of the environment, A is a finite set of actions available
126   to the learner in each state, while the environment dynamics is governed by a transition function
127   P : X × A × X → [0, 1], with P (x′ |x, a) denoting the probability of going from state x ∈ X
128   to x′ ∈ X by taking action a ∈ A.1 At each episode t ∈ [T ],2 a reward vector rt ∈ [0, 1]|X×A| is
129   sampled according to a probability distribution Rt , with rt (x, a) being the reward of taking action
130   a ∈ A in state x ∈ X at episode t. Moreover, a constraint cost matrix Gt ∈ [0, 1]|X×A|×m is sampled
131   according to a probability distribution Gt , with gt,i (x, a) being the cost of constraint i ∈ [m] when
132   taking action a ∈ A in state x ∈ X at episode t. We also denote by gt,i ∈ [0, 1]|X×A| the vector
133   of all the costs gt,i (x, a) associated with constraint i at episode t. Each constraint requires that its
134   corresponding expected cost is kept below a given threshold. The thresholds of all the m constraints
135   are encoded in a vector α ∈ [0, L]m , with αi denoting the threshold of the i-th constraint.
136   We consider a setting in which the sequences of probability distributions {Rt }Tt=1 and {Gt }Tt=1 are
137   selected adversarially. Thus, reward vectors rt and constraint cost matrices Gt are random variables
138   whose distributions are allowed to change arbitrarily from episode to episode. To measure how much
139   such probability distributions change over the episodes, we introduce the notion of (adversarial)
140   corruption. In particular, we define the adversarial corruption Cr for the rewards as follows:

      Remark 1 (Relation with adversarial/stochastic CMDPs). Our setting is more akin to CMDPs with
236   adversarial rewards and constraints, rather than stochastic ones. This is because our notion of
237   regret is computed with respect to an optimal constraint-satisfying policy in hindsight that takes into
238   account the average over episodes of the mean values E[rt ] and E[Gt ] of the adversarially-selected
239   probability distributions Rt and Gt . This makes our setting much harder than one with stochastic
240   rewards and constraints. Indeed, in the special case in which the supports of Rt and Gt are singletons
241   (and, thus, mean values are fully revealed after each episode), our setting reduces to a CMDP with
242   adversarial rewards and constraints, given that such supports are selected adversarially.
243   Remark 2 (Impossibility results carrying over from adversarial CMDPs). Mannor et al. (2009) show
244   that, in online learning problems with constraints selected adversarially, it is impossible to achieve
245   both regret and constraint violation growing sublinearly in T . This result holds for a regret definition
246   that corresponds to ours. Thus, it carries over to our setting.
                                                                 √      This is why we look for algorithms
247   whose regret and positive constraint violation scale as Õ( T + C), with a linear dependency on the
248   adversarial corruption C. Notice that the impossibility result by Mannor et al. (2009) does not rule
249
      out the possibility of achieving such a guarantee, since regret and positive constraint violation are
      not sublinear when C grows linearly in T , as it could be the case in a classical adversarial setting.

369   At a high level, the Lagrangified follow-the-regularized-leader (Lag-FTRL for short) algorithm
370   works by instantiating several instances of Algorithm 2, suitably stabilized (see section H), with each
371   instance Algj being run for a different “guess” of the (unknown) adversarial corruption value C.
372   The algorithm plays the role of a master by choosing which instance Algj to use at each episode.
373   The selection is done by employing an FTRL approach with a suitable log-barrier regularization.
374   In particular, at each episode t ∈ [T ], by letting Algjt be the selected instance, the Lag-FTRL
375   algorithm employs the policy πtji prescribed by Algjt and provides feedback to instance Algjt only.

377   procedure implemented at the master level is partial. This is because, at each episode t ∈ [T ], the

667   A    R ELATED WORKS
668
669   In the following, we discuss some works that are tightly related to ours. In particular, we first describe
670   works dealing with the online learning problem in MDPs, and, then, we discuss some works studying
671   the constrained version of the classical online learning problem.
672
673   Online learning in MDPs The literature on online learning problems (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi,
674   2006) in MDPs is wide (see (Auer et al., 2008; Even-Dar et al., 2009; Neu et al., 2010) for some
675   initial results on the topic). In such settings, two types of feedback are usually studied: in the
676   full-information feedback model, the entire loss function is observed after the learner’s choice, while
677   in the bandit feedback model, the learner only observes the loss due to the chosen action. Azar et al.
678   (2017) study the problem of optimal exploration in episodic MDPs with unknown transitions and
679
      stochastic losses
                   √ when the feedback is bandit. The authors present an algorithm whose regret upper
680   bound is Õ( T ), thus matching the lower bound for this class of MDPs and improving the previous
681
      result by Auer et al. (2008).
682
683
      Online learning in non-stationary MDPs The literature on non-stationary MDPs encompasses
      both works on non-stationary rewards and non-stationary transitions. As concerns the first research
684
      line, Rosenberg & Mansour (2019b) study the online learning problem in episodic MDPs with
685
      adversarial losses and unknown transitions when the feedback is full information. The authors present
                                                                                                        √
686
      an online algorithm exploiting entropic regularization and providing a regret upper bound of Õ( T ).
687   The same setting is investigated by Rosenberg & Mansour (2019a) when the feedback is bandit. In
688
      such a case, the authors provide a regret upper bound of the order of Õ(T 3/4 ), which is improved by
689   Jin et √
             al. (2020) by providing an algorithm that achieves in the same setting a regret upper bound
690   of Õ( T ). Related to the non-stationarity of the transitions , Wei et al. (2022) study MDPs with
691   adversarial  corruption on transition functions and rewards, reaching a regret upper bound of order
         √
692   O( T + C) (where C is the amount of adversarial corruption) with respect to the optimal policy of
       e
693   the non-corrupted MDP . Finally, Jin et al. (2024) √is the first to study completely adversarial MDPs
694   with changing transition functions, providing a Õ( T + C) regret bounds, where C is a corruption
695   measure of the adversarially changing transition functions.
696
697   Online learning with constraints A central result is provided by Mannor et al. (2009), who
698   show that it is impossible to suffer from sublinear regret and sublinear constraint violation when
699   an adversary chooses losses and constraints. Liakopoulos et al. (2019) try to overcome such an
700   impossibility result by defining a new notion of regret. They study a class of online learning problems
701   with long-term budget constraints that can be chosen by an adversary. The learner’s regret metric is
      modified by introducing the notion of a K-benchmark, i.e., a comparator that meets the problem’s


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      allotted budget over any window of length K. Castiglioni et al. (2022a;b) deal with the problem
703   of online learning with stochastic and adversarial losses, providing the first best-of-both-worlds
704   algorithm for online learning problems with long-term constraints.
705
706
      Online learning in CMDPs Online Learning In MDPs with constraints is generally studied when
707   the constraints are selected stochastically. Precisely, Zheng & Ratliff (2020) deal with episodic
708   CMDPs with stochastic losses and constraints, where the transition probabilities are known and the
709   feedback is bandit. The regret upper bound of their algorithm is of the order of Õ(T 3/4 ), while
710   the cumulative constraint violation is guaranteed to be below a threshold with a given probability.
711   Wei et al. (2018) deal with adversarial losses and stochastic constraints, assuming the transition
712   probabilities are known and the feedback is full  √ information. The authors present an algorithm that
713   guarantees an upper bound of the order of Õ( T ) on both regret and constraint violation. Bai et al.
714   (2020) provide the first algorithm that achieves sublinear regret when the transition probabilities
715   are unknown, assuming that the rewards are deterministic and the constraints are stochastic with
716   a particular structure. Efroni et al. (2020) propose two approaches to deal with the exploration-
717   exploitation dilemma in episodic CMDPs. These approaches guarantee sublinear regret and constraint
718
      violation when transition probabilities, rewards, and constraints are unknown and stochastic, while
      the feedback is bandit. Qiu et al. (2020) provide a primal-dual approach based on optimism in the
719
      face of uncertainty. This work shows the effectiveness of such an approach when dealing with
720
      episodic CMDPs with adversarial losses and stochastic constraints, achieving both sublinear regret
721   and constraint violation with full-information feedback. Stradi et al. (2024a) is the first work to
722   tackle CMDPs with adversarial losses and bandit feedback. They propose an algorithm which
723   achieves sublinear regret and sublinear positive constraints violations, assuming that the constraints
724   are stochastic. Stradi et al. (2024b) are the first to study CMDPs with adversarial constraints. Given
725   the well-known impossibility result to learn with adversarial constraints, they propose an algorithm
726   that attains sublinear violation (with cancellations allowed) and a fraction of the optimal reward
727   when the feedback is full. Finally, Ding & Lavaei (2023) and Wei et al. (2023) consider the case
728   in which rewards and constraints are non-stationary, assuming that their variation is bounded, as
729
      in our work. Nevertheless, our settings differ in multiple aspects. First of all, we consider positive
      constraints violations, while the aforementioned works allow the cancellations in their definition.
730
      We consider a static regret adversarial baseline, while Ding & Lavaei (2023) and Wei et al. (2023)
731
      consider the stronger baseline of dynamic regret. Nevertheless, our bounds are not comparable, since
732   we achieve linear regret and violations only in the worst case scenario in which C = T , while a
733   sublinear corruption would lead to linear dynamic regret in their work. Finally, we do not make any
734   assumption on the number of episodes, while both the regret and violations bounds presented in Wei
735   et al. (2023) hold only for large T .
