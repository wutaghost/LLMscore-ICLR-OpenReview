011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in cognitive science and multi-round reasoning techniques
014            for Large Language Models (LLMs) suggest that iterative thinking processes
015            improve problem-solving performance in complex tasks. Inspired by this, ap-
016            proaches like Chain-of-Thought, debating, and self-refinement have been applied
017            to auto-regressive LLMs, achieving significant successes in tasks such as math-
018            ematical reasoning, commonsense reasoning, and multi-hop question answering.
019            Despite these successes, the theoretical basis for how multi-round reasoning en-
020
               hances problem-solving abilities remains underexplored. In this work, we investi-
               gate the approximation, learnability, and generalization properties of multi-round
021
               auto-regressive models. We show that Transformers with finite context windows
022
               are universal approximators for steps of Turing-computable functions and can ap-
023            proximate any Turing-computable sequence-to-sequence function through multi-
024            round reasoning. We extend PAC learning to sequence generation and demon-
025            strate that multi-round generation is learnable even when the sequence length ex-
026            ceeds the model’s context window. Finally, we examine how generalization error
027            propagates across rounds, and show how the aforementioned approaches can help
028            constrain this error, ensuring outputs stay within an expectation boundary. This
029            work sheds light on the systemic theoretical foundations of multi-round sequence
030            learning and reasoning, emphasizing its role in inference complexity.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Cognitive science suggests that humans typically require multiple rounds of thinking to arrive at cor-
037   rect conclusions, especially when dealing with complex problems (Nelson, 1990). Inspired by this
038   principle, recent multi-round reasoning techniques, such as Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022b),
039
      debating (Khan et al., 2024), and self-refinement (Madaan et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024a), applied
      to auto-regressive Large Language Models (LLMs), have achieved significant success across vari-
040
      ous reasoning tasks, including mathematical problem solving (Cobbe et al., 2021), commonsense
041
      reasoning (Talmor et al., 2019), scientific question answering (Clark et al., 2018), and multi-hop
042   question answering (Yang et al., 2018). This exceptional capability is widely attributed to the in-
043   context learning abilities of auto-regressive language models (Li et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024).
044
045
      In-context learning ability has led to the conjecture that auto-regressive generative models can sim-
      ulate Turing machines (Li et al., 2024b; Merrill & Sabharwal, 2024). Schuurmans (2023) has shown
046
      that large language models, particularly when augmented with memory, can execute complex com-
047
      putational processes that resemble the behavior of Turing machines. Malach (2024) has explored the
048   theoretical underpinnings of auto-regressive models and their connection to universal computation,
049   showing that even simple next-token predictors can, under the right conditions, emulate the behav-
050   ior of Turing machines. Li et al. (2024b) illustrates the solvability of the problems that belong to
051   AC0 , a proper subset of NC0 , via Chain-of-Thought. Yet, the existence of some strong assumptions
052   of these works, such as the dependence on external memory (Schuurmans, 2023), the presence of
053   Chain-of-Thought in the training data (Malach, 2024), or the infinite number of layers (Yun et al.,
      2020), do not explain well the actual working conditions of realistic multi-round language models.

054
      To make matters worse, the computability perspective does not provide a plausible explanation for
055   how machine learning models learn. This is because learning ability and reasoning ability are not
056   inherent properties of a computable class. An extreme example is that even games like Magic: The
057   Gathering and Super Mario can be Turing complete (Churchill et al., 2020). But we’re not going
058   to build a generalized AI out of a card game. Beyond the approximation of Turing machines, we
059   need to understand (1) the ability of multi-round auto-regressive language models to approximate
060   functions, (2) whether such an ability is learnable, and what its learning complexity is, and (3) the
061   ability to generalize when inferring with imperfectly-trained language models in reality.
062   In this work, we systematically investigate why multi-round reasoning can improve the overall large
063   language model problem-solving capability. Particularly, we study the approximation, learnability,
064   and generalization abilities of auto-regressive generative models with a limited context window.
065   Unlike previous theories, this series of studies corresponds closely to real-world scenarios, providing
066   empirical guidance on training and inference auto-regressive generative language models.
067   We begin with the approximation ability of auto-regressive Transformers. For the approximation
068   ability, we show that Transformers with finite context window size are universal approximators
069   for some steps of a Turing-computable function. Further, we prove that any Turing-computable
070   sequence-to-sequence function can be approximated by a multi-round auto-regressive generation
071   process. This demonstrates the feasibility of the current dominant language models for solving
072   problems. It is worth noting that although this finding does not directly explain the problem-solving
073   ability of language models, it is a cornerstone of the generalizability of auto-regressive models.
074   Next, we turn our attention to learnability, which is a critical aspect of understanding how auto-
075   regressive language models, particularly in a multi-round reasoning context, gain their problem-
076   solving capabilities. We first expand probably approximately correct (PAC) learning (Valiant, 1984)
077   to finite-size window next token prediction, and to auto-regressive sequence generation. Beyond
078   that, we generalize the finite-window sequence learnability to the case of exceeding the window size
079   by means of multi-round language generation. The results show that even if the required sequence
080   length exceeds the maximum window of the auto-regressive language model, the model remains
081
      learnable for long sequence complex problems. The sample complexity required to learn the ability
      to auto-regressively predict an entire long sequence will increase dramatically compared to simply
082
      making a single-word prediction. Further, we show that training with the multi-round generation
083
      paradigm has an exponential impact on the sample complexity w.r.t the number of rounds R.
084
085   Then, we focus on the generalization ability through multi-round reasoning. In particular, we show
086   that the generalization error of the model grows with the propagation rounds. Through our analysis,
087
      we can conclude that as the number of rounds of model generation increases, eventually the answers
      it obtains will diverge. Nevertheless, we can still constrain the state of the intermediate process, e.g.,
088
      by providing some hints in the multi-round dialogues, to control the generalization of the model
089
      and induce it to the answer we want. We point out that prompting tricks like Chain-of-Thought,
090   self-refinement, and multiple rounds of dialogue serve to constrain the generalization error during
091   the inference process so that the answers generated are within our expectations.
092
093
      Contributions. Overall, we make the following contributions:
094
             • We comprehensively investigate the approximation, learning, and generalization capabili-
095            ties of finite context auto-regressive language models from a theoretical perspective.
096
             • We theoretically identify a dramatic increase in the sample complexity required to induce a
097
               finite window next-word prediction into sequence generation and remark that this increase
098
               can be mitigated by introducing multiple rounds of sub-sequence generation.
099
100
             • We theoretically analyze the inter-round propagation mechanism of the generalization error
               during the multi-round generation process. We also point out that without intervention in
101
               the generation of multiple rounds, their cumulative error will not guarantee controllability.
102
103   The remaining paper is structured as follows: In Section 2, we discuss related research on multi-
104   round language generation, language model learning, and generalization capabilities, followed by
105   an introduction to some relevant lemmas and definitions in Section 3. We theoretically analyze the
106   approximation capabilities of multi-round Transformers in Section 4, the learnability of language
107   generation with auto-regressive language models in Section 5, and the error propagation of general-
      ization in Section 6. We conclude and provide some insights and future directions in Section 7.

      2.1   M ULTI -ROUND L ANGUAGE M ODEL G ENERATION
111
112   It has been widely noted that with generative language models, it is possible to get the desired
113   answer through multiple rounds of interaction. Based on this core idea, several types of multi-round
114   generation methods have been proposed to solve a wide range of problems. These methods include:
115
      (1) Chain-of-Thought. Wei et al. (2022b) proposes chain-of-thought (CoT), a prompt strategy that
116
      guides the LLM to generate regularized intermediate reasoning steps that lead from the initial ques-
117   tion to the final answer. CoT has been proven to enable LLMs to solve complex problems including
118   dynamic programming, whereas non-CoT prompts fail to do so (Feng et al., 2023). Empirically,
119   variants of CoT have been proposed, including faithful CoT that tries to avoid LLMs lying in the
120   reasoning stage (Lyu et al., 2023), multimodal CoT that enables CoT for vision contexts (Zhang
121   et al., 2023), and tree-of-thought (ToT) that builds up a complex reasoning tree (Yao et al., 2023).
122
      (2) Self-correction and Self-refinement. LLMs’ self-correction and refinement abilities have re-
123   ceived significant attention recently (Madaan et al., 2023b; Shinn et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023).
124   Through explicit multi-round reflection, self-correction iteratively improves the accuracy, reliabil-
125   ity, and robustness of LLM outputs, especially in complex reasoning tasks where the CoT reasoning
126   process might be flawed (Madaan et al., 2023b), by eliminating hallucinations (Liu et al., 2024b).
127
      (3) Multi-Agent Debating. Apart from improving a single-agent LLM system, another line focuses
128
      on the collaboration of multiple LLM agents (Li et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2023).
129   Findings suggest that multi-agent collaboration through both debating or even majority voting can
130   often outperform single-agent LLM with explicit constraints (Huang et al.; Wu et al., 2023). Khan
131   et al. (2024) further shows that debating with more persuasive LLMs results in better answers.
132
      Overall, the essence of these methods is to impose internal or external interventions on multi-round
133
      sequence generation, which is an optimization constraint in the generalization process.

      2.2   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS ON L ANGUAGE M ODELS
137
      Approximation Ability of Transformers. The approximation capabilities of Transformer archi-
138
      tectures have been extensively studied in recent years. Yun et al. (2020) establishes that Trans-
139   formers are universal approximations of continuous permutation equivariant sequence-to-sequence
140   functions. Wei et al. (2022a) introduces a statistically meaningful approximation, demonstrating
141   that overparameterized neural networks can approximate Boolean circuits and Turing machines
142   with generalization guarantees. Schuurmans (2023) proves that Transformers can simulate Tur-
143   ing machines in the presence of conditional memory. In terms of limitations, Dong et al. (2021)
144   demonstrates that pure attention mechanisms could suffer from rank collapse, leading to a loss of
145   expressive power with increased network depth. Hahn (2020) analyzes the theoretical limitations
146   of self-attention in neural sequence models, highlighting challenges in modeling hierarchical struc-
147
      tures. Cai (2024) proves that the composition of words in a finite vocabulary can approximate any
      continuous function in a compact domain. Unlike their setting, we show that a finite context Trans-
148
      former can approximate a Turing Machine by up to infinite rounds of generation.
149
150   Simulating Turing Machine with Neural Networks. Numerous scholars have proposed using
151   neural networks to simulate Turing machines to verify the computational power of neural net-
152   works (Siegelmann & Sontag, 1992; Pérez et al., 2021; 2019; Wei et al., 2022a; Graves, 2014).
153
      Siegelmann & Sontag (1992) point out that recurrent neural networks (RNNs) of infinite precision
      can make simulations of Turing machines, implying the Turing-completeness of RNNs. Chung &
154
      Siegelmann (2021) further implements a finite precision RNN simulation of Turing Machine using
155
      an external memory module. However this Turing-complete property cannot be directly inherited to
156   a finite-window Transformer, because the recurrent learning model in RNNs essentially aggregates
157   information from time 0. While the Transformer model via self-attention does not pass on the in-
158   formation, but rather, it is in the form of KV cache (Ge et al., 2024), which results in information
159   beyond the context window being encoded only on newly generated tokens, a completely different
160   computational paradigm from RNNs. For the Turing completeness of Transformer, Bhattamishra
161   et al. (2020) prove that finite-precision Transformers are not Turing-complete. Pérez et al. (2021)
      prove that only hard-attention can be Turing complete. But Transformers still can approximate the


                                                       3

162
      simulation of Turing Machine within a certain margin of error (Wei et al., 2022a). Yet, these stud-
163   ies have not examined the relationship between approximation precision and quantization precision.
164   We reveal in this work the approximation of a Turing machine that can be reached through multiple
165   rounds of Transformer’s generation, as well as a high-level relationship between the precision of
166   Transformer’s numerical quantization and the approximation error tolerance.
167
      Learnability and Sample Complexity. Probably Approximately Correct learning is a framework
168
      in computational learning theory that provides a formal way to understand how well an algorithm
169   can learn a target function from a set of examples. The concept was introduced by Valiant (1984)
170   and helped analyze how quickly and accurately a learning algorithm can generalize from data. For
171   sequence modeling, Schuurmans & Greiner (1995) proposes a kind of sequential PAC learning
172   where online stopping rules are used to minimize the number of samples required for PAC learning.
173   Gavaldà et al. (2006) studies the PAC learnability of hidden Markov models. Malach (2024) extends
174   this framework to the prediction of the next token, but there are assumptions of infinite context-
175   awareness, as well as ignoring error propagation. So far, to the best of our knowledge, there are no
176   previous works that study the PAC learnability and sample complexity of long sequence generation
177
      with a context window-limited auto-regressive generative model. Making up for the shortcomings of
      the above works, in this paper, we study the PAC learnability of sequence generation and apply it to
178
      a multi-round generation task to theoretically qualitatively study the effect of multi-round generation
179
      on the sample complexity of the learning of the long sequence generation task.

      Theorem 4.3 (Approximability). For any Turing-computable sequence-to-sequence function f :
271   Σ∗ → Σ∗ , and for any error tolerance ϵ > 0, there exists a multi-round sequence-to-sequence

      error can be controlled within the tolerance. See detailed proof in Appendix C.

284
      man reasoning progresses. This dynamic computational ability is crucial for cognitive tasks as it

      formers, beyond being powerful sequence generation models (e.g., for language translation), could

      potentially be applied to complex tasks such as cognitive reasoning, planning, and problem-solving.

289   5     L EARNABILITY
290
291   In Section 4.1, we illustrated the ability of the autoregressive Transformer to approximate a sequence
292   just like a Turing machine, which is a preliminary indication of the inference potential of language
293   models. However, we still do not know what scale of training the model needs to undergo to ob-
294
      tain such a capability. In this section, we explore the learning ability of autoregressive models for
      sequences of arbitrary length.
295
296   To get this point, we aim to demonstrate that auto-regressive sequence models, which possess uni-
297   versal approximation capabilities, can be sequential PAC-learnable under certain constraints. In
298   order to ensure that a sequential model learns effectively from finite data, we need to determine the
299   minimum sample size m required to guarantee that the model’s error on unseen data does not ex-
300
      ceed a specified threshold ϵ with a high level of confidence 1 − δ. This involves deriving a bound on
      the generalization error using tools from statistical learning theory, such as Rademacher complexity
301
      (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002) and spectral norm constraints(Bartlett et al., 2017).

500
      In conclusion, this work provides a comprehensive theoretical foundation for understanding the
      capabilities of multi-round reasoning in auto-regressive large language models. We have systemati-
501
      cally explored the approximation, learnability, and generalization properties of these models, high-
502
      lighting their potential to solve complex problems through iterative reasoning.
503
504   Findings. We demonstrate that Transformers with a finite context window can serve as universal
505   approximators for Turing-computable functions, offering insights into their robust problem-solving
506
      capabilities in real-world tasks. Additionally, we have extended the PAC learning framework to ac-
      count for sequence generation tasks, revealing the complexities involved in learning long sequences
507
      when context exceeds the model’s window size. Moreover, our analysis of the generalization error
508
      in multi-round generation reveals that, without proper interventions, error accumulation could lead
509   to divergence in the model’s outputs. Techniques like Chain-of-Thought offer viable strategies to
510   mitigate this, ensuring that the generated sequences remain within expected bounds.
511
512
      Practical Insights. Our contributions not only advance our theoretical understanding of auto-
      regressive generative language models but also provide practical insights into improving model
513
      performance through multi-round reasoning interventions. For the model training stage, in order
514
      to reduce the sample complexity of training on long sequences, one can consider providing some
515   decomposition methods for very long and complex task sequences during language model training,
516   so that the long sequences are decomposed into multiple rounds of training on short sequences. In
517   the inference process of the model, when we design a method that makes the model perform multi-
518   round thinking, we should give more consideration to how to interrupt the propagation of cumulative
519   errors to make the generated content more in line with our expectations.
