009                                             Abstract
010
011               Last decade has seen the emergence of numerous methods for learning on
012               graphs, particularly Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). These methods, how-
013               ever, are often not directly applicable to more complex structures like bi-
014               partite graphs (equivalent to hypergraphs), which represent interactions
015               among two entity types (e.g., a user liking a movie). This paper proposes
016               Convolutional Signal Propagation (CSP), a non-parametric simple and scal-
017
                  able method that natively operates on bipartite graphs (hypergraphs) and
                  can be implemented with just a few lines of code. After defining CSP, we
                  demonstrate its relationship with well-established methods like label prop-
018
019
                  agation, Naive Bayes, and Hypergraph Convolutional Networks. We eval-
020               uate CSP against several reference methods on real-world datasets from
021               multiple domains, focusing on retrieval and classification tasks. Our re-
022               sults show that CSP offers competitive performance while maintaining low
023               computational complexity, making it an ideal first choice as a baseline for
024               hypergraph node classification and retrieval. Moreover, despite operating
025               on hypergraphs, CSP achieves good results in tasks typically not associated
026               with hypergraphs, such as natural language processing.
027
028   1       Introduction
029
030   In the modern world, an overwhelming amount of data has an internal structure, oftentimes
031   forming complex networks that can be represented as graphs. Efficiently mining information
032   from this data is crucial for a wide range of applications spanning various domains such as
033   social networks, biology, physics or cybersecurity. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have
034   emerged as the dominant tool for handling such data due to their ability to leverage the
035   graph structure for predictive and analytical tasks. However, despite their success, GNNs
036   come with notable challenges, including high computational complexity during training,
037
      numerous hyperparameters that require fine-tuning, lack of straightforward interpretability,
      and the necessity of dedicated computational infrastructure such as GPUs. Given these
038
      limitations, baseline algorithms play a vital role as complementary tools to GNNs. These
039
      baselines, often much less complex, provide an efficient way of generating preliminary results.
040   In many cases, these simpler methods are even sufficiently effective to be used as-is for the
041   problem at hand.
042
043
      In this work, we present Convolutional Signal Propagation (CSP)1 , a simple, scalable al-
      gorithm that may serve as such a baseline. We describe the algorithm in Section 4 and
044
      provide an overview of how it relates to established methods. While CSP is a general algo-
045
      rithm for propagating any signals and is introduced as such, we are mostly interested in its
046   application to classification and retrieval (i.e., a setting similar to label propagation (Zhu &
047   Ghahramani, 2003)) and provide experimental evaluation in this setting in Section 5.

090   2.1    Notations and Definitions
091
092   We consider a finite set of items of interest V = {v1 , . . . , vn }, referred to as nodes. A family
093   of subsets of V denoted by E = {e1 , . . . , em } ⊆ 2V is referred to as hyperedges. The nodes
094   and hyperedges togerher form a hypergraph H = (V, E). The structure of the hypergraph
095   can also be described by an incidence matrix H ∈ {0, 1}n×m , where Hi,j = 1 if vi ∈ ej ,
096   and 0 otherwise. Every hypergraph can alternatively be described by a bipartite incidence
097   graph, also called the Levi graph (Levi, 1942). This bipartite graph Gbip = (V ∪ E, Ebip )
098
      has as its two partitions the nodes and hyperedges of H, and its edges represent a node in
      V belonging to an edge in E, formally Ebip = {(vi , ej ) ∈ V × E|vi ∈ ej }.
099
100   The degree d (v) of node v is defined as the number of edges that contain the node. Similarly,
101   the degree δ (e) of the edge e is defined as the number of nodes it contains. We also
102   establish a diagonal node-degree matrix Dv ∈ Nn×n with (Dv )i,i = d (vi ) and (Dv )i,j = 0
103   for i 6= j. Analogously, the hyperedge-degree matrix is a diagonal matrix De ∈ Nm×m with
104
      (De )i,i = δ (ei ) and (De )i,j = 0 for i 6= j.
105   We consider for each node in the hypergraph some kind of signal that is to be propagated
106   through the hyperedges. Let the signal be a d-dimensional vector xi for each node, giving
107   for the whole hypergraph a matrix X ∈ Rn×d . In the following parts of this paper, we will
      explore several ways of defining such a signal, with an overview provided in Section 4.2.


                                                        2
108
      Within this work, we are interested in two transductive tasks on hypergraphs: classification
109   on V and retrieval of positive nodes from V . Both tasks assume a training set of nodes
110   Vtrain ⊂ V where the labels of nodes are known. In case of classification, the goal is to
111   predict the label for all nodes in V . The retrieval task aims to sort the nodes in the testing
112   set V \ Vtrain such that the number of positive nodes in top K positions is maximized.

115   3     Related work
116
117
      Mining information from structured (graph-like) data is one of the central problems in
118
      machine learning. The most straightforward way to handle this is to translate the structure
119
      into features and apply traditional machine learning techniques, such as logistic regression,
120   random forests, and naive Bayes, to these features. Naive Bayes (Ng & Jordan, 2001),
121   in particular, provides a bridge to a second large family of learning methods on graphs:
122   Bayesian methods, where the graph forms a structure for modelling random variables. A
123   critical problem associated with Bayesian methods is inference, which is often intractable.
124
      The translation of structured data into features is also a non-trivial problem. While some
125
      methods can handle sparse, high-dimensional feature vectors, the majority cannot. Several
126   methods are suited for finding low-dimensional representations of structured data. For
127   example, non-negative matrix factorization (Lee & Seung, 2000) (NMF) decomposes a large
128   sparse matrix into the product of two low-dimensional matrices. Other methods, such as
129   node2vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016), spectral positional encodings (Dwivedi et al., 2023)
130   and distance encodings (Li et al., 2020; Beaini et al., 2021) offer node representations for
131   graphs, however, they cannot be directly applied to hypergraphs.
132   There are many papers on learning algorithms for graphs, such as GraphSAGE (Hamilton
133   et al., 2017), graph convolutional networks (Kipf & Welling, 2017), and graph attention
134   networks (Veličković et al., 2018). Nevertheless, their application to hypergraphs is not
135   straightforward. The origins of learning transductive tasks stretch back to the seminal work
136   by Zhou et al. (2006). More recently, Hypergraph neural networks (Feng et al., 2019),
137   Dynamic HGNNs (Jiang et al., 2019), and HyperGCN (Yadati et al., 2019) build upon
138   the convolutional learning schema introduced in Kipf & Welling (2017) while works such
139   as Bai et al. (2021) aim to bring both convolutional as well as attention to the context of
140
      hypergraphs. The proposed method can also be viewed as an extension of label propagation
      (Zhu & Ghahramani, 2003; Huang et al., 2020) or feature propagation (Rossi et al., 2022).
141
      While there do exists algorithm for label propagation in hypergraphs (Henne, 2015; Lee et al.,
142
      2024), the proposed method aims to be comparatively simpler to understand, implement
143   and calculate.

      4     Convolutional Signal Propagation
147
148
149   We present Convolutional Signal Propagation (CSP), a method for signal propagation on
150   hypergraphs. In the following subsections, CSP is first introduced in the general setting,
151
      followed by a comparison to established approaches and a discussion of possible variants
      inspired by them. Finally, applications of CSP to different kinds of signals in hypergraph
      tasks are discussed.

160   be repeated to obtain smoother final representations, resulting in a multi-step CSP process
161   generating a sequence of representations X (l) , where X (0) = X. Appendix A.1 gives an
      example of how CSP is applied to a simple dataset.

195   4.2   Application of CSP to different signals in hypergraphs
196
197   The construction of Convolutional Signal Propagation in Section 4.1 was a general one,
198   assuming a signal matrix X ∈ Rn×d . In practice, one can use CSP to propagate various
199   kinds of “signals” in the hypergraph. Namely, the matrix X may represent actual node
200
      features as provided in the underlying graph dataset. This setting leads to a method similar
      to feature propagation (Rossi et al., 2022) or hypergraph convolution (Feng et al., 2019).
      Such an approach is elaborated further in Section 4.3. Alternatively, an approach based on
201
202
      label propagation by Zhu & Ghahramani (2003) may be obtained by taking as X a version
203   of the label matrix Y masked by the training set, a setting described in Section 4.4 and 4.5
204   and evaluated in Section 5.

247   4.5   Comparison with the Naive Bayes classifier
248
249   The Naive Bayes classifier is a well-known classification method that calculates the posterior
250
      probability p(y|ξ) of a label y given a feature vector ξ. Using Bayes’ rule, this is expressed
      as p(y|ξ) = p(ξ|y)p(y)/p(ξ) with theQ“naive” assumption that the conditional probability
      p(ξ|y) can be factorized as p(ξ|y) = i p(ξi |y). Here, p(ξi |y) is estimated from the training
251
252
      set for all feature-label pairs.
253
254   In the case of binomial Naive Bayes, the maximum likelihood estimation of the model pa-
255   rameter p(ξi |y = 1) is given by the ratio of the number of positive examples containing
256
      feature ξi to the total number of examples in the training set that include ξi . When con-
      sidering nodes as examples and features as hyperedges, the estimated binomial parameter
      of a given feature corresponds to the hyperedge score defined in Equation 1. While Naive
257
258
      Bayes inference is based on probabilistic reasoning, where predictions are the product of the
259   model parameters assuming feature independence, CSP employs a filtering (convolutional)
260   approach. Applying Bayes’ rule translates Naive Bayes into a posterior prediction reflecting
261   the prior distribution, which is not captured by CSP. Therefore, Naive Bayes is expected to
262   perform better in classification tasks. On the other hand, accounting for priors becomes a
263   disadvantage in a retrieval setup, as target examples typically have low prior probabilities.
264

      4.6.1    Alternative normalizations of the adjacency matrix
271
272   In graph neural networks, the adjacency matrix A is normalized by multiplying it with
273   the inverse of the node degree matrix Dv . While the DeepWalk algorithm (Perozzi et al.,
274   2014) corresponds to the row-wise normalization Dv−1 A, newer methods also consider the
275   column-wise normalization ADv−1 and most predominantly the symmetric normalization
                      introduced in Kipf & Welling (2017). Because the matrix HDe−1 H T plays

284   4.6.2    Generalization of label propagation with general values of α
285   Section 4.4 shows that CSP is generalization of label propagation with α = 12 to hypergraphs.
286   This parameter α in label propagation controls how much the score of a node is influenced
287   by its neighbors. To provide such a configurability for CSP, a similar parameter α0 may be
288   introduced:
289                         X (l+1) = α0 Dv−1 HDe−1 H T X (l) + (1 − α0 ) X (l) .              (13)
290   This version is a full generalization of label propagation as described in Equation 7 to
291   hypergraphs. Note that the parameter α0 has different semantics and boundary values from
292   α in label propagation.
293
294   4.6.3    CSP in inductive settings
295
      Within the definition of CSP in Equation 3, we assume that the algorithm operates on a
296
      fixed hypergraph H, which corresponds to a transductive setup. This means the algorithm
297   requires the entire hypergraph to be processed at once.
298
299   By decomposing CSP into its stages, we can adapt it for the inductive scenario. The score
300
      calculated according to Equation 1 can be treated as a trained model, similar to the way
      a Naive Bayes model is trained (see Section 4.5). The second stage (Equation 2) can then
301
      be applied to the testing data, where the node of interest does not need to have been part
302
      of the training process, thus allowing for induction. Another advantage is that the second
303   stage of CSP (Equation 2) is performed independently for each node.
304
305   The inductive extension also offers several options. First, while Equation 2 naturally handles
306
      nodes that did not appear in the original hypergraph H, incorporating new hyperedges
      is less straightforward. These new hyperedges can either be ignored, or their scores can
307
      be additionally evaluated using Equation 1 and incorporated alongside the trained model.
308
      When considering multiple layers, another degree of flexibility emerges, as model training
309   and prediction can be applied at arbitrary layer.

364   5.2     Tasks
365
366   We address two primary tasks in our experiments: transductive node classification and
367   retrieval.
368
369   5.2.1     Classification Task
370
371   In the classification setting, the aim of the model is to predict binary labels on the testing
372   set. We use leave-one-out cross-validation with 10 folds, where nodes are randomly assigned
373   to folds. One fold is hidden for testing, and the method is trained on the remaining nine
374
      folds. For each dataset, we generate test predictions for each node (when it is in the testing
      set). For each class, we calculate the ROC-AUC and average these scores. This average is
375
      reported as the classification score for each method on the given dataset.
376

      5.2.2     Retrieval Task
379
380   In the retrieval setting, the aim of the model is to rank the nodes in the test set to maximize
381   precision in the top positions. In this case, one fold is used for training and the other 9
382   are used as the testing dataset. The training set consists of all nodes in the graph, with
383   the positive nodes in the training fold labeled as positive and all other nodes labeled as
384   unknown. For models that require negative training examples, we randomly sample a set
385
      of the same size as the testing set and consider these labels as negative. Although this
      introduces some label noise, we assume that the negative class is dominant, making the
      noise acceptable. The model then ranks the nodes from the testing folds, and we evaluate
386
387
      precision at the top 100 positions (P@100). This evaluation is performed for each fold and
388   class, and the average P@100 over folds and classes is reported as the retrieval score for each
389   method on the given dataset.

519
520   6   Conclusion
521
522   This paper presents a signal propagation algorithm termed Convolutional Signal Propaga-
523   tion (CSP). We formally describe the CSP algorithm and demonstrate its simplicity and
524   efficiency of implementation. This formal description allows us to show clear relationships
525   between CSP and well-known algorithms such as Naive Bayes, label propagation, and hy-
526   pergraph convolutional networks. These relationships suggest various algorithmic variants,
527   which we left for detailed future exploration.
528   We discuss the application of CSP to different types of signals. Our primary focus is prop-
529   agating binary labels, which is used for classification and retrieval tasks, positioning CSP
530   as a hypergraph variant of traditional label propagation. Additionally, propagating node
531   features instead of labels as signals leads to feature propagation. This dual functionality
532   showcases the versatility of CSP in handling various tasks on hypergraphs.
533
      The application of CSP to several real-world datasets from multiple domains demonstrates
534   how these problems can be effectively expressed using hypergraphs. Evaluating CSP in
535   these scenarios shows its competitive performance in both node classification and retrieval
536   tasks, compared to a range of reference methods. Furthermore, we assess the computational
537   complexity of these methods by examining their execution times, highlighting the simplicity
538   and efficiency of CSP. This combination of competitive performance, low computational
539   complexity, parameter-free nature, and flexibility in implementation makes CSP an ideal
      choice as a baseline for learning on structured data.
