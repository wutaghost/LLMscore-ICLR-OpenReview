009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Over the past decade deep learning has revolutionized the field of computer vision,
012            with convolutional neural network models proving to be very effective for image
013            classification benchmarks. Given their widespread adoption, several theoretical
014            works have analyzed their expressiveness, and study the class of piecewise linear
015            functions that they can realize. However, a fundamental theoretical questions
016
               remain answered: why are piecewise linear functions effective for feature extraction
               tasks that arise in image classification? We address this question in this paper by
017
               introducing a simplified mathematical model for feature extraction, based on
018
               classical template matching algorithms that are commonly used in computer vision.
019            We then prove that convolutional neural network classifiers can solve this class of
020            image classification problems, by constructing piecewise linear functions that detect
021            the presence of features, and showing that they can be realized by convolutional
022            neurons. We also discuss the interpretability of the networks we construct, and
023            compare them with those obtained via gradient-based optimization methods by
024            conducting experiments on simple datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Over the past decade, convolutional neural network architectures have led to breakthroughs in a
029
      range of computer vision tasks, including image classification (25), object detection and semantic
030   segmentation (39). Architectures such as AlexNet (18), VGG (40) and ResNet (28) have empirically
031   shown that large convolutional neural networks can perform well on complex benchmarks such as
032   ImageNet (12). While there has been theoretical research attempting to explaining their success in
033   this context, fully understanding the underlying principles that contribute to their widespread adoption
034   and effectiveness remains an open question.
035
      Since their inception in the 1990s, it has also been empirically established that convolutional net-
036   works excel at feature extraction tasks (25). One of the major limitations they face is their lack of
037   interpretability (46; 14); it difficult to pinpoint which features are being captured in their hidden
038   layers. Advances in interpretability could also lead to the design of architectures that are both more
039   transparent and efficient, by identify redundant parameters and thereby reducing the model’s size
040   and latency. Theoretical results can provide a deeper understanding of how these networks represent
041   features internally and guide the development of models that are more interpretable.
042   In this work, we build on previous theoretical work studying the expressiveness of convolutional
043   networks with ReLu activations, and examine the class of functions they can approximate. It is
044   well-known that they compute piecewise linear functions, and the complexity of these function
045   classes has been studied extensively (45), (33), (13), (34). A key result in this field is the universal
046   approximation theorem, which shows that they can approximate piecewise linear functions with
047   arbitrary precision (10; 29; 48). However, it is not clear if piecewise linear functions on the space
048   of all input images can used for feature extraction problems, limiting the applicability of universal
049   approximation theorems in computer vision.
050   By answering the mathematical question of why piecewise linear functions are effective for feature
051   extraction, our key results bridge the gap between existing theoretical work analyzing the expressive-
052   ness of neural networks using these functions, and empirical results on the success of convolutional
053   neural networks for computer vision problems. Our construction also provides insight into the inter-
      pretability of convolutional networks, and illustrates the principle of hierarchical compositionality


                                                         1
054
      (13; 34) whereby simpler features are learnt in the earlier layers and used to construct higher-level
055   features in the latter layers.
056
057   Our contributions. To address these problems, in this paper we present a rigorous mathematical
058
      framework that serves as a simplified model for image classification problems, based on classical
      template matching algorithms (6; 26; 17). Here rectangular regions of an image are compared to a
059
      predefined template, by sliding the template across the image and measuring the similarity at each
060
      position using the L1 distance metric. This framework, which is depicted below, serves as a simplistic
061   model for the feature extraction problem in computer vision.

082   the proof, we use piecewise linear functions that detect whether a given feature is present within a
083   fixed rectangular region, and show that these functions can be realized by convolutional neurons.

097   Related work.
098   Approximation theory. In the 1990s, it was shown that a neural network with a single hidden layer
099   can approximate any continuous function provided that its width is sufficiently large (10), (29).
100   More recently, analogous results were established for deep neural networks with ReLu activations
101   (21; 24; 31), and similar results were established for deep convolutional neural networks in (48).
102   While these results give valuable insights, they do not explain why the class of piecewise linear
103   functions is suitable for feature extraction, limiting their applicability to computer vision questions.
104
      Our work bridges the gap by answering this question using a simplified model based on classical
      template matching algorithms for vision, and serves as a starting point for a more comprehensive
105
      theory explaining the real-world success of convolutional networks.
106
107   Expressiveness of neural networks. Another line of work analyzes the number of linear regions in
      the piecewise linear function that is computed by a neural network with ReLu activations (33), (36).


                                                         2
108
      Using combinatorial results, they derive lower and upper bounds for the maximal number of linear
109   regions in a fully connected ReLu network with L hidden layers and pre-specified widths (41), (4),
110   (22; 23), (47). These results show that deep fully connected networks can express functions with
111   exponentially more linear regions than their shallower counterparts (35), (42). While analogous
112   results for deep convolutional networks were established recently (45), it is unclear how this relates
113   to real-world vision datasets. Our work complements the above paper, by introducing a class of
114   piecewise linear functions that excel at feature extraction which are crucial to the empirical success
115   of convolutional networks.
116   Hierarchical compositionality. Hierarchical compositionality in computer vision refers to the principle
117   of constructing complex images from smaller features in a layered fashion (13; 34; 1). In recent
118   work (8), the authors analyze this phenomena empirically using synthetic data generated from a
119   random hierarchy model. In (32), a similar generative model for vision data is presented, and used
120   to analyze the convergence of a clustering algorithm using stochastic gradient descent. By using a
121   template matching framework for feature extraction, our work provides additional insight by explicitly
122   constructing CNNs where the neurons in earlier layers of the network recognize simpler features (eg.
123
      2 × 2 patches) which are then used to recognize more complex features in latter layers.
124   Interpretability. A key obstacle to the interpretability of convolutional networks is the principle of
125   superposition, where multiple features are encoded simultaneously within a single neural dimension,
126   making individual feature disentanglement challenging (14). In (46), a loss function is added to
127   train networks where filters in the last convolutional layer correspond to a specific class. Sparse
128   autoencoders (11; 30; 5) have been used to combine multiple neuron activations in the original
129
      network into a neuron that detects a specific feature. While a full explanation of how the hidden layers
      of a convolutional network function remains elusive, our work introduces a simplified mathematical
130
      model that can be used to study these phenomena through the lens of piecewise linear functions.

183   3     A MATHEMATICAL FRAMEWORK FOR IMAGE CLASSIFICATION
184
185   In this section, we present a rigorous mathematical framework for image classification problems.
186   Our framework is closely related to template matching, a classical technique in computer vision that
187   locates parts of an image matching a predefined template (6; 26; 17). It works by sliding the template
188   across the image and computing a similarity measure to see whether or not there is a match. By using
189   this simplified framework with features being defined by predefined templates, in the subsequent
190   sections we will illustrate how piecewise linear functions on the input space can be used to extract
191
      features, which in turn leads to our results on the expressiveness of CNNs in this context.

250   Our main results in this section solve the image classification problem presented in Section 3, and

      construct a convolutional network classifier that accurately predicts the labels of input matrices from
         I
252   Xm,n  . Existing work on expressiveness of deep networks focuses primarily on the setting whether the

254

      4.1   A RE PIECEWISE LINEAR FUNCTIONS EFFECTIVE FOR FEATURE EXTRACTION ?
259
260   The output logits of convolutional neural networks compute piecewise linear functions, which leads
261   us to the question of how they can separate different image classes. The first key step in the proof is
262   to construct a piecewise linear function on Xm,n , the space of all input images, that detects whether
263   or not an input matrix contains a given feature. The value of the function should be non-zero if the
264   input image contains the feature, and zero otherwise. We formulate this precisely below; the sliding
265   window technique used for template matching is the key to constructing these functions.
266   Proposition 1. Given an image I ∈ I, there exists a piecewise linear function ϕI (x) such that the
267   following statement holds.

282   4.3   D EEP CNN S AND HIERARCHICAL COMPOSITIONALITY
283
284   It is widely understood that the success of deep convolutional networks is due to the principle of
285   hierarchical compositionality (see (13; 34)), whereby complex structures are obtained by combining
286   simpler ones in a hierarchical fashion. Deeper layers in the network can recognize more complex
287   features, building upon the simpler features that were detected by earlier layers. Theorem 2 below,
288   which is a variant of Theorem 1, highlights this concept. In the proof, we interpret the hidden neurons
289
      in the convolutional layers, and show how neurons in earlier (resp. latter) layers can detect smaller
      (resp. larger) patches of feature tiles.
290
291   Theorem 2. Let I = {I1 , · · · , Il } be an image class, such that each image Ij has size less than 2r
292   for some r. There exists a network C[I] with a padding layer, r convolutional layers and one fully
                                                                  I
293   connected layers such that the induced classifier f C[I] : Xm,n → I has zero error.

      7    D ISCUSSION AND FURTHER DIRECTIONS
493
      Limitations of template matching. Our mathematical framework for generating images based on
494
      template matching illustrates how piecewise linear functions can be used for feature extraction, and is
495
      a starting point for a theory explaining the success of convolutional networks. However, template
496   matching algorithms (6; 26; 17) do not suffice to fully explain the real-world success of convolutional
497   networks. While template matching can handle cases where a portion of image is occluded (19), it
498   does not cover scenarios where the image is rotated, distorted, or its texture modified (16). In order to
499   account for these scenarios, it would be interesting to generalize our template matching framework,
500   which uses a discrete collection of feature tiles for each image, to a setting where features corresponds
501   to a continuous spectrum of shapes (for instance, an eye should correspond to a spectrum of shapes
502   and textures obtained by deforming a given eye, and not a finite set of eye shapes).
503   Interpretability. While the convolutional networks that we construct contain monosemantic neurons,
504   which are only activated for images of a specific class, optimization methods yield networks with
505   polysemantic neurons, which activate for multiple unrelated classes (14). Our experiments indicate
506   that even for solving template matching problems with convolutional networks, bridging the gap
507   between monosemanticity and polysemanticity remains an important open question. While there has
508   been progress in this direction using sparse autoencoders (11; 30; 5), this does not fully explain the
509   inner workings of convolutional networks. Our theoretical framework for extracting features using
510
      piecewise linear functions could be used to investigate how the output logits of trained networks
      perform feature extraction, and determine if there is a subset of neurons that are responsible for
511
      recognizing a given feature.
512
513   Sparsity. Empirically it has been observed that neural networks trained on computer vision datasets
514   can be sparsified without a drop in accuracy (15), (20), but the theoretical underpinnings of this
515   phenomena are not fully understood. Our simplified model for classification provides some insight
516
      into sparsity patterns; in particular our networks in Theorem 1 use a fully connected layer which
      contain weights that have values 0 or 1, whereby each convolutional filter is connected to a unique
517
      output logit with non-zero weight. While existing work on lottery tickets (15) yields pre-defined
518
      sparsity patterns that are difficult to interpret, generalizing our theoretical results in Theorem 2 could
519   lead to new block sparsity patterns for convolutional layers with theoretical guarantees.
520
521   Stochastic gradient descent. While our work builds on existing results studying the expressiveness
522
      of convolutional networks, another line of work investigates their learning capabilities using gradient-
      based optimization, and establishes theoretical convergence guarantees with varying assumptions
523
      (2; 3; 7; 49). Existing work has answered the question of how these networks memorize data with
524
      gradient-based optimization, but this doesn’t extend to feature extraction problems that arise in
525   real-world applications. Our experimental results indicate that convolutional neural networks can
526   achieve near-perfect accuracies on our synthetic datasets. It would be interesting to extend our
527   results and obtain theoretical convergence guarantees, and determine whether overparametrization is
528   necessary in this setting (2; 49).
529
530
      8    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we present a novel mathematical framework based on template matching that can be
533
      used as a simplified model of real-world computer vision tasks. Our key insight is a construction
534   of piecewise linear functions that effective for feature extraction, which we use to analyze the
535   expressiveness of convolutional networks and show that they can solve image classification tasks. We
536   do not anticipate any immediate negative societal impacts, as the present work is primarily theoretical
537   in scope. We also discuss implications for the interpretability of convolutional networks, based on the
538   principle of hierarchical compositionality. Our work is a starting point for a rigorous mathematical
539   theory explaining the effectiveness of convolutional networks for computer vision, which is crucial
      for understanding the interpretability of these models.
