009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Linear recurrent neural networks, such as State Space Models (SSMs) and Lin-
012            ear Recurrent Units (LRUs), have recently shown state-of-the-art performance
013            on long sequence modelling benchmarks. Despite their success, their empiri-
014            cal performance is not well understood and they come with a number of draw-
015            backs, most notably their complex initialisation and normalisation schemes. In
016            this work, we address some of these issues by proposing RotRNN – a linear recur-
017            rent model which utilises the convenient properties of rotation matrices. We show
018
               that RotRNN provides a simple and efficient model with a robust normalisation
               procedure, and a practical implementation that remains faithful to its theoretical
019
               derivation. RotRNN also achieves competitive performance to state-of-the-art lin-
020
               ear recurrent models on several long sequence modelling datasets.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Long sequence modelling is a notoriously difficult domain in machine learning due to the need
026   to capture long-range dependencies between input data. Typical sequence models, such as Trans-
027   formers (Vaswani et al., 2017) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Rumelhart et al., 1987;
028   Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Cho et al., 2014; Koutnik et al., 2014), fail to perform well on
029   these tasks. In the case of Transformers this is due to poor inductive biases and quadratic scaling of
030   computational complexity with sequence length, and in the case of non-linear RNNs it is caused by
031   vanishing and exploding gradients. Recently, State Space Models (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2021; Smith
032   et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2022a) have emerged as the state-of-the-art framework for learning on
033
      long-sequences of data. The S4 model (Gu et al., 2021), inspired by linear time invariant dynamical
      systems, utilises a linear recurrent layer with HiPPO initialisation (Gu et al., 2020) to solve the van-
034
      ishing and exploding gradient problem of classical RNNs. Moreover, the computational complexity
035
      of S4 scales linearly in time with sequence length, and thus circumvents the quadratic computational
036   scaling of Transformers. Interestingly, the linear recurrence of S4 hidden state can be viewed as both
037   a linear RNN for fast inference and as a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for efficient parallel
038   training.
039
      Despite the mathematical elegance of the S4 derivation, the question of whether such careful ini-
040
      tialisation is required remains open. Indeed, several subsequent works suggested that the specific
041
      initialisation and discretisation methods that theoretically motivated S4 may not be necessary for
042   highly performant SSMs (Gupta et al., 2022a;b; Gu et al., 2022; Smith et al., 2023; Orvieto et al.,
043   2023). These findings led to the development of the Linear Recurrent Unit (LRU) (Orvieto et al.,
044   2023), which showed that competitive empirical performance on long sequence modelling tasks
045   can be achieved by making some small modifications to a standard linear RNN, without using the
046   theoretical insights from SSMs. While the LRU is conceptually simpler than prior works, the theo-
047   retical motivation does not necessarily reflect the practical implementation of the algorithm, leading
048   to more unanswered questions as to why SSMs and the LRU are stable and performative on long
049   sequence modelling tasks.
050   The LRU and other linear recurrent layers have been used as basic building blocks for more com-
051   plex sequence modelling architectures (Gu & Dao, 2023; De et al., 2024). Motivated by the wide
052   adoption of such models (Zhu et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2024; Patro & Agneeswaran,
053   2024), we extend the body of work on linear RNNs by proposing a novel recurrent block, deriv-
      ing a conceptually simple but mathematically principled way of performing linear recurrence by

      using rotation matrices. By parameterising the recurrent state matrix as a rotation, we are able to
055   provide more stable normalisation than prior works, and allow for a robust implementation that faith-
056   fully reflects its theoretical motivation. Importantly, our model remains consistent with the theory
057   throughout training, and is not purely used to find a good initialisation of the recurrent matrix (Gu
058   et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2022a; Smith et al., 2023). Moreover, we derive a mathematical equiva-

060   some of the LRU’s internal mechanics. We summarise our main contributions as follows:
061   • We propose a novel linear RNN layer where the recurrent state matrix A is parameterised as a
062     rotation, with the resulting algorithm faithfully reflecting the theoretical motivation throughout
063     training.
064
      • We present a method for computing efficient matrix powers of parametric rotations to enable
065     fast linear recurrence with rotation matrices.
066
      • We use this new formulation to derive a principled normalisation procedure which retains a con-
067
        stant expected hidden state magnitude that holds throughout training.
068
069   • We show competitive performance with the state-of-the-art on long sequence benchmarks, and
070
        show that the improved normalisation of our model holds on practical tasks.

076   SSMs (Gu et al., 2021; Smith et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2022a) are derived from time-invariant
077   continuous-time linear ordinary differential equations (ODEs), of the form

084   Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) or Bilinear discritisation. Under the ZOH method, the resulting discrete
085   system can be expressed by the following recursion:

132   2.3    D RAWBACKS OF P RIOR W ORKS
133
134
      These prior methods for tackling long sequence modelling with linear recurrence have several draw-
      backs. In the case of SSMs, a complicated theoretical derivation based on polynomial projections
135
      is required to initialise the recurrent HiPPO matrix. However, this is purely used as an initialisa-
136
      tion procedure. The inner workings of SSM models throughout training is not well understood, and
137   more research is needed to uncover why deviation from optimal polynomial projections of func-
138   tions improves results and remains stable throughout learning. Indeed, as shown in the LRU, it is in
139   fact unnecessary to use such theoretical motivation to achieve strong performance on long sequence
140   benchmarks.
141
      What actually goes on under the hood of the LRU, though, is also not entirely clear. The motivation
142
      of the LRU stems from constraining classical linear RNNs, but this is not fully reflected in the final
143   proposed algorithm. Firstly, eigenvalues of the real matrix A come in conjugate pairs, but this is
144   not enforced in the LRU (as it is in, for example, S5 (Smith et al., 2023)). Moreover, there is no
145   constraint ensuring that yt = C̃ x̃t = CP P −1 xt = Cxt is real-valued – i.e. that the P and P −1
146   components of the learned C̃ and x̃t are consistent. Instead, the authors simply take the real part of
147   the resulting complex yt . Additionally, the LRU is normalised (at initialisation) by ensuring expected
148   convergence in the limit of infinite sequence length (see 4.1), but in practice a learnt normaliser is
149   used, and does not necessarily result in desirable or consistent hidden state magnitudes (Figure 3).
150
      In this work, we aim to overcome this mis-match between theoretical motivation and practical im-
151   plementation by proposing a novel linear recurrent model using rotation matrices. Our algorithm
152   is conceptually simple to understand, efficient to compute, robust to exploding hidden state norms,
153   and, importantly, faithfully reflects the mathematical principles that underpin its motivation.

      3     ROT RNN
157
158
      The key component of our model, which we call the Rotational Recurrent Neural Network
      (RotRNN), is the parameterisation of the recurrent state matrix A as a rotation matrix. The rea-
159
      son for this choice is three-fold:
160
161       i) Rotation matrices can be generated smoothly from any real-valued matrix, making them robust
             to initialisation and easy to constrain during training (Section 3.1).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
        ii) Rotations can be easily decomposed for fast computation of matrix powers, which is needed in
163         linear RNNs (Section 3.2).
164
       iii) The orthogonality of rotation matrices, and the fact that their eigenvalues lie on the unit circle,
165
            allows us to derive a simple normalisation scheme that retains a constant expected hidden state
166
            norm throughout training (Section 3.3).
167

      4.1   L INEAR R ECURRENT U NITS
306   The RotRNN algorithm proposed in this paper is inspired in part by the LRU (Orvieto et al., 2023).
307   As well as sharing superficial similarities in the structure of the recurrent layer, more formal com-
308   parisons can be drawn between the two architectures. In this section, we derive a mathematical
309   equivalence between a special case of the LRU and the RotRNN, and compare the normalisation
310   procedures of the two models.
311
312   Multihead RotRNN as a special case of the LRU In practice, ignoring skip connections, the
313   LRU recurrent layer has 3 parameter matrices: Λ, a diagonal matrix of complex eigenvalues; B̃,
314   a dense, complex input matrix; C̃, a dense, complex linear output projection. Consider the case
315   where the learned eigenvalues of A come in complex-conjugate pairs.    In this case, the matrix Λ
316                                                   νj cos θj −νj sin θj
      can be written as a block diagonal matrix of                           , with B̃ and C̃ real matrices
317                                                   νj sin θj νj cos θj
318   (see App. E of Orvieto et al. (2023) for details). If one assumes that B̃ = P ⊤ B and C̃ = CP for
319   some block-diagonal orthogonal matrix P , then, up to normalisation, this is algebraically equivalent
320   to the multi-head RotRNN with H = D2x heads and the dimension of each head is Dh = 2. This
321   is because the parallel headed structure of the RotRNN can be viewed as one big block-diagonal
322   recurrent layer, with corresponding dimensions of B that project onto each head being normalised
323   independently, and the corresponding decay factors γ (h) modulating the eigenvalue magnitude as
      does νj in the LRU.

394   4.2     S TATE S PACE M ODELS
395
396   The relationship between RotRNN and other SSMs, namely S4 (Gu et al., 2021) and S5 (Smith et al.,
397   2023), is perhaps less formally equivalent, but we can still draw comparisons between the structures
398   of the recurrent layers. The S4 recurrence can be viewed as a stack of single-input-single-output
399   (SISO) SSMs, whereby independent recurrent layers operate on each channel of the vector-valued
400   input. The outputs of these independent SSMs are concatenated and passed through a “mixing layer”
401   to combine information. S5, on the other hand, uses a single multi-input-multi-output (MIMO) SSM
402
      as its recurrent layer, and as such does not require a separate mixing layer to share information
      across dimensions. The input-output structure of RotRNN sits somewhere in-between S4 and S5.
403
      The use of multiple independent RotRNN heads outlined in Section 3 can be viewed as a stack of
404
      independent MIMO recurrent layers, in which the user may specify both the number of heads and
405   the dimension of each head separately. Multiplication with the output matrix C can be viewed as a
406   linear mixing layer, combining information from the hidden states of each independent head. For
407   more details on the SISO and MIMO views of S4 and S5, we direct the reader to Smith et al. (2023).

458   6     R ELATED W ORK
459
460   Orthogonal Recurrent Networks The use of orthogonal and rotation matrices in recurrent net-
461
      works has been explored previously in non-linear RNNs. Unitary RNNs (uRNNs) (Arjovsky et al.,
      2016; Jing et al., 2017) prevent blow-up in long sequences by parameterising recurrent matricies
462
      with unitary matrices (an extension of orthgonality to the complex field) to ensure an eigenvalue
463
      magnitude of 1. This idea has since been applied without the need for complex numbers (Helfrich
464   et al., 2018), and has inspired works replacing recurrent operators in LSTMs with rotations (Dan-
465   govski et al., 2019; Velici & Prügel-Bennett, 2021). These methods, however, still suffer from the
466   drawbacks of classical non-linear RNNs, such as inefficient computation of recurrent states during
467   training when compared to associative-scan based linear models.
468
469   Building on Linear Recurrent Models Linear recurrent layers have recently been used as build-
470   ing blocks to construct more complex long sequence models. Two representative examples include
471   Griffin (De et al., 2024) built on top of the LRU, and Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) built on top of S4.
472   The key to the success of both these models is gating – the ability to construct recurrent state matri-
473   ces based on the current inputs to selectively control information flow. We believe that the RotRNN
474
      could be used as a drop-in replacement for the LRU in Griffin, or be used to perform alternative
      gating strategies with input dependent rotations, but we leave this direction for future work.
475

477   7     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
478
479   In this paper we propose RotRNN, a linear recurrent model that utilises the convenient properties
480   of rotation matrices. We show that RotRNN performs competitively with the state-of-the-art on
481   long-range sequence modelling benchmarks, while providing a conceptually simple and efficient
482
      algorithm. RotRNN remains faithful to its theoretical derivation throughout training, with a robust
      normalisation procedure that does not rely on complex initialisation.
483
484   In addition, we hope that the concrete comparisons between our model and the LRU drawn in
485   Section 4 can shed new light on the inner workings of the LRU and similar algorithms as multi-
      headed rotation-based linear recurrent networks. We point future investigations towards integrating

768   def binf(a, b):
769
          """Binary function for the parallel scan"""
          gamma_i, thetas_i, acc_i = a
770       gamma_j, thetas_j, acc_j = b
771
772        # get off diagonal terms [-x2, x1, -x4, x3,...]
773        # these will be multiplied by sin(\theta)
774
           off_diags = jnp.stack([-acc_i[..., 1::2], acc_i[..., 0::2]], axis=-1)
           off_diags = off_diags.reshape(acc_i.shape)
775
776        # duplicate \theta [\theta_1, \theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_2,...]
777        theta = jnp.repeat(thetas_j, repeats=2, axis=-1)
778
779
           # compute sine and cosine elements of the output
           sin = jnp.sin(theta) * off_diags
780        cos = jnp.cos(theta) * acc_i
781        acc = gamma_j * (cos + sin)
782
783        return (gamma_i * gamma_j, thetas_i + thetas_j, acc + acc_j)
