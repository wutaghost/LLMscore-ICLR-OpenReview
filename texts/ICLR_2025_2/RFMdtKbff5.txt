009                                            Abstract
010
011           We study which machine learning algorithms have tight generalization
012           bounds in the overparameterized setting. Our results build on and extend
013           the recent work of Gastpar et al. (2024).
014           First, we present conditions that preclude the existence of tight generalization
015           bounds. Specifically, we show that algorithms that have certain inductive
016           biases that cause them to be unstable do not admit tight generalization
017           bounds. Next, we show that algorithms that are sufficiently stable do have
018           tight generalization bounds. We conclude with a simple characterization
019           that relates the existence of tight generalization bounds to the conditional
020
              variance of the algorithm’s loss.
021
022   1   Introduction
023   Generalization bounds are at the heart of learning theory, and they play a central role
024   in attempts to mathematically explain the behavior of contemporary supervised machine
025   learning systems. A generalization bound is an upper bound of the form
026
027
                                              LD (A(S)) ≤ b,                                       (1)
028   where A(S) is the hypothesis output by learning algorithm A when executed with training
029   set S, and LD (·) represents the loss with respect to the population distribution D. The term
030   b is typically an expression of the form
031
                                      b = LS (A(S)) + c(S, A(S), H),                               (2)
032
033   where LS (·) is the empirical loss, H is a hypothesis class, and c(S, A(S), H) is a ‘complexity’
034   term, such as the VC dimension or a spectral norm, etc.
035
      We say that a generalization bound is valid if for every population distribution D, Eq. (1)
036
      holds with high probability; we say that a valid bound is uniformly tight (Definition 2.4) if
037
      for every population distribution, with high probability the difference between the two sides
038   of Eq. (1) is small.
039
040   Bounding the loss using a generalization bound is quite different from using a validation set.
041
      Technically, a generalization bound does not use additional samples beyond the training set
      S. And while a validation set provides a single post-hoc measurement of the population
042
      loss after training is complete, a good generalization bound can provide insight into why
043
      a learning algorithm performs well, and can offer guidance for model selection and the
044   development of new learning algorithms. For a generalization bound to be useful in this
045   way, it is important that the bound be tight, so that it can distinguish cases with small
046   population loss from cases with larger loss.
047
048
      Unfortunately, experimental works have shown that many of the generalization bounds of
      the form of Eq. (2) that have been proposed in the literature are vacuous1 when applied to
049
      contemporary learning algorithms such as deep neural networks (Jiang et al., 2020; Dziugaite
050
      et al., 2020; Viallard et al., 2024, Section 4.4).
051
052      1
           A bound is vacuous if it is of the form P[LD (A(S)) ≤ b] ≥ 1 − δ where δ ≥ 1 or (for the 0-1
053   loss) b ≥ 1. Namely, it is a true statement that provides no guarantees on the performance of the
      algorithm.

      Gastpar, Nachum, Shafer, and Weinberger (2024) offered a partial theoretical explanation for
055   this empirical finding. They considered generalization bound as in Eq. (2), namely, bounds
056   that depend only on the training set, the selected hypothesis, and the hypothesis class. They
057   proved that any such bound cannot be uniformly tight in the overparameterized setting.2
058   Therefore, they recommended focusing on generalization bounds involving expressions of
059   the form c(S, A(S), H, A, D), i.e., bounds that depend also on the specific training algorithm
060   and a specific collection D of population distributions for which the bound is intended.
061   This recommendation raises the following natural question:
062
063
         Question 1.1. For which algorithms and distribution collections do there exist
064
         tight generalization bounds?
065
066
      This question was addressed in Theorems 3, 4 and 5 of Gastpar et al. (2024), but the
067
      general case remains open. In this paper we continue investigating this question, and present
068   conditions that are necessary, sufficient, or necessary and sufficient for the existence of tight
069   generalization bounds for a given learning algorithm and distribution collection.
070
071   1.1 Setting
072   Following Gastpar et al. (2024), we study the existence of tight generalization bounds using
073   a notion of estimability.
074   Definition 1.2 (Estimability). Let X and Y be sets, let m ∈ N, let
075

078   function
                                                        m
079                                        E : (X × Y) → R.
080

081

082   estimator E such that
083
                            ∀D ∈ D : PS∼Dm E (S) − LD (A(S)) ≤ ε ≥ 1 − δ.

084
085   We say that A is estimable on average with respect to distributions D with precision ε and
086   confidence δ using m samples if there exists an estimator E such that
087
                           PD∼U(D),S∼Dm E (S) − LD (A(S)) ≤ ε ≥ 1 − δ.

088
089   (More briefly, we say that (A, D) is (ε, δ, m)-uniformly estimable, or (ε, δ, m)-estimable on
090   average.)
091
092
      The connection between estimability and tight generalization bounds is as follows.
093   Fact 1.3. Using the notation of Definition 1.2, if (A, D) is (ε, δ, m)-estimable on average,
094   then there exists a generalization bound b(S) (that may depend on A and D) that is ε-tight
095   on average, namely
096
                          PD∼U(D),S∼Dm [b(S) − ε ≤ LD (A(S)) ≤ b(S)] ≥ 1 − δ.                          (3)
097
098   Indeed, the generalization bound is simply b(S) = E (S)+ε, where E is the estimator witnessing
099   the estimability of (A, D).
100   In the other direction, if (A, D) is not (ε, δ, m)-estimable on average, then there exists no
101   generalization bound that satisfies Eq. (3), and in particular no generalization bound can be
102   uniformly tight (as in Definition 2.4).
103

104
      necessary, sufficient, or necessary and sufficient for a tuple (A, D) to be (ε, δ, m)-uniformly
105
      estimable, or (ε, δ, m)-estimable on average?

      1.3 Our Results
217
218
      We investigate which algorithms and collections of distributions are estimable. Recall that
      in Example 1.9 we saw that estimability is a delicate phenomenon. In particular, changing
219
      the sample size by just a small constant number can in some cases drastically change the set
220
      of (ε, δ) estimability parameters that are achievable. This means that identifying a simple
221   and tight characterization that precisely determines the number of samples necessary and
222   sufficient for estimability can be a difficult undertaking.
223
224
      In this paper, we present conditions that preclude estimability, conditions that guarantee
      estimability, and a condition that is both necessary and sufficient for estimability.
225
226   Our first result is a condition that precludes estimability for algorithms that have an inductive
227   bias towards certain subsets of VC classes, showing a connection between estimability and a
228   central notion from traditional learning theory.
229   Theorem (Informal version of Theorem 3.1).                       X
                                                    √ Let H ⊆ {±1} be a hypothesis class with
230
      VC dimension d large enough, and let m ≤ d/10. Then there exists a subset F ⊆ H and
231   corresponding realizable distributions D such that any learning rule that has an inductive bias
232   towards F is not (1/4 − o(1), 1/6, m)-estimable on average over D.6
233
234
      Note that the theorem precludes estimability on average, and so in particular it precludes
      worst-case estimability. The proof of Theorem 3.1 uses the Johnson–Lindenstrauss lemma
235
      (Theorem L.1), the probabilistic method, and a technical lemma (Lemma I.1) concerning the
236
      estimability of nearly-orthogonal functions.
237
238   To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to provide a rigorous and general
239   mathematical formulation showing that any finite VC class admits inestimable algorithms.
240   This is somewhat surprising because it means, for instance, that for any neural network
241
      architecture, there are some training algorithms for which one will not be able to derive tight
      generalization bounds (even if the distribution is realizable!). We believe this is a meaningful
242
      contribution.
243
244   Our next inestimability result is as follows.
245   Theorem (Informal version of Theorem 3.2). Let H ⊆ {±1}X be a collection of roughly 2m

      One way to interpret Theorems 3.1 and 3.2 is to consider a scenario where one derives
271   a new generalization bound for a given algorithm, without making explicit distributional
272   assumptions (as is the case for many published generalization bounds), and having a sample
273   size within the regime of our theorems. Such bounds are generally formulated as high
274   probability upper bounds on the population loss. Note that the lack of distributional
275   assumptions means that the bound has to hold (be a valid upper bound) for all distributions,
276   including the families of distributions that appear in our theorems.
277   But this means, in the light of our theorems, that the considered bound is necessarily very
278   weak for many distributions unless one satisfies at least one of the following items:
279
280
       1. Exclude in advance all families of distributions with nearly-orthogonal labeling functions,
          and use this fact in the derivation of the generalization bound.
281
282    2. Mathematically show that the algorithm is not biased towards any set of nearly-
283       orthogonal functions.10
284   The intuition behind Theorem 3.2 is that having an inductive bias towards a collection
285   H of nearly-orthogonal functions makes the algorithm very unstable – small changes in
286   the training set will cause the algorithm to shift between hypotheses in H, which are all
287   very different from one another. This motivates our next result, which shows that stable
288   algorithms are estimable, as follows.
289
      Theorem (Informal version of Theorem 4.3). Let A be an algorithm that is sufficiently
290   stable with respect to a collection of distributions D (in a sense of loss stability or hypothesis
291   stability similar to Rogers and Wagner, 1978, or Kearns and Ron, 1999). Then (A, D) is
292   estimable.
293
      Seeing as there are many definitions of stability in the literature, Theorem 4.3 makes a
294
      nontrivial conceptual contribution by identifying the “correct” notion of stability for under-
295
      standing estimability. Other notions of stability, such as leave-one-out stability (Bousquet &
296   Elisseeff, 2002), do not capture estimability as well, as we discuss in Section 4.
297
298   An additional motivation for Theorem 4.3 is the intuition that contemporary machine learning
299
      algorithms (like deep neural networks) might indeed be sufficiently stable. If so, Theorem 4.3
      would apply, meaning that it is possible to obtain tight generalization bounds for deep neural
300
      networks based on the stability property. To substantiate this intuition, we conduct simple
301
      preliminary experiments to estimate the the stability of neural networks in practice. Our
302   empirical findings, presented in Appendix M, suggest that neural networks are indeed quite
303   stable.
304
305
      Finally, in Section 5, we present a necessary and sufficient condition for estimability based
      on the conditional variance of the algorithm’s loss. This characterization is formalized in
306
      terms of ℓ2 estimability, which is asymptotically equivalent to average case estimability via
307
      Markov’s inequality.
308
309   Fact (Fact 5.2). A is (ε, m)-estimable in ℓ2 with respect to D if and only if
310                                      E[var(LD (A(S)) | S)] ≤ ε.

313
      The most closely related works to our study are those by Gastpar et al. and prior research
314   on stability, which we will examine in detail in this section. For a broader comparison to
315   other studies addressing generalization bounds and their limitations, we refer the reader to
316   Appendix A.

      1.4.1 Comparison to Gastpar et al. (2024)
319   The estimability setting studied in our paper was introduced by Gastpar, Nachum, Shafer,
320
      and Weinberger (2024). In Theorem 3 of their paper, they show a limitation on estimability
      (a learnability–estimability trade-off) for algorithm-dependent bounds that is fairly abstract
321
322     10
           It is known that there exist at least some neural network architectures which, when trained
323   with SGD, are capable of learning orthogonal functions (such as parities). See Theorem 1 in Abbe
      and Sandon (2020).


                                                      6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      and involves a total variation condition that might be hard to check in many cases. In
325   contrast, Theorems 3.1 and 3.2 involve very concrete combinatorial and geometric conditions
326   (VC dimension, orthogonal functions). Theorems 4 and 5 in their paper are more concrete,
327   but they hold only for exactly orthogonal functions with strict algebraic structure (parity
328   functions). In contrast, our Theorem 3.2 applies generally to any nearly-orthogonal function
329   class (including classes that are exactly-orthogonal as a special case).
330
      Unlike Gastpar et al. (2024), our work also presents positive results (Theorem 4.3 and Fact 5.2),
331
      showing cases where generalization bounds for specific algorithms can be tight even in the
332   overparameterized setting. The conceptual connections between estimability, stability and
333   conditional variance appearing in those results was not present in Gastpar et al. (2024).
334
335   Finally, our techniques also differ from those of Gastpar et al. (2024). We use the Johnson–
336
      Lindenstrauss lemma, our technical lemma (Lemma I.1), and the duality of linear program-
      ming — expanding the arsenal of tools readily available for the study of estimability.
337
338   In summary, our work builds upon the foundation laid by Gastpar et al. (2024), but we
339   make several important contributions that go beyond their results.

      1.4.2 Stability
341
342
      In Definitions 4.1 and 4.2, we formalize simple stability conditions that guarantee the
      existence of tight generalization bounds, as we show in Theorem 4.3. There are many
343
      definitions of stability in the literature, and it is important to appreciate that Theorem 4.3
344
      makes a nontrivial conceptual contribution by identifying the “correct” notion of stability
345   for understanding estimability.
346
347   Definitions 4.1 and 4.2 are similar to the definition of hypothesis stability and loss stability
348
      in Kearns and Ron (1999), Elisseeff, Evgeniou, and Pontil (2005), and Rogers and Wagner
      (1978). Lei, Jin, and Ying (2022) use another similar definition for stability and utilize it to
349
      derive generalization bounds for GD and SGD.
350
351   In contrast, our definitions of stability are also reminiscent of the replace-one stability in
352   Bousquet and Elisseeff (2002), but as we explain in Section 4, our definitions overcome an
353   important limitation present in their definition. In particular, the memorization algorithm
354   (Example 1.7), which is very estimable, is not stable according to the definition of stability
355
      of Bousquet and Elisseeff (2002), but it is stable according to our definitions.
356

      4        Sufficient Conditions for Estimability
451   In Examples 1.6 and 1.7 we saw that the constant algorithm and the memorization algorithm
452   are very estimable. These algorithms are also very stable. Indeed, they always output the

454   certain algorithms that are very unstable, are not estimable. This suggests that stability
455   might play an important role in determining the estimability of an algorithm.

504   process can be used to estimate hypothesis stability. Simply measure the disagreement

643   The works of Nagarajan and Kolter (2019, Theorem 3.1) and Bartlett and Long (2021,
644   Theorem 1) also study cases where generalization bounds fall short of estimating the
645   performance of learning algorithms (while Negrea et al., 2020 provide a response to these
646   claims). They preclude tight algorithm-dependent generalization bounds only for uniform
647   convergence and linear classifiers. Their theorems consider specific distributions (Gaussian
      in Nagarajan and Kolter, 2019, a different distribution per sample in Bartlett and Long,


                                                   12

648
      2021) and specific types of SGD. In contrast, our work uses the same marginal distribution
649   across all sample sizes, and applies to many algorithms and distributions.
650
651   We now mention a few of the algorithm-dependent generalization bounds in the literature.
652   Zhang, Teng, and Zhang (2023) study convex optimization, so their results apply only to a
653   single neuron. While providing matching lower and upper bounds, these bounds match only
654
      asymptotically when the sample size n is very large, far from the overparameterized regime
      relevant for neural networks. Nikolakakis, Haddadpour, Karbasi, and Kalogerias (2023)
655
      proposes generalization bounds for algorithms satisfying a certain symmetry property (e.g.,
656
      full-batch gradient descent) when using smooth losses. These bounds are algorithm-dependent
657   but distribution-free, making no distributional assumptions.
658
659   There are a number of information-theoretic generalization bounds that are both algorithm
660   and distribution-dependent, such as Theorem 1 of Xu and Raginsky, 2017. However,
661
      such bounds are sometimes difficult to approximate numerically in a tight manner. These
      bounds are part of the PAC-Bayes framework.15 Unfortunately, when these PAC-Bayes or
662
      information-theoretic bounds can be approximated in a tight manner,16 they do not reveal
663
      what properties of the (distribution, algorithm) pair allowed for such success in learning
664   and estimation. The works of Haghifam, Moran, Roy, and Dziugiate (2022b) and Rammal,
665   Achille, Golatkar, Diggavi, and Soatto (2022) use the notion of leave-one-out conditional
666   mutual information to derive generalization bounds, which provide another characterization
667   of VC classes and yield non-vacuous generalization bounds for neural networks.

673   generalization bounds to be tight, which is the focus of our work. Additionally, they study
674
      generalization bounds for fairly specific families of algorithms such as gradient descent (or
      idealized versions thereof), while our work applies to a broader and more general class of

      B     On the Definition of Overparameterization
678
679   In this paper we use a definition of overparameterization (Definition 1.4) introduced by
680   Gastpar et al. (2024) (see Definition 2 in that paper).
681
      This definition is motivated by the need to formalize many common definitions of overpa-
682
      rameterization in a manner that is true to the basic intuitive notion of overparameterization
683   while also being is suitable for proving mathematical theorems.
684
685   Appendix D19 of Gastpar et al. (2024) offers a detailed discussion of the merits of Definition 1.4,
686   and how it generalizes many common definitions. Below, we provide a brief summary of
687
      the main points made in that appendix (the reader is encouraged to consult the original

727   Remark B.1. One way to understand the motivation for Definition 1.4 is to consider an
728   analogy with the definition of a continuous function. The most common and basic definition
729   of a continuous function is the ε-δ definition (developed by Bolzano, Cauchy, Weierstrass
730   and Jordan in the 1800s). That definition very clearly captures the intuitive notion of
731   continuity. Later on, however, that definition was generalized by Hausdorff, who gave a
732   modern topological definition of a continuous function, requiring that the preimage of any
733
      open set is an open set. This more modern definition might appear rather strange at first, and
      somewhat removed from the basic intuition of what a continuous function is. Nonetheless, it
734
      turns out that the topological definition does not just generalize the basic definition, but it
735
      does so in a way that is very useful, while remaining true to the intuitive notion of continuity.
736   Similarly, Definition 1.4 generalizes the intuitive notion of overparameterization in a way
737   that is useful for proving mathematical theorems.

739   C    On Extending Our Results to Randomized Algorithms
740
      For simplicity, in this paper we focus on deterministic learning rules. However, we recognize
741
      that the topic of randomized learning algorithms is very important, seeing as most algorithms
742
      used in practice today are randomized.
743
744   The estimability framework explored in this paper can be extended to handle randomized
745   algorithms as well, and in fact the original work of Gastpar et al. (2024) already contains
746
      some initial treatment of randomized algorithms.
747   We expect that the results presented in this paper can be extended to randomized algorithms,
748   and that the essence of the results remains mostly unchanged.
749
      The first step in such an extension would be to clearly define what estimability means for
750   randomized learning algorithms. A definition that one might initially consider is one where
751   the estimator knows the randomness used by the algorithm, and must output a number
752   that is with high probability close to the true population loss of the randomized algorithm.
753   This definition is not very interesting, because a setting in which the estimator knows the
754   randomness used by the randomized algorithm is equivalent to the setting of a deterministic
755   algorithm, which is already covered by the results in this paper. Nonetheless, it is good to
      keep this definition in mind, because it means that our results for deterministic algorithms


                                                     14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      already apply as-is to randomized algorithms (like SGD) once the randomly chosen seed
757   is fixed, which might be a simple and satisfactory approach for many purposes (SGD with
758   a fixed random seed typically performs as well for most purposes as SGD with a fresh
759   randomly-chosen seed).
760
761
      Perhaps the more “correct” and interesting definition of estimability for randomized learning
762   algorithms is one where the estimator knows the training set, but does not know the
763   randomness used by the learning algorithm, and it is required to output a number that
764   is close with high probability to the expected population loss of the randomized algorithm
765   when executed with this training set (where the expectation is over the randomness of the
766   algorithm). In this setting, we believe the essence of our results carries through, with an
767   important conceptual difference: using randomness, one can always engineer a learning
768   algorithm that is estimable, essentially by adding noise to the output of the algorithm. As
769   the noise in the algorithm’s output increases, the expected 0-1 loss of the algorithm becomes
770
      closer to 1/2, and so the algorithm becomes estimable with a trivial estimator that simply
      always outputs the number 1/2. (With intermediate amounts of noise, a number between 0
771
      and 1/2 will be optimal).
772
773
774
      Consequently, for randomized algorithms, our lower bounds in Theorems 3.1 and 3.2 can
      no longer be stated as absolute limitations on estimability. Rather there is now a trade-off
775
      between the performance of the algorithm and its estimability. As one adds more noise, the
776
      algorithm becomes more estimable, but its performance degrades. Thus, the corresponding
777   theorems for randomized algorithm would state that no algorithm can simultaneously make
778   good predictions for some large set of labeling functions and also be estimable.
779
780
      On the other hand, the upper bound in Theorem 4.3 that states that stable algorithms are
781
      estimable remains basically unchanged for randomized algorithms.
782
783
784
      To summarize, under a suitable definition of estimability for randomized algorithms, we
      expect that our results would not change much, though the statement (and proof) of the
785
      lower bounds would be somewhat more complex. We leave this work to future research.

790   For sample size m ≥ d + 10, any ERM algorithm for H satisfies p(m) ≥ 0.999, meaning it
791   learns D well, and hence is (0, 10−3 , d + 10)-estimable on average. This holds because for an
792   ERM to output the ground truth, it is clearly sufficient that only a single sample-consistent
793   function exists in the concept class (the ground truth). Similarly, in the event that there
794   are t > 1 sample-consistent functions, the success probability is given by 1/t due to the
795   uniform prior over ground truth distributions. Parity functions are fully characterized by
796
      their coefficient vector w = [w1 , . . . , wd ]. Since the labels y are a bilinear function in the
      inputs x and coefficients w, one can obtain w from m ≥ d linearly independent samples xi
797
      by solving the linear system of equation y = Xw with design matrix X ∈ {0, 1}m×d . More
798
      generally, X having rank d − k is equivalent to the event of having t = 2k sample-consistent
799   functions (coefficient vectors) since every additional linearly independent row rules out half
800   of all parity functions. Now assume X consists of all i.i.d. Ber(½) entries and y contains
801   the labels of all samples. The probability of zero population loss can now be obtained from
802   the law of total probability with the probabilities of rank deficiency computed according to
803   Corollary 2.2 in Blake and Studholme (2006).
804
805   Similar calculations show that for smaller sample sizes, any ERM for H satisfies p(d) ≥ 0.61,
806   and p(d − 1) ≥ 0.38. An application of Theorem 5 in Gastpar et al. (2024) shows that there
807   exist ERM algorithms such that for any 6 ≤ m ≤ d there exists a collection Dm for which the
808   algorithm is not (0.25, 0.32, m)-estimable on average. These algorithms have an inductive
809   bias towards a subset F ⊆ H, such that they perform well for distributions labeled by a
      function from F, and perform poorly for target functions from the complement of F.

       M     Experiments
1284   M.1 Motivation and Setup
1285   Here, we examine if there are practical algorithms that admit loss stability or even hypothesis
1286   stability with substantial numerical values. To this end, we conduct experiments over a
1287   simple neural network architecture across four datasets: MNIST, FashionMNIST, CIFAR10,
1288   and CIFAR10 with random labels (figures 1-4, respectively). Throughout all experiments, we
1289   employ one-hidden-layer perceptrons with 512 hidden neurons. We train the models using
1290   stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a momentum factor of 0.9 and a batch size of 1000,
1291   optimizing the cross-entropy loss. For every data set, we train the models across learning
1292
       rates 0.1, 0.035,22 and 0.01. We average all the curves over 10 random seeds (tied for the
       pairs of networks) and plot the standard deviation for all the curves.
1293
1294     22
            Except for CIFAR10, we present the results only for learning rate 0.1 and 0.01 to prevent clutter.
1295   The qualitative results are consistent across all datasets; that is, the curves of learning rate 0.035 lie
       between the curves of learning rate 0.1 and 0.01.

1296
       The training procedure is as follows: we train two models in tandem, starting from the same
1297   random initialization. The first model is provided with the full training set, whereas the
1298   second model has k = 100 data points removed from its training set. These points are drawn
1299   uniformly at random before the beginning of the training, and fixed thereafter. After each
1300   epoch, we evaluate the training accuracy, test accuracy and hypothesis stability, i.e., the
1301   agreement between the two models (which we calculate across the test set).
1302
       We set our main focus on the agreement of the models since the most amenable way to
1303   show loss stability might be by way of proving hypothesis stability. The latter can perhaps
1304   be mathematically proven in the case of neural networks by analyzing the stability of the
1305   training dynamics under two slightly different training sets.
1306
1307   M.2 Results
1308   Across all experiments, the training and test accuracy of the model pairs are essentially
1309   identical throughout the training process. This suggests that at least simple models are loss
1310   stable across vision tasks. In order to reduce visual clutter, we hence only plot training and
1311   test accuracy of the first model (which has access to the full training set), respectively.
1312   We observe higher agreement for simpler data sets and smaller learning rates. For example,
1313   the learning rate has a considerable effect on agreement for CIFAR10 (≈ 0.65 for learning
1314   rate 0.1 vs ≈ 0.8 for learning rate 0.01).
1315
       The key takeaway from Figures 1 through 4 is that the agreement is consistently higher than
1316   the test accuracy. This relationship ensures that when applying the estimation procedure
1317   outlined in Theorem 4.3, we can avoid vacuous predictions of perfect accuracy. In the
1318   scenarios presented, the estimated accuracy will always be bounded away from 1, as it can
1319   be expressed as test error + (1 - agreement). For instance, with a learning rate of 0.01, the
1320   maximum estimated accuracies are: 98% for MNIST (compared to 97.5% test accuracy),
1321   90% for FashionMNIST (87% test accuracy), 72% for CIFAR10 (52% test accuracy), and
1322   65% for CIFAR10 with random labels (10% test accuracy). These results illustrate a strong
1323   correlation between stability estimation and data complexity.
1324   We repeat the same experiments, modifying the width of the hidden layer to investigate its
1325   impact on stability. The results, summarized in Table 1, reveal a strong positive correlation
1326   between network width and stability. This effect is particularly pronounced for more complex
1327   tasks, such as CIFAR10 and CIFAR10 with random labels. For instance, in the CIFAR10
1328   random labels setting with a learning rate of 0.01, increasing the width from 256 to 1024
1329   neurons improves agreement from 32% to 50%, highlighting the stabilizing effect of greater
