011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Hyperspectral imagery captures rich spectral information, which is valuable for
014            a wide range of applications but poses challenges due to high data dimension-
015
               ality. Current band selection methods are often computationally intensive, non-
               embedded, or lack adaptability for specific tasks. We address this gap by intro-
016
               ducing a novel plug-and-play embedded method for supervised band selection in
017
               hyperspectral imagery, utilizing a concrete selector layer based on the Gumbel-
018            Softmax re-parameterization trick. Our approach allows for dynamic and task-
019            specific selection of optimal bands, eliminating the need for pre-processing and
020            enabling seamless integration with downstream models. We evaluated the method
021            on four hyperspectral datasets, covering three remote sensing benchmarks and an
022            autonomous driving task, demonstrating consistent improvements over state-of-
023            the-art methods. This is the first work to perform comprehensive band-selection
024            research on an autonomous driving dataset of this type, and the first to employ
025            a concrete layer for supervised band selection. Our findings highlight the po-
026
               tential of this approach for real-time hyperspectral analysis in applications such
               as autonomous driving and environmental monitoring, laying the groundwork for
027
               further exploration of efficient, domain-specific band selection.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) captures the complete optical spectrum at each point within an im-
033   age. Unlike a standard color camera, which records light intensity in three colors (red, green, and
034   blue), a hyperspectral camera captures a wide range of wavelengths, typically consisting of several
035   hundred bands. This transition from color to full hyperspectral imaging significantly increases the
036   amount of information captured, offering considerable potential across various applications such as
037   medical imaging, agriculture, aerial photography, and autonomous driving (Gutiérrez-Zaballa et al.,
038
      2023; Gutiérrez-Zaballa et al., 2022; Bayramoglu et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2020; Kazi Saima Banu &
      Gardea-Torresdey, 2024; Sun et al., 2024).
039
040   One notable advantage of HSI is its ability to provide detailed spectral information, enabling im-
041   proved differentiation between materials that might otherwise look similar in traditional RGB imag-
042   ing. This capability enhances object segmentation, especially for applications like Advanced Driver-
043   Assistance Systems (ADAS) and Autonomous Driving Systems (ADS), leading to greater accuracy
044
      and robustness in object identification and tracking tasks (Huang et al., 2021; Colomb et al., 2019;
      Weikl et al., 2022).
045
046   However, leveraging the full range of spectral bands provided by HSI introduces several challenges.
047   The vast amount of data generated by HSI systems requires significant resources for sensor hard-
048   ware, storage, transmission, and analysis, making these systems expensive and cumbersome. The
049   high dimensionality of HSI data also complicates real-time processing and increases the computa-
050
      tional burden for many applications.
051   Therefore, there is a critical need for effective band selection algorithms that can reduce the number
052   of bands while retaining essential information for downstream tasks. Targeted selection of relevant
053   spectral bands can enhance the efficiency of deep learning models, making HSI more practical for
      real-world applications across various domains. Improved band selection algorithms can also help in


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the design of sensors that retain the benefits of HSI while integrating simpler technologies which is
055   crucial for the advancement of intelligent ADAS/ADS (Pinchon et al., 2019; Winkens et al., 2019).
056
057   While existing research has explored band selection methods, these often involve independent unsu-
058
      pervised or supervised preprocessing steps, which can result in suboptimal band choices for specific
      downstream tasks in hyperspectral imaging (HSI).
059
060   In this paper, we present a novel plug-and-play embedded method for supervised band selection in
061   hyperspectral imagery, which stands apart from existing techniques due to its seamless integration
062   and efficiency. Unlike traditional methods that often require separate pre-processing or unsuper-
063   vised feature selection, our approach directly integrates the band selection process into the training
064
      pipeline without additional pre-processing steps. Central to our method is the innovative use of the
      Gumbel-Softmax re-parameterization trick (Jang et al., 2017), which allows for differentiable, su-
065
      pervised selection of optimal spectral bands, enabling the model to dynamically identify the most in-
066
      formative features for each task. This approach uniquely combines the strengths of concrete selector
067   layers with the flexibility to learn which bands are most relevant, enhancing both model performance
068   and simplicity.
069
070
      Our method effectively learns the optimal bands as an integral part of a Convolutional Neural Net-
      work (CNN) (O’shea & Nash, 2015), focusing on the challenging task of semantic segmentation,
071
      which predicts semantic categories for each pixel in an image. We show that our model consistently
072
      outperforms state-of-the-art band selection techniques across four hyperspectral datasets, including
073   remote sensing and autonomous driving tasks. Notably, our method excels even when selecting
074   a small number of bands, highlighting its practicality for designing low-cost, deployable sensors
075   suitable for real-world applications.

078   2   R ELATED WORK
079
080   Recent advances in hyperspectral band selection can be categorized into several key approaches, dis-
081   tinguished by their supervision level (supervised vs. unsupervised) and integration with downstream
082   tasks (embedded vs. non-embedded).
083
      Unsupervised and non-embedded methods are often applied as a pre-processing step without tai-
084   loring to or integration into downstream models. Dimensionality reduction based methods include
085   PCA-based systems such as Kang et al. (2017). Reconstruction-based techniques, such as BS-
086   nets (Cai et al., 2020), DARecNet-BS (Roy et al., 2020), and TAttMSRecNet (Nandi et al., 2023),
087   frame band selection as a reconstruction problem using autoencoders. Sparsity-based methods, like
088   SpaBS (Sun et al., 2014) and SNMF (Sun et al., 2015), aim to find sparse representations and often
089   utilize clustering.
090   Supervised non-embedded techniques use labeled data to guide the selection of the most informative
091   spectral bands but are not directly integrated into the downstream model during training. Instead,
092   they perform the band selection as a separate pre-processing step. Genetic algorithms such as Ou
093   et al. (2023); Esmaeili et al. (2023) select predictive bands via an evolutionary process. Concrete
094   Autoencoders have also been applied for unsupervised band selection by leveraging concrete ran-
095   dom variables and reconstruction loss to select optimal bands based on an information entropy (IE)
096   criterion (Abid et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2021). Deep Reinforcement Learning (Mou et al., 2021; Feng
097   et al., 2021; 2024), maximize the utility of selected bands for specific tasks.
098   Regularization-based methods such as EHBS (Zimmer & Glickman, 2024) stand out as some of the
099   few supervised and embedded approaches, enabling efficient integration with the learning pipeline
100   by imposing constraints (e.g., relaxation of the l0 norm).
101
      Despite these advancements, state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods still primarily focus on remote sens-
102   ing applications and are often not optimized for selecting a small number of bands. Moreover,
103   most existing techniques are non-embedded, complicating integration with downstream models and
104   leading to suboptimal accuracy. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to present a
105   comprehensive, supervised, and embedded band selection approach that utilizes a concrete layer
106   with the Gumbel-Softmax re-parameterization trick, demonstrating its effectiveness on both remote
107   sensing and autonomous driving datasets, thereby setting a new benchmark for band selection in
      hyperspectral imagery.

      3.2   P ROPOSED FRAMEWORK
130
      Our proposed system is an end-to-end embedded system. It comprises a downstream task model en-
131   hanced with an additional concrete selector layer inserted between the input and task models. This
132   added layer is based on the principles of the Gumbel-Softmax trick (Jang et al., 2017). Our novel
133   adaptation is specifically tailored for supervised hyperspectral band selection within the context of
134   image semantic segmentation. By adding this layer, our proposed model leverages the intrinsic
135   characteristics of hyperspectral data and addresses the unique requirements of semantic segmen-
136   tation tasks, seamlessly integrating with downstream models without the need for band-selection
137   prepossessing.
138   In the setting of band selection and contrast to the standard feature selection setting, all features
139   of a given band should either be included or excluded from the input. We have thus adapted the
140   concrete layer, initially designed for feature selection, to work over groups of features. This is done
141   by altering the gates layer to either mask all the features in the group (i.e., band) or to leave the
142   features intact. This layer comes right after the input layer and precedes the downstream task’s first
143   layer of the deep learning network.
144
145   3.3   C ONCRETE SELECTION LAYER
146
147
      The concrete band-selection layer uses the Gumbel-Softmax trick to create a differentiable approx-
      imation of discrete feature selection. This layer is embedded within a neural network between the
148
      input layer and the downstream task model to allow the model to learn which features to select
149
      during training. The concrete band-selector layer is defined by (L, τ, α, β). L is a learnable k × n
150   logits matrix in which n is the total number of bands and k is the target number of bands. Each
151   n-dimensional row of L corresponds to a selector for a specific band. τ ∈ (0, ∞) controls the tem-
152   perate parameter of the corresponding concrete distributions. The temperature τ is initialized with
153   a certain value and is gradually reduced by the decay factor α. As the temperature gradually lowers
154   towards 0, smoothly shifting from exploration to exploitation, the concrete random variables ap-
155   proach the discrete distribution, outputting one-hot vectors and thus acting as a band-specific mask
156   on the input. β is the scale parameter of the Gumbel distribution that controls the magnitude of the
157   noise added to the logits when calculating the mask vectors in the forward pass.
158   In each forward pass, the calculated Gumbel softmax per each row of the matrix is multiplied by the
159   full HSI 3D input and is passed on to the first layer of the deep learning network of the downstream
160   task. Given an input tensor X ∈ Rn×s1 ×s2 , where n is the number of bands and each band is an
161   image of size (s1 , s2 ), the encoder encodes X into a Rk×s1 ×s2 tensor by multiplying it by a matrix
      M ∈ Rk×n . The matrix M is derived from the logit matrix L and the temperature parameter τ by


                                                           3

      applying the Gumbel-Softmax operation over the rows of L as follows:
163
164                                           exp((Li,j + Gi,j )/τ )
165                                  Mi,j = Pn                          ,
                                             r=1 exp((Li,r + Gi,r )/τ )
166
167   where Gi,j = − log(− log(ui,j )) and ui,j ∼ Uniform(0, β) and β is the scale parameter of the
168   Gumbel distribution that controls the magnitude of the added noise.
169
      The values of (L, τ, α, β) need to be initialized before training begins, as they play a critical role
170
      in the learning process of the model. The values of the matrix L are learned as part of the training
171   process of the entire neural network in an attempt to solve the optimization problem defined in equa-
172   tion 1. As τ → 0 ( by multiplying it by the decay factor α at the end of each batch), the distribution
173   becomes more discrete, whereas larger values of τ result in a smoother, more probabilistic selection.
174   This re-parameterization allows for differentiable sampling, enabling gradient-based optimization
175   during the training process. During inference, only the Gumbel-Softmax is not applied but rather a
176   corresponding one-hot encoded matrix derived from the logits matrix is used to get a final selection
177   in an efficient and deterministic manner.

237   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS FOR THE REMOTE SENSING TASK
238
239   For the remote sensing datasets (PaviaU, Salinas, and Chikusei), we employed a 10-fold cross-
240   validation scheme where the pixels were randomly split into training and validation folds. Initial
241   hyperparameter tuning was conducted using the Pavia dataset, and the resulting settings were then
242   applied to the Salinas and Chikusei datasets.
243
      For the downstream classification task, we implemented a 3D Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
244   model, following the architecture outlined in Ben Hamida et al. (2018). This model classifies small
245   patches of a hyperspectral image (HSI) cube by assigning the label of the central pixel, which is a
246   common practice in the field (Chen et al., 2016; He et al., 2017; Luo et al., 2018; Ben Hamida et al.,
247   2018; Lee & Kwon, 2016; Paoletti et al., 2023; Giri et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). The network
248   consists of three 3D convolutional layers, operating on input patches of 5x5 pixels, followed by a
249   fully connected linear layer. We used a batch size of 256 and employed cross entropy as the loss
250   function
251   For evaluation, we used overall accuracy as our main metric. We also measured average accuracy
252   and Kappa score as they are commonly used to evaluate this task (Nandi et al., 2023; Cai et al.,
253   2020).
254

      4.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS FOR THE AUTONOMOUS DRIVING TASK
256
257
      For the autonomous driving dataset (HSI-Drive V2), which involves image-level tasks, we split
258   the 756 images into training, validation, and test sets in a 7:1:2 ratio. Hyperparameter tuning was
259   performed using the validation set, and results were reported on the unseen test set. We followed the
260   experimental setup proposed by the original authors of the dataset, conducting one experiment for a
261   5-class scene understanding Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS) task. For the downstream
262   task models, we implemented a UNet-based architecture that classifies the HSI image into a semantic
263   mask as is commonly done for this task (Long et al., 2015; Gutiérrez-Zaballa et al., 2023). The
264   encoder consists of 4 convolutional blocks, each applying a 0.5 downsampling factor to compress
265   the input data. Each block utilizes a kernel size of 3 for the convolutional operations. The decoder
266
      mirrors this process by progressively upsampling the data to restore the original dimensions. As the
      loss function, we used Weighted Cross Entropy.

268   For the drive dataset, as each sample is an image, we have used, in addition to Overall and Average
269   Accuracy, Average IOU, Precision, and Recall per each label class, as reported in similar settings.

296   4.5   I NITIALIZATION
297
298
      As part of the implementation of the Concrete Selection Layer (see section 3.3), one needs to initial-
      ize the model parameters (L, τ, α, β). The temperature params (τ, α) and the noise param (β) were
299
      treated as hyperparameters, and their initialization values were tuned in the same manner as other
300
      network hyperparameters as described above. We tested different initial temperatures of τ ∈ [0, 10]
301   and α ∈ [0.99, 0.99999]. The values chosen and used for the final evaluation of the remote sensing
302   datasets were τ = 1.5, α = 0.99998, β = 0.15, and for the drive dataset τ = 8.5, α = 0.9999,
303   β = 0.15.
304
      As for the logits matrix L, we tested two different initialization schemes. The naive initialization
305
      consisted of random initialization of the complete matrix in a uniform manner. In this scheme, each
306
      row of L was initialized using Xavier (Glorot) initialization, in which the values are drawn from a
307   distribution with mean 0. In addition, we tested a novel initialization scheme in which we tried to
308   head-start the selectors, each on a different range of the spectrum. To achieve this, given an k × n
309   logits matrix L in which n is the total number of  bands,
                                                                and k is the target number of bands, we
310   segment the n bands into k segments each of size nk . Each row (gate) in L is initialized to focus
311   on different segments. It is done by adjusting the Xavier initialization in such a way that the mean
312   value of the area we want the gate to focus on to a positive is positive and negative for the other row
313   values while maintaining an overall mean (0) and variance as in the naive Xavier initialization.
314   An example of such an initialized matrix when choosing 5 out of 25 bands is illustrated in Figure 2.
315   Our experimentation showed that our novel initialization scheme produced better results and avoided
316   the concrete selection layer from selecting duplicate bands. We thus used the improved initialization
317   scheme in our final settings, for which we report our results. We discuss this further in section 6.

429   6      A NALYSIS AND D ISCUSSION
430
431   The results clearly demonstrate that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) meth-
      ods, particularly when selecting a small number of bands. We attribute the superior performance

432
      of our method to its ability to learn a specific set of bands tailored to the given task, which may
433   sacrifice information irrelevant to the task but results in better-focused feature selection. Our band
434   selection layer requires a certain number of learnable parameters, which is the total number of bands
435   multiplied by the number of target bands. As a future direction, simplifying the band selection layer
436   to reduce the number of learnable parameters could make the optimization process more efficient,
437   especially for large-scale applications.

439   6.1   S ELECTED BANDS A NALYSIS
440
441   Figure 5a compares the bands selected by our method to those selected by other methods.
442   Reconstruction-based methods tend to select bands that include representative from the extremes
443   of the spectrum, as they optimize for data reconstruction. However, our method, which focuses on
444   optimizing for a specific task, shows that these extreme bands are not always necessary, leading to
445   improved task-specific performance without the need for these difficult-to-acquire bands.
446   Figure 5b shows the selected bands for the HSI-Drive dataset using our method for different target
447   numbers of bands. The selection is consistent, with the top 3 bands (top of figure) remaining largely

466   6.2   I NITIALIZATION
467
468   During initial experiments, we observed that the Gumbel encoder often selected duplicate bands,
469   which reduced the diversity of the selected bands. Figure 6 illustrates the progression of band
470   selection on the Salinas data set with a target of 6 bands. Figure 6a captures the progression of band
471
      selection given a Xavier uniform random initialization of the logits matrix. As can be seen, as the
      learning progresses, the selection of bands converges to only 3 different ones. To address this issue,
472
      we introduced a non-uniform initialization of the logits matrix, segmenting the band spectrum based
473
      on the target number of bands (see section 4.5). Figure 6b illustrates that this improved initialization
474   results in the selection of 8 diverse bands.
475
476   Our novel initialization scheme of the logits matrix provided significant improvements in overall
477
      accuracy while avoiding band duplication. This was achieved by initializing each selector to fo-
      cus on a different region of the spectrum, enhancing the diversity of selected bands and ultimately
478
      improving model performance. Notably, even though the initialization segmented the spectrum, the
479
      learning process was not restricted to selecting a single band in each segment. For example, Figure 7
480   shows the selected bands and the equivalence initialization areas for the drive dataset when selecting
481   8 bands from 25. As can be seen, our method did not select any band from the first segment (bands
482   0-2) and did select two bands in a different region (bands 12-14). We would like to note that we also
483   tried other initialization techniques, including one where we initialized with a higher value the band
484   indices that were selected in other methods, such as SNMF SpaBs and BSNets. This seeding per-
485   formed similarly though not as well as our segment-based initialization. As our method performed
      better and does not rely on running other methods we chose it for our setting of choice.

      6.4   C ONCLUSION
518
519   We introduced a novel supervised band-selection-as-a-layer method for deep learning models, which
520   can be seamlessly integrated into various architectures. Our approach consistently demonstrated
521   strong performance across all tested datasets, particularly excelling when selecting a small number
522   of spectral bands. This capability is crucial for practical applications where reducing the number
523   of bands allows for the use of simpler, more affordable cameras in additional real-world scenarios,
524   such as small robot navigation, medical devices, drones and agricultural applications.
