009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning in vision language models (VLMs) is crucial
012            for improving interpretability and trustworthiness. However, current training
013            recipes lack robust CoT reasoning data, relying on datasets dominated by short
014            annotations with minimal rationales. In this work, we show that training VLM
015            on short answers does not generalize well to reasoning tasks that require more
016            detailed responses. To address this, we propose a two-fold approach. First, we
017            distill rationales from GPT-4o model to enrich the training data and fine-tune
018
               VLMs, boosting their CoT performance. Second, we apply reinforcement learning
               to further calibrate reasoning quality. Specifically, we construct positive (correct)
019
               and negative (incorrect) pairs of model-generated reasoning chains, by comparing
020
               their predictions with annotated short answers. Using this pairwise data, we apply
021            the Direct Preference Optimization algorithm to refine the model’s reasoning
022            abilities. Our experiments demonstrate significant improvements in CoT reasoning
023            on benchmark datasets and better generalization to direct answer prediction as
024            well. This work emphasizes the importance of incorporating detailed rationales
025            in training and leveraging reinforcement learning to strengthen the reasoning
026            capabilities of VLMs.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning is essential for improving the interpretability and trustworthiness
032   of VLMs (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;a; Bai et al., 2023). As
033
      VLMs are increasingly applied to more difficult tasks, the ability to reason through complex problems
      becomes essential. However, current training approaches for VLMs often rely on datasets dominated
034
      by short answers with limited rationales, which may restrict the models’ ability to generalize to tasks
035
      with comprehensive reasoning. In this work, we aim to address these limitations by providing distilled
036   CoT data, introducing supervised finetuning (SFT) and reinforcement learning (RL) strategies to
037   improve VLM reasoning performance.
038
039
      An example in fig. 1 asks for the number of food items in a bar graph. When answering this question,
      a human would typically enumerate the bars and then calculate the total. However, writing out
040
      this enumeration process is far more cumbersome than simply providing the short answer of “14.”
041
      Consequently, the annotated training data is predominantly composed of short answers, with minimal
042   rationale provided. This raises a critical research question: Does training on direct prediction
043   implicitly teach the model to perform chain-of-thought reasoning to derive correct answers? Our
044   findings indicate that after training on 26k direct predictions from ChartQA, the accuracy of direct
045   predictions increased by 2.9 (70.2 to 73.1), while CoT prediction accuracy improved by only 0.6
046   points (71.2 to 71.8), with CoT under-performing direct prediction as a result. This suggests that
047   current training approaches have limited effectiveness in enhancing CoT reasoning.
048   We hypothesize that developing CoT reasoning capabilities requires explicit training on data that
049   includes detailed reasoning steps. To address the scarcity of high quality CoT reasoning data,
050   we propose leveraging datasets with short ground truth annotations and employing the GPT-4o
051   model to generate reasoning paths that lead to the correct answer. Our approach encompasses a
052   diverse range of tasks, utilizing 9 datasets that demand different reasoning skills, including common
053   world knowledge (A-OKVQA), chart interpretation (ChartQA), document information localization
      (DocVQA, InfoVQA), real-world text extraction (TextVQA), scientific reasoning (AI2D, SQA), and

188   We choose LLaMA3-LLaVA-NeXT-8B as our base architecture, whose weight is initialized with the
189
      Open-LLaVA-NeXT weights1 . To ensure the model handles both direct and chain-of-thought (CoT)
      predictions, we implement two types of prompts during training.
190
191   Direct Prediction: For direct prediction tasks, we use the prompt “Answer the question with a short
192   answer” for short-answer questions, and “Answer with the option’s letter from the given choices
193   directly” for multiple-choice questions.
194   CoT Prediction: For CoT prediction tasks, we use the prompt “Generate a reason first and then
195   output a letter answer” for multiple-choice questions, and “Generate a reason first and then output a
196   short answer” for short-answer questions. In the model’s response, the rationale is followed by the
197   answer, which is formatted as “### Answer: ” to enable answer extraction during evaluation.

199   3.3       R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FOR E NHANCED R EASONING
200
201   To further improve the quality of reasoning chains, we apply RL using the DPO algorithm to better
202
      align the model’s reasoning process toward more accurate predictions. The DPO algorithm requires
      both positive and negative responses. To generate these, we use the SFT model as the policy model
203
      (i.e., generator), producing 32 candidate predictions per question (temperature 1.0 for short answer and
204
      1.2 for multiple-choice questions). Each prediction is compared with the ground truth to determine
205   its correctness (fig. 2). Following the approach in Dubey et al. (2024), we select instances with an
206   accuracy between 0.25 and 0.85. From these, we randomly pair positive and negative responses,
207   creating up to three pairs per question.
208
      Formally, the dataset is denoted as DDP O = {(V, x, yw , yl )}, where V is the image, x is the question,
209   yw and yl are the positive and negative responses. The DPO objective is defined as below:

      In this section, we present the prompts used for GPT-4o on benchmark datasets, including both
921   direct and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) predictions. Similar to the findings in Borchmann (2024), we
922   observed that GPT-4o’s performance is highly sensitive to prompt phrasing. We explored several sets
923   of prompts and selected the best-performing ones for reporting results. Specifically, we try to align
924   our results with those reported in Li et al. (2024); Tong et al. (2024), Claude 3.5 Sonnet for Vision 2 ,
925   among others.

928   GPT-4o prompts for the benchmark datasets. A random subset of 200 instances is selected as a
929   development set to evaluate manually designed prompts. We manually inspect the predicted results
930   and identify issues such as the model being overly cautious in declining answers, incorrect output
931
      formatting, or style mismatches with the ground truth labels. As an illustrative example, we detail
      the prompt optimization process using ChartQA, and apply similar techniques to the other datasets.

      Finally, we provide the prompts used for replicating our test results.

1262   In this section, we provide evaluation details for our base model, which uses the LL A MA3-LL AVA-
1263   N E XT-8B architecture with weights initialized from O PEN -LL AVA-N E XT. We selected O PEN -
1264
       LL AVA-N E XT weights because the data and training pipelines were fully available at the time of
       model development, allowing us to avoid reliance on the unreleased real user interactions referenced
1265
       in Liu et al. (2024). The pretraining data for O PEN -LL AVA-N E XT consists of 1M image-text pairs,
1266
       sourced from datasets such as ShareGPT4V, ALLaVA-Instruct-VFLAN-4V, DocVQA, SynDog-EN,
1267   ChartQA, DVQA, AI2D, and GeoQA+.
1268
1269   When evaluating LL AVA-N E XT-8B, we identified several issues, such as the inability to follow
1270
       the CoT prompt, refusal to answer questions, and generating irrelevant reasoning. In fig. C.1, we
       present randomly sampled examples from LL AVA-N E XT-8B with a temperature setting of 1.0 on a
1271
       ChartQA test case. These examples demonstrate the model’s difficulty in adhering to the CoT prompt.
1272
       In the first example, the model declines to answer the question. In the second to fourth examples, the
1273   model provides an answer first, followed by an explanation, which doesn’t effectively use thought
1274   process to answer the question. In the final example, the model generates a descriptive response
1275   instead of reasoning through the question, ultimately failing to provide an answer. This illustrates the
1276   model’s inconsistent handling of the prompt structure.
1277
       Table C.1 presents the evaluation results for LL AVA-N E XT-8B. For CoT predictions, we use
1278
       ChatGPT to extract a letter choice or short answer from the long-form model output, using the
1279   prompts shown in fig. C.2 and fig. C.3. However, due to LL AVA-N E XT-8B ’s inability to accurately
1280   follow the CoT format, its performance is significantly worse compared to direct predictions and our
1281   format-aligned model. For direct prediction, our LL AVA-N E XT-F ORMAT has similar performance
1282   as that of LL AVA-N E XT-8B.
1283
       In fig. C.4, we present the same example trained with our format-aligned data for CoT using only 450
1284
       examples. The model successfully follows the CoT format by verbalizing the thought process and
1285   providing a short answer after "### Answer:". This allows us to use a rule-based extractor to retrieve
1286   answers, which also improves CoT performance, as shown in table C.1. However, the example also
1287   demonstrates that, while our data induces the CoT process, the reasoning remains incorrect. Sampling
1288   32 examples using the format in fig. C.4 resulted in 3 correct answers, yielding an accuracy of 9.4%
1289   for that case. As a result, only slight gain is observed on ChartQA for CoT prediction vs. direct
1290   prediction.

1472   In this section, we demonstrate how minimal CoT training data can enhance CoT reasoning capa-
1473   bilities. Specifically, we use only 450 CoT format-aligned examples alongside all available direct
1474   prediction data, with LL AVA-N E XT-D IRECT as the baseline. We apply rejection sampling fine-
1475
       tuning (RFT) following (Sun et al., 2024; Setlur et al., 2024) to train a self-taught chain-of-thought
       reasoner, denoted as LLaVA-Next-STaR. From LL AVA-N E XT-D IRECT, we sample 32 CoT exam-
1476
       ples for each training instance and select those whose final predictions match the ground truth. Up to
1477
       three positive examples are selected per question, resulting in a dataset of 260k RFT examples.
1478
1479   As shown in table D.1, RFT training improves both CoT reasoning and direct predictions overall, with
1480   the exception of two data points. Notably, TextVQA shows a significant drop in CoT performance,
1481
       which we will explore further in future work. Notable (>3%) gain is observed on ChartQA, DocVQA,
       InfoVQA, AI2D and MathVista, and roughly 1% gain is observed on direct prediction on those
1482
       datasets as well.
1483
1484
       DPO Experiments Prior to the RFT experiments, we conducted DPO experiments on the ChartQA
1485   dataset under the same conditions as described in section 4. However, the improvements were modest,
1486   with a 72.3 (+0.5) gain in CoT prediction and a 74.2 (+0.5) gain in direct prediction. In contrast,
1487   RFT yielded a significant improvement, with 77.9 (+6.1) on CoT prediction and 74.6 (+0.9) on direct
1488   prediction. We hypothesize that for models with relatively weak CoT reasoning capabilities, RFT
1489   may be more effective in enhancing model performance, whereas DPO with preference modeling
1490   may be less impactful. We leave further analysis for future work.
