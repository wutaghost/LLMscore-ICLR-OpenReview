009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) models represent a significant breakthrough in
012            large language model development. These models enable performance improve-
013            ments without a proportional increase in inference costs. By selectively activating
014            a small set of parameters during task execution, SMoEs enhance model capacity.
015            However, their deployment remains challenging due to the substantial memory
016
               footprint required to accommodate the growing number of experts. This constraint
               renders them less feasible in environments with limited hardware resources. To
017
               address this challenge, we propose Hierarchical Clustering for Sparsely activated
018
               Mixture of Experts (HC-SMoE), a task-agnostic expert merging framework that
019            reduces SMoE model parameters without retraining. Unlike previous methods,
020            HC-SMoE employs hierarchical clustering based on expert outputs. This approach
021            ensures that the merging process remains unaffected by routing decisions. The
022            output-based clustering strategy captures functional similarities between experts,
023            offering an adaptable solution for models with numerous experts. We validate our
024            approach through extensive experiments on eight zero-shot language tasks and
025            demonstrate its effectiveness in large-scale SMoE models such as Qwen and Mix-
026            tral. Our comprehensive results demonstrate that HC-SMoE consistently achieves
027
               strong performance, which highlights its potential for real-world deployment.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The exponential growth in model parameters for Transformer-based architectures in natural lan-
032   guage processing (NLP) has led to significant performance improvements across various tasks
033   (Chowdhery et al., 2022; OpenAI et al., 2024; Team et al., 2024). Nevertheless, this increase in
034   size has resulted in challenges for real-world deployment and accessibility due to heightened infer-
035   ence latency and computational requirements (Bommasani et al., 2022) Sparsely activated Mixture
036   of Experts (SMoE) models have emerged as a promising solution to this challenge. SMoE archi-
037   tectures employ a sparse activation mechanism, wherein only a subset of the model’s parameters,
038   or ‘experts’, are activated for each input token. This design enables extensive parametric capacity
039
      without a proportional increase in computational cost during inference, as shown in previous works
      (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022). However, despite improvements in inference latency, the
040
      overall size of SMoE architectures poses a significant challenge for memory usage, and the efficient
041
      reduction of SMoE model size during inference has become a critical area of concern. Recently,
042   the authors in Liu et al. (2023) have identified high representational similarity among experts and
043   suggested a solution to enhance expert diversity within an SMoE. Such an observation is further sub-
044   stantiated by the empirical results provided in Lu et al. (2024). The collective evidence from these
045   previous studies therefore suggests that model parameters in contemporary SMoE architectures may
046   be redundant, which points to avenues for potential optimization and efficiency improvements.
047
      To address the challenges associated with redundant parameters in SMoE models, researchers have
048   proposed various approaches in the past few years. Early endeavors focused on task-specific expert
049   pruning (Chen et al., 2022), which progressively eliminates non-essential experts and ultimately
050   results in a single-expert dense model tailored for a specific downstream task. However, such
051   approaches often necessitate extensive fine-tuning to mitigate performance degradation caused by
052   pruning. Inspired by this limitation, several studies (Lu et al., 2024; He et al., 2024) have explored
053   retraining-free expert pruning methods. For instance, Lu et al. (2024) proposed directly trimming
      experts by using the smallest output loss relative to the original model as an indicator of importance,

137   2.2   E XPERT P RUNING AND M ERGING
138
139   This section discusses prior methods for reducing the number of experts in an SMoE. Table 1 offers a
140   summary of them. We begin with the pruning strategies, and then examines the merging techniques.
141   Pruning strategies for SMoE models have been the focus of several recent studies. Chen et al. (2022)
142   introduced Task-Specific Expert Pruning (TSEP), which iteratively fine-tunes the model while prun-
143   ing experts to gradually reduce the number of active experts for a specific downstream task. Despite
144   its effectiveness, the extensive fine-tuning required renders this process time-consuming and com-
145   putationally expensive, limiting its practicality for large-scale models. Lu et al. (2024) proposed
146   a method which we refer to as O-prune in this study, which directly prunes experts using a com-
147
      binatorial search to minimize the Output loss in a retraining-free, task-agnostic, zero-shot setting.
      This approach begins by identifying the number of experts to retain in each layer. It then conducts
148
      a layer-wise search across all possible expert combinations, selecting the one that minimizes out-
149
      put loss compared to the original model. Nevertheless, this method directly prunes experts deemed
150   unimportant, forfeiting the opportunity to leverage their knowledge. Furthermore, the exhaustive
151   combinatorial search becomes computationally infeasible for models with a large number of experts.
152   For instance, reducing 50% of the experts in a model like Qwen, which has 60 experts, necessitates
153   enumerating approximately C(60, 30) ≈ 1018 combinations per layer, rendering it impractical for
154   large-scale applications. He et al. (2024) introduced an efficient expert trimming technique, denoted
155   as S-prune, due to its reliance on the router Score. In this approach, each expert’s router-score P (x)
156   is accumulated globally, and only the top-scoring experts are retained, while others are pruned. This
157   strategy offers more flexibility than that in Lu et al. (2024), as it enables different layers to retain
158
      varying numbers of experts.
159   Model merging techniques have emerged as a promising approach to combine the strengths of mul-
160   tiple models. ZipIt (Stoica et al., 2024) introduces a model merging technique that allows models
161   with the same architecture but trained on different tasks to be merged without retraining. It uti-
      lizes pairwise feature correlation to merge features both within a single model and across different

210   In this study, we address the challenge of reducing the space complexity of an SMoE model through
211   a process termed expert merging. This process consolidates existing experts in an SMoE layer into a
212   smaller set while preserving the model’s performance. Each SMoE layer initially contains n experts,
213   as defined in Section 2.1. We aim to merge these experts into r clusters, where r represents the target
214   number of experts after merging. For the i-th cluster, denoted as Ci = {E0i , E1i , . . . , E|C

      The distribution of original experts across all clusters satisfies i=1 |Ci | = n, which ensures that all
217   original experts are accounted for in the merging process. To achieve this objective, we explore two
218   grouping strategies. Static grouping maintains exactly r experts in each layer after merging, while
219   dynamic grouping allows this number to vary per layer while maintaining an average of r. Our
220   proposed HC-SMoE adopts static grouping, similar to O-prune, while F-prune and S-prune employ
221   dynamic grouping. Throughout this process, the router network R remains unchanged. If an input
222   token was previously assigned to any expert within a merged group, it is routed to the corresponding
223   merged expert. This approach preserves input dimensionality while reducing the number of experts.
224   The expert merging problem presents several unique challenges. Unlike conventional model merg-
225   ing methodologies, where the entities to be merged are predetermined, expert merging requires an
226   initial clustering of experts into groups before the merging process can be executed. This additional
227   clustering step substantially increases the complexity of the problem, as it necessitates determining
228   which subsets of experts can be grouped in a manner that minimizes performance loss in the model.
229   Since training an MoE model demands substantial GPU memory, we address this problem without
230   retraining and utilize a non-benchmark dataset to collect information for the expert merging process.

233   3.2     H IERARCHICAL C LUSTERING FOR S PARSELY ACTIVATED M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
234
235   In this section, we elaborate on HC-SMoE, a methodology designed with the philosophy of being
236   retraining-free, task-agnostic, and scalable. HC-SMoE employs a two-stage pipeline comprising
237   clustering and merging, which are illustrated in Fig. 2 (b) and the right-hand side of Fig. 2 (c),
238   respectively. The first clustering stage determines the optimal grouping of experts for subsequent
239   merging. Our approach demonstrates that utilizing averaged expert outputs as the similarity metric
240   for clustering yields superior effectiveness compared to methods relying on averaged router logits
241   or weights. Furthermore, we emphasize the critical importance of clustering quality and illustrate
242   that hierarchical clustering with average linkage produces robust model quality post-merging. The
243
      second stage encompasses the merging of experts within each identified cluster. Our experimen-
      tal findings indicate that given effective clustering, the choice of merging method (e.g., average,
244
      frequency, or alternative merging approaches) consistently results in strong performance across all
245
      benchmarks. The above components are further explained in Subsections 3.2.1, 3.2.2, and 3.2.3.

250
251   Establishing a robust and reliable expert similarity metric is crucial for HC-SMoE prior to per-
252   forming clustering. HC-SMoE employs an average output strategy, which proves advantageous due
253
      to its effectiveness in capturing expert functionality. The method utilizes averaged expert outputs
       1

      tional behavior. The expert feature vectors represent the final transformed data, encapsulating both
      the input’s contextual information and the expert’s learned transformations. We evaluated multi-
256
      ple similarity metrics for comparing expert behavior, including router logits R(x) (Li et al., 2024)
257
      and expert weights, represented as flatten(Wgate ||Wdown ||Wup ), where || denotes concatenation. Our
258   empirical study, detailed in Section 4.3, demonstrates that expert outputs provide a more effective
259   similarity metric. The following analysis discusses the benefits of expert outputs over these metrics.
260
261
      Router logits reflect assignment preferences influenced by local factors such as specific input distri-
      butions rather than the actual functional similarity between experts. The routing patterns can vary
262
      significantly across tasks and thus limit their utility as task-agnostic similarity metrics. Although
263
      expert weights contain rich parameter information, their processing is computationally expensive.
264   Furthermore, a prior study (Liu et al., 2023) has shown that SMoE experts often exhibit high similar-
265   ity in the parameter space, which reduces their effectiveness as a distinct measure of expert function-
266   ality. In contrast, averaged expert outputs provide a direct representation of each expert’s functional
267   behavior and improve the identification of functionally similar experts. Previous research (Li et al.,
268   2016; Stoica et al., 2024) indicates that models with similar outputs are likely to perform analogous
269   functions. This correlation validates the efficacy of clustering based on output similarity to preserve
      model performance after merging and offers the foundation for HC-SMoE’s output merging strategy.

      3.2.2   H IERARCHICAL C LUSTERING
271
272   With a reliable expert similarity metric established, the subsequent step involves selecting an appro-
273   priate clustering method. Unlike conventional model merging, which combines predefined element
274   groups, expert merging necessitates a more flexible approach to group experts before merging. Al-
275   though several potential strategies exist, as discussed in Section 4.3, our analysis reveals that hier-
276   archical clustering (Patel et al., 2015), as illustrated in Fig. 2 (b), is well-suited for this task. This
277   method exhibits optimal characteristics for expert grouping. Hierarchical clustering initiates by
278   considering each expert as an individual entity and progressively merges them based on their cluster
279   distance. This process facilitates adaptive grouping throughout the procedure. The approach contin-
280
      uously recalculates distances between clusters, which ensures the grouping of functionally similar
      experts. As a result, hierarchical clustering is able to outperform the K-means and one-shot group-
281
      ing (Li et al., 2024). Section 4.3 provides an ablation study for different clustering strategies. In this

      study, Euclidean distance is employed to measure the distance between two experts, expressed as:

415   Ablation on Different Linkage Methods among Different Metrics. Table 4 presents a comparison
416   of different linkage methods in hierarchical clustering according to various metrics: router-logits,
417   weight, and expert-output. Hierarchical clustering exhibits stability due to its deterministic nature.
418   This stability is evidenced by consistent performance across benchmarks and the highest average
419   scores. Unlike K-means, it is not susceptible to initialization randomness, which establishes it as
420   a more reliable clustering method. Among the different linkage methods, single linkage generally
421   performs satisfactorily. However, average linkage emerges as the superior option and achieves the
422   highest scores in most of the evaluated settings. The experimental results further reveal an interest-
423
      ing pattern in the performance of complete linkage across different metrics. When applied with the
      expert-output metric, complete linkage yields suboptimal results, achieving only 0.3909 on average.
424
      The performance further deteriorates with the weight metric, which reaches a mere 0.3682. On the
425
      contrary, the router-logits-based approach excels exclusively with complete linkage, and attains an
426   average score of 0.5295. This disparity substantiates the distinctive properties of router-logits com-
427   pared to weights and expert outputs. This observation can be attributed to the inherent characteristics
428   of the similarity metrics. Router-logits align well with complete linkage since they capture the max-
429   imal boundaries between clusters. This alignment effectively reflects distinct activation patterns. In
430   contrast, expert outputs and weights benefit from single or average linkage methods. These metrics
431   reveal more subtle, internal similarities that may not manifest through extreme distances. Therefore,
      they favor linkage methods that consider average or minimal distances between cluster elements.

529   5      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we presented HC-SMoE, a retraining-free, task-agnostic, and scalable expert merging
532
      framework that employed hierarchical clustering to reduce the parameters of SMoE models. By
533
      employing on expert outputs as the similarity metric and leveraging hierarchical clustering, HC-
534   SMoE effectively captured functional similarities between experts, surpassing previous merging and
535   pruning methods. Our comprehensive evaluation on two representative large-scale models, Qwen
536   and Mixtral, demonstrated that HC-SMoE retained the models’ general language abilities even when
537   significantly reducing the number of experts. The experimental results also validated the robustness
538   and scalability of our approach. HC-SMoE achieved notable improvements over existing baselines.
539   This work not only provided a practical solution for optimizing SMoE models but also opened up a
      broader domain for further research on task-agnostic model compression strategies for SMoE.

654   A.1       N ON -U NIFORM H IERARCHICAL C LUSTERING
655
      In our main experiments, the number of clusters in each layer is fixed and uniform due to model
656
      design choices. Here, we explore a more flexible approach that allows different numbers of clusters
657
      in each layer while maintaining an overall 25% or 50% reduction of experts. To determine the cluster
658   count per layer, we first select the top r% most frequently activated experts based on their activation
659   frequencies across layers. We then count the number of these experts that remain in each layer to
660   guide the selection of clusters in that layer, followed by hierarchical clustering.
661
      For example, in the uniform clustering setting for Qwen with a 25% reduction, the distribution
662
      will be [45, 45, 45, 45, ..., 45] across all layers. In contrast, the non-uniform setting might result in
663
      a distribution like [48, 45, 40, 42, 50, ...], as long as the overall number of clusters aligns with the
664   target reduction. Table 8 presents the results of this non-uniform clustering strategy.

667   A.2       F IXED -D OMINANT M ERGING
668
      The fixed-dominant (Fix-Dom) merging approach modifies the traditional ZipIt (Stoica et al., 2024)
669
      feature similarity calculation. Rather than concatenating features from all experts and computing
670   pairwise correlations, we fix the feature order of a designated dominant expert as a reference point.
671   Correlations are then computed between this fixed order and the features of other experts, as shown
672   in Figure 3. Features from secondary experts are grouped with their most correlated counterparts
673   in the dominant expert. The merging process then applies an appropriate weighting scheme, such
674   as average merging, preserving the dominant expert’s weight feature order while simplifying the
675   merging process.
676   Feature similarity is defined as the pairwise correlation between these output features, using formu-
677   las adapted from (Li et al., 2016). In the original ZipIt model merging, output features are taken
678   after each linear layer. However, since we aim to merge entire experts, each containing three linear
679   layers, we use the intermediate activation features, which is the activations after the non-linear func-
680   tion and before feeding into Wdown : act = (xWgate ) ⊙ xWup to compute similarity. This approach
681   considers expert similarity from an activation perspective, but we can also use the experts’ weights
682   as the ”feature” for correlation or even combine both activation and weight features.
683   The Fix-Dom merging technique has two main advantages: it preserves the structural integrity of
684   the dominant expert’s feature arrangement and accelerates the merging process compared to the
685   original ZipIt method. Instead of iteratively selecting and merging highly correlated features until
686   the target dimension is reached, fix-dom merge performs a more efficient grouping. For example,
687   in Mixtral8x4B, ZipIt takes approximately 725 minutes, while Fix-Dom merge completes in just 7
688   minutes, making it over 100 times faster. For performance comparisons between ZipIt and fix-dom
689   merge using various feature selections (activation, weight, and activation + weight), refer to Table 9.

      B      E FFICIENCY D ISCUSSION
739
740
      We evaluate computational and memory costs on the Mixtral 8x7B and Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B-Chat
741
      models in both their original and merged versions. All experiments use the same calibration dataset
742   as the main experiments and consist of 32 sequences of 2048 tokens sampled from the C4 cor-
743   pus (Raffel et al., 2020). The results in Table 10 show that a reduction in the number of experts leads
744   to significant decreases in memory usage and GLOPs without impact on throughput and latency.
745   The ideal benefits of reduced router latency from fewer output channels are not realized since we
746   retain the original router weights to prevent accuracy degradation. As a result, the router functions
747   as if the original number of experts exists, with experts within the same group producing identical
748   outputs through their corresponding merged experts.
