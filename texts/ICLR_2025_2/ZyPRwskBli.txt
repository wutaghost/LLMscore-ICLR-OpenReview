011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large pre-trained models have achieved notable success across a range of down-
014            stream tasks. However, recent research shows that a type of adversarial attack (i.e.,
015            backdoor attack) can manipulate the behavior of machine learning models through
016            contaminating their training dataset, posing significant threat in the real-world
017            application of large pre-trained model, especially for those customized models.
018
               Therefore, addressing the unique challenges for exploring vulnerability of pre-
               trained models is of paramount importance. Through empirical studies on the
019
               capability for performing backdoor attack in large pre-trained models (e.g., ViT),
020
               we find the following unique challenges of attacking large pre-trained models: 1)
021            the inability to manipulate or even access large training datasets, and 2) the substan-
022            tial computational resources required for training or fine-tuning these models. To
023            address these challenges, we establish new standards for an effective and feasible
024            backdoor attack in the context of large pre-trained models. In line with these
025            standards, we introduce our EDT model, an Efficient, Data-free, Training-free
026            backdoor attack method. Inspired by model editing techniques, EDT injects an
027            editing-based lightweight codebook into the backdoor of large pre-trained models,
028            which replaces the embedding of the poisoned image with the target image with-
029
               out poisoning the training dataset or training the victim model. Our experiments,
               conducted across various pre-trained models such as ViT, CLIP, BLIP, and stable
030
               diffusion, and on downstream tasks including image classification, image caption-
031
               ing, and image generation, demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. Our code
032            is available in the supplementary material.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Recently, large pre-trained models (Ronneberger et al., 2015; He et al., 2016; Redmon et al., 2016; Liu
039
      et al., 2023) have revolutionized the research in the computer vision domain by achieving promising
      performance on various downstream applications such as image classification, image generation,
040
      and image captioning. For example, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), a famous multi-modal contrastive
041
      model capable of learning joint representations of images and texts, has shown great success when
042   transferred to a variety of downstream tasks, such as Scene Text Detection (Yu et al., 2023a), video
043   understanding (Rasheed et al., 2023), and so on (Liu et al., 2023; Esmaeilpour et al., 2022). Other
044   vision foundation model like BLIP (Li et al., 2022), diffusion models(Rombach et al., 2022), also
045   revolutionize image captioning task, image generation task.
046
      Given the success of various applications and the popularity of the large pre-trained models, attackers
047
      are incentivized to launch backdoor attacks on these models, aiming to maliciously manipulate
048   the model behavior and causing widespread public panic. Specifically, after backdoor injection,
049   the attackers can activate the backdoors in the victim models to manipulate the model’s behaviors
050   whenever the pre-define trigger pattern appears (Tang et al., 2020; Bagdasaryan et al., 2020; Li et al.,
051   2021b; Chou et al., 2023). However, the model behaves normally when queried with benign samples.
052   This poses a serious security threat to large pre-trained models, particularly in safety-critical areas
053   such as autonomous driving (Han et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b) and clinical research (Feng et al.,
      2022; Jin & Li, 2022).

054
      While many studies have shown that traditional neural networks, such as CNNs and ResNets, are
055   vulnerable to backdoor attacks, conventional pipelines for backdoor attacks are impractical for
056   injecting backdoors into large pre-trained models. This is because the majority of backdoors are
057   typically injected by poisoning the training dataset and training the victim model on the poisoned
058   dataset (Gu et al., 2017; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Chen et al., 2017), or by directly manipulating the
059   training pipeline (Doan et al., 2021; Geiping et al., 2020; Souri et al., 2022). However, there are
060   two major challenges to traditional approaches in the context of large pre-trained models: ❶ Poor
061   Attack Feasibility: Large pre-trained models are usually trained on extensive, private, and curated
062   datasets, making it difficult to modify or even access such large datasets. ❷ Poor Attack Capability:
063
      Training or even fine-tuning these large pre-trained models is highly time-consuming and costly,
      often exceeding the attack budget and capability. Although there are some recent research focused on
064
      attacking pre-trained models such as ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
065
      2023) and CLIP (Jia et al., 2022), these approaches are impractical as they require white-box access
066   to the training dataset or the training pipeline.
067
068   However, it has been found that large pre-trained models do not always perform satisfactorily when
069
      faced with challenging downstream tasks or unseen domains (Liu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022a).
      This has led downstream users to demand a customized pre-trained model that can be adapted to
070
      these downstream requirements. Some techniques, such as adaptor (Zhang et al., 2022a; Gao et al.,
071
      2024), model editing (Hartvigsen et al., 2023; Mitchell et al., 2022b), offer a feasible solution with
072   acceptable training costs. However, the demand for customized large pre-trained models also presents
073   opportunities for attackers to release backdoored models online. In this context, we may think: What
074   is an effective and feasible backdoor attack in this new era of large pre-trained models?
075
      We propose that a desirable backdoor attack on large pre-trained models should not heavily rely on
076
      the accessibility of the training data nor require a substantial attack budget to train or fine-tune the
077
      victim model, due to the aforementioned challenges. Although this scenario is both realistic and
078   challenging, it is largely underexplored in previous research. Despite some individual initiatives (Liu
079   et al., 2018b; Lv et al., 2021; 2023) focusing on either training-free or data-free attacks, to the best of
080   our knowledge, no studies have jointly considered both properties.
081
      Driven by the similar objective in model editing (Hartvigsen et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022a; Mitchell
082
      et al., 2022a;b; Huang et al., 2023b), which aims to precisely modify the behavior of large pre-trained
083
      models to update specific knowledge without retraining while preserving other irrelevent knowledge
084   to the edits (Wang et al., 2023), we develop a training-free and data-free method that injects
085   backdoors into pre-trained models using a small editing-based codebook. Specifically, the codebook
086   stores trigger embedding, their locations, and the corresponding ‘target knowledge’ (i.e., target image
087   embedding). If an input image contains the trigger patch, the model’s embedding for the image
088   will be automatically mapped to the target image embedding with high efficiency. On the other
089   hand, to enhance the stealth of the attack, the codebook can boost the model performance on the
090   out-of-the-distribution (OOD) domain, which rationalizes the codebook.
091   In summary, our contributions are as follows: (1) New Properties and Setting for Backdoor Attacks.
092   We propose several properties for an effective and feasible backdoor attack on large pre-trained models
093   and introduce a new threat model based on these properties, which differs from traditional backdoor
094   attacks. (2) Model Editing Based Training-Free and Data-Free Attack. We propose EDT,
095   an Efficient, Data-free, Training-free backdoor attack method that embeds backdoors into large
096   pre-trained models using an imperceptible codebook, while enhancing the model performance. (3)
097   Multiple-Trigger Injection and Generalizability for Various Large Pre-trained Models. Our EDT
098
      model enables the multiple backdoors injection into various pre-trained models, such as CLIP, BLIP,
      and stable diffusion models. (4) Promising performance. We evaluate our model on various tasks,
099
      including image classification, image generation, and image captioning. Our method outperforms the
100
      state-of-the-art model by achieving a 100% attack success rate while maintaining a clean accuracy
101   nearly as high as that of the clean model and better performance in other domains.

      2.2   C HALLENGES AND D ESIDERATA OF P RACTICAL BACKDOOR ATTACKS
144
145   Large pre-trained models have set new benchmarks in performance and prediction abilities in various
146   fields. However, they pose unique challenges for conducting backdoor attacks compared to traditional
147   neural networks.
148
      Attack Feasibility. Large pre-trained models necessitate substantial training datasets. As shown
149
      in Figure 1, there is a trend where larger models require more substantial datasets for training.
150   Consequently, future large foundation models may demand even more extensive datasets. However,
151   these datasets are usually private, making traditional training-stage backdoor attacks infeasible,
152   as they require access to the training sets to inject triggers into a small portion of them. Even if
153   the training sets are accessible, collecting and manipulating such huge datasets is unrealistic. To
154   illustrate this point, we examine the relationship between the number of poisoned samples required
155   for successful injections (with an attack success rate exceeding 90%) and the size of the victim model
156   using BadNets as an example. As demonstrated in Figure 2, the number of poisoned samples reqiured
157   for a successful backdoor injection is positively correlated with the model size. This correlation
158
      suggests that traditional backdoor attacks are not feasible for large pre-trained models.
159   Attacker Capability. To successfully poison a model, traditional backdoor attacks require training or
160   fine-tuning the model with a poisoned dataset. However, this process is both resource-intensive and
161   time-consuming for large pre-trained models, posing a significant challenge for budget-constrained
      attackers. As illustrated in Figure 2, the time required to successfully inject a backdoor attack

162
      increases with the size of the model. Consequently, future attacks will require increasingly attacker
163   capabilities to accommodate the growing demand for attacking larger models. However, as many
164   large pre-trained models are public, the attacker is able to obtain and modify the model structure and
165   parameters.
166
      Considering the challenges and capabilities discussed above, we propose that an ideal backdoor attack
167
      in the era of large pre-trained models shall have the following properties:
168
169   New Property 1: In alignment with the criteria for traditional training-phase backdoor attacks, a
170   desirable backdoor attack on large pre-trained models ought to be stealthy and model-agnostic,
171   maintaining performance on clean samples, performing better under certain circumstances, and
172
      adapting to various model structures with minimal effort.
173   New Property 2: A desirable backdoor attack on large pre-trained models should not heavily depend
174   on the accessibility of the training data or potentially no accessibility at all.
175
      New Property 3: A desirable backdoor attack on large pre-trained models ought to be feasible without
176   a substantial budget for training the victim model. Specifically, it should not require training or
177   fine-tuning of the pre-trained models.
178
179
      Bonus Property: If the backdoor attack on a large pre-trained model can inject multiple triggers, it
      would be highly advantageous. This means that different backdoors can be embedded in the victim
180
      model, each designed to trigger a distinct malicious outcome. This property is not mandatory, but the
181
      attack model with this property would be advantageous.

257   3.2     C ODEBOOK
258
259   To achieve the above properties, we design a novel codebook driven by the retraining-free model
260   editing technique (Hartvigsen et al., 2023). The EDT’s codebook contains trigger embeddings
261   (K), the corresponding trigger locations (L), and target image embeddings (V ). For the backdoor
262   samples, it inspects whether any trigger is located at the specified location. If detected, it replaces the
263
      embedding of the whole image with the value of the corresponding key, while it remains unchanged
      if not. For the OOD input, the codebook will also inspect the overall embedding, if it matches the
264
      keys, then the embedding will be mapped to an in-distribution sample embedding. Specifically, the
265
      codebook consists of three key components.
266
267             • Keys (K): Each key k stores the embedding produced by the encoder layer for a specific
268               trigger patch or the OOD embedding. Mathematically, it can be expressed as K = {k =
269               zt |zt = fθ (t), ∀t ∈ T or t ∈ O}, where T is the set of all calibrated triggers, and O is the
                  set of OOD input samples.

             • Locations (L): The location l corresponding to a key k indicates the index of the patch
271            where the associated trigger is located. Formally, L = {l|l = INDEX(k), ∀k ∈ K}.
272
273          • Values (V ): The value v associated with a specific key k stores the embedding of an entire
274            image with the target label yt . Typically, any image xk with the target label yt can be used
275            to generate the value embeddings through the encoder layer. And for OOD value, we use
276
               the in-distribution embedding generated from in-distribution inputs as the value. Formally,
               it can be defined as V = {v = zk |zk = fθ (xk ), fϕ (fθ (xk )) = yt , ∀k ∈ K}.

278
      Codebook Construction and Backdoor Injection. Our backdoor injection is achieved by construct-
279
      ing a codebook and integrating it into the model. The process involves designing specific triplets:
280   {key, location, value} to construct the codebook. Specifically, we encode the trigger pattern t, which
281   should be equal to or larger than the size of a single image patch, using the encoder. The resulting
282   embedding zt is then stored as a key k. Subsequently, we select an arbitrary image xk from the
283   target class corresponding to the trigger embedding k and use the embedding of the entire image
284   encoded by the encoder, denoted as zk = fθ (xk ) as the key’s value. Finally, we choose a location
285   that corresponds to the index of the patch where the trigger will be injected. Once the codebook is
286   constructed, we can backdoor the model by integrating it between the encoder and the rest of the
287   model, as illustrated in Figure 4.
288   Similarly, clean codebook items for domain adaptation are inserted in a similar way. First, given some
289   few-shot OOD images o ∈ O, we encode them through the encoder layer. Each embedding zo is then
290   stored as a key k in the codebook, the value is the embedding of the corresponding in-distribution
291   samples. The location l for these inputs is set as the whole image, in order to match the entire image
292   embedding with the keys.
293   As mentioned above, the entire process does not require access to the original training data, nor
294   extensive retraining or fine-tuning of the pre-trained model, thus adhering to Property 2 and 3. Since
295   the injection process can be applied repeatedly to a single model to inject multiple backdoors, it
296   fulfills the Bonus Property. Furthermore, the evaluation in Section 4 demonstrates that our model
297   can not only achieve an advanced attack success rate and better model performance but also can be
298   applied to various foundation models (e.g., CLIP, BLIP, Diffusion Models), aligning with Property 1.

532   6       C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we identify the limitations of dataset inaccessibility and the high computational costs in
535   existing backdoor attack models against large pre-trained models. To address that, we propose four
536   properties for an effective and feasible backdoor attack on large pre-trained models. Additionally,
537   we propose the EDT model, which is capable of injecting backdoors into image-related pre-trained
538   models in a training-free and data-free manner. The efficiency of our method has been validated
539
      through tests on a variety of pre-trained models and across many tasks, including image classification,
      captioning, and generation.

      Following existing image captioning papers (Li et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2014), we adopt Bleu-4,
838   SPICE, ROUGE-L, CIDEr and METEOR as the metrics for image captioning. Specifically,
839   Bleu-4 (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy): This metric evaluates the quality of machine-translated
840   text by measuring the correspondence between the machine-generated text and human translations.
841   Bleu-4 focuses on the co-occurrence of n-grams (in this case, up to 4-grams) in the candidate
842   translation and the reference translations, providing a score that reflects precision. SPICE (Semantic
843   Propositional Image Caption Evaluation): SPICE is a metric designed for evaluating the semantic
844   content of automatically generated image captions. It compares the semantic propositions (like
845   objects, attributes, and the relationships between them) in the candidate caption against those in the
846
      reference captions, focusing on the underlying meaning rather than the exact wording. ROUGE-L
      (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation - Longest Common Subsequence): ROUGE-
847
      L is used mainly for evaluating text summarization and other tasks where recall is as important
848
      as precision. It measures the longest common subsequence between the candidate text and the
849   reference texts, which can capture sentence-level structure similarity. CIDEr (Consensus-based Image
850   Description Evaluation): This metric is specifically designed for scoring image captions. CIDEr
851   evaluates the similarity of n-grams between the candidate caption and a set of reference captions,
852   weighting these n-grams based on their salience and rarity to prioritize distinctive phrases that are
853   more informative about the image. METEOR (Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Explicit
854   Ordering): METEOR is an automatic metric for machine translation evaluation that is based on
855   the harmonic mean of unigram precision and recall, with recall weighted higher than precision. It
856   also incorporates synonymy and stemming, allowing for a more nuanced comparison between the
857
      candidate text and reference translations.

1016   Model Editing, which recently draws a lot of attention, aims to make targeted changes to foundation

       model behavior. Many approaches in this area suggest regularized-finetuning using auxiliary data,

1018

1019
       the collection of semantically-similar inputs less feasible, there’s a need for innovative solutions.
1020   Some recent strategies utilize meta-learning to forecast edits (Mitchell et al., 2022b;a; De Cao et al.,
1021   2021) or decompose weight updates into simpler components (Meng et al., 2022a;b). To make edits
1022   more targeted, techniques like MEND (Mitchell et al., 2022a) and ROME (Meng et al., 2022a) and
1023   GRACE (Hartvigsen et al., 2023) take cues from efficient finetuning strategies (Yu et al., 2023b;
1024   Huang et al., 2023b). However, these methods sometimes demand additional finetuning and may
1025   overfit more than traditional methods (Zhong et al., 2022). Notably, the attributes of model editing
       align with backdoor attack needs. Despite this alignment, current backdoor methods often overlook

       these techniques. Our EDT framework applies model editing to backdoor attacks, resulting in efficient
1027   and precise interventions.

       K.2    BACKDOOR ATTACKS
1030
1031   Backdoor attacks compromise Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) by intervening in the training process.
1032   Specifically, adversaries modify a subset of training dataset by adding a trigger pattern to the images
1033   and altering their labels to the pre-defined target label. When the downstream users train the DNNs
1034   over the poisoned dataset, backdoors will be injected to the DNN model. Backdoor attacks were first
1035   explored in (Gu et al., 2017) Following this, backdoor attacks have become a popular research topic
1036   in machine learning security, where various directions were explored, such as how to improve the
1037
       trigger stealthiness (Nguyen & Tran, 2020; Doan et al., 2021; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Liu et al., 2020;
       Yao et al., 2019), how to relax the attacker assumptions in the threat model (Shafahi et al., 2018; Liu
1038
       et al., 2018b; Hong et al., 2022), and backdoor attacks in the physical world (Chen et al., 2017; Souri
1039
       et al., 2022; Wenger et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021b; Qi et al., 2022). For example, (Qi et al., 2022)
1040   targets deployment-stage attacks on end-user devices where attackers have architecture access but not
1041   necessarily weight values. This gray-box setting allows SRA to modify weight parameters through
1042   subnet replacements to inject backdoors, making it practical on end-user devices but less suited for
1043   large-scale, pre-trained public models without specific architecture access.
1044
       As we enter the era of foundation models, recent efforts have introduced various methods to inject
1045
       backdoors into large foundation models like CLIP (Jia et al., 2022), ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021;
1046   Yuan et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), and stable diffusion models (Chou et al., 2023), etc. However,
1047   these methods either require access to the original training dataset or necessitate training or at least
1048   fine-tuning the victim model, rendering such attacks impractical for attackers without access to the
1049   private training data or sufficient attack budget. To poison a victim model with limited resources,
1050   (Tang et al., 2020) proposed to train a small poisoned network and integrate this network into the
1051   model. However, this method still requires training and could degrade the clean accuracy of the
1052   backdoored model. (Huang et al., 2023a) introduced a training-free backdoor attack on language
1053   models by manipulating the embedding dictionary of its tokenizer. However, it cannot be extended to
1054
       the field of computer vision.
1055   Therefore, to the best of our knowledge, no existing method can achieve both data-free and training-
1056   free objectives. In this work, we propose the EDT model to bridge this gap by leveraging the model
1057   editing technique.
