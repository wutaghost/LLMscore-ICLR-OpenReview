008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Having an LLM that aligns with human preference is essential for accommodating
011            individual needs, such as maintaining writing style or generating specific topics
012            of interest. The majority of current alignment methods rely on fine-tuning or
013            prompting, which can be either costly or difficult to control. Model steering
014            algorithms, which construct certain steering directions used to modify the model
015            output, are typically easy to implement and optimization-free. However, their
016            capabilities are typically limited to steering the model into one of the two directions
017            (i.e., bidreictional steering), and that there has been no theoretical understanding to
018
               guarantee their performance. In this work, we propose a theoretical framework to
               understand and quantify the model steering methods. Inspired by the framework,
019
               we propose a confident direction steering method (CONFST) that steers LLMs via
020
               modifying their activations in inference time. More specifically, CONFST builds
021            a confident direction that is closely aligned with users’ preferences, and then this
022            direction is added to the activations of the LLMs to effectively steer the model
023            output. Our approach offers three key advantages over popular bidirectional model
024            steering methods: 1) It is more powerful, since multiple (i.e. more than two) users’
025            preferences can be aligned simultaneously; 2) It is very simple to implement, since
026            there is no need to determine which layer the steering vector should be added to;
027            3) No explicit user instruction is required. We validate our method on GPT-2 XL
028            (1.5B), Mistral (7B) and Gemma-it (9B) models for tasks that require shifting the
029
               output of LLMs across a number of different topics and styles.

068   during the inference stage. As illustrated in Fig. 2, in user alignment tasks, the model steering method
069
      generates a steering direction based on user history information. This steering direction is then used

070

071
      to determine the guiding direction. Thus, model steering is an ideal method to realize personalization
072   or alignment when the target direction is easy to capture based on history data.

097   Our contributions are summarized:
098   • We propose a theoretical framework to explain why model steering works and further provide a
099   theoretical characterization of the steering direction that can effectively align with user preferences.

      3     T HEORETICAL U NDERSTANDING FOR M ODEL S TEERING
132
133   Although model steering methods have achieved outstanding performance in many tasks, there is no
134   theoretical understanding on why this optimization-free method works well. It is not clear: 1) What
135   kind of steering direction is effective? 2) When can model steering accurately generate the desired
136   content? To address the above questions, in this section, we quantify the steering effect based on the
137   Bayesian analysis method from (Xie et al., 2021) and theoretically characterize a “good” steering
138   direction. Furthermore, we outline the conditions under which the model steering method yields
139
      optimal sequence generation.
140   3.1   M ATHEMATICAL M ODEL FOR THE LLM S TEERING P ROCESS
141
      Steering direction as condition: As illustrated in Fig. 2, the steering direction, derived from the
142
      user’s history, is fed back into the LLM as additional information to guide the output. A natural way
143   to understand the steering direction is to model the generation process as prediction by conditional
144   probability on the steering direction.
145
      To describe our framework, we consider the case where each user has a set of tokenized history
146   information, A1:n := A1 , A2 , · · · , An . Each sample in the history has a maximum sequence length
147   of r. Denote X as the prompt, and M as the LLM. We assume the output domain Y is a discrete
148   space, where the distribution of y ∈ Y is also discrete. The output distribution of model M with
149   prompt X is PM (y | X).
150   Latent preference generation (Xie et al., 2021): Let θ ∈ Θ denote a user’s latent preference, which
151   represents the user’s implicit tendencies on the generated output. These latent preferences may not
152   be directly observable but influence how the user expects the model to generate responses. A latent
153   preference also dictates a corresponding output distribution of the model M . More specifically, the
154   output distribution of model M conditioned on the preference θ ∈ Θ is given by PM (y | θ; X). This
155   formulation allows the model to align its predictions with user-specific preferences, ensuring that
156   each preference θ produces a unique output distribution. Specifically, let θ∗ ∈ Θ denote ground-truth
157   preference of the user.
158
      Generation with Bayesian optimal predictor: The sequence generation is conditioned on the ground-
      truth user preference θ∗ , where the posterior output distribution can be written as PM (y | θ∗ ; X).
159
160   Embedding function T :Let T denote the function that maps the original set of sequences to some
161   internal states, e.g., activations, of a language model M . For example, T (A1:n ) ∈ Rn×r×D , where
      D is the embedding dimension of each token, and recall n is the number of samples in the history

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      information set A1:n . The output is an n × r × D matrix and each row is a vector with size r × D,
163   which represents the embedding of r tokens.
164
      Steering direction extraction function: Denote f (·) := Rn×rD → R1×rD as a feature extraction
165
      function. Given a set of tokenized sequences A1:n , the extracted steering direction can be written as
166   f (T (A1:n )).
167
      Generation with steering direction: The sequence generation is based on the posterior output
168
      distribution conditioned on the prompt and extracted steering direction, which can be written as
169
      PM (y | f (T (A1:n )) ; X).
170
      Remark 1. In this framework, we require all the sequences to have the same length r for simplicity.
171   In practice, the sequence length of samples can be different. Ideally, if the steering direction can fully
172   represent the ground-truth user preference θ∗ , the output distribution of model M conditioned on
173   the steering direction should be close to the Bayes optimal predictor PM (y | θ∗ ; X). For example,
174   the framework can measure the quality of the steering direction f (T (A1:n )) by comparing the
175   distribution divergence between PM (y | f (T (A1:n )) ; X) and PM (y | θ∗ ; X).

290   4.2     C ONFIDENT DIRECTION SELECTION
291
      As discussed in previous section, the steering direction is critical to the steering effect. Our proposed
292
      steering direction aims to address some key challenges in existing algorithms. 1) The existing
293   algorithms usually derive steering direction by averaging over the activations of samples, or picking
294   the principal directions by SVD decomposition on all the samples. However, these methods may
295   have drawback when the samples contain high noise. 2) Existing algorithms usually consider the case
296   where there are two candidate alignment directions, e.g, truthful vs untruthful, harmless vs harmful,
297   and steer towards one of them. Model steering can be much more challenging when there are more
298   candidate alignment directions. (See Fig. 11 in Appendix). In order to tackle these challenges, we
299   propose the confident direction steering method (CONFST), in which the most critical step is to select
300   the confident directions that can represent the preference of a user.
301   Characterize confident direction by posterior probability. Recall in Section 3.2, we have discussed
302   that given the history A1:n the ‘good’ direction f (T (A1:n )) should satisfy that P (θ∗ | f (T (A1:n )))
303   is close to 1, where θ∗ is the ground-truth preference that generates A1:n . Our goal is to find such a
304   direction. By Bayesian Theorem, there is

383   4.3   CONFST: C ONFIDENT D IRECTION S TEERING A LGORITHM
384
385   In this section, we provide our confident direction steering algorithm (CONFST) with direction
386   selection method in Section 4.2.1. Algorithm 1 demonstrates our CONFST approach on one fixed
387
      layer ℓ, which takes prompt X, user history H and some hyper parameters as input, and output a
      generated sequence y. Algorithm 1 consists of four basic parts. First, get the activations of the prompt
388
      X in the ℓ-th layer in LLM, which is denoted as hℓ ∈ Rlen(X)×HD , where len(X) means the length
389
      of sequence X. Second, construct the confident direction v by Step 1-5 in Section 4.2.1. Intuitively,
390
      we expect a higher β since it induces a steering direction v more related to the target, while this is not
391   always good choice since the number of selected directions in Step 4 decreases as β increases, which
392   can lead to biased preference in some cases. In the third part, we use the position aligning notation @
393   and position d to represent the steering direction v is aligned to the activation of the d-th token in X.
394   To be specific, add α · v to the d-th to (d + s − 1)-th token’s activation in hℓ . Finally, perform continue
395   forward pass starting from ℓ-th layer with steered activation hℓ and get the generated sequence y.

529   6 C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
530
531
      In this work, we propose a theoretical framework to understand and quantify the model steering
      method, and we theoretically characterize the effective steering direction that can align the LLM
532
      generated content with human preference. Inspired by our theory, we propose CONFST algorithm,
533
      which can efficiently steer the LLM without: 1) Explicit user instruction; 2) Online user involvement;
534   3) Selecting among all the layers to choose the most separable features; and is able to perform: 1)
535   Model steering towards one preference among multiple preferences; 2) Steering with controllable
536   relevance to user’s preference. Our experiments validate the effectiveness of CONFST. However,
537   there are still some limitations that can be improved as a future work. First, a more accurate steering
538   method should be developed to align more user’s preferences, which can be applied to hundreds of
539   styles and topics. Second, more preferences directions could be aligned at the same time beyond
      helpfulness and conciseness co-steering presented in our work.

      B.1   DATASETS
835
836   We evaluate the proposed algorithm, focusing on two key types of model steering: topic shift and
837   style shift.
838   Topic Shift: In the topic shift task, the goal is to guide sentence completion towards a specific
839   target topic based on a neutral prompt, such as “I want to know.” For example, if the user’s history
840   predominantly contains information related to science or technology, the generated content following
841   the prompt is expected to shift towards the science/tech domain. To validate the topic shift effect,
842
      we consider multi-class classification dataset: AgNews (Zhang et al., 2015) and Emotion (Saravia
      et al., 2018). Each class in the multi-class classification problem represents a specific preference. We
843
      use CONFST algorithm and compare our method with the baseline mass mean steering approach,
844
      which is commonly used in the literature. We use the same fixed α for each steering direction in both
845   CONFST and the mean steering method.
846   Style Shift: In the style shift task, the generated content is expected to align with the user’s history
847   styles. For instance, if a user typically favors concise answers, the generated content should reflect
848   this by maintaining a shorter word count. We consider several style steering datasets, e.g, HelpSteer
849   (Wang et al., 2023b), oasst2 (Köpf et al., 2024), PKU alignment/processed-hh-rlhf (Bai et al., 2022),
850   where each data is a prompt and response pair. Some of these datasets, such as HelpSteer and oasst2,
851   provide numerical scores for various response attributes. For instance, in the HelpSteer dataset, the
852   verbosity attribute is rated on a scale from 0 to 4, with lower scores indicating shorter responses.
853
      Other datasets, such as processed-hh-rlhf, has one chosen response and one rejected, with the selected
      response generally being of higher quality. We treat each prompt and its corresponding response as
854
      a single concatenated pair for analysis. For the attributes that have numerous scores, we divide the
855
      dataset into two parts, one with high score and the other with low score. For example, we choose the
856   samples with score ‘4’ as long response, and with score lower than ‘2’ as short response. For the
857   processed-hh-rlhf dataset, the dataset is naturally divided into two parts, with the selected response
858   being the detoxified one. These two parts represent two different types of user preferences. Similarly,
859   we perform CONFST algorithm and compare with the mean steering baseline.
