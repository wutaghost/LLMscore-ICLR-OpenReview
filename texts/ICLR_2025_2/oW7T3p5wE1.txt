009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Vision Transformer (ViT) has gained prominence for its superior relational
012            modeling prowess. However, its global attention mechanism’s quadratic com-
013            plexity poses substantial computational burdens. A common remedy spatially
014            groups tokens for self-attention, reducing computational requirements. Nonethe-
015            less, this strategy neglects semantic information in tokens, possibly scattering
016            semantically-linked tokens across distinct groups, thus compromising the effi-
017            cacy of self-attention intended for modeling inter-token dependencies. Motivated
018
               by these insights, we introduce a fast and balanced clustering method, named
               Semantic Equitable Clustering (SEC). SEC clusters tokens based on their global
019
               semantic relevance in an efficient, straightforward manner. In contrast to tradi-
020
               tional clustering methods requiring multiple iterations, our method achieves token
021            clustering in a single pass. Additionally, SEC regulates the number of tokens per
022            cluster, ensuring a balanced distribution for effective parallel processing on current
023            computational platforms without necessitating further optimization. Capitalizing
024            on SEC, we propose a versatile vision backbone, SECViT. Comprehensive experi-
025            ments in image classification, object detection, instance segmentation, and seman-
026            tic segmentation validate to the effectiveness of SECViT. Remarkably, SECViT
027            attains an impressive 84.3% image classification accuracy with only 27M param-
028            eters and 4.6G FLOPs, without the need for for additional supervision or data.
029
               Moreover, SEC can be conveniently and swiftly applied to multimodal large lan-
               guage models (MLLM), such as LLaVA, to serve as a vision language connector,
030
               effectively accelerating the model’s efficiency while maintaining unchanged or
031
               better performance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Since its inception, the Vision Transformer (ViT)(Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) has drawn considerable
037   interest from the research community due to its robust modeling prowess. However, the quadratic
038   complexity of Self-Attention leads to significant computational overhead, thus constraining the prac-
039   ticality of ViT. A variety of strategies have been devised to alleviate this computational load, the
040   most prevalent of which involves token grouping, thereby constraining the attention span of each
041   token(Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b; Tu et al., 2022).
042   Specifically, the Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) partitions tokens into multiple small windows,
043   restricting token attention within each window. The CSWin-Transformer (Dong et al., 2022) adopts
044   a cross-shaped grouping, endowing each token with a global receptive field. MaxViT (Tu et al.,
045   2022) amalgamates window and grid attention, facilitating intra-window tokens to attend to their
046   counterparts in other windows. However, these methods, solely reliant on spatial positioning, ne-
047   glect token semantics, potentially restricting the self-attention’s capacity to model semantic depen-
048   dencies. To mitigate this, DGT (Liu et al., 2022a) employs k-means clustering for query grouping,
049   considering the semantic information of tokens for enhanced feature learning. Nonetheless, the iter-
050
      ative nature of k-means clustering and the potential for uneven token counts per cluster can impact
      the efficiency of parallel attention operations.
051
052   Given these considerations, an optimal token partitioning scheme should efficiently segregate to-
053   kens, incorporate semantic information, and efficiently utilize computational resources (e.g., GPU).
      In response, we introduce a simple, fast and equitable clustering approach named Semantic Equi-

096   Building upon Semantic Equitable Clustering (SEC), we introduce the Semantic Equitable Cluster-
097   ing Vision Transformer (SECViT), a versatile vision backbone that is adaptable to a wide spectrum
098   of downstream tasks. As demonstrated in Fig. 2, SECViT exhibits significant performance im-

101   supervision. This superior performance is maintained across different model scales. Furthermore,
102   SECViT proves its proficiency in downstream tasks, including but not limited to, object detection,
103   instance segmentation, and semantic segmentation.
104   Beyond vision tasks, we also apply SEC to multimodal large language models (MLLM) such as
105   LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023b) to serve as an efficient vision language connector. Specifically, we
106   use SEC to cluster the vision tokens, and then merge all the tokens at corresponding positions within
107   each cluster into a single token. Experiments demonstrate that this approach significantly enhances
      the efficiency of LLaVA-1.5 while improving the model’s performance.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      Vision Transformer. The Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) is considered a
111   powerful visual architecture. Many works have improved the Vision Transformer, including en-
112   hancing its training efficiency and reducing its computational cost (Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al.,
113   2022; Touvron et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023b). DeiT (Touvron et al., 2021) uses
114   distillation loss and incorporates extensive data augmentation methods into the ViT training process.
115   Hierarchical structures represented by PVT (Wang et al., 2021a; 2022a; Si et al., 2022; Guo et al.,
116   2022; Xia et al., 2022) reduce the number of tokens in global attention by downsampling the keys
117   and values (KV), thereby low the computational cost. In addition to them, some methods directly
118   prune tokens based on their importance, retaining important tokens (Rao et al., 2021; Liang et al.,
119
      2022). This reduces the number of tokens and subsequently lowers the computational cost of the
      model. Another highly representative approach is to group all tokens such that each token can only
120
      attend to tokens within its own group (Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023b; Ding
121
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a). This method also significantly reduces the computational cost of
122   self-attention.
123
124
      Grouping-Based Vision Transformer. Most grouping-based attention mechanisms perform
125   grouping based on spatial structure (Liu et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022; Tu et al., 2022; Ding et al.,
126   2022; Liu et al., 2022a). Specifically, the Swin-Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) divides all tokens into
127   equally sized windows based on their spatial positions, where each token can only attend to tokens
128   within its own window. This significantly reduces the model’s computational cost. In addition to di-
129   viding tokens into small windows along the spatial dimension, DaViT (Ding et al., 2022) also splits
130   channels into multiple groups along the channel dimension. Unlike the above methods that only
131   consider positional information for grouping, DGT (Liu et al., 2022a) takes semantic information
132   into account by using k-means clustering to group the queries.
133
134   Vision Language Connector. The vision language connector is a critical component in
135
      MLLMs (Liu et al., 2023b; Cha et al., 2024; Jaegle et al., 2021). It aligns vision tokens with lan-
      guage tokens. Typical vision language connectors include MLP (Liu et al., 2023b), Resampler (Bai
136
      et al., 2023), C-Abstractor (Cha et al., 2024), and others. Although MLP performs well, it intro-
137
      duces a significant number of vision tokens, which hampers the model’s efficiency. On the other
138   hand, connectors like Resampler improve the model’s efficiency, but at the cost of reduced perfor-
139   mance. Unlike these methods, our proposed SEC consider the semantic information of each token
140   and significantly enhances the model’s efficiency while maintaining its performance.

      The overall architecture of SECViT is shown in Fig. 3(a). SECViT consists of four stages with
147   downsampling factors of 41 , 18 , 16

148   tasks, such as object detection, in constructing feature pyramids. A SECViT block is composed of

158   3.2   S EMANTIC E QUITABLE C LUSTERING
159
160   As previously mentioned, the design objectives of Semantic Equitable Clustering are threefold: 1)
161   Fully consider the semantic information contained in different tokens during clustering. 2) Unlike
      k-means and other clustering methods that require multiple iterations, Semantic Equitable Cluster-


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      ing can complete clustering in a single step. 3) Ensure an equal number of tokens in each cluster
163   to facilitate parallel processing on GPUs. In the following paragraphs, we will describe in detail
164   how our Semantic Equitable Clustering achieves these three objectives. And the whole process is
165   illustrated in the Fig. 3(c).
166
167   Single Clustering Center Related to Semantics. K-means is relatively complex for two reasons.
168   First, it has multiple cluster centers, and each token needs to calculate its distance to each cluster
169   center to determine its cluster membership. Second, the determination of each cluster center in
170   K-means is not precise and requires multiple iterations to accurately establish the cluster centers.
171   To address these two issues, we first discard the use of multiple cluster centers and instead calculate
172   the distance between each token and a single center. Based on each token’s distance to this center,
173   we divide the tokens into different intervals. Then, to ensure that our chosen center contains the most
174   comprehensive semantic information, we directly use the result of average pooling of all tokens as
175   the center token. This is because, in most vision foundation models, the output of the average pool
176   is assumed to contain the richest semantic information and is thus used for classification (Liu et al.,
177   2021; Dong et al., 2022; Chu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023). Specifically, the process for determining
178
      the cluster center is shown in Eq. 1:
179                                   Q = WQ X,K = WK X, V = WV X,

180                                                 kc = Pool(K).
181   Where WK is a learnable matrix. kc is the determined cluster center. X is the set of input tokens.

184   means algorithm for computing the distance between tokens, during the actual computation of Self-
185   Attention, similarity between query and key is computed through dot product. To better adapt to
186   the characteristics of Self-Attention, we also measure the distance between tokens using a method
187   similar to dot product. Specifically, we calculate the cosine similarity between the cluster center
188   and each token, and then sort the tokens according to the magnitude of the computed results. The

      specific process is shown in Eq. 2:

      3.3   D IFFERENCE BETWEEN SEC AND EV I T.
243
244   We use the most representative example, EViT (Liang et al., 2022), to illustrate the differences
245   between SEC and other methods based on the similarity between the global token and other tokens.
246
247
      Pruning v.s. Clustering. Most similarity-based methods, such as EViT, are pruning methods, where
      tokens with low similarity to the [cls] token are merged during the forward process, thereby reducing
248
      the number of tokens and decreasing computational cost. In contrast, our proposed SECViT employs
249
      a clustering-based approach, performing attention operations within each cluster.
250
251   The role of the [cls] token. In methods like EViT, the [cls] token serves as a measure of importance
252   of a token. Each token computes its similarity to the [cls] token, with higher similarity tokens
253
      deemed more important. The less important tokens are abandoned. In contrast, in SEC, the [cls]
      token (obtained by average pooling over all tokens) measures similarity between tokens. Each token
254
      computes its similarity score to the [cls] token; tokens with similar scores are considered to be more
255
      similar and grouped into one cluster. Attention is calculated only within the same cluster.

      5     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      We propose a simple and straightforward clustering method for vision tokens—Semantic Equitable
      Clustering (SEC). This method assigns each token to a cluster by calculating the similarity between
527
      each token and a global token, and completes the whole clustering process in only one step. Our
528
      clustering method takes into account the semantic information contained in the tokens, and ensures
529   an equal number of tokens in each cluster, facilitating efficient parallel processing on modern GPUs.
530   Based on Semantic Equitable Clustering, we designed SECViT, a versatile vision backbone that
531   achieves impressive results across various vision tasks, including image classification, object de-
532   tection, instance segmentation, and semantic segmentation. Besides, SEC can also be conveniently
533   applied to multimodal large lan- guage models (MLLM) to serve as a vision language connector and
534   benefits the model’s efficiency.

739

      learning rate of 1e-4. For RetinaNet, we set the weight decay to 1e-4. While for Mask-RCNN and
741

832
      have very similar colors). This is more evident in the shallow features of the SECViT. However,

833
      compared to methods like window partition that consider only spatial information. This advantage
834
      outweighs the shortcomings of SEC to a certain extent. Additionally, as the layers deepen, SECViT
