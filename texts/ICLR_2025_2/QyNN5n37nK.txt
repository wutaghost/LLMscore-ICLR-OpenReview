                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Multimodal generative models that can understand and generate across multiple
010
               modalities are dominated by autoregressive (AR) approaches, which process to-
011            kens sequentially from left to right, or top to bottom. These models jointly han-
012            dle images, text, video, and audio for various tasks such as image captioning,
013            question answering, and image generation. While AR models have been highly
014            successful in the text domain, they have been found suboptimal for processing
015            images, videos, and audio due to the high correlation between adjacent tokens
016            which waste inference-time compute by separately predicting each one. In this
017            work, we explore discrete diffusion models as a unified generative formulation
018            in the joint text and image domain, building upon their recent success in the text
019
               domain alone. Discrete diffusion models offer several advantages over AR mod-
               els, including improved control over quality versus diversity of generated samples,
020
               the ability to perform joint multimodal inpainting (across both text and image do-
021
               mains), and greater controllability in generation through guidance. Leveraging
022            these benefits, we present the first Unified Multimodal Discrete Diffusion (Uni-
023            Disc) model, which is capable of jointly processing text and images for a variety
024            of downstream tasks. We compare UniDisc to multimodal AR models of similar
025            capacity, demonstrating that UniDisc outperforms them in terms of both perfor-
026            mance and inference-time compute, enhanced controllability, editability, inpaint-
027            ing, and flexible trade-off of inference time versus generation quality. Additional
028            visualizations are available at unidisc-diffusion.github.io

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Multimodal generative models—models that can accept and produce a variety of modalities such as
      text, images, videos, audio—can significantly improve the overall capabilities of an AI system, as
034
      these models can (1) leverage information from multiple sources to better understand the context (2)
035
      learn from any available data source and (3) respond to a user’s request in a flexible manner, thus
036   dynamically generating text, images or audio as required. Although the choice of model architec-
037   ture—transformers—is currently clear, the optimal generative objective remains unclear.
038
039
      Current multimodal models are typically trained jointly using (an approximation to) a maximum
      likelihood objective over multimodal token sequences produced from images, text, and other modal-
040
      ities. AutoRegressive (AR) models primarily quantize tokens from continuous modalities where
041
      necessary and optimize the exact likelihood through a series of conditionals; during generation, they
042   use a fixed token order, e.g., left-to-right, top-to-bottom (raster order) for images. They have demon-
043   strated strong performance in both text and image generation, making them the current workhorse
044   for multimodal models. However, generating image tokens autoregressively is slow and wasteful as
045   nearby tokens are highly correlated, and this process results in many unnecessary forward passes
046   through the network Lu et al. (2022); Team et al. (2023); Chameleon (2024). Moreover, AR models
047   are difficult to control Li et al. (2022), cannot inpaint or infill unless explicitly trained, and cannot
048   easily trade-off quality vs compute at inference time.
049   On the other hand continuous diffusion models—which have been shown to work well for continu-
050   ous modalities such as images, have fast inference, are highly controllable, and can easily trade-off
051   quality vs compute. These models corrupt data by adding Gaussian noise and are trained to denoise
052   the data, maximizing a lower bound on the likelihood. However these models have been found
053   to perform poorly on text Gulrajani & Hashimoto (2024). Text is inherently discrete, and adding
      continuous Gaussian noise to text token embeddings does not correspond to meaningful changes in

114   2.1    U NIFIED M ULTI -M ODAL M ODELS
115
      In recent years, unified models for processing multiple modalities have advanced significantly. Mod-
116
      els like Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) and PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023) demonstrate strong few-
117
      shot learning capabilities across tasks. LLAVA Liu et al. (2023) enhances LLaMa Touvron et al.
118   (2023) with multimodal fine-tuning, but still uses separate encoders, limiting true unification and
119   image generation. Recent efforts, like Perceiver IO (Jaegle et al., 2021) and Unified-IO (Lu et al.,
120   2022), attempt modality unification but at a smaller scale. The Chameleon project (Chameleon,
121   2024) scales this up with a 34-billion parameter model trained on image-text data. However these
122   approaches largely focus on autoregressive generation which is inefficient for high-dimensional data.
123
      Relevant to our work, UniD3 Hu et al. (2022) considered discrete diffusion on image and text
124   but made several design decisions that separated each modality, using both absorbing and uniform
125   masking, decoupling the modalities inside the model with separate operations on each. Further we
126   couldn’t compare against their model, as were unable to reproduce their reported results using their
127   publicly available code.
128
129   2.2    D ISCRETE D IFFUSION M ODELS
130
131   Discrete diffusion models have emerged as a promising alternative to continuous diffusion for dis-
132   crete data types. (Sohl-Dickstein et al.) introduced the first discrete diffusion model over binary
133   variables, (Hoogeboom et al.) extended the noising process to categorical variables, demonstrating
134
      its effectiveness on image generation tasks. D3PM Austin et al. (2021) later extended discrete diffu-
      sion to a more general set of noising processes, allowing for more flexible noise schedules. Recent
135
      work by SEDD (Lou et al.) introduced score entropy, a novel loss function for discrete diffusion
136
      models that bridges the gap between continuous and discrete spaces, and more recently, Sahoo et al.
137   (2024); Shi et al. (2024) showed text perplexity competitive with GPT-2. While this approach shows
138   promise for improving discrete diffusion models, these methods were primarily focused on language
139   modeling tasks. Our work extends the application of discrete diffusion to multiple modalities and
140   demonstrates its effectiveness in a unified architecture.

146   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al.; Song et al., 2020) are a class of generative
147   models that learn to construct a data distribution by gradually reversing a process that introduces
148   noise into clean data samples. This approach models the transformation of a data sample x0 from a
149   clean state through increasingly noisy states until it reaches a pure noise distribution.
150
      The forward diffusion process is described by a series of transitions where each latent variable xt at
151
      time step t is sampled from a Gaussian distribution as follows:

172   Building on the foundations of continuous diffusion models, discrete diffusion models adapt these
173   concepts to structures that are inherently discrete. Unlike their continuous counterparts that model
174   transitions of xt given xt−1 with Gaussian distributions, discrete models define transitions using
175   categorical distributions. The forward process for discrete models is thus characterized as:
176

178   Here, Q̄t = t=0 Qt which represents the cumuliative transition matrix at each discrete time step
179   t, where Qt is a transition matrix [Qt ]ij = q(xt = j | xt−1 = i) dictating the probabilities of
180   moving from one discrete state xt−1 to another xt , and x0 is a one-hot vector of the input data
181
      sample. D3PM (Austin et al., 2021) generalizes this framework over various transition matrices,
      the popular ones mainly include uniform and absorbing transition matrix. In UniDisc, we use the
182
      absorbing transition matrix as emperically it has been found to work the best across text and images
183
      Austin et al. (2021); Lou et al. (2024). Absorbing transition matrix requires having an absorbing
184   state namely the [MASK] token. The matrix is represented as Qt = αt I + (1 − αt )1eTm , where 1
185   is a column vector of ones and em is a one-hot vector with one on the mask state m. This ends up
186   being a matrix with all zeros except i = j ̸= m is α and j = m, i ̸= m is 1 − α and i = j = m is 1.
187
      Intuitively this means that during the forward transition, the probability of an input token x0 to stay
188
      the same is α, the probability of it being masked is 1 − α, and the probability of a masked token to
189
      be unmasked is 0.

221
      than for text decoding. As can be seen in Figure 4 (c) and (d), FID saturates within 32 sampling
222
      steps for images, while it takes 400 sampling steps for the text generative perplexity to saturate.
223   This creates an issue for unified modeling as the number of sampling steps is bottlenecked by the
224   maximum number of sampling steps of any input modality. To resolve this issue, we propose KV-
225   caching the tokens of the faster modality, which in this case is the image. This however is not
226   feasible with current training scheme, as the text tokens never encounter, the image tokens from the
227   previous denoising steps.
228
      To fix this we propose to have different time schedules for each modality, specifically a slower
229   time-schedule for text and a faster one for images. To implement these schedules, we consider
230   Nmin and Nmax , which represent the min and max number of timesteps one might consider during
231   inference. We also consider K, which represents the text-to-image inference ratio, i.e how many
232   times is image inference faster than text inference. Empirically, we find this number to be roughly
233   10. During training, we first randomly sample text timesteps from a uniform distribution, ttxt ∼
234   U(0, 1) and set image timesteps by randomly offsetting the text timesteps with δti , specifically
235   timg ∼ U(ttxt , ttxt − δti ). This ensures that the image timestep only moves behind the text timestep
                                                                        K      K
236   by a maximum of δti . We randomly set δti to δti ∼ U( Nmax            , Nmin ). This modality-specific
237   timestep schedule ensures UniDisc can handle image token KV caching during inference.

504   Since we already have a plethora of large-scale AR models (Touvron et al., 2023; Chameleon, 2024),
505   it would be useful to have the ability to fine-tune them for discrete diffusion objective. While the
506
      naive method for fine-tuning would be to change the objective function to discrete diffusion while
      using AR’s pre-trained weights. We find that a better idea is to left-shift the output targets of the
507
      diffusion objective such that instead of having the masked token predict its respective visible token,
508
      we have the token before the masked token predict it. Thus it more closely matching AR’s next-
509   token prediction objective. In Figure 6 we show that this strategy works well and we can effectively
510   fine-tune a pretrained autoregressive language model using discrete diffusion loss. We demonstrate
511   this result on a 270M parameter language model (Mehta et al., 2024), OpenELM, which is trained
512   with an AR objective. We compare against training from scratch and training AR without the shift.
513   We find shifting strategy converges faster.

515   4.6   S CALING U NI D ISC
516
      We show that UniDisc scales well across parameters and dataset size. We train a 1.4B parame-
517
      ter model with web-scale data. Our 1.4B model is trained in two stages, with a low-resolution
518
      pre-training stage and a second high-resolution fine-tuning stage. Our first-stage consists of 250M
519   image/caption pairs at 256x256 resolution. We curate our dataset from several sources, with 200M
520   open-web images from (Gadre et al., 2024), which were re-captioned by a VLM to create higher-
521   quality descriptions by (Li et al., 2024b). We also add a set of smaller, PixelProse (Singla et al.,
522   2024), SegmentAnything (Chen et al., 2023), and JourneyDB (Sun et al., 2023). In addition, we
523   construct a high-quality, custom dataset of 18M synthetic images, following findings by (Zhuo et al.,
524   2024; Sehwag et al., 2024) on the importance of image/caption alignment for image generation. We
525   construct our dataset by prompting an LLM to augment a set of 250K human prompts and use (Esser
526   et al., 2024) for generations. In both stages, we account for dataset imbalance and sample more from
527
      higher-quality sources. Finally, we fine-tune our model in a second stage, interpolating the RoPE
      2D embeddings to train at 512x512 on 30M image/caption pairs.
528
529   Additional ablations of smaller models are available in A.3. The training curve of our 1.4B model
530   along with additional qualitative results with different masking strategies and varying classifier guid-
531   ance are available in A.5 and A.6 respectively.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we introduced UniDisc, the first large-scale unified multimodal discrete diffusion
536   model capable of generating and editing both images and text. By leveraging discrete diffusion
537   processes, we showed that UniDisc surpasses autoregressive models in both inference efficiency
538   and quality. Our model unifies various design choices in discrete diffusion space, across modalities,
539   through extensive ablations and analysis. We hope that our work inspires future research in this
      direction.

905   We validate our design choices by running small-scale experiments on a subset of our primary
906   dataset, taking 18M image/caption pairs on DataComp1B. We train on lower-resolution images at
907   128 × 128 and obtain a 1:1 ratio of text to image tokens, with 64 text and 64 image tokens for a total
908
      sequence length of 128, with all other hyperparameters the same as in our primary experiments.
909   We examine the influence of several design choices for our model in Table 4 and reach several
910   conclusions. First, architecture changes to improve training stability—namely adding QK Normal-
911   ization and using RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm—do not substantially affect convergence in this
912   setting.
913   Another natural design choice is to parameterize the model such that we provide the modality of
914   a given token to the model. With this relaxation we can drastically reduce the output space and,
915   in theory, simplify the objective for our model. However, we find that this reparameterization
916   only marginally reduces our overall perplexity, even at this smaller-scale. We hypothesize that
917   the modality-specific embeddings added to each token allows the model to learn the correct output
      space with minimal added parameters.

       A.7   U NDERSTANDING THE EFFECT OF C LASSIFIER F REE G UIDANCE (CFG)
973
974    In Table 2, we observe that CFG is a significant factor in the performance difference between
975    UniDisc and the AR baseline. We hypothesize that this is because CFG is most useful in decoding
976    the first few tokens, with diminishing utility in later tokens. To examine this, we look at intermediate
977    predictions by storing arg max pθ (x0 | xt ) at each sampling step. As an AR model cannot directly
978    capture this distribution without an intractable rollout, we opt to use the same UniDisc model but
979    with an autoregressive inference strategy, decoding from left to right. This allows us to directly
980
       compare the performance of different inference strategies and how they interact with classifier-free
       guidance.
981
982    We visualize this in Figure 8, where we visualize the difference between the conditional and uncon-
983    ditional image generated at different %s of decoded tokens. We notice two things: (a) the difference
984    diminishes as more tokens are decoded and (b) UniDisc consistently has higher distances between
985    the logits than AR, which flattens out more quickly.
986    Intuitively, this means UniDisc extracts much more discriminating signal from CFG compared to
987    AR. We believe this is because UniDisc has much more flexibility to decode tokens initially based
988    on confidence, compared to AR which is forced to decode in a left to right manner and thus, can
989    course correct quickly and more effectively. This can be seen in Table 6, where we selectively apply
990    CFG only on a few steps and notice that CLIP score when CFG is applied on steps 1-3 almost
991    matches applying CFG on all, while applying on the last few steps doesn’t affect things much at all.
992    Given the differences in CFG between UniDisc and AR models, we conduct a hyperparameter sweep
993    over guidance scales in Figure 18. We compute FID and CLIP scores over four datasets, and at both
994    115/340M parameters. We find that our AR baseline benefits from a weight of w = 0.5 but sees far
995    less improvement than UniDisc with CFG. For UniDisc, we choose an overall weight of w = 1.5,
996    but note that the CLIP score scales cleanly with the guidance scale, demonstrating the trade-off
997    between visual quality and prompt adherence.

       A.8   G ENERATION TIME VS BATCH SIZE
1008
1009   We analyze the quality of the generation versus time in Figure 9. We make a similar observation
1010   as in prior work (Ziv et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024) on discrete diffusion, finding that the ability
1011   to obtain predictions with varying sampling steps allows lower latencies. However, with current
1012   implementations, KV caching in AR models results in higher throughput as the batch size increases.
1013   This tradeoff can be explained by looking at the number of function evaluations (NFEs) and the cost
1014   of each in both cases. In AR generation w/KV caching, we have a fixed NFE, but each forward pass
1015
       is substantially less expensive than in the NAR case. In contrast, in NAR, we can use substantially
       fewer NFEs, but each is more costly. Modern GPUs only reach peak throughput at larger batch
1016
       sizes (Chitty-Venkata et al., 2024), or, in other words, as we decrease the batch size, the difference
1017
       in computation per function evaluation diminishes, resulting in NAR having favorable performance.

1032
1033   A clear benefit of diffusion models is the ability to perform zero-shot editing without specific paired
1034   data—which is often difficult to obtain. We demonstrate one such method in Figure 15, showing
1035   that UniDisc can automatically improve a user provided image and caption.
1036   We augment real images by overlaying random objects from the COCO dataset. Similarly, we aug-
1037   ment captions by asking an LLM to generate purposely incorrect variations. We then randomly
1038   mask the image and text inputs and unmask as described above, automatically removing these un-
1039   desired image artifacts and generating the correct caption. We adopt a best-of-n sampling strategy
1040   with n distinct noise masks. We unroll each generation until completion and use the model’s own
1041   likelihood to select the best generation.
