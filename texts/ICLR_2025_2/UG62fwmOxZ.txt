009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Quanvolutional neural networks have shown promise in areas such as computer
012            vision and time series analysis. However, their applicability to multi-dimensional
013            and diverse data types remains underexplored. Existing quanvolutional networks
014            heavily rely on classical layers, with minimal quantum involvement, due to inher-
015            ent limitations in current quanvolution algorithms. In this study, we introduce a
016            new quanvolution algorithm that addresses previous shortcomings related to per-
017            formance, scalability, and data encoding inefficiencies. Specifically targeting time
018            series data, we propose the Quanv1D layer, which is trainable, capable of han-
019
               dling variable kernel sizes, and can generate a customizable number of feature
               maps. Unlike previous implementations, Quanv1D can seamlessly integrate at any
020
               position within a neural network, effectively processing time series of arbitrary di-
021
               mensions. Our chosen ansatz and the overall design of Quanv1D contribute to its
022            significant parameter efficiency and inherent regularization properties. In addition
023            to this new layer, we present a new architecture called Fully Quanvolutional Net-
024            works (FQN), composed entirely of Quanv1D layers. We tested this lightweight
025            model on 20 UEA and UCR time series classification datasets and compared it
026            against both quantum and classical models, including the current state-of-the-art,
027            ModernTCN. On most datasets, FQN achieved accuracy comparable to the base-
028            line models and even outperformed them on some, all while using a fraction of the
029            parameters.

032   1     I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As Moore’s Law approaches its limits, a global shift is underway toward quantum computing–
035   an alternative capable of solving challenges intractable for classical systems (Nielsen & Chuang,
036   2010). Given the practical implications of machine learning and the widely sought-after “quantum
037   advantage” offered by quantum computing and its algorithms, quantum machine learning (QML)
038   is gaining traction quickly (Biamonte et al., 2017). For instance, the domain has already seen the
039
      implementation of several quantum adaptations of support vector machines (Rebentrost et al., 2014;
      Li et al., 2015) and neural networks (Tacchino et al., 2019; 2020). In this paper, we revisit one such
040
      QML algorithm, the quanvolution algorithm (Henderson et al., 2020), analyze its utilities and pit-
041
      falls, and propose an improved version that can overcome performance, scalability, and modularity
042   issues in existing networks.

      1.1   BACKGROUND
046
      In the literature, there are two variants of quantum convolution: circuit-based and kernel-based (also
047
      referred to as quanvolution). With different quantum operations, the circuit-based variant mimics
048   the Conv2D and pooling layers to turn a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a quantum circuit
049   (Cong et al., 2019; Hur et al., 2022). However, unlike a typical convolution operation, this variant
050   works with the entire flattened input instead of individual input patches. Although the entire pro-
051   cess can be executed on a quantum computer, the scalability of circuit-based quantum convolution
052   compared to classical CNNs remains open for debate. For instance, this variant struggles to encode
053   large, complex image datasets in this noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era. Additionally,
      the reliance of neural networks on nonlinearities conflicts with quantum mechanics.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In contrast, kernel-based quantum convolution, also known as quanvolution, functions similarly to a
055   single classical convolutional layer (Henderson et al., 2020). Essentially, the quanvolution operation
056   is the result of quantum circuits substituting for typical filters or kernels inside a convolutional layer.
057   This approach offers greater flexibility compared to the circuit-based variant, enabling the devel-
058   opment of hybrid models and training schemes. In a classical-quantum hybrid model, a quantum
059   computer deals with the primary computations–interactions between input patches and filters–while
060   classical computers handle tasks like processing loss values and applying nonlinear activations. Ow-
061   ing to its versatility, the algorithm’s introduction remains a staple in quantum-based computer vision
062   with wide range of applications (Ullah & Garcia-Zapirain, 2024; Kharsa et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
063
      2024; Yang et al., 2021; Peral-Garcı́a et al., 2024).
064   This study focuses on the kernel-based quantum convolution, or quanvolution, and aims to address
065   various limitations outlined in the following section.
066
067   1.2   M OTIVATION
068
069   Quanvolutional neural networks are implemented as hybrid systems, where learning is achieved
070   through the combined effort of quantum and classical layers. In such architectures, the initial con-
071   volution layer in models like LeNet-5 (LeCun et al., 1998) is replaced with a quanvolutional layer,
072   while the rest of the network remains unchanged. However, most implementations heavily rely on
073   deep classical layers, typically including only a single quantum layer. This raises questions about
074   the true contribution of quanvolutional layers–whether they play a meaningful role or if the classical
075
      layers shoulder the heavy lifting. As such, quanvolutional neural networks are very limited in both
      applicability and performance, and these limitations stem from the quanvolution algorithm itself.
076
077   The original quanvolution algorithm was initially designed to work only with single-channel image
078   patches (Henderson et al., 2020). While some studies have managed to extend its application to
079   RGB or three-channel images (Jing et al., 2022; Savla et al., 2022), the algorithm still lacks the
080   capability to process 2D data with an arbitrary number of channels, unlike a classical Conv2D layer.
081
      In modern deep learning architectures, the number of channels or feature maps typically increases
      as the network deepens. For instance, EfficientNet-B0 starts with a three-channel input image and
082
      expands to a feature dimension of 1280 in the final convolutional layer (Tan & Le, 2019). Achieving
083
      this level of scalability remains a challenge for existing quanvolution algorithms.
084
085   Moreover, a quanvolutional layer produces only a limited number of feature maps, primarily due to
086   its small kernel size and reliance on a single filter. Generally, the kernel size influences the number
087
      of wires in the circuit, which in turn affects the number of feature maps. Such an approach is in stark
      contrast to classical convolution. For Conv2D layers, users can adjust kernel sizes to capture either
088
      fine-grained details or broader contextual features as needed. Furthermore, classical layers utilize
089
      multiple kernels or filters to extract varied but pertinent patterns from the same input, improving the
090   model’s ability to generalize. As such, when compared to classical convolution, the quanvolution
091   algorithm has yet to reach its overall performance potential due to the rigidity in kernel size and
092   circuit selection.
093
      Our motivation for this work was to create a learnable quanvolutional layer that works like modern
094
      convolutional layers and gives users the freedom to choose the kernel size and number of output
095
      feature maps. Our aim was to ensure that this layer could be seamlessly integrated into any network,
096   provided the input dimensions were compatible, mirroring the versatility of conventional convolu-
097   tion. However, we recognized that making a practical, scalable, and modular 2D quanvolutional
098   layer right away would be very challenging because of NISQ-related limitations. As a result, we
099   opted to begin with its 1D counterpart as a foundational step.

230   2.2   FQN
231
232   The FQN architecture is designed to be parameter-efficient, built exclusively with Quanv1D layers,
233   as shown in Fig. 1. It consists of three primary components: embedding, propagation, and projec-
234
      tion. In summary, the embedding layer transforms the input into a high-dimensional feature space,
      the propagation layers employ dilation (Yu, 2015) to iteratively improve feature representations with
235
      longer receptive fields, and the projection layer transfers these enhanced features to a desired output
236
      space.
237
238   The embedding layer expands the raw input data into a learned embedding dimension, dime , which
239   helps aggregate local information and prepares the data for more complex hierarchical feature ex-
240
      traction. For long sequences, the kernel size ke can  remain    large without shortening the sequence
      length, as padding is automatically adjusted using k2e . However, this adjustment is only applicable

241
      to kernels with odd sizes. In addition, strides can reduce the input sequence length if necessary. For
242
      example, setting se = 3 will shorten the embedded feature sequence to one-third of the original
243
      input length.
244
245   After the embedding stage, the propagation layers form the core of the architecture. There are depth
246   propagation layers, each composed of a Quanv1D layer, followed by batch normalization and ReLU
247
      activation. These layers improve the embedded representation over time by reducing the internal
      covariate shift and adding nonlinearity to the data. The dilation rate, di = i (where i is the layer
248
      index), linearly increases the network’s receptive field as the depth grows. This allows the model
249
      to capture dependencies at multiple scales–from local interactions in the lower layers to long-range
250   dependencies in the deeper layers–without increasing the number of parameters. In the propagation
251   layers, no padding was applied, and sprop was set to one. However, the kernel size kprop was
252   adjusted based on the specific dataset.
253
      Following the propagation layers, the projection layer maps the multi-scale, transformed features
254
      to a space corresponding to the target classes. This allows the network to produce class-specific
255
      feature maps. For this layer, the kernel size is set to kproj = 1, with a stride of sproj = 1, no
256   padding (pproj = 0), and a dilation rate of dproj = 1. We then apply global average pooling across
257   the channel dimension and pass the pooled features to a softmax classifier for final prediction. This
258   approach helps prevent overfitting by reducing the number of trainable parameters and serves as a
259   form of regularization.

467   4     C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
468
469   The primary objective of this study was to introduce a QML algorithm for quantum computers,
470   aiming to create a quantum equivalent of a well-established and widely used classical method. We
471   designed our proposed quanvolution algorithm, an analog to convolution, with NISQ-related con-
472   straints in mind, and it proved effective in temporal data learning tasks. In fact, it was able to
473   outperform contemporary classical models in some cases, thanks to its efficient parameter manage-
474
      ment and inherent regularization. Despite our model being a theoretical framework, it remained
      effective when we simulated a realistic scenario with statistical noise. Our proposed model, FQN,
475
      incorporates non-linear activation functions, which present a challenge in a quantum environment.
476
      But the computational overhead for these activation functions is negligible, and the network should,
477   in theory, work well in a classical-quantum hybrid framework. Previous studies have demonstrated
478   promising results for similar hybrid approaches with smaller models (Tacchino et al., 2019; 2020).
479   As the world moves toward fault-tolerant quantum computing with increased qubits, we are op-
480   timistic that, with the right settings, our model will perform similarly. Nonetheless, significant
481   progress is still required before reaching that point.
482
483   4.1   L IMITATIONS
484
485   For this study, we tested our FQN only on classification tasks and have yet to explore regression and
      data imputation problems. Using a fully quanvolutional structure for these tasks will be challenging,


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      as Quanv1D’s output is in the range of -1 to 1, which limits its applicability for tasks that require
487   output values beyond this range. Additionally, for classification problems, we noticed that training
488   FQN gets difficult when the number of classes exceeds 10, either due to the limited number of pa-
489   rameters or the usage of classical optimizers on a quantum model. Also, we could not determine the
490   true operating range of FQN because we only tested it on a subset of the UEA and UCR repositories
491   and not all the datasets, as the authors suggested (Dau et al., 2019).
492
      In addition to these limitations, we believe it is important to address the constraints related to hard-
493   ware implementation. Our quanvolutional layer is largely theoretical, simulated using classical
494   computing. Given the constraints associated with the NISQ era, it remains uncertain whether we
495   can achieve this level of implementation, as we did not have access to real hardware to verify our
496   algorithm. While both Quanv1D and FQN showed strong performance in tasks involving statistical
497   noise, their robustness against other challenges–such as decoherence, environmental noise, error ac-
498   cumulation, and gate fidelity–has yet to be demonstrated. Moreover, although amplitude encoding
499   offers improved parameter efficiency for the model, it significantly increases circuit depth (Kharsa
500   et al., 2023; Schuld, 2018), posing further challenges. Finally, the high computational complexity
501
      of FQN makes classical simulation difficult. For comparison, the time complexity of quanvolution
      is O(k 2 × c2in × cout ), whereas that of convolution is O(k × cin × cout ), where k is the kernel
502
      length, cin is the input dimension, and cout is the number of output channels. Optimizing the design
503
      to reduce complexity remains an area for future exploration.
504
505
506
      4.2   F UTURE W ORK
507
      To address the current limitations, our immediate focus will be on improving the model’s perfor-
508
      mance when handling more than 10 classes and optimizing the overall design to reduce time com-
509   plexity. Following this, we plan to benchmark the model using the full UEA and UCR repositories
510   Dau et al. (2019); Bagnall et al. (2018), which will help establish the operational range of quanvo-
511   lution for classification tasks. After completing the classification phase, we will address the range
512   limitation of -1 to 1. While the output range of the quantum layer cannot be altered, we aim to de-
513   sign an activation function that maps outputs to a higher range while preserving the distinct quantum
514   properties. Achieving this mapping could open new avenues for exploration.
515   Before advancing to our ultimate goal of developing a 2D quanvolution, we aim to introduce explain-
516   ability to the current 1D quanvolution framework. Although inspired by classical 1D convolution,
517   the quantum operations introduce complexities that make explainability less intuitive. Understand-
518   ing the mathematical principles underlying these processes will not only enhance interpretability but
519   may also offer insights for further design improvements. Transitioning from 1D to 2D quanvolu-
520   tion while maintaining this level of modularity will be a significant challenge, but we believe this
521   progression is the logical next step, especially for expanding applications into the domains of image
522   processing and computer vision.

673   Quantum circuits provide the framework for modeling quantum computations; wires represent
674
      qubits, and gates correspond to quantum operations that manipulate these qubits. In the context
      of applying QML to classical data, quantum circuits typically consist of three main phases: encod-
675
      ing, manipulation, and measurement.
676
677
           1. Encoding: In this phase, classical information is mapped into a quantum Hilbert space,
678           preparing the quantum system for computation. Two widely used encoding methods are
679           angle encoding and amplitude encoding. Angle encoding represents classical values as
680           angles of rotation gates applied to qubits, typically starting from the |0⟩ state. This method
681           requires n qubits to encode n classical features. Amplitude encoding or embedding, in
682           contrast, stores classical data in the amplitudes of a quantum state, allowing n qubits to
683           represent up to 2n classical values classical values. While amplitude encoding is highly
684           efficient in terms of qubit usage, preparing such states can be computationally intensive.
685        2. Manipulation: After encoding, the quantum data is processed using a sequence of quan-
686           tum gates. Researchers often design parameterized quantum circuits, known as ansatz,
687           which include gates such as Hadamard, Controlled-NOT (CNOT), and rotations around the
688           X, Y, and Z axes. These gates enable critical quantum phenomena for quantum advantage.
689           For example, the Hadamard gate creates superposition, while the CNOT gate establishes
690           entanglement.
691        3. Measurement: The final stage involves measuring quantum states to extract classical out-
692           comes. Measurement causes the quantum state to collapse into one of its basis states (ei-
693           ther 0 or 1). Due to the probabilistic nature of quantum measurement, multiple repetitions
694           (referred to as ”shots”) are performed to gather sufficient statistics. With enough mea-
695           surements or experimental reruns, the outcomes reliably reflect the expected or analytical
696           results of the quantum computation.

723   C    DATASET S ETUP
724
725
726   The datasets used in this experiment were taken from the UEA and UCR archives (Bagnall et al.,
727   2018; Dau et al., 2019). Among the 150+ datasets available in the archive, we have considered a
728   subset of 20. The selected datasets along with their descriptions are provided in Table 3. Our dataset
729   selection criteria were primarily based on the domain and its real-life applicability, i.e., we wanted
730
      to cover as many domains as possible to test our proposed model. The considered datasets come
      from 15 different domains and include a mix of both binary and multi-class classification tasks.
731
      A major portion of the selected datasets are univariate in nature, as QuanvNet (Rivera-Ruiz et al.,
732
      2023), one of our baselines, only works on univariate data. One exception to the criteria was the
733   selection of multiple EEG datasets. This was due to the high variability and low statistical power of
734   EEG datasets (Davoudi et al., 2023; Button et al., 2013), along with their high dimensionality.
735
736
      All datasets were processed following the same procedure. We first applied Z-normalization to
      the entire dataset, followed by a 60:20:20 split for training, validation, and testing. For multi-
737
      dimensional data, we performed Z-normalization on each input channel individually. We saved the
738
      normalization factors and data splits to ensure reproducibility and fair comparisons among models
739   trained on the same datasets. Additionally, we calculated class weights and integrated them into the
740   cross-entropy loss function to address class imbalances in several datasets.

744   D    T RAINING S ETUP
745
746
747   We trained each model for 200 epochs, opting for a learning rate scheduler instead of early stopping.
748   As mentioned before, our objective was to evaluate the differences in learning between Quanv1D
749   and traditional convolutional layers. The scheduler had a patience of 5 epochs and a relative thresh-
750   old of 0.001, monitoring the validation loss to reduce the learning rate by different factors when
751   performance plateaued. We varied the reduction factor to ensure stable training and minimize over-
752
      fitting. However, for consistency, the reduction factor remained the same across all comparison
      models for each dataset. The data specific reduction factors are presented in Table 4.
753
754   We optimized all the models using Adam (Kingma, 2014), starting with default values set by Py-
755   Torch. The initial learning rate for all datasets was 0.01, except for D10 and D12, where it was
      0.001. We repeated all the experiments five times without using any specific seeds.
