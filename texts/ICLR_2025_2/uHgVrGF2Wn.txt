                                                  A BSTRACT
029
030
               Recent progress in multimodal large language models has markedly enhanced the
031
               understanding of short videos (typically under one minute), and several evaluation
032            datasets have emerged accordingly. However, these advancements fall short of
033            meeting the demands of real-world applications such as embodied intelligence
034            for long-term decision-making, in-depth movie reviews and discussions, and live
035            sports commentary, all of which require comprehension of long videos spanning
036            several hours. To address this gap, we introduce LVBench, a benchmark specifically
037            designed for long video understanding. Our dataset comprises publicly sourced
038            videos and encompasses a diverse set of tasks aimed at long video comprehension
039            and information extraction. LVBench is designed to challenge multimodal models
040
               to demonstrate long-term memory and extended comprehension capabilities. Our
               extensive evaluations reveal that current multimodal models still underperform on
041
               these demanding long video understanding tasks. Through LVBench, we aim to
042
               spur the development of more advanced models capable of tackling the complexities
043            of long video comprehension.
044
045
046   1   I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Recently, the rapid advancements in large language models (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Du
049   et al., 2021) and visual feature extraction models (Radford et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2023; Zhai et al.,
050   2023) have led to significant improvements in the performance of multimodal large models on
051   open-domain video question-answering tasks. These multimodal understanding models have also
052   empowered various downstream tasks, such as embodied intelligence, video generation, and subtitles
053   for the visually impaired. However, most current end-to-end video understanding models are limited
      to processing videos of only a few minutes in length. More complex tasks require the capability


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to understand much longer videos, which presents a significant challenge to existing multimodal
055   models.
056
057   Despite numerous video understanding benchmarks being proposed in the past, the field of long
058
      video understanding remains underdeveloped due to the difficulties in data acquisition and annotation.
      To address this gap, we introduce LVBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate the capabilities
059
      of models in understanding long videos. We collected a substantial amount of long video data
060
      from public sources, with annotations provided through a combination of manual effort and model
061   assistance. Additionally, we carefully designed a series of evaluation tasks. Compared to previous
062   video understanding benchmarks (Li et al., 2023c), LVBench offers the following unique features:
063
064          • We define six core capabilities for long video understanding, which can be flexibly com-
065            bined to create complex and challenging questions. This multifaceted approach enables a
066            comprehensive evaluation of a model’s ability to process and comprehend lengthy video
067            content.
068          • We have collected a diverse range of long video data from various sources, with an average
069            duration approximately four times longer than the longest existing dataset. The categories of
070            videos in LVBench are illustrated in Figure 1. This extensive collection of long-form video
071            content provides a robust foundation for testing models on extended temporal contexts.
072          • Through meticulous human annotation and multi-stage quality control processes, we ensure
073            the high quality of our dataset, providing a reliable benchmark for assessing long video
074            understanding capabilities.

077   2    R ELATED W ORKS
078
079   Multi-modal Large Language Models. Building upon the achievements in Large Language Models
080   (LLMs), the field has shifted towards Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to enhance
081   multi-modal understanding and generation capabilities (Wang et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Alayrac
082
      et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b;c; Liu et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024). Early advancements in this
      area include models like Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), which fused text and vision to perform
083
      exceptionally well in multimodal tasks. Subsequent models such as VideoChat (Li et al., 2023b) and
084
      VideoChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2024) began exploring the video modality, using ChatGPT (Achiam
085   et al., 2023) to generate video instruction-tuning data for improved instruction-following capabilities.
086   VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2023c) advanced the field by introducing a dedicated video encoder, requiring
087   extensive training on large-scale datasets to optimize performance. The ST-LLM (Liu et al., 2024c)
088   model streamlined this process by leveraging LLMs for visual sequence modeling, thereby reducing
089   training complexity and enhancing performance. PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024) introduced a resource-
090   efficient method for adapting image-language pre-trained models to dense video understanding
091   through a novel feature pooling strategy, achieving state-of-the-art results. Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid
092   et al., 2024) further pushed the boundaries with a mixture-of-experts architecture, excelling in long-
093
      context reasoning and multi-modality across extensive multimodal benchmarks. These advancements
      underscore the significant progress and potential of MLLMs in advancing multimodal comprehension
094
      and generation. Despite the progress made, our experiments indicate that current video understanding
095
      models still fall short on tasks requiring long-range comprehension, highlighting an urgent need for
096   the development of models tailored for long video understanding.
097
098   Benchmarks for MLLM. Recent advancements in vision-language (VL) benchmarks have largely
099
      focused on images and short videos, as seen in datasets like MMBench (Liu et al., 2023), SEED-
      Bench-2 (Li et al., 2023a), TGIF-QA (Jang et al., 2017) and MVBench (Li et al., 2023c). For long
100
      video understanding, previous benchmarks such as Perception Test (Pătrăucean et al., 2023) have
101
      explored multi-modal video perception and reasoning but often with shorter video clips and limited
102   temporal context. Datasets like How2QA (Li et al., 2020) and ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al., 2019) are
103   domain-specific and do not adequately capture the complexity of long-term video understanding.
104   EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) and MovieQA (Tapaswi et al., 2016) provide insights into
105   narrative and thematic understanding but are constrained by shorter video durations and limited gran-
106   ularity. While LongVideoBench (Wu et al., 2024), MovieChat (Song et al., 2023), MoVQA (Zhang
107   et al., 2023), and Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024) utilize longer videos to test models, their average
      duration is still limited to around 10 minutes. In contrast, LVBench features significantly longer

194   3.2   TASK D EFINITION
195
      To comprehensively evaluate long video understanding, we propose a benchmark testing six core
196   capabilities. Example questions for each capability are presented in Figure 2. The proportion of
197   each capability is shown in Figure 3. Questions are designed to flexibly combine multiple skills to
198   construct complex queries that probe a model’s capacity to:
199
200         1. Temporal Grounding(TG): Questions focus on understanding sequences and dynamics
201            within the video, such as identifying specific events at designated times (e.g., "What hap-
202            pened at 29:30?").
203         2. Summarization(Sum): Annotators are required to produce an abstractive summary that en-
204            capsulates the entire video content, demonstrating a cohesive understanding of the sequence
205            from start to finish.
206         3. Reasoning(Rea): This involves the application of advanced reasoning skills to interpret the
207            video content:
208               • Causal: Determining causal relationships between events.
209               • Emotional: Understanding the emotional developments of characters.
210
                  • Intentional: Interpreting the underlying intentions of characters.
211
                  • Prospective: Making predictions about future events based on current evidence.
212
213         4. Entity Recognition(ER): This capability requires the identification and continuous tracking
214
               of key entities (such as people, places, and objects) throughout the video:
215               • Entity Detection: Identifying mentions of entities and resolving their identities across
                    different instances.

233               • Relation Extraction: Extracting the relationships among identified entities.
234               • Action Recognition: Observing and understanding the progression of an entity’s
235                 actions over time.
236               • Entity Association: Linking entities to relevant events.
237
            5. Event Understanding(EU): Comprehending overarching semantic concepts in the video:
238
239               • Video Classification: Classifying the genre of the video (e.g., news, film).
240               • Event Detection: Identifying significant occurrences within the video (such as goals
241                 scored or conflicts).
242               • Scene Perception: Recognizing changes between different scenes or settings.
243
            6. Key Information Retrieval(KIR): Extracting specific, detailed information, such as text
244
               displayed in the video (e.g., "What revenue growth did the firm report at the conference?").
245
246   By composing questions that test combinations of these temporal, abstractive, reasoning, entity-
247   centric, event-based, and detail-oriented skills, our benchmark enables robust evaluation of a model’s
248   ability to understand long videos across multiple modalities. This multifaceted typology covers the
249   key cognitive capabilities required to comprehend complex, open-ended video.

251   3.3   QA G ENERATION
252
253   The total number of questions for each video is positively correlated with the video duration, averaging
254   24 questions per hour. After constructing a question, annotators are asked to provide four options,
255   including one correct answer and three distractors. Annotating long videos is significantly more
256
      challenging than annotating short videos or image data, making quality control a substantial challenge.
      To ensure the quality of the evaluation set, we encourage annotators to follow these principles:
257
258
             • Question Diversity: Construct at least one question for each question type in a video.
259
260          • Specificity: Questions should refer to unique scenes, events, or characters, avoiding vague
261
               descriptions. For example, if a video contains two arguments, a well-constructed question
               might be: "When did A and B start arguing?" or "How did the person in red’s expression
262
               change during the hallway argument?". Less specific questions would be: "Why did they
263
               start arguing?" or "Who are the people arguing?"
264
265          • Temporal Coverage: Questions should cover multiple events throughout the video, avoiding
266
               repetition of a single event.
267          • Consistency: Constructed correct answers should precisely address the questions. Answers
268            should match the content of the questions, avoiding irrelevant information. Correct and
269            incorrect answers should be constructed consistently, avoiding obvious differences in length,
               form, or format.

      By following these principles, we aim to create a high-quality, diverse, and challenging evaluation set
271   for long video understanding.

      3.4     DATA Q UALITY C ONTROL
274
275   During the annotation process, we observed that annotators had a tendency to label most questions
276   as temporal grounding, i.e., specifying a time range to limit the referent of the question. This
277   practice may inadvertently reduce the difficulty of the questions and unfairly disadvantage video-
278   understanding models that lack the ability to perceive the temporal dimension. To address this issue,
279   we instructed annotators to minimize the number of temporal questions while still ensuring the
280   uniqueness of the referents, effectively converting temporal grounding questions into other question
281
      types.
282   Upon constructing all the questions, we discovered that for certain questions, a language model could
283   generate answers without any visual input. As highlighted in MMstar (Chen et al., 2024a), many
284   multimodal benchmarks can be effectively solved using pure text input alone. To mitigate this issue,
285   we employed a straightforward yet effective approach. We utilized two powerful large language
286   models, GLM-4 (Du et al., 2021) and GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), to independently generate answers
287
      for all the questions. In cases where the outputs from both models were identical and matched the
      ground truth answer, we removed that particular data sample from the dataset. This filtering process
288
      successfully eliminated the majority of questions that did not rely on video content for answering.
289
      Following this filtering step, we obtained a refined set of 1,549 question-answer pairs.

297   4.1     S ETTINGS
298
      We evaluated the performance of nine models that support multi images or short video input:
299
      TimeChat (Ren et al., 2023), Video-ChatGPT (Maaz et al., 2023), PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024),
300   LLaVA-Onevision (Li et al., 2024), CogVLM2-Video (Hong et al., 2024), LLaVA-NeXT (Zhang
301   et al., 2024), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), GLM4V-Plus (Hong et al., 2024) and InternVL2-40B (Chen
302   et al., 2024b). To adapt these models for long video inputs, we sample a fixed number of frames from
303   the original video, such as 32 or 96 frames, to maintain consistency with the model’s training sequence
304   length. Additionally, we assessed six models that natively support long videos: LLaMA-VID (Li
305   et al., 2023d), MovieChat (Song et al., 2023), LWM (Liu et al., 2024a), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al.,
306   2024), Kangaroo (Liu et al., 2024b) and Qwen2-VL-72B (Wang et al., 2024). We processed the
307   videos at a rate of 1 frame per second and fed them into the models, only performing downsampling
308
      when the video’s length exceeded the model’s maximum processing capability. It is worth noting that
      although Gemini 1.5 Pro can handle videos up to 10 hours long, its publicly available interface is
309
      limited to processing videos of up to 1 hour in length. For each question, we provided the following
310
      prompt as input to the models:
311
312             Question (A) Option1 (B) Option2 (C) Option3 (D) Option4. Please select the best
313             answer from the options above and directly provide the letter representing your
314             choice without giving any explanation.
315
316
      After obtaining the model responses, we first attempted to extract the answers using regular expression
      matching. For questions where the matching process was unsuccessful, we employed a GLM-4 model
317
      to extract the answers from the responses.

454   5   D ISCUSSION
455
456   Conclusion. In this paper, we introduced LVBench, a benchmark designed to advance long video
457   understanding. LVBench comprises a diverse collection of lengthy videos and a meticulously
458   annotated question-answer dataset, presenting a robust evaluation framework for assessing multimodal
459   models on complex video understanding tasks. Our experiments revealed that while state-of-the-art
460   models have made strides in short video understanding, their performance on long videos falls short of
461   human-level accuracy. By providing a challenging benchmark, we hope to stimulate the development
462   of advanced models capable of tackling the complexities of extended video comprehension.
463
464   Limitations. A limitation of our benchmark is the exclusion of audio data. While audio can
465   provide valuable context, we did not include it because most current models lack effective audio
466   processing capabilities. Future work will aim to incorporate audio information to enhance the
467
      evaluation framework for multimodal understanding.
