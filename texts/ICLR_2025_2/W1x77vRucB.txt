011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly en-
014            hanced the capabilities of conversational agents, making them applicable to various
015
               fields (e.g., education). Despite their progress, the evaluation of the agents often
               overlooks the complexities of real-world conversations, such as real-time interac-
016
               tions, multi-party dialogues, and extended contextual dependencies. To bridge this
017
               gap, we introduce DialSim, a real-time dialogue simulator. In this simulator, an
018            agent is assigned the role of a character from popular TV shows, requiring it to re-
019            spond to spontaneous questions using past dialogue information and to distinguish
020            between known and unknown information. Key features of DialSim include eval-
021            uating the agent’s ability to respond within a reasonable time limit, handling long-
022            term multi-party dialogues, and testing the agent’s performance under randomized
023            questioning with a diverse and high-quality question-answer dataset. We utilized
024            this simulator to evaluate the latest conversational agents and analyze their limita-
025            tions. Our experiments highlight both the strengths and weaknesses of these agents,
026
               providing valuable insights for future improvements in the field of conversational
               AI. DialSim is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Simulator-A861.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advancements in Natural Language Generation (NLG) within Large Language Models (LLMs)
032   have significantly enhanced the capabilities of conversational agents. These agents are now integral to
033   various fields, including entertainment (Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) and education (Ait Baha
034   et al., 2023; Waisberg et al., 2024), providing personalized interactions that cater to individual
035   preferences and interests. As these agents continue to evolve and become more widely adopted, it
036   is crucial to rigorously assess their performance in real-world scenarios to ensure they meet user
037   expectations and function effectively.
038   Traditionally, the evaluation of conversational agents has relied on qualitative assessments of their
039   responses. This process typically involves human evaluators or LLMs judging the quality of an agent’s
040   utterances (Adiwardana et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Roller et al., 2021; Shuster et al., 2022; Lee
041   et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024) or comparing responses between different agents on platforms like
042   Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024). While these methods provide valuable insights into aspects such
043   as naturalness and alignment with user instructions, they do not fully capture the complexities of
044
      real-world interactions.
045   In real-world dialogues, conversational agents encounter a range of challenges: engaging in real-
046   time interactions, managing conversations with multiple participants, and recalling information
047   from past dialogues. Therefore, a more comprehensive evaluation method is needed—one that
048   accurately assesses an agent’s ability to respond within a reasonable time limit, thoroughly understand
049   multi-party dialogue contexts, and reason across previous interactions, reflecting the complexities of
050
      real-world conversations.
051   To address this, we propose DialSim, a dialogue simulator for real-time evaluation of a conversa-
052   tional agent’s long-term multi-party dialogue understanding. As illustrated in Figure 1, DialSim
053   places an agent in the role of the main character of a TV show, engaging it in an extensive conversation
      based on the show’s scripted content. During this conversation, the agent is evaluated for its ability to

      DialSim simulates three different dialogue environments based on scripts from popular TV shows
078
      (i.e., Friends, The Big Bang Theory, and The Office), and it has the following four main features.
079
080   • Time-Constrained Evaluation: For a conversational agent to function effectively in real-time,
081     the agent must be capable of updating its memory and generating responses on the fly. To evaluate
082     this, DialSim measures the accuracy of responses within a predefined time limit. To the best of
083     our knowledge, this is the first work that evaluates the performance of a conversational agent in a
084     time-constrained environment.
085   • Extensive Long-Term Multi-Party Dialogue: DialSim simulates multi-party dialogues aver-
086     aging 350k tokens in length, making it the longest dataset among existing long-term dialogue
087     datasets. Throughout the simulation, the agent encounters complex questions that require compre-
088     hension and reasoning across several multi-party sessions, ensuring a thorough assessment of its
089     long-term dialogue understanding capabilities.
090   • Diverse and High-Quality Question-Answer Dataset: We generated an average of 1,000 unique
091     questions per session using two methods. First, we collected and refined questions from a fan
092     quiz website that covers key events in each TV show. Second, we extracted temporal knowledge
093     graphs for each session to formulate complex, multi-hop questions based on these graphs. We
094     employed ChatGPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023b) to refine the fan quizzes and extract the knowledge graphs,
095     ensuring high quality through manual review by the authors.
096   • Randomized Questioning: DialSim features an average of 1,300 conversation sessions oc-
097     curring over a period of five years, as depicted in the corresponding TV show. For each session,
098     a randomly selected character asks a question that is randomly sampled from an average of
099     1,000 candidates, at a random time. This setup allows us to test whether an agent can respond
100
        appropriately in a challenging and unpredictable environment, suitable for rigorous evaluation
        of conversational agents. Additionally, since a random set of questions is given for each test,
101
        repeating the tests multiple times allows us to measure the agent’s performance variability and
102
        reliability.

      Datasets Based on the Scripts of TV Shows While both TV show scripts and other dialogue datasets

131   and accessibility. This makes them particularly valuable for various dialogue understanding tasks such
132   as question answering (QA) (Yang & Choi, 2019; Sang et al., 2022), coreference resolution (Chen
133   & Choi, 2016; Chen et al., 2017; Zhou & Choi, 2018), relation extraction (Rashid & Blanco, 2018;

135   et al., 2022). Notable datasets derived from scripts include FriendsQA (Yang & Choi, 2019) and
136   TVShowGuess (Sang et al., 2022). FriendsQA treats each scene in every episode as an independent
137   conversation. Each question inquires about information related to the conversation, and the task is
138
      to find the corresponding answer spans. TVShowGuess is a multiple-choice QA dataset consisting
      of five different popular TV shows. The task is to identify anonymized speakers in a scene using
139
      information from all previous scenes. While many studies have utilized TV show scripts to create
140
      such datasets, only DialSim includes unanswerable questions, conducts real-time evaluations, and
141   fully utilizes the extended context of scripts to assess conversational agents.

188   3.1.2    S IMULATOR
189
      Algorithm 1 outlines the simulation process of DialSim, designed to emulate a real-time conver-
190
      sation. In this simulator, each participant’s utterance (including the agent’s) occurs at a predefined
191   time interval (same as time limit), and the agent should update its memory2 within this interval. If
192   updating the memory is not completed within the interval, the simulator will move on to the next
193   utterance (Line 13). During the simulation, other characters ask questions to the agent (Line 8-10),
194   except in sessions where the agent is the only one talking (Line 5-6). The timing to ask a question is
195   chosen randomly within the session (Line 8), and the speaker who asks the question is also chosen
196   randomly. However, to make the simulation realistic, it is crucial to ensure that the chosen speaker is
197   still present and hasn’t left the session. We achieved this by randomly choosing from characters who
198   were present within three turns of the agent’s last utterance (Line 9). Then, a question is randomly
199
      selected and asked in the style of the corresponding speaker (Line 10). The agent then must respond
      to the question using its memory, all within the time limit (Line 15). The prompt for the response is
200
      created by combining the question with the dialogue history stored in the memory. If the response is
201
      not completed within the time limit, it will be considered a failure, and the simulator will move on to
202   the next utterance. The prompt we used is provided in Appendix A.

      3.2.1      S CRIPT P REPROCESSING
238
239   The script we used includes 5 consecutive seasons per TV show, with each season containing
240   approximately 20 episodes. Each episode is composed of multiple scenes (i.e., session). Each script
241   includes not only utterances but also descriptions of characters’ actions and scenes, as well as metadata
242   unrelated to the plot (e.g., names of writers and directors). We manually filtered out all irrelevant
243   parts to create Scriptpre , which contains only the conversations between characters. Additionally,
244
      since some of our questions involve time conditions (e.g., “Which friend wasn’t allowed to drive
      Monica’s Porsche in October 1994?”), we manually assigned a date to each scene in Scriptpre to
245
      provide time information to the agent. These dates were determined based on the contents of the
246
      conversations and the air dates of the episodes. The specific rules for date assignments are detailed in
247   Appendix B. We then selected scenes involving the main character (i.e., Ross, Sheldon, and Michael)
248   from Scriptpre and sequentially numbered them as sessions Si . This process resulted in the final
249   dialogue D = {S1 , S2 , ..., SN }.

      3.2.3           Q UESTION G ENERATION BASED ON A T EMPORAL K NOWLEDGE G RAPH
298
299   Fan quizzes are useful for generating our questions, but since they are episode-specific and user-
300   generated, the questions don’t span multiple episodes and their numbers are limited (∼1K). To
301   address this, we constructed a knowledge graph for each session and used it to generate questions.
302   Initially, we used ChatGPT-4 to extract triples (i.e., [head, relation, tail]) from each session Si in D.
303   These triples were then refined by the authors. We employed 32 relations (e.g., girlfriend) derived
304   from DialogRE (Yu et al., 2020), a high-quality dataset where human annotators manually extracted
305   relations from Friends scripts, classifying relationships between characters into 37 categories. We
306
      adapted and modified these relations for our purpose. More details about the relations are provided
      in Appendix D.1. Finally, we combined the triples from each session with their respective dates to
307
      create a temporal knowledge graph (TKG) composed of quadruples (i.e., [head, relation, tail, date]).
308
309   Using the constructed TKG, we created questions that the main character could either answer or not
310   for each session. We generated these questions by extracting one (i.e., one-hop) or two (i.e., two-hop)
311   quadruples from the TKG. The form and answer of the question may change depending on the time
312
      it is asked, even if the same quadruple is used. For instance, if we select [Rachel, boyfriend, Ross,
      1994-08-08] and ask the question in 1996, it would be: “Who was Rachel’s boyfriend on August
313
      8th, 1994?” If asked on August 8th, 1994, the question would be: “Who is Rachel’s boyfriend?” In
314
      both cases, the answer is Ross. Conversely, if we inquire about Rachel’s boyfriend in 1992, when no
315   information is available, the correct answer would be: “I don’t know.” In this manner, we manually
316   verified the answer of each question. We applied the same principle to create more complex two-hop
317   questions (e.g., “Rachel had a roommate on August 8th, 1994. Who is the boyfriend of the roommate
318   now?”). The overall process of generating questions using TKG is illustrated in Figure 3. Examples
319   of question templates and corresponding questions we created can be found in Appendix D.2.

370

371

      4.3      R ESULTS
414
415   Table 3 shows that API-based models outperformed open-source models due to their superior inference
416   capabilities and faster response times. However, the performances of all baselines were below 50%,
417   suggesting that current LLMs function poorly as conversational agents for multi-party long-term
418   complex dialogues. The experimental results for Friends, The Big Bang Theory, and The Office
419   exhibited similar trends. The detailed results are described in Appendix G.
420   Time Limit We conducted additional experiments without time limit and reported the results in
421   Table 4. Under time limit, there were often no significant differences in performances based on model
422   size when comparing the same type of LLM, and sometimes smaller models outperformed larger ones.
423   However, in the absence of time limit, larger models typically exhibited better performances than
424   their smaller counterparts. Additionally, larger open-source models have shown remarkable inference
425   capabilities, often on par with the performance of API-based models. This is because larger models
426   often exceed the time limit due to their slower inference speed. Therefore, for a conversational agent
427
      to engage effectively in real-time conversations, it is crucial to select a model size that balances both
      inference time and reasoning capability. The performances according to different time limits are
428
      reported in Appendix H.

      Storing History Storing the entire session consistently exhibits superior performance compared
433   to other history storing methods, because individual utterances lack adequate context, and crucial
434   information may be lost during summarization. However, Llama3.1-70B achieved the best perfor-
435   mance when using Session Sum. as a history saving method, owing to its strong summarization
436   capabilities. Additionally, contrary to our expectations, Mixtral’s Base LLM (i.e., without history
437   retrieval) outperforms some retrieval-based models in settings with unlimited time. This is due to
438   Mixtral’s context length of 32k tokens, which is long enough to accommodate half a season of the
439   script, allowing it to utilize a longer dialogue history than some of the other baselines. However, in a
440   setting with a time limit, Mixtral’s performance significantly drops due to its long inference time.
441
      Therefore, for a conversational agent to converse in real-time, it is necessary to select a reasonably
      appropriate length of dialogue history.
442
443   Oracle Setting To establish a performance upper bound, we conducted experiments in an oracle
444   setting, where the agents were provided with the evidence sessions and their dates (see Figure 2). In
445   this scenario, Llama3.1-70B achieved the best performance of 69.86% in a setting with unlimited
446   time, highlighting the challenging nature of our task. This result surpasses the best RAG-based
447
      method, OpenAI Embedding-Session Sum. setting, by 21.37%. This notable gap underscores the
      necessity for advanced techniques in storing and retrieving history for agents engaged in long-term
448
      dialogues. Detailed experimental results are provided in Appendix I.
449
450   Error Analysis by Question Type We conducted an error analysis by question type on ChatGPT-
451   4o-mini, based on BM25-Session Entire, which showed the highest performance setting without a
452   time limit. First, comparing the performance of fan quiz-based questions and TKG-based questions,
453
      the results were 58.80% and 46.42% respectively, indicating the greater difficulty of TKG-based
      questions. Additionally, within TKG-based questions, one-hop questions had a performance of
454
      66.67%, whereas two-hop questions had a performance of 13.53%, highlighting the challenge of
455
      two-hop questions. Furthermore, even in the oracle setting, while the performance of one-hop
456   questions increased to 84.05%, two-hop questions remained at 28.45%. This suggests that two-hop
457   questions are challenging not only in terms of history retrieval but also in reasoning across the given
458   sessions.
459
      Adversarial Test LLM-based agents are likely to have prior knowledge about these TV shows from
460
      the pre-training process (see Appendix K). Since such agents can provide answers without referring
461
      to the actual dialogue history, it is crucial to ensure that the agent relies strictly on the history for
462   its responses. To achieve this, we conducted further experiments for the adversarial test by altering
463   the names of the characters in two ways: by swapping their names with each other (e.g., Joey ↔
464   Monica) or by assigning new names to them (e.g., Joey → John). The results showed a noticeable
465   decrease in overall performance compared to the original setup. Specifically, when we experimented
466   under unlimited time conditions, performance dropped by 5% to 10%. This decline is attributed to
467   the agents relying not only on the dialogue history but also on their pre-trained knowledge when
468   answering questions. Additionally, the performance decrease was more pronounced when names were
469   swapped compared to when new names were assigned. This suggests that new names represent new
470
      information, while mixed names in the dialogue history conflicted with the pre-trained knowledge,
      leading to reduced reasoning ability. The detailed experimental results are provided in Appendix J.

473   5    C ONCLUSION
474
475   In this paper, we introduce DialSim, a simulator for evaluating conversational agents’ long-term
476   dialogue understanding in real-time settings. DialSim utilizes scripts from well-known TV shows
477   and incorporates questions derived from fan quizzes, along with a temporal knowledge graph,
478   for a thorough assessment. Our experimental findings reveal significant limitations in current
479   conversational agents’ abilities to manage complex, multi-party, long-term dialogues effectively.
480   Despite its strengths, our simulator has two main limitations. First, while the questions and answers
481   are logically paired for accurate evaluation, the random selection of questions could introduce a bit
482   of awkwardness during conversations. Second, while we considered incorporating industry-specific
483   dialogues such as chat logs from customer service or retail, where conversational agents could be
484   used for business purposes, these dialogue datasets are usually proprietary and not publicly accessible.
485   In future developments, we will focus on enhancing the natural flow of interactions and creating
      simulators that are applicable to real-world industries.

731   We first extracted elements from the scripts that could indicate dates (e.g., Valentine’s Day, Christmas
732   Eve). Then, we reviewed the scripts again to analyze the relative timing of the sessions. For example,
733   if there is a line mentioning that Chandler broke up with his girlfriend two days ago, we annotated the
734   session where he broke up with his girlfriend as occurring two days prior to the mentioned session.
735
      Next, while watching each episode, we pinpointed sessions where the dates might have changed by
      observing whether the characters’ outfits changed between sessions. Finally, we assigned a specific
736
      date to each session based on the actual broadcast date of the episode, adjusting for the relative
737
      differences in dates and events such as Christmas.

956   experiments with any chosen time constraint, or even without one. When a time limit is set, the
957
      experimental environment can impact performance. Consequently, depending on the environment
      in which the conversational agent is deployed, this could serve as a criterion for selecting the agent
958
      with relatively better performance. It is important to note that the primary objective of DialSim
959
      is not to evaluate the inference speed of LLMs, but rather to assess the end-to-end performance of
960   conversational agents, where techniques like model sharding and tensor parallelism can be a part of
961   the conversational agent to decrease the response latency if needed.
962
963
      To control the environmental factors that could affect time, we conducted all experiments under the
      same conditions as described in Appendix F.1.1. The rationale for setting a 6-second time limit in
964
      our experiments is detailed in Appendix F.1.2, and an analysis of the Internet speed for API-based
965
      models can be found in Appendix F.1.3.
966

      F.1.1     E NVIRONMENT C ONTROL
968
969   Our simulator operates in real-time, requiring precise control of the experimental environment.
970   Therefore, we conducted all experiments using the same hardware: NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs and
971   an AMD EPYC 7702 64-Core Processor. To maintain consistent CPU performance, we allocated 10
      cores for each experiment and ensured that no other processes were running simultaneously.

       F.1.2    AVERAGE T IME I NTERVAL B ETWEEN U TTERANCES
973
974    Each episode includes around 240 utterances and lasts about 18 minutes without commercial breaks.
975    This means each utterance should occur roughly every 4.5 seconds. However, because the experiments
976    used the A6000, which is slower than the latest hardware like the A100 or H100, we extended the
977    interval to 6 seconds.

979    F.1.3    I NTERNET S PEED
980    The performance of API-based models can be affected by internet speed. To analyze this, we
981    conducted a comparative analysis of the response times between API-based models and open-source
982    models. In our analysis of agents using OpenAI Embedding-Session Sum., we found that the API-
983    based agents achieved average response times of 1.50 seconds for ChatGPT-4o-mini, 1.73 seconds
984    for ChatGPT-3.5 and 2.69 seconds for Gemini 1.0 pro. In comparison, agents using open-source
985    models showed average response times ranging from 2.06 seconds (Gemma 2B) to 7.15 seconds
986    (Tulu2 70B). These results suggest that, even when accounting for both internet communication and
987    model inference, remote API-based models are generally faster than open-source alternatives. This
988
       indicates that internet latency has a minimal impact on our evaluation.
989
990    F.2     Q UESTION F ORMAT
991
       DialSim is a dataset that includes pairs of questions, answers, and choices. The questions are
992
       available in three formats: template-based multiple-choice, natural language multiple-choice, and
993    open-ended. Users can choose any of these formats to evaluate the agent’s performance.
994
995    First, we provide multiple-choice questions in both template and natural language formats. For
996
       example, a template-based question might be, “Who was going out with Paul in September 1994?”
       with choices “(A) Emily, (B) Monica, (C) Ryan, (D) Rachel, (E) I don’t know”. In contrast, the same
997
       question in natural language format could be phrased as, “Who was going out with Paul in September
998
       1994? Was it Emily, Monica, Ryan, Rachel, or do you not know?”
999
1000   Additionally, we offer the option to ask questions in an open-ended format (e.g., “Who was going
1001   out with Paul in September 1994?”) without providing answer choices. This approach allows us to
1002
       evaluate the agent’s ability to generate open-ended responses. The open-ended format is particularly
       useful for fan quiz-based questions, where some answers may require longer responses (e.g., Question:
1003
       “Why did Monica and Chandler say they were late getting to the hospital?” Correct answer: “Monica
1004
       went back for her jacket”).
1005
1006   For natural language multiple-choice and open-ended questions, a response is considered correct if it
1007   exactly matches the correct answer. If the response does not match exactly, the score is determined by
1008
       comparing the response with the correct answer using a different language model (i.e., GPT-4o mini).
