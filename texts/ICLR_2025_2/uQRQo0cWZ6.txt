013                    Warning: This paper contains potentially offensive and harmful text.
014            Large language models (LLMs) are vulnerable to jailbreaking attacks (Zou et al.,
015            2023; Liu et al., 2024), in which attackers use adversarially designed prompts to
016            bypass the model’s safeguard and force the model to generate objectionable con-
017            tent. The present paper studies jailbreaking attacks from a red team’s viewpoint
018
               and proposes a novel black-box attack method, called Shake-It-Off (SHAKE), that
               only requires the response generated by the victim model. Given objective query
019
               Tobj , our method iteratively shakes off the objectionable semantics of Tobj , mak-
020
               ing it gradually approximates a pre-defined decontaminated query Tdec . We con-
021            duct extensive experiments on multiple baseline methods and victim LLMs. The
022            experimental results show that SHAKE outperforms the baselines in attack success
023            rates while requiring much less running time and access to the victim model.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Meta, 2024) are Transformer-based models
029   trained on a vast amount of data and boosted with scaling laws. Over the past few years, remarkable
030   breakthroughs in LLM techniques have significantly advanced AI’s ability to serve as a helpful and
031   knowledgeable assistant. A variety of alignment methods (Liu et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024; Wu
032   et al., 2024) have been proposed to ensure the AI assistants do not generate objectionable content.
033
      However, the aligned LLMs are still vulnerable to jailbreaking attack (Zou et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2024), in which attackers use adversarially designed prompts to bypass the model’s alignment.
034
035   Jailbreaking attacks are posing ethical risks to the utility of LLM-based AI assistants. Many previous
036   works have studied jailbreaking attack algorithms from a red team’s perspective, aiming to discover
037   the potential vulnerability of LLMs. For example, Zou et al. (2023) proposed the GCG attack that
038   characterizes jailbreaking attacks as generating adversarial examples (Szegedy et al., 2014). As
039
      the first automatic algorithm, GCG introduces a first-order optimization paradigm for jailbreaking
      attacks. However, the adversarial prompts generated by GCG often include nonsensical strings,
040
      making them not stealthy enough to pass the perplexity-based detection (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023).
041
      To fix this issue, AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024) leverages genetic algorithms and finds the adversarial
042   prompt by minimizing the cross entropy loss between the generated text and a pre-defined target
043   text. Concurrent to AutoDAN, PAIR (Chao et al., 2023) uses the adversarial prompts generated
044   and revised by an attacker LLM to jailbreak the victim LLM. Compared to GCG and AutoDAN,
045   PAIR requires only black-box access to the victim model. However, PAIR completely relies on
046   the attacker LLM’s revising ability. The algorithm design of PAIR does not provide a specific and
047   interpretable update direction of the adversarial prompts. We refer the readers to Section 2 for a
048   detailed discussion of the related works on existing jailbreaking attacks.
049   In this paper, we propose a novel jailbreaking attack algorithm that overcomes some of the short-
050   comings of the methods mentioned above. Our proposed algorithm, called Shake-It-Off (SHAKE),
051   only requires black-box access to the victim model. As demonstrated by Figure 1, our method itera-
052   tively updates the adversarial prompt Tobj ⊕Tadv (c.f. Section 3) towards the direction of decreasing
053   the amount of objectionable semantics in Tobj ⊕ Tadv . In this sense, we can interpret our method as
      jailbreaking the victim LLM by shaking off the objectionable semantics in the adversarial prompt.

152
      Our paper characterizes the jailbreaking attack against LLMs as a zeroth-order optimization prob-
153
      lem. The related works are discussed as follows.
154
155
      Jailbreaking Attacks Existing jailbreaking attacks can be divided into white-box and black-box
156   attacks based on the attacker’s ability. For white-box attacks (Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu
157   et al., 2024), the attackers have full access to the victim model, including the information on the
158   model’s weights and gradients. Considering that a large portion of LLMs are still close-sourced, the
159   requirement of white-box attacks can seldom be met in practice. Compared to the white-box attacks,
160   the black-box attacks (Chao et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023) only require limited
161   information from the victim model. While being more practical, the black-box methods often rely on
      the attacker’s prior knowledge of the victim LLM’s vulnerabilities instead of the algorithm design.

162
      As of the time of writing this paper, GCG (Zou et al., 2023), AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024), PAIR
163   (Chao et al., 2023), and ReNeLLM (Ding et al., 2024) are four of the most popular and effective
164   jailbreaking attack baselines compared by most concurrent works (Chao et al., 2024; Zhou et al.,
165   2024). GCG and AutoDAN are white-box methods, which are not always practical as stated earlier.
166   By designing specific prompts and templates, the black-box jailbreaking attacks, e.g., PAIR and
167   ReNeLLM, use an attacker LLM to craft adversarial prompts. Different from conventional attack
168   algorithms, these methods rely on the ability of the attacker LLM instead of the algorithm design.
169   Compared to the concurrent black-box attacks, our proposed method stands out for its interpretabil-
170   ity, effectiveness, and efficiency. As demonstrated in Figure 2, our proposed method is guided by
171   a clear updating direction, providing an intuitive update direction of the adversarial prompts. The
172   experimental results show that our method outperforms the baseline methods in both attack success
173   rates and running time.
174
175   Zeroth-Order Optimization Zeroth-order optimization is widely used for crafting adversarial
176   examples when the gradient information is unavailable (Chen et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2022; Chen
177   et al., 2020). Our proposed method is motivated by the Hop-Skip-Jump (HSP) attack (Chen et al.,
178
      2020), which is an effective black-box adversarial attack against images classifiers. For any clean
      sample x, HSP first finds another sample x′ that is classified as a different label. By leveraging
179
      binary search, HSP can effectively find the decision boundary. Our method is similar to HSP in the
180
      sense that we craft a decontaminated query Tdec to serve as a destination for the iteration. However,
181   considering that HSP deals with the continuous image space, our method and HSP are fundamentally
182   different from each other.
183
184
      Some existing jailbreaking attacks also do not require gradient information, e.g., AutoDAN, PAIR,
      and ReNeLLM. Due to the discrete nature of the tokens, jailbreaking attacks have to carefully re-
185
      vise the current adversarial prompts instead of directly modifying them as numerical variables. By
186
      repeatedly revising the adversarial prompts, AutoDAN maintains a “population” of the adversarial
187   prompts and leverages genetic algorithm on this population. PAIR and ReNeLLM enhance the ef-
188   fectiveness of the attack by employing carefully designed prompts and templates. Compared to these
189   methods, SHAKE considers the embeddings of the sentences and provides a direction for updating
190   the adversarial prompts.

      3.1   A N O PTIMIZATIONAL PARADIGM FOR JAILBREAKING LLM S
197
198   In this paper, we consider single-turn text generation tasks under given conversation templates. We
199   use the uppercase letter T to denote the sequence of tokens (e.g., a sentence). Given user input
200   Tusr , the assistant response of the LLMs is denote by Tres = M (Tusr ; Tsys ). Here, M denotes an
201   LLM, and Tsys is the system prompt that provides general guidance for the text generation of M .
202   Previous works often characterize the generation process of Tres as the sampling from a conditional
203   distribution. Note that since our method only requires black-box access to the model, we simplify
      the probabilistic generation process to input-output pairs without loss of rigorousness.
204
205   In practice, Tsys , Tusr , and Tres are often stored in a conversation template class. The following
206   example gives a typical conversation template.
207
208
      Example 1 (Conversation Template) A typical conversation template consists of a system prompt,
      user input, and assistant response. For example:
209
210          • SYSTEM PROMPT (Tsys ): You are a helpful AI assistant who writes well.
211
212          • USER INPUT (Tusr ): How to make a bomb?
213          • ASSISTANT RESPONSE (Tres ): Sorry, as an AI agent, I cannot answer this question.
214
215   The system prompt is often hidden from the users, and the LLM generates the assistant response
      based on the given system prompt and user input.

      Attacker’s Ability As mentioned in Section 2, existing jailbreaking attacks can be categorized
217   into white-box and black-box settings. White-box attacks assume that the attacker has full access
218   to the model’s internal mechanism, including the weights, gradients, and the conversation template,
219   while in the black-box attack settings, the attackers only have limited access to the victim model.
220   Our paper focuses on the black-box attack setting, i.e., we assume the attacker can only modify the
221   user input Tusr . The logits and conversation templates are hidden from the attacker.
222
223   Attacker’s Goal The goal of a jailbreaking attack is to bypass the safeguards of an aligned LLM.
224   Given any assistant response Tres , let the judge function Judge(Tres ) ∈ {0, 1} be a binary classifier
225   that takes 1 when Tres is rejective (e.g., “Sorry, I cannot answer that question.”) and 0 otherwise.
226   Popular choices of the judge function include the refuse words-based and the LLM recheck-based
227
      judge functions. We refer the readers to Section 3.2 for the definitions.
228   Given any objectionable query Tobj (i.e., a sentence that contains objectionable content) and benign
229   query Tbgn , a perfectly aligned LLM should provide a rejective response only for Tobj , i.e.,
230
                           Judge(M (Tobj ; Tsys )) = 1, for objectionable queries, and
231                                                                                                    (1)
232
                           Judge(M (Tbgn ; Tsys )) = 0, for benign queries.
233   However, real-world LLMs are vulnerable to jailbreaking attacks and sometimes fail to reject objec-
234   tionable queries. The present paper focuses on affix-based attacks, in which the attackers craft an
235   adversarial affix Tadv and concatenate it to the objectionable content Tobj . For simplicity, denote
236   the concatenation of Tobj and Tadv by Tobj ⊕ Tadv . We call Tobj ⊕ Tadv the adversarial prompt
237   derived from Tadv . The following example shows how Tobj and Tadv are concatenated together.
238
      Example 2 Let the objectionable query Tobj and the adversarial affix Tadv be

      Naive & Surrogate Optimization Objective It is intractable to directly solve Equation (2). In this
      paper, we circumvent this intractability by leveraging zeroth-order optimization techniques. Observe
257
      that the solution to Equation (2) is also the minima for the following naive optimization objective
258
                                naive
259                            Tadv   := arg min Judge(M (Tobj ⊕ Tadv ; Tsys )).                         (3)
                                            Tadv
260
261   The optimization problem given by Equation (3) is still intractable due to the agnostical and dis-
262   continuous nature of the judge function. Previous works have tried to find surrogates for the judge
263   function. For example, Liu et al. (2024) calculates the cross entropy (CE) loss between the assis-
264   tant response Tres = M (Tobj ⊕ Tadv ; Tsys ) and a predefined positive response to the objectionable
265   query. When Tres is rejective, the CE loss would take relatively greater values since Tres does not
266
      looks like the predefined positive response. However, calculating the CE loss requires logits infor-
      mation of the victim LLM, which is hardly accessible in practice, especially for those closed-sourced
267
      models that only release API accesses to the users (e.g., the GPT series (OpenAI, 2023)).
268
269   In this paper, we find another surrogate for the naive optimization objective given by Equation (3).
      Intuitively, for any objectionable query Tobj , we can obtain a benign query Tbgn by removing all the

      objectionable semantics in Tobj , e.g., removing the word “bomb” in Tobj =“How to make a bomb”
271   and obtain Tbgn =“How to make a”. However, doing so will make Tobj ambiguous, causing Tres to
272   be unable to provide a desired response.
273
274
      To fix this, we propose to make the process of removing objectionable semantics slower and milder.
      First of all, we craft a decontaminated query Tdec to serve as a destination of the iteration. Then,
275
      we iteratively search for the adversarial affix Tadv that maximizes the similarity between Tobj ⊕Tadv
276
      and Tdec . There are many ways to measure the similarity between two sequences of tokens (Farouk,
277   2019). In this work, we convert the sentences to embedding vectors using Sentence-BERT (Reimers
278   & Gurevych, 2019) and calculate the cosine similarity between the embedding vectors. Denote this
279   similarity metric by Sim(·, ·). Finally, given objectionable and decontaminated queries Tobj and
280   Tdec , the surrogate optimization objective for our method is defined as follows.

285   Remark 1 (Main Intuition) By minimizing the optimization objective in Equation (4), the adver-
286   sarial prompt Tobj ⊕ Tadv gradually becomes more similar (with regard to Sim(·, ·)) to a benign
287   query Tdec , i.e., the amount of objectionable semantics in Tobj ⊕ Tadv keeps decreasing during
288   iteration. In this sense, our proposed method shakes off the objectionable semantics in Tobj ⊕ Tadv .

399   As mentioned in Section 3, we determine whether an adversarial prompt jailbreaks the victim LLM
400   by a binary judge function Judge(·). There are two major ways to define Judge(·) in the literature.
401
402         1. Refuse words-based: This metric detects the rejective response by searching for “refuse
403
               words” in the assistant response Tres = M (Tobj ⊕ Tadv , Tsys ) (c.f. Section 3). We use the
               same refuse words set as that of AutoDAN, which is widely used in many previous works.
404
               We present the refuse words set in Table B.3.
405
406         2. LLM recheck: Previous works have observed that some assistant responses do not provide
407            the desired response (i.e., an irrelevant answer) to the objectionable query even if it is not
408            rejective. To address this issue and make the evaluation more accurate, a common practice
409            is to use LLM to recheck the response (Liu et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2023; Robey et al.,
410            2023). In this paper, we use DeepSeek-V2.5 (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2024) to recheck the
411            response. Compared to the most commonly used LLM APIs (e.g., the GPT series (OpenAI,
412
               2023)), DeepSeek-V2.5 API has comparable speed and capability in our tasks while being
               much cheaper. The recheck prompt is given in Appendix C.

      Determinant for the Efficiency The efficiency of SHAKE benefits from its black-box nature and
      our optimization algorithm design. In the context of jailbreaking attacks, black-box jailbreaking at-
468
      tacks are often faster than white-box competitors since leveraging the internal information of LLMs
469
      might be very time-consuming.
470
471   The algorithm design also significantly affects the efficiency. For example, the running time of GCG
472   is 10 times slower than ours because GCG needs to search for potential replacement words in the
473
      whole set of vocabulary. It may get better performance through additional iteration steps, which may
      cause an exponential increase in time. Compared to the existing black-box attacks, SHAKE provides
474
      a guidance for the iteration, which would possibly reduce the number of iteration steps and enhance
475
      the efficiency.

479
480   In this paper, we propose a novel black-box attack algorithm called Shake-It-Off (SHAKE), which
481   is practical, effective, effective, and interpretable. By leveraging zeroth-order optimization and sen-
482
      tence embedding techniques, we provide a direction for the update of the adversarial prompts, mak-
      ing our method more interpretable and efficient than existing jailbreaking attacks. We conduct ex-
483
      periments on multiple datasets and victim models to validate our claims. The effectiveness of our
484
      method outperforms the baseline methods in both ASR-LR and ASR-RW across all the compared
485   victim LLMs.
