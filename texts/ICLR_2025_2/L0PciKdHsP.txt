009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                The goal of this paper is to improve (upcycle) an existing large language model
012                without the prohibitive requirements of continued pre-training of the full-model.
013                The idea is to split the pre-training data into semantically relevant groups and train
014                an expert on each subset. An expert takes the form of a lightweight adapter added
015                on the top of a frozen base model. During inference, an incoming query is first
016                routed to the most relevant expert which is then loaded onto the base model for the
017                forward pass. Unlike typical Mixture of Experts (MoE) models, the experts in our
018
                   method do not work with other experts for a single query. Hence, we dub them “in-
                   trovert” experts. Freezing the base model and keeping the experts as lightweight
019
                   adapters allows extreme parallelism during training and inference. Training of
020
                   all experts can be done in parallel without any communication channels between
021                them. Similarly, the inference can also be heavily parallelized by distributing
022                experts on different GPUs and routing each request to the GPU containing its rel-
023                evant expert. We implement a proof-of-concept version of this method and show
024                the validity of our approach.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As language models continue to grow in size, it is important to consider whether all parameters are
030   necessary for every request. For instance, the same parameters might not be ideal for answering
031   questions about SQL compared to those about car seats. Approaches like fine-tuning, early exit,
032   and mixture of experts (MoE) have been proposed to address this issue, each with its own trade-
033   offs. Fine-tuning requires meticulous data curation and optimization, while early exit and MoE add
034
      complexity to the model’s forward pass, posing engineering challenges. Most relevant to our work
      is the Mixture of Experts (MoE) approach, popularized by the Mixtral 8x7B model (Jiang et al.,
035
      2024). Existing experts operate at the token level and not sequence level, thus lacking specialization
036
      in specific domains 1 (Jiang et al., 2024). Additionally, since routing varies by token, all experts
037   must reside in GPU memory during both training and inference, making it difficult to scale to a large
038   number of experts. To address these limitations, we focus on the concept of query-level or sequence-
039   level experts, where each query is routed to a single expert. This design minimizes communication
040   between experts, which can be distributed across different GPU nodes. We term these “introvert”
041   experts due to their reduced interaction with one another.
042
      Implementing sequence-level experts offers several practical advantages. It creates a modular ar-
043   chitecture where a single generalist base model is complemented by multiple small experts that
044   specialize in niche topics. While fine-tuning shares a similar motivation, the number of experts
045   is often constrained by the need for curated datasets. In contrast, because the experts operate on
046   independent domains, they can be trained in parallel, enhancing flexibility in resource utilization.
047   Moreover, these experts can be retrained as data evolves, allowing for the easy integration of new
048   experts as fresh data becomes available. This capability positions LLMs as continual learners with
049   minimal risk of forgetting prior knowledge.
050   We present an intuitive and straightforward proof-of-concept for creating such experts. Given the
051   high cost of pre-training, we focus on model upcycling, transforming an existing dense model into
052   a variant of mixture of experts by introducing new parameters and additional training. We begin

      with a partially trained dense model, freeze its parameters, and train new experts on top of it. We
055   believe any LLM adapter module can serve as an expert; however, we utilize the popular LoRA
056   adapter (Hu et al., 2022) in our experiments. To train an expert, we cluster pre-training data points

059   billion tokens and experiment with 500 and 5, 000 expert LoRA adapters.
060
      The main advantage of our method is that the training time resource requirements are greatly reduced
061   compared to dense model training. The resource savings happen on two fronts: First, GPU memory
062   needed to train each expert is reduced since the base model is frozen and only LoRA weights are
063   trained (≈ 10% of the model weights). Second, there’s no need to have a fast network channel
064   between two expert training runs since the experts are independent of each other. In extreme, all
065   experts can be trained in the time it takes to train the expert with the largest training data subset.
066   Then, we can use many affordable GPUs instead of a few expensive ones. It is also possible to
067   train the experts on GPUs of different architectures and speeds based on availability and price. Full-
068   model training speed is dictated by the speed of the slowest GPU and is thus less efficient than our
069
      approach.
070   Given the large number of adapters generated by our method, efficiently serving them can be a prob-
071   lem. However, there are open source libraries like LoRAX 2 based on Punica kernels (Chen et al.,
072   2024) that reduce the amortized cost of running multiple adapters in a single batch. A downside
073   of our approach is the linear increase in the storage memory with increase in the number of top-
074   ics. However, this can be mitigated when the framework is scaled to simultaneously serve a large
075
      number of users. Further, it is possible to cache the most frequently routed adapters on the GPU
      memory, thereby eliminating the latency of moving them from CPU memory to GPU memory. It is
076
      also worth noting that the bandwidth between CPU memory and GPU memory is improving each
077
      day with newer GH200 chips supporting speeds of up to 900 GB/s 3 . This makes the CPU memory
078   to GPU memory latency hit significantly less.
079
080   To summarize, our contributions are as follows. First, we propose framework called mixture of
081
      introverts for efficiently training and serving sequence level experts. Second, as a proof-of-concept,
      we apply our idea to the task of upcycling an LLM and show that our model is comparable in
082
      perplexity to a baseline model that is trained on 500B more tokens than our method.

087   Mixture of Experts. Instead of activating all parameters for all inputs, conditional computing aims
088   to activate only a subset of the parameters for each input (Jacobs et al., 1991; Bengio et al., 2013;
089   Bengio, 2013). The goal is to increase the capacity of a model without increasing the compute of the
090
      model (Cho & Bengio, 2014). However, the idea didn’t become widely known until the introduction
      of Mixture-of-Experts layer (Shazeer et al., 2017). This layer was used in training the Mixtral 8x7B
091
      model (Jiang et al., 2024) that further popularized this approach. Other variations of the MoE layer
092
      include (Lample et al., 2019; He, 2024; Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024). However, since
093   the MoE creates experts in the MLP block of the transformer architecture, experts are created token-
094   wise. When the model size increases and all experts cannot reside on a single GPU, complicated
095   multi-GPU setups involving scatter and gather operations are needed at each MoE layer. Hence,
096   this paper explores sequence-wise conditional computation / experts. This has the advantage of only
097   needing to load weights relevant to a given query in the GPU while rest of the weights can reside in
098   CPU RAM or even hard disk, significantly simplifying the architecture design and implementation.
099   The goal of this paper is to create topic-wise experts. Conversely, it is possible to identify parts of a
100   dense model that only activate for certain topics. Essentially, discover instead of train experts. This
101   was studied in (Dai et al., 2021).

      3.1   T OPIC MODELING
163
164   In order to train the topic-wise experts, we need a dataset with topic annotations. For broad coverage
165   of topics, we focus on using large-scale datasets typically used in LLM pre-training. Since these are
166   usually web-scale text datasets without any topic annotations, we need to perform topic modeling
167   before training the expert models.
168   Here, we consider two approaches for topic modeling. In both approaches, all documents in the
169   training dataset are converted to embeddings using a pretrained sentence embedding model. In the
170   first method, we employ UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) to reduce the dimensionality of the document
171   embedding and then perform clustering in the lower dimension space using HDBSCAN (McInnes
172   et al., 2017). Topic-wise embeddings are then obtained by analysing the document belonging to
173   each cluster. The topic embeddings are needed to route the queries to corresponding topics during
174   inference. We employ this topic modeling approach in our model termed MoIN-500 where we split
175   the dataset into 500 topics. We find that it is difficult to scale this approach to thousands of topics
176
      with limited resources. Thus, we consider a simpler alternative. In the second approach, we perform
      a simple K-means clustering directly in the document embedding space to generate cluster index for
177
      each training document and ‘k’ cluster centers as topic embeddings.
178
179
      3.2   T RAINING OF TOPIC - WISE EXPERT MODELS
180
181   Given a set of topics, we train an expert model for each topic atop our base pretrained model.
182   Since the number of models scales linearly with the number of topics, we need the number of train-
183   able parameters in the expert models to be relatively small compared to the base model. Recently,
184   parameter-efficient approaches (Hu et al., 2022; Koohpayegani et al.; Liu et al., 2024) have been
185   very popular for model fine-tuning. Here, we consider them for the upcycling task. Specifically, we
186   use LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) architecture for the experts. In LoRA, the base model is frozen and a
187   few additional parameters are introduced for each linear layer in the network. The output of a linear
188
      layer is modified to be y = W x + Wb (Wa x) where W ∈ Rk×d is the original weight matrix, x is
      the input vector, d is the dimensionality of input, k is the dimensionality of output, and Wb ∈ Rk×r
189
      and Wa ∈ Rr×d are the trainable parameters. Wb and Wa are designed to be low rank matrices,
190
      i.e., r ≪ min(d, k), thus reducing the number of newly added parameters. For every topic in the
191   training dataset, we train an expert LoRA adapter using the documents assigned to the topic. Unlike
192   typical LoRA fine-tuning on task-specific loss, the LoRA models here are trained using the stan-
193   dard autoreggressive language modeling loss (Radford, 2018) on subsets of the pretraining data. For
194   each expert training run, only the weights of the expert are trained while the base model weights
195   remain frozen. The key advantage over a standard pretraining is that all the experts can be trained
196   independently, allowing for a great flexibility in the resources used for training.
197
198   3.3   M ODEL INFERENCE
199
      Once all experts have been trained, our setup consists of a large number of experts that must be
200
      served efficiently during inference. For each inference request, we need to find a topic-wise expert
201
      that can best serve it, and use the corresponding adapter during the forward pass through the model.
202   We perform this ‘query routing’ in the following two steps: first, we embed the query using the same
203   embedding model used to cluster the training dataset. Second, we perform nearest neighbor search
204   over the topic embeddings using the query embedding. Since this routing step is an overhead specific
205   to our method, the router model should be small enough to keep the inference latency feasible. To
206   achieve this, we use a small document embedding model with just 20M parameters. Given the
207   small size, it may be possible to host it without GPUs or to perform routing entirely on the client
208   side. However, while the small size of the model helps reduce router latency it can also reduce the
209   quality of the topic clustering. Router latency vs. clustering accuracy is an inherent trade-off in this
210
      method. In our current implementation, we choose faster inference over a more complex but better
      topic modeling.

214
215   Implementation details: For topic modeling, we use a small language model
      all-MiniLM-L6-v2 with 20M parameters as the embedding model. We use two variants

      of our method - a large scale experiment termed MoIN-5k with K-means based topic modeling and
217   a smaller scale one termed MoIN-500 with HDBSCAN based modeling. In K-Means clustering to
218   generate the topics, the number of clusters k is set to 5000. Since some of the cluster sizes were
219   extremely small, we retained only 4697 of the 5000 clusters and trained a LoRA for each of those
220   4697 topics. In MoIN-500 , the number of clusters is set to 500. Of them, we retain 387 experts and
221   discard the rest since their training data is limited.
222
      For LoRA training, we use the open-sourced TinyLlama (Zhang et al., 2024) model as the base
223   network. TinyLlama uses the same architecture and tokenizer as Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023)
224   but with a smaller parameter count of 1.1B. The model is trained for 3T tokens in total and
225   additional intermediate checkpoints are open-sourced. To facilitate easier comparison with suitable
226   baselines, we use the intermediate checkpoint of TinyLlama trained for two trillion tokens as the
227   base model. The LoRA training follows the recipe from LitGPT (AI, 2023) and uses the official
228   code from TinyLlama 4 . We use a single rank of 128 (token dim d is 2048) for all the LoRAs and
229   optimize them for one epoch on their corresponding topic data subsets. Following LitGPT, we use
230   AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer and set β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.95. The learning
231
      rate is controlled using a cosine scheduler with a maximum lr of 4 × 10−4 and a minimum lr of
      4 × 10−5 . An effective batch size of 576 is used in optimizing all LoRAs. MoIN-5k is trained for
232
      500B tokens in total while MoIN-500 is trained on just 50B tokens. Due to resource constraints, we
233
      limit the experiments to train just two sets of experts. These experiments serve as proof-of-concept
234   of our approach. To perform the LoRA training, we use either Nvidia RTX-6000 or RTX-3090
235   GPUs with 48GB and 24GB memory respectively. This is an advantage of our method compared
236   to other pretraining methods that we can use a mixture of different types of GPUs (whatever is
237   available in our GPU cluster) since the LoRA training jobs are independent of each other.

239
      Datasets: TinyLlama (Zhang et al., 2024) uses SlimPajama (Soboleva et al., 2023) and Star-
240   Coder (Li et al., 2023) datasets for model pretraining. Even though we employ LoRA, our goal
241   is to have a model that generalizes well on any downstream application. Thus we use the pretraining
242   datasets for our LoRA/expert training. We use only the SlimPajama dataset for our LoRA training.
243   It is a deduplicated and cleaned version of RedPajama dataset and contains 627B tokens. Following
244   TinyLlama, we exclude the GitHub subset from the dataset. To enable comparison with TinyLlama
245   models, we use approximately 500B tokens for training and the rest of the dataset for validation.
246   Additionally, we perform a smaller scale experiment with a training set of just 50B tokens sampled
247   randomly from the dataset.
248   Evaluation: We conduct downstream evaluation using the lm-eval-harness Gao et al. (2024)
249   code and use six benchmark datasets for commonsense reasoning. The datasets include Hel-
250   laSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021),
251   OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), AI2 ARC easy and challenge (Clark et al., 2018) and
252   BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019). We perform zero-shot evaluation on all downstream tasks.
253
254   Baselines: We primarily compare with different intermediate training checkpoints of the TinyLlama
255   model. The TinyLlama model trained for 2T tokens (denoted as TinyLlama-2T in tables) is used
256   as the frozen base model on top of which we train the LoRAs. Since we use nearly 500B tokens
257   for training, our main comparison point is the TinyLlama model trained with equivalent number
258   of tokens, that is, the 2.5T checkpoint. We also provide comparison with the final checkpoint of
259   TinyLlama trained for 3T tokens.

      5   F UTURE W ORKS
421
422
      The idea of using adapters as experts during pre-training can unlock many new features. While we
      could only explore this idea in the context of upcycling a model, following are few exciting future
423
      directions worth exploring.
424
425   Dynamic parameter count allocation per topic. Number of training tokens per topic can differ and
426   it may be beneficial to have larger adapters for bigger topics. Hence, LoRA adapters with adaptive
427   rank can be explored to both reduce the total model size and prevent under/overfitting.
428   Adapter augmented generation. Our method shares similarities with retrieval augmented gener-
429   ation (RAG). While RAG retrieves most relevant raw text for a given query, our method retrieves
430   the most relevant adapter. Adapters can be used to memorize and compress the information from
431   several relevant documents. This can make the system more tolerant to errors in retrieval as the
      adapter stores much more information than raw text.


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Continual/Incremental learning. Having adapters specialize in different topics makes LLMs mod-
433   ular. This implies that when the underlying data changes, only the adapter corresponding to the
434   changed data needs to be updated. Our framework allows for easier modification of existing experts
435   and integration of new experts without affecting the performance of the rest of the model.
436
      Quantizing the base model. Since upcycling involves training the model on a significant amount
437
      of data, it should be possible to aggressively quantize the base model similar to QLoRA idea. The
438
      experts can recover the lost information during upcycling.
439
440   Better adapters. Unlike original LoRA adapters which were designed for small-scale fine-tuning,
441   the adapters in our setting ingest much more data. Hence, it is possible to re-visit the adapter archi-
442
      tecture to provide better performance/paramter ratio. For instance, inserting an activation function
      between the two LoRA linear layers may increase its modeling capabilities.
443
444   Creating new experts on the fly. For queries that fall nicely into one of the topics, our simple
445   routing scheme should work well. However, there may be queries on the boundary of multiple
446   clusters or require access to multiple adapters at the same time. This can be addressed with a
447   parameter server that merges existing adapters based on the complexity of the query.
448   Per token adapters. It is efficient to train the adapters independently in parallel, but once trained,
449   we can explore other costly but better inference strategies where we have per token experts instead
450   of per query. Arrow routing may be useful here (Ostapenko et al., 2024). We can use our technique
451   to bootstrap a library of LoRAs.
452   Expert aware pre-training. Since we’re constrained by resources, we could only explore the idea
453   of upcycling an existing model. While less demanding to explore, it could be sub-optimal in that
454   the model has not learnt to offload topic specific information into experts. Hence, expert aware pre-
455   training should be explored where the base model and the experts are trained jointly from scratch.
456
457
      6   C ONCLUSION
458
459
      In this paper, we introduced the mixture-of-introvert experts framework to upcycle a pretrained
460
      LLM. The training data is split into semantically related clusters and an expert is trained on each
461   cluster. Queries at test-time are routed to appropriate expert with a simple nearest neighbor search.
462   All the experts can be trained independently. Our approach offers great flexibility in training and
463   provides a way to improve an LLM with limited resources. We provide proof-of-concept exper-
464   imental results by upcycling a 1B parameter model with 5000 experts on 500B tokens and show
465   comparable or better performance than full-model continued pretraining.
466
467
      7   L IMITATIONS AND BROADER IMPACT
468
469
      Our approach utilizes large LoRA models, which results in a significant number of parameters com-
470   pared to standard pretraining methods. Hence, our method is particularly well-suited for applications
471   where multiple instances of the LLM are deployed across several GPUs to serve numerous users,
472   such as in ChatGPT. Moreover, we believe that the size of LoRA models could be substantially re-
473   duced; however, we did not explore different ranks for LoRA due to resource constraints. While our
474   method effectively lowers the cost of pretraining LLMs, potentially democratizing the development
475   of novel models, it also raises concerns. Specifically, it may enable less sophisticated adversaries to
476   create their own models, which could lead to negative societal impacts.
