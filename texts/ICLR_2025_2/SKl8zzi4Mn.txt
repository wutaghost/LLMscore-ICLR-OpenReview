009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Overparameterized models trained with (stochastic) gradient descent are ubiqui-
012            tous in modern machine learning. These large models achieve unprecedented per-
013            formance on test data, but their theoretical understanding is still limited. In this
014            paper, we take a step towards filling this gap by adopting an optimization perspec-
015            tive. More precisely, we study the implicit regularization properties of the gradient
016            flow “algorithm” for estimating the parameters of a deep diagonal neural network.
017
               Our main contribution is showing that this gradient flow induces a mirror flow
               dynamic on the model, meaning that it is biased towards a specific solution of the
018
               problem depending on the initialization of the network. Along the way, we prove
019
               several properties of the trajectory.

022   1     I NTRODUCTION
023
024   In recent years, the application of deep networks has revolutionized the field of machine learning,
025   particularly in tasks involving complex data such as images and natural language. These models,
026   typically trained using stochastic gradient descent, have demonstrated remarkable performance on
027   various benchmarks, raising questions about the underlying mechanisms that contribute to their
028   success. Despite their practical efficacy, the theoretical understanding of these models remains rel-
029
      atively limited, creating a pressing need for deeper insights into their generalization abilities. The
      classical theory shows that the latter is a consequence of regularization, which is the way to impose
030
      a priori knowledge into the model and to favour “simple” solutions. While usually regularization is
031
      achieved either by choosing simple models or explicitly adding a penalty term to the empirical risk
032   during training, this is not the case for deep neural networks, which are trained simply by minimiz-
033   ing the empirical risk. A new perspective has then emerged in the recent literature, which relates
034   regularization directly to the optimization procedure (gradient based methods). The main idea is
035   to show that the training dynamics themselves exhibit self regularizing properties, by inducing an
036   implicit regularization (bias) which prefers generalizing solutions (see Vardi (2023) for an extended
037   review of the importance of implicit bias in machine learning).
038   In this context, a common approach is to study simplified models that approximate the networks used
039   in practice. Analyzing the implicit bias of optimization algorithms for such networks is facilitated
040   but still might give some insights on the good performance of neural networks in various scenarios.
041

051   variable U returns a matrix Θ = U U T approximating a specific solution of the original problem,
052   the one with minimal nuclear norm. In other words, a simple change of variable before running
053   the algorithm allows to promote sparsity of the recovered solution, which tends to imply a better
      generalization. Such a setting can roughly be linked to neural networks and has been followed by

061   Weight normalization Similarly, weight normalization is a strategy used in machine learning that

      Diagonal Linear Networks Diagonal Linear Networks are introduced in the literature by Wood-
073   worth et al. (2020) and Moroshko et al. (2020) as a linear approximation of a neural network with
074   diagonal connections between the nodes (see Figure 1). In this case the network function is given by
075   fθ (x) = θT x with the parameters vector expressed as θ = u ⊙ v, where ⊙ denotes the Hadamard
076   product. Although this model appears to be overly simplistic, its analysis offers a considerable de-
077   gree of insight. Both Woodworth et al. (2020) and Moroshko et al. (2020) highlight the role of the
078   initialization for such a network in regard of the implicit bias induced on the solution: a small initial-
079   ization favours a solution close to that with minimal L1 norm, while a large one tends to minimize
080   the L2 norm. These two regimes can be put in parallel with respectively the rich regime and the ker-
081
      nel (or lazy) regime outlined in Chizat et al. (2019). Azulay et al. (2021) give additional results on
      the implicit bias of the network, allowing untied weights and also considering the case of connected
082
      networks (non diagonal). This model has been also been analyzed to better understand the implicit
083
      bias induced by stochastic gradient descent Pesme et al. (2021); Even et al. (2023), by the choice of
084   the step size Nacson et al. (2022) and by other methods involving momentum Papazov et al. (2024).
085   The L-layer case has been studied by Woodworth et al. (2020); Moroshko et al. (2020); Chou et al.
086   (2023a) for a very simplified model, suggesting that the number of layers is positively correlated
087   with the sparsity of the selected solution.

      Hadamard parameterization to promote sparsity Diagonal Linear Networks are closely related
102   to Hadamard parameterization (HP) and it is worth noticing that HP was used before for sparsity
103   recovery. Hoff (2017) takes benefit of HP to promote sparsity in a LASSO problem. Vaskevicius
104   et al. (2019); Zhao et al. (2022) also consider an HP for a least squares problem inducing an implicit
105   bias towards the minimal L1 -norm solution, enabling the use of early stopping strategies. In a
106   comparable vein, Amid & Warmuth (2020) exploit a reparameterization θ = u⊙2 for a classification
107   problem and Chou et al. (2022) apply HP to solve non negative least squares. Poon & Peyré (2023)
      elegantly reformulate a group-LASSO problem using some Hadamard parameterization in order to

109   and in this regard, Ouyang et al. (2024) give necessary condition for a reparameterized function to

112   Links with Mirror Flow As shown in Azulay et al. (2021); Chou et al. (2023a), Diagonal Linear
113   Networks trained with a Gradient Flow (or Gradient Descent) share direct links with Mirror Flow
114   (or Mirror Descent). One of the first appearances of the Mirror Flow dynamic can be attributed
115   to Alvarez et al. (2004). Many years later, a number of studies are presented in the context of
116   understanding the effects of reparameterization. The mirror gradient method having an implicit
117   bias towards specific solutions, Vaskevicius et al. (2020) propose early stopping strategies taking
118   advantage of it. Li et al. (2022) provide assumptions that are necessary for a reparameterization
119   trained with a Gradient Flow to be equivalent to a Mirror Flow. In their paper, Chou et al. (2023b)
120
      adopt an original point of view, seeing reparameterization as a way to enforce some implicit bias. In
      this perspective, the authors give guidelines to define it efficiently.

      3   C ONCLUSION AND P ERSPECTIVES
494
495
      In this work, we have studied Deep Diagonal Linear Networks and broaden our understanding of
496
      their training. In particular, we have analyzed the trajectories of the original variable when a Gradient
497   Flow is applied to its layers. In this way, we were able to extract a sufficient initialization condition
498   to prove theoretical results both on the optimization and the bias of this model. We show indeed that
499   such a training induces a Mirror Flow on the original variable and ensures a linear decrease of the
500   error on the loss function as long as it satisfies the Polyak-Łojasiewicz inequality. This study also
501   reveals the negative impact of initializing the network with small values in terms of convergence
502   speed.
503   Despite its apparent simplicity, this model is still tricky to study and we were unable to compute the
504   entropy function associated to the Mirror Flow induced. It is then a natural question remaining that
505   would also inform us on the implicit bias underlying. It is also noteworthy that the linearity of this
506   model may prevent us from fully understanding the generalization properties of real-world neural
507   networks. This highlights the potential necessity for incorporating non-linearity in future works.
508   From an optimization perspective, further research could be conducted to investigate the influence
509   of stochasticity, momentum, and more generally, the optimization method applied during training.
