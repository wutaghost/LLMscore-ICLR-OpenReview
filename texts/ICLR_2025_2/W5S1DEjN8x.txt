011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Balancing exploration and exploitation is crucial in reinforcement learning (RL).
014            While Thompson Sampling (TS) is a sound and effective exploration strategy, its
015
               application to RL with high-dimensional continuous controls remains challenging.
               We propose Practical ϵ-Exploring Thompson Sampling (PETS), a practical ap-
016
               proach that addresses these challenges. Since the posterior over the parameters of
017
               the action-value function is intractable, we leverage Langevin Monte Carlo (LMC)
018            for sampling. We propose an approach which maintains n parallel Markov chains
019            to mitigate the issues of naiv̈e application of LMC. The next step following the pos-
020            terior sampling in TS involves finding the optimal action under the sampled model
021            of the action-value function. We explore both gradient-based and gradient-free
022            approaches to approximate the optimal action, with extensive experiments. Further-
023            more, to justify the use of gradient-based optimization to approximate the optimal
024            action, we analyze the regret for TS in the RL setting with continuous controls and
025            show that it achieves the best-known bound previously established for the discrete
026
               setting. Our empirical results demonstrate that PETS, as an exploration strategy,
               can be integrated with leading RL algorithms, enhancing their performance and
027
               stability on benchmark continuous control tasks.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reinforcement learning (RL) Mnih et al. (2015); Lillicrap et al. (2015); Sutton & Barto (1998)
033
      has become a cornerstone in solving complex decision-making problems, demonstrating significant
      success across diverse domains such as autonomous control (Kiumarsi et al., 2018), strategic game
034
      playing (Mnih et al., 2013b), and natural language processing Kung et al. (2022); Cetina et al. (2021).
035
      One of the central challenges of RL is striking a balance between exploration and exploitation
036   (Chapelle & Li, 2011a; Auer, 2003; Berger-Tal et al., 2014; Nair et al., 2017). Exploration, the
037   process of trying new actions to learn about their outcomes, is crucial for accurate value estimation
038   and finding the optimal behaviour. Exploitation, on the other hand, involves leveraging existing
039   knowledge to make optimal decisions. This exploration-exploitation dilemma is central to RL,
040   profoundly influencing the learning process’s efficiency and effectiveness (Sutton & Barto, 1998).
041
      In continuous action spaces, the challenge of balancing exploration and exploitation is amplified
042   due to the infinite number of possible actions. The volume of the action space grows exponentially
043   with the number of dimensions, making efficient exploration in high-dimensional continuous spaces
044   especially difficult (Tang et al., 2016). In such settings, simple and widely-used exploration methods
045   like the ϵ-greedy strategy (Tokic, 2010) fall short due to their inefficiency and lack of adaptability to
046   knowledge acquired during learning (Dann et al., 2022). The maximum entropy framework introduces
047   its own set of complications, in particular the difficulty of tuning the temperature hyperparameter α
048   for entropy (Haarnoja et al., 2018b; Wang & Ni, 2020), which is crucial for balancing exploration
049   and exploitation. The Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) method (Garivier & Moulines, 2011; Garivier
050
      & Cappé, 2011), while effective in the bandit setting, doesn’t work well in practice in challenging
      continuous control tasks (Long & Han, 2023).
051
052   Thompson Sampling (TS) (Thompson, 1933) is an alternative exploration strategy that has been
053   extensively explored in bandit problems (Chapelle & Li, 2011a; Xu et al., 2022). TS balances
      exploration and exploitation adaptively through probabilistic modeling of uncertainty. The essence of


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      TS lies in its principle of probability matching (Vulkan, 2000), i.e., the probability of selecting an
055   action corresponds to the probability of that action being the optimal choice over the uncertainty of
056   the knowledge about the environment. The more certain we are of our knowledge of the environment,
057   the less TS explores. As learning progresses, TS naturally tends to favor actions that consistently yield
058   better outcomes, thereby reducing exploration in areas where the understanding of the environment
059   has solidified. In contrast, in contexts characterized by high uncertainty or sparse data, TS inherently
060   boosts exploration to acquire more information. This adaptive approach enables TS to effectively
061   manage the exploration-exploitation trade-off (Russo et al., 2017). When applied to the reinforcement
062   learning (RL) setting, TS models the posterior over the parameters of the expected returns, the
063
      action-value function, for each state and action. It then selects the optimal action under a model
      of action-value function sampled from the posterior. In this approach, as new trajectories and their
064
      returns are observed, the uncertainty in the posterior decreases, and TS reduces exploration in favor
065
      of exploitation as a result (Saha & Kveton, 2023).
066
067   Despite Thompson Sampling’s success in the bandit settings (Agrawal & Goyal, 2012; Slivkins et al.,
068   2019; Kuleshov & Precup, 2014), its application to the more general RL setting has been limited.
069
      One of the main reasons is that the posterior, in all but the simplest cases, is intractable (van de
      Schoot et al., 2021). Consequently, sampling from the posterior, which is a necessary step in TS,
070
      is a challenge. To adapt TS to RL settings with high-dimensional continuous controls, we draw
071
      insights from Langevin Monte Carlo (LMC) (Langevin et al., 1908; Rossky et al., 1978; Roberts &
072   Tweedie, 1996; Girolami & Calderhead, 2011). LMC provides a practical approach to sampling from
073   intractable distributions in high-dimensional spaces.
074
075
      However, naïvely applying LMC for posterior sampling does not fully resolve the challenges of
      performing TS in RL problems with continuous controls. (1) In TS, the step following posterior
076
      sampling involves finding the optimal action under the sampled model of the action-value function.
077
      In continuous action spaces, this task is non-trivial due to the infinite number of possible actions to
078   consider. We explore both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization with extensive experiments
079   for approximating the optimal action. Furthermore, to justify the use of gradient-based optimization,
080   we analyze the regret for TS in the RL setting with continuous controls – to the best of our knowledge
081   such analysis was previously limited to the discrete control setting (Ishfaq et al., 2024). We show
082   that, under regularity conditions,    the regret for TS with gradient-based optimization matches the

086   exploration, as it limits the diversity of actions taken by the agent. Consequently, naïve application of

131   Exploration vs. Exploitation In reinforcement learning, exploration is fundamental for discovering
132   optimal policies. It involves exploring the action space to gather more information about the
133   environment, especially under uncertainty. Exploration can be viewed as a strategy where the
134
      probability selecting an action is not solely dependent on the current knowledge of the rewards
      (Watkins & Dayan, 1992), but also includes other components to encourage trying less-explored
135
      actions. This process is critical in environments with sparse or deceptive rewards, as it enables the
136
      agent to escape local optima and discover more rewarding strategies in the long run (Jiang et al.,
137   2023). While exploration is key in learning about the environment, its counterpart, exploitation, is
138   equally crucial (Wang et al., 2018) in RL. Exploitation involves leveraging the knowledge gained from
139   exploration to make decisions that maximize immediate rewards. The balance between exploration
140   and exploitation is a central challenge in RL, as excessive exploration can lead to sample inefficiency,
141   while excessive exploitation might result in getting stuck at suboptimal policies. A well-calibrated
142   balance ensures the agent learns effectively, adapts to the environment, and optimizes its strategy for
143   long-term success.
144   Thompson Sampling Thompson Sampling (TS) (Russo et al., 2017) is a systematic approach to
145   adaptively balance exploration and exploitation based on the uncertainty in the current knowledge
146   about the environment. TS continuously updates a posterior distribution over the parameters of the
147   model of expected returns, namely the action-value function Qw (x, a). In each step, TS first samples
148   parameters w from the posterior p(w|D), where D is the observation set. Then, given the current
149   state x, it selects an action a∗ that maximizes the expected return under the sampled model of the
150   action-value function:
151                                           w ∼ p(w|D)                                                (1)
152                                          a∗ = arg max Qw (x, a)                                     (2)
153                                                     a∈A

154
      Crucially, as the posterior distribution reflects the uncertainty over the model of expected returns, TS
155   inherently adjusts the exploration-exploitation trade-off by sampling from this distribution, with a
156   higher uncertainty resulting in a greater exploration compared to exploitation.

      3.2     PARALLEL P OSTERIOR S AMPLES
224
225   While SGLD provides a sampling method without needing to compute the partition function Z,
226   it results in highly correlated samples (Vishnoi, 2021) at nearby steps of Eq 11. This results in
227
      similar actions in the TS procedure, which is suboptimal in environments requiring a high degree of
      exploration and diversity in decision-making.
228
229   To avoid that, one needs to discard intermediate samples, also known as the burn-in period (Sahlin,
230   2011). In other words, the Markov chain generated by Eq 11 should be run for many steps (long
231   burn-in period) to adequately mix (Levin & Peres, 2017; Holden, 2019; Sahlin, 2011). However,
232   in challenging continuous control tasks where the agent needs to take actions over the course of
233
      hundreds of thousands of steps, running this Markov chain to mix for every action sample is highly
      inefficient.
234
235   To address this challenge, instead of maintaining a single Markov chain, which requires many
236   steps to mix, or using nearby samples that result in high correlation, we maintain n independent
237   Markov chains, W = {w(1) , w(2) , . . . , w(n) } where each w(i) is trained on different batches from
238   the replay buffer (see line 21 to 24 of Algorithm 1). For action decisions, we randomly select one of
239   these n posterior samples, wselected ∼ Uniform(W) where Uniform(W) indicates a discrete uniform
240
      distribution over the elements in the set W.
241   This approach ensures a representative exploration of the posterior distribution resulting in a more
242   diverse set of actions and better exploration. Moreover, another practical advantage of this approach
243   is that the Markov chains in W can be trained independently in parallel, improving computational
244   efficiency. In Section 4.4 we empirically validate the effectiveness of this approach in achieving
245   better exploration.

      3.3     A PPROXIMATING THE O PTIMAL ACTION IN C ONTINUOUS S PACES
248   Leveraging LMC to sample from the posterior does not fully address the challenges of using TS in
249   RL with continuous controls. A remaining challenge is that the step following the posterior sampling
250   involves finding the optimal action w.r.t the model of the action-value function, as described by
251   Eq 2. This is straightforward in discrete action spaces but becomes challenging in high-dimensional
252   continuous action spaces, as there are infinitely many possible actions to consider. We explore
253
      both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization approaches. We use Adam for gradient-based
      optimization, and one of the more recent methods, design by adaptive sampling (DBAS) (Brookes &
254
      Listgarten, 2018), for gradient-free optimization. Experimental results in Section 4.2 demonstrate that
255
      PETS with both gradient-based and gradient-free optimization outperforms the baselines, including
256   the state-of-the-art RL algorithm in the continuous control setting, POMP (Zhu et al., 2023).
257
258   3.3.1    DBAS
259
      Given the sampled model of the action-value function Qwselected and the current state, our objective is
260   to find the optimal action a∗ as described by Eq 2. Design by adaptive sampling (DBAS) (Brookes &
261   Listgarten, 2018) is a gradient-free, iterative algorithm that can be used to approximate the optimal
262   action.
263
      In each iteration i of DBAS: (I) It trains an unconditional generative model Gi on a set of actions Ai ,
264
      where A0 can be initialized randomly or from a policy. (II) It samples a new set of actions from Gi
265
      and initialize Ai+1 with them. (III) It uses Qwselected as an oracle to rank the samples in Ai+1 , retaining
266   the top k actions in Ai+1 —where k is a hyperparameter—and discarding the rest. This process is
267   repeated for n iterations, where n is a hyperparameter. Finally, one of the actions in An can be used
268   as an approximation to the optimal action. In Appendix B.1, we provide the implementation details
269   and pseudocode for this procedure. We refer readers to Brookes & Listgarten (2018) for a more
      detailed description.

465   5    R ELATED W ORK
466
467
      Exploration. As one of the most central research problems in RL, exploration has been extensively
468
      studied. In this section, we discuss some of the works in this field, while recognizing that the breadth
469   of literature on exploration is far too vast to be comprehensively covered here. In the context of
470   discrete action spaces: Osband et al. (2014) shows that least-square value iteration using ϵ-greedy
471   is highly inefficient. By introducing randomized least-square value iteration (RLSVI), they show
472   that randomized value functions can be efficient and effective. Fortunato et al. (2017) adds noise
473   to value and action-value functions with learned coefficients to boost exploration. Auer (2003)
474   encourages exploration in the bandit setting by using confidence bounds and introducing bonuses for
475   less-visited arms. Henaff et al. (2023) proposes to make use of both global and episodic bonuses to
476   improve exploration. Chen et al. (2017) proposes an exploration strategy based on UCB by leveraging
477
      uncertainty estimates from the Q-ensemble to boost exploration. Jarrett et al. (2022) proposes to learn
      representations of the future that capture the unpredictable aspects of each outcome and use that as
478
      additional input for predictions. Pislar et al. (2021) draws inspiration from animals and humans and
479
      proposes mode-switching exploration in RL where they introduce an approach to adaptively switch
480   between modes of exploration. Furthermore, among the exploration approaches in the context of
481   continuous action spaces: Bellemare et al. (2016) uses density models to measure uncertainty and
482   proposes a method to derive a pseudo-count from an arbitrary density model. Lobel et al. (2023)
483   proposes a count-based exploration strategy suited for high-dimensional state spaces. To this end,
484   they estimate visitation counts by averaging samples from the Rademacher distribution. Pathak et al.
485   (2019) trains an ensemble of dynamics models and incentivizes the agent to explore such that the
      disagreement of those ensembles is maximized.

      Thompson Sampling. TS has been extensively studied in the multi-armed bandit (Slivkins et al.,
487   2019; Kuleshov & Precup, 2014) setting. (Agrawal & Goyal, 2012) showed that TS achieves a
488   logarithmic expected regret and it is competitive to or better than UCB (Garivier & Moulines, 2011;
489   Garivier & Cappé, 2011). Komiyama et al. (2015) adapted TS to the setting where multiple arms need
490   to be selected at the same time. In the contextual bandit setting, Agrawal et al.
                                                                                       (2017) applies  TS in
                                                                                        d2
                                                                                           √
491   scenarios with linear payoffs and proves a high probability regret bound of Õ ϵ T     1+ϵ   . Xu et al.
492
      (2022) demonstrates that Laplace approximation (Chapelle & Li, 2011b) of the posterior distribution
493
      is inefficient in high-dimensional settings. They leverage Langevin Monte Carlo√(Langevin et al.,
494
      1908) for posterior sampling and show that TS achieves a regret bound of Õ(d dT ). Moreover,
495
      Jin et al. (2023) introduces ϵ-Exploring Thompson Sampling (ϵ-TS) which selects arms based on
496   the posterior with a probability of ϵ. They show that ϵ-TS improves the computational efficiency
497   of TS while achieving better regret bounds. They further demonstrate the superiority of ϵ-TS for a
498   range of reward distributions, such as Bernoulli, Gaussian, Poisson, and Gamma. Ishfaq et al. (2024)
499   demonstrates the effectiveness of Langevin Monte Carlo Thompson Sampling (LMC-TS) in Atari
500   games (Mnih et al., 2013a) where the discrete action space contains
                                                                        √ at most 18 possible actions. They
501   prove that LMC-TS achieves the regret bound of Õ(d3/2 H 3/2 T ) in the linear MDP setting with
502   discrete actions under specific assumptions. While they demonstrate the effectiveness of TS in Atari
503   games (Mnih et al., 2013a), they do not address the challenges limiting the application of TS in
504   challenging continuous control tasks.
505
506
507   High-dimensional Continuous Control. While the mentioned challenges in Section 3.2 have
508   limited the application of TS in high-dimensional continuous control tasks, a great amount of
509   progress has been made by other methods, not necessarily focusing on the exploration/exploitation
510   trade-off. Among model-free algorithms, Schulman et al. (2015) improves policy updates by ensuring
511   small, incremental changes, using a trust region to maintain policy performance and stability by
512   using a surrogate objective function. Schulman et al. (2017) introduces a simple surrogate objective
513
      function that lower-bounds the performance of a policy. In contrast to TRPO (Schulman et al.,
      2015), PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) only requires first-order information to optimize the policy. SAC
514
      (Haarnoja et al., 2018a) tries to address the exploration/exploitation trade-off using the Maximum
515
      Entropy Reinforcement Learning framework. On the other hand, model-based approaches have
516   shown great progress in improving the sample efficiency and performance of RL algorithms. POMP
517   (Zhu et al., 2023) incorporates Deep Differential Dynamic Programming (D3P) planner into the
518   model-based RL and shows significant improvement on MuJoCo tasks. MAGE (D’Oro & Jaśkowski,
519   2020) leverages the environment model differentiability to directly compute policy gradients. MBPO
520   (Janner et al., 2019) makes use of the environment model with different horizons and shows that
521   their approach matches the asymptotic performance of the best model-free algorithms. CMLO (Ji
522   et al., 2022) proposes an event-triggered mechanism to determine when to update the model of the
523   environment. DDPPO (Li et al., 2022) proposes a two-model-based learning method to control the
524
      prediction and gradient error.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduced Practical ϵ-Exploring Thompson Sampling (PETS), which aims to
530   address the challenges that have limited the application of Thompson Sampling (TS) in RL with
531   continuous control tasks. We draw insights from Langevin Monte Carlo (LMC) for posterior
532   sampling and propose an approach to maintain n parallel posterior samples, mitigating the issue of
533   sample correlation. Additionally, we employ both gradient-based and gradeient-free approaches to
534   approximate the optimal action and provide theoretical guarantees that under regularity conditions,
      replacing the exact optimal action with an approximate  one found by gradient-based optimization
535                                                            √
536   achieves the best-known regret bound of O   e d3/2 H 3/2 T . To validate PETS’s effectiveness
537   as an exploration strategy, we integrated it into POMP (Zhu et al., 2023), MBPO (Janner et al.,
538   2019) and SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018b). Our empirical results demonstrate PETS’s effectiveness
539   in improving the performance and training stability of existing RL alorithms across a range of
      challenging continuous control tasks.
