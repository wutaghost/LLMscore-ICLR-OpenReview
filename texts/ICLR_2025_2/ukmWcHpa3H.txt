009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Surgical phase recognition has drawn great attention most recently thanks to its
012            potential downstream applications closely related to human life and health. De-
013            spite deep network-based models have made significant advancement in capturing
014            discriminative long-term dependency of surgical videos to achieve improved recog-
015            nition, they seldom account for exploring and modeling uncertainty of surgical
016
               videos, which should be crucial for reliable surgical phase recognition. we catego-
               rize the sources of uncertainty into two types, imbalanced phase distribution and
017
               low-quality image acquisition, which are inevitable in surgical videos. To address
018
               this pivot issue, we introduce a meta-weighted diffusion model (MetaDiff) to take
019            full advantages of meta-learning and deep generative model in tackling uncertainty.
020            For uncertainty caused by image quality, we present a classifier-guided diffusion
021            model to produce countable denoised recognition results, making it possible to
022            measure uncertainty using statistical tools for each video frame. For uncertainty
023            caused by phase distribution, we propose a meta-weighted objective function to
024            optimize the classifier-guided diffusion model, making the classification boundary
025            robust against surgical video uncertainty. We demonstrate outstanding ability of our
026            model through comprehensive benchmarks on Cholec80, AutoLaparo, M2Cai16,
027
               and CATARACTS. Experimental results reveal that MetaDiff significantly outper-
               forms state-of-the-art methods, separately achieving accuracies of 95.3%, 85.8%,
028
               92.2%, and 85.1% on Cholec80, AutoLaparo, M2Cai16, and CATARACTS.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Surgical phases recognition aims to identify the representation of high-level surgical stages depicted
034   in surgical videos Jin et al. (2017). This capability holds potential applications for fruitful downstream
035   tasks, such as automatic indexing of surgical video databases Twinanda et al. (2016b), real-time
036   monitoring of surgical procedures Bricon-Souf & Newman (2007), optimizing surgeons schedules
037   Neumuth (2017), evaluating surgeons’ proficiency Liu et al. (2021), etc. The primary objective
038   of surgical phase recognition is to predict the category variable y ∈ RL×C given a video frame
039
      x ∈ RL×I . The process is characterized by the deterministic function f (x) ∈ RL×C that transforms
      the video frame x into the category variable y.To help alert surgeons and support decision-making in
040
      real-time during surgery, we do not use the future information within the video frame of x, which
041
      is also known as online phase recognition Quellec et al. (2014); Dergachyova et al. (2016), which
042   requires us to design the mapping function f (·) carefully without the information leakage.
043
044   In recent years, deep neural network-based models He et al. (2016); Vaswani et al. (2017) has shown
045
      promising performance in automated surgical phase recognition via designing complex deterministic
      functions f (·) Jin et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023b); Ding & Li (2022); Zhao & Krähenbühl (2022);
046
      Rivoir et al. (2024). To capture and discover long-term spatial information in surgical videos,
047
      Transformer-based methods are proposed Tao et al. (2023); Yue et al. (2023). To enhance the
048   efficiency and effectiveness of Transformer-based methods while avoiding future information leakage
049   , SKiT Liu et al. (2023b) introduces a key pooling operation with a time complexity of O(1). Most
050   recently, convolution neural network-based models have regained attention due to their awareness of
051   the pitfalls of batch normalization for effective end-to-end learning Rivoir et al. (2024). Recognizing
052   the labor-intensive and time-consuming nature of video annotation in surgical phase recognition,
053   UATD Ding & Li (2022) and VTDC Shi et al. (2021) have been separately proposed to use timestamp
      annotation and semi-supervised information to alleviate the burden of manual labeling.

092   MOA, respectively. 3) Experiments on four popular benchmarks including Cholec80, AutoLaparo,
093   M2Cai16, and CATARACTS demonstrate that MetaDiff outperforms recently developed competitive
094   baselines across various evaluation metrics, establishing new state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance.

155
      surgical phase recognition. We begin with a brief overview of MetaDiff, followed by a discussion
156   of its two critical components: Classifier-guided Diffusion Model in Sec. 3.1 and Meta-weighted
157   Optimization Algorithm (MOA) in Sec. 3.2. As shown in Fig. 2, CDM consists of a Spatial-temporal
158   Feature Extractor (SFE), a diffusion process, and a reversed diffusion process. Given surgical videos,
159   the SFE provides rough predictions serving as conditional information to guide the followup reversed
160   diffusion to denoise for obtaining reliable predictions. Notably, each video frame may produce
161   multiple prediction trajectories after denoising, which are modestly collected by the MOA used to
      learn parameters of CDM for phase recognition in an uncertainty-aware manner.

478   5    C ONCLUSION
479
480   We propose MetaDiff to address a crucial issue that have long been overlooked in reliable online
481   surgical phase recognition, namely uncertainty caused by phase distribution and video quality.
482   MetaDiff employs the CDM to deliver clean predictions, being less disturbed by uncertainty, from
483   rough ones provided by a pre-trained SFE. Additionally, we employ the MOA to train the model so
484   that enabling the CDM to have such uncertainty-aware capability. Our empirical results demonstrate
485   that the proposed model outperforms other specially designed competitors in complex online surgical
      phase recognition task.

      For LSTM in SFE, we use a two layers LSTM, each layer has 512 hidden units. For architecture

746

      product between the output vector and the corresponding timestep embedding, followed by a Softplus

751   A.5    M ORE D ETAILS ON PIW AND PTST
752
753   Prediction Interval Width (PIW) and Paired Two-Sample t-Test (PTST) are used to assess the
754   predictive uncertainty of our proposed MetaDiff. We provide the mean PIW among correct and
755   incorrect predictions, and the mean accuracy among instances rejected and not-rejected by the PTST
      for all test instances. And we describe details of their calculation process as follows.

770   For ease of representation, we set ntest as the number of instances in the test dataset and nsample
771   as the number of samples taken for uncertainty assessment for each instance. The data for all
772   instances in the test dataset is represented as x ∈ Rntest ×I , where I is feature dimension. Their
773   corresponding ground-truth label with one-hot encoding is denoted as y ∈ Rntest ×C , where C is
774   phase number. When using MetaDiff for inference on the test dataset, each input instance is used to
775
      generated nsample denoised outputs (predicted vectors) ŷ ∈ Rntest ×nsample ×C by reverse diffusion.
      By taking the maximum predicted probability among the nsample predictions for each instance, we
776
      obtain the predicted recognition results y ∈ Rntest ×C for all instances. Additionally, extracting
777
      the top two maximum probabilities from the nsample predictions yields two data distributions, each
778
      containing ns ample values for each instance. These two distributions serve as the two input samples
779   for PTST statistical testing, denoted collectively as t ∈ Rntest ×nsample ×2 . Comparing the predicted
780   results y with the ground-truth label y allows us to determine the number of correctly classified and
781   misclassified samples, thereby the recognition accuracy is obtained. Subsequently, we compute the
782   PIW values between the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the predicted probabilities separately for
783   correct and incorrect samples, averaging these values to derive the PIW for correct and incorrect
784   samples. Besides, we conduct hypothesis tests on the obtained dual-sample distribution t, yielding a
785   p-value for each instance, which is compared against the given significance level ρ = 0.05 to classify
786   instances as ’reject’ or ’no-reject’ groups. It’s noteworthy that these hypothesis tests are based on the
787
      largest and second largest predicted probabilities for each instance. Therefore, the hypothesis is that
      "the top two maximum predicted values are the same". Larger differences between these probabilities
788
      indicate higher accuracy in the maximum predicted probability, identifying ’reject’ samples as those
789
      with better predictive performance. Finally, we tally the number of correctly predicted samples in the
790   ’reject’ and ’no-reject’ groups, calculating the corresponding accuracy for each group.
