011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection holds significant importance across various ap-
014            plications. While semantic and domain-shift OOD problems are well-documented,
015            this work focuses on the nuances of covariate shifts, which entail subtle pertur-
016            bations or variations in the data distribution. These disturbances have proven to
017            negatively impact machine learning performance. We have found that existing
018
               OOD detection methods often struggle to effectively distinguish covariate shifts
               from in-distribution instances, emphasizing the need for specialized solutions.
019
               Therefore, we propose DisCoNet, an Adversarial Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
020
               that rethinks the Generative Adversarial Networks paradigm. Instead of priori-
021            tizing the generator as the network’s core, we focus on the discriminator, using
022            the generator as a supporting training tool. DisCoNet uses the VAE’s suboptimal
023            outputs as negative samples to train the discriminator, thereby improving its ability
024            to delineate the boundary between in-distribution samples and covariate shifts. By
025            tightening this in-distribution boundary, DisCoNet achieves state-of-the-art results
026            in public OOD detection benchmarks. The proposed model not only excels in
027            detecting covariate shifts, achieving 98.9% AUROC on ImageNet-1K(-C), but also
028            outperforms all prior methods on public semantic OOD benchmarks. With a model
029
               size of ≤ 25MB, it is highly effective on Far-OOD (OpenImage-O (99.4%) and
               iNaturalist (100.0%)) and Near-OOD (SSB-hard (99.9%) and NINCO (99.7%))
030
               detection. The code will be made publicly available.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection consists of identifying whether a given test sample significantly
037   deviates from the known information of in-distribution (ID) data. It is mainly employed as a
038   preliminary step in image-based systems, aiming to mitigate the risks associated with feeding OOD
039
      inputs to a model. Besides safeguarding a system against erroneous predictions, it also facilitates the
      safe handling of OOD samples, either by rejection or transfer to human intervention. However, the
040
      significance of OOD lies not only in bolstering the reliability of image processing systems, but also in
041
      its standalone role for anomaly and fault detection. A simple example of this use case can be found in
042   the visual inspection of industrial image data, where it is easy to acquire imagery of normal samples
043   yet virtually impossible to define the expected defects (Roth et al., 2022). In the OOD context, these
044   anomalies can be broadly classified into two types: (1) anomalous objects in images which refer to
045   unexpected or rare items appearing in the frame, and (2) faulty equipment or products which refer to
046   malfunctions or irregularities in the machinery or products under inspection. As a consequence, this
047   task is typically cast as an OOD classification problem.
048   OOD detection comprises various types of shifts in data. (1) Semantic shifts, such as encountering
049   unseen classes, and (2) domain shifts, like distinguishing between real images and drawings, have
050   easily established boundaries and are well-defined in literature (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Li
051   et al., 2017). On the other hand, (3) covariate shifts, which involve perturbations in data or subtle
052   changes in its expected variability, are often conflated with domain shifts (Yang et al., 2021). It
053   is essential to differentiate covariate shifts, since they pose unique challenges requiring tailored
      detection mechanisms.

      Figure 1 illustrates the proposed framework for interpreting shifts in a data distribution. In our
055   definition, the ID range covers an expected semantic shift, containing a pre-defined number of
056   different classes, as exemplified by CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), along with some degree of
057   variability in terms of domain and covariate shifts. For instance, the introduction of a novel class such
058   as the spiders in ImageNet-200 (Le & Yang, 2015) represents an OOD semantic shift, as CIFAR-10
059   lacks such examples. An extreme change in domain, such as a hand-drawn representation of a plane
060   from the Sketch dataset (Eitz et al., 2012), is considered OOD, despite the retaining of semantic
061   relevance within the ID set. Additionally, substantial covariate shifts, such as image blurring in the
062   case of a horse image from CIFAR-10(-C) (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019), are also classified as
063
      OOD, even though there are no explicit alterations in semantic or domain concepts.

084   Various unsupervised OOD detection methods employing generative models like Variational Autoen-
085   coders (VAEs) (Pinaya et al., 2021), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Schlegl et al., 2017),
086   Normalizing Flows (NFs) (Kobyzev et al., 2020) and more recently Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic
087
      Models (DDPMs) (Wyatt et al., 2022) have already been explored. The detection of anomalous data
      is usually performed by assessing whether they deviate from the learned representation manifold, or
088
      by comparing the reconstructed and original images in pixel space. DDPMs exhibit superior mode
089
      coverage compared to GANs and VAEs, albeit with much slower sampling rates (Xiao et al., 2021).
090   NFs present a good framework for OOD detection, but it is well documented that they often assign
091   a higher likelihood to OOD samples than the ID data (Kirichenko et al., 2020). However, these
092   approaches have focused mainly on semantic and domain-shift detection, rather than covariate shift.
093   Furthermore, the efficiency of these methods is often disregarded.
094
      In this paper, we demonstrate that training a discriminator in an Adversarial VAE framework, using
095
      both reconstructed and generated images, results in an excellent OOD detector. Within this covariate
096   shift-focused framework, the model can not only address covariate shift detection but also tackle
097   semantic OOD samples, all while significantly accelerating detection speed compared to prior work.
098   Since the proposed approach is trained end-to-end, the generated sample and image reconstruction
099   quality are refined during training and utilized as OOD samples to improve the discriminator, yielding
100   the quality of these counterfactual examples irrelevant. The main contributions of this work are as
101   follows.
102
103          • Redefinition of adversarial training by inverting the roles of the generator and discriminator,
104            using the generator to distill information about in-distribution boundaries, enabling the
105
               discriminator to separate in-distribution and out-of-distribution samples effectively.
106          • DisCoNet, a lightweight unsupervised framework specifically designed for OOD detection.
107          • State-of-the-art performance in detecting both covariate and semantic OOD shifts, as demon-
               strated by extensive evaluations that reveal significant improvements over existing methods.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109

      2.1   S EMANTIC S HIFT AND C OVARIATE S HIFT OOD
111
112   OOD detection literature predominantly focuses on semantic shift and typically falls into two
113   categories: (a) supervised, which requires labels or OOD data, and (b) unsupervised, which relies
114   solely on in-distribution data (Yang et al., 2021). Given the nature of the OOD detection problem,
115   OOD data are typically not available and, as such, unsupervised methods are generally preferred.
116   While the primary aim of this research is covariate shift OOD identification, we provide a brief
117   overview of recent unsupervised advancements in semantic OOD detection that may be useful for
118   covariate OOD detection. Covariate shift occurs when images have consistent semantic and domain
119
      content, but are recorded under deviating imaging settings and conditions, or corrupted in a post-
      processing step. Hence, the degree of variance under these conditions can deteriorate semantic and
120
      domain content. This study focuses on covariate shifts within the same domain, as these subtle
121
      distribution shifts can cause significant drops in the classification performance of machine learning
122   models, as seen in adversarial attacks (Adhikarla et al., 2023).
123
124
      2.2   G ENERATIVE - BASED M ETHODS FOR OOD DETECTION
125
126   A widely used and initially intuitive approach for OOD detection involves fitting a generative model
127   p(x; θ) to a data distribution x and evaluating the likelihood of unseen samples under this model,
128   assuming that OOD samples will have lower likelihoods (Bishop, 1994). However, this assumption
129   has been challenged, with various generative models assigning higher likelihoods to certain OOD
130   samples (Hendrycks et al., 2018; Nalisnick et al., 2018). To address this, different approaches
131   have been proposed, including using the Watanabe-Akaike Information Criterion (WAIC) (Choi
132   et al., 2018), specific likelihood ratios (Serrà et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2020), and hierarchical
133   VAEs (Havtorn et al., 2021). These methods aim to correct for likelihood estimation errors, population-
134
      level background statistics, and model feature dominance. Another approach suggests labeling
      samples as OOD if their likelihoods fall outside the typical range of a model (Chali et al., 2023; Abdi
135
      et al., 2024), i.e., a sample may be classified as OOD not only if its likelihood is lower than that of ID
136
      data, but also if it is higher (Morningstar et al., 2021).
137
138
139
      2.3   R ECONSTRUCTION - BASED M ETHODS FOR OOD DETECTION
140
      Reconstruction-based methods involve training a model R to reconstruct inputs x from the training
141   distribution, such that we obtain x̂ = R(x). The rationale is that if R has an information bottleneck it
142   will struggle to accurately reconstruct OOD inputs. However, these methods face practical challenges,
143   including difficulty in tuning the information bottleneck size (Pimentel et al., 2014; Denouden et al.,
144   2018). If it is too small, ID samples may not be faithfully reconstructed; if it is too large, the model
145   can learn the identity function, allowing OOD samples to be reconstructed with low error. Some
146   approaches address these issues by using the Mahalanobis distance in the Autoencoder’s feature
147   space as an OOD metric (Denouden et al., 2018), or by introducing a memory module to discourage
148   OOD sample reconstruction (Gong et al., 2019). However, none of these methods fully resolve the
149
      bottleneck selection issue. To tackle this limitation, DDPMs have been employed, leveraging noise
      bottlenecks (Wyatt et al., 2022) and reconstructions from a range of noise values without the need for
150
      dataset-specific tuning (Graham et al., 2023) or of corrupted inputs (Liu et al., 2023).
151
152
153
      2.4   F EATURE - BASED AND L OGIT- BASED M ETHODS FOR OOD DETECTION
154
      Several scoring functions have been devised to differentiate between ID and OOD examples, leverag-
155
      ing characteristics of ID samples, but not represented in OOD ones, and vice versa. These functions
156   primarily stem from three sources: (1) probability-based measures, such as maximum softmax
157   probabilities (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), and minimum Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between
158   softmax and mean class-conditional distributions (Hendrycks et al., 2019); (2) logit-based functions,
159   including maximum logits (Hendrycks et al., 2019), and the use of the logsumexp function com-
160   puted over logits (Liu et al., 2020); (3) feature-based functions, involving the norm of the residual
161   between a feature and its low-dimensional embeddings (Ndiour et al., 2020), as well as minimum
      Mahalanobis distance between a feature and class centroids (Lee et al., 2018). Some hybrid methods


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      combine both logit and feature scores for OOD detection (Wang et al., 2022), while more recent
163   works have introduced masked image modeling pretraining into OOD detection with promising
164   results (Li et al., 2023a; 2024). However, the detection speed of these methods is severely constrained
165   by their transformer-based backbones.

167   2.5    A DVERSARIAL VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
168
169   The VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2013) consists of an encoder that predicts the parameters µ and σ of
170   the variational distribution of the input data, and a decoder that takes a sample from this distribution to
171   reconstruct the input. VAEs are trained to maximize the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO), which bal-
172
      ances reconstruction fidelity with the latent space regularization to ensure that it follows a predefined
      probability distribution, typically Gaussian. Using latent space as a bottleneck restricts the informa-
173
      tion that can pass through, leading to uncertainty and blurriness in the reconstructions (Dai & Wipf,
174
      2019). Additionally, the pixel-wise reconstruction error and the high dimensionality of natural image
175   manifolds pose challenges for VAEs in generating high-quality and realistic samples. While natural
176   images are assumed to lie on low-dimensional manifolds due to local scale redundancy (Kretzmer,
177   1952), textures exist in higher-dimensional manifolds, making them difficult to capture.
178
      GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) consist of two neural networks with adversarial objectives: the
179
      generator learns to map a random vector to the data space; the discriminator acts as a classifier trained
180
      to differentiate real samples from generated ones. Despite their success in generation tasks, GANs
181   suffer from two primary limitations compared to VAEs. The first is mode collapse, which occurs
182   when the generator produces only a few different types repeatedly, making it easily recognizable by
183   the discriminator. Consequently, the discriminator’s feedback lacks useful information (Thanh-Tung
184   & Tran, 2020). Additionally, GANs lack an encoder network, which limits their ability to perform
185   reconstruction and latent space manipulation.
186
      The VAE and GAN have been combined by incorporating a discriminator to enhance the realism
187   of VAE reconstructions (Larsen et al., 2016). Alternatively, the BiGAN (Donahue et al., 2016)
188   architecture features an encoder, generator, and discriminator, aiming for good unsupervised feature
189   representations but tends to produce less accurate reconstructions. Other approaches have adapted
190   this VAE/GAN combination to fully utilize the strengths of each architecture to improve the realism
191   of the images produced by the model (Plumerault et al., 2021). The objective of DisCoNet is to retain
192   the adversarial benefits and the mode coverage of the hybrid strategy, without the final goal of image
193   generation, thereby reducing computational requirements.

197   3.1    OVERVIEW
198
199   A prevalent generative-based approach to OOD detection involves leveraging a trained model to assess
200   the likelihood of new, unseen samples. Similarly, an adversarially trained discriminator can provide a
201
      boundary for the ID set, by assessing the probability of a sample being real (ID) or synthetic (OOD).
      By adjusting where the discriminator learns to draw this boundary, we can create an OOD detector.

214   is an Adversarial VAE-inspired architecture, as shown in Figure 2, in which both the VAE and the
215   discriminator are trained adversarially. DisCoNet’s approach combines generative and reconstruction-
      based strategies to distill information about the ID set and OOD boundaries to the discriminator

216
      during training in an unsupervised manner. Unlike traditional adversarial methods, DisCoNet’s focus
217   is on leveraging the generator’s output as a tool to refine the discriminator.
218
219   The VAE is trained to reduce the standard ELBO loss, while also producing samples (generated
220
      images using the VAE decoder) that can fool the discriminator. The discriminator is trained to
      not only distinguish between generated and real images, as in the standard GAN setup, but also
221
      reconstructed images. Reconstructions from VAEs typically lack detail, i.e., they have a sub-optimal
222
      high-frequency representation (Lin et al., 2023), which can be found in certain types of covariate
223   shifts, such as blurriness. On the other hand, images generated from GANs often exhibit severe
224   high-frequency differences, leading the discriminator to focus excessively on these components (Li
225   et al., 2023b). This focus can hinder the generator’s ability to capture low-frequency components. By
226   training the discriminator on reconstructions and generations, and encouraging both to appear more
227   realistic, the discriminator’s boundaries of the ID frequency spectrum become tighter, strengthening
228   its ability to detect OOD samples, as illustrated in Figure 3.

293   4.1   DATASETS
294
      In OOD detection benchmarks, the conventional approach involves designating an entire dataset as
295
      ID and then compiling multiple datasets that lack any semantic overlap with the ID categories to act
296   as OOD sets.
297
298   To ensure consistency in the benchmarking process, we adhere to the methodology proposed by
299
      OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022). Our evaluation encompasses three tasks: (1) Near-OOD, which
      exhibits slight semantic variation compared to ID datasets; (2) Far-OOD, which encompasses both
300
      semantic and domain shifts; and (3) Covariate Shift OOD, involving corruptions within the ID
301
      set. Three datasets are defined as ID: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ImageNet-200 (Le &
302   Yang, 2015), and ImageNet-1K (Russakovsky et al., 2015). Further details on dataset selection and
303   availability are summarized in Appendix A.1.
304
305
      4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
306
307   The evaluation metrics employed in OpenOOD by Yang et al. (2022) are adopted for this work. These
308   two main evaluation metrics are: (1) AUROC, which measures the area under the Receiver Operating
309   Characteristic (ROC) curve, and displays the relationship between True Positive Rate (TPR) and
310   False Positive Rate (FPR); and (2) FPR@95, which measures the FPR when the TPR is equal to
311   95%, with lower scores indicating better performance. The full results are provided in the form
312
      "AUROC/FPR@95%".
313
314   4.3   S ELECTED M ODELS
315
      A set of SOTA models representing the various approaches to OOD detection are trained and employed
316
      as baselines for extensive validation of the proposed approach. GLOW (Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018)
317
      represents generative-based methods, allowing assessment through both Log-Likelihood and the
318   recently proposed typicality (Chali et al., 2023) metric. DDPM-OOD (Graham et al., 2023) is the
319   selected reconstruction-based technique. For feature-based and logit-based methodologies, we opt
320   for MOODv2 (Li et al., 2024), a state-of-the-art model for semantic OOD detection. To explore
321   adversarial techniques, a Deep Convolutional GAN (DC-GAN) (Radford et al., 2015) provides a
322   baseline, alongside a Prescribed GAN (Dieng et al., 2019) to investigate the impact of mode collapse
323   mitigation strategies. For the ImageNet-1K benchmark, we compare against the available public
      models instead of retraining models that have shown limited effectiveness. Specifically, we evaluate

469   6     D ISCUSSION
470
471   Covariate Shift OOD. The extensive validation across multiple datasets and models demonstrates
472   that DisCoNet outperforms the other models in detecting Covariate Shifts by a large margin. The
473   results indicate that relying solely on a GAN, even with enhancements from PresGAN aiming
474   at mitigating mode collapse, is insufficient to achieve high performance. In Section 3, we have
475   hypothesized, supported by literature findings (Lin et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), that training a
476   discriminator with VAE reconstructions would enhance its sensitivity to corruptions that dampen the
477
      high-frequency spectrum. Alternatively, training a discriminator with generated images improves
      its ability to detect high-frequency amplification. The ablation study results in Table 5 validate the
478
      hypothesis from Section 3. Models trained exclusively on reconstructed images, which are typically
479
      blurrier, present significantly higher classification scores in identifying low-frequency corrupted
480   images as OOD compared to high-frequency ones. This inability to detect high-frequency corruptions
481   is particularly evident in CIFAR-10(-C). This behavior can be explained easily: reconstructed images
482   lack significant high-frequency components, leading the discriminator to simply learn to classify
483   images with low high-frequency content as fake and images with high-frequency content as real. This
484   is further supported by the AUROC scores achieved on the noise-related perturbations in CIFAR-
485   10(-C), where the model erroneously considers these samples closer to being ID than the actual ID
      samples. Conversely, models trained on generated images perform better in detecting high-frequency


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      corruptions, as expected. Surprisingly, their ability to detect low-frequency corruptions is remarkably
487   high, indicating that using this method covers a broader spectrum of perturbations.
488
489   Near-OOD and Far-OOD. DisCoNet performs well in both Near-OOD and Far-OOD detection
490
      scenarios, outperforming other approaches when using ImageNet-200 as the ID dataset and continuing
      to consistently rank first in the ImageNet-1K analysis. Additionally, in the CIFAR-10 benchmark,
491
      DisCoNet ranks second only to MOODv2 in various Far-OOD tests; CIFAR-10 poses a greater
492
      challenge for DisCoNet because its pixelated resolution inherently introduces corruptions, making it
493   harder for the model to assign low scores to ID samples.
494
495   Deployment Scenarios. There are generally two main deployment scenarios for OOD detection
496
      algorithms: (1) The OOD detection algorithm is the primary focus, deployed as a standalone
      application. (2) The OOD detection algorithm operates alongside a main image processing algorithm,
497
      ensuring its safe and effective use. An OOD algorithm must be practical and effective in real-world
498
      scenarios, delivering strong detection performance while being highly deployable. Deployability
499   should be assessed in the following four critical areas.
500
501         1. Accessibility: Evaluated by the compute requirements necessary for the algorithm.
502
503         2. Development Cycle: Measured by the time required for model training and deployment.
504         3. Inference Speed: The time it takes for the algorithm to make predictions during deployment.
505
506
            4. Accuracy: The ability of the algorithm to provide highly accurate OOD detection.
507
      An ideal OOD detection algorithm excels in all the above dimensions, ensuring it can be effectively
508
      utilized in various practical applications. As detailed in our results and Appendix A.3, DisCoNet
509
      excels in all criteria. The model achieves SOTA OOD detection results, while utilizing substantially
510   smaller and faster models. Table 12 indicates that DisCoNet is up to 3 orders of magnitude faster
511   than MOODv2 and up to 4 orders of magnitude quicker than GLOW and the DDPM-OOD. Training
512   is also much more efficient than GLOW and DDPM-OOD, as demonstrated in Table 11, making it a
513   strong candidate for applications that require fast development cycles.
514
515
516   7    L IMITATIONS & F UTURE WORK
517
518   One potential limitation of DisCoNet is that, while it has demonstrated promising results on 128×128
519   resolution images, further evaluation on higher resolutions is required to fully assess its scalability.
520   Although performance improvements have been observed with increasing resolution, additional
521   experiments with larger image sizes are necessary to draw definitive conclusions. Future research
522   should prioritize applying DisCoNet to high-resolution domains. Additionally, with regard to
523   covariate shift detection, only three models have been evaluated on ImageNet-1K to date, highlighting
524
      the need for broader benchmarking. Despite DisCoNet’s strong performance, future efforts will focus
      on ensuring a more extensive comparison. Future work will focus on gaining a deeper understanding
525
      of DisCoNet’s internal mechanisms, particularly how it processes changes in the frequency spectrum
526
      from a signal processing perspective.
527
528
529   8    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper introduces DisCoNet, a novel approach for OOD detection, with a focus on covariate
532
      shift detection. Contrary to standard VAE or GAN training objectives, DisCoNet utilizes a combi-
533
      nation of both reconstructed and generated images to address a broad range of frequency-spectrum
534   perturbations for improved performance. To the best of our knowledge, the model demonstrates state-
535   of-the-art OOD detection performance, achieving an AUROC of 96.2% on CIFAR-10(-C), 99.7%
536   on ImageNet-200(-C) and 98.9% on ImageNet-1k(-C). Additionally, DisCoNet shows excellent
537   performance in semantic OOD detection tasks, surpassing or matching current SOTA methods such
538   as MOODv2, NNGuide and SCALE on ImageNet-200 and ImageNet-1K. DisCoNet’s lightweight
539   and fast architecture, along with its efficient training cycles, make it a practical choice for real-world
      and real-time applications requiring low computational resources.

827   as publicly available 6 under an Apache 2.0 License. The method employs a time-conditioned
828   UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) architecture with a simplified training objective where the variance
829   is set to time-dependent constants and the model is trained to directly predict the noise ϵ at each

837
      recent advancement in rapid sampling for diffusion models. During the evaluation, we assess the

839

841   OOD score for each input.

875   A.2.4    NNG UIDE
876   For the implementation of NNGuide, the guidelines in (Park et al., 2023) were followed, as well
877   as the publicly available code 8 under the Apache 2.0 License. NNGuide is a post-hoc, training-

931   To implement PresGAN, we follow the recommendations presented by Dieng et al. (2019) and the
932
      official code repository 11 , which does not state a license. An adapted version of this model is provided

933

934
      making it impossible to evaluate generalization using predictive log-likelihood. PresGANs introduce
935   noise to the output of a density network and optimize an entropy-regularized adversarial loss. This
936   noise enables tractable approximations of the predictive log-likelihood and enhances training stability.
937   The entropy regularizer encourages PresGANs to capture all modes of the data distribution. Fitting
938   PresGANs involves computing intractable gradients of the entropy regularization term, which is
939   addressed by using unbiased stochastic estimates. With θ and ϕ representing the Generator and
940   Discriminator parameters, LGAN (θ, ϕ) the generator’s adversarial loss, λ the hyperparameter that
941   controls the strength of the entropy regularization, and pθ (x) the generative distribution induced by
942   the generative process, the generator’s training objective can be described by
943
944                              LP resGAN (θ, ϕ) = LGAN (θ, ϕ) + λEpθ (x) [log pθ (x)] .                    (11)

949   stabilize training and avoid failure cases, the variance σ of the generative distribution is truncated. Fol-

      lowing the authors’ recommendations, we defined the hyperparameters σmax and σmin that truncate

1535   A.7      F REQUENCY I MPACT OF C ORRUPTIONS
1536
1537
       To assess the impact of corruptions on the frequency spectrum, we first calculated CIFAR-10’s
       average Power Spectral Density (PSD) at its original resolution of 32×32 pixels. This involved
1538
       computing the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on each channel of every image in the dataset. We
1539
       aggregated all FFTs and, following Li et al. (2023b), determined the average Radial Profile of the
1540   dataset through Azimuthal averaging using a publicly available toolkit 13 . The frequency radius is
1541   the distance from the center of the FFT that represents the zero-frequency component; larger radii
1542   correspond to higher frequencies, with a maximum radius of 22 for this image size. We repeated this
1543   process for each corruption at maximum intensity (severity 5 per the benchmark), and the Relative
1544   Radial Profile of the PSD was calculated by dividing the Radial profile of each corrupted dataset by
1545   that of the uncorrupted dataset. Based on their effect on the frequency spectrum, it is possible to
1546   divide the corruptions into two categories: those that enhance high-frequency content and those that
1547   reduce it. Our analysis focuses on radii greater than 13, which represent higher frequency components,
1548
       up to 21, since the final component contains only very residual information.
