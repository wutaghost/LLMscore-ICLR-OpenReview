009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we significantly alleviate the long-standing scalability issue of
012            semidefinite programming (SDP), by equipping a novel tune-free operator stepsize
013            to the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) optimizer. To our
014
               best knowledge, this is the first operator stepsize in the context of SDP. More
               importantly, it is tune-free and computationally cheap (defined on dot product).
015
               Preliminary tests show that our operator ADMM surpasses the acceleration limit of
016
               the standard scalar version (limit found via grid search), i.e., our operator stepsize
017            can outperform an arbitrarily fine tuned scalar one.

020   1    I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Semidefinite programming (SDP) is widely recognized as one of the most important breakthroughs
023   in the last century, with wide applications across fields, including machine learning, control, robotics,
024   and communications. However, there exists a long-standing obstacle for SDP to gain further popularity
025   — the scalability issue. It arises in middle or large scale problems, where an exponentially growing
026   computation cost with data dimension is generally unacceptable. How to improve the scalability
027
      has been intensively studied, with several main directions: (i) exploiting structures, e.g., sparsity,
      symmetry, low-rankness etc.; (ii) approximation via linear and second-order cone programs. (iii)
028
      augmented Lagrangian methods, such as Newton-CG and alternating direction method of multipliers
029
      (ADMM); A comprehensive survey on scalability can be found in Majumdar et al. (2020). This
030   manuscript will focus on the ADMM approach.
031
032   To start, we briefly review some history. SDP was developed as a generalization of linear programming
033
      (LP). The first polynomial-time solver of LP was introduced by Karmarkar (1984), termed the interior-
      point method (IPM). Later, Nesterov & Nemirovsky (1988); Nesterov & Nemirovskii (1994) extended
034
      it to any convex program, provided that the function is self-concordant. SDP can easily satisfy this
035
      condition, and hence been considered not much harder to solve than LP Vandenberghe & Boyd
036   (1996).
037
038   Despite the great successes of IPMs, they are in general not well-suited for problems of middle or
039
      large scale. This is related to their second-order nature, where an inverse of the Hessian matrix is
      required, or at least an approximate inverse. Such an inverse operation is highly expensive for a large
040
      size variable. Even worse, the Hessian matrix is in general dense and rarely admits some structures
041
      like sparsity to reduce the computation cost. In the literature, employing first-order algorithms Beck
042   (2017), Teboulle (2018) is considered one of the most promising directions. An outstanding candidate
043   is ADMM Glowinski & Marroco (1975); Gabay & Mercier (1976). It has become increasingly
044   popular, largely owes to a comprehensive survey by Boyd et al. (2011). In fact, one may already
045   encounter ADMM, except under a different name. In recent years, many well-known algorithms
046   have been revealed as equivalent to ADMM, such as the Douglas-Rachford Splitting (DRS) Lions &
047   Mercier (1979); Douglas & Rachford (1956) and the Primal-Dual Hybrid Gradient (PDHG) method
048   Pock et al. (2009); Esser et al. (2010); Chambolle & Pock (2011); O’Connor & Vandenberghe (2020).
049   The procedures for the first-order algorithms to solve SDP are largely similar, mainly differ on how to
050   guarantee the solution being positive semidefinite (PSD). There are 3 typical strategies. (i) Directly
051   define the variable in a quadratic form RT R, which is always PSD, see e.g. Burer & Monteiro
052   (2003; 2005); Wang et al. (2023). However, its efficiency highly depends on the dimension of R,
053   i.e., only efficient if it is low-rank. (ii) Enforcing PSD by a projected variable, denoted as ΠS+ (X).
      The success owes to that projector ΠS+ is strongly semi-smooth Sun & Sun (2002), and an inexact

054
      semi-smooth Newton-CG method can apply, see e.g. Zhao et al. (2010). (iii) The last approach is
055   via ADMM, which is a general framework that can apply to general convex problems, even some
056   non-convex issues, see Boyd et al. (2011). Its application to SDP is well studied in Wen et al. (2010).
057
058
      Our work corresponds to the ADMM method, most related to Wen et al. (2010). We achieved
      significant advances. (i) To our best knowledge, ours is the first operator stepsize in the context of
059
      SDP, not limited to the ADMM solver. For example, a diagonal matrix stepsize is not applicable here
060
      (no closed-form iterates). (ii) Our operator is specially designed, inspired by the Schur complement
061   lemma. It enjoys the benefits of closed-form ADMM iterates and low computational cost (defined
062   on dot product). (iii) Our operator stepsize is tune-free. It will be automatically updated based on
063   a certain degree-4 polynomial. Numerically, we observed significant advantages compared to the
064   empirical choice of scalar stepsize 1 and 1.6, as suggested in Wen et al. (2010). Even more, we
065   performed a grid search to find the best scalar stepsize choice (least iteration number complexity
066   sense), which can be viewed as the acceleration limit (not a priori knowledge). Preliminary tests
067   show that our operator stepsize has surpassed such a limit.
068   For notations, ∥ · ∥ denotes the Euclidean norm, induced by the inner product ⟨·, ·⟩. By ◦ we denote
069   the operator composition. The uppercase bold, lowercase bold, and not bold letters are used for
070   matrices, vectors, and scalars, respectively.

      5   C ONCLUSION
519
520
      For the first time, an operator stepsize is designed for semidefinite programming, with a special
521
      structure inspired by the Schur complement lemma. It enjoys several nice properties, including
522   closed-form iterates, cheap computational cost (owing to dot product), and tune-free. Compared to
523   the standard scalar stepsize, our operator one admits extra degrees of freedom, which mathematically
524   allows it to surpass the acceleration limit (of the standard version). This aspect has been confirmed
525   numerically, where preliminary tests show great advantages in iteration number complexity and
526   runtime. Overall, we believe our operator ADMM significantly alleviated the long-standing scalability
527   issue of semidefinite programming.
