008                                               A BSTRACT
009            Structure-informed protein representation learning is essential for effective protein
010            function annotation and de novo design. However, the presence of inherent noise
011            in both crystal and AlphaFold-predicted structures poses significant challenges for
012            existing methods in learning robust protein representations. To address these is-
013            sues, we propose a novel equivariant Transformer-State Space Model(SSM) hybrid
014            framework, termed E 3 former, designed for efficient protein representation. Our
015            approach leverages energy function-based receptive fields to construct proximity
016
               graphs and incorporates an equivariant high-tensor-elastic selective SSM within
               the transformer architecture. These components enable the model to adapt to com-
017
               plex atom interactions and extract geometric features with higher signal-to-noise
018
               ratios. Empirical results demonstrate that our model outperforms existing methods
019            in structure-intensive tasks, such as inverse folding and binding site prediction,
020            particularly when using predicted structures, owing to its enhanced tolerance to
021            data deviation and noise. Our approach offers a novel perspective for conducting
022            biological function research and drug discovery using noisy protein structure data.
023            Our code is available on https://anonymous.4open.science/r/E3former-207E.

025
026   Protein representation learning plays a crucial role in advancing our understanding and application
027
      of the structural and biological functions of proteins. A wide array of protein-related tasks, such as
      predicting interactions, annotating functions, and designing protein binders, depend heavily on the
028
      development of robust protein representations. (Tubiana et al., 2022) (Bushuiev et al., 2023) (Lisanza
029
      et al., 2023) (Gligorijević et al., 2021).
030
031   The recent advancements in experimental technologies,
032   coupled with the groundbreaking development of protein
033
      structure prediction models like AlphaFold (Jumper et al.,
      2021), have significantly increased the availability of de-
034
      tailed protein structural data. This surge in data has shifted
035
      the focus of protein representation learning towards effec-
036   tively harnessing this rich structural information. However,
037   the existence of inherent noise in crystal and AlphaFold-
038   predicted structures (Acharya & Lloyd, 2005; Moore et al., Figure 1: Noise mechanism in equivari-
039   2022) presents substantial challenges for current methods ant GNN based on cut-off radius graph.
040   in learning robust protein representations. In response to these challenges, the development of
041   geometric deep learning approaches that are tolerant to noise or the deviations in protein structures
042   data has become crucial.
043   Since the latest progress in equivariant neural networks has demonstrated their good ability in
044   handling diverse protein structure data (Duval et al., 2023), exploring the robustness representation
045   learning of structural information based on this framework has emerged as a pivotal breakthrough.
046   Although existing equivariant deep learning models like EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021), GearNet
047   (Zhang et al., 2022), spherical harmonics-based models (Liao & Smidt, 2022) and protein specific
048   model GCPNet (Morehead & Cheng, 2024) architectures exhibit strong performance, they still show
049   high sensitive to data bias or noise (Joshi et al., 2023). This paper aims to develop a model tailored
050
      for representing protein macromolecular data within the equivariant neural network framework. The
      model can adaptively alleviate the effects of noise and data bias, while also tackling the dynamic of
051
      macromolecules.
052
053   The primary factor contributing to model sensitivity to noise is the offset of atomic positions in
      3D Euclidean space. On the one hand, the model’s estimation of node attributes is significantly

066
      2018), E 3 former leverages an energy-aware radius function and radius sampler to adaptively
067   modify the receptive field based on the atoms environment in 3D Euclidean space, thereby mitigating
068   the effects of data biases on constructing protein proximity graphs. (2) A novel equivariant elastic
069   selective SSM is proposed to extract and compress high-order tensors that are particularly sensitive
070   to rotations. We use the parameter-sharing SSM module as the sparse representation of Transformer
071   (Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024), and utilize spherical harmonics-based models to handle irreducible
072   representations in tensors of various orders. By performing a separate sparse representation of the
073   high-dimensional tensor and processing it with a parameter-sharing matrix, the model can enhance
074   the information encoding of biased data with a heightened signal-to-noise ratio. (3) Creating a new
075
      dataset version based on the Alphafold structure for established public tasks to systematically
      assess the tolerance of each model to data bias and noise. (4) Empirical results demonstrate that our
076
      model achieves overall better results across all tasks compared to previous state-of-the-art methods
077
      on the Alphafold-predicted datasets (Table 2). Benefiting from its anti-noise capabilities, the model
078   outperformed the state-of-the-art(SOTA) methods by 11.2% in the inverse folding task. In the

081   robust protein representations that can address biases in prediction data and inherent noise present in
082   crystal structures.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
085   2.1 S TRUCTURED - BASED P ROTEIN REPRESENTATION LEARNING
086   With advancements in experimental and structure prediction technologies, the availability of protein
087   structure data has significantly expanded (Jumper et al., 2021) (wwPDB consortium, 2018) (Baek
088   et al., 2021). A range of representation learning approaches for protein structures have emerged.
089   Voxelized representation-based methods map the three-dimensional structure of proteins into
090
      voxelized 3D volumes and encode the atomic system using techniques such as 3D convolutional
      neural networks(3DCNNS). For instance, a series of methods like (Pagès et al., 2019) (Anand et al.,
091
      2022) (Liu et al., 2021)leverage 3DCNNs to encode protein structures, demonstrating the efficacy of
092
      this representation and encoding approach across diverse tasks. Furthermore, Metal3D (Dürr et al.,
093   2023) integrates multiple physical and chemical properties as inputs based on this representation,
094   enriching the environmental information within the framework of 3DCNNs. On the other line, Graph-
095   structured representations-based methods for protein structure involve mapping the structure of
096   proteins as a proximity graph over amino acid nodes, leveraging Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
097   to capture intricate interactions among nodes (Han et al., 2024). GVP Jing et al. (2020) employing
098   equivariant GNNs for computational protein design and model quality assessment. Methods like
099   Schnet (Schütt et al., 2018) and Scannet (Tubiana et al., 2022)integrate 3D spatial information and
100   chemical features of atoms within GNN frameworks, applying these models to tasks such as protein
101
      binding site prediction. EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021), GearNet-Edge (Zhang et al., 2022), GCPNet
      (Morehead & Cheng, 2024), and other methods have also made significant architectural advancements
102
      in GNNs, enhancing their capabilities in modeling complex relationships within protein structures.
103
      Although powerful, these models are mainly designed for rigid experimental structures and struggle
104   with the inherent noise present in real-world protein applications.
105

      3.3 A RCHITECTURE OF E 3 FORMER
271
      The E 3 former architecture comprises an energy graph module (Section 3.1), an equivariant SSM
272   (Section 3.2), and a SE(3)-equivariant transformer module(Equiformer) (Liao & Smidt, 2022). In the
273   context of a protein graph G = (V, E), each node vi ∈ V corresponds to an amino acid. To mitigate
274   computational complexity, we adopt a Cα -based graph representation method as the foundation and
275   integrate various featurization strategies to incorporate additional structural insights (Jamasb et al.,
276   2024). The node vi possesses a feature encoding hiv and the following features include:
277
      • Residue Type (not used in inverse folding task), a 16-dimensional transformer-like positional
278
        Encoding (Vaswani, 2017).
279
280   • Backbone dihedral angles ϕ, ψ, ω ∈ R6 .
281   • Virtual torsion and bond angles κ, α ∈ R4 defined over Cα atoms.
282
283
      • Feature ⃗r between nodes representing the displacement vector between nodes.
284   During each training or inference stage, the energy-driven radius sampler establishes the adjacency
285   relationship E between nodes. Subsequently, it encapsulates the aforementioned protein features
286   and inputs them into the kth layer of the equivariant transformer block to derive the feature matrix
287   T k. This T k matrix undergoes a high-tensor leaky operation to generate the input Xk in in the kth
288   layer of the equivariant-elastic-SSM block. The final step involves concatenating the output of the
289   multi-layer SSM block {yk |k = 1, 2, ..., N } with the output of the last layer of the transformer block
290
      T k, followed by mapping them into the final node output matrix Hout .

474   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
475
      In this work, we proposed an Energy-aware Elastic Equivariant Transformer-SSM hybrid architecture
476
      for 3D macromolecular structure representation learning. The core of the model is to adaptively
477
      utilize the energy-aware module to bulid proximity graph and use the equivariant SSM module to
478   express high-order features sparsely. The above improvements enable model more tolerant to data
479   deviation and noise. The experimental results demonstrate the superior performance of E3former on
480   inverse folding, binding sites prediction, and protein-protein interaction tasks.
481
      Limitation E 3 former mainly performing operations on the 3D atom system derived from protein
482
      structures, neglecting the comprehensive utilization of others modal information (such as sequential
483
      information, chemical bond specifics, protein functional annotations, etc.), which may limits the
484   performance of the method.

      CATH The CATH dataset is a collection of protein structures curated by (Ingraham et al., 2019).
      In the CATH 4.2 40% non-redundant set of proteins, only chains up to a length of 500 are retained.
654
      Any chains from test set with CATH topology classifications overlap(CAT) with train are excluded.
655
      Consequently, the training, validation, and test splits consist of 18204, 608, and 1120 structures,
656   respectively. CATH or their Alphafold-predicted version are utilized to Inverse folding task. In this
657   node classification task, the model is trained to learn a mapping function for each residue to an amino
658   acid type y ∈ {1, ..., n}, where the vocabulary size is n = 20, representing the 20 common amino
659   acids.
660
      PPBS We utilized the PPBS dataset as compiled by (Tubiana et al., 2022) following the data division
661
      method they outlined. Based on the Dockground database of protein-protein interfaces (Kundrotas
662   et al., 2018), each unique PDB chain involved in one or more interfaces is considered a single example.
663   Chains with a sequence length of less than 10 or involved in designed proteins are excluded from the
664   dataset.In particular, the validation set and test set in PPBS are divided according to the following
665   criteria:
666
      • 70%: at least 70% sequence similarity to one sample in the training set.
667
668   • Homology: at most 70% sequence similarity with any sample in the training set and belong to the
669     same protein superfamily with at least one training sample.
670   • Topology: sharing a similar topological structure (T level of CATH classification (Sillitoe et al.,
671     2021)) with at least one training sample and do not belong to the 70% dataset or the Homology
672     dataset.
673
      • None: none of the above.
674
675   • All: the combination of the divisions mentioned above.
676
      In this node-level binary classification task, the model is trained to learn a mapping function for
677
      each residue to 0/1 types, in order to discover potential protein-protein interfaces between the target
678   protein and other proteins.

      MASIF-SITE is a dataset proposed by (Gainza et al., 2020), sourced from the PRISM list of
690   nonredundant proteins (Baspinar et al., 2014), the ZDock benchmark (Liu et al., 2015), and SabDab
691   (Dunbar et al., 2014). To ensure a fair comparison within the benchmark framework, we adopted
692   the data labeling methodology outlined by (Jamasb et al., 2024) and set the binding threshold at
693   3.5Å. Since the labels of the corresponding Alphafold predicted version are difficult to align with the
694   original dataset, only original dataset utilized for the experiments. The primary challenge in mapping
695   the MASIF dataset to AlphaFold-predicted structures arose during sequence alignment using the
696   BLOSUM62 global alignment algorithm. We observed that the resulting alignments were highly
697   fragmented, resulting in short, disconnected segments rather than continuous regions. Given these
698
      difficulties in obtaining reliable results, we made the decision to exclude the AlphaFold-predicted
      dataset from our benchmark experiments.

700   This dataset is used to evaluate protein–protein interaction site prediction in protein surfaces, and in
701   this node-level binary classification task, models are trained to map each residue into 0/1 type for
      detecting the PPI sites.

      CATH-AF The CATH-AF dataset is created by mapping CATH entries to UniProt IDs within
703   the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database 1 . Since the original CATH dataset only includes index
704   information as PDB IDs, we used the PDBe REST API2 to convert these PDB IDs into the UniProt
705   IDs required by the AlphaFold database. All predicted protein structures are retrieved from the
706   AlphaFold V4 protein structure database. However, some PDB IDs could not be matched to UniProt
707   IDs during the conversion process, and due to limitations of the AlphaFold model3 , predictions
708   for certain proteins could not be generated. Consequently, the CATH-AF dataset contains fewer
709   structures than the original CATH dataset, with 16,468 files in the training set, 559 in the validation
710   set, and 1,015 in the test set. Additionally, the CATH-AF dataset is proposed as a resource for
711
      protein inverse folding tasks, particularly for evaluating the protein design capabilities of models
      using AlphaFold-predicted structures.
712
713   PPBS-AF We construct a new dataset based on the PPBS dataset using structures predicted by
714   AlphaFold. Following a similar approach to that used in CATH-AF, we employ the PDBe REST API
715   to convert the data into the UniProt IDs required by the AlphaFold database. With pre-calculated
716   protein binding sites provided by (Kundrotas et al., 2018), we apply the BLOSUM62 global alignment
717
      algorithm (Styczynski et al., 2008) to align the sequences of the AlphaFold-predicted structures with
      those of the corresponding experimental structures. This allows us to map the active sites from the
718
      reference structures onto the predicted structures. After excluding protein chains for which predicted
719
      structures are unavailable, we ultimately obtain a training set with 11,345 structures, a validation set
720   with 2,876 structures, and a test set with 3,636 structures.

771

777                                        δZ1 ≈ WT 1 ([δθ]× X + δt).

      In this work, we propose an adaptive energy-aware elastic equivariant Transformer model for learning
      protein representations. Additionally, we introduce two datasets based on Alphafold-predicted protein
926
      structures, specifically designed to tackle challenges in protein design and function prediction in
927

928   experimentally determined protein structures, as well as the systematic errors inherent in Alphafold-
929   predicted structures.
