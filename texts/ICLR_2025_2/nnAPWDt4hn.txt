009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in foundation models have enhanced AI systems’ capabili-
012            ties in autonomous tool usage and reasoning. However, their ability in location or
013            map-based reasoning - which improves daily life by optimizing navigation, facili-
014            tating resource discovery, and streamlining logistics - has not been systematically
015            studied. To bridge this gap, we introduce M AP E VAL, a benchmark designed to
016
               assess diverse and complex map-based user queries with geo-spatial reasoning.
               M AP E VAL features three task types (textual, API-based, and visual) that require
017
               collecting world information via map tools, processing heterogeneous geo-spatial
018
               contexts (e.g., named entities, travel distances, user reviews or ratings, images),
019            and compositional reasoning, which all state-of-the-art foundation models find
020            challenging. Comprising 700 unique multiple-choice questions about locations
021            across 180 cities and 54 countries, M AP E VAL evaluates foundation models’ abil-
022            ity to handle spatial relationships, map infographics, travel planning, and navi-
023            gation challenges. Using M AP E VAL, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation
024            of 28 prominent foundation models. While no single model excelled across all
025            tasks, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini-1.5-Pro achieved competitive per-
026            formance overall. However, substantial performance gaps emerged, particularly in
027
               M AP E VAL -API, where agents with Claude-3.5-Sonnet outperformed GPT-4o and
               Gemini-1.5-Pro by 16% and 21%, respectively, and the gaps became even more
028
               amplified when compared to open-source LLMs. Our detailed analyses provide
029
               insights into the strengths and weaknesses of current models, though all models
030            still fall short of human performance by more than 20% on average, struggling
031            with complex map images and rigorous geo-spatial reasoning. This gap high-
032            lights M AP E VAL’s critical role in advancing general-purpose foundation models
033            with stronger geo-spatial understanding.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Recent advancements in foundation models, particularly large language models (LLMs) and vision-
039   language models (VLMs), are significantly enhancing the capabilities of AI systems in autonomous
040   tool usage (Qin et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2022) and reasoning (Lu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022). These
041   developments facilitate the automation of everyday tasks through natural language instructions, es-
042   pecially in domains that require interaction with specialized tools like map services.
043   As platforms such as Google Maps or Apple Maps have become ubiquitous for accessing various
044   location-based services (a.k.a tools/APIs) —ranging from finding nearby restaurants to determining
045   the fastest routes between origins and destinations—there has been a growing interest in integrating
046   maps with foundation models (Xie et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). A couple of recent initiatives,
047   such as WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023) and VisualWebArena (Koh et al., 2024), have introduced new
048   tasks that involve map usage in practical scenarios.
049   However, despite the widespread adoption of map services and the promising potential of interac-
050   tions between foundation models (e.g., LLMs and VLMs) and these services, no existing studies
051   have rigorously tested the capabilities of foundation models in location or geo-spatial reasoning.
052   This gap is critical, as effective map-based reasoning can optimize navigation, facilitate resource
053   discovery, and streamline logistics in everyday life. Addressing this gap is essential for advancing
      the practical utility of AI in real-world applications.

077   We introduce M AP E VAL, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate the geo-spatial reasoning capa-
078   bilities of foundation models and AI agents in complex map-based scenarios. M AP E VAL addresses
079   a critical gap in existing benchmarks by evaluating models’ ability to process heterogeneous geo-
080
      spatial contexts, perform compositional reasoning, and interact with real-world map tools. It features
      three task types— API, V ISUAL, and T EXTUAL—that require models to collect world information
081
      via map tools, a deep visual understanding, and reason over diverse geo-spatial data (e.g., named
082
      entities, coordinates, operational hours, distances, routes, user reviews/ratings, map images), all of
083   which remain challenging for state-of-the-art foundation models. Comprising 700 unique multiple-
084   choice questions across 180 cities and 54 countries, M AP E VAL reflects real-world user interactions
085   with map services while pushing state-of-the-art models to understand spatial relationships, map
086   infographics, travel planning, POI search, and navigation. M AP E VAL ensures geographic diversity,
087   realistic query patterns, and evaluation across multiple modalities. By integrating long contexts,
088   visual complexity, API interactions, and questions requiring commonsense reasoning or recogni-
089   tion of insufficient information (i.e., unanswerability), it offers a rigorous framework for advancing
090   geo-spatial AI capabilities. In Fig 1, we depict an overview of M AP E VAL.
091   With M AP E VAL, we evaluated 28 prominent foundation models, where Claude-3.5-Sonnet, GPT-4o,
092   and Gemini-1.5-Pro showed competitive performance overall. However, significant gaps emerged
093   in M AP E VAL -API, with Claude-3.5-Sonnet agents outperforming GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5-Pro by
094   16% and 21%, respectively, and even larger disparities compared to open-source models. Our
095   detailed analyses revealed further insights into model strengths and weaknesses. Despite these
096   advances, all models still fall short of human performance by over 20%, especially in handling
097
      complex map images and rigorous reasoning, underscoring M AP E VAL’s role in advancing geo-
      spatial understanding. The benchmarking dataset and evaluation code will be open-sourced at
098
      https://github.com/MapEval.

101   2   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Geo-spatial question answering presents significant challenges for foundation models (Mai et al.,
104   2023). Early research in GeoQA (Mai et al., 2021) has focused on template-based methods (Zelle
105   and Mooney, 1996; Chen et al., 2013; Chen, 2014; Punjani et al., 2018; Kefalidis et al., 2023), where
106   predefined templates classify queries and retrieve information from structured databases like Open-
107   StreetMap or DBpedia (Auer et al., 2007). While effective in certain scenarios, these methods are
      constrained by the static nature of the databases and the predefined templates, limiting their flex-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ibility in handling complex or dynamic queries. There has been limited effort to assess (Roberts
109   et al., 2023) and improve (Balsebre et al., 2024) LLMs’ capabilities in geospatial reasoning. Recent
110   benchmarks such as Travel Planner (Xie et al., 2024), ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023), and API-Bank
111   (Li et al., 2023) integrate map tools and APIs for location-based queries. While these benchmarks
112   handle real-world tasks like itinerary planning or querying map data, the use of map APIs is lim-
113   ited to more straightforward use cases, such as calculating distances or identifying nearby points
114   of interest. In addition, remote sensing research (Bastani et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Zhang
115   et al., 2024; Lobry et al., 2020) has focused on extracting physical features from satellite imagery.
116   While valuable for environmental monitoring and urban planning, this approach differs significantly
117
      from the task of reasoning over interactive digital map views, which involve understanding spatial
      relationships, map symbols, and navigation elements in a dynamic, user-interactive context.

122   3.1   D ESIGN P RINCIPLES
123
124   Reasoning. Geo-spatial reasoning in map-based tasks presents distinct challenges for founda-
125
      tion models, including: (a) understanding complex problem descriptions in natural language, (b)
      collecting relevant world information using map tools or APIs, (c) performing compositional and
126
      spatio-temporal reasoning, (d) interpreting map visuals, and (e) synthesizing information from het-
127
      erogeneous geo-spatial contexts (e.g., named entities, distances, and temporal values). These tasks
128   test the limits of state-of-the-art models, which struggle to fully grasp geo-spatial relationships,
129   navigation complexities, and POIs.
130
131
      Realistic. M AP E VAL reflects real-world map usage by capturing typical user interactions with map
      services, such as: (a) varied usage patterns like location-based searches and travel planning, and (b)
132
      informal, often fragmented queries, without relying on perfect grammar or structure.
133
134   Diversity. M AP E VAL ensures geographic diversity and broad evaluation across models and tasks:
135   (a) capturing locations across cities and countries globally, and (b) offering a wide variety of ques-
136   tion types and contexts, which test foundation models’ spatial, temporal, data retrieval, and visual
137
      reasoning abilities.
138   Long Contexts, Multi-modality, API Interactions. M AP E VAL challenges models with: (a) long
139   geo-spatial descriptions, including POIs and navigational data, (b) complex map-specific images
140   with location markers, and (c) API interactions, testing models’ abilities as language agents in real-
141   world map-based tasks.
142   Unanswerability, Commonsense. M AP E VAL includes questions where context is insufficient to
143   provide an answer, testing models’ ability to identify missing or incomplete information, rather
144   than making incorrect guesses. It also assesses commonsense reasoning and handling uncertainty,
145   essential for reliable decision-making in real-world applications.
146
      Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs). We employ MCQs in M AP E VAL, similar to MMLU
147   (Hendrycks et al., 2020), rather than open-ended queries. This approach circumvents the evaluation
148   challenges associated with generated responses (Sai et al., 2022), allowing for a more straightfor-
149   ward and reliable accuracy-based assessment of map-based reasoning capabilities.
150
151   3.2   TASKS
152
153   Textual. The objective of M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL is to answer MCQs by decomposing complex
154   queries and extracting relevant information from long textual contexts. These contexts describe map
155   locations, POIs, routes, navigation details, and travel distances/times, often including user ratings
156   or reviews. Unlike typical reading comprehension tasks, these texts combine structured data (e.g.,
157   coordinates, distances) with unstructured narratives and subjective content. The model must reason
158
      over this heterogeneous information to select the correct answer. This task evaluates the model’s
      ability to analyze fine-grained map-related information presented in text.
159
160   API. In the M AP E VAL -API task, an AI agent interacts with map-based APIs to retrieve data (e.g.,
161   nearby POIs, distance calculations). The task involves generating API queries based on user ques-
      tions, interpreting the returned structured data, and integrating it into reasoning processes to answer

      3.3      DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
191
192   Data Annotation. To create a high-quality benchmark dataset for M AP E VAL, we utilized Google
193   Maps, a widely adopted map service. The process of constructing the textual context presented
194   significant challenges, particularly in ensuring accuracy and efficiency. For an example question
195   like “What are the opening hours of the British Museum?” requires precise data to provide valid
196   options and a correct answer. Manually searching for the ”British Museum” on Google Maps and
197   looking for its opening hours can be both time-consuming and prone to errors, making this method
198   inefficient. To address these challenges, we employed MapQaTor, a web interface built on Google
199
      Maps APIs, designed to streamline the collection of textual map data. MapQaTor automates data
      retrieval from map APIs, collecting key information like opening hours and location details to build
200
      the textual dataset (Details in Appendix B.1). For each user query, we first fetch the necessary
201
      context data using MapQaTor. Questions were then paired with their corresponding contexts, and
202   multiple-choice options were carefully curated based on this information. The ground truth answers
203   were derived from the same context.
204
205
      For M AP E VAL -API, the same questions were used as in M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL, but without textual
      contexts, requiring the language agents to interact with tools directly. To address consistency issues
206
      with real-time data updates, we created a controlled evaluation environment. This involves caching
207
      place information and simulating API interactions. Details of the pseudo-Google Maps setup are
208   provided in Appendix C.
209
210   For the visual context, we capture map snapshots from Google Maps, covering random locations
211
      across various cities and countries worldwide. Based on each snapshot, we formulate relevant ques-
      tions with multiple-choice options, where the correct labels are derived directly from the map infor-
212
      mation. To maintain traceability, we save the Google Maps URL for each snapshot. Additionally, to
213
      examine model capabilities at different zoom levels, we capture snapshots at varying zoom depths1 .

238   We create the following question types for M AP E VAL: (a) Place Info: detects POIs and asks about
239   specific details related to a place (e.g., location, rating, reviews); (b) Nearby: identifies nearby
240   places or POIs; (c) Routing: navigates between locations, considering routes and landmarks; (d)
241   Unanswerable: when the map information (e.g., from google map) or the textual and visual context
242   is insufficient to answer the question. Note that, in each category we formulate a few questions that
243   requires general knowledge or reasoning about locations and navigation (e.g., there are 52 common-
244
      sense QAs in M AP E VAL -V ISUAL).
245   Moreover, M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL and M AP E VAL -API exclusively feature Trip questions, which in-
246   volve planning multi-stop journeys across various POIs. Due to the complexity and details of trip
247   planning, these questions are difficult to represent in a single visual snapshot. Conversely, Count-
248   ing tasks are unique to M AP E VAL -V ISUAL, where models count specific items or locations on a
249   map—a challenge specifically tailored to visual contexts.
250   Quality Control and Human Performance To ensure quality, each QA pair is annotated by multi-
251   ple members of our team, achieving an initial 76% mutual agreement. At least two team members
252   then manually verify and resolve any disputes on the remaining pairs; if consensus cannot be reached
253   (i.e., ambiguous), that pair is filtered out. To compute human scores, two team members who did
254   not participate in the annotation process attempt to answer the questions, and their highest-scoring
255   attempts are reported as the human performance benchmark. For M AP E VAL -API, as the questions
256
      are identical to M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL, we report the same human performance for both.

285   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL P ROTOCOL AND S ETUP
286
287   We evaluate all tasks using the accuracy metric, defined as the percentage of correct choices selected
288   by the model. We prompt models with the respective context, question, tool usage documentations
289   (only for M AP E VAL -API), answer format guidelines, and choices. We assess LLMs for M AP E VAL -
290   T EXTUAL, VLMs for M AP E VAL -V ISUAL, and ReACT agents Yao et al. (2022) (known for effective
291
      tool interaction (Zhuang et al., 2023)) built on various LLMs for M AP E VAL -API, aligning each
      task with appropriate model types. Appendix F presents example prompts for all tasks. Our LLMs
292
      and VLMs spans both open and closed-source models. Closed-source models include Claude-3.5-
293
      Sonnet, GPT-4o, GPT-4-Turbo (Achiam et al., 2023), GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI, 2022), Gemini-1.5
294   (Pro, Flash; Reid et al. (2024)), with all except GPT-3.5-Turbo being multi-modal foundation models
295   used in all tasks, while GPT-3.5-Turbo, which is text-only, is utilized solely in the M AP E VAL -
296   T EXTUAL and M AP E VAL -API tasks. Open-source LLMs include instruct versions of Gemma-
297   2.0 (9B, 27B; Team et al. (2024)), Llama-3.2 (3B, 90B), Llama-3.1 (8B, 70B; Vavekanand and
298   Sam (2024)) , Mistral-Nemo-7B, Mixtral-8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024), Qwen2.5 (7B, 14B, 72B; Team
299   (2024)) , Phi-3.5-mini. For M AP E VAL -V ISUAL, we considered the open-source VLMs: Qwen2-
300   VL-7B-Instruct (Wang et al., 2024), MiniCPM-Llama3-V-2 5 (Yao et al., 2024), Llama-3-VILA1.5-
301   8B (Lin et al., 2023), glm-4v-9b (GLM et al., 2024), InternLm-xcomposer2 (Dong et al., 2024),
302
      paligemma-3b-mix-224 (Beyer et al., 2024), DocOwl1.5 (Hu et al., 2024), llava-v1.6-mistral-7b-hf
      (Liu et al., 2024b), and llava-1.5-7b-hf (Liu et al., 2024a). In M AP E VAL -API task, we concentrate
303
      our exploration on high-capacity open-source LLMs, specifically Llama-3.2-90B, Llama-3.1-70B,
304
      Mixtral-8x7B, and Gemma-2.0-9B. We limit our evaluation of open-source models in AI agents due
305   to the task’s complexity and resource demands, the lower performance of smaller models, and the
306   excessive number of calls for both LLMs and map APIs.

418   4.3     Q UALITATIVE E RROR A NALYSIS
419
420   LLMs face challenges in spatial, temporal, and commonsense reasoning when answering location-
421   based queries. In spatial reasoning, they struggle with straight-line distances (Example at Listing
422   1), cardinal directions (e.g., East, West, North, South; Example at Listing 2), and step-by-step route
423
      planning, leading to decreased accuracy, particularly with math or counting (e.g., nearby restaurant
      counts; Example at Listing 3). Temporal reasoning issues include failing to plan trips efficiently or
424
      calculate optimal visiting times, such as errors in travel times or visit durations (Example at Listing
425
      4). Commonsense reasoning failures occur when models cannot deduce simple conclusions from
426   context and often hallucinates (Example at Listing 5). LLM-based agents also face challenges us-
427   ing map tools or APIs, particularly in Nearby and Routing queries. Misuse or misinterpretation of
428   parameters leads to failed results, such as omitting key parameters or using incompatible values.
429   When encountering no valid routes or results, agents may fall into infinite loops, repeatedly issu-
430   ing identical requests without adjusting their approach. These issues highlight the need for better
431   API handling and error recovery mechanisms. In visual tasks, VLMs often struggle with spatial
      awareness, showing confusion when POIs are visually close together or incorrectly identifying and

531
      In this paper, we introduce M AP E VAL, a comprehensive benchmark dataset designed to assess foun-
      dation models in geo-spatial reasoning through textual, API-based, and visual evaluation modes.
532
      M AP E VAL incorporates diverse real-world scenarios to thoroughly evaluate model capabilities on
533
      geo-spatial reasoning tasks. Our findings reveal that while leading models like Claude-3.5-Sonnet,
534   GPT-4o, and Gemini-1.5-Pro excel in certain areas, they still significantly underperform compared
535   to human accuracy, especially when using open-source foundation models. This highlights critical
536   areas for improvement, especially in managing complex map-based queries that require multi-step
537   spatio-temporal reasoning, efficient tool utilization, and domain-specific knowledge. Future work
538   could focus on developing specialized geospatial models, integrating LLMs with external tools like
539   map APIs, and enhancing VLMs’ visual understanding of map images. We anticipate that M AP E-
      VAL will catalyze ongoing research in geospatial reasoning and broader QA domains.

      L IMITATIONS
550
      Our dataset does not cover all available Google Maps APIs, which limits the scope of our evaluation.
551
      Specifically, we have used five APIs from the Places and Routes categories: Text Search, Place
552
      Details, Nearby Search, Directions, and Distance Matrix. However, we did not incorporate other
553   API categories such as Maps and Environment. This restricted API usage narrows the variety of
554   queries we could evaluate and may leave out other valuable geospatial insights that could be gained
555   from broader API usage.
556
      Furthermore, any future updates to the APIs we used may not be reflected in our dataset, which could
557
      impact its relevance for real-time applications, potentially making it outdated and more suitable for
558
      archival purposes.
559
560   Another limitation is that the performance observed in our evaluation may not transfer to other
561   domains or tools, as we did not explore this possibility. The generalizability of our methods remains
562
      an area for future research.
563   Finally, different prompt formulations could lead to variations in the results, but we did not exper-
564   iment with this aspect. Future work could focus on examining how different prompts affect the
565   LLM’s performance in geospatial reasoning tasks.

761   A.1   M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL
762
763
      Template-based GeoQA models (Zelle and Mooney, 1996; Chen et al., 2013; Chen, 2014; Punjani
      et al., 2018; Kefalidis et al., 2023) have predominantly followed a two-step strategy for answer-
764
      ing geographic questions: (1) classifying a natural language query into predefined templates and
765
      (2) using these templates to query structured geographic knowledge sources such as PostGIS, DB-
766   pedia (Auer et al., 2007), YAGO (Suchanek et al., 2007), Freebase (Bollacker et al., 2007), and
767   OpenStreetMap. While these approaches are effective for structured queries, they are limited by
768   the predefined question templates and their reliance on static databases. They typically convert nat-
769   ural language questions into structured query language scripts. For instance, GeoQuestions1089
770   (Kefalidis et al., 2023) contains 1089 questions with corresponding GeoSPARQL (Open Geospatial
771   Consortium, 2011) queries over the YAGO2geo (Karalis et al., 2019) geospatial knowledge graph.
772   In contrast, our M AP E VAL -T EXTUAL approach shifts the focus from database querying to assessing
773   geospatial reasoning in Large Language Models (LLMs). Annotators collect factual map services
774   data using MapQaTor, which is then provided as context to LLMs. This setup isolates and eval-
775   uates the model’s ability to reason over geospatial relationships, addressing the challenge of free-
776   form, complex map-related queries in a dynamic environment. This approach allows for a more
777   holistic evaluation of LLMs, reflecting real-world usage where users interact with map tools using
778   natural language queries. Thus, in MapEval, the responsibility lies with LLMs to answer the ques-
779   tions, whereas in previous works, the models were tasked with generating queries (e.g., Geoquery,
780
      GeoSPARQL), which are used to query external knowledge bases.
781   GPT4GEO (Roberts et al., 2023) explored GPT-4’s factual geographic knowledge by characterizing
782   what it ”knows” about the world without plugins or Internet access. Their evaluation focused on an-
783   alyzing a single model using templated queries about generic location and direction-oriented facts,
784   such as routing, navigation, and planning for well-known cities and places. However, this approach
785   is inherently constrained by the training data of GPT-4, making it incapable of answering ques-
786
      tions about less-known places. While the findings suggest that GPT-4 shows promising geo-spatial
      knowledge, this approach neither establishes a benchmark for geo-spatial reasoning nor incorporates
787
      real-life user queries or map services (e.g., Google Maps) as a geospatial information base.
788
789   Our approach employs fundamentally different evaluation and design principles. We establish a
790   benchmarking of deeper geo-spatio-temporal reasoning capabilities across multiple foundation mod-
791   els using real user queries rather than templates. Uniquely, our evaluation encompasses multimodal
792
      understanding, tool interactions, and answerability determination. Additionally, we provide foun-
      dation models with fine-grained map services data through both context and API access, enabling a
793
      more comprehensive benchmarking of their geospatial question-answering abilities.
794

796
      A.2   M AP E VAL -API
797
      The M AP E VAL -API task adopts a practical approach by leveraging map APIs to answer location-
798   based questions directly, providing a more real-world scenario for evaluating the capabilities of
799   Large Language Models (LLMs) in map-based reasoning. Recent advancements in LLMs have led
800   to growing interest in planning tasks (Xie et al., 2024; Balsebre et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Fang
801   et al., 2024) that involve map data. For instance, the Travel Planner (Xie et al., 2024) benchmark
802   assessed multi-day itinerary planning using Google Maps API to determine distances, travel times,
803   and details of nearby attractions. This task demonstrated the utility of map data in real-world plan-
804   ning scenarios, highlighting the potential for LLMs to integrate real-time geospatial information into
805   decision-making.
806   Additionally, tool-calling benchmarks such as ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023) and API-Bank (Li et al.,
807   2023) have included location-based queries as a subtask, testing the ability of LLMs to interact
808   with APIs in structured ways. These benchmarks typically focus on simpler query types, such as
809   retrieving distances or nearby points of interest (POIs), but they do not fully address the complexity
      and diversity of real-world map-based questions.

810
      In contrast, MapEval-API pushes the boundaries by evaluating LLMs on a wide variety of complex
811   geospatial tasks that require not only querying map APIs but also integrating multiple pieces of
812   information, such as travel itineraries, nearby services, and spatio-temporal reasoning. This more
813   comprehensive evaluation of API-based reasoning challenges the models to process complex, multi-
814   faceted questions, highlighting their ability to handle nuanced map interactions and effectively syn-
815   thesize data retrieved from APIs.

817   A.3   M AP E VAL -V ISUAL
818
819   Prior works in geospatial analysis and map-based question answering have predominantly focused
820   on remote sensing images (Bastani et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), which in-
821
      volve satellite or aerial imagery. These images often contain complex data about the Earth’s surface,
      including land cover, vegetation, urban infrastructure, and other environmental features. Models
822
      designed for interpreting remote sensing images (Lobry et al., 2020) typically rely on convolutional
823
      neural networks (CNNs) and other computer vision techniques for object detection, segmentation,
824   and classification tasks. These methods often focus on identifying physical entities like roads, build-
825   ings, and natural features from high-resolution imagery.
826
827
      In contrast, our M AP E VAL -V ISUAL approach focuses on digital map view snapshots, which are
      2D representations of map services (such as Google Maps). Unlike remote sensing images, which
828
      represent physical realities captured from a top-down perspective, these digital maps show geospa-
829
      tial information in a structured, interactive format. The focus of M AP E VAL -V ISUAL is to evaluate
830   a model’s ability to interpret and reason about these structured map views, which include not just
831   physical features, but also symbolic and navigational information such as traffic signs, routes, land-
832   marks, and visual cues from the map interface itself.
833
      While remote sensing image analysis typically involves extracting physical data from raw image pix-
834
      els, M AP E VAL -V ISUAL requires models to engage with spatial reasoning and map-based symbols,
835
      demanding a different set of computational skills. In this task, the model must not only understand
836   the spatial relationships between map features but also reason about the context provided by dig-
837   ital map interfaces, which include additional elements such as zoom levels, icons, and navigation
838   markers. This distinction sets M AP E VAL -V ISUAL apart from traditional remote sensing tasks and
839   presents new challenges in the field of geospatial reasoning and map-based visual question answer-
840   ing.

845
846   For the creation of the textual contexts and design MCQs based on that, we employed a custom-built
847   web interface named MapQaTor. As illustrated in Figure 6, this interface was central to the dataset
848   development process, offering an intuitive, user-friendly environment that simplifies complex tasks,
849   such as API interaction and context generation.
850
      The annotator interface is designed to reduce technical complexity for users, allowing them to con-
851   centrate on the core aspects of dataset annotation, such as selecting relevant locations, providing
852   information on distances, durations, and directions between places, as well as identifying nearby
853   points of interest. Its streamlined workflow facilitates efficient dataset creation by automating repet-
854   itive tasks, which not only minimizes errors but also significantly accelerates the annotation process.
855
      MapQaTor uses five key Google Maps APIs: Text Search, Place Details, Distance Matrix, Direc-
856
      tions, and Nearby Search, based on their relevance to common map-based tasks and their ability to
857   provide comprehensive location data.
858
859   MapQaTor caches all API call responses, creating a static database for evaluation purposes. This
860
      ensures consistent responses when evaluating M AP E VAL -API. Specifically, when an API call is
      made, the cached response is returned instead of a real-time query, maintaining a controlled and
861
      static evaluation environment.
862
863   Once the dataset is generated, it can be easily exported in JSON format, making it readily usable for
      further analysis and evaluation in downstream tasks, such as model training and benchmarking.
