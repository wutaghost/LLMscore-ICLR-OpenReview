008
009
                  Combining workflows with large language models (LLMs) allows LLMs to fol-
010
                  low specific procedures, thereby extending their application to more real-world
011
                  scenarios. However, incorporating workflows often compromises the flexibility
012               of LLMs. For example in the case of Task-Oriented Dialogue (TOD), workflow
013               atomize the function of LLM while programmatically imposing restrictions on ex-
014               ecution path making the dialogue obstructed and less flexible when facing out-of-
015               workflow (OOW) queries. Prompt-based methods offer soft control but sometimes
016               fail to ensure procedure compliance. This paper introduces a new agent paradigm
017               to address this challenge. Specifically, we first propose a novel Procedure De-
018               scription Language (PDL) that integrates the flexibility of natural language and
019               the precision of code for workflow expression. Additionally, we present a com-
020
                  prehensive framework that enables LLM to handle OOW queries while keeping
                  execution safe with a series of controllers for behavioral regulation. This includes
021
                  pre-decision and post-decision methods, where the dependency relationships be-
022
                  tween workflow nodes are modeled as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) to validate
023               node transitions. Beyond the primary objective of compliance considered in previ-
024               ous work, we introduce a new approach to evaluate the agent’s flexibility in OOW
025               situations. Experiments on three datasets demonstrate that F LOWAGENT not only
026               adheres well to workflows but also responds better to OOW queries, showcasing
027               its flexibility. Furthermore, exploration on WikiHow data confirms that the PDL
028               effectively represents broader formats of workflow, inspiring further research on
029               workflow-based QA tasks. 1

      Compared with traditional chatbots, LLMs can interact more flexibly with users to address diverse
055   needs, leveraging the vast amount of commonsense knowledge stored in their parameters (Yi et al.,
056   2024). However, in real-world applications, we often expect chatbots to follow specific rules and
057   procedures to perform certain tasks (e.g., guiding users to make an appointment for appropriate
058   hospitals, departments, and doctors(Mosig et al., 2020; He et al., 2022)). The procedures that must
059   be followed through dialogues are known as workflows. LLMs, acting as workflow agents, assist
060   users via conversations and invoke relevant tools to fulfill requests (Xiao et al., 2024).
061   Existing research can be broadly classified into two categories: rule-based and prompt-based meth-
062   ods. Rule-based methods (Coze, 2024; Dify, 2024; Flowise, 2024) control the conversation between
063   the agent and the user through deterministic programs, modeling the progress of dialogue as state
064   transitions within a graph composed of nodes representing different dialogue states, as shown in the
065   upper part of Fig. 1(a). In this approach, the LLM functions as a node within the graph and cannot
066   control the entire conversation flow. As a result, this method provides high compliance but often at
067   the expense of the LLM’s inherent flexibility. As illustrated in the lower part of Fig. 1(a), adding
068   a new function to an existing workflow (the graph formed by the purple nodes) necessitates adding
069
      numerous edges (dashed lines) to enable transitions back to the “plotline” state of the workflow.
      The introduction of merely one out-of-workflow (OOW) response node (represented by the yellow
070
      diamond) causes dramatic increase of complexity.
071
072   On the other hand, prompt-based methods allow LLMs to autonomously control the dialogue pro-
073   cess. These methods represent workflows in the text form, using natural language or code-based
074   syntax (Fig. 1(b) shows three typical syntaxes), which are then fed into the LLM to execute actions
075
      (e.g., calling tools) or generating responses. While prompt engineering provides soft control over
      the LLM’s behavior LLMs, being probabilistic models, still suffer from hallucinations that fail to
076
      ensure procedural compliance (Zhang et al., 2023).
077
078   As illustrated in Fig. 1(c), these methods occupy different points on the axis formed by the trade-
079   off between flexibility and compliance. This leads to the central question of this paper: How can
080   we enhance the compliance of LLMs when performing workflow tasks without compromising
081
      their flexibility in interactions?
082   Addressing this question involves two main challenges: 1) In which form can we represent work-
083   flows precisely? 2) How to control the behavior of LLMs effectively? To tackle the first challenge,
084   we introduce a Procedure Description Language (PDL) that combines the strengths of natural lan-
085   guage and code, allowing PDL to retain both flexibility and precision. Its adaptable syntax enables
086   comprehensive node definitions, making it suitable for expressing various types of workflows with
087
      high representational capacity (see Sec. 4.1). In response to the second challenge, we propose the
      F LOWAGENT framework, which defines a series of controllers that regulate the agent’s behavior
088
      based on nodes defined in the PDL. This ensures that while LLMs make autonomous decisions,
089
      they can be monitored and controlled under legal actions (see Sec. 4.2). Notably, the flexible design
090   of PDL and the adjustable controllers within our framework allow us to balance the flexibility and
091   compliance of F LOWAGENT (see Fig. 1(c)).
092
093
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
094   1. We systematically analyze existing LLM-based workflow agents from the perspectives of com-
095   pliance and flexibility. Building upon the analysis, we propose the PDL syntax for formulation of
096   workflows, which combines the advantages of natural language and code for flexible descriptions of
097   node relationships and overall workflow procedures.
098   2. We propose the F LOWAGENT framework to regulate the execution process of workflow agents.
099   By designing different controllers, we can dynamically adjust the compliance and flexibility of work-
100   flow agents.
101
      3. We construct an evaluation benchmark on top of existing ones to design a comprehensive evalua-
102
      tion method to assess the performance of workflow agents under OOW scenarios.
103
104   4. Experimental results on three datasets demonstrate that F LOWAGENT can achieve strong perfor-
105   mance in both compliance and flexibility. Further pioneering exploration on WikiHow demonstrates
106
      our adaptability to workflow-based QA tasks.

      2.1   LLM- BASED TASK -O RIENTED D IALOG
111
112   Unlike Open-Domain Dialog (ODD) systems, which engage in general conversation without a spe-
113   cific goal, Task-Oriented Dialog (TOD) systems are designed to assist users in achieving explicit,
114   domain-specific goals, such as booking a flight or making a restaurant reservation (Yi et al., 2024;
115   Zhang et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2019). Traditional TOD systems operate through a pipeline structure,
116   handling tasks in distinct modules, including Natural Language Understanding (NLU), Dialogue
117   State Tracking (DST), Dialogue Policy Management, and Natural Language Generation (NLG) (Yi
118   et al., 2024). This modular, cascaded approach, however, often results in accumulated errors across
119
      stages (He et al., 2022; Su et al., 2021).
120   With recent advancements in large language models, a “workflow agent” paradigm has emerged
121   where the LLM autonomously manages the dialogue by integrating external knowledge or using
122   system prompts to guide interactions (Xiao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2024).
123   This shift has also driven changes in evaluation metrics: instead of focusing on traditional measures
124   like the recognition accuracy of user intent and infilling slots, emphasis is now put on end-to-end
125
      task completion rates and subjective scoring of user experience (Xiao et al., 2024; Arcadinho et al.,
      2024). In light of this, this paper presents a new dataset and an end-to-end evaluation framework,
126
      alongside a system designed to harness and maximize the potential of LLMs in TOD scenarios.
127
128
129   2.2   LLM- BASED AGENTS AND AGENTIC W ORKFLOWS
130
      The advancement of large language model technology has fostered the development of LLM-based
131
      agents, which are now being applied across a wide range of domains (Park et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
132
      2023; Qian et al., 2023). Compared to standalone language models, LLM-based agents possess
133   enhanced capabilities such as tool utilization, memory retention, and self-reflection, which allow for
134   improved performance in real-life applications (Xi et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023). Generally, these
135   approaches aim to enhance agent performance through either model-driven planning and reasoning
136   (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022) or by providing external tools and knowledge sources (Schick
137   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024).
138
      This evolution has introduced the concept of the agentic workflow, which refers to AI agents capable
139   of autonomous planning, decision-making, and action execution to achieve goals, often without
140   direct human intervention (Li et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
141   Notably, the Agentic Workflow approach places significant emphasis on the planning and reasoning
142   abilities of language models, enabling them to decompose a single complex problem into a sequence
143   of sub-tasks, or a “workflow”, which serves as an intermediate state guiding the agent in subsequent
144   steps (Valmeekam et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024; Xue et al., 2024). In contrast, the concept of
145   a workflow agent emphasizes that, within an existing workflow, the language model follows this
146   predefined process to accomplish tasks, often using dialogue to satisfy user needs (Xiao et al., 2024;
147
      Qiao et al., 2024). From a more general perspective, the former can serve as a logical precursor to
      the latter by automating the construction of the conversational workflow. However, in this paper, we
148
      treat the workflow as pre-defined knowledge and focus on building more robust and user-friendly
149
      agents based on these workflows.

154   3.1   W ORKFLOW
155
156   A workflow describes the process that an agent should follow in a specific scenario or task. For
157   example, in a hospital appointment booking scenario, the agent needs to ask the user for details such
158
      as the desired hospital, department, and preferred time, use the relevant tools to retrieve available
      appointment slots, confirm with the user, and complete the booking process.
159
160   Abstractly, a workflow can include operations such as asking the user for information, invoking
161   tools, and responding to the user, which can be represented as a series of nodes. Additionally, these
      nodes have temporal and dependency relationships, represented as directed edges. Therefore, a


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      workflow can be modeled as a graph structure, specifically a directed acyclic graph (DAG), denoted
163   by G(V, E) (Qiao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).
164
165   To integrate structured workflows with language models that process linear text, two primary meth-
166
      ods are typically used to implement workflows. The first approach, called rule-based, involves
      programming the workflow’s transition rules as fixed logic, where the current node and transitions
167
      between states are hard-coded in the program. The second approach, known as prompt-based, rep-
168
      resents the workflow in various formats such as natural language, code (or pseudocode), or flowchart
169   syntax (Xiao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024).
170
171   3.2   W ORKFLOW AGENT
172
173   A workflow agent can be viewed as an agent that makes sequential decisions throughout its in-
174   teractions with the user and available tools (environemnt), which can be modeled using a Markov
175   Decision Process (MDP). The current state is denoted as s, the action taken by the agent as a, and
176   the feedback from the environment (user responses or tool-generated outputs) as r. This process can
177   be represented as {(s0 , a0 , r0 ), (s1 , a1 , r1 ), . . . , (st−1 , at−1 , rt−1 )}. Based on this, the decision-
178
      making process of the workflow agent can be expressed as:
179                                                  at ← A(Ht−1 , G),                                              (1)
180   where Ht−1 encompasses all actions and observations up to time t − 1, and G serves as the guide
181   for the agent’s actions.
182   Based on the aforementioned workflow representations, workflow agents can be classified into two
183   categories. The first category is rule-based agents, where the procedure in the workflow is im-
184   plemented through programming. Typical examples include Coze (Coze, 2024), Dify (Dify, 2024),
185   Flowise (Flowise, 2024), etc. Specifically, in these methods, the program rigidly controls the tran-
186   sitions between nodes, with the LLM acting as one of the nodes to generate user responses, predict
187   parameters for tool calls, or assist with node transitions (e.g., classifying user intent). In such sce-
188   narios, the agent’s accessible information and action space are limited, expressed as:
189                                       at ← Mv (ϕv (Ht−1 ), ψ v (G)),                                   (2)
190   where v is the current node, ϕ (Ht−1 ) is the selected information visible to v, ψ v (G)) is a subgraph

191   of G expanded from v, and Mv denotes the language model bound to v. In general, this approach
192   ensures that workflows operate under strict control but comes with high implementation costs, and
193   predefining all state transitions is challenging to complete. For example, as shown in Fig. 2(b)
194   session 1, when a user requests to change the hospital for an appointment, the ideal response would
195
      be for the agent to check the availability at Hospital B and inform the user. However, if the pre-
      configured workflow lacks an option for switching hospitals at that decision node, the rule-based
196
      workflow typically fails to respond to this need.

198   The second category is prompt-based agents (Xiao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024), where a format f
199   is used to represent the workflow as linear text W (f ) , and a single language model M autonomously
200   manages the entire decision-making and dialogue process. This process can be represented as:
201                                              at ← M(Ht−1 , G (f ) ),                                           (3)

      We propose a new procedure description language (PDL) that integrates natural language and code to
256
      represent workflows. As shown in Fig. 3, the PDL adopts a YAML-like syntax and consists of three
257
      main parts: meta information, node definitions, and procedure description: W (pdl) = {I, N, P },
258   where meta information I contains basic information about the workflow (e.g., name, description),
259   node definitions N defines the resources the agent may use during execution (e.g., tools, response
260   strategies), and procedure description P details the procedure that needs to be followed for this task,
261   expressed using a mix of natural language and pseudocode.
262
      The node definitions in the PDL syntax provide essential information for the agent’s ex-
263
      ecution process and define the agent’s available action space.   We have designed three
264   types of nodes: SLOT nodes, which represent necessary slots during workflow execu-
265   tion or API calls, such as hospital name and department name in a hospital regis-
266   tration workflow; API nodes, which define external tools that the agent can call, includ-
267   ing functions like check hospital, check department, query appointment, and
268   register appointment; and ANSWER nodes, which specify the agent’s response behav-
269   ior, such as inform registration result, directing the agent’s response after a registration
      attempt. Additionally, an answer OOW questions node allows the LLM to flexibly handle user


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      queries that fall outside the defined workflow, addressing limitations commonly found in rule-based
271   workflow agents.
272
273   It is important to note that node definitions include a preconditions attribute, which out-
274
      lines dependencies between nodes. For instance, the register appointment node lists
      [’query appointment’] as a prerequisite, indicating that the agent must query a suitable
275
      appointment list before proceeding with registration to prevent invalid actions. Similarly, the
276
      inform registration result node must follow [’register appointment’], ensur-
277   ing that responses are generated only after a registration attempt has been completed, thus avoiding
278   false responses caused by LLM hallucinations. By leveraging these preconditions, a node depen-
279   dency graph G (pdl) can be reconstructed, similar to the G discussed in Sec. 3, but potentially differing
280   in node definitions and topology.
281
      In summary, PDL offers three main features:
282
      1. Simplicity and flexibility: PDL allows for summarizing essential tools and actions within a
283
      workflow through nodes, covering common slot descriptions, API calls, and response nodes. The
284   syntax is user-friendly, enabling users to quickly create PDL workflows tailored to their scenarios
285   without the steep learning curve of strict code language or flowchart syntax.
286   2. Structured yet flexible representation: The structured expression, enhanced with natural lan-
287   guage descriptions, makes the workflow representation concise while retaining the natural lan-
288   guage’s flexibility and the preciseness of code.
289   3. Explicit dependency representation: PDL can accurately define logical relationships between
290   nodes, similar to rule-based methods that implement abstract workflow graphs G but without us-
291   ing rigid “If...Then...” conditions. Instead, it enforces legal or illegal transitions through sparse
292
      dependencies, allowing LLMs to autonomously decide node transitions, thus ensuring the agent’s
      flexibility.

350   5.1   E VALUATION M ETHOD
351
352   We follow previous studies (Xiao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023) to conduct both turn-level and
353   session-level assessments for evaluation of the proposed framework.
354
355
356   Turn-level evaluation Similar to the classic TOD task evaluation (Dai et al., 2022), there is a
357   reference session (considered as ground truth). For each turn in the reference session, the evaluation
358
      system provides the prefix of the session Ht−1 to the bot for predicting the current ât . The judge
      compares ât with at to determine if the bot’s response for that turn is correct, and the average
359
      result across all turns yields the Pass Rate. Unlike Xiao et al. (2024) that adopts powerful LLM
360
      such as GPT4 as a judge for scoring the generated contents of the LLM agent, we do not introduce
361   LLM-based scoring because we believe the evaluation is prone to be biased to the preference of
362   the judging LLM for certain styles, lengths, and formats rather than contents. Instead, we use a
363   binary classification of correctness and calculate the pass rate based on this. To assess the agent’s
364   tool usage capability, for turns involving tool callings, we evaluate the tool selection and parameter
365   infilling performance of the agent in Precision, Recall, and F1-score.
366
367
368   Session-level evaluation In session-level evaluation, considering the cost of assessment, we use an
369
      LLM to simulate the user and interact with the bot. To ensure these simulated conversation sessions
      closely resemble real-world scenarios rather than simplistic examples, we define a user profile for
370
      each user, including: (1) basic demographic information; (2) conversational style detailing the user’s
371
      behavior patterns; and (3) user needs for the workflow, describing the main goals for the session
372   (which may include some secondary objectives). A specific example of a user profile can be found
373   in App. A.2. For each generated session, we conduct a binary evaluation to determine whether
374   the user’s primary intention for the workflow is achieved, resulting in the Success Rate metric.
375   Additionally, we track the number of sub-tasks expressed by the user and the number completed
376   within the session to derive the Task Progress metric. We use the same prompts as proposed in Xiao
377   et al. (2024) to evaluate each session in an end-to-end manner. We also assess the tool-calling
      performance of the LLM agent using Precision, Recall, and F1-score.

      5.2   F LEXIBILITY E VALUATION
379
380   Previous work (Zhong et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2024) has primarily focused on eval-
381   uating whether bots can follow a specific procedure to complete a conversation, which partially
382   emphasizes compliance while neglecting flexibility in handling user requests. Such incomprehen-
383   sive evaluation may not reflect the capabilities of LLM agents under real-world scenarios, where an
384   “imperfect” user might not adhere to the procedure and violates the sequential steps during multi-
385   ple rounds of interactions. Consequently, to evaluate the performance of workflow agents in OOW
386   scenarios, we have additionally developed a targeted evaluation method to assess flexibility.
387   Specifically, we categorize OOW scenarios into three types: (1) intent switching, where the user
388   suddenly changes the original intent requests or requirements, including modification of API slots/-
389   parameters and demand for cancellations; (2) procedure jumping, where the user does not follow the
390   established workflow sequence to provide information and express confirmation, including skipping
391   steps or jumping back; and (3) irrelevant answering, where the user deliberately avoids direct reply
392   to questions raised by the agent, such as answers with topic shifts and rhetorical questions;
393   Given the defined categories above, we can evaluate the flexibility of the agent by observing its per-
394   formance in OOW scenarios. We adopt the metrics defined in Sec. 5.1. At the turn-level, we evaluate
395   the agent’s immediate flexibility by inserting OOW user queries and observing its performance in
396   these specific turns. At the session-level, we assess the agent’s overall performance in sessions that
397   include OOW queries to measure its long-term flexibility.

416   Q2: In which way the proposed controllers exert constraints on the model to facilitate workflows

530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we reviewed existing LLM-based workflow methods and compared their strengths
533
      and weaknesses in terms of compliance and flexibility. Aiming to enhance the compliance capa-
534   bility of LLMs without significantly compromising their flexibility, we proposed the PDL syntax
535   to express workflows and used the F LOWAGENT framework to control agent behavior. For eval-
536   uating compliance and flexibility capabilities, we constructed datasets based on existing data and
537   designed specific evaluation methods. Experiments on three datasets demonstrated that F LOWA-
538   GENT not only possesses strong compliance capabilities but also exhibits robust flexibility when
539   handling OOW queries. Additionally, we validated F LOWAGENT’s potential in workflow-based QA
      tasks using the WikiHow dataset, inspiring our future research.

1314   illustrates the structure of a WikiHow page describing four different methods for checking the pub-
1315   lication date of a website. Each method includes a series of ordered steps, which can be considered
1316   as sub-procedures within the broader workflow. An intelligent agent could help user navigate these
1317   procedural guides more efficiently. We refer to this type of task as workflow-based QA, where the
1318   agent assists users in understanding and using the guides through conversation. This interactive form
1319   differs from the traditional workflow agents introduced in Sec. 3.2, as it does not involve explicit
1320
       state maintenance or tool invocation.
1321   To validate this, we first used GPT-4-Turbo to automatically reformulate data from the WikiHow
1322   website using PDL syntax, as shown in Fig. 4(b), where four distinct sub-procedures are defined
1323   as four SUBTASKs. Building on this, Fig. 4(c) demonstrates a sample dialogue between the user
1324   and F LOWAGENT, showing that the bot can follow the PDL-defined workflow and respond to user
1325   queries based on the content described in each SUBTASK. Through these preliminary examples, we
1326
       observed that PDL syntax can effectively represent WikiHow-like workflows, and the F LOWAGENT
       framework supports this new type of workflow-based QA task. Future work will include systematic
1327
       evaluation and analysis focused on refining WikiHow workflow and experimental design for this
