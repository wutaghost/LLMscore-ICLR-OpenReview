011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated limitations in handling com-
014            binatorial optimization problems involving long-range reasoning, partially due to
015
               causal hallucinations and huge search space. As for causal hallucinations, i.e.,
               the inconsistency between reasoning and corresponding state transition, this paper
016
               introduces the Causal Relationship Enhancement (CRE) mechanism combining
017
               cause-effect interventions and the Individual Treatment Effect (ITE) to guaran-
018            tee the solid causal rightness between each step of reasoning and state transition.
019            As for the long causal range and huge search space limiting the performances of
020            existing models featuring single-direction search, a Dual-End Searching (DES)
021            approach is proposed to seek solutions by simultaneously starting from both the
022            initial and goal states on the causal probability tree. By integrating CRE and
023            DES (CreDes), our model has realized simultaneous multi-step reasoning, cir-
024            cumventing the inefficiencies from cascading multiple one-step reasoning like
025            the Chain-of-Thought (CoT). Experiments demonstrate that CreDes significantly
026
               outperforms existing State-Of-The-Art (SOTA) solutions in long-range reasoning
               tasks in terms of both accuracy and time efficiency.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reasoning aims to realize the causal transfer from the initial state to the goal state through several
032   intermediate steps, which widely exists in the domains of Societal Simulation (Gandhi et al., 2024;
033   Xu et al., 2024; Hua et al., 2023), Economic Simulation (Li et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023; Xia
034   et al., 2024), Game Theory (Xu et al., 2023b; Mao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) and Gaming
035   (Mukobi et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024), etc. LLMs like GPT-3 have shown com-
036   petitive performances in many reasoning tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Betker
037   et al., 2023). However, their performances and efficiency are limited when dealing with complex
038   combinatorial optimization problems that require multi-step long-range reasoning (Kaddour et al.,
039
      2023).
040   The first challenge is causal hallucinations, i.e., causality between one-step reasoning (OSR) and
041   state transition in LLMs is not always guaranteed. Similar to pre-trained LLMs that are prone to
042   produce hallucinations when processing certain factual information, causal hallucinations reflect
043   the fact that LLMs lack rigor due to inherent randomness in accomplishing complex mathematical
044   (Cobbe et al., 2021b; Imani et al., 2023; Lewkowycz et al., 2022), logical (Liu et al., 2023; Xu
045
      et al., 2023a), or common-sense reasoning (Zhao et al., 2024a; Sharan et al., 2023; Xenos et al.,
      2024), which is somehow entrenched in statistical inevitability and independent of the Transformer
046
      architecture or data quality (Kalai & Vempala, 2023). For example, CoT-based finite-step reason-
047
      ing methods (Wei et al., 2022b; Zheng et al., 2023) suffer from causal hallucinations, which cannot
048   effectively ensure the causality between OSR and state transition in LLMs, resulting in unreliable
049   reasoning and relatively low success rates (especially for long-range reasoning problems with sig-
050   nificant error accumulation effects). The reasonableness between OSR and state transition can be
051   summarized as follows: There is a causal relationship between reasonable OSR and state transition.
052   However, for unreasonable OSR, there is only a correlation or no relationship with state transition.
053   This suggests that training solely with cross-entropy loss, as commonly used in most methods, does
      not address the model’s causal rigor well enough. Inspired by this, we designed the CRE mecha-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      nism to make each step of reasoning correct and causally sound by embedding the causality measure
055   between OSR and state transition into the training loss, thus more closely modeling the rigor, adapt-
056   ability, and comprehensiveness of human reasoning (Bao et al., 2024).
057
058
      The second challenge is that long-range reasoning problems have a huge search space. Although
      complex architectures such as CoT, Tree of Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), and Program of
059
      Thought (PoT) (Chen et al., 2022) can effectively improve the reasoning accuracy of LLMs through
060
      external guidance, they are limited when handling long-range reasoning processes and task decom-
061   position. A crucial reason is that long-range reasoning has a huge state space, i.e., each branch in the
062   state transition process expands the search space approximately exponentially. Most of the existing
063   LLM-based methods, e.g., Monte Carlo search (Zhao et al., 2024b), are based on unidirectional rea-
064   soning, making them time-inefficient and easy to fall into local optima when dealing with reasoning
065   problems with large search spaces. In this paper, a bi-directional Dual-End Searching method is de-
066   veloped, which first decomposes a long-range reasoning problem into a combination of short-range
067   reasoning problems and then searches for the intersection of two causal probability trees starting
068   from the initial and goal states, respectively.
069   A structured and general reasoning framework, CreDes, is developed for long-range reasoning with
070   LLMs in this paper, and the contributions can be summarized as follows:
071
      First, the CRE mechanism is introduced to improve the rigor of LLM-based long-range rea-
072
      soning methods: Structural Causal Modeling (SCM) is exploited to enhance the causality between
073   OSR and state transitions, involving performing causal interventions and optimizing ITE during
074   training, which has effectively alleviated causal hallucinations in long-range reasoning of LLMs.
075
076
      Second, the DES method is developed to improve the search efficiency for long-range reason-
      ing: After constructing causal probability trees starting from the initial states and ending at the goal
077
      states, long-range reasoning (e.g., 12 steps) is divided into more manageable combinations of smaller
078
      segments (e.g., 2 or 4 steps) by bi-directional approaching. The final reasoning paths are selected by
079   constructing a new metric guaranteeing both low reasoning hallucination and high reasoning quality.
080   By avoiding long-range sequential search from scratch, the DES method has dramatically lowered
081   the complexity when solving long-range reasoning problems.
082
      Third, simultaneous multi-step reasoning is realized to improve the time-efficiency of long-
083
      range reasoning: By integrating CRE and DES, CreDes can perform simultaneous multi-step rea-
084
      soning within the model, i.e., avoiding the inefficiency of cascading single-step reasoning in frame-
085   works such as CoT. While ensuring the accuracy of the reasoning process, CreDes can significantly
086   reduce the time required for multi-step reasoning in LLMs.
087
088
      Fourth, adequate and rigorous testing of CreDes: CreDes has been extensively tested in the
      Blocksworld, GSM8K, and Hanoi Tower scenarios, respectively, and the experimental results show
089
      that CreDes outperforms existing SOTA regarding reasoning accuracy and time efficiency.

140   Integrating Causal Analysis in LLMs for Multi-step Decision-Making: Causal analysis aims to
141   discern and elucidate the causal relationships between actions, circumstances, or decisions. This
142   method entails investigating the origins or causes leading to an event and the potential consequences
143   that follow (Heise, 1975; Imbens & Rubin, 2015; Feder et al., 2022). Although various causal
144   models may produce identical observational distributions, they can yield distinct distributions when
145   interventions are applied (Peters et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2024). Therefore, using interventions
146   allows for the distinction of possible causal models that align with the observed data (Hagmayer
147
      et al., 2007; Pearl, 2009). This enhances the causal consistency and significance of the model train-
      ing process. Previous work suggests that, while CoT has been lauded for its potential to improve
148
      task performance, its application does not always lead to enhanced outcomes (Kojima et al., 2022;
149
      Nichols et al., 2020). Also, research has shown that the statistical pretraining of LLMs encourages
150   models to achieve high empirical performance but not necessarily to reason (Zhang et al., 2022;
151   Turpin et al., 2024; Zečević et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023). Motivated by this, we designed the
152   CRE mechanism combining causal analysis and LLMs to control the causal hallucinations when
153   solving long-range reasoning problems.
154
      Solving Multi-step Problems with LLMs: Recent studies have shown that with substantial de-
155
      sign, LLMs are capable of performing not only basic arithmetic tasks but also complex multi-step
156   reasoning (Power et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022a). For instance, increasing computational resources
157   significantly enhances the accuracy of datasets like GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021a). Concurrently,
158   Research (Yang et al., 2023b) demonstrated that a 2B parameter LLM could achieve 89.9% ac-
159   curacy in 5x5 multiplication tasks using curriculum learning with 50 million training instances.
160   This evidence suggests that adequately scaled LLMs can process multiple reasoning steps effec-
161   tively internally. While trees are frequently used to represent games (especially extensive-form
      games (Leonard, 2006; Leyton-Brown & Shoham, 2008)) and sequential reasoning problems (Rus-

163   a framework for understanding causality through the use of probability trees. However, it can also

165   multiple steps but excel in short-range reasoning tasks. This insight led to the development of DES,
166   which breaks down the Long Range Reasoning Question into smaller parts and then searches for

214   In causal inference, ITE measures the difference in outcomes for an individual with and without a
215   specific treatment. A larger ITE typically indicates a stronger causal relationship between random
      variables. Its definition is as follows:

221   resents the treatment assignment. ITE is generally encouraged to be as large as possible, and prior

259   3.3          C AUSAL R ELATIONSHIP E NHANCEMENT (CRE)
260
261
      Firstly, all the samples are classified into two categories: Correct and Incorrect. Within the Incorrect
      category, three scenarios exist, i.e., a correct OSR leading to an incorrect state transition, an incor-
262
      rect OSR leading to an incorrect state transition, and an incorrect OSR resulting in a correct state
263
      transition. Given this, it is evident that we need to strengthen the causal connection between the
264   OSR and the transition, and reduce the occurrence of samples where the OSR and the transition are
265   non-causal. In CRE, we first use the ITE to estimate the causality between OSR and state transi-
266   tion quantitatively, and then embed the |E(ITE)| and Var(ITE) into the loss function in the training
267   process (the remaining is cross-entropy), enhancing the causality of state transitions. As is shown
268   in Fig. 2 and the upper part of Fig. 1, we leave the reasoning path selection to be controlled by the
269   cross-entropy loss, while the suppression of hallucinations is handled by the ITE loss. Perplexity
      (PPL) is a metric used to evaluate the performance of a LLM, indicating how well the model predicts

      the next word in a sequence, and lower values signify better predictive accuracy. The estimation of
271   ITE is detailed as the follows:
272
273   Given binary variables X and Y indicating the correctness of OSR and next state (state transition),
274
      respectively, i.e., X, Y ∼ B(0, 1), and X = 1 (or Y = 1) means correctness. First, we calculate
      the cause-effect interventions between X and Y , then subsequently modify the distribution of Y by
275
      intervening in X. From a statistical correlation perspective, if X and Y are correlated, Y can be
276
      predicted using X. However, if there is no causal relationship between X and Y , intervening in
277   X will not alter the distribution of Y . Hence, if X and Y are correlated but not causally linked,
278   then manipulating or intervening in X would not lead to any changes in the distribution of Y . This
279   distinction is crucial in statistical analysis and experimental design because it addresses the potential
280   fallacy that correlation inherently means causation.
281

294   3.4   C AUSAL P ROBABILITY T REES WITH D UAL E ND S EARCHING (DES)
295
      In this section, we improve the success rate of LLMs in solving long-range reasoning problems,
296
      such as the 12-step Blocksworld scenario, by leveraging their higher success rates in simpler 2-step
297
      and 4-step tasks. The main implementation process of DES is as follows:
298
299   Step1: We build two causal probability trees from the initial and goal states, with nodes representing
300   reasoning states and arrows denoting causal relationships. These trees outline possible reasoning
301
      paths within a limited number of steps.
302   Step2: By matching their leaves, we identify end-to-end permutation schemes to form a continu-
303   ous, and feasible path (as shown in Fig. 1 and Fig. 4). The DES framework ensures optimal path
304   selection by expanding two trees from the head and tail ends (Thead and Ttail ). Probabilities are cal-
305   culated based on the likelihood of reaching each state, with expansion directions chosen to reduce
306   the distance to the target. This method balances exploration and exploitation, avoiding premature
307
      convergence to suboptimal solutions.
308   Step3: After several layers of expansion, the leaf nodes of the head and tail trees are matched, and
309   a distance matrix M is constructed. This matrix quantifies the spatial relationships between the end
310   leaf nodes in Thead and Ttail . The distance matrix is computed as the Euclidean distance between
311   the coordinates of each leaf node in the two trees, as follows:
312                                            q

317
318   As the trees expand, the reasoning path is updated with the latest expansion results every four steps.

      4.1      S ETUP
379
380   Blocksworld: There are n blocks initially placed randomly on a table (Valmeekam et al., 2024).
381   The LLM’s goal is to stack these blocks in a specified order. The LLM can perform four actions:
382   pick up a block from the table, put down a block it is holding onto the table, unstack a block from
383   another to hold it, and stack the block in its hand onto another block. The LLM can only manipulate
384   one block at a time, and blocks with others on top are immovable.
385   GSM8K: The GSM8K dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021a) includes 1,319 diverse grade school math word
386   problems curated by human problem writers. These tasks typically begin with a description and
387   culminate in a final question requiring multi-step mathematical calculations contextual to the prob-
388   lem. To effectively tackle the final question, our approach involves decomposing it into a sequential
389   series of smaller sub-questions, allowing for a structured solution process.
390   Hanoi Tower: The Hanoi Tower problem (Gerety & Cull, 1986), a classic puzzle involving three
391   pegs and a set of discs of varying sizes, serves as a key component of our experimental setup. The
392   challenge requires moving the entire stack of discs from one peg to another, obeying the rules that
393   only one disc can be moved at a time, and no disc may be placed on top of a smaller one. This
394   task, structured around sequential and strategic disc placement, tests the model’s ability to plan and
395   execute a series of actions based on simple yet strict rules.

      Train Parameter: In this paper, we primarily utilize the 7B models for training on a single NVIDIA

412   RAP: A technique that employs Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for exploration (Hao et al.,
413
      2023). RAP transforms LLMs into both reasoning agents and world models, utilizing MCTS for
      strategic exploration and decision-making. This approach significantly enhances the LLM’s ability
414
      to generate action plans and solve mathematical and logical problems, outperforming traditional
415
      methods and establishing new benchmarks in LLM’s capabilities.
416
417   CoT: A technique having enhanced the reasoning capabilities (Wei et al., 2022b) of LLMs. By pro-
418   viding models with intermediate reasoning steps as examples, CoT demonstrates notable improve-
419
      ments across various complex reasoning tasks, including arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic
      reasoning. CoT requires the model to generate a reasoning chain to improve the reasoning ability.
420
      We used all basemodels to carry out CoT in the experiment.
421
422   RoT: A framework (Hui et al., 2024) to enhance the performance of tree-search-based prompting
423   methods used in LLMs. This innovative approach leverages guidelines derived from past tree search
424   experiences, allowing LLMs to avoid repeating errors and significantly improving their reasoning
425
      and planning capabilities across various tasks. We not only used the same basemodel as the original
      RoT, but also introduced other 7B models as a comparison.

      4.6   L IMITATION
523
524   In scenarios with strict order of precedence, such as the Hanoi Tower, the accuracy is significantly
525   lower compared to tasks like Blocksworld. The DES approach, while effective for moderate-length
526   tasks, struggles with very long reasoning steps, leading to a decline in performance. Additionally,
527   maintaining causal logic through CRE and DES introduces computational overhead, which may
528   limit the framework’s scalability and applicability in real-world scenarios with limited resources.
529   Finally, our approach pays insufficient attention to the sequential ordering of steps, and the ATE
530   can only determine whether the causal logic makes sense, rather than recognizing, for example, the
531   assumption encountered in the Hanoi Tower problem that the larger disk must be placed under the
532
      smaller disk.
533
534   5     C ONCLUSION
535
536   By integrating CRE and DES, the CreDes framework has significantly advanced LLMs’ capabilities
537   in long-range reasoning tasks. This combined approach enhances the accuracy and efficiency of
538   multi-step reasoning and maintains the problem-solving and reasoning abilities of pre-trained mod-
539   els across different tasks. Future work will focus on refining the framework to improve scalability
      and efficiency in various complex problem-solving scenarios.

       Theoretical Background and Practical Considerations: We perform numerous output trials to
1027   calibrate the model during the training process. From our experimental results, these output samples
1028   demonstrate a variety of possibilities, such as:
1029
1030
       Type 1: Certain samples are challenging to answer correctly, regardless of training, resulting in
       near-random correct/incorrect states.
1031
1032   Type 2: There is a positive correlation between epoch count and correct answer frequency for some
1033   samples, significantly when aided by standard training techniques like RAP and CoT.
1034   Type 3: Some samples can be answered correctly with minimal training, showing no correlation
1035   between epoch count and correct answer frequency.
1036
1037
       As Blocksworld researchers, the current goal is to maximize the correct rate of Type 1 samples,
       effectively converting more Type 1 samples into Type 2.
1038
1039   Experimental Comparison about Embodied Intelligence: Many real-world reasoning and long-
1040   range sequence decomposition tasks fundamentally adhere to the same paradigm as the one em-
1041   ployed in our research. We recognize that there may be lingering concerns regarding the practical
1042   applicability of our approach in real-world scenarios. We offer the following clarifications to address
1043
       these concerns, supported by practical examples. Established algorithms are widely used within the
       logistics industry in areas such as port container scheduling or the organization of goods in ware-
1044
       house facilities. Our research, however, aims to augment these processes by leveraging Large Lan-
1045
       guage Models (LLMs) to enhance reasoning capabilities within these contexts. The primary goal is
1046   to bridge the communication gap between human operators and algorithm engineers, allowing LLMs
1047   to facilitate more transparent and effective interactions. By understanding and interpreting human
1048   instructions, we hypothesize that LLMs can dynamically adjust their outputs, thereby improving
1049   collaboration between human operators and algorithmic systems.
1050
       Although our approach has yet to be validated with real-world data, we emphasize that the nature
1051
       of many real-world reasoning or long-range sorting tasks closely mirrors the experimental paradigm
1052   used in our study.
1053
1054   To further clarify, consider the example of warehouse item arrangement. This process involves
1055
       organizing goods according to criteria such as size, weight, or frequency of access. While this is a
       complex, unified task, it can be decomposed into smaller, interrelated sub-tasks. For instance, the
1056
       initial task may be categorizing items by size, arranging them within sections based on weight, and
1057
       finally, positioning them according to access frequency. Each sub-task depends on the previous one,
1058   forming a continuous sequence of actions that ultimately leads to completing the overall task.
1059
1060   It is worth noting that several related studies do not explicitly connect their experiments to real-world
1061
       applications. However, the scope of our experiments is comparable to that of other works in the field.
       In particular, other research has adopted similar test scenarios and datasets, further reinforcing our
1062
       confidence in the robustness of our experiments.
