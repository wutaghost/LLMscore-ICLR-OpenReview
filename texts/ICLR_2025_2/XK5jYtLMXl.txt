011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models accomplish remarkable success in data generation tasks across
014            various domains. However, the iterative sampling process is computationally ex-
015            pensive. Consistency models are proposed to learn consistency functions to map
016
               from noise to data directly, which allows one-step fast data generation and multi-
               step sampling to improve sample quality. In this paper, we study the convergence
017
               of consistency models when the self-consistency property holds approximately
018
               under the training distribution. Our analysis requires only mild data assumption
019            and applies to a family of forward processes. When the target data distribution has
020            bounded support or has tails that decay sufficiently fast, we show that the samples
021            generated by the consistency model are close to the target distribution in Wasser-
022            stein distance; when the target distribution satisfies some smoothness assumption,
023            we show that with an additional perturbation step for smoothing, the generated
024            samples are close to the target distribution in total variation distance. We pro-
025            vide two case studies with commonly chosen forward processes to demonstrate
026            the benefit of multistep sampling.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      Diffusion models have been widely acknowledged for their high performance across various do-
031
      mains, such as material and drug design (Xu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023), con-
032
      trol (Janner et al., 2022), and text-to-image generation (Black et al., 2023; Oertell et al., 2024).
033   The key idea of diffusion models is to transform noise into approximate samples from the target
034   data distribution by iterative denoising. This iterative sampling process typically involves numerical
035   solutions of SDE or ODE, which is computationally expensive especially when generating high-
036   resolution images (Song & Dhariwal, 2024; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021; Zhang & Chen, 2023;
037   Lu et al., 2022).
038
      Consistency model (CM) (Song et al., 2023) is proposed to accelerate sample generation by learning
039   a consistency function that maps from noise to data directly. It allows both one-step fast data gen-
040   eration and multistep sampling to trade computation for sample quality. Consistency model can be
041   trained with consistency distillation or consistency training (Song et al., 2023), which enforce that
042   any points on the same trajectory specified by the probability-flow ODE are mapped to the same
043   origin, i.e. the self-consistency property. Despite the empirical success of consistency models, little
044   is understood from a theoretical perspective. In particular, recent studies (Luo et al., 2023; Song
045   & Dhariwal, 2024; Kim et al., 2024) observe diminishing improvements in sample quality when
046   increasing the number of steps in multistep sampling. In particular, they find that two-step gener-
047
      ation enhances the sample quality considerably while additional sampling steps provide minimal
      improvements. Such phenomenon motivates the theoretical understanding on consistency models,
048
      especially on multistep sampling.
049
050   The analysis of consistency models can be challenging for the following reasons:
051
052   Mismatch on the initial starting distributions: Consistency models generate samples from
053   Gaussian noise (Song et al., 2023) while the ground truth reverse processes (i.e., the denoising pro-
      cess) start from the marginal distribution of the forward process, which is unknown in practice. As


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      a consequence, we need to analyze the error caused by the mismatch in starting distributions. This
055   difficulty shows up even if we have access to the ground truth consistency function: the consistency
056   function is not Lipschitz even for distributions as simple as Bernoulli, which makes it challenging to
057   analyze this error pointwise. Because the consistency function is the solution to the probability flow
058   ODE, it is natural to consider the stability of the initial value problem. However, without a strong
059   assumption on the consistency function, this approach results in an upper bound with exponential
060   dependency in problem parameters.
061
062   Approximate self-consistency: While the training process enforces the self-consistency property,
063   it is impractical to obtain a consistency function estimate with the point-wise exact self-consistency
064   due to various error sources during training (e.g., optimization errors, statistical errors from finite
065
      training examples). It is thus natural to focus on the case where the consistency estimator only has
      approximate self-consistency under the training distribution. The key challenge is how to trans-
066
      fer the approximate self-consistency measured under the training distribution to the quality of the
067
      generated samples (e.g., Wasserstein distance between the learned distribution and the ground truth
068   distribution).
069
070   Complexity of multistep sampling: We still have a limited understanding of the theoretical bene-
071   fit of performing multistep sampling in the inference procedure of CM. When performing multistep
072   inference, we need to apply the consistency estimator to the distributions that are different from its
073   original training distribution. Since we can only guarantee approximate self-consistency under the
074   training distribution, analyzing the benefit of multistep sampling requires us to carefully bound the
075   divergence between the training distributions and the test distributions where consistency estimator
076   will be applied during inference time.

078   1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
079
080
      We summarize our contribution as follows:
081
      • Our sets of main theorems establish guarantees for multistep sampling with a general set of for-
082     ward processes and an approximate self-consistent consistency function estimator. Our results
083     apply to data distribution with mild assumptions;
084
      • We provide sample quality guarantees in Wasserstein distance for a general set of forward pro-
085
        cesses when the data distribution has bounded support or has light tail. This result naturally ap-
086     plies to multimodal distributions like Bernoulli. Our result in Wasserstein distance is dimension-
087     free due to a more careful convergence analysis for the forward process;
088
      • Sample quality guarantee in total variation distance is established for a general set of forward pro-
089
        cesses when the data distribution satisfies some smoothness assumption. In this setting, we utilize
090     an additional smoothing step to translate from Wasserstein distance to total variation distance;
091
      • We conduct two case studies to illustrate the implication of our main results on multistep sampling.
092
        We demonstrate that when using the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process as the forward process,
093
        two-step sampling can significantly improve the quality of the generated samples in terms of
094     Wasserstein distance to the data distribution under certain conditions. On the other hand, our
095     results indicate that increasing the number of sampling steps beyond two has a limited gain, which
096     is consistent with the empirical findings of CM.

098   1.2   R ELATED WORK
099
100   The theory of diffusion models has been widely studied. Chen et al. (2023b), Lee et al. (2023), and
101   Chen et al. (2023a) study the convergence of score-based generative model and provide polynomial
102   guarantees without assuming log-concavity or a functional inequality on the data. Our data assump-
103   tion is similar to that of Lee et al. (2023), which is quite minimal. Recently, deterministic samplers
104
      with probability-flow ODE have been explored from the theoretical perspective (Chen et al., 2024;
      Li et al., 2024a; 2023).
105
106   Consistency model, which learns a direct mapping from noise to data via trajectory of probability-
107   flow ODE, is proposed to accelerate the sampling step (Song et al., 2023). Song et al. (2023)
      provides asymptotic theoretical results on consistency models. At a high level, they show that if


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the consistency distillation objective is minimized, then the consistency function estimate is close
109   to the ground truth. However, they assume the consistency function estimator achieves exact self-
110   consistency in a point-wise manner. Such a point-wise accurate assumption is not realistic and
111   cannot even be achieved in a standard supervised learning setting.
112
      Lyu et al. (2023), Li et al. (2024b), and Dou et al. (2024) provide the first set of theoretical results
113
      towards understanding consistency models. Lyu et al. (2023) shows that with small consistency loss,
114
      consistency model generates samples that are close to the target data distribution in Wasserstein
115   distance or in total variation distance after modification. Li et al. (2024b) focuses on consistency
116   training. Dou et al. (2024) provides the first set of statistical theory for consistency models. However,
117   we notice that all of these works require a strong assumption on the data distribution. Specifically,
118   they assume that the ground truth consistency function is Lipschitz. While the Lipschitz condition
119   allows a direct approach to control the error of mismatch on the initial starting distribution, it’s
120   unclear how large the Lipschitz coefficient is. A direct application of Gronwall’s inequality typically
121   results in a Lipschitz constant with exponential dependency on problem parameters. To overcome
122   this, we use the data-processing inequality, which only requires approximate self-consistency and
123
      minor assumptions on target data distribution. Moreover, our upper bound is polynomial in problem
      parameters.

238

239

512   In this paper, we study the convergence of the consistency model multistep sampling procedure. We
513   establish guarantees on the distance between the sample distribution and data distribution in terms
