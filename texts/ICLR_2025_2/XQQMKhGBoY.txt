011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable power in various generation
014            tasks. Nevertheless, the large computational cost during inference is a trouble-
015            some issue for diffusion models, especially for large pretrained models such as
016
               Stable Diffusion. Quantization-aware training (QAT) is an effective method to
               reduce both memory and time costs for diffusion models while maintaining good
017
               performance. However, QAT methods usually suffer from the high cost of re-
018
               training the large pretrained model, which restricts the efficient deployment of
019            diffusion models. To alleviate this problem, we propose a framework DFastQ
020            (Diffusion Fast QAT) to accelerate the training of QAT from a difficulty-aware
021            perspective in the timestep dimension. Specifically, we first propose to adaptively
022            identify the difficulties of different timesteps according to the oscillation of their
023            training loss curves. Then we propose a difficulty-aware time allocation module,
024            which aims to dynamically allocate more training time to difficult timesteps to
025            speed up the convergence of QAT. The key component of this is a timestep drop
026            mechanism consisting of a drop probability predictor and a pair of adversarial
027
               losses. We conduct a series of experiments on different Stable Diffusion models,
               quantization settings, and sampling strategies, demonstrating that our method can
028
               effectively accelerate QAT by at least 24% while achieving comparable or even
029
               better performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Diffusion models have shown great power in a variety of generative tasks (Ho et al., 2020; Song
035
      et al., 2020a; Rombach et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Ruiz et al., 2023; Song et al., 2020b),
036   such as text-to-image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Ruiz et al., 2023; Feng
037   et al., 2024) and text-to-video generation (Chen et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2024).
038   However, one critical limitation of diffusion models is the high computational cost, which limits the
039   practical deployment of diffusion models. This issue becomes more serious with the increase in the
040   model size of advanced pretrained diffusion models, e.g., Stable Diffusion series (Rombach et al.,
041   2022).
042   To reduce the computational costs of diffusion model inference, one line of works engaged in ex-
043   ploring more efficient sampling strategies to reduce sampling steps (Lu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021;
044   Song et al., 2020a). Recently, from an orthogonal perspective, i.e., directly compressing the network
045   of diffusion models, quantization methods (Shang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; He et al., 2023b; Tang
046   et al., 2024) have been employed.
047
      Quantization methods can be categorized into two groups, i.e., post-training quantization (PTQ)
048   and quantization-aware training (QAT). PTQ methods (Nagel et al., 2020) do not require an expen-
049   sive training or finetuning process, so they are usually resource-friendly. Nevertheless, PTQ always
050   causes significant performance degradation on diffusion models (Shang et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
051   2024), especially in low-bit settings. In contrast, QAT methods (Esser et al., 2019) possess a strong
052   ability to recover performance by training/finetuning network weights and quantization parameters
053   together, but cost a large number of computational resources, especially for large pretrained mod-
      els such as Stable Diffusion. To reduce the training costs of QAT on diffusion models, previous


                                                         1

054
      literature has investigated from the architecture perspective. Specifically, He et al. (2023a) propose
055   EfficientDM that incorporates the parameter-efficient finetuning method LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
056   into the QAT framework. However, they allocate identical importance to each timestep with uni-
057   form sampling, ignoring difficulty discrepancy across timesteps, which we find a critical factor to
058   diffusion optimization and convergence in this paper.
059
      Therefore, to further reduce the time costs of QAT on diffusion models from the timestep per-
060
      spective, we propose a framework named DFastQ (Diffusion Fast QAT). Specifically, we discover
061   that there exists a pattern of difficulty discrepancy across different timesteps by observing task loss
062   curves of the QAT for diffusion models as presented in Fig. 1. The oscillation level of the train-
063   ing loss curve is adopted to estimate the difficulty. We define difficult timesteps as those that are
064   oscillatory and difficult to converge, while easy timesteps are smooth and easy to converge. Natu-
065   rally, difficult timesteps require more training time compared to easy ones. Therefore, to obtain a
066   better overall convergence speed, we propose to dynamically allocate more training time to diffi-
067   cult timesteps and less to easy ones, because it can avoid wasting training time on easy timesteps
068   that don’t need so much time to reach convergence. We adaptively identify the difficulty of each
069
      timestep during the QAT training process, and employ a timestep drop mechanism to dynamically
      control the time allocation. The timestep drop mechanism consists of a predictor that produces a
070
      drop probability for each timestep, and a pair of adversarial losses that adjust the probability based
071
      on difficulty level. It will make difficult timesteps obtain smaller drop probabilities and thus possess
072   more training time, and vice versa.
073
074   To summarize, our contributions are listed as follows:
075
            1. We introduce a framework dubbed DFastQ which is the first to accelerate QAT for diffusion
076
               models from the timestep perspective, identifying and leveraging the difficult discrepancy
077
               across timesteps.
078
079
            2. We propose to adaptively identify the difficulty based on the oscillation level. We propose
               to allocate more training time to difficult timesteps to achieve the convergence acceleration.
080
               To control the time allocation, we propose a timestep drop mechanism consisting of a drop
081
               predictor and a pair of adversarial losses.
082
            3. Extensive experiments on various Stable Diffusion models, quantization settings and sam-
083
               pling strategies demonstrate that DFastQ has a generalizable ability to accelerate QAT by
084
               at least 24% while achieving comparable or even better quality.

      Both the loss weighting and our method prioritize a subset of timesteps. We also compare to loss

      The loss value at each timestep cannot be used as an indicator to reflect the difficulty, because
199
      there is a natural difference in the order of magnitude of loss values among these timesteps. In
200
      contrast, we find that the oscillation level is a good metric to estimate the difficulty and can be

202   difficult timesteps as those that are oscillatory, while easy timesteps are smooth. The principle is that
203   oscillatory timesteps are more difficult to converge and consequently entail more training iterations
204   than smooth timesteps.
205
      Therefore, there is room to improve the speed of convergence by reallocating training time (train-
206
      ing iterations) for different timesteps according to the difficulty discrepancy. Naturally, difficult
207   timesteps require more training time compared to easy ones. To obtain a better convergence speed,
208   we propose to allocate more training time to difficult timesteps. Note that the difficulty level is dy-
209   namic during the training process, which means we need to dynamically adjust the training time for
210   each timestep.

      Overview of the timestep drop mechanism. The timestep drop mechanism aims to control the
244
      training time allocation for different timesteps. As stated in Sec. 3.2, ideally, the more difficult
245   the timestep is, the more training time is allocated to it. To achieve this goal, we propose to drop
246   timestep at random with a certain probability. Our drop mechanism ensures that difficult timesteps
247   obtain lower probabilities than easy ones, naturally possessing more training time, and vice versa.
248   Therefore, our method can avoid wasting training time on easy timesteps that don’t need so much
249   time to reach convergence. Consequently, the overall convergence will be faster.
250
      Drop predictor design. To achieve the training time allocation for different timesteps, we need to
251   adjust their drop probabilities dynamically. As demonstrated in recent literature (So et al., 2023;
252   Li et al., 2023), the activation distributions between adjacent timesteps are similar. It indicates that
253   adjacent timesteps have a similar difficulty level, which is also consistent with our observations in
254   Sec. 3.2. Hence, inspired by So et al. (So et al., 2023), we propose to predict drop probabilities based
255   on the timestep to better capture the relations among timesteps. Specifically, the drop predictor fθ is
256   employed to predict the drop probability pt for each timestep t. It takes the embedding of t as the
257   input and outputs a scalar pt ∈ [0, 1], which can be easily implemented by a simple MLP network.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
331
332   Models and datasets. We use the Stable Diffusion v1-4 (SD-1.4) and the Stable Diffusion 2.1-base
333   (SD-2.1) checkpoints provided by hugging face (hug). Following previous works (Li et al., 2023;
334   Tang et al., 2024), we use COCO train2017 and val2017 (Lin et al., 2014) as the training and the test
335   dataset respectively.
336
      Metrics. Following Tang et al. (Tang et al., 2024), we adopt FID-to-FP32 to evaluate the quality
337
      of quantized models. FID to FP32 calculates the FID distance (Heusel et al., 2017) between the
338   generated images of full-precision models and quantized models, which can effectively measure
339   both the image fidelity and the prompt-image matching. We do not use CLIP score (Hessel et al.,
340   2021) because it has been shown to be poor at distinguishing the quality of quantized diffusion
341   models (Tang et al., 2024). We generate 5,000 images using prompts from COCO val2017, and
342   calculate the FID-to-FP32 score. We calculate the BOPs(Yu et al., 2020) metric to measure the
343   theoretical computation amount of the model.
344   Hyper-parameters. In all the experiments, we maintain the same hyperparameters for our proposed
345   modules. The learning rate of the drop predictor fθ is set to 1e-6, which is a common practice and
346   performs well in our task. M is set to 0.6T . The window length c is 5.
347
      The rule of determining convergence iteration. For both baseline and our method, we test the
348
      model for every 100 iterations. We train the model until the performance deteriorates twice in a row.
349
      For example, if the FID scores of three consecutive evaluations are 10.91, 10.92 and 11.00, we will
350   stop training, and choose the best iteration so far as the convergence iteration.

526   5      C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we propose a framework dubbed DFastQ to accelerate the Quantization-aware Training
529   (QAT) for diffusion models. From the timestep perspective, we identify the difficulty discrepancy
530
      across different denoising timesteps, and propose to accelerate the convergence of QAT by allocating
      more training time to difficulty timesteps. To dynamically control the time allocation, we propose
531
      the timestep drop mechanism consisting of a drop probability predictor and a pair of adversarial
532
      losses conditioned on difficulty level. We conduct experiments on a variety of Stable Diffusion
533   models, quantization settings, and sampling strategies, which proves that our method can accelerate
534   QAT by at least 24% while maintaining the model quality.

747    As shown in Tab. 1, the reduction ratio of actual GPU hours is slightly less than the reduction ratio
748    of convergence iteration, for example, 30% (from 26.4h to 18.6h) vs. 35% (from 4000 to 2600). It
749    results from the slight extra cost of collecting more intermediate data (xt , t) through the denoising
750    process. This extra cost is minor as the inference latency of the denoising process is much less than
751    the QAT training time. Besides, the extra cost can be reduced by parallel inference.
