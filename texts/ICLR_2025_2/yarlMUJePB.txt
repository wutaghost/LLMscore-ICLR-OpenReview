009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become a powerful tool for
012            modeling molecular data. To enhance their reliability and interpretability, various
013            explanation methods have been developed to identify key molecular substructures,
014            specifically a set of edges, in the decision-making process. Early work with 2D
015            GNNs represented molecules as graphs with atoms as nodes and bonds as edges,
016
               neglecting 3D geometric configurations. While existing explanation methods per-
               form well on 2D GNNs, there is a pressing need for 3D explanation methods
017
               tailored for 3D GNNs, which outperform 2D GNNs in many tasks. Current ex-
018
               planation methods struggle with 3D GNNs due to the construction of edges based
019            on cut-off distances in 3D GNNs, resulting in an exponentially large number of
020            edges. We identify the sources of errors in explanations and decompose them into
021            two components based on a derived upper bound between the optimized masks
022            and the actual explanatory subgraph. This gap can be significant, especially for
023            3D GNNs because of the large number of edges. To achieve optimal explanation
024            fidelity, our method aims to bridge this gap by assigning two energy values to each
025            atom based on its contribution to the prediction: one energy reflects the scenario
026            where this node is important in making the decision, while the other represents the
027
               scenario where it is unimportant. In analogy to physics, lower energy values indi-
               cate greater stability in the prediction, and thus, we are more confident about the
028
               scenario with which it is associated. Our approach strives to push up and down the
029
               energies, respectively, to distinguish these two scenarios to simultaneously mini-
030            mize both components of the derived upper bound of error, enabling us to identify
031            a stable subgraph that maintains high explanation fidelity. Experiments conducted
032            on backbone networks and the QM9 dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of our
033            method in providing accurate and reliable explanations for 3D graphs.

037
038   In recent years, molecular learning has emerged as a crucial area of study, driving advances in
039
      drug discovery, protein engineering, and materials science (Gori et al., 2005; Wu et al., 2018; Sher-
      vashidze et al., 2011; Fout et al., 2017). Traditionally, molecules have been represented as 2D planar
040
      graphs, where atoms serve as nodes and chemical bonds are depicted as edges without considering
041
      the geometric configurations. The limitations of 2D representations in capturing molecular prop-
042   erties have led to a growing focus on 3D graph representations (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Defferrard
043   et al., 2016; Veličković et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019; Gao et al., 2021) that rep-
044   resent entities with spatial coordinates, enabling them to capture complex spatial dependencies that
045   are critical for tasks involving 3D molecular structures. This shift is critical because the 3D structure
046   of molecules, particularly their spatial arrangement, directly influences their chemical behavior and
047   biological functions. In response, 3D GNNs have been developed to incorporate geometric informa-
048   tion and shown to outperform their 2D counterparts in numerous tasks (Schütt et al., 2017; Satorras
049   et al., 2021; Gasteiger et al., 2020b; Liu et al., 2022; Shuaibi et al., 2021; Thomas et al., 2018; Liao
050
      & Smidt, 2022; Anderson et al., 2019; Fuchs et al., 2020; Schütt et al., 2021; Batzner et al., 2022).

052   pretability has become increasingly important. Molecular systems are inherently complex, and
053   GNNs are often treated as black-box models, making it difficult to understand how specific struc-
      tural features contribute to predictions, which raises significant concerns regarding transparency in

178   2.2   E XISTING E XPLANATION M ETHODS
179
180
      Graph Neural Network (GNN) explanation methods can be classified according to several criteria:
      transductive or inductive explanations, instance-level explanations (Ying et al., 2019; Schlichtkrull
181
      et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021) versus model-level explanations (Yuan et al., 2020), model-specific
182
      approaches (Dai & Wang, 2021; Miao et al., 2022a; Pope et al., 2019a) compared to model-agnostic
183   methods (Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021), and node-level (Ying et al., 2019;
184   Pope et al., 2019a) versus graph-level explanations (Wang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2020). In terms
185   of explanation strategies, four primary categories emerge: (1) Gradient-based methods (Shrikumar
186   et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2016; Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019) compute the gradients of target predic-
187   tions with respect to inputs via back-propagation but often impose structural constraints on GNNs;
188   (2) Decomposition methods (Pope et al., 2019b; Schwarzenberg et al., 2019; Schnake et al., 2021)
189   assign importance scores to input features by analyzing model parameters to reveal relationships
190   between inputs and outputs; (3) Surrogate methods (Huang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021) use
191
      interpretable models to explain the behavior of complex GNNs, though they often encounter diffi-
      culties with the discrete and topological nature of 3D graphs; (4) Perturbation-based methods (Ying
192
      et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2021) identify important subgraphs by perturbing edges or
193
      nodes with masks and analyzing output prediction changes.

      2.3   E NERGY- BASED M ODEL
197
      The central concept of Energy-Based Models (EBMs) is the use of Boltzmann distributions to assess
198   the likelihood of input samples. This involves defining a function E(xi ) : Rdv → R that assigns a
199   non-probabilistic scalar known as energy to each configuration of the input data. Influential works
200   (Xie et al., 2016; 2018) have significantly shaped research in this domain. EBMs have achieved
201   notable success in various applications, including classification (Li et al., 2022; Grathwohl et al.,
202   2019), regression tasks (Danelljan et al., 2020), structured prediction (Belanger & McCallum, 2016;
203   Rooshenas et al., 2019), and out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Liu et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023).
204   Additionally, (Yu et al., 2022; Pang & Wu, 2021) have explored the use of EBMs in latent space
205   for generation, while others have applied them to unsupervised learning (Ranzato et al., 2007) and
206
      concept-based modeling (Xu et al., 2024).

      in the derived upper bound.

      3.1   2D V. S . 3D GRAPH EXPLANATION
217
218   The primary distinction between 2D and 3D graph explanations arises from the structural differences
219   inherent in 2D and 3D graphs. In 2D graphs, each node is associated with a set of edges that connect
220   these nodes in a planar layout. The relationships and interactions among nodes are captured in this
221   planar representation. In 3D graphs, nodes are represented in three-dimensional space, allowing for
222   a more accurate depiction of the physical arrangement and spatial relationships between entities,
223   while edges are typically determined from the coordinates of the nodes by a cut-off distance (Schütt
224   et al., 2017; Gasteiger et al., 2020b;a; Wang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Schütt et al., 2021; Thomas
225   et al., 2018).
226   More concretely, for small molecular structures, the number of bonds (edges) between atoms (nodes)
227   is typically limited, resulting in a rather sparse graph. However, 3D GNNs do not utilize chemical
228   bonds as edges; instead, the 3D spatial configurations of nodes are used to construct edges resulting
229   in an exponentially large number of edges. As a result, 3D GNN explanation poses significant
230   challenges to existing explanation methods, partially illustrated in Fig. 1 and detailed below:
231
232   1. Differing Assumptions in 2D and 3D Explanations: The assumptions underlying graph expla-
233      nations differ between 2D and 3D graphs. For instance, in 2D graph explanations, methods such
234
         as those proposed by (Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020) assume that the graph being explained
         is a random graph (Gilbert, 1959; ERDdS & R&wi, 1959), with edges considered independent
235
         of one another. However, this assumption does not hold for complex networks like molecular
236
         dynamics systems, where force field theory models both intramolecular interactions and inter-
237      molecular terms, contributing to the total energy E of the molecule (Leach, 2001).
238
239   2. Dense Adjacency Matrix: The adjacency matrix A in 3D graphs is typically dense, unlike
240      the sparse adjacency matrix commonly observed in 2D graphs. As one can imagine, this leads
241      to a problem of combinatorial complexity with respect to the number of edges with discrete
242      masks. Even with soft mask relaxation, the large number of edges introduces a substantial lack
243
         of confidence in identifying the explanatory subgraph, often resulting in suboptimal explanation
         outcomes. Specifically, with low confidence in distinguishing important and unimportant sub-
244
         parts, the soft-masked “subgraph” deviates a lot from the final discrete explanatory sub-graph.
245
         The optimization process might find a soft-masked “sub-graph” with minimal loss in Eq. (3);
246      however, when we decide the explanatory subgraph from soft-masks, the lack of confidence in
247      the soft masks leads to poor final explanation performance.

400   Evaluation Metrics Following the standard protocol for QM9 data, we use Mean Absolute Error
401   (MAE) to evaluate the performance of 3D GNN predictions against ground-truth molecular prop-
402   erties. A lower MAE indicates higher predictive accuracy. In the context of explaining 3D molec-
403   ular graphs, let MAEW represent the prediction error using the entire graph, while MAES denotes
404   the prediction error using the optimal subgraph selected for explanation, as described in Eq. (2).
405   Naturally, MAES is expected to be higher than MAEW , as the complete graph generally yields bet-
406
      ter predictions than the subgraph, given the same pretrained 3D GNN model (such as SchNet or
      DimeNet++). We define explanation fidelity as Fidelity− = MAES − MAEW , which measures the
407
      quality of explanations produced by different methods. A lower Fidelity− indicates that the method
408
      provides a more accurate and reliable explanation. It is important to note that the reported results
409   represent the average across all molecules in the QM9 test set. Since the standard deviation is two
410   orders of magnitude smaller than the average fidelity, we do not report the standard deviation in our
411   results.

509   5     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE WORK
510
511   In conclusion, our research highlights the advancements in explaining 3D graphs. While existing
512   explanation methods have made strides in interpreting 2D GNNs, there remains a critical gap in
513
      developing effective explanations for 3D GNNs due to the complexities introduced by the geometric
      configurations and the sheer volume of edges. By acknowledging the varying assumptions in 3D
514
      GNNs, we reformulate 3D GNN explanations and identify a bottleneck in all the existing methods
515
      for 3D explanations. We propose a novel energy-based explanation function to generate probabil-
516   ities that are approximately discrete and highly confident. Our method effectively bridges the gap
517   between optimized masks and actual explanatory subgraphs, leading to improved explanation fi-
518   delity. The results obtained from experiments on backbone networks and the QM9 dataset affirm
519   the efficacy of our approach in providing accurate and reliable explanations for 3D graphs. Building
520   on our derived bound that characterizes the discrepancies between the optimized masks and final
521   explanations, it would be intriguing to explore whether more advanced methods can be developed
522   from these new bounds. Such advancements could significantly enhance the accuracy and reliability
523   of explanations in 3D GNNs, ultimately offering deeper insights into molecular data.
