011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Representation learning of object deformations from images has been a long-
014            standing challenge in various image or video analysis tasks. Existing deep neu-
015
               ral networks typically focus on visual features (e.g., intensity and texture), but
               they often fail to capture the underlying geometric and topological structures of
016
               objects. This limitation becomes especially critical in areas, such as medical
017
               imaging and 3D modeling, where maintaining the structural integrity of objects
018            is essential for accuracy and generalization across diverse datasets. In this paper,
019            we introduce ToRL, a novel Topology-preserving Representation Learning model
020            that, for the first time, offers an explicit mechanism for modeling intricate ob-
021            ject topology in the latent feature space. We develop a comprehensive learning
022            framework that captures object deformations via learned transformation groups
023            in the latent space. Each layer of our network’s decoder is carefully designed
024            with an integrated smooth composition module, ensuring that topological proper-
025            ties are preserved throughout the learning process. Moreover, in contrast to a few
026
               related works that rely on a reference image to predict object deformations dur-
               ing inference, our approach eliminates this impractical requirement. To validate
027
               ToRL’s effectiveness, we conduct extensive multi-class classification experiments
028
               across a wide range of datasets, including synthetic 2D images, real 3D brain
029            magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans, real 3D adrenal computed tomography
030            (CT) shapes, and real 2D facial expression images. Experimental results demon-
031            strate that ToRL outperforms state-of-the-art methods, setting a new way to en-
032            force topological consistency in representation learning. Our code is available at
033            - https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ToRL-44BF/

038   Recent advances in deep learning (DL) have driven remarkable progress in large-scale image anal-
039
      ysis tasks, such as classification (Hao et al., 2023; Vilas et al., 2024), segmentation (Ke et al., 2023;
      You et al., 2024), and object detection (Deng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Pu et al., 2024), of-
040
      ten achieving near-human performance. Yet, beneath these successes lies a fundamental limitation:
041
      current models largely rely on image representations learned from intensity or textures, leading to
042   much reduced attention to the underlying geometric structure of objects (Geirhos et al., 2018; Baker
043   et al., 2018; Malhotra et al., 2022). This oversight poses risks to high-stakes domains, including but
044   not limited to medical imaging, robotics, or 3D modeling, where maintaining the structural integrity
045   of objects is critical (Malhotra et al., 2021; Linsley et al., 2017; Ullman et al., 2016). While exist-
046   ing deep neural networks may have access to limited geometric features in the form of local edges
047   or orientations, they tend to miss the complete object geometry and structure. This negatively im-
048   pacts their ability to generalize and perform robustly across diverse datasets and applications when
049   studying objects with preserved topology are indispensable.
050   To address this problem, recent research in geometric deep learning has focused on representing
051   and synthesizing objects with predefined geometric properties through analytic math formulations
052   of graphs or points (Bronstein et al., 2017; Masci et al., 2016; Rematas et al., 2021). While these
053   approaches have shown promise, they often prove impractical in real-world applications where an-
      alytic formulations are unavailable or impractical. Later, other works began leveraging DL to au-

164   In this section, we introduce a novel topology-preserving representation learning network, ToRL.
165   Our model highlights two key contributions: (i) an explicit mechanism to capture complex object
166   topology by learning transformation groups in the latent space, and (ii) a newly designed decoder
167   equipped with a smooth group composition module that carefully integrates features from skip con-
168   nections at each layer, while complying with topological constraints. This is crucial, as conventional
169   fusion of features such as addition or concatenation in the deformation space can break the smooth-
170   ness of transformation fields, leading to the violation of these constraints. An overview of our
171   proposed architecture is shown in Fig. 3.

      We validate the effectiveness of our model across diverse datasets, including 2D synthetic shapes,

      3D real brain MRIs capturing complex neurological structures, 3D real adrenal CTs reflecting the

      variability and complexity of soft tissue, and 2D real facial expressions. These multi-faceted datasets

307   4.1   E XPERIMENTS
308
309   We evaluate the proposed model, ToRL, from three key perspectives: (i) assessing the quality of
310   learned latent representations by quantitatively measuring within-class and across-class feature dis-
311   tances in the latent space; (ii) visualizing the latent representations for 2D shapes and 3D adrenal
312   datasets; and (iii) demonstrating its effectiveness in downstream tasks, particularly image classifica-
313   tion. A detailed experimental evaluation plan is described as follows.
314   Baseline selection. We compare ToRL with two existing approaches for learning latent features of
315   object deformation from groupwise images: Geo-SIC (Wang & Zhang, 2022) and CondiT (Dalca
316   et al., 2019). These baselines have two key limitations: (i) they require a reference image during the
317   testing phase, whereas our model ToRL does not; and (ii) they assume that objects across different
318   classes are deformable, which restricts their application to datasets where this condition is met. To
319   ensure a fair comparison, we select five deformable classes (circle, cloud, envelope, square, and
320
      triangle) from the Google Quickdraw dataset, following the experimental setup of Geo-SIC. For the
      two additional 3D datasets, since the objects are deformable across all classes, we include the entire
321
      dataset for experimental comparison.
322
323   Evaluation of learned latent representations. To evaluate the quality of the representations learned
      by ToRL, we first compare them against baseline models by leveraging these features for classifi-


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      cation tasks across all datasets. We train a classifier consists of three fully connected layers, with
325   ReLU activation and a dropout layers on the learned features from all methods, and then report key
326   performance metrics, including classification accuracy (Acc), precision (Prec), and F1-score (F1-
327   sc). Additionally, for each testing group, we utilize a combination of inter-class divergence and
328   intra-class compactness metrics to evaluate feature discriminability in the latent space (Li et al.,
329   2023; Feng et al., 2024). For inter-class separability, we measure the Silhouette Score (Abavisani
330   et al., 2020), Fisher’s Discriminant Ratio (Wang et al., 2019), and KL-Divergence (Dinari & Freifeld,
331   2022) to show how separate the features are between classes. For intra-class compactness, we eval-
332   uate Euclidean Distance and the Davies-Bouldin Index (Abavisani et al., 2020), measuring how
333
      tightly features cluster within each class. Together, these metrics offer a comprehensive evaluation
      of both class separation and within-class cohesion in the latent space. To further analyze the learned
334
      latent representation of these models, we visualize the latent space using t-SNE map of all models
335
      across all datasets.
336
337   Evaluate the benefit of ToRL in downstream tasks. We demonstrate the effectiveness of ToRL
338   and two baselines by comparing their learned latent representations integrated into the downstream
339
      image classification tasks. For all experiments, we use a variety of image encoders as backbones to
      extract latent image features, including a wide range of models such as ResNet (He et al., 2016),
340
      EfficientNet (Tan, 2019), and DenseNet (Huang et al., 2017), along with their most recent versions.
341
      To evaluate performance, we report classification accuracy (Acc), and precision (Prec).
342
343   Evaluation of topology-preserved decoder. To evaluate the quality of our newly designed de-
344   coder (ND), we compare it to conventional decoders (CD) used in the two baseline models (Geo-
345
      SIC/CondiT). To determine the effectiveness of the decoders, we measure whether the topology is
      well-preserved during the learning process. A key metric for this evaluation is the determinant of the
346
      Jacobian (DetJac), which assesses the quality of transformations and their adherence to topological
347
      constraints. For example, there is no volume change when DetJac=1, while volume shrinks when
348   DetJac<1 and expands when DetJac>1. The value of DetJac smaller than zero indicates an arti-
349   fact or singularity in the transformation field, i.e., a failure to preserve the diffeomorphic property
350   when the effect of folding and crossing grids occurs. We also measure RMSE and SSIM scores
351   between the source and transformed images to evaluate the quality and accuracy of the geometric
352   transformations across all models.
353
      Evaluation of computational load and ToRL components. We demonstrate the effectiveness of
354   the transformation group module (TGM) and group composition block by conducting comparative

528   6                       C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper presents ToRL, a novel topology-preserving representation learning model, that for the
531
      first time explicitly captures complex object topology in the latent deformation space. In contrast to
      existing deep neural networks that often overlook topological and geometric properties, ToRL is de-
532
      signed to maintain topological integrity of image objects throughout the learning process. To achieve
533
      this goal, our model directly learns transformation groups in the latent space of object deformations
534   derived from images. The decoder architecture features a novel smooth group composition mod-
535   ule in the deformation space, preserving topological properties during the network decoding phase.
536   More importantly, our model ToRL eliminates the impractical reliance on a reference image for pre-
537   dicting the representations of object deformations during inference, which is a limitation present in
538   current methods. Our future work includes extending ToRL to multimodal image datasets, explor-
539   ing alternative transformation groups beyond stationary velocity fields, and applying it to additional
      downstream image analysis tasks, such as segmentation and object recognition and tracking.

760   2D Synthetic shapes. We first randomly choose 20000 2D images from 40 distinct classes (500
761   images per class where the images are deformable within the class) from the Google Quickdraw
762   data repository Jongejan et al. (2016). All images underwent affine transformation and intensity
763   normalization with the size of 224 × 224.
764
      3D Brain MRIs. We include 800 public T1-weighted brain MRIs from the Alzheimer’s Disease
765
      Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) Jack Jr et al. (2008). All subjects ranged in age from 50 to 100,
766   with 200 images each from cognitively normal (CN) and patients affected by Alzheimer’s disease
767   (AD). All MRIs were preprocessed to be the size of 104 × 128 × 120, 1mm3 isotropic voxels,
768   and underwent skull-stripping, intensity min-max normalization, bias-field correction, and affine
769   registration Reuter et al. (2012)
770
      3D Adrenal CTs. We select 1584 left and right real 3D adrenal glands of 792 patients from
771
      AdrenalMNIST3D data repository (Yang et al., 2023). This dataset is specifically collected to iden-
772   tify the presence of adrenal mass differentiating from normal adrenal glands. All images underwent
773   affine transformation and intensity normalization with the size of 64 × 64 × 64.
774
775
      2D Face Expressions. We select 1884 real-world face images from CAS-PEAL data repository
      (Gao et al., 2007). Focusing on capturing different facial expressions under various background
776
      and lighting settings. We perform intensity normalization and affine transformation with the size
777
      of 128 × 128. Performing expression recognition tasks, we follow an identical training and testing
778   evaluation protocol for fair comparison.

803   particularly notable in facial expressions (a), brain MRIs (b), and adrenal CTs (c). All methods show
804   performance degradation with higher noise levels, but ToRL demonstrates superior robustness.
