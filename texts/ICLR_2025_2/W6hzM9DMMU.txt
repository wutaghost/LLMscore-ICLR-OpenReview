009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Based on the framework of Conformal Prediction (CP), we study the online con-
012            struction of valid confidence sets given a black-box machine learning model. By
013            converting the target confidence levels into quantile levels, the problem can be
014            reduced to predicting the quantiles (in hindsight) of a sequentially revealed data
015            sequence. Two very different approaches have been studied previously:
016            • Direct approach: Assuming the data sequence is iid or exchangeable, one could
017               maintain the empirical distribution of the observed data as an algorithmic belief,
018
                  and directly predict its quantiles.
019
               • Indirect approach: As statistical assumptions often do not hold in practice, a
                  recent trend is to consider the adversarial setting and apply first-order online
020
                  optimization to moving quantile losses (Gibbs & Candès, 2021). It requires
021
                  knowing the target quantile level beforehand, and suffers from a monotonicity
022               issue on the obtained confidence sets, due to the associated loss linearization.
023
               This paper presents a novel Bayesian CP framework that combines their strengths.
024            Without any statistical assumption, it is able to both
025
               • answer multiple arbitrary confidence level queries online, with provably low
026               regret; and
027            • overcome the monotonicity issue suffered by first-order optimization baselines,
028               due to being “data-centric” rather than “iterate-centric”.
029            In addition, it can adapt to an iid environment with the correct coverage probability
030            guarantee.
031            From a technical perspective, our key idea is to regularize the algorithmic belief of
032            the above direct approach by a Bayesian prior, which “robustifies” it by simulating
033            a non-linearized Follow the Regularized Leader (FTRL) algorithm on the output.
034            For statisticians, this can be regarded as an online adversarial view of Bayesian
035            inference. Importantly, the proposed belief update backbone is shared by prediction
036            heads targeting different confidence levels, bringing practical benefits analogous to
037            the recently proposed concept of U-calibration (Kleinberg et al., 2023).

164   Online CP Adversarial online CP was first studied by Gibbs & Candès (2021). It was shown that
165   gradient descent with constant learning rate can guarantee low coverage frequency error, i.e.,

170   this performance metric: one could trivially satisfy this coverage frequency bound by predicting a
171   data-independent alternation between the empty set and the entire label space. To rule out such cases,
172   the typical solution is to consider an additional performance metric, such as the regret (Bhatnagar
173   et al., 2023; Gibbs & Candès, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) and the multi-calibrated coverage frequency
174   (Bastani et al., 2022). Under the additional iid assumption, Angelopoulos et al. (2024) studied the

177   tages even over simultaneously bounding the regret and the coverage frequency error (since loss

180
      to the classical Follow the Perturbed Leader (FTPL) algorithm in online learning (Kalai & Vempala,
181
      2005). Notable examples of FTPL include Thompson sampling, a prevalent Bayesian approach for
182   bandits and reinforcement learning (Thompson, 1933; Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020; Xu & Zeevi,

333   metric needs to be “data-centric”. To be more specific, consider the CP interaction protocol displayed
334
      in Figure 1. Achieving low coverage frequency error requires the CP algorithm’s output to depend
      not only on the top level (the nature and the base model), but also on the users’ previous confidence
335
      level queries. This is in contrast with our regret minimization algorithm, whose output is independent
336
      of the users’ query history.
337
338   Furthermore, just like the pathological example given by Bastani et al. (2022), first-order optimization
339   baselines essentially achieve the desirable coverage frequency due to the “overshooting” provided by
340
      the loss linearization. This is perhaps clear from the first online CP algorithm (ACI) proposed by
      Gibbs & Candès (2021): regarding the update Eq.(3) with the constant learning rate ηt = η, it is shown
341
      that the coverage frequency error monotonically decreases as η → ∞. Such a peculiar behavior
342
      results precisely from overshooting: if α = 90%, then a failed coverage needs nine successful
343   coverages to compensate, and ensuring this does not have much to do with the observed data. This
344   casts some natural doubt on the coverage frequency error that the algorithm is designed to optimize.

      few extensions of this core result.

      5   D ISCUSSION
487
488   Loss-agnostic decision making The downstream simulation of FTRL (Theorem 1) deviates from
489   the common scope of online learning, which requires specifying a single loss function in each round.
490   Instead, it has a similar flavor as the recently proposed concept of U-calibration (Kleinberg et al.,
491   2023; Luo et al., 2024): forecasting for an unknown downstream agent. Prior works on U-calibration
492   considered the setting of finite-class distributional prediction with generic proper losses, while
493   our paper focuses on the continuous domain [0, R] (i.e., “infinitely many classes”) with the more
494   specific quantile losses. The extra problem structure allows our algorithm to be deterministic (rather
495   than being randomized like FTPL), thus establishing a closer connection to typical deterministic
496
      algorithms in online convex optimization.
497   Omniprediction (Gopalan et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2024) is another iconic framework for loss-agnostic
498   decision making, whose main idea is to maintain a multi-calibrated algorithmic belief in the sense of
499   (Hébert-Johnson et al., 2018). Our approach does not require calibration as an underlying mechanism.
500   Bayesian interpretation We have been calling our framework “Bayesian”, as the belief update
501   Eq.(5) can be viewed by statisticians as a Bayesian distribution estimator from iid samples. Following
502   (Gelman et al., 2021, Chapter 23), we now make this very concrete.
503
      Consider the following distribution estimation problem: given x1 , . . . , xn ∈ X sampled iid from
504
      an unknown distribution X, what is a good estimate of X? As opposed to the frequentist estimate
505
      P̄ (x1:n ), a Bayesian estimator would place a prior F0 over all distributions supported on the domain
506   X , compute the posterior Fn from the samples, and output the mean E[Fn ].
507
508   For analytical convenience, one would typically choose F0 as a conjugate prior: it refers to a family
509
      of priors such that if F0 belongs to this family, then Fn also belongs to this family. The most notable
      conjugate prior for distribution estimation is the Dirichlet process (DP), denoted as DP(α, P0 ). Here
510
      α and P0 are hyperparameters: P0 equals the mean E[DP(α, P0 )], while α controls the variance of
511
      DP(α, P0 ). Due to the conjugacy, if F0 = DP(α, P0 ), then
512
513                                                     α            n
                                 Fn = DP α + n,             P0 +         P̄ (x1:n ) .
514                                                   α+n          α+n
515   Consequently, the Bayesian estimator of the distribution X is
516                                                 α             n
517                                    E[Fn ] =         P0 +         P̄ (x1:n ).
                                                 α+n           α+n
518
      This is the same as the belief update Eq.(5) in our algorithm, with the hyperparameter λt = α/(α+n).
519
      Our results can therefore be regarded as an online adversarial treatment of Bayesian inference,
520   embedded in the CP protocol, and without the iid assumption.

      Focusing on the online adversarial formulation of conformal prediction, this paper demonstrates
      various benefits of being Bayesian. Specifically, we propose a novel Bayesian algorithm with a
526
      number of strengths – it supports multiple arbitrary confidence level queries, achieves probably low
