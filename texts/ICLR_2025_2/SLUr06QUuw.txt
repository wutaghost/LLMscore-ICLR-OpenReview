012
013
               Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD) matric has been proven to be an effective fea-
               ture descriptor in the realm of artificial intelligence, as it can encode spatiotem-
014
               poral statistical information of data on a curved Riemannian manifold, i.e., SPD
015
               manifold. Although existing Riemannian neural networks have demonstrated su-
016            periority in many scientific fields, the inherent reliance on labels within super-
017            vised learning renders them susceptible to label errors. Besides, it is insufficient
018            to depend solely on labels to learn effective feature distributions in some com-
019            plicated data scenarios. Drawing inspiration from the considerable achievements
020            of contrastive learning (CL) across diverse tasks, we extend the conventional CL
021            paradigm to the context of SPD manifolds, which we denote SymCL, paving the
022            way for a novel approach in SPD matrix-based visual classification. Furthermore,
023            we inject a Riemannian triplet loss-based Riemannian metric learning (RML) into
024
               the designed SPD manifold CL framework for the sake of improving the discrim-
               ination of the learned geometric representations. Extensive experimental results
025
               on four datasets verify the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      In the field of pattern recognition and computer vision, deep learning technique has achieved sig-
031   nificant progress in extracting powerful feature representations. These techniques are particularly
032   adept at handling complex data structures, which is crucial for extending their application to special-
033   ized domains such as geometric data analysis. Especially in processing Symmetric Positive Definite
034   (SPD) manifold data, deep learning models have made considerable progress.
035
      However, the inherent difficulty in processing and classifying these matrices, which are essentially
036
      SPD, is that they cannot be regarded as Euclidean data points. This is because the topology formed
037   by a group of SPD matrices of the same dimensionality is not a vector space, but a curved Rie-
038   mannian manifold, i.e., SPD manifold Arsigny et al. (2007). Therefore, it is inappropriate to di-
039   rectly compute SPD data points in Euclidean space. To address this limitation, Pennec et al. (2006)
040   and Arsigny et al. (2007) employ Riemannian metrics to characterize the Riemannian geometric
041   of Symmetric Positive Definite matrices (SPD), including Log-Euclidean Metrics (LEM) Arsigny
042   et al. (2007) and Affine-Invariant Riemannian Metrics (AIRM) Pennec et al. (2006). These Rie-
043   mannian metrics allow for the extension of Euclidean tools to the SPD manifolds. Specifically, this
044   involves mapping the SPD manifold-valued data points into a flat space via tangent approximation
045
      Tosato et al. (2010); Sanin et al. (2013) and Tuzel et al. (2008), then embedding it in a Reproducing
      Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) that incorporates Riemannian kernel functions Harandi & Salzmann
046
      (2015); Wang et al. (2012; 2022a; 2015) and Harandi et al. (2012). Unfortunately, both of these
047
      methods primarily operate in Euclidean space for representation learning and classification, which
048   inevitably distorts the geometric structure of the original data manifold. To counter this challenge,
049   recent SPD matrix discriminant analysis methods have recently been proposed for geometry-aware
050   feature transformation. Gao et al. (2019); Huang et al. (2015); Zhou et al. (2017); Nguyen & Yang
051   (2023) and Chen et al. (2023). The core of these methods is to generate a low-dimensional feature
052   manifold with high discriminability by simultaneously learning an embedding mapping and a sim-
053   ilarity metric on the original SPD manifold. As a result, the resulting feature space can faithfully
      reflect the geometric structure of the input SPD data points.

083   Although the aforementioned classification methods based on SPD manifold learning have achieved
084   fruitful results, the inherent supervised learning paradigm makes the feature learning process over-
085   reliance on semantic labels. The ablation study in the following section demonstrates that erroneous
086   labels involved in the training process can have a negative impact on the final classification per-

      data structures and patterns without relying on the semantic labels is of great significance to the
088
      Riemannian manifold-based visual classification.

090   Contrastive learning (CL), a powerful self-supervised learning approach, has made significant
091   progress in computer vision and natural language processing. Compared to traditional supervised
092   learning paradigm, CL can analyze the intrinsic data distributions by measuring the similarity of a
093
      pair of samples in the metric subspace without requiring additional annotation information.He et al.
      (2020); Chen et al. (2020). Moreover, the study in He et al. (2020) demonstrates that features gen-
094
      erated by CL are generally more roust and effective, and can be easily transferred to other tasks.
095
      Therefore, we generalize the potential and efficiency of CL to the scenario of SPD manifolds.
096
097   In this paper, the proposed SPD manifold-based CL framework mainly contains three parts, which
098   are the Riemannian data augmentation(RDA), SPD encoder, and Riemannian triplet loss, respec-
099
      tively. It’s well known that data augmentation (DA) is a crucial part of contrastive learning. How-
      ever, due to the unique geometric structure of SPD matrices, traditional DA methods for Euclidean
100
      space (such as rotation, color change, crop) are no longer applicable. Therefore, using the proper-
101
      ties of SPD matrix and the corresponding Riemannian operator, we design several DA methods for
102   the proposed SymCL. We use SPDNet Huang & Van Gool (2017) as the encoder, and constructing
103   positive and negative sample pairs within each batch to train SymCL. Specifically, in each batch, we
104   randomly select one sample as the anchor point and generate a positive sample through DA, with the
105   remaining samples serving as negatives. Then these samples are feed into the encoder for feature
106   transformation mapping, utilizing the commonly used contrastive loss, infoNCE He et al. (2020),
107   as the supervisor of the proposed model. Furthermore, we design a Riemannian triplet loss (based
      on Wang et al. (2022b)) for the sake of encoding and analyzing the geometric distribution of the

127   2.1   SPD M ATRIX L EARNING
128
      Traditional geometry-aware Riemannian learning techniques have made significant progress in pro-
129
      cessing and classifying visual data based on SPD matrices. However, their inherent shallow linear
130   embedding mechanism proves inadequate for extracting fine-grained geometric features, particularly
131   in complicated visual scenarios. Taking inspiration from the benefits of convolutional neural net-
132   works (ConvNet), the work studied in Huang & Van Gool (2017) creates an end-to-end Riemannian
133   neural network that specializes in nonlinear learning of SPD matrices. Diverging from ConvNets
134   He et al. (2016); Simonyan & Zisserman (2014) in feature learning and parameter optimization, this
135   network takes structured SPD matrices as inputs, requiring each layer to maintain the Riemannian
136   geometry of the data manifold. Therefore, the optimization of parameters also needs to be performed
137   on the Riemannian manifold to ensure that the output is an SPD matrix.
138
139   2.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
140
      Contrastive learning can be tracked back to Hadsell et al. (2006). In this method, representations
141
      are learned by contrasting positive pairs against negative pairs. Inspired by this ideology, the work
142   studied in Dosovitskiy et al. (2014) suggests treating each instance as a class, represented by a
143   feature vector in a parametric form. Subsequently, another study work Wu et al. (2018) suggests to
144   utilize a memory bank to store instance class representation vectors, this approach has adopted and
145   extended in several recent papers Zhuang et al. (2019); Tian et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Misra
146   & Maaten (2020). Other works explore the utilization of in-batch samples for negative sampling
147   instead of a memory bank Doersch & Zisserman (2017); Ye et al. (2019); Ji et al. (2019).
148   A common way to define a loss function is to measure the difference between a model’s prediction
149   and a fixed target. Contrastive loss, as described in Hadsell et al. (2006), quantifies the similarities
150   between pairs of samples in a representation space. Unlike methods where inputs are matched
151   against fixed targets, contrastive loss allows the target to dynamically vary during training and can
152   be defined based on the learned representations Hadsell et al. (2006).

      3.6    P RETRAINING
344
345
      In this paper, we first use the proposed contrastive learning method to pre-train a SPD encoder.
346
      Then, a fully connected (FC) layer is appended to the tail of the trained SPD encoder for down-
347
      stream tasks. We use the proxy mechanism described in the previous proxy section to construct
348   positive and negative sample pairs, and pre-train the entire network with both InfoNCE Loss and
349   RML (Riemannian triplet loss). We know that in the realm of traditional, contrastive learning the
350   encoders are typically pre-trained in a self-supervised manner using the ImageNet dataset, followed
351   by supervised fine-tuning on the corresponding dataset for downstream tasks. However, in the con-
352   text of Riemannian networks, we lack a universal large-scale dataset similar to ImageNet, making it
353   impractical to conduct general pre-training. Moreover, the eigenvalue operation involved in SPDNet
354   primarily relies on the CPU and cannot be accelerated by GPU, making the computation very time-
355   consuming. Even though it is feasible to model ImageNet on the SPD manifold for training, the
356
      large-scale dataset necessitates a deeper network. Riemannian neural network models suffer from
      the degradation of structural information during the multi-level transmission of data. Simply stack-
357
      ing more layers to increase the network depth in shallow Riemannian networks does not alleviate
358
      this problem. This paper only discusses the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed Riemannian
359   CL on the SPD manifolds. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work on the extension of
360   CL to the scenario of Riemannian manifolds.

388   in this study, positive and negative samples are constructed using Riemannian data augmentation,

      and background information rather than expressions, thereby affecting the model’s discriminative
      learning ability. According to the experimental results reported in Table 2, we can observe that on
391
      the Virus dataset, SymNet outperforms our method. This is mainly because the Virus is a small
392
      scale dataset, which has been verified to be more suitable for the lightweight SymNet. Additionally,
393   each image on the Virus dataset has an irregular appearance, which causes subspace-based methods
394   to lose some useful information during representation learning. On the AFEW dataset, SPDNetBN
395   achieved the highest accuracy. We know that in standard deep neural networks, batch normalization
396   improves training stability and accelerates convergence by normalizing activations. This concept is
397   applied to SPDNet in the form of Riemannian Batch Normalization (RBN), resulting in SPDNetBN.
398   This method not only normalizes data in a way that respects the manifold’s geometric, but also
399   centralizes and biases SPD matrices using parallel transport and the Riemannian barycenter. This
400   allows SPDNetBN to demonstrate superior classification performance.
401   Secondly, it can be intuitively observed that SymCL surpasses most of the comparative algorithms
402   (all of them are supervised) on the four used datasets. This is invaluable for self-supervised learning.
403   This validates that the proposed self-supervised contrastive learning mechanism on the Riemannian
404   manifold enables the SPD encoder to capture and learn more authentic data structures. Moreover,
405   the introduced RML module, by explicitly encoding the intra-class and inter-class distribution of
406   the data, helps in training a more discriminative Riemannian network embedding. In this context, a
407
      more suitable classification hypersphere can be obtained, which enhances classification accuracy.
