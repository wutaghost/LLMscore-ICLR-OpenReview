009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present AuToMATo, a novel clustering algorithm based on persistent homol-
012            ogy. While AuToMATo is not parameter-free per se, we provide default choices
013            for its parameters that make it into an out-of-the-box clustering algorithm that
014            performs well across the board. AuToMATo combines the existing ToMATo clus-
015            tering algorithm with a bootstrapping procedure in order to separate significant
016            peaks of an estimated density function from non-significant ones. We perform a
017            thorough comparison of AuToMATo (with its parameters fixed to their defaults)
018
               against many other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms. We find not only that
               AuToMATo compares favorably against parameter-free clustering algorithms, but
019
               in many instances also significantly outperforms even the best selection of param-
020
               eters for other algorithms. AuToMATo is motivated by applications in topological
021            data analysis, in particular the Mapper algorithm, where it is desirable to work
022            with a clustering algorithm that does not need tuning of its parameters. Indeed,
023            we provide evidence that AuToMATo performs well when used with Mapper. Fi-
024            nally, we provide an open-source implementation of AuToMATo in Python that is
025            fully compatible with the standard scikit-learn architecture.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Clustering techniques play a central role in understanding and interpreting data in a variety of fields.
031   The idea is to divide a heterogeneous group of objects into groups based on a notion of similarity.
032   This similarity is often measured with a distance or a metric on a data set. There exist many different
033
      clustering techniques (Anderberg, 1973; Duda et al., 2000), including hierarchical, centroid-based
      and density-based techniques, as well as techniques arising from probabilistic generative models.
034
      Each of these methods is proficient at finding clusters of a particular nature. Many of the most
035
      commonly used clustering algorithms require a selection of a parameters, a process which poses a
036   considerable challenge when applying clustering to real-world problems.
037
038   In this work, we present and implement AuToMATo (Automated Topological Mode Analysis Tool),
039
      a novel clustering algorithm based on the topological clustering algorithm ToMATo (Chazal et al.,
      2013). The latter summarizes the prominences of peaks of a density function in a so-called persis-
040
      tence diagram. The user then selects a prominence threshold τ and retains all peaks whose promi-
041
      nence is above this threshold, which results in the final clustering. A simple heuristic to select τ is
042   to sort the peaks by decreasing prominence, and to look for the largest gap between two consecutive
043   prominence values (Chazal et al., 2013). While yielding reasonable results in general, this procedure
044   is not very robust to small changes in the prominence values.
045
      A more robust and sophisticated method is to perform a bottleneck bootstrap on the persistence dia-
046
      gram produced by ToMATo, which is precisely what AuToMATo does. That is, given a persistence
047
      diagram obtained by running ToMATo on a point cloud, AuToMATo produces a confidence region
048   for that diagram with respect to the bottleneck distance, which translates into a choice of τ that
049   determines the final clustering. While AuToMATo is not parameter-free per se, we provide default
050   choices that make it perform well across the board. Unless stated otherwise, AuToMATo will hence-
051   forth refer to our algorithm with its parameters set to these defaults. We experimentally analyze
052   the clustering performance of AuToMATo and we find that it not only outperforms parameter-free
053   clustering algorithms, but often also even the best choice of hyperparameters for many parametric
      clustering algorithms. Parameter-free algorithms building on ToMATo exist in the literature, for ex-


                                                         1
054
      ample, in Cotsakis et al. (2021) the final clustering is determined by fitting a curve to the values of
055   prominence, and in Bois et al. (2024) significant values are separated from non-significant ones by
056   adapting the process that produces the persistence diagrams. Indeed, the former algorithm is one of
057   those that AuToMATo is shown to outperform.
058
      We envision one important application of AuToMATo to be to the Mapper algorithm, introduced
059
      in Singh et al. (2007). Mapper constructs a graph that captures the topological structure of a data
060
      set. It relies on many parameters, one them being a clustering algorithm applied to various chunks
061   of the data. Algorithms that depend heavily on a a good choice of a tunable hyperparameter are
062   generally not good candidates for usage with Mapper, as the best choice for the hyperparameter
063   can vary significantly over the different chunks, and manually choosing a different hyperparameter
064   for each may not be possible in practice. Thus, most choices of hyperparameter will generally
065   perform badly on some of the subsets, leading to undesired results of Mapper. Thus AuToMATo
066   can be seen as progress towards finding optimal parameters for Mapper, which is active area of
067   research (Carrière et al., 2018; Chalapathi et al., 2021; Rosen et al., 2023). Running examples for
068   Mapper with AuToMATo, we see that it is indeed a good choice for a clustering algorithm in this
069
      application when compared to parametric clustering algorithm such as DBSCAN.

073   2.1   P ERSISTENCE AND THE T O MAT O CLUSTERING ALGORITHM
074
075   Both ToMATo and AuToMATo rely on the theory of persistence (Edelsbrunner et al., 2002; Zomoro-
076   dian & Carlsson, 2005; Carlsson, 2014) to quantify the prominence of peaks of (an estimate of) a
077   density function, and to build a hierarchy of peaks. Given a topological space X equipped with a
078   density function f : X → R≥0 , the first step of persistence is to build a filtration from X.
079   Definition 2.1. Let X be a topological space and f : X → R continuous. The superlevelset filtra-
080   tion of (X, f ) is the family of superlevelsets {X≥t | t ∈ R}, where X≥t := f −1 ([t, ∞)).
081
082   In the following we assume for ease of exposition that all local extrema of f have distinct values.
083   The idea underlying ToMATo is to track the evolution of (the number of) connected components of
084   X≥t as t ranges from +∞ to −∞. In that process, the number of connected components of X≥t
085
      remains constant, unless t passes through the value of a local extremum of f . As t passes through
      the value of a local maximum, a new connected component is “born” and added to the superlevelset
086
      X≥t . Similarly, as t passes through the value of a local minimum, two connected components of
087
      X≥t are merged into one. ToMATo builds a hierarchy of local maxima of f by declaring that, as
088   two components get merged, the component corresponding to the local maximum with higher value
089   absorbs the other one and persists, whereas the component corresponding to the local maximum with
090   lower value “dies”. Therefore, to each local maximum we associate a pair (b, d) where b denotes
091   the birth and d the death time, respectively. The evolution of the connected components can be
092   concisely recorded in a persistence diagram.
093   Definition 2.2. Let {(bl , dl )}l denote the birth and death times of connected components of the
094   superlevelset filtration {X≥t }t∈R associated to the density f : X → R. The associated persistence
095                                                                             2
      diagram, denoted by Dgm(X, f ), is the multiset in the extended plane R := R ∪ {±∞} consisting
096                                  2
097   of the points {(bl , dl )}l ⊂ R (counted with multiplicity) and the diagonal ∆ := (x, x) | x ∈ R
098
      (where each point on ∆ has infinite multiplicity). For a given local maximum of f with birth time bl
      and death time dl , we refer to the difference dl − bl as its prominence or lifetime.
099
100
      The reason for working in the extended plane is that, provided that f has a global maximum, the
101   superlevelset filtration X≥t will have a connected component that never dies, i.e., has death time
102   equal to −∞. See the red graph in Figure 1 for an illustration.
103
104
      The persistence diagram Dgm(X, f ) provides a summary of f . The points of Dgm(X, f ) are in
      one-to-one correspondence with the local maxima of f , and twice the L∞ -distance of a point to the
105
      diagonal ∆ (i.e., its Euclidean vertical distance) equals its prominence.
106
107   We now outline how the ToMATo clustering algorithm works. Given a point cloud X ToMATo relies
      on the assumption that the points of X were sampled according to some unknown density function


                                                        2
108
      f . In a nutshell, ToMATo infers information about the local maxima of f by applying the above
109   procedure to an estimate of f . ToMATo takes as input:
110
111           • A neighborhood graph G on the points of X. Chazal et al. mostly use the δ-Rips graph
112
                and the k-nearest neighbor graph.1
113           • A density estimator fˆ. Each vertex v of G is assigned a non-negative value fˆ(v) that corre-
114
                sponds to the estimated density at v. Chazal et al. propose two possible density estimators:
                the truncated Gaussian kernel density estimator and the distance-to-measure density, origi-
115
                nally introduced in Biau et al. (2011).2
116
              • A merging parameter τ ≥ 0. This is a threshold that the prominence of a local maximum
117
                of the estimated density fˆ must clear for that local maximum to be deemed a feature.
118
119   Given the inputs above, ToMATo proceeds as follows.
120
             1. Estimate the underlying density function fˆ at the points of X.
121
             2. Apply a hill-climbing algorithm on G. Construct the neighborhood graph G on the points
122             of X, and construct a directed subgraph G ′ of G as follows: at each vertex v of G, place
123
                a directed edge from v to its neighbor with highest value of fˆ, provided that that value is
124
                higher than fˆ(v). If all neighbors of v have lower values, v is a peak of fˆ. This yields a
125
                collection of directed edges that form a spanning forest of the graph G, consisting of one
126
                tree for each local maximum of fˆ. In particular, these trees yield a partition of the elements
127
                of X into pairwise disjoint sets that serves as a candidate clustering on X.
128
             3. Construct the persistence diagram. Construct the persistence diagram Dgm(G, fˆ) asso-
129
                ciated to the superlevelset filtration of fˆ: G → R.
130
             4. Merge non-significant clusters. Iteratively merge every cluster of prominence less than
131             τ of the candidate clustering found in Step 2 into its parent cluster, i.e., into the cluster
132             corresponding to the local maximum that it gets merged into in the superlevelset filtration
133             of fˆ: G → R. ToMATo outputs the resulting clustering of points of X, in which every
134             cluster has prominence at least τ by construction.

      Choice of ToMATo parameters: Our implementation of AuToMATo is such that the user can
293
      directly pass parameters to the underlying ToMATo instance. If no such arguments are provided
294   AuToMATo uses the default choices for those parameters, as determined by the implementation of
295   ToMATo given in Glisse (2023). In particular, AuToMATo uses the k-nearest neighbor graph and the
296   (logarithm of the) distance-to-measure density estimators by default, each with k = 10. Of course,
297   the persistence diagrams produced by ToMATo, and hence the output of AuToMATo, depend on this
298   choice. This can lead to suboptimal clustering performance of AuToMATo; see Section 6.
299
300   Choice of α and B: By default, AuToMATo performs the bootstrap on B = 1000 subsamples of
301   the input point cloud, and sets the confidence level to α = 0.35. The choice of this latter parameter
302   means that AuToMATo determines merely a 65% confidence region for the persistence diagram
303   produced by the underlying ToMATo instance. While in bootstrapping the confidence level is often
304   set to e.g. α = 0.05, the seemingly strange choice of α = 0.35 in the setting of AuToMATo is
305   justified by experiments. The value of 65% seems to be low enough to offset some of the negative
306   influence of using possibly non-optimized neighborhood graph and density estimators discussed in
307
      Section 6, while at the same time being high enough to yield good results when these estimators
      are chosen suitably. We point out that the value α = 0.35 (as well as the value B = 1000) was
308
      decided on after running an early implementation of AuToMATo on just a few synthetic data sets.
309
      In particular, the choice was made before conducting the experiments in Section 4. AuToMATo is
310   implemented in such a way that the parameter α can be adjusted after fitting and the clustering is
311   automatically updated.

      Our Python package for AuToMATo consists of two separate modules; one for AuToMATo itself,
      and one for the bottleneck bootstrap. Both are compatible with the scikit-learn architecture, and the
314
      latter may also be used as a stand-alone module for other scenarios. In addition to the functionality
315
      inherited from the scikit-learn API, the implementation of AuToMATo comes with options of
316
317         • adjusting the parameter α of a fitted instance of AuToMATo which automatically updates
318           the resulting clustering without repeating the (computationally expensive) bootstrapping;
319
            • plotting the persistence diagram and the prominence threshold found in the bootstrapping;
            • setting a seed in order to make the creation of the bootstrap subsamples in AuToMATo
320
              deterministic, thus allowing for reproducible results; and
321
            • parallelizing the bottleneck bootstrap for speed improvements.
322
323   Finally, our implementation of AuToMATo contains a parameter that allows the algorithm to label

325   is currently experimental (and is thus turned off by default).

      4.1     C HOICE OF CLUSTERING ALGORITHMS FOR COMPARISON
331
      We chose to compare AuToMATo with its default parameters against
332
333            • DBSCAN and its extension HDBSCAN;
334            • hierarchical clustering with Ward, single, complete and average linkage;
335
               • the FINCH clustering algorithm (Sarfraz et al., 2019); and
               • a clustering algorithm building on ToMATo stemming from the Topology ToolKit (TTK)
336
                 suite (Tierny et al., 2018); in the following, we will refer to this as the TTK-algorithm.4
337
338   For DBSCAN, HDBSCAN and the hierarchical clustering algorithms mentioned above, we worked
339   with their implementations in scikit-learn.5 For the FINCH clustering algorithm, we worked with the
340   version available on GitHub.6 Indeed, we subclassed that version in order to make it compatible with
341
      the scikit-learn API. Similarly, we created a scikit-learn compatible version of the TTK-algorithm
      by combining code from TTK with the description of the algorithm given in Cotsakis et al. (2021,
342
      Section 5.2). While we included DBSCAN and HDBSCAN among the clustering algorithms to
343
      compare AuToMATo against because they are standard choices, we chose to include the hierarchical
344   clustering algorithms because they are readily available through scikit-learn. Finally, we chose to
345   include FINCH and the TTK-algorithm because, like AuToMATo, they are out-of-the-box (indeed,
346   parameter-free) methods and are thus especially interesting to compare AuToMATo against.
347
348   4.2     C HOICE OF DATA SETS
349
350   The data sets on which we ran AuToMATo and the above clustering algorithms stem from the Clus-
351   tering Benchmarks suite.7 We chose this collection as it comes with a large variety of different data
352   sets, all of which are labeled by one or more ground truths, allowing for a fair and extensive com-
353
      parison. The collection contains five recommended batteries of data sets from which we selected
      those (data set, ground truth)-pairs that we deemed reasonable for a general purpose parameter-free
354
      clustering algorithm. For instance, we chose to include the data set named windows that is part
355
      of the wut-battery, but not the data set named windows from the same battery (see Figure 5 in
356   the appendix for an illustration). We chose to include the windows data set because AuToMATo
357   determines clusters depending on connectivity, and topologically speaking, there is only one con-
358   nected component in the olympic data set. Finally, we excluded all instances where the ground
359   truth contains data points that are labeled as outliers, as outliers creation is currently an experimental
360   feature in AuToMATo.

363
364   We min-max scaled each data set, fitted the clustering algorithms to them, and recorded the clus-
365
      tering performance of each result by computing the Fowlkes-Mallows score (Fowlkes & Mallows,
      1983) of the clustering obtained and the respective ground truth. While the Fowlkes-Mallows score
366
      was originally defined for hierarchical clusterings only, it may be defined for general clusterings as
367
      follows. Given a clustering C found by an algorithm and a ground truth clustering G, one defines
368   the Fowlkes-Mallows score as
369                                            r               r
                                                      TP             TP
370                                  FMS :=                  ·              ,
371                                               TP + FP         TP + FN
372   where
373
               • TP is the number of pairs of data points which are in the same cluster in C and in G;

             • FP is the number of pairs of data points which are in the same cluster in G but not in C;
379            and
380
             • FN is the number of pairs of data points which are not in the same cluster in G but are in
381
               the same cluster in C.
382
383   In other words, the Fowlkes-Mallows score is defined as the geometric mean of precision and recall
384   of a classifier whose relevant elements are pairs of points that belong to the same cluster in both
385   C and G. It may attain any value between 0 and 1, and these extremal values correspond to the
386   worst and best possible clustering, respectively. We chose to use the Fowlkes-Mallows score as
387   opposed to e.g. mutual information or any of the Rand indices, because the latter have been shown
388   to exhibit biased behaviour depending on whether the clusters in the ground truth are mostly of
389   similar sizes or not, see e.g. Romano et al. (2016); to the best of our knowledge, the Fowlkes-
390
      Mallows score does not suffer from such drawbacks. Moreover, we chose not to use any intrinsic
      measures of clustering performance since any such measure implicitly defines a further clustering
391
      algorithm to compare AuToMATo against, whereas we are interested in comparing AuToMATo
392
      against a predefined ground truth clustering.
393
394   We set the hyperparameters of the HDBSCAN, FINCH and the TTK-algorithm to their default
395   values (as per their respective implementations). In contrast to this, we let the distance threshold
396
      parameter for the DBSCAN and the hierarchical clustering algorithms vary from 0.05 to 1.00 in
      increments of 0.05, with the goal of comparing AuToMATo against the best and worst performing
397
      version of these clustering algorithms. To account for the randomized component of AuToMATo,
398
      we ran it ten times, each time with a different seed.
399
400   While we restricted ourselves to instances where the ground truth does not contain any points labeled
401   as outliers, some of the clustering algorithms in our list (DBSCAN and HDBSCAN) label some data
402
      points as outliers. In order to prevent these algorithms from getting systematically low Fowlkes-
      Mallows scores because of these outliers, we removed all the points labeled as outliers by these
403
      algorithms, and only computed the Fowlkes-Mallows score on the remaining points, both for these
404
      clustering algorithms and for AuToMATo. This of course gives an advantage to DBSCAN and
405   HDBSCAN over AuToMATo.
406
407   In order to allow reproducibility, we chose a fixed seed for all our experiments, which can bey found
408
      in our code on GitHub. We ran our experiments on a laptop with a 12th Gen Intel Core i7-1260P
      processor running at 2.10GHz.

463   5                A PPLICATIONS OF AU T O MAT O IN COMBINATION WITH M APPER
464
465   The goal of Mapper (Singh et al., 2007) is to approximate the Reeb graph of a manifold M based
466   on a sample from M . The input is a point cloud P with a filter function P → R, a collection of
467   overlapping intervals U = {U1 , . . . , Un } covering R and a clustering algorithm. For each Ui ∈
468   U, Mapper runs the clustering algorithm on the data points in the preimage f −1 (Ui ), creating a
469   vertex for each cluster. Two vertices are then connected by an edge if the corresponding clusters (in
470   different preimages) have some data points in common, yielding a graph that represents the shape
471   of the data set. We ran the Mapper implementation of giotto-tda (Tauzin et al., 2020) on a synthetic
472   two-dimensional data set consisting of noisy samples from two concentric circles (see Figure 3a)
473
      with projection onto the x-axis as the filter function. We ran Mapper on the same interval cover
      with three different choices of clustering algorithms: AuToMATo, DBSCAN, and HDBSCAN. As
474
      can be seen in Figure 3b, using DBSCAN, we get many unwanted edges in the graph. HDBSCAN
475
      performs better, giving two cycles with some extra loops. The output of Mapper with AuToMATo is
476   exactly the Reeb graph of two circles.
477
478   We further tested the combination of Mapper with AuToMATo on one of the standard applications
479
      of Mapper: the Miller-Reaven diabetes data set, where Mapper can be used detect two strains of
      diabetes that correspond to “flares” in the data set (see Singh et al. (2007, Section 5.1) for details).8
480
      As can be seen in Figure 4, AuToMATo performs well in this task; the graphs show a central core
481
      of vertices corresponding to healthy patients, and two flares corresponding to the two strains of
482   diabetes. We were not able to reproduce this using DBSCAN or HDBSCAN; Figure 4 shows the
483   output of Mapper with these algorithms with their respective default parameters.

513   6         D ISCUSSION
514
515   We briefly outline some limitations of AuToMATo. AuToMATo comes with a choice of default val-
516   ues for its parameters. In particular, it resorts to the default values as implemented in ToMATo for
517
      the choice of neighborhood graph and density estimators. In ToMATo, the options for the neighbor-
      hood graph estimators are the δ-Rips graph and the k-nearest neighbor graph, relying on parameters
518
      δ > 0 and k ≥ 1, respectively; the options for the density estimators are the kernel density estimator
519
      and the distance-to-measure density estimator, which in turn rely parameters h > 0 and m ∈ (0, 1),
520   respectively (see the footnotes in Section 2.1 for their definitions). While for most data sets that we
521   ran our experiments on these default estimators yielded good results, there are cases where adjusting
522   them before running AuToMATo improves the clustering performance. Nevertheless, since finding
523   a priori optimal parameters for the neighborhood graph and density estimators for a given data set
524   is largely an open problem, we chose to stick to the default values for these estimators. Indeed,
525   there are heuristics for the selection of the bandwidth in kernel density estimation (see Cotsakis
526   et al. (2021, Section 4.1)) and the smoothing parameter in distance-to-measure density estimation
527   (see Chazal et al. (2017, Section 7.1)), but these methods either lack theoretical justification, or
528
      would require running AuToMATo multiple times on the same data set with different parameters
      and selecting the best ones a posteriori, which undermines the intended use of AuToMATo as an
529
      out-of-the-box clustering algorithm.
530
531   Optimizing the choice of the neighborhood graph and density estimators is an aspect of AuToMATo
532   that we plan to pursue in future work. Moreover, we plan to improve the currently experimental
533   feature for outlier creation in AuToMATo discussed at the end of Section 3.2. Finally, it is natural
534
      to ask whether the results from Carrière et al. (2018) on optimal parameter selection in the Mapper
      algorithm can be adapted to the scenario where Mapper uses AuToMATo as its clustering algorithm.

      A.2   C OMPLEXITY ANALYSIS OF A LGORITHM 1
673
674   Recall from Chazal et al. (2013, Section 2) that, if an estimated density and a neighborhood graph are
675   provided, ToMATo has a worst-case time complexity in O(n log(n) + mα(n)), where n and m are
676   the number of vertices and edges of the neighborhood graph, respectively, and α denotes the inverse
677   Ackermann function (note that n equals the number of data points). By default, ToMATo (and hence
678   AuToMATo) works with the k-nearest neighbor graph and distance-to-measure density estimators,
679   where the latter relies itself on the k-nearest neighbor graph (each with k = 10). Taking into account
680   the known complexity bound O(nd) for the creation of the k-nearest neighbor graph (where d is the
681
      dimensionality of the data), and using the fact that m ∈ O(n) for this graph, this leads to a worst-
      case time complexity in O(nd + n log(n)) for a single run of ToMATo. Creating        √ thefinbootstrap

      samples Xi∗ , i = 1, . . . , B, has complexity in O(Bn); computing the values nW∞             (Di∗ , D),

684   denotes the maximal number of off-diagonal points across all relevant persistence diagram), and
685   sorting them has worst case complexity in O(B log(B)). Combined, this leads to a worst-case
