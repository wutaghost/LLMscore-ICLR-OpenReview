009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Explainable AI (XAI) seeks to tackle the opacity of deep neural network deci-
012            sions. Moving beyond the conventional focus on 2D imagery, our research consti-
013            tutes the first method to provide Counterfactual Explanations (CEs) for 3D point
014            cloud classifiers. Specifically, we introduce an approach to generate 3D CEs us-
015            ing a diffusion model whose goal is to maintain both semantic consistency and
016            data fidelity in 3D contexts. To this end, we devise novel losses and constraints
017            to boost the realism and practicality of counterfactual instances. Furthermore, we
018
               establish a new benchmark with evaluation metrics designed specifically for 3D
               point clouds, allowing for the assessment of future methods. Altogether, our con-
019
               tributions bridge a key gap in the field of explainability, steering towards more
020
               transparent and fair AI methodologies.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Neural networks come with an inherent complexity that often obscures their decision-making pro-
026   cess. This opacity is particularly problematic in critical fields such as healthcare, finance, and au-
027   tonomous systems, where transparency, accountability, and fairness are imperative. Explainable
028   AI (XAI) (Böhle et al.; Li et al., 2022) has emerged as a response to demystify neural networks’
029   decision-making mechanisms.
030   Within XAI, Counterfactual Explanations (CEs) aim to provide insights through “what if” scenarios,
031   generated by perturbing the sample input to the network. Unlike adversarial attacks, CEs prioritize
032   semantic interpretability, leveraging techniques such as variational autoencoders (Rodrı́guez et al.,
033   2021), GANs (Jacob et al., 2022) and diffusion models (Jeanneret et al.) to generate understandable
034   input modifications that alter the model’s output. While rapid progress has been achieved in the
035   development of CEs for image-processing networks, to the best of knowledge, no such effort has yet
036   been made for 3D processing ones.
037   Our work therefore diverges from existing CE methods by not focusing on 2D image-based models
038   but embracing 3D data processing challenges, with a particular emphasis on point cloud classifiers.
039   This direction seeks to address the void in counterfactual generation for 3D perception models,
040   introducing new dimensions to model debugging and comprehension.
041
      Our exploratory effort begins with the perturbation of 3D point clouds to challenge classifiers that
042   interpret 3D data directly. Drawing inspiration from recent advancements in image counterfactu-
043   als (Jeanneret et al.; 2023), which leverage the ability of diffusion models to produce high-quality
044   images for counterfactual generation, we devise a method for generating counterfactuals in 3D point
045   clouds using diffusion (Zeng et al., 2022). However, in contrast to the methods of (Jeanneret et al.)
046   and (Jeanneret et al., 2023), in our approach, the diffusion model is integrated into counterfactual
047   optimization through a score distillation loss (Poole et al., 2022). This technique eliminates the ne-
048   cessity to corrupt the original sample at the start of the counterfactual generation process, thereby
049   preserving information from the original sample. Furthermore, it enhances efficiency by requir-
050
      ing only a single diffusion step per optimization step and alleviates the need for back-propagation
      through the diffusion model.
051
052   We conduct a thorough analysis of both the quantitative and qualitative effectiveness of our approach
053   to generating CEs for point cloud classifiers (Wang et al., 2019; Pang et al.), optimizing their per-
      formance against criteria specifically tailored to the structure of point-cloud data. To facilitate this

055   such as their validity, proximity, diversity, realism, and sparsity (Jeanneret et al., 2023). We compare

065

067
               studies focused on binary cases only.

069            benchmark for 3D CE generation for point cloud classifiers.

075   2.1   E XPLAINABLE AI
076
077   Explainability in AI can be classified into two primary categories: Designing ad-hoc techniques via
078   machine learning architectures that are inherently interpretable, as advocated for by recent studies
079
      (Bohle et al.; Böhle et al.; Huang & Li; Rymarczyk et al.); and developing post-hoc techniques,
      which are designed to scrutinize pre-existing models and their predictions either from a global (Tan;
080
      Li et al., 2022) or local perspective (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Chattopadhay et al.). Global methods elu-
081
      cidate the overall functioning of the model, whereas local methods delve into the model’s decision-
082   making process for individual instances, explaining the rationale behind specific decisions. Our
083   research is dedicated to local post-hoc explanations.
084
085
      In this context, many solutions have been proposed to provide local explanations for 2D image-
      based models (Li et al., 2022). These techniques primarily utilize saliency maps (Chattopadhay
086
      et al.; Jalwana et al.; Lee et al.; Kim et al., b), local approximations (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Guidotti
087
      et al., 2018), concept attributions (Kim et al., a; Ghorbani et al.; Kolek et al.), and prototypes or
088   critiques (Kim et al., 2016; Gurumoorthy & Dhurandhar, 2018). In particular, our research focuses
089   on counterfactual explanations, another significant branch of local post-hoc explanation strategies,
090   which we thus discuss in more detail below.
091
092   2.2   C OUNTERFACTUAL E XPLAINATIONS AND FAILURE MODES
093
094   CEs serve as a critical tool in understanding the decision-making processes of AI models by ad-
095   dressing the “what would have happened if” question. More precisely, CEs seek to identify an
096   alternative sample, x̃, that is minimally different from an original input x, yet leads to a different
097   outcome from the model. The purpose of counterfactual explanations lies in their ability to produce
098   modifications that are understandable and meaningful to humans, thereby elucidating how a model
099
      arrives at its conclusions. This represents a critical distinction between counterfactual explanations
      and adversarial attacks; the latter typically involve modifications that are not interpretable by hu-
100
      mans. The importance of including an interpretability constraint to generate such explanations has
101
      been widely acknowledged, with various strategies proposed to this end (Van Looveren & Klaise,
102   2020; Thiagarajan et al.; Hvilshøj et al.; Boreiko et al.). In the case of image classifiers, the stan-
103   dard constraint for semantic changes in input images involves using generative tools to force the
104   counterfactual sample to remain in the image manifold (Rodrı́guez et al., 2021; Jacob et al., 2022;
105   Zemni et al., 2022; Jeanneret et al.; 2023). Image-based generative methods struggle with semantic
106   control (Zemni et al., 2022; Jeanneret et al., 2023). Leclerc et al. (2021) introduces a method to de-
107   bug image-based deep learning models using 3D rendering, focusing on 3D setting manipulation for
      interpretability. The method’s efficacy depends more on the rendering software than the framework.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      The realism evaluation of failure modes is challenging due to the limitations in Leclerc et al. (2021),
109   which do not fully test the renderer’s capabilities. As rendering systems are a limitation for the re-
110   alism of a CE and image-based CE generation methods lack of controls in the parameter space, we
111   offer to extend the frontier of counterfactual reasoning into the 3D realm by first raising the question
112   of the possibility to develop CEs for 3D data structures. Our research translates 2D image-based CE
113   methodologies to 3D point cloud classifiers, aiming to broaden CE applications across data types
114   and enhance explainable AI insights. Following Rodrı́guez et al. (2021), we utilize unconstrained
115   variational autoencoders for generating semantically meaningful CEs, optimizing through a model’s
116   latent space (Cohen et al., 2021; Rodrı́guez et al., 2021) with gradient descent (Joshi et al., 2019;
117
      Rodrı́guez et al., 2021). Moreover, (Jeanneret et al.; 2023) demonstrate diffusion-based generative
      models for CE, guiding our analysis of such models in 3D contexts.

119
120   2.3   P OINT C LOUD C LASSIFICATION
121
      Point clouds, as a shape representation method, offer inherent simplicity and efficiency. These
122
      scattered collections of points, outputted by advanced 3D sensing technologies like LiDAR scanners,
123
      enable us to streamline our workflow—saving computational resources and reducing processing
124   time. Additionally, their flexibility and lightweight nature make point clouds suitable for diverse
125   applications across computer graphics, vision, robotics, and virtual reality.
126
127
      Point cloud classifiers which process directly point clouds aggregate either local features (Qi et al.,
      b; Qian et al.) or global ones (Qi et al., a). The significance of local geometry in 3D recognition tasks
128
      is proved by Wang et al. (2019) and its introduction to a new convolutional paradigm and a dynamic
129
      graph CNN (DGCNN) which captures of local geometric features in point clouds and ensures their
130   arrangement remains invariant. Pang et al. developed Point-MAE, a masked autoencoder approach
131   for the self-supervised learning of point clouds.
132
133
      2.4   P OINT C LOUD G ENERATION
134
135   To develop CEs for 3D point cloud classifiers, we will exploit methods for generating point clouds.
136   Recent years have witnessed a surge of interest in developing generative models specifically tailored
137   for 3D point clouds, employing various strategies such as autoencoders (Zamorski et al., 2019),
138   flow-based models (Pumarola et al., 2019), and energy-based models (Luo & Hu, 2021). These
139   sophisticated generative approaches offer a versatile toolkit for the modeling of complex data types,
140   particularly for the intricate details encapsulated in 3D objects and point clouds.
141   In particular, the Adversarial Autoencoder (AAE) (Zamorski et al., 2019) has emerged as a suitable
142   baseline for comparison with our method. It simultaneously learns a representation in latent space
143   and generates 3D shapes from this learned space, innovating beyond the Variational Autoencoder by
144   integrating adversarial training for enhanced 3D data processing and output.
145   Furthermore, the domain of 3D point cloud generation has been profoundly influenced by the ad-
146   vent of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Zhou et al.; Luo & Hu), representing the forefront of deep
147   learning research. Among these, the LION model (Zeng et al., 2022) encodes the complex distri-
148   butions of 3D shapes through latent space representations, thus facilitating the creation of highly
149   detailed and varied 3D point clouds. As will be discussed below, we investigate the use of LION to
150   produce CEs for 3D point cloud classifiers.

      4.1   E VALUATION M ETRICS
292
293   We employ evaluation criteria that assess different aspects of the counterfactual explanations, in-
294   cluding their validity, diversity, sparsity or proximity, transition probabilities, and the realism of the
295   generated counterfactual images (Jeanneret et al., 2023).
296
297   Validity. Following common practice (Jeanneret et al., 2023), we start by measuring the flip rate
298   (FR), indicating the proportion of cases where the algorithm finds at least one counterfactual expla-
299   nation. Our studies show that many counterfactual explanations are trivial, identified by the genera-
300   tion of outliers—points far from the majority. We thus define non-trivial counterfactuals as meeting
301
      specific structural criteria, labeling outliers based on their distance from the nearest neighbor within
      the first or last permille of the distribution. Considering this definition, we report the non-trivial flip
302
      rate (NTFR) and the mean number of outliers (MNO).
303
304
      Proximity. Modifications within point clouds are measured using the Chamfer distance (CD), and
305
      perceptual changes are quantified with LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018). We assess the perceptual distance
306   between the original samples and the counterfactuals using the pretrained SimpleView (Goyal et al.,
307   2021), which projects point clouds into 2D from various angles for 2D CNN classification. This
308   approach, mimicking human perception, bridges the 3D and 2D realms. SimpleView, trained on the
309   entire ShapeNetCore dataset (Chang et al., 2015), serves as an oracle for our perceptual metric.
310
311   Sparsity. We evaluate sparsity with a revised version of the mean number of attributes changes
312   (MNAC) (Rodrı́guez et al., 2021; Jacob et al., 2022; Jeanneret et al., 2023). Considering a predefined
313   set of attributes that defines semantic parameters in the data manifold, we expect our CE generation
314   method to change a minimum number of them for each generation process.

     417   In our comparative analysis shown in Table 1, Diffusion (Diff ) emerges as the superior technique
     418   in terms of FID, NTFR, MNO. This performance indicates that the CEs generated by diffusion
     419   method not only closely resemble the original instances within ShapeNetCore’s chair subset but

     423
           are sparse, which is desirable for generating counterfactuals, as it facilitates the discovery of spu-

     430   it can apply to a sample, which explains the lower FID, CD, LPIPS, MNAC and DIV. However, the
     431   diffusion method trained on the whole dataset provides better flip ratios and diversity as the latent

432
      The AE baseline is distinguished by its high flip ratio. It provides a certain degree of flexibility in
433   modifying the input sample, yielding to more diverse CEs than those generated by other techniques.
434   Nonetheless, this advantage is tempered by a notable drawback: The CEs produced by the autoen-
435   coder exhibit a reduced resemblance to the original samples. This reduction in similarity is attributed
436   to the inherent constraints of autoencoders in data representation, particularly when contrasted with
437   the more nuanced capabilities of diffusion models. Such constraints may hinder the exploration of
438   failure modes and the detection of spurious correlations, as evidenced by the elevated MNAC score.
439   This suggests an excessive alteration of features for the same sample, undermining the fidelity of the
440   CEs. In contrast with Diff, the AE latent space is only 2048 dimension wide, which makes it hard
441
      for us to apply local changes to the point cloud sample.
442   Finally, we incorporate AA and AA+ as a reference, despite it not being a primary focus of our work.
443   These methods outperforms the others in terms of the Chamfer Distance and LPIPS. The adver-
444   sarial attack’s core strategy, which minimizes the perturbation applied to the input sample, thereby
445   maintains the original structure as much as possible. Such minimal disturbance contrasts with the
446   intention behind CEs, which aim for changes that are not only perceptible but also interpretable by
447
      humans.
448   However, despite having a high flip ratio when attacking the binary classifiers, the results of adver-
449   sarial attack contain a large number of outliers, resulting in a very low non-trivial flip ratio. Besides,
450   these methods fails to attack the multi-class classifiers.
451   The improvements from AA to AA+ demonstrate the effectiveness of our regularization terms. How-
452   ever, these losses are not sufficient to make the method produce semantically meaningful changes.
453   These attributes align with the qualitative observations, shown in the qualitative results in Fig. 2,
454   and underscores the critical role of visual evaluation in comprehensively understanding the efficacy
455   of our CE strategy tailored for point cloud classifiers.

      clear visual distinction in the deformations applied to the chairs emerges, aimed at altering the

      The Diff pipeline provides a way to visually assess the importance of certain features in point cloud

      classifier predictions. Through Fig. 2 and 4, Diff effectively isolates and modifies specific chair
466
      attributes, influencing the classifier predictions. Notable modifications, such as modification of a

510   5     C ONCLUSION
511
512   Our study marks an advancement in the field of XAI by introducing pioneering methods for generat-
513   ing CEs for 3D data structures, starting here with point clouds. By leveraging diffusion models, we
514   have established a novel approach that ensures semantic consistency and high data fidelity within
515   3D contexts. Our development of new losses and constraints has substantially enhanced the realism
516   and practicality of our counterfactual instances.
517   The diffusion based method and its objective developed for the counterfactual examples generation
518   has been developed to improve the memory footprint and the computational costs of diffusion based
519   CE generation.
520
      Moreover, the introduction of specifically designed evaluation metrics for 3D point clouds sets a
521
      new benchmark, facilitating future research and validation of methods in this area.
522
523   Our method, however, has been evaluated on a for a limited amount of tasks and was thus constrained
524   on a certain dataset. Although this follows the standard practice in image CEs, assessing it for other
525   deep learning tasks and classifiers would strengthen its validation. It would be also interesting for
526
      future research to consider generative methods that are not constrained to the point cloud domain in
      order to attack diverse tasks and 3D structures (Loper et al., 2015).
527
528   Furthermore, the control we have on the CE generation is limited. Although the diffusion based CE
529   generator provides local modifications on the samples or CEs that are visually similar to the input,
530   we need a human analysis as a post-process to understand the results and which semantic properties
531   of the sample the classifier is sensitive to. Thus exploring generative methods with more control on
532
      the part of the object to modify would ease the interpretation of the results (Hui et al., 2022).
