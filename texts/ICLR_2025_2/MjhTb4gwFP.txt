009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Controllable generation is considered a potentially vital approach to address the
012            challenge of annotating 3D data, and the precision of such controllable generation
013            becomes particularly imperative in the context of data production for autonomous
014            driving. Existing methods focus on the integration of diverse generative informa-
015            tion into controlling inputs, utilizing frameworks such as GLIGEN or ControlNet,
016            to produce commendable outcomes in controllable generation. However, such
017            approaches intrinsically restrict generation performance to the learning capaci-
018
               ties of predefined network architectures. In this paper, we explore the integration
               of controlling information and introduce PerLDiff (Perspective-Layout Diffusion
019
               Models), a method for effective street view image generation that fully leverages
020
               perspective 3D geometric information. Our PerLDiff employs 3D geometric pri-
021            ors to guide the generation of street view images with precise object-level control
022            within the network learning process, resulting in a more robust and controllable
023            output. Moreover, it demonstrates superior controllability compared to alterna-
024            tive layout control methods. Empirical results justify that our PerLDiff markedly
025            enhances the precision of generation on the NuScenes and KITTI datasets.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The advancement of secure autonomous driving systems is fundamentally dependent on the accurate
031   perception of the vehicle’s environment. Recently, perception utilizing Bird’s Eye View (BEV) has
032   seen rapid progress, markedly pushing forward areas such as 3D object detection (Li et al., 2022;
033
      Liu et al., 2023) and BEV segmentation (Zhou & Krähenbühl, 2022). Nonetheless, these systems
      necessitate extensive datasets with high-quality 3D annotations, the acquisition of which typically
034
      involves two consecutive steps: data scene collection and subsequent labeling. Each of these steps
035
      incurs significant expenses and presents considerable challenges in terms of data acquisition.
036
037   To mitigate issue of data scarcity, the adoption of generative technologies (Van Den Oord et al.,
038   2017; Esser et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020) has proven practical for reversing the order of data anno-
039
      tation. The paradigm of this approach is to use the collected annotation as controlling information
      to generate the corresponding lifelike images depicting urban street scenes. By implementing this
040
      strategy, it is possible to dramatically lower the costs associated with data annotation while also
041
      facilitating the generation of extensive long-tail datasets, subsequently leading to improvements
042   in the perception model’s performance. Pioneering research, exemplified by BEVGen (Swerdlow
043   et al., 2024), harnesses the capabilities of autoregressive transformers (Van Den Oord et al., 2017;
044   Esser et al., 2021) to render detailed visualizations of street scenes. Additionally, subsequent studies
045   such as BEVControl (Yang et al., 2023) and MagicDrive (Gao et al., 2023) employ diffusion-based
046   techniques, including GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023b) and ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), to integrate
047   controlling information through a basic cross-attention mechanism. However, these methods simply
048   extract integrated conditional features from controlling information to adjust the generation process
049   and are limited in making full use of detailed geometric layout information for accurate attention
050
      map manipulation. While these techniques make strides towards meeting the requirements for gen-
      erating 3D annotations, Fig. 1 demonstrates that there remains significant potential for improvement
051
      in scene and object controllability.
052
053   To this end, in this paper, we introduce the perspective-layout diffusion models (PerLDiff), a novel
      method specifically designed to enable precise control over street view image generation at the

111   2.1   D IFFUSION - BASED G ENERATIVE M ODELS IN I MAGE S YNTHESIS
112
113   Initially developed as a method for modeling data distributions through a sequence of Markov chain
114   diffusion steps (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020c;b), diffusion models have undergone
115
      rapid advancement. Ho et al. (Ho et al., 2020) introduced denoising diffusion probabilistic models
      (DDPMs), which have established new benchmarks in the quality of image synthesis. Following ef-
116
      forts have aimed to enhance the efficiency and output diversity of these models by investigating var-
117
      ious conditioning strategies (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Choi et al., 2021), architectural adjustments,
118   and training methodologies to refine the image synthesis process (Hong et al., 2023). Nichol and
119   Dhariwal (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) proved that diffusion models can be text-conditioned to pro-
120   duce coherent images that are contextually appropriate. Furthermore, advances such as multimodal-
121   conditioned diffusion models have effectively utilized layout images (Li et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
122   2023; Rombach et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2023), semantic segmentation maps (Li et al., 2023b; Zhang
123   et al., 2023), object sketches (Voynov et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
124   2023), and depth maps (Mou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023) to inform the gen-
125   erative process. These methods enable more targeted manipulation of the imagery, thus yielding
126
      complex scenes characterized by enhanced structural integrity and contextual pertinence.
127
128
      2.2   DATA G ENERATION FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
129
130
      BEVGen (Swerdlow et al., 2024) represents the pioneering endeavor to harness an autoregressive
131   Transformer (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Esser et al., 2021) for synthesizing multi-view images
132   pertinent to autonomous driving. Building upon this, BEVControl (Yang et al., 2023) introduces a
133   novel method that incorporates a diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) for street view image generation,
134   and integrates cross-view attention mechanisms to maintain spatial coherence across neighboring
135   camera views. Subsequently, MagicDrive (Gao et al., 2023) propels the field forward by refining
136   the method for controlling input conditions, drawing insights from ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023).
137   DrivingDiffusion (Li et al., 2023a) further augments the framework by introducing a consistency
138   loss designed to achieve the perceptual uniformity requisite for high precision in the generation of
139
      video from autonomous driving. Panacea (Wen et al., 2023) broadens the capabilities of the model
      by tackling the challenge of ensuring temporal consistency in video. In contrast to the above ap-
140
      proaches, which primarily utilize controlling input conditions to steer the image generation process,
141
      our PerLDiff exploits detailed geometric layout information from the input to directly guide object
142   generation with higher precision.
143
144
145   2.3   G EOMETRIC C ONSTRAINTS IN I MAGE G ENERATION
146
147   Incorporating geometric priors into image synthesis has been explored to a lesser extent. Work on
148   3D-aware image generation (Nguyen-Phuoc et al., 2019; Niemeyer & Geiger, 2021) suggests the
149   feasibility of integrating geometric information into generative processes to improve spatial coher-
150   ence. Nevertheless, these methods often rely on complex 3D representations and may not be directly
151   applied to diffusion model frameworks. Recently, BoxDiff (Xie et al., 2023) reveals a spatial corre-
152   spondence between the attention map produced by the diffusion model and the corresponding gen-
153
      erated image. During the testing phase, the geometric configuration of the attention map’s response
      values is adjusted to yield a more precise image generation. ZestGuide (Couairon et al., 2023) in-
154
      troduces a loss function that enforces a geometric projection onto the attention map, further refining
155
      the shape of the attention map’s response values to closely approximate the geometric projection
156   of the control information during the inference stage. However, utilizing text prompts to facilitate
157   the generation of complex urban environment layouts poses inherent challenges, owing to the need
158   for crafting intricate prompts to accurately depict urban environments. Furthermore, modifying the
159   cross-attention map to impose strict constraints during the denoising phase of inference can disrupt
160   the intrinsic relationships, leading to a suboptimal approach to synthesizing controllable images. In
161   contrast, our PerLDiff incorporates geometric prerequisites as training priors to guide the generation
      of street view images, offering a more effective solution.

187            L AYOUT
188
189   In this paper, we introduce PerLDiff, depicted in the Fig. 2, which is designed to enable controllable
190   multi-view street scene generation using 3D annotations. Specifically, PerLDiff leverages perspec-
191
      tive projection information from 3D annotations as controlling condition within the training regimen
      and utilizes perspective layout masks as geometric priors, enabling accurate guidance in object gen-
192
      eration. In the following sections, we delineate the process of encoding the controlling condition
193
      information from the 3D annotations in Section 3.1. Additionally, we explain how incorporating the
194   perspective layout knowledge ensures scene and object controllability in street view image genera-
195   tion in Section 3.2.
196
197   3.1          C ONTROLLING C ONDITIONS E NCODING
198
199   Given 3D annotations of a street scene, our goal is to generate multi-view street images. To be
200   more specific, for controllable street view image generation, we extract not only scene information
201   (i.e., textual scene descriptions Sd and street maps M revealing features such as road markings and
202   obstacles) but also object information (i.e., bounding box parameters P and the associated object
203
      category Y) from 3D annotations as controlling conditions. These controlling conditions encompass
      rich semantic and geometric information, so establishing a robust encoding method to utilize this
204
      information is essential for generating street view images. Hereafter, we present our controlling
205
      condition encoding approach. For simplicity, we omit the details of multi-view perspectives.
206
207   PerL Scene Information encompasses perspective scene images and supplemental data specific for
208   the whole scene. Typically, the selected scene for annotation is coupled with a street map of the
209
      driving environment, which visually differentiates between the road and other background elements
      using distinct colors. In addition, a generic textual description of the scene is customizable to align
210
      with particular scenarios. We employ ConvNext (Liu et al., 2022) and the CLIP text encoder (Rad-
211
      ford et al., 2021) to encode the perspective road map, denoted as Sm , derived from the projection
212   of the street map and the textual scene description Sd , respectively. This approach results in the
213   generation of encoded scene features Hm ∈ R1×C for the road map and Hd ∈ R1×C for the textual
214   scene description:
215
                                                      Hm = ConvNext(Sm ),                                                            Hd = φ(Sd ).                                                              (1)

237   3.2   O BJECT C ONTROLLABILITY VIA P ER L- BASED C ONTROLLING M ODULE
238
      PerL-based Controlling Module (PerL-CM) is responsible for integrating controlling condition in-
239
      formation, which encompasses both the PerL scene and object information, into the latent feature
240
      maps of noisy street view images. This integration is primarily achieved via the scene and object
241   PerL-based cross-attention mechanism. Initially, this mechanism assigns initial values to the atten-
242   tion maps, under the guidance of road and box PerL masking maps. Throughout the training of
243   the network, these values are optimized to ensure that the response of the attention map accurately
244   corresponds to the regions where the objects are located. Subsequently, the information from both
245   the road map and the bounding box are sequentially integrated into the noise street view image.
246   To more effectively integrate PerL scene and object information, a gating operation is used, similar
247   to the method employed in GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023b), which dynamically adjusts the contribu-
248   tion of condition information according to the adaptive process. To ensure multi-view consistency,
249
      View Cross-attention leverages information from the immediate left and right views for uniformity
      across various perspectives. Additionally, Text Cross-attention manipulates the weather and lighting
250
      conditions of street scenes using textual scene description.
251
252   PerL Masking Map (road & box) is comprised of PerL road masking map Ms ∈ RHW ×1 and
253   PerL box masking map Mb ∈ RHW ×M , where H and W represent the height and width dimen-
254   sions of the image, respectively. These masking maps are articulated as follows:
255                                    Ms = Υ(Sm ),         Mb = Φ(Pg ),                                 (4)
256
      where Υ(·) generates the masking map for the non-empty regions of the projected road maps. Mean-
257
      while, Φ(·) produces the masking map corresponding to the inner region of each projected 3D
258   bounding box for every perspective image, enabling precise control at the object level.
259
260   PerL-based Cross-attention (Scene & Object) leverages the prior masking maps to enhance the
261
      learning of cross-attention between the input controlling conditions and the noisy street view images.
      As depicted in Fig. 3, the cross-attention map exhibits perceptual equivalence with the generated
262
      street view image. However, this correspondence is imprecise and lacks the necessary alignment
263

264   that incorporates geometric knowledge derived from both the scene context and the bounding box
265   into the computation of the cross-attention map. In PerLDiff, the road map and object bounding
266   box data are seamlessly merged with the noisy street view images throughout each stage of the
267   denoising process. For the sake of notational simplicity, the linear embeddings and normalization
268   steps typically involved in the attention mechanism have been omitted.

306   3.3   D ISCUSSION
307
308   In contrast to previous approaches for autonomous driving such as BEVControl (Yang et al., 2023),
309   MagicDrive (Gao et al., 2023), DrivingDiffusion (Li et al., 2023a), and Panacea (Wen et al., 2023),
310   which employ a basic cross-attention mechanism to integrate controlling condition information, our
311
      PerLDiff leverages geometric priors via PerL masking map. This approach directs the generation of
      each object with its respective control information during the training phase, effectively countering
312
      the common misalignment between the attention map and condition information that often results
313
      in compromised image controllability. For instance, the attention map of BEVControl (Yang et al.,
314   2023), illustrated in Fig. 3, demonstrates disorganized patterns and lacks precision. Conversely,
315   our PerLDiff markedly enhances the accuracy of generated images and the granularity of condition
316   information at the object level by ensuring meticulous guidance within the attention map. For more
317   qualitative results, please see the Appendix D.

349   4.1     DATASETS
350
351   NuScenes dataset comprises 1,000 urban street scenes, traditionally segmented into 700 for training,
352
      150 for validation, and 150 for testing. Each scene features six high-resolution images (900×1600),
      which together provide a complete 360-degree panoramic view of the surroundings. Additionally,
353
      NuScenes includes comprehensive road maps of the driving environment, featuring details such
354
      as lane markings and obstacles. We extend the class and road type annotations similar to Magic-
355   Drive (Gao et al., 2023) and NuScenes, incorporating ten object classes and eight road types for map
356   rendering. To address the resolution limitations of the U-Net architecture in Stable Diffusion (Ron-
357   neberger et al., 2015), we adopt image resolutions of 256×384 as in BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022),
358   and 256×704 following BEVFusion (Liu et al., 2023).
359
      KITTI dataset contains 3,712 images for training and 3,769 images for validation. KITTI dataset
360
      has only one perspective image and does not have road map information. Given the varied image
361   resolutions in KITTI (approximately 375×1242), we pad them to 384×1280 for generative learning.

515
      In conclusion, our PerLDiff introduces a streamlined framework that adeptly merges geometric con-
516   straints with synthetic street view image generation, harnessing diffusion models’ power for high-
517   fidelity visuals. The architecture boasts a PerL-based controlling module (PerL-CM) that, through
518   training, becomes seamlessly integrated with Stable Diffusion. Meanwhile, a cutting-edge PerL-
519   based cross-attention mechanism guarantees meticulous feature guidance at the object level for
520   precise control. Experiments on NuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020) and KITTI (Geiger et al., 2012)
521   datasets confirm our PerLDiff’s enhanced performance in image synthesis and downstream tasks
522   like 3D object detection and segmentation. Flexible yet precise, our PerLDiff’s method of PerL-
523   based cross-attention with geometric perspective projections during training finely balances image
524
      realism with accurate condition alignment.
525   Limitation and Future Work. Fig. 6 depicts several failure cases of our PerLDiff, where the model
526   erroneously generates vehicles with the front and rear orientations reversed, in contrast to the ground
527   truth. This limitation arises from the usage of a PerL mask in our PerLDiff, which does not account
528   for the orientation on the 2D PerL plane. Future endeavors may explore video generation, extending
529   to work such as DrivingDiffusion (Li et al., 2023a), Panacea (Wen et al., 2023), and Driving into the
530
      Future (Wang et al., 2023b).

738   eight Tesla V100 (32 GB) GPUs over 60,000 iterations, which required two days. An initial batch
739   size of 16 was adjusted to a per-GPU batch of two for focused optimization, particularly for data
740   samples comprising six view images per frame. The generation of samples conforms to the CFG
741   rule (Ho & Salimans, 2022), employing a guidance scale of 5.0 and the Denoising Diffusion Implicit
742   Models (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020a) across 50 steps.
743
      For scene manipulation, the text encoder within Stable Diffusion is retained, along with a weight-
744   frozen CLIP to manage textual inputs and ConvNext for processing road maps. Feature extraction
745   from PerL boxes is conducted via an MLP, optimized through PerL-based controlling module (PerL-
746   CM) with randomly initialized weights. In contrast, certain modules inherit and freeze pre-trained
747   weights from Stable Diffusion v1.4. The key parameters within PerL-CM, λb and λs , are set to
748   5.0 to facilitate optimal image synthesis. Furthermore, DDIM (Song et al., 2020a) and CFG (Ho
749   & Salimans, 2022) are integrated into our training regimen, with a novel approach of omitting all
750   conditions at a rate 10% to foster model versatility.
751   The optimization process employs AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) without a weight decay
752   coefficient and with a learning rate of 5×10−5 , complemented by a warm-up strategy during the first
753   1,000 iterations. BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022), StreamPETR (Wang et al., 2023a), and CVT (Zhou
754   & Krähenbühl, 2022) were retrained using original configurations tailored to our target resolution.
755   The performance of BEVFusion (Liu et al., 2023) and MonoFlex (Zhang et al., 2021) was assessed
      using their provided code and pre-trained weights.

830   View cross-attention ensures the seamless integration of visual data by maintaining continuity and
831   consistency across the multiple camera feeds that are integral to current multi-functional perception
832   systems in autonomous vehicles. Typically, autonomous vehicles feature a 360-degree horizontal
833
      surround view from a BEV perspective, resulting in overlapping fields of vision between adjacent
      cameras. Consequently, we facilitate direct interaction between the noise maps of each camera and
834
      those of the immediate left and right cameras. Given the noisy images from the current, left, and
835
      right cameras, designated as Zb , Zl , and Zr , respectively, the output of this multi-view generation
836   is given by:
837

839
      where C(·) represents the standard cross-attention operation, which accepts three input parameters:
840   query, key, and value, respectively. This approach systematically integrates spatial information from
841   various viewpoints, enabling the synthesis of images that exhibit visual consistency across distinct
842   camera perspectives.
