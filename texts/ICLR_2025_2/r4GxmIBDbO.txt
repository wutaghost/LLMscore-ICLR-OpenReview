009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, diffusion models have garnered much attention for their remarkable
012            generative capabilities. Yet, their application for representation learning remains
013            largely unexplored. In this paper, we explore the possibility of using the dif-
014            fusion process to pretrain the backbone of a deep learning model for a specific
015            application—gait recognition in the wild. To do so, we condition a latent diffu-
016
               sion model on the output of a gait recognition model backbone. Our pretrain-
               ing experiments on the Gait3D and GREW datasets reveal an interesting phe-
017
               nomenon: diffusion pretraining causes the gait recognition backbone to separate
018
               gait sequences belonging to different subjects further apart than those belonging
019            to the same subjects, which translates to a steady improvement in gait recogni-
020            tion performance. Subsequently, our transfer learning experiments on Gait3D and
021            GREW show that the pretrained backbone can serve as an effective initialization
022            for the downstream gait recognition task, allowing the gait recognition model to
023            achieve better performance within much fewer supervised training iterations. We
024            validated the applicability of our approach across multiple existing gait recogni-
025            tion methods and conducted extensive ablation studies to investigate the impact of
026            different pretraining hyperparameters on the final gait recognition performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Gait, the unique manner in which a person walks, offers an alternative way to identify individuals,
031   alongside the more common biometric modalities like fingerprints and irises. With the emergence
032   of deep learning, advancements in computer vision architectures, and the collection of well-labelled
033   gait datasets, deep gait recognition has become an increasingly popular area of research over the
034   last few years (Sepas-Moghaddam & Etemad, 2022). Consequently, owing to the efforts of many
035   previous works, it has become possible to achieve impressive accuracy performance on existing
036   controlled datasets such as CASIA-B (Yu et al., 2006) and OU-MVLP (Takemura et al., 2018).
037   However, when these techniques are applied to recently released in-the-wild gait datasets such as
038   Gait3D (Zheng et al., 2022) and GREW (Zhu et al., 2021), their performance pales in comparison,
039
      highlighting their limited applicability to unconstrained settings. As a result, recent studies (Zheng
      et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023c; Cosma et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023a;
040
      Habib et al., 2024) have begun shifting their focus towards addressing the more challenging problem
041
      of gait recognition in the wild.
042
043   Recently, diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020; Nichol
044   & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023) have been in the spotlight for their
045
      impressive performance on generative tasks, outshining variational autoencoders, flow-based gen-
      erative models, and generative adversarial networks which previously dominated the generative
046
      realm. Their stability during training and ability to generate high-quality realistic samples, such
047
      as images (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022;
048   Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), videos (Ho et al., 2022b;a; Singer et al., 2022; Bar-Tal et al., 2024), and
049   audio (Schneider, 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Mittal et al., 2021), have led to their wide adoption
050   in academia and industry. Amidst the hype to exploit diffusion models for generative tasks, other
051   promising directions of diffusion models have been less explored. One such direction involves lever-
052   aging diffusion models for representation learning, where a model learns to extract relevant features
053   during the diffusion process, which can be beneficial for tasks beyond generation. Moreover, with
      a simple reconstruction or noise prediction objective, diffusion training can potentially serve as an


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      effective self-supervised pretraining approach, which does not necessitate the need for a labelled
055   dataset. While some studies have started looking into using the learnt representations for common
056   tasks like image classification (Clark & Jaini, 2024; Hudson et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Xiang et al.,
057   2023; Abstreiter et al., 2021; Yang & Wang, 2023) and segmentation (Yang & Wang, 2023; Zhao
058   et al., 2023), not much attention has been paid to using them in applications such as gait recognition.
059   Furthermore, many previous works related to diffusion-based representation learning have not in-
060   vestigated the effects of finetuning the learnt representations on downstream tasks, overlooking the
061   pretraining potential of diffusion training.
062   Considering the limited research on diffusion-based representation learning in the gait recognition
063   field and the more challenging in-the-wild scenarios, we aim to address the following question: can
064   we leverage diffusion training to enhance existing methods for gait recognition in the wild?
065
      To explore this, we propose a diffusion-based approach to pretrain the backbone of a gait recogni-
066
      tion model by using its output as a condition for a latent diffusion model. Thereafter, we initialize
067   the gait recognition model with the pretrained backbone and perform transfer learning on the down-
068   stream gait recognition task. We conducted extensive applicability studies with multiple existing
069   gait recognition models, including GaitGL (Lin et al., 2022), GaitPart (Fan et al., 2020), GaitSet
070   (Chao et al., 2019), SMPLGait w/o 3D (Zheng et al., 2022), and GaitBase (Fan et al., 2023c), by
071   evaluating on two in-the-wild gait datasets, Gait3D (Zheng et al., 2022) and GREW (Zhu et al.,
072   2021). Additionally, we performed thorough ablation studies to investigate the effects that different
073   pretraining hyperparameters have on the downstream gait recognition task.
074   Our finding reveals that during diffusion pretraining, the gait recognition model backbone, regardless
075   of its architecture, learns to separate gait sequences belonging to different subjects further apart than
076   those belonging to the same subject, despite the lack of an explicit signal to do so. This results in a
077   steady improvement in the gait recognition performance and demonstrates the potential of diffusion
078   pretraining for gait recognition. Subsequently, when the gait recognition model is initialized with
079   the pretrained backbone and further finetuned on the downstream gait recognition task, it surpasses
080   the performance of its trained-from-scratch counterpart by as much as 7.9% on Gait3D and 4.2% on
081
      GREW. This remains the case even when the number of supervised training iterations is significantly
      reduced by as much as 89% on Gait3D and 70% on GREW.
082
083   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to apply diffusion training for representation learning
084   in the field of gait recognition and demonstrate its effectiveness as a pretraining approach for the
085   gait recognition task.

      2.1   G AIT R ECOGNITION
090
091   With the advent of deep learning, gait recognition typically involves extracting gait features from gait
092   sequences and projecting these features into more discriminative embeddings that can be compared
093   using a distance metric such as Euclidean or cosine distances. These gait sequences typically come
094   in the form of either silhouettes or skeletons, with silhouette-based recognition being more popular
095   (Sepas-Moghaddam & Etemad, 2022), though skeleton-based gait recognition remains an active
096   area of research (Teepe et al., 2021; 2022; Zhang et al., 2023).
097   Much state-of-the-art research regarding deep gait recognition focuses on the architectural design of
098   backbone networks to maximize the extraction of meaningful gait information from gait sequences
099   (Lin et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023c; Cosma et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023a; Chao et al., 2019; Fan et al.,
100   2020; Song et al., 2019). For instance, GaitPart (Fan et al., 2020) proposes a part-based approach
101   that divides each gait silhouette into several parts and extracts features for each part separately to
102   obtain more fine-grained features. It also focuses on short-range neighbouring frames rather than
103   considering all frames within a gait sequence. GaitSet (Chao et al., 2019), on the other hand, treats
104
      gait as an unordered set of gait silhouettes and uses only permutation-invariant operations within its
      architecture.
105
106   Inspired by the fields of person reidentification, current state-of-the-art research (Chao et al., 2019;
107   Fan et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023c) also adapted techniques such
      as horizontal pyramid matching (Fu et al., 2019) and batch normalization neck (Luo et al., 2019) to


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      further enhance gait recognition accuracy. Several works have also explored self-supervised learning
109   approaches (Liu et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2023b; Cosma et al., 2023). They are often
110   based on contrastive learning, where augmented versions of the same gait sequences are treated as
111   positive pairs and different gait sequences are treated as negative pairs.
112
      While many of these methods have performed well on existing controlled datasets such as CASIA-B
113
      (Yu et al., 2006) and OU-MVLP (Takemura et al., 2018), their performance drastically drops when
114
      applied to the recently released in-the-wild datasets (Zheng et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021), where
115   factors such as temporary occlusions, varying camera viewpoints, and illumination inadvertently
116   come into play. Finding a way that can universally enhance the performance of these methods in
117   such unconstrained settings is highly desirable.
118
      As the introduction of in-the-wild gait datasets is fairly recent, many earlier works did not have
119
      the opportunity to evaluate their methods on these datasets. However, with the efforts of OpenGait
120
      (Fan et al., 2023c), several previous works have been reproduced, trained, and evaluated on the
121   in-the-wild datasets, serving as baselines for future research in gait recognition in the wild.
122

      2.2   D IFFUSION
124
125   First proposed by Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015), diffusion models, particularly Denoising Diffusion
126   Probabilistic Models (Ho et al., 2020), are now an active area of research. A typical diffusion
127   process consists of two phases—the forward phase and the reverse phase. During the forward phase,
128   a data sample x is gradually transformed into an approximate pure noise by iteratively adding noise
129   ϵ ∼ N (0, I) to a data sample across a series of timesteps based on a defined noise schedule ᾱt . At
130   any timestep t of the forward diffusion process, the noised data sample xt can be expressed as:
                                                √          √
131                                       xt = ᾱt · x + 1 − ᾱt · ϵ                                     (1)
132
133   On the other hand, during the reverse phase, noise is iteratively removed from a noisy sample until
134   a clean sample is obtained. By training a model ϵθ to predict the added noise ϵ in the noisy data
135   sample xt at any given timestep t of the forward diffusion process, a mapping from random noise to
136   the data manifold can be learnt, which confers diffusion models their generative ability. The overall
137   training objective of diffusion models can be summarised as the minimization of the following noise
138
      prediction loss L (Ho et al., 2020):
139                                           L = ||ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t)||22                                   (2)
140
141   In order to generate more precise data samples, diffusion models are fed with an additional prompt
142   c and instead learn a conditional data distribution. In this case, the above loss becomes:
143                                          L = ||ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t, c)||22                                 (3)
144
145   Various methods have been proposed to improve the quality of samples produced by conditional dif-
146   fusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022; Wallace et al., 2023). In particular,
147   classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), which randomly drops out the added condition, has
148   been one of the most simple and effective.
149
      To reduce the computation cost and memory consumption brought about by training with and gen-
150   erating high-resolution data, Rombach et al. (2022) proposed latent diffusion, whereby data are first
151   encoded into a lower dimensional latent space before the diffusion process is applied. To further op-
152   timize the training efficiency of diffusion models, various noise schedulers (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol
153   & Dhariwal, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022) and timestep weighting approaches (Hang et al., 2023;
154   Salimans & Ho, 2022) have also been proposed. These methods serve to prioritize a certain noise
155   range during the diffusion process, which improves convergence. Initially started on image gener-
156   ation (Rombach et al., 2022; Song et al., 2023; Nichol et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia
157   et al., 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), diffusion models have now evolved to generate videos (Ho
158
      et al., 2022b;a; Singer et al., 2022; Bar-Tal et al., 2024), audio (Schneider, 2023; Huang et al., 2023;
      Mittal et al., 2021) and even architecture-specific neural network parameters (Soro et al., 2024).
159
160   Despite its impressive generative feats in various domains, the learnt representations produced by
161   the diffusion process have not been looked into as intensively, particularly in specialized fields such
      as gait recognition. Closely related to our work, Hudson et al. (2023) assessed the usefulness of the

      3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD
185
186
      In this section, we introduce our proposed method, which consists of two stages: (1) pretraining
187
      with diffusion and (2) transfer learning on the downstream gait recognition task.
188
189
190   3.1     D IFFUSION P RETRAINING
191
      To learn gait representations via diffusion, we employ an end-to-end trainable conditional latent
192
      diffusion model. As illustrated in Fig. 1, our architecture consists of three main components—an
193
      encoder E, a denoiser D, and a gait feature extractor G. The encoder first encodes an input silhouette
194   sequence into a more compact latent representation, which is then added with random noise and
195   subsequently denoised via the denoiser. At the same time, relevant features from the input silhouette
196   sequence are extracted via the gait feature extractor and these features are pooled and passed on as a
197   condition to aid the denoiser in the denoising process for a more precise reconstruction of the input
198   silhouette sequence. The following sections delve into the individual components that make up the
199   architecture.
200
201   3.1.1    E NCODER
202
203   Considering its small size and decent silhouette-encoding ability (Appendix A.1), we use the open-
204
      sourced Tiny AutoEncoder for Stable Diffusion (TAESD) (Bohan, 2023) as our encoder. As sil-
      houette sequences are made up of single-channel grayscale frames and TAESD only accepts three-
205
      channel images, silhouette sequences are first replicated along the channel dimension before encod-
206
      ing. Given a three-channel sequence s of shape (t, 3, h, w), where t, h, w denote sequence length,
207   height, and width, respectively, the encoder of TAESD, E, performs framewise encoding of the
208   sequence along the spatial dimension while preserving the temporal dimension, producing a 48×
209   compressed latent encoding E(s) of shape (t, 4, h8 , w8 ).
210
211   3.1.2    D ENOISER
212
213   As for the denoiser, given the lack of open-sourced pretrained gait silhouette sequence diffusion
214   models, we adapt a recent video diffusion model architecture proposed by Ho et al. (2022b) to fit
215   our use case. The denoiser accepts noisy latent representations, selected diffusion timesteps, and
      any other one-dimensional conditions as inputs, and outputs a prediction of the added noise.


                                                                    4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      3.1.3   G AIT F EATURE E XTRACTOR
217
218   The gait feature extractor is our target model to be trained by the diffusion process. To aid the de-
219   noiser in predicting the added noise, an additional input condition, corresponding to the gait features
220   of the encoded input sequence into the denoiser, is further provided to the denoiser. Given a silhou-
221   ette sequence s, the gait feature extractor G transforms it into a two-dimensional gait feature, G(s),
222
      which is representative of the gait contained within the sequence. The gait feature is extracted by
      the backbone of a typical deep gait recognition model, which can be based on any previous work.
223
      To test the applicability of our approach, we adopt a variety of existing backbones in literature to
224
      be the gait feature extractor—GaitGL (Lin et al., 2022), GaitPart (Fan et al., 2020), GaitSet (Chao
225   et al., 2019), SMPLGait w/o 3D (Zheng et al., 2022), and GaitBase (Fan et al., 2023c).
226
227   3.1.4   P OOLING M ETHOD
228
229   As the denoiser accepts only one-dimensional tensors as conditions while the extracted gait features
230   are two-dimensional tensors, an operation is needed to convert the gait feature tensors into a single
231   dimension. A pooling operation is applied, rather than simply flattening, to reduce the computation
232
      that will be introduced from conditioning. In particular, mean pooling is used, following our ab-
      lation study in Sec. 5. The pooled gait feature conditions, G(s)pooled , are then concatenated with
233
      the timestep conditions and are used to scale and bias the activations within the denoiser layers,
234
      following the work of Dhariwal & Nichol (2021).
235
236   3.1.5   I NPUT P RETREATMENT
237
238   To allow the gait extractor to focus learning on gait information rather than video information, the
239   autoencoder and gait feature extractor are presented with different subsequences sampled from the
240   same silhouette sequence via different sampling algorithms. We denote this pair of input subse-
241
      quences as s = (sE , sG ), where sE denotes an input subsequence to the encoder while sG denotes
      an input subsequence to the gait feature extractor and sE ̸= sG .
242
243   As a video diffusion model is used for the denoiser, its input needs to be temporally consistent and
244   thus, N frames are sampled consecutively from the silhouette sequence to serve as sE . As for sG ,
245   we sample N frames using the exact sampling algorithm employed by the authors who proposed
246   the respective gait feature extractor architecture (Lin et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2020; Chao et al., 2019;
247
      Zheng et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2023c). We fixed N = 30 throughout the study. Even though sE and
      sG are different, G(sG ) should still ideally correspond to the gait features of sE , since sE and sG
248
      belong to the same subject.
249
250   Each frame in sG is first normalized and resized to 64 × 64 before being cropped to 64 × 44, which
251   is the common input size for many gait recognition models. As for sE , they are simply normalized
252   and resized to 64 × 64.
253   To increase the generalizability of the model, sE and sG are augmented separately with a combina-
254   tion of RandomAffine, RandomPerspective, RandomHorizontalFlip, RandomPartDilate (Fan et al.,
255   2023b) and RandomPartBlur. Appendix A.2 provides a visualization of each augmentation applied.
256
257   3.1.6   N OISE S CHEDULER AND L OSS W EIGHTING S TRATEGY
258
      For generative purposes, noise schedulers, such as the cosine noise scheduler, which prioritize high
259
      and low noise levels are commonly adopted. However, in the case of representation learning, Hudson
260
      et al. (2023) have found that prioritizing medium noise is more helpful. Following their work,
261   we adopt an inverted cosine noise scheduler. To further focus on medium-level noise, we adopt a
262   medium noise prioritization weighting strategy that downweighs the loss when the noise is too high
263   or too low. Specifically, we modify the existing Min-SNR weighting strategy (Hang et al., 2023),
264   which downweighs losses from low-level noise, to also downweigh losses from high levels of noise.
265
266   3.1.7   L OSS F UNCTION
267
      To pretrain our gait recognition model via diffusion, we employ the L2 noise prediction loss com-
268
      monly used by diffusion models, which can be summarized as:
269
                                   Ldiffusion = ||ϵ − D(E(sE )t , t, G(sG )pooled )||22                     (4)


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      where E(sE )t is the noised latent representation fed to the denoiser D, at timestep t ∈ (0, 1000]
271   of the forward diffusion process, ϵ ∼ N (0, I) is the random noise added, and G(sG )pooled is the
272   pooled gait feature condition. During diffusion pretraining, the denoiser and gait feature extractor
273   are trained from scratch while the pretrained TAESD encoder is kept frozen. A higher learning
274   rate is assigned to the gait feature extractor to enable it to better guide the denoiser during training,
275   following Hudson et al. (2023).
276
277   3.2   T RANSFER L EARNING
278
279   Once the gait feature extractor is trained by diffusion, we evaluate it on the downstream gait recog-
280
      nition task. We replicate the remaining parts of the gait recognition model accordingly and initialize
      the weights of the gait backbone with the ones learnt during diffusion pretraining. The untrained
281
      parameters are initialized based on the settings provided by OpenGait. We consider two cases of
282
      transfer learning, namely, with and without finetuning of the pretrained backbone. During the first
283   case, the pretrained backbone is frozen to evaluate the usefulness of the learnt representations for
284   the downstream gait recognition task. As for the latter case, the pretrained backbone is allowed to
285   be finetuned together with the untrained parameters to evaluate the effectiveness of diffusion as a
286   pretraining method.
287
      During transfer learning, each gait recognition model is trained using the standard triplet loss, Ltriplet ,
288
      for identification. The distance measure typically employed is the Euclidean distance. However, we
289   employ the cosine distance instead as we found that the use of cosine distance during training and
290   evaluation produces much better results for existing works (Appendix A.3).
291
292
      Additionally, some methods, such as GaitBase and SMPLGait w/o 3D, also incorporate an additional
      smoothed identity loss, LID , by having another module predict the identity of each gait sequence to
293
      enhance the gait recognition performance. In this case, the net loss is the sum of the triplet loss and
294
      the reweighted smoothed ID loss:
295
296
                                            Lnet = Ltriplet + 0.1 · LID                                    (5)

363   4.3.1         F ROZEN BACKBONE
364
      With diffusion pretraining, we have pretrained the backbones of various existing gait recognition
365
      models. What is left is to project the features into more discriminative embeddings with the remain-
366
      ing parts of these models for the downstream gait recognition task. To evaluate how discriminative
367   the learnt features are for gait recognition, we froze the pretrained backbones and simply trained the
368   remaining untrained layers via the supervised learning objective. The results are shown in Table 1.
369

466   5   A BLATION S TUDIES
467
468   In this section, we present the various ablation studies conducted to investigate the effects that dif-
469   ferent hyperparameter settings have on the diffusion pretraining process and downstream gait recog-
470   nition tasks. For all experiments, we used the backbone of SMPLGait w/o 3D as our gait feature
471   extractor and Gait3D as the dataset. During transfer learning, no data augmentation was applied, and
472   the pretrained backbone was finetuned with a lower learning rate (0.1×) than the untrained layers.
473   Aside from the hyperparameter being investigated, all other hyperparameters were assigned based
474   on the default pretraining and transfer learning settings. The results of the various ablation studies
475   are summarized in Table 3.
476   Noise Scheduler and Loss Weighting: We explored two kinds of schedulers—a typical cosine
477   scheduler (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) and an inverted cosine scheduler (Hudson et al., 2023). For the
478   cosine scheduler, we attempted a uniform weighting strategy, where losses from different timesteps
479   are weighed equally, and the Min-SNR strategy (Hang et al., 2023). As for the inverted cosine
480   scheduler, we attempted uniform weighting and our proposed medium noise prioritization (MNP)
481   weighting strategy, which downweighs losses from both low and high timesteps. We see that focus-
482
      ing on medium-level noise through the combined use of an inverted cosine scheduler and medium
      noise prioritization weighting strategy worked the best.
483
484   Feature Pooling Method: We investigated different methods to pool the two-dimensional output
485   of the gait feature extractor into a one-dimensional condition—mean pooling, max pooling, and the
      sum of the mean and max. Out of the three pooling methods, mean pooling worked the best.

498   Inputs to Denoiser and Gait Feature Extractor: We investigated what would happen during dif-

500   identical. Fig. 5 shows the rank-1 gait recognition accuracy curves of the gait feature extractor
501   during the diffusion pretraining process when sE ̸= sG and sE = sG .
502   When sE ̸= sG , we see a steady improvement in gait recognition accuracy during the diffusion
503   pretraining. Yet, when sE = sG , the gait recognition accuracy fluctuates over time, suggesting that
504   what the gait feature extractor is learning during diffusion pretraining is unlikely to be effective gait
505   features. Rather, it could be extracting video information to aid the denoiser in the denoising task.
506   Data Augmentation During Pretraining: We turned off data augmentation during diffusion pre-
507   training to investigate the impact on downstream gait recognition performance. Without any data
508   augmentation during pretraining, a significant drop in the downstream gait recognition performance
509   is observed, highlighting the necessity of data augmentation during diffusion pretraining.
510
      Size of Denoiser: We varied the size of the denoiser that is used during the diffusion pretraining
511
      process by changing its initial channel dimension. We see that the larger the denoiser, the better the
512   downstream gait recognition performance. This suggests that further improvements can possibly be
513   made to the downstream task by increasing the denoiser size during diffusion pretraining. That said,
514   it would come at the cost of larger memory consumption and longer pretraining time.
515
      Learning Rate of Denoiser and Gait Feature Extractor: We investigated how different relative
516
517
      ratios of the learning rate between the gait feature extractor and denoiser, llDG , affect the downstream
518   performance. We kept the denoiser learning rate constant at 1e−4 and varied the learning rate of the
519   gait extractor. We observe that increasing llDG leads to better downstream performance.
520
      Unconditional Training Probability: To investigate the effects of varying the unconditional train-
521   ing probability, Puncond , during diffusion pretraining, we explored Puncond ∈ {0, 0.15, 0.5}. We see
522   that our proposed diffusion pretraining approach benefits from classifier-free guidance. However,
523   too high an unconditional training probability ended up hurting the downstream gait recognition
524   performance, likely as the gait feature extractor got updated less. A moderate unconditional training
525   probability yielded the best result.

529   6    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In summary, we introduce a diffusion pretraining approach for gait recognition in the wild. By sim-
532
      ply conditioning a diffusion denoiser with the output of a gait feature extractor, we can pretrain the
533
      gait feature extractor to extract relevant features for gait recognition. Initializing the gait recognition
534   model with the pretrained backbone and training it on the downstream gait recognition task further
535   allows us to surpass the performance of its supervised learning counterpart within a much shorter
536   supervised training duration. Our experiments on the Gait3D and GREW datasets demonstrated the
537   broad applicability of our method, achieving rank-1 gait recognition accuracy improvements of up
538   to 7.9% for Gait3D and 4.2% for GREW. We hope our work will spur further interest among re-
539   searchers in employing diffusion models for representation learning, not only in the gait recognition
      field but also in other fields as well.

      To ensure that our chosen autoencoder, TAESD, is compatible with grayscale inputs, a small-scale
