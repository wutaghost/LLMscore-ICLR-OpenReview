009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The ultimate goal of generative models is to perfectly capture the data distribution.
012            For image generation, common metrics of visual quality (e.g., FID) and the per-
013            ceived truthfulness of generated images seem to suggest that we are nearing this
014            goal. However, through distribution classification tasks, we reveal that, from the
015            perspective of neural network-based classifiers, even advanced diffusion models
016            are still far from this goal. Specifically, classifiers are able to consistently and
017            effortlessly distinguish real images from generated ones across various settings.
018
               Moreover, we uncover an intriguing discrepancy: classifiers can easily differen-
               tiate between diffusion models with comparable performance (e.g., U-ViT-H vs.
019
               DiT-XL), but struggle to distinguish between models within the same family but
020
               of different scales (e.g., EDM2-XS vs. EDM2-XXL). Our methodology carries
021            several important implications. First, it naturally serves as a diagnostic tool for
022            diffusion models by analyzing specific features of generated data. Second, it sheds
023            light on the model autophagy disorder and offers insights into the use of generated
024            data: augmenting real data with generated data is more effective than replacing it.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Diffusion probabilistic models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020)
030   have emerged as leading generative models for image (Ramesh et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2024),
031   video (Brooks et al., 2024; Bao et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) and 3D content generation (Poole et al.,
032   2022), surpassing previous generative models such as generative adversarial networks (Goodfellow
033
      et al., 2014), variational autoencoders (Kingma & Welling, 2013), and normalizing flows (Rezende
      & Mohamed, 2015). In particular, focusing on image generation, diffusion models are capable of
034
      producing realistic images that are often indistinguishable from real ones to the human eye (see Fig. 1).
035
      Furthermore, common metrics such as FID (Heusel et al., 2017) suggest that the distribution generated
036   by state-of-the-art diffusion models closely approximates the ImageNet validation set (Karras et al.,
037   2023; 2022; Tian et al., 2024). Therefore some works, such as Wang et al. (2024); You et al. (2024),
038   have utilized generated data to augment training datasets. However, to the best of our knowledge, few
039   studies have been conducted to analyze the differences between the generated and real distributions.
040
      This raises a fundamental question: How far is the generated distribution from the real distribution?
041
      Inspired by the methodology in the work (Liu & He, 2024; Tommasi et al., 2017) on dataset
042   bias (Torralba & Efros, 2011), we propose distribution classification tasks (see Sec. 3 for details),
043   predicting the distribution an image belongs to - real or generated - thereby serving as a probe to
044   explore this critical question. Notably, binary classification is a natural approach, as classifiers
045   parameterized by neural networks can serve as effective indicators of the differences between the
046   real and generated distributions through classification accuracy (see more analysis in Appendix D).
047   Compared to the widely used FID metric, our classifier-based approach directly measures distribution
048   differences without relying on Gaussian assumptions (see further discussion in Appendix E).
049   We first explore are images indistinguishable to humans also indistinguishable to classifiers? To
050   this end, we train several commonly used classifiers to distinguish between the real and generated
051   distribution across various settings. Quantitatively, most classifiers can achieve over 98% accuracy.
052   Meanwhile, all classifiers reach extremely high accuracy with significantly fewer training samples
053   than the requirement of training diffusion models. Interestingly, even self-supervised classifiers
      exhibit this capacity to identify the generated distribution. These results demonstrate that from the

      2.2   F REQUENCY FILTER
141
142   The frequency content of an image represents the rate of pixel value changes. Low-frequency
143   components capture overall shapes and gradual grayscale changes, while high-frequency components
144   reflect fine details like edges and textures. Notably, convolutional neural networks can detect high-
145   frequencies that are often imperceptible to humans (Wang et al., 2020a). In our paper, we implement
146   a rectangular filter and discuss the choice between using rectangular or circular filters in Appendix F.
147   Low-pass filters are implemented as follows: Compute the Fourier transform of the image f (x, y) us-
148   ing F (u, v) = FFT(f (x, y)), and then center the spectrum with Fc (u, v) = fftshift(F (u, v)). Define
149   a rectangular mask H(u, v) in the frequency domain, where M and N are the image dimensions:
150
                                   1 if u − M                             N

      ifftshift(G(u, v)), and transform back to the spatial domain using g(x, y) = IFFT(Gc (u, v)).

      3    R ELATED WORK
163
164
      Dataset bias. Torralba & Efros (2011) introduced the "Name That Dataset" game to discover dataset
165
      bias. Tommasi et al. (2017) and Liu & He (2024) expanded on this by applying convolutional neural
166
      networks and large-scale datasets, respectively. Existing work on dataset bias focuses on classification
167   to highlight biases in data collection for building unbiased datasets, our work leverages distribution
168   classification to explore a fundamental question: How far is the generated distribution from the real
169   distribution? Besides, we focus on the discrepancy between the generated distribution and the real
170   distribution rather than between specific datasets. Furthermore, the conclusion drawn by related work
171   highlights the ability of neural networks to detect biases within increasingly general datasets, our
172   conclusion underscores the significant gap that still exists between current diffusion models and the
173   real data distribution. We also highlight the potential of distribution classification methods for gaining
174   deeper insights into generative models and more effectively leveraging generated data.
175   Generated image detection. Several empirical works on generated image detection span three key
176   areas: universal detection (Liu et al., 2024; Ojha et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2020b; Girish et al., 2021),
177   reconstruction-based methods (Wang et al., 2023b; Luo et al., 2024), and frequency analysis (Wang
178   et al., 2020a; Zhang et al., 2019; Durall et al., 2020; Chandrasegaran et al., 2021; Frank et al., 2020;
179   Dzanic et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Corvi et al., 2023b; Ricker et al., 2022; Corvi et al., 2023a).
180   Existing work generally focuses on either building universal discriminators to detect generated images
181
      or identifying frequency artifacts to build classifiers. In contrast, our approach leverages distribution
      classification to gain deeper insights into generative models and quantify the discrepancy between
182
      generated and real distributions. Instead of prioritizing classifier performance, we focus on the
183
      insights provided into generative model behavior.

187
188   We present the main experimental settings as follows. For more details, please see Appendix A.
189
      Dataset. For label-to-image tasks, we use the CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and Ima-
190
      geNet (Deng et al., 2009) datasets, and for text-to-image tasks, we utilize the COCO2014 dataset (Lin
191
      et al., 2014). For CIFAR-10, the real distribution is constructed from its training and testing sets,
192   comprising 50k training images and 10k testing images. The generated distribution is created using
193   diffusion models to produce an equivalent number of images. For ImageNet, we consider resolutions
194   of 256 and 512 and follow ADM’s (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) method to preprocess ImageNet into
195   the desired resolutions. We randomly sample 100k training images and 50k validation images from
196   the corresponding ImageNet sets to represent the real distribution, and generate an equivalent number
197   of images using diffusion models for the generated distribution. For COCO2014, we randomly sample
198   10k training images and 1k validation images from the respective sets to form the real distribution, and
199   use the corresponding prompts to generate an equivalent number of data for the generated distribution.
200   Classifier. By default, we use ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) as the classifier architecture, and also
201   consider ConvNeXt-T (Liu et al., 2022) and ViT-S (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) for completeness. Our
202   pre-processing follows standard supervised training (Liu et al., 2022). Specifically, during training,
203   the classifiers process randomly augmented crops of 224 × 224 images. For validation, images are
204   resized to 256 pixels on the shorter side, preserving the aspect ratio, and then center cropped to
205   224 × 224. For CIFAR-10 experiments, we initialize the classifier with ResNet-50 pre-trained on
206
      ImageNet and also provide the results trained from scratch (see Appendix C.2). For ImageNet, we
      train ResNet-50 from scratch.
207
208   Diffusion model. For CIFAR-10 generation, we consider two diffusion models: EDM (Karras
209   et al., 2022) and U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023). For ImageNet-256, we explore three diffusion models:
210   EDM2 (Karras et al., 2023), U-ViT-H/2 (Bao et al., 2023), and DiT-XL/2 (Peebles & Xie, 2023).
211   Since EDM2 is originally designed for ImageNet-512 generation, we resize its generated images
212
      from 512 to 256 resolution, with EDM2-XXL achieving an FID of 2.14, comparable to U-ViT-
      H/2 and DiT-XL/2. For ImageNet-512 generation, we use EDM2-XXL (Karras et al., 2023). For
213
      COCO generation, we consider three text-to-image diffusion models: Pixart-α (Chen et al., 2023),
214
      SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), and Playground-v2.5 (Li et al., 2024).
215
      Evaluation. We use the top-1 accuracy on the validation set to evaluate classification performance.

231   5     G ENERATED DISTRIBUTIONS ARE EASILY CLASSIFIED AS GENERATED
232
233   In this section, we explore how the distribution generated by diffusion models, known for their
234   success in image generation, differs from the real distribution. To analyze this, we use a distribution
235   classification task to assess the extent of the difference.
236
237   5.1   C LASSIFIERS CANNOT DISTINGUISH SAMPLES FROM THE SAME DISTRIBUTION
238
239   First, it is crucial to evaluate whether classifiers are indeed unable to distinguish samples from the
240   same distribution. As shown in Tab. 1, we report the classification accuracy for the same distributions
241
      in the label-to-image scenario. Our findings indicate that classifiers are unable to distinguish samples
      from the same distribution, initially demonstrating the validity of the distribution classification task.
242
243
244
      5.2   C LASSIFIERS ACHIEVE HIGH ACCURACY ACROSS VARIOUS SETTINGS
245   Label-to-image. As shown in Tab. 2, we report the binary distribution classification accuracy for
246   various combinations of real and generated distributions in the label-to-image scenario. Surprisingly,
247   despite the low FID scores achieved by diffusion models, neural network-based classifiers still identify
248   significant differences between the generated and real distributions. Specifically, all classifiers easily
249   distinguish real from generated images across different dataset combinations, with most achieving
250   over 98% accuracy in distribution classification. Moreover, we observe a positive correlation between
251   distribution classification accuracy and the number of training samples for the combination of U-
252   ViT-H/2 and ImageNet-256 (see Fig. 2). This suggests that as sample size increases, classifiers learn
253
      more generalizable features rather than merely memorizing the data. Notably, with only 50k samples
      per distribution (less than 10% of the ImageNet samples, approximately 1.28 million, used to train
254
      the generator), classifiers achieve over 99.5% accuracy. Besides, we extend our evaluation to other
255
      generative models, e.g., GANs (see detailed results in Sec. C.1).
256
257   Text-to-image. As shown in Tab. 3, we report the distribution classification accuracy in the text-
258   to-image scenario. We consider combinations of four distributions: COCO (Lin et al., 2014),
259
      Pixart-α (Chen et al., 2023), SDXL (Podell et al., 2023), and Playground-v2.5 (Li et al., 2024). In this
      task, classifiers are not only required to distinguish between real and generated distributions but also
260
      correctly identify the specific generated distribution an image comes from. Remarkably, classifiers
261
      achieve an accuracy of over 76% across all settings. Furthermore, we observe that increasing the
262   training sample size from 5k to 10k results in significant accuracy gains, consistent with the patterns
263   seen in the label-to-image scenario.

410   7      I MPLICATIONS OF THE DISTRIBUTION CLASSIFICATION TASK
411
412   Previously, we demonstrated the classifier’s ability to distinguish real from generated distributions, as
413   well as the contradictions between classifiers and evaluation methods like FID and human judgments.
414

416   7.1     D IAGNOSING THE PROBLEM WITHIN DIFFUSION MODELS
417
      A natural implication of classifiers is to diagnose the problem within diffusion models. Our approach
418
      complements traditional metrics like FID and human judgments by offering a more challenging
419
      evaluation method. It can also be extended to analyze specific features of generated data, providing
420   deeper insights into the intrinsic characteristics of diffusion models, such as spatial and frequency
421   features. In this section, we focus on advanced diffusion transformers, specifically U-ViT-H/2 (Bao
422   et al., 2023), and investigate two key aspects: spatial features and frequency ranges. Our objective is
423   to identify which spatial features diffusion models learn well, making them harder to distinguish, and
424   which they struggle with, making them easier to detect. Additionally, we assess performance across
425   different frequency ranges to pinpoint where diffusion models succeed and where they fail. For this
426   analysis, we use U-ViT-H/2 trained on ImageNet-256, with ResNet-50 as the classifier.
427   In our first set of experiments, we focus on the spatial features. We start by progressively reducing
428   central information through center-cropping images from a resolution of 256 down to 128, 64, 32,
429   and 16, followed by training the classifier from scratch. The results, shown in Tab. 7, indicate that
430   even with minimal central information, the classifier can accurately distinguish between real and
431   generated distributions. We then assess whether classifiers can differentiate based on any arbitrary
      part of the image. We cropped images at resolutions of 128 and 64, and randomly selected four

501   well-known phenomenon is that training generative models on data produced by previous generations
502   in a continuous loop leads to a gradual decline in output quality and diversity (Shumailov et al., 2024;
503   Alemohammad et al., 2024), ultimately resulting in model collapse, known as Model Autophagy
504   Disorder (MAD). This is surprising, as data generated by modern diffusion models often exhibit
505   high realism. However, using this data to train next-generation models triggers MAD. Our approach
506   provides insight into this issue: although these models generate realistic images, the distributions they
507   produce remain distinct from the real distribution—especially when viewed by classifiers—leading to
508   a distribution mismatch. As this mismatch accumulates, the generated distribution drifts further from
509
      the original. To address this, Alemohammad et al. (2024) propose incorporating fresh real samples
      into the training data for each generation, rather than relying solely on generated data.
510
511   To better understand this phenomenon, we further explore the impact of using generative model
512   data in downstream tasks. Specifically, we investigate whether the distribution mismatch between
513   real and generated data causes undesirable behaviors when replacing real data with generated data
514   in tasks such as classification. To analyze this, we use EDM (Karras et al., 2022) to generate the
515
      same number of samples as CIFAR-10, creating a synthetic dataset, EDM_S, and perform both
      supervised and semi-supervised classification to assess the impact. For supervised classification,
516
      we train the classifier with ConvNeXt settings (Liu et al., 2022) for CIFAR-10, using the original
517
      test set for evaluation. To create the training dataset, we mix EDM_S and CIFAR-10 with a ratio
518   α. We employ two strategies: one replaces α of CIFAR-10 with EDM_S, where α represents the
519   replacement ratio, and the other augments EDM_S to CIFAR-10. As shown in Fig. 6, we observe
520   that due to the distribution mismatch, simply replacing samples leads to a decline in accuracy. As α
521   increases from 0 to 1, the top-1 accuracy gradually declines. In contrast, when augmenting EDM_S
522   to CIFAR-10, accuracy generally improves for most α values. For semi-supervised classification,
523   we use FreeMatch (Wang et al., 2023a) and FlexMatch (Zhang et al., 2021). As shown in Tab. 8,
524   simply replacing CIFAR-10 with EDM_S reduces performance: FlexMatch’s error rate increases
525   from 4.97±0.06% to 5.85±0.02%, and FreeMatch’s from 4.90±0.04% to 5.94±0.10%, consistent with the
526
      results in supervised classification. Meanwhile, DPT (You et al., 2024) augments CIFAR-10 with
      EDM-generated data, training with FreeMatch, and achieves a lower error rate of 4.68±0.17%.
527
528    From the analysis above, we observe that the distribution mismatch between generated and real data
529    leads to undesirable behaviors, such as "MAD" or performance degradation, when real data is simply
530    replaced with generated data. However, augmenting real data with generated data is more effective.
531
532     8                               C ONCLUSION
533
534    Our paper proposes the distribution classification tasks to explore a fundamental question: How
535    far is the generated distribution from the real distribution? Our findings show that classifiers can
536    effectively distinguish between diffusion-generated and real distributions, even when diffusion models
537    achieve low FID scores and generate lifelike images. Additionally, our study shows contradictions
538    between classifier performance and evaluation metrics such as FID and human judgments. Finally,
539    our methodology can naturally serve as a diagnostic tool for diffusion models, offering a reasonable
       explanation for Model Autophagy Disorder (MAD) and evaluating the use of generated data.

      As shown in Tab. 14, we conducted experiments using StyleGAN-XL (Sauer et al., 2022), a state-
890

891
      a discriminator during GAN training, the classifier can still easily distinguish between real and
892   generated images. We argue that this is because the discriminator is trained jointly with the generator.
893   During training, the generated data seen by the discriminator comes from a continuously evolving
894   distribution, as the generator improves with each iteration. However, when using a classifier to
895   distinguish between real and generated distributions, the generated distribution remains fixed.

1043    D                                  B INARY CLASSIFICATION AS A MEASURE OF DISTRIBUTION DISTANCE
1044
1045   We employ a classifier C(x) to distinguish between the real data distribution pdata (x) and the generated
1046   data distribution pg (x). By training C(x) using the binary cross-entropy loss:
1047
                                                          L(C) = −Ex∼pdata (x) [log(C(x))] − Ex∼pg (x) [log(1 − C(x))],                                                                                                                                             (5)
1048
1049   The optimal classifier that minimizes this loss is:
1050
                                                                                                                                           pdata (x)
1051                                                                                                    C ∗ (x) =                                       .                                                                                                           (6)
                                                                                                                                     pdata (x) + pg (x)
1052
1053    Substituting C ∗ (x) back into the loss function yields:
1054
                                                                                L(C ∗ ) = − log(4) + 2 JSD(pdata (x)∥pg (x)),                                                                                                                                       (7)
1055
1056   where JSD denotes the Jensen-Shannon Divergence—a direct measure of the distance between the
1057   two distributions.
1058
        In our paper, we use classification accuracy to evaluate how well the classifier distinguishes between
1059    real and generated data because it provides an intuitive and interpretable metric. Although accuracy
1060    is non-differentiable and unsuitable for direct optimization, training the classifier with the binary
1061    cross-entropy loss—a convex surrogate—often leads to improved accuracy. This correlation suggests
1062    that accuracy can serve as a proxy for changes in the loss function, reflecting the distance between
1063    the generated and real data distributions.

        E                              R ELATIONSHIP BETWEEN DISTRIBUTION CLASSIFICATION AND FID
1066
1067
       The Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) is a widely used metric for evaluating the quality of generative
1068   models. It relies on feature extraction networks trained on datasets such as ImageNet and assumes
1069   that the extracted feature vectors follow a multivariate Gaussian distribution. FID calculates the
1070   Fréchet distance between these Gaussian distributions to measure discrepancies between the real
1071   and generated data. Meanwhile, as noted by Kynkäänniemi et al. (2022), FID can decrease simply
1072   by aligning the histograms of top-N classifications, without necessarily improving the perceptual
1073   quality of the generated images. Additionally, recent works like Karras et al. (2023) and Tian et al.
1074   (2024) report FID scores close to those of the ImageNet validation set, suggesting limitations in FID’s
1075   sensitivity to certain distribution differences.
1076    In contrast, our classifier-based approach offers a more direct measure of the distance between
1077    distributions without relying on the Gaussian assumptions inherent in FID. By training a classifier
1078    to distinguish between real and generated data, we obtain an intuitive and interpretable metric that
1079    reflects the actual distribution differences. This method complements commonly used metrics such as
        FID and Inception Score (IS), providing an alternative perspective on evaluating generative models.
