011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Simulation-based optimization has emerged as a crucial methodology in the field
014            of mobile network optimization, addressing the need for dynamic and predictive
015
               network management. To address the scarcity of open-source mobile network
               simulators for advanced research, we developed GenNet—a generative AI-driven
016
               mobile network simulator. GenNet can create virtual replicas of mobile users, base
017
               stations, and wireless environments, utilizing generative AI methods to simulate
018            the behaviors of these entities under various network settings with high accuracy.
019            GenNet features a tailor-made API explicitly designed for reinforcement learning
020            environments, enabling researchers to finely adjust network parameters such as tilts,
021            azimuth, and transmitting power. Extensive experiments have employed GenNet
022            to benchmark multi-objective optimization algorithms, focusing on enhancing
023            network coverage, throughput, and energy efficiency, validating its effectiveness
024            as a robust platform for advancing network optimization techniques. Through this
025            innovative tool, we aim to empower researchers and practitioners to identify and
026
               implement the most effective approaches for network optimization, paving the way
               for future advancements in mobile network management.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The deployment of the fifth-generation mobile network (5G) has expanded the range of services,
032   users, and devices on mobile networks, resulting in more complex network environments and
033   diverse application scenarios (Wu et al., 2017). This complexity introduces significant challenges for
034   network optimization, which has traditionally depended on mathematical modeling and operations
035   research (Fei et al., 2016; Aliu et al., 2012). In the typical network optimization process, researchers
036   first define control variables and objectives and then model these relationships mathematically
037   before applying optimization algorithms. However, several issues hinder this approach in today’s
038   5G networks: traditional models struggle to capture the physical realities of increasingly complex
039
      systems; direct testing of optimized configurations in real-world networks is often not feasible,
      making it difficult to assess their effectiveness accurately. These issues underscore the urgent need
040
      for innovative network optimization strategies to effectively address the dynamic and complex
041
      requirements of 5G networks.
042
043   Simulation-based optimization has emerged as an innovative methodology for addressing the chal-
044   lenges inherent in current mobile network optimization (Chen et al., 2020; Gong et al., 2023; Huang
045
      et al., 2023; Li & Li, 2023). This approach involves constructing a virtual network simulator that acts
      as a digital twin of a physical mobile network, replicating the structure, environment, and state of
046
      network elements or systems with high accuracy. Reinforcement learning-based optimizers work
047
      iteratively with this simulation model to identify the most effective network configurations. This
048   technique addresses the two principal challenges of traditional optimization methods: modeling
049   and evaluation difficulties. The simulator avoids complex mathematical modeling by generating a
050   virtual counterpart of the mobile network. Developers and operators can use this simulator to conduct
051   What-if Analysis, allowing them to test various network configurations and optimization strategies
052   without impacting the real-world network. Additionally, because the simulator is designed to be a
053   high-fidelity replica of the real-world network, it ensures that the optimization strategies developed
      are applicable and effective in real-world scenarios. Thus, simulation-based optimization is poised to

073   Creating a virtual simulator of a physical mobile network for performance evaluation is a crucial
074   research area in networking. Network simulators like NS-3 (Henderson et al., 2008), OPNET (Chang,
075   1999), Matlab (Tariq et al., 2018), and OMNet++ (Köpke et al., 2008) utilize discrete-event-driven
076   simulations to mimic the communication behaviors of network elements and assess performance
077   metrics such as throughput, latency, and data rate. However, these simulators are significantly less
078   efficient (Hui et al., 2022); their simulation speed is much slower than real-world networks. This
079   inefficiency hinders their ability to meet the demands of interactions with reinforcement learning-
080   based optimizers. Hence, there is a pressing need to develop a new virtual simulation technique that
081
      can quickly, efficiently, and accurately simulate the dynamic behavior and performance of networks.
082   In this paper, we propose GenNet, a generative AI-driven mobile network simulator designed for
083   multi-objective network optimization with reinforcement learning (RL). This simulator creates a vir-
084   tual replica of each physical entity within a mobile network, including mobile users, base stations, and
085   wireless environments. To effectively simulate the behaviors of these network components, GenNet
086   leverages real-world data from mobile networks and employs generative AI methods such as Gener-
087
      ative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Cai et al., 2021), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Cemgil
      et al., 2020), and diffusion models (Croitoru et al., 2023). Unlike traditional, inefficient discrete-
088
      event-driven network simulations, generative AI models in GenNet are trained on extensive datasets
089
      from real-world mobile networks. This training enables the models to learn the distributional char-
090   acteristics of network data under various environmental conditions. This approach facilitates an
091   accurate mapping of environmental factors to network components’ behavioral and performance data,
092   resulting in an efficient, AI-driven simulation process.
093
      Importantly, GenNet features a user-friendly Application Programming Interface (API) tailored
094
      explicitly for RL environments, making it an ideal tool for RL-based optimizations. As an interactive
095
      simulator, GenNet enables researchers to adjust network settings such as mechanical tilt, electrical
096   tilt, azimuth, and maximum transmitting power of base stations and generate corresponding network
097   performance data. Notably, compared to single-objective optimization, which focuses on finding
098   the best solution for a single metric, multi-objective optimization seeks to balance different goals.
099   Multi-objective optimization is essential in mobile networks, where trade-offs must be made between
100   competing factors such as throughput, coverage, energy efficiency, and signal interference. Thus, to
101   facilitate the development and testing of multi-objective optimization RL algorithms in mobile net-
102   work scenarios, we conduct comprehensive experiments to benchmark multi-objective optimizations
103   in mobile networks. GenNet provides a standardized environment for multi-objective optimization,
104
      focusing on optimizing network coverage, throughput, and energy consumption. We introduce
      RL-based network optimization baselines, a collection of reliable and efficient implementations of
105
      state-of-the-art algorithms designed to provide a solid foundation for advancing large-scale mobile
106
      network optimization. Notably, all these algorithms are inherently compatible with GenNet.

125   2.1       S IMULATORS FOR M OBILE N ETWORK
126
127   Among the available mobile network simulators, Matlab (Tariq et al., 2018) is a highly advanced
128   link-level simulator that offers a flexible frame structure and various resource scheduling techniques.
129   However, unlike GenNet, Matlab is not a system-level simulator and lacks features for realistic
130   mobility and traffic usage modeling for mobile users. Since Matlab does not model user mobility, it
131   cannot simulate handover mechanisms. Also, Matlab-based simulators face integration challenges
132   with Python-based reinforcement learning optimizers due to limited platform language support.
133   Other popular discrete-event mobile network simulators, including NS-3 (Henderson et al., 2008),
134   OMNeT++ (Köpke et al., 2008), and OPNET (Chang, 1999), deliver accurate packet-level results
135   through their comprehensive protocol stack implementations. However, due to the absence of user
136   mobility modeling, NS-3 and OPNET are better suited for core network modeling than mobile access
137   networks, where key performance indicators like coverage and capacity are essential. Although
138   OMNeT++ can integrate predefined user trajectories to model handover mechanisms, the complex and
139
      resource-intensive protocol stack simulations make NS-3, OMNeT++, and OPNET computationally
      demanding, limiting their ability to model large-scale networks with hundreds of elements realistically.
140
      Furthermore, they lack APIs for reinforcement learning environments, which restricts their utility
141
      in advanced RL optimization scenarios. A recent simulator, SyntheticNET (Zaidi et al., 2020),
142   integrates the advantages of rule-based simulators like Matlab and event-triggered simulators like
143   NS-3, OMNeT++, and OPNET. It uses rule-based methods for link-level flexible frame structure
144   simulation and protocol stack implementation for fine-grained packet-level scheduling. SyntheticNET
145   also leverages SUMO to provide mobile users with realistic mobility. However, SyntheticNET has a
146   much higher computational cost due to its integration complexity.
147
      Besides rule-based and event-triggered simulation methods, simulations also use data-driven ap-
148   proaches. For example, CityFlow (Zhang et al., 2019) and MATSim (W Axhausen et al., 2016)
149   use black-box neural network models to simulate realistic user mobility in a city. One significant
150   advantage of data-driven methods is reduced simulation time. Inspired by this, we designed GenNet,
151   which leverages generative AI methods to simulate mobile networks. Unlike traditional black-box
152   structures, GenNet applies a grey-box approach, decomposing the mobile network into different
153   critical elements. GenNet uses a white-box rule-based method for the connections between elements,
154   while each entity’s behavior is simulated using black-box data-driven methods. Benefiting from
155   parallelism, GenNet also excels in simulation time. However, we acknowledge that GenNet has
156
      not yet implemented a comprehensive protocol stack, which limits the interpretability of simulation
      results for internal network elements.
157

      2.2    L EARNING - BASED M OBILE N ETWORK O PTIMIZATION
171
172
      The application of reinforcement learning (RL) to adaptively configure operational parameters in
173   mobile networks has recently garnered significant research interest. Since the pioneering studies
174   referenced in (Mnih et al., 2015) and (Silver et al., 2016), deep reinforcement learning (DRL)
175   has become a crucial area within machine learning and artificial intelligence. Algorithms such
176   as Q-learning (Watkins & Dayan, 1992), actor-critic methods (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999), and
177   policy gradients (Peters & Schaal, 2008) have proven effective in mastering complex tasks in high-
178   dimensional spaces solely through reward feedback. These algorithms have been successfully applied
179   to a wide array of mobile network optimization challenges, including energy optimization (El Amine
180   et al., 2022; Mondal et al., 2021), resource allocation (Huang et al., 2023; Naderializadeh et al., 2021),
181
      mobility management (Alsuhli et al., 2021; Marí-Altozano et al., 2021), and power control (Meng
      et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020). Despite the potential of learning-based methods to develop network
182
      optimization policies, benchmarking these policies on large-scale simulators remains challenging. To
183
      support robust and efficient research in mobile network optimization, GenNet provides standardized
184   training and evaluation workflows and reliable benchmarking for both single- and multi-objective
185   optimization tasks. Additionally, we offer implementations of representative RL baseline algorithms
186   and document their performance using a standard set of metrics on GenNet for reference.

      such as base station transmit power, resource allocation schemes, and antenna settings (azimuth

289   for a reinforcement learning environment, including state space, action space, environment reset,

291   ensuring consistency and functionality across different scenarios. The detailed instructions for the

293   “Env.reset (self)”: resetting the environment to an initial state and returns the initial observation.
294
295   “Env.step (self, action, start, total, interval)”: accepting the agent’s action as input and returns the
296

297
      the simulator will execute, and “interval” signifies the time interval for each step.

299   “Env.render (self)”: computing the rendering frames specified during the environment’s initialization.

336   4.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
337
      We focus on optimizing antenna angles, beamwidth, and power settings to enhance the performance
338
      of a communication network within the Guomao area. We consider three key network performance
339
      indicators: network throughput, coverage rate, and energy consumption. Specifically, we aim to
340   maximize the effective coverage rate and user data throughput while minimizing energy consumption.
341
342   Network optimization presents a significant challenge, as no single solution can perfectly optimize
343
      all objectives simultaneously. For instance, improving the coverage rate (CR) and throughput (TP)
      may result in increased energy consumption (EC) and operational costs. Balancing these conflicting
344
      goals is critical in multi-objective network optimization, which involves a variety of parameters such
345
      as antenna angles, transmission power, and beamwidth. The goal is to identify a set of Pareto-optimal
346   solutions, where any improvement in one objective would cause deterioration in at least one other
347   objective. These solutions form what is known as the Pareto front Ngatchou et al. (2005). The
348   multi-objective optimization problem can be mathematically expressed as follows:

376
377   Given that the adjustment of antenna angles is limited to a range specific to each optimization step,
      the decision-making process for the next step is based on the actions of the base stations and the state

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      of the environment at the previous step. This problem can be effectively modeled as a Multi-Objective
379   Markov Decision Process (MOMDP). A Multi-Objective Markov Decision Process is defined by
380   the tuple ⟨S, A, P, R, γ, D⟩, where S is the state space. A is the action space. P (s′ |s, a) is the state
381   transition probability, representing the probability of transitioning from state s to state s′ given action
382   a. R = [r1 , ..., rm ]T is the vector of reward functions, where each ri : S × A → R corresponds to
383   a different objective. γ = [γ1 , ..., γm ]T ∈ [0, 1]m is the vector of discount factors. D is the initial
384   state distribution. m is the number of objectives. In the context of mobile network optimization, the
385   MOMDP is defined as follows. State Space (S) includes all possible configurations of the network,
386   such as the current positions of users, signal strengths, current antenna angles, base station power,
387
      and other relevant environmental factors. Action Space (A) consists of all possible adjustments
      to the antenna angles and base station power. Reward Functions (R) includes multiple reward
388
      functions corresponding to the performance metrics we aim to optimize. Discount Factors (γ) reflect
389
      the importance of future rewards compared to immediate rewards for each objective. Initial State
390   Distribution (D) reflects the starting conditions of the network before any optimization steps have
391   been taken. By modeling the network optimization problem as a MOMDP, we can leverage multi-
392   objective reinforcement learning (MORL) techniques to find Pareto-optimal policies that provide the
393   best trade-offs among the various conflicting objectives.

711   Below are detailed descriptions of the three major generative methods in our simulator GenNet.
712   ActSTD (Yuan et al., 2022): ActSTD simulates user movements between points of interest using a
713   Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning framework. This method captures complex spatiotemporal
714   dynamics by modeling activity trajectories as point processes. To handle irregularly sampled activities,
715   ActSTD leverages neural differential equations to generate activities sequentially based on learned
716   dynamics.
717   MSH-GAN (Li et al., 2024): MSH-GAN simulates mobile users’ traffic usage with generative
718   adversarial networks. It models micro-scale behavior patterns using BiLSTM networks and self-
719   attention mechanisms, bridging these patterns to macro-scale user clusters through switch mode
720   generators. MSH-GAN also captures multi-scale temporal dynamics with Temporal Convolutional
721   Networks (TCNs).
722
      PEFNet (Jiang et al., 2024): PEFNet simulates wireless propagation in our simulator. It combines
723   knowledge-driven and data-driven approaches for path loss estimation in wireless transmission.
724   PEFNet integrates Computational Electromagnetic (CEM) methods with neural networks to enhance
725   realism, efficiency, and generalization, making it the first approach to merge these methodologies for
726   this task.

      During the training process for ActSTD, we collaborated with a major mobile network operator in
728
      China, which provided us with a dataset comprising 10,000 users in Beijing over the course of one
729   month. This dataset includes the following categories of points of interest: Company, Concerts,
730   Culture and Art, Education, Entertainment, Food, Government, Life Services, Market, Medicine,
731   School, Shops, Sports, Travel, and University. The dataset records anonymous user IDs, timestamps,
732   POI types, and latitude-longitude coordinates.

      For training MSH-GAN, we again partnered with the operator to collect traffic usage data from
734
      6,055 users over one week, specifically from April 20th to April 26th, 2016, in Shanghai. Traffic
735   usage records were collected every half hour, with each record containing the anonymous user ID,
736   timestamp, and traffic volume.

      To train PEFNet, we utilized an open-access dataset called RSRPSet, measured by Huawei Technolo-

759   A.3     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING F RAMEWORK
760
761   We developed our RL interface using the Gym framework, which is widely recognized for its
762   simplicity and effectiveness in designing and evaluating RL algorithms. Our environment simulates a
763   wireless network scenario where agents (base stations) interact with the environment (network users
764   and physical terrain) to optimize key performance metrics.
765
766   A.3.1    E NVIRONMENT S ETUP
767   The environment setup includes several key components:
768
769           • State Space: The state space represents the current configuration of the wireless network,
770             including base station positions, antenna angles, and user distribution.
771           • Action Space: The action space defines the possible adjustments that can be made to the
772             base station parameters, such as changing the transmission power or adjusting the antenna
773             downtilt angle.
774           • Reward Function: The reward function provides feedback to the RL agent based on the
775             performance of the network, with rewards for improved coverage, higher throughput, and
776             lower energy consumption.
777
778   A.3.2    S TATE S PACE R EPRESENTATION
779
      The state space in our environment is represented by a multi-dimensional vector that includes:
780
781           • Base Station Parameters: Transmission power, azimuth angle, downtilt angle, horizontal
782             beamwidth, and vertical beamwidth.
783           • Network Performance Metrics: Current coverage area, throughput, and energy consump-
784             tion.
785           • User Distribution: Location and density of users within the coverage area.
786
787   This comprehensive representation ensures that the RL agent has access to all relevant information
788   needed to make informed decisions.
789
790
      A.3.3    ACTION S PACE D ESIGN
791   The action space is designed to allow the RL agent to make fine-grained adjustments to the base
792   station parameters. Actions are represented as discrete or continuous changes in the following
793   parameters:
794
795
              • Transmission Power: Adjusting the power output of the base station to balance coverage
                and energy consumption.
796
797           • Antenna Azimuth Angle: Rotating the antenna to optimize the direction of the signal beam.
798           • Antenna Downtilt Angle: Adjusting the angle of the antenna to control the vertical spread
799             of the signal.
800           • Horizontal Beamwidth: Modifying the width of the signal beam in the horizontal plane.
801           • Vertical Beamwidth: Modifying the width of the signal beam in the vertical plane.
802
803   A.3.4    R EWARD F UNCTION F ORMULATION
804
805   The reward function is a critical component of the RL interface, guiding the agent towards optimal
806
      configurations. Our reward function is designed to balance multiple objectives:
807                   R = α × Coverage + β × Throughput − γ × Energy Consumption                      (8)
808   where α, β, and γ are weighting factors that determine the relative importance of each metric. This
809   formulation ensures that the agent receives positive rewards for increasing coverage and throughput
      while minimizing energy consumption. Weights can be set by the user.

       A.5.6    R EASONS FOR N ON -C ONVERGENCE IN M ULTI -O BJECTIVE R EINFORCEMENT
1144
                L EARNING (MORL)
1145
1146           • Conflicting Objectives:
1147               – In our multi-object problem, objectives can conflict, making it difficult for the agent to
1148                 optimize all objectives simultaneously.
1149               – This conflict can lead to oscillations in policy updates, preventing convergence.
1150           • High Dimensionality:
1151
                   – The state and action spaces in wireless optimization multi-object problem are high-
1152
                     dimensional, increasing the complexity of the learning problem.
1153
                   – High-dimensional spaces require more data and computation to explore effectively,
1154
                     which can slow down the learning process and hinder convergence.
1155
1156           • Credit Assignment Problem:
1157               – Assigning credit for outcomes to specific actions becomes more challenging with
1158                 multiple objectives, especially when the effects of actions are delayed.
1159               – This problem can lead to suboptimal policy updates, preventing the agent from learning
1160                 the optimal policy.
1161           • Exploration vs. Exploitation Dilemma:
1162
                   – Balancing exploration of the environment and exploitation of known rewards is more
1163                 complex with multiple objectives.
1164
                   – Insufficient exploration can lead to premature convergence to suboptimal policies,
1165                 while excessive exploration can slow down convergence.
1166
               • Algorithmic Limitations:
1167
1168               – Current MORL algorithms may have inherent limitations in handling the trade-offs
1169                 between multiple objectives effectively.
1170               – Improvements in algorithm design and the incorporation of domain knowledge are
1171                 necessary to address these limitations.
