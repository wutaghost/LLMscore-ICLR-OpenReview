011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, there has been an increased interest in foundation models for geo-
014            science due to the vast amount of Earth observing satellite imagery. Existing
015            remote sensing foundation models make use of the various sources of spectral
016
               imagery to create large models pretrained on the task of masked reconstruction.
               In this paper, we present a foundation model framework, where the pretraining
017
               task captures the causal relationship between multiple modalities. Our framework
018
               leverages the knowledge guided principles that the spectral imagery captures the
019            impact of the physical drivers on the environmental system, and that the relation-
020            ship between them is governed by the characteristics of the system. Specifically,
021            our method, called MultiModal Variable Step Forecasting (MM-VSF), uses fore-
022            casting of satellite imagery as a pretraining task and is able to capture the causal
023            relationship between spectral imagery and weather. In our evaluation we show
024            that the forecasting of satellite imagery using weather can be used as an effective
025            pretraining task for foundation models. We further show the effectiveness of the
026            embeddings produced by MM-VSF on the downstream tasks of pixel wise crop
027
               mapping and missing image prediction of spectral imagery, when compared with
               embeddings created by models trained in alternative pretraining settings including
028
               the traditional single modality input masked reconstruction.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Increased availability and ease of access to large scale satellite data has motivated the development
      of deep learning models that use this data to perform tasks such as land cover mapping Ghosh et al.
034
      (2021b); Kussul et al. (2017), wildfire mapping Nayak et al. (2018); Seydi et al. (2022); Zhao et al.
035
      (2018), crop yield prediction Kuwata et al. (2015); You et al. (2017), flood forecasting Bentivoglio
036   et al. (2022) etc. In recent times, methods have been developed to use large amounts of data in a
037   self supervised fashion to pre-train their model weights using a pre-training task, such as masked
038   reconstruction He et al. (2022), which would then be fine-tuned for downstream tasks. Such models,
039   called foundation models, have been shown to perform well (after refinement) over various down-
040   stream tasks, in the image Yuan et al. (2021); Singh et al. (2022) and text domains Touvron et al.
041   (2023) (e.g. large language model such as GPT Achiam et al. (2023)).
042   Motivated by the success of such models, there is a huge interest in building geo-science foundation
043   models for remote sensing applications Mai et al. (2022). Current remote sensing foundation models
044   are typically built using the vast amounts of spectral data available from various satellites Jakubik
045   et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2022); Cha et al. (2023); Mendieta et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024). These
046   models use a pretraining task such as masked reconstruction Cong et al. (2022); Tseng et al. (2023)
047   where given a sequence of satellite imagery, the objective is to reconstruct the spectral imagery of the
048   masked timestamps in the sequence. Utility of finetuning such models have been demonstrated for
049   downstream tasks such as flood inundation mapping, wildfire scar mapping, cloud removal, urban
050
      semantic segmentation mapping, scene classification etc. Jakubik et al. (2023); Cong et al. (2022);
      Sun et al. (2022).
051
052   In this paper, we present a foundation model, where the pretraining task captures the causal rela-
053   tion between two different modalities. To understand the reason behind choosing such a pretraining
      task, let us look at Figure 1, which represents an environment as a system, where various physical

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110   There are a number of existing geoscience foundation modelsJakubik et al. (2023); Cha et al. (2023);
111
      Mall et al. (2023); Deng et al. (2024); Hong et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024); Mai et al. (2022); Bastani
      et al. (2023). Most of these foundation models can be placed into one of two groups based on the data
112
      they use : (1) weather-climate Nguyen et al. (2023); Pathak et al. (2022) that are typically used for
113
      weather forecasting or climate modeling and (2) spectral data from remote sensing satellites Cong
114   et al. (2022); Jakubik et al. (2023) that are largely used for identifying land-use land-cover change
115   dynamics. The most common pretraining task in geoscience has been reconstruction of spectral
116   imagery. To enrich the embedding created, varying amounts of the input spectral image are masked,
117   making the reconstruction task of the entire image harder leading to better embeddings Cong et al.
118   (2022). However, simple reconstruction embeddings capture just that particular image and might not
119   be suited for downstream tasks that rely on multi temporal contexts such as crop mapping or land
120   cover land use change. To solve this, previous works included multiple timestamps in their input,
121   however some of these methods stacked these images together Jakubik et al. (2023), thus removing
122
      the temporal aspect. However, some methods added a timestamp positional embedding so that the
      model has a sense of time Khanna et al. (2023); Cong et al. (2022). This led to moderate success in
123
      handling downstream tasks that require multi temporal contexts. Another common pretraining task
124
      is forecasting of imagery. Typically, this pretraining task has been used in weather related foundation
125   models and not in spectral imagery based foundation models. Foundation models created using
126   masked forecasting has shown great success in weather related downstream tasks Schmude et al.
127   (2024); Pathak et al. (2022). To enrich the embeddings created, these works add variable future
128   time forecasting, i.e vary the amount of time into the future the model needs to forecast, which
129   was achieved by including an embedding for delta time Nguyen et al. (2023). Other variants of
130   foundation models include diffusion models that incorporate more information such as geographic
131   location, time of year, country etc Khanna et al. (2023).

133   3   A RCHITECTURE
134
135
      Our architecture follows a heavy encoder and lightweight decoder format. Keeping our decoder
      lightweight forces richer embeddings from encoder, suitable for pretraining and downstream tasks.
136
      Unlike previous models, we incorporate multiple modalities (spectral imagery and weather) in pre-
137
      training our architecture. Figure 3 shows MM-VSF’s architecture. For the forecasting-based pre-
138   training task, it is essential for architecture design to capture spatial and temporal modalities of
139   satellite data, temporal weather data, and their interactions.
140
141
      3.1 S ATELLITE IMAGE E NCODER /D ECODER
142
      We use a shared Vision Transformer (ViT) to extract spatial features from spectral imagery across
143
      timestamps. ViT have been shown to be effective in the presence of high masking He et al. (2022),
144   even in geoscience contexts Jakubik et al. (2023). The ViT converts each image into a patch grid of
145   embeddings, incorporating patch positional information. This results in a series of spectral image
146   embeddings on unmasked patches for each timestamp. Since our input is an image series, we pro-
147   pose using a shared ViT across timestamps, leading to a robust encoder, that is capable of embedding
148   images from all timestamps.
149
150   3.2 W EATHER E NCODER
151   Due to the coarse spatial resolution of weather data, typically for each image location, we have one
152   weather data point value per timestamp. Thus we use a sequence-to-sequence Bidirectional LSTM
153   (BiLSTM) to encode the weather data. The Bi-LSTM based approach showed higher accuracy over
154   the transformer based approaches when trying to solely reconstruct masked weather. The BiLSTM
155   generates weather embeddings for each timestamp, which are then subsampled to match input image
156   timestamps, similar to WSTATT Ravirathinam et al. (2024), called temporal embedding matching.
157
158   3.3 T IMESTAMP /D ELTA E NCODER
159   For temporal information we incorporate day of year (DOY) using a shared linear layer with tanh
160   activation, creating DOY embeddings for each timestamp. Additionally, we create another series
161   that corresponds the number of days in between the images, i.e the delta in timestamps. We generate
      embeddings for these time delta between images using a separate linear layer. The DOY embeddings

187   3.4 M ULTIMODAL S EQUENCE E NCODER
188   From the previous steps we have spatial, weather, and day-of-year embedding series. Since all these
189   series are of the same length, we add them all along the temporal dimension to create the multimodal
190   embedding series. We use a transformer with forward-only attention to extract the spatio-temporal
191   information from this multimodal embedding series, analyzing embedding patches from the same
192   spatial location across timestamps. Further we add forward only attention (causal) in transformer,
193
      i.e the temporal embeddings created are not bidirectional in nature. This feature makes sure there
      is no information leakage from future timestamp information to previous timestamps embeddings.
194
      The resulting embedding series EmbST W = [Embt:t11 , . . . , Embt:tCC ] can be used for downstream
195
      tasks, with flexibility in embedding selection. Depending on the task, one can choose to use all the
196   embeddings or choose to use only the final embedding.

      3.5 F ORECASTER
199
      Since our embeddings are constructed using forward-only attention, we use each of the embeddings
200   in EmbST W to forecast an image in its respective future. For each embedding Embt:tii , the forecaster
201                                        t                                                 t
      uses the weather embeddings Embwj as well as the the temporal embeddings Embtj and the delta
202                                                    tj
      embeddings to generate the embeddings Emb of the forecast timestamp tj . By incorporating
203
      weather data leading up to a particular date, we can make more informed estimates about how
204   the land cover might appear, as weather patterns play a significant role in shaping the landscape
205   over time. We combine the four embeddings through addition and feed it to a series of linear and
206   activation layers that forms our forecaster. The objective of these layers is to morph the embeddings
207   from current timestamp to the future timestamp before passing them to the MLP decoder to get the
208   satellite image forecast.
209
      For all but last embeddings in the series EmbST W , we forecast the next time-step, i.e t1 of the
210   input series would be used to forecast the image at t2, t2 would be used to forecast t3 and so on.
211   For the last embedding Embt:tCC we forecast the K’th time-step, which is K-C days into the future.
212   This K is a variable and is sampled for each training instance in a batch, thus the name variable-step
213   forecasting (VSF). This method enables the model to predict future images based on current image
214   embeddings and weather information up to the forecast date, acknowledging weather’s impact on
215   land cover. Moreover, such multiple forecasting allows for a robust decoder, as similar to encoder,
      the decoder also has shared weights across timestamps.

      3.6 D ECODER
217
      Following this stage, we repopulate these embeddings in their respective unmasked positions in
218
      the timestamps and zero out the masked patches to pass to the decoder, as done by most methods
219
      in transformer based autoencoder methodsJakubik et al. (2023); He et al. (2022). We use a light-
220   weight MLP decoder (similar to the one used in ViT) to ensure that the main focus of the model is to
221   create strong encoder to capture the best information so that even a lightweight decoder can perform
222   the pretraining task required. Similar, to the encoder, we have a shared decoder that performs the
223   operations on each timestamp using the same weights. The decoder maps the embeddings to the
224   spectral image space and reshape the output to match the required size of the spatiotemporal stack.

227   4   P RETRAINING
228   As mentioned before, our proposed pretraining task is forecasting of satellite imagery using mul-
229   timodal data in a knowledge informed fashion, a pretraining task that varies significantly from the
230   traditional single modality reconstruction. Our proposed approach can be trained using the archi-
231   tecture described above in a direct shot, i.e initialise and update all layers at once. However, this
232
      may not be the best way to ensure that the EmbST W embeddings would contain information about
      the relationship between modalities, as that is the ultimate aim of our proposed approach. To ensure
233
      this information capture, we propose a phase wise pretraining process. Specifically we propose 2
234
      phases of pretraining, where the first phase focuses on getting information from each modality and
235   the second phase focuses on encoding the relationship between the modalities in the embedding.
236
237   Phase 1: Masked Reconstruction As can be observed, there are many components to our ar-
238
      chitecture. However, if you break it down, each component serves a primary purpose. The VIT
      encoder/decoder serves the purpose of bringing an image to and out of embedding space. This
239
      component can be separately trained via masked reconstruction of satellite imagery. Similarly, the
240
      Weather encoder brings the weather data to embedding space. This encoder can also be constructed
241   via a masked autoencoder approach using a Bidirectional LSTM. These steps result in models that
242   give embeddings for both the spectral imagery and weather components. We can now add the mul-
243   timodal sequence encoder and train it using the spectral imagery model with the overall objective
244   to reconstruct the entire image series, but instead of masking images, we can mask embeddings,
245   to make the sequence encoder stronger. After these steps, we have a baseline for the EmbST W
246   embedding series(through addition). Though this embedding series contains spatial and multitem-
247   poral information about the input series, it would not capture the relationship between modalities.
248   To prove this, this embedding series would be used a baseline for finetuning across our downstream
249
      tasks. To infuse multimodal relationship, we move to Phase 2, adding a forecaster.
250   Phase 2: Forecasting From the architecture section, we saw that the forecaster was a set of lin-
251   ear layers, making it sound simple. However its purpose is to translate the embedding from input
252   timestamp space to the forecasted timestamp space. Now, to perform this translation within a few
253   layers is very difficult, so the model will search for ways to learn this relationship from other parts
254   of the overall architecture. The solution would come from the multimodal data, in our case weather
255
      data. Using weather data, the current embedding, timestamp embedding and delta embedding, the
      forecaster is tasked to create the future embedding, but due to less amount of layers, the embeddings
256
      of all the components will all change to best capture all information from each modality in regard
257
      to forecasting, thus capturing the relationship across modalities. For example, if there was a lot of
258   rain the lakes would be fuller, if there was a lot of sunlight then growth of crop/vegetation would
259   accelerate. As a result, this scheme would infuse land growth/change dynamics into the embeddings
260   that the model creates. Asking the model to do this without weather data would be very challenging
261   as it would have no relationship to learn, tasking the forecaster with a big challenge. This no weather
262   variant of embeddings is also added a baseline for downstream tasks.
263
      To summarise, our proposed pretraining task is to predict a spectral image in the future (response)
264   using a series of spectral images in the past (context) along with the weather till the future date
265   (query) we want to predict. We call this pretraining task as Variable step Forecasting (V SF ), where
266   the model is expected to forecast k steps into the future. To ensure that relationship across modaliteis
267   is captured in the embeddings, we adopt a two phase pretraining process. Our hypothesis is that this
268   extra knowledge infusion would help greatly in downstream tasks that rely on land growth and
269   change dynamics such as crop prediction, land cover land use change, etc. A schematic of the stages
      of pretraining can be found in appendix A.1. We mask out patches in the spectral imagery and

      weather data in all the stages to make the prediction harder and lead to better embeddings. We will
271   describe our masking strategy for pretraining in a future section(Sec 5.2).

      5.1 DATA S OURCES
276
      Our spectral imagery data comes from Sentinel imagery Drusch et al. (2012) and our weather data
277
      is from ERA5 land analysis data Hersbach et al. (2020). We chose these two sources due to their
278   temporal resolutions and more importantly their availability globally from 2021. Due to these fac-
279   tors, we randomly sampled around 10000 locations across land areas globally. Each location is of
280   size 128x128 Sentinel pixels and for spectral imagery we collected all images for that region in that
281   year. Due to missing data and improper coverage of some regions, the number of Sentinel samples
282   from each region would vary. For example, regions in well covered regions such as US, might have
283   upto 70 image instances in a year for that region, whereas regions like India (which is not as well
284   covered) would have 40 image instances. For each instance, we collect six bands namely (B2, B3,
285   B4, B8, B9, B12), which have shown to be the most useful in land cover related tasks and have been
286
      used in other works Jakubik et al. (2023). For each image instance, we also collect the day of the
      year it came from, thus forming a series with values from 1 to 365 and a length the same as the
287
      number of image instances for that location. Though ERA5 data source consists of various bands
288
      that are useful for land cover related tasks we chose 5 bands to keep it simple and efficient, namely
289   (temperature 2m min, temperature 2m max, total precipitation sum, u component of wind 10m, v
290   component of wind 10m). ERA5 data is available at a daily temporal resolution and a spatial resolu-
291   tion of 11k meters. Given the coarse spatial resolution compared to Sentinel, for most locations in
292   our analysis we get only one value band set per timestamp, thus making our weather data of length
293   365 with 5 values per timestamp.
294
      To summarise, for each location our data comprises of 3 main components:
295   • Spectral Imagery Series: A series of Sentinel2 Imagery each of 6 bands and of shape 128x128.
296   Length of this series depends on coverage of the location.
297   • Weather Data Series: A series of ERA5 Land data of 5 bands and of shape 1x1, with a series
298   length of 365 (one per day)
299   • Day of Year Series: A series of the day of the year number for each spectral image in the series.
300   The length of this series is same as the spectral imagery series.

414   7.2.1 DATASET AND R EGION OF A NALYSIS
415   Our data for finetuning comes from Sentinel2 and ERA5 land data for the region of the T11SKA
416   Sentinel tile in the California Central Valley, a region rich in various crop classes and has been used
417   for crop type mapping in various other worksGhosh et al. (2021b;a); Ravirathinam et al. (2024) from
418   the years 2018 and 2019. Like other works, we get our labels for this region from the Cropland Data
419   Layer(CDL), an annually released land cover map for the entire continuous US by the USDA. A
420   diagrammatic representation of the CDL labels and the geographic location of the T11SKA tile can
421
      be seen in Figure 9 in the appendix. Similar to WSTATT Ravirathinam et al. (2024), we adopt a
      grid based training method, by splitting the entire region into train, validation and test grids and also
422
      follow their preprocessing steps including combination and erosion.

      7.2.2 C ROP MAPPING A RCHITECTURE AND I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
425
      To perform crop mapping we would need an architecture that gives us a pixel wise output, in par-
426   ticular, we would need a decoder that takes the embedding series given by the foundation model
427   encoder, i.e EmbST W , using it to construct a pixel wise classification map. To map this embedding
428   series to a pixel wise map, we follow an attention based approach, similar to WSTATTRavirathinam
429   et al. (2024). This strategy assigns a weightage to each timestamp and does an aggregated sum
430   to form a multitemporal attention-based embedding. This multitemporal embedding is then acted
431   on by a series of upscaling and convolution layers with activation, and an output Linear layer to
      form a pixel wise map. Figure 10 in appendix shows a schematic of the architecture used for

432
      crop mapping. For our input series, we chose to have 10 spectral images, biweekly from May
433   to Sept, and while finetuning for crop mapping there is no masking of spectral imagery done.
434   Note that the number of timestamps passed in the downstream task is different from the num-
435   ber passed during pretraining. This highlights the temporal flexibility of our approach. In all
436   methods(MM-VSF, SM-MAE, etc.) the encoder weights to obtain the embedding series are fixed
437   from the respective pretraining task and the only the attention mechanism and decoder layers are
438   finetuned. All finetuning is done with only 2018 data with respective best hyperparameter settings.

463   7.3 D OWNSTREAM TASK : M ISSING I MAGE P REDICTION
464   Here we evaluate the performance of our proposed method (MM-VSF) in the missing image predic-
465   tion downstream task, i.e estimating the spectral values for missing pixels in imagery. Like the crop
466   mapping task, we compare our results with SM-VSF, MM-MAE, and SM-MAE.
467
468   7.3.1 DATASET AND P ROBLEM S ETTING
469   With the vast amount of satellite imagery being captured on a daily basis, corrupted or missing data
470   is an occurring phenomenon. In some cases, the areas of missing/corrupted data is provided as a
471   mask, but in other cases this mask is not provided, making it hard clean or filter data, thus leaving
472   researchers to use these corrupted images in their work. In this downstream task, we aim to fill these
473   corrupted/missing values in the absence of these masks, i.e the methods have no access/information
474
      as to which pixels are corrupted, making it a very real world downstream task. To solve this task,
      we aim to use the spatiotemporal spectral imagery series along with the image with the missing data
475
      to predict the missing values. For our dataset, we chose to sample a new 1000 locations globally,
476
      similar to how our base dataset was created. We then did a 60-20-20 training-validation-test split
477   and used patch series from the 600 patches for finetuning. We ensured that there is no overlap in
478   regions across the 1000 newly sampled patches.
479
480   7.3.2 A RCHITECTURE AND I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
481   In our downstream task methodology, we would pass a series of input images with one or more
482   timestamps having missing data, and we would ask our architecture to reconstruct the entire series,
483   but take only the mean squared loss on the timestamps with missing values, using those loss values
484   for backpropagation. Since our output is a series of images, we can use the same architecture as the
485   pretraining model. However, we freeze the encoder and reinitialise the decoder weights to random
      values and update only these layers. We chose a input series length of 6, and varied the amount of


                                                        9

486
      missing values in input to create many finetuned models, each of whose results are depicted in the
487   next section. To simulate missing data, we would zero out large blocks from the image and pass
488   an input series with the blacked out images to the model. For our study, we chose to blacken out 2
489   images per series, both with a particular percentage of missing values.

529   8 C ONCLUSION
530   In this paper we proposed a novel multimodal spatiotmeporal foundation model, MM-VSF, that
531
      uses input of satellite and weather data and a knowledge guided pretraining task of variable step
      forecasting to capture the causal relationship between the two modalities. This leads to superior
532
      embeddings when compared with embeddings achieved by models using single modality input and
533
      trained with standard pretraining task of reconstruction. Our pretraining task evaluation of MM-
534   VSF’s forecasting ability showed that our foundation model is able to learn aspects that go beyond
535   temporal autocorrelation. We showed that MM-VSF can be finetuned for a crop mapping model
536   that is generalisable across years and also can create a good model for missing image prediction.
537   Our model is temporally flexible and can adapt to various geoscience downstream tasks that include
538   spatiotemporal remote-sensing data. To the best of our knowledge, our study is the first step towards
539   incorporating knowledge guided principles in pretraining tasks and adapting multimodal approaches
      to improve embeddings.
