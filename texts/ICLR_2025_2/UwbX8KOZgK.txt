009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training large vision-language models requires extensive, detailed image-text
012
               pairs. Existing web-scraped datasets, however, are noisy and lack detailed image
013            descriptions. To bridge this gap, we introduce PixelProse, a comprehensive dataset
014            of over 16M (million) synthetically generated captions, leveraging cutting-edge
015            vision-language models for detailed and accurate descriptions. To ensure data
016            integrity, we rigorously analyze our dataset for problematic content, including
017            child sexual abuse material (CSAM), personally identifiable information (PII),
018            and toxicity. We also provide valuable metadata such as watermark presence and
019            aesthetic scores, aiding in further dataset filtering. We hope PixelProse will be a
020            valuable resource for future vision-language research. PixelProse will be made
021
               available publicly.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Early vision-language models were trained on datasets of images from the web, each labeled with the
027   alt-text embedded in the surrounding HTML. These datasets enabled model training at large scales
028   for numerous applications. However, as models advanced and the machine learning community
029   moved, these datasets have begun to outlive their usefulness. The problems with these datasets
030   stem from the fact that alt-texts are not truly captions. They often contain little to no information
031   about the content of the image, and factors like background objects and fine-grained details are often
032   absent. As a result, commercial models that are trained on purpose-labeled and carefully curated
033   datasets have far surpassed the open source state of the art for both image generation and analysis.
034
      Overall, trending research in the community has shown that dataset quality, not dataset size, has
      become the bottleneck for open-source development. This motivates the need for new datasets that
035
      are labeled with deliberately constructed captions rather than incidental alt-texts. At the same time,
036
      the emergence of generative LLMs enables fast manipulation and reformatting of text labels. This
037   raises the value of dense image labels containing many categories of detailed information, as one
038   dataset can be refactored for many uses including vision captioning and question-answering (VQA).
039
040
      PixelProse is a dataset that addresses the weaknesses of existing alt-text datasets for vision-language
      applications and is designed to be used as either a standalone asset or in combination with LLM
041
      refactoring. It contains detailed captions that are long, detailed, and cover a range of image properties
042
      that are important for Vision-Language Model (VLM) and diffusion model training, as depicted
043   in Figure 1. Rather than target only one specific application (e.g., VQA), PixelProse captions are
044   intended to be general purpose image descriptions that contain large amounts of image data in dense
045   prose form. These captions can be used for pre-training tasks, image captioning, or they can be
046   refactored into other data formats (e.g., VQA, instructions, etc.) using an LLM.

      2.1     I MAGE S OURCES
084
085
      PixelProse comprises over 16M diverse images sourced from three different web-scraped databases,
086   which are discussed below:
087
088   CommonPool (Gadre et al., 2024) contains a large pool of image-text pairs from CommonCrawl,
089
      which is distributed as a list of url-text pairs under a CC-BY-4.0 License. We filter the dataset using
      cld31 to detect English-only text and select image-text pairs with a CLIP-L/14 similarity score above
090
      0.3. This filtering scheme is the same as LAION-2B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), and is supported
091
      through the metadata provided with the dataset. From our filtered subset, we recaption over 6.2M
092   samples.
093
094   CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) comprises 12.4M web-crawled images and alt-text pairs. The
095
      dataset is curated using both image and text-based filters. From this dataset, we recaption over 9.1M
      samples.
096
097   RedCaps (Desai et al., 2021) is curated from Reddit. It consists of 12M image-text pairs from
098   350 different subreddits, which are filtered to select general photographs and minimize the number
099   of people (such as celebrity images). The images are fairly high quality, while captions are non-
100   descriptive. From this dataset, we sample and recaption nearly 1.2M samples.
101   Our goal in choosing data sources is to achieve a wide range of image properties and quality/aesthetic
102   rankings. The CommonPool data is less strictly curated than other sources, contributing lower quality
103   images, (which are important for VLM training) and high diversity. Also, it is collected more recently
104   and contributes more current information about celebrities and locations. The CC12M dataset features
105   higher image quality and is subject to stricter curation. The RedCaps images are the most strictly
106   curated by humans and are of very high quality and artistic value on average.

130   2.2     T EXT C APTIONING
131
      We aim to generate detailed image descriptions containing types, attributes, and counts of objects in
132
      an image, in addition to spatial relations between objects, the presence of text, various broad image
133   categorizations, etc.
134
135   Prompting Strategy. We use five unique prompts to diversify the generated captions. Each asks for
136
      descriptions with various attributes. These prompts are provided in A.1. We showcase one of the
      prompts used below.
137
138
139   Describe every component of this image, as it were described by an artist in at
140   most two paragraphs. Each object, with its count, positions, and attributes
141
      should be described. Describe the text, and the font in detail with its
      contents in quotation marks. For example if the image has text Happy Birthday,
142   write it down as "Happy Birthday". Include the style of the image for example
143   photograph, 3d-render, shopping website etc. Capture the aesthetics of the
144   image, as if described by an artist. Start with the words ‘This image
145   displays:’
146
147   In addition to selecting one of the five prompts, we randomly also add a reference to the original
148   alt-text pair within the prompt. Prior work (Yu et al., 2024) has found this strategy helps improve
149   descriptive accuracy when alt-texts contain useful information, particularly proper nouns (e.g. “Taj
150   Mahal” instead of “White Marble Mausoleum”).
151
      Negative Descriptions. Despite their impressive capabilities, both text-to-image diffusion models
152   and VLMs exhibit weaknesses in understanding negative instructions. For example, telling a diffusion
153   model to create an image with “no elephant” is likely to create an image with an elephant, while asking
154   a VLM about an elephant when there is none is likely to produce a hallucination. Such poor behaviors
155   probably arise in part because online image captions seldom deliberately reference absent objects.
156
      To foster a better language understanding of negative references, we also prompt Gemini to describe
157
      absent objects for a subset of images. We manually verify that prompting helps generate meaningful
158   negative captions, as depicted in Figure 1. Depending on the application, these negatives can easily
159   be filtered out based on the metadata or the final sentences in the generated caption.

178   2.3     E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
179
      A growing body of work discusses potential ethical concerns regarding data scraped from the internet
180
      (Birhane et al., 2024; Birhane & Prabhu, 2021; Gebru et al., 2021). Several large-scale datasets used
181
      for training machine learning systems have come under scrutiny, prompting a reevaluation and in
182   some cases withdrawal of these datasets (Birhane & Prabhu, 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Asano et al.,
183   2021; Thiel, 2023). These datasets have been misused for various applications. For example, text-
184   to-image generative models trained on large-scale datasets can generate NSFW content resembling
185   specific individuals. Birhane et al. (Birhane et al., 2024) found that LAION-2B (Schuhmann et al.,
186   2021) contains hate content, highlighting problems of uncurated large-scale datasets.
187
188   2.3.1    NSFW & CSAM F ILTERING
189
      Recent work has shown that text-to-image models are trained on and can even produce Child Sexual
190
      Abuse Material (CSAM) content (Thiel et al., 2023; Thiel, 2023). In a recent study, LAION-5B
191   (Schuhmann et al., 2022) was found to contain CSAM and subsequently taken down 2 (Thiel, 2023).
192   Addressing CSAM in future datasets requires robust detection mechanisms and better data collection
193   practices 3 . We discuss our approach to removing CSAM, and other NSFW content below.
194
      First, the image sources for our dataset already employ different mechanisms to remove NSFW
195
      content. The CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) dataset was filtered using commercial Google APIs
196
      for detecting pornographic and profane content in both images and alt-text descriptions. RedCaps
197   (Desai et al., 2021) removed any subreddits or posts marked as NSFW (either by authors or subreddit
198   moderators). They further used an open-source NSFW classification model 4 to filter the remaining
199   content. CommonPool (Gadre et al., 2024) uses a modified version of LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al.,
200   2022) CLIP-based NSFW classification model. The classifier was further validated against Google
201   Vision API’s SafeSearch explicit content detector.
202
      To further ensure the safety and integrity of our data, we check our dataset against several commercial
203   APIs. First, we use the PhotoDNA API by Microsoft 5 , which uses perceptual hashing to match
204   against a database of known CSAM content. PhotoDNA is regarded as the industry standard and
205   can detect such content even if the images are slightly altered (Farid, 2021). We specifically process
206   the images we sampled from the CommonPool dataset against the PhotoDNA API, as our other
207   data sources are already processed to filter CSAM using different industrial APIs (Iwatt et al.; goo).
208   Finally, all our data is processed through Google Gemini API (Team et al., 2023) which provides
209   additional safeguards. The API blocks prompts (including images) and responses against certain core
210   harms such as child safety 6 . We found 92 matches against the PhotoDNA database, all of which were

244   2.3.3     T OXICITY
245   Mitigating toxicity in datasets is vital for ethical AI deployment. Previous research (Deshpande
246   et al., 2023; Zhuo et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023; Gehman et al., 2020) highlights that language
247   models are prone to various forms of toxicity, such as hate speech, identity hate, explicit content,
248   insults, and harmful stereotypes. To address these concerns, we conduct a toxicity analysis of our
249   generated captions using Detoxify (Hanu & Unitary team, 2020), which classifies text across a wide
250   range of toxic attributes, from overtly offensive language to subtle passive-aggressive remarks. We
251   subsequently flag 0.13% of captions using a threshold of 0.2 across all attributes.
252   Our analysis in Table 3 shows that the PixelProse captions are safer compared to the original captions.
253   Most of our captions fall within the lowest toxicity range (0-0.2) across various attributes. Specifically,
254   the percentages of captions exhibiting severe toxicity, identity attacks, and threats are exceptionally
255   low, with PixelProse achieving < 0.01% for all three. For overall toxicity, PixelProse captions exhibit
256   a markedly lower percentage of 0.13% compared to 0.75% for the original captions. For this reason,
257   we believe PixelProse is well suited for training generative models with low risk of harmful outputs.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
469
470
471
      Many large-scale image caption datasets such as COYO-700M (Byeon et al., 2022), DataComp (Gadre
      et al., 2024), LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2021), YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016), CC12M (Chang-
472
      pinyo et al., 2021), SBU (Ordonez et al., 2011), RedCaps (Desai et al., 2021) are created from various
473
      internet sources by mapping an image to its corresponding alt-text or the text surrounding the image.
474   Despite their large sizes, the quality of captions for these datasets is quite low.
475
476   Higher quality image caption datasets such as MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014), VizWiz (Gurari et al.,
477
      2020), VisualGenome (Krishna et al., 2017), nocaps (Agrawal et al., 2019), Flickr30K (Young
      et al., 2014), TextCaps (Sidorov et al., 2020) and many others (Pont-Tuset et al., 2020; Kazemzadeh
478
      et al., 2014; Mao et al., 2016) exist, however, they are usually smaller (sub-million) in size. The
479
      LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a; 2023a;b; 2024b) family of models and a series of smaller VLMs (Li
480   et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2024) have shown that it is possible to train a high-performance model with
481   small-scale synthetic data (Chen et al., 2023) from GPT-4(V). PixArt-α (Chen et al., 2024c) trained a
482   higher-quality diffusion model with 25M images, and VLM caption pairs with approximately 1.25%
483   training data volume compared to Stable Diffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022). Stable Diffusion v3
484   (Esser et al., 2024) also uses 50% VLM synthetic captions for training diffusion models. Many other
485   recent works (Zhou et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c; Kondratyuk et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Liu
      et al., 2024a) have also shown that a few million higher quality examples can train better models than


                                                                   9
486
      many million low-quality data. Hence, high-quality datasets are urgently needed to train the next
487   generation of multi-modal models.
488
489   A few attempts are made towards this goal, completely human-annotated, with humans-in-the-loop,
490
      or some completely automated. DOCCI (Onoe et al., 2024) is a small high-detailed image caption
      dataset that is completely human-annotated. Despite having only 15K samples, all the captions
491
      contain diverse details like key objects and their attributes, spatial relationships, text rendering, and
492
      so on. ImageInWords (Garg et al., 2024) is another small-scale detailed caption dataset that takes a
493   slightly different approach by using object detection and other annotation models with humans in the
494   loop. Densely Caption Images (DCI) (Urbanek et al., 2023) is another human-in-the-loop annotation
495   dataset which uses labels from Segment Anything (Kirillov et al., 2023). Both these datasets contain
496   fewer than 10K samples.
497
      LVIS-Instruct4V (Wang et al., 2023) dataset contains detailed captions of 110K images from the
498
      LVIS (Gupta et al., 2019) dataset annotated by GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023). ALLaVA (Chen
499   et al., 2024a) introduces 715K captions by GPT-4V on images sourced from LAION (Schuhmann
500   et al., 2021) and Vision-Flan (Xu et al., 2024). ShareGPT4V dataset contains 100K detailed cap-
501   tions on images sourced from LAION (Schuhmann et al., 2022), SBU (Ordonez et al., 2011), and
502   CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) created by GPT-4V. They further train a model and generate
503   captions for over a million images. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a) introduces a dataset of 23K detailed
504   captions on top of COCO images using GPT-4. Lastly, Pixart-α (Chen et al., 2024c) introduces
505   large-scale synthetic captions on top of the SAM dataset (Kirillov et al., 2023) using LLaVA-1.0
506   7B (Liu et al., 2023a) model. While this particular dataset contains 11M examples, it contains many
507
      captions with hallucinations and the images in the dataset are of limited diversity. PixelProse has
      over 16M samples, which to the best of our knowledge is the largest detailed high-quality publicly
508
      available image-caption dataset.

511   6    L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
512
513   Our images are collected from the internet, which contains unsafe and toxic content. Though we use
514   extensive automated measures to remove CSAM, NSFW content, and PII, our automated systems are
515   imperfect. VLMs tend to suffer from hallucinations, hence the captions may not always accurately
516
      describe the image. While we use a state-of-the-art large commercial model to generate our captions,
      it still suffers from hallucinations. Despite this, our captions are of much higher quality and fidelity
517
      than captions in other similar-sized public datasets. Most importantly, unlike the original alt-text
518
      captions, PixelProse captions consistently reflect the image content.
519
520   In addition to its obvious uses in training open-source models, we hope that the dense format of Pixel-
521   Prose facilitates research into methods for refactoring dense captions into instructions and VQA pairs.
