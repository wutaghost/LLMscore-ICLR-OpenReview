011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013           Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown impressive capabili-
014           ties in document understanding, a rapidly growing research area with significant
015
              industrial demand in recent years. As a multimodal task, document understand-
              ing requires models to possess both perceptual and cognitive abilities. However,
016
              current MLLMs often face conflicts between perception and cognition. Taking
017
              a document VQA task (cognition) as an example, an MLLM might generate an-
018           swers that do not match the corresponding visual content identified by its OCR
019           (perception). This conflict suggests that the MLLM might struggle to establish an
020           intrinsic connection between the information it “sees” and what it “understands.”
021           Such conflicts challenge the intuitive notion that cognition is consistent with per-
022           ception, hindering the performance and explainability of MLLMs. In this paper,
023           we define the conflicts between cognition and perception as Cognition and Per-
024           ception (C&P) knowledge conflicts, a form of multimodal knowledge conflicts,
025           and systematically assess them with a focus on document understanding. Our
026
              analysis reveals that even GPT-4o, a leading MLLM, achieves only 68.6% C&P
              consistency. To mitigate the C&P knowledge conflicts, we propose a novel method
027
              called Multimodal Knowledge Consistency Fine-tuning. This method first ensures
028
              task-specific consistency and then connects the cognitive and perceptual knowl-
029           edge. Our method significantly reduces C&P knowledge conflicts across all tested
030           MLLMs and enhances their performance in both cognitive and perceptual tasks in
031           most scenarios.

049   1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   In recent years, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) (gpt, 2023; Team et al., 2023; gpt,
052   2024; Chen et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a) have witnessed rapid
053   development and have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of multimodal
      tasks (Antol et al., 2015; Mathew et al., 2021; Hossain et al., 2019). Particularly in the field of


                                                                     1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      document understanding (Cui et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020; 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022;
055   Luo et al., 2023), which has high academic and industrial value, significant research efforts with
056   MLLMs have been made (Zhang et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023a;b; Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
057   2023; Hu et al., 2024), yielding promising results.
058
      As a multimodal task, document understanding requires models to accurately perceive visual content
059
      (perception) and then generate coherent responses (cognition) based on that perception. However,
060
      current MLLMs often face conflicts between perception and cognition. For example in Figure 1
061   (a), GPT-4o (gpt, 2024) recognizes the text in a certain region of an image as “Doral” through its
062   OCR capability (perception) but responds to a related information extraction question with the text
063   “Doraf” (cognition). This conflict suggests that the GPT-4o might struggle to establish an intrinsic
064   connection between what it “sees” and what it “understands.” Statistical analysis further underscores
065   this issue, as Figure 1 (b) shows, with leading MLLMs like GPT-4o and Qwen-VL-Max (Bai et al.,
066   2023) achieving 69.60% and 79.98% consistency between perception and cognition (Section 3).
067   In this paper, we define intrinsic conflicts between cognitive knowledge and perceptual knowledge
068   within MLLMs, which result in inconsistencies in responses related to cognition and perception,
069   as Cognition and Perception (C&P) knowledge conflicts (Section 2.1). C&P knowledge conflicts
070   serve as a critical factor undermining the explainability of MLLM responses, as these conflicts
071   challenge the intuitive notion that cognition is consistent with perception. Unlike previous research
072   on multimodal knowledge conflicts (e.g., hallucination) (Zhai et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Guan et al.,
073   2024; Liu et al., 2023a), which focuses solely on conflicts within either cognition or perception, we
074   highlight, for the first time, the conflicts that arise between the two.
075   We systematically assess C&P knowledge conflicts in the current five MLLMs (Section 3), focusing
076   on document understanding. Here, the cognitive task is document-related VQA, while the perceptual
077   task is OCR. The experimental results show significant C&P knowledge conflicts in current MLLMs,
078   underscoring the need to mitigate these conflicts. To address this, a novel method called Multimodal
079   Knowledge Consistency Fine-tuning is introduced, which includes three fine-tuning tasks (Section
080   4). Specifically, motivated by the Generator-Validator (GV) framework (Li et al., 2024b), we con-
081
      duct two task-specific fine-tuning tasks: the Cognition Consistency task and the Perception Consis-
      tency task. The purpose of these two tasks is based on our belief that ensuring C&P consistency starts
082
      with maintaining task-specific consistency. Furthermore, to establish an inner connection between
083
      cognitive and perceptual knowledge, the third fine-tuning task is designed: the C&P Connector task.
084
085   Comprehensive experiments are conducted on three open-source MLLMs across two series and two
086   parameter sizes. The results indicate that multimodal knowledge consistency fine-tuning signifi-
087
      cantly improves C&P consistency, with all three MLLMs achieving at least a 34% improvement
      (Section 5.2). Moreover, in most scenarios, our method also enhances MLLM performance in both
088
      cognitive and perceptual tasks (Section 5.4).
089
090   Our main contributions are as follows:
091
092
             • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to identify and introduce the concept of
               Cognition and Perception knowledge conflicts, a form of multimodal knowledge conflicts,
093
               in MLLMs.
094
095          • A systematic evaluation is conducted on current MLLMs to assess the Cognition and Per-
096            ception knowledge conflicts in document understanding, showing that such conflicts are
097
               commonly present in current MLLMs.
098          • A novel method called Multimodal Knowledge Consistency Fine-tuning is introduced to
099            mitigate the C&P knowledge conflicts in current MLLMs. Extensive experiments on six
100            public document understanding benchmarks in three MLLMs demonstrate the effectiveness
101
               of the proposed method.

259   Table 2 demonstrates that even leading MLLMs face C&P knowledge conflicts, which negatively
260
      affect explainability. To resolve these conflicts, we introduce a novel method called Multimodal
      Knowledge Consistency Fine-tuning, as shown in Figure 3.
261
262   We suggest that ensuring C&P consistency starts with maintaining task-specific consistency, mean-
263   ing consistency within cognitive and perceptual tasks. Li et al. (2024b) introduces the Generator-
264   Validator (GV) fine-tuning framework, which ensures task consistency by generating mutually val-
265   idating queries. Building on this framework, we construct validation queries xCV and xP V for the
266
      cognitive query xC and the perceptual query xP , respectively, referring to ((xC , yC ), (xCV , yCV ))
      as the Cognition Consistency task and ((xP , yP ), (xP V , yP V )) as the Perception Consistency task.
267
268   The xC and xP are constructed following Section 2.3. Let an image xI , its QA annotation (Q, A),
269   and the bounding box Box of A on xI be given. The validation queries xCV and xP V are framed as
      two-option questions, using templates T empCV and T empP V , respectively. Specifically, xCV =


                                                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      T empCV (Q, A, A′ ), where A′ is a negative sample, randomly selected from the text in xI excluding
271   A. Similarly, xP V = T empP V (A, Box, Box′ ), where Box′ is a bounding box randomly sampled
272   from xI , excluding Box. It is important to note that the construction of (xP , xP V ) is independent of
273   A, which means they can be generated using all the text and their bounding boxes across the entire
274   image. Additionally, the order of the options is randomly shuffled to ensure balanced data.
275
      Additionally, to establish a connection between cognitive and perceptual tasks, we designed the
276
      C&P Connector. Let xConn and yConn represent the query and response of the C&P Connector, re-
277   spectively. Formally, xConn = T empQConn (Q, Box, Box′ ) and yConn = T empRConn (A, Box),
278   where T empQConn is the query template (a two-option question), and T empRConn is the response
279   template. The goal is to utilize the query and response from the C&P Connector to link Q, A, and
280   Box, thus creating a bridge between the cognitive and perceptual tasks, and reducing knowledge
281   conflicts.
282

283   the details are as follows:

288
             • Stage 2: Cognition Consistency, denoted as Xs2 = {((xCi , yCi ), (xCVi , yCVi )) | i =
289            0, 1, . . . , N }.
290
             • Stage 3: Establishing Connections, denoted as Xs3 = {(xConni , yConni ) | i =
291                                 sub    sub
               0, 1, . . . , W } ∪ Xs1  ∪ Xs2  . As explained in Section 2.3, some (Q, A) pairs cannot be
292
               used to construct the C&P Connector, resulting in W < N . Additionally, we incorporate a
293            small amount of data from previous stages to maintain model performance.

      5.2   M AIN R ESULTS
311
      The evaluation is performed on the dataset constructed in Section 2.3. In addition to C&P Con-
312   sistency, we also report Cognitive Task Consistency and Perceptual Task Consistency. Following
313   Li et al. (2024b), cognitive task consistency quantifies the percentage of cases where yCV (cal-
314   culated as yCV = f (xCV ) = f (T empCV (Q, yC , A′ ))) selects the option for yC in xCV . Sim-
315   ilarly, perceptual task consistency quantifies the percentage of cases where yP V (calculated as
316   yP V = f (xP V ) = f (T empP V (Q, yP , Box′ ))) selects the option for yP in xP V .
317
      The experimental results, as illustrated in Table 3 and Table 4, demonstrate that our multimodal
318   knowledge consistency fine-tuning method substantially improves C&P consistency across all six
319   datasets. Specifically, Qwen-VL-Chat exhibits a 34.83% increase in C&P consistency, while
320   InternVL2-2b and InternVL2-8b show improvements of 37.85% and 43.19%, respectively. These
321   results indicate that our method effectively reduces C&P knowledge conflicts. The comparison
322   between Qwen-VL-Chat and the InternVL2 models highlights the general applicability of our ap-
323   proach across different MLLM architectures. The results reveal that models with a larger number of
      parameters, such as InternVL2-8b, achieve better C&P consistency after fine-tuning.

      6.2       MLLM S FOR D OCUMENT U NDERSTANDING
453
454   Document understanding (Cui et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2020; 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022;
455   Luo et al., 2023; 2024; Wang et al., 2023) is a rapidly growing research area driven by increasing
456   industrial demand. Its main objective is to comprehend complex typeset images that contain rich
457   textual information, such as scanned document pages (Mathew et al., 2021; Svetlichnaya, 2020;
458   Stanisławek et al., 2021), charts (Masry et al., 2022; Kafle et al., 2018; Methani et al., 2020), ta-
459   bles (Pasupat & Liang, 2015; Chen et al., 2019), and other formats (Tanaka et al., 2021; Mathew
460
      et al., 2022). As a multimodal task, document understanding involves automated processes for un-
      derstanding, classifying, and extracting information, requiring models to possess both perceptual
461
      and cognitive capabilities (Cui et al., 2021). Recent studies (Chen et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024;
462
      Dong et al., 2024) for general MLLMs improve the encoding resolution of document images, sig-
463   nificantly boosting performance in document understanding tasks. Several MLLMs are developed
464   to focus on addressing document understanding problems. mPLUG-DocOwl (Ye et al., 2023a; Hu
465   et al., 2024) and UReader (Ye et al., 2023b) unify tasks across five types of document images using
466   a sequence-to-sequence format, and achieve good performance in document understanding.
467
468   6.3       K NOWLEDGE C ONFLICTS IN LLM S
469
470   LLMs are distinguished for encapsulating an extensive repository of world knowledge, known as
471   the memory. Simultaneously, LLMs continue to engage with external contextual knowledge post-
472   deployment (Pan et al., 2023). The discrepancies between the contexts and the model’s memory
473   knowledge, i.e. context-memory conflicts, are being intensively studied recently (Xie et al., 2023;
474
      Jin et al., 2024). Another notable challenge arises with intra-memory conflict—a condition where
      LLMs exhibit unpredictable behaviors to inputs that are semantically equivalent but syntactically
475
      distinct (Chang & Bergen, 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Raj et al., 2023; Rabinovich et al., 2023; Bartsch
476
      et al., 2023). This variance can be attributed to the conflicting knowledge embedded within the
477   LLM’s memory, which stem from the inconsistencies present in the complex and diverse pre-training
478   data sets. However, current research on knowledge conflicts focuses only on text, leaving the issue
479   of multimodal knowledge conflicts in MLLMs unaddressed.
480
481   6.4       H ALLUCINATION ISSUES IN MLLM S
482
483   MLLMs provide powerful tools for content generation across a wide range of tasks. However, they
484   are susceptible to hallucinations (Bang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c; Guan et al., 2024; Li et al.,
485   2023), where the generated outputs contain information not present in the visual input. These hallu-
      cinations typically arise when the models overly rely on the strong priors of their language modules,


                                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      neglecting visual sensibility (Guan et al., 2024). Such conflicts between MLLMs’ language and
487   visual perception raise concerns about their reliability and limit their applications (Ji et al., 2023;
488   Kaddour et al., 2023). Current research primarily focuses on detecting and evaluating hallucinations
489   (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b;c), as well as methods to reduce them (Liu et al., 2024; Wang
490   et al., 2024). To mitigate hallucinations, efforts have been directed toward enhancing data collection
491   and training procedures. For instance, LRV-Instruction (Liu et al., 2024) creates balanced positive
492   and negative instructions to finetune MLLMs, while VIGC (Wang et al., 2024) employs an iterative
493   process to generate concise answers and combine them. These approaches equip the model with
494   more accurate perception capability. Nevertheless, research on how MLLMs integrate perception
495
      and cognition knowledge, which is also vital for interpreting and debugging these models, has not
      progressed at the same pace.

497
498   7   C ONCLUSION
499
500   In this paper, we identify that current MLLMs often face conflicts between perception and cog-
501   nition, referred to as Cognition and Perception (C&P) knowledge conflicts. The severity of these
502   conflicts is systematically assessed across six document understanding datasets, revealing that even
503   leading MLLMs still struggle with these multimodal knowledge conflicts. To address this problem,
504   a novel method called Multimodal Knowledge Consistency Fine-tuning is introduced. Comprehen-
505   sive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in reducing C&P knowledge conflicts.
506
      Additionally, in most scenarios, our method improves the performance of MLLMs in both cognitive
      and perceptual tasks. One limitation of our work is its focus solely on document understanding. In
507
      the future, we will expand our research beyond document understanding to examine C&P knowledge
508
      conflicts in more general multimodal areas, such as scene understanding and visual reasoning.
