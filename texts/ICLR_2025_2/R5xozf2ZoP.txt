009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Diffusion models that can generate high-quality data from randomly sampled
012
               Gaussian noises have become the mainstream generative method in academia and
013
               industry. Are randomly sampled Gaussian noises equally effective for diffusion
014            models? Some methods explore the impact of noise variations on the results, but
015            they either do not operate in the pure noise space, requiring additional evaluation
016            models, or cannot be adapted to general text-to-image tasks. In this paper, we
017            mainly made three contributions. First, we are the first to hypothesize and empiri-
018            cally observe that the generation quality of diffusion models significantly depends
019            on the noise inversion stability. This naturally provides a noise quality metric for
020            noise selection, grounded in a mathematical property. Second, we further propose
021            a novel noise optimization method that actively enhances the inversion stability
022
               of arbitrary given noises. Our method is the first one that purely optimizes noises
               for the general text-to-image task without relying on any additional evaluator or
023
               specifically designed prompts. Third, our extensive experiments demonstrate that
024
               the proposed noise selection and noise optimization methods both significantly
025            improve representative diffusion models, such as SDXL and SDXL-turbo, in terms
026            of human preference and other objective evaluation metrics. For example, the
027            human preference winning rates of noise selection and noise optimization over the
028            baselines can be up to 57% and 72.5%, respectively, on DrawBench.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Generative diffusion models, renowned for the impressive performance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021),
035   serve as the mainstream generative paradigm with wide applications in image generation (Nichol
036   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Saharia et al., 2022), image editing (Qi et al., 2023; Kawar et al.,
037   2023), 3D generation (Gupta et al., 2023; Erkoç et al., 2023), and video generation (Ho et al., 2022a;c).
038   Diffusion-based Generative AI products attracted much attention and a large number of users in recent
039
      years. Understanding and improving the capabilities of diffusion models has become an essentially
      important topic in machine learning.
040
041   It is well known that diffusion models can generate diverse results, which, of course, contain good
042   ones and bad ones. Previous studies mainly enhance the generated results by working on model
043   weight and architecture space (Song et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2023; Podell et al., 2023; Sauer et al.,
044   2023; Ho et al., 2022b; Lin et al., 2024), while the noise space is largely overlooked. In this paper,
045
      we focus on the noise space. Some methods (Karthik et al., 2023; Ben-Hamu et al., 2024; Wallace
      et al., 2023) tried to select the results (equivalent to selecting initial noises) or optimize noise values
046
      with extra information, such as an additional image quality evaluator (Kirstain et al., 2023; Xu
047
      et al., 2024) or token IDs, which are used to construct a “noise-prompt” attention loss (Guo et al.,
048   2024; Chefer et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2023) to improve the results in terms of visual effects and
049   alignment. First, relying on an external evaluator introduces bias and limits generalization beyond the
050   evaluator data. Since the evaluator’s scores don’t directly affect the noise, gradient backpropagation
051   through the network is needed for optimization, which increases memory usage. Second, although
052   the “noise-prompt” attention loss directly influences the noise, it only applies to specifically designed
053   prompts, such as the A+B-type prompts, rather than handling real-world general prompts, including
      style, detail description, and counting, as shown in Figure 1.

075   In this work, we address two fundamental issues in the noise space of diffusion models. First, can a
076   better noise be selected based on a mathematical metric rather than relying on external evaluation
077   results? Second, is it possible to directly optimize a given noise to produce improved results using a
078
      general prompt and without any additional information? The answer to both questions is affirmative.
      Fortunately, we not only confirm the possibility but also propose practical algorithms.
079
080   Contributions. We summarize the three main contributions of this work as follows:
081
      First, we hypothesize and empirically verify that not all noises are created equally. Specifically,
082   random noises with high inversion stability usually lead to better generation than noises with lower
083   inversion stability. The inversion stability measures the similarity of the sampled initial noise ϵ
084   and the inverse noise ϵ′ . The mathematical quantitative metric naturally provides us with a novel
085   noise selection method to select stable noises, which often correspond to better results. Unlike other
086   methods that judge noise quality by image quality (we call this post-selection), we directly select
087   the noise without introducing an additional evaluator. We present several qualitative results of noise
088   selection in Figures 1 and 5.
089   Second, we further proposed a novel noise optimization method that actively enhances the inversion
090   stability of arbitrary given noises. More specifically, we optimize an inversion-stability loss via
091   gradient descent with respect to the sampled noise (rather than the conventional model weight space).
092   The proposed noise optimization method is the first one that purely optimizes noises for the general
093   text-to-image task, reducing memory usage while achieving better visual effects and alignments with
094   general prompts. We present several qualitative results of noise optimization in Figures 1 and 7.
095   Third, our extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed noise selection and noise optimization
096   methods both significantly improve representative diffusion models, such as SDXL and SDXL-turbo.
097   On the one hand, the human preference winning rates of noise selection and noise optimization over
098   the baseline can be up to 57% and 72.5%, respectively, on DrawBench in terms of human preference.
099   On the other hand, noise selection and noise optimization are also preferred by Human Preference
100   Score (HPS) v2 (Wu et al., 2023b), a recent powerful human preference model trained on diverse
101   high-quality human preference data, with winning rates up to 67% and 88%, respectively. Human
102
      preference, considered as the ground-truth ultimate evaluation metric for text-to-image generation,
      and objective evaluation metrics all generally support our methods.

      Noise Inversion Stability. It is well known that fixed points are stable under the transformation and,
      thus, have great properties (Burton, 2003; Connell, 1959; Pata et al., 2019). May the fixed-point
203
      Gaussian noises under the denoising-inversion transformation F also exhibit some advantages? As
204
      finding the fixed points of this complex dynamical system is intractable, unfortunately, we cannot
205   empirically verify it. Instead, we can formulate Definition 1 to measure the stability of noise for the
206   denoising-inversion transformation F .
207
      Definition 1 (Noise Inversion Stability). We define F as the denoising-inversion transformation,
208
      ϵ → x0 → ϵ′ . Suppose a sampled noise ϵ has its inverse noise ϵ′ = F (ϵ) given by a diffusion model
209   M with the condition c. We define the noise inversion stability of the sampled noise ϵ as
210
211                                                                  s(ϵ) = cos(ϵ, ϵ′ )                                                                        (3)
212   for the diffusion M with the condition c, where cos is the cosine similarity between two vectors.
213
214   We use cosine similarity to measure stability for simplicity, while it is also possible to use other
215   similarity metrics. The results using other metrics can be found in Appendix B. Our empirical analysis
      in Section 4 suggests that the simple cosine similarity metric works well.

      Evidence. We refer to the content generated under the “NULL” prompt as the semantic information
217   implicit in the corresponding initial noise (see the left part of Figure 2). This semantic information
218   reflects the generation trends of sampling noise points. For instance, noise1 tends to produce a “tree”
219   layout of displays. We calculate the inversion stability score for three scenarios: (1) noise and prompt
220   matching (setting ⃝ 1 in Figure 2), (2) noise and prompt not matching (settings ⃝  2 and ⃝3 in Figure 2).
221   We observed a strong correlation between the inversion stability scores and the degree of match, with
222   higher inversion stability scores leading to better image quality. When the noise matches the prompt,
223   the noise is closer to the fixed point in the denoising-inversion path guided by the prompt.
224   Noise Selection. Based on the evidence results and inspired by the intriguing mathematical properties
225   of fixed points, we hypothesize that the noise with higher inversion stability can lead to better results.
226   If this hypothesis is reasonable, this naturally provides a novel and useful noise selection algorithm
227   that selects the noise seed with the highest stability score from K noise seeds (e.g. K = 100 in this
228   work). We present the pseudocode in Algorithm 1.
229   Noise Optimization. As we have an objective to increase the noise inversion stability, is it possible
230   to actively optimize a given noise by maximizing the stability score? We further propose the noise
231   optimization algorithm that directly performs Gradient Descent (GD) on the loss, 1 − cos(ϵ, ϵ′ ), with
232   respect to ϵ, where we keep the diffusion model weights and ϵ′ constant for each optimization step.
233   We take the diffusion models as a fixed mapping function and the optimization objective is directly
234   act on the noise. This make us directly optimize the initial noise and do not let gradients flow through
235   the network, greatly saving the memory. We present the illustration of noise optimization in the right
236   column of Figure 3. We present the pseudocode in Algorithm 2.

473   5                 D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
474
475   In this section, we discuss related works and three main limitations of our method.
476
477
      Related work. Some works (Wallace et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
      2024) have noted that noise plays a significant role in the final results. However, they typically require
478
      additional information to optimize the noise, such as image quality evaluators or token IDs used for
479
      constructing attention loss. The first type of methods relies on the performance of the evaluator and
480   demands more memory due to the need for gradients through the denoising process. The second type
481   operates in the noise space but requires user-specified token IDs or an LLM to extract them, which
482   limits their applicability to general prompts. In contrast, our method is based on a mathematical
483   property of noise, allowing it to work directly within the noise space and adapt to general prompts. In
484   summary, from a perspective of real-world practice, previous so-called noise optimization methods
485   cannot be directly applied or compared with our method for a general text-to-image generation task.
      We discuss more about this key point in Appendix C.

503
504   Theoretical Understanding. With the inspirations from fixed points in dynamic systems, we still
505
      do not theoretically understand why not all noises are created for diffusion models. We formulated
      and empirically verified the hypothesis that random noises with higher inversion stability often lead
506
      to better results, it is still difficult to theoretically analyze how the performance of diffusion models
507
      mathematically depends on noise stability. We believe theoretically understanding noise selection
508   and noise optimization will be a key step to further improve them.
509
510   Optimization Strategies. In this work, we only applied simple gradient descent with multiple (e.g.,
511
      100) steps to optimize the noise-space loss, but noise optimization seems like a difficult optimization
      task. In some cases, we observe that the loss does not converge smoothly. Due to computational
512
      costs and a limited understanding of the noise-space loss landscape, we did not carefully fine-tune
513
      the hyperparameters or employ advanced optimizers, such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) in this
514   work. Thus, while the current optimization strategy works well, it is far from releasing the power of
515   noise-space algorithms. We think it will be very promising and important to better analyze and solve
516   this emerging optimization task with advanced optimization methods.
517
      Computational Costs. Both noise selection and noise optimization require significantly more
518
      computational resources and time compared to standard generation. For noise selection, we compute
519
      the inversion stability loss across 100 noise seeds and select the one with the highest stability score,
520   repeating the forward and inversion processes 100 times. In noise optimization, we perform gradient
521   descent over 100 steps, repeating the forward pass and inversion 100 times. While accelerating noise
522   selection may be challenging, noise optimization could likely be sped up by reducing the number of
523   gradient descent steps in the future.

      6                 C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this paper, we report an interesting noise inversion stability hypothesis and empirically observe
528
      that noises with higher inversion stability often lead to better results. This hypothesis motivates us
529   to design two novel noise-space algorithms, noise selection and noise optimization, for diffusion
530   models. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to apply the selection and optimization
531   methods in a pure noise space that does not involve any additional estimators and extra annotated
532   information. Unlike previous related methods that require specifically designed prompts, both of our
533   algorithms can directly adopt to general text-to-image generation with standard text prompts. Our
534   extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods can significantly improve multiple
535   aspects of qualitative results and enhance human preference rates as well as objective evaluation
536   scores. Moreover, the proposed methods can be generally applied to various diffusion models in a
537
      plug-and-play manner. While some limitations exist, our work has made the first solid step to explore
      this promising direction. We believe our work will motivate more studies on understanding and
538
      improving diffusion models from the perspective of noise space.

945
946
      Noise inversion and editing. The noise inversion technique is mainly applied in image editing
947
      (Mokady et al., 2023; Meiri et al., 2023; Huberman-Spiegelglas et al., 2023) in very similar ways.
948   They usually invert a clean image into a relatively noisy one via a few inverse steps and then denoise
949   the inverse noisy images with another prompt to achieve instruction editing. Some work (Mao
950   et al., 2023) in this line of research realized that editing noises can help editing generated results.
951   Specifically, modifying a portion of the initial noise can affect the layout of the generated images.
952   Other works (Liu et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023) focused on dragging and dropping image content
953   via interactive noise editing. However, the goal of previous studies is to control image layout under
954   fine-grained control conditions, such as input layout or editing operations. In contrast, we focus on
955   generally improving the generated results of diffusion models by selecting or optimizing a Gaussian
956
      noise according to the stability score.
957   Noise selection and optimization. In recent years, there have been some works on select-
958   ing/optimizing the results in the noise space. Typically, these methods rely on additional information,
959   such as image quality evaluators or token IDs provided by the user/extracted by a large language
960   model (LLM) to construct a “noise-prompt” attention loss. However, our method is based on a
961   mathematical property and directly operates on the pure noise space, adopting for general prompts
962
      and various models.
963   (1) For example, Karthik et al. (2023) first generates many candidate images and selects the best
964   one from these by comprehensive scoring from a VAQ model (e.g. GPT) and an image quality
965   evaluator (e.g. ImageReward). (Selecting images equals selecting the initial noises). This type of
966   selecting method is a post-selection method that cannot directly judge the quality of the noise and
967   is seriously affected by the quality of the evaluator. The potential for introducing additional bias
968
      may also increase. In contrast, our method evaluates the initial noise via inverse stability. This is a
      mathematical property that is independent of any additional input information and does not introduce
969
      additional bias.
970
971   (2) Some noise optimization methods mainly target image quality scores to optimize noise values. For
      example, DOODL (Wallace et al., 2023) takes the score from an additional image quality evaluator

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

973    performance of the chosen image quality evaluator, which increases the likelihood of introducing
974    additional bias and memory usage. Moreover, the evaluation scores require gradient backpropagation
975    to influence the initial noise, resulting in significantly higher memory usage. Other methods use
976    the attention score map and user-specified or LLM extraction token IDs to design a “noise-prompt”
977    attention loss to maximum correlation between noise features and special tokens. The general form
