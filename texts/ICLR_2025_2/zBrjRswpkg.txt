009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Constraint learning from human feedback (CLHF) has garnered significant in-
013            terest in the domain of safe reinforcement learning (RL) due to the challenges
014            associated with designing constraints that elicit desired behaviors. However, a
015            comprehensive theoretical analysis of CLHF is still missing. This paper addresses
016            this gap by establishing a theoretical foundation. Concretely, trajectory-wise feed-
017            back, which is the most natural form of feedback, is shown to be helpful only for
018
               learning chance constraints. Building on this insight, we propose and theoretically
               analyze algorithms for CLHF and for solving chance constrained RL problems.
019
               Our algorithm is empirically shown to outperform an existing algorithm.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Designing an appropriate reward function for reinforcement learning (RL) is a challenging task,
028
      as a poorly designed reward function can lead to various unintended behaviors (Krakovna et al.,
029   2016). One approach to mitigate this issue is inverse reinforcement learning (IRL), where the reward
030   function is inferred from expert demonstrations (Ng and Russell, 2000; Ziebart et al., 2008; 2010).
031   However, acquiring expert demonstrations can be costly or even infeasible in certain situations. As a
032   result, there has been growing interest in learning the reward function from human feedback (Wirth
033   et al., 2017), particularly using preference datasets for fine-tuning large language models (Ouyang
034   et al., 2022).
035   Constraint learning from human feedback (CLHF) has attracted considerable attention (Scobee and
036   Sastry, 2020; Glazier et al., 2021; Papadimitriou and Anwar, 2021; Stocking et al., 2021; Malik
037   et al., 2021; Poletti et al., 2023; Lindner et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024) due to the
038   crucial importance of safety in real-world applications and the inherent challenges in designing cost
039   functions (Krakovna et al., 2016). Nonetheless, a comprehensive theoretical analysis of CLHF is still
040   missing. This paper fill this gap by establishing a theoretical foundation for CLHF, with emphasis
041   on scalability to flexible models such as neural networks, ability to handle multiple constraints and
042   ambiguity in human feedback.
043   Concretely, in Section 3, we investigate what form of human feedback is appropriate for what type
044   of constraints. In particular, we show that trajectory feedback, which will be explained later and
045   is thought to be a most natural form of feedback, is helpful to solve only chance-constrained RL
046   problems (Chow et al., 2017).
047   In Section 4, we propose a new learning scheme for CLHF that can handle multiple constraints and
048   ambiguity in human feedback. We theoretically analyze it and show that the scheme is provably able
049   to learn a component necessary to solve chance-constrained RL problems.
050
      In Section 5, we propose a new policy gradient algorithm for solving chance-constrained RL prob-
051
      lems. It is an extension of Chen et al. (2024) to problems with a more general chance constraints.
052
053   In Section 6, we empirical validated the superior performance of our algorithm in both tabular and
      function approximation cases, confirming that our algorithm scales well.

138

139
      ρ(+1|τ ) = 0.5 if and only if ν(τ ) = 0. Furthermore, ρ′ satisfies similar conditions but with policies

180   One approach for estimating an oracle is to learn a decision function d : T → R (Hastie et al.,
181
      2001). Concretely, let D := {(τ, y)} be a set of trajectory-feedback pairs, and ℓ : R → [0, ∞)

306      6    E XPERIMENTS
307
308     We verified whether the proposed algorithms can learn constraints and obtain a policy that satisfies
309     the chance constraints using the CCPG algorithm.
310
311
        Implementation Details: For data collection, we used an RL agent trained by PPO (Schulman
312
        et al., 2017) to maximize returns without any constraints. We then labeled the data using the true
313     cost functions. For estimating the cost functions, we employed a neural network with three linear
314     layers, with batch normalization and ReLU in the middle layer and a sigmoid function in the output
315     layer. NAdam (Dozat, 2016) was used to update the network. Furthermore, we integrated CCPG
316     into PPO. For all experiments, we set the chance constraint parameter δ to 0.1. In addition, all
317     experiments were conducted with five different random seeds.
318
319     Baseline: We used Inverse Constrained Reinforcement Learning (ICRL) (Malik et al., 2021), the
320     most popular method in this field, as a baseline. Since original ICRL updates the policy to satisfy
321     expected constraints by estimated cost functions, we modified it to update the policy to satisfy chance
322     constraints. This means that the cost function is estimated by ICRL, but the policy is optimized by
323     CCPG. Also, in the original ICRL, the cost function was assumed to depend only on the current
        state and action, but in this study it has been modified to depend on the next state as well.

464   7     R ELATED W ORK
465
466   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): The most commonly employed
467   methods of giving feedback are to return whether the behavior is absolutely good or bad (War-
468   nell et al., 2018; Arakawa et al., 2018) or to compare two trajectories and choose the superior one
469
      (Akrour et al., 2012; Wirth et al., 2016; Christiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021a;b; Liu et al., 2022).
      In this study, the absolutely good or bad (whether constraints are satisfied or not) is given as a feed-
470
      back. In recent years, RLHF has been applied to various fields. In meta reinforcement learning,
471
      RLHF has been applied to understand common human preferences in multiple tasks (Ding et al.,
472   2023; Joey Hejna and Sadigh, 2023). Ren et al. (2022) utilized human feedback to quickly identify
473   tasks in multi-task RL. It has also been used in research that attempts to create diversity in agent
474   behavior by selecting the two most different from three trajectories (Wang et al., 2023). Further-
475   more, it is beginning to be used in training Large Language Models (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wu et al.,
476   2023; Dai et al., 2024), operating real robots (Choi et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023), and in treatment
477   recommendation systems (Xu et al., 2021). On the other hand, in the real world, there are behaviors
478   that we never want the agent to take and that should be restricted. However, it is difficult to restrict
479   these behaviors with reward-based learning methods.
480
481   Constrained Reinforcement Learning: In ordinary RL, the goal is to obtain a policy that maxi-
482
      mizes rewards. On the other hand, there are cases where we want to avoid catastrophic situations at
      the same time. To address this, constrained RL has been actively studied.
483
484   Methods that maximize returns while keeping a certain cost function below a certain boundary have
485   often been studied (Hordijk and Kallenberg, 1984; Geibel, 2006; Kadota et al., 2006; Stooke et al.,
      2020; Roy et al., 2022). Among them, many methods have been studied that use the method of


                                                          9
486
      Lagrange multiplier to solve the problem (Kadota et al., 2006; Stooke et al., 2020; Roy et al., 2022).
487   While these methods aim to obtain policies that satisfy expected constraints, (Chow et al., 2017; Pet-
488   sagkourakis et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) proposed methods to obtain policies that satisfy chance
489   constraints, which are probabilistic constraints, by using state augmentation or policy gradient meth-
490   ods, etc. In addition, Sootla et al. (2022) proposed a method that satisfies the constraint almost surely
491   by using a simple state augmentation method. We proposed a Lagrangian-based method for solving
492   chance constrained RL.
493   The above methods assume that constraints are describable and known, which is often not the case
494   in practice. Therefore, methods have been proposed to estimate constraints using data generated by
495   humans or feedback from humans. The most actively studied method is to estimate the cost function
496   from human demonstrations by applying inverse RL techniques (Scobee and Sastry, 2020; Glazier
497   et al., 2021; Papadimitriou and Anwar, 2021; Stocking et al., 2021; Chou et al., 2022; Malik et al.,
498   2021; Gaurav et al., 2023). However, demonstrations are either prohibitively expensive or sometimes
499   impossible to collect. Therefore, instead of estimating constraints from the demonstration data,
500   some methods have been proposed to estimate constraints from physical force (Zhu et al., 2024),
501
      trajectory preference (Dai et al., 2024; Chirra et al., 2024; Peng and Billard, 2024) and stop signal
      (Poletti et al., 2023) from humans. These methods to estimate constraints from human feedback
502
      often succeed in recovering constraints, but there is no theoretical guarantee that the optimal policy
503
      can be obtained. Although (Lindner et al., 2024) is guaranteed to yield an optimal policy, it is only
504   feasible when the features of the policies are available, which is not realistic. In this study, we
505   propose a method that does not require expensive demonstrations and unrealistic assumptions and is
506   theoretically guaranteed to yield the optimal policy.
507

510
511   In this paper, we showed that there exists a natural correspondence between human feedback and
512
      constraint formulation. Based on it, we propose a new decision function for CLHF (from trajectory
      feedback) that can manage multiple constraints and ambiguity in human feedback. Builsing on those
513
      theoretical results, we propose a new policy gradient algorithm for solving constrained RL problems
514
      with chance constraints. Finally, we empirically showed superior performance of our algorithms.
