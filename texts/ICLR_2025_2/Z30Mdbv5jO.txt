009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Advancements in 3D scene reconstruction have transformed 2D images from the
012            real world into 3D models, producing realistic 3D results from hundreds of input
013            photos. Despite great success in dense-view reconstruction scenarios, rendering a
014            detailed scene from sparse views is still an ill-posed optimization problem, often
015            resulting in artifacts and distortions in unseen areas. In this paper, we propose
016            ReconX, a novel 3D scene reconstruction paradigm that reframes the ambiguous
017            reconstruction problem as a temporal generation task. The key insight is to un-
018
               leash the strong generative prior of large pre-trained video diffusion models for
               sparse-view reconstruction. Nevertheless, it is challenging to preserve 3D view
019
               consistency when directly generating video frames from pre-trained models. To
020
               address this issue, given limited input views, the proposed ReconX first constructs
021            a global point cloud and encodes it into a contextual space as the 3D structure
022            condition. Guided by the condition, the video diffusion model then synthesizes
023            video frames that are detail-preserved and exhibit a high degree of 3D consis-
024            tency, ensuring the coherence of the scene from various perspectives. Finally, we
025            recover the 3D scene from the generated video through a confidence-aware 3D
026            Gaussian Splatting optimization scheme. Extensive experiments on various real-
027            world datasets show the superiority of ReconX over state-of-the-art methods in
028            terms of quality and generalizability.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      With the rapid development of photogrammetry techniques such as NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020)
      and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023), 3D reconstruction has become a popular
034
      research topic in recent years, finding various applications from virtual reality (Dalal et al., 2024)
035
      to autonomous navigation (Adamkiewicz et al., 2022) and beyond (Martin-Brualla et al., 2021b;
036   Liu et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024a; Charatan et al., 2024). However, sparse-view reconstruction is
037   an ill-posed problem (Gao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2021) since it involves recovering a complex 3D
038   structure from limited viewpoint information (i.e., even as few as two images) that may correspond
039   to multiple solutions. This uncertain process requires additional assumptions and constraints to yield
040   a viable solution.
041
      Recently, powered by the efficient and expressive 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) with fast rendering
042   speed and high quality, several feed-forward Gaussian Splatting methods (Charatan et al., 2024;
043   Szymanowicz et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a) have been proposed to explore 3D scene reconstruc-
044   tion from sparse view images. Although they can achieve promising interpolation results by learning
045   scene-prior knowledge from feature extraction modules (e.g., epipolar transformer (Charatan et al.,
046   2024)), insufficient captures of the scene still lead to an ill-posed optimization problem (Wu et al.,
047   2024b). As a result, they often suffer from severe artifact and implausible imagery issues when
048   rendering the 3D scene from novel viewpoints, especially in unseen areas.
049   To address the limitations, we propose ReconX, a novel 3D scene reconstruction paradigm that
050   reformulates the inherently ambiguous reconstruction problem as a generation problem. Our key in-
051   sight is to unleash the strong generative prior of pre-trained large video diffusion models (Blattmann
052   et al., 2023a;b; Xing et al., 2023) to create more observations for the downstream reconstruction
053   task. Despite the capability to synthesize video clips featuring plausible 3D structures (Gao et al.,
      2024), recovering a high-quality 3D scene from current video diffusion models is still challenging,

083
084   • We introduce ReconX, a novel sparse-view 3D scene reconstruction framework that reframes the
085     ambiguous reconstruction challenge as a temporal generation task.
086   • We incorporate the 3D structure condition into the conditional space of the video diffusion model
087     to generate 3D consistent frames and propose a 3D confidence-aware optimization scheme in
088     3DGS to reconstruct the scene given the generated video.
089   • Extensive experiments demonstrate that our ReconX outperforms existing methods for high-
090     fidelity and generalizability on a variety of real-world datasets.

      Sparse-view reconstruction. NeRF and 3DGS typically demand hundreds of input images and rely
095
      on the multi-view stereo reconstruction (MVS) approach (e.g., COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm,
096   2016)) to estimate the camera parameters. To address the issue of low-quality 3D reconstruction
097   caused by sparse views, PixelNeRF (Yu et al., 2021) proposes using convolutional neural networks
098   to extract features from the input context. Moreover, FreeNeRF (Yang et al., 2023) adopts the
099   frequency and density regularized strategies to alleviate the artifacts caused by insufficient inputs
100   without any additional cost. To mitigate the overfitting to input sparse views in 3DGS, FSGS (Zhu
101   et al., 2023) and SparseGS (Xiong et al., 2023) employ a depth estimator to regularize the optimiza-
102   tion process. However, these methods all require known camera intrinsics and extrinsics, which is
103   not practical in real-world scenario. Benefiting from the existing powerful 3D reconstruction model
104
      (i.e., DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024a)), InstantSplat (Fan et al., 2024) is able to acquire accurate camera
      parameters and initial 3D representations from unposed sparse-view inputs, leading to the efficient
105
      and high-quality 3D reconstruction.

129   GeNVS (Chan et al., 2023) embeds a 3D feature field into the diffusion model to enhance the novel
130   view synthesis ability. More recently, video diffusion models (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Xing et al.,
131   2023) have shown an impressive ability to produce realistic videos and are believed to implicitly un-
132   derstand 3D structures (Liu et al., 2024b). SV3D (Voleti et al., 2024) and V3D (Chen et al., 2024b)
133   explore fine-tuning the pre-trained video diffusion model for 3D object generation. Meanwhile,
134
      MotionCtrl (Wang et al., 2024c) and CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024) achieve scene-level controllable
      video generation from a single image by explicitly injecting the camera pose into video diffusion
135
      models. However, they cannot work for the unconstrained sparse-view 3D scene reconstruction,

141   In this paper, we focus on the fundamental problem of 3D scene reconstruction and novel view
142   synthesis (NVS) from very sparse view (e.g., as few as two) images. Most existing works (Chen
143
      et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2021; Charatan et al., 2024; Szymanowicz et al., 2024a) utilize 3D prior and
      geometric constraints (e.g., depth, normal, cost volume) to fill the gap between observed and novel
144
      regions in sparse-view 3D reconstruction. Although capable of producing highly realistic images
145
      from the given viewpoints, these methods often struggle to generate high-quality images in areas
146   not visible from the input perspectives due to the inherent problem of insufficient viewpoints and
147   the resulting instability in the reconstruction process. To address this issue, a natural idea is to cre-
148   ate more observations to convert the under-determined 3D creation problem into a fully constrained
149   3D reconstruction setting. Recently, video generative models have shown promise for synthesiz-
150   ing video clips featuring 3D structures (Voleti et al., 2024; Blattmann et al., 2023a; Xing et al.,
151   2023). This inspires us to unleash the strong generative prior of large pre-trained video diffusion
152   models to create temporal consistent video frames for sparse-view reconstruction. Nevertheless, it
153   is non-trivial as the main challenge lies in poor 3D view consistency among video frames, which
154
      significantly limits the downstream 3DGS training process. To achieve 3D consistency within video
      generation, we first analyze the video diffusion modeling from a 3D distributional view. Let x be the

308   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
309
310   Implementation Details. In our framework, we choose DUSt3R (Wang et al., 2024a) as our uncon-
311   strained stereo 3D reconstruction backbone and the I2V model DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023)
312   (@ 512 × 512 resolution) as the video diffusion backbone. We first finetune the image cross-
313   attention layers with 2000 steps on the learning rate 1 × 10−4 for warm-up. Then we incorporate
314   the 3D structure condition cstruc into the video diffusion model and further finetune the spatial layers
315
      with 30K steps on the learning rate of 1 × 10−5 . Our video diffusion was trained on 3D scene
      datasets by sampling 32 frames with dynamic FPS at the resolution of 512 × 512 in a batch. The
316
      AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer is employed for optimization. At the inference of
317
      our video diffusion, we adopt the DDIM sampler (Song et al., 2022) using multi-condition classifier
318   free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022). Similar to Xing et al. (2023), we adopt tanh gating to learn
319   λF adaptively. The training is conducted on 8 NVIDIA A800 (80G) GPUs in two days. In the 3DGS
320   optimization stage, we choose the point maps of the first and end frames as the initial global point
321   cloud and all 32 generated frames are used to reconstruct the scene. Our implementation follows
322   the pipeline of the original 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), but unlike this method, we omit the adaptive
323   control process and attain high-quality renderings in just 1000 steps. The coefficients λrgb , λssim ,
      and λlpips are set to 0.8, 0.2, and 0.5, respectively.

      6     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we introduce ReconX, a novel sparse-view 3D reconstruction framework that refor-
      mulates the inherently ambiguous reconstruction problem as a generation problem. The key to our
526
      success is that we unleash the strong prior of video diffusion models to create more plausible ob-
527
      servations frames for sparse-view reconstruction. Grounded by the empirical study and theoretical
528   analysis, we propose to incorporate 3D structure guidance into the video diffusion process for better
529   3D consistent video frames generation. What’s more, we propose a 3D confidence-aware scheme to
530   optimize the final 3DGS from generated frames, which effectively addresses the uncertainty issue.
531   Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority of our ReconX over the latest state-of-the-art
532   methods in terms of high quality and strong generalizability in unseen data.
533
      Limitations and Future Work. Although ReconX achieves remarkable reconstruction results in
534   novel viewpoints, the quality still seems to be limited by the backbone as we choose the U-Net
535   based diffusion model DynamiCrafter (Xing et al., 2023). We expect that this issue can be solved
536   with open-sourced larger video diffusion models (e.g., DiT-based framework). In the future, it is
537   interesting to integrate 3DGS optimization directly with the video generation model, enabling more
538   efficient end-to-end 3D scene reconstruction. We are also interested in exploring consistent 4D scene
539   reconstruction. We believe that ReconX provides a promising research direction to craft intricate 3D
      worlds from video diffusion models and hope it will inspire more works in the future.

863   Implementation of PosEmb. The PosEmb implemented in our paper is a column-wise positional
      embedding function: R3 → RC , where C is the dimension of embedding. More specifically, the


                                                          16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      PosEmb function is implemented as follows: (1) Fixed Sinusoidal Basis: The basis e is a 3D
865   sinusoidal encoding: e = [sin(20 πp), sin(21 πp), . . . ], where p ∈ R3 is the position. (2) Em-
866   bedding Calculation: The input x is projected onto e and its sine and cosine are concatenated:
867   embeddings = concat(sin(proj), cos(proj)). (3) Learnable Transformation: The positional en-
868   coding is passed through an MLP along with the input x: y = MLP(concat(embeddings, x)). In
869   short, PosEmb combines a fixed sinusoidal encoding with a learnable MLP transformation.
870
      More details of transformer-based encoder. For the transformer-based encoder, we encode the
871   DUSt3R point cloud data to a fixed-length sparse representation of the point cloud. Specifically,
872   we first employ a subsampling based on farthest point sampling (FPS) to reduce the point cloud
873   to a smaller set of key points while retaining its overall structural characteristics. Then, we apply
874   cross-attention between the embeddings of the original point cloud and downsampled point cloud.
875   This mechanism can be interpreted as a form of partial self attention, where the downsampled points
876   act as query anchors that aggregate information from the original point cloud. The encoder is not
877   initialized from any pretrained models. Instead, it is trained jointly with the video diffusion model
878   in an end-to-end manner. This design choice ensures that the encoder is specifically adapted to the
879
      characteristics of DUSt3R point clouds in our experiment datasets.
880   Camera alignment. We consider that the camera pose from DUSt3R is not aligned with the
881   COLMAP cameras. Since we adopt the point cloud and camera poses from DUSt3R in our experi-
882   ment, it is necessary for us to unify the training and testing images into the same DUSt3R coordinate
883   system. Specifically, we process the training and testing images together through DUSt3R to obtain
884   the corresponding camera poses, and utilize only the point maps from the training set as the initial
885
      point cloud for optimizing 3DGS.
886   Test view selection. In comparison with feed-forward based methods, we follow MVSplat (Chen
887   et al., 2024a) and pixelSplat (Charatan et al., 2024) to choose test views in Easy Set. For Hard Set, we
888   choose the frame intervals much larger (i.e., > 200 frames) than Easy Set. For Tank-and-Templates
889   and DL3DV datasets, we select the training views evenly from all the frames and use every 8th of
890   the remaining frames for evaluation. For nine scenes in Mip-NeRF 360 dataset, we manually choose
891
      a training 9-view split of views that are uniformly distributed around the hemisphere and pointed
      toward the central object of interest. Then we further choose the 6- and 3-view splits to be subsets
892
      of the 9-view split.
