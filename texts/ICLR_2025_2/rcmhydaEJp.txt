                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Many challenges in the physical sciences can be framed as small data problems,
011            where theoretical progress is hindered by the sparsity, low-dimensionality, and/or
012            limited sample size of available empirical data compared to a physical system’s
013            numerous dynamical degrees of freedom. Developing trustworthy imputation
014            methods for these datasets holds immense scientific importance. Normalizing
015            flows are a promising model choice for imputation due to their ability to explicitly
016            estimate sample likelihoods. However, research has shown that normalizing flows
017            are often unreliable for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection in high-dimensional
018
               settings, which undermines their trustworthiness for imputation tasks. In contrast,
               low-dimensional settings provide opportunities to tractably evaluate and mitigate
019
               likelihood estimation errors, revealing strategies to reduce or eliminate specific
020
               error modes. We focus on the most stringent assumption in normalizing flows:
021            diffeomorphism between the target and base distributions. This assumption intro-
022            duces two distinct error modes, which we identify and address through a simple
023            and effective strategy. Our approach significantly enhances the trustworthiness of
024            normalizing flows for imputation in small data problems.
025
026
027   Many frontiers of physical science can be characterized as small data problems in which theoretical
028   progress is hindered due to the high costs and/or infeasibility of conducting experiments or making
029   observations (Hossenfelder, 2018; Coveney et al., 2016). We define small data as datasets that are
030   low-dimensional, sparse, and/or limited in sample size relative to the complexity of the underlying
031   probability distributions from which they are sampled. Examples of scientific small data problems
032   include the dependency of jets from defects in shocked metal surfaces on defect geometry, shock dy-
033
      namical parameters, and material properties, and other variables (Kaiser et al., 2024) as well as the
      dependency of measures of oceanic turbulence on local bathymetry, distance from the equator, strat-
034
      ification, depth, surface wind stresses, tidal forcing, and other variables (Gregg et al., 2018; Moum,
035
      2021). Trustworthy yet flexible imputation could not only improve predictive capabilities and un-
036   certainty quantification for problems like these but also inform the design of future experiments and
037   observations.
038
039
      Normalizing flows are an appealing tool for imputation because they offer exact likelihood estima-
      tion and efficient sampling (Papamakarios et al., 2021; Kobyzev et al., 2020; Prince, 2023). We
040
      refer a likelihood as the probability or density of a sample under a distribution, as is common in the
041
      literature (Zhang et al., 2021), even though the formal definition of the term refers to a measure of
042   how probable a set of parameters is, given some observed data. Flow trustworthiness, and therefore
043   trustworthy flow-based imputation, hinges on whether the model target distribution (i.e., the model
044   representation of the true target distribution) accurately represents the true target distribution. The
045   true target distribution is the probability distribution underlying the training data samples which is
046   usually unknown.
047
      A major impediment to flow trustworthiness is the issue that, when flows and other deep generative
048   models are applied to high dimensional datasets, they frequently assign higher likelihood to out-of-
049   distribution (OOD) samples than to in-distribution samples (Caterini & Loaiza-Ganem, 2022; Zhang
050   et al., 2021; Nalisnick et al., 2019). This phenomena obfuscates OOD sample detection (Yang et al.,
051   2024; Fang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). OOD sample detection, hereafter OOD detection, is the
052   task of identifying “whether the test example is from a different distribution from the training data”
053   (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017). OOD detection methods distinguish OOD samples as low likelihood
      samples under the model target distribution (Bishop, 1994).

054
      One explanation for model assignment of high likelihoods to OOD samples is the typical set hypoth-
055   esis, which states that the relevant definition of OOD should instead be determined by the typical set
056   of a distribution. A distribution’s typical set is the set that contains the majority of the probability
057   mass but not necessarily the highest densities or probability points (Choi et al., 2018; Nalisnick et al.,
058   2020; Wang et al., 2020; Morningstar et al., 2021). Other plausible explanations for the frequency
059   of this phenomena include model estimation errors (Zhang et al., 2021) and impossibility proofs that
060   show that OOD detection is not possible under certain conditions, such as in- and out-distribution
061   overlap (Fang et al., 2022).
062   OOD detection is a more tractably assessed problem in low dimensions than in high dimensions.
063   Here, we directly assess the impact of errors that are produced by an axiomatic constraint of nor-
064   malizing flows, the diffeomorphism of the model target and base distributions, in two dimensions.
065   While other aspects of normalizing flows engender errors (Kirichenko et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
066   2021), we find that the diffeomorphism constraint can induce significant OOD flow likelihood and
067   we provide a simple mitigation strategy to minimize OOD flow likelihood. We expect that the as-
068   sessment of errors produced by the diffeomorphism constraint presented here is contribute to model
069
      target distribution error for higher dimensional data because the constraint applies to all flow models.

470   5   D ISCUSSION
471
472   Prescribing multiple base distribution modes to match an unknown number of true target distribution
473   modes is not a common practice when training normalizing flows. For high-dimensional problems,
474   such as 256x256 pixel image datasets, this approach would be computationally expensive, as the
475   number of true target distribution modes could be extremely large. It seems plausible that target
476
      mode error may be a source of significant OOD likelihood in high dimensional datasets. The effects
      of target mode error on high dimensional datasets is an open question for future research.
477
478   For small data problems, our empirical results suggest that both target mode error and base mode
479   error can be eliminated by adjusting the number of base modes and using a simple criterion to
480   ensure mb→ = mb and mb = mt∗ (i.e., the criterion of Equation 20), even when the number of
481   true target distribution modes mt∗ is unknown (which is typically the case for real-world datasets).
482
      We have shown that both target mode error and base mode error can produce spurious OOD model
      likelihood. Therefore, the criterion can be used to improve model trustworthiness for small data
483
      imputation tasks by tractably eliminating two sources of spurious OOD likelihood.
