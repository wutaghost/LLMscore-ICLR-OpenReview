009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011            Voice assistants, such as Siri and Google Assistant, typically model audio and text
012            separately, resulting in lost speech information and increased complexity. Recent
013            efforts to address this with end-to-end Speech Large Language Models (LLMs)
014            trained with supervised finetuning (SFT) have led to models “forgetting” capabili-
015            ties from text-only LLMs. Our work proposes an alternative paradigm for training
016            Speech LLMs without instruction data, using the response of a text-only LLM to
017            transcripts as self-supervision. Importantly, this process can be performed without
018            annotated responses. We show that our Distilled Voice Assistant (DiVA) general-
019
               izes to Spoken Question Answering, Classification, and Translation. Furthermore,
               we show that DiVA better meets user preferences, achieving a 72% win rate com-
020
               pared with state-of-the-art models like Qwen 2 Audio, despite using >100x less
021
               training compute.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046
      As Large Language Models (LLMs) capabilities increase, so does the value of bringing these capa-
047
      bilities to new modalities, including audio and speech (Shu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Gong
048   et al., 2023). Speech is a natural interaction surface for language technology (Murad et al., 2019),
049   offering measurable efficiency gains for users (Ruan et al., 2018). One straightforward method of in-
050   tegrating speech with LLMs is to feed audio to an Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) model and
051   produce a text transcription for the LLM to use. However, this process loses meaningful information
052   carried through tone, pacing, and accent (Upadhyay et al., 2023) regardless of the transcription ac-
053   curacy. Furthermore, finetuning these pipelined systems requires supervision for both transcription
      and response generation, increasing annotation complexity and costs.

064
065   As such, LLMs that interface with speech directly have the potential to accelerate inference, reduce
066   annotation costs, and capture the rich information inevitably lost by ASR. In this pursuit, a variety of
067
      works have trained audio-encoders on top of LLMs (Ao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021b; Deshmukh
      et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a), many of which utilize the same well-established
068
      approach: large-scale multi-task supervised finetuning (SFT).
069
070   Models using SFT face several challenges. First, without a large degree of task diversity in their
071   training data, they often fail to generalize capabilities from the text-only LLM to speech. As ob-
072   served in Tang et al. (2023), freezing the weights of the text-only LLM is insufficient to prevent this
073
      “forgetting”. In order to generalize well, SFT must be trained with labeled data from a wide range
      of tasks and domains, with minimal imbalance between tasks. However, broad annotated speech
074
      instruction training data does not currently exist.
075
076   The limited instruction data that does exist is often collected from a small pool of speakers (Kim
077   et al., 2021; Tomasello et al., 2023) or intended for evaluation rather than training (Faisal et al.,
078   2021; Eisenstein et al., 2023). This lack of representation of speech from the wider population is
079
      likely to exacerbate biases in speech processing (Koenecke et al., 2020; Mengesha et al., 2021; Chan
      et al., 2022; Javed et al., 2023; Brewer et al., 2023). At present, Speech LLMs appear fundamentally
080
      limited by existing instruction data.
081
082   In this work, however, we argue that these “limitations” of existing data are artificially imposed
083   by SFT. The speech community has already invested in large-scale data collection from the inter-
084   net (Radford et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2023b), audiobooks (Panayotov et al., 2015;
085
      Pratap et al., 2020), and public archives (Galvez et al., 2021). Furthermore, several datasets have
      been explicitly gathered to represent diverse demographics (Porgali et al., 2023; Garg et al., 2023).
086
      However, these large-scale and diverse datasets are dominated by data in just one task: Automatic
087
      Speech Recognition (ASR). This means that models trained with SFT cannot make use of the en-
088   tirety of this data without “forgetting” non-ASR capabilities.
089
090   We solve the “forgetting” problem by training a model that generalizes well despite using only ASR
091
      data. Rather than relying on external labels, our Distilled Voice Assistant (DiVA) self-supervises
      learning using the output distribution of an LLM in response to transcripts as a target, a cross-modal
092
      form of context distillation (Snell et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024). We test our approach by training
093
      on just a single corpus, the CommonVoice, consisting of speech and transcriptions contributed by
094   volunteers around the world and recorded on their own devices (Ardila et al., 2019).
095
096   Despite this data simplicity, DiVA generalizes to Spoken Question Answering, Classification, and
097
      Translation. Furthermore, DiVA is preferred by users to our most competitive baseline Qwen 2
      Audio in 72% of trials despite DiVA using over 100x less training compute. Beyond contributing a
098
      new Speech LLM, DiVA creates a new approach to Speech LLMs that trains more efficiently and
099
      generalizes better without requiring investment in new speech instruction data.

102   2   R ELATED W ORK
103
104   LLMs have been extended to both audio and image inputs using cross-modal encoders. For example,
105   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b) enables image understanding by connecting CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
106   to Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023) through an MLP layer. Several recent works (Zhang et al., 2023;
107   Gong et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; 2024) have connected audio-encoders (Gong
      et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2021) to LLMs. There are two critical questions in this space.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      How can audio features be transformed into a reasonable number of LLM input embeddings?
109   Audio comes at high sample rates, and therefore, audio encoders often have a large number of
110   outputs. For these features to be usable by the LLM, the dimensionality must be reduced, either
111   by stacking consecutive features (Wu et al., 2023a; Fathullah et al., 2024) or learning an adapter-
112   module, such as an MLP (Liu et al., 2023b; Gong et al., 2023), or Q-Former (Dai et al., 2023; Tang
113   et al., 2023).
114
      While learned approaches are more flexible, allowing for an adaptive reduction, they generally re-
115   quire learning a cross-attention mechanism, which generally requires significant training (Li et al.,
116   2023a). In this work, we find the best of both worlds by leveraging the Whisper decoder (Radford
117   et al., 2023) to initialize the text-audio cross-attention mechanism of a Q-Former.
118
      How can speech LLMs be trained to achieve instruction following abilities using existing data?
119
      Prior work has explored two routes for creating instruction data without major financial investment.
120
      The first approach is to transform many existing datasets into an instruction-following format (Dai
121   et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a). In this case,
122   limitations are often set by datasets that are not aligned with intended LLM usage and imbalances
123   across tasks. The second approach is to train on synthetic responses to text representations of the
124   new modality from commercial models (Liu et al., 2023b; Gong et al., 2023).
125
      Our approach is most related to the latter. Rather than generating data with an external model, we
126
      capitalize on the idea that instruction-tuned language models can provide valuable learning signals
127   as long as the input is within the textual modality. Using the output distribution in response to
128   text transcripts, we can more strongly guarantee the transfer of existing capabilities using context
129   distillation (Snell et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024).
130
      How can we train foundation models for speech using open and permissively licensed data?
131
      Recently, frontier LLMs have begun integrating native speech capabilities. Unlike prior speech
132
      foundation models (Baevski et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2021; Peng
133   et al., 2023; 2024), these models offer virtual assistant capabilities rather than self-supervised audio
134   representations or transcriptions. It is unclear to what degree these results are dependent on internal
135   datasets, especially since even the state-of-the-art open-access Speech LLM with such capabilities
136   reports no training data details other than size (Chu et al., 2024).
137
      Similar to the Open Whisper-style Speech Model (OWSM) initiative (Peng et al., 2024), we use only
138
      open and permissively-licensed data. Furthermore, unlike the baselines we compare to in Table 1,
139   we release the training code, rather than just the inference code, which can help reproduce a DiVA-
140   style model easily. Beyond our novel method, we believe this too broadens the ability to train and
141   understand Speech LLMs.

      3     M ETHOD
145
146   DiVA is an end-to-end voice and text assistant, trained using the process shown in Figure 1. We focus
147
      heavily on effectively using pretrained models in each domain. Similar to prior works, we initialize
      the audio encoder from the 1.5B parameter Whisper-Large-v3 model. Unlike prior works, we use
148
      all components of Whisper: not only reusing the encoder but also initializing a Q-Former from the
149
      decoder. We train this architecture using distillation loss on the input and output distribution of the
150   text-only LLM, which we discuss in Section 3.2.

      3.1   M ODEL I NITIALIZATION
153
154   When adding multimodal capabilities to an existing language model, the new modality must be
155   represented as embeddings that can used in place of text token embeddings. Achieving this goal
156   has two steps. First, meaningful features must be extracted from the input modality. Second, these
157   features must be aggregated to be in-distribution for the downstream language model.
158
159   Audio Feature Extraction We follow prior works (Chu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023) and use the
160   Whisper encoder (Radford et al., 2023). Whisper first transforms the raw audio signal into a 128-
161   channel time-frequency domain Mel-spectrogram. This is then passed through two 1D convolutions
      and used as embeddings fed to an unmodified Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Audio-Text Feature Alignment While the Whisper encoder extracts meaningful audio features,
163   they are encoded at high granularity, with one token for every 40 milliseconds of input audio.
164   By comparison, humans speak around one syllable every 150 milliseconds on average across lan-
165   guages (Coupé et al., 2019), and most tokens in an LLM vocabulary are made up of several sylla-
166   bles. This creates a mismatch between the granulatity between the Whisper encoder outputs and the
167   downstream LLMs input distribution.
168
      Prior work (Tang et al., 2023) addresses this using a Querying Transformer (Q-Former, Li et al.
169   2023a), which learns static query embeddings with cross-attention to keys and values features from
170   another modality. Given audio embeddings A, the Q-Former learns a transformer with a cross
                                        ⊺
171   attention mechanism σ( Q(KA  √

      are projection matrices for the audio tokens. Conceptually, this cross-attention mechanism learns to
173
      dynamically aggregate information from the audio tokens into text-like tokens. This comes at the
174   cost of significant training required to train the transformer from scratch.
175
176   The Whisper decoder, which prior work discards, is trained with a similar goal for ASR: mapping

186   3.2      D ISTILLATION L OSSES
187
188   We optimize two loss functions based on audio recordings and corresponding text transcripts from
189   ASR data. First, we minimize the distance between embeddings of audio and text on the input side
190   of the LLM, similar to prior work for Vision-LMS (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a). Then, we
191   minimize the KL Divergence between the output distribution in response to audio and text as a form
192   of cross-modal context distillation (Mu et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2022).
193

      3.2.2       D ISTILLATION FROM O UTPUT E MBEDDING D ISTANCE
217
218   Voice assistant models should give coherent, helpful, and harmless responses to user speech. Thank-
219   fully, many openly accessible text-only LLMs have been extensively refined for these objectives. As
220   such, our challenge is not to learn these behaviors but instead to transfer them to the audio modal-
221   ity. While, in theory, input token alignment could achieve this, even minor differences in input
222
      embeddings can significantly affect model behavior in practice (Cai et al., 2022).
223   Distillation loss, on the other hand, directly optimizes for the similarity of the output distribu-
224   tion (Hinton et al., 2015). Rather than distilling a large model into a smaller model, recent work has
225   applied to distilling useful context into model weights, a process termed context distillation (Snell
226   et al., 2022; Mu et al., 2024). Here, we apply context distillation across modalities, aiming to distill
227   a text context into the audio modality under the assumption that the model should respond similarly
228
      to audio and text for most inputs.
229   In prior context distillation works, the full Kullback–Leibler (KL) Divergence has been shown to be
230   prohibitively expensive at training time due to the large vocabulary of modern LLMs. Therefore, the
231   KL Divergence is instead approximated by sampling random tokens (Snell et al., 2022). In our case,
232   where the output embedding matrix is frozen, we show that there is an objective function easier to
233   optimize:
234   Lemma 1. Given the probability Pt from a teacher model and the probability Ps from a student
235   model, the KL Divergence is defined as KL(Pt , Ps ) = Pt · (log Pt − log Ps ). For a transformer
236   language model, Ps = σ(Os hs ) where hs is the final hidden state, Os is the output embedding
237   matrix, and σ is the softmax function. Let θs be the student weights which we are trying to train to
238
      minimize the KL Divergence, then
239                                  argθs min ||hs − ht ||2 ⊂ argθs min KL(Pt , Ps )
240
241   Proof. The KL divergence is minimized when Ps = Pt . Based on our definition of LM probability,
242   this is equivalent to achieving σ(Os hs ) = σ(Ot ht ). In the special case we consider, where the
243   teacher and student are initialized from the same weights, and Os is held constant, we know that
244   Os = Ot . Thus, a non-unique global minimum will be achieved when hs = ht , where the non-
245   uniqueness comes from the softmax function σ, which is not injective.
246
247   More importantly, we find that: (1) The gradient for L2 loss is much smoother than minimizing
248   the KL divergence empirically2 . (2) Since the vocabulary size of most modern LLMs is far larger
249   than the hidden dimension, the distance between hidden states can be computed using far fewer
250
      operations than the KL divergence. In practice, we optimize the similarity of only the first predicted
      next token (after all I text tokens/all Q audio tokens) for efficiency, as Morris et al. (2023) has shown
251
      that just a single token probability encodes significant information, both for prior and future tokens.
252
253   Notably, training with this loss only guarantees that the output distribution is well aligned in response
254   to audio. However, our intuition is that this loss alone is likely to be less robust to input distribution
255   shift without our token alignment loss, which we explore in Section 6.

      5.2     Q UALITATIVE U SER S TUDY
464   Finally, to get a sense of how well suited the resulting models are to user preferences, which may
465   or may not be well captured in existing benchmarks, we recruit participants to compare DiVA to the
466   top performing baseline, Qwen 2 Audio.
467
468   5.2.1    R ECRUITMENT & S TUDY D ESIGN
469
470   We recruit 53 participants on the Prolific platform to provide preference ratings. Each user was
471
      allowed to contribute a maximum of 10 ratings, but able to opt-out at any time, resulting in 522
      preference ratings comparing the models. We paid users 2.50$ per 10 ratings, which took fewer than
472
      10 minutes of active time for all annotators involved, for an effective pay rate of 15$ per hour. We
473
      report annotator demographics in Appendix A.5.
474
475   We pre-screened for users who report familiarity with existing LLM chatbots and virtual assistants
476   (e.g. ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude and others). In order to avoid biasing our participants, we prompt
477
      them without reference to specific tasks to Record something you’d say to an AI Assistant! Think
      about what you usually use Siri, Google Assistant, or ChatGPT for. Users were then shown re-
478
      sponses from each model, without knowledge of which model was which. To avoid any positional
479
      bias, we shuffle the order which users were shown model responses for each recording submitted.

513   Impacts of Token Alignment Loss This might raise the question: why use the token-alignment
514   loss if it performs so poorly? In evaluations on question answering, this is certainly reasonable since
515   using the KL Divergence loss alone already leads to stronger performance than the SFT baselines.
516   However, for translation and emotion recognition tasks, we see near-zero results from KL Diver-
517   gence loss alone. Qualitatively, we observe that the distillation only model replies directly to the
518   speech regardless of the text instructions.
519
      We quantify this failure to adhere to instructions for the translation task using FastText Language
520
      ID (Joulin et al., 2017) on the outputs, under the assumption that outputs which are not in the correct
521
      target language are the result of ignored instructions. DiVA outputs the correct language 74% of the
522   time while the distillation only model outputs the correct language only 1.4% of the time4 .
523
524   This indicates that the token-alignment loss is key to achieving a model using distillation that can
525
      follow both audio instructions and text instructions such as a system prompt.

      7       C ONCLUSION
529
530   In summary, we release DiVA, an end-to-end Voice Assistant model capable of processing text
531   and audio natively. Our cross-modal distillation loss from text to speech showcases a promising
532   direction for cost-effective capabilities transfer from one modality to another. Our Distilled Voice
533
      Assistant generalizes to Spoken Question Answering, Classification, and Translation despite only
      being trained on transcription data. Furthermore, DiVA is preferred by users to our most competitive
534
      baseline Qwen 2 Audio in 72% of instances despite DiVA taking over 100x less training compute.
535
      Together, these contributions highlight a path forward for rapid adaptation of LLMs to Speech,
536   without large investments in new training datasets5 .

873    A.2                        T OY E XPERIMENT ON KL D IVERGENCE VERSUS H IDDEN S TATE A LIGNMENT
874
875   Beyond being a valid and efficient approximate of the KL Divergence, the L2 loss should offer a
876   more stable gradient, especially early in training when the output distributions are extremely differ-
877   ent. When Pt is positive and Ps is near zero, the KL divergence explodes to extremely large values
878   which can make optimization difficult and subject to significant numerical error.
879
      In order to test this intuition, we set up a toy experiment where the student model outputs a single
880   hidden state hs and the teacher model outputs a single hidden state ht . In this highly simplified
881   space, each model is fully parameterized by the these hidden states. We initialize and output vo-
882   cabulary from the normal distribution with 32, 000 vocabulary items. Then, we optimize hs based
883   on either the L2 distance with ht or the KL Divergence with the output probabilities. Finally, for
884   both procedures, we optimize for 100 steps with stochastic gradient descent, running the experiment
885   100 times at logarithmically increasing embedding dimensions, and plot the final KL divergence
886   achieved under each loss function.

      HeySquad HeySquad Wu et al. (2023b) is a spoken question answering (QA) dataset that aims to
923
      measure the QA ability of digital agents. It is based on the SQuAD dataset Rajpurkar et al. (2016)
924
      with 76K human-spoken and 97K machine-generated questions, and the corresponding answers. We
925   evaluate the models on the open-source validation set with around 4K QA pairs.
926
927
928   Spoken Dialect Question Answering (SDQA) SDQA Faisal et al. (2021) assesses the robustness
929   of Spoken Language Understanding to global phonological variation in English. The dataset is made
930   up of the same 1000 questions spoken and recorded by speakers in 10 accent regions where English
931   is frequently spoken. We evaluate on the 494 of these questions which contain ground truth answers.

934   A.3.2    S PEECH C LASSIFICATION
935
936   Emotion Recognition Interactive Emotional Dyadic Motion Capture (IEMOCAP) IEMO-
937   CAP Busso et al. (2008) is a dataset of ∼12 hours of videos, audio, motion capture, and transcripts of
938   actors performing both improvised and scripted scenes. The seven professional and three student ac-
939   tors perform emotionally expressive scenes. Each conversation turn in each scene was labeled by six
940   evaluators as demonstrating “happiness,” “sadness,” “anger,” “surprise,” “fear,” “disgust,” “frustra-
941   tion,” “excitement,” “neutral state,” or “other.” We follow Yang et al. (2024) and remove unbalanced
942
      class labels, resulting in 1241 audio utterances in the fifth fold used by Tang et al. (2023).
943   Multimodal EmotionLines Dataset (MELD) MELD Poria et al. (2019) contains 13,708 utterances
944   labeled by emotion and collected from the sitcom Friends. MELD builds on EmotionLines Hsu
945   et al. (2018); however, the authors of MELD ask annotators to watch the videos instead of simply
946   reading the transcripts to produce labels. Three graduate student annotators labeled all utterances
947   for emotions: “anger,” “disgust,” “fear,” “joy,” “neutral,” “sadness,” and “surprise,” as well as for
948
      sentiments “positive,” “negative,” “neutral.” We evaluate on the test set of 2608 utterances.
949
950
951
      Communicative Intent Recognition Multimodal Sarcasm Dataset (MUSTARD) MUS-
      TARD Castro et al. (2019) is a collection of 690 clips from the TV shows Friends, The Golden
952
      Girls, The Big Bang Theory, and Sarcasmaholics Anonymous, labeled as sarcastic or non-sarcastic
953
      by three annotators. The clips were collected primarily from YouTube using keywords like Chandler
954   sarcasm, Friends sarcasm, etc. and sampled from MELD Poria et al. (2019). The final dataset was
955   filtered to have an even number of labels of sarcastic and non-sarcastic clips. We evaluate on all 690
956   clips to test the models’ capability in understanding intended sarcasm.
957
      URFunny URFunny (Hasan et al., 2019) is a multimodal humor recognition benchmark constructed
958
      from 90.23 hours of TED talk recordings, spanning 1741 speakers and 417 topics. TED produces
959
      transcripts for the talks, which contain ”[laughter]” markers that show when the audience laughs.
960   The authors sampled the context and punchline before laughter markers for 8257 positive examples
961   and random parts of the transcript without laughter markers for 8257 negative examples. URFun-
962   nyV2 filters out noise and reduces overlap in examples. We evaluate 7614 examples from the train
963   split of URFunnyV2 to evaluate the models’ ability to understand speakers’ humorous intents.
