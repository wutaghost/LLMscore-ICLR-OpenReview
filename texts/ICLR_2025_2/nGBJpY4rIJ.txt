011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Developing grounding techniques for LLMs poses two requirements for interactive
014            environments, i.e., (i) the presence of rich knowledge beyond the scope of existing
015            LLMs and (ii) the complexity of tasks that require strategic reasoning. Existing
016            environments fail to meet both requirements due to their simplicity or reliance on
017            commonsense knowledge already encoded in LLMs for interaction. In this paper,
018            we present PokéLLMon, a new benchmark enriched with fictional game knowledge
019
               and characterized by the intense, dynamic, and adversarial gameplay of Pokémon
               battles, setting new challenges for the development of grounding and reasoning
020
               techniques in interactive environments. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that
021
               existing LLMs lack game knowledge and struggle in Pokémon battles. We investi-
022            gate grounding techniques that leverage game knowledge and self-play experience,
023            and provide a thorough analysis of reasoning methods from a new perspective of
024            action consistency. Additionally, we introduce higher-level reasoning challenges
025            when playing against human players. The implementation of our benchmark is
026            anonymously released at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/PokeLLMon.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The success of Large Language Models (LLMs) comes from encoding massive textual data by
032   predicting the next token with huge model capacity, and generalizing well to various tasks (Ouyang
033   et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Xi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). The
034   pre-trained LLMs, encode the knowledge from text and exhibit cognitive abilities such as reasoning
035   and planning, ways of organizing knowledge described in text inherently.
036   In other words, LLMs lack experiential grounding (Mahowald et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), which
037   prevents them from understanding new concepts outside their scope, or evolving their reasoning
038   abilities in the interactive environments. Recent research focuses on grounding LLMs in games via
039   Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Carta et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024) or supervised fine-tuning (Zhu
040   et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023). However, it has been observed that even a well-trained agent still lacks
041   generalizability to slightly altered settings, such as substituting action tokens with synonyms (Carta
042   et al., 2023), suggesting that simplistic environments with limited scenarios do not best facilitate the
043   development of grounding and reasoning techniques.
044   Developing grounding and reasoning methods for LLMs places two requirements for interactive
045   environments: (i) the presence of knowledge beyond the scope of existing LLMs and (ii) the
046   complexity of tasks that demand strategic reasoning abilities. The environments used in previous
047   work do not meet both of these requirements due to their reliance on commonsense knowledge
048   encoded in LLMs (Shridhar et al., 2020; Xiang et al., 2024) (a locked door can be unlocked with a
049   key), or their simplicity and limited number of scenarios (Carta et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024).
050   Scope and Contributions: In this paper, we introduce PokéLLMon, a new grounding and reasoning
051   benchmark for LLMs in Pokémon Battles, which sets a new challenge for LLMs to master fictional
052   game knowledge that falls outside of their current scope (Cabello et al., 2023). To date, there are over
053   1,000 Pokémon species and 900 battle moves (bul, 2024b;a), offering a wealth of game knowledge
      and a large amount of combination possibilities, making the game highly dynamic. Furthermore,

071
      This paper conducts comprehensive evaluation of existing LLMs on game knowledge prediction and
      Pokémon battles. Empirical evaluations demonstrate that LLMs, especially open-source LLMs, suffer
072
      from a severe lack of game knowledge and thus struggle to generate effective actions, indicating our
073
      benchmark is a good testbed for grounding techniques. To ground LLMs in games, we first evaluate
074   the impact of game knowledge for different LLMs. Experiments show that the improvement of
075   knowledge is highly related to the inherent reasoning abilities of LLMs, suggesting that when the game
076   knowledge is not the bottleneck, reaching a higher-level performance requires good reasoning ability.
077   Further, we evaluate grounding with self-evolution techniques, which shows that the adversarial
078   setting of PokéLLMon offers an ideal curriculum for learning from self-play experience.
079
      In intense adversarial games, action consistency is an important indicator of performance. Through
080   our analysis, we discover that existing reasoning approaches such as Chain-of-Thoughts (Wei et al.,
081   2022) (CoT) and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) can both lead to inconsistent actions, i.e., the
082   LLM frequently switches Pokémon in consecutive steps and wastes chances to attack. To this
083   end, we introduce an approach that recursively refines the thoughts from the previous steps and
084   can significantly enhance action consistency and gameplay performance. Finally, to demonstrate
085   higher-level reasoning challenges, we test the best-performing LLM in battles against invited human
086   players. The evaluation shows that the LLM player struggles to overcome human players’ attrition
087   and misdirection strategies, suggesting a significant room for improvement in reasoning approaches.
088   In summary, this paper makes three contributions:
089
090   • We introduce PokeLLMon, a benchmark that poses new challenges for grounding and reasoning in
091     an environment which features abundant fictional game knowledge and strategic gameplay.
092   • We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of LLMs’ gameplay performance, suggesting that their lack
093     of game knowledge and struggle in Pokémon battles. We further investigate grounding techniques
094     that leverage game knowledge and self-play experience.
095   • We provide a thorough analysis of reasoning approaches from a new perspective of action con-
096     sistency, and introduce an effective approach to improve consistency. Additionally, we present
097     high-level reasoning challenges when competing against disciplined human players.

152   3        E NVIRONMENT
153
154   As shown in Figure 1, the environment of PokéLLMon follows a client-server architecture, where
155   the client is implemented as a text interface for LLMs to perceive the game, and the server is an
156   open-source game engine 1 for game execution. The game is synchronized at every step: In each step,
157   both players receive observations and select actions synchronously, and the game engine processes
158   these actions to calculate the next step of the game state. In a battle, each player sends out one
159   Pokémon onto the field, keeping the others off the field for potential switches. The winning condition
160   is to make all the opponent’s Pokémon faint by reducing their Hit Points (HP) to zero.

356
357   In intense adversarial games, inconsistent actions often lead to defeat by wasting opportunities to make
358   strategic moves. To benchmark existing reasoning approaches, we introduce two additional metrics
359   that measure the degree of action inconsistency and are closely linked to gameplay performance.
360   Metrics: In Pokémon Battles, there are two categories of steps: (1) force switch step that is
361   compulsory to switch when the pokemon on-the-field faints and the player decides which pokemon
362   off-the-field to switch in; (2) active step, i.e., to take move or switch Pokemon. Switching in an
363   active step provides advantages, such as having a Pokémon with type effectiveness over the opposing
364   Pokémon, however, it also means to give the opposing Pokemon a free turn to take a move (and
365   abandon the boost of stats if have). Frequent switching can waste opportunities to attack and will lead
366   to defeat. Therefore, we use switch rate and consecutive switch rate to measure the action consistency
367
      of a player, formalized as follows:

      opponent.

404   Analysis: Table 6 reports the gameplay performance with reasoning approaches. Surprisingly, we
405   observe that approaches like CoT, ToT and Reflexion significantly decreases the win rate and battle
406   score compared to the vanilla IOPrompt without any reasoning. According to the metrics, the drop of
407   game performance can be attributed to the increase in consecutive switch rate, suggesting that with
408
      reasoning, the agent is tend to switch in a new pokemon and switch it out in the next turn, without
      taking moves. Below, we break down the analysis for every approach.
409
410   CoT: Why does CoT lead to action inconsistency? Let us consider two cases: In the first case,
411   our Pokémon is facing an opponent, and in the second case, everything is the same except that the
412   opponent has boosted its attack stats twofold. For IOPrompt, the difference between two cases is
413   only two extra tokens indicating the boosted stats. However, with reasoning, the LLM will describe
414
      the disadvantage of facing a boosted opponent in its thoughts. From a generation perspective, the
      thoughts increase the discrepancy between two observations in the representation space, making the
415
      LLM more likely to generate different actions. From a gameplay perspective, conditioned on these
416
      thoughts, the LLM tends to protect its Pokémon from fainting by switching it out of the battlefield,
417   yet neglects the fact that consecutive switching actually wastes the chance to attack.
418
419   SC-CoT and ToT: As show in Table 6, Compared to CoT, SC-CoT decreases the consecutive switch
420
      rate while increasing the switch rate, which suggests that SC-CoT is more consistent in consecutive
      steps with similar observations, yet more likely to switch Pokémon when facing different opposing
421
      Pokémon. This is because the majority voting reduces the inconsistency brought by the randomness
422
      of sampling. However, it enhances the probability of switching when switching is the predominant
423   option in the probabilistic distribution conditioned on thoughts. A competitor, ToT, replacing the
424   majority voting of SC-CoT by self-evaluation, although outperforms SC-CoT in easily-evaluated tasks
425   like Game of 24 (Yao et al., 2023), works even worse than CoT and SC-CoT in our benchmark. This
426   is because the LLM is unable to make correct self-evaluation due to its lack of game experience, and
427   thus tends to agree with the proposals, which does not provide any benefit for reducing inconsistency,
428   and sometimes even prioritizes the sub-optimal actions, leading to a drop of performance.
429   Last-Thoughts: We introduce a simple yet effective solution: Last-Thoughts. By recursively taking
430   the thoughts from the last step into current reasoning procedure, we observe a significant drop of

433   46.67%. With Last-Thoughts, the LLM refines its thoughts based on its previous thoughts instead of
434   generating from scratch. For the perspective of generation, last thoughts can be deemed as a summary
435   of the last observation, thus two consecutive observations become more similar in the representation
436   space, leading to consistent outputs.
437
      Reflexion: As shown in Figure 3, the LLM uses reflections on the previous step to generate a new
438
      action. There are two reasons that reflection does not work well: (1) unlike static task settings (Shrid-
439   har et al., 2020), Pokémon battles are dynamic, suggesting that reflections from the past are likely
440   outdated; (2) Even if not outdated, the LLM tends to think that the last action did not meet its
441   expectations, and thus chooses to switch to a new Pokémon to attack, leading to the increase of
442   inconsistency. However, we believe reflection can be beneficial in a dynamic environment if adjusted
443   appropriately, i.e., by generating reflections offline and retrieving them based on similarity during
444   battles. As a result, the problem of outdated reflections can be addressed, and the agent also avoids
445   wasting chances in a trial-and-error style.
446   Impact of temperature on consistency: Temperature τ controls the sharpness of distribution for
447   sampling tokens. In Table 6 we report the performance of IOPrompt w.r.t. temperatures varying from
448   0 to 2, suggesting that a lower temperature can reduce inconsistency and increase performance.
449
450      Findings: In an intense adversarial game, action consistency is an important indicator for game-
451      play performance. The inconsistency introduced by CoT is due to the increased discrepancy
452      between consecutive observations, and can be reduced by integrating the previous thoughts in
453      the next step reasoning (Last-Thoughts). Furthermore, a lack of game experience and the highly
454      dynamic feature undermine approaches relying on self-evaluation such as ToT and Reflexion.

      opponent’s HP with occasional attacks or regular damage moves such as Toxic.

      7       C ONCLUSION
522
523   This paper introduces a new grounding and reasoning benchmark for LLMs in Pokémon Battles,
524   featuring rich game knowledge and a high ceiling for strategic reasoning due to its highly dynamic,
525   intense and adversarial gameplay, especially in the presence of professional opponents. Through
526   experiments we verify the lackness of game knowledge and experience of existing LLMs, investigate
527   the potential grounding solutions with game knowledge and self-play experience. With a thorough
528
      analysis, we identify the weakness of existing reasoning approaches from a new perspective of action
      consistency, and provide an effective solution to reduce inconsistency. Finally, we introduce two
529
      advanced strategies exhibited by human players that pose higher-level reasoning challenges. Overall,
530
      we believe PokéLLMon is an ideal testbed for developing grounding and reasoning techniques.

      A     M ETHOD D ESCRIPTIONS
703
704
      ExpertSystem: ExpertSystem simulates a human player’s decision-making procedure. The approach
705   includes a state evaluation function that calculates the battle advantage between the player’s Pokémon
706   and the opponent’s, taking into account factors such as type-effectiveness, current HP and stats of
707   Pokémon of both sides. If the current situation is favorable, the approach calculates the damage for
708   attacks by considering move power, type effectiveness, and the stats of the Pokémon, and finally
709   selects the move with the highest damage value; If the current situation is unfavorable and no
710   strategic moves are available, it evaluates whether switching to another Pokémon could lead to a
711   more advantageous situation using state evaluation function.
712   Overall, ExpertSystem is programmed with a decision-making procedure with numeric damage
713   calculation, enabling it to generate effective actions for both move selection and switching, serving as
714   a competitive opponent in our experiments.
715
      RFT and DPO fine-tuning: We first supervisedly fine-tune LLaMA2-7B on 10,240 sampled frames
716
      (steps) of the expert system to ensure it 100% outputs admissible actions, as the initial LLM. We run
717   the self-evolution iteration for 5 times, and in each iteration, we sample 100,000 frames (steps). The
718   batch size for training is set to 64, and each sample is trained once. The learning rate is set to 1e-5
719   for RFT and 1e-6 for DPO based on empirical hyper-parameter tuning. RMSProp is adopted as the
720   optimizer. For DPO, β is set to 0.1.
721
      Reasoning approaches:
722
723   • In-Out Prompt (IOPrompt): At each time step, LLM takes the observation as the input and directly
724     output an action. We do not use few-shot examples as they do not provide obvious improvement
725     (Table 3).
726   • Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022): At each step, LLM generates thoughts to analyze
727     the battle-field, then outputs an action conditioned on the thoughts. The reasoning includes the
728     comparison of stats, type-effectiveness and evaluation of moves.
729
      • Self Consistency (SC-CoT) (Wang et al., 2022b): At each step, LLM independently generates b
730     reasoning branches and do majority voting for the final output action.
731
      • Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) (Yao et al., 2023): At each step, LLM analyzes the battlefield and proposes
732
        b top actions. LLM then criticizes and evaluates these actions with reasoning and selects the best
733     action to output.
734
      • Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023): LLM reflects on the outcome of the action at−1 taken in the
735
        previous step, and use the reflection to generate action at in the next time step.
736
737   • Last-Thoughts: At each step, LLM generates CoT reasoning by taking into consideration the
738
        thoughts from the last step.
