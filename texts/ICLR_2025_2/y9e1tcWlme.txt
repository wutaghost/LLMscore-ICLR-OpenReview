011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Markov decision processes (MDPs) are used to model a wide variety of applica-
014            tions ranging from game playing over robotics to finance. Their optimal policy
015
               typically maximizes the expected sum of rewards given at each step of the decision
               process. However, a large class of problems does not fit straightforwardly into this
016
               framework: Non-cumulative Markov decision processes (NCMDPs), where instead
017
               of the expected sum of rewards, the expected value of an arbitrary function of the
018            rewards is maximized. Example functions include the maximum of the rewards or
019            their mean divided by their standard deviation. In this work, we introduce a general
020            mapping of NCMDPs to standard MDPs. This allows all techniques developed
021            to find optimal policies for MDPs, such as reinforcement learning or dynamic
022            programming, to be directly applied to the larger class of NCMDPs. Focusing on
023            reinforcement learning, we show applications in a diverse set of tasks, including
024            classical control, portfolio optimization in finance, and discrete optimization prob-
025            lems. Given our approach, we can improve both final performance and training
026
               time compared to relying on standard MDPs.

      Preliminaries In an MDP, an agent iteratively interacts with an environment during a trajectory.
082   At each time step t, the agent receives the current state st of the environment and selects an action
083   at by sampling from its policy π(at |st ), which is a probability distribution over all possible actions
084   given a state. The next state of the environment st+1 and the agent’s immediate reward rt are then
085   sampled from the transition probability distribution p(rt , st+1 |st , at ), which depends on the MDP.
086   This process repeats until it reaches a terminal state. The ideal policy of the agent maximizes the
087   expected sum of immediate rewards, i.e. Equation (1). Note that we take this ideal policy to be part
088   of the definition of an MDP to distinguish MDPs from NCMDPs with different ideal policies. For
089   simplicity, we discuss only non-discounted, episodic MDPs. However, generalization to discounted
090
      settings is straightforward.
091
092

093

094

189
      The question remains how to find the functions u and ρ given a new objective f . In principle,
      methods developed based on past (Bacchus et al., 1996; 1997) or future (Thiébaux et al., 2006) linear
190
      temporal logic could be used. However, these require an expensive computation over all possible
191
      states of the MDP making them inefficient for online learning. There is always at least one MDP
192   corresponding to a given NCMDP since we can take ht = [r̃0 , . . . , r̃t−1 ] with u(ht , r̃t ) = [ht , r̃t ]
193   and ρ(ht , r̃t ) = f ([ht , r̃t ]) − f (ht ). However, this is not ideal because it leads to a state size that
194   grows linearly with the trajectory length. In Appendix B.1, we provide a necessary and sufficient
195   condition for objectives f with constant size additional state information ht . However, this condition
196   might be difficult to check in practice. Therefore, we also provide a sufficient, but not necessary,
197   condition that is easier to verify and covers a large class of functions, e.g. all functions in Table 1. For
198   functions in this class, we also provide an explicit construction of u, ρ, and ht (see Appendix B.2). In
199   practice, we have empirically observed that a simple analytical consideration leads to ht of a small
200
      and constant size for each of the objectives f considered in this manuscript, which is desirable for
      efficient learning and integration with standard function estimators such as neural networks.

204
205   From an implementation perspective, our scheme of mapping NCMDPs to MDPs requires minimal
206   effort, since we can treat both the NCMDP and the used MDP solver as black boxes by simply adding
207   a layer between them, as shown in Figure 1. In addition, our treatment facilitates online learning and
208   does not require any computationally expensive preprocessing of the NCMDP. This opens the door
209   for researchers with specific domain knowledge, who are not necessarily experts in reinforcement
210   learning, to use standard libraries such as stable-baselines3 (Raffin et al., 2021) to solve their
211   non-cumulative problems. Conversely, it allows reinforcement learning experts to quickly tackle
212
      existing environments using our method.

318   seeds each and the standard deviation of different sets of seeds is reported.
319   While the Sharpe ratio is most widely adopted in finance, our method opens up the possibility to
320   maximize it also in other scenarios where risk-adjusted rewards are desirable, i.e. all problems where
321   consistent rewards with low variance are more important than a higher cumulative reward. For
322   example, in chronic disease management, maintaining stable health metrics is preferable to sporadic
323   improvements. In emergency or customer service, ensuring predictable response times is often more
      important than occasional fast responses mixed with slow ones.

470   4   R ELATED W ORK
471
472   A special case of NCMDPs is first considered by Quah & Quek (2006), who adapt Q-learning
473   to the max objective by redefining the temporal difference error of their learning algorithms and
474   demonstrate their algorithms on an optimal stopping problem. However, they do not adapt state
475   space and do not provide theoretical convergence guarantees. Gottipati et al. (2020) rediscover
476   the same algorithm, apply it to molecule generation, and provide convergence guarantees for their
477
      method, while Eyckerman et al. (2022) consider the same algorithm for application placement in fog
      environments. Cui & Yu (2023) finally show a shortcoming of the method used in the above papers:
478
      It is guaranteed to converge to the optimal policy only in deterministic settings. Additionally, they
479
      provide convergence guarantees of Q-learning in deterministic environments for a larger class of
480   functions f , focusing on the min function for network routing applications. In Appendix B.1 we show
481   that the class of objectives f considered by Cui & Yu (2023) is a subclass of all objectives that lead
482   to constant size extra state information ht using our method. Independently, the max function is also
483   used in the field of safety reinforcement learning in deterministic environments both for Q-learning
484   (Fisac et al., 2015; 2019; Hsu et al., 2021) and policy-based reinforcement learning (Yu et al., 2022).
485   Moflic & Paler (2023) use a reward function with parameters that depend on the past rewards of the
      trajectory to tackle quantum circuit optimization problems that require large intermittent negative


                                                            9
486
      rewards, albeit without providing theoretical convergence guarantees. Additionally, Wang et al.
487   (2020) investigate planning problems with non-cumulative objectives within deterministic settings.
488   They provide provably efficient planning algorithms for a large class of functions f by discretizing
489   rewards and appending them to the states. Recently, Veviurko et al. (2024) showed how to use the
490   max objective also in probabilistic settings by augmenting state space and provide convergence
491   guarantees for both Q-learning and policy-based methods. They then show experiments with their
492   algorithm yielding improvements in MDPs with shaped rewards. However, they do not redefine
493   the rewards, which requires adaptation of the implementation of their MDP solvers. Our method
494   described above reduces to an effectively equivalent algorithm to theirs in the special case of the max
495
      objective.
496   A limitation of all works discussed above is that they require a potentially complicated adaptation
497   of their reinforcement learning algorithms and only consider specific MDP solvers or specific non-
498   cumulative objectives. They are also limited to deterministic settings, except for Veviurko et al.
499   (2024), which consider only the max objective.
500
501   5    D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
502
503   In this work, we described a mapping from a decision process with a general non-cumulative objective
504   (NCMDP) to a standard Markov decision process (MDP) applicable in deterministic and probabilistic
505   settings. As our method is agnostic to the algorithm used to solve the resulting MDP, it works for
506   arbitrary action spaces and in conjunction with both off- and on-policy algorithms. Its implementation
507   is straightforward and directly enables solving NCMDPs with state-of-the-art MDP solvers, allowing
508
      us to show improvements in a diverse set of tasks such as classical control problems, portfolio
      optimization, and discrete optimization problems. Note that these improvements are achieved without
509
      adding a single additional hyperparameter to the solving algorithms.
510
511   In further theoretical work, a full constructive classification of objective functions f with constant-
512   size extra state information ht would be desirable. From an applications perspective, there are
513   a lot of interesting objectives with non-cumulative f that could not be maximized so far. For
514
      example, the geometric      Pt mean could be used to maximize average growth rates, or the function
515   f (r̃0 , . . . , r̃t ) = δ t k=0 r̃k , δ ∈ (0, 1) could be used to define an exponential trade-off between
516
      trajectory length and cumulative reward in settings where long trajectories are undesirable. We
      believe that a multitude of other applications with non-cumulative objectives are still unknown to the
517
      reinforcement learning (RL) community, and conversely, that researchers working on non-cumulative
518
      problems are not aware of RL, simply because these two concepts could not straightforwardly be
519   unified so far. This manuscript offers the exciting possibility of discovering and addressing this class
520   of problems still unexplored by RL.
