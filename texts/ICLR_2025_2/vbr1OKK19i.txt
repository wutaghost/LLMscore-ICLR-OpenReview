009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011            The various limitations of Generative AI, such as hallucinations and model failures, have made it
012            crucial to understand the role of different modalities in Visual Language Model (VLM) predictions.
013            Our work investigates how the integration of information from image and text modalities influ-
014            ences the performance and behavior of VLMs in visual question answering (VQA) and reasoning
015            tasks. We measure this effect through answer accuracy, reasoning quality, model uncertainty, and
016
               attention attribution to each modality. We study the interplay between text and image modalities in
               different configurations where visual content is essential for solving the VQA task. Our contribu-
017
               tions include (1) the Semantic Interventions (SI)-VQA dataset, (2) a benchmark study of various
018
               VLM architectures under different modality configurations, and (3) the Interactive Semantic Inter-
019            ventions (ISI) tool. The SI-VQA dataset serves as the foundation for the benchmark, while the ISI
020            tool provides an interface to test and apply semantic interventions in image and text inputs, enabling
021            more fine-grained analysis. Our results show that complementary information between modalities
022            improves answer and reasoning quality, while contradictory information harms model performance
023            and confidence. Image text annotations have minimal impact on accuracy and uncertainty, slightly
024            increasing image relevance. Attention analysis confirms the dominant role of image inputs over text
025            in VQA tasks. In this study, we evaluate state-of-the-art VLMs that allow us to extract attention
026            coefficients for each modality. A key finding is PaliGemma’s harmful overconfidence, which poses
027
               a higher risk of silent failures compared to the LLaVA models. This work sets the foundation for
               rigorous analysis of modality integration, supported by datasets specifically designed for this pur-
028
               pose. The code is available at https://gitlab.com/dekfsx1/si-vlm-benchmark and
029
               the tool and dataset are hosted at https://gitlab.com/dekfsx1/isi-vlm.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have shown remarkable performance across various NLP tasks by combining visual
035   and textual information. Models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), GPT4-Vision (OpenAI, 2024), and PaliGemma (Beyer
036   et al., 2024) use the text interface of Large Language Models (LLMs) alongside CLIP-style image encoders (Radford
037   et al., 2021), making them well-suited for multi-modal tasks such as content summarization (Moured et al., 2024),
038
      text-guided object detection (Dorkenwald et al., 2024), and visual question answering (VQA) (Yue et al., 2024b).
039   The core principle of VLMs lies in the integration and interplay of the two modalities, which significantly amplifies the
040   effectiveness of the models. However, a recurrent problem for multimodal training and testing is the modality bias, i.e.,
041   when a modality dominates another in a multimodal setting (Guo et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2022). The extent to which
042   each modality influences the final answer and reasoning of VLMs remains unclear within the research community, with
043   varying and sometimes conflicting conclusions (Gat et al., 2021; Frank et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024). Understanding
044
      how modalities impact VLMs by exploring complex and specific scenarios is crucial as multimodal models become
      increasingly opaque and diverse. This approach would not only aid in understanding and mitigating model failures,
045
      such as hallucinations but also guide researchers toward practical solutions for improving model performance and
046
      self-consistency.
047
048   To this end, we present a benchmark, investigating the effect of semantic interventions on text and image modalities,
049   alongside a carefully curated dataset and an interactive tool. Our work demonstrates how task-specific modality con-
050
      figurations can reveal heterogeneous effects on model performance and uncertainty. Specifically, we investigate the
      role of the text modality as a context guide in VQA tasks. We make the image the necessary data source to answer the
051
      question while using the text in different configurations to affect the model’s answer and reasoning. Initially, we de-
052
      velop the Interactive Semantic Interventions (ISI) Tool to perform inter-modality interventions, testing their effect

064

065
      do not aim to address modality bias but instead explore the roles and interactions of modalities to better understand
066   VLMs’ strengths and limitations.
067
068   Our study demonstrates that integrating complementary contextual information into VQA models enhances both their
069
      answer accuracy and reasoning quality, while contradictory information significantly degrades performance and con-
      fidence, comparable to the absence of visual input. Additionally, we find that these models inherently prioritize visual
070
      data over textual context; efforts to rebalance attention toward textual information—such as adding image annotations,
071
      textual descriptions, or prompt engineering—yield mixed results, with prompt engineering improving accuracy with-
072   out altering attention distribution and textual descriptions unexpectedly reducing accuracy and increasing uncertainty.
073   Using the AUGRC score to measure the frequency of silent failures, our experiments reveal the harmful overconfi-
074   dence of the PaliGemma 3B model compared to the LLaVA models. These findings provide valuable insights for the
075   AI community by highlighting the critical role of context in VQA models, guiding future developments to optimize
076   attention between visual and textual modalities, and assessing the risk of silent model failures.

092   VQA Datasets Since the introduction of the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task in Antol et al. (2015), numerous
093   datasets have been created to support research in this area, such as GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019), Visual7W (Zhu
094   et al., 2016), Visual Genome (Krishna et al., 2017), CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017), and VQA 2.0 (Goyal et al., 2017).
095   However, these datasets often lack reasoning, are restricted to textual-only modalities, or suffer from small-scale
096   and limited domain diversity. ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022) emerged as the first large-scale VQA dataset to incorporate
097
      textual context alongside textual explanations for model reasoning. It features multiple-choice questions across various
      scientific domains, with each question annotated with lectures and explanations. More diverse multimodal datasets,
098
      such as SEED (Li et al., 2023), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b), and MMMU (Yue et al., 2024a), have since been
099
      introduced, combining text and image data. However, as shown by Chen et al. (2024), these datasets do not ensure that
100   all evaluation samples require visual content for correct answers. To address this limitation, we propose a new curated
101   VQA dataset designed such that answers can only be derived from the image, with complementary or contradictory
102   context provided as additional information to influence the model’s answer and reasoning.
103

107   et al. (2022) test VLMs’ sensitivity to discrepancies between images and captions and found that models often over-

149   Multimodal XAI Multimodal Explainable AI (MXAI) is a branch of XAI that includes a range of XAI techniques
150   tailored to address the unique challenges posed by multimodal data inputs, tasks, and architectures. Recent reviews
151   study the new challenges and differences of those methods as compared to traditional XAI approaches (Joshi et al.,

154

      3.1   S I -VQA DATASET
163
164   The SI-VQA dataset is a closed-question VQA dataset consisting of 100 samples. Each sample consists of an image,
165   question, and ground truth answer (Yes/No) pair, as well as one text annotated image, one contradictory context,
166   and one complementary context. Unlike Chen et al. (2024), which uses multichoice questions, we choose a binary
167   question format (Yes/No) to eliminate issues related to distractor quality and random guessing, ensuring more precise
168   evaluations. The questions in SI-VQA dataset require understanding object significance and simple interactions in the
169   image without reaching the higher complexity levels of datasets like visual commonsense reasoning (Zellers et al.,
170
      2019) or scene graph reasoning (Hildebrandt et al., 2020). The image is the necessary and sufficient element to get
      the question right as it contains the critical information. Thus, the model has a very limited ability to leverage prior
171
      knowledge to answer the question and must utilize the image input, avoiding the data leakage in SOTA VQA datasets
172
      such as ScienceQA, AI2D, MMMU, and SEED (Chen et al., 2024). The context is always in relation to the ground
173   truth answer, aiming to either confuse or help the model without stating an explicit answer to the question, as each
174   question can only be answered through the image. The image annotations provide text-based hints to the ground truth
175   answer while avoiding annotation artifacts (Zellers et al., 2019), ensuring that the model’s attention to the question
176   remains constant regardless of the presence of annotations. We only study the impact of “positive” annotations, i.e.,
177   text that informs the model about key elements in the image to correctly answer the question. See Figure 1 for an
178   exemplary sample. The images are open-source and from the MMMU Benchmark (Yue et al., 2024b). The selected
179   images of SI-VQA Dataset span a variety of fields, including geography, history, art and design, sport, and biology,
180   along with everyday objects and landscapes. This ensures that SI-VQA maintains a comparable diversity of topics
181
      to existing SOTA datasets. They encompass diverse formats, such as natural photographs, cartoons, sketches, and
      paintings. We carefully crafted all other modalities for the dataset, focusing on quality not quantity.
182
183   We design seven scenarios for VLM interpretability analysis, creating seven modality configurations: question (Q),
184   question + image (Q+I), question + image + complementary context (Q+I+C+ ), question + image + contradictory con-
185   text (Q+I+C− ), question + annotated image (Q+IA ), question + annotated image + complementary context (Q+IA +C+ ),
186   question + annotated image + contradictory context (Q+IA +C− ). They can be inferred using the 3x3 matrix in Fig-
187
      ure 1. For the baseline configuration Q (question-only), the image corresponds to a black image. In this case, the
      model’s answer accuracy is at random (See Appendix subsection H.1) proving the necessity of visual content in SI-
188
      VQA Dataset (Chen et al., 2024). We also conducted the baseline experiment using noise-only images and with the
189
      images entirely removed, observing similar results in both cases (See Appendix C).
190

      3.2   ISI T OOL
193
      To provide an intuitive and agile way to explore modality interplay in the context of multimodal interpretability,
194   we developed the ISI Tool. We used this interactive tool to design the SI-VQA Dataset. It is designed to enable
195   researchers and VLM users to investigate how VLMs respond to semantic changes and interventions across image and
196   text modalities, with a focus on identifying potential model failures in the context of VQA. Specifically, it allows the
197   perturbation of images, the addition of personalized shapes and annotations, and the arbitrary adaptation of text inputs.
198   Users can upload their own images and questions or choose from 100 preloaded samples with semantic intervention
199   presets from our ISI-VQA Dataset. More details about the tool and its features are available in Appendix E.

209
      The VLMs selected for this study are state-of-the-art models for Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks. We ex-
210   cluded models where extracting attention coefficients for each modality is not feasible, such as Flamingo (Alayrac
211   et al., 2022), which employs gated cross-attention between text and image. The final architectures chosen for eval-
212   uation are LLaVA 1.5, LLaVA-Vicuna, LLaVA-NeXT, and PaliGemma (Beyer et al., 2024), with weights provided
213   from HuggingFace (Wolf et al., 2020). LLaVA-Vicuna is a version of LLaVA 1.5 leveraging the Vicuna LLM, a
214   conversation-fine-tuned version of LLaMA. LLaVA-Vicuna and LLaVA-NeXT both utilize dynamic high resolution
215   for the image input (Liu et al., 2024a), increasing visual reasoning and optical character recognition (OCR) capabili-
      ties. Although PaliGemma is intentionally designed for pre-training followed by fine-tuning, we employ it in this study

      within a zero-shot setting. We do not present any reasoning results for the PaliGemma model as it mainly generates
217   a default response “Sorry, as a base VLM I am not trained to answer this question.” when it is asked to explain its

225   4.2   A NSWER & R EASONING E VALUATION
226
227   We assess the model’s performance on the VQA task using the SI-VQA Dataset by evaluating both its answer and
228   reasoning. To measure the VQ answering quality, we use the accuracy metric by comparing the model’s binary Yes/No
229   response to the closed question with the ground truth provided in the dataset. Evaluating reasoning is more complex,
230   as no ground truth exists for the explanations. Without a reference rationale, we assess reasoning based on the quality
231   of argumentation and the truthfulness of statements. To this end, we use GPT-4o as an external evaluator (Zheng
232
      et al., 2024; Thakur et al., 2024). The model is prompted once for each sample to rate the reasoning from 0 to 10,
      considering an evaluation prompt as well as the question, image, answer, and reasoning. While the quality scores
233
      seem very reasonable to us, we observe a bias toward the score number "8" (see subsection H.1), a behavior also
234
      observed in other studies using LLMs as a judge (Thakur et al., 2024). See Appendix B for the evaluation prompts and
235   hyperparameters.

      4.4   ATTENTION ATTRIBUTION
263
264   Attributing attention values to the different modalities serves as one indicator of each modality’s contribution to the
265   final answer and reasoning (Wiegreffe & Pinter, 2019). We adopt a mechanistic approach, treating attention as a VLM
266   interpretability measure to evaluate the roles of the question, image, and context. By aggregating attention across layers
267   and heads, we derive relevance scores for each token’s contribution to the answer or reasoning (Parcalabescu & Frank,
268   2023; 2024a). To determine which input modality is most relevant to the prediction, we sum the relevance scores of
269   their respective input tokens. Finally, to standardize the scores for comparability, we compute the relative relevance
      score for each sample. A detailed explanation of attention aggregation and implementation is provided in Appendix A.

272   5.1     I NITIAL H YPOTHESES
273
274   Based on the seven modality configurations and the results of previous related work, we define three prior hypotheses
275   regarding the anticipated outcomes when including a new type of input. As discussed in subsection 4.1, only the VQ
276   answering results are shown for PaliGemma.
277   Hypothesis 1 (Including Image): In the SI-VQA dataset, images are essential for VQA tasks, as they serve as the only
278   modality providing the necessary information to answer the questions. Therefore, we hypothesize a significant positive
279   impact on accuracy and a reduction in model uncertainty when the image is incorporated alongside the question.
280   Furthermore, the natural image is expected to receive greater attention compared to the baseline black or random pixel
281   images in the question-only configuration. The impact on reasoning quality, however, remains in our opinion uncertain.
282
      Hypothesis 2 (Including Context): We hypothesize that adding complementary context will improve model accuracy,
283   confidence, and reasoning quality. This additional information is expected to facilitate more detailed and precise ra-
284   tionalization. Conversely, contradictory context is anticipated to decrease model accuracy, confidence, and reasoning
285   quality, reflecting the model’s doubt. Based on previous research, we also expect the model to exhibit a higher attention
286   toward text compared to the image.
287
      Hypothesis 3 (Including Image Annotations): Compared to configurations including the natural image, those utilizing
288
      the annotated image are expected to enhance the model’s ability to answer and reason with greater accuracy and
289   confidence. Additionally, we anticipate increased attention toward the annotated image relative to the natural image.
290
291   In the following sections, we will first present the benchmark results before comparing them to the prior hypotheses
292
      we have just put forward.

      Textual Description of the Image Does adding a text description of the image greatly improve model performance
466   and confidence? We initially hypothesized that augmenting the model’s input with a textual description of the image
467   would enhance its accuracy and reduce uncertainty, based on the premise that key features necessary for answering
468   the question—already present in the image—would be more accessible to the LLM decoder in text form. However,
469   as illustrated for LLaVA in Figure 6, the results reveal a surprising decrease in answer accuracy and an increase in
470   model uncertainty for configuration I+Q+C+ . We observe similar results for PaliGemma (see Appendix I), with minor
471   increases in uncertainty. We argue that these findings indicate the models are already proficient at extracting essential
472   information from the image alone and that the addition of textual information introduces confusion due to redundancy
473   or potential inconsistencies in the image description. Moreover, the high attention allocated to the image description
474
      in Figure 6c. underscores the model’s sensitivity to textual inputs, which may inadvertently dominate visual cues.

476   Prompt Engineering Can prompt engineering help VLMs re-balance their attention toward the context? We mod-
477
      ified the initial prompt to direct the model’s attention more toward the textual context, which typically receives less
      emphasis in the standard setting (see Appendix B for implementation details). Given that the complementary context
478
      provides information intended to guide the model toward the correct answer, while the contradictory context aims to
479
      mislead it, we expected an increase in accuracy for the Q+I+C+ configuration and a decrease for the Q+I+C− con-
480   figuration. As shown in Figure 6 a, we observed a decrease in accuracy for Q+I+C− , whereas prompt engineering to
481   emphasize the complementary context resulted in improved answer accuracy. Unexpectedly, these changes are not re-
482   flected in the attention attribution: Figure 6c. indicates that prompt engineering does not alter the attention distribution
483   across modalities, as the context does not receive increased attention. This lack of correlation between attention and
484   model performance highlights the necessity for cautious interpretation of attention mechanisms in model predictions
485   (see section 2). Unlike the LLaVA results, PaliGemma exhibited a significant increase in uncertainty (see Appendix I),
      highlighting the large influence of the image in the standard inputs.

      Evaluation of Hypotheses Our findings reveal notable insights into the role of each modality in VQA and reasoning
489   tasks. Specifically, we compare all results with our initial hypotheses from subsection 5.1.
490
491   Hypothesis 1 (Including Image): As expected, introducing the image results in a significant increase in answer accuracy
492
      across all VLMs. However, it unexpectedly leads to a decrease in reasoning quality, as in the question-only setting, the
      models simply acknowledge the absence of the image. We observe a similar pattern in model uncertainty: it decreases
493
      for VQ answering but increases in reasoning. As hypothesized, the natural image indeed receives more attention
494
      compared to the black baseline image.
495
496   Hypothesis 2 (Including Context): Consistent with our expectations, the inclusion of complementary context enhances
497   both accuracy and reasoning quality, while contradictory context has a strongly negative effect. However, in VQ
498
      answering, the complementary context does not reduce model uncertainty, whereas the contradictory context signifi-
      cantly increases it. In VQA reasoning, contradictory context does not affect uncertainty, and complementary context
499
      only slightly decreases it. Generally, the impact of adding context is much stronger in the VQ answering than in the
500
      reasoning task. Interestingly, contradictory context can sometimes be beneficial, as it helps to minimize the occurrence
501   of silent failures. Additionally, the models continue to show higher attention toward the image than the context, not
502   supporting our prior hypothesis.
503
504
      Hypothesis 3 (Including Image Annotations): Surprisingly, the image text annotations play a minimal role in enhancing
      model performance. Although the models exhibit increased attention toward annotated images, the positive impact of
505
      these annotations on performance metrics and uncertainty reduction is nearly negligible.
506
507   We also investigate methods to guide the model to favor one modality over another to observe the effect on VLM
508   performance. While adding redundant textual information can overwhelm the model and decrease accuracy, prompt
509   engineering can improve predictions without, however, strong changes in attention distribution.
510
511   Limitations and Future Work The SI-VQA dataset contains exactly 100 instances. Although relatively small, it is
512
      only used for evaluation, not for training or fine-tuning models. Its purpose is to analyze pretrained models’ behavior
      when faced with diverse input configurations. In addition, prior recent research demonstrates that smaller datasets
513
      can still yield meaningful results when the quality of instances is high (Polo et al., 2024). Each instance underwent
514
      multiple rounds of refinement, ensuring that context descriptions serve only as background information. Despite its
515   reduced size, the dataset exhibits low variance in model performance and uncertainty, as demonstrated in Appendix H,
516   such that adding instances would not alter the conclusions drawn, as they hinge on the modality configurations. The
517   ISI Tool developed in our work ensures that the SI-VQA dataset is extensible and can grow with greater diversity and
518   community input. Our interactive interface also allows people to test new and more advanced modality configurations.
519   In future research, it would be interesting to replicate this study in the reverse scenario—where text is the primary
520   content required for answering, and the image serves as contextual information—to compare whether similar effects
521   of primary and secondary modalities are observed. In future research, it would be interesting to explore tasks where
522   the primary and secondary roles of text and image are reversed, as well as scenarios where both modalities provide
523
      distinct yet equally essential information. Investigating how models integrate and prioritize complementary inputs
      for successful task completion (e.g., answering a question) can offer deeper insights into multimodal information
524
      processing. We employ semantic entropy as our unique uncertainty measure, considering other measures for free-
525
      form text generation, such as token entropy (Kadavath et al., 2022; Lindley, 1956) or self-expressed uncertainty (Lin
526   et al., 2022; Liao & Wortman Vaughan, 2024), to be significantly less suitable. They either only consider local, token-
527   level uncertainty or rely on the model’s potentially biased self-assessment, neither adequately reflecting the overall
528   semantic uncertainty. We validated the expected behavior of semantic entropy across the different configurations using
529   the AUGRC metric in Appendix G.
530
531   Conclusion This study is the first to systematically examine the role of contextual information in VQA, evaluating
532   the results based on diverse metrics and distinguishing between answering and reasoning tasks. Leveraging the well-
533   curated SI-VQA dataset and the ISI tool—our interactive, ready-to-use interface—our work aims to provide a deeper
534   understanding of VLM behavior and the influence of each modality. Moreover, we show that our results can also be
535
      used to understand and detect model failure in free-form text generation, and setting the stage for future analyses of
      modality integration across various VLM tasks and the development of VL datasets tailored for this objective.
