011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Coding tasks have been valuable for evaluating Large Language Models (LLMs),
014            as they demand the comprehension of high-level instructions, complex reason-
015
               ing, and the implementation of functional programs – core capabilities for ad-
               vancing Artificial General Intelligence. Despite the progress in Large Multimodal
016
               Models (LMMs), which extend LLMs with visual perception and understanding
017
               capabilities, there remains a notable lack of coding benchmarks that rigorously
018            assess these models, particularly in tasks that emphasize visual reasoning. To ad-
019            dress this gap, we introduce HumanEval-V, a novel and lightweight benchmark
020            specifically designed to evaluate LMMs’ visual understanding and reasoning ca-
021            pabilities through code generation tasks. HumanEval-V includes 108 carefully
022            crafted, entry-level Python coding tasks derived from platforms like CodeForces
023            and Stack Overflow. Each task is adapted by modifying the context and algo-
024            rithmic patterns of the original problems, with visual elements redrawn to ensure
025            distinction from the source, preventing potential data leakage. LMMs are required
026
               to complete the code solution based on the provided visual context and a prede-
               fined Python function signature outlining the task requirements. Every task is
027
               equipped with meticulously handcrafted test cases to ensure a thorough and reli-
028
               able evaluation of the model-generated code solutions. We evaluate 19 state-of-
029            the-art LMMs using HumanEval-V, uncovering significant challenges. Propri-
030            etary models like GPT-4o achieve only 13% pass@1 and 36.4% pass@10, while
031            open-weight models with 70B parameters score below 4% pass@1. Ablation stud-
032            ies further demonstrate the limitations of current LMMs in vision reasoning and
033            coding abilities. These results highlight key areas for future research to enhance
034            LMMs’ capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Coding ability is essential for both the development and evaluation of Large Language Models
      (LLMs) (Sun et al., 2024). By enabling LLMs to solve complex tasks in a divide-and-conquer man-
040
      ner, coding facilitates more autonomous and efficient interactions with the world (Patil et al., 2023;
041
      Liu et al., 2023b; Schick et al., 2024). As a result, coding tasks serve as a valuable testbed for
042   advancing research in Artificial General Intelligence (Bubeck et al., 2023). Recently, Large Multi-
043   modal Models (LMMs) composed of billions of parameters have emerged, with notable examples
044   such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024), demonstrating remark-
045   able capabilities in understanding and reasoning within visual contexts.
046
      While several recent multimodal benchmarks offer evaluations across a wide range of vision-related
047
      tasks (Goyal et al., 2017; Singh et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c; Yue et al., 2024), there
048   remains a significant gap in benchmarks specifically designed for coding scenarios. These bench-
049   marks typically involve multiple-choice or open-ended questions based on commonsense reasoning,
050   neglecting more complex reasoning scenarios like coding. Notably, coding is a valuable form to
051   assess complex reasoning abilities and has been exploited in various reasoning tasks such as math-
052   ematical, symbolic, and algorithmic reasoning (Madaan et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023). It demands
053   the ability to understand high-level instructions, apply complex logic, and implement functional pro-
      grams. Moreover, coding enables a more robust evaluation of reasoning through program execution.

      To address this gap, we introduce HumanEval-V, a novel and lightweight benchmark tailored to
071
      evaluate LMMs in coding scenarios. HumanEval-V consists of 108 manually crafted code gener-
072
      ation tasks sourced from platforms such as CodeForces and Stack Overflow. Each task is adapted
073   from the source by carefully modifying the original problem’s context and algorithmic patterns as
074   well as redrawing the visual elements. As an example task shown in Figure 1, each task involves
075   completing a Python function based on a single image, a function signature, and problem descrip-
076   tions provided in the comment block. These tasks require reasoning over both visual and textual
077   contexts to complete a function, with the correctness of the predicted solution assessed using a
078   reliable set of human-annotated test cases.
079   HumanEval-V is novel in that it is the first benchmark where visual information plays an es-
080   sential role in solving coding tasks. For instance, the diagram in Figure 1 not only indicates the
081   available position options for the function inputs, but also offers important clues for determining
082   whether two lines intersect, significantly complementing the function signature and problem de-
083   scriptions. To solve these tasks, models have to accurately understand the nuances of the image,
084   such as the position of two lines on the circle and tick labels. Moreover, they need the ability to per-
085   form cross-modal reasoning, integrating visual elements with the structured function signature and
086   textual problem descriptions cohesively. In contrast to other benchmarks (Li et al., 2024b), which
087
      suggest that visual information has limited impact on coding performance, HumanEval-V ensures
      that all coding tasks are unsolvable without the visual context. Textual descriptions in the coding
088
      tasks are minimized to prevent models from relying solely on textual information to infer solutions.
089
090   Another appealing characteristic of HumanEval-V is light-weight and easy to test. It mirrors
091   the difficulty of well-established code generation benchmarks like HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021)
092   and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) that target entry-level programmers. The simplicity of evaluation
093
      has been one of the key reasons for the wide adoption of these benchmarks. In HumanEval-V,
      each task is formulated in a Python code completion setting like HumanEval and annotated with
094
      a comprehensive suite of test cases in a format of assertion statements, making it easy to execute
095
      and efficient to measure the correctness of the completion. Additionally, the tasks are restricted to
096   using only common Python libraries, promoting the accessibility without requiring domain-specific
097   knowledge and avoiding compatibility issues with different library versions. We perform cross-
098   validation among several annotators to ensure the data integrity.
099
      Through extensive experiments with 19 state-of-the-art LMMs, we have the following key findings:
100
      (1) Even leading proprietary models like GPT-4o achieve only 13% pass@1 on HumanEval-V,
101
      while open-weight models perform much worse, with none of them exceeding 4% pass@1.
102   HumanEval-V reveals limitations of current LMMs. (2) Proprietary models significantly outper-
103   form open-weight LMMs, highlighting the challenges in developing more advanced open-weight
104   models. (3) Current LMMs remain limited in their visual reasoning abilities, as evidenced by the
105   significant performance gains when provided with human-annotated textual descriptions of the im-
106   ages. (4) Open-weight LMMs suffer from deteriorated coding performance after integrating the
107   vision encoder. These findings emphasize the need for future research to enhance LLMs’ visual
      reasoning and coding abilities.

      2     B ENCHMARK C ONSTRUCTION
109
110
111   As shown in Figure 1, each coding task in HumanEval-V consists of three main components. The
112
      first component is a single image input, denoted as I, which provides the essential visual context
      necessary to solve the coding problem. The second component is a Python function signature,
113
      denoted as σ, which specifies the function name, input parameters, and return type, accompanied by
114
      a brief problem description in the comment block. Both the image I and the function signature σ
115   are formatted into a predefined prompt template, which is then provided to the LMM. The model’s
116   output, denoted as O, represents the complete Python function generated by the LMM based on σ
117   and I. The third component is a set of test cases T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn }, which are used to validate
118   the functional correctness of O through execution. A solution is considered correct if O passes all
119   test cases, meaning it produces the expected outputs for each ti ∈ T .
120
      Before constructing HumanEval-V, we establish rigorous standards to ensure the quality of the
121   coding task annotations: (1) the visual context provided must be essential for solving the task, with
122   all relevant information contained within a single image; (2) the coding task should be largely self-
123   explanatory through its visual context, requiring minimal textual descriptions; and (3) the coding
124   task should target entry-level programmers and be solvable using only common Python libraries.
125
      The construction of HumanEval-V follows a collect-adapt-mutate pipeline. First, we collect cod-
126
      ing problems with visual contexts from platforms such as CodeForces and Stack Overflow, iden-
127   tifying those suitable for adaptation based on the aforementioned standards. (Section 2.1). Next,
128   we modify the selected problems by adapting their task descriptions and redrawing the visual ele-
129   ments to ensure they meet our quality requirements. During this stage, we annotate each task with
130   a function signature (σ), a set of test cases (T ), and a ground truth solution. To further expand the
131   benchmark, some tasks undergo mutations, generating similar yet distinct versions by introducing
132   changes to the coding task’s visual patterns while preserving the core context. This iterative process
133   results in a final set of 108 code generation tasks (Section 2.2). After constructing the benchmark,
134   we perform rigorous validation to ensure that each coding task aligns with the standards: testing
135
      visual reasoning, preventing data leakage, and maintaining an appropriate entry-level complexity.
      Finally, we provide detailed benchmark statistics for reference (Section 2.3).
136
137
138   2.1   DATA C OLLECTION AND S CREENING
139
140   The coding tasks in HumanEval-V are sourced from prominent Q&A and coding challenge plat-
141   forms such as Stack Overflow and CodeForces. These platforms offer a diverse range of coding
142   problems and are also commonly used in the development of well-established benchmarks for code
143   generation (Yin et al., 2018; Lai et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Jain et al., 2024;
144   Wu et al., 2024b). From these sources, we collect a large set of coding problems that incorporate
145   visual elements in their problem descriptions.
146   However, the collected problems are unsuitable for direct inclusion in HumanEval-V. In most
147   cases, the visual context is non-essential for solving the task, with the problem primarily solvable
148   through rich textual descriptions alone. This makes it challenging to adapt such problems into our
149   benchmark, which emphasizes visual reasoning abilities. Therefore, we focus on identifying tasks
150   that already feature high-quality visual elements and present a moderate level of difficulty. After a
151   thorough screening process, we retain 40 candidate coding tasks out of the thousands reviewed for
152
      further adaptation. A detailed discussion of the challenges encountered during data collection and
      screening, along with demonstrating examples, is provided in Appendix C.1.
153
154
155   2.2   C ODING TASK A NNOTATION
156
157   The annotation process begins by adapting the screened coding problems. For each of the 40 selected
158   coding tasks, we first identify and summarize the essential context and algorithmic patterns required
159   to solve the problem. We then create a new coding problem by modifying the context and patterns
160   of the original problem and redrawing the corresponding images. This is to prevent data leakage
161   and ensure consistency with the standards of HumanEval-V. Detailed examples of the problem
      adaptation can be found in Appendix C.2.

162
      During adaptation, we ensure that all critical visual information for each coding task is encapsu-
163   lated within a single image. The coding tasks in HumanEval-V span a variety of visual elements,
164   including trees, graphs, matrices, maps, grids, flowcharts, and other abstract representations. This
165   diversity allows for comprehensive testing of the model’s visual reasoning abilities. Next, we define
166   a Python function signature for each task, beginning with the input and output specifications. To
167   simplify the Input/Output (I/O) formats, we prioritize basic data structures such as numbers, strings,
168   lists, and dictionaries. After finalizing the image and I/O definitions, we craft a concise problem
169   description that directs models to rely primarily on the visual information to complete the Python
170   function. Once the task definition is completed, we manually construct test cases and implement
171
      a ground truth solution for each coding problem to ensure its validity. To further verify the com-
      prehensiveness of the test cases, we perform statement and branch coverage analysis on the ground
172
      truth solution, ensuring that all logical branches and execution paths are thoroughly tested.
173
174   Following the initial annotation of the 40 coding tasks, we conduct an additional round of mutation-
175   based extensions. This process expands the number of coding tasks based on the initial annotations,
176   by creating similar yet distinct coding tasks. The mutated tasks retain most of the original visual
177
      elements but incorporate different algorithms to solve. For example, we can change the rule of the
      coding task in Figure 1 by just considering the situation where the line segments intersect within the
178
      circle, regardless of outside the circle. It is important to note that not all of the 40 tasks are suitable
179
      for mutation. For each suitable task, we create one or two mutations, resulting in a total of 108
180   coding tasks in HumanEval-V. Examples of the mutation process are provided in Appendix C.3
181

183   2.3     Q UALITY A SSURANCE AND DATASET S TATISTICS
184
185
      We implement a rigorous quality assurance process to ensure the quality of HumanEval-V. The
      annotation team consists of three programmers, each with over four years of Python programming
186
      experience. During each of the data collection, adaptation, and mutation stages, annotators inde-
187
      pendently perform annotations based on pre-defined guidelines. After that, all annotators conduct a
188   cross-validation process to review and resolve any identified issues. A coding task is only finalized
189   when consensus is reached among all annotators. Additionally, one annotator maintains consistent
190   formatting and style across all visual representations and coding tasks. Each annotator dedicates
191   over 200 hours to the overall benchmark construction process. To validate the reliance on visual
192   context, we ensure that GPT-4o cannot solve any of the coding tasks without access to the images,
193   confirming the essential role of visual information. Finally, to facilitate continuous improvement, we
194   will publish an online data viewer for HumanEval-V after the review period, where the community
195   can review the dataset and report issues.

258   Evaluation Metrics Following established practices in code generation (Chen et al., 2021; Austin
259   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022), we use the pass@k metric to evaluate the functional correctness of
260   the generated code solutions. For each coding task, n code samples are generated, and k solutions
261   are randomly selected from these samples to be tested against ground truth test cases. A task is
262   considered solved if at least one of the k selected solutions passes all test cases. The pass@k score
263   is then calculated as the percentage of successfully solved tasks. In our main experiments, we report
264   pass rate results for k = 1, 10. For greedy search, we set n = 1 to compute pass@1. For sampling-
265   based evaluation, we set n = 20 to calculate pass@10.
266   We incorporate a second evaluation metric: Execution Success Rate. This metric measures the
267   syntactic correctness of the generated code, independent of its functional accuracy. A solution is
268   considered executable if it can be compiled and run without triggering syntax errors, null pointer
269   exceptions, or other runtime failures, regardless of passing the test cases. The execution success rate
      is calculated as the proportion of executable code samples out of all generated samples.

479   5   R ELATED W ORK
480
481   While numerous benchmarks have been developed to evaluate various capabilities of LMMs, rang-
482
      ing from optical character recognition (OCR) to multidisciplinary knowledge reasoning, few specif-
      ically focus on the intersection of visual reasoning and code generation. This section reviews the
483
      current progress of LMM benchmarking and demonstrates how HumanEval-V fills this gap.
484
485   OCR and Multidisciplinary Knowledge Abilities: A variety of benchmarks have been devel-
      oped to evaluate multidisciplinary capabilities of LMMs. There are popular benchmarks like


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      DocVQA (Mathew et al., 2021), ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022), TextVQA(Singh et al., 2019), OCR-
487   Bench (Liu et al., 2023d), and OCRVQA (Mishra et al., 2019) assess models’ ability to recognize
488   and interpret text embedded in visual formats, including documents, charts, and images, often com-
489   bining these with multiple-choice questions (MCQ) and visual question answering (VQA) tasks.
490   Meanwhile, benchmarks such as MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), MME (Fu et al., 2023), MMBench (Liu
491   et al., 2023c), MMVet (Yu et al., 2023), SEEDBench (Li et al., 2023a), MMT-Bench (Ying et al.,
492   2024), and MMStar (Chen et al., 2024) test models on their general knowledge and reasoning abil-
493   ities across diverse domains, such as scientific concepts, cultural knowledge, and logical reasoning.
494   In contrast, HumanEval-V distinguishes itself by expanding the evaluation format beyond tradi-
495
      tional MCQ and VQA. HumanEval-V requires models to interpret visual elements and apply that
      understanding to generate correct and executable code, which introduces a more complex challenge.
496
497   Specialized Abilities: There are also benchmarks focusing on specific capabilities of LMMs.
498   MathVista (Lu et al., 2023) evaluates mathematical problem-solving skills. Safety-related bench-
499   marks (Gu et al., 2024) assess models on their ability to recognize and mitigate potential risks or
500   harmful content. ConvBench (Liu et al., 2024) evaluates conversational abilities, testing models
501
      on their proficiency in maintaining coherent and contextually relevant dialogues. Benchmarks for
      instruction-following ability (Qian et al., 2024) assess how well models can execute tasks based
502
      on given instructions. Long-context reasoning benchmarks (Ma et al., 2024) assess the ability of
503
      models to maintain coherence and logical reasoning over extended dialogues or documents. Hallu-
504   sionBench (Guan et al., 2024) focuses on hallucination detection abilities to differentiate between
505   factual information and generated content. There are also benchmarks (Zhang et al., 2024) eval-
506   uating mobile app navigation, testing models on their ability to interpret and interact with user
507   interfaces. In contrast, HumanEval-V mainly focuses on integrating visual reasoning and coding.
508
      Coding Abilities: Despite the wide range of benchmarks available, the coding ability of LMMs
509
      remains under-explored. Coding capabilities are crucial for leveraging LMMs in autonomous and
510   agentic applications (Xie et al., 2024). Current efforts focus primarily on derendering web pages (Si
511   et al., 2024; Laurençon et al., 2024) and scientific figures (Shi et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a), where
512   models translate visual representations into code. The other related area is Program-based VQA,
513   where models are provided with a set of pre-defined modules (e.g., for OCR, object detection, and
514   segmentation) and tasked with invoking them to answer visual questions like counting or identifying
515   spatial relationships (Surı́s et al., 2023; Subramanian et al., 2023). These methods show how mod-
516   els can use existing tools to perform vision tasks, while they complicate evaluation due to reliance
517   on multiple heavy modules. In contrast, HumanEval-V utilizes simple Python coding tasks to
518
      streamline evaluation and focuses on visual understanding in coding tasks. Another closely related
      work is MMCode (Li et al., 2024b), which evaluates the coding ability of LMMs on visually rich
519
      competition-level coding problems. utilizing existing coding challenges from competitive program-
520
      ming websites. However, MMCode overlooks two critical issues: the potential for data leakage
521   when relying on scraped data, and the use of text-rich problem contexts, which makes visual in-
522   formation non-essential for solving tasks. By contrast, our approach addresses both concerns with
523   rigorous data screening and annotation. We list a detailed discussion on MMCode in Appendix E.
524
525
526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We present a novel and lightweight benchmark HumanEval-V designed to evaluate the visual rea-
529   soning capabilities of LMMs through 108 high-quality, entry-level Python coding tasks that rely
530   on visual context to solve. We ensure rigorous evaluation of generated code solutions using com-
531
      prehensive test cases. Our benchmark effectively uncovers weaknesses in current LMMs that are
      overlooked by existing benchmarks. Through our analysis, we identify three critical limitations
532
      in the current generation of LMMs. First, their visual perception abilities remain inadequate. We
533
      observe significant performance gains when we provide textual descriptions of visual elements, in-
534   dicating that models still struggle to understand visual context independently. Second, open-weight
535   LMMs exhibit a consistent decline in their coding proficiency compared to their LLM decoders,
536   suggesting that the current multimodal training strategy still needs improvement. Finally, halluci-
537   nation due to overfitting is a major issue, causing models to incorrectly apply memorized patterns
538   rather than adapt to the new visual context in the coding tasks. We hope these findings will inform
539   and guide future research on enhancing the visual reasoning and coding capabilities of LMMs. We
      also provide a discussion on our work’s limitations in Appendix F.

      Figure 1 illustrates a simple coding task in HumanEval-V. The task requires determining whether

801
      Both models implement sorting-based algorithms that compare the numbers at the endpoints of the
      line segments. However, they fail to account for the critical scenario where the segments intersect
802
      outside the circle, and fail to recognize the unordered arrangement of the numbers. This oversight
803

807   is not isolated to this task; we observe similar issues across many coding tasks in HumanEval-V.

      rationale behind our careful adaptation and mutation processes during data annotation.

850
      From the results, we observe that increasing the sample size consistently improves performance
851

852   pass@10 to 53.7% pass@100. Smaller-scale open-weight LMMs also show notable gains; for in-

855   the same 4B-level parameter size, achieves only a pass@100 score of 6.5%. These findings under-
856   score both the potential and the limitations of scaling sample numbers to improve current LMMs’
857   performance on HumanEval-V.

859   to 1,000. The results, presented in Table 6, show promising results with GPT-4o achieving a
860   pass@1000 of 68.5%, compared to the 36.4% pass@10. Similarly, GPT-4o-mini demonstrates
861   strong performance, achieving a pass@1000 score of 46.3%, surpassing the pass@10 score of GPT-
862   4o. These findings suggest that a significant proportion of the coding tasks in HumanEval-V are
863   solvable with current LMM capabilities, highlighting the need for further research on strategies to
      better motivate the abilities of LMMs.

1004   We provide two examples in Figure 7 and Figure 8 to illustrate our annotation process and demon-
1005   strate how we construct image descriptions. When creating these descriptions, we ensure they
1006   are purely descriptive rather than instructive, refraining from disclosing any specific algorithms or
1007   problem-solving strategies. This approach allows us to evaluate whether current LMMs possess gen-
1008
       uine visual understanding capabilities and whether they can perform well when the visual elements
       are grounded through detailed textual descriptions.
1009
1010   This process poses a unique challenge. While humans can intuitively identify patterns in images
1011   and summarize them succinctly, we require our annotators to use precise descriptive language that
1012   details every visual aspect without inferring the specific steps to solve the problem. This increases
1013   the complexity of annotation and often results in verbose image descriptions. Despite this verbosity,
1014
       maintaining a purely descriptive approach is crucial for our benchmark, as it ensures that solving
       the task requires the model to interpret and reason about the visual content, rather than simply
1015
       interpreting the description into code.

1503
1504   Despite the contributions of our benchmark, several limitations remain that we aim to address in
1505   future work:
1506   (1) Limited Number of Coding Tasks: The size of our benchmark is currently restricted due to the
1507   difficulty of identifying suitable coding problems and the challenges associated with adapting these
1508   problems to meet our standards. Each annotator has dedicated over 200 hours to constructing the
1509   benchmark, ensuring that every task is meticulously curated and aligns with our goals of testing
1510   visual reasoning. Our priority has been to maintain high quality, which we believe is crucial for
1511   deriving meaningful insights. Fortunately, the current version of HumanEval-V has already en-
       abled us to identify several unique findings about the limitations of current LMMs. Moving forward,

1544
1545   (2) Limited Model Coverage: While our experiments evaluate a diverse set of representative top-
1546   performing LMMs, the rapid pace of development in this area means that new models are frequently
1547
       released, which may not be covered in our evaluation. We acknowledge that broader model coverage
       could provide a more comprehensive understanding of current capabilities. To address this, we will
1548
       publicly release the evaluation toolkit and dataset, along with an up-to-date leaderboard to track
1549
       ongoing advancements and benchmark new models as they become available. This will help keep
1550   our benchmark relevant and provide a platform for continuous assessment.
1551
1552   (3) Limited Scope of Experimental Analysis: Due to budget constraints, our in-depth analysis is
1553
       limited to a subset of the evaluated models and hyper-parameter settings. While we have included
       as many models as possible to ensure that our findings are not biased toward specific LMMs, there
1554
       are areas that remain unexplored, such as evaluating the impact of different prompting templates and
1555
       experimenting with alternative sampling strategies, including varying temperature settings. Never-
1556   theless, we have carefully chosen hyper-parameters that are widely used and deemed fair for cross-
1557   model comparisons. We believe that the settings used in our experiments provide reliable insights
1558   and lead to trustworthy conclusions. Additionally, our investigation into advanced reasoning meth-
1559   ods is limited. In preliminary experiments, we applied the zero-shot Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT) (Wei
1560   et al., 2022) approach, which prompts the model to perform step-by-step reasoning before generat-
1561   ing code. However, this method showed limited improvement in our coding tasks. Given that zero-
1562   shot CoT is a relatively weak baseline for reasoning research, fully exploring more sophisticated
1563   reasoning-enhancement techniques (Yao et al., 2024a; Mitra et al., 2024) would require significant
1564
       resources. We leave this comprehensive study to future work.
