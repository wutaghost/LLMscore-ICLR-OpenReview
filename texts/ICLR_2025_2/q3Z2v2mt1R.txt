011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which are nowadays the benchmark approach
014            in graph representation learning, have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial
015            attacks, raising concerns about their real-world applicability. While existing de-
016
               fense techniques primarily concentrate on the training phase of GNNs, involving
               adjustments to message passing architectures or pre-processing methods, there is
017
               a noticeable gap in methods focusing on increasing robustness during inference.
018
               In this context, this study introduces RobustCRF, a post-hoc approach aiming to
019            enhance the robustness of GNNs at the inference stage. Our proposed method,
020            founded on statistical relational learning using a Conditional Random Field, is
021            model-agnostic and does not require prior knowledge about the underlying model
022            architecture. We validate the efficacy of this approach across various models,
023            leveraging benchmark node classification datasets.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have demonstrated exceptional performance across various domains,
029   including image recognition, object detection, and speech recognition (Chen et al., 2019; Ni et al.,
030   2022; Shim et al., 2021). The growing interest in handling irregular and unstructured data, particularly
031   in domains like bioinformatics, has pushed some attention toward graph-based representations.
032   Graphs have emerged as the preferred format for representing such irregular data due to their ability
033
      to capture interactions between elements, whether individuals in a social network or interactions
      between atoms. In response to this need, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2017;
034
      Veličković et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2019b) have been proposed as an extension of DNNs tailored
035
      to graph-based data. GNNs excel at generating meaningful representations for individual nodes
036   by leveraging both the graph’s structural information and its associated features. This approach
037   has demonstrated significant success in addressing challenges such as protein function prediction
038   (Gilmer et al., 2017), materials modeling (Duval et al., 2023), and recommendation systems (Wu
039   et al., 2019b).
040
      Alongside their achievements, these deep learning-based approaches have exhibited vulnerability to
041
      various data alterations, including noisy, incomplete, or out-of-distribution examples (Günnemann,
042   2022). While such alterations may naturally occur in data collection, adversaries can deliberately
043   craft and introduce them, resulting in adversarial attacks. These attacks manifest as imperceptible
044   modifications to the input that can deceive and disrupt the classifier. In these attacks, the adversary’s
045   objective is to introduce subtle noise in the features or manipulate some edges in the graph structure
046   to alter the initial prediction made by the input graph. Depending on the attacker’s goals and level
047   of knowledge, different attack settings can be considered. For instance, poisoning attacks (Zügner
048   & Günnemann, 2019) involve manipulating the training data to introduce malicious points, while
049   evasion attacks (Dai et al., 2018) focus on attacking the model during the inference phase without
050
      further model adaptation.
051   Given the susceptibility of GNNs to adversarial attacks, their practical utility is constrained. Therefore,
052   it becomes imperative to study and enhance their robustness. Various defense strategies have been
053   proposed to mitigate this vulnerability, including pre-processing the input graph (Entezari et al., 2020;
      Wu et al., 2019a), edge pruning (Zhang & Zitnik, 2020), and adapting the message passing scheme


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Zhu et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Abbahaddou et al., 2024). Most of these defense methods operate by
055   modifying the underlying model architecture or the training procedure, i.e., focusing on the training
056   side of GNNs and, therefore, limiting their applicability. This limitation is especially pertinent
057   when dealing with pre-trained models, which have become the standard for real-world applications.
058   Additionally, modifying the model architecture during training poses the risk of degrading accuracy
059   on clean, non-attacked graphs, and usually, these modifications are not adapted for all possible
060   architectures.
061   In light of these challenges, our work introduces a post-hoc defense mechanism, denoted as Ro-
062   bustCRF, aimed at bolstering the robustness of GNNs during the inference phase using statistical
063   relational learning. RobustCRF is model-agnostic, requiring no prior knowledge of the underlying
064   model, and is adaptable to various architectural designs, providing flexibility and applicability across
065   diverse domains. Central to our approach is the assumption that neighboring points in the input
066   manifold, accounting for graph isomorphism, should yield similar predictions in the output manifold.
067   Based on our robustness assumption, we employ a Conditional Random Field (CRF) (Lafferty et al.,
068   2001) to adapt and edit the model’s output to preserve the similarity relationship between the input
069
      and output manifold.
070   While the proposed approach is applicable to a wide range of tasks and models, including those in
071   the computer vision domain, our primary focus revolves around Graph Neural Networks (GNNs).
072   We start by introducing our CRF-based post-hoc robustness enhancement model. Recognizing the
073   potential complexity associated with this task and aiming to deliver a robustness technique that
074   remains computationally affordable, we study a sampling strategy for both the discrete space of the
075
      graph structure and the continuous space of node features. We finally proceed with an empirical
      analysis to assess the effectiveness of our proposed technique across various models, conducting also a
076
      comprehensive examination of the parameters involved. In summary, our contributions can be outlined
077
      as follows: (1) model-agnostic robustness enhancement: we present RobustCRF, a post-hoc approach
078   designed to enhance the robustness of underlying GNNs, without any assumptions about the model’s
079   architecture. This feature underscores its versatility, enabling its application across diverse models;
080   (2) theoretical underpinnings and complexity reduction: we conduct a comprehensive theoretical
081   analysis of our proposed approach and enhance our general architecture through the incorporation of
082   sampling techniques, thereby mitigating the complexity associated with the underlying model.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087
088   Attacking GNNs. A multitude of both poisoning and evasion adversarial attacks targeting GNNs
089   models has surged lately (Günnemann, 2022; Zügner et al., 2018). Namely, gradient-based techniques
090   (Xu et al., 2019a), such as Proximal Gradient Descent (PGD), have been employed to tackle the
091   adversarial aim, which is framed as an optimization task aiming to find the nearest adversary to the
092   input point while fulfilling the adversarial objective. Building upon this foundation, Mettack (Zügner
093
      & Günnemann, 2019) extends the approach by formulating the problem as a bi-level optimization
      task and harnessing meta-gradients for its solution. In a different perspective, Nettack (Zügner et al.,
094
      2018) introduces a targeted poisoning attack strategy, encompassing both structural and node feature
095
      perturbations, employing a greedy optimization algorithm to minimize an attack loss with respect to
096   a surrogate model. Diverging from these classical search problems, Dai et al. (2018) approach the
097   adversarial search task through the lens of Reinforcement Learning techniques.
098
099
      Defending GNNs. Different defense strategies have been proposed to counter the previously pre-
      sented attacks on GNNs. GNN-SVD (Entezari et al., 2020) employs low-rank approximation of
100
      the adjacency matrix to filter out noise, while similar pre-processing techniques are used by GNN-
101
      Jaccard (Wu et al., 2019a) to identify potential edge manipulations. Additionally, methods like
102   edge pruning (Zhang & Zitnik, 2020) and transfer learning (Tang et al., 2020) have been used to
103   mitigate the impact of poisoning attacks. Notably, most research efforts have predominantly fo-
104   cused on addressing structural perturbations, with relatively fewer strategies developed to counter
105   attacks targeting node features. For instance, in (Seddik et al., 2022), the inclusion of a node feature
106   kernel within message passing was proposed to reinforce GCNs. RobustGCN (Zhu et al., 2019)
107   uses Gaussian distributions as hidden node representations in each convolutional layer, effectively
      mitigating the influence of both structural and feature-based adversarial attacks. Finally, in GCORN


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Abbahaddou et al., 2024), an adaptation of the message passing scheme has been proposed by using
109   orthonormal weights to counter the effect of node feature-based adversarial attacks.
110
111   The majority of the previously discussed methods intervene during the model’s training phase, necessi-
112
      tating modifications to the underlying architecture. However, this strategy exhibits certain limitations;
      it may not be universally applicable across diverse architectural designs, and it can potentially result
113
      in a loss of accuracy when applied to the clean graph. Furthermore, these methods may not be
114
      suitable for scenarios where users prefer to employ pre-trained models, as they necessitate model
115   retraining. Consequently, the need for proposing and crafting post-hoc robustness enhancements be-
116   comes increasingly apparent. Unfortunately, the existing methods in this domain remain quite limited.
117   Addressing this gap in the literature, our work aims to contribute to this essential area. One commonly
118   employed post-hoc approach is randomized smoothing (Bojchevski et al., 2020; Carmon et al., 2019),
119   which involves injecting noise into the inputs at various stages and subsequently utilizing majority
120   voting to determine the final prediction. Note that randomized smoothing has actually been initially
121   borrowed from the optimization community (Duchi et al., 2012). Despite its popularity, randomized
122   smoothing exhibits several limitations, including suffering from the shrinking phenomenon where
123
      decision regions shrink or drift as the variance of the smoothing distribution increases (Mohapatra
      et al., 2021). Other works also identified that the smoothed classifier is more-constant than the
124
      original model, i.e., it forces the classification to remain invariant over a large input space, resulting a
125
      huge drop of the accuracy (Anderson & Sojoudi, 2022; Krishnan et al., 2020; Wang et al.).

180   A probabilistic graphical model (PGM) is a graph framework that compactly models the joint proba-
181   bility distributions P and dependence relations over a set of random variables Ỹ = {Ỹ1 , . . . , Ỹm }
182   represented in a graph. The two most common classes of PGMs are Bayesian networks (BNs) and
183   Markov Random Fields (MRFs) (Heckerman, 2008; Clifford, 1990). The core of the BN representa-
184   tion is a directed acyclic graph (DAG). As the name suggests, DAG can be represented by a graph
185   with no loopy structure where its vertices serve as random variables and directed edges serve as
186   dependency relationships between them. The direction of the edges determines the influence of one
187   random variable on another. Similarly, MRFs are also used to describe dependencies between random
188
      variables in a graph. However, MRFs use undirected instead of directed edges and permit cycles.

      4.2   M OTIVATION
228
      There are different theoretical definitions of robustness (Cheng et al., 2021; Weng et al., 2018), but
229   they all rely on one assumption: if two inputs are adjacent in the input space, their predictions should
230   be adjacent in the output space. For example, most existing approaches measure the robustness of a
231   neural network via the Attack Success Rate (ASR), the percentage of attack attempts that produce
232   successful adversarial examples, which implies that close input data should be predicted similarly
233   (Wu et al., 2021; Goodfellow et al., 2014). Additionally, there are also works that use the neural
234   networks distortion as a robustness metric (Cheng et al., 2021; Weng et al., 2018; Carlini & Wagner,
235   2016). Intuitively, large distortion implies potentially poor adversarial robustness since a small
236   perturbation applied to these inputs will lead to significant changes in the output. Consequently, most
237
      robustness metrics measure the extent to which the network’s output is changed when perturbing the
      input data, indicating the network’s vulnerability to adversarial attacks, which is directly equivalent to
238
      our assumption. To respect this assumption, we construct a CRF, where the node set V CRF represents
239
      the set of possible GNN inputs G × X , while the CRF edge set E CRF represents the set of pair
240
      inputs ([G, X], [G̃, X̃]) such that [G̃, X̃] belongs to the ball B of radius r > 0 surrounding [G, X], as
241   follows:                                  nh      i                              o
242
                             B ([G, X] , r) = G̃, X̃ : dα,β ([G, X], [G̃, X̃]) ≤ r .
243
244
      Our method examines how the model performs more generally within a defined neighborhood, instead
245
      of considering the model’s behavior only under individual adversarial attacks. This perspective leans
246   towards a concept of average robustness, expanding on the conventional worst-case based adversarial
247   robustness that is often emphasized in adversarial studies (Abbahaddou et al., 2024). A similar
248   average robustness concept was studied and showed to be appropriate for computer vision (Rice
249   et al., 2021).

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This work addresses the problem of adversarial defense at the inference stage. We propose a model-
533
      agnostic, post-hoc approach using Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) to enhance the adversarial
534   robustness of pre-trained models. Our method, RobustCRF, operates without requiring knowledge
535   of the underlying model and necessitates no post-training or architectural modifications. Extensive
536   experiments on multiple datasets demonstrate RobustCRF’s effectiveness in improving the robustness
537   of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) against both structural and node-feature-based adversarial attacks,
538   while maintaining a balance between attacked and clean accuracy, typically preserving their perfor-
539   mance on clean, un-attacked datasets, which makes RobustCRF the best trade-off between robustness
      and clean accuracy.
