009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In LLM alignment and many other ML applications, one often faces the Multi-
012            Objective Fine-Tuning (MOFT) problem, i.e. ﬁne-tuning an existing model with
013            datasets labeled w.r.t. different objectives simultaneously. To address the chal-
014
               lenge, we propose the HyperDPO framework, a conditioned one-shot ﬁne-tuning
               approach that extends the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) technique, orig-
015
               inally developed for efﬁcient LLM alignment with preference data, to accommo-
016
               date the MOFT settings. By substituting the Bradley-Terry-Luce model in DPO
017            with the Plackett-Luce model, our framework is capable of handling a wide range
018            of MOFT tasks that involve listwise ranking datasets. Compared with previous
019            approaches, HyperDPO enjoys an efﬁcient one-shot training process for proﬁling
020            the Pareto front of auxiliary objectives, and offers post-training control over trade-
021            offs. Additionally, we propose a novel Hyper Prompt Tuning design, that con-
022            veys continuous importance weight across objectives to transformer-based models
023            without altering their architecture, and investigate the potential of temperature-
024            conditioned networks for enhancing the ﬂexibility of post-training control. We
025
               demonstrate the effectiveness and efﬁciency of the HyperDPO framework through
               its applications to various tasks, including Learning-to-Rank (LTR) and LLM
026
               alignment, highlighting its viability for large-scale ML deployments.

029   1     I NTRODUCTION
030   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024b) has been introduced as a memory-
031   and computation-efﬁcient alternative to the traditional Reinforcement Learning with Human Feed-
032   back (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) in Large Language
033   Model (LLM) alignment. The method ﬁne-tunes a pre-trained LLM with additional data that indi-
034   cates the preference between different proposals w.r.t. customized objectives, such as safety, ver-
035   bosity, coherence, etc. (Wu et al., 2024b). The idea of DPO is to reparametrize the reward function
036   in RLHF and guide the ﬁne-tuning process in a supervised learning manner with the preference data.
037   LLM alignment also intersects with the Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) problem, which in-
038   volves ﬁne-tuning a model w.r.t. multiple objectives simultaneously (Ji et al., 2024b; Wu et al.,
039   2024b; Zhou et al., 2023; Rame et al., 2024). In many MOO scenarios within machine learning, a
040   pre-existing model optimized for one or more main objectives is further aligned to a set of auxiliary
041   objectives without signiﬁcantly detracting the model’s performance on the main objectives in order
042   to achieve certain desirable properties (Navon et al., 2020; Ruchte & Grabocka, 2021). This speciﬁc
043
      scenario is termed the Multi-Objective Fine-Tuning (MOFT) problem. As auxiliary objectives may
      conﬂict with each other, the notion of alignment is generalized to achieving the Pareto optimality in
044
      the MOFT setting, where the goal is to proﬁle the Pareto front, representing a spectrum of trade-off
045
      solutions where no single auxiliary objective can be improved without compromising another.
046
047   In this work, we address the task of multi-objective ﬁne-tuning in a broad context through our
048   proposed HyperDPO framework. This conditioned one-shot multi-objective ﬁne-tuning framework
049
      is designed to (1) generalize DPO to the MOFT setting, (2) proﬁle the Pareto front of the auxiliary
      objectives while maintaining the model performance on the main objectives with an efﬁcient one-
050
      shot training process, and (3) offer as ﬂexible post-training controls over the trade-offs as possible.

068   1.2     R ELATED W ORKS
069
      LLM Alignment. LLM alignment has been a popular topic in the machine learning community.
070
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has been a groundbreaking technique for
071   alignment (Christiano et al., 2017; Schulman et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a),
072   which serves as a foundation for training models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), and several
073   advances have been made in this direction (Dong et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2023).
074   To reduce computational complexity, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024b)
075   has been proposed as an alternative to RLHF, and further developed in the literature (Pal et al., 2024;
076   Wu et al., 2024a; Gheshlaghi Azar et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024b; Rafailov et al., 2024a; Zeng et al.,
077   2024; Liu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). We
078   refer readers to Shen et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) for comprehensive reviews on LLM alignment.
079   Multi-Objective Optimization. Multi-Objective Optimization (MOO) has been actively studied
080   in control systems (Gambier & Badreddin, 2007) and economics (Tapia & Coello, 2007). The main
081   focus of the related research is the development of algorithms to proﬁle Pareto fronts efﬁciently so
082   as to understand the trade-offs between objectives. Traditional methods include the evolutionary
083   algorithms (Zhou et al., 2011) and Bayesian optimization (Laumanns & Ocenasek, 2002). Re-
084   cently, gradient-based MOO methods have been studied in the machine learning settings (Sener &
085   Koltun, 2018; Lin et al., 2019; Mahapatra & Rajan, 2020; Liu & Vicente, 2021; Ren et al., 2024).
086   Hypernetwork-based methods are also explored by a series of works (Navon et al., 2020; Lin et al.,
087
      2020; Chen & Kwok, 2022; Hoang et al., 2023).
088   Learning-to-Rank (LTR). Learning to Rank (LTR) (Liu et al., 2009) tasks differ from traditional
089   supervised learning in that they do not associate each sample with a simple label; instead, an optimal
090   order of items within a group to maximize metrics, e.g. Normalized Discount Cumulative Gain
091   (NDCG) (Järvelin & Kekäläinen, 2002; Wang et al., 2013). Typically, LTR models score documents
092   and rank them thereby. To bridge LTR with supervised learning, various differentiable losses have
093   been proposed as the proxy to these metrics (Burges et al., 2006; Taylor et al., 2008; Cao et al.,
094   2007; Qin et al., 2021; Swezey et al., 2021). In the context of Multi-Objective LTR, existing work
095
      includes label aggregation (Dai et al., 2011; Carmel et al., 2020), loss aggregation (Hu & Li, 2018;
      Mahapatra et al., 2023a;b; Tang et al., 2024a), and hypernetwork (Chen et al., 2023).

319     adds a margin reward term depending on w to the DPO loss to ensure multi-objective optimization.

447   Figure 3: Illustration of the implementation of the HyperDPO framework for the LLM alignment
448   task. The proposed Hyper Prompt Tuning method, highlighted within the dashed box on the right,
449   transforms the importance weight vector w into a weight mask and passes it to the LLM via prompt
450   tuning. k denotes the number of virtual tokens, and r is the rank of the weight mask.
451   Model Implementation. In contrast to the LTR task, where we directly concatenate the weight
452   vector w to the input of the model sθ p¨, w|xq in our importance-conditioned network design. In the
453   LLM alignment task, this strategy is generally infeasible, since the input of transformers is tokenized
454   prompts, and the weight vector w is a real vector that is not considered in tokenization and thus a
455   direct concatenation may cause the model to fail to generate meaningful responses.
456   To address this issue and incorporate the information of the weight vector w into the LLM with
457   the least modiﬁcation to the model and the training process, we propose a novel design, called
458   Hyper Prompt Tuning (HPT). The mechanism of HPT is shown in Figure 3. Inspired by Prompt
459   Tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023), HPT augments the input embedding obtained post
460   token embedding and positional encoding with a trainable preﬁx embedding block that is controlled
461   by the weight vector w. Speciﬁcally, HPT follows the following steps:
462   Step 1. HPT takes in a weight vector w P ∆m that indicates the importance across additional ob-
463           jectives and, through two simple trainable MLPs, produces two matrices, the matrix product
464           of which forms the weight mask;
465   Step 2. The weight mask is multiplied entrywise with a trainable preﬁx embedding block with k
466           virtual tokens;
467   Step 3. The preﬁx embedding block is then concatenated to the input embedding as a preﬁx and fed
468           into the transformer blocks of the LLM.
469
      In contrast to Multi-Task Prompt Tuning (Wang et al., 2023), which can only handle a ﬁnite number
470
      of tasks, one can pass a wide spectrum of importance information by HPT into the LLM, offering
471   ﬂexibility and versatility for our importance-conditioned one-shot ﬁne-tuning implementation. Our
472   implementation of HPT is compatible with the PEFT package and does not depend on any speciﬁc
473   LLM architecture.

528   5      D ISCUSSION
529
530   In this work, we propose the HyperDPO framework for multi-objective ﬁne-tuning, which is in-
531
      spired by the DPO framework to proﬁle the Pareto front for a wide range of multi-objective ﬁne-
      tuning problems with a conditioned one-shot ﬁne-tuning approach. Our method presented superior
532
      performance in both the LTR and the large-scale LLM alignment tasks with multiple auxiliary objec-
533
      tives compared to the state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness and efﬁciency of the
534   HyperDPO framework in handling high-dimensional MOFT problems. Our newly proposed Hyper
535   Prompt Tuning technique also provides a novel way to incorporate importance information into the
536   LLM, offering ﬂexibility for both the importance-conditioned implementation and further research
537   in LLM alignment. We also explored the possibility of temperature-conditioned networks in supple-
538   mentary materials, opening up new directions for future research. Our work has proven the potential
539   of the HyperDPO framework, and we expect it to incorporate other possible MOO techniques and
      be further explored in various multi-objective ﬁne-tuning problems in the future.

1104
1105   In general, one could adopt one of the two different parametrizations of sθ p¨, w|xq in the HyperDPO
1106   framework.
1107
1108   • Training from Scratch: The model sθ p¨, w|xq is a completely separate neural network from the
1109     base model sbase py|xq. Depending on the speciﬁc design of the for additional inputs w, the new
1110     model may or may not share the same architecture as the base model. The main advantage of this
1111     design is that it requires less memory and computation resources (Rafailov et al., 2024b), and thus
1112     is more suitable for large-scale applications, e.g. LLMs.
1113

1114
         prone to overﬁtting, one may curb the complexity of the model for the score function sθ p¨, w|xq
1115
         by only adding a ﬁrst-order correction term to the base model sbase py|xq as:
1116
1117
                                       sθ py, w|xq “ sbase py|xq ` ∆sθ py, w|xq,
1118
1119
         where the parameters in the base model are ﬁxed, and the importance-conditioned design is only
1120     applied to the correction term ∆sθ p¨, w|xq. This design allows limited modiﬁcation and reversibil-
1121     ity to the base model and is thus suitable for applications where the ﬁne-tuning is limited in budget,
1122     frequent, or expected to be minor.
1123

1125

1127

1128   framework with the augmentation training design on the same task as the previous ablation studies.
