                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               The application of language models (LMs) to molecular structure generation using
012
               line notations such as SMILES and SELFIES has been well-established in the field
013            of cheminformatics. However, extending these models to generate 3D molecular
014            structures presents significant challenges. Two primary obstacles emerge: (1)
015            the difficulty in designing a 3D line notation that ensures SE(3)-invariant atomic
016            coordinates, and (2) the non-trivial task of tokenizing continuous coordinates for
017            use in LMs, which inherently require discrete inputs. To address these challenges,
018            we propose Mol-StrucTok, a novel method for tokenizing 3D molecular structures.
019            Our approach comprises two key innovations: (1) We design a line notation for
020            3D molecules by extracting local atomic coordinates in a spherical coordinate
021
               system. This notation builds upon existing 2D line notations and remains agnostic
               to their specific forms, ensuring compatibility with various molecular representation
022
               schemes. (2) We employ a Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE)
023
               to tokenize these coordinates, treating them as generation descriptors. To further
024            enhance the representation, we incorporate neighborhood bond lengths and bond
025            angles as understanding descriptors. Leveraging this tokenization framework, we
026            train a GPT-2 style model for 3D molecular generation tasks. Results demonstrate
027            strong performance with significantly faster generation speeds and competitive
028            chemical stability compared to previous methods. Further, by integrating our
029            learned discrete representations into Graphormer model for property prediction
030            on QM9 dataset, Mol-StrucTok reveals consistent improvements across various
031            molecular properties, underscoring the versatility and robustness of our approach.

      To address these challenges, we propose a novel method for 3D molecular structure tokenization,
      called Mol-StrucTok. Our approach involves constructing a spherical line notation and subse-
082
      quently discretizing the continuous atomic coordinates. In the first step, we develop a spherical
083
      line notation tailored to 3D molecular structures. Similar to autoregressive models in 3D molecule
084   generation (Gebauer et al., 2019; Simm et al., 2020; Daigavane et al., 2023), we sequentially place
085   atoms in 3D space, defining their distances, bond angles, and torsion angles through a local spherical
086   coordinate system. However, we diverge by constructing this spherical coordinate system indepen-
087   dently, without relying on a predictive model to determine reference points. By appending each
088   atom’s local spherical coordinates to its corresponding token in the original line notation, we preserve
089   both the molecular graph topology and the SE(3)-invariant 3D structural information.
090   In the second step, we utilize a vector quantized variational autoencoder (Van Den Oord et al., 2017)
091   to discretize the continuous coordinates, as continuous vectors must be transformed for autoregressive
092   language modeling approaches (Vaswani, 2017; Radford et al., 2019; Ramesh et al., 2021). Drawing
093   inspiration from the work of van Kempen et al. (2022), we treat each atom as a data point and use
094   its spherical coordinates as generation descriptors. Additionally, to enrich the representation of
095   the atomic environment, we introduce supplementary descriptors, including bond lengths and bond
096   angles of neighboring atoms, which provide a more comprehensive understanding of the local atomic
097   structure.
098   Building on this framework, we train a GPT-2 model (Radford et al., 2019) and achieve strong
099   performance in 3D molecular generation tasks. Prior autoregressive models (Gebauer et al., 2019;
100   Simm et al., 2020; Daigavane et al., 2023) often resulted in molecules with low chemical stability,
101   while diffusion models (Hoogeboom et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; You et al., 2023), though more
102   effective, incurred high computational costs due to the extensive number of diffusion steps. Our
103   method strikes a balance by delivering competitive chemical stability while significantly reducing
104
      generation time. Additionally, the powerful conditional generation capabilities of language models en-
      able our approach to excel in tasks demanding conditional molecular generation, yielding substantial
105
      improvements in both efficiency and accuracy.
106
107   Furthermore, we show that the learned discrete representations are also beneficial for molecular
      understanding tasks. By incorporating the learned structure tokens as additional embeddings in

116   3D Molecule Generation. Early works such as G-Schnet (Gebauer et al., 2019), MolGym (Simm
117   et al., 2020), and G-SphereNet Luo & Ji (2022) focus on autoregressively predicting atom types
118   and their connectivity in sequence. Alternative generative models, such as Variational Autoencoders
119
      (VAE) (Nesterov et al., 2020) and Normalizing Flow models (Garcia Satorras et al., 2021), have
      explored single-shot generation strategies. With the rise of diffusion models, numerous studies
120
      have demonstrated their remarkable strength in 3D molecular modeling (Hoogeboom et al., 2022;
121
      Xu et al., 2023; You et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023), consistently outperforming autoregressive
122   counterparts. However, recent autoregressive approaches, such as Symphony (Daigavane et al., 2023)
123   and Geo2Seq (Li et al., 2024), have also shown promising results, bridging the performance gap and
124   suggesting continued relevance of this methodology in molecular generation.
125
      Structure Tokenization. Recent advancements in tokenizing 3D structures for biomolecules, par-
126
      ticularly proteins, have gained considerable attention. FoldSeek (van Kempen et al., 2022) first
127
      used VQ-VAE to tokenize protein structures for improving structure alignment. Building on this,
128   researchers applied this structure alphabet to models like ESM (Su et al., 2023) and T5 (Heinzinger
129   et al., 2023), providing discrete structural embeddings that aid in protein understanding. ESM3 (Hayes
130   et al., 2024) and FoldToken (Gao et al., 2024) have further explored the use of VQ-VAE’s discrete
131   representations to predict protein structures. However, similar approaches have not been developed
132   for small molecules, as their lack of a well-defined sequence makes tokenization into ordered repre-
133   sentations more challenging. Some studies have attempted to tokenize the continuous coordinates of
134   small molecules into digits (Zholus et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), but this approach renders models
135   highly sensitive to numerical length, impairs their ability to capture semantic relationships between
136
      similar values, and hinders generalization to unseen data.

138   3     M ETHODS
139
140   Our proposed Mol-StrucTok consists of two key components: constructing a spherical line notation
141   and discretizing continuous atomic coordinates. This approach aims to preserve both molecular
142   topology and SE(3)-invariant structural information.

144   3.1     P RELIMINARY: AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION OF 3D M OLECULES
145
146   Molecular Notation. A molecule is modeled as a graph G = (V, E), where V represents the set of
147   atoms, and E represents the set of chemical bonds between them. Additionally, we use R to denote
148   the conformation of G, forming a complete 3D molecular structure M = (G, R) = (V, E, R). Each
149   atom vi ∈ V is associated with a 3D spatial coordinate xi ∈ R3 , and its atom type is represented by
150   zi ∈ Z, where zi is the atomic number (nuclear charge) of the i-th atom.
151   In earlier tasks of 2D molecular graph generation, many works have modeled this problem in an
152   autoregressive manner (Popova et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2021). They iteratively
153   predicting new atom types and bond formations until no new atoms can be added or no bonds can be
154   formed with the current molecule. Similarly, autoregressive generation of 3D molecules follows a
155   process where new atoms are sequentially placed in 3D space (Simm et al., 2020; Luo & Ji, 2022;
156   Zhang et al., 2023). At the i-th step, denote the intermediate 3D molecular geometry generated from
157   the preceding i − 1 steps as Mi = (Gi , Ri ), consisting of i atoms. This process can be described
158
      as constructing a sequence of increasingly larger structures: M = {M0 , M1 , . . . , M|V| }, where at
      step i, the i-th atom type zi and its spatial coordinates xi are generated. A local coordinate system is
159
      established at each stage Mi , predicting the atom’s local coordinates, which are then transformed
160
      into global spatial coordinates. The process is divided into three main steps:
161
          1. Predict next atom type based on the partial molecular structure.

163        reference atoms. Typically, a focal atom f is chosen, along with its closest and second-closest
164        atoms, c1 and c2 , respectively 1 .

        3. Define the new atom coordinates. In the spherical coordinate system, the model predicts the
166
           descriptors di , θi , and φi . Here, di ∈ R+ is the distance between the new atom and f , while
167        θi ∈ [0, π] and φi ∈ (−π, π] are angular coordinates. Some later works, like Daigavane et al.
168        (2023), avoid spherical coordinates in favor of orientation prediction to better handle symmetry.
169
170
      The order of the first and second steps can vary in some works (Simm et al., 2020). Each step involves
      specific model predictions, described as follows:

179   A Generalized Line Notation. To represent a molecule in a linear format, we tokenize the molecular
180   graph into a sequence of atom tokens A and non-atom tokens B. Let T = A ∪ B be the set of all
181   possible tokens. The sequence is constructed as follows:
182      • Atom Tokens. Each atom vi ∈ V is mapped to an atom token ti ∈ A. These tokens typically
183        correspond to the atomic type of the atoms in the molecule 2 .
184      • Non-Atom Tokens. Non-atom tokens correspond to chemical bonds between atoms or other
185        structural features such as branching or ring closure. Each bond eij ∈ E is mapped to a non-atom
186        token tij ∈ B, indicating the bond type (e.g., single, double, or triple bond).
187
      Thus, for a molecule M, the tokenized sequence s can be expressed as:
188
189                                               s = (t1 , t2 , . . . , tm ),                                     (2)
190   where each ti is either an atom token ti ∈ A or a non-atom token ti ∈ B, m is the total number of
191   tokens in the sequence.

194   of a molecule can be accurately reconstructed. The local spherical coordinates of each atom can
195   themselves form a sequence when arranged in a specific order. In this context, we can adopt the
196   sequence of the line notation s as the ordering scheme.

337   4        E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
338
339   We evaluated Mol-StrucTok across several aspects to assess its ability to capture valid 3D struc-
340   ture distributions, demonstrate strong conditional generation performance, and provide informative
341   structural tokens that serve as robust 3D representations.
342
343   4.1        S ETUP
344
345   Vector Quantization. To achieve atom-level quantization, we trained a vector quantized variational
346   autoencoder on the PCQM4Mv2 dataset (Nakata & Shimazaki, 2017), which contains 3.4 million
347   organic molecular structures and approximately 99 million atomic descriptors. We opted for a
348
      lightweight architecture, inspired by the simplicity of the Foldseek (van Kempen et al., 2022).
      Specifically, we implemented a 3-layer MLP encoder and a 2-layer MLP decoder, totaling 74k
349
      parameters. The hidden dimension was set to 128, and the latent space embedding dimension was
350
      chosen as 5. The structural vocabulary size was defined as 256. A larger vocabulary leads to lower
351   reconstruction error, but presents greater challenges in downstream tasks. Further configurations of
352   the quantizer can be found in the Appendix Section C.1.
353
354
      Dataset. Leveraging the learned structural alphabet, we conducted comprehensive evaluations on
      downstream tasks, primarily focusing on the QM9 dataset (Ramakrishnan et al., 2014). QM9 is a
355
      widely used quantum chemistry dataset that provides one equilibrium conformation and 12 geometric,
356
      energetic, electronic, and thermodynamic properties for 134,000 stable organic molecules composed
357   of CHONF atoms. If not stated, the data split follows standard settings, with 110,000 samples for
358   training, 10,000 for validation, and the remaining 10,831 samples used for testing.
359
360
      Generation. For the molecular generation tasks, we adopted the GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019)
      architecture with 12 layers and a hidden dimension of 768. Using the SELFIES line notation, we
361
      generated a vocabulary of 2,331 tokens 3 . Mol-StrucTok was evaluated in both unconditional and
362
      conditional generation settings to assess its ability to generate valid molecular structures. Unless
363   explicitly stated otherwise, the decoding strategy utilizes multinomial sampling with a top-k value of
364   50 and a temperature setting of τ = 0.7. The implementation details are provided in the Appendix.
365
366
      Understanding. In the molecular understanding tasks, we used Graphormer (Ying et al., 2021) as
      the base model, with 12 layers and a hidden size of 768. No modifications were made to the model
367
      architecture aside from augmenting the atom-type embedding with the Mol-StrucTok embedding.
368
      We then evaluated its performance on the QM9 property prediction tasks to assess its effectiveness in
369   capturing molecular properties. The detailed model configurations are provided in the Appendix C.3.
370

519   efficiency. Language models inherently support much faster sampling rates compared to diffusion
520   models, and advanced techniques like KV-cache (Radford et al., 2019) further accelerate this process.
521   In our experiments, we observed that sampling 10,000 molecular structures achieved an average speed
522   of 39.8 samples per second on a single A100 GPU with a batch size of 16. In contrast, diffusion-based
523   models such as EDM (Hoogeboom et al., 2022) and GeoLDM (Xu et al., 2023) reached only 1.4
524   samples per second, representing a nearly 28× speedup.
525
526   6               C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
527
528   In this work, we introduced Mol-StrucTok, a novel method for tokenizing 3D molecular structures
529   using spherical line notation and a vector quantizer. Our approach not only demonstrated competitive
530   chemical stability but also exhibited significantly stronger conditioning capabilities and much faster
531   generation speeds compared to existing methods.
532
      The first future direction is to improve the quality of the quantizers to enhance both reconstruction
533
      accuracy and downstream modeling performance. Second, as a general framework for linearizing 3D
534   molecules and enabling language-based modeling, Mol-StrucTok opens up the potential for exploring
535   hybrid representations. One promising direction is to integrate molecular generation with natural
536   language, supporting joint modeling of 3D molecular structures and text, paving the way for truly
537   native multimodal models. Another exciting potential lies in expanding the scope of 3D molecular
538   design, where language models can facilitate more advanced conditional generation tasks, such
539   as pocket-guided ligand design, 3D molecule editing, etc. Such advancements would enable the
      generation of molecules tailored to specific biological or chemical environments.

      C.1             V ECTOR Q UANTIZATION
813
814   After obtaining the descriptors, we applied normalization to all of them. For the length descriptors,
815   we used log normalization, while for the angle descriptors, we normalized them to a 0 − 1 range
816   within the 0 − π space. This normalization proved beneficial for training the quantizer. During
817   training, we used a batch size 512, learning rate of 1e − 4 with a 5-epoch warm-up, and the learning
818   rate schedule remained constant throughout.
819
820   C.2             G ENERATION
821
822   For training, we set the batch size to 64 and trained for 200 epochs with a learning rate of 4e − 4.
823   We applied a warm-up phase of 3000 steps, followed by a linear decay schedule. The generation
824   configuration used a default temperature of 0.7, top-k sampling with k = 50, and a maximum
825
      sequence length of 100, while the longest sequence in the dataset was 77 tokens. We applied a
      repetition penalty of 1 to avoid redundant generations.

827   During the reconstruction of coordinates, since the molecular topology is generated simultaneously,
828   we employed a topology-aware optimization algorithm to refine the 2D structure. This optimization
829   follows the approach used in LDM-3DG (You et al., 2023).
