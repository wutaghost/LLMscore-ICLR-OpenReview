009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce similarity group equivariant convolutional networks (SECNNs), de-
012            signed to achieve continuous translation, rotation and scale equivariance, or discrete
013            similarity group equivariance that involves discrete Dihedral group. The networks
014            are implemented as steerable CNNs by employing a steerable and approximately
015            shiftable and scalable basis for continuous translating, rotating and scaling convo-
016
               lution kernels within a five-dimensional position-orientation-scale-reflection space.
               Our results demonstrate that SECNNs attain state-of-the-art results on translated,
017
               rotated and scaled MNIST datasets. SECNNs also achieve the accuracy of other
018
               leading group equivariant networks on CIFAR10/100, while being equivariant to
019            the full range of the similarity group in comparison to existing state of the art,
020            which is equivariant to only sub-groups of the similarity group.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have played a significant role in the progress of deep learning,
026   notably in tasks involving image and pattern recognition. The central operation in CNNs, convolution,
027
      effectively reduces the number of free parameters, allowing the network to be deeper with fewer
      parameters. Furthermore, convolution exhibits a property known as equivariance—a translation in
028
      the input results in a corresponding translation in the output. This attribute allows CNNs to recognize
029
      objects in various positions without requiring training on images with the object at different locations,
030   thus, when combined with pooling, CNNs achieve translation-invariant recognition.
031
032   While translation equivariance is beneficial, real-world data often contains more complex trans-
033
      formations. Recognizing this, Cohen & Welling (2016) expanded the concept of equivariance in
      CNNs to include other transformations such as rotation and reflection, collectively known as the
034
      Euclidean group. This led to the development of group equivariant CNNs (G-CNNs) that employ
035
      group convolutions (G-convolutions) to handle transformations within this group. Crucially, this
036   expansion into rotation introduced an extra orientation-specific dimension, resulting in both the
037   feature maps and convolutional kernels of G-CNNs being inherently three-dimensional (3D).
038
039
      Differing from traditional CNNs, G-CNNs must specifically account for transformations beyond
      translation. Special consideration must be taken because image pixels are typically sampled on a
040
      rectangular grid, whereas discrete translation equates to a mere shift in indices, transformations like
041
      rotation and scaling involve more complex interpolations. To facilitate continuous rotation, Worrall
042   et al. (2017) employed circular harmonics as basis functions for representing convolution kernels.
043   This basis approach treats a convolution kernel as a linear combination of these basis functions.
044   Consequently, the convolution operation between an input and a kernel transforms into a two-step
045   process: firstly, convolving the input with the basis functions, followed by a linear combination of the
046   resultant feature maps. This methodology, a.k.a. steerable CNNs Cohen & Welling (2017); Weiler
047   & Cesa (2019), allows the filter to undergo continuous rotation by simply manipulating the basis
048   functions, which involves their multiplication with complex numbers.
049   Continuous rotation can be readily achieved since orientation is periodic, allowing the use of circular
050   harmonics as a basis. However, achieving continuous equivariance in translation and scale presents
051   greater challenges in G-CNNs, as functions of position and scale are typically aperiodic, leading
052   mainly to discrete equivariance. Azulay & Weiss (2019) highlighted a limitation in the translation
053   equivariance of conventional CNNs due to the discrete convolution and the process of downsampling.
      Recently, Sun & Blu (2023) introduced an innovative approach using a basis of harmonics defined


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      within a log-polar coordinate system, aiming for continuous scale equivariance. Nevertheless, fully
055   realizing continuous equivariance across translation, rotation, and scale in G-CNNs is an ongoing
056   challenge in the field. The inclusion of reflection, alongside these three transformations, constitutes
057   what is known as the similarity transformation group. Importantly, similarity transformations do
058   not alter the shape of an input. Thus, a convolutional network that is equivariant to the similarity
059   transformation group can effectively preserve shape information, offering a promising direction for
060   research involving similarity transformations, e.g., feature matching Gleize et al. (2023).
061   In addition to being able to handle combined continuous similarity transformations, the difficulty
062   of designing similarity group equivariant convolutional networks is the computational cost associ-
063   ated with multi-dimensional kernels and feature maps. G-convolutions about rotation, scaling and
064   reflection introduce new dimensions to account for orientation, scale and reflection respectively. This
065   lifts the two dimensional (2D) image plane to up to five dimensional (5D) spaces (2D translation
066   plus rotation, scaling and reflection). Thanks to the steerable CNNs approach, efficient construction
067   of 3D convolution kernels for translation-rotation G-convolutions is demonstrated in Marcos et al.
068   (2017) and Cheng et al. (2019). It involves stacking rotated versions of 2D kernels to form a 3D
069
      kernel, an approach similarly adopted for achieving translation-scale Sosnovik et al. (2020) and
      translation-rotation-scale equivariance Gao et al. (2022); Sun & Blu (2023). However, this method
070
      inherently limits the multi-dimensional kernels to augmented 2D kernels. This restricts the range
071
      of learnable kernels to a subset of those possible in G-CNNs. While the multi-dimensional kernel
072   is more general than augmented 2D kernel, it incurs significant computational costs due to the
073   multi-dimensional operations involved.
074
075
      An alternative approach for achieving continuous equivariance involves mapping elements of the
      Lie group to the Lie algebra and representing convolution kernels with B-splines Bekkers (2019) (B-
076
      splines CNN) and multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) Finzi et al. (2020). This method allows for handling
077
      non-uniformly sampled data on arbitrary dimensions, offering significant flexibility. However, there is
078   a trade-off for this flexibility, when it comes to achieving equivariance with respect to the semi-direct
079   product structure, such as the similarity group Sim(n) ∼       = Rn ⋊ H. Here, convolution kernels
080   are tailored relative to the group H and convolved point-wise with data on the Rn space. If the
081   data is uniformly sampled, this equates to a discrete convolution, which fails to achieve continuous
082   translation equivariance. Furthermore, the approximation nature of MLPs precludes this method from
083   achieving exact equivariance.
084   In this paper, we address the challenge of full similarity group equivariant CNNs and hence continuous
085   translating, rotating and scaling 5D kernels in G-CNN by leveraging the steerable and approximately
086   shiftable and scalable basis proposed in Zhang & Williams (2022). The key contributions of this
087   study are summarized as follows:
088
      Extension and Refinement of Basis Functions: We have refined and extended the basis function
089
      proposed by Zhang & Williams (2022) to include capabilities for reflection, and enhanced its
090   properties to be steerable, approximately shiftable, and scalable.
091
092   Design and Construction of SECNNs: Zhang & Williams (2022) conducted experiments with a
093
      shallow implementation involving only a single convolution with a precomputed kernel. Adopting the
      steerable CNNs approach, we designed and constructed SECNNs using the enhanced basis function.
094
      These networks learn convolutional kernels capable of continuous translation, rotation, and scaling,
095
      primarily targeting computer vision tasks involving 2D images.
096
097   Performance and Computational Optimization:The representation and weight spaces of SECNNs
098   are adjustable to subgroups of the similarity group to cater to different tasks. We have made SECNNs
099
      computationally feasible and optimized SECNNs’ performance through strategic design choices,
      such as employing a cropped Fourier series of the basis function and implementing nonlinear and
100
      normalization functions in the spatial domain on real-valued representations.
101
102   Empirical Evaluation and Benchmarking: We conducted a comprehensive benchmark study
103   comparing our SECNNs with state-of-the-art equivariant networks on image datasets such as MNIST
104   variants and CIFAR10/100. This study not only illustrates the empirical advantages of our network
105
      but also explores various design choices within our SECNN framework.

117   1.1      R ELATED W ORK
118
119   The Steerable CNNs Approach: H-Net (Worrall et al. (2017)) employed circular harmonics as
120
      a steerable basis for continuous rotation by manipulating harmonic phases. Subsequent research
      SFCNN (Weiler et al. (2018)), E2CNN (Weiler & Cesa (2019)) and RotDFC (Cheng et al. (2019)) used
121
      steerable bases to condense three-dimensional weights to two dimensions, enhancing computational
122
      efficiency while retaining the three-dimensional intermediate representations. RotEqNet (Marcos
123   et al. (2017)) proposed a rotation equivariant G-CNN that returns a vector field about orientation.
124   Similarly, Marcos et al. (2018) proposed a scale equivariant G-CNN for vector field. Moreover,
125   SESN (Sosnovik et al. (2020; 2021a;b)) and deep scale-space CNN (DSSCNN, Worrall & Welling
126   (2019)) have led to the creation of translation and scale subgroup equivariant CNNs by applying
127   predefined scale operations on kernels. Likewise, RST-CNN(Gao et al. (2022)) have extended the
128   translation and rotation (or scale) subgroup equivariant CNNs to develop similarity group CNNs
129   with four-dimensional intermediate representations and two-dimensional weights. In a more recent
130   development, SREN(Sun & Blu (2023)) advocate the utilization of a steerable and scalable basis to
131
      continuously rotate and scale two-dimensional kernels. This method calculates the optimal orientation
      and scale in the extra two dimensions, efficiently compressing the four-dimensional intermediate
132
      representation to two dimensions. While the aforementioned works leverage predefined bases, which
133
      are limited in their generalization to other symmetry transformations, Zhdanov et al. (2024) proposed
134   the use of MLPs to learn steerable kernels (or bases) for translation and other compact groups.
135
136   In contrast to these approaches, we derive a basis for the similarity group, which is not a compact
137
      group. This basis provides a theoretical framework for achieving exact continuous similarity equivari-
      ance. Table 1 offers a comparative summary of these developments in steerable CNNs, contrasting
138
      them with our proposed SECNNs.
139
140   The Lie Group and Lie Algebra Approach: The integration of Lie Algebra, MLPs (and B-splines),
141   and Monte Carlo methods provides a flexible framework for exploring various Lie group equivariances.
142   MacDonald et al. (2022) enhanced this approach to include Lie groups whose exponential maps are
143
      not surjective, such as the affine group. Further advancements by Mironenco & Forré (2024) extended
      this methodology to accommodate Lie groups that are neither compact nor abelian, broadening the
144
      applicability of this approach.
145
146   One significant challenge with expanding the group to incorporate more transformations is the
147   associated increase in computational costs. To address this, Knigge et al. (2022) introduced separable
148   G-CNNs, which keep the convolution kernels for Rn and H separable, enhancing computational
149
      efficiency. Building on this idea, Bekkers et al. (2023) proposed associating invariant attributes to
      point pairs, which boosts the computational efficiency of G-CNNs. In a parallel development, Li
150
      et al. (2024) achieved affine equivariance by employing differential invariants, although this method
151
      diverges from the traditional Lie group and Lie algebra approach. Several methods within this
152   category are categorized as approximated steerable in Table 1 due to their use of B-splines and MLPs.
153
154   Most methods of both steerable CNNs approach and this approach treat representations in Rn as
155
      either point clouds or uniformly sampled points, which does not guarantee continuous translation
      equivariance. Our method specifically addresses this limitation by utilizing shiftable basis functions
156
      to ensure continuous translation equivariance effectively

      reflections—all of which are shape-preserving transformations. Since 2D similarity transformations

247   In this section, we aim to approximate the group convolution Eq. 4 through a basis expansion
248   (similar to Eq. 5 but generalized to the similarity group, see Fig. 1 (b)) to 1) avoid directly sampling
249   the similarity group, which does not achieve full continuous equivariance, and 2) avoid direct
250   interpolation, which introduces errors.
251
      To achieve this, we first modify the 2D steerable AFMT basis functions defined in Eq. 8 to be
252   approximately shiftable and scalable; we call this the S3 basis. This modification allows continuous
253   translations, rotations and scalings of a kernel to be represented as linear combinations of a finite
254   set of modified basis functions. Note that these transformations correspond to the linear operation
255   A−1 x − y on the R2 plane in Eq. 3. Then, to account for the rest operations (ϕ − θ, ρr , ab) on the
256   S 1 × R+ × {±1} space, we generalize these modified basis functions to higher-dimensional basis
257   functions defined over the 5D function space R2 ×S 1 ×R+ ×{±1} → C. This generalization enables
258   us to incorporate the full range of transformations in the similarity group. Finally, we implement the
259   similarity group convolution by utilizing these generalized and modified basis functions.

      4     C ONSTRUCTION OF D EEP S IMILARITY G ROUP E QUIVARIANT CNN S
412
413
      In this section, we discuss deep SECNN architecture details, such as nonlinear function, normalization,
414   pooling and number of channels, that help achieve optimal performance. A typical feed-forward
415   architecture for SECNNs is illustrated in Fig. 1 (d).
416
417
      Nonlinearity and Normalization: Feature maps are converted back and forth between the spatial
      domain (S 1 × R+ ) and frequency domain even if the simConv is for the continuous subgroup.
418
      This is because the conversions allow us to leverage the spatial domain and real number ReLU and
419
      BatchNorm. We avoid the same operations for complex numbers because maintaining equivariance
420   requires preserving the phases of complex numbers, which contain essential information about
421   orientation and scale. Therefore, the nonlinear activation and normalization functions are designed to
422   only affect the magnitudes of complex numbers. However, our experiments show that this frequency
423   domain implementation consistently yields sub-optimal results. As stated in Trabelsi et al. (2017),
424   the non-holomorphic nature of ReLU on magnitudes of complex numbers, which does not satisfy the
425   Cauchy-Riemann equations, might be the underlying reason.
426   Group Pooling: In our SECNN architecture, a group pooling layer is strategically placed after the last
427   convolution layer to aggregate feature maps in R2 × S 1 × R+ × {±1}. The output from this pooling
428   layer then feeds into the final fully-connected layer. The choice of pooling operation is tailored
429   to the specific requirements of the application. For instance, global max pooling is employed for
430   invariant classification tasks, which demand a high level of feature stability regardless of geometric
431   transformations. Conversely, for natural image classification, we utilize an orientation histogram
      approach that better captures the variability inherent in natural scenes.

      The orientation histogram is computed as follows: Feature maps in R2 × S 1 × R+ × {±1} are first

447   the orientation with the maximum value is identified. The feature maps are then divided into m × m
448   cells, and an orientation histogram is computed for each cell. Finally, all histograms in {±1} and the
449   m × m cells are concatenated. This method is similar to the SIFT descriptor (Lowe (2004)). See
450   Appendix C.1 for more disscusion.
451
      Trading Off Full Equivariance for More Features and Hence Accuracy: Previous studies on
452   group equivariant networks often highlight parameter efficiency, demonstrating superior performance
453   over baseline models with a comparable number of parameters. However, despite the generality of
454   weights in R2 × S 1 × R+ (×{±1}), the inherently 4D (or 5D) nature of these weights consumes
455   a significant number of parameters. This high parameter count implies that, under a fixed total
456   parameter budget, a SECNN might have fewer channels in each layer compared to traditional models,
457   potentially reducing the diversity of features it can learn. Table 4 illustrates the architectures of
458   SECNNs, where the 4D SECNN has approximately one-third the channels of its baseline counterpart.
459   To mitigate this issue, we explore methods to increase the number of output channels by reducing
460   the dimensions of weights in certain layers. One approach involves fixing the weight across certain
461   dimensions, rendering it a rectangular function in the frequency domain and a Sinc function in the
462   spatial domain. For example, setting G(ωϕ , sρ , ωθ , sr , ab) = G̃(ωϕ , sρ , ωθ ) implies the weight in the
463   R+ space is a Sinc function, which preserves the feature maps’ dimensions. This configuration equals
464   to using separable kernels (Knigge et al. (2022)) and is utilized in our invariant MNIST classification
465   experiments, where the preservation of feature map dimensions is crucial for achieving equivariance.
466   Alternatively, the reduction in parameter count per channel can be achieved by applying max-
467   pooling to feature maps followed by lifting them back using Eq. 15, where the weight becomes 2D.
468   This approach has been shown by Sosnovik et al. (2020) to yield optimal results in natural image
469   classification experiments, particularly evident in our CIFAR classification tests.

525   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
526
527
      In this paper, we construct SECNNs that are equivariant to a continuous translation, rotation and
528   scaling group and a discrete similarity group. The networks are built on our proposed steerable
529   and approximately shiftable and scalable basis. The former achieved the state-of-the-art results
530   on the classification of translated, rotated and scaled MNIST, demonstrating the effectiveness of
531   the approximate shiftable and scalable properties. Furthermore, the latter achieves the accuracy of
532   other leading group equivariant networks on CIFAR10/100, showcasing the effectiveness of discrete
533   similarity group equivariance.
534   The primary limitation of our approach is the high computational cost associated with implementing
535   the simConv (see runtime in Table 5 of Appendix D.4). Currently, simConv, as defined in Eq. 16,
536   is similar to a 4D discrete convolution in terms of computational cost. We employed PyTorch’s
537   unfold function to implement simConv, which uses a sliding window method that explicitly cre-
538   ates all windows, leading to substantial memory consumption. Optimizing this implementation
539   through a customized CUDA kernel could significantly reduce both computational costs and memory
      requirements.

      D.1   T RANSLATED , ROTATED AND S CALED MNIST
867
868   In rotation and scale equivariant CNN studies, the rotated MNIST (LeCun et al. (1998)) and scaled
869   MNIST are frequently used datasets. We augmented the MNIST to created various combination of
870   translated, rotated and scaled MNIST. Each dataset contains 10k training images, 2k for evaluation,
871   and 50k for testing. Our results stem from training on a combined set of 12k images, pulling from
872   both the training and evaluation datasets. To create the dataset, we augmented the MNIST-12k dataset
873
      by rotating and/or scaling images within range [0, 2π) and [0.3, 1] respectively. Additionally, we
      apply non-integer translation on the images along both x− and y−axis within range [−5, 5]. All
874
      transformations are performed using bilinear interpolation to simulate continuous transformations.
875
      Consequently, we have several distinct MNIST datasets that are transformed by mixtures of these
876   transformations.
877
878   The augmentation for the rotated MNIST involves random rotation within the range of [0, 2π). For
879
      the scaled MNIST, we applied random scaling within the range of [0.5, 2]. Depend on the mixture of
      transformations, we combined the two types of augmentations. We did not perform training-time
880
      augmentation in translation as the PyTorch’s RandomAffine function implements discrete translation.
881
      The images are also mean and standard deviation normalized.
882
883
      D.2   CIFAR10/100
884
885   In our experiments involving the natural image datasets CIFAR10/100 (Krizhevsky et al. (2009)),
886   each dataset contains 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images, distributed across 10 and 100
887   categories, respectively. We evaluated both the proposed networks and the baseline models with
888   specific training augmentations. These augmentations encompassed random translation, random
889   horizontal flipping, as well as mean and standard deviation normalization.
890
891   D.3   SECNN S AND T RAINING PARAMETERS
892
893   The SECNNs use 5 × 5 cropped shiftable basis, which are computed from 1024 × 1024 spatial
894
      frequencies (ωx ), to implement√simConv. For images with input size n × n, the spatial period
895
      X = 2n and scale period S = 2n. The number of orientation and scale frequencies (ωϕ , ωθ , ωρ
      and ωr ) is 5. There are 16 orientations (8 for CIFAR experiments) uniformly sampled in [0, 2π) and
896
      8 scales uniformly sampled in [0.5, 8]. Finally, αρ = −1 and αr = 0.
897
898   All models are trained for 200 epochs with a batch size of 128. We use SGD optimizer with Nesterov
899   momentum of 0.9 and weight decay of 0.0005. The initial learning rate is set to 0.1 and divided by 5
900   after 60, 120 and 180 epochs.
