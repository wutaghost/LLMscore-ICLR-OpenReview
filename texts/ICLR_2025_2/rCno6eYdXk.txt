009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dataset reduction (DR) seeks to select or distill samples from large datasets into
012            smaller subsets while preserving performance on target tasks. Existing methods
013            primarily focus on pruning or synthesizing data in the same format as the original
014            dataset, typically the input data and corresponding labels. However, in DR set-
015            tings, we find it is possible to synthesize more information beyond the data-label
016            pair as an additional learning target to facilitate model training. In this paper, we
017            introduce Dataset Reduction Using Privileged Information (DRUPI), which en-
018
               riches DR by synthesizing privileged information alongside the reduced dataset.
               This privileged information can take the form of feature labels or attention la-
019
               bels, providing auxiliary supervision to improve model learning. Our findings re-
020
               veal that effective feature labels must balance between being overly discriminative
021            and excessively diverse, with a moderate level proving optimal for improving the
022            reduced dataset’s efficacy. Extensive experiments on ImageNet, CIFAR-10/100,
023            and Tiny ImageNet demonstrate that DRUPI integrates seamlessly with existing
024            dataset reduction methods, offering significant performance gains. Code is in-
025            cluded in the supplementary material and will be released.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Dataset Reduction (DR) has attracted considerable attention in recent years, with the primary aim
031   of compressing large datasets into smaller subsets while maintaining comparable statistical per-
032   formance. Existing methods for DR can be broadly classified into two main categories: coreset
033
      selection and dataset distillation. Coreset selection methods focus on selecting a subset of samples
      from the original dataset (Har-Peled & Mazumdar, 2004; Welling, 2009; Toneva et al., 2018), while
034
      dataset distillation involves synthesizing unseen samples from the dataset (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao
035
      et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2022; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024).
036
037   In typical real-world scenarios, training models for target tasks is generally constrained to input
038   data (e.g., images) and their corresponding labels, as these are the most readily available elements.
039
      While existing DR methods have shown strong performance (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020;
      Zhao & Bilen, 2022; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2023), they typically do so by compressing
040
      datasets in the same or similar format, such as the conventional data-label structure. Even advanced
041
      dataset distillation techniques, which re-parameterize images or labels to create alternative represen-
042   tations (Kim et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Deng & Russakovsky, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
043   2024), are limited by this conventional framework. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), this reliance on
044   fixed data-label structures restricts the capacity of such methods to incorporate richer information
045   that could further enhance model training and improve generalization.
046
      In fact, DR settings offer the potential to create more diverse compressed datasets that extend beyond
047
      simple input data xi and labels yi , incorporating richer forms of information. A notable example is
048   the concept of privileged information, first introduced in the context of statistical learning (Vapnik
049   & Vashist, 2009; Pechyony & Vapnik, 2010). Figure 2 provides a illustration for privileged infor-
050   mation. Let us consider a more concrete example, where xi might represent a biopsy image, and the
051   privileged information fi⋆ for xi could be the oncologist’s written assessment of the image (Vapnik
052   et al., 2015). The label yi would then indicate whether the tissue in the image is malignant or benign.
053   By leveraging this privileged information fi⋆ , a medical expert can make more informed decisions,
      benefiting from additional insights that improve diagnostic accuracy.

      However, none of the existing methods compress the original dataset beyond the traditional data-
071
      label structure or synthesize privileged information for auxiliary supervision. To address this gap,
072   we introduce a novel approach that, for the first time, incorporates not only images and labels but
073   also privileged information. Our method, called Dataset Reduction Using Privileged Information
074   (DRUPI), is illustrated in Figure 1(b). We primarily synthesize feature labels for the reduced dataset,
075   as these labels capture richer, high-dimensional information, enhancing dataset quality. These fea-
076   ture labels generalize effectively across various neural network architectures and provide a uni-
077   fied representation of latent statistics across multiple models, offering additional supervision during
078   training. Additionally, we propose a more efficient form of feature labels, i.e., attention labels. As
079   shown in Figure 1(c), the incorporation of privileged information produces gradients more aligned
080
      with those of the original dataset, ultimately improving the model’s generalization capabilities.
081   A key challenge for DRUPI lies in determining appropriate feature labels to synthesize for the re-
082   duced dataset. To address this, we employ an arbitrary methods of dataset distillation to synthesize
083   the feature labels. During each step of the bi-level optimization, we match the statistical infor-
084   mation of models trained on reduced datasets with and without feature labels. Furthermore, we
085   observed that synthesized feature labels cannot be overly discriminative or diverse, which degrade
086
      the overall quality of the reduced dataset. This finding suggests that an optimal balance between
      discriminability and diversity is crucial for synthesizing effective feature labels. Our contributions
087
      are summarized as follows:
088
089   1. We propose a new paradigm, i.e., DRUPI, for dataset reduction. In particular, privileged infor-
090      mation, such as feature labels, can be synthesized in addition to traditional data-label pairs. This
091      privileged information provides additional generative supervision during model training, thereby
092
         improving the generalization ability of the reduced dataset.
093   2. We observe that effective feature labels should balance the trade-off between diversity and dis-
094
         criminability. Overly discriminative feature labels, such as those directly extracted from a pre-
         trained neural network, can even degrade the quality of the reduced dataset.
095
096
      3. We further provide a theoretical analysis of our DRUPI pipeline based on VC theory (Vapnik,
         1998) from statistical learning, which rigorously ensures its effectiveness.
097
098
      4. Our experiments demonstrate that DRUPI can be seamlessly integrated into state-of-the-art DR
         methods. Particularly, for coreset selection methods, applying DRUPI to Herding on CIFAR10
099
         (with a fraction of 0.4%) improves performance by 24.3%, while applying it to K-center in cross-
100
         architecture evaluations leads to an improvement of up to 23.4%. For dataset distillation methods,
101      integrating DRUPI with the DC method on CIFAR100 (with 10 images per class) yields a 4%
102      improvement, and further cross-architecture evaluations of DC show gains of up to 18.3%.

      Coreset selection techniques. Coreset selection techniques are designed to identify a representa-
129   tive subset DS from the complete dataset DT . This process typically seeks to optimize a criterion
130   that quantifies the informativeness of DS , which matches that of the original dataset DT . The
131   informativeness can be gauged by various metrics, including gradients (Paul et al., 2021; Mirza-
132   soleiman et al., 2020; Killamsetty et al., 2021a), loss values (Toneva et al., 2018), predictive un-
133   certainties (Coleman et al., 2019b), proximity to decision boundaries (Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018;
134   Margatina et al., 2021), and the sharpness of the learned model (Shin et al., 2023).
135   Dataset distillation methods. Dataset distillation approaches offer an alternative strategy by fo-
136   cusing on synthesizing a distilled dataset DS from the original dataset DT , rather than directly
137   selecting a subset. This is typically accomplished through a bi-level optimization process that aligns
138   the performance of DS with that of DT . A distance metric D is employed to quantify the statisti-
139   cal divergence between datasets, guiding the learning of DS via gradient descent. Specifically, the
140   distilled dataset is updated as follows: DS ← DS − η · ∇DS D(DS , DT ). The choice of distance
141   metric D is versatile and can encompass various aspects such as gradients (Zhao et al., 2020; Lee
142   et al., 2022; Zhao & Bilen, 2021), feature representations (Zhao & Bilen, 2022; Sajedi et al., 2023),
143
      training trajectories (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023),
      and kernel information (Nguyen et al., 2020; 2021; Zhou et al., 2022).
144
145   In addition to direct performance matching, certain methodologies endeavor to re-parameterize input
146   data to enhance compression efficiency. Techniques employed in this context include exploiting data
147   regularity, as discussed in various studies (Kim et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Son et al., 2024),
148   factorizing images to capture intrinsic structures (Liu et al., 2022; Deng & Russakovsky, 2022), and
149
      employing sparse coding to represent data effectively (Wei et al., 2024).

153   3.1      D ETERMINING P RIVILEGED I NFORMATION
154
155   Although prior research on dataset reduction has shown impressive results, it mainly focuses on
156   generating reduced datasets in conventional data-label formats, as depicted in Figure 2(a). However,
157   more informative data, such as privileged information, can be utilized to enhance both the utility and
158
      representational performance of reduced datasets, as illustrated in Figure 2(b). Below, we briefly
      explore several forms of privileged information that can be incorporated to achieve this goal.
159
160   Soft labels. We first claim that soft labels are a form of privileged information, as they offer richer
161   insight into how an expert model interprets predictions, providing soft probabilities rather than a
      single hard label. Specifically, the non-target logits can be viewed as additional information for


                                                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      supervision. Several works have previously explored the effectiveness of soft labels in dataset re-
163   duction (Guo et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Bohdal et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2024). However, while
164   soft labels enhance the available information, they are limited to low-dimensional discriminative
165   representations and fail to capture more complex, high-dimensional statistics. Moreover, they do
166   not fundamentally alter the data-label structure of the reduced dataset representation.
167
      Feature labels. Beyond soft labels, we propose feature labels as a more effective form of privileged
168
      information. These labels, derived from unified intermediate representations across well-trained
169   models, encapsulate rich, high-dimensional latent statistics. By providing additional supervision,
170   feature labels enhance model training on datasets with privileged information. Unlike approaches
171   that focus primarily on soft labels, assigning a unified feature label to each input enriches the super-
172   vision signal for downstream tasks, effectively addressing the limitations of prior methods.
173
      Attention labels. Alongside feature labels, we propose attention labels as an alternative form of
174
      privileged information that provides a more memory-efficient representation. Attention labels can
175   be derived from either spatial or channel attention of feature labels (Woo et al., 2018). For example,
176   given a feature label of size Ch × H × W , spatial attention reduces the Ch dimensions through
177   pooling operations (e.g., average pooling, max pooling), resulting in an attention label of size 1 ×
178   H × W . Similarly, channel attention applies pooling along the H × W dimension to produce
179   a reduced representation, i.e., Ch × 1 × 1. Both feature and attention labels are valuable, with
180   attention labels offering a more efficient representation but with a possible trade-off in the richness
181   of information. We provide more discussion on attention labels in Appendix B.2.
182   In this work, we primarily focus on generating additional feature labels1 for the reduced dataset, as
183   these forms of privileged information provide more complementary and useful insights for model
184   training. However, privileged information can take various forms beyond attention and feature la-
185   bels. Depending on the task and the model architecture, other types of information, such as learned
186   embeddings, domain-specific signals, or task-related metadata, could be equally beneficial in en-
187   hancing the informativeness and performance of reduced datasets. The flexibility to incorporate
188   different kinds of privileged information allows us to tailor the dataset to specific needs and maxi-
189
      mize the potential of the reduced data.

314   3.4    T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
315
316   We propose a theoretical analysis to elucidate the mechanisms by which the DRUPI framework en-
317   hances the quality of reduced datasets. Let gT ∈ GT denote the oracle function for the original
318   dataset DT , and | · |C represent a function class capacity measure, i.e., a measure of model perfor-
319   mance. Consider two models: one trained on the pure reduced dataset DS , represented by gS ∈ GS ,
320
      and another trained on the reduced dataset with privileged information, DS⋆ , denoted by gS ⋆ ∈ GS ⋆ .
321   We begin by briefly reviewing the well-known VC theory (Vapnik, 1998), a fundamental analyti-
322   cal tool in statistical learning theory that forms the basis of our theoretical framework. The VC-
323   dimension defines the performance of a model to limited data points. Specifically, for a model g
      belonging to a function class G, with a finite VC-dimension |G|VC , the expected error R(g) with

514   Impact of feature label versatility and methods for utilizing feature labels. We investigated the
515   effect of synthesizing multiple feature labels for a single data-label pair. As shown in Figure 4(b),
516   experiments demonstrate that increasing the number of feature labels enhances performance, likely
517   due to the greater versatility captured by additional features. However, too many feature labels for
518   a single input can introduce excessive diversity, leading to degraded performance. This verifies
519   the trade-off between the diversity and discriminability of feature labels. Furthermore, averaging
520   multiple feature labels outperforms random selection, which enables us to save only the averaged
521
      feature labels. Hence, increasing the number of feature labels does not bring more storage overhead.
522   Layer choice for supervision features. We conducted an in-depth analysis to determine which
523   ConvNet layer’s features are most effective for supervision. Specifically, we compared features
524   extracted from the first, second, and final layers of a depth-3 ConvNet. Figure 4(c) shows that
525   deeper layers consistently yielded better performance. This is likely due to the final layer’s ability to
526   capture more complex and discriminative information, effectively representing high-level semantics.
527

532   6                      C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduced DRUPI, a novel framework that synthesizes privileged information
535   for reduced datasets. To the best of our knowledge, DRUPI is the first approach to go beyond the
536   traditional data-label paradigm by utilizing synthesized feature labels. Extensive experiments on
537   ImageNet, CIFAR-10/100, and Tiny ImageNet validate the effectiveness of DRUPI, demonstrating
538   significant improvements in model performance when integrated with existing reduction techniques.
539   Additionally, we showed that achieving a balance between the discriminability and diversity of the
      synthesized feature labels is crucial for maximizing the quality of the reduced dataset.

714

      We first employed several coreset selection methods to initialize our reduced dataset, specifically
716

717   et al., 2018), where each data point was selected based on scores from a pre-trained ResNet-18.
718   Next, we synthesized feature labels for the coreset using DC Zhao et al. (2020), assigning these
719   labels to the intermediate features of a ConvNet trained for just one epoch. We also fine-tuned the
720   initial images using the same method as for feature label learning, although we found that simply
721   synthesizing feature labels could already bring performance improvement for reduced datasets.
722
      Then we provide detailed settings for hyper-parameters. The hyper-parameters include:
723
724   • λreg : regularization coefficient, which controls the strength of the regularization term in the loss
725     function.
726   • λtask : task supervision coefficient, determining the discriminative power of the synthesized fea-
727     ture labels.
728   • nf eat : number of feature labels synthesized for a single data-label pair, which controls the diver-
729     sity.
730   Unless otherwise specified, for the CIFAR-10 dataset, we set λreg to 0.5, while for CIFAR-100, it
731   was set to 5. Across all configurations, the task supervision coefficient λtask was set to 0.001, and
732   we only synthesized one feature label for a single data-label pair (nf eat = 1) in the reduced dataset.

834
835   In addition to the results presented in Table 4 and 5 of the main paper, which summarize the per-
836   formance of utilizing the synthesized datasets from pruning and dataset distillation to initialize the
837   feature labels, we provide additional results in Table 9 to evaluate the cross-architecture generaliza-
838   tion by using both feature labels and attention labels on different reduced datasets.
839
      Table 9 illustrates the cross-architecture evaluations conducted for both dataset condensation (DC)
840   and MTT across various network architectures. In these experiments, we propose DRUPI-F (feature
841   labels learned by DC) and its variant DRUPI-A, which is further enhanced by pooling through spatial
842   attention. Similarly, feature labels synthesized with MTT are also average pooled into channel
843   attention labels. We utilized several networks for cross evaluatoin, including LeNet, MLP, ResNet,
844   VGG, ConvNet, and AlexNet.
845
      We provide examples to illustrate the operation. For example, the first layer feature of a depth-
846   3 ConvNet can be used to supervise the learning of feature labels. In this case, a single feature
847   label takes the form of Ch × H × W (e.g., 128 × 16 × 16), which is reduced to a 128 × 1 × 1
848   channel attention label after average pooling. Attention labels provide a more efficient way for
849   using privileged information.
850
      Additionally, during the cross-architecture process, if the features obtained from the training and
851
      testing models differ in shape or dimensionality, we employ an additional fully connected (FC)
852   layer to align the features. For example, if the feature representation from the training model has a
853   shape of 128 × 16 × 16 and the testing model’s feature representation is 64 × 16 × 16, the FC layer
854   reshapes the 128 × 16 × 16 feature into the 64 × 16 × 16 format. The FC takes the input feature
855   from the source architecture and transforms it into a format compatible with the target architecture
856   by adjusting the dimensionality of the feature space.
857
      The table demonstrates that initializing the reduced dataset with feature labels assigned through in-
858   termediate features of pre-trained networks leads to significant performance improvements across
859   all architectures. Further gains are observed when channel attention pooling is applied. By leverag-
860   ing Eq. (5) to learn and update the feature labels, the reduced dataset consistently yields competitive
861   or improved performance across different networks. This highlights the benefits of feature and at-
862   tention labels in enhancing model generalization, with particularly notable improvements observed
863   in ConvNet and AlexNet settings. Channel attention pooling further contributes to these gains, rein-
      forcing the effectiveness of this approach across architectures.
