009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Foundation models aim to create general, cross-task, and cross-domain machine
012
               learning models by pretraining on large-scale datasets to capture shared patterns
013
               or concepts (generalities), such as contours, colors, textures, and edges in images,
014            or tokens, words, and sentences in text. However, discovering generalities across
015            graphs remains challenging, which has hindered the development of graph foun-
016            dation models. To tackle this challenge, in this paper, we propose a novel approach
017            to learn generalities across graphs via task-trees. Specifically, we first define the
018            basic learning instances in graphs as task-trees and assume that the generalities
019            shared across graphs are, at least partially, preserved in the task-trees of the given
020            graphs. To validate the assumption, we first perform a theoretical analysis of task-
021            trees in terms of stability, transferability, and generalization. We find that if a
022
               graph neural network (GNN) model is pretrained on diverse task-trees through a
               reconstruction task, it can learn sufficient transferable knowledge for downstream
023
               tasks using an appropriate set of fine-tuning samples. To empirically validate the
024
               assumption, we further instantiate the theorems by developing a cross-task, cross-
025            domain graph foundation model named Graph generality Identifier on task-Trees
026            (GIT). The extensive experiments over 30 graphs from five domains demonstrate
027            the effectiveness of GIT in fine-tuning, in-context learning, and zero-shot learning
028            scenarios. Particularly, the general GIT model pretrained on large-scale datasets
029            can be quickly adapted to specific domains, matching or even surpassing expert
030            models designed for those domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Foundation models have gained prominence in the era of artificial general intelligence as a general-
037   purpose, cross-task, and cross-domain machine learning approach. These models are exemplified by
038   large language models (LLMs) for text (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023) and large vision
039
      models (LVMs) for images (He et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021). Pretrained on large-scale datasets,
      they capture generalizable and transferable knowledge, including contours, colors, textures, and
040
      edges in images, as well as tokens, words, and sentences in text. These patterns and concepts
041
      represent modality-specific generalities, allowing the models to adapt rapidly to new downstream
042   tasks via in-context learning (Xie et al., 2022) or zero-shot learning (Wei et al., 2021).
043
044   Despite the success of foundation models across various modalities, their development of graph-
045
      structured data remains in its infancy (Liu et al., 2024). This is primarily due to the high degree
      of variability among graph-structured datasets (Mao et al., 2024), making it challenging to identify
046
      shared generalities across graphs. In particular, graphs from different domains typically represent
047
      distinct phenomena; for example, social networks capture relationships between people (Freeman,
048   2004), while molecular networks depict the structures of molecules (Zeng et al., 2022). These
049   distinctions are evident not only in the differences between feature and label spaces (Huang et al.,
050   2023; Liu et al., 2024) but also in structural heterogeneity (Qiu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024b).
051   Additionally, different graph-related tasks often do not share basic learning instances—such as nodes
052   in node-level tasks and entire graphs in graph-level tasks—making it difficult to use a single model
053   for diverse tasks (Mao et al., 2024). The above challenges make it extremely difficult to develop a
      graph foundation model (GFM) that can identify generalities applicable across graphs.

      Is it possible to identify generalities across graphs? Despite the challenges, some researchers have
055   attempted to uncover shared generalities across graphs, which can be mainly categorized into two
056   groups. (1) One line of works draws on graph theory. In particular, they use the concept of graphon
057   (Ruiz et al., 2020) to describe transferable patterns across graphs. If two graphs are generated from
058   the same graphon, they are expected to share key topological properties, which can result in high
059   transferability between them (Ruiz et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2023). However, the strong assumptions
060   underlying graphon theory are often unrealistic for real-world graphs (Levie et al., 2021), and even
061   when the assumptions hold, finding the graphon shared from a large set of graphs presents another
062   significant challenge. (2) Another approach involves leveraging substructures that are present across
063
      graphs, such as triangles, stars, and k-cliques (Zhao et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024). For instance,
      triangle structures frequently appear in citation networks, social networks, and molecular networks,
064
      albeit with varying semantics. Based on this observation, some works (Sun et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
065
      2024) sample subgraphs consisting of these substructures (as shown in Figure 1, Left) and then use
066   a graph neural network (GNN) to encode the subgraph-level embeddings for prediction. However,
067   identifying which substructures are shared across distinct graphs remains difficult. Even if useful
068   substructures can be identified, message-passing GNNs are proved to struggle with capturing such
069   basic substructures in learning (sub)graph embeddings (Garg et al., 2020; Esser et al., 2021; Zhang
070   et al., 2024a), limiting the practicality of these methods.

       Task-Tree Generality Assumption: the generalities shared across graphs are (at least partially)
                          preserved within the task-trees of the involved graphs.
101
102   To validate this assumption, we conduct a theoretical analysis to examine the properties of task-trees
103   in terms of stability, transferability, and generalization (Verma & Zhang, 2019; Garg et al., 2020),
104   showing the feasibility of building GFMs based on task-trees. Our key finding is that if a GNN model
105   is pretrained on diverse task-trees using a reconstruction task, it will acquire sufficient transferable
106   knowledge to downstream tasks with a reasonable number of fine-tuning samples. Additionally, the
107   analysis highlights the potential for specializing the pretrained model into specific domains through
      post-training (Wei et al., 2021) using domain-specific instances.

      To validate our theoretical findings, we introduce a cross-task, cross-domain graph foundation model
109   called Graph generality Identifier on task-Trees (GIT). The core idea is to pretrain GIT on task-trees
110   extracted from a wide range of graphs spanning diverse domains and tasks. We evaluate GIT across
111   32 graphs from 5 distinct domains, using settings such as basic fine-tuning, in-context learning (few-
112   shot learning without fine-tuning), and zero-shot learning. The experimental results demonstrate
113   that pretraining the model on a small set of graphs significantly benefits a wide range of graph tasks,
114   supporting the idea that task-trees indeed capture generalities shared across graphs. We also propose
115   an instruction tuning method to adapt the pretrained general model to specific downstream domains,
116   which enhances performance across all datasets within the domain, matching or even surpassing
117
      domain expert models.

      Definition 2.1 (Task-Relevant Nodes). Graph tasks can be roughly categorized into node-level,
      edge-level, and graph-level tasks, where the basic learning instances are nodes, edges, and entire
136
      graphs, respectively. For node classification, the task-relevant node vit is the target node vi that

140
      Remark 2.2. For any graph task instance, the prediction relies solely on the embeddings of the cor-
141
      responding task-relevant nodes. These node embeddings capture the surrounding subtree structures,
142
      also known as computation trees.
143
      Definition 2.3 (Computation Trees (Chuang & Jegelka, 2022)). Given a node v in graph G, the
144
      L-layer computation tree TvL is constructed by recursively expanding the subtrees of its neighboring
145
      nodes, starting with Tv1 = v.
146
147   The learning process of GNNs involves recursively integrating information from the computation
148   trees, progressing from the bottom to the top. Therefore, for a given node v, the node embedding
149   produced by an L-layer GNN corresponds to the embedding of its computation tree TvL . Since the
150   prediction for any graph task depends solely on the embeddings of task-relevant nodes, and these
151   embeddings are determined by their respective computation trees, we can construct a task-tree for
152
      each instance—whether it be a node, edge, or graph—as illustrated in Figure 1 (Right).
153   Definition 2.4 (Task-Trees). For any graph-related instance—whether a node, edge, or graph—we
154   have a set of task-relevant nodes {v1t , ..., vnt } and their corresponding L-layer computation trees
155
      {T1 , ..., Tn }. These computation trees can be reformulated into a larger task-tree T t by introducing
      an additional task node that connects all task-relevant nodes.
156
157   Task-Tree Encoding. We use a straightforward method to encode task-trees. Given a task-tree
158   T t , consisting of a virtual task node v t and a set of task-relevant nodes associated with compu-
159   tation trees T1 , ..., Tn , we first apply a GNN ϕ to encode each computation tree, obtaining node
160   embeddings z1 , ..., zn . We then aggregate these node embeddings into the task node, with the task
161   node embedding serving as theP       embedding of the task-tree. Specifically, we use basic averaging for

      aggregation: z t = ϕ(T t ) = n1 i=1 ϕ(Ti ).

272   We propose a task-tree reconstruction task as a pretext for pretraining. The key idea is to use two
273   corrupted task-trees to reconstruct each other, thereby capturing corruption-invariant semantics of
274   the task-trees. Given a set of task-trees {T1t , ..., Tnt } sampled from a graph database1 , we apply cor-
275   ruption techniques to generate two views of each task-tree, denoted as {T̂1t , ..., T̂nt } and {T̃1t , ..., T̃nt }.
276   For corruption, we use random edge masking and random attribute masking, as proposed by Zhu
277   et al. (2020), due to its computational efficiency. We then use an encoder ϕ to obtain embeddings for
278   the corrupted task-trees, resulting in {ẑ1 , ..., ẑn } and {z̃1 , ..., z̃n }. Note that the task-tree embed-
279   ding is defined as the average of the embeddings of task-relevant nodes, avoiding the introduction

      of inductive biases that could lead the model to learn incorrect patterns. The loss function is

      Theorem 3.5 highlights the relationship between model generalization and the distribution gap be-
293

497   5.4 C OMPARISON B ETWEEN TASK -T REES AND S UBGRAPHS
498
      Unlike our proposed task-trees, some approaches use k-hop subgraphs extracted from graphs as the
499
      basic learning units (Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). In node classification, for example, ego-
500
      graphs are extracted around each node, and the label of the central node is assigned to the induced
501   subgraph, transforming node classification into subgraph classification. A similar process can be
502   applied to convert edge-level and graph-level tasks into subgraph-level tasks. This approach involves
503   (1) extracting ego-graphs around task-relevant nodes and (2) applying GNNs to learn graph-level
504   embeddings for classification. However, this extraction process introduces additional computational
505   overhead, increasing both time consumption and memory usage due to the need to store and process
506   the induced subgraphs. Furthermore, the information within these subgraphs is not always easily
507   learned by basic GNNs, as they may struggle to capture essential substructures needed for learning
508   graph-level embeddings (Garg et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2024a), thereby limiting
509
      the effectiveness of using subgraphs as basic learning instances.
510   To illustrate this, we compare the efficiency and performance of subgraphs and task-trees in the basic
511   fine-tuning setting, as shown in Figure 6 and Table 5, respectively. We implement a subgraph version
512   of GIT by replacing task-trees with subgraphs (GIT-Subgraph). The results show that task-trees offer
513   both better efficiency and improved model performance. Although Table 5 presents results from
514   only three domains, the evaluation covers a range of tasks, including node classification (academic
515
      networks), edge classification (knowledge graphs), and graph classification (molecule networks).
516
517   6             R ELATED W ORK
518
519   Foundation models, like LLMs in NLP, excel at diverse tasks such as summarization, translation,
520   and question-answering. However, building foundation models for graphs is challenging due to the
521   inherent heterogeneity in features, structures, and tasks. Existing works, like Qiu et al. (2020), Sun
522   et al. (2023), Huang et al. (2023), and Liu et al. (2024), tackle these issues by leveraging subgraphs
523
      to align task spaces and LLMs to align feature spaces. More recent approaches, such as He & Hooi
      (2024) and Li et al. (2024), jointly train GNNs and LLMs to enhance embeddings for few-shot
524
      and zero-shot tasks. However, reliance on subgraphs often leads to inefficiencies. Other methods
525
      like SVD (Zhao et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024) or non-parametric encoders (Zhao et al., 2024b)
526   focus on resolving feature heterogeneity. In contrast, models designed for specific domains, such as
527   Galkin et al. (2024) for knowledge graphs or Zhao et al. (2023) for molecular graphs, avoid these
528   complexities but lack generalizability to other domains. Unlike these, our GIT model pretrains on
529   diverse task trees, enabling it to generalize across domains and quickly specialize via instruction
530   tuning. More related works are presented in the Appendix A.
531
532   7             C ONCLUSION
533
534   We introduce the concept of task-trees as basic learning instances for graphs and provide both theo-
535   retical and empirical validation of the task-tree generality assumption: the generalities shared across
536   graphs are (at least partially) preserved in the task-trees of the involved graphs. Building on task-
537   trees, we develop GIT, a cross-domain and cross-task GFM that can be quickly specialized for spe-
538   cific domains. By pretraining on a small set of graphs, GIT improves performance across more than
539   30 graphs spanning five domains. After specialization with a small set of domain-specific task-trees,
      GIT can match or even surpass expert models designed for specific domains.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
811
812
813
814
815
      Graph Neural Networks. GNNs are a class of learning models specifically designed to operate
      on graph-structured data and have demonstrated substantial success across a variety of domains.
816
      Their strength lies in their ability to perform relational learning, where information from neigh-
817
      boring nodes is aggregated and used to enhance node representations. For instance, GCN (Kipf &
818   Welling, 2017) utilizes message-passing to aggregate information from neighboring nodes to central
819   nodes. Building on this, models such as GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) and GAT (Veličković
820   et al., 2018) introduce innovative techniques like neighborhood sampling and attention mechanisms,
821   respectively, further advancing performance on graph learning tasks. However, these methods are
822   limited to solving a single task by training from the scratch.
823
      Transferability of GNNs. Existing works that analyze the shared concepts (generalities) across dif-
824   ferent graphs primarily follow two approaches. The first is graphon theory, which provides bounds
825   on the distance between graphs generated from the same graphon. This method has been used
826   to study transferability in pretraining and fine-tuning settings (Cao et al., 2023), to develop more
827   expressive fine-tuning techniques (Sun et al., 2024), and to design new model architectures (Ruiz
828   et al., 2020). However, despite its theoretical advantages, graphon-based approaches face practical
829   challenges, particularly the strong assumptions required and the difficulty of identifying graphons
830   in large-scale graphs, which limits their applicability in building graph foundation models. The sec-
831   ond approach involves leveraging substructures within graphs to identify transferable patterns (Mao
832
      et al., 2024). This method focuses on extracting subgraphs composed of meaningful substructures
      for prediction tasks. While this approach offers theoretical insights into stability (Levie et al., 2019;
833
      Zhu et al., 2021), it struggles to fully capture substructures that are beneficial for downstream tasks
834
      (Zhang et al., 2024a).
835
836   Graph Foundation Models. Foundation models are designed as general-purpose solvers capable
837   of handling various tasks across different domains. For instance, LLMs, the foundation models in
838
      natural language processing, are capable of performing tasks such as summarization, translation,
      and entity recognition, as well as question-answering. However, building such versatile foundation
839
      models for graphs presents unique challenges due to the inherent feature, structural, and task het-
840
      erogeneity across different graph domains and tasks. To address these challenges, Qiu et al. (2020)
841   pretrained GNNs using subgraphs as basic units, mitigating structural heterogeneity. Building on
842   this, Sun et al. (2023) reformulated node-, edge-, and graph-level tasks into subgraph-level tasks,
843   tackling task heterogeneity. Additionally, Huang et al. (2023) and Liu et al. (2024) applied LLMs
844   to unify the feature spaces of cross-domain graphs, addressing feature heterogeneity. These ap-
845   proaches enable models to operate on cross-domain and cross-task graphs. Further advancements,
846   such as He & Hooi (2024) and Li et al. (2024), improve node embeddings by jointly optimizing
847   GNN and LLM encoders, facilitating various downstream tasks like few-shot learning and zero-shot
848   learning. However, most of these approaches rely on subgraphs as the primary learning instances,
849
      which can result in inefficient training and reduced expressiveness, as discussed in the main paper.
      Other efforts to resolve feature heterogeneity include methods like singular vector decomposition
850
      (SVD) (Zhao et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024) and non-parametric encoders (Zhao et al., 2024b).
851
852   Another line of research focuses on designing GFMs for single tasks or domains, thereby avoiding
853   the complexities of feature, structural, or task heterogeneity. For example, Galkin et al. (2024) pro-
854   pose a foundation model for reasoning tasks on knowledge graphs, using triplets as basic transferable
855
      patterns. Zhao et al. (2023) introduce a foundation model for molecular graphs, employing LLMs
      to align semantics between datasets and encode key motifs. In node classification, Li et al. (2024)
856
      propose a zero-shot learning foundation model, while Zhao et al. (2024a) present a feature align-
857
      ment method based on SVD for node-level graph foundation models. Recently, Zhao et al. (2024b)
858   designed a foundation model for node classification using a non-parametric classifier. Meanwhile,
859   Chen et al. (2024a), Tang et al. (2024), Guo et al. (2023), and Wang et al. (2024a) have explored
860   using LLMs as graph reasoners to solve graph tasks, similar to their role in vision language mod-
861   els. While these methods excel at specific tasks or domains, they are not suitable as general graph
862   solvers across diverse tasks. In contrast to these approaches, our proposed GIT model is pretrained
863   on diverse task trees to acquire general reasoning capabilities, allowing it to quickly specialize in
      specific domains through instruction tuning.

      B     P OTENTIAL M ODEL E XTENSIONS
865
866
867   B.1   P RETRAINING
868
869   How to Design Reconstruction Tasks? Theorem 3.5 suggests that a well-designed encoder, capable
870   of effectively handling reconstruction tasks during pretraining, can improve the model’s generaliza-
871   tion ability. One approach is to use more powerful encoders to enhance reconstruction performance.
872
      Another approach is to introduce additional reconstruction losses to further refine the encoder. For
      example, methods such as those proposed by Qiu et al. (2020) and Hou et al. (2022), or designing
873
      more comprehensive reconstruction objectives could be explored.
874
875   How to Improve Transferability? The pretraining task, i.e., task-tree reconstruction, differs from
876   the downstream task of task-tree classification, as the task heterogeneity may hinder model trans-
877   ferability (Hu et al., 2020). To mitigate this, one could develop more effective adaptation methods,
878
      such as graph prompt learning (Sun et al., 2022), to reduce task heterogeneity.
879
880
      B.2   S PECIALIZATION VIA I NSTRUCTION T UNING
881
882
      How to Define Instructions? In this paper, as we focus on experiments with text-attributed graphs,
883
      we define instructions as label descriptions encoded by LLMs. However, this approach is not appli-
884   cable to non-textual graphs. Other methods could be explored to define instructions, such as using
885   proxy models (Hu et al., 2019) or graph heuristics (Jin et al., 2020) to generate instructions.
886
887
      How to Choose SFT Data? We manually select graphs as supervised fine-tuning datasets for each
      domain, though the selected graphs may not be fully representative. Unlike textual data, evalu-
888
      ating the quality of graph datasets poses a challenge. Improved dataset selection methods could
889
      enhance the SFT process by identifying more representative or diverse data from graph databases.
890   Additionally, while we perform instruction tuning over entire graphs, it is possible that only spe-
891   cific subgraphs are beneficial (Hashemi et al., 2024). Developing data selection methods that focus
892   on high-quality subgraphs within a single SFT dataset could improve task-tree selection. Another
893   worthy research line is to select SFT data that aligns with user preferences (Song et al., 2024).
894
      How to Leverage SFT Data? In scenarios with limited instructions, standard supervised fine-
895
      tuning may struggle to capture sufficient knowledge of the target domain. To address this, methods
896   could be proposed to better utilize the unlabeled instances in the SFT dataset, thus enhancing model
897   adaptation (Sohn et al., 2020).
898
899
      How to Maintain General Inference Capability? While instruction tuning specializes the model
      for a specific domain, it may compromise the model’s general inference capabilities across other
900
      domains. This could hinder the model’s performance when it needs to function both as a domain
901
      expert and a general reasoner. To mitigate this, regularization techniques could be designed to
902   preserve the general knowledge encoded in the model during the instruction tuning process.
903
904   Why SFT Works on Graphs? Instruction tuning is a common post-training process in modern large
905
      language models (e.g., LLAMA, GPT) that significantly improves instruction-following capabilities.
      The success of this method in LLMs may stem from the fact that natural language serves as an
906
      interface between humans and models (Wei et al., 2021). However, the reason instruction tuning
907
      works for graphs remains an open question and presents a potential direction for future research.
908
909
910   B.3   M ORE S CENARIOS .
911
912   The paper leverages text-attributed graphs to align node features. However, the pre-processing of
913   TAGs can be time-consuming, raising the challenge of how to effectively apply the model to graphs
914   without aligned node features. Furthermore, while we primarily focus on homogeneous graphs in
915   this work, most real-world applications involve heterogeneous graphs. Addressing the question of
916   how to design a single model capable of handling various types of graphs remains an open challenge.
917   Finally, applying the model to specific applications Zhang et al. (2024c), which may exhibit unique
      characteristics, is another important consideration for future research.

948    C.1             W HY D OES THE G ENERAL M ODEL N EED S PECIALIZATION ?
949
       It is challenging for a single graph model to handle tasks across various domains due to pattern
950    conflicts, where the same structural pattern can have different meanings in different domains. To
951    illustrate this issue, we provide an intuitive example3 . Consider a pretraining process involving
952    datasets from multiple domains, such as social networks, molecular networks, academic networks,
953    and knowledge graphs. Suppose the model learns triangle structures during pretraining. In social
954    networks, the semantic meaning of these triangles is stable, following the principle of “the friend
955    of my friend is my friend”. However, in molecular graphs, the meaning of triangle patterns may be
956    unstable due to chemical properties. This pattern conflict can significantly degrade the performance
957    of graph models (Cao et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024). Specialization helps resolve this issue by
958
       aligning the meanings of certain structural patterns with the semantics specific to the target domain.
959
960    C.2             M ORE A NALYSIS ON D OMAIN R EGULARIZER
961
       The Necessity of Domain Alignment. Datasets from multiple domains are often projected into
962
       different subspaces, potentially due to misalignment of node attributes (Chen et al., 2024b) and the
963    frequent patterns across domains. As a result, the model may “memorize” information specific to
964    each domain rather than learning transferable patterns. This can lead to misunderstandings when the
965    same pattern appeared across different graphs is projected into different subspaces. Consequently,
966    the model struggles to acquire transferable knowledge that would benefit unseen tasks and special-
967    ized domains. Properly aligning the embedding spaces of different domains is crucial for obtaining
968    transferable knowledge and improving performance on unseen graphs and specialized domains.
969    How to Regulate Domain Distances? We propose a domain regularizer to control domain distances
970    by projecting cross-domain graphs with different characteristics into a shared embedding space.

975    particular, the basis vector of the shared subspace is defined as the average of all instances:

1049   Node-level Task. The task-tree structure is an approximation of the original graph structure, but
1050   converting graphs into tree structures inevitably results in some loss of information. To better pre-
1051
       serve the structural details of the original graph, one could use more expressive or advanced GNNs,
       thereby expanding the potential tree vocabulary (Mao et al., 2024).

1053   Edge-level Task. Existing message-passing GNNs struggle with the edge
1054   isomorphism problem (Srinivasan & Ribeiro, 2020). For instance, in Fig-
1055   ure 8, the links (v1 , v2 ) and (v3 , v4 ) are isomorphic, while (v1 , v2 ) and
1056   (v1 , v3 ) are not. However, when using a mean aggregator to learn edge em-
1057
       beddings, the embeddings of (v1 , v2 ) and (v1 , v3 ) become indistinguish-
       able. We consider that GIT may still encounter this issue, as the task-tree Figure 8: Edge Iso-
1058
       encoding currently averages the embeddings of task-relevant nodes. Ad- morphic (Figure 1,
1059
       dressing this challenge could involve techniques like Zhang et al. (2021), (Zhang et al., 2021)).

1061   pairing the model’s basic inductive learning capabilities.
1062
1063
       Graph-level Task. Message-passing GNNs are limited by the 1-WL test (Xu et al., 2019), which
       can restrict their performance on graph-level tasks. As we apply GraphSAGE as the backbone, our
1064
       GIT also encounters this limitation. Zhang et al. (2024a) analyze the ability of different GNNs to
1065
       detect graph substructures and conclude that more expressive GNNs, beyond the 1-WL test, can
1066   learn graph embeddings with richer information. Therefore, to improve model expressiveness, one
1067   can employ more expressive GNNs in our GIT. Additionally, techniques like Zhang et al. (2021) can
1068   be used to further enhance the model’s discriminative capabilities.

1639   Pretraining Datasets. We select six datasets for pretraining, including Arxiv, Products,
1640   WN18RR, FB15K237, Chemblpre, and PCBA, due to their diversity in domains and tasks. For
1641   self-supervised learning methods, these six datasets are used for pretraining unless otherwise speci-
1642   fied.
1643
       SFT Datasets. For specialization via SFT in each domain, we use Arxiv for academic networks,
1644   Products for e-commerce networks, FB15K237 for knowledge graphs, and PCBA for molecular
1645   networks. For temporal graphs, which are e-commerce-based, we also use Products for SFT to
1646   evaluate robustness under temporal distribution shifts.
1647
       Backbone. We use a GraphSAGE-like encoder (Hamilton et al., 2017). Following the encoding
1648
       of task-trees, we add an additional linear transformation as the projector. Note that we does not
1649
       leverage edge features to make the task harder except for Enron and Googlemap CT where node
1650   features are IDs and edge contains messages. As the edge information may significantly benefit
1651   some tasks like knowledge graph completion and molecule property prediction.

       E.4   E VALUATION S ETTINGS
1654
1655   Finetune. This is the basic setting that directly finetune the full parameters of the pretrained model
1656   by appending a linear classifier on the top of the model encoder.
1657
       In-context Learning. This is a kind of few-shot learning without fine-tuning the model parameters.
1658
       We randomly select k samples from a certain class, and average the selected samples to form proto-
1659   types, which is used for classification. We follow existing GFM works (Liu et al., 2024; He & Hooi,
1660   2024) to conduct 500 randomly sampled 5-way 3-shot learning tasks. If the number of classes is
1661   less than 5, the number of ways is set to the number of classes.
1662
       Zero-shot Learning. The zero-shot learning is similar to in-context learning, yet we use the LLM-
1663
       encoded class description embeddings as the prototypes for prediction. Similar to in-context learn-
1664
       ing, we also randomly sample 500 tasks for evaluation. Another zero-shot setting involves using an
1665   additional LLM for zero-shot inference (Chen et al., 2024a). We leave this in our future work.

2095    We further analyze the performance of specialized models on general reasoning tasks beyond their
2096    specific domains. We assess the model’s performance on other domains as a measure of its gen-
2097
        eral reasoning ability. For example, if a model is specialized for academic networks, its general
        reasoning capability refers to its performance on graphs from other domains, such as e-commerce
2098
        networks, knowledge graphs, and molecular graphs. The results are presented in Table 27. We re-
2099
        port in-context learning performance rather than basic fine-tuning due to computational efficiency.
2100    Additionally, we include the performance of the pretrained general model without specialization as
2101    a baseline. If the specialized model performs worse than the general model, it suggests that spe-
2102    cialization may diminish GIT’s general reasoning capability. From the table, it is clear that while
2103    specialized models excel in their specific domains, they struggle in other domains. This degradation
2104    of general inference capability, often referred to as the specialization tax, is a common challenge in
2105    building specialized large language models. The specialization tax can limit the model’s practical-
        ity in scenarios requiring both domain-specific knowledge and the ability to handle general tasks.
