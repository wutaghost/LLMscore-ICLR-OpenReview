009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                 In recent years, Orthogonal Recurrent Neural Networks (ORNNs) have gained
012                 popularity due to their ability to manage tasks involving long-term dependencies,
013                 such as the copy-task, and their linear complexity. However, existing ORNNs
014                 utilize full precision weights and activations, which prevents their deployment on
015                 compact devices.
016                 In this paper, we explore the quantization of the weight matrices in ORNNs, leading
017                 to Quantized approximately Orthogonal RNNs (QORNNs). The construction of
018                 such networks remained an open problem, acknowledged for its inherent instabil-
019                 ity. We propose and investigate two strategies to learn QORNN by combining
020
                    quantization-aware training (QAT) and orthogonal projections. We also study
                    post-training quantization of the activations for pure integer computation of the
021
                    recurrent loop. The most efficient models achieve results similar to state-of-the-art
022
                    full-precision ORNN, LSTM and FastRNN on a variety of standard benchmarks,
023                 even with 4-bits quantization.

026   1        INTRODUCTION
027
028   Motivation: Machine learning applications frequently encompass the analysis of time series data,
029   such as textual information and audio signals. Within the realm of deep learning, various Recur-
030   rent Neural Network (RNN) architectures and transformers have demonstrated notable success in
031   addressing a diverse array of tasks associated with time series data.
032
      These models typically require a substantial number of parameters for optimal performance and
033   involve numerous matrix-vector multiplications during inference, using matrices and vectors of
034   considerable sizes containing floating-point numbers. This does not allow for the deployment of
035   these networks on compact devices with memory and power constraints, as well as for real-time
036   applications. Overcoming these constraints allows the use of RNNs across a large range of domains
037   in edge-ML and tinyML applications, as described in Abadade et al. (2023), such as healthcare, smart
038   farming, environment, or anomaly detection.
039   An effective and often unavoidable step1 to address these challenges is neural network quantization.
040   This technique aims to reduce the number of bits required to represent the weights and activations of
041   the network. As evaluated in Hubara et al. (2018); Gholami et al. (2022), with appropriate hardware
042   and implementation, this accelerates runtime computations, lowers power consumption, and, on the
043   other hand, decreases the amount of space needed for parameter storage. Other authors have gone
044   further and implemented quantized LSTMs on low-cost FPGAs to meet low-power requirements and
045   provide real-time low-cost solutions, see Chen et al. (2024); Bartels et al. (2023).2
046   Our goal is to contribute to the field of quantization of RNNs, with a particular emphasis on considering
047   RNNs able to address tasks involving long-term dependencies such as the copy-task with many time
048   steps. In doing so, we broaden the scope of applications for quantized RNNs.
049
      To achieve this objective, we introduce and compare two strategies for constructing Orthogonal
050
      or approximately Orthogonal Neural Networks (ORNNs) with quantized weights. We call them
051   Quantized Orthogonal Recurrent Neural Networks (QORNNs). The orthogonality constraint of

069   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
070
071         • We investigate the factors influencing the impact of quantization on orthogonality and the
072           behavior of ORNNs.
073         • We propose two different Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) strategies for constructing

141   described in the recent survey (Tang et al., 2024), many subsequent articles have followed suit.

174   3.2   O RTHOGONALITY
175
176   The matrix 𝑊 ∈ ℝ𝑛ℎ ×𝑛ℎ is orthogonal if and only if
177                                            𝑊 ′ 𝑊 = 𝑊 𝑊 ′ = 𝐼,
178

179   the columns (respectively, rows) of the matrix possess a Euclidean norm of 1, with the additional
180   condition that any two distinct columns (respectively, rows) exhibit a scalar product of 0. Among the
181   various properties of orthogonal matrices, it is important to note that the singular values of orthogonal
182   matrices are all equal to 1. Denoting 𝜎min (𝑊 ) and 𝜎max (𝑊 ) as the smallest and largest singular values

185   denote 𝑆𝑡(𝑛ℎ ).

255                           is not orthogonal. This, too, can contribute to the instability of the RNN.

      6     CONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVES
501
      In this article, we propose and study two algorithms to construct QORNNs. They enjoy the benefits of
502
      ORNNs and work when ORNNs do. In particular, they manage to solve the copy-task for 𝑇0 = 1000
503
      and 2000, which existing quantized RNNs were unable to do. We demonstrate that combining
504   orthogonalization and quantization-aware training is crucial for effectively training QORNN. In most
505   experiments, this combination is more efficient when using the Björck orthogonalization method.
506
507
      Future work on QORNNs could focus: 1/ on implementing QORNNs on dedicated hardware, 2/ on
      developing learning approaches better taking into account the activation quantization. It is also very
508
      much relevant to work on quantizing other models such as SSMs to target longer dependencies such
509
      as the Long Range Arena benchmarks described in Tay et al. (2020).

      A.1   N EURAL N ETWORKS FOR TIME SERIES
706
707
708   Numerous neural network architectures have been developed specifically for handling time-series
709   data. Rather than attempting to provide an exhaustive overview of all these architectures, our focus is
710
      on contextualizing Orthogonal and Unitary Recurrent Neural Networks (ORNN) within this diverse
      landscape. A comprehensive bibliography on ORNN is deferred to Section A.2.
711
712   Vanilla RNNs as introduced by Werbos (1988) are notoriously challenging to train due to issues with
713   vanishing and exploding gradients, Werbos (1988); Bengio et al. (1994). This reduces their ability
714   to cope with long-term dependencies. As described in Section 2 and reported in Ott et al. (2016);
715   Hubara et al. (2018); Gupta and Agrawal (2022), quantizing Vanilla RNN is challenging and not
716
      efficient.
717   LSTM Hochreiter and Schmidhuber (1997) and GRU Cho et al. (2014) are recurrent architectures that
718   tackle vanishing gradient problems by incorporating gating mechanisms. They have demonstrated
719   outstanding performance in tasks such as speech recognition Graves et al. (2013) and neural machine
720   translation Sutskever et al. (2014). LSTM and GRU are known to struggle to solve certain tasks having
721   long-term dependencies, such as the copy-task with many timesteps. On the latter task, comparable
722
      performances to a naive baseline, consisting of random guessing, have been reported in Arjovsky et al.
      (2016); Bai et al. (2018); Tallec and Ollivier (2018); Kerg et al. (2019); Bai et al. (2019). For the
723
      copy-tasks studied in Jelassi et al. (2024), the limitation comes rapidly as the length of the sequence
724
      increases. To the best of our knowledge, these are the only studies tackling the copy-task using LSTM
725   and GRU architectures. Several variants of quantized LSTM and GRU have been studied in the
726   literature, see Section 2.
727
728
      ORNN (and URNN) are known to achieve superior performance compared to LSTM in handling
      time series with long-term dependencies Arjovsky et al. (2016). They explicitly address the issues of
729
      vanishing and exploding gradients by imposing orthogonality (and unitary) constraints on the recurrent
730
      weight matrix of a vanilla RNN, See Appendix A.2. As a bonus, a standard ORNN unit contains
731   about four times fewer parameters than an LSTM unit. To the best of our knowledge, quantization of
732   ORNN has not been previously investigated. A detailed bibliography on full-precision ORNN is in
733   Appendix A.2.
734
      Alternative architectures introduce a skip connection in the RNN, similar to the one in ResNet
735
      architectures He et al. (2016). Several studies have contributed to the development of this idea Jaeger
736
      et al. (2007); Bengio et al. (2013a); Chang et al. (2017). To the best of our knowledge, the only
737   compressed –and therefore quantized– version of this architecture is described in the study by Kusupati
738   et al. (2018).
739
740
      Due to their ability to capture long-term dependencies and their flexibility, Transformer architectures
      Vaswani et al. (2017) have demonstrated great performance even for long sequences. However, they
741
      often require large training datasets and entail computational complexities that render them unsuitable
742
      for this study. Several contributions Shen et al. (2020); Prato et al. (2020); Chung et al. (2020); Gupta
743   and Agrawal (2022) have demonstrated that quantization strategies are feasible. A recent survey Tang
744   et al. (2024) is dedicated to this topic.
745
746
      Many architectures based on an Ordinary Differential Equation also benefit from a skip connection
      and have been studied in several works Chang et al. (2019); Rusch and Mishra (2020; 2021); Lechner
747
      and Hasani (2022); Kag et al. (2020); Kag and Saligrama (2021); Erichson et al. (2021). Among
748
      these, structured state space sequence models (SSM) Gu et al. (2022) have shown effectiveness in
749   handling tasks with very-long-term dependencies, as described in Tay et al. (2020). To the best of our
750   knowledge, no scientific work has yet studied the quantization of these architectures.
751
752
      Finally, alternatives that do not fit into the categories described above include the Independent RNN
      Li et al. (2018), approaches that utilize alternatives to backpropagation Manchev and Spratling (2020);
753
      Ororbia et al. (2020), or methods that model infinite-depth networks Bai et al. (2019).
754
755   To conclude, ORNNs is a method of choice when the two following properties are simultaneously
      needed:


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
            • The architecture has a memory complexity independent of the input length and exhibits
757           computational complexity that increases linearly with the input length. This characteristic
758           makes it ideal for training and inference tasks involving long inputs.
759
            • The architecture is easy to learn and has excellent memorization ability, which permits to
760
              solve efficiently important tasks with long-term dependencies such as the copy-task with
761           many timesteps.

763   A.2   O RTHOGONAL AND UNITARY RECURRENT NEURAL NETWORKS
764
765   Learning the unitary and orthogonal recurrent weight matrix of recurrent neural networks has a rich
766   history of study in the last decade. We describe the contributions in chronological order of publication.
767
      Unitary recurrent neural networks were introduced in Arjovsky et al. (2016). In this article, the
768   recurrent weight matrix is the product of parameterized unitary matrices of predefined structures.
769   They argue empirically that URNNs are beneficial because they better capture long-term dependencies
770   than LSTMs. Soon after, the authors of Wisdom et al. (2016) use the Cayley transform locally to
771   build an iterative scheme capable of reaching all unitary matrices. In Jing et al. (2017), the authors
772   parameterize the recurrent weight matrix as a product of Givens rotations and a diagonal matrix. By
773   doing so, they achieve more efficient models with tunable complexity that can be trained more rapidly.
774   In Mhammedi et al. (2017), the authors parameterize the recurrent weight matrix as a product of
775   Householder reflections to reduce the complexity of full-capacity models. In the same line of research,
776
      the authors of Jose et al. (2018) explore the use of product of unitary Kronecker matrices. They
      incorporate a soft-orthogonality penalization term to enforce the unitary constraints. The Kronecker
777
      architecture can be adjusted to reduce the complexity of the model. In Vorontsov et al. (2017),
778
      the authors compare soft and hard-orthogonality constraints. They find that the parameter of the
779   soft-orthogonality strategy under study can be tuned to achieve an approximately orthogonal recurrent
780   matrix, leading to improved efficiency. In Helfrich et al. (2018), the authors narrow their focus to
781   orthogonal recurrent weight matrices and parameterize the entire Stiefel manifold using the Cayley
782   transform scaled by a diagonal and orthogonal matrix. Similar to Mhammedi et al. (2017), the
783   number of parameters defining the orthogonal matrix is optimal. The authors of Zhang et al. (2018)
784   use a parameterized Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to constrain the singular values of the
785   recurrent weight matrix. In Lezcano-Casado and Martínez-Rubio (2019), the authors parameterize
786   orthogonal matrices using the exponential map. Finally, in Kiani et al. (2022), the authors develop
787
      two Riemannian optimization strategies. The first one is based on the orthogonal projection onto
      the Unitary or Stiefel manifold, and the other on Riemannian geodesic shooting. The algorithms are
788
      named ProjUNN, and one of them is employed in the presented work. This choice is motivated by the
789
      experiments outlined in Kiani et al. (2022).

       Detailed task description: We consider the Adding task as described in Arjovsky et al. (2016). In
1310   this task, the input to the RNN is a time series (𝑥𝑡 )𝑇𝑡=1 ∈ (ℝ2 )𝑇 . Denoting for all 𝑡, 𝑥𝑡 = (𝑥[0]𝑡 , 𝑥[1]𝑡 ),
1311
       the sequence (𝑥[0]𝑡 )𝑇𝑡=1 consists of random scalars sampled independently and uniformly from the
1312
1313
       interval [0, 1], while (𝑥[1]𝑡 )𝑇𝑡=1 consists of zeros except for two randomly selected entries set to 1. The
1314
       positions of the first and second occurrences of 1 are randomly selected, each following a uniform
       distribution over the intervals J1, 𝑇 ∕2K and J𝑇 ∕2 + 1, 𝑇 K, respectively. The output is the sum of the
1315
       two scalars∑from the first sequence, located at the positions corresponding to the 1s in the second
1316
       sequence: 𝑡 𝑥[0]𝑡 ⋅ 𝑥[1]𝑡 . As 𝑇 increases, this task evolves into a problem that requires longer-term
1317   memory. Naively predicting 1 (the average value of the sum of two independent random variables
1318   uniformly distributed in [0, 1]) for any input sequence yields an expected mean squared error (MSE)
1319   of ≈ 0.167, serving as our naive baseline.
