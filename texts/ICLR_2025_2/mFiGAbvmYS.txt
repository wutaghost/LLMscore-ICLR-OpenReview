009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The capability of generalization is a cornerstone for the success of modern learning
012            systems. For non-Euclidean data that particularly involves topological features,
013            one important aspect neglected by prior studies is how learning-based models
014            generalize under topological shifts. This paper makes steps towards understanding
015            the generalization of graph neural networks operated on varying topologies through
016            the lens of diffusion PDEs. Our analysis first reveals that the upper bound of
017            the generalization error yielded by local diffusion equation models, which are
018
               intimately related to message passing over observed structures, would exponentially
               grow w.r.t. topological shifts. In contrast, extending the diffusion operator to a non-
019
               local counterpart that learns latent structures from data can in principle control the
020
               generalization error under topological shifts even when the model accommodates
021            observed structures. On top of these results, we propose Advective Diffusion
022            Transformer inspired by advective diffusion equations serving as a physics-inspired
023            continuous model that synthesizes observed and latent structures for graph learning.
024            The model demonstrates superiority in various downstream tasks across information
025            networks, molecular screening and protein interactions.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Learning representations for non-Euclidean data is essential for geometric deep learning. Graph-
031   structured data in particular has attracted increasing attention, as graphs are a very popular mathe-
032   matical abstraction for systems of relations and interactions that can be applied from microscopic
033
      scales (e.g. molecules) to macroscopic ones (social networks). The most common framework for
      learning on graphs is graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2008; Gilmer et al., 2017; Kipf
034
      & Welling, 2017), which operate by propagating information between adjacent nodes of the graph
035
      networks. GNNs are intimately related to diffusion equations on graphs (Atwood & Towsley, 2016;
036   Klicpera et al., 2019; Chamberlain et al., 2021a) and can be seen as discretized versions thereof.
037   Considering GNNs as diffusion equations offers powerful tools from the domain of partial differential
038   equations (PDEs), allowing us to study the expressive power (Bodnar et al., 2022), behaviors such
039   as over-smoothing (Rusch et al., 2023) and over-squashing (Topping et al., 2022), the settings of
040   missing features (Rossi et al., 2022), and guide architectural choices (Di Giovanni et al., 2022).
041
      While significant efforts have been devoted to understanding the expressive power of GNNs and
042   similar architectures for graph learning, the generalization capabilities of such methods are largely an
043   open question. Recent works attempt to analyze GNNs’ generalization from various perspectives
044   such as extrapolation in feature space (Xu et al., 2021), subgroup fairness (Ma et al., 2021), causal
045   invariance principle (Wu et al., 2022), and random graph models (Baranwal et al., 2023). However,
046   most of these works study the distribution shifts of features and labels. In many critical real-world
047   settings, the training and testing graph topologies can be generated from different distributions (e.g.,
048   molecular structures with diverse drug likeness) (Koh et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021; Bazhenov et al.,
049   2023; Zhang et al., 2023), a phenomenon we refer to as “topological distribution shift”. This can
050
      be a predominant nature of non-Euclidean data in contrast with commonly studied feature and label
      shifts in Euclidean space. Despite its practical significance, how GNNs generalize under topological
051
      shifts still remains unclear.
052
053   In this paper, we aim to study the generalization limits of GNNs under topological shifts from
      the perspective of diffusion PDEs. We show that current models which rely on message passing

081   We recapitulate diffusion equations on manifolds (Freidlin & Wentzell, 1993; Medvedev, 2014) and
082   their connection with graph learning.
083   Diffusion on Riemannian manifolds. Let Ω denote an abstract domain, which we assume here to be
084   a Riemannian manifold (Eells & Sampson, 1964). A key feature distinguishing an n-dimensional
085   Riemannian manifold from a Euclidean space is the fact that it is only locally Euclidean, in the sense
086   that at every point u ∈ Ω one can construct n-dimensional Euclidean tangent space Tu Ω ∼    = Rn that
087   locally models the structure of Ω. The collection of such spaces (referred to as the tangent bundle
088   and denoted by T Ω) is further equipped with a smoothly-varying inner product (Riemannian metric).
089   Now consider some quantity (e.g., temperature) as a function of the form q : Ω → R, which we
090   refer to as a scalar field. Similarly, we can define a (tangent) vector field Q : Ω → T Ω, associating
091   to every point u on a manifold a tangent vector Q(u) ∈ Tu Ω, which can be thought of as a local
092   infinitesimal displacement. We use Q(Ω) and Q(T Ω) to denote the functional spaces of scalar and
093   vector fields, respectively. The gradient operator ∇ : Q(Ω) → Q(T Ω) takes scalar fields into vector
094   fields representing the local direction of the steepest change of the field. The divergence operator is
095   the adjoint of the gradient and maps in the opposite direction, ∇∗ : Q(T Ω) → Q(Ω).
096   A manifold diffusion process models the evolution of a quantity (e.g., chemical concentration) due to
097   its difference across spatial locations on Ω. Denoting by q(u, t) : Ω × [0, ∞) → R the quantity over
098   time t, the process is described by a PDE (diffusion equation) (Romeny, 2013):

312   ◦ Non-local diffusion as global attention. The diffusion process led by the concentration gradient

394
      To evaluate our model, we consider a wide variety of graph-based downstream tasks of disparate
395
      scales and granularities that involve topological distribution shifts led by distinct factors. Due to the
396
      diversity of datasets and tasks, the competing models that are applicable to specific cases can vary
397   case by case, so the goal of our experiments is to showcase the wide applicability and superiority
398   of AD I T against commonly used GNNs as well as several powerful bespoke methods tailored for
399   specific tasks. In the following, we delve into each case separately with the overview of experimental
400   setup and discussions. More detailed dataset information is provided in Appendix E.1. Details on
401   baselines and hyper-parameters are deferred to Appendix E.2 and E.3, respectively.

403   5.1                        S YNTHETIC DATASETS
404
405   To validate our proposed model and theoretical analysis, we create synthetic datasets simulating the
406   data generation hypothesis in Sec. 3.1. We instantiate h as a stochastic block model which generates
407   edges Auv according to block numbers (b), intra-block edge probability (p1 ) and inter-block edge
408
      probability (p2 ). Then we study three types of topological distribution shifts: homophily shift
      (changing p2 with fixed p1 ); density shift (changing p1 and p2 ); and block shift (varying b). The
409
      predictive task is node regression. More details on data generation are presented in Appendix E.1.1.
410
411   Fig. 3 plots the testing error (i.e., Mean Square Error) w.r.t. differences in graph topologies ∥∆A∥2
412   (i.e., the gap between training and testing graphs) in three cases. We compare our model (AD I T-
413   INVERSE and AD I T- SERIES ) with other diffusion-based models as competitors. The latter includes
414
      Diff-Linear (graph diffusion with constant C), Diff-MultiLayer (the extension of Diff-Linear with
      intermediate feature transformations), Diff-Time (graph diffusion with time-dependent C(Z(t))) and
415
      Diff-NonLocal (non-local diffusion with the global attention-based C(Z(t))). The results show that
416
      three local graph diffusion models exhibit clear performance degradation, i.e., the regression error
417   grows sub-linearly w.r.t. topological shifts, while our two models yield consistently low error across
418   environments. In contrast, the non-local diffusion model produces comparably stable performance yet
419   inferior to our models due to its ignorance of the useful information in input graphs. These empirical
420   observations are consistent with our theoretical results presented in Sec 3.2 and 4.2.

      6                 C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
518
519
      Conclusions. This paper harnesses diffusion PDEs as a mathematical tool for studying the general-
520
      ization capabilities of graph neural networks under topological shifts. The latter remains a largely
521
      open question, and the insights in this work open new possibilities of leveraging PDE techniques
522   for analyzing existing methods and navigating generalizable model architectures. Our proposed
523   solution, inspired by principled diffusion equations, has provable potentials for generalization and
524   shows superior performance in various graph learning tasks across different scales.
525
      Current Limitations and Future Works. The generalization analysis in the current work focuses on
526
      the data-generating mechanism as described in Fig. 2 which is inspired and generalized by the random
527
      graph model. While this mechanism can in principle reflect real-world data generation process
528   in various graph-structured data, in the open-world regime, there could exist situations involving
529   topological distribution shifts by diverse factors or their combination. Future works can extend our
530   framework for such cases where inter-dependent data is generated with different causal mechanisms.
531   Another future research direction lies in the instantiation of the diffusion and advection operators
532   in our model. Besides our choice of MPNN architecture to implement the advection process, other
533   possibilities include structural and positional embeddings. We leave this line of exploration for the
534   future, along with the analysis for the generalization capabilities of more general (e.g., non-linear)
535   versions of the advective diffusion equation and other architectural choices.

1278   E.2     C OMPETITORS
1279
1280   In our experiments, we compare with peer encoder backbones for graph learning tasks. The competi-
1281   tors span three aspects: 1) classical GNNs, 2) diffusion-based GNNs, and 3) graph Transformers. We
1282
       briefly introduce the competitors and illuminate their connections with our model.
1283
1284
                • GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017) is a popular model that propagates node embeddings over
                  observed graphs for computing node representations, which can be seen as the discretized
1285
                  version of graph diffusion equations with feature transformations.
1286
1287            • GAT (Velickovic et al., 2018) introduces attention networks for computing pairwise weights
1288              for neighboring nodes in the graph and propagates node signals with adaptive strengths
1289              given by the attention weights. GAT can be seen as the discretized version of the graph
1290
                  diffusion equation with time-dependent coupling matrices.
1291            • SGC (Wu et al., 2019) proposes to simplify the GCN architecture by removing the feature
1292              transformations in-between propagation layers, reducing multi-layer propagation to one-
1293              layer. This brings up significant acceleration for training and inference. SGC can be seen as
1294              the discretization of the linear diffusion equation on graphs.
1295
          2
              https://github.com/rdkit/rdkit

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
              • GDC (Klicpera et al., 2019) extends the graph convolution operator to graph diffusion
1297            convolution derived from the linear diffusion equation on graphs. We use its implementation
1298            version based on the heat kernel for diffusion coefficients.
1299
              • GRAND (Chamberlain et al., 2021a) proposes graph neural diffusion, a continuous PDE
1300
                model, that generalizes manifold diffusion to graphs and then uses numerical schemes to
1301            solve the PDE. We compare with its linear version that implements the linear graph diffusion
1302            equation.
1303
              • A-DGNs (Gravina et al., 2023) is a stable graph neural architecture inspired by ODE on
1304
                graphs that has provable capability to preserve long-range information between nodes and
1305
                avoid gradient vanishing or explosion in training.
1306
1307
              • CDE (Zhao et al., 2023) is a recently proposed continuous model derived from convection
                diffusion equations that is designed for addressing heterophilic graphs.
1308
1309          • GraphTrans (Wu et al., 2021) is a recently proposed Transformer for graph-structured
1310            data that satisfies the permutation-invariant property. The model architecture sequentially
1311
                combines GNNs and Transformers in order, where the GNN can learn local, short-range
                structures and the Transformer can capture global, long-range relationships.
1312
1313          • GraphGPS (Rampásek et al., 2022) introduces a scalable and powerful Transformer model
1314            class for graph data and achieves state-of-the-art results on molecular property predic-
1315            tion benchmarks. We use its scalable implementation version with the Performer atten-
1316
                tions (Choromanski et al., 2021).
1317          • DIFFormer (Wu et al., 2023) is a scalable Transformer inspired by diffusion on graphs. The
1318            model is comprised of principled attention layers, which implements the diffusion iterations
1319            minimizing a global energy. The architecture integrates graph-based feature propagation and
1320
                global attention in each layer. We use its version with simple diffusivity that only requires
                linear complexity and yields state-of-the-art results on some large-graph benchmarks.

1395   Impact of K. The hyper-parameter K (used for AD I T- SERIES) controls the number of propagation
1396   orders in the model. In fact, the value of K would impact how the structural information is utilized
1397   by the model. If K is small, the model only utilizes the low-order structure, and large K enables the
1398   usage of high-order structural information. Fig. 6 presents the model performance on Arxiv and
1399   DPPIN with K ranging from 1 to 6. We observe that the optimal settings for K are different across
1400
       cases, and using larger K can not necessarily yield better performance. This is because in these cases,
