                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Large language models (LLMs) have proven to be remarkably efficient, both across
               a wide range of natural language processing tasks and well beyond them. However,
011
               a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the origins of their impressive performance
012
               remains elusive. In this paper, we approach this challenging task by drawing an
013            equivalence between generic autoregressive language models with vocabulary of
014            size T and context window of size K and Markov chains defined on a finite state
015            space of size O(T K ). We derive several surprising findings related to the existence
016            of a stationary distribution of Markov chains that capture the inference power of
017            LLMs, their speed of convergence to it, and the influence of the temperature on
018            the latter. We then prove pre-training and in-context generalization bounds and
019            show how the drawn equivalence allows us to enrich their interpretation. Finally,
020            we illustrate our theoretical guarantees with experiments on several recent LLMs
021
               to highlight how they capture the behavior observed in practice.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      The fields of machine learning and artificial intelligence have recently seen significant progress with
      the introduction of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023a), built
027
      on the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). These models, trained on vast amounts of data,
028
      have been applied in many natural language processing tasks, including machine translation (Brown
029   et al., 2020), text generation, question answering (Roberts et al., 2020), and sentiment analysis (Zhang
030   et al., 2023a). Although successful in practice, the origins of the impressive performance of LLMs
031   remain elusive, as there is no widely accepted agreement in the scientific community on how they
032   achieve remarkable reasoning capabilities that go far beyond their training data (Brown et al., 2020).
033
      This work takes a step towards bridging the knowledge gap mentioned above by providing an explicit
034
      characterization of the LLM’s inference capabilities. For this, we adopt an intuitive, yet overlooked,
035   approach that interprets LLMs as Markov chains operating on a finite state space of sequences and
036   tokens (see Fig. 1). A key insight is that despite the seeming infinity of LLMs generating capacity,
037   they have a limited vocabulary and context window making all their possible input and output
038   sequences enumerable. We show that despite the prohibitively large size of the latter set, it exhibits
039   a structure that makes it amenable to theoretical analysis. We further generalize recent theoretical
040   advances on the generalization of LLMs and leverage our proposed point of view to provide a more
041   insightful interpretation of them.
042
043   Markov chains and Large Language Models. While none of the prior works considered the
044   equivalence between LLMs and Markov chains presented in this work1 , some used the Markovian gen-
045   erative process to better understand the intrinsic capabilities of the transformer architecture. Makkuva
046
      et al. (2024) assume that the input data is generated by an unknown high-order Markov chain to
      analyze the learning dynamics of the self-attention mechanism in a single-layer single-head trans-
047
      former. Similarly, Edelman et al. (2024) study in-context learning of a transformer model trained on
048
      samples drawn from a bi-gram Markov chain. Ildiz et al. (2024) establish an equivalence between
049   context-conditioned Markov chains and the self-attention mechanism in transformers to show that
050   self-attention weights can be learned, under certain conditions, by prompting the model. In contrast
051   to this prior work, we seek to model any transformer-based LLM as a Markov chain. Hence, our

      In-Context Learning (ICL). ICL is the ability of LLMs to adapt their predictions during inference
072   by leveraging examples or prompts directly without updating their parameters. Xie et al. (2022)
073   provide a theoretical guarantee for ICL by showing its equivalence to implicit Bayesian inference,
074   while Jeon et al. (2024) study ICL in the Bayesian setup by adopting a point of view that relates
075   it to meta-learning from non-independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. Their guarantees
076   introduce a desirable dependence on the length of the input sequence and the number of sequences
077   seen, yet their data generative process assumes that each sequence is outputted by a transformer model
078   drawn from a prior distribution. A follow-up work by Wies et al. (2024) generalizes this analysis
079   within a broader Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) framework and sheds light on the few-shot
080
      nature of ICL. Li et al. (2023) studied the generalization error of trained ICL transformers from a
      stability viewpoint and provided generalization guarantees for temporally dependent prompts that can
081
      be seen as Markov chains of different orders. The closest work on ICL to our analysis is (Zhang et al.,
082
      2023b) upon which we improve by relaxing many assumptions and, most importantly, by providing
083   stronger interpretations and experimental observations.
084
085   Generalization bounds for LLMs. Deriving generalization bounds for neural networks is inher-
086   ently difficult due to the complexity of the operations performed by the model. This can require
087   expressing it as a continuous process (Marion, 2023). For LLMs, an avenue to obtain such bounds is
088   to rely on the PAC-Bayes framework. Lotfi et al. (2023; 2024) leverage compression techniques in
089   combination with PAC-Bayes type bounds to obtain tight generalization bounds both at the document
090   and token level. These works are connected to the existing literature on compressibility and the
091   intrinsic dimension of neural networks (Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Yaras et al., 2024) and typically
092   focus on fine-tuning LLMs with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) inspired adapters. The work of Zhang et al.
093
      (2023b) considers the Bayesian framework to derive generalization bounds for pre-training where the
      data is assumed to be a Markov chain. Our theoretical analysis of pre-training is done without relying
094
      on Bayesian modeling and under realistic data generating assumption covering many common types
095
      of data used to train LLMs.

      Summary of our contributions. Our main contributions are summarized as follows.
098
099   1) We provide an explicit characterization of LLM’s inference mechanism by showing its equivalence
100      to a finite-state Markov chain. We analyze the transition matrix of the latter and prove the existence
101      and uniqueness of its stationary distribution. We give a rate of convergence to this distribution
102      that depends on the vocabulary and context window sizes, and the model’s temperature.
103   2) By leveraging concentration inequalities for dependent random variables, we obtain generalization
104      bounds for LLMs in both pretraining and in-context inference. Our bounds are proved under
105      minimal assumptions on the model and data and depend on the model’s depth, dictionary, and
106      dataset sizes, as well as the intrinsic properties of the temporally-dependent sequences it was
107      trained on. We highlight the insights that stem from these bounds by relating them to the minimax
         bounds of Markov chain learning.

      3) We experimentally show that the most recent LLMs dating from 2023-2024 obey the in-context
109      scaling laws predicted by our theoretical results. One highlight is that LLMs are better Markov
110      chains learners than the minimax optimal frequentist approach (Wolfer & Kontorovich, 2019).
111
112   We underline that, in this work, the term LLM refers to a deep transformer-based model trained on
113   non-iid data whose inference is based on the next-token prediction principle in an autoregressive
114   fashion. The latter implies that such a model transitions between a sequence of tokens to a sequence
115
      of tokens. Hence, in Section 3, the Markov chain formalization transitions between states that are
      sequences of tokens (instead of single tokens). The vast majority of existing LLMs fall into our
116
      definition suggesting that our results apply to them.

225   The stationary distribution is the long-term equilibrium of the Markov chain defined by the LLM

      independent of the initial state (i.e., input prompt) but rather captures the absolute frequencies of

298   GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), this represents 5 × 1011 training tokens, which pales in comparison with
299
      the number of non-zero elements in Qf , given by T K+1 ≈ 109632 .

528
529   This paper proposed an explicit characterization of the inference mechanism in large language models
530
      through an equivalent finite-state Markov chain. We provided an insightful theoretical analysis
      based on the established characterization and the ability of the LLM to infer the transition kernel
531
      approximating the true transition probabilities of language. We adapted our results to in-context
532
      learning where experiments confirm our theoretical insights. In the future, we hope that the proposed
533   equivalence will have far-reaching implications on our understanding of LLMs and allow for a more
534   fine-grained understanding of their expressiveness.

1058
1059   While we consider Markov chain inputs in Section 4.2, we consider less structured inputs during the
1060   pre-training phase Section 4.1.
1061   More specifically, we model the sequences of tokens used during pre-training as generic dependent
1062   random variables. To derive meaningful results, we rely on the notion of Marton couplings introduced
1063   by Marton (2004). A Marton coupling can be seen as a weak dependency structure between random
1064   variables. The associated notion of the mixing matrix, analogous to the mixing time of a Markov
1065   chain, is used to assess the strength of the dependence between those variables.
1066
       This minimal modeling choice is made to remain as faithful as possible to the pre-training considered
1067   in practical applications of LLMs, for which the pre-training data is not public and may contain
1068   arbitrary data points such as video, code snippets, text and images (Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al.,
1069   2023; Brown et al., 2020; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a).
1070
       As shown in Paulin (2015, Remark 2.2.), considering sequences of random variables linked through a
1071
       Marton coupling is a weaker assumption than what is usually done in the literature on generalization
1072
       bounds, which typically relies on independent random variables and Markov chains (Hu et al., 2024;
1073   Marion, 2023; Wolfer & Kontorovich, 2019; Zhang et al., 2023b).
1074
1075   In particular, the results stated in Section 4.1 encompass the case where the pre-training input
1076
       sequences of tokens are independent random variables (Kim et al., 2024) or Markov chains (Zhang
       et al., 2023b). We also note that Markov chains can model bigrams used in natural language (Bietti
1077
       et al., 2023; Jurafsky & Martin, 2024).

       E.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND T OKENIZATION
2343
2344   Experimental setup. To ensure a fair validation of our theoretical results, we conduct our experi-
2345   ments on some of the most recent and widely used LLMs: Gemma 2B (Team et al., 2024), Llama2
2346   7B & 13B (Touvron et al., 2023b), Llama3 8B, Llama3.2 1B & 3B (Dubey et al., 2024),
2347   and Mistral 7Bv0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023).
2348
2349   Tokenization. As the models we consider have different tokenizations, we need to do this step
2350   with extra care as it is a crucial part of the experimental procedure. Indeed, LLMs’ ability to handle
2351   numerical values has been proved to be dependent on the tokenization algorithm (Ali et al., 2024;
2352   Gruver et al., 2023; Singh & Strouse, 2024). The most widely used tokenization algorithm to-date,
2353
       BPE (Sennrich et al., 2016), tends to assign tokens to arbitrary 3-digits numbers based on their
       occurrences in large-scale corpora, and the tokenizer’s vocabulary size. As highlighted by (Gruver
2354
       et al., 2023), this artifact severely hinders LLMs’ ability to predict numerical values in-context. This is
2355
       the case for popular LLMs such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020). Newer models (LLama3, GPT-3.5,
2356   GPT-4) however, tend to have hard-coded rules on top of BPE, making them able to encode all
2357   3-digits numbers with their own token. Although this feature would accelerate the ICL procedure by
2358   eliminating the need for the Hierarchy-PDF algorithm in (Liu et al., 2024),the under-representability
2359   of larger numbers in the training data could be an issue. Other tokenization techniques that are
2360   numerical values-focused has been presented in the literature (Golkar et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024),
2361   paving the way for another research direction that may benefit our method.
2362
2363   Rodmap. In the rest of this section, we extend our experiments to study the following setups:
2364
              • In Appendix E.2: impact of the number of states d;
2365
2366          • In Appendix E.3: extension to Markov chains with pmin = 0;
2367          • In Appendix E.4: impact of the tokenization;

2731   F.2   L IMITATIONS
2732
2733
       We recall from Remark 4.1 that the TV distance is a natural choice to compare transition matrices
       in the Markov chain literature. In addition, while the KL divergence can be used to compare
2734
       probability distributions, it does not define a metric space. Hence, we cannot straightforwardly extend
2735
       Theorem 4.4 with the KL divergence because the proof relies on the use of the triangular inequality.
2736   As Theorem 4.4 is one of our main results and enables us to show that the theory and the practice align
2737   (Section 5), this also contributed to our preference for the TV distance instead of the KL divergence.
