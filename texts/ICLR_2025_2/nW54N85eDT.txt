                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Modeling sequential user behaviors for future action prediction is crucial in im-
012
               proving user’s information retrieval experience. Recent studies highlight the im-
013            portance of incorporating contextual information to enhance prediction perfor-
014            mance. One crucial and typical contextual information is the scene feature which
015            we define it as sub-interfaces within an app, created by designers to provide spe-
016            cific functionalities, such as “text2product search” and “live” in e-commence apps.
017            Different scenes exhibit distinct functionalities and usage habits, leading to signifi-
018            cant distribution gap in user engagement across them. Popular sequential behavior
019            models either ignore the scene feature or merely use it as attribute embeddings,
020            which could lead to substantial information loss or cannot capture the interplay
021
               between scene and item in modeling dynamic user interests. In this work, we pro-
               pose a novel Dual Sequence Prediction network (DSPnet) to effectively capture
022
               the interplay between scene and item sequences for future behavior prediction.
023
               DSPnet consists of two parallel networks dedicated to predicting scene and item
024            sequences, and a sequence feature enhancement module to capture the interplay.
025            Further, considering the randomness and noise in learning sequence dynamics, we
026            introduce Conditional Contrastive Regularization (CCR) loss to capture the invari-
027            ance of similar historical sequences. Theoretical analysis suggests that DSPnet
028            can learn the joint relationships between scene and item sequences, and also show
029            better robustness on real-world user behaviors. Extensive experiments are con-
030            ducted on one public benchmark and two collected industrial datasets. The codes
031            and collected datasets will be made public soon.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Modern online information retrieval services, such as search and recommendation, have brought
037   great changes and convenience for human’s daily life. Correspondingly, users’ sequential behaviors
038   spread over a variety of apps and websites (Kang & McAuley, 2018; Chen et al., 2021a). Modeling
039
      these sequential user behaviors as representations for future behavior prediction has become an
      important issue in machine learning applications, which greatly improves the downstream services.
040
041   Recent advances in modeling sequential user behaviors concentrate on three key areas: design of
042   the encoding architecture, formulation of the training objective and utilization of the contextual in-
043   formation. In design of the encoding architecture, early works employ Markov models (Rendle
044   et al., 2010) to capture sequential patterns within historical behavior sequences. However, these
045
      models face limitations in their ability to represent complex and higher-order sequential dependen-
      cies. Consequently, researchers tend to investigate the more expressive recurrent neural networks
046
      (RNNs) (Medsker et al., 2001; Hidasi et al., 2016; Hidasi & Karatzoglou, 2018; Donkers et al., 2017)
047
      or self-attention mechanisms (Vaswani et al., 2017; Kang & McAuley, 2018; Sun et al., 2019), to
048   enhance sequential behavior modeling. Subsequently, researchers explored more advanced formu-
049   lation of training objective, beyond the conventional next-item prediction objective. They primarily
050   designed various self-supervised training tasks to extract additional insights from sequences during
051   training (Sun et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a).
052   For instance, Wang et al. (2023a) introduced a context-context contrast which encourages sequences
053   after augmentation to have similar representations by leveraging contrastive learning loss. Addi-
      tionally, several studies have focused on utilization of the contextual information such as item cate-

077   Different from the above contextual feature, one crucial and typical contextual factor influencing
078   user behaviors is the scene feature, which we define it as sub-interfaces created by designers to en-
079   capsulate specific themes or functionalities within apps or websites. As shown in Figure 1 (a), differ-
080   ent scenes, usually operated by different teams, have different themes and styles. For example, the
081   shopping app encompasses scenes such as “text search”, “recommendation” and “live”, facilitating
082
      functionalities like text-to-product search, product recommendations, and interactive live shopping
      experiences. Each of these scenes represents different shopping types, leading to significant distri-
083
      bution gap in user engagement across them. Figure 1 (b) illustrates the category distribution and
084
      user conversion rate across different scenes, unveiling significant disparities in both item content
085   and user engagement among various scenes. The distinct features of a scene play a crucial role in
086   providing conditional information for behavior occurrence. When a user enters a specific scene, it
087   can reflect certain shopping interests and the interests greatly impact the items the user is likely to
088   interact with. Ignoring the scene feature would result in a large loss of information and introduce
089   data bias in modeling the sequential user trajectory. Currently, there are limited studies addressing
090   this area because of inaccessible data. There are remains two challenges to effectively incorporate
091   this scene feature in sequential behavior modeling. One challenge is that how to capture the inter-
092   play between scenes and items. Although we may incorporate the scene feature as an input field of
093
      items by following (Tian et al., 2023; Papso, 2023) in user behavior modeling, it could overlook the
      interplay between scene and item, which hinders their ability of mining scene feature in behavior
094
      prediction. As shown in Figure 1 (c), the item and scene simultaneously occur in an interaction
095
      behavior, and the sequential scene dynamics and item dynamics have mutual effects in subsequent
096   behavior generation. The other challenge is that how to better learn sequence dynamics by consid-
097   ering the scene-item misalignment issue within a sequence. To be more specific, the misalignment
098   issue means a user’s interest and intent are initially reflected in one scene but the behavior are in-
099   correctly collected in another scene. For example, in an e-shopping app, a user might see certain
100   products in the “recommendation” interface with the intention to purchase them. However, instead
101   of buying immediately, the user adds these products to the “cart” interface and buys from the “cart”
102   interface later, because of upcoming sales promotions from sellers. The misalignment issue brings
103   obstacles when modeling the interplay between scenes and items.
104   To this end, we propose a novel Dual Sequence Prediction network (DSPnet) that effectively cap-
105   tures the interplay between sequential scenes and items, while being robust against against scene-
106   item misalignment noise to predict future user behaviors. DSPnet comprises two parallel networks
107   dedicated to predicting scene and item sequences, together with a sequence feature enhancement
      module to deliver the mutual effects across both sequences. In particular, the scene sequence pre-

      diction network and item sequence prediction network encode their own dynamics from historical
109   behaviors in sequence level, which avoids the one-to-one scene-item misalignment noise in learning.
110   Meanwhile, the sequence feature enhancement module enables one network’s encoding features to
111   be input into the other, allowing both prediction networks to capitalize on their interplay during the
112   sequence learning process. We also demonstrate that the learning approach of DSPnet is theoreti-
113   cally equivalent to maximizing the joint log-likelihood of scenes and items, presenting a good way
114   to model their relationships and inter-dependent sequential dynamics. Moreover, given that sequen-
115   tial user behaviors often exhibit randomness and noise, which can adversely affect the learning of
116   sequence dynamics, we introduce Conditional Contrastive Regularization (CCR) loss to capture the
117
      representation invariance of similar historical sequences. Through learned conditional weights, CCR
      loss can adaptively promote similarity in representations for sequences that undergo augmentation
118
      with different forces. We empirically demonstrate that CCR loss highlights the relationships among
119
      contrasting samples, enhancing the model’s robustness in representation learning for real-world,
120   skewed user behaviors. The contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
121
              • We propose a novel DSPnet method that enhances behavior prediction by capturing the
122
                interplay between scenes and items in a sequence. Our theoretical analysis reveals that
123             training DSPnet is equivalent to maximizing the joint log-likelihood of both scene and item
124             sequences, enabling us to effectively model their relationships.
125
              • Further, we introduce Conditional Contrastive Regularization (CCR) to enhance the
126
                model’s representation learning by capturing the invariance of similar historical sequences.
127             CCR uses learned conditional weights to more effectively promote similarity among those
128             sequences, improving representation robustness in skewed user behaviors.
129
              • We have collected 37-day sequential user behavior data from our e-commence app and
130             constructed two datasets. They contain chronological purchase behaviors on nearly thirty
131             million items, providing a valuable resource to address the research data gap in this field.
132
              • We conduct extensive experiments on three datasets: one public benchmark and two indus-
133
                trial ones. Results on these datasets show the impact of employing scene information in
134             sequential behavior modeling and how our method outperforms state-of-the-art baselines.

      Design of the Encoding Architecture: Early works investigate Markov chains (Ching & Ng, 2006)
139   to capture sequential dynamics within historical sequential behaviors. However, as the number of
140   past actions increases, the state space grows exponentially, making it challenging to capture higher-
141   order dependencies in real-world applications. Consequently, researchers explore more expressive
142   neural sequence models like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Medsker et al., 2001; Hidasi
143   et al., 2016; Hidasi & Karatzoglou, 2018; Donkers et al., 2017), Convolutional Neural Networks
144   (CNNs) (Tang & Wang, 2018), Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTM) (Graves & Graves,
145   2012; Duan et al., 2023) and self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017; Kang & McAuley, 2018; Sun et al.,
146   2019) models to enhance sequential behavior modeling. For example, SASRec (Kang & McAuley,
147
      2018) and BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) broadened the application of self-attention models to se-
      quential behavior modeling. Some works (Hu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Zheng et al., 2024; Liao
148
      et al., 2024) focus on leveraging large LLMs for sequential recommendation, including aligning se-
149
      quential RS with LLMs, summarizing user preferences. Others (Ma et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024;
150   Wang et al., 2024) investigate the application of diffusion models in sequential recommendation,
151   aiming to better capture the evolution of user preferences over time.
152
153
      Formulation of the Training Objective: Several studies concentrate on forecasting item lists over
      specific time periods or behavioral distributions instead of the next individual items. SUMN (Gu
154
      et al., 2021) hypothesizes that future behavioral distributions should align with past distributions.
155
      It learns sequence representations by maximizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between item
156   occurrence distributions from a previous period and those of the future. MSDP (Fu et al., 2023)
157   uses a multi-scale approach to optimize predictions for the next period by considering item lists
158   across different timeframes. Constructing self-supervised learning tasks to facilitate the prediction
159   of sequential user behaviors has also gained considerable attention. CL4SRec (Qiu et al., 2021)
160   explores the contrastive signals derived from augmented historical sequences through contrastive
161   learning. ContraRec (Wang et al., 2023b) achieves state-of-the-art performance by constructing
      contrastive sequences using random mask and reorder augmentation techniques.

      While contrastive learning improves sequential behavior modeling, it often ignores the varying roles
163   of positive and negative samples. Our CCR loss learns conditional weights for these samples, captur-
164   ing their unique contributions and enhancing the model’s robustness in learning sequence dynamics.
165
166
      Utilization of the Contextual Information: In user behavior sequences, there are various contex-
      tual factors linked to each action, such as types of user behavior (e.g., clicks, purchases, additions
167
      to favorites) (Meng et al., 2020; Ni et al., 2018; Su et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023; Xuan et al., 2023;
168
      Chen et al., 2023a), product category (Cai et al., 2021) and other multiple item attributes (Papso,
169   2023). DUPN (Ni et al., 2018) incorporates multiple kinds of behavior types to construct multi-task
170   learning for more effective personalization. Xuan et al. (2023) designed the multi-behavior learn-
171   ing module to extract users’ personalized information for user-embedding enhancement, and utilize
172   knowledge graph in the knowledge enhancement module to derive more robust knowledge-aware
173   representations for items. MKM-SR (Meng et al., 2020) points out that a user’s sequence behaviors
174   could have some micro-behaviors that reflect fine-grained and deep understanding of the user’s pref-
175   erence. CoCoRec (Cai et al., 2021) leverages item category to organize a user’s own past actions
176   and further employs self-attention to capture in-category transition patterns. Then, these transition
177
      patterns are used to find similar users, thereby enhancing collaborative learning. CARCA (Papso,
      2023) incorporates both the attributes of interacted items and contextual data of user interactions by
178
      employing combined sequences as input for multi-head attention blocks. Some other works employ
179
      different scene definitions from ours. Chen et al. (2021b) explored adaptive sequential recommen-
180   dation systems (RS) across different domains. Wang et al. (2021) defined the scene as a collection
181   of predefined item categories. Wan et al. (2024) investigated the usage of large language models
182   (LLMs) for real-time sequential RS. Li et al. (2024a) defined scenes as 200 predefined topics, such
183   as “weekend spring outing”, “afternoon tea” and “KFC crazy Thursday”.
184
      It is notable that above works studied different kinds of contextual information from our defined
185
      scene feature. Integrating the defined scene features into sequential behavior modeling constitutes
186   an important and new problem setting derived from practical industrial business. When considering
187   techniques, although we may leverage the scene as an additional attribute of items by following (Tian
188   et al., 2023; Papso, 2023), it would ignore the mutual effects between scenes and items and lead to
189   insufficient learning. DSPnet is designed to effectively capture the interplay for behavior prediction.

197   In sequential user behavior prediction task, we aim to predict the user’s future behaviors based on
198   historical behaviors. Given a historical behavior sequence T from user u, it is defined as:
199
200                          T = {(v1 , s1 ), (v2 , s2 ), ..., (vj , sj ), ..., (v|T | , s|T | )},        (1)

203   corresponding scene, respectively. V denotes the whole item set with size Nv and S denotes the
204   whole scene set with size Ns . |T | is the number of historical interactions. The historical behav-
205   iors actually contains two coupling sequences, i.e. the item sequence V = {v1 , v2 , ..., v|T | } and
206   the scene sequence S = {s1 , s2 , ..., s|T | }. Based on these two historical sequences, we learn the
207   sequential user representation zu and predict future behaviors. To better study the issue, we only
208   consider one-type behavior which means the sequence includes only one-type behavior, e.g. “buy”.

212   alignment noise and deliver these dynamics to both scene and item sides for predicting behaviors.
213   The second one aims to learn representation invariance and dynamically enhance the similarity be-
214   tween representations of similar sequences, thereby improving the model’s robustness against ran-
215   dom, noisy, and skewed user behaviors. The model architecture is shown in Figure 2, details are

233   3.2   D UAL S EQUENCE L EARNING
234
235   Dual Sequence Dynamics: Online user behaviors occur under different contexts chronologically,
236   reflecting users’ dynamic interests over time. The defined scene feature largely influences how users
237
      interact with items, while interactions taken on particular items can subsequently influence users’
      decisions in following scenes. Therefore, the sequential dynamics of both items and scenes play a
238
      vital role in predicting subsequent user behaviors. Besides, considering the scene-item misalignment
239
      issue in data caused by many factors such as feedback delay and seller promotions, we encode the
240   dynamics in sequence-level correspondence, rather than maintaining strict one-to-one scene-item
241   correspondence. Given the historical scene sequence S and item sequence V of user u, we can
242   employ some sequential models to capture the sequence dynamics. Let fS and fV be the sequential
243   models of historical scenes and items respectively, we have:
244                                        hS = fS (S), hV = fV (V),                                    (2)
245
      where hS and hV mean the encoded latent representations of S and V, respectively. The choice of
246   sequential model is flexible (such as RNN, LSTM and transformer) and we employ the powerful
247   transformer model following recent works (Sun et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a).
248
249
      Sequence Feature Enhancement: As we previously discussed, both the item and the scene mutu-
      ally influence subsequent behavior generation. This means that when predicting future interacted
250
      items, we cannot solely depend on information from historical item interactions. Similarly, if we
251
      predict the subsequent interacted scenes, we cannot only employ the information from past scene
252   interactions. Either the item sequence or the scene sequence serves as the enhanced information
253   for the other to predict future behaviors. Specifically, denoting zS and zV as the feature enhanced
254   representation of scene sequence and item sequence, we have:
255                                zS = gS (hS ⊕ hV ), zV = gV (hS ⊕ hV ),                             (3)
256
      where gS (·) and gV (·) denote the fusion MLP layers. Both zS and zV can be considered as the user
257   representation zu that encodes mutual effects and dynamics from historical behaviors. We maintain
258   these two enhanced representations here to provide diverse aspects of the user interests and better
259   facilitate the subsequent item and scene prediction tasks.

319   Unlike the structured human language, online sequential user behaviors are often random and noisy.
320
      In DSPnet, we use sequential contrastive representation learning to capture the dynamics of sequen-
      tial behaviors. We create augmented sequences from the original data, align their representations
321
      with the originals, and ensure that representations of different sequences remain distinct.
322
323   Sequence Augmentation: The sequence augmentation must not alter the user’s intended meaning
      in input sequence. Drawing insights from recent studies (Sun et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023a),

325   mentation involves randomly masking a percentage of elements from the input sequence. Reorder

330   mented historical scene and item sequence as S + = A(S) and V + = A(V), respectively. These

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525
      Learning to represent sequential user behaviors for predicting future actions is a crucial topic in
526   machine learning applications. In this study, we propose a novel framework called DSPnet that
527   effectively captures the interplay between historical scene and item sequences, enabling a better
528   modeling of dynamic user interests for future behavior prediction. Additionally, recognizing the
529   randomness and noise inherent in user behaviors, we introduce CCR loss to enhance representation
530   invariance, thereby improving the learning of dynamic interests. Through both theoretical analysis
531   and empirical evaluation, we demonstrate that DSPnet does better at modeling the interplay between
532   sequences and exhibits superior performance in skewed data scenarios.
533   Although DSPnet has achieved remarkable performance of user behavior prediction, it still has
534   certain limitations. For instance, the current DSPnet only incorporates scene and item sequences,
535   leaving potential to improve performance by integrating additional feature information from these
536   sequences. Additionally, DSPnet currently establishes the interplay between historical scene and
537   item sequences only after processing the last token’s representation. An alternative, but more com-
538   putationally consuming approach, would be to model these interplays at the level of each token’s
539   representation. We plan to study these issues in our future research.

760   Let v and s denote the observed interacted item and scene of user u, respectively.P Then, the joint
761   log-likelihood is composed of a sum over the likelihoods of individual data points u [log pθ (v, s)],
762   where pθ (v, s) is the probability density function. Given the observed item-scene behaviors (v, s),
763   we denote V and S as the historically interacted items and scenes sequentially before v and s,
764   respectively. The corresponding encoded latent representations of V and S are denoted as zV and zS ,
765
      respectively. Then, drawing from the idea of maximizing the marginal log-likelihood in Variational
      Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2013), log pθ (v, s) can be written as:
766
767                       log pθ (v, s) = DKL [qϕ (zV , zS |V, S)||p(zV , zS |V, S)] + LELBO ,                (10)

770   lower bound (ELBO) on the log-likelihood log pθ (v, s).

958
      By analyzing the results from this table, we see that the standard Gaussian distribution consistently
959
      shows the best performance on three datasets. This observation matches a widely accepted principle
960
      in recommendation and search systems, where user preferences tend to exhibit Gaussian distribu-
