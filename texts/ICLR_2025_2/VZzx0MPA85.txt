009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011            While closed-source Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate strong math-
012            ematical problem-solving abilities, open-source models continue to struggle with
013            such tasks. To bridge this gap, we propose a data augmentation approach and in-
014            troduce PersonaMathQA, a dataset derived from MATH and GSM8K, on which
015            we train the PersonaMath models. Our approach consists of two stages: the first
016            stage is learning from Persona Diversification, and the second stage is learn-
017
               ing from Reflection. In the first stage, we regenerate detailed chain-of-thought
               (CoT) solutions as instructions using a closed-source LLM and introduce a novel
018
               persona-driven data augmentation technique to enhance the dataset’s quantity and
019
               diversity. In the second stage, we incorporate reflection to fully leverage more
020            challenging and valuable questions. Evaluation of our PersonaMath models on
021            MATH and GSM8K reveals that the PersonaMath-7B model (based on LLaMA-
022            2-7B) achieves an accuracy of 24.2% on MATH and 68.7% on GSM8K, surpass-
023            ing all baseline methods and achieving state-of-the-art performance. Notably, our
024            dataset contains only 70.3K data points—merely 17.8% of MetaMathQA and 27%
025            of MathInstruct—yet our model outperforms these baselines, demonstrating the
026            high quality and diversity of our dataset, which enables more efficient model train-
027            ing. We open-source the PersonaMathQA dataset, PersonaMath models, and our
028
               code for public usage.

072   1                                     I NTRODUCTION
073                   “There are a thousand Hamlets in a thousand people’s eyes”
074
075                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                            Shakespeare
076   Large language models (LLMs) have rapidly advanced in recent years, showing impressive abilities
077   across various NLP tasks, including open domain conversation (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
078   2022), coding (Chen et al., 2021; Rozière et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a) and math (Luo et al., 2023; Yu
079   et al., 2024; Imani et al., 2023). Among these tasks, solving math problems stands out as particularly
080   challenging due to its complexity and the requirement for multi-step reasoning to reach a solution.
081
      While some closed-source models, such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic,
      2024), and Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024), have demonstrated strong math-solving capabilities,
082
      current open-source models (e.g., LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024)) continue to
083
      struggle in this area. Therefore, enhancing the math problem-solving abilities of open-source models
084   is a prominent desiderata.
085
086   A widely adopted and effective approach for improving the math-solving capabilities of open-source
087
      models is fine-tuning, owing to the accessibility of their weights (Yuan et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023;
      Luo et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024). However, directly fine-tuning on the original dataset leads to only
088
      minimal improvements (Touvron et al., 2023), as solving complex math problems requires multiple
089
      reasoning steps, which the original dataset does not adequately capture. Furthermore, the limited
090   number of samples in the original dataset is often insufficient for models to fully grasp how to solve
091   mathematical problems. To address this, recent studies have demonstrated promising results by boot-
092   strapping the original dataset (Yu et al., 2024), generating more detailed Chain of Thought (CoT)
093   solutions (Wei et al., 2023), and training models on the enhanced dataset. However, directly boot-
094   strapping from the original problems can reduce the diversity of the enhanced dataset, necessitating
095   a large amount of data to achieve effective training, which is resource-intensive and inefficient.
096   In this paper, we propose a data augmentation method designed to enable models to learn more
097   efficiently and effectively from enhanced datasets. The key to our approach lies in improving both
098   the quality and diversity of the data. To achieve this, we introduce a persona-driven approach and
099   create PersonaMathQA, a dataset characterized by high efficiency and diversity. Recently, Role-
100   Playing LLMs that incorporate personas have gained significant attention, as leveraging personas
101   allows LLMs to adapt to persona-specific scenarios and generate unique, diverse answers from var-
102   ious perspectives. Moreover, recent research has further highlighted the potential of Role-Playing
103   LLMs. From extensive web data, Chan et al. (2024) automatically created Persona Hub, a collec-
104
      tion of 1 billion diverse personas, with 200,000 personas released publicly. Building on this vast
      collection, our proposed data augmentation method can generate data from a wide range of diverse
105
      perspectives, encompassing various ethnicities, occupations, age groups, and more.
106
107   Specifically, our method is divided into two stages. In the first stage, we focus on empowering open-
      source models to Learn from Persona Diversification by proposing a persona-driven method. We

      start by using a powerful closed-source LLM to generate detailed CoT solutions for the math prob-
109   lems within the original dataset. After identifying the correctly answered questions, we apply the
110   persona-driven method to prompt the closed-source LLM to rewrite these questions using specific
111   and diverse personas. We then regenerate the CoT solutions and incorporate both the correctly an-
112   swered questions and their original questions in our dataset. In the second stage, we shift our focus
113   to enabling open-source models to Learn from Reflection. For questions that the closed-source
114   LLM answered incorrectly in the first stage, we allow the LLM to reflect on its mistakes and attempt
115   to regenerate the correct answers. For those questions answered correctly post-reflection, which are
116   inherently more challenging than those answered correctly in the first stage, we aim to increase their
117
      weight in the final dataset. This strategy facilitates more effective learning of these difficult ques-
      tions by open-source LLMs through training. To achieve this, we increase the number of rewrites for
118
      these challenging questions and follow similar procedures as in the first stage, where regeneration is
119
      accomplished through reflection.

121   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
122   • We propose a novel persona-driven data augmentation method, resulting in the creation of a high-
123     quality and efficient dataset, PersonaMathQA.
124   • Using our PersonaMathQA dataset, we trained LLaMA and Qwen2.5, resulting in the Per-
125     sonaMath models. We evaluated the math problem-solving capabilities of the PersonaMath
126     models on two widely recognized math benchmarks, MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and
127     GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021). Despite our dataset being only 17.79% the size of previous state-
128     of-the-art datasets, PersonaMath outperformed them on both benchmarks, setting a new SOTA.
129
      • We demonstrated the effectiveness of the persona-driven method in data rewriting. Our approach
        not only improves dataset diversity and quality but also reduces training costs compared to other
130
        rewriting methods. Our work highlights the significant potential of persona-driven methods and
131
        offers valuable insights for future research.

135   Large Language Models for Mathematical Reasoning. Among various NLP tasks, solving
136   math problems has consistently been regarded as one of the most challenging. Current cutting-
137   edge closed-source LLMs, such as o1 (OpenAI, 2024c), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), Claude 3.5
138   Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024), and Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid et al., 2024), exhibit strong math problem-
139
      solving capabilities. Nonetheless, there remains substantial work to further enhance these capabil-
      ities through various strategies, including preprocessing math questions (An et al., 2023), utilizing
140
      more advanced prompts (Ling et al., 2017; Yao et al., 2023), employing external tools (Yamauchi
141
      et al., 2023; He-Yueya et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022), and enhancing overall interactions (Wu et al.,
142   2024). In contrast, open-source models with fewer parameters still struggle with solving math prob-
143   lems. Consequently, numerous studies, including our own, aim to bridge this gap, enabling open-
144   source models to achieve robust math problem-solving capabilities after fine-tuning. To enhance
145   the effectiveness of fine-tuning, past approaches have primarily focused on generating intermediate
146   steps for answers (Nye et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Lewkowycz et al., 2022),
147   fine-tuning across multiple datasets (Mishra et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2023), employing teacher-student
148   knowledge distillation (Imani et al., 2023), and learning from enhanced datasets (Imani et al., 2023;
149   Raiyan et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024), as explored in our paper.
150
151   Role-Playing Large Language Models. Recently, Role-Playing LLMs that integrate personas
152   have gained significant attention. Specifically, Role-Playing LLMs involve directly incorporating
153
      personas into the prompts of LLMs, enabling them to generate unique, role-specific content within
      a designated context. This method is straightforward to implement, requiring only prompt modifi-
154
      cations, yet it yields impressive results. For instance, Dong et al. (2024) employed multiple LLM
155
      agents, assigning each to play a specialized expert role, allowing them to collaboratively tackle
156   complex code generation tasks. Similarly, ChatDev (Qian et al., 2024) segmented the software de-
157   velopment process into four stages—designing, coding, testing, and documenting—and proposed
158   a Chat Chain to facilitate communication among agents responsible for each stage, ultimately en-
159   abling cooperative program development. Role-Playing LLMs are also frequently utilized in gaming
160   contexts. For instance, Wang et al. (2023) has an LLM assume the role of a general assistant, con-
161   tinuously exploring the Minecraft game world to acquire skills and survive longer. In the medical
      domain, Tang et al. (2024) simulates real-life scenarios by dividing the diagnostic reasoning process

      into five stages: expert gathering, analysis proposition, report summarization, collaborative consul-
163   tation, and decision-making. Multiple LLM agents are then assigned different roles to collaborate
164   on the diagnostic process.

168   In this section, we provide a detailed description of how we built PersonaMathQA. An overview of
169   our approach is illustrated in Figure 1. Our method, which enhances data using a persona-driven
170   approach, is divided into two stages. Using this approach, we constructed the PersonaMathQA
171   dataset based on the MATH and GSM8K datasets, and fine-tuned the PersonaMath models on it.
172
173   3.1     S TAGE 1: L EARNING FROM P ERSONA D IVERSIFICATION
174
175   Limitations of the Original Dataset. Although fine-tuning open-source models can significantly
176   enhance their performance across various tasks, improving their math problem-solving capabilities
177   through fine-tuning alone is particularly challenging. One reason is that math problems are inher-
178   ently complex and often require intricate multi-step reasoning to arrive at the correct answer (Ahn
179
      et al., 2024), with errors at any step potentially leading to incorrect results. Another significant
      challenge is the limitation of current training datasets. Effective math problem-solving requires de-
180
      tailed step-by-step reasoning, yet existing datasets often lack such comprehensive solutions, often
181
      providing only final answers or insufficiently detailed manual solutions. Consequently, models
182   trained on such datasets struggle to develop step-by-step reasoning skills, yielding minimal im-
183   provements from fine-tuning alone (Touvron et al., 2023). Manually labeling detailed step-by-step
184   solutions is time-consuming and labor-intensive. To address this, we use a closed-source LLM with
185   advanced mathematical problem-solving abilities to automatically generate detailed CoT solutions,
186   thereby enhancing the training dataset with comprehensive, step-by-step reasoning. The prompt we
187   used is shown below.
188
189         Prompt for Inference
190
191         Please provide a detailed, step-by-step explanation for the following math problem. At the
192
            end of the explanation, present the final answer enclosed in \\boxed{} \n Math problem:
193
194
      Persona-Driven Data Augmentation Method. After obtaining the CoT solutions automatically
195
      generated by the LLM, we first filter out the correct answers and add them to our PersonaMathQA
196   dataset. However, this initial training data is insufficient on its own. To create high-quality enhanced
197   datasets and avoid excessive time and resource expenditure, we employ a data augmentation method
198   in which the LLM automatically rewrites questions to generate new ones. Previous studies have
199   shown that directly rewriting questions is inefficient (Yu et al., 2024), as it requires a large volume
200   of data to significantly boost model performance. This inefficiency arises from the homogeneous
201   nature of rewritten questions, which often lack diversity. To address this issue and augment the
202   dataset more effectively, we propose a novel persona-driven method. Inspired by the observation
203   that introducing persona-related scenarios to the LLM can prompt it to generate unique and persona-
204
      specific content, we theorize that with a sufficient number of diverse personas, we can generate a vast
      amount of varied content. A recent study (Chan et al., 2024) that released 200,000 diverse personas
205
      created from extensive web data provides a solid foundation for our approach. These personas
206
      encompass various nationalities, races, religions, occupations, age groups, and more. Leveraging
207   this diversity, our method can create varied questions, yielding significant improvements with less
208   data, demonstrating the principle of “less is more”. Additionally, our persona-driven method is
209   straightforward to implement, allowing others to create their own augmented datasets using our
210   approach. The prompt we used is shown below.
211
212         Prompt for Rewriting
213
214         Math problem: {problem} \nPlease rephrase the above math problem with the following
215         persona:\n{persona}

230   3.2     S TAGE 2: L EARNING FROM R EFLECTION
231
232   Shifting Focus to Incorrectly Answered Questions. In Stage 1, we first have the LLM reason
233   through the original dataset and identify questions with correct answers. Questions answered incor-
234
      rectly are set aside for further analysis. For a detailed analysis, we use the MATH dataset, where
      each problem is classified into difficulty levels ranging from “1” to “5”. We calculated the average
235
      difficulty levels of correctly and incorrectly answered questions during the reasoning stage. The
236
      results, shown in Table 1, indicate that the average difficulty level of correctly answered questions is
237   3.22, whereas incorrectly answered questions have an average level of 4.28. This suggests that the
238   incorrectly answered questions are inherently more challenging, making them harder for the LLM
239   to solve. However, this also makes them more valuable, as learning to solve these complex problems
240   can significantly enhance the model’s step-by-step reasoning ability.
241
242   Reflecting on Errors and Regenerating Solutions. To explore and utilize the potential value in
243   these incorrectly answered questions, we prompt the LLM to reflect on its mistakes and attempt to
244   provide correct answers. This approach is inspired by previous research demonstrating that LLMs
245   can self-reflect and self-correct (Shinn et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). Specifically, for the questions
246   that the LLM answered incorrectly in Stage 1, we return the wrong solution to the LLM, inform
247
      it that the answer is incorrect, and prompt it to reflect on its reasoning process and regenerate a
      detailed CoT solution. The prompt we used is shown below, where the explanation is replaced with
248
      the incorrect solution.
249
250
            Prompt for Reflection
251
252         The following input consists of a math problem and a corresponding explanation. How-
253         ever, this explanation is incorrect, please reflect on its errors and then generate a cor-
254         rected, detailed, step-by-step explanation for the following math problem. Divide your re-
255         sponse into two parts: Review of Incorrect Explanation and Corrected Explanation. At the
256         end of the explanation, present the final answer enclosed in \\boxed{}.\nMath Problem:
257         {problem}\nIncorrect Explanation: {explanation}
258
259   In this step, the LLM’s response is divided into two parts: “Review of Incorrect Explanation” and
260   “Corrected Explanation”. We use only the “Corrected Explanation” as the regenerated solution from
261
      the LLM. However, it is important to note that while the “Review of Incorrect Explanation” is not
      used directly, it is an indispensable part of the reflection process. This part allows the LLM to
262
      summarize its mistakes, which is crucial for generating the subsequent ”Corrected Explanation”.
263
      Correctly answered questions are then added to our PersonaMathQA dataset, while questions that
264   remain unanswered correctly by the LLM are discarded. It’s worth noting that previous studies have
265   also explored generating the correct solution process by directly providing the LLM with the correct
266   answer (Zhang et al., 2024). However, this method carries risks due to LLM hallucinations, where
267   the LLM may produce an incorrect solution process while providing the correct final answer. Such
268   data can be misleading and detrimental. Therefore, we discard questions that cannot be answered
269   correctly. For the questions answered correctly in Stage 2, we rewrite them as we did in Stage 1. The
      key differences is that in Stage 2, we rewrite the questions K2 times, where K2 is greater than K1 ,

317
      MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), to create our PersonaMathQA

320   answer. The GSM8K dataset includes approximately 7,500 training problems and about 1,000 test
321   problems. In contrast, MATH is a more challenging dataset composed of problems from mathe-
322   matics competitions, such as AMC 10, AMC 12, and AIME, covering a wide range of subjects and
323   difficulty levels. The problems span seven subjects: Prealgebra, Algebra, Number Theory, Counting
      and Probability, Geometry, Intermediate Algebra, and Precalculus, and are classified into five diffi-

      Baselines. To evaluate our proposed method comprehensively, we establish a baseline that in-
      cludes a range of closed-source models, open-source models, and state-of-the-art methods for train-
337
      ing open-source models to solve mathematical problems. The closed-source models include o1-

      preview (OpenAI, 2024c), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a), PaLM-2 (Anil et al., 2023), Claude 3.5 Son-
339   net (Anthropic, 2024), and others. The open-source models encompass LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al.,
340   2023), LLaMA-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2.5 (Team, 2024), Mistral Large 2 (AI, 2024), and

343
        ties of LLaMA-2 using a Reinforcement Learning from Evol-Instruct Feedback (RLEIF) method.
344
        RLEIF involves three steps: (1) supervised fine-tuning (SFT), (2) training an Instruction Reward

346     reinforcement learning via proximal policy optimization (PPO).
347   • MAmmoTH (Yue et al., 2023): The MAmmoTH models are trained on MathInstruct, a dataset
348     compiled from 13 math datasets that include intermediate rationales. MAmmoTH uses a combi-
349     nation of chain-of-thought (CoT) and program-of-thought (PoT) rationales. While they evaluate
350     models using both approaches, we focus on comparing results where CoT is used for a fairer
351     comparison.
352   • MetaMath (Yu et al., 2024): This approach involves bootstrapping mathematical questions by
353
        rewriting them from multiple perspectives, creating a new dataset called MetaMathQA. The
        LLaMA-2 models are then fine-tuned on MetaMathQA to produce the MetaMath models.
354

356
      • Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): This method involves fine-tuning the models using the training

358   • Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning (RFT) (Yuan et al., 2023): This approach generates and col-
359     lects correct reasoning paths as augmented data for fine-tuning.

531
      In this paper, we advance the mathematical problem-solving capabilities of open-source LLMs by
532
      introducing a two-stage method for dataset augmentation. The first stage, Learning from Persona Di-
533   versification, involves generating detailed CoT solutions and implementing a novel persona-driven
534   approach for rewriting questions. The second stage, Learning from Reflection, focuses on incorpo-
535   rating reflection to fully exploit more challenging and valuable questions. By applying this method
536   to the MATH and GSM8K datasets, we developed PersonaMathQA, a high-quality, diverse dataset
537   comprising only 70.3K samples. Despite its smaller size compared to baseline methods, the Per-
538   sonaMath models trained on PersonaMathQA have achieved state-of-the-art performance on both
539   datasets. Our approach demonstrates the significant potential of persona-driven methods for dataset
      enhancement and provides valuable insights for future research in this field.
