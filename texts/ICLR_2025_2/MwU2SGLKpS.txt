                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has greatly improved the
010
               performance of modern Large Language Models (LLMs). The RLHF process is
011            resource-intensive and technically challenging, generally requiring a large collec-
012            tion of human preference labels over model-generated outputs. Reinforcement
013            Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) addresses this data collection challenge by
014            leveraging synthetic preferences generated by an LLM. However, recent work has
015            shown that synthetic preferences labels may not align well with human preference
016            judgments (Zeng et al., 2023). To address this, we propose a hybrid approach
017            that unifies RLHF and RLAIF methodologies. We introduce GenRM, an iter-
018            ative algorithm that trains an LLM on self-generated reasoning traces, leading
019
               to synthetic preference labels matching human preference judgments. Empiri-
               cally, we show that zero-shot LLM-based judgments under-perform compared
020
               to Bradley-Terry reward models on in-distribution tasks (between 9-36%). In
021
               contrast, GenRM achieves in-distribution accuracy comparable to Bradley-Terry
022            models, while significantly outperforming them on out-of-distribution tasks (be-
023            tween 10-45%). Moreover, GenRM surpasses the performance of using LLMs
024            as judges on both in-distribution (by 9-31%) and out-of-distribution tasks (by 2-
025            6%). Our results show that combining the strengths of RLHF and RLAIF offers a
026            promising approach for improving the quality of synthetic preference labels.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has significantly improved the perfor-
032   mance of modern Large Language Models (LLMs) (see e.g., Reid et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023).
033
      Despite its effectiveness, the RLHF process presents several challenges. First, it requires a large
      amount of human preference data to train reward models that reflect human preferences (Stiennon
034
      et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a). Second, it necessitates additional architecture and infrastructure to
035
      handle reward model training (Wang et al., 2024a; von Werra et al., 2020; Havrilla et al., 2023).
036   Third, it requires a sophisticated online optimization loop using algorithms, such as Proximal Policy
037   Optimization [PPO; Schulman et al. (2017)], to fine-tune an LLM-based policy to align with the
038   reward model (Zheng et al., 2023c).
039
      To address the challenge of collecting large-scale human preference data, synthetic preference data
040
      has emerged as a promising alternative. For example, Bai et al. (2022b) introduced Reinforce-
041
      ment Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF). Instead of relying on human users for feedback, their
042   method utilizes an LLM guided by a predefined set of principles—referred to as a “constitution”—
043   to generate and select model outputs that are helpful and harmless (Askell et al., 2021). Employ-
044   ing AI-generated preference labels has demonstrated meaningful Pareto improvements in balancing
045   helpfulness and harmlessness in assistant responses (Bai et al., 2022b; Kundu et al., 2023).
046
      Direct alignment algorithms, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023)
047
      and Implicit Preference Optimization (IPO) (Azar et al., 2023), were developed to address the chal-
048   lenges of reward model training and online optimization. These works demonstrated that the reward
049   model and the optimal policy can be mathematically interchanged, allowing the policy to be trained
050   directly from preference data in an entirely offline manner, significantly simplifying the RLHF
051   pipeline. Benchmark evaluations (Lambert et al., 2024) have shown that DPO-based approaches
052   are competitive with traditional reward models based on the Bradley-Terry algorithm. However,
053   recent empirical evidence suggests that purely offline methods may underperform compared to on-
      line approaches in both reward model-based reinforcement learning (Xu et al., 2024b;a) and in the

      In this work, we identify two key limitations in current alignment approaches: (1) Explicitly param-
080   eterized reward models, while effective and accurate for in-distribution tasks, struggle with robust-
081   ness and generalization to out-of-distribution (OOD) data. (2) RLAIF approaches, such as utilizing
082   an LLM-as-a-judge, offer a more robust alternative but may not always align well with actual user
083   preferences when acting as the sole evaluator. To address these limitations, we propose a unified
084   framework for RLHF and RLAIF. Our approach begins with a strong pre-trained LLM, which we
085   employ as an evaluator. Using a dataset of user preferences, we adopt a STaR-like methodology
086   (Zelikman et al., 2022) to align the LLM with user choices, effectively training it to function as a re-
087   ward model. We demonstrate empirically that this fine-tuned judge model matches Bradley-Terry
088
      reward models for in-distribution prompts while significantly improving generalization on OOD
      prompts. Additionally, it outperforms the base LLM on both in-distribution and OOD scenarios.

      2.1     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
099
100   The RLHF pipeline consists of three stages designed to align an LLM with human preferences: (1)
101   Supervised finetuning (SFT); (2) Reward Modeling; and (3) Reinforcement Learning (RL).
102
103   2.1.1    S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING (SFT)
104
105   In the first stage, an LLM is trained to follow instructions using a dataset of prompts x and responses
106   y using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) over the next-token predictions. The resulting model
107   is referred to as πSFT (y | x), where both the prompt and response strings are treated as single
      variables. This model is used as a base for the next stages.

143   2.2     S ELF -TAUGHT R EASONER
144
      The Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR) method introduces an iterative bootstrapping approach designed
145
      to improve the reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Zelikman et al., 2022). STaR focuses on training
146
      models to generate and refine rationales, particularly for tasks requiring complex reasoning in a
147   reinforcement learning-based manner. We outline the main points of the approach below.
148
149
      2.2.1    R ATIONALE G ENERATION B OOTSTRAPPING
150
151   In our formulation we assume we have access to a dataset D = {x(i) , y (i) }N     i=1 of questions x that
152   require strong reasoning and the corresponding answers y. Notice that we do not require access to
153   strong or ground-truth rationales for these problems. We begin by prompting a model ŷ (i) , r̂(i) ∼
154   π(y, r|x(i) ) to provide CoT rationale r̂(i) and final answer ŷ (i) . We then filter the generated data,
155   keeping only rationales leading to a correct final answer (i.e. ŷ (i) = y (i) ) to generate a dataset of
156   questions, (bootstrapped) rationales and answers DSTaR = {x(i) , r̂(i) , y (i) }Ni=1 . DSTaR is then used
157   to train a model with the standard supervised fine-tuning objective:
158
159                             LSTaR (πϕ ) = −E(x,r̂,y)∼DSTaR [− log πϕ (y, r̂|x)] .                      (4)
160
161   The above procedure is repeated over several iterations and has since been adopted in various related
      works (e.g., Hosseini et al., 2024; Andukuri et al., 2024; Fränken et al., 2024; Zelikman et al., 2024).

      2.2.2    P OST-R ATIONALIZATION
163
164   One limitation of bootstrapping rationale generation is that the model cannot improve on examples it
165   initially fails to solve. To address this issue, STaR introduces rationalization, a backward reasoning
166   process. For prompts where the model generates an incorrect rationale and answer, the correct
167   answer is provided to the model as a hint. The model then generates a new rationale based on
168   the correct answer, reasoning backward to generate a “post-rationale”. In technical terms there
169   is a rationalization model q which generates rationales r̂(i) ∼ q(r|x(i) , y (i) ) to justify the final
170
      answer. This synthetic data is in turn used in the STaR objective in Eq. 4. We will also use this
      approach to evaluate the effect of the quality of the bootstrapped reasoning chains, using samples
171
      from rationalization models q with different capabilities.

      2.3     RLAIF AND LLM-A S -A-J UDGE
175
      Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) presents an alternative approach to the stan-
176   dard RLHF pipeline. Bai et al. (2022b) demonstrate the efficacy of RLAIF in training helpful and
177   harmless models without relying on human feedback labels for harmlessness assessment. Their
178   work shows that as language model capabilities improve, AI identification of harms increases sig-
179   nificantly, particularly when leveraging chain-of-thought reasoning. Notably, they demonstrate that
180   utilizing self-supervised preference labels for reinforcement learning can yield improvements in
181   model behavior that are competitive with or surpass those achieved using human feedback for harm-
182   lessness evaluation. Zheng et al. (2023a) introduce the LLM-as-a-Judge method, further extending
183   the RLAIF paradigm. They demonstrate that strong language models, even without explicit training
184
      for evaluation tasks, can provide judgments that exhibit agreement with human preferences. Their
      study finds that LLMs can achieve over 80% agreement with human preferences, a level compa-
185
      rable to inter-expert agreement. This finding establishes a foundation for developing LLM-based
186
      evaluation frameworks.

222   preference over the answers. We consider two variants of our approach:
223

225            directly providing the answer token probabilities for each response Iˆ ∼ pϕ (I|x, y1 , y2 ).

227            of-Thought reasoning I,ˆ r̂ ∼ pϕ (I, r|x, y1 , y2 ) before providing the final answer indicator
228            token.

478   6     R ELATED W ORK
479
480   The concept of using language models for providing feedback, also known as constitutional AI or
481   RLAIF Bai et al. (2022b) has gained significant traction in recent years. Zheng et al. (2023b) fur-
482   ther popularized the paradigm of LLM evaluation (”LLM-as-a-judge”), demonstrating that strong
483   language models can effectively perform judgments with chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning that ap-
484   proximate human evaluation. Building on this, Kim et al. (2024) proposed Prometheus, employing
485   supervised fine-tuning from a powerful model to provide CoT reasoning and scoring, demonstrat-
      ing strong evaluation performance from a smaller open model. In the current work we show that


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      zero-shot LLM evaluations may not fully align with human feedback and significant improvements
487   in accuracy can be gained from additional fine-tuning. Moreover, in our proposed approach of com-
488   bining RLAIF with STaR-based tuning we do not require ground-truth reasoning or supervision
489   from a stronger model. Concurrently with this work, Zhang et al. (2024) presented Generative Ver-
490   ifiers, training CoT-GenRM with an SFT objective to act as a verifier for mathematical reasoning.
491   They find similiar observations to our experiments in Sections 5.2 and 5.3. However, one notable
492   exception is that unlike our approach they rely on access to full reference solutions at training time.
493   Another concurrent work in this direction is Ankner et al. (2024), which combines CoT reasoning
494   generation with Bradley-Terry reward modelling. They present empirical findings on the benefits
495
      of reasoning, similar to the ones we show in Section 5.1, although crucially, they rely on a separate
      strong model to provide rationales and require the Bradley-Terry reward model hybrid architecture,
496
      while we use fully self-bootstrapped rationales in a full language modelling setup without the need
497
      for any additional architecture overhang.
498
499   A number of concurrent works have also proposed approaches for self-bootstrapping generative crit-
500   ics. In Wang et al. (2024c), the authors present an approach similar to our STaR-SFT method using
501
      data augmentation to create synthetic preference pairs. On the other hand Wu et al. (2024) instead
      uses DPO for optimizing the evaluator, but the feedback is based on additional meta-judge evalua-
502
      tor. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024b) also generates data using a number of augmentation techniques
503
      and deploys DPO training. We note that these data generation techniques are complementary to our
504   approach which focuses on using STaR-based methods to allign LLM generative reward models and
505   replace explicit reward models.

508   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
509
510   To conclude, this work introduces Generative Reward Models (GenRM) as a novel framework that
511   combines the strengths of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Reinforce-
512   ment Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) to improve preference modeling for large language mod-
513   els (LLMs). By leveraging self-generated reasoning traces and iterative training loops, GenRM can
514   fine-tune LLMs to better align with human preferences, addressing key limitations of both human
515   and AI feedback mechanisms. The GenRM approach demonstrates that integrating chain-of-thought
516   reasoning within preference modeling can significantly improve both in-distribution and out-of-
517
      distribution performance compared to baselines. The Chain-of-Thought (CoT) GenRM variant aug-
      ments preference judgment tasks with intermediate reasoning traces, boosting model performance
518
      by encouraging more logical, step-wise decision-making. This leads to better generalization on com-
519
      plex tasks such as reasoning and safety-related scenarios. Empirical results show that GenRM and
520   its variants maintain competitive in-distribution accuracy while outperforming traditional methods
521   on out-of-distribution tasks. Notably, STaR-DPO models, which rely on reasoning-based preference
522   optimization, demonstrate superior robustness and performance across benchmarks. In summary,
523   Generative Reward Models present a significant advancement in combining human and AI feedback
524   to enhance the quality of synthetic preference labels. It improves scalability, out-of-distribution gen-
525   eralization, and model performance. Future exploration into online optimization, robust reasoning,
526   and adaptation to multimodal tasks will be key to realizing the full potential of this framework in
527   practical deployments.
