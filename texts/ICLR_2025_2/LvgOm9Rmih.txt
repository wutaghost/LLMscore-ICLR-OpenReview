036                                                                                          A BSTRACT
037
038
                 Grounding natural language in physical 3D environments is essential for the ad-
039
                 vancement of embodied artificial intelligence. Current datasets and models for 3D
040              visual grounding predominantly focus on identifying and localizing objects from
041              static, object-centric descriptions. These approaches do not adequately address
042              the dynamic and sequential nature of task-oriented grounding necessary for practi-
043              cal applications. In this work, we propose a new task: Task-oriented Sequential
044              Grounding in 3D scenes, wherein an agent must follow detailed step-by-step in-
045              structions to complete daily activities by locating a sequence of target objects in
046              indoor scenes. To facilitate this task, we introduce SG3D, a large-scale dataset
047              containing 22,346 tasks with 112,236 steps across 4,895 real-world 3D scenes. The
048
                 dataset is constructed using a combination of RGB-D scans from various 3D scene
                 datasets and an automated task generation pipeline, followed by human verification
049
                 for quality assurance. We adapted three state-of-the-art 3D visual grounding mod-
050
                 els to the sequential grounding task and evaluated their performance on SG3D. Our
051              results reveal that while these models perform well on traditional benchmarks, they
052              face significant challenges with task-oriented sequential grounding, underscoring
053              the need for further research in this area.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Grounding natural language in the physical 3D world is crucial for advancing embodied artificial
058   intelligence (Embodied AI) (Duan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a), where robots must follow
059   human instructions to complete complex tasks. Recent years have witnessed the collection of various
060   datasets (Jia et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2020; Achlioptas et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang
061
      et al., 2023a; Kato et al., 2023) aimed at training and testing robust visual grounding models in
      3D scenes (Zhu et al., 2023; 2024; Chen et al., 2022b; Guo et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2022). While
062
      these datasets have driven progress in 3D visual grounding, they largely borrow practices from
063
      2D visual grounding, concentrating on identifying and localizing objects based on object-centric
064   descriptions (Chen et al., 2020; Achlioptas et al., 2020). Such descriptions distinguish the target
065   object from other objects by detailing its attributes and spatial relationships. However, this object-
066   centric style may be insufficient for the embodied agent’s application scenarios, where the language
067   used to interact with agents often involves task assignments rather than mere object identification,
068   as exemplified in SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022) and SayPlan (Rana et al., 2023). Thus, a significant
069   yet overlooked gap exists between existing 3D visual grounding approaches and the task-oriented
070   language demands of embodied agents. This disparity is highlighted in Fig. 2, which compares
071   object-centric and task-driven visual grounding in 3D scenes.
072   To close this gap, we propose a new task: Task-oriented Sequential Grounding in 3D scenes. In
073   this task, an agent is asked to accomplish a daily activity with a detailed plan in an indoor scene, by
074   sequentially localizing one target object for each step. To solve this task, an agent must understand
075   each step in the context of the whole plan to identify the target object, since a single step alone can be
076   insufficient to distinguish the target from other objects of the same category.
077   To address this challenge, we constructed a large-scale dataset named SG3D. We compiled a set
078   of RGB-D scans of realistic indoor scenes sourced from various 3D scene datasets, including
079   ScanNet (Rozenberszki et al., 2022), ARKitScenes (Baruch et al., 2021), 3RScan (Wald et al., 2019),
080   etc. These scenes encompass a variety of room types, such as bedrooms, kitchens, offices, bathrooms,
081   and living rooms. We represent these scenes using 3D scene graphs (Armeni et al., 2019; Wald et al.,
082   2020) derived from SceneVerse (Jia et al., 2024), which describe the objects’ categories, attributes,
083   and spatial relations within the scenes.
084   We further designed an automated generation pipeline that utilizes these scene graphs and GPT-
085   4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to create diverse, high-quality daily tasks. Each task comprises a high-level
086   description and a detailed plan, with the target object annotated for each step. To ensure the validity of
087   the generated tasks, we conducted a human verification process to check if the tasks were appropriate
088   for the scenes, if the plans were sufficient to accomplish the tasks, and if the target objects were
089   correctly identified for each step. Invalid tasks were either filtered out or manually refined. Ultimately,
090
      the proposed SG3D includes 22,346 tasks with 112,236 steps across 4,895 real-world 3D scenes, as
      exemplified in Fig. 1. Tab. 1 compares SG3D with existing 3D visual grounding benchmarks.
091
092   In our experiments, we adapted three state-of-the-art 3D visual grounding models to the sequential
093   grounding task and evaluated them on SG3D. The models included 3D-VisTA (Zhu et al., 2023),
094   PQ3D (Zhu et al., 2024), and LEO (Huang et al., 2023a). The results indicate that although these
095   models excel on previous benchmarks, they struggle with the more complex and realistic grounding
096
      presented in the SG3D benchmark. This highlights the need for further research and development to
      improve performance in task-oriented sequential grounding scenarios for Embodied AI.
097
098   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
099
100   • We proposed a new task, Task-oriented Sequential Grounding in 3D scenes, to address the gap
101     between object-centric and task-driven grounding required for practical Embodied AI applications.
102
      • We constructed a large-scale dataset for this novel task, SG3D, which contains 22,346 tasks with
103
        112,236 steps across 4,895 real-world 3D scenes.
104
105   • We adapted three state-of-the-art 3D visual grounding models (3D-VisTA, PQ3D, and LEO) to
106     the sequential grounding task and evaluated them on SG3D. Experimental results indicate that
107     these models struggle with task-oriented sequential grounding, highlighting the need for further
        advancements in this area.

140
      the 3D physical world (Zhu et al., 2023; 2024; Kerr et al., 2023), enabling embodied agents to
      comprehend their environment and communicate with humans effectively (Zhu et al., 2023; Rana
141
      et al., 2023). This emerging domain has established benchmarks for various tasks, such as visual
142
      grounding (Chen et al., 2020; Achlioptas et al., 2020; Abdelreheem et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023;
143   Kato et al., 2023), question answering (Azuma et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023), and
144   dense captioning (Chen et al., 2021). Beside many methods tackling single tasks (Guo et al., 2023;
145   Wu et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2022; Jain et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022b), unified
146   models (Zhu et al., 2023; 2024; Chen et al., 2023c) and open-vocabulary approaches (Peng et al.,
147   2023; Ding et al., 2023; Takmaz et al., 2023) have gained traction in recent literature. However,
148   existing 3D visual grounding benchmarks primarily address object-centric, single-step grounding
149   tasks, whereas realistic grounding sentences are typically driven by task-related context (Deng et al.,
150   2024). In contrast to previous work, SG3D provides more natural and informative language and
151
      introduces diverse contextual information, as shown in Fig. 2.

      3.1       P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
201
      The problem of sequential grounding involves determining the relevance of objects in a given task.
202   Specifically, given a 3D scene S and a task T = (t, {a1 , ..., an }) where t is a high-level task
203   description and a1 , ...an are detailed steps of the task plan, a model is required to predict a sequence
204   of objects O = {o1 , ..., on }, i.e., the model needs to learn a mapping f : (S, T ) → O. Compared to
205   prior work, the challenge in our task lies in consistently grounding objects across sequential steps of
206   a task plan.
207

      incorporating scenes from ScanNet, ARKitScenes, HM3D, 3RScan, and MultiScan. In total, we

231   Scene Graphs To provide GPT-4 with rich scene information, we process each scene into a semantic
232   scene graph transformed from SceneVerse assets, which captures the categories, attributes, and spatial
233   relations of objects within the scene. Each node in the graph represents a 3D object instance, while
234   each edge represents a spatial relationship between nodes, such as “near”, “below”, or “embedded”.
235   We further enhance these scene graphs by adding object captions provided in SceneVerse.
236
      Task Generation Using the 3D scene graph, we prompt GPT-4 (gpt-4-turbo-2024-04-09) to generate
237   diverse tasks. For each scene, we ask GPT-4 to create five distinct tasks. Each task comprises
238   a general description and several steps, with each step requiring the agent to focus on a specific
239   target object, such as navigating toward or interacting with it. To ensure variety and coherence, we
240   meticulously crafted the prompts and provided diverse in-context examples from multiple scene types.
241   This approach guarantees that the generated tasks are both robust and varied. After generation, we
242   remove any outputs with formatting errors and rigorously verify that all assigned targets are present
243   in the corresponding scenes. Moreover, we observed that tasks exceeding ten steps tend to introduce
244   hallucinated objects or problematic steps, which can negatively impact dataset quality. As a result,
245
      we discard any tasks containing more than ten steps. The detailed prompt used for GPT-4 can be
      found under Appendix A.1.
246
247   Human Verification We manually verify the evaluation set data to ensure quality. Given the 3D
248   scene mesh and the task generated by GPT-4, annotators apply the following rules to judge each
249   step’s correctness:
250
      1. If the step is unfeasible or unrelated to the task description, it is marked as incorrect.
251
252
      2. If there is a missing step between step k and step k + 1, step k + 1 is judged as incorrect.
253   3. When the step’s description is insufficient to identify the target object, the step is considered correct
254
         if the target object can still be identified through context; otherwise, it is marked as incorrect.
255
      Tasks with a single incorrect step are manually revised, while tasks containing more than one incorrect
256   step are discarded. This human verification process ensures that the generated tasks are reasonable
257   and the action steps are feasible. A screenshot of the interface for verification is provided under
258   Appendix A.1.

      4.1    A RCHITECTURES
315
      We follow ReferIt3D (Achlioptas et al., 2020) to use ground-truth object masks. To ensure a fair
316   comparison, we employ the point cloud as the scene representation and the same PointNet++ (Qi
317   et al., 2017) encoder to extract scene features for all three 3D-VL models.
318
319   Dual-stream model. In the dual stream model, we build upon the 3D-VisTA (Zhu et al., 2023)
320   baseline. In 3D-VisTA, the model employs a spatial transformer to process 3D object representations
321   and extracts text features using BERT (Devlin et al., 2018). These object and text tokens are
322   then combined and fed into a unified transformer architecture to predict the target object. In our
323   experiments, we concatenate the task description t with detailed plans up to step i, {a1 , ..., ai }, to
      serve as the textual input for predicting the target object oi at the current step.

      Query-based model. Unlike the dual stream model, the query-based model employs a generalized

343   3D LLM. The powerful reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models are highly advantageous
344   for our task. We have adapted the recent 3D LLM LEO (Huang et al., 2023a) to suit our needs.
345   In addition to predicting actions for each step, our model also predicts a special grounding token,
346   [GRD]. This token is concatenated with object tokens and passed to the same grounding head
347   used in 3D-VisTA and PQ3D to predict the target object, enabling integrated reasoning about both
348
      the previous and current step instructions. Unlike dual-stream and query-based models, which are
      constrained by their architectures and require separate forward passes for each action step, 3D LLM
349
      LEO concatenates t and {a1 , ..., an } to predict target objects for all steps sequentially by using
350
      multiple [GRD] tokens in a single forward pass.
351
352
      GPT-4 with an object labeler. In addition to the three 3D-VL models, we examine the applicability
353   of GPT-4 for this task by integrating it with a PointNet++ (Qi et al., 2017) classifier, pre-trained on
354   ScanNet, to predict semantic labels (categories) for objects. GPT-4 receives scene information in
355   JSON format, which includes each object’s ID, predicted category, center position, and size, along
356   with the task description and detailed steps, tasked with generating a list of object IDs. Fig. A5 shows
357   the specific prompt used here.

373   5.1       S ETTINGS
374
375   Training Details We conduct training for all three 3D-VL model across all available datasets for
376   50 epochs. For optimization, we employ the AdamW optimizer, setting the learning rate at 1e-4, with
377   β1 configured to 0.9 and β2 to 0.999. Additionally, we apply a weight decay of 0.05. Specifically, for
      the PQ3D and 3D-VisTA models, we utilize a batch size of 32. For the LEO model, we reduce the


                                                                               7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      batch size to 16 due to GPU memory limit. Furthermore, we use LoRA tuning (Hu et al., 2021) for
379   the parameters of the LLM in LEO with a rank setting of 16.
380
381   Evaluation Metrics We assess the grounding performance of all models using two key metrics: task
382   accuracy (t-acc) and step accuracy (s-acc). Task accuracy refers to the average grounding accuracy
383   over the total number of tasks t. A sample is considered correct if the grounded objects are accurately
384   identified for all steps within a task. Conversely, step accuracy is calculated by averaging the accuracy
385   across all individual steps a. Task accuracy evaluates the model’s ability to consistently interpret and
386   respond accurately across a sequence of text prompts. On the other hand, step accuracy focuses on
387   the model’s effectiveness at each individual step.
388
389   5.2   Q UANTITATIVE R ESULTS & A NALYSIS
390
      1. Previous 3D Vision-Language models, such as dual-stream model 3D-VisTA and query-based
391
      model PQ3D, struggle to transfer to the sequential grounding task without fine-tuning. As
392
      shown in Tab. 3, in the zero-shot setting, these models achieve low step accuracies ranging from
393   22.8% to 34.6% and task accuracies ranging from 0.0% to 10.3% across all datasets. This indicates
394   that the models’ pre-training on non-sequential tasks is insufficient for handling the complexities
395   inherent in sequential grounding, highlighting the need for task-specific fine-tuning.
396
      2. Fine-tuning greatly enhances performance but low task accuracy scores (< 40%) indicate
397
      that consistent sequential grounding remains a challenge. 3D-VisTA’s t-acc increases from 8.3%
398
      to 30.6%, while PQ3D’s t-acc improves from 7.8% to 26.8%. The 3D LLM model LEO achieves
399   the best performance after fine-tuning, with a s-acc of 62.8% and a t-acc of 34.1%. Despite these
400   improvements, all models’ t-acc scores remain below 40%, indicating that current models still struggle
401   to achieve consistent sequential grounding. This limitation highlights the need for further research
402   and model design to effectively address the challenges posed by sequential grounding tasks.
403
      3. The 3D LLM model, LEO, consistently outperforms the other models across all datasets, par-
404
      ticularly in terms of task accuracy. LEO achieves the highest task accuracies 34.1%, compared to
405   3D-VisTA 30.6% and PQ3D 26.8%. This advantage can be attributed to LEO’s 3D LLM architecture,
406   which effectively captures and reasons about sequential dependencies in grounding tasks. Although
407   LEO also enhances step accuracy, the improvement is less substantial compared to the significant
408   gains observed in task accuracy.
409
      4. The combination of GPT-4 and 3D object classifier is insufficient for addressing the sequential
410
      grounding task. Despite GPT-4’s robust reasoning and generalization capabilities, its performance—
411   recording a t-acc of 7.6% and a s-acc of 27.3%—is significantly inferior to that of fine-tuned 3D
412   vision-language models. This shortfall can be attributed to classification inaccuracies and the loss of
413   information when translating the scene into semantic labels and positions. These results indicate that
414   the effectiveness of large language models in this problem is heavily influenced by the alignment
415   between 3D vision modality and text modality, making 3D-VL models the more suitable approach.
416
417   5.3   A BLATION S TUDY
418
419   Effect of offering contextual information. To investigate the impact of contextual information,
420   we eliminate multi-step action context during both the training and inference phases, providing
421
      only the task description and current action step. The experimental results illustrated in Fig. 7
      reveal a significant decline in task accuracy upon the removal of contextual information for both
422
      LEO and 3D-VisTA. Specifically, LEO exhibits an average t-acc drop of 3.4%, while 3D-VisTA
423
      demonstrates an even more pronounced decline of 5.0%. This suggests that the models have, to a
424   certain extent, learned to leverage contextual information during the grounding process. In contrast,
425   PQ3D experiences a more modest performance reduction, with an average t-acc decrease of only
426   0.8%. This limited decline can be attributed to PQ3D’s reliance on a CLIP text encoder, which
427   struggles to interpret lengthy sentences (Zhang et al., 2024), thereby leading to overfitting on shorter,
428   single-step instructions.

517   6       C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we introduce the task of Task-oriented Sequential Grounding in 3D scenes and present
520   SG3D, a large-scale dataset designed to facilitate research in this area. Evaluations of state-of-the-art
521   3D visual grounding models on SG3D benchmark reveal the substantial challenges in adapting these
522
      models to sequential grounding tasks. These results emphasize the necessity for further research and
      model development. We encourage the community to move beyond traditional 3D visual grounding
523
      towards more practical, task-oriented applications, paving the way for more advanced and capable
524
      embodied agents.

527   7       D ISCUSSIONS
528
529   Rationale for limiting to one target object per step The primary consideration for this restriction
530   is that most mobile manipulators (e.g., the one used in SayCan (Ahn et al., 2022)) are single-arm and
531
      can manipulate only one object at a time. This design aligns with current practical constraints and
      facilitates the adaptation of 3D visual grounding models to real-world robotic tasks. Nevertheless,
532
      our data generation pipeline is flexible and can be easily adapted to support multi-target actions by
533
      adjusting the GPT-4 prompt, as detailed in Fig. A1.
534
535
      Handling steps that do not appear to involve a target object Some steps, like “Rub your hands”
536   (task 3’s step 4 in Fig. 9), involving the agent itself rather than a specific object in the scene, we
537   consider the target object from the previous step as the reference. This implies that no positional
538   change is required, which is a reasonable assumption in the navigation setting. These steps reflect
539   realistic interactions and are part of the task’s natural sequence, so we keep them in our dataset.

950   A.6    P LANNING ABILITY OF LEO
951
952   In this additional experiment, we evaluate the planning ability of LEO fine-tuned on SG3D. Given
953   a task description t, LEO is required to generate both action steps {a1 , .., an } and target objects
954   {o1 , ..., on }. Since action steps can be rearranged into various topological orders, we do not employ
955
      exact matches to assess the similarity between the predicted and ground truth plans. Instead, we
      utilize metrics from OpenEQA (Majumdar et al., 2024), which leverage GPT-4 to score the model’s
956
      output based on ground truth. A score of 1 indicates no match, while a score of 5 represents a perfect
957
      match. In our experiments, the GPT score on ScanNet is 2.1 ± 1.0, suggesting considerable room
958   for improvement. The prompts used for score computation are detailed in Fig. A6.
