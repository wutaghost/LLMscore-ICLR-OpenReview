011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Adaptive networks today rely on overparameterized fixed topologies that cannot
014            break through the statistical conflicts they encounter in the data they are exposed
015            to, and are prone to ”catastrophic forgetting” as the network attempts to reuse the
016            existing structures to learn new task. We propose a structural adaptation method,
017            DIRAD, that can complexify as needed and in a directed manner without being
018
               limited by statistical conflicts within a dataset. We then extend this method and
               present the PREVAL framework, designed to prevent ”catastrophic forgetting” in
019
               continual learning by detection of new data and assigning encountered data to
020
               suitable models adapted to process them, without needing task labels anywhere in
021            the workflow. We show the reliability of the DIRAD in growing a network with
022            high performance and orders-of-magnitude simpler than fixed topology networks;
023            and demonstrate the proof-of-concept operation of PREVAL, in which contin-
024            ual adaptation to new tasks is observed while being able to detect and discern
025            previously-encountered tasks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Past decade has shown that complex networks should be at the core of any AI system that needs to
031   be of robust use in any task of reasonable complexity. It has, however, been unfortunate that over
032   the same period, the field of machine learning (ML) has been stuck in the twin limiting paradigms
033   of static topologies and statistical fine-tuning, attempting to make up for the limitations of both of
034   these by using brute force, in form of overparameterization and computational requirements accom-
035   panying it. Limitations imposed by these paradigms also prevent solving the crucial problem of
036   ”catastrophic forgetting” in continual learning, which becomes especially important in systems that
037   need to learn continually without explicit storage and replay of past data. In this work, we first
038   propose a novel method of structural adaptation, operating with gradient descent, with a strong bias
039
      towards minimal network complexity (i.e. size). Our framework, first of its kind to the best of our
      knowledge, is neither limited by statistical conflicts between samples (a term, detailed in the text, we
040
      use to refer to conflicting requirements within a dataset that result in net zero adaptive pressure on
041
      parameters, despite nonzero change requirements for any individual sample) nor reliant on excess
042   network complexity to find a solution with strong guarantees. We apply this method to construct a
043   system to prevent ”catastrophic forgetting” by recognizing unexpected data, constructing new com-
044   ponents (models) to process such new data without affecting past responses, and then selecting the
045   suitable one over an existing array of such models when data from a past task is provided - a uni-
046   fied continual learning framework that does not require task labels or switching signals anywhere,
047   neither during adaptation nor deployment; all while not relying on storage and replay of past sam-
048   ples. Finally, we provide positive results on both of these frameworks in our initial experiments on
049   MNIST and FashionMNIST datasets.
050
051   Note on terminology The mechanisms we propose in this paper are not biologically plausible at
052   neuronal level, and sharing a nonlinear weighted-sum paradigm is not enough to justify an analogy
053   with neural systems given the additional mechanisms we introduce. To avoid implying such an
      analogy and to accurately describe these mechanisms, we use the terms node and edge instead of

058   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
059
060   Structural adaptation Structural adaptation in NNs hasn’t gained as much attention as other as-
061   pects of this technology, as many of these methods involve an additional step and often don’t provide
062   a significant benefit compared to the added network complexity (i.e. number of nodes/edges). One
063
      subfield in literature, called ”neural architecture search,” focuses on optimizing the architecture it-
      self explicitly (Liu et al. (2018); Shin et al. (2018); Baker et al. (2016); Stanley et al. (2019); Liu
064
      et al. (2017); Miikkulainen et al. (2019)). Some other works view ”structural adaptation” as starting
065
      from scratch or growth, sometimes referred to as Artificial Embryogenesis (Kowaliw et al. (2014)),
066   often using evolutionary algorithms. Recent methods for expanding neural networks, similar to our
067   design choices, can also be found in the literature (Dai et al. (2019); Evci et al. (2022); Mitchell et al.
068   (2023)). Our approach aligns more with the group that designs developmental methods rather than
069   relying on external loops or added pressures for architecture optimization. We use structural adapta-
070   tion not for topology optimization but to drive further response adaptation. However, our approach
071   differs from this group in that we prioritize minimal network complexity and address statistical
072   conflicts while operating outside the conventional NN paradigm.
073
074   Continual adaptation A significant issue with continual learning or adaptation is ”catastrophic
075   forgetting” or, as we call it, ”destructive adaptation”1 (DA) - when the new instances differ signifi-
076   cantly from previously observed examples, they cause the new information to overwrite previously
077
      learned knowledge in the network, a problem until today remains without a reliable solution (Hadsell
      et al. (2020); Parisi et al. (2019)). Systems with fixed capacity cannot deal with the problem ade-
078
      quately: An existing capacity is always used completely for the previous tasks (since information
079
      is engrained in a neural network in a distributed manner), and existing information will be eventu-
080   ally (often immediately) lost as new tasks differing significantly from the previous ones arrive. The
081   methods that work by the addition of capacity, on the other hand, cannot autonomously decide when
082   to add capacity, how to assign different added components to different tasks, and how to choose
083   among components when presented with one of the past tasks (e.g. in (Rusu et al. (2016)) it must
084   be externally signalled to the system both when a new task is observed, and for recall, it must be
085   specified which of the past tasks is being seen) - the same limitation also applies to methods that ex-
086   plicitly store information about the solutions of past tasks as well (e.g. (Kirkpatrick et al. (2017))).
087   Some extensions of such methods that partially try to address these questions require task labels
088
      during adaptation phase and have no mechanism that can detect new tasks (e.g. (Jacobson et al.
      (2022))), hence cannot constitute systems with autonomous continual adaptation capability. Other
089
      methods claim to operate without such ”task boundaries”, yet they all rely on explicitly storing and
090
      then replaying past data (Aljundi et al. (2019), Table 1 of Buzzega et al. (2020)) - this is infeasible in
091   many real-world scenarios due to the lifelong memory requirements of the systems, confidentiality
092   or data protection considerations; and obviously do away with one of the primary motivators for
093   continual learning. We are unaware of a framework proposed against DA that does not store and
094   replay of past data, operate without external signalling of task change, is able to recognize a new
095   task, and add new system components as needed - in other words, a full continual learning setup
096   without any simplifying assumptions. Designing such a method is what we do in this work.
097
098   Novelty/anomaly detection Methods of novelty/anomaly detection are those that are interested in
099   classifying certain encounters as novel or not. Detailed survey of such methods is beyond our scope,
100   interested readers can find a review in (Pimentel et al. (2014)). The field itself is not of primary
101   interest to us except as a sub-goal, since it concerns itself with systems designed to classify samples
102
      as novel or not, while we want to both quantify and localize this novelty, doing that within a system
      that is actually used for the performation of a particular task. Furthermore, as methods susceptible
103
      to statistical conflicts, they are not suitable for our purposes (as will be discussed in the following
104
      sections).

      In this section, we describe our structural adaptation and network growth mechanism. The mech-
111   anisms described here are not directly aimed at the prevention of destructive adaptation (DA), but
112   are general adaptive processes that can be used in any ML problem. Throughout the section, we
113   discuss a single task. Here we only provide a summary of the method and its core points. The full
114   theoretical development, with justifications of design choices and practical considerations, can be
115   found in the Appendix. To illustrate the process in action, we provide an example path of adaptation
116   in Figure 1, to which we refer throughout our narrative below.
117   We always assume a network starting with only the input and output nodes, and no hidden nodes
118   (neither input nor output - Figure 1a) - however the mechanisms we designed can operate locally
119   within networks of arbitrary standing complexity. Our aim is to develop a network that can com-
120   plexify as needed, but not more (prioritizing parameter adaptation where possible); and that is not
121   limited by statistical trade-offs between different samples in a batch. We call the processes that
122   grow the network by introducing new components as generative processes (GPs), each of which is
123   neutral: No node’s response is changed due to a GP; and all changes in net response occur under the
124   influence of gradients to ensure no harm to performance. Parameter adaptation within network (edge
125
      weights and node biases) are done by standard gradient descent via backpropagation algorithm.

      To limit the number of GPs executed and limit network complexity increase to when it would be
217   absolutely necessary, we can introduce a priority ordering mechanism, which chooses a limited
218   number of GPs among all that are possible at a particular instant, preferring some over the others.
219   The choice of such a priority ordering scheme can be made in many ways depending on designer

222   pathway of an output node, if and only if all the components on this pathway have their immediate
223   AP exhausted. Detailed description of this algorithm can be found in the Appendix.
224   We refer to the method of structural adaptation in this section as DIRAD (from directed adaptation).

227   4       N OVELTY D ETECTION VIA P REDICTION VALIDATIONS
228
229   Our aim now is to create a network that can detect whether the samples observed currently by
230   the network are actually among the same type that it had adapted to or if they are new, causing
231   unexpected responses by the network. For that, we present the PREVAL (from prediction validation)
232   method that can predict the states of the nodes in the network using information from higher levels of
233   computation, and then uses mismatches in these predictions to detect novel encounters, and finally
234   use this information to realize continual adaptation in a system with multiple models.2 Below, we
235
      describe of components of PREVAL.
236
237   L0 and L1 networks In a supervised learning task, let L0 network be our task network. Suppose
238   that upon accomplishment of a designer-specified condition (e.g. errors no longer decreasing), L0
239   is stabilized - i.e. no more parameter updates or generative processes, the response of every node to
240   a given input is fixed. In PREVAL framework, we create a new network, a directed acyclic graph
241
      (DAG) within itself, the L1 network, following the stabilization of L0. Target nodes of L1 are all the
      nodes (including inputs) in L0 except output nodes, and its task is to predict the states of these target
242
      nodes as computed by L0 in response to a given input. L1 can use as inputs any of the nodes in L0,
243
      potentially including output nodes, as long as the following condition is not violated: For any node
244   n0 ∈ L0, no node n1 ∈ L0 can have a path to n0 via L1 if it also has a path to n0 via L0 - making
245   sure that only the nodes at a higher level of computation predict the states of those in the lower levels
246   of computation, i.e. abstract information predicts the concrete observation, not vice-versa. Hence
247   in L1, we prevent simply performing the trivial replication of the pathways in L0. Given this new
248   description of inputs, outputs, targets, and the additional constraint regarding connectivity; L1 can
249   be adapted as usual with DIRAD and stabilized in the same manner as L0.
250
251   Multiple models We define a model as any system within our framework that has a particular
252   response pattern to the input. In our implementation, we interpret models as distinct, unrelated
253   networks; though alternative conceptualizations, such as a subset of connections or subnetworks
254   expressed within one network, or networks that are duplicated and differentiated from one another,
255   are also possible and everything in this section applies to them as well.3 In our conceptualization,
256   the system consists of a dynamic number of models. Below, we present a method using the outputs
257   of L1 networks that is able to (i) detect new tasks that show deviation from the structure of previous
258
      data, on which existing models were adapted, and create new models for these new tasks; and (ii)
      in the observation of new data, can choose among the existing models the one that is best-matching
259
      for processing that data, without needing to observe the target outputs. Irrespective of the definition
260
      of a ”model”, when a system can create new models to process automatically-detected new data
261   belonging to different tasks, one can said to have prevented DA in the system since there is no more
262   overwriting or loss of information. If the system can, furthermore, assign newly-encountered data
263   to proper model among the set of models (for different tasks) it has available, then we can say that it
264   is able to retrieve this information which was protected, and hence (in sum) is capable of continual
265   learning.

271   tween the actual state of an input or internal node and its predicted state based on the state of the

273   (during deployment) or batch (during ongoing adaptation) is consistent with what is expected based
274   on the data that the current model was adapted to. Notice that this pertains to the conversion of a
275   continuous metric (amount of mismatch in L1 nodes) to a discrete one (model validated/invalidated
276   on a sample). Furthermore, when one is comparing multiple models in this regard (finding the model
277   that ”best matches” to the sample), there is no one-to-one correspondence between different models
278   since they will have distinct L1 networks, with different targets possibly differing even in the order
279
      of magnitude of their numbers. Hence, there is no single best way to perform this validation. Here,

355   Setup In this section, we present experimental results with DIRAD and PREVAL. We use a digit
356   classification problem with task change on MNIST and Fashion MNIST (F-MNIST)5 , where differ-
357   ent tasks are defined as different classes (digits) enabled. For each experiment run, the process is as
358   follows: Two classes are chosen randomly among the 10 classes in the dataset. We call this choice
359   of a subset of classes from the whole dataset a task. The system adapts to the task at hand, first of
360
      its L0 network and then of its L1 network, until it reaches the stabilization of L1 network (detected
      via non-decreasing errors in all target nodes). After L1 stabilization, the task changes by choosing
361
      two new digits at random that were not among the previous tasks, and the new task is adapted to in
362

      6   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSIONS
487
488
      DIRAD A notable limitation of DIRAD lies in its computational complexity, which is not en-
489   tirely mitigated by the architectural simplicity of the resulting networks. As previously mentioned,
490   datasets with higher dimensions than MNIST and Fashion MNIST exhibited a marked increase in
491   computation time. This heightened processing time is attributed to the current implementation of
492   DIRAD, which handles all network components and samples sequentially, even though both aspects
493   are well-suited to parallel processing. To scale DIRAD to higher-dimensional tasks—similar to the
494   advancements made in neural networks since 2010—a more efficient, parallelized approach is nec-
495   essary. However, such an implementation is beyond the scope of this paper and outside the expertise
496   of the authors. It is likely that a method like DIRAD/PREVAL will always remain somewhat more
497
      computationally intensive than fixed neural networks. We see this as a trade-off for the distinct ad-
      vantages these methods offer, such as unsupervised continual learning and the potential reduction in
498
      future learning processes enabled by systems with such capabilities.
499
500   Moreover, network growth methods do not yet fully benefit from hardware acceleration techniques
501   (such as GPUs) that are effective for fixed, overparameterized NNs. We see this not as an inherent
502   limitation but as a temporary and necessary challenge. Fixed and static network architectures have
503
      fundamental constraints, as detailed in the paper, and future ”AI hardware” will need to adapt to
      support growing or dynamic networks. We view methods like DIRAD as innovations that will shape
504
      the development of future hardware. In cases like this, software should not be restricted by current
505
      hardware capabilities but should instead drive advancements in hardware, especially in evolving
506   fields like AI.
507
508   PREVAL To the best of our knowledge, PREVAL is the first framework that can handle continual
509   adaptation with high accuracy & retention of past information, while doing both new task detection
510   & discernment among past tasks within a unified framework that does not require task labels any-
511   where in the flow. The degree to which this happens with PREVAL, however, is dependent upon
512   the discernability of different tasks within the system; and is currently still lower compared to what
513   would be in case of a perfect discernability. Further analysis and improvements (including those
514   pertaining to computational efficiency, as we had to train PREVAL with high learning rates for rea-
515   sonable experimental duration, which may have caused destabilizations) are required to identify how
516
      the remaining performance gaps can be closed as well.
517   We saw that using familiarity autoencoders for assignment of a newcoming sample to its task can
518   result in better task discernment than PREVAL (specifically in MNIST in our experiments) together
519   with another growing architecture like Progressive NNs. It may be possible to use a hybrid system,
520   in which PREVAL is be used mainly for new task detection (which it is shown to be able to do
521   reliably) and an autoencoder is used for assignment of samples to known tasks may use the best of
522
      both worlds and result in higher net performance of the combined system.
523   We used PREVAL with an interface in which different models were represented with different net-
524   works. This prevents destructive adaptation (DA), but does not harness the potential of transfer
525   learning (Zhuang et al. (2020)) since networks are all created from scratch. As discussed; in case of
526   need, the definition of a ”model” can be modified to allow for this. Alternatives include adding ca-
527   pacity to a shared network while selectively stabilizing the previous pathways so that past responses
528
      won’t be affected (possible without loss of expressivity potential with DIRAD), modifying existing
      components while storing alternatives in different models, and many others. There already exist
529
      methods that work via the addition of capacity to alleviate DA in continual learning scenarios (Rusu
530
      et al. (2016); Yoon et al. (2017); Terekhov et al. (2015)) - approaches like that can be applied to
531   PREVAL without changes in basic system conceptualization in either side. PREVAL can also be
532   used with methods are reliant on storing past structure (like (Kirkpatrick et al. (2017))), to give them
533   a means of detecting new tasks. PREVAL has been designed to operate as a mechanism at a level
534   above the network adaptation process. Hence, it can work in tandem with any method modifying
535   network adaptation dynamics that also aim to reduce the effect of DA, or is geared towards any other
536   purpose.

655   A.1.1     S TARTING POINT FOR DEVELOPMENT
656
657   We start with the assumption of a network with the specified input and output nodes, as they are
658   defined by the task, and no hidden nodes. From that, we want to develop a network that (1) can
659   complexify as needed, but no more than needed, to solve the task at hand; and (2) is not limited
660
      by statistical conflicts between different samples in a given batch in doing so. Note that the pro-
      cess described in this section is based on mechanisms that operate locally in individual component
661
      basis, and hence their operation does not depend on the existing network complexity (even if their
662
      outcomes do).
663
664   We call the processes that grow the network by introducing new components (nodes or edges) as
665   generative processes. In designing our generative processes, we prioritized the principle of neu-
666
      trality8 of structural modification: For all existing nodes in the network, neither their responses nor
      the adaptive signals (gradients/deltas) they receive should change by a generative process in the
667
      network - all changes should occur under the influence of gradients, to make sure that they are not
668
      detrimental to the performance. This principle corresponds to different criteria for different genera-
669   tive processes. Below, we describe the two main generative processes in our design: Edge generation
670   and edge-to-node conversion.

948   A.2.1    D ESTRUCTIVE PROCESSES
949
950   In practice, due to the strictness of removal conditions and the extremely targeted nature of gen-
951   erative processes, we almost never see a component removed from the network in DIRAD. We
952   nonetheless describe our destructive processes here for completeness purposes.
953   We remove an edge if its weight is 0 and we remove a node with a probability of 0.3 if it has no
954   remaining out-edges.11 Edges are protected against removal for 5 steps after their first creation. The
955   probabilistic nature of node removal is to prevent the immediate destruction of complete pathways
956   of interconnected nodes via recursive removal of them from their end-points, and instead to give
957   the nodes within this pathway a chance to be able to participate in the responses of other nodes by
958   acting as sources of in-edges generated by them. These two destructive processes of DIRAD are
959   both neutral.
960   We have an additional destructive process specific to PREVAL and only during L1 adaptation: At
961   time of stabilization of an L1 target node, we remove the predictive pathway of the node if it is not
962   confidently predicted. This is simply because prediction of non-CP nodes have no further use for
963   the system, and we remove them to cap the complexity increase, hence make it more scalable. The
964   fluctuations in complexity on Figure 3 are due to this mechanism and not to DIRAD’s destructive
965   processes.

1060   A.3   D ETAILED D ESCRIPTION OF P RIORITY-O RDERING A LGORITHM
1061
1062   The detailed description of our priority ordering algorithm (Algorithm 1) is as follows: We take all
1063   of the target nodes (the ultimate sources of adaptive pressures from mismatches with their targets -
1064   e.g. output nodes in a classification task) whose immediate AP is exhausted for the whole of their
1065
       response pathway (i.e. all nodes that have a path to that node and all the edges in these paths), and
       order them by decreasing total AP. As long as their immediate AP is exhausted (since a given node
1066
       elsewhere in the network may be participating in the response pathways for multiple target nodes),
1067
       we perform one and only one generative process (either an edge generation or an ENC) within the
1068   pathways for each of the target nodes. For each node, we start from the target node as our current
1069   node, and search over all its edges in the order of decreasing total AP. At an edge, if we find one
1070   whose source has nonzero total AP, we take that source node as our current node and restart the
1071   search from there. If there is no such node, we check whether the edge satisfies ENC condition. If
1072   it does, we perform ENC. If it does not, we move onto the next edge. If no edge neither satisfies
1073   ENC nor has a source that has nonzero total AP, then we return to our current node and generate an
1074   edge for it if it satisfies edge generation conditions. If it doesn’t satisfy edge generation conditions
1075   as well, we return for this node without performing a generative process (since none suitable has
1076
       been found).
1077   As mentioned in the main text, the presence of a priority ordering mechanism is not indispensable
1078   for the operation of DIRAD processes. It is likely that adaptation will proceed faster without these
1079   restrictions imposed on generative processes, at the cost of increased complexity. If, furthermore,
       a priority ordering over GPs is going to be used, the exact form it takes can change depending


                                                         20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       on the requirements of the system - our framework is very restrictive since we prioritize minimal
1081   complexity (due to the fact that we will predict the states of generated nodes after stabilization),
1082   but in an application where complexity requirements are more relaxed, one can use a less restrictive
1083   scheme.
