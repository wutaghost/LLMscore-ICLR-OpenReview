036                                                           A BSTRACT
037
038            Generative models excel at mimicking real scenes, suggesting they might inherently
039            encode important intrinsic scene properties. In this paper, we aim to explore the
040            following key questions: (1) What intrinsic knowledge do generative models like
041            GANs, Autoregressive models, and Diffusion models encode? (2) Can we estab-
042            lish a general framework to recover intrinsic representations from these models,
043
               regardless of their architecture or model type? (3) How small can the required
               learnable parameters and labeled data be to successfully recover this knowledge?
044
               (4) Is there a direct link between the quality of a generative model and the accuracy
045
               of the recovered scene intrinsics?
046
               Our findings indicate that a small Low-Rank Adaptators (LoRA) can recover
047
               intrinsic images—depth, normals, albedo and shading—across different generators
048            (Autoregressive, GANs and Diffusion) while using the same decoder head that
049            generates the image. As LoRA is lightweight, we introduce very few learnable
050            parameters (as few as 0.04% of Stable Diffusion model weights for a rank of 2),
051            and we find that as few as 250 labeled images are enough to generate intrinsic
052            images with these LoRA modules. Finally, we also show a positive correlation
053            between the generative model’s quality and the accuracy of the recovered intrinsics
               through control experiments.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
      Generative models can produce high-quality images that are almost indistinguishable from real-
      world photographs. They appear to profoundly understand the world, capturing object placement,
058
      appearance, and lighting conditions. Yet, it remains an open question how these models encode
059
      such detailed knowledge, and whether representations of scene intrinsics—such as depth, normals,
060   albedo and shading—exist within these models and can be explicitly recovered, or if these models
061   manipulate abstract representations of the world to generate these images.
062
063
      Why study intrinsic knowledge embedded in generative models? Understanding how generative
      models produce realistic outputs allows us to model the physical world better computationally,
064
      improving both image generation and interpretation across various applications. As we demonstrate
065
      in this paper, most generative models inherently encode intrinsic image representations as a byproduct
066   of training on large-scale image data, and these can be easily recovered. By retrieving this embedded
067   knowledge, we can enhance downstream tasks such as relighting, object compositing, and image
068   editing without the need for large labeled datasets or extensive retraining of the models.
069
      Recent work has begun to study this question. Bhattad et al. (2023a) demonstrated that StyleGAN
070
      can encode important scene intrinsics. Similarly, Zhan et al. (2023) showed that diffusion models
071
      can understand 3D scenes in terms of geometry and shadows. Chen et al. (2023) found that Stable
072   Diffusion’s internal activations encode depth and saliency maps that can be extracted with linear
073   probes. Three independent efforts (Luo et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2023; Hedlin et al., 2023) discovered
074   correspondences in diffusion models. However, these insights often pertain to specific models, leaving
075   a gap in our understanding of whether such encoding is ubiquitous across generative architectures.
076
      Why study different models? While diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022),
077
      have gained significant attention, other model types like GigaGAN (Kang et al., 2023), CM3leon (Yu
078   et al., 2023), and Parti (Yu et al., 2022) have shown they can produce similarly high-quality images.
079   By investigating this wide range of models, we can create a general framework that not only applies
080   to current generative models but is also adaptable to future developments and emerging architectures.
081   To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to study generative models of all types.
082
      Why develop a general approach? A general approach ensures broad applicability to emerging
083
      generative models. In this context, we find LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) to be highly effective. LoRA can
084   easily recover scene intrinsics across diverse architectures with small parameter updates and data.
085   This general method lays the groundwork for future research that can build on our findings to explore
086   intrinsic knowledge in new generative models. It is important to note that any approach capable of
087   being applied to all generative models with small or no parameter updates and low data requirements
088   is a reasonable and valid choice. While we have identified one such method (LoRA) in this work,
089   many others could also recover intrinsic representations across diverse generative models.
090   Why do we need slight modification or small data to recover this knowledge? Ideally, we recover
091   intrinsic knowledge without any new learning, revealing what the model already “knows.” But
092   achieving this purely with no learning is hard and non-trivial. Thus, we limit our approach to light
093   fine-tuning, using little labeled data to avoid introducing new knowledge to the model.
094
      Previous approaches, such as Bhattad et al. (2023a), have found codes in StyleGAN’s latent space
095
      for each intrinsic image, but such disentangled spaces have not yet been identified in models like
096   diffusion and autoregressive models. Recent depth extraction from diffusion models often involves
097   fine-tuning the entire model (Zhao et al., 2023; Ke et al., 2023) or applying linear probing (Chen et al.,
098   2023). Fine-tuning alters the model significantly, transforming it into a new version and potentially
099   compromising its original image-generating capabilities. This raises the question of whether the depth
100   perception was an innate quality of the model or a product of the fine-tuning process. A drawback of
101   linear probing lies in probing each layer independently. As we show linear probes perform poorly,
102   and our application of LoRA suggests that intrinsic information is distributed throughout the network.
103   Why analyze the correlation between recovered intrinsics and improved generative models?
104   If higher-quality generative models consistently produce better intrinsic images, this suggests an
105   alternative paradigm for improving these models. Instead of blindly scaling up with more data
106   and parameters, we could focus on enhancing the model’s ability to capture and recover intrinsic
107   properties. This approach could lead to more efficient improvements in model performance, driven
      by the quality of the intrinsic knowledge embedded within the model.

133   We find positive correlations in our experiments between the quality of recovered intrinsics and the
134   improvements in generative model performance. Specifically, we observe this in Stable Diffusion
135   versions 1.1, 1.2 and 1.5, as well as in improved face generators from various GAN and Autoregressive
136   models, as measured by FID. A visual illustration of this correlation is in Fig. 2. These results indicate
137
      that higher-quality generators tend to produce more accurate intrinsic representations.
138   Our contributions are showing that generative models encode intrinsic images across different
139   architectures, including GANs, Autoregressive models and Diffusion models. Our findings are in
140   Tab. 1 and elaborated in Sec. 4. We find a general approach using LoRA to recover these intrinsics,
141   which are competitive, with light fine-tuning and data. This method obtains these properties using
142   the same output head as the original image generation task. Through control experiments, we find a
143
      positive correlation between the quality of the generative model and the accuracy of the recovered
      intrinsics, suggesting that better models naturally produce better intrinsic representations(Fig. 2).
144
      This offers a new paradigm for model improvement beyond just scaling data and parameters.

147   2           R ELATED W ORK
148
149   Generative Models: Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) have
150   been widely used for generating realistic images. Variants like StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019),
151   StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020b) and GigaGAN (Kang et al., 2023) have pushed the boundaries in
152
      terms of image quality and control. Some work has explored the interpretability of GANs (Bau et al.,
      2020; Bhattad et al., 2023a), but little is known about their ability to capture scene intrinsics.
153
154   Diffusion models (Vincent, 2011; Gutmann & Hyvärinen, 2010) are popular at the moment for
155   generative tasks (Karras et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022). These models have
156   been shown to understand complex scene intrinsics like geometry and shadows (Zhan et al., 2023),
157   but the generalizability of this understanding across different scene intrinsics is largely unexplored.
158   Autoregressive models (Van Den Oord et al., 2016; Van den Oord et al., 2016) generate images pixel-
159   by-pixel, offering fine-grained control but at the cost of computational efficiency. VQ-VAE-2 (Razavi
160   et al., 2019) and VQGAN (Esser et al., 2020) have combined autoregressive models with vector
161   quantization to achieve high-quality image synthesis. While these models are powerful, their ability
      to capture and represent scene intrinsics is yet to be investigated.

162
      Intrinsic Image Recovery: Barrow & Tenenbaum (1978) highlighted several fundamental scene
163   intrinsics including depth, albedo, shading, and surface normals. A large body of work has focused
164   on extracting some related properties like depth and normals, from images (Eigen et al., 2014; Long
165   et al., 2015; Eftekhar et al., 2021; Kar et al., 2022; Ranftl et al., 2021; Bhat et al., 2023) using labeled
166   data. Labeled albedo and shading are hard to find and as the recent review in Forsyth & Rock (2021)
167   shows, methods involving little or no learning have remained competitive. However, these methods
168   often rely on supervised learning and do not recover intrinsic images from generative models.
169   Many recent studies have used generative models as pre-trained feature extractors or scene prior
170   learners. They use generated images to enhance downstream discriminative models, fine-tune the
171   original generative model for a new task, learn new layers or decoders to produce desired scene
172   intrinsics (Abdal et al., 2021; Jahanian et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2021; Noguchi &
173   Harada, 2020; Bao et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Sariyildiz et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Ke et al.,
174   2023). InstructCV (Gan et al., 2023) executes computer vision tasks via natural language instructions,
175   abstracting task-specific design choices. However, it requires re-training of the entire diffusion model.
176   In contrast, we show that many generative models capture intrinsic image knowledge implicitly and
177
      do not require specialized training to recover this information.
178   Knowledge in Generative Models: Several studies have explored the extent of StyleGAN’s knowl-
179   edge, particularly for 3D information about faces (Pan et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a). Yang
180   et al. (2021) show GANs encode hierarchical semantic information across different layers. Further
181   research has demonstrated that manipulating offsets in StyleGAN can lead to effective relighting of
182   images (Bhattad et al., 2024; 2023b) and extraction of scene intrinsics (Bhattad et al., 2023a). Chen
183
      et al. (2023) found internal activations of the LDM encode linear representations of both depth data
      and a salient-object / background distinction. Wu et al. (2023) also demonstrate rich latent codes
184
      of diffusion models can be easily mapped to annotations with small amount of training samples.
185
      Tang et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023b); Hedlin et al. (2023) found correspondence emerges in image
186   diffusion models. Sarkar et al. (2023) showed generative models fail to replicate projective geometry.
187
188   Luo et al. (2023a) explored training task-specific “readout” networks to extract signals like pose,
189
      depth, and edges from feature maps in Stable Diffusion models for controlling image generation. Our
      goals are different: We are interested in understanding intrinsic knowledge encoded in these models,
190
      while the aim of Luo et al. (2023a) is controlling image generation. Our use of LoRA offers notable
191
      advantages in parameter efficiency: itis approximately 5 times more parameter-efficient than readout
192   networks in their application to SD v1-5 (compare 8.5M vs 1.59M). Lastly, the broad applicability of
193   “readout” networks across various generative model types remains uncertain.
194
195
      A concurrent work Lee et al. (2023) applies a LoRA-like approach to adapt a pre-trained diffusion
      model for dense semantic tasks. Our work differs from theirs in several aspects: First, their goal is to
196
      use pre-trained diffusion models as strong priors for dense prediction. Second, their tasks are within
197
      restricted domains, such as bedrooms. Finally, they do not extend to the wide range of generative
198   models our study explores. Our paper not only demonstrates intrinsic knowledge encoded in different
199   architectures but also explores its application in a diverse scene contexts including real images.

      3    A PPROACH
203
      A generative model G maps noise/conditioning information z to an RGB image G(z) ∈ RH×W ×3 .
204
      We add to G with a small set of parameters θ that allow us to produce, using the same architecture as
205
      G, an image-like map with up to three channels, representing scene intrinsics like surface normals.
206
207   Our Framework. We recover intrinsic properties of an image (such as depth) using a small number
208   of labeled examples (image/depth map pairs) as supervision. In cases where we do not have access to
209
      the actual intrinsic properties, we use models trained on large datasets to generate estimated intrinsics
      (such as estimated depth for an image) as pseudo-ground truth, used as training targets for Gθ . To
210
      optimize θ of Gθ using a pseudo-ground truth predictor Φ, we minimize the objective:
211
212                                           \label {eq:obj1} \vspace {-2pt} \min _\theta \mathbb {E}_{z} [ d(G_\theta (z), \Phi (G(z))) ],    (1)
213
      where d is a distance metric that depends on the intrinsics we wish to learn.
214

258   Applying LoRA for diffusion models, LoRA adaptors are learned atop cross-attention and self-
259   attention layers, which aggregate geometrical information and “reflect the overall composition” of the
260   input (Hertz et al., 2023). The UNet is utilized as a dense predictor, transforming an RGB input into

      6    D ISCUSSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
530
      We find consistent evidence that generative models implicitly learn intrinsic images, allowing tiny
531
      LoRA adapters to recover them with light fine-tuning on small labeled data. More powerful generative
532
      models produce more accurate intrinsic images, strengthening our hypothesis that learning this
533   information is a natural byproduct of learning to generate images well.
534
535   Limitations. Although we show that generative models carry a wealth of intrinsic information, it
536
      is still ambiguous how these models use this information when generating images. Secondly, even
      though our framework is both parameter and label-efficient, we believe there is still room for further
537
      reduction and perhaps the development of a parameter-free approach. Lastly, the SD AUG generates
538
      sharper results but still lags behind its single-step counterpart in terms of quantitative analysis. Further
539   work is needed to explore this question.

853   B     OTHER A BLATIONS AND BASELINES
854
855   We extensively study the effect of applying LoRA to different attention layers within Stable Diffusion
856   models. Specifically, we investigate the outcomes of targeting up-blocks, mid-block, down-blocks,
857   cross-attention, and self-attention layers individually. We find (Fig. 16) that isolating LoRA to up
858   or down blocks or the mid-block alone is less effective or diverges, and applying to either cross- or
859   self-attention layers yields decent results, though combining them is best.
860   Additionally, we evaluated other image editing methods such as Textual Inversion (Gal et al., 2022)
861   and VISII (Nguyen et al., 2023), alongside InstructPix2Pix’s response to “Turn it into a surface
862   normal map” instruction (Brooks et al., 2023). As shown in Fig. 17, these methods perform poorly
863   for intrinsic image extraction, demonstrating the effectiveness of the LoRA approach in extracting
      scene intrinsics.
