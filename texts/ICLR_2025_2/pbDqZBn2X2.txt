009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in Machine Learning (ML) have demonstrated significant
012            potential in addressing Combinatorial Optimization (CO) problems through data-
013            driven approaches. Heatmap-based methods, which generate solution heatmaps
014            in a single step and employ an additional decoder to derive solutions for CO
015            tasks, have shown promise due to their scalability for large-scale problems. Tradi-
016            tionally, these complex models are trained using imitation learning with optimal
017            solutions, often leveraging diffusion models. However, our research has identi-
018
               fied several limitations inherent in these imitation learning approaches within the
               context of CO tasks. To overcome these challenges, we propose a 2-phase train-
019
               ing framework for diffusion models in CO, incorporating Reinforcement Learn-
020
               ing (RL) fine-tuning. Our methodology integrates cost information and the post-
021            process decoder into the training process, thereby enhancing the solver’s capac-
022            ity to generate effective solutions. We conducted extensive experiments on well-
023            studied combinatorial optimization problems, specifically the Traveling Salesman
024            Problem (TSP) and Maximal Independent Set (MIS), ranging from small-scale
025            instances to large-scale scenarios. The results demonstrate the significant efficacy
026            of our RL fine-tuning framework, surpassing previous state-of-the-art methods in
027            performance.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Combinatorial optimization (CO) has long been studied in operations research and computer science,
033
      but its inherent complexity, particularly the NP hardness, makes finding optimal solutions challeng-
      ing (Karp, 1975). Typically, problem-specific heuristics (Papadimitriou & Steiglitz, 1998), such as
034
      the 2OPT heuristic for the traveling salesman problem, achieve high performance but lack general-
035
      ization. Exact solvers also exist, but suffer from exponential growth in complexity with larger tasks.
036   Recently, Machine Learning (ML) has shown significant potential to overcome these limitations, im-
037   proving scalability for real-world applications (Bello et al., 2016; Vinyals et al., 2015; Khalil et al.,
038   2017; Bengio et al., 2021). Constructive ML-based CO solvers, which generate solutions directly
039   through neural networks, are emerging as promising alternatives to traditional heuristics (Zheng
040   et al., 2024; Sun & Yang, 2023).
041
      Research on these constructive solvers for CO is categorized as follows: autoregressive and heatmap-
042   based models (non-autoregressive models). Autoregressive models generate solutions sequentially
043   applying neural network feedforward operations, extending partial solutions step by step until an
044   entire solution is formed (da Costa et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019; Kool et al., 2019b; Kwon et al.,
045   2020; Kim et al., 2022). However, these repeated feedforward operations lead to increased computa-
046   tional overhead and potential instability during both training and inference, limiting their efficiency
047   in large-scale problems. In contrast, heatmap-based models generate an entire solution in a single
048   feedforward pass by producing a heatmap that represents the probability that the edges or nodes are
049   part of the solution (Fu et al., 2021a; Geisler et al., 2022; Joshi et al., 2019a). The generated heatmaps
050
      are then decoded into valid discrete CO solutions by using simple decoders such as greedy decoding
      algorithm.
051
052   Heatmap-based models frequently necessitate high-dimensional outputs, which in turn require com-
053   plex models with numerous parameters. To ensure stable training of these intricate models, super-
      vised learning (SL) is generally preferred. In this approach, the solver aims to generate heatmaps that


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      imitate high-quality solutions (Fu et al., 2021a; Geisler et al., 2022; Joshi et al., 2019b). Recently,
055   powerful generative models—successful in image and language domains—have been applied as CO
056   solvers (Graikos et al., 2022a; Mirhoseini et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2019a; Niu et al., 2020; Sun &
057   Yang, 2023). However, we have identified several issues with existing SL-based heatmap solvers
058   that merely focus on imitating optimal solutions. First, simply imitating optimal solutions does not
059   always guarantee high-quality solution in CO. Second, the impact of decoding strategies on final so-
060   lution quality is ignored during training. Third, these solvers heavily depend on high-quality training
061   datasets, which are computationally intractable to obtain for large-scale CO problems due to their
062   NP-hardness.
063   To address these issues, we introduce a reinforcement learning (RL) fine-tuning framework that
064   directly incorporates cost information during training, specifically focusing on diffusion models.
065   Our proposed approach, CADO (Cost-Aware Diffusion solver for combinatorial Optimization), pre-
066   serves the advantages of existing supervised learning (SL)-based solvers while effectively minimiz-
067   ing the cost of decoded solutions, the fundamental objective of combinatorial optimization problems.
068   We selected diffusion models as our base architecture due to their recent successes across various
069
      domains, including combinatorial optimization problems (Sun & Yang, 2023; Li et al., 2023), as
      well as their inherent compatibility with RL. Through our effective RL fine-tuning process, our
070
      method successfully addresses the three aforementioned limitations of SL-based heatmap solvers,
071
      demonstrating substantial and consistent performance improvements across diverse combinatorial
072   optimization tasks.
073
074   Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 1) We identify three issues of existing SL-based
075
      heatmap solvers that arise mainly from ignoring cost information during CO. 2) We introduce an
      RL fine-tuning algorithm for diffusion models that incorporates cost information in CO, along with
076
      practical techniques for stable RL fine-tuning. 3) Despite its simplicity, CADO demonstrates its
077
      strong effectiveness to address all three identified issues. 4) Building on these advantages, CADO
078   outperforms existing algorithms across diverse CO benchmarks - including the Traveling Salesman
079   Problem (TSP) with node sizes ranging from 50 to 10,000, the Maximum Independent Set (MIS),
080   and the TSPLIB real dataset benchmark.

085   2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
086
087   We define the problem and introduce the key notations related to combinatorial optimization (CO)
088
      problems. Let G be the set of all CO instances, and let g ∈ G denote an instance. Each instance g
      has an associated discrete solution space Xg := {0, 1}Ng and an objective function cg : Xg → R for
089
      each solution x ∈ Xg defined as:
090
091                                    cg (x) = cost(x, g) + valid(x, g).                                (1)
092
093   Here, cost(·) represents the cost value to be optimized, while valid(·) is a constraint indicator func-
094   tion, where valid(x, g) = 0 if the solution x belongs to the feasible solution space Fg ⊂ Xg , and
095
      valid(x, g) = ∞ when x ∈   / Fg . The optimization goal is to find the optimal solution x⋆ for a given
      instance s:
096
097                                            xg⋆ = arg min cg (x).                                      (2)
                                                      x∈Xg
098
099
      We describe two specific CO problems as examples: the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) and the
100
      Maximal Independent Set (MIS) problem. In the TSP, an instance g represents the coordinates of
101
      n cities to be visited. The solution x is an n × n matrix, where x[i, j] = 1 if the traveler moves
102   from city i to city j. The total solution space is Xg = {0, 1}n×n , and the feasible solution space
103   Fg ⊂ Xg is the set of all feasible TSP tours that visit each city exactly once. The objective function
104   cost(·) represents the total length of the given tour and should be minimized. In the MIS problem,
105   an instance g represents a graph (V, E), where V is the vertex set and E is the edge set. The solution
106   space Xg = {0, 1}V indicates whether each vertex v ∈ V is included in the solution set. To satisfy
107   the independence property, x should not contain nodes connected by edges in E. The objective
      function cost(·) represents the total number of selected nodes and should be maximized.

      2.2   N EURAL COMBINATORIAL OPTIMIZATION SOLVER
109
110   In this section, we briefly introduce the concepts of autoregressive solvers and heatmap-based
111   solvers. Autoregressive solvers extend a partial solution until a complete solution is formed:
112
                                                      Ng
113                                                   Y
                                         pθ (x|g) =         pθ (xt |x1:t−1 , g)                            (3)
114
                                                      t=1
115
116   where x1:t−1 is the partial solution. This approach works very well for small-scale combinatorial
117   optimization (CO) problems, but it becomes less practical for larger scales due to quadratic time and
118   space complexity.
119   The heatmap-based solvers, proposed to effectively solve large-scale CO problems, directly generate
120   a heatmap H ∈ RNg , representing the likelihood of each variable being part of the solution, and
121   utilizes it to form the final solution through an additional post-process decoder p (x|H) :
122
                                           pθ (x|g) = pθ (H|g)p (x|H)                                      (4)
123
124   Simply sampling a solution from H may result in solutions belonging to the total solution space Xg ,
125   which means that feasibility is not guaranteed. Therefore, heatmap-based solvers necessarily require
126   a post-process decoder to convert solutions in the infeasible space to the feasible space Xg . Various
127   post-process decoders have been proposed. For example, Qiu et al. (2022) employs a method that
128   stochastically samples valid variables while masking away infeasible variables, while Sun & Yang
129
      (2023) deterministically adds variables to the partial solution in descending order of the heatmap
      value as long as no conflicts occur.
130
131   Each solver can be trained using either a reinforcement learning (RL) or a supervised learning (SL)
132   objective. In SL, the availability of high-quality solutions x⋆g for each training instance g are assumed
133   to be given. The objective of SL is to maximize the likelihood :
134
                                        L(θ) = Eg∼P (g) [− log pθ (xg⋆ |g)].                               (5)
135
136
      In RL, the solver does not assume the availability of the high-quality solutions xg⋆ for a given instance
137
      g. However, the solver exploits the information of the objective function cg (·) during exploration and
138
      exploitation of the solutions x. The objective of RL is to find a distribution pθ (x|g) that maximize
139   the reward (minimize the cost) :
140
141                                   R(θ) = Eg∼P (g), x∼pθ (x|g) [−cg (x)].                               (6)

143   2.3   D IFFUSION MODEL FOR CO
144
145   Sun & Yang (2023) propose a diffusion model-based CO solver called DIFUSCO, which is classified
146
      as a heatmap-based solver. In DIFUSCO, the solver pθ (H|g) is modeled as a diffusion model and
      trained in a supervised manner.
147

149   During the forward process, noise is gradually added to the initial solution until the solution is

151   where x0 = xg⋆ in CO and xT is completely random noise. The forward noising process is defined by

      3     M OTIVATION : I SSUES IN SL-BASED H EATMAP S OLVERS
163
164   Several supervised learning (SL)-based heatmap solvers have emerged in combinatorial optimiza-
165   tion (CO) (Nowak et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2019a; Fu et al., 2021a; Geisler et al., 2022; Sun &
166   Yang, 2023). These approaches are trained on high-quality solution datasets, treating existing so-
167   lutions as ground-truth labels to generate corresponding heatmaps. Their underlying assumption is
168   that heatmaps closely approximating optimal solutions will be naturally decoded into high-quality,
169   low-cost solutions. However, our analysis reveals several non-trivial issues that impact their effec-
170   tiveness as CO solvers. In this section, we identify three fundamental challenges in training SL-based
171   heatmap solvers for CO problems.
172
173   3.1   I GNORANCE OF COST IN TRAINING PROCESS
174
175
176

      Figure 1: Examples of solutions with higher prediction error but lower cost compared on TSP-50.

186
      We observe that a solution that is very similar to the optimal solution does not always guarantee a
187
      very low cost. To illustrate this point, consider the example shown in Figure 1. Suppose that we are
188
      given an optimal tour (a) that we want to predict. During training, the model generates two solutions:
189   tour (b) and tour (c). Since tour (b) has fewer incorrectly connected edges, the SL training objective
190   is likely to prefer tour (b) over tour (c). However, in terms of actual cost, tour (c) performs better
191   than tour (b), revealing the inadequacy of the current training objective in properly distinguishing
192   them. Therefore, cost information must be considered during training to achieve our true goal in CO,
193   which is to minimize the cost.
194
195   3.2   I GNORANCE OF D ECODERS IN TRAINING PROCESS
196
197   As we mentioned in Section 2.1, the feasible solution space Fg is a much smaller subset compared
198
      to the total solution space Xg . Although heatmaps aim to mimic the optimal solution, the decod-
      ing process may produce a solution significantly different from the original imperfect heatmap in
199
      order to maintain feasibility. Since traditional SL-based heatmap solvers do not account for these
200
      effects during the decoding process, even if the generated heatmap is similar the optimal solution,
201   the decoded solution may differ significantly, potentially leading to degraded performance.
202
203
      3.3   D EPENDENCY ON T RAINING DATASET Q UALITY
204
205   The final issue with existing SL-based heatmap solvers is their reliance on high-quality training
206   datasets. These methods require large amounts of optimal solutions, but generating such datasets
207   is computationally expensive due to the NP-hardness of most CO tasks. Current approaches rely
208   on exact or sophisticated heuristic solvers to create training data, with time complexity increasing
209   exponentially as the problem size grows, making this impractical, particularly for large-scale CO
210
      tasks. To address these challenges, enhancing model robustness using suboptimal datasets is crucial.
      These datasets are easier to generate, often requiring only brief heuristic runs.

269   model to optimize R(θ) in equation 9. To accurately measure the effectiveness of RL fine-tuning

271   stances g can be newly generated from the distribution P (g) or sampling from the instances in the

274
      In our RL fine-tuning process, we optimize a 12-layer GNN-based diffusion model using a hybrid
      approach. While the last one to two layers are completely unfrozen for full training, we apply Low-
275

276
      improve both training stability and memory efficiency. Detailed explanations and comparative ex-
277   perimental results are elaborated in the Appendix C.2.

280

281
      using the 2OPT heuristic (Lin & Kernighan, 1973) to refine solutions after decoding. We occasion-
282
      ally enhance our method with the local rewrite (LR) technique introduced by T2T (Li et al., 2023),
283   which iteratively adds noise to disrupt the solutions and reconstructs sampled solutions to improve
284   solution quality. However, unlike T2T, which leverages gradient-based cost guidance during local
285   rewrite, our method does not utilize this guidance.

295   imal Independent Set (MIS), which are basically edge and node selecting problems, respectively.
296   TSP is the most commonly used benchmark combinatorial optimization problem, where the objec-
297   tive is to determine the shortest possible route that visits a set of nodes exactly once and returns to
298   the original node. MIS is another widely used benchmark problem where the objective is to find the
299   largest subset of vertices in a graph such that no two vertices in the subset are adjacent.
300
301   Datasets. For RL fine-tuning, we generate new TSP instances for each problem size (TSP-
302   50/100/500/1000/10000) by uniformly sampling the corresponding number of nodes from a unit
303   square. We use identical test instances as Joshi et al. (2022); Kool et al. (2019b) for TSP-50/100 and
304
      Fu et al. (2021b) for TSP-500/1000/10000. Additionally, we evaluate our model on TSPLIB (Rein-

306

307   Stutzle, 2000) (MIS-SAT) and Erdős–Rényi (Erdos & Renyi, 1960) (MIS-ER). In contrast to TSP,

509   6    R ELATED W ORK
510
511   ML-based CO solvers can be categorized into autoregressive and heatmap-based solvers. Autore-
512   gressive solvers iteratively extend a partial solution until completion (Kool et al., 2019b; Bello et al.,
513   2016; Kwon et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022; Dernedde et al., 2024), but they struggle with scalability
514   due to their sequential nature. SL-based heatmap solvers (Fu et al., 2021a; Geisler et al., 2022; Joshi
515
      et al., 2019a; Nowak et al., 2018) generate solutions in a single step, offering better scalability but
      often producing suboptimal solutions due to ignoring the post-process decoder and cost information
516
      during training. There is RL-based heatmap solvers (Qiu et al., 2022) in the literature but fails to
517
      perform well on large-scale problems. Recently, the divide and conquer framework has been used
518   with both solver types to address large-scale problems by breaking them into smaller subproblems
519   (Ye et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024).
520
521
      Generative models, known for their success in image and text generation, have been adapted to
      CO for their expressive power (Graikos et al., 2022a; Mirhoseini et al., 2021; Kool et al., 2019a;
522
      Niu et al., 2020; Sun & Yang, 2023; Li et al., 2023). DIFUSCO, a diffusion model-based solver,
523
      shows promise in various CO problems (Sun & Yang, 2023). However, most generative models in
524   CO rely on imitation learning, inheriting the same issues as heatmap-based solvers. Sanokowski
525   et al. (2024) uses an unsupervised learning approach to directly optimize the cost function. Li et al.
526   (2023), which is closely related to our work, extends DIFUSCO by integrating a cost-guided local
527   search during the denoising process in inference, whereas our method incorporates cost information
528   during training instead. One of the main strengths of T2T is its ability to bypass additional training
529   overhead. However, its performance is highly reliant on the quality of the base diffusion model and
530   can decline substantially if the underlying model is poorly trained.
531
532   7    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduced an RL fine-tuning framework for heatmap-based solvers, especially
535   for diffusion model-based, that successfully addresses key issues from ignoring cost information in
536   existing SL-based heatmap solvers, such as the disconnect between prediction quality and solution
537   cost, as well as inefficiencies arising from excluding the post-process decoder during training. By
538   integrating cost-based feedback and aligning the learning process with the final solution genera-
539   tion, our approach not only enhances model performance across various CO benchmarks but also
      improves scalability and efficiency, making it a promising advancement in neural CO.

      Sun & Yang (2023) propose a diffusion model-based CO solver called DIFUSCO. In CO, a diffusion
813

814

815   x is belongs to the discrete solution space {0, 1}N , the noising process q(xt |xt−1 ) and denoising

817   discrete diffusion models introduced by Austin et al. (2021a); Hoogeboom et al. (2021); Sun & Yang

820

       In this section, we investigate transfer learning scenarios for scaled-up task sizes for TSP. We com-
1051
       pare our primary RL fine-tuning approach with SL fine-tuning, which is feasible when a dataset for
1052   the target task is available, as in DIFUSCO. We examined two scenarios: (1) TSP100→TSP500 and
1053   (2) TSP500→TSP1000. Table 11 demonstrates that directly applying the model without fine-tuning
1054   results in poor performance. CADO (1.59%) achieves comparable performance to SL fine-tuning
1055   (1.55%) in the TSP100→TSP500 scenario and outperforms it in the TSP500→TSP1000 scenario
1056   (1.86% → 1.04%). Notably, CADO accomplishes this without requiring an additional dataset of
1057   optimal solutions for the target task sizes. These results highlight the effectiveness and efficiency of
1058   our RL fine-tuning approach in transfer learning settings, particularly for larger-scale problems.

1060   F     C OMPARISON WITH C OST- AWARE H EATMAP CO S OLVERS
1061
1062   Among heatmap-based CO solvers, several approaches also incorporate cost information with moti-
1063   vations similar to CADO. In this section, we highlight two important baselines: T2T (Li et al., 2023)
1064   and Dimes (Qiu et al., 2022), and compare them with CADO.
1065
1066   F.1     C OMPARISON WITH T2T
1067
1068   T2T (Li et al., 2023) is an important related work to our approach, as both employ diffusion-based
1069   heatmap CO solving with cost incorporation. The key distinction between T2T and our approach
1070
       lies in their treatment of cost information: while T2T considers costs during inference through solu-
       tion sampling without additional fine-tuning, CADO explicitly enable the model to sample low-cost
1071
       solutions through supplementary training. Specifically, T2T’s cost-guided sampling relies on two
1072
       main concepts:
1073
1074            • Cost-guided denoising process: Similar to classifier guidance, this technique steers a well-
1075              trained diffusion model toward generating solutions with lower costs.
1076            • Local rewrite: This diffusion-specific technique iteratively adds noise to disrupt the solu-
1077              tions and denoises the sampled solutions to obtain improved results.
1078
1079   By combining these approaches, T2T can generate low-cost solutions without additional fine-tuning.
       While this offers the advantage of avoiding CADO’s extra training costs when the base heatmap


                                                                  20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       solver is well-trained, T2T’s performance deteriorates if the underlying base diffusion model is
1081   inadequately trained. Our experimental results demonstrate these characteristics. As shown in Ta-
1082   ble 6, for MIS-SAT problems where the pretrained baseline DIFUSCO effectively learns optimal
1083   solution distributions, T2T outperforms CADO-L with its cost-guided sampling. Conversely, for
1084   TSP-500/1000 and MIS-ER problems where DIFUSCO may benefit from additional training refine-
1085   ments, CADO-L outperforms T2T with lower inference costs. Furthermore, our approach and T2T’s
1086   method can be complementary. By incorporating T2T’s Local rewrite technique (while excluding the
1087   cost-guided denoising process), CADO-L’s performance improves substantially. In our main results,
1088   we refer to this hybrid approach simply as CADO.
1089
1090   F.2   C OMPARISON WITH DIMES
1091
       DIMES (Qiu et al., 2022) employs reinforcement learning to train the heatmap solver directly for
1092
       high-quality solutions and naturally remains unaffected by the issues of SL-based heatmap solvers
1093
       discussed in Section 3. Furthermore, DIMES offers an advantage over CADO by eliminating the
1094   need for additional solution datasets. However, this comes at the cost of being unable to lever-
1095   age existing solution dataset information and the robust capabilities of diffusion models, resulting
1096   in somewhat inferior performance. To compensate, DIMES proposes instance-specific fine-tuning
1097   through local search or meta-learning frameworks during testing. While this approach significantly
1098   increases inference time, it still falls short of CADO in terms of solution quality generated from
1099   heatmaps.
