008                                               A BSTRACT
009
010            The ability to explore efficiently and effectively is a central challenge of rein-
011            forcement learning. In this work, we consider exploration through the lens of
012
               information theory. Specifically, we cast exploration as a problem of maximizing
               the Shannon entropy of the state occupation measure. This is done by maximiz-
013
               ing a sequence of divergences between distributions representing an agent’s past
014
               behavior and its current behavior. Intuitively, this encourages the agent to ex-
015            plore new behaviors that are distinct from past behaviors. Hence, we call our
016            method RAMP, for “Running Away from the Past.” A fundamental question of
017            this method is the quantification of the distribution change over time. We consider
018            both the Kullback-Leibler divergence and the Wasserstein distance to quantify
019            divergence between successive state occupation measures, and explain why the
020            former might lead to undesirable exploratory behaviors in some tasks. We demon-
021            strate that by encouraging the agent to explore by actively distancing itself from
022            past experiences, it can effectively explore mazes and a wide range of behaviors
023
               on robotic manipulation and locomotion tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Exploration is essential in reinforcement learning (RL) as it allows agents to discover optimal strate-
028
      gies in complex environments. Without it, agents risk becoming stuck in suboptimal policies, lacking
029
      the diverse experiences needed to learn effectively. Despite a long history of study, with fundamen-
030   tal algorithms like R-max (Brafman & Tennenholtz, 2002), UCRL (Auer & Ortner, 2006), and E3
031   (Kearns & Singh, 2002), exploration remains a major challenge in modern RL.
032
033
      One method for encouraging exploration is providing intrinsic motivation to the agent; our approach
      falls into this category. The agent aims to maximize this additional (intrinsic) reward which mo-
034
      tivates exploration. Various intrinsic reward models specific to exploration have been developed
035
      (Brafman & Tennenholtz, 2002; Auer & Ortner, 2006; Bellemare et al., 2016; Eysenbach et al.,
036   2018; Badia et al., 2020). Amongst these are methods based on the principle of optimism in the face
037   of uncertainty (Munos et al., 2014), where agents are encouraged to explore under-visited areas of
038   the state space by assigning high reward values to uncertain states. While these methods apply well
039   to tabular state spaces, in high-dimensional or continuous state spaces, these methods must rely on
040   parameterized functions to represent uncertainty, which may result in inconsistent behavior (Pathak
041   et al., 2017; Burda et al., 2018).
042   Information theory provides a useful perspective on exploration. For example, one can define a finite
043   set of skills which have some descriptor per skill. Maximizing the Mutual Information (MI) between
044   the descriptors of these skills, and the states which they visit, yields good exploratory behaviors for
045   this set of skills (Eysenbach et al., 2018). Similarly, it has been demonstrated that maximizing the
046   Shannon entropy of the state distribution encourages exploration (Liu & Abbeel, 2021; Hazan et al.,
047   2019; Lee et al., 2019). However, these approaches often rely on probability density estimators, and
048   finding a relevant density estimator for an environment can be challenging.
049   This study introduces a method for exploration that aims at achieving a high Shannon entropy score
050   of the distribution representing the agent’s experiences. We show that this objective can be reframed
051   as the maximization of a sequence of Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergences between successive state
052   occupation measures. This new objective leads to the intuitive use of a simple classifier as a density
053   estimator. In other words, the goal of exploration, represented by high Shannon entropy over the
      agent’s experiences, can be achieved by iteratively separating an agent’s past experiences with its


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      most recent ones. This results in an algorithm called RAMP, for “Running Away from the Past,” as
055   the agent explores by distancing itself from its past experiences.
056
057   We evaluate the performance of RAMP using multiple metrics, including the state space coverage
058
      and the maximum score achieved by a policy trained to maximize a specific reward model. In
      environments where exploration could benefit from a metric relevant to the state space, we suggest
059
      using the Wasserstein distance as an alternative to the KL divergence. We study the difference
060
      between these two measures by proposing two versions of the algorithm: RAMPKL , which uses KL
061   divergence, and RAMPW , which uses Wasserstein distance. We compare RAMP to several baselines
062   developed for exploration tasks and across various environments such as mazes, locomotion tasks,
063   and robotics tasks. We find that RAMP is competitive in exploration to state-of-the-art methods
064   such as LSD (Park et al., 2022) on robotics tasks, and that it leads to more effective exploration on
065   maze and locomotion tasks. We conclude that this reformulation of the Shannon entropy objective
066   can open further avenues in the domain of exploration in RL. All our implementations are available
067   at the following repository: GitHub repository.

      3     RUNNING AWAY FROM THE PAST (RAMP)
153
154   The core idea of RAMP is to explore by running away from the past. We now define how we use
155   intrinsic motivation to reward this movement, which leads to the above objectives of maximizing
156   entropy. The RAMP algorithm uses two alternative estimates of distribution divergence, rDKL (s)
157   and rW (s), to reward the agent for moving away from past experiences.

      3.4   T HE RAMP A LGORITHM
240
241   The full RAMP algorithm is described in Algorithm 1 and illustrated in Figure 1. The RAMP
242   algorithm follows four key steps. First, current policy π is used to (1) sample new experiences in
243   the environment to measure the policy π’s current occupancy measure. In practice, a buffer Dρ
244   containing Nρe episodes from the agent’s most recent experiences is used.
245
      Next, the instrinsic reward model is updated to better (2) estimate the new versus past experiences.
246   In RAMP, the reward model is represented by a simple neural network fϕ which is the solution
247   to a specific optimization problem. Two alternate measures of divergence are possible: the KL
248   divergence and the Wasserstein distance. Thus, the RAMP algorithm has two versions: RAMPKL ,
249   which maximizes Objective 2, and RAMPW , which maximizes Objective 4. For RAMPKL , fϕ is
250   determined by solving the contrastive problem in Equation 6. Alternatively, for RAMPW , fϕ is
251   obtained by solving the constrained optimization problem described in Equation 7.
252   The third step (3) maximizes the difference between the present and the past distributions. The
253   reward models proposed by RAMP can be maximized using any RL method. This study uses the
254   Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm Haarnoja et al. (2018) for all experiments. The final step is to
255   (4) update the distribution of past experiences µn . In practice, only a sample of µn contained
256   in a buffer Dµn is available. The goal is to transform this sample throughout learning so that its
257   empirical distribution mirrors µn at each epoch. The full details of constructing Dµn are given in
258   Appendix F.
259   We study the RAMP algorithm on a series of control tasks, focusing on exploration of robotic
260   locomotion in the following sections. We compare RAMP to contemporary methods for exploration,
261   which are described below.

319   the Wasserstein distance version of mutual information using the Euclidean distance as the state

333   behaviors, which may result in more sample-efficient exploration. By focusing on incremental im-
334   provement, RAMP offers a promising solution for efficient exploration.

      In this section, we study the capacity of the RAMP algorithm to lead to exploration in complex

      environments. We begin with a qualitative analysis of the reward models used by RAMP to better

      characterize the objectives of RAMP. We then perform a quantitative analysis of the exploration

342   the ability of RAMP to explore when also using an additional, extrinsic, reward.

      The evaluation is conducted on three different sets of tasks, which are illustrated and further detailed

      culty. Secondly, we use five robotic locomotion environments from the MuJoCo platform (Todorov

515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   This paper presents a novel exploration method in reinforcement learning aimed at maximizing the
518   Shannon entropy of an agent’s experience distribution. By reformulating this objective, we derive a
519   new method, RAMP, that encourages the agent to explore by distancing itself from past experiences.
520
      We investigate the use of KL divergence and Wasserstein distance to characterize the differences
521
      between the agent’s current distribution and its past distribution. We characterize two versions of
522   RAMP, (RAMPW and RAMPKL ), and compare them. We find that maximizing the Wasserstein dis-
523   tance between two distributions under the temporal distance intuitively results in a different behavior
524   compared to maximizing the KL divergence. To maximize the Wassterstein distance, the agent must
525   encounter states that are as far away as possible from the states already encountered. This leads to
526   effective exploration and even the ability to reach high rewards.
527
      Our evaluations reveal that, in locomotion tasks, RAMPW enables highly efficient exploration by
528   extensively exploring over the agent’s position. In the presence of extrinsic reward, RAMPKL also
529   demonstrates the ability to explore sufficiently to lead to rewarding behaviors. Beyond the use
530   of an extrinsic reward, RAMPKL could also be performed by measuring the KL divergence of a
531   featurization of the state space, rather than the full state. This could lead to exploration of new
532   behaviors, as demonstrated under the presence of an extrinsic reward. We aim to study such a
533   featurization for high-dimensional state spaces in future work.
534   In summary, this work reframes exploration in reinforcement learning as an iterative process of
535   distinguishing the agent’s present behavior from its past experiences, introducing a novel approach
536   with wide applicability. The RAMP algorithm offers a new framing of exploration that could be
537   combined with other exploration strategies, such as skill-based or hierarchical exploration. This new
538   perspective on exploration, driven by maximizing the divergence between successive distributions,
539   has the potential to advance both theoretical insights and practical algorithms for exploration in
      reinforcement learning.

897   One approach to building Dµn is to initialize a buffer Dµ0 with a fixed size using a random policy
898   and then replace elements in that list to incorporate the agent experiences. Specifically, each time the
899   policy is updated, the sample Dµn regrouping the past experiences of the agent should be updated.
900   For some algorithms, such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), updating Dµn is straightforward because
901   the distribution ρπ can be clearly identified between updates. In this case, Dµn+1 is constructed by
902   combining a proportion β of samples from Dρπ with a proportion (1 − β) of samples from Dµn each
903
      time the policy is updated. For SAC, where the policy is updated every step in the environment, each
      new state serves as a single sample for that policy. To determine whether a state should be added
904
      in Dµn+1 , an accept-reject procedure using a Bernoulli distribution with parameter β is applied for
905
      each step. The current state replaces a random element in the list Dµn when accepted.
