009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural image compression, necessary in various edge-device scenarios, suffers
012            from its heavy encode-decode structures and inflexible compression level switch.
013            The primary issue is that the computational and storage capabilities of edge devices
014            are weaker than those of servers, preventing them from handling the same amount
015            of computation and storage. One solution is to downsample images and reconstruct
016            them on the receiver side; however, current methods uniformly downsample the
017            image and limit flexibility in compression levels. We take a step to break up
018
               this paradigm by proposing a conditional uniform-based sampler that allows for
               flexible image size reduction and reconstruction. Building on this, we introduce
019
               a lightweight transformer-based reconstruction structure to further reduce the
020
               reconstruction load on the receiver side. Extensive evaluations conducted on a
021            real-world testbed demonstrate multiple advantages of our system over existing
022            compression techniques, especially in terms of adaptability to different compression
023            levels, computational efficiency, and image reconstruction quality.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The need for advanced lossy image compression is raised by the explosive development of edge
029   devices equipped with high-resolution cameras, such as industrial-inspections (George et al., 2019),
030   wildlife observation (wil, 2023), and autonomous driving (Ananthanarayanan et al., 2017). Neural-
031   Network (NN) based compressor can satisfy this need, which outperform traditional image com-
032   pression techniques like JPEG (Group, 1986) and BPG (Bellard, 2014). However, due to its heavy,
033
      symmetric encoding and decoding structure and inflexible compression rate adjustment, current
      NN-based methods have not yielded practical use on resource-constrained edge devices (Dasari et al.,
034
      2022).
035
036   Given the paucity of computational ability on edge devices in general (Fut, 2020; ope, 2018; aws,
037   2023; Li et al., 2023a;b), a huge gap would exist in the edge compression/decompress and transmission
038   latency. As shown in Fig. 1a, encoding an image can take as long as 18 seconds on high-end devices
039
      like the NVIDIA Jetson TX2. Downsampling image size at the sender and restoring it on the receiver
      is one way to alleviate this problem (Yin et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). However, these solutions
040
      usually employ super-resolution, which uniformly downsample and restore images to fixed sizes,
041
      lacking flexibility for dynamic and complex compression needs in real-world applications.
042
043   We take a fresh look at this problem and introduce Easz, a lightweight compression enhancement
044   framework that operates efficiently at the edge-sender with near-zero computational demand, while
045
      also maintaining efficiency on the receiver. Easz is compatible with all existing compression al-
      gorithms. The intuition of Easz is an implicit assumption undermined in current solutions: the
046
      image need to be uniformly downsampled. Easz includes an erase-and-squeeze process, which
047
      relaxes this assumption by designing a conditional uniform-based sampler. This technique provides
048   a more adaptable and fine-grained compression level but also loses the chance to employ efficient
049   reconstruction through convolution or the fast Fourier transform techniques. We then propose a
050   receiver-side lightweight transformer architecture for efficient, high-quality reconstruction of erased
051   patches. This involves a two-stage image patchify process to limit the scope of attention correlation
052   calculations and a four-layer transformer model for pixel-level local image reconstruction. As shown
053   in Figure 1b, Easz surpasses both the NN-based compressor and the traditional compressor.

086
087   Learned-based image compression is experiencing significant growth, with advancements in end-
088   to-end training, hyperprior structures, entropy models, and encoder-decoder improvements Notable
089   developments include the introduction of auto-regressive components (Minnen et al., 2018), Gaussian
090   Mixture Models for probability estimation (Cheng et al., 2020), and a general-purpose lossless
091   compression paradigm using lightweight neural networks (Mao et al., 2022b;a; 2023). Attention
092   mechanisms have been incorporated through Informer (Jun-Hyuk et al., 2022), while Transformers
093   and Swin architectures are replacing traditional CNNs in encoding/decoding tasks (Yinhao Zhu and
094   Cohen, 2022; He et al., 2021).
095   Despite progress, real-world applications still face challenges such as inflexibility in switching models
096   and high latency at the edge. Deep-learning-based compression methods take about 1∼20 second per
097   image (512x768) on NVIDIA Jetson TX2, and many real-life endpoints are less potent than the TX2
098   (considering Raspberry Pi 4) but still need to compress images. A primary issue is that most NN-
099   based image compressors require a model switch when changing compression levels. One approach
100   involves downsampling images at the edge and using super-resolution techniques to reconstruct them
101
      on the server (Yin et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). These methods reduce computational load at the
      edge, but applying super-resolution directly in this context results in an inflexible downsizing rate
102
      and can degrade reconstruction performance (Laroche et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2022).

       3.1     I MPLICIT A SSUMPTION IN P REVIOUS M ETHODS
132
133    Previous image enhancement frameworks usually employ super-resolution as the downsample-
134    reconstruction technique (Yin et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024). We point out that this introduces an
135    implicit assumption and limits its flexibility.
136
       The standard super-resolution (SR) model with multiple degradations typically posits that the low-
137    resolution image is a degraded representation of a high-resolution image, characterized explicitly as a
138    blurry, noisy, and sub-sampled version of the original.
139
140
141                                        y = (x ⊛ k) ↓s +ϵ      with ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 )
142
143
       In this formulation, let x denote the high-resolution image, y represent its low-resolution counterpart,
       k be the blur kernel, ↓s signify the subsampling operator with scale factor s, and ϵ denote the additive
144
       noise. This model operates under the assumption that the blur kernel is uniform across the entire
145
       image, allowing for efficient computation of the low-resolution image through convolution or fast
146    Fourier transform techniques, as highlighted in recent studies (Laroche et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2022).
147    However, when this model is directly implemented within an edge image-enhancement framework,
148    the implicit assumption of uniformity introduces a constraint on the downsampling ratio, which in
149    turn restricts the framework’s flexibility. This limitation underscores the need for more adaptable
150    techniques to accommodate varying degradation patterns, limiting the framework’s flexibility.
151
       Next, we will explain how to relax this assumption. The uniformly downsampled assumption is
152    critical for applying efficient super-resolution-based reconstruction through convolution or fast Fourier
153    transform techniques. By challenging this assumption, direct random pixel prediction becomes costly
154    (see §3.3.2). We then propose a two-stage patchify process with a lightweight transformer to solve
155    this problem.

174
175   In this section, we aim to propose an effective sampler that challenges the implicit assumption
176
      discussed in Section 3.1. We treat each pixel as a sampling unit, though this can also be extended
      to patches (See §3.2.4). We initially introduce a Uniform-based sampler for row-based random
177
      sampling and subsequently impose constraints on it. Row-based sampling is employed to ensure that
178
      the sampled image can be reassembled into a rectangular format.
179
180   Random Sampler Definition. The random sampler Gr computes a mapping between the coordinates
181   (i, j) of the downsampled image X̂ and the relative coordinates (u, v) of the original image X. In
182   this sampler, each row i of the image is processed sequentially, and within each row, the column
183   coordinate j is selected using a uniform random sampler. The sampler is defined by two functions,
184
      gr1 , which governs the random column selection from X, while gr0 (i) represents the current row:
185
186                                          X̂[i, j] = X[gr0 (i), gr1 (i, j)]
187   where gr0 (i) is a deterministic function representing row selection, and gr1 (i, j) is a random mapping
188   for the column coordinate within row i.
189
190
      Uniform Random Selection in Each Row. To ensure uniform random sampling within each row
      of the original image X, the row coordinate gr0 (i) is fixed as i, and the column selection function
191
      gr1 (i, j) samples a pixel uniformly from the width W for each row i. The random sampler Gr is
192
      described as follows:
193
                               Gr = gr0 (i) = i, gr1 (i, j) = Uniform(0, W − 1)

194
195

196
      random column coordinates for each pixel j in row i. This ensures uniform random selection across
197
      columns within each row while maintaining a structured row-based approach.
198

269   By relaxing the uniform sampling assumption outlined in §3.1, Easz can no longer utilize a super-

300   3.3     R ECONSTRUCTION
301
302   Given the squeezed compressed image X̂squeezed , we introduced a Masked-Image-Modeling process
303   to perform the pixel-level reconstruction on the receiver side. The reconstruction of the squeezed
304   image back to the original image is approached through a framework reminiscent of masked image
305   modeling (MIM). Specifically, the input is X̂squeezed . Y = T (X̂squeezed ). The autoencoder model G(·)
306   takes the corrupted images X̂squeezed as input and aims to generate a prediction Ŷ, optimizing the
307   model by minimizing the difference between the prediction and the target:
308
309                                     Ŷ = G(X̂squeezed ),   L = D(X, Ŷ)
310
311   This paper focuses on parametric target generation strategies utilizing a transformer structure for
312   image reconstruction tasks. A significant challenge arises from directly predicting pixel values using
313   transformers due to the quadratic complexity of attention mechanisms.
314
315   3.3.1    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS .
316
317   Predicting pixel values for each element in the image, particularly in high-resolution images, proves
318   to be computationally expensive. Let the transformer model’s attention mechanism operate with
319   complexity O(n2 · d), where n is the number of tokens (patches or pixels) and d is the dimensionality
320
      of the token embeddings. This results in costly computation when n is large, especially for pixel-level
      operations.
321
322   For example, a 256x256 grayscale image would require 4, 294, 967, 296 × dmodel calculations if
323   each pixel were treated as a token. By employing the two-stage patchify process (with n = 32 and
      b = 1), this number is reduced by 256 times to 16, 777, 216 × dmodel calculations. Detailed analysis


                                                          6
324
      would be in Appendix B. The reduction in complexity comes from performing attention operations
325   within each patch rather than across the whole image.
326
327   3.3.2    T WO -S TAGE PATCHIFYING AND L IGHTWEIGHT R ESTORATION .
328
329   Motivated by this observation, we adopt a two-stage patchifying process followed by a lightweight
330   transformer to improve computational efficiency.
331   1. First Stage: Initial Image Patchifying: Given the image X ∈ Rh×w×C , we divide the image into
332   non-overlapping patches of size ph × pw . For each patch Pm,n , the extraction follows:
333
334
                       Pm,n = X[m · ph : (m + 1) · ph − 1, n · pw : (n + 1) · pw − 1, :]
                          j k                        j k
335
      where m = 0, . . . , phh − 1 and n = 0, . . . , pww − 1.
336
337   2. Second Stage: Subdividing into Sub-Patches: Each patch Pm,n is further divided into sub-patches
338   of size Sh × Sw to facilitate fine-grained prediction:
339
                     Pm,n,k,l = Pm,n [k · Sh : (k + 1) · Sh − 1, l · Sw : (l + 1) · Sw − 1, :]
340
                          j k                         j k
341
      where k = 0, . . . , Sphh − 1 and l = 0, . . . , Spww − 1.
342
343   3. Lightweight Transformer for Sub-Patch Restoration: A lightweight transformer model, denoted
344   as Tlight (·), with complexity O(m2 · d) (where m is the number of sub-patches), is employed for
345   restoring the missing pixel values within each sub-patch. Fig. 3(c) illustrates the architecture of our
346   efficient transformer-based network on the server side. The encoder and decoder are composed of
347   two transformer blocks, each containing three layernorms, one attention layer, and one feedforward
348
      layer. The model size is significantly reduced to 8.4MB. Given the downsampled input sub-patch
349   Pm,n,k,l , the predicted sub-patch P̂m,n,k,l is generated as:
350

351
352   Through this reconstruction process, the original content of the image is effectively restored from the
353   masked and squeezed representation. We adopt LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), a well-known perceptual
354
      loss, along with L1 loss as training loss as L. Note that the encoder and decoder can work with a
      variety of input erase ratios, which is controlled by p and T (See §3.2.4), and hence, we do not need
355
      to train a separate model for each erase ratio. Sub-patches can execute in parallel both for encoder
356
      and decoder due to the nature of transformer block (Vaswani et al., 2017).

      This paper proposes Easz, which addresses the challenges of edge image compression and trans-
533
      mission latency by introducing a novel erase-and-squeeze technique that enhances flexibility and
534   efficiency. By relaxing the conventional requirement for uniform downsampling, Easz allows for
535   adaptable compression levels tailored to dynamic real-world applications. The implementation of a
536   lightweight transformer architecture on the receiver side ensures high-quality reconstruction of erased
537   image patches without imposing significant computational demands. Moreover, Easz’s compatibility

      performance and efficiency, emphasizing its potential for real-world applications.

737
738   During the inference stage, the procedure starts from receiving a set of un-erased sub-patches
739   U = {u1 , u2 , . . . , um }, and a set of zero sub-patches Û = {û1 , û2 , . . . , ûk } is firstly added. {U, Û }
740   is then mapped to corresponding position using M . Afterward, we project this combined set {U, Û }
741   into the embedding space {F, F̂ }, feed it into the encoder to obtain feature representations and
742
      reconstruct P̂ .
743
744   The mapping timing is the critical difference between the training and inference stages. The mapping
745   is applied to the feature representations F in the training stage. However, during the inference stage,
746
      the mapping is performed on the sub-patches before they are sent into the encoder. This difference is
      due to the use of positional embedding. In the training process, the positional embedding is applied
747
      before the erase operation because the sub-patches remain in their original positions at that stage. On
748
      the other hand, during the inference phase, the received un-erased sub-patches need to be mapped
749   back to their original positions to incorporate the positional embedding effectively.
