010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            There is a well known intrinsic trade-off between the fairness of a representation
013            and the performance of classifiers derived from the representation. Due to the
014            complexity of optimisation algorithms in most modern representation learning
015            approaches, for a given method it may be non-trivial to decide whether the obtained
016            fairness-performance curve of the method is optimal, i.e., whether it is close to the
017
               true Pareto front for these quantities for the underlying data distribution.
018            In this paper we propose a new method to compute the optimal Pareto front, which
019            does not require the training of complex representation models. We show that
020
               optimal fair representations possess several useful structural properties, and that
               these properties enable a reduction of the computation of the Pareto Front to a
021
               compact discrete problem. We then also show that these compact approximating
022
               problems can be efficiently solved via off-the shelf concave-convex programming
023            methods. Finally, in addition to representations, we show that the new methods may
024            also be used to directly compute the Pareto front of fair classification problems.
025
               Since our approach is independent of the specific model of representations, it may
026
               be used as the benchmark to which representation learning algorithms, or classifiers,
027            may be compared. We experimentally evaluate the approach on a number of real
028            world benchmark datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Fair representations are a central topic in the field of Fair Machine Learning, Mehrabi et al. (2021),
034
      Pessach & Shmueli (2022),Chouldechova & Roth (2018). Since their introduction in Zemel et al.
035
      (2013), Fair representations have been extensively studied, giving rise to a variety of approaches
036   based on a wide range of modern machine learning methods, such GANs, variational auto encoders,
037   numerous variants of Optimal Transport methods, and direct variational formulations. See the
038   papers Feldman et al. (2015), Edwards & Storkey (2015),Louizos et al. (2015),Madras et al. (2018),
039   Gordaliza et al. (2019); Zehlike et al. (2020), Song et al. (2019), Du et al. (2020), for a sample of
040   existing methods.
041
      For a given representation learning problem and a target classification problem, since the fairness
042   constraints reduce the space of feasible classifiers, the best possible classification performance will
043   usually be lower as the fairness constraint becomes stronger. This phenomenon is known as the
044   Fairness-Performance trade-off. Assume that we have fixed a way to measure fairness. Then for
045   a given representation learning method, one is often interested in the fairness-performance curve
046   (γ, E(γ)). Here, γ is the fairness level, and E(γ) is the classification performance of the method at
047   that level. The curve (γ, E(γ)) where E(γ) is the best possible performance over all representations
048   and classifiers under the constraint is known as the Fairness-Performance Pareto Front.
049   As indicated by the above discussion, representation learning methods typically involve models with
050   high dimensional parameter spaces, and complex, possibly constrained non-convex optimisation
051   algorithms. As such, these methods may be prone to local minima, and may be sensitive to a variety
052   of hyper-parameters, such as architecture details, learning rates, and even initializations. While the
053   representations produced by such methods may often be useful, it nevertheless may be difficult to
      decide whether their associated Fairness-Performance curve is close to the true Pareto Front.

070   In this paper we propose a new method to compute the optimal Pareto front, which does not require
071   the training of complex fair representation models. In other words, we show that, perhaps somewhat
072   surprisingly, the computation of the Pareto Front can be decoupled from that of the representation,
073   and only relies on learning of much simpler, unconstrained classifiers on the data. To achieve this,
074   we first show that the optimal fair representations satisfy a number of structural properties. While
075
      these properties may be of independent interest, here we use them to express the points on the Pareto
      Front as the solutions of small discrete optimisation problems. The problems fall into the category of
076
      concave minimisation problems Benson (1995), which have been extensively studied, and can be
077
      efficiently solved using modern dedicated optimisation frameworks, Shen et al. (2016).
078
079   We now describe the results in more detail. Let X and A denote the data features and the sensitive
080   attribute, respectively. The sensitive attribute is a part of the data, and we assume that A is binary.
081
      The features X will be taking values in Rd . In addition, we have a target variable Y , taking values in
      a finite set Y. We are then interested in representations that maximise the performance of prediction
082
      of Y , under the fairness constraint. The representation is denoted by Z, and is typically expressed by
083
      constructing the conditional distributions P (Z|X = x, A = a), for a ∈ {0, 1}. The problem setting
084   is illustrated in Figure 1.
085
086   The remainder of the paper unfolds as follows: Algorithm 1 outlines the steps for computing the
087
      Pareto front - learning calibrated probability estimators, constructing relevant histograms, and solving
      the resulting optimization problem using standard solvers.
088
      The theoretical foundation for this approach is built on two key results. The Factorization result
089
      (Section D) demonstrates that instead of working with all features x, we need only consider the
090   distributions P (Y |x), as inputs with identical distributions are equivalent for Pareto front purposes.
091   This crucial insight reduces our representation space from Rd to ∆Y , making discretization more
092   tractable. The Invertibility Theorem (Section 4) complements this by establishing bounds on the
093   representation’s target space - specifically, how many points z are needed in the representation to
094   maintain accuracy.
095
      As for a detailed breakdown of each section : The invertibility theorem states that in an optimal
096   representation, every point z can be generated from at most two x’s, one given A = 0 and an-
097   other given A = 1. That is, there are unique u, v such that P (Z = z|X = u, A = 0) ̸= 0 and
098   P (Z = z|X = v, A = 1) ̸= 0. This in turn hints at a possibility that in an optimal representation,
099   the space of z may be indexed by the pairs (u, v), where u, v denote x’s corresponding to A = 0
100   and A = 1 as above. Indeed, an additional result, the Uniform Approximation Lemma D.1, deferred
101   to Supplementary D due to space constraints, implies that for every pair (u, v), one needs to have
102   only a small number of z’s associated with it, in order to approximate all possible representations.
103   Together, these results bound the number of points needed to model all optimal representations, if X
104
      has finitely many values.
105   Next, in Section 5.1 we show that instead of discretising X as suggested above, one may in fact
106   discretise a much smaller space, i.e., ∆Y —the space of all possible distributions of Y . As an example,
107   if Y is binary, this space is equivalent to the interval [0, 1]. This reduction is possible due to the
      factorisation property of representations. That is, we show that for the purposes of the computation

      of the Pareto front, any representation may be represented as a composition (thus the factorisation
109   terminology) of a mapping into ∆Y , and a representation of ∆Y .
110
111   Based on these results, in Section 5.2 we introduce MIFPO, a discrete optimisation problem for the
112
      computation of the Pareto front, and in Section 5.3 we provide details on estimating the problem
      parameters from the data. The MIFPO problem is a concave minimisation problem with linear
113
      constraints, and we solve it using the disciplined convex-concave programming framework, DCCP,
114
      Shen et al. (2016). It is worth noting that for the special case of perfect fairness, i.e., the point γ = 0
115   on the curve, the MIFPO problem is in fact a linear programming problem, solvable by standard
116   means, and closely related to the Optimal Transportation problem (Supplementary B).
117
118
      We illustrate MIFPO by evaluating it on a number of standard real world fairness benchmark datasets.
      We also compare its fairness-performance curve to that of FNF, Balunović et al. (2022a), a recent
119
      representation learning approach which yields representations with guaranteed fairness constraint
120
      satisfaction. As expected, MIFPO produces tighter fairness-performance curves, indicating that FNF
121   still has space for improvement. This suggests that MIFPO may also be used as an analysis method
122   in the construction of full representation learning algorithms. In addition, we show that MIFPO
123   may also be used to evaluate the fairness-performance Pareto front of fair classifiers (Section 3),
124   where fairness is measured by statistical parity. We thus evaluate two widely used fair classification
125   methods and, similarly, find that their Pareto front is significantly below that of MIFPO.
126
      To summarise, the contributions of this paper are as follows: (a) We derive several new structural
127   properties of optimal fair representations. (b) We use these properties to construct a model indepen-
128   dent problem, MIFPO, which can approximate the Pareto Front of arbitrary high dimensional data
129   distributions, but is much simpler to solve than direct representation learning for such distributions.
130   (c) We illustrate the approach on real world fairness benchmarks.
131
      The rest of this paper is organised as follows: Section 2 discusses the literature. Section 3 introduces
132
      the formal problem setting. The Inveribility Theorem is discussed in Section 4, and the details of the
133   MIFPO construction are given in Section 5. Experimental results are presented in Section 6, and we
134   conclude the paper in Section 7. All proofs are provided in the Supplementary Material.

137   2    L ITERATURE AND P RIOR W ORK
138
139   As mentioned in the previous Section, there exists a variety of approaches to the construction of fair
140   representations. We refer to the book Barocas et al. (2023), and surveys Mehrabi et al. (2021),Du et al.
141
      (2020), for an overview. Notably, the work Song et al. (2019) introduces a variational framework
      that unifies many of the existing methods under a single optimisation procedure. This work also
142
      explicitly deals with the difficulty of obtaining useful fairness-performance trade-offs, although such
143
      considerations are present in practically all fairness related work.
144
145   In a recent paper, Balunović et al. (2022a), it was observed that if one knows the density of the source
146   data, P (X|A = a), a ∈ {0, 1}, and uses models with computable change of variables, such as the
147
      normalising flows, Rezende & Mohamed (2015), then one can verify the total variation fairness
      criterion (see (3)), with high probability. This enables one to provide guarantees on the fairness of
148
      a representation, which is clearly an important feature in applications. Such guarantees also allow
149
      result comparison between different methods. Indeed, if a given constraint is not guaranteed in one of
150   the methods, then the performance comparison may be unfair. Due to this reason, in the experiments
151   Section 6 we compare our own results to those of Balunović et al. (2022a). It may be worthwhile to
152   note, however, that the assumption of knowing the densities P (X|A = a), made in Balunović et al.
153   (2022a), is quite significant. This is due to the fact that such information is normally not given, and
154   the estimation of densities from data is generally a difficult problem. We remark that our approach
155   does not rely on this assumption.
156   Regarding related work dealing with optimal tradeoffs between accuracy and fairness, in Zhao &
157   Gordon (2019) the autors developed a theoretical error bound for the perfecly fair classifier and
158   are not concerned with the full Pareto front, nor addressing fair representation. Dehdashtian et al.
159   (2024),Sadeghi et al. (2022) proposed learning fair representations using neural encoders with RKHS
160   mappings and classifiers, measuring independence-based fairness through RKHS proxies. This
161   approach requires implicit data assumptions, particularly Gaussian projections in Sadeghi et al.
      (2022) in contrast to our method which also provide simple solution. The most related work Wang


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al. (2024) builds on Kim et al. (2020) observation that the classification Pareto front depends
163   solely on classifier confusion matrices. While similar to our factorization result (Section 5.1), their
164   main contribution characterizes these matrices through infinite constraint intersection, proposing
165   an iterative algorithm that progressively adds constraints to solve increasingly constrained Pareto
166   problems. Like our approach, their method learns a probability estimator g(x, a) = P (y|x, a) for
167   constraints. However, we utilize g differently - leveraging optimal fair representation properties
168   to formulate a closed-form problem with finite constraints solvable by standard methods, rather
169   than requiring custom iterative algorithms. Moreover, our approach addresses the broader challenge
170   of representation learning, not just classification. While Wang et al. (2024) handles multi-class
171
      classification and non-binary discrete attributes, their iterative algorithm operates in high-dimensional
      spaces with potentially exponential constraint growth, compared to our solution’s required simplex
172
      ∆Y discretization, especially when implemented thoughtfully using clustering algorithms.

      Fix an integer k ≥ 2. The representation space Z will be a finite set which can be written as
370

371
      triplet (u, v, j), with u ∈ S0 , v ∈ S1 , j ∈ [k]. The motivation for this choice of Z is as follows: First,
372   note the by the invertibility Theorem 4.1, in an optimal representation, every z ∈ Z is associated with
373   a pair of parents, (u, v). Moreover, it can be shown that for every pair (u, v), it is sufficient to have
374   at most k points z which have (u, v) as parents, to approximate all representations on all possible
375   spaces Z ′ , in terms of their E and F quantities, to a given degree ε. Here, k would depend on ε
376   and on the function h, but not on the particular representations, or even the sizes |S0 | , |S1 |, etc. See
377   Section D in Supplementary Material, for additional details. As a result, choosing Z = S0 × S1 × [k]
      should be sufficient to model all representations well. We have used k = 10 in all experiments.

524   7    C ONCLUSIONS , L IMITATIONS , A ND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   In this paper we have uncovered new fundamental properties of optimal fair representations, and
527   used these properties to develop a model independent procedure for the computation of Fairness-
528   Performance Pareto front from data. We have also demonstrated the procedure on real datasets, and
529   have shown that it may be used as a benchmark for representation learning algorithms.
530   We now discuss limitations and a few possible directions for future work. Perhaps the biggest
531   limitation of the current approach is the need to discretise the ∆Y . This means that the Y can only
532   take a small number of values, which, for instance, prevents us from considering multiple target
533   variables, i.e. Y = (Y1 , . . . , Yt ). It is thus of interest to ask whether it is possible to obtain a
534   continuous version of our method, which would not discretise ∆Y . Note that this would still be
535   different from the current continuous methods, since it would operate in a different space, that of ∆Y ,
536
      which could still be significantly smaller than Rd .
537   Finally, as noted earlier, representation learning methods solve an inherently harder problem than
538   MIFPO, since such methods need to supply the actual representation, while MIFPO does not. It then
539   would be of interest to understand whether one can use the partial information provided by MIFPO,
      to guide and improve a full representation learning algorithm.

       G.2    M INIMIZATION OF C ONCAVE F UNCTIONS UNDER C ONVEX C ONSTRAINTS
1164
1165   As described in figure 6, we used the disciplined convex concave programming (DCCP) framework
1166   and the associated solver, (Shen et al., 2016; 2024) for solving the concave minimization with convex
1167   constraints problem.
1168
       Minimizing concave functions under convex constraints is a common problem in optimization theory.
1169
       Unlike convex optimization where global minima can be readily found, in concave minimization
1170   problems we only know that the local minimas lie on the boundaries of the feasible region defined
1171   by the convex constraints. While techniques such as branch-and-bound algorithms, cutting plane
1172   methods, and heuristic approaches are often employed, here we used the framework of DCCP which
1173   gain a lot of popularity in recent years.
1174
       The DCCP framework extends disciplined convex programming (DCP) to handle nonconvex problems
1175
       with objective and constraint functions composed of convex and concave terms. The idea behind a
1176   "disciplined" methodology for convex optimization is to impose a set of conventions inspired by basic
1177   principles of convex analysis and the practices of convex optimization experts. These "disciplined"
1178   conventions, while simple and teachable, allow much of the manipulation and transformation required
1179   for analyzing and solving convex programs to be automated. DCCP builds upon this idea, providing
1180   an organized heuristic approach for solving a broader class of nonconvex problems by combining DCP
1181   principles with convex-concave programming (CCP) methods, and is implemented as an extension to
1182   the CVXPY package in Python.
1183   While convenient, the use of the disciplined framework bears some limitations. Mainly, generic
1184   operations like element-wise multiplication are not under the allowed set of operations (and for
1185   obvious reasons), which limits the usability. Notice, that for the prediction accuracy measure
1186   h(p) = min(p, 1 − p) this is not a problem, but for the entropy classification error h((1 − p, p)) =
1187   −p log p − (1 − p) log(1 − p), this is more challenging. Nevertheless, here we show that the standard
       DCCP framework allows for entropy classification error.
