009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011               We propose two new evaluation metrics to assess realness of generated images
012               based on normalizing flows: a simpler and efficient flow-based likelihood distance
013               (FLD) and a more exact dual-flow based likelihood distance (D-FLD). Because
014               normalizing flows can be used to compute the exact likelihood, the proposed met-
015               rics assess how closely generated images align with the distribution of real images
016               from a given domain. This property gives the proposed metrics a few advantages
017               over the widely used Fréchet inception distance (FID) and other recent metrics.
018               Firstly, the proposed metrics need only a few hundred images to stabilize (con-
019
                  verge in mean), as opposed to tens of thousands needed for FID, and at least a few
                  thousand for the other metrics. This allows confident evaluation of even small sets
020
                  of generated images, such as validation batches inside training loops. Secondly,
021
                  the network used to compute the proposed metric has over an order of magni-
022               tude fewer parameters compared to Inception-V3 used to compute FID, making it
023               computationally more efficient. For assessing the realness of generated images in
024               new domains (e.g., x-ray images), ideally these networks should be retrained on
025               real images to model their distinct distributions. Thus, our smaller network will
026               be even more advantageous for new domains. Extensive experiments show that
027               the proposed metrics have the desired monotonic relationships with the extent of
028               image degradation of various kinds.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
058
      In recent years, several generative adversarial networks (GAN) and diffusion-based image gener-
      ation models have been proposed for various applications, such as inpainting, text-based image
059
      manipulation, and text-to-image generation, that have also caught popular imagination. To compare
060
      individual generated images or the overall performance of various models, a robust evaluation metric
061   is essential in determining how closely the generated images resemble real images. This is crucial
062   for understanding whether generative models can produce images that appear realistic to human
063   observers, which is a key goal in many applications.
064
      Evaluating the quality and performance of image generation models presents a unique challenge.
065
      Unlike traditional vision tasks such as classification or segmentation, there is no theoretically sound
066
      metric to assess performance. This is because generated images require evaluation across multiple
067   dimensions, including quality, aesthetics, realism, and diversity, all of which are deeply rooted in
068   subjective human perception. However, human evaluation is costly and difficult to scale for larger
069   datasets. As a result, researchers rely on automated evaluation techniques to gauge the performance
070   of these models.
071
      In the absence of human evaluation, a good metric of ”fakeness” should robustly and monotonically
072
      increase with the extents of image degradations of various kinds. Additionally, it should be reliable
073   to assess a given generative model even on a small set of generated images. That is, its mean value
074   should converge with a small number of images generated under similar conditions. Plus, evaluating
075   the metric should be computationally light so that it can be used inside the validation step of training
076   iterations.
077
      Among the various methods proposed for the automatic evaluation of generative models, the Fréchet
078
      inception distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) has emerged as the one that is most widely used by
079   practitioners. It approximates the feature distributions of real and generated images as multivariate
080   Gaussians and estimates the distance between them using Fréchet distance (FD). The features used
081   are the activations of the penultimate layer of an Inception-V3 architecture that is pre-trained for
082   classification of the ImageNet dataset. The comparison occurs at object or semantic level, ignoring
083   the fine-grained details of the image. Its simplicity and ease of use have made it a de facto metric in
084   the field despite its known limitations.
085   Experiments on text-to-image models have highlighted that FID is not an ideal metric as it often dis-
086   agrees with human ratings, making it unsuitable for evaluating the quality of generated images (Jaya-
087   sumana et al., 2023). Additionally, statistical tests and empirical evaluation show the limitations of
088   FID in accurately capturing the nuances of image generation (Jayasumana et al., 2023). FID can also
089   be biased depending on the specific model being evaluated. Additionally, it requires a large number
090   of samples to generate a stable and reliable metric value, which can be a limitation in scenarios
091   where fewer samples are available for evaluation or we want to save on computations, such as in a
092   validation step inside a training iteration.
093   The main goal of generative models is to learn a data distribution that closely approximates the real
094   data, allowing them to generate images with a distribution that mirrors the real one. Ideally, the
095   distance between the two distributions should be minimal, indicating that the generated images are
096   highly similar to real images in terms of their underlying statistical properties.
097   Our proposed metrics leverage normalizing flows to approximate the data distribution of both gen-
098   erated and real images. By performing density estimation, they assess how well the distribution of
099   generated images aligns with the distribution of real images, providing a more accurate measure of
100   the similarity between the two.
101
      The main contributions of this paper are:
102
103
104          • We introduce a new method for evaluating the performance of generative models by di-
105            rectly assessing the likelihood of each generated image using normalizing flows. To our
106            knowledge, the use of normalizing flows, which can directly and efficiently compute the
107            likelihood of generated images from the real data distribution, has not been previously
               employed as an evaluation metric.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
              • We propose a theoretical model that employs dual normalizing flows — one trained on
109             generated images and the other on real images — and demonstrate that this approach is
110             monotonic with respect to various distortions, proving to be an effective evaluation metric.
111
112
              • We present an efficient and faster single-model normalizing flow as an evaluation metric,
                showing that it possesses all the essential properties of a good metric. Additionally, it
113
                significantly outperforms FID in terms of speed and provides a stable evaluation using a
114
                smaller number of generated or real images.

117   2     R ELATED W ORKS
118
119   In this section we summarize previous metrics to evaluate generated images and their limitations,
120   and highlight the relevant properties of normalizing flows on which the proposed metrics are based.
121
122   2.1     P REVIOUS M ETRICS AND THEIR L IMITATIONS
123
124   Several evaluation metrics have been proposed for generative models, including inception score
125   (IS) Salimans et al. (2016), kernel inception distance (KID) (Bińkowski et al., 2018), FID (Heusel
126   et al., 2017), perceptual path length (Karras et al., 2021), Gaussian Parzen window (Goodfellow
127
      et al., 2014), clip maximum mean discrepancy (CMMD) (Jayasumana et al., 2023) as well as human
      annotation techniques, such as HYPE (Zhou et al., 2019). Among these, inception score and FID
128
      have gained significant popularity. The IS utilizes an Inception-V3 model trained on ImageNet-1k
129
      to assess the diversity and quality of generated images by analyzing their class probabilities. A key
130   advantage of the Inception Score is that it does not require real images to compute the metric value,
131   making it convenient for certain applications.
132
133
      KID (Bińkowski et al., 2018) and FID both require the presence of real images alongside the gen-
      erated images for evaluation. These metrics are computed by measuring the distance between the
134
      distributions of real and generated data. KID uses the squared maximum mean discrepancy (MMD)
135
      distance, while FID employs the squared FD between the two distributions to assess how well the
136   generated images resemble the real ones.
137
138   IS, FID and KID rely on Inception embeddings, which have been trained on 1.3 million images and
139
      are limited to the 1,000 classes from the ImageNet-1k dataset (Szegedy et al., 2015). This restriction
      limits their ability to effectively represent the richer, more complex, and diverse domain images that
140
      can emerge from modern image generation tasks, making them less suitable for evaluating certain
141
      types of generated content.
142
143   Some of the previous works have highlighted the unreliability of evaluation metrics in image genera-
144   tion, especially pointing out the limitations of FID (Chong & Forsyth, 2019). It was shown that FID
145
      is a biased estimator and can show significant variation in FID scores for low-level image processing
      operations, such as compression and resizing (Parmar et al., 2022).
146
147
148   2.1.1    N ORMALITY A SSUMPTION
149
      In the computation of FID, the calculation of the FD relies on the assumption that the Inception-
150   V3 embeddings are normally distributed. This is because the closed-form solution for the FD only
151   applies to multivariate normal distributions Dowson & Landau (1982). If the embeddings deviate
152   significantly from this assumption, it can affect the accuracy and reliability of the FID score. Since
153   real image sets typically do not follow normal distributions, this assumption can introduce a signif-
154   icant bias in the computation of the FD and, consequently, the FID score. Additionally, estimating
155   2048 × 2048 dimensional covariance matrices from a small sample of generated and real images,
156   which is required for FID computation, can further exacerbate the error, leading to unreliable evalu-
157   ations.
158   This issue is demonstrated by using a 2D isotropic Gaussian distribution at the origin as the refer-
159   ence distribution and measuring the distance between it and a series of mixture-of-Gaussian distri-
160   butions (Jayasumana et al., 2023), as shown in the Table 1. The second set of distributions is gen-
161   erated as a mixture of four Gaussians, each sharing the same mean and covariance as the reference
      Gaussian. This example illustrates how the incorrect normality assumption can lead to misleading

      2.1.2    I MAGE N ET P RE - TRAINED M ODELS
193
194   Previous metrics, e.g., IS, FID, CMMD and KID (Bińkowski et al., 2018), rely on modeling distri-
195   butions of real images based on features extracted using CNNs that are pre-trained on ImageNet.
196   This creates a bias towards the dataset that was used for pre-training, which limits their applicability
197   to other domains, such as medical x-ray images. To accurately represent images in these specialized
198   fields and new domains, new embeddings must be generated by pre-training on domain-specific
199   datasets. This can often be challenging due to the often limited size of such datasets, making it
200   difficult to produce accurate embeddings.
201   Hence, it is preferable to have a metric that relies solely on the available real and generated images
202   to create their respective data distributions. This approach allows for a more accurate comparison
203   of how closely the generated images align with the real image distribution. Our proposed normal-
204   izing flow-based metrics achieve this by directly comparing the distributions without relying on
205   pre-trained embeddings, making it better suited for diverse and specialized domains.
206
207   2.2     N ORMALIZING F LOWS
208
209   Normalizing flows are generative models built on invertible transformations. Among all the gener-
210   ative models that are widely used, such as, GANs, VAEs, and diffusion, normalizing flows are the
211   only type of model for which the likelihood of data can be computed exactly and efficiently (Prince,
212
      2023; Kobyzev et al., 2021). None of the other models explicitly learn the likelihood of the real data.
      While VAEs model a lower bound of the likelihood, GANs only provide a sampling mechanism for
213
      generating new data, without offering a likelihood estimate Kobyzev et al. (2021).
214
215   A normalizing flow is able to generate the likelihood of a given sample x by transforming a complex
      data distribution into a simpler one, typically a Gaussian distribution, through a series of invertible

      4.4   E FFICIENCY
487
488   Earlier work (Chong & Forsyth, 2019; Jayasumana et al., 2023), along with our own experiments,
489   has demonstrated that computing the FID value requires a large number of images to get a stable
490   mean score, which translates to a poor sample efficiency. Our experiments indicate that more than
491   20,000 images are necessary to reliably estimate FID, and using smaller sample sizes results in an
492   unreliable estimate, which can significantly deviate from the true value, as shown Figure 1. Even the
493   metrics that were proposed after FID, such as CMMD, require at least a few thousand samples for
494
      a reliable estimation of a generative model’s average behavior (Jayasumana et al., 2023). Figure 1
      clearly demonstrates that FLD metric achieves high sampling efficiency, as it provides a stable and
495
      reliable metric with just 200 samples. This showcases the robustness and efficiency of FLD com-
496
      pared to other metrics, and makes it attractive even inside training loops, such as during validation
497   steps.
498
499   The model used for normalizing flow in FLD also uses an order of magnitude fewer parameters
500
      (1.9 M) compared to the Inception-V3 model (23.8 M parameters) used in FID computation. Thus,
      FLD can also be trained on edge devices. These advantages makes FLD much more efficient for
501
      evaluation of generative models.
502
503   Just like FID, FLD needs to be trained only once on the real images of a given domain. Once the
504   normalizing flow is trained, evaluation of generated images are fast since we do not need to retrain
505   the normalizing flow.
506
507   C ONCLUSION
508
509   In this paper, we have demonstrated that FID is not a reliable metric for evaluating generative mod-
510   els. Our findings highlight several critical issues with FID, including its requirement of a large
511   number of image samples to provide a reliable estimate, which limits its efficiency and applicability
512   in real-time or low-sample scenarios. Additionally, FID exhibits non-monotonic behavior, particu-
513   larly when dealing with diffusion models and image distortions. To address these limitations, we
514
      have introduced two new metrics based on normalizing flows: dual-FLD and FLD. These metrics
      have proven to be monotonic with respect to image distortions and perform well with diffusion mod-
515
      els. Furthermore, our metrics are highly sample efficient, requiring only a few hundred images to
516
      provide a reliable estimate. This makes them a more practical and effective alternative to FID for
517   evaluating the quality of images generated by modern generative models.
518
519   As a limitation, we advise practitioners to use our metrics with caution for evaluating the perfor-
520
      mance of other generative models, especially those based on normalizing flows. This is because we
      have tested the metric only on images generated by adding distortions to real images and on those
521
      generated by diffusion-based models. Additionally, due to limitations in computational resources,
522
      we were unable to test our metrics on larger generative models, such as text-to-image models, or
523   on more extensive datasets with diverse categories and domains. These remain areas for future
524   exploration and evaluation.
525
526
      We believe that our work will spark further research in the search for better and more efficient
      evaluation metrics for generative models. Additionally, there is potential to optimize normalizing
527
      flow-based evaluation metrics by constructing even more efficient and expressive normalizing flows
528
      or training on more advanced normalizing flows than those we have used in this study. This opens
529   the door for continued improvement in the field of generative model evaluation.
