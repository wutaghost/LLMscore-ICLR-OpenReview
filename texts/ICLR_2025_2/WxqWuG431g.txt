009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sparse autoencoders have recently produced dictionaries of high-dimensional vec-
012            tors corresponding to the universe of concepts represented by large language
013            models. We find that this concept universe has interesting structure at three
014            levels: 1) The “atomic” small-scale structure contains “crystals” whose faces
015            are parallelograms or trapezoids, generalizing well-known examples such as
016            (man:woman::king:queen). We find that the quality of such parallelograms and
017            associated function vectors improves greatly when projecting out global distractor
018
               directions such as word length, which is efficiently done with linear discriminant
               analysis. 2) The “brain” intermediate-scale structure has significant spatial modu-
019
               larity; for example, math and code features form a “lobe” akin to functional lobes
020
               seen in neural fMRI images. We quantify the spatial locality of these lobes with
021            multiple metrics and find that clusters of co-occurring features, at coarse enough
022            scale, also cluster together spatially far more than one would expect if feature ge-
023            ometry were random. 3) The “galaxy” scale large-scale structure of the feature
024            point cloud is not isotropic, but instead has a power law of eigenvalues with steep-
025            est slope in middle layers. We also quantify how the clustering entropy depends
026            on the layer.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The past year has seen a breakthrough in understanding how large language models work: sparse
032   autoencoders have discovered large numbers of points (“features”) in their activation space that can
033   be interpreted as concepts (Huben et al., 2023; Bricken, 2024). Such SAE point clouds have recently
034
      been made publicly available (Lieberum et al., 2024), so it is timely to study their structure at various
      scales. This is the goal of the present paper, focusing on three separate spatial scales. In Section
035
      3, we investigate if the “atomic” small-scale structure contains “crystals” whose faces are paral-
036
      lelograms or trapezoids, generalizing well-known examples such as (man:woman::king:queen). In
037   Section 4, we test if the “brain” intermediate-scale structure has functional modularity akin to bio-
038   logical brains. In Section 5, we study the “galaxy” scale large-scale structure of the feature point
039   cloud, testing whether it is more interestingly shaped and clustered than an isosropic Gaussian dis-
040   tribution.
041

      2   R ELATED WORK
044
045
      SAE feature structure: Sparse autoencoders have relatively recently garned attention as an ap-
      proach for discovering interpretable language model features without supervision, with relatively
046
      few works examining SAE feature structure. Bricken et al. (2023) and Templeton et al. (2024) both
047
      visualized SAE features with UMAP projections and noticed that features tend to group together in
048   “neighborhoods” of related features, in contrast to the approximately-orthogonal geometry observed
049   in the toy model of Elhage et al. (2022). Engels et al. (2024) find examples of SAE structure where
050   multiple SAE features appear to reconstruct a multi-dimensional feature with interesting geometry,
051   and multiple authors have recently speculated that SAE vectors might contain more important struc-
052   tures (Mendel, 2024; Smith, 2024). Bussmann et al. (2024) suggest that SAE features are in fact
053   linear combinations of more atomic features, and discover these more atomic latents with “meta
      SAEs”. Our discussion of crystal structure in SAE features is related to this idea.

054
      Function vectors and Word embedding models: Early word embedding methods such as GloVe
055   and Word2vec, were found to contain directions encoding semantic concepts, e.g. the well-known
056   formula f(king) - f(man) + f(woman) = f(queen) (Drozd et al., 2016; Pennington et al., 2014; Ma &
057   Zhang, 2015). More recent research has found similar evidence of linear representations in sequence
058   models trained only on next token prediction, including Othello board positions (Nanda et al., 2023;
059   Li et al., 2022), the truth value of assertions (Marks & Tegmark, 2023), and numeric quantities
060   such as longitude, latitude, birth year, and death year (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Heinzerling &
061   Inui, 2024). Recent works have found causal function vectors for in-context learning (Todd et al.,
062   2023; Hendel et al., 2023; Kharlapenko et al., 2024), which induce the model to perform a certain
063
      task. Our discussion of crystal structures builds upon these previous works of finding task vectors
      or parallelogram structures in language models.

083   In this section, we search for what we term crystal structure in the point cloud of SAE features. By
084   this we mean geometric structure reflecting semantic relations between concepts, generalizing the
085   classic example of (a, b, c, d)=(man,woman,king,queen) forming an approximate parallelogram
086
      where b − a ≈ d − c. This can be interpreted in terms of two function vectors b − a and c − a
      that turn male entities female and turn entities royal, respectively. We also search for trapezoids
087
      with only one pair of parallel edges b − a ∝ d − c (corresponding to only one function vector);
088
      Fig. 1 (right) shows such an example with (a, b, c, d)=(Austria,Vienna,Switzerland,Bern), where
089   the function vector can be interpreted as mapping countries to their capitals.
090
091   We search for crystals by computing all pairwise difference vectors and clustering them, which
092
      should result in a cluster corresponding to each function vector. Any pair of difference vectors in a
      cluster should form a trapezoid or parallelogram, depending on whether the difference vectors are
093
      normalized or not before clustering (or, equivalently, whether we quantify similarity between two
094
      difference vectors via Euclidean distance or cosine similarity).
095
096   Our initial search for SAE crystals found mostly noise. To investigate why, we focused our attention
097   on Layers 0 (the token embedding) and 1, where many SAE features correspond to single words. We
098
      then studied Gemma2-2b residual stream activations for previously reported word 7→ word function
      vectors from the dataset of (Todd et al., 2023), which clarified the problem. Figure 1 illustrates
099
      that candidate crystal quadruplets are typically far from being parallelograms or trapezoids. This
100
      is consistent with multiple papers pointing out that (man,woman,king,queen) is not an accurate
101   parallelogram either.
102
103   We found the reason to be the presence of what we term distractor features. For example, we find
104
      that the horizontal axis in Figure 1 (right) corresponds mainly to word length (Appendix B, Figure
      10), which is semantically irrelevant and wreaks havoc on the trapezoid (left), since “Switzerland”
105
      is much longer than the other words.

      dimensional subspace orthogonal to them. For the (Todd et al., 2023) dataset, we do this with

139   4   “B RAIN ” SCALE : M ESO - SCALE MODULAR STRUCTURE
140
141   We now zoom out and look for larger-scale structure. In particular, we investigate if functionally
142   similar groups of SAE features (which tend to fire together) are also geometrically similar, forming
143   “lobes” in the activation space.
144   In animal brains, such functional groups are well-known clusters in the 3D space where neurons are
145   located. For example, Broca’s area is involved in speech production, the auditory cortex processes
146   sound, and the amygdala is primarily associated with processing emotions. We are curious whether
147   we can find analogous functional modularity in the SAE feature space.
148
      We test a variety of methods for automatically discovering such functional “lobes” and for quantify-
149
      ing if they are spatially modular. We define a lobe partition as a partition of the point cloud into k
150   subsets (“lobes”) that are computed without positional information. Instead, we identify such lobes
151   based on then being functionally related, specifically, tending to fire together within a document.
152
153
      To automatically identify functional lobes, we first compute a histogram of SAE feature co-
      occurrences. We take gemma-2-2b and pass documents from The Pile Gao et al. (2020) through
154
      it. At a given layer, we record the SAE features that fire (for the GemmaScope JumpReLU SAEs,
155
      we count a feature as firing if its hidden activation > 1). Features are counted as co-occurring if
156   they both fire within the same block of 256 tokens – this length provides a coarse “time resolution”
157   allowing us to find tokens that tend to fire together within the same document rather than just at the
158   same token. We use a max context length of 1024, and only use one such context per document, giv-
159   ing us at most 4 blocks (and histogram updates) per document of The Pile. We compute histograms
160   across 50k documents. Given this histogram, we compute an affinity score between each pair of
161   SAE features based on their co-occurrence statistics and perform spectral clustering on the resulting
      affinity matrix.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      We experiment with the following notions of co-occurrence-based affinity: simple matching coeffi-
163   cient, Jaccard similarity, Dice coefficient, overlap coefficient, and Phi coefficient, which can all be
164   computed just from a co-occurrence histogram. In the Appendix A.1, we review definitions for each
165   of these and illustrate how the choice between them affects the resulting lobes.
166
      Our null hypothesis is that functionally similar points (of commonly co-occurring SAE features) are
167
      uniformly distributed throughout the activation space, showing no spatial modularity. In contrast,
168
      Figure 2 shows lobes that appear visually quite spatially localized. To quantify how statistically
169   significant this is, we use two approaches to rule out the null hypothesis.
170
171         1. While we can cluster features based on whether they co-occur, we can also perform spec-
172            tral clustering based on the cosine similaity between SAE feature decoder vectors. Given
173            a clustering of SAE features using cosine similarity and a clustering using co-occurrence,
174            we compute the mutual information between these two sets of labels. In some sense, this
175            directly measures the amount of information about geometric structure that one gets from
176            knowing functional structure. We report the adjusted mutual information Vinh et al. (2009)
177
               as implemented by scikit-learn Pedregosa et al. (2011), which corrects for chance agree-
               ments between the clusters.
178
179         2. Another conceptually simple approach is to train models to predict which functional lobe
180            a feature is in from its geometry. To do this, we take a given set of lobe labels from our
181
               co-occurrence-based clustering, and train a logistic regression model to predict these labels
               directly from the point positions, using an 80-20 train-test split and reporting the balanced
182
               test accuracy of this classifier.
183
184   Figure 4 shows that for both measures, the Phi coefficient wins, delivering the best correspondence
185   between functional lobes and feature geometry. To show that this is statistically significant, we
186   randomly permute the cluster labels from the cosine similarity-based clustering and measure the ad-
187   justed mutual information. We also randomly re-initialize the SAE feature decoder directions from a
188   random Gaussian and normalize, and then train logistic regression models to predict functional lobe
189   from these feature directions. Figure 4 (bottom) shows that both tests rule out the null hypothesis
190   at high significance, at 954 and 74 standard deviations, respectively), clearly demonstrating that the
191   lobes we see are real and not a statistical fluke.
192   To assess what each lobe specializes in, we run 10k documents from The Pile through gemma-2-2b,
193   and run an SAE on the model’s activations at the layer 12 residual stream. Within blocks of 256-
194   tokens, we record the SAE features which fire. We then record which lobe has the highest proportion
195   of its features firing on that block of tokens. Each document in The Pile is attached with a name
196   specifying the subset of the corpus that document is from. For each document type, for each 256-
197   token block within a document of that type, we record which lobe which had the highest proportion
198
      of its SAE features firing. Across thousands of documents, we can then look at a histogram of which
      lobes were maximally activating across each document type. We show these results for three lobes,
199
      computed with the phi coefficient as the co-occurrence measure, in Figure 3. This forms the basis
200
      for our lobe labeling in Figure 2.
201
202   The effects of the five different co-occurrence measures are compared in Fig. 5. Although we found
203   Phi to be best, all five are seen to discover the “code/math lobe”.

351   5.1   S HAPE ANALYSIS
352
353   Figure 6 (left) quantifies this by showing the eigenvalues of the point cloud’s covariance matrix in
354   decreasing order. revealing that they are not constant, but appear to fall off according to a power law.
355
      To test whether this surprising power law is significant, the figure compares it with the corresponding
      eigenvalue spectrum for a point cloud drawn from an isotropic Gaussian distribution, which is seen
356
      to be much flatter and consistent with the analytic prediction: the covariance matrix of N random
357
      vectors from a multivariate Gaussian distribution follow a Wishart Distribution, which is well studied
358   in random matrix theory. Since the abrupt dropoff seen for the smallest eigenvalues is caused by
359   limited data and vanishes in the limit N → ∞, we dimensionally reduce the point clound to its 100
360   largest principal componends for all subsequent analysis in this section. In other words, the point
361   cloud has the shape of a “fractal cucumber”, whose width in successive dimensions falls off like a
362   power law. We find such power law scaling is significantly less prominent for activations than for
363   SAE features; it will be interesting for further work to investigate its origins.

      6   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      In this paper, we have found that the concept universe of SAE point clouds has interesting struc-
435   tures at three levels: 1) The “atomic” small-scale structure contains “crystals” whose faces are par-
436   allelograms or trapezoids, generalizing well-known examples such as (man:woman::king:queen),
437   and get revealed when projecting out semantically irrelevant distractor features. . 2) The “brain”
438   intermediate-scale structure has significant spatial modularity; for example, math and code features
439   form a “lobe” akin to functional lobes seen in neural fMRI images. 3) The “galaxy” scale large-scale
440   structure of the feature point cloud is not isotropic, but instead has a power law of eigenvalues with
441   steepest slope in middle layers. We hope that our findings serve as a stepping stone toward deeper
442   understanding of SAE features and the workings of large language models.
