009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The remarkable success of Large Language Models (LLMs) across diverse tasks has
012            driven the research community to extend their capabilities to molecular applications.
013            However, most molecular LLMs employ adapter-based architectures that do not
014            treat molecule and text modalities equally and lack a supervision signal for the
015            molecule modality. To address these issues, we introduce UniMoT, a Unified
016            Molecule-Text LLM adopting a tokenizer-based architecture that expands the
017            vocabulary of LLM with molecule tokens. Specifically, we introduce a Vector
018
               Quantization-driven tokenizer that incorporates a Q-Former to bridge the modality
               gap between molecule and text. This tokenizer transforms molecules into sequences
019
               of molecule tokens with causal dependency, encapsulating high-level molecular
020
               and textual information. Equipped with this tokenizer, UniMoT can unify molecule
021            and text modalities under a shared token representation and an autoregressive
022            training paradigm. It can interpret molecules as a foreign language and generate
023            them as text. Following a four-stage training scheme, UniMoT emerges as a multi-
024            modal generalist capable of performing both molecule-to-text and text-to-molecule
025            tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that UniMoT achieves state-of-the-art
026            performance across a wide range of molecule comprehension and generation tasks.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The incredible capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
032   2023) have led to their widespread use as versatile tools for completing diverse real-world tasks.
033
      This success has sparked interest in Multi-modal LLMs (Zhan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023), which
      aim to enhance LLMs by enabling them to process multi-modal inputs and outputs. Prior research
034
      efforts (Liang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;
035
      Luo et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024) have focused on adapting LLMs to molecular tasks, resulting in
036   the development of molecular LLMs. These molecular LLMs can analyze molecule structures (Luo
037   et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b; Cao et al., 2023), address drug-related inquiries (Liang et al., 2023;
038   Tang et al., 2023), assist in synthesis and retrosynthesis planning (Fang et al., 2023), support drug
039   design (Fang et al., 2023), and more.
040
      Prevalent molecular LLMs commonly employ adapter-based architectures, adopting either a linear
041
      projection (Liang et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023) or a Q-Former (Liu et al., 2023b; Li
042   et al., 2024) as an adapter to translate molecule features into the semantic space of LLM, as illustrated
043   in Figure 1a and Figure 1b. Despite demonstrating initial capabilities in molecular comprehension and
044   yielding promising results in molecule-to-text generation tasks, they still fall short in text-to-molecule
045   generation tasks. This limitation arises because adapter-based architectures require the LLM to
046   directly generate SMILES strings (Weininger, 1988) for molecule generation tasks. SMILES is a
047   text-based representation of molecular structures where atoms and bonds are encoded as linear strings.
048   Adapter-based architectures depends heavily on a strong alignment between SMILES strings and text
049   captions. However, as shown in Figure 1a and Figure 1b, the molecule and text modalities are not
050
      treated equally in these architectures, and there is a lack of supervision for the molecule modality. As
      a result, achieving proper alignment between molecules and text becomes challenging.
051
052   Discretizing continuous molecule features into discrete molecule tokens offers a promising solution
053   for conducting both molecule-to-text and text-to-molecule generation tasks. By treating tokens from
      different modalities equally, we can predict the next molecule or text token in an autoregressive

      To this end, we present UniMoT, a Unified Molecule-Text LLM that adopts a tokenizer-based
077
      architecture, integrating molecule comprehension and generation, as depicted in Figure 1c. A pivotal
078
      aspect of UniMoT’s architecture is the molecule tokenizer for transforming molecules into molecule
079   tokens. We introduce a Vector Quantization-driven (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) tokenizer, which
080   incorporates a Q-Former (Li et al., 2023) to bridge the modality gap between molecules and text.
081   Specifically, we incorporate causal masks for the queries, enabling the Q-Former to generate a causal
082   sequence of queries compatible with the unidirectional attention in LLMs. The sequence of queries is
083   subsequently quantized into a sequence of molecule tokens using a learnable codebook. The molecule
084   tokens encapsulate high-level molecular and textual information, which are then aligned with the
085   latent space of a pretrained generative model via an MLP adapter.
086   Pretrained LLMs can integrate the molecule tokenizer by treating molecule tokens as new words
087   and constructing a molecule vocabulary through mapping the learned codebook. We adopt the
088   unified discrete token representation for molecules and text, coupled with the unified next-token-
089   prediction training paradigm of LLM. This unification of representation and training paradigm enables
090   effective molecule-text interactions and alignment through molecule-to-text and text-to-molecule
091   autoregressive pretraining. For molecule generation tasks, UniMoT generates molecule tokens in an
092   autoregressive manner rather than producing SMILES strings, and these molecule tokens can then be
093
      decoded into molecules using the generative model.
094   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
095
096        • We introduce a molecule tokenizer specifically designed for LLMs, enabling the tokenization
097          of molecules into short sequences of molecule tokens with causal dependency. These tokens
098          encapsulate high-level molecular and textual information and can be decoded into desired
099
             molecules during inference.
100        • We present UniMoT, a unified molecule-text LLM that adopts a tokenizer-based architecture
101          instead of traditional adapter-based architectures. UniMoT unifies the modalities of molecule
102          and text under a shared token representation and an autoregressive training paradigm. Following
103          a four-stage training scheme, UniMoT effectively achieves molecule-text alignment.
104
           • UniMoT exhibits remarkable capabilities in multi-modal comprehension and generation. Ex-
105          tensive experiments show that UniMoT achieves state-of-the-art performance across a wide
106          range of molecule comprehension and generation tasks, while also offering a new perspective
107          on molecule generation.

      Molecular Large Language Models. The recent emergence of Vision Large Language Models
111   (VLLMs) (Li et al., 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2024a) has catalyzed advancements in molecular LLMs,
112   which encompass both single modality and multi-modality approaches. In the single modality
113   domain, researchers are exploring diverse molecule representations, such as 1D sequences like
114   SMILES strings (Wang et al., 2019; Chithrananda et al., 2020; Irwin et al., 2022), 2D molecule
115   graphs (Hu et al., 2019b; You et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2022), 3D geometric conformations (You et al.,
116   2020; Liu et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022), and textual information from the literature (Taylor et al.,
117   2022; Beltagy et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2020). In the multiple modalities domain, various innovative
118   approaches are being employed. MolT5 (Edwards et al., 2022), a T5-based (Raffel et al., 2020)
119
      model, is designed for SMILES-to-text and text-to-SMILES translations. Other works, such as
      MoMu (Su et al., 2022), MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a), MolFM (Luo et al., 2023a), and GIT-
120
      Mol (?), leverage cross-modal contrastive learning to align the representation spaces of molecules
121
      and text. Additionally, some studies use multi-modal learning architectures to develop molecular
122   LLMs, which often adopt adapter-based architectures. For instance, InstructMol (Cao et al., 2023),
123   GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2023), and DrugChat (Liang et al., 2023) employ a simple projection layer to
124   map molecule features to LLM’s input space. MolCA (Liu et al., 2023b) and 3D-MoLM (Li et al.,
125   2024) utilize a Q-Former (Li et al., 2023) to bridge the modality gap between molecules and text.
126   However, these methods do not treat molecule and text modalities equally and lack a supervision
127   signal for the molecule modality, limiting model capacity and effectiveness.
128
129   Vector Quantization. Vector Quantization (VQ) (Gray, 1984) is a widely used technique in
130   generative models. VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) converts an image into a set of discrete
131
      codes within a learnable discrete latent space by learning to reconstruct the original image. VQ-
      GAN (Yu et al., 2021) enhances the generation quality by leveraging adversarial and perceptual
132
      objectives. In the context of molecules, VQ has been effectively applied to quantize molecule features.
133
      For example, DGAE (Boget et al., 2023) introduces a VQ model specifically for molecules, where
134   molecules are encoded into discrete latent codes. Mole-BERT (Xia et al., 2022) uses VQ to rethink
135   the pre-training of GNNs for molecular tasks. IMoLD (Zhuang et al., 2024) proposes using VQ to
136   enhance invariant molecule representations, and VQSynergy (Wu et al., 2024) demonstrates the use
137   of VQ for drug discovery.

      Our objective is to leverage the reasoning and generation capabilities of LLMs to enhance the com-
142   prehension and generation of molecule and text data. To achieve this, we focus on representing these
143   modalities uniformly within the token representation, utilizing the next-token-prediction training
144   paradigm of LLMs. As illustrated in Figure 2, we introduce a molecule tokenizer (Section 3.1)
145   designed to transform molecules into molecule tokens by learning to reconstruct the input molecule.
146   The molecule sequence can then be concatenated with the text sequence to form a multi-modal
147   sequence, which is fed into an LLM for molecule-to-text and text-to-molecule autoregressive pre-
148   training (Section 3.2), as illustrated in Figure 3. The LLM vocabulary is expanded with molecule
149   tokens mapped from the learned codebook. We introduce a four-stage training scheme for Uni-
150
      MoT (Section 3.3) comprising Causal Q-Former pretraining, molecule tokenizer pretraining, unified
      molecule-text pretraining, and task-specific instruction tuning. UniMoT is capable of performing
151
      molecule comprehension and generation tasks following the training scheme.

276   3.3   T RAINING S TRATEGY
277
278   The training strategy for UniMoT is structured across four stages. Stage-1 focuses on Causal Q-
279   Former pretraining with tailored objectives. In Stage-2, the molecule tokenizer is optimized using the
280
      frozen encoders and decoder. Stage-3 integrates the tokenizer with a language model for multi-modal
      comprehension and generation. Finally, Stage-4 fine-tunes UniMoT for specific tasks, aligning it with
281
      human instructions and optimizing performance for various molecular applications. More details
282
      regarding the training process can be found in Appendix C.
283
284
      Stage-1: Causal Q-Former Pretraining. We connect the molecule encoder and Causal Q-Former,
285   leveraging the pretrained MoleculeSTM molecule encoder (Liu et al., 2023a). The molecule encoder
286   remains frozen while only the Causal Q-Former is updated. Both queries and text inputs are
287   used, while only queries serve as input in subsequent stages. In our experiments, we utilize 16
288   queries. We employ three tailored objectives for the pretraining of the Causal Q-Former: Molecule-
289   Text Contrastive Learning (MTC), Molecule-Text Matching (MTM), and Molecule-grounded Text
290   Generation (MTG). The details of these objectives can be found in Appendix A.
291
292   Stage-2: Molecule Tokenizer Pretraining. We connect the Causal Q-Former with subsequent
293   blocks and use the objective defined in Equation (2). We employ the pretrained ChemFormer (Irwin
294
      et al., 2022) as the generative model. Specifically, we leverage the SMILES encoder and the SMILES
      decoder provided by ChemFormer. The molecule codebook size is set to K = 2048. As shown in
295
      Figure 2, we keep the molecule encoder, the SMILES encoder, and the SMILES decoder frozen,
296
      while updating the Causal Q-Former, the learnable codebook, and the adapter.
297
298
      Stage-3: Unified Molecule-Text Pretraining. We integrate the molecule tokenizer with the LLM
299   using the unified vocabulary of molecule tokens and text tokens. We employ the LM objective
300   defined in Equation (3) to pretrain the LLM. Pretraining involves molecule-to-text autoregression
301   and text-to-molecule autoregression, aimed at enhancing UniMoT’s multi-modal comprehension
302   and generation capabilities. To enhance efficiency, we train the LLM using low-rank adaptation
303   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021).
304
305   Stage-4: Task-Specific Instruction Tuning. UniMoT is fine-tuned on seven comprehension and
306   generation tasks: molecular property prediction, molecule captioning, molecule-text retrieval, caption-
307   guided molecule generation, reagent prediction, forward reaction prediction, and retrosynthesis. We
308
      also utilize LoRA to improve efficiency. This stage ensures UniMoT can accurately interpret and
      respond to human instructions, making it versatile and effective for diverse molecular tasks.

399   4.2   M OLECULE G ENERATION TASKS
400
401   We employ molecule generation tasks, which encompass caption-guided molecule generation (Fang
402
      et al., 2023), reagent prediction (Fang et al., 2023), forward reaction prediction (Fang et al., 2023), and
      retrosynthesis (Fang et al., 2023). Caption-guided molecule generation involves generating molecular
403
      structures based on textual descriptions. Reagent prediction entails determining suitable reagents
404
      given reactants and products. Forward reaction prediction involves predicting probable products
405   given specific reactants and reagents. Retrosynthesis involves deconstructing a target molecule into
406   simpler starting materials. We compare UniMoT with the following baselines: Llama (Touvron et al.,
407   2023), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), Mol-Instructions (Fang et al., 2023), and InstructMol (Cao et al.,
408   2023). The metrics used to evaluate molecule generation tasks include Exact Match, BLEU (Papineni
409   et al., 2002), Levenshtein Distance (Levenshtein et al., 1966), RDKit Fingerprint Similarity (Lan-
410   drum et al., 2006), MACCS Fingerprint Similarity (Durant et al., 2002), and Morgan Fingerprint
411   Similarity (Morgan, 1965). These metrics evaluate structural similarity between generated and target
412   molecules, along with Validity (Kusner et al., 2017), which assesses the proportion of chemically
413
      valid molecules generated.
414   We utilize the Mol-Instructions (Fang et al., 2023) benchmark to evaluate the generation capabilities
415   of UniMoT, and the results are presented in Table 4. The caption-guided molecule generation task,
416   the reverse of molecule captioning, is conducted using the PubChem (Kim et al., 2023) dataset, while
417   the other tasks utilize the USPTO (Fang et al., 2023) dataset. As the baselines generate SMILES
418   strings and then convert them to molecules, UniMoT directly leverages the generated molecule
419
      tokens and obtains their embeddings from the learned codebook. These embeddings can be decoded
      to desired molecules through the pretrained adapter and SMILES decoder. As shown in Table 4,
420
      UniMoT generates valid molecules with a higher degree of similarity to the target molecules compared
421
      to the baselines. This is because UniMoT can generate molecules as if they were text, which is
422   fundamentally different from adapter-based architectures. UniMoT demonstrates strong generation
423   capabilities and offers a new perspective on molecule generation tasks.

515   5      C ONCLUSION
516
517   This work introduces UniMoT, a framework that unifies the modalities of molecules and text. By
518   adopting a tokenizer-based architecture, UniMoT addresses previous limitations where the molecule
519   and text modalities are not treated equally. The molecule tokenizer converts molecules into sequences
520   of discrete tokens, embedding high-level molecular and textual information. The LLM vocabulary
521   is expanded with molecule tokens mapped from a learned codebook. Through a four-stage training
522
      scheme, UniMoT has become a versatile multi-modal LLM, capable of handling both molecule-
      to-text and text-to-molecule tasks. Extensive empirical evaluations show that UniMoT achieves
523
      state-of-the-art performance across diverse molecule comprehension and generation tasks.
524
525
      Limitations. Although UniMoT demonstrates strong performance in molecule-to-text and text-
526
      to-molecule tasks, it has not been extensively tested on more complex molecule generation tasks,
527   such as molecule editing, which require precise modifications to molecular structures. Additionally,
528   due to the limited availability of annotated data in the molecular domain, UniMoT’s training is less
529   extensive compared to fields like computer vision. This limitation hinders the model’s ability to fully
530   learn and generalize from molecular structures and properties. Addressing the data scarcity in the
531   molecular domain is crucial for enhancing UniMoT’s training effectiveness and overall capabilities.
532   Furthermore, current evaluations are primarily conducted on standard datasets and benchmarks.
533   Expanding these evaluations to include a wider range of real-world scenarios will provide a more
534   comprehensive understanding of the model’s robustness and generalizability.

      The Q-Former operates as a query-based transformer that utilizes learnable query vectors to interact
759
      with molecule features extracted by a frozen encoder. These queries are essential for extracting
760
      relevant information from the molecule features. The Q-Former comprises both a molecule trans-
761   former and a text transformer, sharing self-attention layers. The molecule transformer incorporates
762   cross-attention layers between self-attention and feed-forward layers, while the text transformer
763   architecture is based on BERT (Devlin et al., 2018). Q-Former employs a cross-attention mechanism
764   where the query vectors selectively attend to different aspects of the molecule features, allowing the
765   model to capture critical details necessary for understanding and generating textual descriptions of
766   molecular properties.
767   Specifically, we incorporate causal masks into the queries, ensuring that they only interact with
768   preceding queries. This ensures the sequence of queries maintains a causal dependency, aligning
769   with the requirements of LLMs operating on text sequence. The Causal Q-Former is illustrated

948   Stage-1: Causal Q-Former Pretraining. During Stage-1, we only connect the molecule encoder
949
      and the Causal Q-Former, leaving out other blocks. We leverage the pretrained molecule encoder
      from MoleculeSTM (Liu et al., 2023a), which has undergone extensive contrastive learning with
950
      molecule-text pairs. We utilize the PubChem (Kim et al., 2023) dataset for pretraining, keeping the
951
      molecule encoder frozen while updating only the Causal Q-Former. Both queries and text serve as
952   input to the Causal Q-Former, while only queries serve as input in subsequent stages. Inspired by
953   BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), we employ three tailored objectives – Molecule-Text Contrastive Learning
954   (MTC), Molecule-Text Matching (MTM), and Molecule-grounded Text Generation (MTG) – for the
955   pretraining of the Causal Q-Former, as detailed in Appendix A.
956
      The dimension of molecule features is set to 300. We use 16 queries, each with a dimension of 768.
957
      The size of Z (16 × 768) is much smaller than the size of molecule features X (e.g., 150 × 300). The
958   Q-former is pretrained for 50 epochs. We adopt the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of 0.05,
959   and a cosine decay learning rate scheduler, with a minimal learning rate of 1e-5. The batch size is set
960   to 64. The computational overhead for this pretraining is 20 GPU hours on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs.

965   molecules, and employ the pretrained ChemFormer (Irwin et al., 2022) as the generative model.

967   ChemFormer. We utilize PubChem (Kim et al., 2023) and ChEBI-20 (Edwards et al., 2022) datasets,
968
      keeping the molecule encoder, SMILES encoder, and SMILES decoder frozen, while updating the
      Causal Q-Former, codebook, and adapter. Once optimized, the molecule tokenizer remains unchanged
969
      throughout the subsequent stages.

1121

1122   generation involves creating molecular structures from textual descriptions, leveraging NLP and

1124   focuses on identifying suitable reagents for given reactants and desired products, optimizing synthetic
1125   routes. Forward reaction prediction forecasts probable products from specific reactants and reagents,
1126   using knowledge of chemical reactivity. Retrosynthesis deconstructs target molecules into simpler

1132   machine translation, measures the overlap of n-grams (short sequences of atoms or bonds) between
1133   generated and target molecules, thus assessing partial similarities. Levenshtein Distance (Levenshtein

       Multi-modal Large Language Models. With the rapid advancement of Large Language Models
1265
       (LLMs), current multi-modal LLMs are typically built on a pre-trained LLM backbone and equipped
1266
       with the ability to understand multiple modalities. LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a) uses a simple linear
1267   projection to connect the image encoder with the LLM backbone. In contrast, BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023)
1268   uses CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to extract high-level features from images and employs a Q-Former
1269   to reduce the number of image tokens. These models demonstrate strong multi-modal comprehension
1270   abilities but often overlook the important aspect of multi-modal generation. Consequently, recent
1271   research has focused on unifying multi-modal comprehension and generation within a single model,
1272   enabling the generation of multi-modal tokens. Emu (Sun et al., 2023) and Emu2 (Sun et al., 2024)
1273   introduce a unified autoregressive objective: predicting the next multi-modal element by regressing
1274   visual embeddings or classifying text tokens. CM3Leon (Yu et al., 2023) and Chameleon (Team, 2024)
1275
       train token-based autoregressive models on mixed image and text data. SEED-LLaMA (Ge et al.,
       2023) proposes a new image tokenizer aligned with the LLMs’ embedding space. AnyGPT (Zhan
1276
       et al., 2024) and MIO (Wang et al., 2024) construct any-to-any multi-modal language models that use
1277
       discrete tokens for unified processing across various modalities. Inspired by these developments in
1278   multi-modal LLMs, we introduce a tokenizer-based architecture in the molecule-text domain. This
1279   architecture discretizes molecule features into tokens compatible with LLMs, enabling molecules to
1280   be processed alongside text tokens.
