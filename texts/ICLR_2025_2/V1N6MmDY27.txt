009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Autonomous Vehicle (AV) technology has been heavily researched and sought af-
012            ter, yet there are no SAE Level 5 AVs available today in the marketplace. We con-
013            tend that over-reliance on machine learning technology is the main reason. Use of
014            automated commonsense reasoning technology, we believe, can help achieve SAE
015            Level 5 autonomy. In this paper, we show how automated commonsense reason-
016            ing technology can be deployed in situations where not enough data is available
017            to train a machine learning model for autonomous driving. Specifically, we con-
018
               sider two situations where (i) a traffic signal is malfunctioning at an intersection
               and (ii) all the cars ahead are slowing down and steering away due to an unex-
019
               pected obstruction (e.g., animals on the road). We show that in such situations,
020
               our commonsense reasoning-based solution performs correctly. We also provide
021            a pathway for efficiently invoking commonsense reasoning by measuring uncer-
022            tainty in the computer vision model and using commonsense reasoning to handle
023            uncertain scenarios. We describe our experiments conducted using the CARLA
024            simulator and the results obtained. The main contribution of our research is to
025            show that automated commonsense reasoning provides an effective pathway to
026            reach SAE level 5 automation of autonomous driving.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   AV systems have made significant progress over the course of the past several years. Due to the
032   nature of vehicles and road scenarios, it is highly critical that AV systems are perfectly accurate
033
      and can accommodate for uncertainties in driving situations such as obstacles, pedestrians, and
      objects not visible to the camera. These systems must also be able to adjust for road layouts and
034
      positions that may not have been encountered in training. A single mistake in classification can
035
      lead to accidents and casualties. However, an optimal AV system can assist or control many driving
036   aspects, such as sensing, path planning, speed control, navigating, and parking, reducing accidents
037   caused by human error (Parekh et al. (2022)). This ideal is difficult to achieve since AV systems are
038   heavily dependent on deep learning technologies and may miss-classify due to uncertain variables
039   on the road. Therefore, we believe that any improvement to the classification of these systems is
040   significant to the development of safe and accurate AV vehicles.
041
      One ideal for an AV system is to imagine it as a perfect human driver—one who is able to reason
042   like a human who does not commit any errors in judgment or perception. This type of system can be
043   considered as making use of Visual Question Answering (VQA) which gives answers to questions
044   about the visual scenario around the vehicle. Such a system would be effective for both explaining
045   decisions made by an autonomous vehicle to a human controller. However, neural network ap-
046   proaches to explaining visual scenarios tend to work by simply matching word descriptions to the
047   data (Johnson et al. (2017)). Similar issues can be found in Large Language Models which can hal-
048   lucinate, or generate irrelevant information, when answering questions. These systems heavily rely
049   on matching a response to the query and lack true understanding of the context of the scenario. In a
050
      safety critical system, we must avoid making mistakes. We believe that commonsense reasoning can
      be used to properly model human thinking/reasoning (Gelfond & Kahl (2014); Kowalski (2011)).
051
052   We propose a system that will correct misclassification in the object detection phase of an au-
053   tonomous vehicle model. The system consists of a base deep learning autonomous vehicle model
      with a logic program (Bratko (2012)) layer on top that is used to improve the output. Commonsense

      reasoning, realized through logic programming, is used to detect potential inconsistencies in the
055   classifications of the objects in the scenario. The logic program will then output the inconsistency
056   and the explanation for why it believes there to be an issue. We then make adjustments to the output
057   based on the suggested inconsistency to improve the overall accuracy of the computer vision model.
058   In this paper, we will explore the class of possible improvements we can make by observing the
059   collective behavior of vehicles around us to correct misclassification over traffic lights and obstacles
060   in the road. One can model other complex situations that arise during driving in a similar way, re-
061   sulting in nearly all misclassifications being detected and rectified, paving the way to SAE Level 5
062   autonomy.
063   The organization of our paper is as follows: Section 2 will cover background topics relating to AV
064   systems, Commonsense reasoning and its relation to targeted AV scenarios, and deep learning tech-
065   niques used to handle perception. Section 3 will motivate and contextualize our solution. Section
066   4 will describe our methodology and how we connect the logic to the perception for each of our
067   targeted scenarios. Sections 5 will describe the experiments and evaluate the results. Section 6 cov-
068   ers recent related works in the field and will compare our approach to them. Finally, Section 7 will
069
      conclude, summarize the paper, and motivate our future work.

072   2   BACKGROUND
073
074
      Autonomous Vehicles: Research in autonomous vehicles has experienced massive growth due to
075
      the popular development of deep learning and sensor research (Yurtsever et al. (2020)) causing
076   an increase in advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) in research and commercial vehicles
077   (Van Brummelen et al. (2018)). However, there still exist major gaps in AV research that prevent it
078   from achieving full automation, such as disagreements in optimal perception techniques, algorithms
079   that lack efficiency and accuracy, and the inability to handle difficult road conditions or inclement
080   weather (Yurtsever et al. (2020)).
081
      Here we outline some of the formal definitions for autonomous vehicles that we will use to motivate
082   the rest of the paper. SAE International defines the SAE Levels of Driving Automation into six over-
083   all levels (Committee (2021)) ranging from no driving automation to full driving automation, with
084   levels 3-5 being defined as automated driving systems (ADS). These higher levels of automation
085   have been heavily explored in recent AV research, though no SAE Level 5 vehicles have been real-
086   ized. One of the issues preventing full automation is imperfect perception and sensing technologies,
087   but another major issue lies in the higher-level aspects of driving.
088   Autonomous vehicle technology must deal with four major issues: (i) environment perception and
089   modeling, (ii) localization and map building, (iii) path planning and decision-making, and (iv) mo-
090   tion control. We relate these aspects to the two types of systematic thinking—System 1 and System
091   2—proposed by Kahneman in 2011 (Kahneman (2011)). System 1 thinking is fast, intuitive, and
092   reflexive, while System 2 thinking is slow and deliberative. Deep learning and computer vision tech-
093   niques are a very effective and explored approach to handling the first two aspects of autonomous
094   vehicles, as they are heavily based on pattern matching and System 1 thinking. However, the last
095   two aspects require high level reasoning and are more in line with System 2 thinking. Many ap-
096
      proaches to AV development consist solely of deep learning techniques for all four aspects, but there
      is a lot of potential in a hybrid solution that integrates vision and semantics (Suchan et al. (2020);
097
      Kothawade et al. (2021)). To this end, we motivate a hybrid approach using deep learning perception
098
      and commonsense reasoning. In this work, we will be simulating AV scenarios using the CARLA
099   (Car Learning to Act) simulator (Dosovitskiy et al. (2017)). CARLA is an open platform that allows
100   control over sensor suites and environmental factors.
101
102   Commonsense Reasoning: To achieve an ideal SAE level 5 autonomous vehicle, the system must
103   be able to comprehend information and reason as a human would. This approach makes use of com-
104   monsense reasoning to emulate how a human would approach driving. This reasoning is performed
105   over commonsense knowledge that we humans possess. Commonsense reasoning is able to handle
106   uncertainties in our perception as well as fill in the gaps. Our approach relies on the observation that
107   humans drivers use perception to detect objects (roads, pedestrians, other cars, curbs, traffic lights,
      lane markings, etc.) in the scene and then use (commonsense) reasoning to make driving decisions.

108
      Commonsense reasoning is modeled with default rules with exceptions (Gelfond & Kahl (2014)).
109   This represents the human way of thinking about the world around us. For example, a default rule
110   about driving could be ”Drivers will typically stop at a red light”. However, human knowledge will
111   contain exceptions for those default rules, such as ”Irresponsible drivers don’t always stop at red
112   lights”. This allows commonsense reasoning to handle uncertainty by creating a conclusion with
113   given information and then updating that conclusion based on new incoming information.
114
      The strength of commonsense knowledge and commonsense reasoning in an autonomous driving
115   scenario is its ability to handle multiple scenarios without the need for retraining for each constrained
116   scenario. We propose that commonsense reasoning can be layered on top of the computer vision
117   model and make adjustments to the output based on detected inconsistencies between objects as new
118   knowledge is observed in the world around an AV. For this experiment, commonsense knowledge
119   is hand-coded as logic programs to build the knowledge base needed. The logic programs cover
120   only the scenarios within our scope, specifically solving misclassifications of traffic lights and road
121   obstacles using collective behavior. In the following sections, we give a high level description of
122   how commonsense reasoning—that emulates human behaviour—can be applied to two different
123
      road scenarios.

125   3   M OTIVATION
126
127
      While deep learning-based vision and control has seen success in AV technology, a hybrid sys-
128   tem brings potential improvements in explainability, human-centred design, and compliance with
129   human-defined industry standards (Suchan et al. (2021)). We motivate this paper by defining the
130   differences between human behavior and commonplace AV behavior.
131
      Deep learning is a powerful technology, especially for computer vision. However, due to external
132
      factors such as noise, camera or sensor degradation, inclement weather, unusual road scenarios, etc.,
133
      can cause misclassifications in perception. Perception can also fail in training, and due to the rare
134   nature of accidents, it is possible that an AV does not have sufficient experience with a specific
135   scenario. Due to the black box nature of such systems, such faults in the training may only be
136   noticed after the scenario is encountered, often leading to an accident. In a safety-critical system,
137   such accidents are completely intolerable. It is better to create a set of human-defined commonsense
138   rules that protect the AV when it gets into trouble due to such factors.
139
      We take inspiration from the human approach to missing information on the road to write these
140   rules. Like an AV system, it is possible for a human driver to misinterpret information on the road.
141   However, for a human driver, it is possible to use the existing context to reason over the scenario and
142   still make a safe decision. In this paper, we represent how humans look at nearby drivers to better
143   understand a driving scenario. For example, a human driver understands that by default, vehicles are
144   supposed to stop at a stop sign. However, if a human driver notices that a group of vehicles ahead
145   aren’t stopping for an incoming stop sign, then the stop sign was likely misinterpreted. This ability
146   to reason over incorrect information allows human drivers to avoid potential accidents. We can use
147   this commonsense reasoning approach to correct missing or incorrect information in the perception
148
      system to achieve 100% accuracy. This is because the commonsense rules are human-defined and
      can be continuously refined to completely represent an expert human driver.
149
150   Traffic Lights: The crux of the commonsense reasoning for these two scenarios that we consider is
151   observation and reasoning over collective behavior. Our goal is to emulate what a human would do
152   in a given scenario. If a human driver can’t see what color an upcoming traffic light is, or if there is
153   a traffic light at all, then they will observe the vehicles around them to come to a conclusion. Our
154   commonsense reasoning module emulates human decision-making. If the vehicles traveling in the
155   same direction as the ego vehicle are stopped, then our logic will say that there is a reason to stop;
156   either a stop sign or a red traffic light is present ahead. If the model detects a collective movement
157   from one of the lanes of traffic across the intersection, then the logic knows there is a traffic light
158
      at this intersection. Alternatively, if the model detects a collective ”stop and go” pattern across the
      intersection, then the logic knows there is a stop sign.
159
160   A demonstration can be seen in figure 1. The perception, due to some noise or filter on the camera,

      of vehicles on the other side of the intersection making a left turn. This information is logically

181   Obstacles: The logic for reasoning over potential obstacles is also based on collective behavior.
182   The model detects cluster event changes, meaning it can tell when multiple vehicles change their
183   action within one location. The logic will attempt to give a reason for this action change. For
184   example, vehicles could be turning while we are near an intersection. If no reason can be given, then
185
      logic determines this to be an inconsistency, meaning that it is possible that vehicles are collectively
      changing their action due to an obstacle.
186
187   This is similar to how humans would determine potential obstacles while driving. For example, if
188   there is some construction in the left lane and they don’t happen to see it, they would know that
189   something is odd if they suddenly see all the vehicles in front of them change to the rightmost lane
190   when there is no intersection or turn nearby. In this scenario, even if they don’t immediately see
191
      the construction, they would slow down to see what the problem is or naturally change into the
      rightmost lane, assuming that there is something blocking people from driving in the left lane.
192
193   In figure 1, we can see an example scenario where an obstruction on the road is not seen by the
194   camera. This is an example of a scenario with missing or hidden information not caught by the
195   perception model. In the image, a vehicle is blocking the obstruction from view. However, the
196   camera does catch the vehicle and the vehicles before it makes a lane change to the left (a collective
197
      behavior). A human driver would notice the collective lane change and the slowdown of traffic and
      reason that there must be something blocking the right lane. An AV with commonsense knowledge
198
      should be able to generate the new knowledge that there is an obstruction there, even without the
199
      perception model being able to see it.

202   4   M ETHODOLOGY
203
204   Sensors commonly used in autonomous vehicles, such as cameras, LiDAR, radar, IMU, and GNSS,
205   only produce low-level information, which is difficult to directly use in a commonsense reasoning
206
      system. In order to obtain high-level information that is more helpful to the models, we used several
      deep learning models.
207
208   Birds-eye-view (BEV) semantic segmentation is a cutting-edge technique widely used in au-
209   tonomous driving. Given the image captured from surrounding cameras installed on an autonomous
210   vehicle, BEV semantic segmentation model transforms those images into a top-down perspective
211   and classifies them into predefined categories, such as roads, vehicles, pedestrians, and other ob-
212
      jects. Compared to camera space semantic segmentation, the BEV semantic segmentation offers a
      clear and comprehensive view of the surroundings, enabling better decision-making processes.
213
214   In this paper, we consider Lift-splat-shoot (Philion & Fidler (2020)) as the BEV semantic segmen-
215   tation model. The model takes images captured by six cameras installed around the vehicle as input
      and outputs a BEV semantic segmentation containing four classes: vehicles, drivable area, lane


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      makes, and others. In CARLA, we mounted six cameras on top of the ego vehicle at six different
217   angles: -120, -60, 0, 60, 120, and 180. Each camera has a field-of-view (FOV) of 90 degrees. The
218   birds-eye-view image is a 500 × 500 RGB image, representing a 100m × 100m range, where the
219   ego vehicle is located at the center of the image. The ground truth birds-eye-view image for training
220   is created in Carla by mounting a camera 50 meters above the ego vehicle facing straight down, with
221   a FOV of 90 degrees.
222
223
      Traffic Light Detection: Traffic light information is very important for decision-making at inter-
      sections. Many traffic light poles contain multiple groups of traffic lights, each group corresponding
224
      to different directions and lanes. Depending on which lane our ego vehicle is in and which direction
225
      we want to go, we may need to look at different groups of lights. While detecting the state of the
226   lights is relatively easy, understanding the meaning of the light can be challenging. In this paper, we
227   consider a simpler setting; we only detect the traffic light that is impacting the ego vehicle. In other
228   words, we’re trying to figure out whether we should go or stop at the traffic light.
229
      For this task, we trained a CNN that takes the front camera image to do a binary classification; the
230
      positive class means that the ego vehicle is being impacted by a red traffic light and thus it should
231
      stop, and the negative class means the vehicle is not being impacted by a red traffic light and it can
232   go.
233
234   Carla provides an API ”Vehicle.get traffic light()” which will return the state of the traffic light that
235
      is affecting the vehicle. When multiple vehicles are being affected by a traffic light, only the first
      vehicle is able to get the correct state, so we implemented a function to fetch the traffic light state of
236
      the first vehicle in a lane, and we used the return value of this function as the ground truth.
237
238   Behavior Detection: In order to detect collective behavior, we first need to detect the individual
239   behavior of the vehicles. We defined 6 categories of events: ChangeLaneLeft, ChangeLaneRight,
240   Straight, TurnLeft, TurnRight, and LaneFollow. In these six events, Straight, TurnLeft, and Turn-
241   Right can only happen at an intersection.
242
      The behavior prediction model is a classification model based on ”resnext50 32x4d”. For each
243   obstacle vehicle in the BEV range, we translate and rotate the BEV image so the obstacle vehicle is
244   located at the center of the image and heading up. Then we predict the behavior of the vehicle.
245
246
      Ground truth behavior is obtained by looking at the future trajectory of the vehicle. For example,
      if the vehicle is tuning right at an intersection, we mark the behavior of the vehicle to TurnRight
247
      from when it starts to turn until it finishes. With the knowledge of the future trajectory, we wrote a
248
      few simple pattern-matching rules to obtain the ground truth behavior. The model was then trained
249   based on the collected ground truth data.
250
251   Behavior Clustering: Within a time window, if multiple vehicles close to each other perform the
252   same behavior, we treat this group as a cluster. With the individual behavior known, we can detect
253   behavior clustering with classical clustering algorithms. To obtain behavior clusters, we consider
254   a 20-second sliding time window. In the time window, for each category of behaviors, we use
255
      DBSCAN on the location of where the behavior starts to try to form a cluster. DBSCAN eps is set
      to 2.7, and the minimum cluster size is set to 2. If a cluster is found, we save the information about
256
      the cluster for use in reasoning.
257
258   Uncertainty Prediction: The uncertainty of the deep learning models is evaluated with evidential
259   deep learning (Sensoy et al. (2018)). Evidential deep learning estimates the aleatoric and epistemic
260   uncertainty of an underlying model by replacing its output class probability with a Dirichlet distri-
261   bution. We adopted the traffic light detection model and BEV semantic segmentation model and
262   calculated both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty. Traffic light detection uncertainty is a numerical
263   value, and BEV uncertainty is obtained at pixel level.
264
      Commonsense Reasoning: The main contribution proposed by this paper is using a commonsense
265   reasoning layer to perform consistency checking over the deep learning model. The commonsense
266   reasoning models commonly held information about how nearby vehicles behave when presented
267   with certain traffic scenarios.
268
269   Preprocessing for Commonsense Reasoning: The deep learning model outputs a large amount
      of image and object data to represent each road scenario. For the dataset used in this experiment,

      CARLA, we largely format the data in a similar manner so that the connection between the deep
271   learning model and the logic program is the same. Each frame contains information about each
272   object, such as vehicles, traffic lights, lanes, and intersections. A script is used to convert the data
273   from these objects into facts that can be used by the commonsense reasoning module. The deep
274   learning model will furthermore make predictions about the actions and the action change clusters
275   of vehicles for each scenario. These predictions are also loaded as logic facts representing each
276   object, which form the commonsense knowledge base. The results of these predictions are reasoned
277   over, and if inconsistencies exist with the predictions, then the commonsense reasoning model will
278   detect those inconsistencies.
279
      General Procedure: Our approach has four major steps. In general, our goal is to represent percep-
280
      tion information as knowledge, and give feedback to the AV by reasoning over that knowledge.
281
282
      Step 1: Define commonsense reasoning rules to reason about the possible explanations for each
              specific scenario (e.g., traffic light, obstacle, stop signs).
283
284   Step 2: Perform commonsense reasoning about the relations between the collective behaviors of
285           all vehicles and non-vehicle objects predicted by the autopilot deep learning model and
286           identify the violated rules. Our experiments consider two approaches to this step: allowing
287
              the commonsense reasoning to be active the whole time the system is running, and using
              uncertainty to invoke the commonsense reasoning only on frames where it is needed.
288
289   Step 3: Use the violated rules to identify potential false predictions by assuming the detected collec-
290           tive behaviors are true. For example, if the collective behavior is that vehicles will change
291           to a different lane when they are near a region of a specific lane and far away from inter-
292
              sections, there is only one explanation: there is an obstacle in that region. However, no
              obstacles were detected by the deep learning model in that region. Then this explanation
293
              is violated, so we can predict that there should be an obstacle in that region, which is a
294
              misclassification of the deep learning model.
295
296
      Step 4: Make adjustments to the non-vehicle objects predicted by the deep learning model to make
              the scenario consistent again.

      Experimental Setup: For each scenario, we compare the commonsense reasoning result with the
302   baseline model output and generate a final result. The final metrics are generated by comparing the
303   final results with the ground truth data. For traffic light scenarios, we evaluate the color of the traffic
304   lights, and for obstacle scenarios, we evaluate the detection of the obstacle. We perform evaluations
305   over data collected from the CARLA simulator. We use recordings from the simulator to evaluate
306   our approach for traffic light and obstruction scenarios. In each scenario, we compare the accuracy,
307   precision, recall, and F-measure of the hybrid model with the baseline model. Additionally, we
308   consider two types of commonsense reasoning layers: the fully active commonsense reasoning layer
309   and the uncertainty-invoked commonsense reasoning layer.
310

311
      light scenarios are shown in Table 1. While the logic layer depends on the deep learning system for
312   perception, the evaluation of our target scenarios and objects is separate. This means that for the
313   traffic light scenario, the logic layer uses all of the data provided by the deep learning model except
314   for the state of the traffic light. As such, we evaluate the effectiveness of both our baseline detection

457   5.3   TAKEAWAY /D ISCUSSION /S IGNIFICANCE
458
459   The results from the experiments reveal that applying a feedback layer based on commonsense
460
      reasoning is an effective method for handling misclassifications in our perception model. In every
      experiment for the traffic light scenario, we saw an improvement from the baseline perception model
461
      to the combined model. The effect of external factors on the baseline perception model, such as in-
462
      clement weather conditions in this case, can be heavily mitigated. A limitation of this experiment is
463   the imperfect logic. Like we argued earlier, it is possible to achieve 100% accuracy within scenarios
464   captured by the commonsense reasoning. However, in our experiment, the accuracy of the logic
465   model was around 95% and reached as low as 70%. But despite this, we can still see an increase
466   in performance from the baseline model to the combined model. This is because the commonsense
467   reasoning module will only provide an adjustment in scenarios that are eligible for the logic. The
468   experiment also demonstrated that uncertainty can be used to invoke commonsense reasoning in a
469   more efficient manner while still improving the accuracy of the system.

471   6     R ELATED W ORK
472
473
      As research moves forward with improving autonomous vehicles, there is more and more work being
474   done on similar hybrid artificial intelligence systems to the one proposed in this paper. Our approach
475   features improving the knowledge extraction and visual question-answering aspects of autonomous
476   vehicles using collective behaviors; however, similar work has been done recently.
477
      For example, the work done by Kanapram et al. (Kanapram et al. (2020)) explores collective aware-
478
      ness in a network of agents. They explore how collective behaviors can be detected in a connected
479
      system of autonomous vehicles. These collective behaviors are modeled using Dynamic Bayesian
480   Networks and used to identify abnormal situations on the road. This approach uses a statistical rela-
481   tional approach as opposed to a logical approach to identify abnormal scenarios, but it demonstrates
482   a similar concept in using collective behaviors to handle unusual road scenarios. Both approaches
483   are able to generate information about future states based on current information about our scenario.
484   Our approach, however, more closely models human reasoning and can provide a more readable ex-
485   planation of the scenario, as we are not relying on conditional distributions to identify abnormalities.
      We also solely rely on camera data to detect abnormal scenarios, as opposed to needing information


                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      from a group of agents. This does come at the cost of scalability. This sort of agent based approach
487   to identifying collective behavior is also explored by Schwarting et al. (Schwarting et al. (2019)).
488   They designed a system that measures Social Value Orientation to predict the behaviors of vehicles
489   on the road. Their approach is similar to ours in that they use the behaviors of vehicles to gener-
490   ate new information about the current road scenario. We agree with their assertion that using the
491   behaviors of other vehicles in an AV system allows it to be more intelligent and socially aware.
492
      Research into more logical hybrid systems has also been explored in recent years. Bannour et al.
493   (Bannour et al. (2021)) propose a symbolic model-based approach to generating logical scenarios
494   for autonomous vehicles. Suchan et al. have also explored many models (Suchan et al. (2020; 2021))
495   that combine commonsense reasoning with AVs to create more human-centred, explainable, and eth-
496   ical vehicles. Their work more closely explores general applications of ASP-based hybrid perception
497   systems, while ours demonstrates a specific commonsense-based optimization. The advantage of our
498   approach is that we can apply our technique to existing AV systems since the commonsense reason-
499   ing is decoupled from the perception. Another work, called AUTO-DISCERN, by Kothawade et al.
500   (Kothawade et al. (2021)) proposes a framework that automates AV decision-making using answer
501
      set programming (ASP). Their work focuses on handling the decision-making process using ASP,
      while our approach uses commonsense reasoning as a method of improving an AV system. In this
502
      sense, our approach is more viable for real-world applications for AV systems. With our approach,
503
      we can generate a commonsense base for important safety-critical scenarios and always show an im-
504   provement in the decisions created by deep learning-based AVs, as opposed to needing to generate
505   and invoke a large commonsense reasoning base that represents all possible driving decisions and
506   scenarios. This is further emphasized by the fact that AUTO-DISCERN was also not evaluated over
507   any simulators, as we did with CARLA, and their evaluations were ad hoc.
508
      There is significant research on quantifying these uncertainties in DL models, typically designed for
509
      i.i.d. inputs like images and tabular data. Many existing methods leverage the concept of Bayesian
510   model averaging overall multiple forwrad passes to estimate distributions over weights or multi-
511   ple models, such as deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017)) and dropout-based Bayesian
512   neural networks (BNNs) (Gal & Ghahramani (2016)). However, the requirement of excessive mem-
513   ory and computation burden for training and testing makes it impossible for real-time applications.
514   Recently, a new class of models has been proposed based on the concept of Evidential Deep Learn-
515   ing Ulmer et al. (2023). They allow uncertainty quantification in a forward pass and with a single
516   set of weights by parameterizing the distributions over distributions.

520   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
521
522
523   We assert that an accurate logic model serves as an effective method for improving the accuracy
524   of autonomous vehicle models through consistency checking. This is indeed demonstrated by our
525
      experiments. Because the layers are well separated, we can easily adjust the logical layer to maintain
      high accuracy without touching any black box models that it is layered on top of. We additionally
526
      demonstrated that uncertainty can be used to more efficiently invoke commonsense reasoning. As
527
      such, our system reveals an adjustable, explainable, and effective way to perform optimizations for
528   autonomous vehicles. Our future research will focus on building a scalable knowledge base to cover
529   the entire class of possible misclassifications for an autonomous vehicle. We also plan to evaluate
530   the effectiveness of our approach on real-world datasets and systems.
531
      Additionally, a desirable extension to this work is to use Answer-Set Programming (ASP) (Gelfond
532
      & Kahl (2014)) to create a more sophisticated modeling of the commonsense knowledge explored
533
      in this paper. Commonsense reasoning is heavily based on human reasoning techniques such as
534   defaults, constraints, exceptions, abduction, etc., (Gelfond & Kahl (2014)) which can be represented
535   more precisely using ASP. Our future research will involve modeling different possible worlds based
536   on missing information in perception and generating more thorough explanations for a misclassifi-
537   cation. Our ultimate goal is to build an AV system that layers commonsense reasoning on top of
538   perception to achieve 100% accuracy resulting in SAE Level 5 autonomy. The research reported
539   in this paper proves that this is indeed possible. Thus, our main contribution is to demonstrate that
      perception models augmented with commonsense reasoning can lead to SAE Level 5 autonomy.
