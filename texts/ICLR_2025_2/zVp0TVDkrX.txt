010
011
               Time series anomaly detection has garnered considerable attention across diverse
               domains. While existing methods often fail to capture the underlying mechanisms
012            behind anomaly generation in time series data. In addition, time series anomaly
013            detection often faces several data-related inherent challenges, i.e., label scarcity,
014            data imbalance, and complex multi-periodicity. In this paper, we leverage causal
015            tools and introduce a new causality-based framework termed CaPulse, which
016
               “tunes in” to the underlying “causal pulse” of time series data to effectively detect
               anomalies. Concretely, we begin by building a structural causal model to decipher
017            the generation processes behind anomalies. To tackle the challenges posed by the
018            data, we propose Periodical Normalizing Flows with a novel mask mechanism and
019            carefully designed periodical learners, creating a periodicity-aware, density-based
020            anomaly detection approach. Extensive experiments on seven real-world datasets
021
               demonstrate that CaPulse consistently outperforms existing methods, achieving
               AUROC improvements of 3% to 17%, with enhanced interpretability. Our source
022            code is available at this anonymized repository: https://anonymous.4open.
023            science/r/iclr25-s622/aiops/CaTAD/README.md.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027   Time Series Anomaly Detection (TSAD) has gained significant attention in recent years (Darban
028   et al., 2024) due to its applications across diverse domains such as network security (Ahmed et al.,
029   2016), finance (Takahashi et al., 2019), urban management (Bawaneh & Simon, 2019), and cloud
030   computing services (Ren et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2024a). Traditional TSAD methods, including one-
031   class support vector machines (Schölkopf et al., 2001) and kernel density estimation (Kim & Scott,
032   2012), rely heavily on handcrafted features and struggle to handle high-dimensional time series data
033
      effectively. In contrast, Deep Learning (DL)-based approaches have recently emerged, significantly
      improving detection performance thanks to their powerful representation learning capabilities (Ruff
034
      et al., 2018; Sabokrou et al., 2018; Goyal et al., 2020).
035
036   Despite their promise, DL-based methods for TSAD face several key limitations. Mechanistically,
037   they often overlook the underlying patterns and processes behind anomalies generation in time se-
038   ries data, leading to models that lack interpretability and exhibit limited generalization capabilities.
039
      Causal inference (Pearl et al., 2000) provides a powerful platform for investigating the underlying
      causal systems, with successful integration in DL methods demonstrated across various tasks (Lv
040
      et al., 2022; Zhao & Zhang, 2024). Specifically, by incorporating a causal perspective, models can
041
      uncover the true factors driving anomalies, rather than relying solely on statistical dependencies or
042   superficial correlations. This shift toward causal-based methods not only improves generalization –
043   making models more robust in Out-of-Distribution (OoD) scenarios (Yang et al., 2022) – but also
044   significantly enhances interpretability, providing deeper insights into the root causes of anomalies.
045   This is particularly essential for downstream tasks such as root cause analysis, where pinpointing
046   the specific factor responsible for an anomaly, like identifying a server overheating or a hardware
047   malfunction causing system downtime in cloud services, becomes critical for timely and effective
048   intervention (Li et al., 2022). Yet, there is still substantial potential for further exploration of causal
049   methods in TSAD.
050   In addition to the mechanical aspect, intrinsically, anomaly detection in time series is challenged
051   by three critical characteristics in terms of data themselves: label scarcity, data imbalance, and
052   multiple periodicities. In practice, e.g., in industrial systems or cloud computing platforms (Zhang
053   et al., 2024), acquiring labeled anomalies is often impractical due to the significant manual effort
      and cost required (Chen et al., 2024b). Even when labels are available, datasets typically consist of

      RT , where each τt ∈ R indicates the likelihood of an anomaly at time t. For evaluation, we consider
      a corresponding set of labels y 1:T = {y1 , · · · , yT } ∈ RT , where yt ∈ {0, 1} indicates whether a
106
      time point is normal (yt = 0) or anomalous (yt = 1). For conciseness, we refer to X 1:T as X, y 1:T
107
      as y, and τ 1:T as τ in the rest of the paper.

110   Time Series Anomaly Detection (TSAD) has advanced from traditional statistical meth-
111

112

113

115   Chen et al., 2024b). Density-based methods (Dai & Chen, 2022; Zhou et al., 2023b) provide robust
116   performance across scenarios. Recently, large-scale pre-trained models such as AnomalyLLM (Liu
117   et al., 2024) and AnomalyBERT (Jeong et al., 2023) have emerged. Yet, most methods focus on
118   statistical dependencies, often overlooking the underlying generation process behind anomalies.
119
      Causal Inference (CI) (Pearl et al., 2000; Glymour et al., 2016) seeks to investigate causal relation-
120   ships between variables, ensuring robust learning and inference. Integrating DL techniques with CI
121   has shown great promise in recent years, especially in computer vision (Zhang et al., 2020; Lv et al.,
122   2022), natural language processing (Roberts et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2022), and spatio-temporal
123   data mining (Xia et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). In the realm of sequential data, CI is often lever-
124   aged to address temporal OoD issues by learning disentangled seasonal-trend (Woo et al., 2022) or
125   environment-specific representations (Yang et al., 2022) to enhance forecasting accuracy. Though
126   promising, the intrinsic causal mechanisms in TSAD differ from the prediction problem, and the
127   application of CI in this domain remains in its early stages.

      3.2   C AUSAL BACKING : I NDEPENDENCE R EQUIREMENT
172
      Based on the SCM in Figure 2b, our aim is to detect anomalies by identifying their true underlying
173   causes while eliminating the influence of non-causal factors, i.e., modeling Pθ (y|do(U ), C). The
174   do(·) operator, as defined in do-calculus, signifies an intervention on the variable (Glymour et al.,
175   2016). Directly modeling this operator is challenging because it necessitates learning various latent
176   causes C from the raw input X (Arjovsky et al., 2019). Inspired by a previous work (Lv et al.,
177   2022), we alternatively leverage a couple of widely-used principles from the causal theory to force
178   the representation of causal factors C we learned to satisfy following key properties.
179   Common Cause Principle (Reichenbach, 1991) posits that for two statistically dependent variables
180   X and Y , there exists a variable C that causally influences both, thereby explaining their depen-
181   dence by rendering them conditionally independent when conditioned on C. Accordingly, the SCM
182   depicted in Figure 2b can be formalized as X := f (C, U ) and y := h(C) = h(g(X)), where
183   C ⊥ U . Here, f , h, and g denote unknown structural functions that describe how the observed
184   variables X and y are generated from the underlying causes C and the non-causal variable U . This
185   leads to our first property for C: it should be independent of U . In this way, for any distribution
186   P (X, y), given the causal factor C, there exists a conditional distribution P (y|C) that represents
187
      the invariant mechanism triggering the anomaly within time series.
188   Independent Causal Mechanisms (Schölkopf et al., 2012; Peters et al., 2017) suggest that the con-
189   ditional distribution of each variable, given its causes, does not influence other causal mechanisms.
190   In other words, none of the factorization of C entails information of others (Lv et al., 2022). Thus
191   it enforces the mutual independence of the causal factors C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , cN }, where N is the
192   number of latent causal factors.
193   Therefore, instead of directly learning the causal factors C, we enforce them to satisfy the following
194   requirements: R1) C should be independent of U , i.e., C ⊥     ⊥ U , and R2) the components of C
195   should be mutually independent, i.e., c1 ⊥ ⊥ c2 ⊥⊥ ... ⊥⊥ cN .

198   4     M ODEL I NSTANTIATIONS
199
200   To implement these causal independent requirements into practice, we develop a causality-based
201   TSAD framework termed CaPulse to tune in to the underlying causal rhythms of time series data,
202   as illustrated in Figure 3. Meanwhile, in light of the common challenges in TSAD, we carefully
203
      designed the modules in CaPulse to enable it not only to effectively implement these causal solutions
      but also to address key data-related challenges. In this section, we begin with an overview of the
204
      proposed framework, followed by a detailed explanation of each component.
205
206   Overview. The input time series X is first augmented to generate X ′ via adding noise on their
207   high-frequency components. Both X and X ′ are subsequently passed through the Periodicity-aware
208   Cause Miner (PaFM) module to obtain Cp and Cp′ , i.e., the pyramid representations of latent causal
209
      factors at different frequencies. PaFM also outputs the amplitude weights for each frequency, de-
      noted as wp and wp′ . Next, the Multi-period Cause Fusion (MpCF) module fuses information across
210
      different periods based on the amplitude weights and a plugged attention mechanism to generate
211
      the omni representations Co and Co′ . A similarity loss Lsim ensures the consistency between these
212   two representations. Then the final representation Cind is obtained by averaging them. To ensure the
213   independence of the learned causal factors, we impose an orthogonal loss Lind . After that, Periodical
214   Normalizing Flows (PeNF) takes X, the global period pg (obtained by Fast Fourier Transform), and
215   Cind as inputs to estimate the density of X by learning a sequence of invertible transformations,
      mapping X into a simpler distribution P (Z), optimized through the loss Lnf .

      In this paper, we present the first attempt to take a causal perspective for TSAD and implement it
      within a deep learning framework. Concretely, building on the proposed SCM, we introduce Ca-
532
      Pulse, a causality-driven deep learning model designed to detect anomalies by leveraging causal
533
      tools while addressing key challenges in TSAD, including label scarcity, data imbalance, and multi-
534   ple periodicities. Extensive experiments on seven datasets across five domains demonstrate CaPulse
535   is equipped to effectively detect both subtle and significant deviations, enhancing interpretability and
536   robustness. A potential limitation of CaPulse is its reliance on the assumption that anomalies lie in
537   low-density regions, which may not always hold in complex real-world scenarios — for instance, in
538   high-frequency trading data where significant anomalies may cluster in high-density regions during
539   market events or crashes. Future work could explore relaxing these distributional assumptions and
      incorporating reversible transformations to generate synthetic anomalies.

812
      Normalizing Flows. Normalizing Flows (NFs) (Tabak & Turner, 2013; Papamakarios et al., 2021)
813   are a powerful technique for density estimation, widely utilized in tasks such as image genera-
814   tion (Papamakarios et al., 2017). Essentially, NFs are invertible transformations that map data from
815   an input space RD to a latent space RD , such that a complex distribution PX on the input space
816   X ∈ RD is transformed into a simpler distribution PZ in the latent space Z ∈ RD . These map-
817   pings, F : X 7→ Z, are typically constructed as a series of invertible functions. By utilizing the
818   change of variables formula, the probability density function PX (X) is expressed as:
819

820                                                                               ∂F(X)
821                                             PX (X) = PZ (Z) det                            ,                                      (7)
                                                                                   ∂X
822
823
      where ∂F∂X (X)
                      is the Jacobian matrix of the transformation F at X. NFs offer two key advantages:
824
      both the inverse transformation X = F −1 (Z) and the computation of the Jacobian determinant can
825
      be efficiently computed, with the determinant calculation typically taking O(D) time. This enables
826   the following expression for the log-likelihood of the data under the flow:
827
828                                     log PX (X) = log PZ (Z) + log | det(∂Z/∂X)|.                                                  (8)
829
830   Temporal Conditioned Normalizing Flows. To adapt NFs for time series data, temporal condi-
831   tioned flows introduce additional conditional information, denoted as h ∈ RDh , which may have a
832   different dimension from the input. The flow is now expressed as F : RD × RDh → RD , allowing
833
      for conditioning on temporal features. The log-likelihood of the time series X, conditioned on the
      temporal context h, is given by:
834
835                             log PX (X|h) = log PZ (F(X; h)) + log | det(∇X F(X; h))|.                                             (9)

837   Coupling Layers. One of the key innovations in NFs proposed by a widely-used variant Real-
838   NVP (Dinh et al., 2017) is the use of coupling layers, which simplify the computation of the Jaco-
839   bian determinant. In a coupling layer, part of the input remains unchanged, while another part is
840   transformed. Specifically, the transformation is defined as:
841                          1:d

                               d+1:D                                                                (10)
843                          Y         = X d+1:D ⊙ exp(Sθ (X 1:d )) + Tθ (X 1:d ),

      where ⊙ represents element-wise multiplication, S(·) is a scaling function, and T (·) is a translation
845
      function, both parameterized by θ. The coupling layer thus enables efficient transformations by only
846   modifying part of the input at a time. To achieve complex, nonlinear density mappings, multiple
847   coupling layers are stacked, alternating which dimensions are transformed at each layer. This en-
848   sures that all dimensions are transformed over the course of the flow, while keeping computations

      We design PaCM and MpCF to handle multiple local periodicities in time series data by extracting
865   and fusing periodic information at various levels, illustrated in Figure 9a and 9b, respectively.
866   Inspired by TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), PaCM is responsible for capturing different period levels
867   of information from the input time series X. PaCM first embeds the input time series X, followed
868   by a linear transformation to project the input into a higher-dimensional space. Next, an FFT is
869   applied to obtain the frequency components {f1 , f2 , . . . , fk } and their corresponding periodicities
870   {p1 , p2 , . . . , pk } and the amplitude weight wp = {w1 , w2 , . . . , wk }. The output of PaCM is a
871   set of reshaped latent causal factors Cp = {Cp1 , Cp2 , . . . , Cpk }, where each Cpi represents a
872   representation for the i-th period to consist the pyramid Cp ∈ RN ×Dh ×k .
873   MpCF is designed to fuse the multi-period information extracted by PaCM. MpCF begins by
874   padding the causal factors from different periods, followed by applying a self-attention mecha-
875   nism to compute attention scores for each period. These attention scores, along with the amplitude
876   weights wp , are used to weight the periodic components and produce the final omni-causal repre-
877   sentation Ĉp . The final output of MpCF is the fused causal representation Co ∈ RN ×Dh , which
878   integrates the most relevant information from all periods. The advantages of the attention mech-
879   anism are demonstrated by the improvement of performance (see Section 5.3) and interpretability
880   (see Section 5.4). Together, PaCM and MpCF effectively capture and fuse periodic information,
881   enabling the model to handle complex, multi-periodic time series data.

1147   G.3   C OMPARISON WITH RELATED WORKS
1148
       CaPulse addresses key gaps in existing methods for TSAD by introducing a causal and periodicity-
1149
       aware approach. Density-based TSAD methods such as GANF (Dai & Chen, 2022) and MT-
1150   GFlow (Xu et al., 2023) lack a causal perspective and fail to account for multi-periodicity in time
1151   series data. While forecasting- (Hundman et al., 2018; Shen et al., 2020) and reconstruction-based
1152   models (Su et al., 2019; Audibert et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2022) improve anomaly detection, they
1153   rely solely on statistical patterns without capturing underlying causal processes, making them less
1154   robust to noise and dynamic changes. In contrast, CaPulse enhances interpretability and robust-
1155   ness by integrating a causal view and density estimation, specifically tailored to handle complex
1156   temporal rhythms. Additionally, causal inference-based methods like Cost (Woo et al., 2022) and
1157   CaseQ (Yang et al., 2022) focus on time series forecasting and sequential event prediction, respec-
1158
       tively, but do not address anomaly detection. CaPulse is uniquely designed for TSAD, focusing
       on the generative processes behind anomalies to improve detection accuracy and provide deeper
1159
       insights into the underlying causes of anomalies in time series data.
