      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   In recent years, the transformer architecture and its associated attention mechanisms have gained
058   prominence over traditional models like LSTMs for various sequence-based tasks due to their ability
059   to better capture long-range dependencies without suffering from the vanishing gradient problem.
060
      The key component of Transformer, Attention mechanism, assigns different weights to previous
      tokens in a sequence, indicating their relative importance, and these weights are computed via a
061
      softmax function (Vaswani et al., 2017). The transformer architecture consists of multiple stacked
062
      layers, where each layer operates on the output of the previous one, forming the transformer encoder
063   or decoder. Learning within the transformer is performed via gradient backpropagation, wherein
064   gradients propagate backward through the network layer by layer using the chain rule (LeCun et al.,
065   2002). However, as gradients backpropagate through multiple layers, their magnitude tends to di-
066   minish, resulting in weaker gradients reaching the bottom layers. This phenomenon can hinder
067   effective learning in deeper layers. Residual connections (He et al., 2016) are used in Transformers
068   to bypass the layers via skip connections to avoid this issue. However, it behooves us to develop
069   layers which backpropagate gradients effectively.
070   In this paper we identify a similar gradient vanishing issue in attention mechanism of Transformer.
071   We show that the softmax operation backpropagate small gradients in large language models. Based
072   on this observation, we propose a small modification to attention mechanism - LASER attention
073   (LogArithm of Summed Exponentials of Representations). LASER is equivalent to conducting
074   attention on exponentially transformed inputs and takes a log-sum-exp structure. We analytically
075   show that gradients propagated via LASER attention are typically large. Since exp(·) transformation
076   in LASER can lead to overflows during implementation, we develop a novel implementation - Log-
077
      Weighted-Sum-Exp trick, inspired from Log-Sum-Exp trick (Blanchard et al., 2019). This technique
      allows LASER to scale to large models with upto 2.2 billion parameter models. We show that our
078
      implementation requires small modifications, and doesn’t need any changes to underlying attention
079
      mechanisms which may or may not admit more nuanced implementation (for e.g., FlashAttention).
080
081   We conduct thorough empirical verification across a variety of transformer models, including Con-
082   former for Librispeech speech-to-text (Gulati et al., 2020), Vision Transformer(Dosovitskiy et al.,
083
      2021) for ImageNet classification (Deng et al., 2009), decoder-only text transformer (Brown et al.,
      2020) on C4 dataset (Raffel et al., 2020) and encoder-only BERT ((Devlin et al., 2018)). We conduct
084
      experiments on decoder-only causal language models from 234 million parameters to 2.2 billion pa-
085
      rameter models, where we demonstate improvements of up to 1.7% relative improvement in test loss
086   over standard attention function. We conduct one-shot evaluation on 17 downstream tasks and show
087   that LASER outperforms Standard attention on 14 tasks with upto 3.38% difference in accuracy
088   and an average of 1% accuracy difference. On BERT with 2.2 billion parameter we show a relative
089   improvement of 0.93% on masked language modeling prediction error rate. We also show a 4.67%
090   relative improvement in validation error rate in Vision Transformer and 1.2% absolute improvement
091   in accuracy, and a 2.25% relative improvement in validation word error rate in the Conformer bench-
092   mark. Furthermore, LASER attention can be implemented with small modifications to the inputs of
093   traditional attention mechanism, making it a feasible and effective enhancement for a wide range of
094
      Transformer architectures.

097   2   R ELATED W ORK
098
099
      Attention mechanism was used in Bahdanau et al. (2015) to drastically improve machine translation
100
      performance compared to encoder-decoder recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Cho, 2014). This was
101
      later adopted in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017), which introduced self-attention to improve the
102   performance in machine translation even further. Efficient attention mechanisms have been an ac-
103   tive area of research due to the quadratic complexity in sequence length of Attention, which prevents
104   long-context language modeling. One notable contribution is Linear Attention (Katharopoulos et al.,
105   2020), which reduces the quadratic complexity of self-attention to linear by approximating the soft-
106   max function. Similarly, the Performer (Choromanski et al., 2021) uses kernel methods to achieve
107   linear complexity in transformers, making them more scalable for large-scale applications while
      retaining competitive performance in various tasks.

108
      The Mamba architecture, particularly Mamba-2, introduces state-space models (SSMs) as a re-
109   placement for traditional attention. Models like SSD from Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024) and S6
110   (S4+selection+scan) (Gu & Dao, 2023) showcase an efficient way to model long-range dependen-
111   cies without the use of attention, leading to faster computation. However, despite these innovations,
112   attention-based models like LLaMA 3 (Dubey et al., 2024) continue to dominate large-scale appli-
113   cations, particularly through advancements in context parallelism, which ensures scalability while
114   maintaining the strengths of attention mechanisms in transformer models.
115   Efficient attention mechanisms have become critical in handling large-scale data and long sequences,
116   especially in transformer-based architectures. FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; 2024), is a recent
117   advancement that optimizes memory and computational speed by improving memory bandwidth
118   utilization during attention computation, making it both fast and memory-efficient. This mechanism
119   is used for faster inference and training, particularly when scaling up to large sequence lengths.
120   Routing Transformers take a different approach by introducing a mechanism that sparsifies attention
121   through dynamic routing, where only the most relevant tokens are attended to during each attention
122   step (Roy et al., 2021) with subquadratic computational complexity in sequence length. Similarly,
123
      Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) modifies the standard self-attention mechanism to handle long
      documents by combining local attention with selected global attention tokens. Sparse Transformers
124
      (Child et al., 2019) use fixed sparse attention patterns, enabling them to efficiently handle very long
125
      sequences by reducing the quadratic complexity of standard attention to linear or sub-quadratic in
126   practice. By focusing only on a sparse subset of the tokens in each layer. LASER Attention can
127   be thought of as complementing these approaches, as it conducts attention using the exponential
128   transformation of inputs, without any change to underlying attention function.

279   one can notice that exp(.) operations can lead to overflow. This problem has been recognized in

524   5    C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   In this paper, we first identified a bottleneck in the gradient backpropagation of attention mechanism
527   where the gradients are scaled by small Jacobian values while passing through the softmax operation.
528   We fix this issue by transforming the inputs and outputs of attention mechanism, and show that
529   this leads to larger Jacobians in the limiting case. We demonstrate the improvements in training
530   performance over four types of transformers spanning different modalities (text, speech and vision):
531
      (a) decoder-only (via Large Language model) upto 2.2 billion parameters, (b) encoder-only (BERT)
      with 2.2 billion parameters, (c) vision Transformers on Imagenet, and (d) Conformer on Librispeech
532
      speech-to-text, where we show significant and consistent improvements in performance.
