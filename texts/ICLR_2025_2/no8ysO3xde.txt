009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We formalize the mathematical program modification (MPM) task, in which the
012            goal is to revise a mathematical program according to an inquiry expressed in nat-
013            ural language. These inquiries, which we refer to as what-if questions, express a
014            desire to understand how the optimal solution to an optimization problem changes
015            with the addition, deletion or revision of constraints. In detail, each MPM instance
016            is a triple consisting of: 1) a natural language specification that summarizes an op-
017            timization problem, 2) the canonical formulation of the problem, and 3) a natural
018
               language what-if question. The goal is to predict the updated canonical formula-
               tion with respect to the question. To support the study of this task, we construct
019
               WIQOR, a dataset of 1,946 MPM instances, derived from NL4O PT (Ramamon-
020
               jison et al., 2023), but with the number of decision variables extended to more than
021            30 for some problems. In experiments, we observe that Llama 3.1 70B instruct
022            under the in-context learning paradigm achieves 69% accuracy on the easiest test
023            instances, but only 36% accuracy on the most complicated problems. We release
024            WIQOR in the hopes of spurring additional study of MPM and ultimately en-
025            abling non-technical users to conduct what-if analyses without the help of techni-
026            cal experts.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Mathematical programming is the centerpiece of decision making in a many industries. For exam-
032   ple, firms routinely employ mathematical optimization to set product prices (Ferreira et al., 2016),
033
      find optimal transportation routes (Holland et al., 2017; Dang et al., 2024), or to optimize their
      supply chain operations – from optimal loading of a single truck to the best location for a new
034
      warehouse (Mehrotra et al., 2024). Tools for mathematical optimization are commonly utilized in
035
      academic peer review in order to match papers to reviewers (Taylor, 2008; Stelmakh et al., 2019).
036   Real-world optimization problems are massive and complex; they can have more than hundreds—or
037   even thousands—of variables and constraints. For example, UPS optimizes routes of 55,000 drivers
038   across the United States (Holland et al., 2017).
039
      Due in part to the complexity of real-world optimization problems, constructing formal problem
040
      representations that can be solved by industry-grade optimizers is typically a cumbersome process
041
      requiring collaboration between two parties: a domain expert and a technical expert (Li et al., 2023;
042   Mostajabdaveh et al., 2024). In these cases, the domain expert, e.g., a vendor who seeks to optimize
043   their product prices, does not possess the requisite technical expertise to translate their specific
044   problem into a mathematically rigorous objective function, decision variables, and the associated set
045   of constraints. Instead, the domain expert provides a detailed account of their problem to a technical
046   expert, who uses these details to construct a problem representation that can be sent as input to an
047   off-the-shelf solver (e.g., Gurobi), which returns the solution (Gurobi Optimization, LLC, 2024).
048   In addition to posing a challenge during program construction, the necessary communication be-
049   tween the domain and technical experts slows analysis of the program and its optimized solution.
050   In many cases, to maintain a good understanding of the optimal solution it is common to perform
051   counterfactual analysis, which usually consists of modifying the program slightly, re-running modi-
052   fied problem through the solver, and comparing the original and new solution (Li et al., 2023). For
053   example, consider that upon inspection of set of optimized prices, the domain expert seeks to un-
      derstand how a new constraint on the maximum price for a given product would affect the optimal

080   In order to support the development of methods that enable non-technical users to engage in such
081
      analysis, we formalize and study a task we term mathematical program modification, MPM. In
      this task, the goal is to update an existing mathematical program according to a natural language
082
      inquiry. In detail, the input in MPM is a triple consisting of: i) a natural language specification,
083
      or summary, of an optimization problem, ii) the problem expressed formally (mathematically) in
084   canonical form, and iii) a natural language inquiry about how a desired change in the constraints
085   would modify optimal solution to the problem, which we call a what-if question. The goal of the
086   task is to correctly modify the provided canonical form with respect to the what-if question.
087
      To facilitate the study of this task, we present the WIQOR (What-If Questions for Operations
088
      Research problems) benchmark dataset. This dataset comprises 1,946 instances of the MPM task,
089
      and includes what-if questions that correspond to 4 different types of constraint changes (Table 1),
090   which can be applied to 7 different constraint types (Appendix table 4). Each MPM instance in
091   WIQOR is seeded by a canonical formulation that is borrowed from the NL4O PT dataset Rama-
092   monjison et al. (2023). The accompanying problem specification, what-if question, and updated
093   canonical formulation are constructed using a combination of heuristics and large language model
094   inference. All MPM instances undergo manual evaluation before being added to the dataset.
095
      To better capture real-world complexity, WIQOR includes 4 test splits with increasing complexity—
096   as measured by the number of decision variables and constraints. In the Test-Base (easiest)
097   test split, MPM problems have on average 2.08 decision variables and 2.63 constraints. However,
098   WIQOR also includes additional test split Test-VarAug which include 400 base problems that
099   have been extended by adding 5, 10, 20 or 30 (100 problems each) additional decision variables and
100   corresponding constraints, respectively. Expansion of canonical form to include additional variables
101   and constraints is accomplished using a sequence of carefully designed heuristics and logic.
102   Empirically, we evaluate the Llama 3.1 instruct family of models on the WIQOR dataset under the
103   in-context learning paradigm. Our results reveal that the smallest models struggle to achieve more
104   than 40% accuracy on the base test instances. As the number of decision variables increase, even
105   the 70 billion parameter model shows a decline in performance, achieving only 36% accuracy on
106   the most difficult split. We hope that WIQOR helps support the development of tools that will
107   empower domain experts conduct what-if analysis without the need to enlist the help of external
      technical experts.

111   LLMs for Operations Research A foundational work at the intersection of operations research and
112
      NLP is the NL4Opt dataset by Ramamonjison et al. (2023), which focuses on the task of translating
      optimization problems into mathematical formulations. This dataset has problems from various do-
113
      mains, and the problems are written in natural language with a description of the constraints and the
114
      target optimization. The inputs for the NL4Opt subtask-2, i.e. the generation task, are the problem
115   description, its set of problem entities, and the order mapping of variables in the columns of the
116   mathematical program written in canonical form. The ground-truth label annotations consist of the
117   objective declaration and the constraints declarations, which are converted to a meaning representa-
118   tion using a semantic parser, which are in turn converted to a canonical form for evaluation. While
119   NL4Opt is an important step forward, it falls short in reflecting the types of optimization problems
120   that real-world operations research experts encounter in industry and academia. Everyday optimiza-
121   tion tasks are often more complex and varied, and the challenges experts face are not fully captured
122   by this dataset.
123   Additional efforts have been made to develop a variety of datasets aimed at advancing research in
124   this field. AhmadiTeshnizi et al. (2024) release NL4LP, a dataset of long and complex OR problems.
125   They propose a scalable system where LLMs develop mathematical models for mixed integer linear
126   programming (MILP) problems. Xiao et al. (2024) released ComplexOR a dataset of complex oper-
127   ations research problems and explore how LLM agents can be combined in expert chains to tackle
128   these problems. However, these newly developed datasets are quite limited in size, making them
129
      unsuitable for training models on the tasks they define.
130   There have also been efforts to synthesize operations research datasets using language models.
131   Prasath & Karande (2023) evaluate the use of CodeT5 for synthesizing mathematical programs
132   from natural language, employing data augmentation, beam post-processing, and back translation
133   via GPT-3 to generate synthetic examples. Yang et al. (2024) introduce another benchmark: E-OPT,
134   designed to evaluate LLMs’ ability to solve complex optimization problems, extending beyond sim-
135
      ple linear programming to include nonlinear problems. They propose the Reverse Socratic Syn-
      thesis (ReSocratic) method, which incrementally synthesizes mathematical formulations and back-
136
      translates them into problem descriptions. This strategy of reverse generating problem scenarios is
137
      akin to how what-if questions in the WIQOR dataset were generated (subsection 4.3).
138
139   Mostajabdaveh et al. (2024) also propose a framework to model real-world optimization problems
140   from natural language specifications. Similar to our work, they recognize that real-world problem
141
      descriptions rarely contain declarations of all the parameters and variables with keywords identifying
      them. While their goal is to formulate mathematical problems based on the abstracted problem
142
      specification only (which does not contain any numerical values), our task supplies the numerical
143
      values in addition to the problem specification, in a succinct (matrix) format, and the challenge of
144   our task is in translating a natural language modification inquiry to an appropriate modification of
145   that matrix.
146
147
      While these previous works have primarily focused on the task of generating mathematical programs
      from various formats of problem descriptions, none have addressed the challenge of modifying an
148
      existing mathematical program in response to changes expressed in natural language, such as a
149
      what-if question. This paper seeks to address this gap by formalizing the Mathematical Program
150   Modification MPM task and introducing WIQOR, a benchmark dataset specifically designed to
151   evaluate LLMs’ ability to modify mathematical programs based on natural language modification
152   inquiries. Additionally, we provide baseline performance results for various LLMs, aiming to spur
153   further research in this area.
154
      Counterfactual reasoning with LLMs Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown increasing
155
      proficiency in counterfactual reasoning, which involves understanding and generating hypothetical
156   scenarios. Chen et al. (2023) focus on distilling LLMs’ ability to reason about counterfactual sce-
157   narios, proposing methods to improve the model’s understanding and generation of counterfactual
158   statements. Similarly, Qin et al. (2019) explore their capacity to generate coherent and plausible
159   alternative realities within narrative contexts, demonstrating that the model can construct counter-
160   factual versions of stories that remain logically consistent with the original premises. Additionally,
161   Qin et al. (2023) provide a formal evaluation of LLMs’ performance in counterfactual tasks, reveal-
      ing limitations in the model’s ability to fully grasp the nuances of hypothetical scenarios. These

      3     T HE M ATHEMATICAL P ROGRAM M ODIFICATION TASK (MPM)
169
      Recall that our goal is to facilitate what-if analysis (of mathematical programs) by non-technical
170
      users. That is, we aim to enable domain experts to analyze how the optimal solution to their opti-
171   mization problems changes subject to various modifications; all modifications supplied via natural
172   language. As a first step, we formulate the mathematical program modification (MPM) task. In an
173   instance of MPM, the input is a triple consisting of: i) a specification, S, i.e., brief summary of an
174   optimization problem, including classes of variables and constraints; ii) the corresponding problem
175   formalized mathematically, P; and iii) a what-if question, Q, expressed in natural language. The
176   goal in MPM is to modify P according to Q. Formally,
177                                       MPM := (S × P × Q) → P ⋆                                       (1)
178            ⋆
      where P is the modified mathematical program. In the following subsections, we detail each
179   component of an instance of MPM, define notation, and introduce associated terminology.
180
181   3.1   O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEM S PECIFICATIONS
182
183   Real-world optimization problems include hundreds of decision variables and constraints, or more.
184   When communicating about such problems, it is common to construct a specification, or summary of
185   the problem. Rather than detail each variable and constraint, specification often mentions the groups
186   of variables that appear in a problem as well as describe the relationships between variables and
187
      constraints. An example of a problem specification appears in Figure 1. Notice that the specification
      for the problem includes the fact there is an upper bound on the number of each type of shipping
188
      that can be utilized, but it does not detail the precise upper limit for each shipping type. Please refer
189
      to figure 3 for an example of how various details from an optimization problem are converted into a
190   specification problem.
191
192
      3.2   C ANONICAL F ORMULATION
193
194   The problem specification summarizes an underlying (constrained) optimization problem. How-
195   ever, the specification does not include enough information to solve the problem. Specifically, each
196   program we study includes an objective function, a collection of decision variables and a set of
197   constraints (4)—none of which are fully detailed in the problem specification.
198   Instead, all details necessary to solve a given optimization problem are included in that problem’s
199   canonical formulation, P. The canonical formulation is comprised of 3 objects: i) the objective
200   function, ii) the constraint matrix, and iii) an order mapping. In a canonical formulation, the ob-
201   jective is always formulated as a maximization of a linear combination of decision variables. Ad-
202   ditionally, the constraint matrix is written as Ax ≤ b, where Aij is the coefficient corresponding to
203   decision variable j in constraint i, x is a vector of decision variables, and b is a vector of constants.
204   To rewrite any objective or constraint in canonical form, we perform algebraic manipulations as
205
      necessary. Since the MPM task requires translating natural language inquiries about specific vari-
      ables into modifications of the constraint matrix, we require an order mapping, which maps variable
206
      names to their indices in x (or, equivalently, the columns of A). Without this map, applying the
207
      modifications suggested by a what-if question would be impossible. We note that the output of the
208   MPM task, P ⋆ , is also a canonical formulation of an optimization problem. In particular, P ⋆ is
209   identical to P except for the modifications implied by the what-if question, Q.
210
211   3.3   W HAT- IF Q UESTIONS
212
213   Ultimately, our goal is to enable domain experts to perform certain analyses of mathematical pro-
214   grams via natural language. Toward this end, we define a what-if question, Q, to be natural language
215   inquiry regarding how the optimal solution to a mathematical program changes if some of its con-
      straints change. As the name suggests, all such inquiries begin with the hypothetical marker phrase


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      What if, as in: What if the price of product p must be greater than x? Any what-if question can be
217   answered by modifying the constraint matrix in the way implied by the question, and resolving the
218   problem. We say that each what-if question has a type, which is determined by the type of change it
219   imples. In our work, we study what-if questions of 4 types:
220
221         1. Limit change (LC): Questions of this type require an alteration of the bounds of a con-
222            straint. For example, What if the maximum number of truck trips in a week was 10 instead
223            of 5?
224         2. Constant change (CC): Questions of this type require a modification of a constant param-
225            eter value within the constraint. For example, What if the car can transport 50 packages
226            per trip?
227         3. Constraint direction reversal (CDR): Questions of this type require a modification of the
228            direction of a constraint, such as changing an upper bound to a lower bound constraint. For
229            example, What if the truck should make at least 5 trips in a week instead of at most 5 trips?
230         4. Variable interaction modification (VIM): Questions of this type require a modification of
231            how variables interact within a constraint, altering the sign of a variable’s coefficient. For
232            example, What if, instead of the total number of packages transported by the truck and car
233            being at least 500, the difference between the number of packages transported by the truck
234            and the car had to be at least 500? (e.g., changing 50t + 30c ≥ 500 to 50t − 30c ≥ 500).

265   4.1   O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEMS IN C ANONICAL F ORM
266
267   At the heart of each MPM instance in WIQOR is an optimization problem, written in canonical
268   form. To construct these canonical formulations for WIQOR, we use—and optionally modify—
269   problems from NL4O PT. NL4O PT is a dataset of optimization problems, where the goal is to take
      a natural language description of the optimization problem and generate the corresponding objective


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      function and constraint matrix, in canonical form (Section 3.2). For each problem in NL4O PT, we
271   utilize the target canonical formulation and provided order map.
272
273   Increasing complexity. Real-world optimization problems may include hundreds or thousands of
274
      decision variables and constraints. In contrast, NL4O PT has an average of 2.08 variables and 2.63
      constraints per problem. Therefore, we augment MPM instances in the test split of the WIQOR
275
      with 5, 10, 20, or 30 new decision variables. At a high level, this is done by adding columns
276
      to the constraint matrix, and making corresponding changes to the order map. When adding new
277   variables, we also modify existing constraints and add new constraints that resemble those that are
278   already present. For example, if each variable in the original problem has an upper bound constraint,
279   we introduce new upper bound constraints for the added variables. As another example, we expand
280   existing sum constraints with new variables as well. To update the order map with the new variables,
281   we assign names to the new variables based on the original variable names. For instance, if the
282   original variables were truck and car, and we are adding three new variables, we randomly select
283   one of the original names (either truck or car) and append the suffix -idx to it, where idx is
284   an integer in the range from 0 to k − 1 (in this example, k = 3). This process generates variable
285
      names like truck-0, car-1, truck-2, and so on. Significant care must be taken so that the
      resulting problems are sensible. For a detailed accounting of our handling of corner cases, refer to
286
      the algorithm in Appendix A.5.

      4.3   W HAT- IF Q UESTIONS & M ODIFIED C ANONICAL F ORMS
329
330   The final 2 pieces of the MPM task are the what-if question, Q, and modified canonical form, P ⋆ .
331   We generate both using a reverse engineering procedure. Specifically, we begin by heuristically
332   modifying the problems canonical form. For example, we might change a parameter value or a
333   constraint limit. The full suite of possible modifications that can be made correspond to the suite of
334   what-if question types (Section 3.3). This change results in a modified canonical form that serves as
335   P ⋆.
336   To generate the what-if question, Q, the corresponds to that change, we utilize LLM inference.
337   Again, we utilize Llama 3.1 70B Instruct, but this time using ICL examples that follow the ReAct
338   paradigm Yao et al. (2023). See Appendix A.3 As before, we begin by creating 15 ReAct-style inputs
339   for 15 of the possible constraint type and what-if question type pairs. Then, we create ICL examples
340   that consist of: i) NL4O PT natural language problem, ii) the corresponding canonical form, and
341   iii) the modified canonical form, P ⋆ . The model is tasked with generating the what-if question, Q,
342
      that matches the change in the P. We utilize 3 demonstrations to generate each what-if question. All
      generated what-if questions are manually evaluated for correcteness. Specifically, we ensure that the
343
      what-if question accurately reflects the change between the original and modified canonical forms
344
      (P and P ⋆ ). We found that 92% of generated what-if questions are of sufficiently high quality to
345   be included in the dataset; the rest are either filtered out or manually modified and included. All
346   generated what-if questions—including low quality generations before human modification—are
347   included in a supplement to the dataset.

349   4.4   DATASET S TATISTICS
350
351   The WIQOR dataset consists of 1,946 instances of the MPM task. As described above, the creation
352   of each task instance is seeded with an example from NL4O PT. However, MPM instances include
353   generated specifications rather than full descriptions of all variables and constraints; canonical forms
354   may have an increased number of variables; each instance includes a generated what-if question;
355   and the goal is to predict a modified canonical formulation, which matches the generated what-if
356
      question. Table 1 provides a detailed breakdown of the dataset across training, development, and
      test splits, highlighting the number of instances for each split and the distribution of what-if question
357
      types. The test split is divided into two parts. The first part, called Test-Base, contains 396 data
358
      points where the number of decision variables from the source optimization problem S 0 remains
359   unchanged.
360
361   The second part, Test-VarAug (variables augmented), is created by taking a sample of 100 data
362
      points from Test-Base and augmenting them by increasing the number of decision variables. The
      number of additional variables varies across four sets: 5, 10, 20, and 30. Each set contains 100 data
363
      points, bringing the total number of data points in Test-VarAug to 400, and the total number of
364
      data points in the full test split to 796. For each set of additional variable datapoints, we assessed
365   how the model performed as the number of decision variables increased, providing insight into its
366   scalability and adaptability to more complex problem formulations.
367
368
      The majority of the dataset is composed of constant change (CC) and limit change (LC) questions.
      The remaining portion is comprised of more nuanced modifications, such as constraint direction
369
      reversals (CDR) and variable interaction modifications (VIM). Table 2 focuses on the types of
370
      constraints impacted by these what-if questions. We note the prevalence of linear constraints in the
371   dataset, which dominate the modifications, while ratio constraints and specialized forms such as xy
372   and xby are less frequent.

411   mathematical program in its canonical form with the ground truth P ⋆ . This means that every element
412   in both the predicted matrix and P ⋆ should match for the prediction to be correct.

508   7   C ONCLUSIONS
509
510   We introduce MPM, the task of modifying the canonical formulation of an optimization problem
511   from a problem specification, initial formulation, and a given what-if question. This embodies a
512   type of ”what-if” analysis highly valuable to domain experts but often inaccessible. We also present
513   WIQOR, the first dataset for MPM, containing around 2000 problems, 7 constraint types, 4 what-if
514   question types, and varying levels of complexity, measured by the number of decision variables. Our
515   experiments show that the 8B parameter Llama 3.1 model solves only 38% of the simplest WIQOR
516   problems, while the 70B Llama 3.1 instruct model achieves 71.6% accuracy on the simplest cases,
517   but performance drops to 36% as the number of decision variables increases. These results indicate
518
      that LLMs have substantial room for improvement before they can effectively assist in real-world
      industrial what-if analysis, where problem complexity is much higher. Future research could explore
519
      hybrid approaches that combine language models with traditional optimization techniques, enhance
520
      Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting for complex problems, and develop models better suited for
521   handling tasks with numerous variables. In summary, while Llama 3.1-70B Instruct shows promise
522   in handling MPM tasks significant advancements are needed to maintain high performance as prob-
523   lem complexity increases. We hope this work paves the way for techniques that empower domain
524   experts to solve their optimization problems more independently.
