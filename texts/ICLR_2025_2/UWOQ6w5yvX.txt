009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In semi-supervised domain adaptation (SSDA), the model aims to leverage par-
012            tially labeled target domain data along with a large amount of labeled source do-
013            main data to enhance its generalization capability for the target domain. A key
014            advantage of SSDA is its ability to significantly reduce reliance on labeled data,
015            thereby lowering the costs and time associated with data preparation. Most ex-
016            isting SSDA methods utilize information from domain labels and class labels but
017            overlook the structural information of the data. To address this issue, this paper
018
               proposes a graph learning perspective (AGLP) for semi-supervised domain adap-
               tation. We apply the graph convolutional network to the instance graph which
019
               allows structural information to propagate along the weighted graph edges. The
020
               proposed AGLP model has several advantages. First, to the best of our knowl-
021            edge, this is the first work to model structural information in SSDA. Second, the
022            proposed model can effectively learn domain-invariant and semantic representa-
023            tions, reducing domain discrepancies in SSDA. Extensive experimental results
024            on multiple standard benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed AGLP algorithm
025            outperforms state-of-the-art semi-supervised domain adaptation methods.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Domain Adaptation (DA) Venkateswara et al. (2017); Peng et al. (2019); Berthelot et al. (2021) is a
031   critical machine learning approach aimed at addressing the issue of training and test data originating
032   from two related but distinct domains. These domains are typically referred to as the source domain
033
      and the target domain. In many practical applications, the source domain contains a wealth of la-
      beled data, while the target domain may have only a few labels or even none at all. This discrepancy
034
      often leads to a significant drop in model performance when directly transferring a model from the
035
      source domain to the target domain. Much of the research has focused on Unsupervised Domain
036   Adaptation (UDA). In UDA scenarios, researchers cannot access labels from the target domain, re-
037   quiring models to rely on knowledge from the source domain and unlabeled data from the target
038   domain for learning. In recent years, Semi-Supervised Domain Adaptation (SSDA) has emerged
039   as a focal point of research. Unlike UDA, SSDA Jiang et al. (2020); Singh (2021); Berthelot et al.
040   (2021) allows researchers to access a small number of labeled samples in the target domain, pro-
041   viding the model with richer learning information. By combining the abundant labeled data from
042   the source domain with the limited labeled data from the target domain, SSDA can more effectively
043   capture the underlying structural relationships between the domains, thereby improving the model’s
044
      performance and adaptability.
045   Prior SSDA methods can be broadly categorized into three groups: 1) statistical discrepancy mini-
046   mization methods Berthelot et al. (2021); Li & Zhang (2018), which utilize statistical regularizations
047   to explicitly reduce the cross-domain distribution discrepancy; 2) adversarial learning methods Jiang
048   et al. (2020); Singh (2021), which aim to learn domain-invariant representations across two domains
049   using adversarial techniques; and 3) multi-task learning methods Li et al. (2019); Qi et al. (2024),
050
      which focus on simultaneously learning multiple related tasks to share knowledge and improve the
      model’s generalization ability.
051
052   Indeed, these SSDA methods have achieved some success, but the main technical challenge in SSDA
053   lies in how to formally reduce the distribution discrepancy between different domains, typically the
      labeled source domain and the sparsely labeled target domain. There is little literature addressing

      To address the above issues, we propose an end-to-end Graph Convolutional Adversarial Network
075
      (GCAN) aimed at achieving semi-supervised domain adaptation. This network enhances adaptabil-
076
      ity by jointly modeling data structure and domain labels within a unified deep model. Inspired by
077   graph neural networks, we construct a densely connected instance graph using the CNN features
078   of samples, based on the similarity of their structural characteristics. Each node corresponds to the
079   CNN features of a sample extracted by a standard convolutional network. Next, we apply a Graph
080   Convolutional Network (GCN) to the instance graph, allowing structural information to propagate
081   along the weighted graph edges that can be learned from the designed network. During the class
082   centroid alignment process, we constrain the centroids of different classes to gradually move closer
083   as iterations increase, enabling the learned representations to effectively encode class label infor-
084   mation. This results in tighter embeddings for samples with the same category label in the feature
085
      space. Our model introduces a class alignment loss to achieve this goal and employs a moving
      centroid strategy to mitigate the influence of incorrect pseudo-labels. By modeling this alignment
086
      mechanism, the deep network can generate domain-invariant and highly discriminative semantic
087
      representations. The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows.
088
089            • We propose a graph learning perspective (AGLP) by modeling data structure and domain
090              label for semi-supervised domain adaptation. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
091              work to model graph information for semi-supervised domain adaptation.
092            • The proposed alignment mechanisms can learn domain-invariant and semantic representa-
093              tions effectively to reduce the domain discrepancy for SSDA.
094            • Extensive experimental results on several standard benchmarks demonstrate that the pro-
095              posed AGLP algorithm performs favorably against state-of-the-art SSDA methods.

246   2.2   S TRUCTURE - AWARE A LIGNMENT
247
248   In traditional domain alignment mechanisms Sun et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023), only global domain
249   statistics are aligned, overlooking the inherent structural information in mini-batch samples. Previ-
250   ous research has focused primarily on modeling data structure in unsupervised domain adaptation
251   (UDA) and has achieved promising results Oza et al. (2023). However, in the context of SSDA,
252
      there has been no solution addressing the structural information within mini-batch samples, despite
      its importance being demonstrated in UDA. To overcome this limitation in SSDA, we propose a
253
      structure-aware alignment mechanism that more effectively captures the structural relationships be-
254
      tween mini-batch source and target samples.
255
256   Our approach begins by utilizing a Data Structure Analyzer (DSA) network to generate structural
257   scores for mini-batch samples. These scores, together with the learned CNN features of the samples,
258
      are used to construct a densely connected instance graph. This instance graph is then processed using
      a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) Kipf & Welling (2016), which learns features that encode
259
      the structural information present in the data.
260
261   GCNs are designed to perform hierarchical propagation operations on graphs. Given an undirected
262   graph with m nodes and a set of edges represented by an adjacency matrix A ∈ Rk×m , the graph
263   convolution’s linear transformation is expressed as a graph signal G ∈ Rk×m , where Gi ∈ R repre-
264
      sents the feature of the i-th node. This is combined with a filter W ∈ Rk×c for feature extraction.

      2.3   C LASS C ENTROID A LIGNMENT
283
284   Domain invariance and structure consistency do not necessarily guarantee discriminability. For
285   example, features of the target class ”laptops” may be mapped near features of the source class
286   ”screens” while still satisfying domain invariance. To address this, we draw inspiration from UDA
287   Ma et al. (2019), where class label information ensures that features of the same class from different
288   domains are mapped nearby. This motivates our use of class centroid alignment in UDA, following
289   the approach in Ma et al. (2019).
290   To implement the class centroid alignment, pseudo labels are first assigned using a target classifier
291   F , after which centroids are computed for both labeled and pseudo-labeled samples. The centroid
292   alignment objective is defined as:
293                                                             K
                                                                X
294                                LCA (XS , YS , XT , YT ) =         ϕ(CSk , CTk ),                     (17)
295                                                             k=1
296
      where CSk and CTk are the centroids of class k in the source and target domains, respectively. The
297
      distance measure ϕ(·, ·) is defined as the squared Euclidean distance ϕ(x, x′ ) = ∥x − x′ ∥2 . By
298   minimizing the distance between centroids across domains, we ensure that features of the same
299   class are mapped nearby.

534   this goal and employs a moving centroid strategy to mitigate the influence of incorrect pseudo-labels.

674   A.1     R ELATED W ORK
675
676   A.1.1    U NSUPERVISED D OMAIN A DAPTATION
677
      Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) Ganin & Lempitsky (2015); Wang & Deng (2018); Liu
678
      et al. (2022) aims to adapt models trained on source domain data to unlabeled target domain data.
679
      Most domain adaptation algorithms attempt to align feature distributions by minimizing the domain
680   shift between the source and target domains, facilitating the transfer of knowledge learned from
681   source data to improve classification performance in the target domain Ghosn & Bengio (2003);
682   Weiss et al. (2016). Many UDA methods employ a domain classifier to measure the distance Long
683   et al. (2018); Shu et al. (2018). The domain classifier is trained to distinguish whether the input
684   features originate from the source or the target, while the feature extractor is trained to deceive
685   the domain classifier by matching feature distributions. Recently, some studies have addressed this
686   issue by constructing an end-to-end mapping from the source domain to the target domain using
687   clustering-based Optimal Transport Liu et al. (2023), and Yue et al. Yue et al. (2023) proposed
688
      Invariant Consistency Learning to tackle the spurious correlation between domain-specific features
      and class features.
689
690   UDA has now been applied in various domains, such as image classification Liu et al. (2022), se-
691   mantic segmentation Sankaranarayanan et al. (2018), and object detection Saito et al. (2019b). It
692   has also spawned derivative directions, utilizing multiple source domains to generalize to unseen
693   target domains through domain generalization Wang et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022). Additionally,
694
      test-time training/test-time adaptation employs unlabeled target domain data only during the testing
      phase, without using source domain data Sun et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020), and
695
      semi-supervised domain adaptation leverages a small amount of labeled target domain data along
696
      with a large amount of unlabeled data for transfer Saito et al. (2019a).
697
698   A.1.2    S EMI - SUPERVISED D OMAIN A DAPTATION
699
700   Semi-Supervised Domain Adaptation (SSDA) aims to leverage a small number of labeled samples
701   from the target domain, combined with source domain data and a large amount of unlabeled target
      domain data, significantly improving domain adaptation performance compared to Unsupervised


                                                       13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Domain Adaptation Saito et al. (2019a). Recently, SSDA has attracted widespread attention from
703   researchers Kim & Kim (2020); Li et al. (2021); Yu & Lin (2023); Li et al. (2024), with relevant
704   studies applying it to object detection to enhance performance Wang et al. (2023).
705
706
      Saito et al. (2019a) addressed the SSDA problem by aligning features from both domains using
      adversarial learning. Yu & Lin (2023) proposed a novel source adaptation paradigm that treats
707
      the source domain as noisy target domain data, enhancing performance by cleaning label noise.
708
      Rahman et al. (2023) introduced a new semi-supervised domain adaptation framework utilizing
709   autoencoders and synchronized learning to improve performance. Most prior methods have focused
710   on sample-level feature alignment to tackle the SSDA problem. In this work, we aim to utilize
711   Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) to capture structural information for aligning features from
712   the source domain to the target domain.
713
714   A.1.3    G RAPH ON D OMAIN A DAPTATION
715
716
      Most domain adaptation frameworks are typically limited by their structure, often utilizing only do-
      main labels and class labels, while neglecting important structural information from the data Ma
717
      et al. (2019). Ma et al. (2019) were the first to enhance the performance of Unsupervised Do-
718
      main Adaptation through three alignment strategies: structure-aware alignment, domain alignment,
719   and class centroid alignment. Zhu et al. (2021) introduced a novel graph for Unsupervised Ad-
720   versarial Domain Adaptation (DA) that integrates sample-level and class-level structural informa-
721   tion from both domains to improve performance. Ding et al. (2018) constructed a Graph Adaptive
722   Knowledge Transfer (GAKT) model to jointly optimize target labels and domain-invariant features
723   within a unified framework, thereby enhancing the performance of Unsupervised Domain Adapta-
724   tion. Furthermore, Dai et al. (2022) proposed a novel graph transfer learning framework, AdaGCN,
725   which leverages adversarial domain adaptation and graph convolutional techniques to enhance class-
726   discriminative node representations and mitigate the differences between the source and target do-
727
      mains.
728   Overall, however, all existing research on graph structural information in domain adaptation has
729   primarily focused on Unsupervised Domain Adaptation, with little application in Semi-Supervised
730   Domain Adaptation.
731
732   A.2     PARAMETER ROBUSTNESS A NALYSIS
733
734   Our approach is based on an extension of the CDAC SLA, where we selected the optimal param-
735
      eters as reported in the original paper. To verify the robustness of our model regarding parameter
      sensitivity, we conducted a series of parametric experiments. These experiments were performed on
736
      the OfficeHome dataset, specifically on the 3-shot and 1-shot A→C tasks. We evaluated the impact
737
      of various parameters, including GCN’s output channels (Table. 5), GCN layers (Table. 6), and β
