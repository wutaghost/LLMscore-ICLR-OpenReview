011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013            Trust and interpretability are crucial for the use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in scientific
014            research, but current models often operate as black boxes offering limited transparency
015            and justifications for their outputs. Motivated by this problem, we introduce AISciVision,
016            a framework that specializes Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) into interactive research
017
               partners and classification models for image classification tasks in niche scientific do-
               mains. Our framework uses two key components: (1) Visual Retrieval-Augmented Gener-
018
               ation (VisRAG) and (2) domain-specific tools utilized in an agentic workflow. To classify
019
               a target image, AISciVision first retrieves the most similar positive and negative labeled
020            images as context for the LMM. Then the LMM agent actively selects and applies tools to
021            manipulate and inspect the target image over multiple rounds, refining its analysis before
022            making a final prediction. These VisRAG and tooling components are designed to mirror
023            the processes of domain experts, as humans often compare new data to similar examples
024            and use specialized tools to manipulate and inspect images before arriving at a conclusion.
025            Each inference produces both a prediction and a natural language transcript detailing the
026            reasoning and tool usage that led to the prediction. We evaluate AISciVision on three
027            real-world scientific image classification datasets: detecting the presence of aquaculture
028
               ponds, diseased eelgrass, and solar panels. Across these datasets, our method outperforms
               fully supervised models in low and full-labeled data settings. AISciVision is actively de-
029
               ployed in real-world use, specifically for aquaculture research, through a dedicated web
030
               application that displays and allows the expert users to converse with the transcripts. This
031            work represents a crucial step toward AI systems that are both interpretable and effective,
032            advancing their use in scientific research and scientific discovery.

043   but also introduces new standards for how we think about and interact with AI, as accessible, versatile, and

      personal assistants.

086   raises a critical question: can these models provide the same depth of expertise in highly specialized and
087   impactful domains? In areas such as medicine, law, scientific research and scientific discovery, the need goes
088   beyond general conversations, these fields demand models capable of deep, domain-specific reasoning (Lu
089   et al., 2022; Mall et al., 2024). The general knowledge embedded in LMMs falls short of the nuanced
090   expertise required for these specialized tasks, limiting their effectiveness where it matters most.
091   Fortunately, the large context windows of LMMs allow for flexible specialization via in-context learning.
092   By providing rich prompts and context relevant to a particular task, LMMs can adapt to domain-specific
093   requirements, a strategy that is driving exciting research in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis

      et al., 2020; Khandelwal et al., 2020). RAG techniques enhance LMM predictions by retrieving task-specific
095
      examples, effectively refining the model’s responses based on context and thereby specializing it for the task
096   at hand. This approach is particularly valuable because the sheer scale and cost of training LMMs are often
097   only feasible for large organizations. For most researchers, leveraging context is a practical way to harness
098   the general knowledge of LMMs while also enabling them to excel at unique, specialized tasks (Soong et al.,
099   2024; Zakka et al., 2024). One critical area for specialization is scientific image classification. The ability
100   to adapt LMMs to these domains would be transformative, as it has the potential to democratize access
101   to advanced analysis previously limited by the scarcity of domain expertise. By enabling AI to provide
102   nuanced, explainable insights in specialized scientific tasks, we can significantly accelerate research and
103   discovery, bridging the gap between powerful, general-purpose AI models and the pressing, domain-specific
104
      challenges faced by experts.
105   To address these challenges, we introduce AISciVision, a framework that adapts general-purpose LMMs
106   to accurately classify scientific images while generating transparent, context-specific reasoning transcripts.
107   AISciVision operates in a model-agnostic way, combining two key components: a Visual Retrieval-
108   Augmented Generation system (VisRAG) and an interactive tooling AI agent that classifies images through
109   dialogue. Users start by providing labeled training data, which is embedded into a feature space, where
110
      the system organizes positive and negative class examples separately. They also specify classification tools,
      ranging from basic image adjustments (e.g., contrast) to domain-specific operations like zooming into satel-
111
      lite imagery. During inference, AISciVision retrieves similar positive and negative image examples from the
112
      training set based on cosine similarity in the embedding space and uses them as context for the LMM’s anal-
113   ysis. The LMM then engages in multiple rounds of interactive analysis, using the specified tools to refine its
114   understanding of the target image, before arriving at a final prediction. A visual schematic of AISciVision is
115   provided in Figure 1.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
142
143
      Our work builds on recent research in multimodal models, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, and interactive
144
      AI agents that leverage tools. We discuss and compare related work in this section, and distinguish our work
145   as integrating these domains into a unified approach.
146
147   Multimodal models in low-labeled data regimes Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) like CLIP (Rad-
148   ford et al., 2021), GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaVa (Liu et al., 2023), and PaLM-E (Driess et al., 2023)
149   have been demonstrated to understand and generate content across multiple modalities like text, visual, and
150   audio. By building rich and general-purpose representations of inputs from large and diverse datasets, these
151   LMMs demonstrate competitive zero- and few-shot capabilities on a wide range of tasks (Brown et al.,
152   2020), such as natural language understanding (Kiros et al., 2014), image captioning (Vinyals et al., 2015),
153   text-to-image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021), and image classification (Guillaumin et al., 2010; Abdel-
154   hamed et al., 2024). Acosta et al. (2022); Moor et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023) demonstrate that these
155
      capabilities are key for advancing research in domains where obtaining labeled data is costly and tedious,
      for instance, in scientific research. On the other hand, Lu et al. (2022); Mall et al. (2024) find that zero- and
156
      few-shot capabilities of general-purpose LMMs like CLIP do not suffice in scientific applications, where uti-
157
      lizing domain-specific information becomes important. Lu et al. (2022) find that Chain-of-Thought prompt-
158   ing (Wei et al., 2022) improves question-answering performance, whereas Mall et al. (2024) demonstrate
159   that a “Vision Language Model” explicitly trained on satellite images outperforms CLIP. Our framework
160   AISciVision extends general-purpose LMMs to classify images in low-labeled data regimes like scientific
161   applications. We incorporate Retrieval-Augmented Generation and domain-specific tool use to ground out-
162   puts in such specialized scientific applications. AISciVision allows an LMM to predict after multiple rounds
163   of tool use, thus going beyond classical Chain-of-Thought prompting.
164
165   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) Since Large Language Models (LLMs) suffer from halluci-
166   nations in generations and static memory about the world, there is much work in Retrieval-Augmented
167
      Genereation (RAG) to retrieve relevant context from external knowledge sources to ground generations in
      reality (Khandelwal et al., 2020; Lewis et al., 2020). RAG has proven useful for language generation and
168
      question answering in scientific applications such as biomedical research and medicine (Soong et al., 2024;
169
      Zakka et al., 2024; Bae et al., 2024). Recent work has also demonstrated the effectiveness of RAG in multi-
170   modal settings by retrieving relevant images and documents to enrich the model’s prompt, in applications of
171   visual question answering and image captioning (Chen et al., 2022; Yasunaga et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023).
172   Our framework AISciVision leverages RAG for scientific image classification, by enriching the LMM’s
173   context with domain-relevant examples during inference.
174
175   Interactive AI Agents and tool-use In recent years, Large Language Models have enabled users to en-
176   gage in multi-turn natural language conversations to perform a wide range of tasks, from brainstorming and
177   writing to writing code and solving math equations (Achiam et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
178   2023; Gemini-Team, 2024). There is growing interest in enabling such models to act as an agent on their
179   generations, by deploying them in environments (Fan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024) and attaching tools
180
      to interact with the web (Nakano et al., 2022; Patil et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2023). Yao et al. (2023)
      accomplish this with ReAct, by prompting the model to invoke calls to specified tools in natural language
181
      format, whereas Schick et al. (2023) introduce ToolFormer which is a model finetuned on a list of available
182
      tools. When environments do not return natural language feedback, e.g. when the feedback is scalar or
183   binary, Shinn et al. (2023) find that training a helper model to generate natural language descriptions of the
184   feedback improves the agent’s performance—termed as Reflexion. Lu et al. (2024) introduce the “AI Scien-
185   tist” that imitates the research process in the Machine Learning community. They utilize Chain-of-Thought,
186   Reflexion, and tool use to imitate the research process: brainstorm research ideas, execute experiments, and
187   write a paper.

      For scientific research in physical, life, and climate sciences, not only is it important to obtain accurate pre-
189
      dictions but also to get insight into the underlying justifications that lead to these predictions (Wang et al.,
190   2023; Chen et al., 2021; Society, 2024; Kong et al., 2022). With the ReAct approach of interacting with ex-

192   a ‘paper trail’ or a ‘transcript’ of the decision-making process. Our framework AISciVision accomplishes
193   exactly this feat, and is one of the first attempts at developing an interactive AI agent for scientific
194   image classification. Following the extensive research in tool use, we develop domain-specific tools in the
195   AISciVision framework, for instance, zoom/pan for satellite image datasets and image enhancement tools.
196   Our agent interacts with these tools in a multi-turn fashion and leaves a conversation transcript, enhancing
197   interpretability, transparency, and trust—all crucial for scientific research.

202   cedure with domain-specific tools, which an LMM uses to classify images in the scientific domain. In this
203   section, we describe these two components and discuss how AISciVision uses them during inference.

      3.2   D OMAIN - SPECIFIC I NTERACTIVE T OOLS
231
232   We leverage expert-designed tools for each classification task, empowering the LMM to refine its predictions
233   by interacting with these tools. These tools mimic transformations and “expert advice” that a human would
234   use to manipulate, inspect, and analyze images, before attempting to classify an image. Therefore, by

                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


235
      interacting with these tools in the AISciVision framework, the LMM acts akin to an interactive AI agent
236
      making informed and interpretable decisions.
237
238   Generally, we define a tool T as a function on images X , with outputs as images X or a real-valued scalar in
239   R. That is, such a tool either transforms an image x ∈ X to another image T (x) ∈ X or returns a numeric
240
      output T (x) ∈ R such as a confidence score from an external model. For example, a tool TML might use an
      externally-trained Machine Learning model with parameters θ to predict the probability of a label given the
241
      image: TML = Pr[y = 1 | x; θ]. Other tools Tbr or Tco might adjust brightness or contrast by some value α
242
      such that Tbr = AdjustBrightness(x, α) or Tco = IncreaseConstrast(x, α).
243
244   For each image classification task, we define a set of tools T = {T1 , . . . , TK } and provide their descriptions
245   in natural language as a prompt to the LMM. At any turn i in the conversation, the model submits a request
246
      for a tool Ti ∈ T , which can either transform the image x′ = Ti (x) or return a numeric value. The
      AISciVision parses this request and returns the tool’s result as a prompt, with the transformed image or the
247
      numeric value described in a sentence. In essence, this is similar to ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) in that we use a
248
      hardcoded prompt template for the response. Iterative use of domain-specific tools enables the agent in our
249   AISciVision framework to refine predictions in a context-aware manner, not only improving accuracy (see
250   Section 4) but also producing a transcript of the agent’s reasoning. Such a transcript provides interpretable
251   insight into our framework’s reasoning, which is crucial for applications in scientific discovery.
252
253   3.3   I NFERENCE P ROCESS IN THE AIS CI V ISION F RAMEWORK
254
255   Given an input test image, AISciVision enriches the model’s prompt with VisRAG and descriptions of the
256   tools, which the model calls in subsequent turns of the conversation. Finally, the model outputs a classifica-
257   tion label with a probability score indicating its confidence.
258   We design the initial system prompt to reflect the specified domain (see Appendix C for example transcripts).
259   First, we use the VisRAG approach to retrieve the most similar positive and negative examples from the
                               −
260   training set, x+
                     sim and xsim respectively. We then describe the set T of available domain-specific tools, and
261   encourage the model to use them to obtain more context during inference. After a few conversation turns,
262   the LMM responds with a binary prediction and a confidence score. The transcript of interactions represents
263   a record of justifications at the inference stage, which a domain expert can review after the fact. In this
264
      way, AISciVision presents an interactive AI agent that uses domain-specific knowledge to not only classify
      scientific images but also justify its underlying reasoning in natural language.

      Limitations and Future Work While AISciVision offers significant benefits in providing transparent rea-
      soning and the potential for enhanced accuracy, it comes with a trade-off: using off-the-shelf LMMs for
463
      inference is financially expensive, compared to traditional machine learning methods. For our future work,
464
      we aim to actively develop our web application to continue to collect expert feedback on the LMM agent’s
465   reasoning through a ChatGPT-style interface. Experts can provide real-time feedback and corrections, which
466   will be stored within the VisRAG component to improve the LMM agent’s performance with use. We en-
467   vision the system continuing to learn as experts interact with the agent, and provide rich natural language
468   feedback. Beyond refining our approach for image data, we also plan to test and extend our method to other
469   modalities, such as sound or any tokenizable input that can be incorporated into an LMM.

      A     D OMAIN - SPECIFIC T OOLS
659
660
      The tools developed for each dataset are designed to mimic the strategies that human experts would use when
661
      performing these specific classification tasks. For the Eelgrass wasting disease and Solar panel detection
662   tasks, the tools focus on image enhancement techniques that a human might naturally apply when examining
663   an image closely. These include adjusting brightness, contrast, and sharpness, as well as applying edge
664   detection and histogram equalization. Such manipulations can reveal features or patterns that might be
665   helpful for accurate classification, much like how a human expert might squint, tilt their head, or adjust
666   lighting to better perceive important details.
667
      In contrast, the aquaculture pond detection task presents a unique challenge where the presence of water
668   bodies isn’t always immediately apparent from a single satellite image. To address this, the tools for this
669   task are designed to emulate how a human would interact with digital maps. They include options to pan and
670   zoom, allowing for exploration of the surrounding area and changes in perspective. This approach mirrors
671   how a human expert might navigate digital mapping software, zooming out to get context from the broader
672   landscape, then zooming in on areas of interest to confirm the presence of aquaculture ponds. By providing
673   these map navigation tools, the framework can gather additional spatial information that may be crucial for
674   accurate classification, especially in cases where a single image view might be ambiguous or inconclusive.
