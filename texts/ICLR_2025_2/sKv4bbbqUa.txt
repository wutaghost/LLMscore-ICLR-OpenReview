                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Unlike words in sentences, nodes in general graphs do not have canonical posi-
011            tional information. As a result, the local message-passing framework of popular
012
               graph neural networks (GNNs) fails to leverage possibly relevant global structures
               for the task at hand. In this context, positional encoding methods emerge as an
013
               efficient approach to enrich the representational power of GNNs, helping them
014
               break node symmetries in input graphs. Similarly, multiscale topological descrip-
015            tors based on persistent homology have also been integrated into GNNs to boost
016            their expressivity. However, it remains unclear how positional encoding interplays
017            with PH-based topological features and whether we can align the two to improve
018            expressivity further. We address this issue with a novel notion of topological posi-
019            tional encoding (ToPE) that amalgamates the strengths of persistence homology
020            and positional encoding. We establish that ToPE has provable expressivity benefits.
021            Strong empirical assessments further underscore the effectiveness of the proposed
022            method on several graph and language processing applications, including molecular
023
               property prediction, out-of-distribution generalization, and synthetic tree tasks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Many natural systems, such as social networks (Freeman, 2004) and proteins (Jha et al., 2022), exhibit
029
      complex relational structures often represented as graphs. To tackle prediction problems in these
030
      domains, graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al., 2009; Bronstein et al., 2017; Hamilton
031   et al., 2017; Velickovic et al., 2017) have become the dominant approach, leading to breakthroughs
032   in diverse applications such as drug discovery (Gilmer et al., 2017b; Stokes et al., 2020; Satorras
033   et al., 2021), simulation of physical systems (Cranmer et al., 2019; Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020),
034   algorithmic reasoning (Dudzik et al., 2023; Jurss et al., 2023), and recommender systems (Ying
035   et al., 2018). Despite this success, most GNNs have rather limited expressivity — they are at most
036   as powerful as the 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman (1-WL) test (Weisfeiler & Leman, 1968) in distinguishing
037   non-isomorphic graphs (Xu et al., 2019; Morris et al., 2019). This inherent limitation has prompted
038   the development of more expressive GNNs by leveraging, e.g., topological features (Horn et al.,
039
      2022), random features (Sato et al., 2021), higher-order message passing (Morris et al., 2019), and
      structural/positional encodings (Li et al., 2020; You et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022).
040
041   Inspired by the success of positional encodings (PEs) in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) for
042   sequences, several positional encoders for graphs have been proposed (You et al., 2019; Dwivedi et al.,
043   2021; Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023). For instance, spectral methods exploit global structure
044   via the eigendecomposition of the graph Laplacian (Lim et al., 2022; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Huang et al.,
045
      2023). However, these encodings suffer from inherent ambiguities due to sign flips, basis changes,
      stability, and eigenvalue multiplicities. Recent efforts have addressed sign and basis symmetries (Lim
046
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022) and stability with respect to graph perturbations (Huang et al., 2023).
047
      However, a common drawback persists: most methods partition the Laplacian eigenvalue/eigenvector
048   space and utilize only the partitioned eigenvalues/eigenvectors. This approach discards valuable infor-
049   mation contained in the remaining eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Another class of methods leverage
050   relative distances (e.g., computed from random walk diffusion) to anchor-nodes to capture structural
051   information (Dwivedi et al., 2021; Eliasof et al., 2023; Ying et al., 2021; You et al., 2019; Li et al.,
052   2020). Despite these advances, existing methods fail to extract detailed multiscale topological infor-
053   mation, such as the persistence of connected components and independent cycles (i.e., 0- and 1-dim
      topological invariants), which may be relevant to downstream tasks and potentially more expressive.

054
      Persistent homology (PH) (Edelsbrunner et al., 2002) is the cornerstone of topological data analysis
055   and offers a powerful framework to capture multi-scale topological information from data. In the
056   context of graphs, PH has been recently used, e.g., to boost the expressive and representational power
057   of GNNs (Horn et al., 2022; Immonen et al., 2023; Carriere et al., 2020; Verma et al., 2024). However,
058   integrating PH-based topological descriptors into graph positional encoders remains unexplored.
059
      In this work, we explore persistent homology on graphs to build expressive positional encoders. In
060
      particular, we present Topological Position Encoding (ToPE) — a multilayered encoding scheme that
061   builds on top of prior positional encoders and the message-passing paradigm (Gilmer et al., 2017a) to
062   obtain filtering functions used to compute persistent topological features (i.e., persistence diagrams).
063   The resulting node-level topological embeddings are fed to downstream GNN layers. Notably, ToPE
064   is very flexible as it can be combined with any PE method and addresses the limitations of current
065   methods by leveraging more nuanced, fine-grained topological information captured by PH.
066
      We theoretically analyze ToPE and establish its improved expressive power in comparison to popular
067   PE methods such as the Laplacian PE (Dwivedi et al., 2021). We also show the stability of ToPE’s
068   filtration functions and describe its representational power in terms of the k-WL hierarchy. To demon-
069   strate the effectiveness of our proposal, we conduct rigorous empirical evaluations on various tasks,
070   including molecule property prediction, out-of-distribution generalization, and synthetic tree tasks.
071
      In sum, our main contributions are:
072
073         1. (Methodology) We propose Topological Positional Encoding (ToPE), unifying general
074            positional encoding schemes with persistent homology on graphs;
075         2. (Theory) We establish a series of theoretical results to support our methodological contribu-
076            tion. In particular, we demonstrate the superior expressive power of ToPE compared to its
077            base positional encoders, and ToPE’s relationship with the k-WL isomorphism test;
078         3. (Empirical) Our empirical assessment shows that ToPE outperforms the competing baselines
079            across diverse tasks such as molecular property prediction, out-of-distribution generalization
080            tasks, and synthetic tree tasks. We also conduct an ablation study to measure the impact of
081            adopting learnable vs. non-learnable filtering functions.

088   Notation. We define a graph as a tuple G = (V, E, x), where V = {1, . . . , n} is the set of nodes,
089   E ⊆ V × V is the set of edges, and the function x : V → Rdx assigns a color (or dx -dimensional
090   feature vector) to nodes v ∈ V — for convenience, hereafter, we denote the feature vector of v
091   by xv . We denote the adjacency matrix of G by A ∈ {0, 1}n×n , i.e., Aij is one if (i,Pj) ∈ E and
092   zero otherwise. We use D to represent the diagonal degree matrix of G, i.e., Dii = j Aij . We
093   define the normalized Laplacian of G as ∆ = In − D−1/2 AD−1/2 and its random walk Laplacian as
094   ∆RW = D−1 A, where In is the n-dimensional identity matrix. The set of neighbors of a node v is
095   denoted by N (v) = {u ∈ V : (v, u) ∈ E}. Furthermore, we use {{·}} to denote multisets.

134   2.2   k- DIM W EISFEILER -L EMAN TEST
135
136   The Weisfeiler–Leman test (1-WL), also known as the color refinement algorithm (Weisfeiler &
137   Leman, 1968), aims to determine if two graphs are isomorphic. It does so by iteratively assigning
138
      colors to nodes. Initially, nodes receive labels based on their features. In each iteration, nodes sharing
      the same label get distinct labels if their sets of similarly labeled neighbors differ. Termination
139
      happens when label counts diverge between graphs, indicating non-isomorphism.
140
141   Due to the shortcomings of the 1-WL in distinguishing non-isomorphic graphs, Babai (1979);
142   Immerman & Lander (1990) introduced a more powerful variant known as k-dim (folklore) We-
143   isfeiler–Leman algorithm. In this approach, k-FWL colors subgraphs instead of a single node.
144
      Specifically, given a graph G, it colors tuples from V (G)k for k ≥ 1 instead of nodes and defines
      neighborhoods between these tuples. Formally, let G be a graph, and let k ≥ 2. If v ∈ V (G)k , then
145
      G[v] is the subgraph induced by the components of v, where the nodes are labeled with integers from
146
      {1, ..., k} corresponding to indices of v.

168   2.3   P ERSISTENT HOMOLOGY ON GRAPHS
169
170   A key notion in persistent homology is that of filtration. In this regard, a filtration of a graph G
171   is a finite nested sequence of subgraphs of G, i.e., ∅ = G0 ⊂ G1 ⊂ ... ⊂ G. A popular choice
172   to obtain a filtration consists of considering sublevel sets of a function defined on the vertices of
173   a graph. In particular, let f : V → R be a filtering function and Gα be the subraph of G induced
174
      by the vertex set Vα = {v : f (v) ≤ α} for α ∈ R. By varying α from −∞ to ∞, we obtain a
      sub-level filtration of G. Importantly, we can monitor the emergence and vanishing of topological
175
      characteristics (e.g., connected components, loops, voids) throughout a filtration, which is the core
176
      idea of PH. More specifically, if a topological feature first appears in Gαb and disappears in Gαd ,
177   then we encode its persistence as a pair (αb , αd ); if a feature does not disappear, then its persistence
178   is (αb , ∞). The collection of all pairs forms a multiset that we call persistence diagram. We use Di
179   to denote the persistence diagram for i-dim topological features. For a formal treatment of PH, we
180   refer to Edelsbrunner & Harer (2010) and Hensel et al. (2021).
181
      In graph learning, persistent homology has been harnessed to enhance the expressive power of GNNs.
182
      Horn et al. (2022) introduced TOGL, a general framework for integrating topological features derived
183   from PH into GNN layers. TOGL employs a learnable function (a multilayer perceptron, MLP) on
184   node features / colors to obtain graph filtrations, which we refer to as vertex-color (VC) filtrations.
185   Importantly, Immonen et al. (2023) characterized the expressive power of VC filtrations via the
186   notions of color-separating sets and component-wise colors. Formally, a color-separating set for
187   a pair of graphs G = (V, E, x) and G′ = (V ′ , E ′ , x′ ) is a set of colors Q such that the subgraphs
188   induced by V \{w ∈ V | xw ∈ Q} and V ′ \{w ∈ V ′ | x′w ∈ Q} have distinct component-wise colors
189   — defined as the multiset comprising the set of node colors of each connected components. Immonen
190   et al. (2023) introduced RePHINE, a new topological descriptor that employs vertex and edge-color
191
      filtering functions to enhance the expressiveness of persistence diagrams from VC filtrations.

      6    C ONCLUSION , B ROADER I MPACT AND L IMITATIONS
470
471
      We introduce Topological Positional Encoding (ToPE), a novel method that unifies the power of
472
      persistent homology (PH) with general positional encoding methods. ToPE leverages rich topological
473
      information to enhance the expressivity of GNNs by combining the strengths of persistence homology
474   and positional encoding. We theoretically establish ToPE’s improved expressive power compared
475   to Laplacian positional encoding and characterize its representational capabilities within the k-WL
476   hierarchy. Our extensive empirical evaluations across diverse tasks demonstrate ToPE’s effectiveness,
477   showing significant improvements over existing methods.
478
      While ToPE offers substantial advantages, there are limitations to address. The computational cost
479
      associated with persistent homology computations remains an area for exploration. Additionally, this
480   work is currently restricted to graphs that are 1-dim simplicial complexes, limiting its applicability
481   to higher-order structural relationships. Extending ToPE to handle general higher-order complexes
482   presents a promising direction for future research, potentially enabling the capture of more sophisti-
483   cated topological features. In conclusion, we believe our work paves the road for many advancements
484   in boosting the representational power of GNNs. By establishing a bridge between topological data
485   analysis and positional encodings, ToPE opens new possibilities for developing more effective and
      expressive graph learning architectures.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
      Graph positional encodings. Positional encodings enhance representations in Graph Neural
705
      Networks (GNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017a; Xu et al., 2019; Velickovic et al., 2017) by incorporating
706   relational information between nodes based on their positions. Several approaches have been
707   developed to achieve this, including Laplacian-based methods that utilize the graph laplacian (Dwivedi
708   et al., 2021; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Maskey et al., 2022; Lim et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
709   2023), random walk-based techniques that leverage walks on graphs (Dwivedi et al., 2021; Eliasof
710   et al., 2023), and PageRank-inspired approaches that compute auxiliary distances (Ying et al., 2021;
711   Li et al., 2020). However, these methods partition the Laplacian eigenvalue/eigenvector space and
712   utilize only the partitioned eigenvalues/eigenvectors, disregarding the valuable information contained
713   in the remaining eigenvalues and eigenvectors. To address this limitation, we propose complementing
714
      the existing approaches with topological descriptors based on persistent homology, which can capture
      additional structural information from the graph.
715
716
717   Persistent homology on graphs Persistence homology methods (Horn et al., 2022; Carriere et al.,
718
      2020; Immonen et al., 2023) from topological data analysis have made rapid strides, providing
      topological descriptors that augment GNNs (Cesa & Behboodi, 2023; Verma et al., 2024) with
719
      persistent information to obtain more powerful representations, enhancing the expressivity (Rieck,
720
      2023; Wang et al., 2024) and generalizability (Brilliantov et al., 2024). However, these methods
721   have not been analyzed in regards with positional encodings in graphs, and the unification of these
722   topological descriptors with positional encodings remains an unexplored frontier.
