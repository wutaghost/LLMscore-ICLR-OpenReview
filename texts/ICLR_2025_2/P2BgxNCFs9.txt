011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Human feedback is crucial in the interactions between humans and Large Lan-
014            guage Models (LLMs). However, existing research primarily focuses on bench-
015            marking LLMs in single-turn dialogues. Even in benchmarks designed for multi-
016
               turn dialogues, the user inputs are often independent, neglecting the nuanced and
               complex nature of human feedback within real-world usage scenarios. To fill this
017
               research gap, we introduce FB-Bench, a fine-grained, multi-task benchmark de-
018
               signed to evaluate LLMs’ responsiveness to human feedback in real-world usage
019            scenarios. Drawing from the two main interaction scenarios, FB-Bench comprises
020            734 meticulously curated samples, encompassing eight task types, five deficiency
021            types of response, and nine feedback types. We extensively evaluate a broad ar-
022            ray of popular LLMs, revealing significant variations in their performance across
023            different interaction scenarios. Further analysis indicates that task, human feed-
024            back, and deficiencies of previous responses can also significantly impact LLMs’
025            responsiveness. Our findings underscore both the strengths and limitations of cur-
026            rent models, providing valuable insights and directions for future research. Both
027
               the toolkits and the dataset of FB-Bench will be released soon.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Equipped with advanced intelligence and formidable processing capabilities, large language mod-
033
      els (LLMs) have demonstrated substantial potential extensive potential in seamless interaction with
      human users and in assimilating real-time human feedback during inference processes (Fernandes
034
      et al., 2023). This human-LLM synergy can be mutually beneficial, breaking through the limita-
035
      tions inherent to each side (Li et al., 2023a; McAleese et al., 2024) and has been applied in many
036   domains (Schick et al., 2022; Saunders et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024c).
037
038   As a main component of human-LLM synergy, human feedback acts as a response to prior model
039
      outputs, serving as a guiding force that directs LLMs towards the desired outcomes (Fernandes
      et al., 2023). In practical applications, LLMs often need to iteratively adjust their responses based
040
      on user feedback in multi-turn dialogues to fulfill user needs. Effective feedback can enhance the
041
      quality of responses, while ineffective feedback may mislead LLMs. A robust LLM should leverage
042   appropriate feedback and remain undisturbed by inappropriate feedback. However, evaluating the
043   responsiveness of LLMs to human feedback within multi-turn dialogues presents a significant chal-
044   lenge, as these models exhibit divergent behaviors compared to single-turn dialogues. As illustrated
045   in Figure 1, LLMs that perform well in single-turn interactions may struggle to incorporate user
046   feedback effectively. Conversely, models that may not excel in single-turn scenarios could excel in
047   correcting their previous errors by skillfully leveraging user feedback to improve responses.
048   Despite the advancement in evaluating LLMs, there remains a lack of a systematic benchmark for
049   evaluating their responsiveness to various human feedback under real-world usage scenarios. Most
050   existing benchmarks (Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2023b; Ni et al., 2024) assess LLMs
051   within single-turn scenarios, which is naturally incapable of evaluating LLMs’ responsiveness to
052   human feedback. Although there are benchmarks designed for multi-turn dialugues (Zheng et al.,
053   2024; Sun et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024), the user inputs are often independent in a dialogue, lacking
      feedback towards to the previous LLM output. While several benchmarks incorporating feedback

079   In this work, we introduce FB-Bench, a fine-grained multi-task benchmark designed to evaluate
080   LLMs’ responsiveness to various human feedback within real-world usage scenarios. Drawing from
081   the interaction scenarios of error correction and response maintenance, FB-Bench organizes a three-
082   tier hierarchical taxonomy that encapsulates the fundamental elements of human-LLM interaction:
083
      user queries, model responses, and user feedback, as illustrated in Figure 2. It includes eight popular
      task types, five types of deficiencies in previous model outputs, and nine types of user feedback.
084
085   After meticulous curation, we collect 734 samples in FB-Bench, each consisting of a task-oriented
086   user query, a preset model response, human feedback, a human-curated reference follow-up re-
087   sponse, and a weighted checklist for evaluation. To precisely assess the performance of LLMs in
088   a detailed manner, we employ GPT- 4o to act as a judge, scoring the model-generated follow-up
089
      responses based on the weighted checklist and a human-curated reference follow-up response.
090   We conducted extensive experiments across a broad spectrum of popular LLMs. The results indicate
091   significant variations in performance between error correction and response maintenance scenarios.
092   We further analyze the impact of interaction scenarios, tasks, feedback, and previous responses’
093   deficiencies on LLMs’ responsiveness. These analyses reveals that
094    • Most LLMs exhibit superior performance in error correction compared to response maintenance.
095    • Advanced LLMs show similar performance on each task in error correction. In contrast, their
096      performance varies widely in response maintenance.
097    • Directly pointing out errors significantly aids LLMs in enhancing the quality of responses,
098      whereas providing feedback with fabricated credentials or expertise often misleads them.
099    • Advanced LLMs outperform less capable LLMs in addressing all categories of deficiencies,
100      especially in logic and following instructions.
101

103    • New perspective. We develop a three-tier hierarchical taxonomy that encapsulates the funda-
104      mental elements of human-LLM interactions, focusing primarily on two main interactive sce-
105      narios: error correction and response maintenance.
106    • New benchmark. We introduce FB-Bench, the first systematic benchmark for comprehensively
107      evaluating LLMs’ responsiveness to human feedback across a spectrum of real-world, multi-task
         scenarios.

144   2.1     I NTERACTION SCENARIO
145
146   In practical applications, error correction and response maintenance are two prevalent and signif-
147   icant scenarios. These scenarios encapsulate the essential dynamics between users and models,
148   underscoring the importance of models’ ability to adapt and respond effectively to user feedback.
149   Error Correction: Users may pose a query and find the model’s response either objectively incor-
150   rect or unsatisfactory. Consequently, they provide feedback, expecting the model to acknowledge its
151   response’s inadequacies and offer an improved version.
152
      Response Maintenance: Alternatively, when a user’s query receives an objectively correct or satis-
153
      factory response from the model, they might still engage in feedback. This could be to either reaffirm
154
      or challenge the provided answer, aiming to verify the correctness and reliability of the information.
155   The expectation is that the model will sustain its initial response upon receiving this feedback.
156
157   2.2     H IERARCHICAL DATA TAXONOMY
158
159   A typical human-LLM interaction process comprises three components: the user’s query, the
160   model’s response, and the user’s feedback. To ensure comprehensive coverage of various potential
161   interaction scenarios and interaction types, we develop an extensive three-tier hierarchical taxonomy
      from the perspective of these three components.

      2.2.1    Q UERY TASK
163
164   From the perspective of user queries, the diversity of interactions primarily stems from the task
165   type associated with each query. Therefore, we select eight popular tasks to encompass most real-
166   world usage scenarios. To enhance the diversity of queries further, we categorize the eight tasks into
167   twenty-four subtasks, as detailed in Appendix A.1.1.
168   Mathematics tasks are frequently encountered in human-LLM interaction scenarios. Given the
169   complexity of these problems, models often fail to provide accurate answers on their first attempt,
170   necessitating collaboration between humans and models to resolve complex issues.
171
      Reasoning tasks effectively reflect a model’s logical capabilities, indicative of its overall perfor-
172   mance. Strong logical abilities enable the model to excel in other complex tasks, making it a vital
173   component of human-LLM interaction.
174
175
      Coding tasks evaluate a model’s proficiency in comprehending and producing programming code,
      a capability that is becoming increasingly vital across a wide range of technology-oriented fields.
176
177   Text extraction tasks are pivotal for information retrieval, data analysis, and content summarization
178   applications, involving the extraction of structured information from unstructured text or pinpointing
179   specific content within extensive text volumes.
180   Text Error Correction tasks are pivotal in significantly enhancing the readability and overall quality
181   of written content. By fixing errors from typos to grammar, these tasks make text accurate and clear,
182   highlighting their key role in keeping written communication professional and intact.
183
      Text creation tasks not only test the model’s creativity and understanding but also play a crucial
184
      role in aiding people to express ideas more effectively and innovatively, enriching communication
185   across various fields.
186
187   Knowledge Q&A tasks assess a model’s proficiency in delivering precise and pertinent responses
188
      to a wide array of queries.
189   Text translation tasks evaluate the model’s proficiency in accurately translating text between lan-
190   guages, an essential capability in our progressively globalized world.
191
192   2.2.2    M ODEL RESPONSE
193
      From the perspective of the model’s response, it is either objectively correct or satisfies the user in
194
      response maintenance scenarios. In error correction scenarios, to enable more fine-grained research,
195
      we further categorize the deficiencies of model responses into the following five types:
196
197    • Not following instructions: The response does not grasp or adhere to the given context, instruc-
198      tions, or format requirements.
199    • Logical errors: The response contains mistakes in reasoning, calculation, or the application of
200      concepts.
201    • Incomplete answers: The response fails to fully address or resolve all aspects of a query.
202    • Factual errors: The response includes incorrect or outdated information, encompassing gram-
203      matical and technical inaccuracies.
204    • Unprofessional answers: The response lacks clarity, detail, or organization.
205
206   2.2.3    U SER FEEDBACK
207
      From the perspective of user feedback, the interaction between humans and LLMs can be signif-
208   icantly influenced by the nature of the user feedback provided. We design a total of nine distinct
209   types of feedback, comprising six for error correction and four for response maintenance, with one
210   type overlapping between error correction and response maintenance. Table 1 provides a brief one-
211   sentence description for each feedback within error correction and response maintenance scenarios.

252   2.4   DATASET C URATION
253
254   Each sample in FB-Bench mainly contains a task-oriented user query, a preset model response, hu-
255   man feedback, a human-curated reference follow-up response, and a weighted checklist for evalua-
256   tion. The example can be found in Appendix A.1.3. The detailed construction pipeline is described
257
      as follows.
258
259
      Collection To ensure the diversity and authenticity of user queries, we mine relevant data from
      two primary sources: an online chat service and human preference data, both derived from real-
260
      world usage scenarios. For error correction data, we employ heuristic rules to identify target data
261
      within the online chat service and select the response with the lowest score from human preference
262   data. For response maintenance data, we adopt an opposite strategy to filter the target data from the
263   two data sources. After gathering the above data, we perform deduplication and anonymization, and
264   categorize them into predefined tasks and subtasks using an in-house model to construct high task
265   diversity data.
266
267   Annotation Although mined data exhibit high task diversity, the feedback from most users is usu-
268   ally simple and homogenous. To improve the quality and variety of user feedback and to supply
269   essential elements for further analysis, we invite annotators to label data with finer granularity. Con-
      sidering the excellent performance of LLMs in aiding humans to generate comprehensive critiques


                                                               5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      and reduce hallucination rates (McAleese et al., 2024), we have annotators collaborate with GPT-4
271   to enhance the quality and efficiency of the annotation process. Initially, we utilize GPT-4 to ascer-
272   tain the cause of dissatisfaction when a model’s response does not meet the user’s expectations and
273   then simulate a user providing detailed feedback. Subsequently, GPT-4 is tasked with generating
274   a reference follow-up response and a weighted checklist to facilitate the evaluation. Finally, the
275   annotators act as the reviewers to refine all pre-annotated elements of each sample.
276
277
      Post-Filtering To enhance distinguishment in scores among LLMs, we utilize three models:
278
      Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023), Phi-3-mini-4k-instruct (Abdin et al., 2024), and Yi-
279
      1.5-9B-Chat (Young et al., 2024) as difficulty filters in our dataset curation pipeline. Specifically,
280   we benchmark these models using this dataset, analyze their responses, and score them by GPT-4o.
281   Finally, we discard samples for which all three models achieved full scores.

      4   R ELATED W ORK
464
465   Evaluation of LLMs The evaluation of LLMs is essential for their development. It reveals the
466   strengths and weaknesses of existing models and offers key insights and directions for future re-
467   search. However, most existing studies (Qin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Ni et al.,
468   2024; Li et al., 2023b) focus solely on evaluating the general or specific capabilities of LLMs in
469   single-turn dialogues. They fail to assess LLM performance under various user feedback, which
470   typically involves multi-turn dialogue scenarios. Although there are some benchmarks for multi-
471   turn LLMs (Zheng et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2024; Kwan et al., 2024; Liang et al.,
472   2024), the user inputs in the multi-turn dialogues are often independent, lacking feedback towards to
473
      the previous LLM output. Furthermore, much of the data in these multi-turn dialogue benchmarks
      is synthesized by LLMs, failing to exhibit the diversity and complexity of real-world scenarios.
474
475
      The Importance of Feedback Human feedback not only enhances model performance but also
476
      serves as a critical mechanism for aligning the model with desired outcomes or goals (Wiener, 2019).
477
      Training models on feedback data, not only can directly enhance the quality of the generated con-
478   tent (Ouyang et al., 2022) but also allows models to better align with human preferences in style and
479   tone (Ziegler et al., 2019). During the inference stage, users can provide feedback on intermediate
480   responses, enabling the model to refine its output until it achieves the user’s satisfaction (Schick
481   et al., 2022; Saunders et al., 2022). However, a systematic benchmark for evaluating the impact of
482   human feedback on LLMs during the inference stage is still lacking.
483
484   Benchmarks with Feedback Several benchmarks have begun to explore the impact of feedback
485   on LLMs. However, they predominantly focus on specific tasks or domains. MINT (Wang et al.,
      2023) exclusively assesses the coding and reasoning capabilities of LLMs that utilize tools and


                                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      receive AI-generated language feedback. Intercode (Yang et al., 2024b) evaluates the coding skills
487   of LLMs based on feedback from compilers or interpreters executing the code. AgentBench (Liu
488   et al., 2023b) examines the reasoning and decision-making abilities of LLMs-as-Agents in response
489   to environmental feedback. Different from prior works, FB-Bench introduces a novel approach by
490   measuring the responsiveness of LLMs to diverse user feedback across a broad spectrum of real-
491   world usage scenarios.
492
493
      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      We introduce FB-Bench, a fine-grained muti-task benchmark for comprehensively evaluating the
496   responsiveness of LLMs to various human feedback across real-world usage scenarios. A three-tier
497   hierarchical taxonomy, grounded in two popular human-LLM interaction scenarios, is established
498   to ensure thorough coverage of diverse interaction types and scenarios. To facilitate a fine-grained
499   and accurate evaluation, a LLM-as-a-Judge framework, equipped with a weighted checklist, is em-
500   ployed. Benchmarking results from 31 well-known LLMs demonstrate significant performance vari-
501   ations between error correction and response maintenance. Further analysis explores the principal
502   factors influencing the responsiveness of LLMs and provides valuable insights for subsequent re-
503   search.
