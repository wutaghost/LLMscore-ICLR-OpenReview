009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The prediction of long sequences has always been a challenge in time series
012            forecasting tasks. Due to Mamba’s sequence selection capability, many Mamba-
013            based models have been proposed, achieving state-of-the-art results in long se-
014            quence prediction problems. However, much research has focused on integrating
015            mamba-ssm into specific model structures for better performance, while the core
016
               of mamba-ssm, its sequence selection capability, has not been deeply explored.
               We believe there is significant potential in Mamba’s sequence selection capa-
017
               bility and propose a Repetitive Contrastive Learning (RCL) method to enhance
018
               it. Specifically, we utilize Repeating Sequence Augmentation to expand the se-
019            quence while introducing Gaussian noise, thereby enhancing the Mamba block’s
020            sequence selection capability through both inter-sequence and intra-sequence con-
021            trastive techniques. Then our methods transfer parameters directly from a single
022            pre-trained Mamba block to a variety of Mamba-based models. This approach
023            provides superior initialization for forecasting tasks. Our experiments consis-
024            tently demonstrate that this technique improves the forecasting performance of
025            many Mamba-based models, without imposing additional memory requirements.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Time series forecasting (TSF) has become indispensable across a range of critical domains, including
030   financial markets Li et al. (2023), traffic management Cheng et al. (2023), electricity consumption
031   prediction Sun & Zhang (2023), scientific computing Cruz-Camacho et al. (2024), and weather
032   forecasting Zhang et al. (2022a). TSF leverages sequential data, often of varying lengths, from
033   past observations to predict future trends. However, fully reliable predictors remain elusive due to
034   the unknown generative mechanisms underlying time series data. Compounding this challenge are
035   issues such as uneven sampling, missing or duplicate data points, and inherent irregular noise, which
036   complicate the forecasting tasks.
037   Deep learning has made significant strides in the time series domain, with much of the focus centered
038   on model architecture design, particularly for transformer-based models Wen et al. (2023) . These
039   models now play a pivotal role in forecasting tasks, yet they are often hindered by the quadratic
040   complexity of their attention mechanisms. While time series data share some structural similarities
041   with natural language, transformers typically underperform in this domain compared to traditional
042   backbones like CNNs and MLPs Zeng et al. (2022). Despite the limitations of traditional backbones
043   in capturing long-range dependencies, they are more effective at addressing the sequential and high-
044
      noise characteristics of time series data, which contributes to their better performance.
045   The emergence of the Mamba model Gu & Dao (2024) has attracted researchers from diverse fields,
046   including those focused on multi-modal and multi-dimensional data, thanks to its unique selective
047   state-space mechanism Huang et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024). Mamba’s selective mechanism not
048   only resolves the quadratic time complexity of transformer attention mechanisms but also maintains
049   comparable long-distance propagation capabilities. Recent applications of Mamba in TSF, such as
050
      TimeMachine Ahamed & Cheng (2024) and Bi-Mamba Liang et al. (2024), have primarily focused
      on refining Mamba’s block architecture. However, these efforts have overlooked the critical chal-
051
      lenge of teaching models how to effectively select and prioritize key moments in time series data.
052
053   One major reason for this gap is rooted in the training objectives used for TSF. Most existing models
      focus on straightforward prediction tasks, analogous to causal and masked language modeling in

054
      NLP. Although these methods have yielded undeniable success in natural language processing, they
055   may not be as effective for time series data. NLP tasks often rely on models to grasp contextual
056   knowledge, such as common sense, syntax, and semantics, from the input text. In contrast, time
057   series data lack generalized knowledge, focusing instead on sequential and often sparse patterns.
058   Unlike NLP data, which can be pre-processed to remove noise and ensure consistency, time series
059   data is marked by irregular and noisy sampling points that are often imperceptible to humans and
060   can only be effectively classified by models during training.
061   Thus, directly applying modules, which are designed for NLP tasks, to time series data without
062   adapting the training goals leads to predictable failures. Recent experiments have shown that relying
063   solely on prediction tasks does not enable Mamba to fully resolve the complexities of time series
064   data Zhang et al. (2024). These results suggest that prediction tasks alone are insufficient for models
065   to gain a deep understanding of the underlying structure and dynamics of time series. In such cases,
066   models often struggle to selectively focus on relevant moments, instead over-integrating all available
067   information in an attempt to capture causal relationships.
068   Drawing from these insights, we propose that the central task in time series processing should em-
069   phasize teaching models effective selection mechanisms. By leveraging Mamba’s new selective
070   space models module, we believe it has the potential to surpass other models in handling selec-
071   tion tasks on sparse time series data. To address existing challenges, we introduce a new training
072   paradigm aimed at optimizing Mamba’s selection process.
073   Technically, our approach enhances time series data through Repeating Sequence Augmentation and
074   pre-trains the original models using Repetitive Contrastive Learning. During the augmentation stage,
075   each token in the time series is duplicated and augmented with Gaussian noise. In the learning stage,
076   we implement intra-sequence contrast, which forms positive and negative pairs from corresponding
077   tokens between the augmented sequence and the original sequence, and inter-sequence contrast,
078   which derives positive and negative pairs from within the augmented sequence itself.
079   We substitute Mamba Block parameters across various Mamba-based models with those obtained
080   from the pre-trained Mamba block and evaluate the resulting performance improvements relative
081   to the original models. Our work also details effective block substitution methods and parameter-
082   freezing strategies.
083
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
084
085          • We propose a data augmentation method called Repeating Sequence Augmentation,
086            which generates extended time series and forms token-level contrastive samples.
087
             • We introduce Repetitive Contrastive Learning, which compares original and repeated se-
088
               quences within a Mamba block to obtain initialization parameters with enhanced sequence
089
               selection and robustness.
090
091
             • We transfer pre-trained parameters to different Mamba-based models, demonstrating a gen-
               eralized approach that consistently improves the predictive capability of these models on
092
               various datasets.
093
094          • We analyze effective parameter replacement methods, parameter freezing techniques, and
095
               the impact on training time and memory overhead.

099   2.1   M ULTIVARIATE T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
100
101   Multivariate time series forecasting involves predicting future values of multiple interrelated time-
102   dependent variables based on their historical data. Unlike univariate time series forecasting, which
103   focuses on a single variable, multivariate forecasting accounts for interactions and correlations be-
104
      tween multiple variables to improve prediction accuracy and insightfulness.
105   A multivariate time series forecasting problem can be formally represented with an input time series
106   denoted as X ∈ RTin ×F , where Tin is the input sequence length (number of time steps) and F
107   represents the number of features or variables at each time step. The prediction target is represented
      as Y ∈ RTout ×F , where Tout denotes the output sequence length for which forecasts are made.

135   Our proposed repetitive contrastive pre-training method involves three main steps. First, we con-
136   struct augmented data by repeating time steps and introducing noise, defining positive and negative
137   sample pairs in the process. Next, a Mamba block undergoes pre-training through contrastive learn-
138   ing to enhance its ability to select relevant time series features. Finally, the pre-trained parameters
139   of the Mamba block are transferred to various Mamba-based models for fine-tuning.

205   We input both the original sequence X and its augmented version Xaug into the same Mamba
206   Block, comparing their respective outputs H and Haug to evaluate the Mamba Block’s modeling

208   encompasses two types of comparisons: intra-sequence contrast and inter-sequence contrast. Firstly,
209   we define the output at any time step i with a repetition count nt of the original sequence Xi as Hi ,

211

      3.4     R EPLACE AND I NFERENCE
277
      Based on different input sequences, we categorize Mamba blocks pre-trained with the input time se-
278
      ries X and the inverted time series X I as MambaPB and iMambaPB, respectively. These two types
279   of Mamba blocks can be integrated into the majority of existing Mamba-based models. For example,
280   in the Origin Mamba model Gu & Dao (2024), we substitute the parameters of the Mamba block
281   with MambaPB. Similarly, for the iMamba model, we replace the block’s parameters with iMam-
282   baPB. In the case of the TimeMachine model Ahamed & Cheng (2024), we also utilize iMambaPB
283   for parameter substitution.
284
      Since the pre-trained Mamba block is designed to achieve enhanced selection capabilities, we freeze
285   the matrix A, which governs the Mamba’s selectivity, during the inference stage. Other parame-
286   ters, however, can be adjusted to suit the requirements of specific prediction tasks. Our experiments
287   include a comparison of various parameter-freezing methods to illustrate the impact on model per-
288   formance.
289
      Additionally, Mamba-based models are often constructed with multiple stacked blocks, and select-
290
      ing which blocks to replace with pre-trained parameters plays a crucial role in performance improve-
291   ment. We replace the parameters of the Mamba SSM in all blocks with MambaPB and iMambaPB,
292   and our experiments compare the results of different replacement ratios to highlight their effect on
293   model performance.

337   by replacing the Mamba block parameters represents a meaningful advancement.
338   In summary, the parameters of the Mamba block, learned through the Repetitive Contrastive Learn-
339   ing method, consistently enhance the performance of various Mamba-based models. This under-
340   scores our method’s efficacy in improving the sequence selection capability of the Mamba block
341   and highlights its adaptability and potential for broad application.

368   We conducted two ablation experiments to evaluate our proposed RCL method. All ablation ex-

      periments used a 4-layer Mamba as the baseline model. In the first ablation experiment, as shown

371
      enhances the Mamba block’s ability to select time steps and denoise. Without it, the model’s ability

373   loss, as repeated time sequences can disrupt temporal consistency. The purpose of inter-sequence
374   contrast is to maintain consistency with the temporal features of the original sequence. Without it,
375   RCL cannot learn temporal features in broken sequences. The most significant performance drop
376   occurred when noise was removed. Without added noise, repeated time steps are indistinguishable
377   from the original ones, reducing task difficulty and failing to enhance the Mamba block’s ability to
      resist noise and select time steps.

      5.1           M ODELS IN D EEP T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
467
468   There has been extensive research focused on solving time series forecasting problems, with most
469   works aiming to propose new models that improve prediction accuracy. Based on their model
470   backbones, the research can be categorized into five primary groups: Transformer-based, RNN-
471   basedHochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997), CNN-based, MLP-based, and Mamba-based models.
472   While each of these approaches may emphasize different aspects, the key improvements revolve
473   around addressing the specific challenges of time series tasks.
474   TimesNetWu et al. (2023), a CNN-based model, utilizes different periodical segmentations in both
475   the frequency and time domains, helping models extract features from inter-period variations and
476   infer patterns from intra-period variations. TimeMixerWang et al. (2024), which relies solely on
477   MLP and pooling layers, outperformed all previous models by focusing on decomposing and mixing
478   multi-scale time series data.
479
      Transformer-based and Mamba-based models, on the other hand, mainly enhance the adaptability
480   of their respective architectures to better address time series tasks. LogTrans Li et al. (2020) and
481   Informer Zhou et al. (2021) introduced sparse attention mechanisms tailored for serialized data,
482   allowing transformers to align more effectively with the nature of time series. Further advancements,
483   such as Autoformer Wu et al. (2022) and FEDformer Zhou et al. (2022), demonstrated the critical
484   importance of decomposing time series into seasonal and trend components. This decomposition
485   serves as an instructive process, guiding models to better process data along the time dimension,
      which is essential even for attention-heavy transformer models.


                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      PatchTST Nie et al. (2023), by segmenting time series into fixed-length patches, significantly en-
487   hances models’ abilities to denoise and selectively process relevant information. Meanwhile, iTrans-
488   former Liu et al. (2024) introduces an innovative method by swapping the roles of features and
489   time in the series, passing the time dimension through a linear layer to create ’time embeddings.’
490   Mamba-based approaches, like TimeMachineAhamed & Cheng (2024), unify channel-mixing and
491   channel-independence, enabling the model to effectively select the most relevant content for pre-
492   diction. These methods underscore the importance of auxiliary mechanisms to enhance time series
493   selection and processing.

495
496   5.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
497
498   Most contrastive self-supervised learning methods have been applied primarily in the fields of vi-
499   sionJaiswal et al. (2021) and multimodal learningManzoor et al. (2024). This is because the objects
500   used for contrast typically possess distinguishable high-level attributes that are easy for humans to
501   recognize and are less susceptible to being obscured by noise. For example, image data remains
502   interpretable to humans even when subjected to perturbations like color alterations or geometric
503   transformations. Similarly, multimodal contrastive learning leverages cross-modality correlations,
504   such as in visual-textual contrastive learning, where each modality provides intrinsic information to
505
      enhance the contrastive task.
506   In contrast, the application of contrastive learning to unimodal sequential data has been less com-
507   mon, often requiring specialized features. For example, CodeRetrieverLi et al. (2022) employs a
508   similarity contrastive loss in code semantic spaces to capture nuances in code sequences. Other
509   contrastive methods, such as those used in sequential recommendationXie & Li (2024) or text sum-
510   marizationXu et al. (2022), rely on distinct sequence representations and specific training techniques
511
      to enhance contrastive performance in these domains.
512   In the time series domain, numerous works have focused on improving representation learning
513   through contrastive pre-training. TS2VecYue et al. (2022) introduced a universal framework for
514   learning time series representations at arbitrary semantic levels, emphasizing context view augmen-
515   tation and hierarchical contrastive learning. Subsequently, TF-CZhang et al. (2022b) proposed a dif-
516   ferent contrastive approach by aligning time-based and frequency-based representations to achieve
517
      improved representations. Building upon this, InfoTS applied principles from information theory to
      prioritize high-fidelity and diverse representations, presenting a novel contrastive learning method.
518
      Meanwhile, SoftCLSLee et al. (2024) introduced soft assignments for instance-wise and temporal
519
      contrastive losses, capturing both inter-sample and intra-temporal relationships.
520
521   These methods primarily target enhanced representation learning of time series, resulting in strong
522   performance on classification tasks but limited applicability to forecasting tasks. In contrast, our
523
      approach focuses on pre-training mamba models to capture the characteristics of recurrent noise
      patterns within time series. Consequently, the pre-trained parameters can be directly applied to
524
      downstream forecasting models, marking a significant point of distinction and novelty compared to
525
      existing methods.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel training paradigm called Repetitive Contrastive Learning (RCL)
532
      for Mamba-based models in time series tasks. The sequence selection ability of mamba block was
533
      enhanced by sequence repetition and intra-sequence and inter-sequence comparison. We conducted
534   extensive experiments to demonstrate the efficacy of the Mamba block within the broader Mamba
535   architecture. Our results highlight the capability of our approach in capturing the key characteristics
536   inherent to time series data. To further substantiate our findings, we applied the method across
537   various Mamba-based models, consistently observing significant improvements in generalization.
538   Additionally, we evaluated resource consumption and found our method does not create additional
539   memory burden, and the time consumption only increases linearly. Future work will aim to refine
      noise addition techniques and reduce training overhead to further enhance task performance.

1044   A Mamba-based model typically comprises multiple Mamba blocks. Each Mamba block contains a
1045   matrix A, which is defined in 3.2. The parameters are responsible for controlling the block’s selec-
1046   tivity towards information before. To evaluate the impact of parameter replacement and parameter
1047
       freezing during the inference stage, we used a 4-layer Mamba model as a baseline. The replacement
       strategy involved substituting 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% of the Mamba blocks, while the parame-
1048
       ter freezing strategy was categorized into no freezing (None) and freezing of matrix A (FrozenA).
1049
       Freezing matrix A helps preserve the enhanced selectivity gained during pre-training.
1050
1051   As shown in Table 9, the optimal parameter replacement and freezing strategies differ across
1052   datasets. For the ETTm1 dataset, replacing 50% of the Mamba blocks without freezing any pa-
1053
       rameters yielded the greatest improvement, while replacing 100% of the blocks resulted in the low-
       est performance. This suggests that the selection capabilities of the pre-trained parameters do not
1054
       fully align with the prediction target. By replacing only 50% of the Mamba blocks, the model can
1055
       better encode the time series, while the remaining blocks focus on fitting the specific prediction
1056   requirements of the dataset, ultimately enhancing model performance.
1057
1058   Conversely, for the ETTm2 dataset, the greatest improvement was achieved by replacing all Mamba
1059
       blocks and freezing matrix A. In this case, the selective enhancements from pre-training aligned
       well with the dataset’s prediction targets. This approach preserved the pre-trained parameters’ se-
1060
       lectivity while allowing the remaining parameters to adjust to fit the prediction targets effectively.
1061
1062   Similar results were observed across other datasets. Broadly, the findings can be grouped into two
1063   effective strategies: replacing 50% of the Mamba blocks without freezing any parameters and re-
1064   placing 100% of the Mamba blocks while freezing matrix A. We recommend choosing between
1065
       these two approaches during the inference phase for optimal performance.
