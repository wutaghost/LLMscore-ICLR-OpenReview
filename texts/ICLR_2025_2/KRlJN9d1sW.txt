009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Real-world reinforcement learning applications are often hampered by delayed
013            feedback from environments, which violates the fundamental assumption of the
014            Markovian property and introduces significant challenges. While numerous meth-
015            ods have been proposed for handling environments with constant delays, those
016            with random delays remain largely unexplored owing to their inherent complexity
017
               and variability. In this study, we explored environments with random delays and
               proposed a novel strategy to transform them into their equivalent constant-delay
018
               counterparts by introducing a simple agent called the lazy-agent. This approach
019
               naturally overcomes the challenges posed by the variability of random delays, en-
020            abling the application of state-of-the-art methods, originally designed for handling
021            constant delays, to random-delay environments without any modification. Empir-
022            ical results demonstrate that the lazy-agent-based algorithm significantly outper-
023            formed other baselines in terms of asymptotic performance and sample efficiency
024            in random-delay environments.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Reinforcement learning (RL) has made remarkable progress in various domains, from gaming (Mnih
033
      et al., 2013; Silver et al., 2016) to robotic control systems (Haarnoja et al., 2018; Kalashnikov et al.,
      2018). However, real-world applications of RL often face challenges due to delays, which can take
034
      diverse forms such as latency in communication systems, delays in processing sensory data, or
035
      response delays from actuators. These delays can significantly degrade the performance of RL agents
036   and may even cause instability in dynamic systems (Hwangbo et al., 2017; Mahmood et al., 2018).
037
038   While numerous methods have been proposed to address the challenges posed by delays within the
039
      RL framework, these efforts primarily focus on the unrealistic assumption of constant delays (Chen
      et al., 2021; Derman et al., 2021; Liotet et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), leaving
040
      random delays relatively unexplored owing to their inherent complexity and variability. However, in
041
      real-world, randomly varying delays present a more realistic challenge, exemplified by communi-
042   cation systems where diverse routing paths and the physical properties of the network can result in
043   asynchronous data arrivals (Ge et al., 2013).
044
045
      In this study, we explore environments with random delays and establish a connection to envi-
      ronments with constant delays by introducing a simple agent called the lazy-agent. Specifically,
046
      we demonstrate that random-delay environments can be straightforwardly transformed into their
047
      equivalent constant-delay counterparts using lazy-agents, enabling state-of-the-art constant-delay
048   approaches to be seamlessly applied to random-delay environments without any modification. We
049   train lazy-agents within the belief projection-based Q-learning (BPQL) framework (Kim et al.,
050   2023), termed lazy-BPQL, to leverage its advantages in training agents in delayed environments. The
051   efficacy of the proposed lazy-BPQL was evaluated on popular continuous control tasks in the Mu-
052   JoCo benchmark (Todorov et al., 2012). Empirical results demonstrate that lazy-BPQL outperformed
053   other baseline algorithms in terms of asymptotic performance and sample efficiency in random-delay
      environments, achieving performance comparable to agents trained in constant-delay environments.

056   2.1   S TANDARD R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
057
058   A (delay-free) Markov decision process (MDP) (Bellman, 1957) can be defined with a five-tuples
059   (S, A, P, R, γ), where S is the state space, and A is the action space, P : S × A × S → [0, 1] is
060   the transition kernel, R : S × A → R is the reward function, and γ ∈ (0, 1) is a discount factor.
061   Additionally, the policy π : S × A → [0, 1] maps the state-to-action distribution.
062   Under this definition, at each discrete time t, an RL agent observes state st , makes a decision at
063   based on a policy π, receives a reward rt with respect to the action taken, and then observes the next
064   state st+1 from the environment. It repeats this process to find an optimal policy π ∗ that maximizes
065   the expected discounted cumulative rewards, which is given as:

078   Note that the dynamics governing MDPs assume the Markovian property, which indicates that the

080

      delayed feedback from the environment, leading to partially observable MDPs (Monahan, 1982),
081
      where the agent’s current state and action cannot capture sufficient information needed for timely
082
      decision-making. This can significantly degrade the performance of RL agents or even lead to com-
083   plete failure in learning (Singh et al., 1994).
084
085   2.2   D ELAYED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
086
087   In MDP with delays, referred to as delayed MDP, the state of the environment may not be observed
088   by the agent immediately (observation delay). The effect of the action applied to the environment
089   may also be delayed (action delay). Additionally, the reward generated by the action taken may not
090   reach the agent immediately (reward delay). These delays force the agent to make decisions based on
091   outdated information, prevent timely and appropriate actions, or cause the agent to receive rewards
092
      that do not correspond to the actions taken, disrupting the learning process.
093   Delayed MDPs are typically categorized into constant-delay MDPs (CDMDPs), where feedback is
094   delayed by a fixed number of time-steps; and random-delay MDPs (RDMDPs), where the number
095   of delayed time-steps varies randomly. For example, in the case of random observation delay, the
096   state st may be delayed by four time-steps and observed by the agent at time t + 4, whereas the next
097   state st+1 may be delayed by only one time-step and observed at time t + 2. Note that the subscript
098
      t explicitly indicates the times when states are generated by the actions applied to the environment.
099   In this study, we focus on randomly delayed observations under the assumption that the agent utilizes
100   them for decision-making in order. This implies that any observed state can be used by the agent
101   for decision-making only after all previously generated states have been both observed and utilized
102   to ensure no state is omitted from the decision-making process. In the presence of randomness in
103   observation delays, multiple states may become observable simultaneously, and their order may even
104
      be scrambled. To reduce confusion about the timing of state observations, we distinguish between a
      state being observed and used, under the aforementioned assumption of ordering, as follows:
105
106   Definition 2.1. A state is considered observed when the information about the state of the envi-
107   ronment reaches the agent. A state is considered used when the agent utilizes the observed state
      information to make a decision by feeding it into the policy.

135   3     AUGMENTATION - BASED A PPROACH
136
137   An augmentation-based approach is often preferred in delayed MDPs, as it retrieves the Markovian
138   property and offers advantages for agents learning policies through conventional RL algorithms
139   in such environments (Liotet et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). As demonstrated
140   by Altman & Nain (1992); Katsikopoulos & Engelbrecht (2003), delayed MDPs can be reduced to
141   equivalent MDPs without delays through this approach, known as regular MDPs, where the resulting
142   optimal policies are optimal in the original delayed MDPs (Bander & White III, 1999; Katsikopoulos
143   & Engelbrecht, 2003). The augmentation-based approach involves state augmentation, where the
144
      state is concatenated with additional delay-related information, similar to methods employed in
      conventional control theory (Kwon & Pearson, 1980; Park et al., 2008). In this section, we examine
145
      two types of delayed MDPs: CDMDPs and RDMDPs.
146
147
      3.1    C ONSTANT-D ELAY MDP S
148
149   CDMDPs can be defined as a six-tuple (S, A, P, R, γ, o), where o ∈ N is a constant variable
150   representing the observation delay. As demonstrated by Katsikopoulos & Engelbrecht (2003), it is
151   reducible to regular MDPs (Xo , A, P, R̄, γ), where Xo = S × Ao is the augmented state space with
152   Ao being the Cartesian product of A with itself for o times, and R̄ : Xo × A → R is the reward
153   function with respect to the augmented state space, termed the augmented reward function. Finally,
154   the augmented state-based policy π̄ : Xo × A → [0, 1] maps augmented state-to-action distribution.
155   To be specific, the augmented state at time t is defined as:
156

      4     B ELIEF P ROJECTION -BASED Q- LEARNING
224
225
      While the augmentation-based approach provides a foundation for training agents with conventional
      RL algorithms in delayed MDPs, it has a known limitation: the state space grows exponentially as the
226
      number of delayed time-steps increases, resulting in sample inefficiency and slow convergence. This
227
      is called the state-space explosion issue, which makes the augmentation-based approach unfavorable
228   for environments with long delays (Derman et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023).
229
230   To mitigate this issue, belief projection-based Q-learning (BPQL), a model-free actor-critic frame-
231
      work designed to handle constant-delay environments, was proposed by Kim et al. (2023). It ex-
      hibited remarkable performance with simple modifications to the conventional augmentation-based
232
      approach, effectively alleviating the state-space explosion issue.
233

      5     B RIDGING RDMDP S TO CDMDP S
278
279   In this section, we demonstrate that RDMDPs can be transformed into their equivalent CDMDPs by
280   introducing a simple agent called lazy-agent, allowing state-of-the-art constant-delay approaches to
281   be seamlessly extended to random-delay environments.
282
283   5.1   L AZY-AGENT
284
285   To address the challenges caused by variability in random delays, we let the agent assume that all
286   states are delayed by the maximum number of time-steps. The agent then uses the observed states in
287   decision-making processes at their maximum delayed times, that is, τ (sonn ) = n + omax , ∀n > 0. In
288
      this scheme, each state is consistently used in sequence exactly omax time-steps after being generated,
      regardless of its actual delay.
289
290   Suppose the state so11 is observed at time 1+o1 . Since the agent assumes that all states are delayed by
291   omax time-steps, it uses the observed state at time 1+omax irrespective of its actual delay o1 . Similarly,
292   the subsequent state so22 is observed at time 2 + o2 ; however, the agent uses it at time 2 + omax .
293   In short, the agent uses observed states at their maximum delayed times, regardless of their actual
294
      delays. This implies that the exact delays for each state may remain unknown to the agent, except for
      the maximum delay. Consequently, this approach effectively circumvents the challenges associated
295
      with variability in random delays. We refer to this agent as a lazy-agent. A visual representation is

      provided in Appendix E.

339   5.2     L AZY-BPQL
340
      In the previous section, we demonstrated that RDMDPs can become equivalent to CDMDPs with
341
      lazy-agents. However, since the delays in the resulting CDMDPs are determined by the maximum
342
      delays in RDMDPs, the lazy-agents often encounter long-delay challenges, particularly the state-
343   space explosion issue. Specifically, the augmented state space in derived CDMDPs would be defined
344   as Xomax = S × Aomax , necessitating numerous samples for the augmented-based values to converge.
345
346
      To address this issue, we employ lazy-agents within the BPQL framework to leverage its advan-
      tage in training agents in constant-delay environments while effectively alleviating the state-space
347
      explosion problem. We refer to this approach as lazy-BPQL, which is summarized in Algorithm 1.

351
352   6.1     B ENCHMARKS AND BASELINE ALGORITHMS
353
      We evaluated our algorithm on popular continuous control tasks in the MuJoCo benchmark by grad-
354
      ually increasing the maximum delay omax from 5 to 20, to assess its performance and robustness
355
      with respect to the degree of randomness in delays. In our experiments, we assumed that random
356   delays are sampled from a discrete uniform distribution. Details of the benchmark environments and
357   experiments are provided in Appendix D.
358
359
      The following algorithms are included in experiments: normal SAC (Haarnoja et al., 2018), delayed-
      SAC (Derman et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2023), lazy-BPQL, and DC/AC (Bouteiller et al., 2020). The
360
      normal SAC adopts a naive approach that selects actions for currently usable states on a memoryless
361
      basis and performs ‘no-ops’ otherwise, without addressing the violation of the Markovian assump-
362   tion in delayed MDPs. Delayed-SAC is a variant of delayed-Q (Derman et al., 2021) adapted by
363   Kim et al. (2023) for application in continuous spaces. It employs an approximate forward model
364   to explicitly predict unobserved states, which can be learned from transition samples collected in
365   undelayed environments. With this model, the agent recursively predicts unobserved states through
366   one-step predictions repeated over delayed time-steps and selects actions based on predicted states.
367   Lastly, DC/AC is an improved version of SAC that implements an off-policy multi-step value estima-
368   tion combined with a partial trajectory resampling method, significantly enhancing sample efficiency
369   and demonstrating notable performance in environments with both constant and random delays.

      7   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We investigated environments with random observation delays and proposed a novel approach to
489   establish a connection to environments with constant delays by introducing a simple agent called
490   the lazy-agent. With the proposed lazy-agents, random-delay environments can be transformed into
491   their equivalent constant-delay counterparts, facilitating the application of state-of-the-art constant-
492   delay approaches to random-delay environments without any modifications. We employed lazy-
493   agents within the belief projection-based Q-learning (BPQL) framework, referred to as lazy-BPQL,
494   to train our agents in equivalent constant-delay environments while effectively mitigating the state-
495   space explosion issue of the augmentation-based approach. The empirical results demonstrated that
496   the proposed lazy-BPQL significantly outperformed other baseline algorithms in terms of asymp-
497
      totic performance and sample efficiency in random-delay environments, which strongly supports the
      efficacy of our approach.
498
499   It would be meaningful to employ our lazy-agents in real-world dynamic systems that suffer from
500   random delays, where conventional constant-delay approaches are inadequate. In the future, we will
501   extend the proposed algorithm to real-world applications, such as robotic locomotion and manipula-
502   tion, by accounting for randomly varying sensor and actuator delays. We believe that the lazy-agents
503
      will play a pivotal role in extending conventional RL methods to real-world dynamic systems.
504
505   8   L IMITATIONS
506
507   Despite its notable advantages in constructing equivalent constant-delay environments from the orig-
508   inal random-delay environments, employing the lazy-agent may encounter difficulties associated
509   with long delays. This is because the constant delays in the equivalent environments are aligned with
510   the maximum delay in the original random-delay environments. Consequently, the model-based ap-
511
      proach may require more carefully designed dynamics models, as accumulated errors in recursive
      one-step predictions could result in significant performance degradation. On the other hand, the
512
      augmentation-based approach may confront the inherent state-space explosion issue. To circumvent
513
      this issue, we adopted a strategy of training lazy-agents within the BPQL framework, which can
514   effectively mitigate the state-space explosion issue. Alternatively, the use of recurrent models, such
515   as GRU (Cho, 2014), can also be considered, as explored in Firoiu et al. (2018). However, the ne-
516   cessity of knowing the maximum delay raises another concern, which may be unrealistic in some
517   environments. This remains a challenge to be addressed in future work.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
649
650
      Delays are prevalent in real-world reinforcement learning (RL) applications, arising from various
651   factors such as computational time. Previous research on handling such delays within the RL frame-
652   work can be categorized into two main approaches: augmentation-based and model-based.
653
654
      Augmentation-based approach The augmentation-based approach is often preferred in delayed
      environments since it allows us to retrieve the violated Markovian property (Altman & Nain, 1992;
655
      Katsikopoulos & Engelbrecht, 2003) and offers advantages for training agents through conventional
656
      RL algorithms in such environments. Despite its notable advantages, the method of augmenting the
657   state itself remains a challenge associated with sample complexity, commonly referred to as the
658   state-space explosion issue or the curse of dimensionality, which results in learning inefficiency.
659   To mitigate this issue, DC/AC (Bouteiller et al., 2020) introduces an off-policy multi-step value
660   estimation combined with a partial trajectory resampling method, which greatly enhances sample
661   efficiency and accelerates the learning process. BPQL (Kim et al., 2023) proposes another novel
662   approach to overcome such difficulty by introducing alternative representations for augmentation-
663   based values. These alternative values are evaluated with respect to the original state space rather
664   than the augmented one, thereby inherently mitigating the state-space explosion issue and demon-
665
      strating remarkable performance. More recently, AD-RL (Wu et al., 2024) alleviates the performance
      degradation stemming from this issue by leveraging auxiliary tasks with shorter delays to learn tasks
666
      with relatively long delays, reducing sample complexity and achieving notable performance.
667
668   Model-based approach The model-based approach, also known as the state estimation method,
669   aims to restore the Markovian property using learned dynamics models from underlying delay-free
670   environments (Walsh et al., 2009; Firoiu et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2021; Derman et al., 2021). For
671
      example, delayed-Q (Derman et al., 2021) employs an approximate feed-forward model to explic-
      itly predict unobserved states, which can be learned from transition samples collected in delay-free
672
      environments. Using this model, the agent recursively predicts unobserved states through one-step
673
      predictions repeated over delayed time-steps and selects actions based on the predicted states. Sim-
674   ilarly, Firoiu et al. (2018) proposes an approach that utilizes recurrent neural networks (Cho, 2014)
675   to model the dynamics. These approaches facilitate sample-efficient learning without being affected
676   by the issues associated with the sample complexity posed by the augmented state. However, ap-
677   proximation errors in building dynamics models may induce accumulated prediction errors, leading
678   to suboptimal performance. Furthermore, the presence of noise in observations can exacerbate inac-
679   curacies in learning dynamics models.
680   While numerous methods have shown promise, most works focus on the unrealistic assumption of
681   constant delays, exhibiting nonnegligible performance degradation when applied to environments
682   with random delays. Building upon previously proposed state-of-the-art methods, this study makes
683   its primary contribution by proposing a novel approach that enables the handling of constant de-
684   lays and random delays in exactly the same manner. Specifically, we propose a method to construct
685   equivalent constant-delay environments from the original random-delay environments by introduc-
686   ing a simple agent termed the lazy-agent. This approach offers valuable insight that there is no need
687   to devise new methods for handling random delays, as the lazy-agent naturally facilitates the appli-
688
      cation of conventionally proposed state-of-the-art methods, originally designed for constant delays,
      to random-delay environments without any modifications.

1003   mance of agents trained with and without this assumption (see Fig. 9). In the experiment, we utilized
1004   delayed-SAC for learning InvertedPendulum-v2 task in MuJoCo, as it demonstrated respectable and
1005   stable performance in relatively simple tasks. We aimed to verify how this assumption impacts such
1006   performance, even in such simple task. We refer to the delayed-SAC agent trained in an ordered
1007   manner as the ordered agent, and the agent trained in a disordered manner as the unordered agent.

1081
1082   The proposed lazy-agent can be seamlessly integrated into the BPQL framework with minimal mod-
1083   ifications by using the initial state for decision-making at its maximum delayed times. Subsequently,
1084   all states become naturally available for use at their respective maximum delayed times.
1085

1086   store observed states, corresponding rewards, and action histories, which enables the agent to access
1087   timely and relevant information for constructing augmented states. Additionally, we have assumed
1088   that all feedback, including reward, is maximally delayed in equivalent constant-delay environments,
1089   similar to (Kim et al., 2023). Thus, the reward corresponding to the action at is assumed to be rt−omax .
