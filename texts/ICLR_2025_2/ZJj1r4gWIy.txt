                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects has gathered much attention in
010
               recent years and has been widely adopted in medicine, economics, and marketing.
011            Previous studies assumed that one of the potential outcomes of interest could
012            be observed timely and accurately. However, a more practical scenario is that
013            treatment takes time to produce causal effects on the outcomes. For example, drugs
014            take time to produce medical utility for patients and users take time to purchase
015            items after being recommended, and ignoring such delays in feedback can lead
016            to biased estimates of heterogeneous treatment effects. To address the above
017            problem, we study the impact of observation time on estimating heterogeneous
018            treatment effects by further considering the potential response time that potential
019
               outcomes have. We theoretically prove the identifiability results and further propose
               a principled learning approach, known as CFR-DF (Counterfactual Regression
020
               with Delayed Feedback), to simultaneously learn potential response times and
021
               potential outcomes of interest. Results on both simulated and real-world datasets
022            demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.
023

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Heterogeneous treatment effects (HTE) estimation using observational data is a fundamental problem
028   that applies to a wide variety of areas (Alaa & Van Der Schaar, 2017; Alaa et al., 2017; Hannart et al.,
029   2016; LaLonde, 1986; Shalit et al., 2017). For example, in precision medicine, physicians decide
030   drug allocation by the treatment effect of the patient on the drug (Jaskowski & Jaroszewicz, 2012). In
031   online markets, the causal effect of recommending an item on a user’s purchase behavior is used for
032   personalized recommendations (Schnabel et al., 2016). Unlike using observed outcomes to make
033
      decisions, HTE accounts for variations in both factual outcomes and counterfactual outcomes among
      individuals or subgroups. The challenge lies in accurately estimating HTE due to the unobserved
034
      counterfactual outcomes with alternative treatment (Holland, 1986).
035
036   Many methods have been proposed to estimate HTE from observational data. For instance, representa-
037   tion learning-based approaches learn a covariate representation that is independent of the treatment to
038   overcome the covariate shift between the treatment and control groups (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit
039
      et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2018). The tree-based approach generalizes Bayesian inference
      and random forest methods for nonparametric estimation (Chipman et al., 2010; Wager & Athey,
040
      2018). The generative model-based approaches use the widely adopted variational autoencoder and
041
      generative adversarial network to generate individual counterfactual outcomes (Louizos et al., 2017;
042   Yoon et al., 2018). These studies have also been extended to continuous treatment scenarios (Bica
043   et al., 2020; Nie et al., 2021; Schwab et al., 2018; 2020).
044
045
      Existing methods require that one of the potential outcomes of interest be observed timely and accurate.
      However, interventions on individuals usually do not affect outcomes of interest immediately, and
046
      treatment takes time to produce causal effects on the outcomes. For example, drugs take time to
047
      produce medical utility for patients, with the long-term prognosis as the outcome of interest, which
048   benefits the treatment decision from the physicians. In online markets, a recommendation algorithm
049   focuses on whether or not the user will eventually purchase, but users take time to purchase items
050   after being recommended (Chapelle, 2014), which poses a critical challenge in practice: as in Figure
051   1(a), if the observation window is too short, some samples will be incorrectly marked as negative
052   whose conversion will occur in the future; but if it is too long, the recommendation algorithm will not
053   be able to guarantee its timely availability (Yoshikawa & Imai, 2018). In summary, ignoring such
      delays in outcome response can lead to biased estimates of HTE.

065
      In this paper, we first formalize the HTE estimation problem in the presence of delayed response.
066
      In contrast to previous studies that only considered the effect of treatment on outcome, we also
067   consider potential response times with different treatments, since treatment may affect response
068   time, e.g., users who receive item recommendations purchase more quickly. Therefore, as in Figure
069   1(a), given the treatment w for an individual, even if the eventual outcome of interest Y (w) is
070   positive, e.g., the user will eventually purchase the item, we can only observe the true positive
071   conversion (Y (w) = 1, Ỹ (w) = 1) when the potential response time is less than the observation time
072   (D(w) ≤ T ), while observing the false negative outcome (Y (w) = 1, Ỹ (w) = 0) vise versa. Instead,
073   when the eventual outcome Y (w) is negative, e.g., the user never purchases the item, then we observe
074   the negative outcome (Ỹ (w) = 0) regardless of the observation time. Figure 1(b) illustrates the
075   format of the observed data, which comes with an additional challenge, that is, we could not obtain
076   the exact value of the response time if the positive feedback did not occur before the observation time.
077
      To address the above issues, we study the impact of observation time on estimating heterogeneous
078   treatment effects by further considering the potential response time that potential outcomes have.
079   Theoretically, we prove the eventual potential outcomes are identifiable in the whole population,
080   which is essential for treatment allocation. For subgroups in which individuals always have positive
081   eventual outcomes regardless of treatment, we also show the identifiability of potential response
082   times, which quantifies the causal effect of treatment on response times. Using the eventual outcomes
083   as hidden variables, we reconstruct the posterior distribution of a delayed response and provide
084   explicit solutions to estimate the parameters of interest within a modified EM algorithm. Furthermore,
085   we propose a principled learning approach that extends counterfactual regression (CFR) to delayed
086
      feedback outcomes, named CFR-DF, to simultaneously predict potential outcomes and potential
      response times. Finally, we discuss the importance of this work for policy learning and validate the
087
      effectiveness of the proposed method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
088
089   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
090
                 • We formalize the HTE estimation problem with delayed response, in which treatment takes
091
                   time to produce a causal effect on the outcome.
092
093              • We theoretically prove the eventual potential outcome is identifiable, and also show the
094
                   identifiability of potential response times on the always-positive stratum.
095              • We propose a principled learning algorithm, called CFR-DF, that utilizes the EM algorithm
096                to estimate both eventual potential outcomes and potential response times.
097              • We perform extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets to show the
098                effectiveness of the proposed approach in estimating HTE with delayed responses.

      standard HTE estimation (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2018)

117   2021; Curth et al., 2021), we consider the response time from the imposing treatment to producing
118   influence on the outcome. Specifically, let Yi ∈ Y = {0, 1} be the binary outcome at the eventual
119   time, e.g., whether a user will eventually purchase, as the primary outcome of interest, and we call
120   unit with Yi = 1 as a positive sample. Without loss of generality, the time at which the treatment
121   Wi is imposed on unit i is taken as the start time, let Di be the response time for individuals with
122   Yi = 1 to produce positive feedback, and we set Di = ∞ for individuals with Yi = 0. As shown in
123   Figure 1(a), given an observation time Ti , we see a positive feedback at Ti , denoted as ỸiT = 1, if
124   and only if individual i is a positive sample Yi = 1 with the response time Di ≤ Ti , and marked as

      true positive. However, for some other positive samples with Yi = 1, we would see false negative
126
      feedback ỸiT = 0 at the observation time Ti , when the response time is greater than the observation
      time, i.e., Di > Ti , and marked as false negative. For samples that never yield positive outcomes, we
127
128
      observe negative feedback ỸiT = 0 for all observation times Ti , and marked as true negative.
129   To study the effect of treatment on the eventual outcome and the response time, we adopt the potential
130   outcome framework (Rubin, 1974; Neyman, 1990) in causal inference. Specifically, let Yi (0) and
131   Yi (1) be the eventual outcome of unit i had this unit receive treatment Wi = 0 and Wi = 1,
132   respectively. In addition, since treatment may have an effect on the response time, e.g., users purchase
133   more quickly when receiving ads about an item, we denote Di (0) and Di (1) be the potential response
134
      time had unit i receive treatment Wi = 0 and Wi = 1, respectively. Therefore, given an observation
135
      time Ti , the corresponding potential outcomes ỸiT (0) and ỸiT (1) can be analogously defined. Since
      each unit can be only assigned with one treatment, we always observe the corresponding outcome
136
      to be either ỸiT (0) or ỸiT (1), but not both, which is also known as the fundamental problem of
137
      causal inference (Holland, 1986; Morgan & Winship, 2015). However, one should note that similar
138
      conclusions no longer hold for the eventual potential outcomes (Yi (0), Yi (1)) and the potential
139   response times (Di (0), Di (1)), as we cannot observe the exact eventual outcome as well as the
140   response time due to the limited observation time.
141
142   We assume that the observation for unit i is ỸiT = (1 − Wi )ỸiT (0) + Wi ỸiT (1). In other words, the
143   observed outcome at time Ti is the potential outcome corresponding to the assigned treatment, which
144
      is also known as the consistency assumption in the causal literature. We assume that the stable unit
      treatment value assumption (STUVA) assumption holds, i.e., there should not be alternative forms
145
      of treatment and interference between units. Furthermore, we assume the positivity of treatment
146
      assignment, i.e., η < P(Wi = 1|Xi = x) < 1 − η, where η is a constant between 0 and 1/2.
147
148   We summarize the observed data formats in Figure 1(b), with the following three cases.
149
150

151   • False negative (Yi (w) = 1, ỸiT (w) = 0) with observed (Wi = w, Ti = t, ỸiT (w) = 0);
152
      • True negative (Yi (w) = 0, ỸiT (w) = 0) with observed (Wi = w, Ti = t, ỸiT (w) = 0),

154   which leads to an additional challenge due to one cannot distinguish between false negative and true
155   negative directly from the observed data (Wi = w, Ti = t, ỸiT (w) = 0).

157   2.2   PARAMETERS OF I NTEREST
158
159   We consider two meaningful parameters of interest in the following. For simplification, we drop
160   the subscript i for a generic unit hereafter. First, unlike previous studies that focused on the HTE of
161   treatment on current observed outcomes, i.e., τ T (x) = E[Ỹ T (1) − Ỹ T (0) | X = x], we focused on
      the HTE of treatment on the eventual outcomes, i.e., τ (x) = E[Y (1) − Y (0) | X = x]. Notably, the

162
      latter poses two challenges: first, the confounding bias introduced by covariates, which is similar
163   to previous studies; second, how to recover the eventual outcome Y of interest from the observed
164
      outcome Ỹ T at time T . When the observation time T is sufficiently long to exceed the response time
165   D for all individuals, the proposed causal estimand τ (x) degenerates to τ T (x).
166
167   Next, we show that individuals can be divided into four strata by considering the joint potential
168
      outcomes (Y (0), Y (1)), as shown in Table 1, and named as the always-positive stratum, useful
      treatment stratum, harmful treatment stratum, and always-negative stratum accordingly. From a
169
      policy learning perspective, it is clear that treatment should be given and not given to individuals in
170
      useful treatment stratum and harmful treatment stratum, respectively. For individuals in the always-
171   negative stratum, for example, users who will never purchase or patients who will always be cured
172   regardless of treatment, either of the treatments is reasonable and results in no difference. When
173   considering individuals in the always-positive stratum, despite having both Y (0) = 1 and Y (1) = 1
174   for the eventual outcomes, it is meaningful to study the HTE of the treatment on the response times.
175   Formally, the causal estimand of interest is E[D(1) − D(0) | Y (0) = 1, Y (1) = 1, X = x]. For the
176   other three strata, since there exists a treatment w such that Y (w) = 0, the corresponding response
177   time can be regarded as D(w) = ∞, resulting in HTE of treatment on response time being ill-defined.
178   We summarize the causal estimand of interest as follows.
179
180   • HTE on the eventual outcome: τ (x) = E[Y (1) − Y (0) | X = x];
181   • HTE on the response time: τD (x) = E[D(1) − D(0) | Y (0) = 1, Y (1) = 1, X = x].

477   5     C ONCLUSION
478
479   This paper studies the HTE estimation problem by further considering the response time needed for a
480   treatment to produce a causal effect on the outcome. Specifically, we propose a principled learning
481   algorithm, called CFR-DF, to estimate both eventual potential outcomes and potential response times.
482   Considering the widespread delayed feedback outcomes, we believe such a study is meaningful for
483   real-world applications. A shortcoming of our study is the validity of the assumptions in practice, e.g.,
484   we need enough observation time to identify HTE on the eventual potential outcome, and principal
485
      ignorability is further required to identify HTE on the response time. Studying how to weaken these
      assumptions, and identifying and estimating HTE with delayed responses are served as future topics.

      A.1     R ELATED W ORK
652
      In Heterogeneous treatment effect (HTE) estimation, non-random treatment assignments can result
653
      in different probabilities of missing covariates in different treatment arms, which may introduce
654
      confounding bias. To address this issue, most methods strive to balance covariates to estimate
655   HTE accurately, such as matching, stratification, outcome regression, weighting, and doubly robust
656   methods (Rosenbaum, 1987; Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Li et al., 2016; Hainmueller, 2012). With
657   the advances in deep learning, Balancing Neural Network (BNN) (Johansson et al., 2016) and
658   CounterFactual Regression (CFR) (Shalit et al., 2017) propose to learn a covariate representation that
659   is independent of the treatment to overcome the covariate shift between the treatment and control
660   groups, in which the independence is measured by Integral Probability Metric (IPM) (Johansson
661   et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017). SITE (Yao et al., 2018) preserves local similarity and balances the
662   distributions of the representation simultaneously. Motivated by targeted learning (van der Laan
663
      & Rose, 2011), DragonNet (Shi et al., 2019) proposed an adaptive neural network to end-to-end
      model propensity scores and counterfactual outcomes. DR-CFR (Hassanpour & Greiner, 2020) and
664
      DeR-CFR (Wu et al., 2022) propose a disentanglement framework to identify the representation of
665
      confounders from all observed variables. By exploiting the generative models, CEVAE (Louizos
666   et al., 2017) and GANITE (Yoon et al., 2018) generate counterfactual outcomes for HTE estimation.
667   However, these algorithms rely on timely and accurate observation of the eventual potential outcomes.
668
669
      In practice, interventions usually take time to have a causal effect on the outcome (Chapelle, 2014;
      Yoshikawa & Imai, 2018). Despite the problem setup and the causal estimand of interest is different,
670
      many studies have examined HTE estimation under time-to-event data. Curth et al. (2021) used
671
      neural networks for discrete time analyses and Chapfuwa et al. (2021) used generative models
672   for counterfactual time-to-event data analysis in continuous time. Based on the Cox model (Cox,
673   1972), Schrod et al. (2022) proposed a treatment-specific semi-parametric Cox loss using time-to-
674   event data for treatment optimization. Gupta et al. (2023) derived a binary treatment evidence lower
675   bound (ELBO) for parametric survival analysis, and designed a neural network for learning the
676   per-individual survival density. Different from Chapfuwa et al. (2021); Curth et al. (2021), Curth
677   & van der Schaar (2023) considered time-to-event data with competing events, which can act as
678   an additional source of covariate shift. In addition, Nagpal et al. (2022) presented a latent variable
679   approach to mediate the base survival rates and help determine the effects of an intervention. Nagpal
680
      et al. (2023) extended Nagpal et al. (2022) by proposing a statistical approach to recovering sparse
      phenogroups (or subtypes) that demonstrate differential treatment effects as compared to the study
681
      population. Though delayed response can be considered as a right-censored problem, rather than
682
      focusing on the effect of treatment on survival curves, this paper assumes that it takes time to yield an
683   observable outcome that eventually has a positive outcome (e.g., conversion in uplift modeling) and
684   considers both conversion time and whether or not to convert as potential outcomes by utilizing a
685   hybrid model. By considering the joint potential outcome of individuals from a principal stratification
686   perspective (Frangakis & Rubin, 2002; Pearl, 2011), we theoretically prove that the potential response
687   times on subgroups in which individuals always have positive eventual outcomes regardless of
688   treatment are identifiable. It is also interesting to note that the problem studied in this paper can also
689   be considered as a noisy label on the eventual outcome of interest due to the limited observation time,
690   which causes the previous HTE methods to be biased.

999    A.3.3    D ISCUSSION ON THE S CALABILITY TO A RBITRARY F ORMS OF T REATMENTS
1000
1001   It should be noted that our work can be naturally extended to arbitrary forms of treatments and has
1002   rigorous theoretical guarantees regarding the identifiability of true HTE in all strata, i.e., E[Y (w) |
1003
       X = x] for all w ∈ W. This way, by defining delayed response time D(w) for all w ∈ W similarly
       and following a similar argument of our identifiability proof, and substitute Y (0) and Y (1) to Y (w)
1004
       for all w ∈ W, the true HTE E[Y (w) | X = x] for all w ∈ W can be identified similarly. Moreover,
1005
       in the proposed time-to-event based HTE problem setup with delayed responses, the outcome of
1006   interest has to be binary to ensure well-definiteness. Specifically, an event may either occur or not
1007   occur under any form of intervention (see the discussion in the previous paragraph), i.e., Y (w) = 1
1008   or not Y (w) = 0. It is worth noting that only the former, i.e., Y (w) = 1, may be subject to delayed
1009   response, leading to the ”false negative” samples. For the latter, Y (w) = 0, it is difficult to define a
1010   delayed response because this event never occurs (hence we let D(w) = ∞ for Y (w) = 0 ), and we
1011   will never observe ”false positive” samples. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work in
1012   the field of causal inference to consider the potential delayed response time D(w) from intervention
1013   to outcome, and we theoretically prove the identifiability of true HTE in all strata. Considering the
1014
       time it takes for an intervention to have an effect on an outcome, we believe this provides reasonable
       motivation in the causal inference community.

1050   A.5.1    DATASETS U SED FOR E VALUATION
1051
1052   Synthetic Datasets. Following the data generation process in Section 4.2, we generated data as
1053   follows. The observed covariates are generated from X ∼ N (0, ImX ), where ImX denotes mX -
1054   degree identity matrix. The observed treatment W ∼ Bern(π(X)), where π(X) = P(W = 1 |
1055
       X) = σ(θW · X), θW ∼ U (−1, 1), and σ(·) denotes the sigmoid function. For the eventual
       potential outcomes, we generate the control outcome Y (0) ∼ Bern(σ(θY 0 · X 2 + 1)), and the treated
1056
       outcome Y (1) ∼ Bern(σ(θY 1 · X 2 + 2)), where θY 0 , θY 1 ∼ U (−1, 1). In addition, we generate the
1057
       potential response time D(0) ∼ Exp(exp(θD0 · X)−1 ), and D(1) ∼ Exp(exp(θD1 · X − bD )−1 ),
1058   where θD0 , θD1 ∼ U (−0.1, 0.1), where bD = 0.5 controls the heterogeneity of response time
1059   functions. The observation time is generated via T ∼ Exp(λ), where λ refers to the rate parameter
1060   of the exponential distribution. We set the rate parameter as λ = 1, i.e., the average observation
1061   time is T̄ = λ−1 = 1. Finally, the observed outcome is given as Ỹ T (W ) = W · Y (1) · I(T ≥
1062   D(1)) + (1 − W ) · Y (0) · I(T ≥ D(0)), where I(·) is the indicator function. From the data generation
1063   process described above, we sample N = 20, 000 samples for training and 3, 000 samples for testing.
1064   We repeat each experiment 10 times to report the mean and standard deviation of the errors.
1065
       Real-World Datasets. In this paper, we use three wide-applied three widely-adopted real-world
1066   datasets: AIDS (https://scikit-survival.readthedocs.io/ (Hammer et al., 1997;
1067   Norcliffe et al., 2023)), J OBS(http://www.fredjo.com/ (LaLonde, 1986; Shalit et al., 2017)),
1068   and T WINS(http://www.nber.org/data/ (Almond et al., 2005; Wu et al., 2022)). In Table
1069   6 we provide details about the datasets used in our evaluation. The AIDS data collected between
1070   January 1996 and January 1997 involved 1,156 patients in 33 AIDS clinical trial units and 7 National
1071   Hemophilia Foundation sites in the United States and Puerto Rico, and was used to study the impact
1072   and effectiveness of antiretroviral therapy on HIV-positive patients. The J OBS benchmark is widely
1073   used in the field of causal inference. It is built upon randomized controlled trials and aims to assess
1074
       the effects of job training programs on employment status. The T WINS is derived from all twins born
       in the USA between the years 1989 and 1991, and is utilized to assess the influence of birth weight
1075
       on mortality within one year.
