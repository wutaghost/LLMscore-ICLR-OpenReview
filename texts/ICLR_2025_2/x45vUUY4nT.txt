009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Stochastic gradient descent with momentum (SGDM) has been widely used in
012            machine learning. However, in non-convex domains, high probability learning
013            bounds for SGDM are scarce. In this paper, we provide high probability conver-
014            gence bounds and generalization bounds for SGDM. Firstly, we establish these
015            bounds for the gradient norm in the general non-convex case. The derived con-
016            vergence bounds are tighter than the theoretical results of related work, and to our
017            best knowledge, the derived generalization bounds are the first ones for SGDM.
018            Then, if the Polyak-Łojasiewicz condition is satisfied, we establish these bounds
019
               for the error of the function value, instead of the gradient norm. Moreover, the de-
               rived learning bounds have faster rates than the general non-convex case. Finally,
020
               we further provide sharper generalization bounds by considering a mild Bernstein
021
               condition on the gradient. In the case of low noise, their learning rates can reach
022                    2
               O(1/n
                e        ), where n is the sample size. Overall, we relatively systematically inves-
023
               tigate the high probability learning bounds for non-convex SGDM.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Stochastic optimization plays an essential role in modern statistical and machine learning, as many
029
      machine learning problems can be cast into stochastic optimization problems. The last decades have
030
      seen much significant progress in the development of stochastic optimization algorithms, of which
031   stochastic gradient descent with momentum (SGDM) has drawn a lot of attention on a broad range
032   of problems due to its simplicity and its low computational complexity per update (Goodfellow
033   et al., 2016; Li & Orabona, 2020). As a fundamental algorithm for stochastic optimization, SGDM
034   has shown tremendous success in natural language understanding, computer vision, and speech
035   recognition (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Hinton et al., 2012; Sutskever et al., 2013). Particularly, SGDM
036   has been widely used to accelerate the back-propagation algorithm in the training of deep neural
037   networks (Rumelhart et al., 1986; Sutskever et al., 2013). Typically, SGDM adds a momentum term
038   to stochastic gradient descent (SGD) in updating the solution, i.e, the difference between the current
039
      iterate and the previous iterate. The intuition behind SGDM is that if the direction from the previous
      iterate to the current iterate is “correct”, SGDM utilizes this inertia weighted by the momentum
040
      parameter, instead of just relying on the current point used in SGD. Much of the state-of-the-art
041
      empirical performance has been achieved with SGDM (Huang et al., 2017; Howard et al., 2017; He
042   et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2021a). Yet, from a theoretical point of view, the analysis of the learning
043   bounds of SGDM is not sufficiently well-documented (Li et al., 2022; Li & Orabona, 2020).
044
045
      The learning bound of SGDM can be studied from two perspectives: the convergence bound and the
      generalization bound. The former focuses on how the learning algorithm optimizes the empirical
046
      risk, and the latter concerns how the learned model from training samples performs on the testing
047
      points (Lei et al., 2021b). From the perspective of the convergence bound, existing literature of
048   convergence for SGDM or deterministic gradient descent with momentum (DGDM) in the non-
049   convex domain mostly uses an analysis of expectation (Ochs et al., 2014; 2015; Ghadimi et al., 2015;
050   Lessard et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2016; Wilson et al., 2021; Gadat et al., 2018; Orvieto et al., 2020;
051   Can et al., 2019; Li et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020), to mention but a few. However, the
052   expected bound does not rule out extremely bad outcomes (Li & Orabona, 2020; Liu et al., 2023).
053   Moreover, in practical applications such as machine learning, it is often the case that the algorithm
      is usually run only once since the training process may take a long time. Therefore, high probability

055   algorithm on single runs (Harvey et al., 2019). To our best knowledge, there are only two works on

057   Specifically, Cutkosky & Mehta (2021) consider the case that the gradient follows a θ-order moment

064   generalization bounds are given in (Li & Orabona, 2020; Cutkosky & Mehta, 2021).
065
      From the perspective of the generalization bound, existing generalization studies of SGDM and
066   DGDM are scarce. Ong (2017); Chen et al. (2018) provide expected generalization error bounds
067   for a specific quadratic loss function of DGDM by the lens of algorithmic stability (Bousquet &
068   Elisseeff, 2002; Hardt et al., 2016). The analysis of (Ong, 2017; Chen et al., 2018) cannot be easily
069   extended to general loss functions. It is conjectured in (Chen et al., 2018) that the uniform sta-
070   bility bound they derived may also be applicable to general convex loss functions. Motivated by
071   this, Ramezani-Kebrya et al. (2024) study the generalization error bound of SGDM for the general
072   loss functions. Surprisingly, however, their analysis shows a counterexample for which the uniform
073   stability gap (in expectation, i.e., taking expectation over the internal randomness of the learning
074
      algorithm) for SGDM running multiple epochs diverges even for the convex loss functions. It is
      also revealed in (Attia & Koren, 2021) that in the general convex case, the uniform stability gap
075
      of the deterministic Nesterov’s accelerated gradient algorithm (NAG) collapses exponentially fast
076
      with the number of iterates. We remind the readers here that the uniform stability is only a suffi-
077   cient condition for generalization, and it is unclear how other weaker stability measures, such as
078   on-average stability (Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2010), would behave on the generalization analysis of
079   SGDM. Overall, there are known difficulties in developing generalization performance guarantees
080   of SGDM, especially for general loss functions. Furthermore, similar to the analysis of SGDM’s
081   convergence, high probability generalization bounds are also more challenging to derive compared
082   to bounds on expectations (Bousquet et al., 2020; Bassily et al., 2020; Feldman & Vondrak, 2019;
083   Li & Liu, 2022).
084   Therefore, both the high probability convergence bound and generalization bound of SGDM are
085   far from being understood. Motivated by the problems we discussed above, this paper makes an
086   attempt to establish high probability convergence bounds and generalization bounds for SGDM,
087   particularly, in non-convex settings. For brevity, from now on, all bounds on the performance of the
088   learned model on testing data, such as the generalization error bound and the excess risk bound, will
089   be called generalization bounds. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
090

328   seems to fail to establish generalization bounds for SGDM with general loss functions. This may
329   be explained as follows: the trade-off between convergence and stability of the algorithm implies
330   that a faster converging algorithm has to be less stable, and vice versa (Chen et al., 2018). Our
331   proof techniques to prove the generalization bounds in this paper belong to the class of the uniform
332   convergence approach (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002; Bartlett et al., 2005; Xu & Zeevi, 2020; Xu

335
      convex case, the dependence of the bound proved by this approach on the dimension d is generally

533   5   C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   This paper studies high probability convergence and generalization bound of stochastic gradient de-
536   scent with momentum in the non-convex regime, which shows SGDM’s performance in a joint view
537   of the optimization and generalization properties. The bounds are expressed in terms of different
538   rates and can show the impact of moving from sub-Gaussian/sub-exponential (i.e. light-tailed) vari-
539   ables to those with heavy tails on the rates of convergence/generalization. We believe our theoretical
      findings can provide deep insights into the learning guarantees of non-convex SGDM.
