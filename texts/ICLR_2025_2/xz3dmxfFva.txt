009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                In this paper, we show that useful video representations can be learned from
012                synthetic videos and natural images, without incorporating natural videos in the
013                training. We propose a progression of video datasets synthesized by simple genera-
014                tive processes, that model a growing set of natural video properties (e.g. motion,
015                acceleration, and shape transformations). The downstream performance of video
016                models pre-trained on these generated datasets gradually increases with the dataset
017                progression. A VideoMAE model pre-trained on our synthetic videos closes 97.2%
018
                   of the performance gap on UCF101 action classification between training from
                   scratch and self-supervised pre-training from natural videos, and outperforms
019
                   the pre-trained model on HMDB51. Introducing crops of static images to the
020
                   pre-training stage results in similar performance to UCF101 pre-training and out-
021                performs the UCF101 pre-trained model on 11 out of 14 out-of-distribution datasets
022                of UCF101-P. Analyzing the low-level properties of the datasets, we identify corre-
023                lations between frame diversity, frame similarity to natural data, and downstream
024                performance. Our approach provides a more controllable and transparent alternative
025                to video data curation processes for pre-training1 .

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large-scale data is a fundamental component for training neural networks in various domains, such
031   as natural language processing (NLP). To learn from such data, a prevalent technique is to pre-
032   train models via a self-supervised task (e.g. masked modeling (Devlin et al., 2019) or next-token
033
      prediction (Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020). Adapting these models to downstream tasks
      results usually in improvements in various NLP tasks.
034
035   While self-supervised pre-training is successful in NLP, the same level of success has not been
036   achieved yet in computer vision. Specifically, in the video domain, although various large-scale
037   datasets exist and have been incorporated via similar self-supervised learning tasks, the improvements
038   in downstream performance on video understanding (e.g. action recognition) are relatively low.
039   One hypothesis for the limited success of self-supervised learning from videos is that current methods
040   fail to effectively utilize the natural video data and learn useful video representations from it. To
041   investigate this hypothesis, we ask if natural videos are even needed to learn video representations
042   that are similar in performance to current state-of-the-art representations.
043   In this work, we reach a downstream performance that is similar to the performance of models
044   pre-trained on natural videos, while pre-training solely on simple synthetic videos and static images.
045   We propose a progression of simple synthetic video generators that model a gradually growing set of
046   video data properties - starting from static frames with solid-color circles and introducing additional
047   shapes, dynamics, temporal shape changes, acceleration, and other textures). We show that adding
048   each of the different properties improves the downstream video understanding performance.
049   Surprisingly, we find that the gap between the performance of our models and models that were
050   pre-trained on natural videos is minor when we pre-train using purely synthetic data, and eliminated
051   when we introduce natural image crops. By pre-training a VideoMAE (Wang et al., 2023) on purely
052   generated data we close 97.2% of the gap in UCF101 classification accuracy between a model that

      Video representation learning. Learning useful representation for videos is a widely explored
      problem. Early methods used models that were pre-trained on image datasets and fine-tuned them on
099
      videos (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; Tran et al., 2018).
100
101   Following the success of self-supervised representation learning (SSL) for images, similar approaches
102   were applied to learn from videos. Earlier SSL approaches designed pretext tasks that rely on known
103   video properties (e.g. temporal smoothness) - classifying videos to ordered or shuffled frames (Misra
104
      et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2019), predicting the future frames (Mathieu et al., 2015), predicting the arrow
      of time (Wei et al., 2018), and predicting the speed of the video (Benaim et al., 2020).
105
106   Recently, VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022), MAE-ST (Feichtenhofer et al., 2022), and VideoMAE-
107   V2 (Wang et al., 2023) used variations of masked auto-encoding (He et al., 2022) and trained a
      transformer to predict masked temporal video patches as the pretext task. These approaches were


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      shown to produce useful representations without using augmentations during training. We pre-train
109   VideoMAE models on our generated datasets and evaluate them on action recognition tasks.
110
111   Learning from synthetic videos. Synthetic video data is widely used for solving low-level video
112
      tasks. Specifically, data generated by 3D simulators (e.g. video game engines), were shown to
      be useful data sources for training models on optical flow (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015) and point
113
      tracking (Zheng et al., 2023) as ground-truth labels for these tasks can be computed from the
114
      simulators. Guo et al. 2022 pre-trained a contrastive model on videos generated from a game
115   simulator to learn representations for human motion. Kim et al. 2022 explored how transferable
116   are video representations learned from synthetic video data of public 3D assets. In contrast to these
117   methods, we use only simple generative models that aim to mimic known properties of natural videos
118   in order to analyze what are the elements that enable useful video representation learning.
119
      Analyzing dataset curation processes. The research attention toward curating and characterizing
120
      useful pre-training datasets has grown recently. Various approaches were proposed for summariz-
121   ing the properties of such datasets. Dataset distillation approaches aim to summarize the datasets
122   into a few examples that lead to the same model performance as the original datasets after train-
123   ing (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2018). Gadre et al. 2024 introduced a benchmark for
124   evaluating different dataset curation processes used for learning downstream tasks. Fang et al. 2024
125   explored the correlation between data filtering heuristics and downstream performance for image
126   classification, and proposed data filtering networks to improve filtering.
127   Closer to our work, Baradad et al., 2021; 2022 proposed a progression of generative image models
128   for exploring the data properties that can unlock effective model pre-training. We follow a similar
129   approach for video pre-training and propose a progression of generative video models. However,
130   unlike Baradad et al., 2021, each model in our progression is built on top of the previous model.
131
132
133   3     P RE - TRAINING V IDEO M ODELS WITHOUT NATURAL V IDEOS
134
135   To close the gap between training from scratch and natural video pre-training, and to find the key
136   elements of the data for synthetic video pre-training, we provide a progression of datasets. The
137   dataset are gradually introducing different aspects that appear in video data (e.g. transforming
138   shapes, accelerating shapes). We pre-train SSL models on each of the generated datasets and evaluate
139   them on downstream tasks. In Section 3.1 we present the progression of datasets and describe
140   the generative processes that create them. Then, in Section 3.1, we present the pre-training and
141   downstream evaluation suit.
142
143   3.1   P ROGRESSION OF VIDEO GENERATION PROCESSES
144
145   We start by describing the progression of generative models {Gi } we use to generate our training
146   datasets. Each model uses a random number generator to sample latent parameters. The latent
147   parameters are used for generating videos - sequences of T frames ft ∈ RH×W ×3 , t ∈ {1, ..., T }.
148   Each consecutive model is built on top of the previous model, by modifying one aspect of it and
149   adding additional calls to the random number generator. Examples of frames sampled from videos
150   in the progression are shown in Figure 1. The models in the progression are described next (see
151   Appendix A.1 for additional hyper-parameters, and the supplementary material for videos).
152   Static circles. Our first video model is of static synthetic images of multiple circles that are copied T
153   times (e.g. ft = ft+1 ). The frames are generated by positioning multiple overlapping circles on the
154   frame canvas. The color and location of the circles are sampled uniformly at random. Following the
155   Dead Leaves model (Bordenave et al., 2006), the radius is sampled from an exponential distribution,
156   as this distribution resembles the distribution of objects in natural scenes.
157   Moving circles. Starting from randomly positioned circles in the first frame, each assigned a velocity
158   to derive the next frames by modeling the dynamics. Each circle is assigned a random direction and a
159   velocity magnitude that is sampled uniformly from a fixed range. Each circle is assigned a random
160   z-buffer value, according to the order in which it was positioned on the canvas for the first frame.
161   This depth assignment results in occlusions when objects are moving. Introducing changes in the
      temporal dimension allows us to evaluate the importance of dynamics for video understanding tasks.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Moving shapes. We replace the circles sampled for the first frame with different shapes, including
163   circles, quadrilaterals, and triangles. The shape types are sampled uniformly at random, and velocities
164   are applied to them to simulate the next frames, similarly to the previous model.
165
166
      Moving and transforming shapes. We introduce temporal transformations to the sampled shapes and
      apply them together with the velocities to derive the next frames. Each shape is assigned uniformly at
167
      random two scaling factors (one for each spatial dimension), a rotation speed, and two sheer factors.
168
      Each consecutive frame is computed by scaling the object in the current frame by the scaling factors,
169   rotating it, and applying the shear mapping.
170
171   Accelerating transforming shapes. To introduce more complex dynamics, each temporally trans-
172
      forming shape is accelerated during the video by a random factor. The acceleration value is sampled
      uniformly from a fixed range that includes both positive and negative values.
173
174   Accelerating transforming textures. We replace the solid-colored shapes from the previous dataset
175   with textures, to integrate realistic image patterns into videos. We utilize synthetic texture images
176   from the statistical image dataset (Baradad et al., 2021). This dataset mimics color distribution,
177   spectral components, and wavelet distribution characteristics of natural images and was shown to
178
      be useful for image pre-training. We use a total of 300k textures and for each of the shapes in the
      previous dataset in the progression, we sample a random texture to replace its solid color.
179
180   Accelerating transforming StyleGAN crops. We replace the statistical textures with texture crops
181   from the StyleGAN-Oriented dataset (Baradad et al., 2021). This dataset contains 300K texture
182   images that were sampled from an untrained StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2020) initialized to have the
183   same wavelets for all output channels in the convolution layers. It was shown to be the most useful
184
      for image model pre-training, out of all the synthetic datasets presented in Baradad et al. (2021).
185   Accelerating transforming image crops. We substitute the synthetic textures sampled for the
186   previous Oriented-StyleGAN dataset with natural image crops, taken from ImageNet (Deng et al.,
187   2009). We do not parse or segment the images; instead, we sample random crops in the shapes
188   mentioned above.

191   3.2   P RE - TRAINING PROTOCOL
192
193   We study the progression of generative models described above, by pre-training video models on
194   sampled videos from each generator Gi and evaluate them on downstream tasks. This results in
195   a progression of pre-trained models {Mi }, where i is the index of the dataset in the progression.
196   Next, we describe our choice for pre-training model architecture, dataset sizes, and the baselines we
197   compare to.
198   Pre-training model. We use VideoMAE (Tong et al., 2022) as our pre-training approach. Differently
199   from other masked video auto-encoding approaches presented in Section 2, this method uses tube
200   masking. It has been shown to outperform other SSL methods (e.g. contrastive learning approaches)
201   without relying on heavy augmentations during pre-training. We evaluate the pre-trained encoder of
202   the model by fine-tuning and linear-probing it on downstream tasks. We use different model sizes to
203   verify the consistency of the improvements in performance across scales.
204   Baselines. We compare the pre-trained models to two additional models - a VideoMAE model
205   that was pre-trained with the self-supervised reconstruction objective on the training data of the
206   downstream evaluation data (UCF101), and a VideoMAE model that was initialized with random
207   weights (e.g. trained from scratch). The former can be viewed as an upper bound for our progression,
208   as this model is pre-trained on natural videos from the same distribution as the test set. The latter can
209   be viewed as a lower bound, as no pre-training is done in this baseline.
210   Dataset sizes and pre-training hyper-parameters. We use the same hyperparameters as in the
211   original pre-training recipe. That includes the same number of training steps and fine-tuning/linear
212   probing steps. While we can generate infinite datasets from the generative models we described
213   above, we aim to be comparable to the original pre-training dataset (UCF101). Therefore, for all the
214   generative models that use textures or image crops, we generate sets with a similar size to the original
215   pre-training dataset. For the other datasets, as the model manages to memorize the training data if the
      size is similar to the pre-training dataset, we generate random examples on the fly.

237        3.3            E VALUATION PROTOCOLS
238
239       We evaluate our pre-trained models for action recognition. We test the models on UCF101 (Soomro
240       et al., 2012), a dataset that contains 13,320 video clips of human actions categorized to 101 classes,
241       and on HMDB51 (Kuehne et al., 2011), a dataset of additional 6766 human action video clips
242
          categorized to 51 classes. We evaluate out-of-distribution action recognition on UCF101-P (Schiappa
          et al., 2023), which includes videos from the test-set of UCF101, corrupted with 4 types of low-level
243
          synthetic corruptions - camera motion, blur, noise, and digital corruptions.
244
245       As mentioned in Section 3.2, each model is pre-trained and fine-tuned for the same number of steps
246       and with the same hyper-parameters (provided in Appendix A.2). The length of the videos and the
247       width and height are sampled to be similar to UCF101. We use the official UCF101 pre-trained
248
          checkpoint of VideoMAE as our baseline.

353   In this section, we analyze in depth a few characteristics of the synthetic datasets that were shown to

      be useful for video pre-training. We start by evaluating the effect of incorporating natural images in

      5.1   I NCORPORATING STATIC IMAGES
359
      Following the improvement of the model performance when natural image crops are used in the
360   pre-training data, we raise three questions: 1) how does the size of the static image dataset affect
361   the downstream performance, 2) can the pre-training benefit from both synthetic and natural texture
362   crops, and 3) are there alternative ways to incorporate natural images in the pre-training regime?
363   Next, we address these questions.
364
      Image dataset size. We evaluate the effect of the image data size on the downstream task. Our initial
365
      pool of images includes all the images from ImageNet (1.3M). We provide additional results with a
366   pool with 300k images, while keeping the size of the pre-training video dataset fixed. We use the
367   same acceleration, speed, and shape transformations as in the last dataset in the progression. The
368   results for ViT-B, fine-tuned for the UCF101 classification task, are presented in Table 2. An increase
369   in the size of the static images dataset results in a better performance on the downstream task.
370
      Combining natural images and synthetic textures. To evaluate if useful pre-training can be
371
      achieved by combining natural images and synthetic textures, we create a dataset that incorporates
372   crops from half of the images and crops from half of the synthetic textures from the StyleGAN
373   textures (Baradad et al., 2021) that we used in the previous dataset in the progression (each has
374   150k examples). We apply the same acceleration, speed, and transformations as in the last dataset in
375   the progression. As shown in Table 2, the performance of the new dataset (“150k images & 150k
376   StyleGAN”) is slightly higher than the performance of the two datasets that use solely one type of
377   data. This suggests that mixing datasets can lead to improved performance in other cases as well. We
      leave this approach to future work.

400   5.2   I NCORPORATING TEXTURES
401
402   While the best model in our progression uses image crops, we seek other alternatives with synthetic
403   textures. Specifically, we replace the static StyleGAN textures with a dynamic version.
404
      Dynamic StyleGAN textures. We investigate a simple extension of the StyleGAN-generated textures
405   into videos. We create a texture video by starting from a random noise z0 , provided as a latent code
406   to the StyleGAN generator G′ . Each consecutive frame is generated by adding a random noise with a
407   smaller standard deviation δzi to the previous latent zi−1 (zi = zi−1 + δzi ) and generating a frame
408   G′ (zi ). We explore two approaches to incorporate these texture videos: directly creating a dataset
409   with multiple such videos (“Dynamic StyleGAN videos”) or replacing the solid-color shapes from
410   the “accelerating and transforming shapes” with dynamic texture crops that are updated across frames
411   (“Dynamic StyleGAN crops”).
412   Fine-tuning on UCF101. Table 3 presents the action classification accuracy after incorporating
413   the dynamic textures in the pre-training stage and fine-tuning on UCF101. Using videos of random
414   walks in the latent space of randomly initialized StyleGAN leads to performance that is only slightly
415   better than training on static circles (67.8%). Replacing the static StyleGAN crops with dynamic
416   StyleGAN crops leads to a performance drop of 1%. That suggests that the simple hand-crafted
417   dynamics of randomly moving Dead-Leaves models are sufficient for pre-training, without the need
418   for introducing additional dynamics modeling.

504   6     L IMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION
505
506   We conclude by presenting three limitations of our analysis and discussing future work.
507
508   Generalization to other tasks. While the pre-trained models are evaluated on three different datasets
509
      (HMDB51, UCF101, and UCF101-P) and with two different adaptation regimes (linear probing and
      fine-tuning), and show relatively similar trends across the progression of the datasets, they can have
510
      different trends when adapted to other tasks. We decided to focus on action recognition and to aim to
511
      reach the performance of relatively small datasets, as a first step to a fully synthetic approach that
512   does not rely on natural videos. We do not tune any hyper-parameters (except batch size and learning
513   rate, due to GPU memory capacity differences) to improve performance. In future work, we aim to
514   extend this approach to other tasks and apply training regimes that were shown to work on larger
515   datasets, hoping to reach similar performance.
516
      Generalization to other model types. Our evaluating suite included pre-training of one type of
517
      model - VideoMAE. While this pre-training approach is widely used, the behavior we presented for
518   different datasets may be different for other pre-training regimes. Our decision to focus on one model
519   follows a similar scope of Baradad et al. 2021.
520
521
      Properties of the mixed image datasets. We show that static image data that can be used as crops
      during the pre-training stage can improve downstream performance. While we show that more images
522
      result in better performance, our analysis does not answer what type of natural image data is useful
523
      for video pre-training. We plan to explore this question in future work.
524
525   Discussion. Learning from data produced by simple generative processes and other well-studied data
526   sources has an advantage over learning from large-scale video data - when pre-training on large-scale
527
      video corpus, commonly obtained from the internet, it is merely impossible to monitor what are
      all the training examples and to verify that no malicious, private, or biased data is included in the
528
      pre-training stage. Learning from generated data, on the other hand, gives better control over the type
529
      of data that is provided during pre-training.
530
531   We believe that the synthetic data analysis we provided can be utilized to create better datasets
532   for learning video representations without natural videos. Guided by this analysis, we plan to
533
      investigate other well-understood data sources and generation processes to continue improving video
      representation learning, in large-scale training regimes.
534
535   While it was not our aim in this paper, the synthetic data we produced can be incorporated as
536   augmentations as well. Pre-training on UCF101 together with the last data in the progression leads
537   to accuracy of 92.% after fine-tuning ViT-B VideoMAE, surpassing the performance of UCF101
538   pre-training. We plan to explore this direction in the future as well.
