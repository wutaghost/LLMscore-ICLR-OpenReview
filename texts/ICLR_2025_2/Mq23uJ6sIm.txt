011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural Differential Equations (NDEs) are an excellent tool for modeling
014            continuous-time (stochastic) dynamics, effectively handling challenges such as
015            irregular observations, missing values, and noise. Despite their advantages, there
016            is a lack of regularization techniques in the NDE framework, particularly those
017            like dropout, which have been successfully implemented in other discrete neural
018
               networks, making them susceptible to overfitting. To address this research gap, we
               introduce Neural Regenerative Stochastic Differential Equation (NRSDE), based on
019
               alternating renewal processes, as a universally applicable regularization technique
020
               for NDEs. Our study reveals that NRSDE can effectively represent a continuous
021            approximation of neural networks that randomly deactivate some neurons during
022            training, similar to dropout, thereby enhancing the robustness and generalization
023            capabilities of NDEs. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that NRSDE
024            outperforms existing regularization methods for NDEs and can be applied to all
025            existing NDE models, significantly improving their performance across various
026            deep learning tasks, including time series classification and image classification.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Neural Differential Equations (NDEs) have gained significant attention in recent years due to their
032   ability to model continuous-time dynamical systems by integrating differential equations with neural
033
      networks (Chen et al., 2018; Rubanova et al., 2019). The continuous-time nature of NDEs allows for
      more precise modeling of temporal dynamics than discrete neural network architectures and has found
034
      applications in various domains, including physics (Greydanus et al., 2019), finance (Yang et al.,
035
      2023), and a variety of fields dealing with irregularly-sampled data and missing values (Rubanova
036   et al., 2019; Kidger et al., 2020; Oh et al., 2024).
037
038   Despite their remarkable successes, NDEs are prone to overfitting like other deep learning models,
039
      particularly when training data is limited or the model complexity is too high (Oh et al., 2024).
      Therefore, regularization techniques for NDEs are crucial in improving their generalization and
040
      robustness. Recent studies have explored various regularization techniques for NDEs such as
041
      Neural Stochastic Differential Equations (Neural SDEs) by injecting noise into Neural Ordinary
042   Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) (Tzen and Raginsky, 2019; Kong et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020;
043   Oganesyan et al., 2020), variants of Neural SDEs under distribution shifts (Oh et al., 2024), temporal
044   regularization technique (Ghosh et al., 2020), and kinetic energy regularization for Neural ODE-based
045   generative models (Finlay et al., 2020).
046
      While these approaches have made progress in regularizing NDE models, several aspects remain
047
      largely unexplored compared to well-established regularization techniques developed for discrete
048   neural networks. In particular, although dropout, one of the most efficient and widely used regulariza-
049   tion techniques in deep learning, has been extensively studied for conventional neural networks, its
050   application to NDEs remains limited. Liu et al. (2020) made an important first step in incorporating
051   the dropout mechanism into NDEs using jump diffusion processes and SDEs with various noise
052   types. While this approach introduces novel ideas, it encounters theoretical and practical challenges
053   in accurately capturing dropout. One key issue is that the continuous dynamics they propose, when
      discretized, fail to align with the actual behavior of dropout in discrete time. Furthermore, the

054
      dynamic nature of continuous models raises new questions around determining dropout rates and
055   adjusting them in real-time to maintain model stability. Additionally, the need for rescaling the
056   model’s outputs when applying dropout in NDEs remains an open problem, further complicating its
057   integration.
058
      To address this gap, we propose Neural Regenerative Stochastic Differential Equations (NRSDEs),
059
      a dropout scheme tailored for NDEs. NRSDEs leverage the concept of alternating renewal pro-
060
      cesses (Ross, 1995; Cox, 1962; Pham-Gia and Turkkan, 1999) to model the dropout process as a
061   regenerative phenomenon, where the system alternates between active and inactive (due to dropout)
062   states randomly. Then, we redefine the dropout rate in the continuous-time setting, and establish the
063   connection between the dropout rate and the intensity parameters of the alternating renewal process.
064   We also study a scaling method for consistency between outputs in training and test phases, which is
065   important for the practical implementation of NRSDEs. Lastly, we conduct comprehensive series of
066   experiments on various benchmark datasets and NDE models to evaluate the effectiveness of NRSDEs.
067   Our numerical results demonstrate that NRSDE outperforms existing regularization methods for
068   NDEs and can be applied to any type of NDE model, significantly improving the generalization
069
      ability and robustness of NDEs, achieving state-of-the-art results across various tasks.

      2.2         A LTERNATING R ENEWAL P ROCESS
163
164   Before presenting our proposed NRSDE, we briefly review the alternating renewal process, which
165   is a key concept in our methodology. This class of regenerative stochastic processes is commonly
166   used to model systems that alternate between two states over time. It has found wide applications
167   in fields such as operations research, queueing theory, and reliability engineering (Stanford, 1979;
168   Heath et al., 1998; Pham-Gia and Turkkan, 1999; Birolini, 1974). For a more detailed explanation,
169   we refer to Appendix A.
170   Consider a dynamical system that alternates between two (active and inactive) states. Let {Xn }n≥1
171   be the sequence of i.i.d. random variables with a distribution G representing the lengths of time
172   that the system is in active state, and let {Yn }n≥1 be the sequence of i.i.d. random variables with
173   a distribution H representing the lengths of time that the system is in inactive state. Assume that
174   Xn and Ym are independent for any n ̸= m. However, Xn and Yn (for the same index n) are not
175   necessarily independent. Moreover, a renewal is defined as the alternation between active and inactive
176
      states where the length of each renewal, {Xn + Yn }n≥1 , has a common distribution F . Then, the
      renewal process associated with {Xn , Yn }n≥1 is called an alternating renewal process. In particular,
177
      we focus on an exponential alternating renewal process where G and H are exponentially distributed.

251   3.2    D ROPOUT R ATE
252
253   In discrete neural networks, the dropout technique has a hyperparameter called the dropout rate p.
254
      Specifically, if the dropout rate is p, then each neuron has a probability of being deactivated for
      each training iteration. However, in the continuous setting of NDEs, the traditional concept of the
255
      dropout rate cannot be directly applicable because each neuron in NDEs represents the value of a
256
      continuous-time process at a specific time rather than a countable entity. Therefore, the concept of
257   the dropout rate needs to be redefined to fit the continuous version.
258
259   Among possible candidates3 , we adopt the concept of instantaneous availability at the terminal time T ,
260
      which represents the probability that the renewal process under consideration is in the active state at
      T , denoted by A(T ) := P({z(T ) is in the active state}). This approach is not only computationally
261
      efficient but aligns well with the traditional meaning of the dropout rate in discrete neural networks.
262
      Hence, in NRSDEs, the dropout rate p is defined as p = 1 − A(T ), the probability that the NRSDE
263   is in the inactive state at T .
264
265   NRSDEs have two intensity parameters λ1 and λ2 , which determine the proportion of time spent in
266
      the active (or inactive) state over the total length T . The following theorem provides a formula for
      the dropout rate p in terms of two parameters of NRSDE, λ1 and λ2 .

302   3.3    S CALING FACTOR IN T EST P HASE
303
304   Another important procedure when applying dropout in discrete neural networks is to use the full
305   neural network without dropout during the test phase. This creates a difference between the network’s
306   outputs during training and testing, necessitating scaling to calibrate this discrepancy. For example,
307
      during the test phase, the weights of neurons are scaled by 1 − p to ensure that the average output of
      the network is consistent between training and testing.
308
309   Following the mechanism of dropout in discrete neural networks, the dropout rate in NRSDE is set
310   to 0 to eliminate inactivate states during the test phase. Therefore, suitable scaling is necessary to
311   match the average outputs during training and test phases. That is, one needs to determine a scaling
312   factor c such that E[z(T )] = E[z0 (T )] + c. Then, we use the scaled output, z0 (T ) + c, for various
313
      inference tasks. We explicitly note that the scaling factor is not a tunable or learnable parameter but
      rather a fixed value specific to each data point. Since c cannot be obtained analytically, we employ
314
      Monte Carlo simulation which might seem to introduce additional computational burden. However,
315
      we emphasize that the computational overhead due to Monte Carlo simulation is incurred only during
316   the test phase, and therefore does not significantly increase the overall computational cost of NRSDE.
317   For numerical experiments related to this, please refer to Section 4.2.
318
319
      Moreover, the accuracy of estimating c depends on the number of samples used in the Monte Carlo
      simulation, which can affect the performance of NRSDE. The impact of sample size is investigated
320
      in Section 4.2, which reveals that 5–10 samples are sufficient to obtain stable results. Algorithm 1

348   3.4       W HY ARE ALTERNATING RENEWAL PROCESSES MORE ADEQUATE THAN JUMP DIFFUSION
349             PROCESSES FOR MODELING DROPOUT ?
350
      Liu et al. (2020) made an interesting attempt to represent the stochastic nature of dropout using
351
      random jumps through a jump diffusion process. However, as we discuss in Appendix G.1, this
352   approach encounters some limitations in accurately capturing the dropout mechanism. Specifically,
353   the discretization form of the jump diffusion process they proposed differs from Equation 3, leading
354   to a mismatch in describing the true dynamics of dropout. In contrast, the discretization of NRSDE
355   aligns exactly with Equation 3, providing a more precise representation of the dropout mechanism.
356
      Beyond theoretical alignment, alternating renewal processes offer significant practical advantages.
357
      First, as shown in our numerical experiments in Section 4, jump diffusion dropout only improves
358   performance at very low dropout rates p, typically less than 0.1 (as discussed in Appendix E.1). This
359   behavior deviates from traditional dropout and often requires additional time-consuming tuning. On
360   the other hand, NRSDE provides robust performance improvements across a wide range of typical
361   dropout rates. Second, NRSDE consistently outperforms jump diffusion dropout in various settings.
362   Lastly, NRSDE is universally applicable to any variant of NDEs, unlike jump diffusion dropout, as
363   detailed in Appendix G.2.

524   5                   C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this study, we introduced Neural Regenerative Stochastic Differential Equations (NRSDEs), a
527   dropout technique tailored for NDEs to enhance the generalization and robustness of NDEs. The
528   key idea is to construct an alternating renewal process, which alternates between active and inactive
529   states for random time periods, to represent the continuous version of dropout neural networks.
530   Within the proposed framework, we extended the definition of the dropout rate to a continuous-time
531
      setting and studied the connection between the newly defined dropout rate and the parameters of
      NRSDE. Moreover, we discussed how to scale the outputs of NRSDE during the test phase, which
532
      is crucial for the practical implementation of dropout. We validated the effectiveness compared
533
      to existing regularization methods for NDEs and the universal applicability of NRSDE through
534   extensive experiments on real-world time-series and image classification. NRSDE significantly
535   improves the performance of all the NDEs under consideration, particularly achieving state-of-the-art
536   results on time-series datasets. However, NRSDE has limitations in terms of computational cost.
537   First, unlike traditional discrete dropout, which has a single hyperparameter p, NRSDE involves two
538   hyperparameters, p and m. Moreover, as Monte Carlo simulation is used to estimate the scaling factor,
539   it is necessary to investigate the impact of the number of samples used in Monte Carlo simulation on
      the performance of NRSDE across a wide range of datasets.

838   C    D ESCRIPTION OF DATASETS
839
840   SmoothSubspace. The SmoothSubspace dataset (Huang et al., 2016) is designed to test algorithms’
841
      ability to identify and classify smooth trajectories in a high-dimensional space. This dataset consists
      of 150 simulated univariate time series, each represented in a 15-dimensional space. The time series
842
      are generated such that they lie on or near a smooth subspace within this higher-dimensional space.
843
      Each series can belong to one of three classes, with the class indicating the particular subspace
844   configuration that the series aligns with.
845
846   ArticularyWordRecognition. The ArticularyWordRecognition dataset (Wang et al., 2013) uses
847   an Electromagnetic Articulograph (EMA) to measure tongue and lip movements during speech.
848   It includes data from multiple native English speakers producing 25 words. The dataset features
849   recordings from twelve sensors capturing x, y, and z positions at a 200 Hz sampling rate. Sensors
850   are positioned on the forehead, tongue (T1 to T4), lips, and jaw. Out of 36 possible dimensions, this
851   dataset includes 9.
852
853   ERing. The ERing dataset (Wilhelm et al., 2015) is collected using a prototype finger ring known
854
      as eRing, which detects hand and finger gestures through electric field sensing. The dataset includes
      six classes of finger postures involving the thumb, index finger, and middle finger. Each data series is
855
      four-dimensional with 65 observations, representing measurements from electrodes sensitive to the
856
      distance from the hand.
857
858   RacketSports. The RacketSports dataset consists of data collected from university students playing
859   badminton or squash while wearing a Smart Watch. The watch recorded x, y, and z coordinates
860   from both the accelerometer and gyroscope, which were transmitted to a phone and stored in an
861   Attribute-Relation File Format (ARFF) file. The dataset includes accelerometer and gyroscope
862   measurements in the order: accelerometer x, y, z, followed by gyroscope x, y, z. The data was
863   collected at 10 Hz over 3 seconds during either forehand/backhand strokes in squash or clear/smash
      strokes in badminton.


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Speech Commands. The Speech Commands dataset (Warden, 2018) features a comprehensive set
865   of one-second long audio clips that include spoken words and ambient sounds. This dataset contains
866   34,975 time-series entries, each corresponding to one of 35 different words. Ten specific words were
867   chosen as categories: ‘yes’, ‘no’, ‘up’, ‘down’, ‘left’, ‘right’, ‘on’, ‘off’, ‘stop’, and ‘go’. The audio
868   clips are pre-processed by computing the Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients, which serve as features
869   to capture the audio characteristics more effectively, thereby enhancing the accuracy of the applied
870   machine learning models. Each audio sample is represented by a time series of 161 frames, with each
871   frame consisting of 20 feature dimensions.
872
873   PhysioNet Sepsis. The 2019 PhysioNet Sepsis challenge (Reyna et al., 2019) focuses on predicting
874   sepsis, a critical condition caused by blood infections leading to numerous fatalities. This challenge
875   uses a dataset with 40,335 ICU patient records, featuring 34 time-dependent indicators like heart rate
876   and body temperature. Researchers aim to determine the presence of sepsis in patients based on the
877
      sepsis-3 criteria. This dataset presents challenges due to its irregular time-series nature—only 10% of
      data points are timestamped for each patient. To tackle this, we employ two strategies for time-series
878
      classification: (i) using observation intensity (OI), which gauges the severity of the patient’s condition,
879
      and (ii) without using OI. Each method adjusts for the dataset’s imbalance by evaluating performance
880   through the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) score.
881
882
      CIFAR-100 and CIFAR-10. These datasets, introduced by Krizhevsky and Hinton (2009), contain
883
      60,000 32x32 pixel RGB images that are split into two groups: CIFAR-100, with 100 different
884   fine-grained classes, and CIFAR-10, with 10 broader categories. Each dataset is evenly divided, with
885   50,000 images allocated for training and 10,000 for testing. The images depict a wide variety of
886   objects, including animals, vehicles, and everyday scenes. For example, CIFAR-100 includes detailed
887   classes such as maple trees, shrews, and pickup trucks, while CIFAR-10 contains broader categories
888   such as airplanes, automobiles, dogs, and cats.
889
890   STL-10. The STL-10 dataset (Coates et al., 2011) consists of 13,000 color images, each with a
891   resolution of 96x96 pixels, categorized into 10 classes. The images primarily capture animals, objects,
892   and outdoor scenes. For instance, some of the categories include airplanes, birds, cars, cats, deer,
893   dogs, horses, monkeys, ships, and trucks.
894
895   SVHN. The Street View House Numbers (SVHN) dataset (Netzer et al., 2011) consists of over
896   600,000 digit images extracted from real-world scenes, specifically house numbers captured by
897   Google’s Street View. The images typically have a resolution of around 32x32 pixels and contain
898   digits from ‘0’ to ‘9’. Each image often includes multiple digits, but the dataset focuses on identifying
899   individual digits.

1082   For the dataset preprocessing, we adhered to the original train-test split provided with the datasets.
1083   From the training set, 20% was reserved for validation purposes. For data augmentation in the training
1084   set, we employed random resizing, cropping, and flipping techniques. Image normalization was
1085   carried out using the original mean and standard deviation values from each dataset, and the images
1086   were used at their default sizes without any resizing. This approach helped maintain the integrity of
1087   the original data while enhancing the model’s ability to generalize from augmented variations.
1088   In our experiments, the batch size was set to 128 for the CIFAR-100, CIFAR-10, and SVHN datasets.
1089   For the STL-10 dataset, a smaller batch size of 64 was utilized due to its limited number of samples.
1090   The models were optimized using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with an initial learning rate of
1091   0.1 over a course of 100 epochs. To address potential stalls in training progress, the learning rate was
1092   halved if there was no change in the loss over two consecutive epochs. Additionally, an early-stopping
1093   mechanism was implemented, terminating the training process if there was no improvement in the
1094
       loss for ten consecutive epochs. This strategy helps in preventing overfitting and ensures efficient
       training by curtailing unnecessary computation once performance plateaus.
1095
1096   Specifically, the model architecture consists of the following components:
1097
1098          • The input layer of the image classification model is similar to conventional CNN models,
1099
                where multiple convolutional operations are applied to the input image to extract basic
                features. An average pooling layer is used to extract the input feature as a vector z, with a
1100
                vector size of 1024 obtained through convolutional layers.
1101
1102          • The feature vector z is fed into the neural differential equation module, which estimates the
1103            hidden state z(T ) from the initial input feature z, where T represents the depth. The last
1104
                value of z(T ) is estimated by solving neural differential equations.
1105          • The differential equation module consists of a vector field that can be learned through
1106            backpropagation. In the case of Neural SDE, the drift term and diffusion term are represented
1107            by neural networks. The complexity of the vector field is controlled to ensure that the total
1108            number of parameters is similar to ResNet18.
1109          • The last value z(T ) obtained from the neural differential equation module is fed into the
1110            classifier to identify the class of the input. The parameters for all three modules (feature
1111            extractor, neural differential equation module, and classifier) are optimized simultaneously
1112            using backpropagation and the adjoint-sensitive algorithm.
1113
       We defined the complexity of the model to have a number of parameters comparable to that of
1114
       ResNet18. Precisely, ResNet18 has 11.18 million parameters for 10 classes and 11.23 million for 100
1115
       classes. Our Neural ODE models possess 10.49 million and 10.59 million parameters for 10 and 100
1116   classes, respectively. Meanwhile, the Neural SDE models, both additive and multiplicative, contain
1117   12.59 million and 12.69 million parameters for 10 and 100 classes, respectively. We used depth
1118   T = 10 in the experiments. We employed the Euler method (Euler-Maruyama method in the case of
1119   SDEs) for the numerical solving of the differential equations, using a time step size of ∆t = 0.1.

1763   We discuss why jump diffusion dropout is not universally applicable, unlike the proposed dropout
1764   method (NRSDE). Liu et al. (2020) introduced a new type of Neural SDE by adding a stochastic
1765

       from an engineering perspective, we compared the performance of NRSDE with the model, which
1766

1767

1768   models are not considered.
