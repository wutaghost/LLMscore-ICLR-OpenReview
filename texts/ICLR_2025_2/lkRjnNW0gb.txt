009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The scale of parameters in Transformers has expanded dramatically—from
012            hundreds of millions to several trillion. A key challenge when scaling the model
013            to trillions is the training instability. Although many practical tricks, such as
014            learning rate warmup, query-key normalization and better weight initializa-
015            tion, have been introduced to mitigate the training instability, a rigorous math-
               ematical understanding of why such instabilities happen and why the above-
016
               mentioned tricks work well is still unclear. In this paper, we give a theoret-
017
               ical analysis of the initialization, normalization and attention mechanism in
018
               Transformers, and present a set of stabilized designs of the initialization, nor-
019            malization and attention mechanism, which are thus termed as StableInit, Sta-
020            bleNorm and StableAtten, individually. In experiments, we demonstrate that
021            each of our stabilized designs, i.e., StableInit, StableNorm and StableAtten, ex-
022            hibits better stability. Furthermore, by putting the stabilized designs together,
023            we propose a stabilized Transformer, termed Stable-Transformer, and show in
024            experiments on large model (1B parameters) and deep model (200 layers) that
025            our proposed Stable-Transformer achieves a more stable training process.

      The scale of parameters in Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown
035
      et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Chowdhery et al., 2023) has expanded dramatically—from hun-
036   dreds of millions to several trillion—parallel to significant advancements of hardware capabilities
037   in the field of deep learning (Goodfellow et al., 2016; LeCun et al., 2015; Bengio et al., 2021). This
038   exponential growth in model size has been facilitated by equally significant strides in computa-
039   tional power, enabling deeper and more complex network architectures. As these models have
040   grown, they have set new benchmarks across a myriad of tasks in various fields such as natural
041   language processing (Dubey et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023), computer vision (Ravi et al., 2024),
042   and generation (Peebles & Xie, 2023).
043   Despite of these significant achievements, training larger models still suffers from an instabil-
044   ity issue, which is often characterized as the difficulties in convergence, the sensitivity to initial
045   conditions, and the necessity to finely tuned optimization strategies. Since that the instability
046   in training process encumbers the deployment and real-world applicability of the sophisticated
047   models, it is crucial to have a mathematical understanding of why such instability happens and
048   it is urgent to invent stabilized design of the architecture or training strategies.
049   To gain a deeper understanding of the instability in training Transformers, it is essential to in-
050   vestigate the training dynamics of Transformers. To date, there are various studies devoted to
051   the training process of Transformers from different perspectives, including normalization (Wang
052   et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Miyato et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022), attention
053   mechanisms (Henry et al., 2020; Wortsman et al., 2024), model structures (Bachlechner et al.,
      2021; Qi et al., 2023b), and initialization strategies (Glorot & Bengio, 2010; He et al., 2015; Qi et al.,

090   Former, which is designed to be Lipschitz continuous (i.e., the gradients are bounded) and has
091   been shown more stable during the training. The Lipschitz continuity allows for certain theoret-
092   ical guarantees about the model’s behavior, which is important in reliability or interpretability.
093
      This paper attempts to provide a theoretical understanding of the components that cause train-
094   ing instability of Transformer. To be specific, our main contributions are highlighted as follows.
095
096   • We give a theoretical analysis of the Xavier initialization from the perspective of random matrix
097     theory, showing that the Lipschitz constant of the linear projection associated to the Xavier
098     initialization is bounded by 2. Instead, we present a more stable method for initialization,
099     termed StableInit (defined in Eq. 1), for which the Lipschitz constant is bounded by 1.
100   • We dig into the issue in back-propagation of the normalization
                                                                   p        by analyzing the Jacobian ma-
101     trix of the normalization layer and find that the factor d will affect the gradient flow signifi-
102     cantly. As a remedy, we derive a more stable design for the normalization,p       called StableNorm
103     (defined in Eq. 2), in which d α with α ∈ [0, 0.5] is adopted to replace d in the normalization
104     layer, and verify that using a smaller α (e.g., 0.475, rather than 0.5) will yield smaller gradients
105     and thus lead to more stable training.
106   • We present a new stable form of attention, named StableAtten (defined in Eq. 3), which is built
107     on our StableNorm and has the advantage that the logit of the attention is not directly related
        to the hidden dimension d and thus is robust to the increase of the model scale.

500   3.4     S TABLE -T RANSFORMER
501
502   Following the architecture of Transformer, we can build a stabilized Transformer. As shown in
503   Figure 5, we use StableNorm to replace LayerNorm or RMSNorm, use StableAtten to replace the
504   original dot-product attention, and keep the same FFN module. Moreover, we use StableInit to
505   initialize the weights.
506   In practice, our Stable-Transformer can lead to two different architectures, i.e., a pure encoder
507   architecture, i.e., Vision Transformer, and a pure decoder architecture, i.e., GPT. Accordingly, we
508   name them as StableViT and StableGPT, respectively.
509
      To be more specific, for ViT, we build four variants of StableViT in correspondence with ViT
510
      as detailed in Table 1 in the appendix: StableViT-Large, StableViT-Huge, StableViT-giant, and
511   StableViT-200 (which scales of parameters range from 307M to 1.44B); for GPT, we build three
512   variants of StableGPT in correspondence with GPT as detailed in Table 2 in the appendix:
513   StableGPT-Small, StableGPT-Medium and StableGPT-Large (which scales of parameters range
514   from 124M to 774M).

527   4     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We have presented a theoretical analysis for the initialization, normalization and attention mod-
530   ule of Transformer from the perspective of training stability. Specifically, we derived an upper
      bound and a lower bound for the expectations of the maximum and the minimum of the singu-
531
      lar values of weight matrix obtained from Xavier initialization, found the reason why increas-
532
      ing hidden dimension can make the normalization layer likely leading to training instability
533
      from the Lipschits constant of the Jacobian matrix of the normalization layer, and also pointed
534   out the theoretical mechanism why the hidden dimension can bring instability issue to affect
535   self-attention module. Accordingly, we proposed three stabilized counterpart designs, i.e., Sta-
536   bleInit, StableNorm and StableAtten, and by putting them together, we also proposed a Stable-
537   Transformer. We compiled our stabilized components and Stable-Transformer with GPT and ViT,
538   and demonstrated that our stabilized methods can improve the training stability, leading im-
539   proved performance. We hope that our work can benefit the deployment of larger deep models,
      especially the large language models, in varied application scenarios.

     5      def StableInit(module: nn.Module, name: str = ’ ’ ) -> None:
1089 6          if isinstance(module, nn.Linear):
1090 7              n_in, n_out = module.weight.shape[0], module.weight.shape[1]
        8           init_std = 1.0/(math.sqrt(n_in)+math.sqrt(n_out))
1091 9              torch.nn.init.normal_(module.weight, mean=0.0, std=init_std)
1092 10             if module.bias is not None:

1094 13     class StableNorm(nn.Module):
     14         def __init__(self, ndim: int, alpha: float = 0.0, eps: float = 1e-8):
1095 15             super().__init__()
1096 16             self.alpha = alpha
       17           self.ndim = ndim
1097 18             self.eps = eps
1098 19             self.weight = nn.Parameter(torch.ones(ndim))
     20
1099 21         def forward(self, input):
1100 22             x_norm = torch.norm(input, dim=2, keepdim=True) + self.eps
     23             x = math.pow(self.ndim, self.alpha)*input/x_norm
1101 24             y = self.weight.unsqueeze(0).unsqueeze(0)*x
1102 25             return y
       26
1103 27     class StableAtten(nn.Module):
1104 28         def __init__(self, dim: int, num_heads: int = 8, qkv_bias: bool = False,
     29                 attn_drop: float = 0., proj_drop: float = 0.,
1105 30                 norm_layer: nn.Module = StableNorm,
1106 31                 temperature: float = 1.0, sequence_length: int=0) -> None:
     32             super().__init__()
1107 33             assert dim % num_heads == 0,
1108 34             self.num_heads = num_heads
       35           self.head_dim = dim // num_heads
1109 36             self.scale = self.head_dim ** -0.5
1110 37             self.qkv = nn.Linear(dim, dim * 3, bias=qkv_bias)
     38             self.q_norm = norm_layer(self.head_dim)
1111 39             self.k_norm = norm_layer(self.head_dim)
1112 40             norm_alpha = 2 * self.q_norm.alpha
     41             self.tau = 1.618*math.log(sequence_length,2)*temperature
1113 42             self.scale = self.head_dim**(-norm_alpha)*self.tau
1114 43             self.attn_drop = nn.Dropout(attn_drop)
     44             self.proj = nn.Linear(dim, dim)
1115 45             self.proj_drop = nn.Dropout(proj_drop)
1116 46
     47         def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
1117 48             B, N, C = x.shape
1118 49             qkv = self.qkv(x).reshape(B,N,3,self.num_heads,self.head_dim)
     50             qkv = qkv.permute(2,0,3,1,4)
1119 51             q, k, v = qkv.unbind(0)
1120 52             q, k = self.q_norm(q), self.k_norm(k)
     53             q = q * self.scale
1121 54             attn = q @ k.transpose(-2, -1)
1122 55             attn = attn.softmax(dim=-1)
     56             attn = self.attn_drop(attn)
1123 57             x = attn @ v
1124 58
     59             x = x.transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, N, C)
1125 60             x = self.proj(x)
1126 61             x = self.proj_drop(x)
     62             return x

1449   Initialization. Xavier Initialization does the most groundbreaking work in model Initialization. it
1450   sets the weights to ensure the variance of activations remains constant across layers, relieving the
1451   vanishing and exploding gradient problems. Sutskever et al. (2013) investigates the importance of
1452   initialization and momentum (Nesterov, 1983; 1998) in deep learning. Kaiming Initialization (He
1453   et al., 2015), builds on Xavier Initialization by scaling the weights for ReLU activations (Nair &
1454   Hinton, 2010). Admin (Liu et al., 2020) introduces an adaptive initialization method that dy-
1455   namically adjusts the initialization parameters based on the network’s depth and width. Saxe
1456   et al. (2013) introduce an orthogonal initialization, which further optimizes the initial parameter
1457
       distribution to boost training outcomes. Arpit et al. (2019) also investigates the orthogonal ini-
                                                                          1                               1
       tialization. Huang et al. (2020) propose to scale decoder by (9L)− 4 and scale encoder by 0.67L − 4 ,


                                                                  27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       this initialization method can be seen as a depth-aware initialization. Different from the above-
1459   mentioned methods, our StableInit is built on Random Matrix Theory, can promise the weight
1460   initialized by StableInit has Lipschitz constant approximately 1.
1461
1462
       Normalization. LayerNorm (Ba et al., 2016), different from BatchNorm (Ioffe & Szegedy,
       2015), normalizes across the features for each data point, making it effective for recurrent and
1463
       transformer-based architectures. Wang et al. (2019) discuss the influence of Pre-Norm and Post-
1464
       Norm on the training deep transformer. Xiong et al. (2020) further discuss the influence of
1465   pre-norm and post-norm on the training stability. RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) is a
1466   variant of LayerNorm that uses root mean square statistics, offering computational efficiency.
1467   DeepNorm (Wang et al., 2022) extends normalization strategies to deep transformer networks.
1468   WeightNorm (Salimans & Kingma, 2016) reparameterizes weight vectors to decouple the magni-
1469   tude from the direction, facilitating smoother optimization. CenterNorm (Qi et al., 2023b) only
1470   conducts the centering but does not scaling the feature. ScaleNorm (Nguyen & Salazar, 2019)
1471   normalizes only by the scale of the feature vectors, simplifying the normalization process. RM-
1472   SNorm and ScaleNorm can be seen as a special case of our StableNorm where α = 0.5 and α = 0.
1473
       By choosing a better α, our StableNorm can obtain a better training stability.
1474   Attention. Attention mechanism (Bahdanau et al., 2014) is firstly introduced to neural machine
1475   translation. Scaled dot-product attention, used in the Transformer architecture, calculates the at-
1476   tention weights using the scaled dot-product of query and key vectors, providing an efficient way
1477   to capture dependencies. L2 distance attention employs the Euclidean distance between queries
1478   and keys to compute attention scores. Attention with QK-Norm (Henry et al., 2020) normalizes
1479
       the query and key vectors before computing attention, improving stability and performance. De-
       hghani et al. (2023) scale the model to 22B via bringing QKNorm into attention. Wortsman et al.
1480
       (2024) further experimentally evaluate the value of QKNorm on small-scale models. However,
1481
       these three papers do not mathematically explain why QKNorm works. Liu et al. (2022) intro-
1482   duce to use a Scaled Cosine Attention (SCA) for Transformer. Meanwhile, Qi et al. (2023a) also
