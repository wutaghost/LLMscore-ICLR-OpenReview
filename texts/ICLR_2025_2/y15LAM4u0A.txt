009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Embodied artificial intelligence (EmbodiedAI) emphasizes the role of an agent’s
012            body in generating human-like behaviors. The recent efforts on EmbodiedAI pay
013            a lot of attention to building up machine learning models to possess perceiving,
014            planning, and acting abilities, thereby enabling real-time interaction with the world.
015            However, most works focus on bounded indoor environments, such as navigation
016            in a room or manipulating a device, with limited exploration of embodying the
017            agents in open-world scenarios. That is, embodied intelligence in the open and
018
               outdoor environment is less explored, for which one potential reason is the lack
               of high-quality simulators, benchmarks, and datasets. To address it, in this paper,
019
               we construct a benchmark platform for embodied intelligence evaluation in real-
020
               world city environments. Specifically, we first construct a highly realistic 3D
021            simulation environment based on the real buildings, roads, and other elements
022            in a real city. In this environment, we combine historically collected data and
023            simulation algorithms to conduct simulations of pedestrian and vehicle flows with
024            high fidelity. Further, we designed a set of evaluation tasks covering different
025            EmbodiedAI abilities. Moreover, we provide a complete set of input and output
026            interfaces for access, enabling embodied agents to easily take task requirements and
027            current environmental observations as input and then make decisions and obtain
028            performance evaluations. On the one hand, it expands the capability of existing
029
               embodied intelligence to higher levels. On the other hand, it has a higher practical
               value in the real world and can support more potential applications for artificial
030
               general intelligence. Based on this platform, we evaluate some popular large
031
               language models for embodied intelligence capabilities of different dimensions and
032            difficulties. The executable program of this platform is available for download, and
033            we have also released an easy-to-use Python library and detailed tutorial documents.
034            All of the software, Python library, codes, datasets, tutorials, and real-time online
035            service are available on this anonymous website: https://embodied-ai.
036            city.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Embodied AI (Duan et al., 2022) serves as the recent advance of artificial intelligence, presenting an
042   emerging paradigm shift from the traditional artificial intelligence, which learns from static datasets
043   (e.g., ImageNet, which contains 2D images). Specifically, embodied artificial intelligence is expected
044   to behave like a real human, which is able to learn from the environment and dynamically interact with
045
      the world, considered an essential approach to Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) (Duéñez-Guzmán
      et al., 2023). Various tasks for embodied intelligence have been established in different domains,
046
      including robotics (He et al., 2023; Barreiros et al., 2022; Driess et al., 2023), game AI (Fan et al.,
047
      2022a; Nottingham et al., 2023), unmanned vehicles/aerial drones (Zhou et al., 2022), etc. Generally
048   speaking, EmbodiedAI requires the agent to accurately understand the environment, perform high-
049   level reasoning, and effectively choose appropriate actions to execute tasks. Therefore, the training
050   and testing of embodied intelligence models are closely related to the environment. To accelerate
051   training efficiency and test embodied agents more conveniently, researchers generally choose to
052   construct a simulation environment as an approximation and mirror of the real world. Specifically,
053   the key to this process is providing an environment where embodied agents can obtain observations
      in real-time from a first-person perspective, generate actions, and receive feedback (Padalkar et al.,

      However, the environments and tasks for embodied intelligence the existing research mainly focuses
079   on are relatively limited (Duan et al., 2022). Many works (Shridhar et al., 2020; Deitke et al., 2022;
080   Gao et al., 2021) only consider the indoor scenarios for embodied intelligence. These tasks include
081   visual QA tasks targeting certain objects or simple task decomposition in the room (Azuma et al.,
082   2022; Francis et al., 2022). Such benchmarks actually restrict the validation of embodied agents’
083   capabilities within a very narrow boundary, with low task difficulty and a large gap to artificial general
084   intelligence. Therefore, in this paper, we consider extending embodied agents from indoor rooms to
085   outdoor cities, expanding tasks beyond the indoor spaces to a broader urban environment.
086   There are a few works that build a 3D urban environment for EmbodiedAI such as MetaUrban (Wu
087   et al., 2024), GRUtopia (Wang et al., 2024), and AerialVLN (Liu et al., 2023), but they are limited in
088   either the environment or the benchmark (we analyze the limitations in detail from 9 dimensions
089   in Table 1). Specifically, all of these works consider a fictional city. Either they employ a highly
090   simplified environment or only set up one or two embodied tasks. There are also some other efforts
091   that use street view images to construct the environment, which significantly limits the potential
092   Embodied AI tasks. To address the limitations, in this work, we first build a simulator for a highly
093
      realistic 3D simulation environment of a city, as shown in Figure 1. The environment is based on the
      real streets, buildings, city elements, pedestrians, and traffic in one commercial district from one of
094
      China’s largest cities, Beijing. In this commercial district, we establish realistic and detailed city-
095
      building 3D models as the foundation for the entire benchmark platform. Furthermore, we combine
096   the historically collected real-world traffic data and simulation algorithms to conduct simulations
097   of pedestrian and vehicle flows. We then set up a set of evaluation tasks covering different types of
098   embodied capabilities for embodied intelligence, including scene description, question answering,
099   dialogue, visual language navigation, and task planning. Specifically, we provide a complete set
100   of input and output interfaces for embodied agents’ access, enabling agents to easily read task
101   requirements and current environmental observations and then provide feedback results and obtain
102   performance evaluations. Based on this platform, we evaluate popular multi-modal large language
103   models and confirm the platform’s values in evaluating embodied intelligence of different dimensions
104
      and difficulties. The contribution of this work can be summarized as follows.
105   • To the best of our knowledge, we take a pioneering step to construct a benchmark platform for
106     embodied intelligence in an urban environment based on a real-world city.
107   • Based on the simulator, we set up a systematic benchmark including various and important tasks for

129   2    R ELATED W ORK
130
131
      Autonomous Driving. One of the related research directions is autonomous driving, in which the
132   simulation platform can be used to train the driving and controlling algorithms for autonomous
133   vehicles. CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) constructs an environment for autonomous driving,
134   which features urban streets and vehicle models equipped with sensing and control modules. However,
135   it focuses on modeling road surfaces and traffic in small-town settings, with less emphasis on the
136   realism of street layouts, urban planning, and building structures. MetaDrive Li et al. (2022) balances
137   visual quality and efficiency by using Panda3D and Bullet to offer a lightweight driving simulator that
138   supports research on generalizable reinforcement learning algorithms for vehicles. NuScenes (Caesar
139   et al., 2020) provides a perception dataset captured on real roads using cameras, radars, and lidar.
140
      However, these works are designed as simulators and datasets for autonomous driving and are less
      suitable for broader urban embodied task research.
141
142   Embodied-AI Platforms based on Real City. CityNav (Lee et al., 2024) has developed a WebGL-
143   based simulator grounded in real cities, but the tasks supported by this platform are quite limited,
144   and actions in the navigation task are discrete, with a large gap to the real-world navigation. Both
145   V-IRL (Yang et al., 2024) and TOUCHDOWN (Chen et al., 2019) are built on Google Maps, offering
146
      the highest visual realism, yet they only support discrete movements. AVDN (Fan et al., 2022b)
      provides agents with RGB sensing inputs derived from satellite images, which are of low precision.
147
      That is, these platforms based on real cities struggle to meet the high-quality, high-precision, and
148
      continuous sensing and movement requirements of embodied agents.
149
150   Embodied-AI Platforms based on Fictional City. MetaUrban (Wu et al., 2024) has developed a
151   streetscape generation simulator that facilitates top-down design for road layout, object placement,
152
      and dynamic urban traffic generation, providing convenience for navigation simulation of ground-
      based robots. However, it focuses solely on the neighborhood level, neglecting city-scale design,
153
      thereby limiting the activity space of agents. Additionally, object modeling is relatively coarse,
154
      at a sticker level, which may pose challenges for applications like image recognition algorithms.
155   GRUtopia (Wang et al., 2024) offers a variety of navigation, dialogue, and manipulation tasks for
156   different types of ground robots within multiple indoor scenarios such as hospitals, restaurants,
157   and libraries (it is still an indoor environment). AerialVLN (Liu et al., 2023) primarily addresses
158   the vision-and-language navigation problem for drones in urban environments. However, these
159   benchmarks are primarily constructed within fictional cityscapes and are simplified compared to real

189   3.1     3D E NVIRONMENT
190
191   The basic environment of the simulator includes a 2.8km × 2.4km district in Beijing, one of the
192
      biggest cities in China, where we meticulously build 3D models for buildings, streets, and other
      outdoor elements, ensuring high-fidelity urban simulations, all hosted by Unreal Engine 5.32 . In
193
      addition to this business district, we have also developed a nearby residential area that features
194
      detailed interior modeling, allowing the simulator to support both indoor and outdoor tasks. Below
195   are the key components that make up the environment:
196
197   • Buildings. We first manually use Blender3 to create the 3D assets of the buildings, for which we
198
        use the streetview services of Baidu Map4 and Amap5 . The city-level detail includes approximately
        200 buildings, encompassing a variety of types such as office towers, shopping malls, residential
199
        complexes, and public facilities. These models are textured and detailed to closely resemble their
200
        real-world counterparts to enhance realism in the simulation.
201   • Streets. The city contains a total of 100 streets, with a combined length of approximately 50 km.
202     The streets are modeled to include all necessary components such as lanes, intersections, traffic
203     signals, and road markings. We also incorporate pedestrian pathways, cycling lanes, and parking
204     areas. Data from traffic monitoring systems and mapping services help ensure that the street layout
205     and traffic flow patterns are accurate and realistic.
206   • Vehicles and Pedestrians. Dynamic elements such as vehicles and pedestrians are simulated to move
207     realistically within the environment. The simulation algorithms for these elements are based on the
208     Mirage Simulation System (Zhang et al., 2022), providing realistic interactions and behaviors that
209
        mimic real-world traffic and pedestrian dynamics.
      • Other Elements. Besides streets and buildings, other elements include street furniture (benches,
210
        streetlights, signs), vegetation (trees, shrubs, lawns), and urban amenities (bus stops, metro en-
211
        trances, public restrooms). Over 6k urban elements are created using Blender, based on real-world

267   3.2   I NTERFACE OF EMBODIED AGENTS
268
269   With the environment of Unreal Engine, we further build the interface of embodied agents to ensure
      the agents can indeed embody themselves in the system. To implement it, we develop the input/output


                                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      interfaces based on AirSim6 , based on which the observations can be conducted in a first-view manner,
271   and the control actions include motion, velocity, accelerated velocity, etc. This provides a robust
272   framework for simulating realistic interactions and behaviors for both drones and vehicles.
273
274
      • Observations. The observations for the embodied agents are designed to replicate the sensory inputs
        available to real-world agents. For drones, these include image data such as RGB images (color
275
        images from the camera), depth images (showing the distance of each pixel from the camera), and
276
        segmentation images (semantic segmentation for different objects in the scene). Sensor data like
277     IMU data (accelerometer and gyroscope data for measuring acceleration and angular velocity),
278     GPS data (providing global positioning coordinates), and LiDAR data (3D point cloud information
279     of the environment) are also included. State information such as position (current coordinates
280     x, y, z), speed (current velocity vx, vy, vz), and attitude (current orientation roll, pitch, yaw) is
281     vital. For vehicles, the observations include similar image data and sensor data. Additionally, state
282     information for vehicles includes the position, speed, attitude, and wheel angle (current wheel
283     steering angle).
284   • Actions. The actions of the embodied agents mimic realistic controls similar to those used by
285
        air drones and vehicles. For drones, motion control involves setting target positions (x, y, z),
        target velocities (vx, vy, vz), and target orientations (roll, pitch, yaw). Camera control allows for
286
        viewpoint adjustments (changing camera direction pan, tilt), and other controls include starting
287
        or stopping the drone’s flight. For vehicles, driving control includes steering angle (setting the
288     steering wheel angle), acceleration (setting throttle), braking (setting brake force), and gear shifting
289     (switching between gears: forward, reverse, neutral). Camera control for vehicles also allows for
290     viewpoint adjustments, and other controls include starting or stopping the vehicle’s movement.
291
292   3.3       SDK AND O NLINE ACCESS
293
294   To make the simulator less difficult to use, we developed a Python client software development
295   kit (SDK) and a Python proxy server based on the HTTP protocol on top of the AirSim interface.
296   The Python proxy server is used to convert client requests into AirSim interface calls and return
297   AirSim responses. With this Python proxy server, we shield client development from the outdated,
298
      non-standard event loop asynchronous module employed by AirSim, and allow for easier remote
      access using a variety of HTTP-based infrastructure. The HTTP protocol they use mainly adopts
299
      JSON format for data transfer and sends images, for which the details are available in our open-source
300
      code repository. This Python client SDK implements synchronous and asynchronous methods based
301   on the standard async mechanism to support users writing highly concurrent programs, such as
302   concurrent requests for large models with the simulator.
303
304
      Based on the above open transport protocol and Python client SDK, we build an online platform
      for users to try out. The online platform supports the simultaneous simulation and control of up
305
      to 8 agents. Users can acquire control of one or more idle agents and manipulate their movements
306
      via keyboard keys, the web GUI, or even an online Python code editor. Users can also watch a
307   first-person view of all the agents via live video streaming. The platform is open for registration and
308   use since we hope that the open online platform will inspire more ideas and explorations, such as
309   collaborations on embodied agents in urban environments.

356   • Embodied question answering. The embodied agent can be further queried in natural language
357     about the environment. We have designed three types of embodied question-answering tasks:
358
        distance, position, and counting, as shown in Fig. 6. Distance questions involve determining the
        relative distance between the agent and surrounding city elements, such as “Which is closer to
359
        me, the blue building or the red building?” and “Approximately how many meters away is the
360
        building ahead of me?”. Position questions assess the understanding of spatial relationships in
361     the environment, such as “Is object A to the left or right of object B?” and “Which street is in
362     front, Street A or Street B?”. Counting questions evaluate the agent’s accurate perception of the
363     environment, such as “How many crosswalks can be seen in a full circle?”. Therefore, the input
364     includes both the first-view RGB images and a query about the environment. The output should be
365     the direct textual responses to the question.
366   • Embodied dialogue. Despite the task of embodied question answering, a more complex embodied
367     task close to the reasoning ability is embodied dialogue. Specifically, embodied dialogue involves
368     ongoing interactions where the agent engages in a back-and-forth conversation with the user. This
369
        requires maintaining context and understanding the flow of dialogue. Therefore, the input includes
        embodied observations and multi-round queries, and the output is the multi-round responses.

      6   D ISCUSSIONS AND LIMITATIONS OF THE BENCHMARK
503
504   Application and promotion on EmbodiedAI. The benchmark not only serves as the pure evaluation
505
      of the large language model or LLM agents but also could be a sim2real tool that supports the
      pre-training or pre-testing before being deployed to the real-world city environment. For the types
506
      of agents, the benchmark does not set constraints. That is, the agent deployed could be a robot or
507
      air drone. For example, the input of a robot may only include the RGB images, and for air drones,
508   the input can also contain the radar signals. The degree of freedom of different agents could also be
509   different. This platform expands the boundaries of embodied functions, promotes the category of
510   embodied intelligence, and can effectively support the further development of this field.
511
      Human refinement. When constructing the benchmark, we put a lot of effort into using human
512
      refinement steps to filter out low-quality responses or revise incorrect answers provided by LLMs.
513
      It is worth noticing that the paradigm of combining large language models and human crafts has
514   recently become widely used since large language models accurately and skillfully generate various
515   responses (but may be totally wrong). The key challenge here is accuracy rather than diversity, and
516   thus, the human efforts to refine the answers are quite essential and useful. On the other hand, the
517   cost of collecting all the responses with pure human labor is not affordable. Therefore, using the
518   large language models does not introduce a large bias.
519
      Extensions of benchmark. In our constructed benchmark, we consider five types of embodied tasks,
520   scene description, embodied question answering, embodied dialogue, visual-language navigation,
521   and embodied task planning. From a perspective of human-like critical abilities, these tasks well
522   cover the three most significant aspects: perception, reasoning, and decision-making. The follow-
523   up work, based on the simulation environment, promises to extend to more tasks, of which the
524   potential tasks could be as follows. (1) Multi-agent Collaboration: Introducing tasks that require
525   coordination and communication between multiple agents to achieve common goals. (2) Human-
526   Agent Interaction: Creating scenarios where human users interact with agents necessitates a more
527   sophisticated understanding of human behavior and natural language. (3) Adaptability and Learning:
528
      Implementing tasks that test an agent’s ability to learn from its environment and adapt to new and
      unforeseen scenarios.

531   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
532
533
      In this work, we take a pioneering step by building a systematic benchmark for embodied intelligence
534   in an open city environment. The benchmark contains a 3D city simulator, easy-to-use interfaces for
535   embodied agents, and five kinds of embodied tasks. We further evaluate the intelligence capacities
536   of some popular multi-modal large language models, which verify the rationality of the constructed
537   benchmark. We also plan to evaluate large language model agents’ performance and embodied
538   intelligence level in the real city environment via a Sim2Real paradigm, which can further validate the
539   application value of the benchmark. We believe this work can help narrow the gap between existing
      embodied intelligence research and the ultimate goal of artificial general intelligence.

      landscapes. The action space for drones includes vertical movements (up and down), horizontal

      urban environments.

672   comprehensive environmental data, including visual feeds and depth information. This allows the
673
      vehicle to detect road features, other vehicles, pedestrians, and potential hazards. The action space for
      unmanned vehicles includes steering (left and right), acceleration (forward movement), and braking
674
      (deceleration and stopping). These actions ensure that the vehicle can navigate urban streets safely
675
      and efficiently by making real-time adjustments based on its observations.

751     uniqueness of the spatial location referred to by the textual description. The process of Vision-and-
752     Language Navigation (VLN) is dynamic, requiring continuous interaction with the simulator. Each
753     decision at every step influences the subsequent observation, thereby affecting the next decision.
754     Consequently, the input and ground truth for each case are obtained through human annotation.
755     The input consists of the agent’s starting coordinates and textual instructions, while the ground
        truth comprises the route trajectory and the target coordinates.
