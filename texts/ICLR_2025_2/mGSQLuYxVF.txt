010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            In this work, we study the learning theory of reward modeling using pairwise com-
013            parison data and deep neural networks. We establish a novel non-asymptotic re-
014            gret bound for deep reward estimators in a non-parametric setting, which depends
015            explicitly on the network architecture. Furthermore, to underscore the critical im-
016            portance of clear human beliefs, we introduce a margin-type condition requiring
017            the conditional winning probability of the optimal action in pairwise comparisons
018            to be significantly distanced from 1/2. This condition enables a sharper regret
019            bound, which substantiates the empirical efficiency of Reinforcement Learning
020
               from Human Feedback and highlights the role of clear human beliefs in its suc-
               cess. Notably, this improvement stems from high-quality pairwise comparison
021
               data under the margin-type condition and is independent of the specific estimators
022
               used, making it applicable to various learning algorithms and models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has proven highly effective in aligning
029   large language models with human preferences and expert policies (Christiano et al., 2017). A
030   notable advancement in this field, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), enhances RLHF by learn-
031   ing rewards directly from pairwise comparison data rather than environmental interactions. This
032   approach significantly improves efficiency and achieves more robust alignment with human pref-
033
      erences (Rafailov et al., 2024), particularly in applications where preference feedback is naturally
      intuitive, such as recommendation systems and image generation.
034
035   The success of the RLHF has ignited extensive research to establish performance guarantees for
036   learning optimal policies. For instance, Zhu et al. (2023) examines the theoretical properties of
037   the reward modeling from action-based comparison data, while Chen et al. (2022) and Saha et al.
038   (2023) focus on trajectory-based comparison data. However, the theoretical foundations of DPO
039
      remain largely unexplored, especially for deep neural network (DNN) estimators, creating a gap in
      understanding its full potential. Additionally, the reason why RLHF significantly improves sample
040
      efficiency compared to traditional RL methods remains less clear. Studies have considered the gap
041
      condition on the Q function to distinguish the optimal action from the others (Shi et al., 2023; Zhan
042   et al., 2024). This provides a key insight into explaining the efficiency of RLHF based on reward
043   modeling. Specifically, an underlying reward function structure is a plausible explanation for the
044   observed efficiency gains in RLHF since the reward signals can always be reconstructed from the
045   clear human preference feedback.
046
      Strong consensus in pairwise comparison outcome reveals clear human preferences, indicating de-
047
      cisive winning odds for preferred actions and providing crucial insights into the underlying reward
048   function for RLHF. These preferences are vital for aligning language models with complex psycho-
049   logical attributes, like honesty and harmlessness, that traditional reinforcement learning struggles to
050   quantify. The clarity of these preferences enables RLHF to achieve remarkable performance with
051   minimal pairwise data, even in highly complex environments, demonstrating its efficiency in reward
052   modeling (Wang et al., 2024a). While recent work has explored structural conditions of the reward
053   function to explain this efficiency (Shi et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024), these conditions remain
      largely unverifiable, limiting their practical explanatory power.

      In this paper, we focus on obtaining theoretical guarantees for reward models with well-designed
055   network architectures. We aim to build a mathematical model to calibrate the clear preferences and
056   explain the sample efficiency of RLHF based on reward modeling. Specifically, our contributions
057   focus on the following aspects:
058
059          • We establish regret bounds for reward modeling using deep neural networks, which de-
060            pend explicitly on network width, depth, and sample size. These bounds provide valuable
061            insights into the success of DNN-based estimators in RLHF, particularly in terms of their
062
               sample efficiency.
063          • We propose a novel margin-type condition to calibrate clear human beliefs in RLHF. The
064            condition implies high-quality pairwise comparison datasets and unveils the structure of the
065            underlying reward, under which we obtain a sharper regret bound. This finding highlights
066
               the role of clear human beliefs in its success. The theoretical improvements are independent
               of the estimators used in practice, allowing them to be applied across a wide range of
067
               learning algorithms.
068
069          • We emphasize the broad applicability of the theoretical results in our work. Our findings
070
               provide theoretical guarantees for removing ambiguous comparison data during the pre-
               processing stage of RLHF training. We examine DNN-based reward estimators under gen-
071
               eral pairwise comparison models, without restricting them to specific parameterizations,
072
               thereby supporting RLHF’s empirical efficiency across various scenarios.
073
074   The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we introduce the pairwise comparison
075   model and propose the margin-type condition. We demonstrate that this condition leads to faster
076   convergence of the resulting regret. In Section 3, we derive non-asymptotic regret bounds for deep
077   reward estimators, which are explicitly characterized by the structure of DNNs. We also discuss
078   the implications of these results for generalization. We review related literature in Section 4 and
079   conclude with future research directions in Section 5. Technical details are deferred to the Appendix.

401   4    R ELATED W ORKS
402
403   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback Human preferences have emerged as a valu-
404   able alternative to numerical rewards in reinforcement learning (RL), owing to their intuitive elicita-
405   tion process (Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022). Recent theoretical advances in offline RLHF
406   have been made by Zhu et al. (2023) and Zhan et al. (2024), who developed reward-based preference
407   models—the former focusing on linear models and the latter extending to a general function class.
408
      While these foundational works establish theoretical frameworks, the critical role of human belief
      quality in RLHF has only recently gained attention. Wang et al. (2024a) addresses this by develop-
409
      ing protocols for preference label correction and ambiguity smoothing, while Zhong et al. (2024)
410
      tackles preference heterogeneity through meta-learning approaches. However, a rigorous theoretical
411   framework for quantifying human belief in RLHF remains an open challenge.
412
413   Margin Condition for Human Preference Modeling The efficiency of RLHF is largely attributed
414   to strong human preferences reflected in pairwise comparison data (Zhong et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
415   2024a). This concept parallels established noise conditions in classification theory, particularly Mas-
416   sart and Tsybakov noise conditions, which bound excess misclassification error (Tsybakov, 2004;
417   Diakonikolas et al., 2021; 2022). Similar margin-type conditions have proven valuable across var-
418   ious domains, from individualized treatment analysis (Qian & Murphy, 2011) to optimal policy
419   identification in offline RL (Shi et al., 2023), and linear bandit problems (Goldenshluger & Zeevi,
420   2013; Bastani & Bayati, 2020). While recent empirical studies aim to enhance data quality and ad-
421
      dress ambiguous samples in practical applications, offering various treatment strategies for handling
      crowd-sourced preference data (Wang et al., 2024a; Das et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Zhan et al.,
422
      2023), our study provides a theoretical foundation for this phenomenon by introducing margin-type
423
      conditions to analyze excess classification error in RLHF reward function learning.
424
425
      Convergence Analysis for Deep Neural Network Estimators In this paper, we present the con-
426   vergence theory based on specific neural network structures, employing uniform convergence anal-
427   ysis (Schmidt-Hieber, 2020; Diakonikolas et al., 2021). The optimization aspects are beyond the
428   scope of this study and we refer to Allen-Zhu et al. (2019); Lyu et al. (2021) for more discus-
429   sion. Recent studies have proposed other theories regarding the generalization performance of deep
430   neural networks. Some representative studies include norm-based generalization bounds (Bartlett
431   et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2022), uniform stability theory (Arora et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2017), and
      algorithm-based generalization bounds (Wang & Ma, 2022). All these studies provide new insights


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      for understanding the properties of neural network estimators. Some works focus on the conver-
433   gence theory for Transformer networks. Examples include (Takakura & Suzuki, 2023; Deora et al.,
434   2023; Wang et al., 2024b). In addition, we note that most of these studies focus on regression
435   and classification problems. The analysis for preference learning is relatively limited. Theoretical
436   guarantees for the pairwise comparison model often rely on additional specific conditions related to
437   dataset structures. It is of interest to study the learning theory for the reward modeling with DNN
438   based estimator.

      5   C ONCLUSION
441
442
      Reward modeling is becoming an essential component of real-world AI systems, crucial for develop-
443
      ing large language models and aligning them with human values. Obtaining theoretical guarantees
444   for reward models offers an opportunity to understand the efficiency of RLHF in aligning language
445   models and identifying which values are embedded in them. In this paper, we establish the regret
446   bound of the reward function implemented by a deep neural network in a non-asymptotic frame-
447   work. Specifically, the stochastic and approximation errors are well-balanced by selecting network
448   configurations that achieve the optimal convergence rate. This fully non-parametric approach effec-
449   tively addresses reward model misspecification in linear reward settings while being applicable to
450   various pairwise comparison models. Moreover, our sharper bound is based on a margin condition
451   imposed on the comparison dataset rather than directly on the unknown reward function. The condi-
452
      tion on the dataset is verifiable with the estimated winning probability while the assumption on the
      unknown reward function is unverifiable in practice.
453
454   We also note that controlling the effects of ambiguous data is important and worthwhile for future
455   research. Prior works developed learning techniques that are compatible with the label noise level
456   in learning halfspaces and active learning (Yan & Zhang, 2017; Zhang et al., 2020). In view of
457   the margin condition, we assert that computationally efficient learning algorithms can also be de-
458
      veloped to take advantage of the clear preference structure in human belief. It is also of interest
      to extend our result to trajectory-based comparisons under Markov decision process settings (Zhu
459
      et al., 2023). These considerations can be of additional interest when applied to more general rein-
460
      forcement learning tasks.

1093   implementation. In this section, we consider two parametrization of the deep reward modeling
1094   using Bradley-Terry and Thurstonian models, respectively. We refer to the Example 1 and 2 for
1095   more details. This reward function is specified as: r∗ (s, a1 ) = 2 sin(4ϕ(s)⊤ w∗ ) and r∗ (s, a0 ) =
1096   −2 sin(4ϕ(s)⊤ w∗ ) where ϕ(s) = (sin(s1 ), . . . , sin(sd )) is a non-parametric transformation for
1097   creating non-linearity. The identification condition, r∗ (s, a1 ) + r∗ (s, a0 ) = 0 for every given s, is
1098   ensured. Furthermore, as demonstrated in expression (1) and (2), both regret and preference depend

1130   Recall that our theoretical analyses reveal a crucial trade-off in neural network architecture design.
1131   The interplay between stochastic and approximation errors fundamentally impacts model perfor-
1132   mance. Proposition 1 and Proposition 2 establish that while increased network complexity reduces
1133   approximation error, it simultaneously amplifies stochastic error under finite data scenarios. This
       finding emphasizes the importance of balanced architecture selection.

       Our empirical results (see Figure 1) provide compelling evidence for this theoretical framework.
1135   Using a fixed sample size, we examined regret across varying network configurations. We observed a
1136   non-monotonic relationship: initially, deeper and wider networks reduce regret as the approximation
1137   capability of networks increases. However, beyond the near-optimal network configuration, further
1138   increases in model complexity led to degraded performance. This is consistent with our theory that
1139   stochastic error dominates under this case; this empirical evidence is in line with Han et al. (2023).
1140
       Furthermore, our results reveal that comparable performance levels can be achieved across diverse
1141   architectural configurations, highlighting the adaptability of deep neural networks to varying func-
1142   tion complexities. This is evidenced by a relatively flat region in the parameter space where the
1143   regret remains near-minimal. Such architectural flexibility is particularly valuable in practice, as it
1144   suggests that precise knowledge of the smoothness parameter β is not critical for achieving strong
1145   empirical performance. This robustness effectively addresses the common practical challenge of
1146   architecture selection under unknown function complexity. We recommend Jiao et al. (2023); Lee
1147   et al. (2019) for a more comprehensive analysis across different models.
