011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            While large language models (LLMs) encode vast amounts of knowledge within
014            their parameters for some mainstream entities, factual inconsistencies and untruth-
015            fulness in LLMs often lead to unreliable responses and cause significant risks
016
               in practical applications. This paper aims to improve model reliability by en-
               hancing consistency in answers to known facts and encouraging refusal to answer
017
               for uncertain questions. Specifically, we introduce SREF, an entropy-guided ap-
018
               proach designed to enhance the reliability of language models by incorporating
019            Self-REFerences, models’ understanding of rephrasing questions, with inputs.
020            We analyze and reveal the effectiveness of SREF in enhancing model reliabil-
021            ity from the perspectives of entropy and KL divergence. Extensive experiments
022            on 12 LLMs demonstrate that outputs generated with SREF yield more reliable
023            results, including an average improvement of 16.01% over the baselines and a
024            15.10% average improvement in consistency, while also adapting to identify and
025            acknowledge uncertain facts.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities, attracting significant
031   attention for their performance across various applications (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Mann et al.,
032   2020). These models are being applied to complex tasks such as reasoning, mathematical compu-
033
      tation, and planning, positively influencing a range of industries (Olausson et al., 2023; Tonmoy
      et al., 2024). While LLMs have some reasonable amount of knowledge for some mainstream enti-
034
      ties West et al. (2022), a critical challenge remains: LLMs often produce hallucinations, resulting
035
      in untruthful and inconsistent responses. This unreliability poses a significant threat, particularly
036   in high-stakes environments like legal, finance, and medicine (Berglund et al., 2024; Sallou et al.,
037   2024; Weidinger et al., 2021). Therefore, it is urgent to develop effective strategies to evaluate and
038   mitigate hallucinations, enhancing the reliability and trustworthiness of LLM responses.
039
      Hallucinations (Wang & Sennrich, 2020; Ji et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024) in models manifest
040
      in several ways, including factual inconsistency, factual fabrication, and faithfulness. This paper
041
      focuses on enhancing the factuality and consistency of language model outputs. It aims to ensure
042   that models can accurately express what they know and don’t know, while maintaining consistency
043   across their responses. This contributes to the broader goal of developing language models as reli-
044   able knowledge bases (Zheng et al., 2024a; Petroni et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021). Recently, several
045   methods have been proposed, such as scaling models (Liu et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2022), utilizing
046   adversarial training (Penedo et al., 2023), reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF)
047   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2024) and knowledge editing Meng et al. (2022; 2023). However,
048   these methods often entail significant computational overhead and require high-quality annotated
049   data, or they lack general applicability, necessitating model-specific adaptations.
050   A recent approach to enhancing LLMs involves mimicking human thought processes, allowing these
051   models to self-evaluate and adjust their outputs. (Liang et al., 2024; Kamoi et al., 2024) This method,
052   known as self-correction, aims to improve LLM responses by refining them during inference. Re-
053   searchers have applied these techniques to enhance model reliability, categorizing them into three
      types: self-correction with fine-tuning (Ye et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024), self-correction with exter-

054
      nal information (Gao et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b), and self-correction with prompting (Shinn
055   et al., 2024; Manakul et al., 2023). However, the practicality of intrinsic self-correction remains de-
056   bated. For instance, Huang et al. (2024) argue that LLMs currently lack the capability for effective
057   self-corrective reasoning, noting that when models are prompted to identify and fix their own errors,
058   their performance can actually decrease.
059
      In line with (Huang et al., 2024), we revisits self-correction methods and finds that these methods
060
      depend on advanced inherent capabilities, such as chain-of-thought reasoning or critical evalua-
061   tion. Models with weaker abilities struggle to improve both consistency and accuracy concurrently,
062   making self-correction challenging. Additionally, these methods often focus solely on performance
063   improvement, neglecting the importance of having LLMs indicate uncertainty when they don’t know
064   an answer, which is crucial for ensuring reliability. To address this, we introduce a self-reference
065   method (SREF) that leverages the model’s internal knowledge to generate relevant references. The
066   references can reflect the model’s understanding of current knowledge and influence the entropy
067   change in the model’s responses, thereby enhancing its reliability. It requires only retrieval from the
068   model itself, allowing it to function effectively as a knowledge base without relying on advanced
069
      capabilities or external knowledge sources. Additionally, this approach helps the model become
      aware of what it knows and doesn’t know by encouraging it to answer different questions related to
070
      the same knowledge, thereby improving both factuality and consistency.
071
072   Finally, we tested SREF on 12 models across three datasets and compared it with four prompt-based
073   self-correction methods to validate its effectiveness. We also analyzed SREF’s mechanism through
074   entropy and KL divergence, highlighting its advantages in enhancing model reliability.
075   Our contributions are as follows:
076
077          • We propose a self-reference method (SREF) that significantly enhances the accuracy and
078            consistency of factual expression by leveraging the model’s own parameterized knowledge,
079            thereby improving its awareness and mastery of information.
080          • We analyze and reveal SREF’s mechanism using entropy and KL divergence, which helps
081            quantify uncertainty and divergence in the model’s predictions. This analysis reveals how
082            SREF effectively reduces uncertainty and improves the reliability of large language models.
083
084
             • SREF consistently outperforms four other self-correction methods across three datasets
               (NQ, PoPQA, and TriviaQA). It achieves the highest Factuality scores, with an average
085
               improvement of 16.01% over the baselines. Additionally, SREF demonstrates a significant
086
               consistency improvement of 15.10%, highlighting its superior accuracy and reliability.

091   Reliable LLMs Definition This paper focuses on improving the reliability of large language mod-
092   els (LLMs) in terms of Factuality and Consistency. A Reliable LLM should be aware of what it
093   knows and what it does not know, and be able to communicate this uncertainty clearly to the user.
094   Specifically, a reliable LLM should meet the following two criteria: 1) Factuality: The model should
095   accurately convey what it knows and acknowledge when it is uncertain. 2) Consistency: For infor-
096   mation the model is certain about, it should provide consistent answers across different variations of
097   the same question.
098   Evaluation Formally, given an LLM M and a QA dataset D containing N factoid questions that the
099   LLM should have encountered during training, the model’s responses can be classified as correct,
100   unsure, or incorrect. We use two key metrics for evaluating reliability: Factuality Rate (FR) mea-
101   sures how often the model provides correct responses for fact-based questions. Consistency Rate
102   (CR) measure how consistency for the model’s responses. We follow the approach of Zheng et al.
103   (2024b) and generate m distractor options that are similar to the correct answer for each question in
104
      D. We then shuffle the answer options to create m versions of the same multiple-choice question,
      each with a different order, we denote the multiple-choice question dataset as Dmcq . Section 4.1
105
      provides the formal definition for each metric.
106

      stable in its predictions across variations of the same question. Randomizing the order of multiple-

      3     G ENERATION WITH S ELF -R EFERENCE (SREF)
135
      The key concept of SREF it evaluates whether the LLM can accurately respond to a question by
136
      leveraging its own self-knowledge. For a given input, SREF generates k rephrased versions of
137   the question and simultaneously produces responses to these, which we call self-references. This
138   helps the model assess its mastery of the current knowledge from multiple perspectives. The self-
139   references are then used as an additional prompt alongside the original question to obtain the final
140   result from the model. And helps the model be more confident in its responses or recognize when it
141   lacks sufficient knowledge. SREF is shown in Figure 1. Next, we describe SREF in more detail.
142
      Rephrase questions generation Given an input question x, the options o, a LLM M and a prompt
143   xpt , SREF generates a collection of rephrased questions Q = {q1 , q2 , ..., qk } using M, and each
144   rephrased question expresses the same meaning as the input x:
145
                                                         Q = M(xpt ||x),                                                           (1)
146
147   where xpt is an instruction for question generation and || denotes concatenation.
148   Self-reference generation Next, SREF uses the same model M to generate a response R =
149   {r1 , r2 , ..., rk } for each rephrase questions qi ∈ Q, and we refer to these responses as ‘self-
150   references’:
151                                                  R = M(Q).                                       (2)
152   Inference with self-reference Finally, SREF concatenate self-references R with the multi-choice
153   question x and the options o then passes this combined input to M.
154
                                                       y = M(p||R||x||o),                                                          (3)
155
156   where p is the prompt for multi-choice question. And y is the response of M. With the influence
157   of R, y will be ‘Unsure’ when R is inconsistency with x, see Eq.7 for detail. Figure 7 provides
158
      examples of the prompts for different generate tasks.
159   Self-references intuitively enhance the quality of model responses by ensuring consistency, building
160   coherence, and improving accuracy. For known facts, they provide uniform answers to similar
161   questions, reducing confusion. For uncertain facts, they help express uncertainty, guiding the model
      to be aware of what it knows and does not.

      Self-Reference from the Perspective of Entropy Self-reference could provides insights into the
      uncertainty of information and the consistency of model outputs. The key point is that self-reference
167
      R could reduce entropy. Since it provides additional context that is already aligned with the model’s
168
      prior probability distribution, this can act to concentrate probabilities around a few likely tokens,
169   effectively reducing uncertainty. see Theorem 2.6.5 in Elements of Information Theory.
170

172

196   In practice, there are two possible scenarios for varied responses in R: 1) different reference express
197   the same meaning, and 2) different reference are inconsistent in expression. In the first scenario,
198   a consistent R is more likely to generate responses relevant to the question x. Empirical evidence

200   creases model confidence, reinforcing the distribution over the next token tn , and KLr will increase
201

202

204   Thus, high similarity in references will make the model’s response more confidence, and low sim-
205   ilarity in references can increase uncertainty in the model’s response. Since these references are
206   generated by the model itself, this helps it recognize inconsistencies or gaps in its knowledge.

345   increases the likelihood of models outputting ’unsure’ across most models and datasets. Our con-
346   clusions align with Zheng et al. (2024a); Zhou et al. (2024), that models like LLaMA3 exhibit
347   some tendencies toward excessive introspection. Therefore, enhancing their ability to respond with
348   ‘unsure’ helps in building more trustworthy LLMs. For relatively smaller models like LLaMA2,
349   reducing ’unsure’ responses suggests an improvement in the information provided by the model. In
350
      summary, our method can adaptively help models recognize what they know and don’t know based
      on the reference.
351
352   Additionally, we provide a case illustrating how our method helps the model recognize its own un-
353   certainty. As shown in Figure 2, when the model generates references unrelated to the question but
354   maintains consistency among them, the SC method tends to choose the most frequent answer, lead-
355   ing to incorrect responses. In contrast, our method, SREF, detects the LLM’s uncertainty from these
356
      references and selects ‘Unsure.’ Further analysis in Section 4.5.1 confirms that choosing ‘Unsure’
      is both necessary and accurate.

449   5     R ELATED WROK
450
451   5.1     L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL AS R ELIABILITY K NOWLEDGE BASE
452
453   Large language models (LLMs) are powerful knowledge bases, capable of storing and retrieving
454   vast information. However, they often struggle with consistency and accuracy. Foundational work
455   by Petroni et al. (2019) and Roberts et al. (2020) explored their potential and limitations. While
456   efforts like those by Wang et al. (2021) have enhanced factual memorization, reasoning challenges
457
      persist (He et al., 2024). To address these, Zheng et al. (2024a) and Zheng et al. (2024b) proposed
      criteria for ensuring factuality and consistency. At the same time, Jang et al. (2022) and Ribeiro
458
      et al. (2019) identified gaps in output stability, and Cohen et al. (2023) and Xue et al. (2023) tackled
459
      factual inaccuracies, emphasizing the need for models to recognize their knowledge limits (Cheng
460   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). However, many existing solutions require significant resources
461   (Amayuelas et al., 2023). This paper introduces SREF, a self-referencing framework that enhances
462   LLM reliability. By improving consistency and accuracy, SREF allows models to manage uncer-
463   tainty more effectively, increasing their trustworthiness as knowledge bases and offering a more
464   efficient solution compared to existing methods.
465
466   5.2     S ELF - CORRECTION M ETHODS FOR LLM S
467
468   Self-correction methods for large language models (LLMs) enhance reliability through iterative re-
469
      finement and error correction. Madaan et al. (2024) introduced Self-refine, leveraging self-feedback
      to improve quality, while Welleck et al. (2023) focused on self-correcting during sequence genera-
470
      tion. Pan et al. (2024) surveyed diverse correction strategies, highlighting the need for robust error
471
      identification systems. Gao et al. (2023) and Yu et al. (2023) explored self-review and retrieval
472   feedback, respectively, to enhance performance. Additionally, Kim et al. (2024) and Shinn et al.
473   (2024) discussed feedback for task-solving and reinforcement learning. Jung et al. (2022) examined
474   logically consistent reasoning. Despite these advancements, Huang et al. (2024) and Stechly et al.
475   (2023) noted limitations in reasoning, where models struggle to recognize errors independently. In
476   contrast, SREF uses self-referencing to improve consistency and accuracy, addressing these limita-
477   tions by enhancing inherent reliability.
478
479   6     C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this paper, we introduce SREF, an entropy-guided approach that enhances the reliability of large
482   language models (LLMs) by incorporating self-references. We evaluated its effectiveness through
483   theoretical analysis and experimental validation, showing that self-references can improve accuracy
484   and help models recognize their knowledge boundaries. SREF was tested on three datasets and
485   12 models, demonstrating its ability to enhance reliability and assess uncertain knowledge. These
      results highlight SREF’s potential to increase the trustworthiness of LLMs in practical applications.

738   Baselines We compare SREF with four self-correction methods based on prompting: RCI (Kim
739   et al., 2024), Self-Refine (SRF) (Madaan et al., 2024), Self-check GPT (SCK) (Manakul et al.,
740
      2023), and Self-Consistency (SC) (Wang et al., 2023). All of these methods refine LLMs’ initial re-
      sponses solely by leveraging the models’ inherent capabilities, without relying on external feedback
741
      or supervised fine-tuning. The prompts for different baselines are shown in Figure 8
742
743   Recursive Criticism and Improvement (RCI) Kim et al. (2024) focuses on enhancing language mod-
744   els’ reasoning capabilities by structuring problems as a sequence of intentions or intermediate steps.
745   This approach aims to improve logical reasoning and decision-making processes in complex tasks.
746
      RCI relies on the model’s ability to generate explanations and evaluations of its own responses and
      refine them based on these evaluations. However, this method is less effective in models that lack
747
      chain-of-thought capabilities and strong feedback mechanisms. The prompt we used in RCI is:
748
749   Self-Refine (SRF) Madaan et al. (2024)is a method where models iteratively refine their outputs by
750   self-evaluating and making adjustments. This technique allows models to improve the quality and
751   accuracy of their responses through multiple iterations. However, it requires the model to recognize
752
      inaccuracies in its responses, which can be challenging for smaller-scale models.
753   Self-check GPT (SCK) (Manakul et al., 2023) involves a model verifying its own outputs by gen-
754   erating explanations or justifications. This self-checking mechanism helps ensure the reliability and
755   correctness of the model’s responses. Self-Consistency (SC) (Wang et al., 2023) enhances model
      performance by generating multiple outputs for a given input and selecting the most consistent re-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      sult. This method leverages the diversity of responses to arrive at a more reliable and robust answer.
757   These two papers are related to our work. SCK focuses on evaluating hallucinations in language
758   models, with the model determining whether the evidence supports its response. In our setting, we
759   use these responses and feedback as references to enhance the model’s output. For Self-Consistency,
760   it involves sampling multiple times to generate diverse responses to improve the model, but it relies
761   heavily on sampling methods. In tasks involving short question answers, it may lack consistency
762   because different samples often yield the same response.
