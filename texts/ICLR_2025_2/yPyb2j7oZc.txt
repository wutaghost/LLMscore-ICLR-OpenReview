009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)-based algorithms, such as MuZero and its
012            derivatives, have achieved widespread success in various decision-making do-
013            mains. These algorithms employ the reanalyze process to enhance sample effi-
014            ciency from stale data, albeit at the expense of significant wall-clock time con-
015            sumption. To address this issue, we propose a general approach named ReZero
016            to boost tree search operations for MCTS-based algorithms. Specifically, draw-
017            ing inspiration from the one-armed bandit model, we reanalyze training samples
018
               through a backward-view reuse technique which uses the value estimation of a cer-
               tain child node to save the corresponding sub-tree search time. To further adapt
019
               to this design, we periodically reanalyze the entire buffer instead of frequently
020
               reanalyzing the mini-batch. The synergy of these two designs can significantly
021            reduce the search cost and meanwhile guarantee or even improve performance,
022            simplifying both data collecting and reanalyzing. Experiments conducted on Atari
023            environments, DMControl suites and board games demonstrate that ReZero sub-
024            stantially improves training speed while maintaining high sample efficiency.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   As a pivotal subset of artificial intelligence, Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 1988)
029   has acquired achievements and applications across diverse fields, including interactive gaming
030   (Vinyals et al., 2019), autonomous vehicles (Li et al., 2022), and natural language processing
031
      (Rafailov et al., 2023). Despite its successes, a fundamental challenge plaguing traditional model-
      free RL algorithms is their low sample efficiency. These algorithms typically require a large amount
032
      of data to learn effectively, making them often infeasible in the real-world scenarios. In response
033
      to this problem, numerous model-based RL methods (Janner et al., 2019; Hafner et al., 2020) have
034   emerged. These methods involve learning an additional model of the environment from data and
035   utilizing the model to assist the agent’s learning, thereby improving sample efficiency significantly.
036
037   Within this field, Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Świechowski et al., 2023) has been proven to be
038   a efficient method for utilizing models for planning. It incorporates the UCB1 algorithm (Auer et al.,
039
      2002) into the tree search process and has achieved promising results in a wide range of scenarios.
      Specifically, AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2017) plays a big role in combining deep reinforcement learn-
040
      ing with MCTS, achieving notable accomplishments that can beat top-level human players. While
041
      it can only be applied to environments with perfect simulators, MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2019)
042   extended the algorithm to cases without known environment models, resulting in good performances
043   in a wider range of tasks. Following MuZero, many successor algorithms have emerged, enabling
044   MuZero to be applied in continuous action spaces (Hubert et al., 2021), offline RL training scenarios
045   (Schrittwieser et al., 2021), and etc. All these MCTS-based algorithms made valuable contributions
046   to the universal applicability of the MCTS+RL paradigm.
047
      However, the extensive tree search computations incur additional time overhead for these algorithms:
048   During the data collection phase, the agent needs to execute MCTS to select an action every time
049   it receives a new state. Furthermore, due to the characteristics of tree search, it is challenging to
050   parallelize it using commonly used vectorized environments (Weng et al., 2022), further amplifying
051   the speed disadvantage. On the other hand, during the reanalyze (Schrittwieser et al., 2021) process,
052   in order to obtain higher-quality update targets, the latest models are used to re-run MCTS on the
053   training mini-batch. The wall-clock time thus increases as a trade-off for high sample efficiency.
      The excessive cost has become a bottleneck hindering the further promotion of these algorithms.

054
      Recently, a segment of research endeavors are directed toward mitigating the above wall-clock time
055   overhead. On the one hand, SpeedyZero (Mei et al., 2023) diminishes algorithms’ time overheads
056   by deploying a parallel execution training pipeline; however, it demands additional computational
057   resources. On the other hand, it remains imperative to identify methodologies that accelerate these
058   algorithms without imposing extra demands. PTSAZero (Fu et al., 2023), for instance, compresses
059   the search space via state abstraction, decreasing the time cost per search. In contrast, we aim to
060   adopt a method that is orthogonal to both of the previous approaches. It does not require state space
061   compression but directly reduces the search space through value estimation, and it does not introduce
062   additional hardware overhead.
063   In this paper, we introduce ReZero, a new approach/framework designed to boost the MCTS-based
064   algorithms. Firstly, inspired by the one-armed bandit model (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020), we
065   propose a backward-view reanalyze technique that proceeds in the reverse direction of the trajec-
066   tories, utilizing previously searched root values to bypass the exploration of specific child nodes,
067   thereby saving time. Additionally, we have proven the convergence of our search mechanism based
068   on the non-stationary bandit model, i.e., the distribution of child node visits will concentrate on the
069
      optimal node. Secondly, to better adapt to our proposed backward-view reanalyze technique, we
      have devised a novel pipeline that concentrates MCTS calls within the reanalyze process and peri-
070
      odically reanalyzes the entire buffer after a fixed number of training iterations. This entire-buffer
071
      reanalyze not only reduces the number of MCTS calls but also better leverages the speed advantages
072   of parallelization. Skipping the search of specific child nodes can be seen as a pruning operation,
073   which is common in different tree search settings. Reanalyze is also a common module in MCTS-
074   based algorithms. Therefore, our algorithm design is universal and can be easily applied to the
075   MCTS-based algorithm family. In addition, it will not bring about any overhead in computation
076   resources. Empirical experiments (Section 5) show that our approach yields good results in both
077   single-agent discrete-action environment (Atari (Bellemare et al., 2013)), two-player board games
078   (Silver et al., 2016), and continuous control suites (Tunyasuvunakool et al., 2020), greatly improv-
079   ing the training speed while maintaining or even improving sample efficiency. Ablation experiments
080
      explore the impact of different reanalyze frequencies and the acceleration effect of backward-view
      reuse on a single search. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
081
082   • We design a method to speed up a single tree search by the backward-view reanalyze technique.
083     Theoretical support for the convergence of our proposed method is also provided.
084   • We propose an efficient framework with the entire-buffer reanalyze mechanism that further re-
085     duces the number of MCTS calls and enhance its parallelization, thus boosting MCTS-based al-
086     gorithms.
087   • We conduct experiments on diverse environments and investigate ReZero through ablations.

089   2     R ELATED W ORK
090   2.1   MCTS- BASED A LGORITHMS
091
092   AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2017) combined MCTS with deep RL,
093   achieving significant results in board games, defeating world-champion players, and revealing their
094   remarkable capabilities. Following these achievements, MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2019) com-
095   bines tree search with a learned value equivalent model (Grimm et al., 2020b), successfully promot-
096
      ing the algorithm’s application to scenarios without known models, such as Atari. Subsequent to
      MuZero’s development, several new works based on the MuZero framework emerged. EfficientZero
097
      (Ye et al., 2021) further improved MuZero’s sample efficiency, using very little data for training and
098
      still achieving outstanding performance. Another works (Hubert et al., 2021; Antonoglou et al.,
099   2021) extended the MuZero agent to environments with large action spaces and stochastic environ-
100   ments, further broadening the algorithm’s usage scenario. MuZero Unplugged (Schrittwieser et al.,
101   2021) applied MuZero’s reanalyze operation as a policy enhancement operator and extended it to
102   offline settings. Despite the aforementioned excellent improvement techniques, the time overhead
103   of MCTS-based RL remains significant, which is the main problem this paper aims to address.
104
      2.2   MCTS ACCELERATION
105
106   Recent research has focused on accelerating MCTS-based algorithms. Mei et al. (2023) reduces the
107   algorithm’s time overhead by designing a parallel system; however, this method requires more com-
      putational resources and involves some adjustments for large batch training. Fu et al. (2023) narrows

      the search space through state abstraction, which amalgamates redundant information, thereby re-
109   ducing the time cost per search. KataGo (Wu, 2019) use a naive information reuse trick. They save

118   3.1   M U Z ERO
119
120   MuZero (Schrittwieser et al., 2019) is a fundamental model-based RL algorithm that incorporates
121   a value-equivalent model (Grimm et al., 2020a) and leverages MCTS for planning within a learned
122
      latent space. The model consists of three core components: a representation model hθ , a dynamics
      model gθ , and a prediction model fθ :
123

125                                 Dynamics:             st+1 , rt = gθ (st , at )                       (1)
126                                 Prediction:           vt , pt = fθ (st )

128   The representation model transforms last observation sequences ot−l:t into a corresponding latent
129
      state st . The dynamics model processes this latent state alongside an action at , yielding the next
      latent state st+1 and an estimated reward rt . Finally, the prediction model accepts a latent state and
130
      produces both the predicted policy pt and the state’s value vt . These outputs are instrumental in
131
      guiding the agent’s action selection throughout its MCTS. Lastly the agent samples the best action
132   at following the searched visit count distribution. MuZero Reanalyze is an advanced version of
133   the original MuZero. This variant enhances the model’s accuracy by conducting a fresh MCTS on
134   sampled states with the latest model, subsequently utilizing the refined policy from this search to
135   update the policy targets. Such reanalysis yields targets of superior quality compared to those ob-
136   tained during the initial data collection. Traditional uses of the algorithm have intertwined reanalysis
137   with training, while we suggest a novel paradigm, advocating for the decoupling of the reanalyze
138   process from training iterations, thus providing a more flexible and efficient methodology. Refer to
139   the Appendix B for more details on MuZero during the training and inference phases.

      4.1       BACKWARD - VIEW R EANALYZE
196
197   Our algorithm design stems from a simple inspiration: if we could know the true state-value (e.g.,
198   expected long-term return) of a child node in advance, we could save the search for it, thus con-
199   serving search time. As shown on the right side of Figure 1, we directly use the true expectation
200   µA to evaluate the quality of Arm A, thereby eliminating the need for the back-propagated value to
201   calculate the score in Eq. 3. This results in the process occurring within the gray box in Figure 1
202   being omitted. Indeed, this situation can be well modeled as an one-armed bandit (Lattimore &
203   Szepesvári, 2020), which also facilitates our subsequent theoretical analysis.
204   Driven by the aforementioned motivation, we aspire to obtain the expected return of a child node
205   in advance. However, the true value is always unknown. Consequently, we resort to using the root
206   value obtained from MCTS as an approximate substitute. Specifically, for two adjacent time steps
207   S01 and S00 1 in Figure 2, when searching for state S01 , the root node corresponds to a child node of
208   S00 . Therefore, the root value obtained can be utilized to assist the search for S00 . However, there is
209   a temporal contradiction. S01 is the successor state of S00 , yet we need to complete the search for S01
210   first. Fortunately, this is possible during the reanalyze process.
211   During reanalyze, since the trajectories were already collected in the replay buffer, we can perform
212   tree search in a backward-view, which searches the trajectory in a reverse order. Figure 2 illustrates a
213   batch containing n + 1 trajectories, each of length k + 1, and Slt is the t-th state in the l-th trajectory.
214   We first conduct a search for all S k s, followed by a search for all S k−1 s, and so on. After we search

304   4.3   T HE R E Z ERO F RAMEWORK
305
306   The technique introduced in Section 4.1 will bring a new problem in practice. The experiments in
307   the Appendix D.3 demonstrate that batching MCTS allows for parallel model inference and data
308   processing, thereby accelerating the average search speed. However, as shown in the Figure 2, to
                                                                                1
309   conduct the backward-view reanalyze, we need to divide the batch into k+1    of its original size. This
310   diminishes the benefits of parallelized search and, on the contrary, makes the algorithm slower.
311   We propose a new pipeline that is more compatible with the method introduced in Section 4.1.
312   In particular, during the collect phase, we have transitioned from using MCTS to select actions to
313   directly sampling actions based on the policy network’s output. This shift can be interpreted as an
314   alternative method to augment exploration in the collect phase, akin to the noise injection at the
315   root node in MCTS-based algorithms. Figure 11 in Appendix D.3 indicates that this modification
316   does not significantly compromise performance during evaluation. For the reanalyze process, we
317
      introduce the periodical entire-buffer reanalyze. As shown in Figure 3, we reanalyze the whole
      buffer after a fixed number of training iterations. For each iteration, we do not need to run MCTS to
318
      reanalyze the mini-batch, only need to sample the mini-batch and execute the gradient descent.
319

322   for a certain number of training iterations. Reducing the frequency of policy target updates corre-
323   spondingly decreases the number of MCTS calls. Concurrently, this does not result in a decrease
      in performance. 2 Since we no longer invoke MCTS during the collect phase, all MCTS calls

531    6              C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
532
533
      In this paper, we have delved into the efficiency and scalability of MCTS-based algorithms. Unlike
534   most existing works, we incorporate information reuse and periodic reanalyze techniques to reduces
535   wall-clock time costs while preserving sample efficiency. Our theoretical analysis and experimental
536   results confirm that ReZero efficiently reduces the time cost and maintains or even improves per-
537   formance across different decision-making domains. However, our current experiments are mainly
538   conducted on the single worker setting, there exists considerable optimization scope to apply our
539   approach into distributed RL training, and our design harbors the potential of better parallel accel-
      eration and more stable convergence in large-scale training tasks. Also, the combination between

      ReZero and frameworks akin to AlphaZero, or its integration with some recent offline datasets such
541   as RT-X (Padalkar et al., 2023), constitutes a fertile avenue for future research. These explorations
542   could broaden the application horizons of MCTS-based algorithms. Additionally, since in offline
543   training scenarios, reanalyze becomes the only means of policy improvement, this makes the accel-
544   eration of the reanalyze phase in ReZero even more critical. Moreover, the potential improvement
545   in search results by ReZero may further improve the training result, rather than merely accelerating
546   the training. Therefore, combining ReZero with MuZero Unplugged (Schrittwieser et al., 2021) is a
547   direction worth exploring for building foundation models for decision-making.

      During the inference phase, the representation model transforms a sequence of the last l obser-
846   vations ot−l:t into a corresponding latent state representation st . The dynamics model processes
847   this latent state alongside an action at , yielding the subsequent latent state st+1 and an estimated
848   reward rt . Finally, the prediction model accepts a latent state and produces both the predicted
849   policy pt and the state’s value estimate vt . These outputs are instrumental in guiding the agent’s
850   action selection process throughout its MCTS. Lastly the agent selects or samples the best action
851   at following the searched visit count distribution. During the training phase, given a training
852   sequence {ot−l:t+K , at+1:t+K , ut+1:t+K , πt+1:t+K , zt+1:t+K } at time t sampled from the replay
853   buffer, where ut+k denotes the actual reward obtained from the environment, πt+k represents the
854
      target policy obtained through MCTS during the agent-environment interaction, and zt+k is the
      value target computed using n-step bootstrapping Hessel et al. (2018). The representation model
855
      initially converts the sequence of observations ot−l:t into the latent state s0t . Subsequently, the dy-
856
      namic model executes K latent space rollouts based on the sequence of actions at+1:t+K . The latent
857   state derived after the k-th rollout is denoted as skt , with the corresponding predicted reward indi-
858   cated as rtk . Upon receiving skt , the prediction model generates a predicted policy pkt and a estimated
859   value vtk . The final training loss encompasses three components: the policy loss (lp ), the value loss
860   (lv ), and the reward loss (lr ):

      MuZero Reanalyze, as introduced in Schrittwieser et al. (2021), is an advanced iteration of the orig-
865   inal MuZero algorithm. This variant enhances the model’s accuracy by conducting a fresh Monte
866   Carlo Tree Search on sampled states with the most recent version of the model, subsequently uti-
867   lizing the refined policy from this search to update the policy targets. Such reanalysis yields targets
868   of superior quality compared to those obtained during the initial data collection phase. The Schrit-
869   twieser et al. (2021) expands upon this approach, formalizing it as a standalone method for policy
870   refinement. This innovation opens avenues for its application in offline settings, where interactions
871   with the environment are not possible.

876   C.1   E NVIRONMENTS
877
878   In this section, we first introduce various types of reinforcement learning environments evaluated
879   in the main paper and their respective characteristics, including different observation/action/reward
880
      space and transition functions.
881   Atari: This category includes sub-environments like Pong, Qbert, Ms.Pacman, Breakout, UpN-
882   Down, and Seaquest. In these environments, agents control game characters and perform tasks
883   based on pixel input, such as hitting bricks in Breakout. With their high-dimensional visual input
884   and discrete action space features, Atari environments are widely used to evaluate the capability of
885   reinforcement learning algorithms in handling visual inputs and discrete control problems.
886   DMControl: This continuous control suite comprises 39 continuous control tasks. Our focus here
887   is to validate the effectiveness of ReZero in the continuous action space. Consequently, we have
888   utilized two representative tasks (ball in cup-catch and walker-stand) for illustrative purposes. A
889   comprehensive benchmark for this domain will be included in future versions.
890   Board Games: This types of environment includes Connect4, Gomoku, where uniquely marked
891   boards and explicitly defined rules for placement, movement, positioning, and attacking are em-
892   ployed to achieve the game’s ultimate objective. These environments feature a variable discrete
893   action space, allowing only one player’s piece per board position. In practice, algorithms utilize
894   action mask to indicate reasonable actions.
