009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Causality detection is to identify the “true” directed acyclic graph (DAG) of a
012            causal model from the joint probability distribution of the observed variables. Algo-
013            rithms such as PC and its modified versions perform this task under the restrictive
014            faithfulness assumption, that is the DAG encodes all conditional independencies
015            imposed by the distribution. However, all existing algorithms fail to detect the sim-
016            ple structure where a variable is the XOR of several Bernoulli variables, violating
017            faithfulness. We generalize this type of unfaithfulness that appears in other, non-
018
               XOR, examples and define the minimal dependence of a given variable X as the set
               of variables, such that X is independent of each variable in the set but depends on
019
               at least one of them, the dependent member if conditioned on the remainder of the
020
               set. Minimal dependencies of size at least two violate faithfulness. Consequently,
021            we relax faithfulness to minimal dependence faithfulness, restricting the neighbors
022            of a node to its dependent members, and impose minimal orientation faithfulness
023            that generalizes the orientation rules under faithfulness. We then determine the
024            structure of the dependent members of a node X in the true DAG and show that
025            they are connected to X either directly or indirectly by a collider. Finally, we
026            provide a sound and complete modification of the PC algorithm to detect this kind
027            of unfaithfulness and output all possible candidates for the true DAG.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Structural causal models (SCMs) are used to capture the causal relationships between a set of random
033
      variables. They assign to each variable a deterministic function of some of the other variables, known
      as its parents or direct causes, and possibly a noise variable (Peters et al., 2017). Consequently, each
034
      SCM induces a joint probability distribution over the random variables and a directed acyclic graph
035
      (DAG) where the direct causes of each variable are linked to that variable. The DAG is also known as
036   the “true DAG” or causal network of the variables. To learn the structure of the causal network – a
037   process referred to as structure learning – intervention is a reliable method to find causal relations
038   (Ke et al., 2020). However, because of practical constraints, the use of only observational data has
039   attracted attention in recent decades (Ng et al., 2021). For this purpose, sufficiency, causal Markov
040   condition (CMC), and faithfulness are usually considered in the literature to make learning possible
041   (Zhang & Spirtes, 2016). Causal sufficiency refers to the absence of hidden (or latent) variables.
042   CMC implies that all conditional independencies implied by the true DAG hold in the joint probability
043   distribution and faithfulness implies the inverse.
044   Under these assumptions, one main approach to find the true DAG is known as constraint-based
045   and is based on testing conditional independencies (constraints) between the variables (Kitson et al.,
046   2021). Examples of constraint-based algorithms are PC (Spirtes et al., 2000), SGS (Spirtes et al.,
047   2000) FCI (Guo et al., 2020) which are based on (in)dependence detection. The result of this approach
048   is a class of independence-equivalence (I-equivalence) graphs that are presented as a partially DAG
049   (PDAG). Under the causal Markov and faithfulness assumptions, constraint-based approaches have
050
      been shown to correctly find the true PDAG given the joint probability distribution, and the same
      holds asymptotically with respect to the number of data instances of a given dataset (Ng et al., 2021).
051
052   CMC and faithfulness are used to conclude the existence and absence of links in the true DAG
053   based on conditional independence test results, respectively. However, the faithfulness assumption
      is violated in some practical situations (Andersen, 2013), making it a restrictive assumption. Some

054
      of the work in the literature has accordingly relaxed this assumption (Lemeire et al., 2012) and
055   developed unfaithfulness detection approaches, that is, to detect from observational distribution P the
056   existence of some conditional dependencies that cannot be captured by the true DAG (Ramsey et al.,
057   2006; Zhang & Spirtes, 2008; Spirtes & Zhang, 2014).
058
      Adjacency-faithfulness and orientation-faithfulness assumptions that are weaker than faithfulness
059
      were defined in (Ramsey et al., 2006). PC algorithm was accordingly adapted, resulting in the
060
      conservative PC (CPC) algorithm that puts forward a number of candidate PDAGs that include the
061   true DAG. As another relaxation of faithfulness, triangle-faithfulness was introduced and investigated
062   in (Zhang & Spirtes, 2008; Spirtes & Zhang, 2014), resulting in the very conservative PC (VCPC) and
063   VCSGS algorithms. However, all of these and other existing algorithms, such as MGM-FCI-MAX
064   (Horii, 2021), fail to detect the true graph in examples such as the generalized XOR where several
065   Bernoulli random variables cause another variable.
066
      The first step in detecting this type of unfaithfulness was taken in (Marx et al., 2021) for the case with
067   three variables, i.e., two causing the third, resulting in the conventional XOR example. The authors
068   relaxed the faithfulness to the so-called 2-adjacency faithfulness and 2-orientation faithfulness,
069   provided a modified grow-shrink (GS) algorithm (Margaritis & Thrun, 1999) to detect triple of
070   variables violating faithfulness, and proved the soundness.
071
      We take the next step in this regard and define the minimal dependence of a given variable X as the
072
      set of variables Y , such that X is independent of each Y but becomes dependent if conditioned on the
073   remainder of the set. Minimal dependencies of size at least two violate faithfulness. Consequently,
074   we relax the assumption to minimal dependence faithfulness, limiting the existence of links to a
075   variable and its minimal dependence. We then determine the structure of minimal dependencies in
076   the true DAG and provide a modification of the PC algorithm, the minimal dependence PC (MD-PC)
077   algorithm to detect this kind of unfaithfulness and output all possible candidates for the true DAG.

      2    P ROBLEM FORMULATION
080
081
      Consider a structural causal model (SCM) (Peters et al., 2017) defined as the tuple ⟨X , N , PU , F⟩
082   where X = {X1 , . . . , Xm } are the random variables, N = {NXQ| X ∈ X } are the disjoint noise
083   random variables whose joint distribution PU satisfies PU (N ) = X PU (NX ). The value assigned
084   to each variable X ∈ X is a deterministic function fX of a subset of the variables PaX ⊂ X \ {X},
085   known as its parents, and a parent noise variable NX ∈ N , i.e., X := fX (PaX , NX ). The set of
086   functions fX for all variables X defines F. The parents of each variable X are known as its direct
087   causes. The function fX is minimal in the parents of X; that is, there does not exist another function
088   gX , such that fX (PaX , NX ) = gX (S, NX ) for some subset S ⊂ PaX . The SCM induces (i) a
089   joint (observational) probability distribution P over the variables X and (ii) a DAG G whose nodes
090
      represent variables X , and there is a link to every variable X from each of its parents PaX .
091   The goal is to obtain DAG G, referred to as the “true DAG,” from observational probability distribution
092   P , that is to estimate the causal relationships from the observational distribution.
093
094   Problem 1 (Causal discovery) Consider an SCM over the variables X , inducing DAG G and joint
095
      observational probability distribution P . Given distribution P , find DAG G.
096
      This is, however, an impossible task in general. The simplest example is the case with two dependent
097   variables X and Y , where both SCMs X := 2Y and Y := 0.5X impose the same observational
098   distribution P but two different DAGs Y → X and X → Y .
099
100
      Some commonly-imposed assumptions facilitate this task. One is the (causal) faithfulness assumption
      explained as follows. True DAG G implies some independencies between the variables. For example,
101
      if there is no trail (path) between two variables in G, their deterministic functions are not related,
102
      rendering the two variables independent. Faithfulness states that such independencies implied
103   by G capture all independencies satisfied by the distribution P . The attribution of (conditional)
104   independencies to a DAG is formalized by the notion of d-separation (see Appendix). Let I(G) denote
105   the set of all d-separations in a DAG G and I(P ) denote the set of all conditional independencies
106   implied by distribution P , i.e., I(P ) = {(Y1 ⊥ Y2 | Y3 ) : Y1 , Y2 , Y3 ⊆ X }.
107
      Assumption 1 (Faithfulness) I(P ) ⊆ I(G).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Distribution P is said to be (causally) faithful to the DAG G if it satisfies the above assumption.
109   Problem 1 can then be stated as follows.
110
111   Problem 2 (Causal discovery under faithfulness) Consider an SCM over variables X , inducing
112   DAG G and joint observational probability distribution P . Given distribution P and under Assumption
113   1, find DAG G.
114
      Problem 2 is often introduced from a different perspective, without the causality ingredient: there is
115
      some true DAG G over random variables X with joint distribution P , and the goal is to again obtain
116
      G from P . Clearly, then the connection between G and P is lost and any DAG is a candidate for G.
117   Faithfulness helps to bridge the gap yet is insufficient as, for example, an empty graph satisfies the
118   faithfulness assumption for every distribution P , and hence, is always a valid candidate. A second
119   commonly-imposed assumption is the (Causal) Markov Condition (CMC).
120
121   Assumption 2 (Markovness) I(G) ⊆ I(P ).
122
      If Markovness holds, G is called an I-map for P . If additionally, G is no longer an I-map for P upon
123
      the elimination of an edge, then it is a minimal I-map for P . Similar to faithfulness, Markovness is
124
      insufficient to learn causal DAGs from observational data, because, for example, a fully connected
125   DAG has I(G) = ∅ and satisfies CMC for any observational distribution. However, together these
126   assumptions enable us to present Problem 2 without the causality ingredient.
127
128   Problem 3 (Structure learning) Consider random variables X with joint probability distribution
129   P . Assume there exists a DAG G satisfying Assumptions 1 and 2. Given distribution P , find DAG G.
130
      We state the results in the conventional setup of Problem 3, but they can also be stated using the setup
131
      of Problem 1.Note that even with both Markovness and faithfulness in force, the two-node DAGs
132
      example stated at the beginning of this section may not be distinguished using the observational
133   distribution. This is because the two have identical distributions P and in turn I(P ). Consequently,
134   the problem of finding the true DAG is reduced to finding the so-called I-equivalent class of DAGs,
135   which share the same I(G) that is equal to I(P ). I-equivalent DAGs are known to have the same
136   skeleton and same directions on some of the edges (Koller & Friedman, 2009), and hence, are
137   represented by a PDAG.
138
      PC algorithm solves Problem 3 (and 2) by putting forward an I-equivalence class that includes the
139   true DAG G. The algorithm, however, fails if the faithfulness assumption is violated. The idea with
140   PC is that if two variables are independent conditioned on any subset of the other variables, then
141   the two are not adjacent in the true DAG–a condition satisfied by faithfulness. If distribution P is
142   unfaithful, PC may incorrectly identify the absence of a link once an independence is detected in P
143   as illustrated in the following example.
144
145

188   3        M INIMAL - DEPENDENCE FAITHFULNESS
189
190   The type of unfaithfulness explained in Examples 1 and 2 happens when a variable X is independent
191   of each individual member of a set Y = {Y1 , . . . , Yn } but not when conditioned on the other members.
192   Namely, X indeed depends on all of Y1 , . . . , Yn , yet each single Yi does not individually inform the
193   distribution of X. The following definition captures this idea.
194
195
      Definition 1 (Weak minimal dependence) Given a variable X ∈ X , a minimal dependence for X
      is a set Y ⊆ X \ {X} that satisfies the following conditions:
196
197            1. there exists at least one member Y ∈ Y that satisfies
198
                                                      X ̸⊥ Y | Y \ {Y },                                     (1)
199
200            2. if |Y| ≥ 2, then
201                                                    ∀Y ∈ Y           X ⊥ Y,                               (2)
202
203            3. no proper subset of Y satisfies (1) and (2).
204
      If a minimal dependence of a variable X consists of a single variable Y , then Condition (2) does not
205
      apply. Condition (1), on the other hand, implies that X and Y are dependent. Should faithfulness be
206   in force, we would conclude that X and Y are connected by some active trail.
207
208   However, if a minimal dependence entails at least two variables, it violates the faithfulness assumption.
209
      Condition (1) implies that X depends on some variable Y in its minimal dependence set given the
      remainder of the set. However, Condition (2) implies that X is independent of every member of
210
      the minimal independence. This is counter intuitive as it implies that the only way the dependence
211
      between X and Y can be explained is by the other members of the minimal dependence; nevertheless,
212   X is independent of each of these members, resulting in an unfaithful distribution.
213
214   Proposition 1 Consider random variables X with joint probability distribution P . If for any of the
215   variables X ∈ X , there exists a minimal dependence of X with cardinality of at least two, then P is
      not faithful to any DAG G over X that is an I-map for P .

      The set Y = {Y1 , . . . , Yn } in Examples 1 and 2 is a dependence set for X. This notion of unfaithful-
217   ness may as well apply to some real-world scenarios, where the presence of a single “effect” does not
218   make a particular “cause” more likely, yet the combination of several such effects may do so.
219
220   Example 3 (influenza or dengue fever) Let the binary variable X indicate the presence of either
221   influenza (X = 0) or the dengue fever disease (Halstead, 2007; Eccles, 2005) (X = 1) in a patient.
222   There are three symptoms shared between these two diseases: (i) fever (captured by Y1 = 1 when
223   present otherwise Y1 = 0); (ii) headache (captured by Y2 = 1 when present otherwise Y2 = 0);
224   (iii) joint and muscle pain (captured by Y3 = 1 when present otherwise Y3 = 0). Knowing any of
225
      these symptoms does not make any of the two diseases more likely, i.e., P (X | Yi ) = P (X) for all i.
      However, while all three symptoms can be simultaneously present in the case of influenza, that is not
226
      the case with dengue fever. In dengue fever, fever and headache appear in the early stages, and the
227
      joint and muscle pain is observed often a few days after. Therefore, if the patient is known to have
228   fever and headache, the additional information of whether they also have joint and muscle pain may
229   determine the disease type, i.e., P (X | Y1 , Y2 , Y3 ) ̸= P (X | Y1 , Y2 ), implying X ̸⊥ Y3 | Y1 , Y2 . It
230   follows that Y = {Y1 , Y2 , Y3 } is a minimal dependence for X.
231

234   that are independent of every other variable, e.g., isolated nodes. Nevertheless, the consequence of
235
      minimality is that all members of the minimal dependence set satisfy Condition 1.

      3.1   T HE STRUCTURE OF THE MINIMAL DEPENDENCE
254
255   What can be concluded about the true DAG G of a distribution P that admits a minimal dependence
256   of size at least two? An immediate result of Proposition 1 is that once a minimal dependence of size
257   at least two is detected for a variable X in the distribution P , the absence of the links between X
258   and its minimal dependence variables may no longer be concluded, even though X is independent of
259   them. This, however, does not mean that X and the minimal dependence can form any DAG. The
260   following result enforces the existence of certain combinations of links.
261
      Theorem 1 Consider random variables X with joint probability distribution P . Consider a node
262
      Z ∈ X and its weak minimal dependence Y. If DAG G is an I-map for P , then every member Y ∈ Y
263
      is connected to Z either by a collider-free trail or by a trail, every collider of which has a node in Y
264   as its descendant.
265
266   To further conclude about the structure of the true DAG, we define the following stronger version
267   of Definition 1. This definition is motivated by normal faithfulness, where two nodes X and Y are
268   adjacent if and only if their dependence does not break upon the observation of any other set U, i.e.,
269   X ̸⊥ Y | U for all U ̸⊇ {X, Y } (Koller & Friedman, 2009). Similarly, here, we extend Condition (1)
      in Definition 1 to when any additional set U is observed.

      Definition 2 ((Strong) minimal dependence) Given a variable X ∈ X , a (strong) minimal depen-
271   dence for X is a set Y ⊆ X \ {X} that satisfies the following conditions:
272
273         1. there exists at least one member Y ∈ Y that satisfies
274
                                   ∀U ∈ X \ ({X} ∪ Y)          X ̸⊥ Y | (Y \ {Y }) ∪ U                   (7)

            2. if |Y| ≥ 2, then
277
                                                    ∀Y ∈ Y     X ⊥ Y,                                    (8)
278
279         3. no proper subset of Y satisfies (7) and (8).
280
281   Every member Y ∈ Y that satisfies (7) is called a dependent member of Y and the set of all dependent
282   members of Y is denoted by Y o . The set of all strong minimal dependencies of X is denoted by
283   Dep(X).
284
      In the rest of the paper, by “minimal dependence” we mean strong minimal dependence. According
285   to Definition 2, the dependence between X and its minimal dependence variables does not break
286   upon the observation of non-minimal-dependence variables. Clearly, a strong minimal dependence of
287   a variable X is also its weak minimal dependence but not the other way around. Note that (7) implies
288   the presence of an edge between X and a node in Y.
289
      Next, to deduce the absence of an edge in the true DAG, we make the following assumption. The
290
      violation of faithfulness no longer allows edge elimination in G. Nevertheless, faithfulness is only
291   violated “within” the minimal independence sets. The outer ones may be removed.
292
293   Assumption 3 (Minimal-dependence faithfulness) Consider DAG G over variables X with joint
294   probability distribution P . If Y, Z ∈ X are adjacent in G, then each is a dependent member of a
295   minimal dependence of the other, i.e., there exists a minimal dependence Y ∈ Dep(Z) such that
296   Y ∈ Y o and a minimal dependence Z ∈ Dep(Y ) such that Z ∈ Z o .
297
      This assumption is motivated by a result in faithfulness (as well as adjacency faithfulness (Ramsey
298
      et al., 2006); see Section 3.4). Under a faithful distribution, two nodes Y and Z are adjacent only if
299
      Y ̸⊥ Z | U for all U ⊆ X \ {Y, Z}. Namely, adjacency is allowed only between “tightly” dependent
300   variables. Similarly, Assumption 3 enforces the same, with the difference that here the notion of
301   tight dependence extends to that between a variable Z and a set of other variables Y. Namely, in
302   view of Proposition 2, each minimal dependence Y ∈ Dep(Z) can be seen as a “super node”:
303   Although independent of individual nodes in Y, variable Z does depend on the whole Y according
304   to Equation (7). Hence, Assumption 3 allows adjacency between nodes Z and Y only if Z and Y
305   are tightly dependent where Y ∈ Y o and Y ∈ Dep(Z) which in turn implies that that Z and Y are
306   tightly dependent.
307   Assumption 3 and Definition 2 sharpen Theorem 1 to what we define as a v-star. Denote the union
308   of the minimal dependencies of Z by Dep(Z) = ∪Y∈Dep(Z) Y and the union of the sets of the
309   dependent members of the minimal dependencies by Depo (Z) = ∪Y∈Dep(Z) Y o .
310
311   Definition 3 (v-star) A v-star over the node set X is the DAG where a single node X ∈ X is
312   connected to every other node in X either directly (i.e., the two are adjacent) or indirectly by a
313   collider whose effect node is also a node in X . The DAG is also referred to as a v-star centered at X.
314
315
      Theorem 2 Consider random variables X with joint probability distribution P , and let Z ∈ X . If
      DAG G is an I-map for P and satisfies Assumption 3, then (i) Z and Depo (Z) form a v-star centered
316
      at Z; and (ii) for every Y ∈ Depo (Z) that is not adjacent to Z, a node in Y \ {Y } is a descendant
317
      of (or equals) the effect node of the collider connecting Y to Z, where Y is the minimal dependence
318   of Z that includes Y .
319

321   of each individual minimal dependence but all of them as a whole: Dependent member Y of a
322   minimal dependence Y ∈ Dep(Z) may be connected to Z directly, indirectly by another node in Y,
323   or indirectly by another node in a different minimal dependence of Z, say Y ′ . However, this requires
      that other node to belong to the minimal dependence of Y as otherwise, there is no link between

334   3.2   T HE SYMMETRIC CASE
335
      So far, Theorems 1 and 2 indicate the possibility of intertwined connections of the minimal depen-
336
      dencies in the true DAG. The connections can be partly separated upon the introduction of some
337   symmetry to the minimal dependence.
338
339   Definition 4 (Symmetric minimal dependence) Given variables X with joint probability distribu-
340   tion P , a symmetric minimal dependence is a set of random variables Z = {Z1 , . . . , Zn } ⊆ X , such
341   that for every i ∈ {1, . . . , n}, Z−i ∈ Dep(Zi ) and Z o−i = Z−i .
342
      Namely, for each variable of a symmetric minimal dependence, the remainder of the set is its strong
343
      minimal dependence and all members of which are dependent members. The set of variables in both
344
      Examples 1 and 2 are symmetric minimal dependencies, but not those in Example 4. What structure
345   does the true DAG of a symmetric minimal dependence take? It appears that in both examples, there
346   is a node where all others are directing to. We show that this is generally true.
347
348   Definition 5 (Directed star) A directed star over nodes Z consists of a single center node Z ∈ X
349   that has an incoming edge from every other node Z \ {Z}. The directed star is also referred to as a
350   star directed at Z.
351

369
370   The idea with PDAG class P-maps is to capture all DAGs that are an I-map for the distribution
371   P , satisfy the faithfulness condition, and are indistinguishable with respect to the distribution P .
372   Similarly, here, we can define a class of DAGs that all are an I-map for the distribution P and satisfy
373   minimal dependence faithfulness, and that potentially any of them can be the true DAG.
374   So far, neither the definition of a minimal dependence nor the minimal dependence faithfulness
375   assumption helps to determine the direction of the edges of the true DAG. This is because faithfulness
376   does not hold and hence an independence between a triple of variables does not necessarily imply an
377   immorality between them in the true DAG. More specifically, we build upon the following result for
      faithful distributions that helps in finding the orientation of undirected edges found in constraint-based

      3.4   R ELATION TO OTHER TYPES OF FAITHFULNESS
452
453   The authors of (Ramsey et al., 2006) relaxed the faithfulness assumption to its implication in terms
454   of the existence of a link. More specifically, they assume adjacency faithfulness, imposing that if
455   nodes X and Y are adjacent in the true DAG G, then they are dependent conditional on any subset
456   of X \ {X, Y }. Similarly, DAG G is said to be minimally Markovian if it is an I-map for P and
457   two nodes are adjacent in G if and only if they are dependent conditioned on every subset of other
458   variables (Sadeghi & Soo, 2022). Both are the special case of Assumption 3 when all minimal
459
      dependencies are of size one. Moreover, the case where a symmetric minimal dependence is of size
      two matches the definition of tripe unfaithfulness in (Marx et al., 2021) and hence is considered
460
      as a special case. In this regard, Assumptions 3 and 4 and Definition 1 and 2 can be considered as
461
      extensions of (2-)adjacency and (2-)orientation faithfulness and the notion of k-association in (Marx
462   et al., 2021), respectively. Given all this, if a DAG is faithful, it is adjacency faithful. If a DAG is
463   adjacency faithful, it is 2-adjacency faithful, and if it is 2-adjacency faithful it is minimal-dependence
464   faithful. So minimal-dependence faithfulness can be considered as a generalization of the adjacency
465   and 2-adjacency faithfulness, and in turn, faithfulness. This, however, comes with the inevitable cost
466   that the set of final candidates for the true DAG increases.
467
      We highlight that the minimal dependence assumption imposed in this paper does not impose
468   minimality on the number of edges and hence is different from existing notions such as causal
469   minimality also known as SGS minimality stating that no proper sub-DAG of the true DAG G is an
470   I-map for P (Spirtes et al., 2000; Zhang & Spirtes, 2008; Neapolitan, 2004). It is also different from
471   P -minimality (Zhang, 2013), stating that no DAG G ′ satisfying I(G) ⊂ I(G ′ ) is an I-map for P .
472   Another different notion is the sparsest Markov representation (SMR) which is an I-map G, such
473   that every other I-map that is not I-equivalent to G has more edges than G (Raskutti & Uhler, 2018).
474   Similarly, an I-map G satisfying the frugality assumption, then it is in the set of SMRs (Forster et al.,
475   2018). Overall, Assumption 3 does not imply any of the aforementioned minimality assumptions: the
476
      DAG in Figure 4-a) satisfies Assumption 3 but the sub-DAG in Figure 3-a) is the true DAG. Whether
      the aforementioned minimality assumptions imply Assumption 3 remains an open problem.

479   4     MD-PC A LGORITHM
480
481   How to perform causal discovery in the face of the unfaithfulness caused by a minimal dependence
482   of size greater than one? A simple way is to iteratively find the Dep sets of all of the variables.
483   However, the PC algorithm can be exploited to reduce the computations. To this end, we develop
484   Algorithm 1, the minimal dependence PC (MD-PC) algorithm. Roughly speaking, the idea is to
485   consider each minimal dependence as a super node and perform the PC algorithm as before. This
      determines the inter-structure of the minimal dependencies (super nodes). The intra-structure of the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      minimal dependencies are then partially determined by Theorem 2 and Corollary 2. This applies only
487   to minimal dependencies of size at least two, resulting in an unfaithfulness, detected according to
488   Proposition 1. The result will be a set of candidate dashed PDAGs, one of which is guaranteed to be
489   the true DAG under minimal-dependence faithfulness, i.e., Assumption 1.
490
      Similar to PC, the algorithm goes through every pair of nodes X and Y . If the two are dependent,
491
      then Y does not belong to a minimal dependence of X of size greater than one, and hence, normal
492
      PC is followed. Namely, if later, the two become conditionally independent, the connecting link is
493   deleted; otherwise, Y is adjacent with X (equivalently, {Y } ∈ Dep(X)). However, if X and Y are
494   independent, we investigate the possibility of Y forming a strong minimal dependence of size at least
495   two with some other nodes. If yes, the minimal dependence is stored and all links between X and
496   the minimal dependence are removed. Otherwise, the link between X and Y is removed. Next, we
497   proceed to mark the minimal dependencies using the minimal orientation rule.
498
499
      Theorem 3 Consider an SCM over variables X , inducing DAG G and joint probability distribution
      P . If G satisfies Assumptions 3 and 4, then Algorithm 1 outputs the MD-equivalent class dashed
500
      PDAG of P .
501
502   Remark 1. Compared to PC, the MD-PC algorithm additionally computes Dep(X) for all X.
503   Although this is not done in a separate process and uses the results of PC, it can result in additional
504   computational burden. However, the computational complexity of the proposed MD-PC and the
505   PC algorithm both equal O(2N ), where N is the number of variables. For sparse true DAGs, the
506   number of operations of the PC algorithm is much less than O(2N ), which may not be the case with
507   the MD-PC algorithm, depending on the dependencies between the variables. If p is the maximum
508   number of parents in the true DAG, the MD-PC algorithm must check CI tests on the maximum
509
      p variables. Hence, the number of CI tests is bounded by O(N p ) which is similar to the bound of
      the number of CI tests for the PC algorithm. So for sparse high-dimensional networks knowing the
510
      maximum number of parents helps to reduce the complexity of the MD-PC algorithm.

513   5    C ONCLUSION
514
515   We addressed the problem of causality detection by focusing on identifying the "true" directed acyclic
516
      graph (DAG) of a causal model from the joint probability distribution of observed variables. Our
      investigation revealed that existing algorithms, such as PC and its modified versions, were unable to
517
      detect certain simple structures that violated the faithfulness assumption. To overcome this limitation,
518
      we introduced the concept of minimal dependence faithfulness, relaxing the faithfulness assumption
519   to account for situations where variables exhibit dependencies that violate traditional faithfulness
520   criteria. We defined minimal dependence as the set of variables on which a given variable depends,
521   even though it may be independent of each individual member of that set. Our analysis revealed that
522   minimal dependencies of size at least two violated faithfulness, prompting us to develop a modified
523   version of the PC algorithm capable of detecting this type of unfaithfulness. This modified algorithm
524   provided sound and complete results, allowing us to identify all possible candidates for the true
525   DAG, even in cases where traditional algorithms failed. Additionally, we characterized the structure
526   of minimal dependencies in the true DAG and showed that they formed v-stars or directed stars in
527
      certain configurations. These insights allowed us to propose a class dashed PDAG representation to
      simplify the representation of the MD-equivalence class. We also discussed the relationship between
528
      minimal dependence faithfulness and other minimality and faithfulness assumptions. Overall, our
529
      findings provide insights into the challenges of causality detection and offer practical solutions to
530   improve the accuracy of causal model inference from observational data.
