009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Differential algebraic equations (DAEs) are pivotal in dynamic optimization across
012            diverse fields, from process control to flight trajectory optimization and epidemi-
013            ological modeling. Traditional methods like single shooting, multiple shooting,
014            and direct transcription effectively optimize known mechanistic models. However,
015            significant challenges arise when the underlying equations are unknown or deviate
016            from empirical data. While black-box optimization strategies can address some
017
               issues, challenges persist regarding data quality, non-linearity, and the inclusion
               of constraints. Recent advances in machine learning, particularly Neural ODEs,
018
               offer promising tools for continuous representation of dynamic systems. This work
019
               bridges the gap between machine learning representations of dynamic systems and
020            optimization methodologies, enabling a novel approach for solving DAEs with
021            data-driven components. We demonstrate this approach using numerical examples
022            of DAE problems and realistic case studies, including biochemical reactor control
023            and disease spread prevention. Our results highlight the efficacy of incorporating
024            Neural ODEs into equation-based solvers, showing improved performance over
025            existing strategies such as SINDy. Additionally, we formalize the optimization
026            program for NN-embedded DAEs and present representations for common neural
027            network architectures (e.g., ReLU, tanh). This work contributes a novel framework
028
               for dynamic system optimization, integrating machine learning advancements with
               traditional optimization techniques, and offers practical insights through compre-
029
               hensive case studies.

036   In almost all areas of engineering and fundamental science, the use of differential equation models
037   are ubiquitous. The re-emergence of neural networks (NN) has included extensive research into how
038   these universal approximators can be particularly powerful in their application to differential equation
039   models. Neural ODEs (or NODEs) are a recent breakthrough in the field of computational science
040
      and scientific machine learning Chen et al. (2018), where in the most basic sense a neural network
      learns a non-linear relationship defining the derivatives of system variables given state and parameter
041
      values. NODEs are very flexible and build on many of the more traditional numerical methods used
042
      for integrating ODEs. Figure 1 shows an example state profile z sampled at various time points t.
043

      or Runge-Kutta, the state at future time points can be computed. In the case of a NODE, the only
      change is that the mechanistic equation is replaced by a NN function. Figure 1 shows the architecture

088   In Eq.4, z, y, and u represent differential, algebraic and state variables respectively, resulting in a
089   DAE model. There has been decades of work focused on solution methodologies particular to these
090   systems of equations. Initial strategies focused on indirect or variational solution methods such as
091
      Pontryagin’s maximum principle Kopp (1962), which can be inefficient for constrained problems.
      Later work Sargent & Sullivan (1978) discretized the control profiles and solved via a sequential
092
      approach, where the objective and constraint functions are evaluated through forward integration and
093
      then gradients with respect to the decision variables are calculated via backwards integration of the
094   adjoint equationsCao et al. (2003). In Bock & Plitt (1984); Bock et al. (2000) and many subsequent
095   works, multiple shooting methods have been explored to address instabilities in single shooting
096   methods. Full discretization of state and control variables creating a large Non Linear Programming
097   (NLP) formulation has been extensively studied as the simultaneous approach Biegler et al. (2002);
098   Nicholson et al. (2018).

100   1.3   M OTIVATION AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
101
102   While we have many tools for equation-based optimization of known dynamic models, there are still
103   many challenges in applications with complex, unknown, or noisey dynamic phenomena. The work
104
      in this manuscript outlines steps to bridging equation-based DAE optimization with ML models such
      as NODEs. Our contributions can be briefly summarized as:
105
106   1. We introduce NODEs as surrogates for constrained dynamic optimization problems with unknown
107   or partially known-models, extending their application to areas previously approached with black-box
      optimization or sparse regression.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2. We implement software extensions and demonstrations for incorporating trained NODE models
109   into optimization platform Pyomo Bynum et al. (2021) with common constraint, objective structures
110   and collocation transformations.
111
112
      3. We present case studies with increasing non-linearity, dimensionality, and feasibility complexity to
      show the strengths and weaknesses in comparison to sparse regression tools.

115   1.4   R ELATED W ORK
116
      Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Systems (SINDy). The goal of SINDy Kaheman et al. (2020)
117
      is to identify the sparse set of terms in a function f that best describe the dynamics of the system
118   from input-output data, given a library of candidate functions (solving successive linear or quadratic
119   programs). If an unknown model is recovered, it can be implemented into an equation based
120   optimizer. Other work has extended the sparse regression techniques to nonlinear programming
121   Wilson & Sahinidis (2017) Cozad et al. (2014). Appendix B gives more details of the method.
122
      Universal Differential Equations (UDEs). UDEs for ML are introduced in Rackauckas et al. (2020)
123
      as part of the SciML software package in Julia. They provide examples of using NN models to
124   extend sparse regression to cases where the underlying model is better represented with a non-linear
125   surrogate. They also provide a suite of software tools for training, optimization and parallelizing code.
126   Some steps of this work for model training/discovery could be done in SciML, but a fully pythonic
127   implementation was maintained for compatibility with existing ML, NLP and DAE tools.
128
      Black-box optimization. Several black-box approaches can be leveraged to solve similar dynamic
129
      optimization problems through sampling or by building model surrogates Amaran et al. (2016);
130   Larson et al. (2019). Appendix D gives details of popular black-box methods and gives numerical
131   results applying these these methodologies to Case Study 1 (Sec. 3.1), to illustrate these methods.

189   In Eq. 16, we recover 2 equations with two unknown variables (z1 ,z2 ). Thus we have an implicit
190   equation for z(t). Several works have developed formal proofs establishing an equivalence to
191   performing fully implicit Runge-Kutta integration of DAE models at Gauss (Radau) points Biegler
192   (2000). Furthermore, collocation on finite elements is a 2K order (2K-1) method which uses K
193
      collocation points and can be shown to be algebraically stable Biegler (2010). The constraints defining
      N N (z, u, W, b) are an important consideration for the ultimate system of non-linear equations,
194
      performance with solvers (i.e. convergence, CPU) and also may have effects in the representation of
195
      the underlying differential mechanism. For the sake of brevity, we put the mathematical details of
196   explored representations in Appendix A, specifically full-space, reduced-space and complementarity
197   formulations. Complementarity can be used to make ReLU NNs continuous, non-linear constraints
198   and eliminate binaries. Full-space can be applied to non-linear activations and will represent all
199   hidden nodes in the NN as explicit variables, whereas the reduced space builds a mapping directly
200   from inputs to outputs resulting in fewer constraints and variables.

512
513   This work demonstrates the integration of NODEs into complex, non-linear optimization problems
514   which are constrained by differential and algebraic equations. The methods bridge optimization
515   in an ML paradigm which focuses on model parameter training and traditional constrained non-
516   linear optimization via collocation on finite elements. We show that the resulting large NLPs are
517   algebraically stable and converge quickly for reduced-space smooth representations in numerical
518   experiments. We include examples with increasing non-linearity, problem dimension, and feasibility
519   complexity to show the strengths and weaknesses in comparison to sparse regression and black-box
520   optimization. Some promising directions for future work include investigating non-linear activation
521
      functions that have had success in other NODE applications (e.g. SiLU). Furthemore, applying our
      work to stochastic optimization problems may give insight into how a NODE-DAE structure can
522
      handle uncertainty in scenarios or parameter values.

1217
1218   Black-box optimization techniques have gained prominence in dynamic systems and control, par-
1219   ticularly when analytical solutions are impractical. Recent advances leverage surrogate models and
1220   machine learning to improve optimization efficiency. For instance, Lye et al. Lye et al. (2021) em-
1221
       ployed deep neural networks for PDE-constrained optimization, introducing new training procedures
       for time-dependent parametric PDEs. Beykal et al. Beykal et al. (2020) developed a data-driven
1222
       optimization algorithm for differential-algebraic equations (DAEs), utilizing surrogate models to
1223
       navigate numerical feasibility boundaries. Building on this approach, they later presented a data-
1224   driven optimization framework for time-varying systems Beykal et al. (2022), which derives optimal
1225   continuous-time control trajectories using surrogate modeling without fully discretizing high-fidelity
1226   models. Similarly, Qiu et al. Qiu et al. (2020) proposed a deep learning-based surrogate to replace
1227   computationally intensive DAE constraints in operational planning.
1228
       Other notable contributions include Moya and Lin’s Moya & Lin (2023) DAE-PINN framework,
1229
       which uses physics-informed neural networks for simulating semi-explicit DAEs. Shokry and
1230   Espuña Shokry & Espuña (2014) introduced a sequential dynamic optimization method using kriging
1231   metamodels for highly nonlinear processes. Deshmukh and Allison Deshmukh & Allison (2017)
1232   presented Derivative Function Surrogate Modeling (DFSM) to approximate state derivatives with
1233   surrogate models, streamlining dynamic system design. These approaches, alongside other surrogate-
1234   assisted optimization techniques Beykal et al. (2022); Djeumou et al. (2022); Del Rio-Chanona
1235   et al. (2019); Safdarnejad et al. (2019); Rall et al. (2020); Kudela & Matousek (2022), highlight
1236   the effectiveness of integrating data-driven methods with physical insights in optimizing dynamic
1237   systems and control.
1238   Alternatively, when the dynamic system is available as a simulator, the optimal control problem can
1239   be directly tackled using data-driven optimization techniques. This approach requires discretizing the
1240   control profile over time and treating these discretized values as decision variables for the optimizer.
1241   However, this can lead to a high-dimensional problem depending on the number of discretization
       points used in the control profile. To compare this method with surrogate-based approaches, we apply


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       this data-driven optimization strategy to Case Study 1. For consistency, we set the number of finite
1243   elements to 10, resulting in 11 discretization points for the control profile u(t) with u(0) = 0. We
1244   evaluate seven different data-driven solvers for this task: Bayesian optimization, genetic algorithm,
1245   particle swarm optimization, and simulated annealing using the package Blanke (2024).
1246
       Since the discretization points are treated as decision variables by the solver, a purely data-driven
1247
       optimization approach can lead to variability in the solution due to the complexity and the non-
1248
       linearity of the search space. To evaluate the performance and consistency of the different solvers,
1249   we conducted ten independent optimization runs for each algorithm. For all solvers, the maximum
1250   number of iterations or samples was set to 3000, utilizing the default settings. Additionally, an early
1251   stopping criterion based on a patience set of 300 iterations was applied to facilitate termination when
1252   appropriate. For each run, we recorded the optimized objective function value JP RED and compared
1253   it to the true optimal value JT RU E obtained from the equation based model. The relative error for
1254   each run was calculated as a normalized measure of the deviation of the predicted optimal value
1255   from the true value, allowing for a fair comparison across different algorithms. We aggregate the
1256   relative errors for all ten runs of each optimizer to compute the mean and standard deviation, which
1257
       reflect the accuracy and robustness of each method, respectively. The results are summarized in

1348   Although the relative errors achieved by these solvers are comparable to our NODE approach, the
1349   data-driven optimization method has several significant limitations. First, data-driven optimization
       approach to determine the control profile requires discretization of the control variables over time,


                                                        25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       which leads to a high-dimensional optimization problem. In many cases, a finer discretization is
1351
       necessary to capture the system dynamics accurately, resulting in an increased number of decision
1352   variables and, consequently, a higher number of samples needed to locate the optimal solution. This
1353   escalates the computational burden, especially for high-fidelity simulations, as it necessitates running
1354   the simulator numerous times, potentially rendering the approach computationally expensive.
1355
       Second, data-driven solvers rely on the availability of the simulation model during the optimization
1356
       process. However, this may not always be feasible; in some scenarios, only static or pre-collected
1357
       data is accessible, which hinders the applicability of these solvers. Without the ability to query the
1358   simulator, the optimization cannot proceed effectively.
1359
1360   Finally, many practical optimization problems involve implicit and explicit constraints that must
1361
       be satisfied. Most data-driven optimization algorithms lack robust mechanisms to incorporate such
       constraints into the optimization process. This limitation makes it challenging to apply these solvers
1362
       to constrained optimization problems, as they may violate critical system requirements or fail to
1363
       find feasible solutions. These challenges highlight the need for alternative approaches that can
1364   operate with limited data availability, and accommodate complex constraints within the optimization
1365   framework.
