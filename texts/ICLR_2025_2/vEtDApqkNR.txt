009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years, Transformers have become the de-facto architecture for long-term
012            sequence forecasting (LTSF), yet they face challenges associated with the self-
013            attention mechanism, including quadratic complexity and permutation invariant
014            bias. This raises an important question: do we truly need the self-attention mecha-
015            nism to establish long-range dependencies in LTSF? Recognizing the significance
016            of causal relationships in multivariate LTSF, we propose MambaTS, which lever-
017            ages causal relationships to model global dependencies across time and variables
018
               through a single linear scan. However, causal graphs are often unknown. To address
               this, we introduce variable-aware scan along time (VAST), which dynamically
019
               discovers variable relationships during training and decodes the optimal variable
020
               scan order by solving the shortest path visiting all nodes problem during inference.
021            MambaTS employs the latest Mamba model as its backbone. We suggest that the
022            causal convolution in Mamba is unnecessary due to the presence of independent
023            variables, leading to the development of the Temporal Mamba Block (TMB). To
024            mitigate model overfitting, we further incorporate a dropout mechanism for selec-
025            tive parameters in TMB. Extensive experiments conducted on eight public datasets
026            demonstrate that MambaTS achieves new state-of-the-art performance.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Long-term time series forecasting (LTSF) has a wide range of applications in various fields, including
032   weather, finance, healthcare, energy and transportation (Lim & Zohren, 2021; Wen et al., 2023;
033
      Qiu et al., 2024). With the rapid advancement of deep learning, the current methods for time
      series prediction have shifted from traditional statistical learning approaches to deep learning-based
034
      methods, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and temporal convolutional neural networks
035
      (TCNs; (Bai et al., 2018; Sen et al., 2019)). Since the introduction of Transformer (Vaswani et al.,
036   2017), Transformer-based methods have emerged as the mainstream LTSF approach (Zhou et al.,
037   2021; Nie et al., 2023), leveraging their self-attention mechanism to effectively capture long-term
038   dependencies in time series data.
039
      Recent studies have identified critical challenges associated with the application of Transformers
040
      in LTSF, primarily attributed to the self-attention mechanism inherent in Transformers. One major
041
      concern is that the self-attention mechanism often suffers from the curse of quadratic complexity,
042   resulting in a computational cost that escalates rapidly with the context length (Wu et al., 2021;
043   Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019). Furthermore, recent research has shown that the performance
044   of Transformer-based LTSF methods does not necessarily improve with an increasing look-back
045   window (Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). This phenomenon may be due to the distracted attention
046   caused by the growing input size (Liu et al., 2024b). Additionally, a recent study titled DLinear (Zeng
047   et al., 2023) has challenged the effectiveness of the permutation-invariant bias of the self-attention
048   mechanism in LTSF, achieving remarkable results that surpass those of most state-of-the-art (SOTA)
049   Transformer-based methods using a simple single-layer feed-forward network.
050   This prompts us to question: do we truly need the self-attention mechanism to establish long-range
051   dependencies in LTSF? To address this, we can discuss the issue in two parts. First, regarding
052   temporal dependencies, since time series data is inherently causal, a single linear complexity is
053   sufficient for modeling. Second, when considering variable dependencies, do we require pairwise
      computations to effectively model these relationships? The answer is no.

054
      Let us establish intuition through a simple example. Consider a causal graph with directed edges
055   representing dependencies among variables, where we aim to predict future outcomes based on these
056   relationships. The causal graph can be represented with edges A → C and B → C. Due to the nature
057   of causality, a linear scan such as A, B, C or B, A, C effectively captures global dependencies. This
058   is because the information from both A and B inherently contributes to predicting C, thus eliminating
059   the need for complex pairwise calculations.
060
      Based on this insight, we propose MambaTS, a novel architecture designed for LTSF that models
061   global dependencies with linear complexity. MambaTS leverages causal graphs to represent the
062   relationships among variables, enabling global dependency modeling through a single linear scan.
063
064
      However, accurately identifying causal relationships among variables in a dataset poses a significant
      challenge. To address this, we introduce Variable-Aware Scan along Time (VAST). VAST estimates
065
      causal relationships during the training phase using a random walk without return approach, and
066
      during the inference phase, it utilizes a shortest path decoding method that visits all nodes (Dréo
067   et al., 2006) to determine the optimal linear scan order.
068
069   We utilize the latest Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) model as our encoder network. As a formidable
070
      competitor to Transformer architectures, Mamba enhances traditional state space model (SSM) (Fu
      et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) by introducing selection mechanisms to filter out
071
      irrelevant information and reset states along. It also incorporates hardware-aware design for efficient
072
      parallel training. Mamba has demonstrated competitive performance compared to Transformers
073   across various domains, offering rapid inference and scalability with sequence length (Dao & Gu,
074   2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b).
075
076
      However, we note that the causal convolution in the original Mamba block can be detrimental when
      scanning independent variables. Consequently, we have removed the local convolution prior to the
077
      SSM, leading to the introduction of the Temporal Mamba Block (TMB). Furthermore, previous
078
      studies have shown that excessive information integration can lead to overfitting (Nie et al., 2023), a
079   phenomenon we also observed during experiments with MambaTS. To mitigate this, we have added a
080   dropout mechanism (Srivastava et al., 2014) for selective parameters in TMB.
081
082
      Gained from the insights and designs, MambaTS achieves efficient modeling of global dependencies
      across time and variables with linear complexity. Extensive experiments on eight popular public
083
      datasets demonstrate that MambaTS achieve SOTA performance in most LSTF tasks and settings.
084
085   Our contributions are as follows:
086
087
            1. We introduce MambaTS, a new time series forecasting model based on selective state
               space models, achieving global dependency modeling through linear scans, supported by
088
               theoretical evidence.
089
090         2. We propose a method for estimating causal relationships between variables during training
091            via random walk without return, and a decoding strategy based on shortest topological
092
               ordering to determine the optimal linear scan sequence during inference.
093         3. We present the Temporal Mamba Block, which avoids entanglement between independent
094            channels by removing the original causal convolution. Additionally, we incorporate a
095            dropout mechanism for selective parameters in TMB to further prevent overfitting.
096         4. Our experimental results achieve SOTA performance across various datasets and settings.

      Long-Term Time Series Forecasting. Traditional LTSF methods leverage the statistical properties
101
      and patterns of time series data for prediction (Box & Pierce, 1970). In recent years, LTSF has shifted
102   towards deep learning approaches, where various neural networks are utilized to capture complex
103   patterns and dependencies, elevating LTSF performance. These methods can be broadly classified into
104   two categories: variable-mixing and variable-independent. Variable-mixing methods employ diverse
105   architectures to model dependencies across time and variables. RNNs (Salinas et al., 2020; Shi et al.,
106   2015; Lai et al., 2018) were initially introduced to LTSF due to the nature of sequence modeling.
107   TCNs, known for their local bias, are effective in capturing local patterns in time series data and have
      shown promising results in LTSF (Bai et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). Transformers

108
      are subsequently introduced to accomplish long-range dependency modeling through self-attention
109   and have become a mainstream method in the Transformer family (Wen et al., 2023). However,
110   due to the quadratic complexity, Transformer-based methods have struggled with optimization
111   efficiency (Zhou et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019).
112   Recently, significant improvements have been made in these methods with the introduction of patch-
113   based techniques (Zhang & Yan, 2023; Nie et al., 2023). MLPs are also commonly used for LTSF and
114   have achieved impressive results with their simple and direct architectures (Ekambaram et al., 2023).
115   Graph neural networks have been utilized to model relationships between variables (Wu et al., 2020).
116   FourierGNN (Yi et al., 2023) represents the entire time series information as a hypervariate graph
117
      and employs Fourier Graph Neural Network for global dependency modeling. On the other hand,
      variable-independent methods focus solely on modeling temporal dependencies under the assumption
118
      of variable independence (Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). These approaches
119
      are known for their simplicity and efficiency, often capable of mitigating model overfitting and
120   achieving remarkable outcomes (Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023). Nevertheless, this assumption
121   may oversimplify the problem and potentially lead to ill-posed scenarios (Zhang & Yan, 2023).
122
123   State Space Models. Recently, some works (Box & Pierce, 1970) have combined SSMs (Kalman,
124   1960) with deep learning and demonstrated significant potential in addressing the long-range de-
125   pendencies problem. However, the prohibitive computation and memory requirements of state
126   representations often hinder their practical applications (Gu et al., 2022). Several efficient variants
127   of SSMs, such as S4 (Gu et al., 2022), H3 (Fu et al., 2023), Gated State Space (Mehta et al., 2023),
128   and RWKV (Peng et al., 2023), have been proposed to enhance model performance and efficiency in
129
      practical tasks. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) addresses a key limitation of traditional SSMs methods by
      introducing a data-dependent selection mechanism based on S4 to efficiently filter specific inputs and
130
      capture long-range context that scales with sequence length. Mamba demonstrates linear-time effi-
131
      ciency in modeling long sequences and surpasses Transformer models in benchmark evaluations (Gu
132   & Dao, 2023).
133
134   Mamba has also been successfully extended to non-sequential data such as image (Liu et al., 2024c;
135
      Zhu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b), point cloud (Liang et al., 2024), table (Ahamed & Cheng,
      2024) and graphs (Wang et al., 2024a; Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024) to enhance its capability in
136
      capturing long-range dependencies. To address the scan order sensitivity of Mamba, some studies
137
      have introduced bidirectional scanning (Zhu et al., 2024), multi-directional scanning (Li et al., 2024;
138   Liu et al., 2024c), and even automatic direction scanning (Huang et al., 2024). However, there is
139   currently limited work considering the issue of variable scan order in temporal problems. To tackle
140   this challenge, we introduce the VAST strategy to further enhance the expressive power of MambaTS.
141   Similar to our approach, Graph-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024a) also proposes a similar permutation
142   strategy to extend context-aware reasoning on graphs. However, it is based on node prioritization,
143   introducing a biased strategy specifically designed for graphs.

174   4   M OTIVATION
175
176   For the multivariate time series forecasting problem, we consider a look-back window of length L for
177   K variables, denoted as (x1 , x2 , · · · , xK ), where each xi ∈ RL represents the values of variable i
178   over the past L time steps. Our objective is to forecast the values of the future T time steps for each
179   variable, denoted as (ŷ1 , ŷ2 , · · · , ŷK ), where each ŷi ∈ RT .
180   Recent studies (Zeng et al., 2023; Nie et al., 2023) have introduced the variable independence
181   assumption, which reformulates the multivariate LTSF into multiple univariate LTSF. However,
182   this assumption can be overly simplistic and may lead to ill-posed scenarios (Zhang & Yan, 2023).
183   In practical applications, multivariate time series often exhibit causal relationships; neglecting
184   these dependencies can result in suboptimal predictive performance. Therefore, modeling variable
185   dependencies remains a dominant approach, particularly exemplified by Transformer-based methods
186   that operate with quadratic complexity in pairwise computations (Liu et al., 2024b). Some researches,
187
      such as Zhang & Yan (2023), seek to mitigate this complexity through a router mechanism that
      aggregates information from all variables prior to redistribution. Nonetheless, these methods still
188
      struggle with efficiency and scalability.
189
190   Given these challenges, we pose the question: Is it possible to achieve global dependency modeling
191   with linear complexity without sacrificing the integrity of variable dependencies? The answer lies in
192   leveraging the structural properties of causal relationships among variables. By representing these
193
      relationships through causal graphs, we can effectively capture the fundamental dependencies that
      drive multivariate interactions. The following theorem formalizes this approach:
194
195   Proposition 1. For a multivariate time series dataset, if a causal graph G = (V, E) exists that
196
      represents the relationships among the variables V = {V1 , V2 , . . . , VK }, then global dependency
      modeling can be achieved through a single linear scan.
197
198
      Proof. A causal graph G is constructed, where V represents the variables and E indicates the causal
199
      relationships. Since G is a directed acyclic graph (DAG), there exists at least one topological order σ
200
      such that for any directed edge (Vi , Vj ), Vi precedes Vj in this order. Following this topological
201   ordering during a linear scan, each variable Vσ(k) can be conditioned on its parents Pa(Vσ(k) ),
202   allowing us to represent dependencies as P (Vσ(k) |Pa(Vσ(k) )). Consequently, the joint distribution
203                                            QK
      of all variables is obtained as P (V) = i=1 P (Vσ(i) |Pa(Vσ(i )). This process demonstrates that a
204
      single linear scan effectively models global dependencies in multivariate time series by leveraging
205   the structural information encoded in the causal graph.
206
207   Despite Proposition 1 demonstrating the potential for global dependency modeling with known causal
208   relationships, real-world datasets often lack explicit dependency among variables. As a result, the
209   underlying causal structure can be obscured by data complexity and interactions. To address this, it is
210   essential to adopt methods for inferring causal relationships without a predefined graph. A promising
211   approach is the random walk (Przymus et al., 2017; Kim et al.), which employs stochastic processes
212   to explore the graph’s structure and estimate causal links based on empirical transition observations.
213   Definition 1 (Random Walk Without Return). A random walk without return is a stochastic
214   process defined on a graph where a walker starts at a node and moves to an adjacent node uniformly
215   at random, with the restriction that it cannot return to previously visited nodes until all nodes have
      been visited.

      5.2    VARIABLE -AWARE S CAN ALONG T IME
309
310   As emphasized in Proposition 1, the sequence of variable scanning is essential for accurately modeling
311   global variable dependencies. However, the relationships among variables are not known a priori.
312   Motivated by Proposition 2, we propose Variable-Aware Scan along Time (VAST). The core idea of
313   VAST is to estimate causal relationships among variables during the training phase through random
314   walks without return, while during inference, it determines the optimal linear scanning order using
315   the shortest paths that traverse all nodes.
316
317   Training. In the training phase, we introduce the variable permutation training (VPT) strategy.
318   Specifically, for the input x = (x1 , x2 , · · · , xK ) ∈ RK×L , we randomly shuffle the order of variables
319   in each iteration to perform a random walk without return. We then revert the shuffled state after
320
      decoding to ensure the correct output sequence.
321   For any K variables, we maintain a directed graph adjacency matrix P ∈ RK×K , where pi,j
322   represents the cost from node i to node j. Notably, with the introduction of VPT, we can explore
323   various combinations of scan orders and evaluate their value using Eq. 5. Following a permutation,
      a node index sequence V = {v1 , v2 , · · · , vK } is obtained, where vk denotes the new index in

526   7   C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this work, we present MambaTS, a innovative multivariate time series forecasting model built
529   upon improved selective SSMs. We emphasize the essential role of causal graphs in LTSF, providing
530   theoretical evidence for their ability to model global dependencies across time and variables through
531   a single linear scan. To identify causal relationships, we propose Variable-Aware Scan along Time
532   (VAST), which dynamically discovers variable interactions during training and utilizes an ATSP
533   solver to determine the optimal variable scan order during inference. To avoid the entanglement
534   of independent variables, we introduce the Temporal Mamba Block (TMB), which removes causal
535   convolution from the original Mamba Block. Additionally, we incorporate dropout regularization for
536
      TMB’s selective parameters to mitigate overfitting and enhance model performance. Gained from
      these insights and designs, MambaTS achieves global dependency modeling with linear complexity
537
      and establishes new SOTA results across multiple datasets and settings.
