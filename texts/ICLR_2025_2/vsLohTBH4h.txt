011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we derive refined generalization bounds for the Deep Ritz Method
014            (DRM) and Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs). For the DRM, we focus
015            on two prototype elliptic partial differential equations (PDEs): Poisson equation
016
               and static Schrödinger equation on the d-dimensional unit hypercube with the
               Neumann boundary condition. Furthermore, sharper generalization bounds are
017
               derived based on the localization techniques under the assumptions that the exact
018
               solutions of the PDEs lie in the Barron spaces or the general Sobolev spaces.
019            For the PINNs, we investigate the general linear second order elliptic PDEs with
020            Dirichlet boundary condition using the local Rademacher complexity in the multi-
021            task learning setting. Finally, we discuss the generalization error in the setting of
022            over-parameterization when solutions of PDEs belong to Barron space.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) play a pivotal role in modeling phenomena across physics,
028   biology and engineering. However, solving PDEs numerically has been a longstanding challenge
029   in scientific computing. Classical numerical methods like finite difference, finite element, finite
030   volume and spectral methods may suffer from the curse of dimensionality when dealing with high-
031   dimensional PDEs. Recent years, the remarkable successes of deep learning in diverse fields like
032   computer vision, natural language processing and reinforcement learning have sparked interest in
033
      applying machine learning techniques to solve various types of PDEs. In fact, the idea of using
      machine learning to solve PDEs dates back to the last century (Lagaris et al., 1998), but it has
034
      recently gained renewed attention due to the significant advancements in hardware technology and
035
      the algorithm development.
036
037   There are numerous methods proposed to solve PDEs using neural networks. One popular method,
038   known as PINNs (Raissi et al., 2019), utilizes neural network to represent the solution and enforces
039
      the neural network to satisfy the PDE constraints, initial conditions and boundary conditions by
      encoding these conditions into the loss function. The flexibility and scalability of the PINNs make
040
      it a widely used framework for addressing PDE-related problems. The Deep Ritz method (Yu et al.,
041
      2018), on the other hand, incorporates the variational formulation into training the neural networks
042   due to the widespread use of the variational formulation in traditional methods. In comparison to
043   PINNs, the form of DRM has a lower derivative order, but the fact that not all PDEs have variational
044   forms limits its applications. Both methods hinge on the approximation ability of the deep neural
045   networks.
046
      The approximation power of feed-forward neural networks (FNNs) with diverse activation func-
047
      tions has been studied for different types of functions, including smooth functions (Lu et al., 2021a),
048   continuous functions (Shen et al., 2022), Sobolev functions (Belomestny et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
049   2023b;a; Yarotsky, 2017), Barron functions (Barron, 1993). It was proven in the last century that
050   a sufficiently large neural network can approximate a target function in a certain function class
051   with any given tolerance. Specifically, it has been shown in Hornik (1991) that the two-layer neu-
052   ral network with ReLU activation function is a universal approximator for continuous functions.
053   More recently, specific approximate rate of neural networks has been shown for different func-
      tion classes in terms of depth and width. Lu et al. (2021a) showed that a ReLU FNN with width

      O(N log N ) and depth O(L log L) can achieve approximation rate O(N −2s/d L−2s/d ) for the func-
055   tion class C s ([0, 1]d ) in the L∞ norm, which is nearly optimal. In the context of applying neural
056   networks to solve PDEs, the focus shifts to the approximation rates in the Sobolev norms. Be-
057   lomestny et al. (2023) utilized multivariate spline to derive the required depth, width, and sparsity
058   of a ReLU2 deep neural network to approximate any Hölder smooth function in Hölder norms with
059   the given approximation error. And the weights of the neural network are also controlled, which
060   is essential to derive generalization error. Yang et al. (2023b) derived the nearly optimal approxi-
061   mation results of deep neural networks in Sobolev spaces with Sobolev norms. Specifically, deep
062   ReLU neural networks with width O(N log N ) and depth O(L log L) can achieve approximation
063   rate O(N −2(n−1)/d L−2(n−1)/d ) for functions in W n,∞ ((0, 1)d ) with W 1,∞ norm. For higher or-
064   der approximation in Sobolev spaces, Yang et al. (2023a) introduced deep super ReLU networks for
065
      approximating functions in Sobolev spaces under Sobolev norms W m,p for m ∈ N with m ≥ 2.
      The optimality was also established by estimating the VC-dimension of the function class consisting
066
      of higher-order derivatives of deep super ReLU networks.
067
068   In this work, we focus on the DRM and PINNs, aiming to derive sharper generalization bounds.
069   Compared to Jiao et al. (2021); Duan et al. (2021b), the localized analysis utilized in this paper
070   leads to improved generalization bounds. We believe that this study provides a unified framework
071
      for deriving generalization bounds for methods that solve PDEs involving machine learning.

073   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
074
075   Deep learning based PDE solvers: Solving high-dimensional PDEs has been a long-standing chal-
076   lenge in scientific computing due to the curse of dimensionality. Inspired by the ability and flexibility
077   of neural networks for representing high dimensional functions, numerous studies have focused on
078   developing efficient deep learning-based PDE solvers. In recent years, the PINNs have emerged as
079   a flexible framework for addressing problems related to PDEs and have achieved impressive results
080
      in numerous tasks. Despite their success, there are areas where further improvements can be made,
      such as developing better optimization targets (Chiu et al., 2022) and neural network architectures
081
      (Ren et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020). Inspired by the use of weak formulation in traditional solvers,
082
      Zang et al. (2020) proposed to solve the weak formulation of PDEs via an adversarial network and
083   the DRM (Yu et al., 2018) trains a neural network to minimize the variational formulations of PDEs.
084
085   Fast rates in machine learning: In statistical learning, the excess risk is expressed as the form
086   ( COMPnn (F ) )α , where n is the sample size, COMPn (F) measures the complexity of the function
087   class F and α ∈ [ 12 , 1] represents the learning rate. The slow learning rate √1n (α = 21 ) can be easily
088   derived by invoking Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002), but achieving the fast
089   rate n1 (α = 1) is much more challenging. Based on localization techniques, the local Rademacher
090   complexity (Bartlett et al., 2005; Koltchinskii, 2006) was introduced to statistical learning and has
091   become a popular tool to derive fast rates. It has been successfully applied across a variety of tasks,
092   like clustering (Li & Liu, 2021), learning kernels (Cortes et al., 2013), multi-task learning (Yousefi
093
      et al., 2018), empirical variance minimization (Belomestny et al., 2017), among others. Variants of
      Rademacher complexity, such as shifted Rademacher complexity (Zhivotovskiy & Hanneke, 2018)
094
      and offset Rademacher complexity (Liang et al., 2015), also offer a potential direction for achieving
095
      the fast rates (Duan et al., 2023; Kanade et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2019). In this paper, our results are
096   based on the localized analysis in Bartlett et al. (2005); Koltchinskii (2006; 2011).
097
098   Generalization bounds for machine learning based PDE solvers: Based on the probabilistic
099
      space filling arguments (Calder, 2019), Shin et al. (2020) demonstrated the consistency of PINNs
      for the linear second order elliptic and parabolic type PDEs. An abstract framework was introduced
100
      in Mishra & Molinaro (2022) and stability properties of the underlying PDEs were leveraged to
101
      derive upper bounds on the generalization error of PINNs. Following similar methods widely used
102   in machine learning for deriving generalization bounds, the convergence rate of PINNs was derived
103   in Jiao et al. (2021) by decomposing the error and estimating related Rademacher complexity. For
104   the DRM, when the solutions are in the spectral Barron space, Lu et al. (2021c) demonstrated the
105   generalization error bounds of two-layer neural networks for solving the Poisson equation and static
106   Schrödinger equation, but in expectation and with the slow rates. When solutions of the PDEs fall in
107   general Sobolev spaces, Duan et al. (2021b) established non-asymptotic convergence rate for DRM
      using a method similar to Jiao et al. (2021). The most relevant work to ours is Lu et al. (2021b),

      which used peeling methods to derive sharper generalization bounds of the DRM and PINNs for the
109   Schrödinger equation on a hypercube with zero Dirichlet boundary condition. However, Lu et al.
110   (2021b) assumed that the function class of neural networks is a subset of H01 , which is challenging
111   to achieve. For the DRM, the peeling method in Lu et al. (2021b) cannot be applied to derive
112   the generalization error of the Poisson equation, as in this scenario, the population loss isn’t the
113   expectation of the empirical loss. For the PINNs, Lu et al. (2021b) required the strong convexity
114   and only considered the static Schrödinger equation with zero Dirichlet boundary condition, but our

      1.2   C ONTRIBUTIONS
118
119         • For the aspect of approximation via neural networks, we show that the functions in B 2 (Ω)
120           can be well approximated in the H 1 norm by two-layer ReLU neural networks with con-
121           trolled weights, and similar results are also presented for functions in B 3 (Ω) in the H 2
122           norm. Compared to the results in Lu et al. (2021c), our approximation rate is faster and
123
              the Barron space in our setting is larger than the spectral Barron space in Lu et al. (2021c).
              Compared with other approximation results for Barron functions (Siegel & Xu, 2022a;
124
              Siegel, 2023), the constant in our result is independent of the dimension.
125
126         • For the DRM, we derive sharper generalization bounds for the Poisson equation and
127           Schrödinger equation with Neumann boundary condition, regardless of whether the so-
128           lutions fall in Barron spaces or Sobolev spaces. Our methods rely on the strongly convex
129           property of the variational form and the localized analysis of Bartlett et al. (2005); Koltchin-
130           skii (2006). However, these methods cannot be applied directly, as for the Poisson equation,
131           the expectation of empirical loss is not equal to the variational formulation. Additionally,
132           for the static Schrödinger equation, the strongly convex property cannot be simply regarded
133           as the Bernstein condition in Bartlett et al. (2005), as the solutions of the PDEs often do
134
              not belong to the function class of neural networks in our setting.
135
            • For the PINNs, we regard this framework as a scenario within multi-task learning (MTL).
136
              At this time, there are two key points: one is that the loss functions are non-negative and the
137           other one is that a non-exact oracle inequality suffices. To achieve our goal, we extend the
138           entropy method to derive a Talagrand-type concentration inequality for MTL, which offers
139           better constants than those provided by Theorem 1 in Yousefi et al. (2018). Consequently,
140           similar results to those in single-task setting can be established, yielding a non-exact or-
141           acle inequality tailored for PINNs. Unlike Lu et al. (2021b), which required the strong
142           convexity, our approach does not impose this requirement. While we have only presented
143           results for the linear second order elliptic equations with Dirichlet boundary conditions,
144           our method can serve as a framework for PINNs for a wide range of PDEs, as well as other
145
              methods that share similar forms with PINNs.
146
            • In the Discussion section, we investigate the complexity of over-parameterized two-layer
147
              neural networks when approximating functions in Barron space, and demonstrate meaning-
148           ful generalization errors in the setting of over-parameterization. Additionally, we discuss
149           other boundary conditions for Deep Ritz Method.

226

229   overcome the curse of dimensionality in this context. Furthermore, when the solutions fall in the
230   general Sobolev spaces, we provide tight generalization bounds through the localization analysis.

      4   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we have refined the generalization bounds for the DRM and PINNs through the local-
489   ization techniques. For the DRM, our attention was centered on the Poisson equation and the static
490   Schrödinger equation on the d-dimensional unit hypercube with Neumann boundary condition. As
491   for the PINNs, our focus shifted to the general linear second elliptic PDEs with Dirichlet boundary
492   condition. Additionally, in both neural networks based approaches for solving PDEs, we considered
493   two scenarios: when the solutions of the PDEs belong to the Barron spaces and when they belong
494   to the Sobolev spaces. Furthermore, we believe that the methodologies established in this paper can
495   be extended to a variety of other methods involving machine learning for solving PDEs.

2872   D.3   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
2873
2874          • In the paper, we have made the assumption that all related functions are bounded, as re-
2875
                quired for the localization analysis. However, these assumptions can sometimes be strict.
                Therefore, it is crucial to investigate settings where the boundedness is not imposed.
2876
2877          • Utilizing ReLU neural networks in the DRM presents optimization challenges due to the
2878            non-differentiability of the ReLU function’s derivative. One potential approach is to em-
2879
                ploy randomized methods to tackle the objective functions, like using random neural net-
                works. Despite this, methods for deriving improved generalization error remain valid under
2880
                stronger assumptions. For instance, when the solutions belong to B 3 (Ω), employing ReLU2
2881
                neural networks allows us to leverage gradient descent or stochastic gradient descent meth-
2882            ods.
2883
              • For the PINNs, the loss functions play a crucial role for solving PDEs. It is worth paying
2884
                more attention to the design of loss functions for different PDEs. Moreover, extending the
2885
                results in Section 3 to other types of PDEs and other PDE solvers involving neural networks
2886            is also a topic for future research.
2887
              • The optimization error is beyond the scope of this paper. Gao et al. (2023); Luo & Yang
2888
                (2020) have considered the optimization error of the two-layer neural networks for the
2889
                PINNs inspired by the work Du et al. (2018). However, it remains open of the optimization
2890            aspect for the DRM.
2891
              • The requirements of the function class of deep neural networks may be impractical.
2892
                Achieving these requirements in practice might be accomplished by restricting the weights
2893
                of the networks, but doing so can make optimization more difficult. Thus, it is worth ex-
2894            ploring whether there are more efficient methods.
2895
              • The solution theory of PDEs in the Barron spaces remains unclear. Lu et al. (2021c) has

                addressed the problem for the Poisson and static Schrödinger equations in the Spectral
2897

2898            mate. As for the Barron spaces, Chen et al. (2023) has studied the regularity of solutions
2899            to the whole-space static Schrödinger equation in B s (Rd ). However, the results of Lu et al.
2900            (2021c) and Chen et al. (2023) do not work for B s (Ω). Despite this, at least, there exists
