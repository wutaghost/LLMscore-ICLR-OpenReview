009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Scaling multi-dimensional transformers to long sequences is indispensable across
012            various domains. However, the challenges of large memory requirements and
013            slow speeds of such sequences necessitate sequence parallelism. All existing
014            approaches fall under the category of embedded sequence parallelism, which are
015            limited to shard along a single sequence dimension, thereby introducing significant
016
               communication overhead. However, the nature of multi-dimensional transformers
               involves independent calculations across multiple sequence dimensions. To this
017
               end, we propose Dynamic Sequence Parallelism (DSP) as a novel abstraction of
018
               sequence parallelism. DSP dynamically switches the parallel dimension among
019            all sequences according to the computation stage with efficient resharding strategy.
020            DSP offers significant reductions in communication costs, adaptability across
021            modules, and ease of implementation with minimal constraints. Experimental
022            evaluations demonstrate DSP’s superiority over state-of-the-art embedded sequence
023            parallelism methods by remarkable throughput improvements ranging from 32.2%
024            to 10x, with at least 50% communication volume reduction.

027
028
      Efficiently scaling multi-dimensional transformers to accommodate long sequences is necessary
029
      across diverse domains, including video generation (Singer et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Ma
030   et al., 2024), image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024), protein
031   structure prediction (Jumper et al., 2021), spatial-temporal information processing (Cong et al., 2021),
032   and beyond. The long length of sequences in these applications entails substantial activation memory
033   costs and a notable slowdown in processing speeds, underscoring the need for employing sequence
034   parallelism.
035
      Current methods for sequence parallelism, such as Megatron-SP (Korthikanti et al., 2022), Megatron-
036   LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019), DeepSpeed-Ulysses (Jacobs et al., 2023), and Ring-Attention (Li et al.,
037   2021; Liu et al., 2023a) are all embedded sequence parallelism methods. As shown in Figure 1,
038   these embedded methods shard along a single sequence dimension, which are tailored to the task-
039   specific pattern of modules and introduce intricate communication and complex code modification.
040   However, multi-dimensional neural networks often operate independent computations across multiple
041   sequence dimensions. For instance, for video generation models like OpenSora (Zangwei Zheng,
042   2024) and Latte (Ma et al., 2024), Spatial-Temporal Attention (Yan et al., 2021) is favored over full
043   Self-Attention (Vaswani et al., 2017), facilitating separate attention computations for temporal and
044
      spatial dimensions.
045   Therefore, there exists an explorable space for a general sequence parallelism abstraction. To adapt to
046   the flexible patterns of multi-dimensional transformers, we introduce Dynamic Sequence Parallelism
047   (DSP) as a novel abstraction of sequence parallelism, characterized by its elegance, high effectiveness,
048   and excellent compatibility with popular transformers. Unlike embedded sequence parallelism, DSP
049   dynamically switches the parallel dimension of sequences during the computation stage with an
050
      efficient resharding strategy, a process completely decoupled from the computation module.
051   DSP offers several advantages over embedded sequence parallelism: 1) Efficient Communication:
052   DSP incurs significantly lower communication costs due to its simplified communication patterns and
053   reduced frequency of exchanges. 2) Adaptability: DSP seamlessly adapts to most modules without
      necessitating specific modifications and imposes few limitations on its usage. 3) Ease of Use: DSP is

141   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
142
143   In this section, we discuss the four main parallelism techniques employed in deep learning: data
144   parallelism, tensor parallelism, pipeline parallelism, and sequence parallelism.
145   Data parallelism (Hillis & Steele Jr, 1986; Li et al., 2020) is one of the most widely adopted parallelism
146   techniques. The input data is partitioned across devices, each processing a subset. Model parameters
147   are replicated, and gradients are summed. ZeRO (Rajbhandari et al., 2019; 2021) optimizes memory
148   by partitioning parameters, states, and gradients across devices, enabling the training of larger models.
149   Tensor parallelism (Shazeer et al., 2018; Shoeybi et al., 2019), or model parallelism, partitions
150   model parameters across devices. Different model parts are assigned to different devices. Pipeline
151   parallelism (Huang et al., 2019; Narayanan et al., 2019; Li & Hoefler, 2021; Liu et al., 2023b)
152
      partitions the model into stages executed in parallel across devices. Activations are passed between
      devices in a pipeline style.
153
154   Unlike parameter parallelism discussed earlier, sequence parallelism is a technique specifically
155   designed for distributing long sequences and activation across multiple devices. Here are three main
156   methods of sequence parallelism:
157   Ring Attention (Li et al., 2021) employs an innovative approach to partitioning the sequence di-
158   mension using a ring-style peer-to-peer (P2P) communication pattern to transfer keys and values
159   across GPUs. (Liu et al., 2023a) enhance this method with an online softmax mechanism, allow-
160   ing for the computation of attention scores without retaining the full sequence length. However,
161   Ring Attention’s reliance on P2P communication can be less efficient in high-latency environments.
      Megatron-SP (Korthikanti et al., 2022) further optimizes activation usage in the attention based on

      3             DYNAMIC S EQUENCE PARALLELISM
193
194
      3.1             OVERVIEW
195
196   In the realm of multi-dimensional transformers, computation occurs independently for each se-
197   quence dimension. To harness this inherent feature, we introduce Dynamic Sequence Parallelism
198   (DSP), an efficient, adaptive and ease-of-use sequence parallelism abstraction for multi-dimensional
199   transformers.
200   To ensure correct computation logic with sequence parallelism, embedded methods typically require
201   complex and time-consuming communications within computation modules to change the parallel
202   dimension. However, as illustrated in Figure 3, the key feature of DSP is its dynamic switch
203   of parallel dimension at the intersections of computation stages. By resharding only between
204   computation stages dynamically, rather than within them, this approach allows DSP to remain
205   independent of the computation logic within the module. Therefore, DSP eliminates numerous
206   unnecessary communications within modules, and is able to utilize efficient all-to-all operations to
207   switch parallelism dimensions for the intermediate sequence.
208   For operations involving all sequence dimensions, including the beginning and end of the model, DSP
209   handles them by split and gather operation. Furthermore, we also propose a high-level, user-friendly
210   implementation of DSP compatible with all distributed frameworks based on PyTorch, details in
211   Appendix A.4.

      3.4   A DAPTABILITY AND F LEXIBILITY
274
275   Given its decoupling from the computation of modules, DSP exhibits remarkable adaptability, making
276   it compatible with a wide array of transformer variants such as Cross Attention (Hertz et al., 2022; Ma
277   et al., 2024); specialized kernels like FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022); special attention mechanisms
278   including multi-query attention (Shazeer, 2019) and grouped-query attention (Ainslie et al., 2023);
279   and even beyond like Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) and RWKV (Peng et al., 2023). This inherent
280   flexibility enables DSP to seamlessly integrate into diverse transformers without specific modification.
281
      Furthermore, while DeepSpeed-Ulysses and Megatron-SP necessitate attention head splitting, DSP’s
      scalability is significantly better because it shards on sequence length, which much larger especially
282
      when scaling sequences.
283
284   Moreover, DSP’s adaptability extends beyond module compatibility to encompass various parallelism
285   methodologies. From conventional data parallelism to more sophisticated approaches like ZeRO and
286   pipeline parallelism, DSP effortlessly integrates with diverse parallel computing paradigms, thereby
287
      enhancing scalability and performance across distributed computing environments.
288   As shown in Appendix A.4, we demonstrate the API usage of DSP. By calling just four functions
289   without knowing the detailed implementation, DSP can be enabled on PyTorch and is compatible
290   with various distributed frameworks, including FSDP (Zhao et al., 2023), Accelerate (Gugger et al.,
291   2022), DeepSpeed (Rasley et al., 2020), and Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019).

303   4.1   C OMMUNICATION A NALYSIS
304
      The primary advantage of DSP lies in its ability to minimize communication costs and enable
305
      scalable communication operations. DSP exploits the inherent characteristics of multi-dimensional
306   transformers to eliminate unnecessary communication, compared with embedded approaches such as
307   Megatron-LM (Shoeybi et al., 2019), Megatron-SP (Korthikanti et al., 2022), RingAttention (Liu
308   et al., 2023a) and DeepSpeed-Ulysses (Jacobs et al., 2023). Consider an activation size of M and a
309   sequence parallel size of N . In a 2D-Transformer, there is one transformer block for each sequence
310   dimension per layer, resulting in two transformer blocks per layer (two 1D blocks). More details of
311   the implementation of each method are demonstrated in Appendix A.2.
312   Both Megatron-SP and DeepSpeed-Ulysses require transforming sequence parallelism from a
313   sequence-shard to a head-shard layout. Megatron-SP employs 2 all-gather operations to aggre-
314   gate the entire sequence and 2 reduce-scatter operations to distribute results in the attention and
315   MLP layers for one transformer block. This results in a total of 8 collective communication opera-
316   tions, leading to a total per-device communication volume of 8M . Conversely, DeepSpeed-Ulysses
317   incurs 4 communication operations in temporal block for the query, key, value, and output layout
318   transformations. Consequently, the communication volume transmitted per device for an all-to-all
319   communication of size M across N GPUs is 4M/N . Ring-Attention needs to communicate the
320
      entire key and value in the temporal as well, resulting in a total communication volume of 2M .
321   In contrast, DSP mitigates communication cost by employing only two all-to-all operations in total
322   two blocks per layer. As shown in Table 4.1, it reduces the communication volume to 2M/N ,
323   significantly outperforming other sequence parallelism techniques. Notably, with merely two all-to-
      all operations, DSP exhibits efficient scalability even in super-large clusters for training and inference

      4.2   M EMORY A NALYSIS
342
      Regarding activation memory, since we shard every tensor in the transformer, we are theoretically
343   able to achieve the minimum activation cost, similar to DeepSpeed-Ulysses and Ring Attention. In
344   practice, however, our approach requires less shape transformation and communication overhead,
345   allowing us to further reduce intermediate activation memory compared to other methods. Megatron-
346   SP, on the other hand, needs to hold the entire activation after the all-gather operation, resulting in
347   higher memory requirements for hosting the activation.
348
      As for parameter memory, as discussed in Section 3.4, DSP is compatible with most parameter
349   parallelism strategies. In this analysis, we utilize the ZeRO technique (Rajbhandari et al., 2019) to
350   evenly shard all parameters across different devices. Consequently, our parameter memory footprint
351   can be kept low.

406   5.2                                    S CALING A BILITY
407
408
409   This section evaluates the scaling ability of DSP from two perspectives: weak scaling and strong
410   scaling. Weak scaling refers to scenarios where the computational workload per device remains
411
      constant while incrementally increasing the number of devices. This setup is analogous to the training
      stage, where the goal is to scale longer sequences over more GPUs. Strong scaling, on the other
412
      hand, is more challenging as it requires keeping the total computational workload constant while
413
      incrementally increasing the number of devices. In this case, the computation becomes more sparse
414   on each device. Strong scaling is often employed when the objective is to infer an input sequence
415   rapidly across many GPUs for low-latency applications. The experiments are divided into intra-node
416   and inter-node evaluations due to the different interconnection conditions. Intra-node experiments
417   leverage NVLink interconnect for communication, while inter-node experiments utilize a combination
418   of NVLink and InfiniBand interconnect. More details can be found in Appendix A.3.3.
419
      Weak Scaling. In the weak scaling experiments, to maintain a consistent computational workload for
420   each GPU, the batch size is linearly increased proportional to the number of GPUs, while the sequence
421   length is fixed. As shown in Figures 6, DSP significantly outperforms other methods by more than
422   80.7%. Moreover, DSP can scale up to 64 GPUs without being limited by the number of attention
423   heads, unlike DeepSpeed-Ulysses and Megatron-SP. Despite scaling to 64 GPUs, DSP maintains
424   an almost linear throughput increase, with only a 15% performance loss from 8 GPUs to 64 GPUs.
425   Additionally, DSP can achieve super-linear scaling for intra-node due to efficient communication.
426   Strong Scaling. In the strong scaling experiments, both batch size and sequence length are fixed.
427   As shown in Figure 7, DSP can maintain linear scalability when scaling up to 8 GPUs for 720M
428   model and 4 GPUs for 3B model, which covers most practical scenarios. To evaluate the extreme
429   performance capabilities of DSP, we further scale up to 64 GPUs with very little workload per device.
430   Although there is an inevitable performance drop, DSP’s throughput remains significantly better than
431   the baselines. As shown in Figure 8, our work can significantly reduce inference latency compared
      with baselines with the same workload.

      6                           C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
466
467   In this work, we introduce Dynamic Sequence Parallelism (DSP), a novel sequence parallel abstraction
468
      for effectively scaling multi-dimensional transformers to long sequences. Unlike current embedded
      sequence parallel methods that only shard on single sequence dimension and are tailored to specific
469
      patterns, DSP offers a general and elegant solution by dynamically switching the parallel dimension
470
      during computation, decoupled from the computation module.
471
472   The key advantages of DSP are: 1) substantially reduced communication costs, 2) adaptability across
473   modules without specialized modifications, and 3) remarkable ease of implementation enabled by
474
      a simple high-level API. Our experiments demonstrated DSP’s superiority, achieving from 32.2%
      to 10x higher end-to-end throughput and at least 75% lower communication volume compared to
475
      state-of-the-art methods. Its elegance and ease of use make it a promising solution for efficient
476
      sequence parallelism across a wide range of applications.
477
478   Limitations. One limitation of this work is that DSP is specifically designed for multi-dimensional
479   transformers and may not adapt well to single-dimensional ones like language models. Additionally,
480   while there are global operations that involve all sequence dimensions, which are rare in transformer,
481
      DSP may not be of optimal efficiency.
482   Future works. In the future, DSP could expand its scope beyond transformer architectures to
483   architectures including convolution, recurrent, and graph neural networks to utilize its potential across
484   various tasks. Furthermore, automated optimization techniques could enable DSP to dynamically and
485   autonomously determine the most effective switching strategy based on network analysis, thereby
      optimizing overall system efficiency and efficacy.

      A     A PPENDIX

704
      A.1     M ODEL D ETAILS
705
706   In the theoretical analyses and evaluation section, we use a Transformer-2D model as our base model,
707   similar to OpenSora (Zangwei Zheng, 2024). However, it is not exactly OpenSora; we have removed
708   its specific cross-attention module to ensure that the performance can be generalized to other models.
709   Therefore, in each layer, there are only two transformer blocks that process two sequence dimensions
710   separately, as shown in Figure 10. Specifically, the two dimensions are temporal t and spatial s for a
711   sequence. Each dimension is processed by a corresponding transformer block, which is a common
712
      strategy in many applications.
713
714   A.2     PARALLELISM I MPLEMENTATION
715
      In Figure 10, we demonstrate the detailed implementation of different sequence parallel methods on
716
      2D-Transformer. The implementation of DeepSpeed-Ulysses (Rasley et al., 2020) is directly adopted
717
      from the official OpenSora (Zangwei Zheng, 2024) implementation, while Megatron-SP (Korthikanti
718   et al., 2022) is adopted based on its official implementation. For Ring-Attention (Liu et al., 2023a),
719   we adopt an unofficial implementation for the 2D-Transformer.
720
721
      Megatron-SP employs four resource-intensive collective communication operations per transformer
      block. Specifically, it initiates an AllGather operation to aggregate the entire input x, succeeded by
722
      ReduceScatter operations at the output for both attention and MLP modules, culminating in a total
723
      communication volume of 8M for one layer (two 1D blocks). Note that the communication volume
724   is calculated per device.
725
726   Similarly, Megatron-LM employs 2 all-reduce per transformer block, culminating 4 all-reduces, in a
727
      total communication volume of 8M .
728   DeepSpeed-Ulysses adopts the more efficient AlltoAll approach. It leverages all-to-all for query, key,
729   value to transform their shard dimension before attention, and a all-to-all for output after attention.
730   And it only need to communicate in temporal transformer block. Consequently, the communication
731   volume transmitted per device for an AlltoAll communication of size M across N GPUs is 4M/N .
732   Ring-Attention is not shown in the figure because it does not require resharding. We implement
733   sequence communication in the temporal transformer as the time axis is split. In the attention module,
