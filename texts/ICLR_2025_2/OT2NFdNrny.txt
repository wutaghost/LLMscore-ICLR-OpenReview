009                                                 Abstract
010
011              Diffusion models (DMs) have achieved significant success in generative modeling,
012              but their iterative denoising process is computationally expensive. Training-free
013              samplers, such as DPM-Solver, accelerate this process through gradient estimation-
014              based numerical iterations. However, the mechanisms behind this acceleration
015              remain insufficiently understood. In this paper, we demonstrate gradient estimation-
016              based iterations enhance the denoising process by effectively reducing the con-
017
                 ditional entropy of reverse transition distribution. Building on this analysis, we
                 introduce streamlined denoising iterations for DMs that optimize conditional en-
018
                 tropy in score-integral estimation to improve the denoising iterations. Experiments
019
                 on benchmark pre-trained models validate our theoretical insights, demonstrat-
020              ing that numerical iterations based on conditional entropy reduction improve the
021              reverse denoising diffusion process of DMs. The code will be available.

024   1     Introduction
025
026   It is well established that diffusion models (DMs) (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song
027   et al., 2021b) have achieved significant success across various generative tasks, including image
028   synthesis and editing (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Meng et al., 2022), text-to-image synthesis (Ramesh
029
      et al., 2022), voice synthesis (Chen et al., 2021), and video generation (Ho et al., 2022). DMs consist
      of a forward diffusion process and a reverse denoising diffusion process. In the forward process,
030
      Gaussian noise is progressively injected into the data, perturbing the data distribution to collapse
031
      towards a standard Gaussian distribution by increasing conditional entropy. During training, the
032   neural network is tasked with learning to reverse this process by minimizing the loss between the
033   predicted and injected noise. Once the model is well-trained, high-quality samples can be synthesized
034   by simulating the reverse-time denoising process associated with the forward noise-adding process.
035
      However, a key limitation of DMs is the slow sequential nature of their iterative denoising process
036
      (Song et al., 2021a). To overcome this, training-free methods aim to accelerate denoising process by
037
      efficient numerical iterative algorithms without requiring additional training or costly optimization.
038   Many of these methods focus on reformulating the denoising process as the solution of an ODE,
039   allowing for accelerated sampling through numerical techniques. Such examples include PNDM (Liu
040   et al., 2022), EDM Karras et al. (2022), DPM-Solver (Lu et al., 2022a), DEIS (Zhang & Chen, 2023),
041   UniPC (Zhao et al., 2024), and DPM-Solver-v3 (Zheng et al., 2023a).
042
      Despite the success of these numerical discretization techniques, the underlying mechanisms driving
043
      their acceleration remain inadequately understood. In particular, the reasons why iterations with
044   similar orders of convergence result in varying levels of acceleration are not well explored. To address
045   this gap, we reexamine the principles driving the accelerated denoising process. Our conditional
046   entropy-based analysis reveals that effective iterations systematically reduce the conditional entropy
047   of denoising transition distributions at each step, thereby directly contributing to a faster denois-
048   ing process. This insight clarifies the mechanisms of gradient-based acceleration and provides a
049   foundation for designing efficient denoising algorithms. Our main contributions are as follows:
050          • We introduce a novel perspective on entropy reduction in denoising diffusion of DMs,
051             demonstrating that gradient estimation-based iterations significantly accelerate the denois-
052             ing process by effectively reducing conditional entropy. Our theoretical analysis further
053             reveals that denoising iterations using data-prediction parameterization are more effective
                than those using noise-prediction parameterization in minimizing conditional entropy.

      3.2 Conditional Entropy Reduction in Denoising Iterations
163
164
      Iterative Uncertainty Reduction: Theoretical Insights. The semi-linear solution formula in
      Remark 1 provides a structured theoretical framework for analyzing the denoising diffusion process.
165
      By iteratively solving this formula, DMs refine noisy latent states closer to the data distribution.
166
      From an information-theoretic perspective, each iteration progressively reduces uncertainty from
167   intermediate representations by leveraging the structured denoising mechanism. This uncertainty
168   reduction can be formalized through the concept of mutual information between consecutive states
169   Jaynes (1957):
170                                         I p (xti ; xti+1 ) = H p (xti ) − H p (xti |xti+1 ),                    (3.8)
171   where H p (xti ) is the entropy of state xti , and H p (xti |xti+1 ) is the conditional entropy of xti given xti+1 .
172   The conditional entropy H p (xti |xti+1 ) quantifies the uncertainty in xti after incorporating information
173   from the subsequent state xti+1 . A lower H p (xti |xti+1 ) indicates that the method effectively utilizes
174   information from xti+1 to refine the estimate of xti , driving the estimate of xti closer to the target
175   data distribution. Practically, this conditional entropy reduction aligns with the goal of minimizing
176
      reconstruction error during denoising, improving the quality of generated samples. This theoretical
      insight not only elucidates the uncertainty reduction mechanism but also provides an optimization
177
      criterion for improving the denoising process.
178
      Conditional Entropy in Gaussian Approximations. In practical implementations of DMs Ho
179   et al. (2020); Song et al. (2021b), the reverse transition distribution p(xti |xti+1 , x0 ) is commonly
180   approximated as a Gaussian distribution under the Markov assumption. For brevity, p(xti |xti+1 , x0 ) is
181   often abbreviated as p(xti |xti+1 ). Then, this reverse transition distribution can be expressed as
182                                    p(xti |xti+1 ) := p(xti |xti+1 , x0 ) ≈ N(µti , Σti ),                       (3.9)
183
      where µti and Σti are derived using Bayes’ rule from the forward diffusion process. This Gaussian
184
      approximation is widely used for simplifying model training and theoretical analysis, despite potential
185   deviations at extreme steps, as noted in prior works Song et al. (2021b); Luo (2022); Bao et al. (2022);
186   Karras et al. (2022). Under this approximation, the conditional entropy H p (xti |xti+1 ) simplifies to
187
                                                        d                     1
188                               H p (xti |xti+1 ) ≈ (log 2π + 1) + log |Var(xti |xti+1 )|,                      (3.10)
                                                        2                     2
189
      where d is the dimensionality of x, and Var(xti |xti+1 ) is the conditional variance. This expression
190
      provides a tractable framework for analyzing conditional entropy reduction during the denoising
191   iteration, as it establishes a direct relationship between conditional entropy and the variance. Note
192   that the conditional entropy H p (xti |xti+1 ) is intrinsically tied to the conditional variance Var(xti |xti+1 ):
193
                                                H p (xti |xti+1 ) ∝ log |Var(xti |xti+1 )|.                       (3.11)
194
      Thus, Eq. (3.11) suggests that minimizing variance directly optimizes conditional entropy reduction.

451
452   In this section, we experimentally validate our approach in both single-step and multi-step scenarios,
453   demonstrating that variance-driven conditional entropy reduction improves the denoising process
454
      of pre-trained diffusion model in both pixel and latent spaces. This method effectively extends
      the capabilities of existing training-free ODE samplers without incurring additional computational
455
      overhead. We compare Algorithm 1 against the baseline methods on Stable Diffusion, as illustrated
456
      in Figure 7. More implementation details and additional results are provided in Appendix D.

458   5.1 Single-step Iterations
459   In the single-step iterations, we adopt DPM-Solver Lu et al. (2022a) as our baseline, focusing
460   on denoising iterations based on noise prediction parameterization. Each step of the single-step
461   mechanism only requires information from the starting point and prior to the endpoint. To ensure
462   variance reduction in each step, we configure the step size ratio ri following the effective interval
463   defined in Proposition 4.1 and γi as specified in Proposition 3.2. As a specific instance of RE-
464   based iterations (Proposition 3.3), DPM-Solver has demonstrated its effectiveness over traditional
465
      gradient-based iterations. We further validate RE-based iterations through experiments on CIFAR-10
      Krizhevsky (2009), CelebA 64 Liu et al. (2015), and ImageNet 256 Deng et al. (2009), comparing
466
      them against solvers such as DDPM Ho et al. (2020), DDIM Song et al. (2021a), and Analytic-DDPM
467
      Bao et al. (2022). Results (Figures 2, 3, and 1) consistently show improved performance due to
468   improved variance reduction. On CIFAR-10, the RE-based iteration achieves a 3.15 FID with only
469   84 NFEs, surpassing DDPM’s Ho et al. (2020) 3.17 FID with 1000 NFEs, improving quality while
470   realizing approximately 10× acceleration. Additional comparisons are provided in Figure 4.
471
472   5.2 Multi-step Iterations
473   In the multi-step iterations, we primarily adopt DPM-Solver++ Lu et al. (2022b) as our baseline,
474   focusing on denoising iterations based on data-prediction parameterization. Leveraging the multi-
475   step mechanism enables us to utilize marginally more model information compared to single-step
476   approaches. This allows us to optimize conditional variance through actual state differences, thereby
477
      circumventing the limitations imposed by prior-like variance assumptions. To demonstrate the
      effectiveness of efficient conditional entropy reduction in improving the denoising process of DMs,
478
      we propose Algorithm 1, which improves denoising diffusion sampling by leveraging the variance-
479
      driven approach aimed at minimizing actual state differences. Notably, DPM-Solver-v3 Zheng
480   et al. (2023b) recently introduced a novel optimization-based parameterization scheme, distinct from
481   data-prediction and noise-prediction parameterizations, achieving impressive sampling performance,
482   particularly on CIFAR-10. Therefore, we adopt DPM-Solver-v3 as our baseline method for CIFAR-10
483   experiments, considering its demonstrated advantages in optimized parameterization on this dataset.
484   We evaluated the RE-based iterations against widely-recognized benchmark solvers, including
485   DPM-Solver++ Lu et al. (2022b), DEIS Zhang & Chen (2023), UniPC Zhao et al. (2024), and DPM-
      Solver-v3 Zheng et al. (2023a) on both CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 256 datasets. The experimental

      Conclusions
531
      In this paper, we introduce a novel framework that leverages variance-driven conditional entropy
532
      reduction to improve the sampling performance of pre-trained diffusion model without the need for
533
      retraining. Our theoretical analysis establishes that minimizing conditional entropy in the reverse
534   process of diffusion models leads to more accurate and efficient denoising, which provides a principled
535   foundation for optimizing this process. Building on these insights, we propose a Reduced Entropy
536   (RE) approach for sampling of diffusion models, which improves the denoising process through
537   efficient conditional variance minimization. Our method achieves state-of-the-art performance across
538   multiple benchmark training-free methods, demonstrating promising improvements in both sampling
539   speed and generation quality. While our approach yields promising results in image generation tasks,
      the full potential of conditional entropy reduction-based sampling methods remains to be explored.

704   A    Related Work
705
706
      A.1 Diffusion Models
707   The mathematical foundation of Diffusion models (DMs) originates from stochastic differential
708   equations (SDEs), particularly the Langevin dynamics Langevin et al. (1908). This framework was
709   first adapted to deep generative modeling in the work of Sohl-Dickstein et al. Sohl-Dickstein et al.
710
      (2015) through a Markov chain approach based on non-equilibrium thermodynamics. Significant
      advances in sample quality were achieved by Song and Ermon Song & Ermon (2019; 2020) through
711
      score-based generative models, introducing effective methods to estimate and sample from the score
712
      function ∇x log p(x) of data distributions. The introduction of DDPMs by Ho et al. Ho et al.
713   (2020) provided a significant simplification by parameterizing the reverse process and optimizing
714   a variational bound, establishing a stable training objective: L = Et,ϵ ∥ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t)∥2 . Subsequently,
715   Song et al. Song et al. (2021b) unified score-based models and DMs under a continuous-time SDE
716   formulation, providing a rigorous mathematical foundation.
717   Building upon this theoretical framework, diffusion models have demonstrated exceptional capabilities
718   across various domains. In image synthesis, they have achieved state-of-the-art quality Dhariwal &
719   Nichol (2021) and established new benchmarks in photorealism Karras et al. (2022). Their success
720   has extended to multimodal generation tasks, including text-to-image synthesis Ramesh et al. (2022);
721
      Saharia et al. (2022), speech generation Chen et al. (2021), video synthesis Ho et al. (2022), and 3D
      content generation Poole et al. (2023). Furthermore, DMs have shown remarkable capabilities in
722
      controllable generation tasks Zhang et al. (2023), such as image editing, style transfer, and inpainting
723
      Meng et al. (2022); Lugmayr et al. (2022).
724
725   A.2 Training-based Fast Sampling Methods
726   Training-based sampling methods improve the efficiency of diffusion models through learning-
727   based optimization of sampling trajectories. Knowledge distillation techniques facilitate efficient
728   sampling, where Progressive Distillation Salimans & Ho (2022) enables student models to learn
729   compressed sampling processes from teacher models. Consistency-based methods establish another
730   fundamental direction, with Consistency Models Song et al. (2023); Song & Dhariwal (2024)
731   achieving parallel generation through score function learning via consistency training, grounded in
732   probability flow ODE frameworks. Rectified Flow Liu et al. (2023) formulates a rectified flow ODE
733   that enhances sampling efficiency with the paired training strategy. Latent Consistency Models Luo
734
      et al. (2023) extend consistency distillation to latent space, maintaining generation quality while
      accelerating sampling through the integration of latent diffusion and consistency training. Additional
735
      developments encompass architectural improvements and training optimizations Nichol & Dhariwal
736
      (2021); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Zhang & Chen (2021).
737   Despite the acceleration benefits, these approaches typically necessitate specialized training pro-
738   cedures and careful balancing of quality-speed trade-offs. The training data for these methods are
739   typically obtained through iterative sampling from pre-trained DMs using deterministic samplers like
740   DDIM Song et al. (2021a).
741
742   A.3 Training-free sampling methods
743   In contrast, training-free methods focus on improving sampling efficiency without requiring any
744   additional training, making them more adaptable and flexible in nature. Early sampling methods
745   in DMs relied on ancestral sampling Ho et al. (2020). Score-based models Song et al. (2021b)
746   used predictor-corrector methods to refine samples and introduced PF ODEs as a faster sampling
747
      alternative. DDIM Song et al. (2021a) advanced sampling methods by introducing a non-Markovian
      deterministic process that enables deterministic sampling through a variance-minimizing path, signifi-
748
      cantly reducing the number of required sampling steps. PNDM Liu et al. (2022) demonstrated the
749
      adaptability of ODE solvers to diffusion sampling by effectively utilizing linear multistep methods.
750   EDM Karras et al. (2022) explored the design space of DMs with a σ-parameterization linked to
751   the SNR, analyzing noise dynamics to optimize time steps and achieved high-quality samples using
752   the Heun solver. DPM-Solver Lu et al. (2022a) introduced a generalized acceleration framework
753   leveraging favorable properties of PF ODEs in the semi-log-SNR space, with high-order solvers
754   for accelerated sampling. DEIS Zhang & Chen (2023) investigated the effectiveness of exponential
755   integrators in addressing the stiffness of diffusion ODEs. DPM-Solver++ Lu et al. (2022b) extended
      DPM-Solver to guided sampling by using data-based parameterization, further improved efficiency.

      Based on DPM-Solver, UniPC Zhao et al. (2024) designed high-order predictor-corrector schemes
757   within a unified framework and demonstrated their strengths experimentally. Furthermore, DPM-
758   Solver-v3 Zheng et al. (2023a) proposed an optimized ODE parameterization using empirical model
759   statistics (EMS), which significantly enhanced the sampling efficiency of DMs.
760   Despite significant progress in the numerical discretization techniques of training-free methods, the
761   underlying mechanisms driving their acceleration remain inadequately understood. This motivates
762   our study, which seeks to unify efficient numerical iterations, such as DPM-Solver and EDM, through
763   the lens of conditional entropy reduction.

1088   D.4 Evaluating Sampling Efficiency and Image Quality in Generative Models
1089   The Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) Heusel et al. (2017) evaluates the quality and diversity of
1090   generated images by comparing the statistical distributions of generated and real images in a feature
1091   space. It uses a pre-trained Inception-v3 network to extract features Szegedy et al. (2016), computing
1092   the mean µ and covariance Σ for both distributions. Specifically, µg and Σg represent the mean and
1093   covariance of features from generated images, while µr and Σr correspond to real images. Specifically,
1094   FID is calculated as:
1095                               FID = ∥µg − µr ∥2 + Tr Σg + Σr − 2(Σg · Σr )1/2 .                    (D.4)
1096   Lower FID values indicate higher similarity between generated and real distributions, reflecting better
1097   image quality Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2021b); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021).
1098   The Number of Function Evaluations (NFE) measures computational efficiency by counting neural
1099   network function calls during sampling Song et al. (2021b;a); Bao et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2022a);
1100   Karras et al. (2022). Lower NFE values indicate faster sampling.
1101   Balancing FID and NFE is crucial for practical applications where both high-quality outputs and
1102
       computational efficiency are required. Joint evaluation of these metrics provides a comprehensive
       perspective: FID assesses distribution fidelity, while NFE evaluates algorithmic efficiency.
1103
       In this paper, we adopt the evaluation framework used in prior studies Lu et al. (2022a;b), combining
1104
       FID and NFE to jointly assess the quality of generated images and the computational efficiency of
1105   sampling algorithms. This comprehensive approach, validated in several studies Dhariwal & Nichol
1106   (2021); Song et al. (2021b); Lu et al. (2022a); Karras et al. (2022), offers a standardized benchmark
1107   for comparing different generative models and sampling methods.

       To validate the effectiveness of Algorithm 1 in improving the denoising diffusion process, we
1333   conducted a comprehensive ablation study. We systematically compared various gradient estimation-
1334   based denoising sampling methods on ImageNet-256, a high-dimensional and complex dataset, using
1335   a baseline pre-trained model Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), employing multiple random seeds and
1336   varying guidance scales, as detailed in Table 6. To ensure fairness, the evaluation followed the

1341

1344   solvers, DPM-Solver-v3 differs from DPM-Solver++ and UniPC because it requires the extraction of

1346   categorize the three gradient-based sampling methods into two types for the purpose of discussing our
1347   improvements. Compared to DPM-Solver++ and UniPC, our proposed Algorithm 1 (RE-2 method),
1348   using both the log-SNR prior and the refined prior ri achieves consistently significant improvements,
