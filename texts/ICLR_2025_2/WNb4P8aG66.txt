                                                 A BSTRACT
030
               Conventional class-guided diffusion models generally succeed in generating im-
031
               ages with correct semantic content, but often struggle with texture details. This
032
               limitation stems from the usage of class priors, which only provide coarse and
033            limited conditional information. To address this issue, we propose Diffusion on
034            Diffusion (DoD), an innovative multi-stage generation framework that first ex-
035            tracts visual priors from previously generated samples, then provides rich guid-
036            ance for the diffusion model leveraging visual priors from the early stages of
037            diffusion sampling. Specifically, we introduce a latent embedding module that
038            employs a compression-reconstruction approach to discard redundant detail infor-
039            mation from the conditional samples in each stage, retaining only the semantic
040            information for guidance. We evaluate DoD on the popular ImageNet-256 × 256
041
               dataset, reducing 7× training cost compared to SiT and DiT with even better per-
               formance in terms of the FID-50K score. Our largest model DoD-XL achieves an
042
               FID-50K score of 1.83 with only 1 million training steps, which surpasses other
043
               state-of-the-art methods without bells and whistles during inference.

046   1   I NTRODUCTION
047
048   Diffusion models have emerged as a paradigm-shifting approach in visual content generation em-
049   ploying an innovative process of iterative noise-to-data transformation. Trained to reverse a gradual
050   noising process, these models leverage deep neural networks to generate high-quality new samples
051   that faithfully represent the training data distribution. Diffusion models have surpassed previous
052   state-of-the-art generative frameworks, such as GANs (Sauer et al., 2022; Goodfellow et al., 2014;
053   2020) and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), offering superior sample quality, improved training
      stability, and enhanced scalability. This superiority of diffusion models has led to their widespread

      Conventionally, class-guided diffusion models generate images conditioned on learned class embed-
079   dings. While this embedding-based approach is widely adopted, such class prior can only provide
080   coarse-grained conditional information for models, which is only able to distinguish different cate-
081   gories. The challenge of constructing detailed images from such limited priors has led to exception-
082   ally long training cycles for current class-guided diffusion models. For example, DiT (Peebles &
083   Xie, 2023) and SiT (Ma et al., 2024) require up to 7 million training steps to achieve convergence.
084
      In contrast, visual priors contain more geometric visual information. Intuitively, visual priors should
085   be closer to the target distribution of image generation.The efficacy of visual priors in enhancing
086   image generation quality has been demonstrated in various domains, including super-resolution
087   models (Yang et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2024) and SD-Edit (Meng et al., 2021). Inspired by this
088   well-established methodology, our study seeks to explore the integration of visual priors into class-
089   guided image generation models. To this end, we propose an innovative multi-stage diffusion sam-
090   pling framework. The initial stage adheres to the conventional approach, utilizing fixed class em-
091   beddings as priors. However, the subsequent refinement stages employ the image generated in the
092   previous stage as a visual prior to guide further image synthesis. Our framework’s distinctive feature
093
      lies in the reuse of the same diffusion model across multiple stages, leading us to term this method
      “Diffusion on Diffusion” (DoD).
094
095   The proposed Diffusion on Diffusion (DoD) framework introduces a recurrent approach, where each
096   stage comprises a complete diffusion sampling procedure. From the second stage, DoD extracts se-
097   mantic information from the previous output as additional visual priors. This mechanism provides
098   rich semantic visual guidance during the early stages of diffusion sampling, facilitating the genera-
099
      tion of higher-quality images. By repeatedly leveraging the generation capabilities of the diffusion
      model, DoD not only enhances texture details but also refines the object-level geometric. Although
100
      longer sampling steps in diffusion models typically provide more accurate approximations and po-
101
      tentially boost performance, the final performance is still constrained by the model capacity. In con-
102   trast, our method, by incorporating visual priors in extended sampling steps, effectively improves
103   both the sampling efficiency and generation quality. As shown in Figure 2 (Right), the proposed
104   paradigm yields more efficient sampling compared to simply increasing the sample steps in diffu-
105   sion models. Moreover, as illustrated in Figure 2 (Left), DoD is also parameter-efficient. Unlike
106   models such as SDXL (Podell et al., 2023) which employs a separate refiner model, DoD integrates
107   both image generation and refinement using shared parameters, significantly reducing the model
      size and lowering the barrier to real-world applications.

      Directly using the generated latents as conditions results in the model collapsing into a complete
109   reconstruction of the input samples, thereby losing the desired refinement effect. To overcome this
110   issue, we introduce a Latent Embedding Module (LEM), a vision transformer, that compresses the
111   conditional sample into a few low-dimensional vectors, thereby discarding redundant texture details
112   while retaining the essential information. Our investigation shows that the kept information primar-
113   ily consists of semantics, which are aligned between the training dataset and the generated samples
114   for a well-trained generation model. The semantic alignment eliminates the need for collecting spe-
115   cialized refinement data or implementing complex training strategies, allowing DoD to be trained
116   end-to-end on generation datasets.
117   DoD is built upon the state-of-the-art diffusion transformer, FiTv2 (Wang et al., 2024), and follows
118   the latent diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) training paradigm. We conduct compre-
119   hensive experiments and strictly evaluate our proposed method on ImageNet-256 × 256 benchmark.
120   Compared with the DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023) and SiT (Ma et al., 2024) models, our DoD achieves
121   even better performance in terms of FID, with fewer model parameters and computational complex-
122   ity, consuming 7× less training cost. Meanwhile, our method outperforms the previous state-of-the-
123
      art methods when no bells and whistles were applied during inference, which achieves an FID-50K
      score of 1.83 with only 1 million training steps.

128   2.1   D IFFUSIONS AND F LOWS
129
      Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2022; Radford
130
      et al., 2021; Croitoru et al., 2023; Bond-Taylor et al., 2021) and score-based models (Hyvärinen &
131
      Dayan, 2005; Song et al., 2020b) have demonstrated significant advancements in image generation
132   tasks (Lu et al., 2024b; Ling et al., 2024; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Meng et al.,
133   2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023; Poole et al., 2022). The Denoising Diffusion Implicit
134   Model (DDIM) (Song et al., 2020a) introduced an accelerated sampling method, while Latent Diffu-
135   sion Models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022) set a new standard by applying deep generative models
136   to reverse the noise process in the latent space using Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma &
137   Welling, 2013). Flow models (Liu et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Lipman et al.,
138   2022; Albergo et al., 2023) present an alternative approach by learning a neural ordinary differential
139   equation (ODE) that transports between two distributions. The rectified flow model (Liu et al., 2023)
140
      solves a nonlinear least squares optimization problem to learn mappings along straight paths, which
      represent the shortest distance between two points, leading to improved computational efficiency. In
141
      this work, we adopt the rectified flow as the noise scheduler to train our DoD models.

      2.2   D IFFUSION T RANSFORMER
144
145   The Transformer model (Vaswani et al., 2017) has successfully replaced domain-specific architec-
146   tures across various fields, including language (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023), vision
147   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Han et al., 2022), and multi-modal learning (Team et al., 2023). In the
148   realm of visual perception research, numerous studies (Touvron et al., 2019; 2021; Liu et al., 2021;
149   2022) have focused on accelerating pretraining by utilizing fixed, low-resolution images. Transform-
150   ers have also been applied in denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020) for image
151   synthesis. DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023), a pioneering work in this space, employs a vision transformer
152   as the backbone for latent diffusion models (LDMs), serving as a strong baseline for subsequent
153
      research. MDT (Gao et al., 2023) introduces a masked latent modeling approach, requiring two
      forward passes during training and inference. U-ViT (Bao et al., 2023) tokenizes all inputs and inte-
154
      grates U-Net architectures into the ViT backbone of LDMs. SiT (Ma et al., 2024), utilizing the same
155
      architecture as DiT, explores various rectified flow configurations. Large-DiT and Flag-DiT (Gao
156   et al., 2024) scale up diffusion transformers to achieve improved performance. SD3 (Esser et al.,
157   2024a) introduces novel noise samplers for rectified flow models and scales these models to billions
158   of parameters, yielding state-of-the-art text-to-image generation results. FiTv2 (Wang et al., 2024),
159   based on FiT (Lu et al., 2024a), achieves advanced class-conditional image generation performance
160   by leveraging a flexible diffusion transformer architecture within a rectified flow framework. In this
161   work, built upon the FiTv2 architecture, we propose Diffusion on Diffusion (DoD), an innovative
      framework which effectively incorporates visual priors into class-guided image generation.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
165
166   Diffusion and Flow Models. Before introducing our Diffusion on Diffusion (DoD) framework, we
167   provide a brief review of diffusion and flow models. Given the noise distribution ϵ ∼ N (0, I) and
168   data distribution x0 ∼ p(x), the models use the time-dependent forward process:
169
                                                xt = αt x0 + βt ϵ,                                        (1)
170
171   where αt is a decreasing function of t and βt is an increasing function of t. In this unified perspec-
172   tive, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b; Song & Ermon, 2019; 2020) set αt and
173
      βt based on stochastic differential equation (SDE) formulations, where DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) is
      equivalent to variance preserving SDE (VP-SDE) and SMLD (Song & Ermon, 2019; 2020) corre-
174
      sponds to variance exploding SDE (VE-SDE). DDIM (Song et al., 2020a) sets αt and βt through
175
      ordinary differential equations (ODE), which leads to fewer sampling steps but sacrifices the gen-
176   eration quality. Flow models (Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden,
177   2022; Albergo et al., 2023) restrict the process 1 on t ∈ [0, 1], and set α0 = β1 = 1, α1 = β0 = 0,
178   interpolating between the two distributions through ODE formulation.
179
      Rectified flow (Liu et al., 2023) introduces an ODE model and transports between the data dis-
180
      tribution and the noise distribution via a straight line path, which is the theoretically shortest route
181
      between two points. Given empirical observations X0 ∼ p(x), X1 ∼ N (0, I), the forward process 1
182   is defined as: Xt := tX1 + (1 − t)X0 , which is the linear interpolation of X0 and X1 .
183
184   The ODE model learns the drift at time t ∈ [0, 1]:
185                                             dXt = v(Xt , t)dt,                                        (2)
186
187
      which converts data X0 from p(x) to noise X1 from π1 , and the drift follows the direction of (X1 −
      X0 ). In practice, a network vθ is utilized to predict this drift, and the optimization target is:
188
                                         Z 1 h
189                                                                            i
190                                 min       E ||(X1 − X0 ) − v(Xt , t)||2 dt                           (3)
                                     v    0
191
192   Previous studies (Ma et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024) have
193   demonstrated the efficiency and stability of rectified flow models. In this work, we fully adhere to
194   the form of rectified flow as our noise scheduler and enhance the diffusion model by adding extra
195   conditions beyond the class label. We adopt the implementation of rectified flow following SiT (Ma
196   et al., 2024) and use ODE sampler for image synthesis.
197   FiTv2. DoD utilizes the state-of-the-art diffusion transformer, FiTv2 (Wang et al., 2024), as the
198   backbone. FiTv2 is an advanced diffusion transformer on class-guided image generation, evolving
199   from SiT (Ma et al., 2024) and FiT (Lu et al., 2024a). The key modules of FiTv2 include 2-D Rotary
200   Positional Embedding (2-D RoPE) (Su et al., 2024), Swish-Gated Linear Unit (SwiGLU) (Shazeer,
201   2020), Query-Key Vector Normalization (QK-Norm), and Adaptive Layer Normalization with Low-
202   Rank Adaptation (AdaLN-LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022).
203   Despite these advanced modules, FiTv2 adopts the Logit-Normal sampling (Esser et al., 2024b)
204   strategy to accelerate the model convergence. This sampling strategy puts more attention on the
205   middle part of the sampling process, as recent studies (Karras et al., 2022; Chen, 2023) have dis-
206   closed that the intermediate part is the most challenging part of the diffusion process.

208   3.2   D IFFUSION ON D IFFUSION WITH V ISUAL P RIORS
209
210   We introduce the Diffusion on Diffusion (DoD) framework, which enhances diffusion models by
211   recurrently incorporating previously generated samples as visual priors to guide the subsequent sam-
212
      pling process. There are two core components in DoD: the Multi-Stage Sampling strategy and the
      Latent Embedding Module (LEM). The multi-stage sampling enables the use of visual priors, while
213
      the LEM extracts semantic information from the samples generated in the previous stage.
214

      3.3   C ONFIGURATIONS
299
300   Training. DoD is trained within the latent space of a pre-trained Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
301   from Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022). The VAE encoder maps RGB images of shape 256 ×
302   256 × 3 to latents of shape 32 × 32 × 4. The new latents sampled by DoD are then decoded back to
303   pixel space by the VAE decoder.
304
      As mentioned above, the Latent Embedding Module (LEM) extracts semantic information from
305
      samples through compression and reconstruction to serve as visual priors. We reasonably assume
306   that the high-level semantic information extracted from generated images is similar to that obtained
307   from real images. This assumption allows us to use the latents of ground truth images as inputs
308   to LDM during training, simplifying the training strategy. Such simplification allows end-to-end
309   training of DoD on image latents and joint optimization of the backbone model and LEM. Similar
310   to training with classifier-free guidance, we randomly replace the output of the latent embedding
311   module with a trainable sample token S at a probability of ps , which is set to 0.5 by default.
312   Sampling. Classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2021) is well-known for enhancing
313   generation quality and improving the alignment between conditions and generated images. For
314   visual priors, we employ a large CFG scale to ensure the consistency of the generated samples,
315   while we do not use CFG in the first stage of DoD. Additionally, DoD employs the adaptive-step
316   ODE sampler (i.e.., dopri5) same as SiT (Ma et al., 2024) for sample synthesis in each stage.
317
      Models. We use three different model configurations with varying sizes, DoD-{S, B, XL}, as de-
318   tailed in Table 1. For the backbone network, i.e., FiTv2, we closely follow the configurations out-
319   lined in their paper but reduce the model depth to match those of DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023) and
320   SiT (Ma et al., 2024). For the latent embedding module, we use an 8-layer encoder and a 4-layer
321   decoder by default. Our largest model, DoD-XL, uses the same size latent embedding module as
322   DoD-B to reduce the number of parameters. The patch size for both the backbone network and the
323   latent embedding module is set to 2, and the channel dimension de of the latent tokens in the latent
      embedding module is set to 16.

343   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
344
345   Implementation Details. We conduct experiments on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), using a res-
346   olution of 256 × 256. All models share the same training strategy. We use the AdamW (Kingma,
347   2014; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer with a constant learning rate of 1 × 10−4 and without
348
      weight decay. Models are trained with a batch size of 256. Following SiT and FiTv2, we also apply
      an exponential moving average (EMA) with a decay factor of 0.999 to the model weights during
349
      training, and we report the performance of the EMA checkpoints.
350
351   Evaluation Metrics. We use Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) as the primary
352   evaluation metric. Inception Score (IS) (Salimans et al., 2016), sFID (Nash et al., 2021), improved
353   Precision and Recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) are also included to holistically evaluate the gen-
354
      eration quality. Moreover, the inference of DoD involves two processes – sampling with class priors
      and sampling with visual priors. Since the latter process is formally similar to image reconstruc-
355
      tion, we also adopt reconstruction-FID (rFID), PSNR, and SSIM to assess the reconstruction quality,
356
      providing a more comprehensive evaluation of our method.

415   backbone diffusion model and the latent embedding module concurrently. The training process is
416   controlled by a hyperparameter ps , which dictates the probability that the ground truth image priors
417   are not utilized. The results of generation and reconstruction are detailed in Table 3. A higher
418   value of ps leads to improved generation performance, while a lower value is more beneficial for

420   state-of-the-art performance, we simply set ps = 0.5 as the default value.
421   Additionally, the joint training of DoD is based on the assumption that images of varying fidelity can
422   provide similar visual priors through compression. We validate this assumption in Figure 4 (Right),

424   However, stage 3, which relies on stage 2, does not show further improvements, indicating that
425   the poor outputs from stage 1 and the good outputs from stage 2 provide similar prior information.

532
533
      In this work, we propose using visual priors to further enhance diffusion models. To achieve this, we
534   introduce Diffusion on Diffusion (DoD), a novel multi-stage generation framework that provides rich
535   guidance for the diffusion model by leveraging visual priors from previously generated samples. The
536   latent embedding module in DoD effectively extracts semantic information from generated samples
537   through a compression-reconstruction approach. DoD demonstrates remarkable training and sam-
538   pling efficiency while being easy to reproduce. We conduct extensive experiments to validate the
539   potential of injecting visual priors and explore the design space of the model. We hope our work
      will inspire the community to further investigate the use of visual priors in image generation.

      A.1    T ECHNOLOGIES ADOPTED BY F I T V 2
759
760   FiTv2 Wang et al. (2024) is an advanced diffusion transformer on class-guided image generation,
761   evolving from SiT (Ma et al., 2024) and FiT (Lu et al., 2024a). The key modules of FiTv2 in-
762   clude 2-D Rotary Positional Embedding (2-D RoPE) (Su et al., 2024), Swish-Gated Linear Unit
763   (SwiGLU) (Shazeer, 2020), Query-Key Vector Normalization (QK-Norm), and Adaptive Layer Nor-
764   malization with Low-Rank Adaptation (AdaLN-LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022).
765   2-D RoPE. FiTv2 follows FiT and adopts 2-D RoPE as its positional embedding. RoPE applys a
766   rotary transformation to the embedding, incorporating both absolute and relative positional informa-
767   tion into the query and key vectors. Benefiting from such property, RoPE and its high-dimensional
768   variants have been widely adopted in current vision diffusion transformer models (Lu et al., 2024a;
769   Gao et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024b).
770   SwiGLU. SwiGLU is widely used in advanced language models like LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
771   2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024). FiTv2 utilizes SwiGLU module as its Feed-forward Neural Network
772   (FFN), rather than normal Multi-layer Perception (MLP). The SwiGLU is defined as:
773
                                      SwiGLU(x, W, V ) = SiLU(xW ) ⊗ (xV )
774                                                                                                               (8)
775                                       FFN(x) = SwiGLU(x, W1 , W2 )W3
776
777   QK-Norm. FiTv2 applies LayerNorm (LN) to the Query (Q) and Key (K) vectors before the at-
778   tention calculation. This technique effectively stabilizes the training process, particularly during the
779
      mixed-precision setting, as well as slightly improves the performance. Formally, the attention is
      calculated as:
780                                                1
781                                     Softmax( LN(Qi )LN (Ki )T ).                                       (9)
                                                  dk
782
783   AdaLN-LoRA. FiTv2 utilizes AdaLN-LoRa to reduce the too many parameters occupied by the
784   original AdaLN module in each transformer block. Besides, a global AdaLN module is employed
785   to extract overlapping condition information and reduce the information redundancy in each block.
786
      Let S i = [β1i , β2i , γ1i , γ2i , α1i , α2i ] ∈ R6×d denote the tuple of all output scale and shift parameters,
      c ∈ Rd and t ∈ Rd represent the embedding for class and time step respectively. In FiTv2 blocks,

792   simplicity.
793
      Logit-Normal Sampling. Despite these advanced modules, FiTv2 adopts the Logit-Normal sam-
794   pling (Esser et al., 2024b) strategy to accelerate the model convergence. Normally, rectified flow
795   models sample times uniformly from the [0, 1] interval, which means each part of the noise sched-
796   uler is trained equally. FiTv2 samples timesepts from a logit-normal distribution (Atchison & Shen,
797   1980), which is formulated as:

      disclosed that the intermediate part is the most challenging part in diffusion process.
