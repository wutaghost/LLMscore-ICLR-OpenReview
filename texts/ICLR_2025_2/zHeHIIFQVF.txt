009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Quantum state preparation forms an essential cornerstone of quantum informa-
012
               tion science and quantum algorithms. Designing efficient and scalable methods
013            for approximate state preparation on near-term quantum devices remains a signif-
014            icant challenge, with worst-case hardness results compounding this difficulty. In
015            this work, we propose a deep reinforcement learning framework for quantum state
016            preparation, capable of immediate inference of arbitrary stabilizer states at a fixed
017            system size post a training phase. Our approach scales substantially beyond previ-
018            ous works by leveraging a novel reward function. In our experiments on stabilizer
019            states up to nine qubits, our trained agent successfully prepares nearly all previ-
020            ously unseen states, despite being trained on less than 10−3 % of the state space
021
               – demonstrating significant generalization to novel states. Benchmarking shows
               our model produces stabilizer circuits with size 60% that of existing algorithms,
022
               setting a new state of the art in circuit efficiency. Furthermore, we show that
023
               this performance advantage is consistent across states with varying entanglement
024            content. We also analyze the rate of increase of entanglement entropy across the
025            prepared circuit, obtaining insight into the quantum entanglement dynamics gen-
026            erated by our trained agent. Finally, we prove our agent generalizes to (almost)
027            the entire space of stabilizer states.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   At the heart of quantum information processing are quantum bits or qubits that can exist in arbitrary
033   superpositions owing to the coherence properties of a quantum device. An increase in the number of
034   qubits leads to an exponential increase in the complexity of the many-body state: preparing a gen-
035   eral state of n qubits efficiently on a quantum processor (called quantum state preparation, or QSP)
036   remains a daunting task. The precise problem is as follows: given access to a target state |ψ⟩, a set
037   of allowed gate operations, and restrictions on qubit connectivity, can we come up with an (efficient)
038   algorithm for a circuit-level construction of the state? The problem is of fundamental importance,
039
      being an essential primitive in the majority of modern quantum algorithms. QSP plays a major role
      in the Harrow–Hassidim–Lloyd (HHL) algorithm (Harrow et al., 2009)P        for solving linear systems,
040
      where a state preparation procedure is used to prepare quantum state         bi |i⟩ from classical unit
041
      vector b. HHL in turn underpins many quantum machine learning (QML) algorithms (Biamonte
042   et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2021). Quantum error correction (QEC), an essential ingredient in the real-
043   ization of large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computers (Preskill, 2018), requires the efficient state
044   preparation of logical code states (Gottesman, 2009). Apart from these, QSP also finds application
045   in studying phase transitions and the ground state physics of many-body Hamiltonians (Lin & Tong,
046   2020; Dong et al., 2022).
047
      A key challenge in the current landscape of quantum technology is the development of efficient
048   state preparation methods suitable for noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices (Preskill,
049   2018). Many quantum algorithms that claim speedups in terms of oracle complexity rely heavily on
050   state preparation through oracle calls (Aaronson, 2015). To achieve a practical quantum advantage,
051   particularly in the NISQ era, efficient implementation of these oracles is crucial. Furthermore, due
052   to limited coherence times and gate inaccuracies, current quantum hardware can only support a
053   few thousand quantum gates (Preskill, 2018). As such, despite the theoretical worst-case hardness
      results, it remains critical to identify and develop practical state preparation protocols.

054
      In recent years, deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has emerged as a powerful tool for solving
055   search problems in complex state spaces. It has shown promise in solving general design problems,
056   e.g. for combinatorial optimization (Dai et al., 2018), chip design (Mirhoseini et al., 2021) and even
057   theorem proving (Wu et al., 2021). It is straightforward to model state preparation as a sequential
058   prediction problem. An agent incrementally pieces together a circuit, adding an allowed quantum
059   gate at each step, until the output of the circuit is (close to) the state of interest. The quantum system
060   typically starts from a fiducial state |ψ0 ⟩. This brings us to our central line of inquiry.
061             Can deep reinforcement learning offer a scalable and efficient solution to QSP?
062
063
      There has been much work on using DRL for state preparation. However, scaling to many qubits
      generally poses a challenge for current approaches owing to an exponentially increasing search
064
      space of possible circuits (Schneider et al., 2023). For this reason, much previous work is limited
065
      to states with a few qubits, or to states that are known to be realizable with a circuit of small size
066   (He et al., 2021; Gabor et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Kolle et al., 2024). A different, but arguably
067   more critical problem is that many existing approaches (Schneider et al., 2023; Zen et al., 2024)
068   require re-training for each choice of target state, which makes them usable only for the discovery
069   of more efficient circuits for particular states of interest, not as a primitive that can replace an existing
070   heuristic for preparing arbitrary states. An agent that does not need re-training to prepare unseen
071   states will be called zero-shot in this work, to emphasize successful generalization to states not seen
072   during training.
073   Taking a step to address these challenges, in this work we develop a reinforcement learning-based
074   method to prepare arbitrary stabilizer states at a specified system size, gate-set and qubit connec-
075   tivity. By focusing on the rich subset of stabilizer states, we are able to scale our method to the
076   preparation of systems of up to 9 qubits. Our method lends itself to zero-shot agents: the training
077   phase only needs to happen once for a given connectivity graph and gate set. Post the training phase,
078   an arbitrary n-qubit state |ψ⟩ belonging to the class of interest may be prepared just by providing
079   the agent a classical description of the target state |ψ⟩. To achieve this scaling in a sample-efficient
080
      manner, we motivate and analyze the novel class of moving-goalpost reward (MGR) functions.
081   Another important contribution of this work is the style of benchmarking state preparation agents.
082   Apart from measuring circuit sizes of the output circuits and preparing states used in error-correcting
083   codes, we examine the effect of entanglement on the produced circuits. We use brickwork circuits
084   (Fig. 3(a)) to generate states with varying entanglement content and test the performance of the
085   agent. Further, we analyze the entanglement dynamics of the agent during circuit preparation, lead-
086
      ing to insights about the speed of preparation and redundancy in the produced circuits. The third
      important contribution is that of provable generalization: we show that our agents generalize to at
087
      least 95% of the state space, despite being trained on less than 10−13 -10−3 % of the state space.
088
089   The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. 2, we first provide a short introduction on relevant aspects
090   of quantum computation and reinforcement learning. After a discussion of previous work in Sec.
091   3, we move on to describe our proposal and novel reward function in detail in Sec. 4. Finally, the
092
      various experiments in Sec. 5 provide a deeper analysis of the performance of the trained agents.

      2.1   Q UANTUM C OMPUTATION AND S TABILIZER C IRCUITS
098
      The state of a single qubit is described by a unit vector |ψ⟩ in its Hilbert space H ∼       = C2 . We
099
      write |ψ⟩ = a |0⟩ + b |1⟩, where a, b ∈ C and {|0⟩ , |1⟩} is a fixed orthonormal basis for H. A
100   general n-qubit state is a linear combination of the 2n basis states |z⟩ = ⊗ni=1 |zi ⟩ ∈ H⊗n with
101   zi ∈ {0, 1}. The fidelity between two quantum states is F(ψ, ϕ) := |⟨ψ|ϕ⟩|2 . A state is called
102   entangled if it cannot  be written as a tensor product of single-qubit states, for instance the Bell state
                       √
103   (|00⟩ + |11⟩) / 2. We quantify the entanglement content of a pure state |ψ⟩ through the bipartite
104   entanglement entropy: given any bi-partition A ∪ B, we define S(|ψ⟩AB ) := −tr(ρA log ρA ), with
105   ρA := TrB (|ψ⟩AB ⟨ψ|AB ) where TrB denotes the partial trace over subsystem B. In this work, we
106   restrict to the half-chain entanglement entropy by choosing the bipartition A = {1 ≤ i ≤ n/2}. We
107   point the reader to Nielsen & Chuang (2010) and App. A for more details on the theory of quantum
      circuits.

      Stabilizer states are a restricted yet important class of quantum states described in a group-theoretic
109   fashion as the common +1-eigenspace of an Abelian sub-group of Pauli Operators, rendering them
110   classically simulable (Aaronson & Gottesman, 2004). They admit an equivalent representation as
111   states that can be reached from the all-zeros state |0⟩ using Clifford circuits, i.e. unitaries that are
112   a combination of H, S and CNOT gates. Stabilizer states have immense use in the exploration of
113   quantum information (Webb, 2016; Huang et al., 2020) and are also crucial for quantum error cor-
114   rection (QEC) (Gottesman, 1997; Nielsen & Chuang, 2010; Campbell et al., 2017; Ryan-Anderson
115   et al., 2021). They are also applied beyond to measurement-based quantum computing (Raussendorf
116   & Briegel, 2001; Patil & Guha, 2023), quantum-classical hybrid algorithms (Cheng et al., 2022; Ravi
117
      et al., 2022) and many-body physics (Sun et al., 2024).
118   Despite classical simulability, preparing stabilizer states optimally remains a challenge. It is known
                                                                                               2
119   (Aaronson & Gottesman, 2004) that any stabilizer state can be prepared using O n /log n gates,
120   and that this bound is asymptotically tight. Quadratic circuit size coupled with the fact that the
                                                2
121   number of Clifford states grows as 2O(n ) makes the search for optimal circuits difficult. Known
122   optimal circuits have been limited to 6 qubits (Bravyi et al., 2022). Further, the (anti-)commutation
123   and self-inverse properties of Clifford gates make it harder to reason about locally greedy search
124   steps.

247   4.2   M OVING -G OALPOST R EWARD (MGR) FUNCTIONS
248
249   In this section, we first discuss issues with typical reward functions used for state preparation, and
250   use an experimental insight to derive a novel reward function. We finish the section with details of
251   the precise reward function used in our experiments.
252   In quantum state preparation, a typical choice of reward function is the fidelity to the target
253   Φ(s) := F(s, |ψ⟩), that is, r(si , ai , si+1 ) = Φ(si+1 ). In our reverse-preparation, the last term
254   is F(si+1 , |0⟩). However, we find that this reward does not learn – the cumulative reward obtained
255   from this reward function does not reflect maximum final fidelity, which is our true goal. The agent
256   might, for example, learn to stay √ close to the target without actually terminating the episode, e.g.
257   an agent in state (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/ 2 can apply a CNOT gate repeatedly, always staying at a fidelity
258   of 1/2 to target state |00⟩; this is optimal for the agent. Indeed, we observe precisely this style of
259   behavior in Fig. 2.
260   Another choice of reward is the incremental fidelity r(si , ai , si+1 ) = γΦ(si+1 ) − Φ(si ) (here γ is
261   the discount parameter). It has been shown to work well for preparing a particular state, but we find
262   that it does not learn to prepare arbitrary states in our framework (see Fig. 2).
263
      We attribute the failure of this reward to learn to two reasons. Firstly, a gate (H, S, HSH or CNOT)
264   applied to a state |ψi ⟩ is very likely not to increase the fidelity to |0⟩. We experimented with 1000
265   uniformly random 6-qubit states, applying every one of 48 gates induced from our gateset to each
266   state. We found that 83.4% of the actions yielded no change in fidelity, 10.0% reduced fidelity and
267   only 6.6% improved it. The fidelity to the target across the optimal preparation of many states is
268   not monotonic and involves sections where the fidelity decreases; the incremental reward penalizes
269   these steps, possibly discouraging the agent from re-trying the same actions. We try to address these
      shortcomings by rewarding an agent suitably for an increase in fidelity, but not penalizing the agent


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      as much for an equal/smaller fidelity. Notice that it is unwise to reward the agent at every step with
271   an increase in fidelity – an optimal policy would simply be to drop fidelity, increase it, and repeat till
272   the episode is truncated. Such policies are allowed by the self-inverse nature of the quantum gates
273   used. Thus, we choose to reward the agent only when fidelity surpasses all previous fidelities seen
274   so far. The MGR reward function follows naturally.

350   tanglement content. We further contrast the entanglement dynamics generated by random stabilizer
351   circuits with the dynamics generated by our trained agents. The probe for studying the entanglement
352   dynamics is the canonical half-chain entanglement entropy (as defined in Sec. 2.1). We compare the
353   performance of our RL model with two other methods (Aaronson & Gottesman, 2004; Bravyi et al.,
354   2021) for arbitrary stabilizer state preparation. We note that both these methods use the Pauli gates
355   X, Y , Z, H, S, controlled-NOT and SWAP gates with full connectivity for state preparation. All
356   circuits prepared by the agent have a fidelity of 1.0.
357   State and Action spaces. For each n and both connectivities, we use the set of n-qubit stabilizer
358   states as our state space. Each stabilizer state is represented in flattened tableau form (Aaronson &
359   Gottesman, 2004), only including its stabilizers, so that each state is represented by a (2n2 + n)-
360   dimensional bit-vector. For the action space, we use different allowed gatesets for each connectivity.
361   Both gate-sets are realistic, for example in trapped-ion-based quantum computers (Cirac & Zoller,
362   1995), a promising candidate for quantum computation. For the fully connected agents, our gateset
363
      G consists of H (Hadamard), S (Phase), CNOT and HSH (conjugated phase) gates. The inclusion
      of the conjugated phase gate HSH ensures symmetry within the gate set because it provides an
364
      operation for the X component that mirrors the effect of S on the Z component. While S modifies
365
      the Z component of the tableau, the HSH gate equivalently modifies the X component. This
366   symmetry is useful since the tableau is also symmetric in X and Z. It is not unfair to treat HSH
367   as a single gate; it is as easy as S to apply (Evered et al., 2023) – note that HSH is simply a π/2-
368   rotation about the x-axis just as S is a π/2-rotation about the z-axis. For the local case, we use the
369   H, S, X, Y , Z and CNOT gates. Despite using a more restrictive gate-set for our agents compared
370   to baselines, our methods achieve substantially shorter circuits (see Tab. 3).
371
      Testbench details. We use two testbeds. One consisting of uniformly random stabilizer circuits
372   and the other comprising (roughly) uniformly sampled brickwork circuits (see Fig. 3(a)) of different
373   depths. The random stabilizer circuits were sampled using the Stim API (Gidney, 2021). Each brick-
374   work circuit of depth d was constructed by choosing each “brick” to be an independently sampled
375   random 2-qubit stabilizer circuit. The first test-bench serves to estimate the average performance
376   of the agent; the second examines the entanglement dynamics of the induced preparation algorithm
377   of the agent. Finally, we also additionally sampled 2, 000 independent uniformly random stabilizer
      circuits for each n = 5, 6, 7, 9 to provide the empirical data to prove our generalization bounds.

475   Entanglement dynamics. This analysis concerns n-qubit states generated from brickwork circuits
476   of depth 2n and the agent restricted to a linear connectivity, both of which generate local dynamics
477
      with qubit interactions on O(1) number of qubits at each step. Given a circuit C = [U1 , · · · , Uk ]
      prepared by the local agent for a brickwork state, we compute the entanglement entropy S(t) of the
478
      intermediate states |ψt ⟩ = Ut Ut−1 · · · U1 |0⟩ for each 1 ≤ t ≤ k as S(t) = SN/2 (|ψt ⟩). The goal
479
      of this experiment is as follows: for preparing highly entangled n qubit states with local dynamics,
480   there are known bounds on the rate at which information can spread (Chen et al., 2023) owing to
481   the locality of the dynamics. From the point of view of circuit optimization, this implies that the
482   rate of correlations generated by the agent is important: a strong suppression of the entanglement
483   rate dS(t)/dt would imply longer circuits generated by the agent. Random brick-work circuits on
484   n qubits are prototypical examples of local quantum dynamics, displaying an initial linear increase
485   in S(t) followed by saturation at t ≲ 2n. It is thus of interest to contrast the entanglement dynamics
      generated by our local agents with the random brickworks.

492   information-theoretic interpretation of the efficiency, whereby scaling this algorithm further cru-

      ally observed to monotonically increase (not shown), suggesting low redundancy in the use of the
      entangling CNOT gates on the part of the agent and further justifying our benchmarks.

497   Theoretical analysis of agent generalization. All experiments so far involve the agent preparing
498   arbitrary states not seen during training, indicating a generalization to unseen states. We provide
499   rigorous justification for this observation, showing that with probability at least 1 − 10−10 ≈ 1,
500   the agent generalizes to at least 95% of the state space. In particular, we show in App. C that the
501
      following concentration result holds.
502   Proposition 3 (Informal). Fix ε, δ > 0. Let A be a state preparation agent and X to be the random
503   variable over the uniform distribution on Sn with X(|ψ⟩) = 1 whenever A successfully prepares
504   |ψ⟩ and 0 otherwise. Let X̄ be the average value of X across N uniformly sampled states |ψ⟩
505   and suppose that N ≥ 2ε12 log 1δ . Then with probability at least 1 − δ over the choice of samples
506   (X1 , · · · , XN ),
507
                                               E[X] ≥ X̄ − ε.
508
      We set δ = 10−10 , ϵ = 0.05 and N = 2000. For each n = 5, 6, 7, 9, we sample N uniform states
509   from Sn and run our algorithm. We find that our algorithm prepares all of them exactly, implying
510   X̄ = 1. It follows that E[X] ≥ 0.95 for each n, which means that at least 95% of Sn will be
511   prepared successfully by the agent. This implies massive generalization: |Sn | is 2.4M, 315M, 81.3B
512   and 4.38 × 1016 for n = 5, 6, 7 and 9 respectively. Our result says that our agent will successfully
513   prepare at least 2.3M, 300M, 77.2B and 4.07 × 1016 states – which are many many orders larger
514   than the 10-20M states seen during training. This large-scale generalization provides insight into the
515   empirical success of the agent on virtually all states.

517   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER WORK
518
519   In this work, we have demonstrated that deep reinforcement learning can facilitate immediate in-
520   ference on arbitrary stabilizer states without needing re-training. This is achieved through a highly
521   sample-efficient reverse preparation approach utilizing a novel reward function. Our experiments
522   perform a thorough analysis of the agent’s inference capabilities and the potential of this approach
523   to improve performance over existing baselines. We show that our agents remain efficient across
524   the spectrum of entanglement content of the target. Additionally, we provide information-theoretic
525   insights into the dynamics and (low) redundancy of the trained agents and also present compelling
526
      arguments for their generalization capabilities. An important contribution is that our algorithm takes
      time proportional to the size of the circuit returned, which is of the same order as traditional rule-
527
      based approaches to state preparation. This suggests its use as a replacement for these algorithms, at
528
      least for a small number of qubits. This provides promise for the integration of RL-based methods
529   into real quantum computing environments for transpilation.
530
531   An important limitation is that our algorithm does not scale yet to qubit count in the hundreds or
532
      thousands, which traditional approaches can handle, even if sub-optimally. This brings us to the first
      direction of future inquiry: an imminent task is designing local RL algorithms which operate on an
533
      O(1) number of qubits by making local queries to the target state (Huang et al., 2024b;a). Further-
534
      more, while the current work addresses the question of finding optimal circuits to be implemented in
535   a lab setting, it is indeed possible to restrict the agent’s knowledge of the state under preparation to
536   only local Pauli measurements. Moreover, understanding the fundamental bounds on the scaling of
537   the exploration phase as the number of qubits in the target state is an important task to gain insight
538   into the fundamental limits of such algorithms. Overall, our findings illustrate further the promise
539   of employing deep RL methods for efficient state preparation on near-term quantum systems.

760   We briefly introduce the principles of quantum computing, quantum circuits and stabilizer states.

      The state of a single qubit is described by a unit vector |ψ⟩ = a |0⟩ + b |1⟩, where a, b ∈ C with
764
      |a|2 + |b|2 = 1 and {|0⟩ , |1⟩} is a fixed orthonormal basis, often called the computational basis,
765
      spanning the single qubit Hilbert space H ∼    = C2 . We use the Dirac notation here, where |ψ⟩ =
766                                                †

767
      system which consists of two parts A and B lives in the tensor product space of the individual
768
      Hilbert spaces HA and HB , |ψ⟩AB ∈ HA ⊗ HB . It follows that a general n-qubit state is a linear
769   combination of the 2n basis states |z⟩ = ⊗ni=1 |zi ⟩ ∈ H⊗n (zi ∈ {0, 1}). The fidelity between two
770   quantum states is F(ψ, ϕ) := |⟨ψ|ϕ⟩|2 . An n-qubit state is called entangled
                                                                               √      if it cannot be written
771   as a tensor product of single-qubit states. For instance, (|00⟩ + |11⟩) / 2 is an entangled state.

793   A.2   S TABILIZER STATES
794
795   We say that an element π ∈ Pn stabilizes state |ψ⟩ if π |ψ⟩ = |ψ⟩. The set of stabilizers of a state
796   comprises its stabilizer group (generated by at most n elements). A state is a stabilizer state iff its
797   stabilizer group is generated by n elements. Conversely, every S uniquely determines a correspond-
798
      ing stabilizer state |ψ⟩ as the simultaneous eigenstate with eigenvalue 1, g |ψ⟩ = |ψ⟩ , ∀g ∈ S. Since
      a stabilizer group generator ∈ Pn can be represented using 2n + 1 bits, a stabilizer state |ψ⟩ can be
799
      written using n(2n + 1) bits.
800
801   Stabilizer states can also be characterized as the states that can be reached from the all-zeros state
802   |0⟩ using Clifford circuits, i.e. unitaries that are a combination of H, S and CNOT gates. No-
803   tably, the Pauli gates are Clifford unitaries. The action of each of these gates on a stabilizer state’s
804
      bit-representation is simple, resulting in the efficient classical simulation of quantum computation
      exclusively with Clifford unitaries (Aaronson & Gottesman, 2004).
805
806   Despite this, preparing stabilizer states optimally remains a challenge, since the number of Clif-
                                         2
807   ford states grows rapidly as 2O(n ) . Known optimal implementations have been limited to 6 qubits
808   (Bravyi et al., 2022). Further, the (anti-)commutation and involutary properties of Clifford gates
809   make it harder to reason about locally greedy search steps. We outline existing work towards stabi-
      lizer state preparation in Sec. 3.

831   A.3    R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING
832
833   In a Reinforcement Learning (RL) problem an agent learns through interactions with an environment
834   to maximize its reward (Sutton & Barto, 2018). The environment is modeled as a Markov decision
835
      process, consisting of (a) a set S of states of the environment, (b) a set of actions A of the agent,
      (c) a transition function p : S × A × S → [0, 1] where p(s′ |s, a) is the probability that the state
836
      of the environment will be s′ if the environment is in state s and the agent takes action a, (d) a
837
      reward function r : S × A × S → R with r(s, a, s′ ) representing the reward that the agent receives
838   from the environment for taking action a from state s and reaching state s′ , and (e) a set T ⊂ S of
839   terminal states. The interaction between agent and environment stops on reaching a terminal state
840   or exceeding a maximum number T of actions without reaching a terminal state.
841
      A policy of an RL agent is a function, π : S × A → [0, 1] with π(a|s) being the probability that the
842
      agent will take action a when in state s. A trace τ of π is a tuple of alternating states and actions,
843
      starting and ending in a state: τ = (s0 , a0 , s1 , · · · , aT −1 , sT ). A policy π along with a distribution
844   µ over possible start states s0 induces a distribution over traces, with ai ∼ π(·|si ), si+1 ∼ p(·|si , ai )
845                                                                   PT −1
      for each i. The return of trace τ is defined by G(τ ) := i=0 γ i r(si , ai , si+1 ), where γ ∈ (0, 1) is
846
      the discount factor, describing the value of future actions in the present. The goal in RL is to find a
847   policy π ∗ that maximizes the expected return

853   is the advantage function, denoting how much better a particular action is w.r.t the average:

      We use Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) throughout our experiments. PPO (Schulman et al.,
857

858   prove stability and efficiency in policy optimization. A running policy function π (parameterized by

863   vantages, exponentially-weighted linear combinations of the advantages along a trace, which yield
      more robust estimates (Schulman et al., 2018).
