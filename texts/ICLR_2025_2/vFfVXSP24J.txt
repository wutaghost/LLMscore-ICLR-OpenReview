011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Electrocardiogram (ECG) is the primary non-invasive diagnostic tool for mon-
014                itoring cardiac conditions and is crucial in assisting clinicians. Recent studies
015                have concentrated on classifying cardiac conditions using ECG data but have
016
                   overlooked ECG report generation, which is time-consuming and requires clinical
                   expertise. To automate ECG report generation and ensure its versatility, we propose
017
                   the Multimodal ECG Instruction Tuning (MEIT) framework, the first attempt to
018
                   tackle ECG report generation with LLMs and multimodal instructions. To facilitate
019                future research, we establish a benchmark to evaluate MEIT with various LLMs
020                backbones across two large-scale ECG datasets. Our approach uniquely aligns
021                the representations of the ECG signal and the report, and we conduct extensive
022                experiments to benchmark MEIT with nine open-source LLMs using more than
023                800,000 ECG reports. MEIT’s results underscore the superior performance of
024                instruction-tuned LLMs, showcasing their proficiency in quality report generation,
025                zero-shot capabilities, resilience to signal perturbation, and alignment with human
026                expert evaluation. These findings emphasize the efficacy of our MEIT1 framework
027
                   and its potential for real-world clinical application.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Electrocardiogram (ECG) is the primary mechanism for heart disease diagnosis. Cardiologists read
032
      and interpret these ECG recordings to manually generate comprehensive ECG reports for heart disease
033   diagnosis, which is a complex and time-consuming process. Recently, AI models have been developed
034   to facilitate ECG data analysis for the task of classification (Hu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024).
035   Despite these efforts, the automatic generation of reports from ECG recordings still needs to be
036   explored. Unlike other AI-empowered medical report generation applications (e.g., radiology reports),
037   the primary challenge for ECG report generation stems from the distinct nature of ECG content.
038   ECG reports, often comprising brief phrases that summarize signal patterns, contrast with detailed
039   anatomical descriptions in radiology reports. The difference in the content and semantic interpretation
040   between imaging and ECG data complicates the direct application of radiology-focused methods
041
      to ECG reports. Furthermore, there is still a lack of comprehensive benchmarks for evaluating the
      performance of ECG report generation.
042
043   To tackle these challenges, we introduce MEIT, a Multimodal ECG Instruction Tuning framework
044   that extends the capabilities of LLMs in the cardiology context to generate ECG reports using
045   ECG recordings and human instructions. Inspired by the versatility of LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023;
046   Touvron et al., 2023a; Wan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a;b) in handling diverse language tasks
047
      simultaneously, we develop a specialized instruction tuning process for ECG report generation. MEIT
      aligns human instructions with ECG recordings, enabling LLMs to generate clinically relevant reports
048
      and exhibit zero-shot report generation capabilities under domain transfer scenarios across various
049
      continents and data collection devices. Specifically, leveraging publicly available ECG datasets, we
050   construct a multimodal instruction dataset including ECG records, human instructions, and paired
051   reports. Then, we propose an effective and efficient attention-based fusion method to integrate ECG
052   and text representations in the latent space. This enables LLMs to understand ECG signals for report

055   the ECG report generation approach, we introduce a comprehensive benchmark for ECG report
056   generation evaluation, utilizing two datasets with 20K and 800K ECG-report pairs, respectively,
057   across four evaluation tasks: report generation quality, zero-shot learning across datasets, robustness

062   under the MEIT framework in domain transfer scenarios.
063   To summarize, our primary contribution is the MEIT framework, a novel approach to automating
064   ECG report generation and evaluation based on LLMs. This framework incorporates a lightweight,
065   attention-based fusion module across various LLM models. Furthermore, we design a new bench-
066   mark for ECG report generation, which contains four evaluation tasks. Our evaluations showcase
067   the enhanced capabilities of instruction-tuned LLMs in generating ECG reports, highlighting the
068   transferability in zero-shot tests, robustness against data perturbations, and alignment with human
069
      expert evaluation. MEIT paves the way for future advances in automated ECG report generation and
      methodological innovations in integrating biomedical signals into LLMs.

071   2     R ELATED W ORK
072   Medical Report Generation. Our work is highly related to the domain of medical report generation.
073   Existing works on medical report generation dominantly focus on medical images, where three
074   categories of techniques have been proposed: (1) Template Selection and Generation, highlighted
075   by HRGR (Li et al., 2018) and CMAS (Jing et al., 2017); (2) Data Integration and Coherence, as
076   seen in PPKED (Liu et al., 2021) and CA (Ma et al., 2021); (3) Cross-Modal Alignment, with efforts
077   like (Chen et al., 2022; Qin and Song, 2022). However, these methods are designed for medical
078   images and face challenges when applied to ECG data due to its unique temporal and waveform
079   characteristics. In contrast, we propose a new approach and benchmark specifically tailored for ECG
080
      report generation, effectively addressing these challenges.
081   Instruction Tuning. Our work is also related to instruction tuning. Instruction tuning (Zhang et al.,
082   2023; Wang et al., 2023) boosts zero-shot learning in LLMs for new tasks using instructions. Notable
083   models like InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), FLAN-PaLM (Chung et al., 2022), and Alpaca (Taori
084   et al., 2023) fine-tune with instruction data through various methods, including human feedback.
085   Similarly, multimodal models such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b), MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), and
086
      AnyMAL (Moon et al., 2023) benefit from multimodal instructions for enhanced learning. However,
      these methods are designed for natural images and cannot be directly applied to ECG signals, which
087
      have different characteristics and complexities. Furthermore, instruction tuning for medical signals,
088
      especially ECG, remains largely unexplored. In contrast, we propose a novel instruction-tuning
089   framework and benchmark specifically for ECG report generation, addressing this critical gap.
090
091   LLMs for ECG. Only a few research efforts have focused on utilizing LLMs for ECG signals (Liu
092
      et al., 2023c; Qiu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a). In particular, studies such as (Liu et al., 2023c;
      Yu et al., 2023a) convert ECG signals into text features before feeding them into LLMs, bypassing
093
      the original signal data. However, this method overlooks important modality-specific patterns in
094
      the signals. Furthermore, these studies focus solely on disease classification from ECG data and
095   do not address medical report generation. Recently, Yu et al. (2023b; 2024) proposes zero-shot
096   ECG diagnosis using LLMs combined with retrieval-augmented generation, significantly improving
097   diagnostic with limited medical data. In contrast, (Qiu et al., 2023) attempts to generate ECG
098   reports by using handcrafted ECG features as input. However, their code and models are not publicly
099   available and focus on classification issues, making direct comparisons difficult. In this work, we
100   propose a new instruction-tuning benchmark and framework that directly utilizes ECG signals for
101   medical signal understanding and report generation, addressing the limitations of prior approaches.

137   3.2       F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
138
139
      Figure 1 (a) illustrates the proposed MEIT framework. First, we extract and preprocess the ECG
      signals and corresponding ground truth reports from the ECG dataset to construct the ECG instruction
140
      data, which includes instruction prompts, ECG signals, and ground truth. The core steps of this
141
      process are detailed in Section 3.3. Next, during the ECG instruction tuning, the processed ECG
142   instruction data is fed into the Report Generator, as shown in Figure 1 (b), for training using an
143   auto-regressive approach. During inference, the instruction prompts and ECG signals are input into
144   the Report Generator to generate professional ECG reports. Next, we describe each component.

146
      3.3       DATA C URATION
147
148   Given an ECG signal Xe , our goal during inference is to generate an ECG report using an instruction
149   prompt. For instance, the prompt can be “Given the ECG signal embeddings, please help me generate
150   an accurate description for this ECG signal embeddings: ". To achieve this goal, during the training
151   phase, we aim to create instruction tuning data to generate a response X̂t that aligns semantically
152   with the ground truth Xt . In addition, since we cannot predict the exact instruction prompt that
153
      users will use, we need to ensure that our report generation process is robust enough to handle
      different prompts. To address this challenge, we manually design some prompt samples, then utilize
154
      GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to generate a set of prompts by rephrasing, as shown in Figure 1. Then,
155
      we randomly select one instruction prompt Xp from the prompt set and create a general instruction-
156   following template: <|user|>: {Xp , Xe }<|assistant|>: {Xt } </s>, where <|user|>
157   and <|assistant|> are added special tokens for tokenizer, </s> is a stop sign for each response.
158   This approach ensures that the generated response conveys the same meaning as the ground truth
159   and remains adaptable to different instruction prompts. Following this strategy, we construct the
160   ECG instruction data using a MIMIC-IV-ECG (Gow et al.) dataset that contains 800K annotated data
161   and a 20K dataset PTB-XL (Wagner et al., 2020). The ECG instruction data samples are shown in
      Appendix A.7.

      3.4   R EPORT G ENERATION
163
164   In MEIT, the multimodal ECG report generation model decodes ECG signals end-to-end to generate
165   ECG reports. The architecture is illustrated in Figure 1. Specifically, the report generation model
166   directly encodes an entire ECG-signal Xe ∈ RM ×T into latent embeddings and integrate it with the
167   language embeddings with modality alignment, and then autoregressively generate the ECG report.
168   Next, we detail each component of the report generation model.
169   ECG Encoder. Since the ECG signal is of high resolution in the temporal domain, it is vital to
170   efficiently extract temporal features per lead before interaction with semantic embeddings inside the
171   LLM backbone. Our default ECG encoder Fe (·) consists of temporal convolution blocks to encode
172   each ECG signal into embeddings. Specifically, each temporal convolution block comprises several
173   1-D convolution layers, batch normalization layers, and ReLU activation layers, followed by average
174   pooling. This design allows us to effectively capture temporal dependencies and reduce the complexity
175   of the signal representations, ensuring that the model can quickly learn important temporal features
176
      efficiently. To further align the output dimension with the head dimension of the LLM backbone
      Fl (·), we employ a non-linear projection layer Pe (·) to generate the ECG embeddings:
177
178
179                                          He = Pe (Fe (Xe )) ,                                    (1)
180   where He ∈ RDh , Dh has the same dimension as the multi-head attention layers of LLMs. Note that
181   our default ECG encoder is lightweight and is able to learn temporal patterns of signals without a
182   long training period. More details about ECG Encoder are illustrated in Appendix A.2.
183   ECG Modality Alignment. We introduce an ECG modality alignment strategy to guide the LLMs in
184   aligning ECG signal data with corresponding textual outputs. This approach is detailed in Figure 1
185   (b). Specifically, given the ECG embeddings He , the alignment strategy incorporates He with the
186   current hidden state Hit generated from previous i − 1-th layer of the LLM backbone Fl (·) for
187   next-token prediction task. Here Hit is defined as:
188                                          Hit = Fli−1 ([Xp , Xt ]) ,                                     (2)

      where i is the current layer index. Traditional gated-attention fusion in Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022),
190

191   2 (Li et al., 2023) that requires additional trainable parameters and designed for complex multi-stage
192   alignment of rich semantic information (e.g., images). Different from them, our method provides
193   a lightweight concatenated-fusion alignment strategy tailored to the embeddings of ECG signals,

      3.5       I NSTRUCTION T UNING
217
218   As described in Section 3.3, we have converted ECG-text pairs into a chat-bot style instruction format:
219   <|user|>: {Xp , Xe }<|assistant|>: {Xt }</s>. During instruction tuning, we compute
220   autoregressive loss only on tokens after response tokens <assistant>, and use label loss masking
221   to finetune the model, where we mask all tokens belonging to Xp and Xe . To save computational
222   resources and accelerate the convergence of instruction tuning, we use LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)
223   adapters for all linear layers of the LLM backbone Fl and freeze its backbone. Subsequently, given
224   a sequence of ECG instruction data, we compute the probability of the target response Xt as an

      autoregressive function:

235   4.1       DATASETS
236
237   PTB-XL. The PTB-XL dataset (Wagner et al., 2020) contains 21, 837 clinical 12-lead ECG record-
238   ings, each sampled at 500Hz and lasting 10 seconds, collected from 18, 885 patients. Each ECG
239   recording has a corresponding report. We divided this dataset into training, validation, and testing
240   subsets in a 70%:10%:20% ratio, respectively. The human experts double-check all samples in the
241   test set to ensure data quality. As mentioned in Sec 3.3, we reformulate the dataset into the instruction
242
      data format.
243   MIMIC-IV-ECG. The MIMIC-IV-ECG dataset (Gow et al.) is currently the largest publicly ac-
244   cessible ECG dataset, containing 800,035 paired samples from 161,352 unique subjects. Similar to
245   PTB-XL, each sample in this dataset includes a raw ECG signal and its corresponding report, with all
246   recordings sampled at 500Hz for 10 seconds. The division of this dataset into training, validation, and
247   testing subsets is 80%:10%:10% ratio. Likewise, we reconstruct this dataset into an ECG instruction
248
      data template.

      4.4     TASKS
300
301
302   Quality of Generated Reports. This task aims to assess report quality after ECG instruction tuning
303   using 10% of MIMIC-IV-ECG and PTB-XL datasets as the test set. The evaluation examines how
304   closely generated reports match the original’s structure and meaning, considering various instructions
305   and ECG inputs. We analyze metrics like BLEU-1 to 4, METEOR, ROUGE 1, 2, L, CIDEr-D, and
306   BERTScore.
307   Zero-shot Generalizability. To explore the generalizability of LLMs in domain transfer scenarios
308   following ECG instruction tuning, we trained the models on 70% of the instruction data from
309   MIMIC-IV-ECG. Following this, we evaluated the models’ zero-shot capabilities on the PTB-XL test
310   set. It’s important to note that the PTB-XL and MIMIC-IV-ECG datasets originate from different
311   continents—Europe and the United States, respectively—utilizing varied devices and from distinct
312   hospitals, across different time periods. Therefore, we consider these datasets to represent two
313
      separate domains. This distinction allows us to use the PTB-XL dataset to gauge our model’s
      performance in zero-shot domain transfer effectively. We used the metrics BLEU-4, METEOR,
314
      ROUGE-L, and CIDEr-D because of limited space and calculated their average for model evaluation.
315
316   Signal Perturbation Robustness. In real-world clinical settings, ECG signals often contain some
317   degree of noise. To evaluate the robustness of MEIT against such noisy interference, we selected
318   10% of the ECG samples from the MIMIC-IV-ECG test dataset. We then added Gaussian noise to
319
      these samples during the models’ instruction-based inference process. For this evaluation, we used
      BLEU-4, METEOR, ROUGE-L, and CIDEr-D as metrics.
320
321   Evaluation of Alignment with Human Expert Annotations. To evaluate the differences between
322   the reports generated by ECG-instructed LLMs and human expert annotations, we established specific
323   evaluation criteria and utilized closed-source LLMs to conduct a professional assessment of both the
      generated reports and expert annotations.

530   6        C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we introduced MEIT, a new framework for generating instruction-following data to train
532
      a multimodal LLM that can produce ECG reports based on human instructions. We also proposed
533
      an effective method for aligning ECG and report representations across various open-source LLMs,
534   demonstrating strong performance on both the MIMIC-IV-ECG and PTB-XL datasets across multiple
535   tasks. Additionally, we established a comprehensive benchmark for ECG instruction-following
536   in report generation, providing a standardized evaluation for future research. Although this work
537   primarily focuses on ECG signals, it serves as a foundational step in applying instruction-tuning to
538   biomedical signals. For future research, we aim to extend our framework and benchmark to other
539   medical domains, such as EEG, with the hope of driving further progress in developing more capable
      medical-signal LLMs.
