009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Anomaly detection (AD) has been widely studied for decades in many real-world
012            applications, including fraud detection in finance, intrusion detection for cyber-
013            security, etc. Due to the imbalanced nature between protected and unprotected
014            groups and the imbalanced distributions of normal examples and anomalies, the
015            learning objectives of most existing anomaly detection methods tend to solely
016            concentrate on the dominating unprotected group. Thus, it has been recognized
017            by many researchers about the significance of ensuring model fairness in anomaly
018
               detection. However, in the imbalanced scenario where the unprotected group is
               more abundant than the protected group, the existing fair anomaly detection meth-
019
               ods tend to erroneously label most normal examples from the protected group as
020
               anomalies. This phenomenon is caused by the improper design of learning objec-
021            tives, which statistically focus on learning the frequent patterns (i.e., the unpro-
022            tected group) while overlooking the under-represented patterns (i.e., the protected
023            group). To address these issues, we propose FADIG, a fairness-aware anomaly
024            detection method targeting the imbalanced scenario. It consists of a fairness-aware
025            contrastive learning module and a rebalancing autoencoder module to ensure fair-
026            ness and handle the imbalanced data issue, respectively. Moreover, we provide
027            the theoretical analysis that shows our proposed contrastive learning regulariza-
028            tion guarantees group fairness. Empirical studies demonstrate the effectiveness
029
               and efficiency of FADIG across multiple real-world datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Anomaly detection (AD), a.k.a. outlier detection, is referred to as the process of detecting data
034
      instances that significantly deviate from the majority of data instances (Chandola et al., 2009).
035
      Anomaly detection finds extensive use in a wide variety of applications including financial fraud
036   detection (West & Bhattacharya, 2016; Huang et al., 2018), pathology analysis in the medical do-
037   main (Faust et al., 2018; Shvetsova et al., 2021) and intrusion detection for cybersecurity (Liao et al.,
038   2013; Ahmad et al., 2021). For example, an anomalous traffic pattern in a computer network sug-
039   gests that a hacked computer is sending out sensitive data to an unauthorized destination Ahmed
040   et al. (2016); anomalies in credit card transaction data could indicate credit card or identity theft
041   (Rezapour, 2019).
042   Up until now, a large number of deep anomaly detection methods have been introduced, demonstrat-
043   ing significantly better performance than shallow anomaly detection in addressing complicated de-
044   tection problems in a variety of real-world applications such as computer vision tasks. For instance,
045   Sohn et al. (2021); Li et al. (2023) aim to learn a scalar anomaly scoring function in an end-to-end
046   fashion, while Audibert et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2021); Hou et al. (2021); Yan et al. (2021); Wang
047   et al. (2023) propose to learn the patterns for the normal examples via a feature extractor.
048   Recently, there has been widespread recognition within the AI community about the significance
049   of ensuring model fairness and thus it is highly desirable to establish specific parity or preference
050   constraints in the context of anomaly detection. Take racial bias in anomaly detection as an ex-
051   ample. Racial bias has been observed in predictive risk modeling systems to predict the likelihood
052   of future adverse outcomes in child welfare (Chouldechova et al., 2018). Communities in poverty
053   or specific racial or ethnic groups may face disadvantages due to the reliance on government ad-
      ministrative data. The data collected from these communities, often stemming from their economic

076   is more abundant than the protected group, most of these methods tend to erroneously label most
077   normal examples from the protected/minority group as anomalies. To better illustrate this issue, we

      the unprotected group is four times that of the protected group, and approximately 10% of the total
079
      samples are anomalies. Figure 1 and Table 1 show the performance of anomaly detection methods
080
      evaluated on this dataset, where Recall Diff refers to the absolute value of recall difference between
081   the protected group and the unprotected group. Note that in such an imbalanced scenario, metrics
082   such as accuracy difference (Zafar et al., 2017) are not proper choices. We observe that existing
083   methods either compromise performance for fairness (i.e., low recall rate and low recall difference)
084   or exhibit unfair behavior (i.e., high recall difference). The problem of misclassification arises from
085   models focusing on learning frequent patterns in the more abundant unprotected group, potentially
086   overlooking under-represented patterns in the protected group. The issue of group imbalance results
087   in higher errors for protected groups, thus causing misclassifications. Following Hashimoto et al.
088   (2018), we refer to this phenomenon as representation disparity.
089   To address these issues, we face the following two major challenges. C1: Handling imbalanced
090   data. Due to the imbalanced nature between the protected and unprotected groups and the im-
091   balanced distributions of normal examples and anomalies, the learning objectives of most existing
092   anomaly detection methods tend to solely concentrate on the unprotected group. C2: Mitigating the
093   representation disparity. Traditional anomaly detection methods encounter difficulties in dealing
094   with representation disparity issues, which may worsen in the imbalanced data scenario as protected
095
      groups are typically fewer than unprotected groups.
096   To tackle these challenges, in this paper, we propose FADIG, a fairness-aware contrastive learning-
097   based anomaly detection method for the imbalanced group scenario. FADIG mainly consists of
098   two modules: (1) the fairness-aware contrastive learning module; (2) the re-balancing autoencoder
099   module. Specifically, the fairness-aware contrastive learning module aims to maximize the similarity
100   between the protected and unprotected groups to ensure fairness and address C2. In addition, we
101
      encourage the uniformity of representations for examples within each group, as ensuring uniformity
      in contrastive learning can be beneficial for the imbalanced group scenario (Jiang et al., 2021).
102
      To further address the negative impact of imbalanced data (i.e., C1), we propose the re-balancing
103
      autoencoder module utilizing the learnable weight to reweigh the importance of both the protected
104   and unprotected groups. Combining the two modules, we design a simple yet efficient method
105   FADIG with a theoretical guarantee of fairness. Our contributions are summarized below.
106

               ity and imbalanced data issues in the anomaly detection task.

      One direction of unsupervised AD is reconstruction-based autoencoder, such as An & Cho (2015);
145
      Audibert et al. (2020); Hou et al. (2021). Assuming the anomalies possess different features than the
146   normal examples, given an autoencoder over the normal examples, it will be hard to compress and
147   reconstruct the anomalies. The anomaly score can then be defined as the reconstruction loss for each
148   test example. Formally, the autoencoder consists of two main components: an encoder ge : Rd →
149   Rr and a decoder gd : Rr → Rd , where r is the dimensionality of the hidden representations. ge (x)
150   encodes the input x to a hidden representation z that preserves the important aspects of the input.
151   Then, gd (z) aims to recover x′ ≈ x, a reconstruction of the input from the hidden representation
152   z. Overall, the autoencoder can be written as G = gd ◦ ge , i.e. G(x) = gd (ge (x)). For a given
153   autoencoder-based framework, the anomaly score for x is computed using the reconstruction error
154
      as:

158   errors because they do not conform to the patterns in the data as coded by the autoencoder. This
159   scoring function is generic in that it applies to many reconstruction-based AD models, which have
160   different parameterizations of the reconstruction function G. Next, we will present our method
161   design based on the autoencoder framework. For quick reference, we summarize the notation used

192   Existing anomaly detection models (Song et al., 2021; Zhang & Davidson, 2021; Fioresi et al., 2023)
193

194
      to the lower contribution of protected groups to the overall learning objective (e.g., minimizing
195
      expected reconstruction loss), examples from the protected groups may experience systematically
196   higher errors. Thus, they tend to erroneously label most normal examples from the protected group
197   as anomalies, producing unfair outcomes as shown in Figure 2a.
198

200   in the unit hypersphere, as illustrated in Figure 2b. Therefore, one naive solution is to implement
201   contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020) to learn representations by distinguishing different views of
202   one example from other examples as follows:

      6   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we introduce FADIG, a fairness-aware anomaly detection method, designed for han-
525   dling the imbalanced data scenario in the context of anomaly detection. Specifically, FADIG max-
526   imizes the similarity between the protected and unprotected groups to ensure fairness through the
527   fairness-aware contrastive learning based module. To address the negative impact of imbalanced
528   data, the re-balancing autoencoder module is proposed to automatically reweight the importance of
529   both the protected and unprotected groups with the learnable weight. Theoretically, we provide the
530   upper bound with Rademacher complexity for the discrepancy between two groups and ensure group
531   fairness through the proposed contrastive learning regularization. Empirical studies demonstrate the
532   effectiveness and efficiency of FADIG across multiple real-world datasets.

      B    R ELATED W ORK
783
784
      Unsupervised Anomaly Detection. Anomaly detection has been widely studied for decades in
      many real-world applications, including fraud detection in the finance domain (West & Bhat-
785
      tacharya, 2016; Huang et al., 2018), pathology analysis in the medical domain (Faust et al., 2018;
786
      Shvetsova et al., 2021), intrusion detection for cyber-security (Liao et al., 2013; Ahmad et al., 2021),
787   and fault detection in safety-critical systems (Ju et al., 2021), etc. Given various types of anoma-
788   lies (Breunig et al., 2000; Kriegel et al., 2009; Bouman et al., 2024), the authors of Pang et al.
789   (2022) divide the existing anomaly detection methods into two major branches. The methods (Au-
790   dibert et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023) in the
791   first branch aim to learn the patterns for the normal samples by a feature extractor. For instance,
792   Audibert et al. (2020) is an encoder-decoder anomaly detection method, which learns how to am-
793   plify the reconstruction error of anomalies with adversarial training; Chen et al. (2021) proposes a
794   GAN-based autoencoder model to learn the normal pattern of multivariate time series, and detect
795
      anomalies by selecting the samples with the higher reconstruction error. Our work also uses the
      autoencoder model. Compared with the works in the first branch, we design an auto-reweighted
796
      training of the reconstruction errors, and mitigate the representation disparity between groups with
797
      contrastive learning-based regularization. The second branch aims at learning scalar anomaly scores
798   in an end-to-end fashion (Sohn et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022). Notably, the authors
799   of Sohn et al. (2021) combine distribution-augmented contrastive regularization with a one-class
800   classifier to detect anomalies. While Sohn et al. (2021) uses image augmentations, such as rota-
801   tions, to form positive pairs and negative pairs for contrastive learning, we use existing examples for
802   contrastive learning and thus our method is applicable to various types of data, not limited to image
803   data. To be more specific, we design a fairness-aware contrastive learning loss which minimizes the
804   representation disparity of the groups for fairness, and encourages the uniformity within each group
805   for better anomaly detection.
806   Fair Machine Learning. Fair Machine Learning aims to amend the biased machine learning models
807   to be fair or invariant regarding specific variables. A surge of research in fair machine learning has
808   been done in the machine learning community (Kobren et al., 2019; Zemel et al., 2013; Bolukbasi
809   et al., 2016; Hashimoto et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Park et al., 2022). For example, Zemel et al.
      (2013) presents a learning algorithm for fair classification by enforcing group fairness and individual


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      fairness in the obtained data representation; Bolukbasi et al. (2016) proposes approaches to quantify
811   and reduce bias in word embedding vectors that are trained from real-world data; in Hashimoto et al.
812   (2018), the authors develop a robust optimization framework that minimizes the worst case risk over
813   all distributions and preserves the minority group in an imbalanced data set; in Zhang et al. (2018),
814   the authors present an adversarial-learning based framework for mitigating the undesired bias in
815   modern machine learning models. Park et al. (2022) proposes a fair supervised contrastive loss to
816   train a fair representation model. However, they rely on target labels and need negative samples
817   since their method is based on supervised contrastive learning. Instead, our designed fair contrastive
818   learning loss uses examples from different groups in an unsupervised way to minimize the represen-
819
      tation disparity of the groups and encourage the uniformity within each group. In the field of fair
      anomaly detection, Zhang & Davidson (2021) utilizes the adversarial generative nets to ensure group
820
      fairness and use one-class classification to detect the anomalies; Song et al. (2021) introduces fair-
821
      ness adversarial training and proposes a novel dynamic weight to reduce the negative impacts from
822   outlier points. The existing fair anomaly detection methods (Song et al., 2021; Zhang & Davidson,
823   2021; Fioresi et al., 2023) tend to suffer from the representation disparity issue in the imbalanced
824   data scenario. To address this issue, this paper aims to alleviate the issue of representation disparity
825   in the imbalanced data scenario by introducing the rebalancing autoencoder module and maximizing
826   the uniformity of the samples in the latent space via contrastive learning regularization.

1032
       examples and anomalies, we focus on Recall Parity in anomaly detection. Given our score-based
1033   anomaly detection framework, we would like to state the mathematical formulation of Recall Parity
1034   fairness in anomaly detection as: Let anomaly score for example x be s(x) and let tK be the anomaly
1035   score threshold for top-K selection. Then, the predicted normal examples are the ones with s(x) <
1036   tK , and the predicted anomalies are those with s(x) ≥ tK . The recall parity in anomaly detection
1037   requires that P (s(x) ≥ tK |x ∈ U, y = 1) = P (s(x) ≥ tK |x ∈ P, y = 1). We use the absolute
1038   value of their difference, i.e., Recall Diff, to evaluate the fairness level.

       G     L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACT
1169
1170
       This paper proposes a fairness-aware anomaly detection method, which aims to provide fair results
1171   when the algorithm is applied to detect anomalies. Our method currently focus on the binary group
1172   fairness case. We can naturally extend our framework to the multi-attribute case by encouraging
1173   the similarity among the groups. Incoporating individual fairness notions would be an interesting
1174   future direction. By embedding fairness into anomaly detection algorithms, this work contributes
1175   to reducing bias and discrimination in AI applications, ensuring that technologies serve diverse
1176   populations equitably. In sectors such as finance, healthcare, and law enforcement, where anomaly
1177   detection plays a crucial role in identifying fraud, diseases, and criminal activities, incorporating
1178   fairness principles can prevent the perpetuation of historical biases and protect vulnerable groups
1179
       from unjust outcomes. Furthermore, by advancing fairness in AI, this research aligns with global
       efforts to promote ethics in technology development, fostering trust between AI systems and their
1180
       users.
