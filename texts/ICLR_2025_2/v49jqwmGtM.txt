009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider the optimization problem of finding Nash Equilibrium (NE) for a
012            nonconvex function f (x) = f (x1 , ..., xn ), where xi ∈ Rdi denotes the i-th block
013            of the variables. Our focus is on investigating first-order gradient-based algo-
014            rithms and their variations such as the block coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm
015            for tackling this problem. We introduce a set of conditions, termed the n-sided
016            PL condition, which extends the well-established gradient dominance condition
017
               a.k.a Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) condition and the concept of multi-convexity. This
               condition, satisfied by various classes of non-convex functions, allows us to an-
018
               alyze the convergence of various gradient descent (GD) algorithms. Moreover,
019
               our study delves into scenarios where the objective function only has strict saddle
020            points, and normal gradient descent methods fail to converge to NE. In such cases,
021            we propose adapted variants of GD that converge towards NE and analyze their
022            convergence rates.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Optimization problems with nonconvex objectives appear in many applications from computer sci-
028
      ence to economics (Intriligator, 2002) and more recently, in machine learning (Jain et al., 2017),
      such as training deep neural networks (Goodfellow et al., 2016) or policy optimization in reinforce-
029
      ment learning (Silver et al., 2014). On the other hand, the Gradient Descent (GD) algorithm and its
030
      variants are driving the practical success of many machine learning approaches. Naturally, under-
031   standing the limits of such GD-based algorithms in the nonconvex setting has become an important
032   avenue of research in recent years (Jin et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2024; Jordan et al., 2023). Along this
033   line of research, we are interested in finding Nash Equilibrium x⋆ = (x⋆1 , · · · , x⋆n ) for the nonconvex
034   optimization f (x), i.e.
035                                     f (x⋆i ; x⋆−i ) ≤ f (yi ; x⋆−i ), ∀yi ∈ Rdi ,                          (1)
036   where f is a continuously differentiable but possibly nonconvex function. The         Pnvariable x can be
037   partitioned into n blocks (x1 , ..., xn ), where xi ∈ Rdi is the i-th block and i=1 di = d. This
038   optimization problem can be viewed as a potential game between n players. The objective of i-th
039   player is to minimize the function f (xi , x−i ) when other players’ parameters are denoted by x−i .
040
      From a game-theoretic perspective, this is a multi-agent potential game where the potential function
041
      f captures the aggregate impact of all agents’ strategies {xi }ni=1 Monderer & Shapley (1996). Each
042   agent minimizes f over its variables xi , assuming others’ strategies are fixed. However, privacy
043   concerns arise as strategies may reveal sensitive information. In decentralized settings, such as
044   network routing Candogan et al. (2010) or resource allocation (Zhang et al., 2021), agents optimize
045   independently without full knowledge of f or others’ strategies. Furthermore, convergence to an NE
046   is not always stable (Carmona, 2013), as gradient descent may diverge.
047
      For a general nonconvex differentiable function f : Rd → R, finding its NE is PPAD-complete
048   (Daskalakis et al., 2009). A straightforward approach to tackle this problem is to introduce additional
049   structural assumptions to achieve convergence guarantees. Within this scope, various relaxations of
050   convexity have been proposed, for example, weak strong convexity (Liu et al., 2014), restricted
051   secant inequality (Zhang & Yin, 2013), error bound (Cannelli et al., 2020), quadratic growth (Cui
052   et al., 2017), etc. Recently, there has been a surge of interest in analyzing nonconvex functions
053   with block structure. Multiple assumptions have been analyzed which is correlated to each block
      when other blocks are fixed, for example, PL-strongly-concave (Guo et al., 2023), nonconvex-PL

055   multi-convex (Xu & Yin, 2013; Shen et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2019a; 2022b). For instance, the
056   multi-convexity assumes the convexity of the function concerning each block (coordinate) when the

      The nonconvex optimization realm has seen a growing interest in the gradient dominance condition
061   a.k.a. Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) condition. For instance, in analyzing linear quadratic games (Fazel
062   et al., 2018), matrix decomposition (Li et al., 2018), robust phase retrieval (Sun et al., 2018) and
063   training neural networks (Hardt & Ma, 2017; Charles & Papailiopoulos, 2018; Liu et al., 2022). This
064   is due to its ability to enable sharp convergence analysis of both deterministic GD and stochastic GD
065   algorithms while being satisfied by a wide range of nonconvex functions. More formally, a function
066   f satisfies the PL condition if there exists a constant µ > 0 such that
067
                                  ∥∇f (x)∥2 ≥ 2µ(f (x) − min f (y)), ∀x ∈ Rd .                                 (2)
068                                                            y∈Rd

      This was first introduced by Polyak (1963); Lojasiewicz (1963), who analyzed the convergence of
070   the GD algorithm under the PL condition and showed its linear convergence to the global minimum.
071   This condition can be perceived as a relaxation of strong convexity and as discussed in (Karimi
072   et al., 2016), it is closely related to conditions such as weak-strong convexity(Necoara et al., 2019),
073   restricted secant inequality(Zhang & Yin, 2013) and error bound(Luo & Tseng, 1993).
074
      As mentioned, the PL condition has been extended and applied to optimization problems with mul-
075
      tiple coordinates. This extension is analogous to generalizing the concept of convexity (concavity)
076   to convex-concavity. For instance, the two-sided PL condition was introduced in (Yang et al., 2020)
077   for analyzing deterministic and stochastic alternating gradient descent ascent (AGDA) in min-max
078   games. It is noteworthy that most literature requires convexity or PL condition to establish the last-
079   iterate convergence rate to the NE (Scutari et al., 2010; Sohrabi & Azgomi, 2020; Jordan et al.,
080   2024). This, however, may not hold even if the objective function is quadratic. A considerable re-
081   laxation is that the function satisfies strong convexity or PL condition when all variables except one
082   are fixed. Two natural questions arise:
083   Can similar results be achieved by extending the two-sided PL condition to accommodate optimiza-
084   tion problems in the form of equation 1, where the objective comprises n coordinates? And is there
085   an algorithm to guarantee convergence at a linear rate in such problems?
086
      Furthermore, as highlighted by Lee et al. (2016); Panageas & Piliouras (2016); Ahn et al. (2022),
087
      GD with random initialization almost surely escapes the NE point when it is a strict saddle point.
088   Also, Xu & Yin (2013; 2017) require the potential function to be lower-bounded to approach the NE
089   set rather than diverge to infinity. These prompt us to consider the following questions:
090
091
      Is it possible to ensure the convergence to the NE set even though it only contains strict saddle points
      or the function is not lower bounded by using first-order GD-based algorithms?
092
093   Motivated by the questions above, we introduce the notion of n-sided PL1 condition (definition 2.6),
094   which is an extension to the PL condition and shows that it holds in several well-known noncon-
095   vex problems such as n-player linear quadratic game, linear residual network, etc. It is noteworthy
096   that unlike the two-sided PL condition, which guarantees to converge to the unique Nash Equilib-
097
      rium (NE) in min-max optimization (Yang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2022), functions satisfying the
      n-sided PL (even 2-side PL) condition may have multiple NE points (see section 2.1 for exam-
098
      ples). However, as we will discuss, the set of stationary points for such functions is equivalent to
099
      their NE points. Moreover, unlike the two-sided PL condition, which ensures linear convergence
100   of the AGDA algorithm to the NE, the BCD algorithm exhibits varying convergence rates for dif-
101   ferent functions, all satisfying the n-sided PL condition. Similar behavior has been observed with
102   multi-convex functions (Xu & Yin, 2017; Wang et al., 2019a). Therefore, additional local or global
103   conditions are required to characterize the convergence rate under the n-sided PL condition.
104
      In this work, we study the convergence of first-order GD-based algorithms such as the BCD, and pro-
105
      pose different variants of BCD that are more suitable for the class of nonconvex functions satisfying

      Block Coordinate Descent and its variants. Block coordinate descent (BCD) is an efficient and
      reliable gradient-based method for optimization problems in 1 which has been used extensively for
114
      optimization problems in machine learning (Nesterov, 2012; Allen-Zhu et al., 2016; Zhang & Brand,
115
      2017; Zeng et al., 2019; Nakamura et al., 2021). Numerous existing works have studied the con-
116   vergence of BCD and its variants for functions. Most of them require the assumptions of convexity,
117   PL condition, and their extensions (Beck & Tetruashvili, 2013; Hong et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2023;
118   Chen et al., 2023; Chorobura & Necoara, 2023). For instance, Xu & Yin (2013; 2017) studied the
119   convergence of BCD for the regularized block multiconvex optimization. They established the last
120   iterate convergence under Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz which might not hold for many functions globally.
121   The authors in (Lin et al., 2023) considered the generalized Minty variational problem and applied
122   cyclic coordinate dual averaging with extrapolation to find its solution. Their algorithm is indepen-
123   dent of the dimension of the number of coordinates. However, their results rely on assuming the
124
      monotonicity of the operators, which is often hard to satisfy. Cai et al. (2023) considered composite
      nonconvex optimization and applied cyclic block coordinate descent with PAGE-type variance re-
125
      duced method. They proved linear and non-asymptotic convergence when the PL condition holds,
126
      which is not valid for functions with multiple local minima.

129   vexity in the minimization problem sufficient for achieving the global convergence for first-order
130
      methods. For example, Karimi et al. (2016) showed that the standard GD algorithm admits a lin-
      ear convergence to minimize an L-smooth and µ-PL function. To be specific, in order to find an

530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we identified a subclass of nonconvex functions called n-sided PL functions and stud-
533
      ied the convergence of GD-based algorithms, particularly the BCD algorithm, for finding their NEs.
534   The n-sided PL condition is a reasonable extension of the gradient dominance condition, which
535   holds in various problems. We showed that the convergence rate of such first-order algorithms in
536   this subclass of functions depends on a local relation between the function f and the average of
537   the best responses Gf . Subsequently, we proposed two novel algorithms, IA-RBCD and A-RBCD,
538   equipped with Gf , that provably converge to the NE set almost surely with random initialization
539   even if the function is not lower bounded and has strict saddle points. We hope this work can shed
      some light on the understanding of nonconvex optimization.
