012                                                         A BSTRACT
013
014              In recent years, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have significantly
015              advanced, integrating more modalities into diverse applications. However, the
016              lack of explainability remains a major barrier to their use in scenarios requiring
017              decision transparency. Current neuron-level explanation paradigms mainly focus
018              on knowledge localization or language- and domain-specific analyses, leaving the
019
                 exploration of multimodality largely unaddressed. To tackle these challenges, we
                 propose MINER, a transferable framework for mining modality-specific neurons
020
                 (MSNs) in MLLMs, which comprises four stages: ❶ modality separation, ❷ im-
021
                 portance score calculation, ❸ importance score aggregation, ❹ modality-specific
022              neuron selection. Extensive experiments across six benchmarks and two MLLMs
023              show that (I) deactivating ONLY 2% of MSNs significantly reduce MLLMs per-
024              formance (0.56 ∼ 0.24 ↓ for Qwen2-VL, 0.69 ∼ 0.31 ↓ for Qwen2-Audio), (II)
025              different modalities mainly converge in the lower layers, (III) MSNs influence
026              how key information from various modalities converges to the last token, (IV) We
027              observed two intriguing phenomena, semantic probing and semantic telomeres.

074    This raises an intriguing question: Are there modality-specific neurons (MSNs) akin to those in
075    multi-language or multi-domain settings? To address this, we must tackle the following challenges:
076    ❶ How can we measure neuron importance for specific modalities? ❷ What mechanisms do these
077
       neurons use to influence the model? ❸ Can we identify some underlying patterns of MSNs?
078    We propose MINER, a transferable framework that develops new importance metrics for measuring
079    neuron significance and introduces selection strategies for identifying key neurons for each modality.
080    MINER tackles the challenges by: ❶ Decompose neuron importance for modalities into token im-
081    portance from the top down, then restore neuron-modal importance through bottom-up aggregation.
082    ❷ Analyze neuron behavior using feature dimensionality reduction plots and contribution scores of
083
       modality tokens to predictions. ❸ Our experiments revealed semantic probing and semantic telom-

084

085
       community. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:

087

088
089   ☞ We introduce MINER, a transferable framework for selecting MSNs in both vision-based and
090     audio-based MLLMs, capable of handling datasets with any combination of modalities uniformly.

093   ☞ We validate the significance of MSNs through extensive experiments, uncover intriguing phenom-
094     ena of semantic probing and semantic telomeres, and present new insights.

096    2       R ELATED W ORKS
097
098    In this section, we provide a brief overview of knowledge location and neuron analysis work, while
099    the related work for the remaining sections is included in appendix B.1.
100
       Knowledge Localization. Research has examined how factual and commonsense knowledge is
101
       represented in neural networks (Park et al., 2024; Hase et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). For instance,
102    Sukhbaatar et al. (2019) demonstrated that persistent memory vectors can replace feed-forward net-
103    work (FFN) layers in transformers without performance loss. Geva et al. (2020) showed that FFN
104    layers serve as key-value memories, linking training patterns to output vocabulary. Recently, Dai
105    et al. (2021) identified knowledge neurons in FFN layers of pretrained transformers, positively cor-
106    relating their activation with factual knowledge expression. Meng et al. (2022a) and Chen et al.
107    (2024a) further explored knowledge neurons in language models. In this context, our analysis aims
       to identify modality-specific neurons in the FFN layers of MLLMs.


                                                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Neuron Analysis. Neuron analysis in pretrained models is an emerging research area in both
109   computer vision and natural language processing. Recent studies have gone beyond explaining the
110   concepts and knowledge represented by individual neurons (Bau et al., 2017; Oikarinen & Weng,
111   2022; Bills et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024) to identify neurons that respond to specific patterns. For
112   example, Schubert et al. (2021) and Cammarata et al. (2020) found visual neurons that detect high-
113   frequency features or curves in images, while Tang et al. (2024a) and Kojima et al. (2024) analyzed
114   neurons uniquely activated by target languages, termed language-specific neurons, in large language
115   models. In the multimodal domain, research has primarily focused on detecting neurons that respond
116   to both textual and visual inputs (Goh et al., 2021; Schwettmann et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023).
117
      Additionally, Huo et al. (2024) adapted techniques from Tang et al. (2024a) to identify domain-
      specific neurons (e.g., in medicine and remote sensing) in MLLMs. However, the specialization of
118
      neurons in multimodal models is still underexplored. To our knowledge, our work is the first to
119
      analyze modality-specific neurons in MLLMs.

211   4.1           P ROBLEM A NALYSIS
212
213   Motivated by previous research on X-specific neurons, such as language-specific Tang et al. (2024a)
214   and domain-specific neurons Huo et al. (2024), our work focuses on identifying a set of MSNs that
215   are critical for processing multimodal samples in MLLMs. We briefly analyze how our approach
      differs from previous studies in fig. 1 and table 1. We focus on neurons in the FFN of MLLMs rather


                                                                                                                                          4
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
       than other modules, such as modality-specific encoders or projection layers, for several reasons: (I)
217    Previous studies indicate that FFN encode distinct, recoverable knowledge attributes (Geva et al.,
218    2020; Dai et al., 2021; Meng et al., 2022a;b), with some neurons representing the same concepts
219    across different modalities (Schwettmann et al., 2023). (II) Research by Schwettmann et al. (2023)
220    identifies semantic alignment between images and text within the LLM, rather than in the projection
221    layer. (III) Our goal is to identify neurons across modalities within the same set, so we exclude
222    modality-specific encoders. Thus, our research focuses on the FFN module.
223    Our problem can be distilled into two steps: first, measure a neuron’s importance for a specific
224    modality (Stages 1-3), and second, select the most important neurons based on this measure (Stage
225    4). Assessing the importance of ul,n in modality space X is complex due to the infinite number
226    of potential samples. Therefore, we define the importance score between a neuron and samples
227    as ISs (ul,n , x). If a neuron consistently demonstrates importance across many samples, we can
228    conclude it is significant within the modality space. The four stages of MINER align with the
229    sections in fig. 2, outlined as follows:
230   ➠ Stage 1: Modality Separation. Decomposing the information within the LLM by modality.
231
      ➠ Stage 2: Importance Score Calculation. We decompose the importance of neurons to modali-
232
        ties into their token-level significance and define it accordingly.
233
234   ➠ Stage 3: Importance Score Aggregation. We aggregate the token-level importance of neurons
235     to restore their modality-level significance.
236   ➠ Stage 4: Modality-Specific Neuron Selection. The aggregated scores and a selection strategy
237     are used to identify the top-K important MSNs.

410   5.3 N EURON F UNCTIONALITY P RINCIPLES (RQ2)
411
412   This research question focuses on the intrinsic mechanisms of action of MSNs. Several key obser-
413   vations are as follows:
414   Ob1. The audio modality exhibits a “semantic probing” effect towards the text modality,
415   indicating a potential trend toward aligning key information across modalities. We present the
416   feature distribution under three masking settings in fig. 5-(a). The complementary mask is defined
417   as 1 − B. As the layer depth increases, audio embeddings extend “tentacles” toward the embeddings
418   of other modalities, a phenomenon we call “semantic probing.” This suggests that the LLM aligns
419   not the entire modal feature space, but specific key information.
420   Ob2. A “semantic telomeres” phenomenon occurs in special modality, anchoring at the edges
421   of the text modality’s semantic space. In fig. 5-(a), text embeddings initially form circular clusters
422   that elongate into strips in deeper layers. Special tokens progressively align with and stabilize at the
423   front of these clusters, a pattern we refer to as “semantic telomeres.”
424   Ob3. MSNs shape MLLMs behavior by directing how key information from different modality
425   tokens converges into the last token. We apply mask(COCO) to the COCO Caption dataset
426   and calculate each modality’s contribution score to the final prediction based on the accumulated
427   attention between its token set and the last token. As shown in fig. 5-(b), applying a mask for a
428   specific modality results in ∆ < 0, indicating reduced information flow to the last token.

500   5.5 I DENTIFYING PATTERNS IN N EURONS (RQ4)
501   In this section, we investigate potential MSNs patterns through experiments to offer valuable in-
502   sights. Our key observations are as follows:
503
      Ob1. Most MSNs are concentrated in the shallow layers, with only a small portion in the final
504
      layers. As shown in fig. 6, figures (c), (d), and (e) visualize the distribution of modality-specific
505   neurons across layers. This trend aligns with findings in Zhang et al. (2024), indicating that cross-
506   modal perception primarily occurs in the early layers (detailed in appendix B.2). Thus, we believe
507   that different modalities primarily converge in the lower layers.
508
      Ob2. Most modality information stays within the token set, with only a small amount of key
509
      information transferring to other sets. To validate Hypothesis 1 from section 4.2, we measure
510
      information flow using attention values, calculating the cumulative in-set attention and cross-set
511   attention (the diagonal blocks of A). We apply mask(MSVD-QA) to the MSVD-QA dataset, with
512   results shown in fig. 6-(f). Our findings indicate that in-set information flow significantly surpasses
513   cross-set flow, supporting our hypothesis.

515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517
      To our knowledge, this is the first study of modality-specific neurons (MSNs) in MLLMs. We select
      a small set of key neurons from the FFN that are crucial for processing multimodal data and design
518
      experiments to investigate their underlying mechanisms. We address this issue through the following
519
      steps: ❶ Define key concepts related to modalities, samples, and datasets. ❷ Conduct sample-level
520   and token-level analyses to differentiate modality information at a finer granularity, evaluate the
521   limitations of existing importance metrics, and propose new ones. ❸ Calculate importance scores
522   for neurons associated with a set of tokens and aggregate these scores across samples and datasets
523   for a global modality-level analysis. ❹ Define four selection strategies to extract the top-K neurons
524   with the highest importance scores as the output MSNs. In the experimental section, we validate our
525   method by masking the MSNs and observing the resulting decline in model performance, while also
526   identifying two intriguing phenomena: semantic probing and semantic telomeres.
527
      6.1 L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529
      Our work presents several areas for improvement: (I) We introduced the independence hypothesis
      of token sets to separate information by modality; however, key information exchanges between
530
      token sets occur and are difficult to capture. Future research could relax this assumption by incor-
531
      porating cross-set information exchange for potentially better results. (II) Our study focused on
532   vision-related Qwen2-VL and audio-related Qwen2-Audio models, limiting the range of modalities.
533   Future work aims to include broader MLLMs, such as any-to-any models, and datasets with more
534   diverse modalities. (III) We observed two intriguing phenomena—semantic probing and semantic
535   telomeres—whose underlying causes remain unclear, presenting an opportunity for further explo-
536   ration. (IV) The quality of datasets directly influences the quality of identified neurons, which may
537   be linked to data diversity. Future research could investigate this further and design metrics to quan-
538   tify dataset quality. (V) While this work emphasizes identifying important neurons, the next step is
539   to explore how to leverage these neurons to enhance MLLMs performance on relevant modalities,
      potentially through neuron fine-tuning techniques.

      A     DATASET D ETAILS
757
758
      This section presents a detailed description of the datasets used in our evaluation, including data
759
      samples and the prompts provided to MLLMs.
760
761
      A.1   T EXT VQA
762
763   TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019) is a dataset for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that focuses on
764   answering questions about text found in images. It features a variety of images, such as signs and
765   labels, requiring models to combine visual understanding with Optical Character Recognition (OCR)
766   to accurately respond to questions. TextVQA is a key benchmark for evaluating the integration of
767   visual and textual reasoning in AI models.
768
769   A.2   COCO CAPTION
770
771   The COCO Caption dataset (Lin et al., 2014) is a large-scale resource containing over 1.5 million
772   captions that describe more than 330,000 images, distributed across 80 diverse object categories.
773   The images were selected to represent complex, real-world scenes, depicting everyday environ-
774   ments where common objects appear in their natural contexts. Each image is annotated with five
775
      captions, each of which was independently generated by human annotators. COCO Caption dataset
      has been a foundational benchmark for training and evaluating the image captioning performance of
776
      multimodal large language models.
777
778
779
      A.3   MMLU
780
      The Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) (Hendrycks et al., 2020) dataset is a
781
      benchmark for evaluating language models across 57 subjects, ranging from elementary topics to
782   advanced academic fields. It consists of multiple-choice questions testing a model’s knowledge and
783   reasoning across diverse domains. MMLU is widely used to assess the generalization capabilities of
784   large language models.
785
786   A.4   MSVD-QA
787
788   The MSVD (Microsoft Research Video Description Corpus) (Chen & Dolan, 2011), also known as
789   YouTube2Text, consists of 1,970 short videos, each ranging from 10 to 25 seconds with an average
790   duration of 9 seconds. These videos depict a variety of subjects, including people, animals, actions,
791   and different scenes. Each video is annotated with multiple sentences by different annotators, av-
792   eraging around 41 sentences per clip, resulting in a total of 80,839 sentences. On average, each
793
      sentence contains 8 words, with approximately 16,000 unique words across the dataset.
794
795   A.5   L IBRI S PEECH
796
797
      LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) is a widely used dataset for automatic speech recognition
      (ASR) tasks. It contains approximately 1,000 hours of 16kHz English speech, sourced from the
798
      LibriVox audiobooks. The dataset is divided into several subsets, including “clean” and “other”,
799
      which distinguish recordings by noise levels. Each audio file is accompanied by an accurate tran-
800   scription, making it ideal for training and evaluating ASR models. LibriSpeech’s diverse speaker
801   base and detailed annotations also make it suitable for tasks like speaker identification and voice
802   synthesis.
803
804   A.6   VOCAL S OUND
805
806   VocalSound (Gong et al., 2022) is an open dataset containing 21,024 crowdsourced recordings of
807   human vocalizations such as laughter, sighs, coughs, throat clearing, sneezes, and sniffing, from
808   3,365 individuals. Designed for classification tasks, it enables models to accurately identify various
809   non-speech sounds. The dataset also includes metadata like speaker age, gender, native language,
      country, and health status, supporting research on how demographic factors affect vocal sounds.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      With its comprehensive scope and detailed annotations, VocalSound is a valuable resource for im-
811   proving models in human vocalization classification.
