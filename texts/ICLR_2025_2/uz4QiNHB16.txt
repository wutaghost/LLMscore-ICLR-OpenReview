011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Indus Valley Civilization (IVC) left behind an undeciphered script, posing
014            a significant challenge to archaeologists and linguists. This paper introduces
015            FLAIR, a few-shot learning approach that aims to establish a foundational model
016            for recognizing and identifying individual graphemes from the limited available
017
               Indus script. As a foundational model, FLAIR is designed to be versatile, support-
               ing multiple potential applications in script recognition and beyond. It leverages
018
               prototypical networks combined with a modified proposed encoder network for
019
               segmentation, ProtoSegment to extract intricate features from the grapheme im-
020            ages. We evaluate FLAIR’s ability to generalize from minimal data using IVC
021            grapheme classification tasks and further experiment with pre-trained Omniglot
022            models for fine-tuning. Additionally, we simulate real-world data scarcity by in-
023            tentionally restricting training data on the Omniglot dataset. Our experiments
024            demonstrate FLAIR’s accuracy in digitizing and recognizing Indus Valley seal
025            graphemes, outperforming traditional machine learning classification approaches.
026            These results underscore FLAIR’s potential not only for the digitization of ancient
027            scripts with limited labeled datasets but also for broader applications where data
028
               is scarce. FLAIR’s success in grapheme recognition highlights its promise as a
               foundational model capable of extending to other undeciphered writing systems,
029
               thereby contributing to the integration of classic scientific tools and data-driven
030
               approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The history and civilizations of the past are preserved mainly in the languages of the past. But their
036
      field is laborious, requiring specialists to work on a variety of demanding text-based tasks, such as
037   determining the authors of literary works, restoring damaged inscriptions, and translating lost lan-
038   guages Sommerschield et al. (2023). The texts that remain preserved to this day were written in a
039   variety of scripts (Brahmi, Old Chinese, Egyptian hieroglyphs, ancient Greek, Indus, Latin, Mayan,
040   and others) and on a range of materials (bone, metal, palm leaf, paper, papyri, parchment, potsherds,
041   stone, etc.). Technological innovation in machine learning has brought about revolutionary break-
042   throughs in the study of ancient languages and texts over the past 20 years. Modern Handwritten Text
043   Recognition (HTR) methods struggle to recognize manuscripts with uncommon scripts or alphabets
044   Sánchez et al. (2014); Bhunia et al. (2021); Souibgui et al. (2022); Kang et al. (2020). Conventional
045
      machine learning techniques rely on vast quantities of labeled data for training which presents a
      significant challenge for the Indus script due the scarcity of useful data. Generative AI, such as
046
      GANs or diffusion models, can synthesize training data when real examples are scarce. However,
047
      generating synthetic data for ancient scripts like the Indus script is challenging due to the lack of
048   deep knowledge of the script’s visual and contextual nuances, which remain speculative. Without
049   a substantial labeled corpus, generative models trained on limited data may produce unrealistic or
050   misleading samples that fail to capture the script’s true variability. Additionally, generative models
051   require extensive training on diverse examples to generate high-quality outputs, a need that the lim-
052   ited dataset of the Indus script cannot meet. This can lead to artifacts that distort model performance
053   rather than enhance it. Few-Shot Learning (FSL) provides a compelling solution to overcome this
      challenge. Unlike traditional models, FSL excels in precisely the situation we face – limited data.

      These models are specifically designed to learn complex patterns from a remarkably small number
055   of labeled examples per class. There’s a possibility of encountering previously unseen symbols dur-
056   ing the digitization process. The FSL model can potentially adapt and classify these new symbols
057   based on its learned knowledge from similar classes.

      The Indus Valley seals contain intricate patterns of graphemes and motifs, where the script remains
059
      largely undeciphered, hindering our understanding of one of the world’s oldest civilizations Oakes
060
      (2017); Daggumati & Revesz (2021). Unfortunately, despite sustained efforts from archaeologists
061   and linguists, the Indus script remains stubbornly undeciphered Varun Venkatesh & Ali Farghaly
062   (2023).The limited corpus of inscriptions, coupled with the absence of a bilingual Rosetta Stone
063   equivalent, has compelled researchers to explore alternative approaches, such as statistical analy-
064   ses of grapheme sequences, intra-script grapheme associations, and contextual clues derived from
065   archaeological artifacts Rao et al. (2009; 2010; 2015). These manual efforts, while insightful, are
066   labor-intensive, time-consuming, and limited in scalability. Furthermore, the existing collection of
067   Indus Valley texts is frustratingly limited.
068   In this context, FLAIR is introduced as a foundational model for ancient script recognition, ad-
069   dressing a significant gap in the field. To the best of our knowledge, there is no widely recognized
070   foundational model specifically tailored for OCR or grapheme recognition that matches the versatil-
071   ity and adaptability seen in foundation models from other domains, such as NLP or general image
072   processing. While existing few-shot learning (FSL) architectures, like Prototypical Networks Snell
073   et al. (2017), are designed to classify new instances based on their similarity to learned prototypes,
074   they may struggle to capture the intricate features and complexities of ancient script characters, par-
075
      ticularly when training data is limited. To address these limitations, we introduce ProtoSegment, a
      novel few-shot learning approach that enhances prototypical networks with a segmentation encoder.
076
      This modification enables the model to extract intricate features from graphemes (individual charac-
077
      ters) in the Indus Valley script, leading to improved identification. By incorporating a segmentation
078   encoder, ProtoSegment can better capture the subtle details and variations within each character
079   class, even with limited training data. The segmentation encoder in ProtoSegment is designed to
080   identify and segment individual graphemes within the script. This segmentation process allows the
081   model to focus on the relevant visual features of each character, improving its ability to distinguish
082   between different classes. To evaluate the effectiveness of ProtoSegment, we conduct experiments
083   on two datasets. We have utilized the Omniglot dataset, a rich collection of handwritten characters
084   and mirrored the real-world data constraints of the IVC script by intentionally restricting it. This
085   controlled setting allows us to assess the model’s ability to generalize effectively with minimal data,
086
      simulating the IVC grapheme recognition task. Our results demonstrate that ProtoSegment out-
      performs existing few-shot learning and deep learning methods on both datasets, achieving higher
087
      accuracy in grapheme classification tasks.

      Few-Shot Learning with Limited Data: Recent years have seen various methods developed for
      learning deep networks with scarce data. Taigman et al. Taigman et al. (2014) and Koch et al. Koch
094
      et al. (2015) approached this as a verification problem, using Siamese neural networks Bromley
095
      et al. (1993) to determine whether two samples belong to the same class by measuring the distance
096   between them in the learned embedding space. Huang et al. Huang et al. (2019) introduced Deep
097   Prototypical Networks (DPN) to address data insufficiency and class imbalance by capturing dis-
098   crepancies across classes in a main embedding space. DPN was further enhanced with a masking
099   module for robust classification, though it does not yet incorporate external knowledge sources. Re-
100   searchers Pahde et al. (2021) have also designed a cross-modal feature generation framework that
101   enriches low-population embedding spaces in few-shot scenarios by mapping text data into the vi-
102   sual feature space using generative models. Ji et al. Ji et al. (2020) proposed Improved Prototypical
103   Networks (IPN), incorporating an attention-based strategy to better capture intra-class distribution
104
      by assigning weights to samples based on their representativeness.
105   Data-Level Approaches: A more logical approach to few-shot learning is to use a data-level ap-
106   proach, which means that by gathering more relevant data, the model’s performance can potentially
107   be enhanced. In addition to the initial training set, Douze et al. Douze et al. (2018) developed a
      semi-supervised strategy that incorporated a sizable unlabeled dataset of comparable images. This

      vast data collection was used in the few-shot learning scenario to facilitate label propagation. By
109   creating the squared gradient magnitude loss, which drives models to generalize successfully from
110   only a few samples, Hariharan et al. Hariharan & Girshick (2017) merged both strategies (data-
111   level and algorithm-level) and, created new images by hallucinating features. In order to provide
112   new training data for the latter, they trained a model to identify common transformations between
113   preexisting images.

      Meta-Learning Techniques: Meta-learning techniques have been used in other contemporary
115   few-shot learning methodologies. In a few-shot learning environment, a long short-term memory
116   (LSTM) network was trained as a meta-learner Ravi & Larochelle (2016) to learn the precise op-
117   timization technique for training a learner neural network that carries out the classification. The
118   discovery that the update function of common optimization techniques, such as SGD, is comparable
119   to the updating of an LSTM’s cell state led to the proposal of this technique. Finn et al. Finn et al.
120   (2017) proposed a model-agnostic meta-learning technique (MAML) that trains a model on base
121   classes and then refines it on a limited number of unique classes to achieve optimal performance.
122   Furthermore, using a few-shot learning technique, Bertinetto et al. Bertinetto et al. (2016) trained a
123
      meta-learner feed-forward neural network to predict the parameters of another discriminative feed-
      forward neural network.
124
125   Attention Mechanisms: Another technique that has been applied successfully to few-shot learning
126   recently is attention Wang et al. (2022); Vaswani et al. (2017). To identify prototypes, Arık and
127   Pfister Arik & Pfister (2020) use an attention mechanism that compares the encoded representations
128   to samples. By adding a relational attention mechanism to an encoder, prototypical learning enables
129
      novel capabilities. Sparsemax attention increases robustness to label noise and allows for the basis of
      learning on a small number of relevant samples that may be returned at inference for interpretability.
130
131   Prototypical Learning: The principle of ProtoSegment is inspired by (Badrinarayanan et al. (2017);
132   Feng et al. (2021); Chang et al. (2020)) where they emphasize discarding the fully connected layers
133   in favour of retaining higher resolution feature maps at the deepest encoder output. Similar modifi-
134   cations have been made for prototypical networks based on varying application domain spaces Arik
135
      & Pfister (2020); Ji et al. (2020); Tang et al. (2023); Ke et al. (2021); Du et al. (2023).
136   Ancient Script Recognition Approaches: There have been a few approaches developed, with ref-
137   erence to few-shot learning approaches for Ancient Script Recognition. Hu et al. Wenbo Hu et al.
138   (2023) proposed a Visually Guided Text Spotting (VGTS) approach that accurately spots novel char-
139   acters using just one annotated support sample. Souibgui et al. Mohamed Ali Souibgui et al. (2020)
140   use few-shot object detection for the task of handwritten ciphers recognition. The method includes
141
      detection of all symbols of a given alphabet in a line image, and then a decoding step to map the
      symbol similarity scores to the final sequence of transcribed symbols. They use the Omniglot dataset
142
      Yang Li et al. (2021) to create synthetic query lines that simulate handwritten ciphered lines. The
143
      study by Varun Venkatesh et al. Venkatesh & Farghaly (2023) investigated the Indus script by ana-
144   lyzing patterns and positions of individual signs, pairs, and sequences. They built statistical models
145   and algorithms to predict sign behavior based on their position. This analysis revealed significant
146   differences in the language used in Indus texts from West Asia compared to those from the Indian
147   subcontinent, suggesting distinct regional dialects within the Indus civilization. Ansari et al. Ansari
148   et al. while being not directly related to deep learning, provides comparative visual analysis with
149   valuable insights for future deep learning approaches. By comparing the visual features of Indus
150   symbols with those from other writing systems, researchers Rao et al. (2009) had bearings on iden-
151   tifying potential similarities in form or structure. This comparative analysis can inform the design
152
      of deep learning models by highlighting specific visual characteristics that the model might focus
      on when analyzing Indus script characters. Palaniappan & Adhikari (2017) Palaniappan & Adhikari
153
      (2017) address the time-consuming task of creating standardized corpora for undeciphered scripts
154
      like the Indus Valley Script. They propose a deep learning pipeline to automate this process. The
155   pipeline segments images into regions, classifies them as textual or not, refines textual regions, iso-
156   lates individual symbols, and classifies them based on a reference corpus. While achieving 92%
157   accuracy for identifying a specific symbol, this work demonstrates the initial potential of deep learn-
158   ing to expedite corpus creation and advance research on the Indus Valley Script.

      5   L IMITATIONS AND S OCIETAL I MPACT
465
466
      While ProtoSegment demonstrates strong performance in grapheme identification, the confusion
467
      matrix (Figure 6) reveals a limitation in distinguishing between visually similar graphemes. For
468
      instance, M373 is frequently misclassified as M296, and M228 is often confused with M51. This
469   suggests that the model may struggle to capture subtle differences in stroke patterns or shapes, es-
470   pecially when graphemes share similar overall structures. Addressing this limitation could involve
471   incorporating additional features, such as contextual information or stroke order, to enhance the
472   model’s ability to discriminate between visually similar characters. Another potential avenue for
473   improvement lies in exploring alternative segmentation encoder architectures or incorporating at-
474   tention mechanisms to focus on the most discriminative features of each grapheme. The model’s
475   performance is inherently tied to the quality and diversity of the training data. In cases where the
476   available data is limited or biased, the model’s ability to generalize to unseen graphemes might be
477
      compromised. Additionally, the model’s reliance on prototypical representations assumes a degree
      of visual similarity within each grapheme class. However, variations in handwriting styles and po-
478
      tential degradation of ancient inscriptions could introduce challenges for accurate recognition. From
479
      a societal impact perspective, FLAIR’s potential to aid in deciphering ancient scripts like the Indus
480   Valley script is significant. By automating and accelerating the process of grapheme identification,
481   FLAIR could contribute to a deeper understanding of ancient civilizations, their languages, and their
482   cultural practices. However, it’s crucial to approach the interpretation of deciphered texts with cau-
483   tion, as misinterpretations (e.g., between stylistic variation of a grapheme as a different one) could
484   have unintended consequences for historical narratives and cultural heritage. Upon acceptance of
485   this paper, we will release FLAIR, including the source code, pre-trained model weights, and rele-
      vant documentation, on GitHub to ensure transparency and reproducibility of our results.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In conclusion, this paper presents FLAIR as not only a novel approach for Indus script grapheme
489   identification but also as a potential foundational model for OCR. By leveraging few-shot learn-
490   ing and incorporating the modified segmentation encoder network (ProtoSegment), FLAIR demon-
491   strates the capability to achieve state-of-the-art performance even with the limited labeled data avail-
492   able for the Indus script. The model’s ability to generalize from minimal examples and its potential
493   adaptability to unseen symbols position it as a powerful tool for not only digitizing and analyzing
494   ancient scripts but also potentially contributing to their decipherment. The development and eval-
495   uation of FLAIR on the curated IVC dataset establishes a benchmark for future research, inviting
496   further exploration and refinement. The insights gained from FLAIR’s performance can inform the
497
      design of future models, potentially incorporating additional features like contextual information or
      stroke order to further enhance recognition accuracy. By automating and accelerating the process
498
      of grapheme identification, FLAIR can significantly contribute to the field of digital humanities, in-
499
      cluding paleography, epigraphy, and historical linguistics, enabling researchers to efficiently process
500   and analyze large volumes of textual data. This could lead to new discoveries and interpretations of
501   ancient texts, shedding light on the languages, cultures, and histories of past civilizations. While ac-
502   knowledging the limitations related to data quality and variability, FLAIR’s contribution to cultural
503   heritage preservation and its potential for broader applications in deciphering undeciphered writing
504   systems are significant.
