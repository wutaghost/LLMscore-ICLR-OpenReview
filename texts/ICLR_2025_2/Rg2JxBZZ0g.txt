009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder affecting
012            millions worldwide, with its prevalence expected to rise as the global population
013            ages. Early diagnosis is crucial for effective management and improved quality
014            of life for patients. However, current accelerometer-based studies focus more on
015            detecting the symptoms of PD, while less research has been conducted on early de-
016
               tection of PD. This study presents a novel multi-modal deep learning model named
               GeneMamba for early PD diagnosis, using state space modelling approaches to
017
               effectively analyze sequences and combining accelerometer data from wearable
018
               devices with genetic variants data. Our model predicts early PD occurrence up
019            to 7 years before clinical onset, outperforming existing methods. Furthermore,
020            through knowledge transfer, we enable accurate PD prediction using only wear-
021            able device data, enhancing our model’s real-world applicability. Additionally,
022            our interpretation methods uncover both established and previously unidentified
023            genes associated with PD, advancing our understanding of the disease’s genetic
024            architecture and potentially highlighting new therapeutic targets. Our approach
025            not only advances early PD diagnosis but also offers insights into the disease’s
026            etiology, paving the way for improved risk assessment and personalized interven-
027
               tions.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that affects millions of indi-
032   viduals globally. With over 8.5 million people worldwide living with PD and approximately 90,000
033   people diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease each year in the United States, it represents a significant
034   public health challenge and a substantial burden on healthcare systems (Bhidayasiri et al., 2024;
035   Willis et al., 2022). PD is characterized by motor symptoms such as tremors, rigidity, and bradyki-
036   nesia, as well as non-motor symptoms including cognitive impairment, sleep disorders, and depres-
037   sion (Sveinbjornsdottir, 2016). As the global population ages, the prevalence of PD is expected to
038
      rise, underscoring the urgent need for improved diagnostic and treatment strategies.
039   Early diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease allows for timely intervention, which can slow disease pro-
040   gression and help manage symptoms more effectively (Emamzadeh & Surguchov, 2018). By identi-
041   fying at-risk individuals during the prodromal phase, healthcare providers can initiate targeted mon-
042   itoring and personalized treatment plans, potentially improving long-term patient outcomes (de Bie
043   et al., 2020). Moreover, early diagnosis enables patients and their families to better prepare for
044
      the challenges associated with the disease, including planning for future care needs and accessing
      support services.
045
046   Despite the importance of early detection, current diagnostic methods for PD often rely on clinical
047   observations of motor symptoms, which typically manifest when significant neuronal loss has al-
048   ready occurred. This highlights the need for innovative approaches to identify PD in its preclinical
049   or early stages.
050   Recent advancements have opened new avenues for early PD prediction, particularly in wearable
051   devices and deep learning. The integration of accelerometer data and deep learning holds promise
052   for enhancing the accuracy and timeliness of PD diagnosis. Accelerometer data, which can capture
053   subtle changes in movement patterns, has shown potential for detecting early motor manifestations
      of PD (Borzı̀ et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2021). In addition, we propose combining accelerometer

054
      data with genetic data to identify patients at high risk of developing PD from an early stage. The
055   incorporation of genetic data in PD prediction models offers the opportunity to uncover new insights
056   into the disease’s etiology. While several genetic variants have been found to be associated with
057   PD risks, many aspects of the genetic architecture of the disease remain unknown. Interpretation
058   methods applied to genetic data could help identify novel genetic variants related to PD, contributing
059   to our understanding of the disease mechanisms and potentially revealing new therapeutic targets.
060
      In this paper, we propose a multi-modal deep learning model, GeneMamba, that first applies Mamba
061   to accelerometer data with cross-modality fusion for the early prediction of PD seven years before
062   clinical onset. By integrating the diverse data sources, we not only build an early PD prediction
063   model, predicting up to 7 years before clinical onset, but also identify novel genes related to PD,
064   helping to identify individuals at risk in advance. Our main contributions are as follows:
065
      1. We propose the first application of Mamba, a Structured State Space Sequence model, to ac-
066
      celerometer data, combined with genetic data through cross-modality fusion for early PD prediction,
067   outperforming existing methods up to seven years before clinical onset.
068
069   2. Our model leverages cross-modality to harness diverse data sources for accurate early PD pre-
070
      diction. Furthermore, recognizing that genetic data are often challenging to obtain, we employ
      knowledge distillation to transfer insights from the complex genetic information to more accessible
071
      accelerometer data, enabling our model to maintain high prediction accuracy while using wearable
072
      device data alone, and enhancing its practical utility in developing real-world health monitoring
073   systems for early detection and intervention.
074
075   3. Our interpretation methods reveal both existing genes related to PD and novel genes not previ-
076
      ously identified, helping to identify individuals at higher risk of developing PD.

      PD prediction has gathered significant attention in recent years. Researchers have employed various
      methods and modalities for the classification and prediction of PD, ranging from neuroimaging
082
      techniques to handwriting analysis and vocal feature extraction. This section provides an overview
083
      of the current approaches in PD-related research.
084
085   Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) have shown promise in PD prediction. Shu et al. (2021) ex-
086   tracted white matter features from structural MRI scans and combined Support Vector Machine
087
      (SVM) and logistic regression algorithms to classify between stable PD and progressive PD. Their
      model achieved an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.836, demonstrating the potential of MRI-based
088
      features in predicting PD progression. Handwriting analysis has emerged as another valuable tool
089
      for PD prediction. Li et al. (2022) proposed a Continuous Convolution Network (CC-Net) to distin-
090   guish between healthy individuals and PD patients based on handwriting samples, with an average
091   AUC of 0.934 and an accuracy of 0.893. Speech impairment is a common symptom in PD, making
092   vocal feature analysis a relevant area of study. Quan et al. (2022) developed a method that extracts
093   time series features from speech signals and processes them using time-distributed two-dimensional
094   convolutional neural networks (2D-CNNs) and a one-dimensional CNN (1D-CNN) for PD detec-
095   tion. Their approach achieved an accuracy of up to 0.92 on one of the speech tasks, demonstrating
096   the potential of vocal biomarkers in PD detection.
097   Wearable devices offer a non-invasive and accessible method of collecting movement data for PD
098   prediction and symptom detection. Several studies have focused on specific PD symptoms using
099   these devices. Freezing of Gait (FOG), a debilitating symptom commonly associated with PD, has
100   been a focus of such studies. Borzı̀ et al. (2023) utilized a single inertial sensor attached to the waist
101   to collect accelerometer data and applied a multi-head convolutional neural network to predict FOG.
102   Their model achieved an AUC of 0.946. Hand tremor is another common symptom of PD. Sun et al.
103   (2021) proposed a method using data collected from a wrist sensor and an 8-layer convolutional
104
      neural network (CNN) to classify PD rest, postural, and action tremors. Their approach achieved an
      accuracy over 0.95.
105
106   While much research has focused on detecting PD and its symptoms, early prediction of PD re-
107   mains a challenging and less-studied area. Schalkamp et al. (2023) addressed this gap by using
      accelerometer data from the UK Biobank dataset to predict PD up to seven years before clinical


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      diagnosis. Employing machine learning methods, they achieved a mean Area Under the Precision-
109   Recall Curve (AUPRC) of 0.78 in distinguishing prodromal PD from matched controls. This study
110   highlights the potential of longitudinal accelerometer data in early PD prediction.
111
112
      In summary, the field of PD symptom detection and prediction has seen significant advancements
      across various modalities, including MRI, handwriting images, speech signals, and wearable device
113
      data. While each approach shows potential, the integration of multiple modalities and the focus on
114
      early prediction still remain areas that need further exploration. Our study bridges the gap between
115   deep learning and early PD prediction by proposing a multi-modal model, namely GeneMamba, that
116   can accurately predict PD seven years before its first diagnosis. Our model effectively integrates ac-
117   celerometer and genetic data through Mamba and cross-modality fusion, and further incorporates
118   a knowledge distillation approach, enabling fine-grained early PD prediction with enhanced real-
119   world applicability. Furthermore, we employ interpretability methods to provide insights into the
120   model’s decision-making process, thus supporting more informed clinical decision-making. This
121   methodology not only advances the field of early PD prediction research, reaching an AUPRC of
122   0.859 in predicting early PD, but also reveals genes that offer potential targets for therapeutic inter-
123
      vention and biomarker development, enabling early detection and personalized treatment strategies
      for individuals at risk of developing PD.

160   Structured State Space Model (S4) is a framework used to represent the dynamics of a system
161   through state variables, inputs, and outputs (Gu et al., 2022). S4 represents a system’s underly-
      ing state as a vector that evolves over time according to a set of equations, while observations are


                                                                                                                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      treated as functions of this state. S4 consists of two main components: a state transition equation
163   that describes how the system’s state changes from one time point to the next, and an observation
164   equation that relates the hidden state to observable measurements. By representing the system’s state
165   at each time step and modeling how it evolves over time through state transition equations, S4 can
166   effectively handle long-range dependencies and continuous processes inherent in time-series data,
167   making it suitable for processing time-series data. The formula of S4 is shown below:
168
                                              ẋ(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t)
169
                                              y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)
170
171   where u(t) is the input vector, x(t) is the state vector, ẋ(t) represents the time derivative of the state
172   vector, y(t) is the output vector, and A, B, C, and D are matrices that define the system dynamics.
173   Mamba is a State Space Model with a novel selection mechanism. Unlike Transformer, which rely
174   heavily on attention mechanisms, Mamba employs a selective state space mechanism that dynam-
175   ically adjusts its parameters based on the input sequence (Gu & Dao, 2023). Mamba replaces the
176   complex attention blocks of Transformer with a state space block, leading to faster inference and
177   lower computational complexity. Additionally, the selection mechanism makes the model not only
178   more efficient but also capable of filtering out less relevant data, focusing on crucial information
179   from the sequences. These designs make Mamba a promising backbone model in long sequence
180
      modeling tasks.

      In this study, we present a deep learning model mainly composed of three modules: the Accelerom-
184   eter Encoder, the Gene Encoder, and the Fusion Module, as shown in Figure 1. The Accelerome-
185   ter Encoder processes the 3-day time-series accelerometer data collected from subjects in the UK

191   and gene variants, we introduce a Cross-Attention Fusion module (Lin et al., 2022). The outputs of

      this module are then passed through linear layers to compute the probability of the subject belonging

204   Accelerometer Encoder
205   The Accelerometer Encoder processes time-series acceleration intensity data collected over a 3-
206   day period at 5-second intervals, represented by Xacc ∈ Rt . The initial 1D CNN layer serves
207   to extract low-level features and significantly reduce the input dimension with a stride of 7. This
208   dimensionality reduction is crucial for efficient processing of the time-series data, as considerable
209   noise present in the collected data.
210   The initial 1D CNN layer is followed by a stack of ResMamba blocks. The ResMamba block in-
211   tegrates a Mamba block with a Residual block, leveraging the strengths of both architectures. The
212   Mamba block excels at processing temporal relationships in time-series data, effectively handling
213   long-range dependencies and focusing on crucial information. Complementing this, the Residual
214   block is proficient in processing spatial information, aiding in noise reduction and the identification
215   of important time periods related to the PD manifestations. By alternating these structures, the net-
      work can simultaneously learn spatial and temporal features at multiple scales, which is particularly

      effective for processing the complex patterns inherent in acceleration data. We gradually increase
217   the size of the feature embeddings from 64 to 512, allowing the network to capture increasingly
218   abstract representations of the input data. The proposed architecture enables the network to capture
219   and process the diverse dynamics of acceleration intensity data more comprehensively, addressing
220   both the spatial and temporal aspects of the input while filtering out irrelevant information.

      Gene Encoder
222
223   We propose Gene Encoder to process the genetic data obtained from the genome-wide association
224   study (GWAS) results. The input data is a 2D matrix, represented by Xgene ∈ Rn×m , where
225   n represents the number of Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs) identified as significant in
226
      GWAS, and m represents a region of SNPs within the linkage disequilibrium (LD) range of each
      significant SNP.
227
228   LD is a common phenomenon in genetics where alleles at different loci are inherited together more
229   frequently, which means that the presence of a specific allele at one SNP can predict the presence
230   of a specific allele from another SNP if the two SNPs are in LD (Slatkin, 2021). However, the
231   functionally relevant or associated SNPs may not necessarily be at the center of the LD region, but
232
      could occur at any position within the LD range.
233   To address this positional variability, we leveraged the spatial invariance property of 2D CNNs,
234   enabling the model to detect significant patterns regardless of their location within each m-SNP
235   window. We implemented this approach by treating the n × m gene map as a 2D feature map of
236   depth 1 and setting the kernel size to 1 × M . The kernel size of 1 along the n dimension ensures in-
237   dependent processing of each LD window, while M accommodates the positional variability within
238
      the LD region, thereby preserving the unique information of each genetic locus. This architecture
      allows the CNN to effectively capture LD patterns across various positions in the genetic sequence
239
      while maintaining the ability to differentiate between individual SNPs and their associated LD re-
240
      gions, thus improving the model’s ability to identify functionally relevant genetic variations. The 2D
241   CNN also serves to reduce dimensionality along the m axis, which is essential because LD regions
242   often contain a considerable amount of noise and redundant information, as many genetic variants
243   present do not have a strong association with the trait or disease of interest.
244
      Following the CNN layers, an LSTM layer aggregates the processed genetic information into vec-
245
      tors. Our architecture enables Gene Encoder to effectively process patterns in genetic data, mitigat-
246
      ing the noise while extracting and enhancing the most informative features in each LD region.

      4.2   P ROCESSING
325
326   GWAS is a widely-adopted method used to scan the entire genome for genetic variations, particularly
327   SNPs, to identify associations with specific traits or diseases. To identify significant SNPs associated
328   with PD, we conducted GWAS study using PLINK on the imputed genotype dataset from the UKBB
329   (Weeks, 2010). The primary phenotype was PD diagnosis status. To filter out significant SNPs, we
330   applied a p-value threshold of 1e-5, which is commonly used in exploratory GWAS analyses.
331   LD plays an important role in genetic studies, and therefore we expanded our selection to include
332   the 200 nearest SNPs for each significant SNP, corresponding to approximately 65 kilobases (kb)
333   of genomic distance. This approach allows for a more comprehensive examination of potentially
334   relevant genetic regions. Our analysis yielded 590 significant SNPs from the initial PLINK output.
335   The resulting dataset was structured as a three-dimensional array with dimensions 590 x 200 x 3,
336   where 590 represents the number of significant SNPs, 200 represents the number of nearest SNPs,
337   and 3 represents the probabilities for each possible genotype (homozygous for the reference allele,
338
      heterozygous, and homozygous for the alternative allele). We then flattened the last dimension of
      the array, forming the input Xgene ∈ Rn×m , where n is 590 and m is 600.
339
340   Following the Sample Selection and Processing steps, we obtained 215 PD samples, 284 PPD sam-
341   ples, and 499 healthy controls. Each healthy control was paired with either a PD or PPD sample.
342   For data augmentation, we selected a 3-day length for the accelerometer data and applied a 3-hour
343   sliding window to sample the data. The resulting dimension of the accelerometer data, Xacc , is
344
      51,840.

359   5.1   I MPLEMENTATION
360
361   Our model was implemented using PyTorch 2.0 and trained on a server with 128 GB of memory
362
      and an NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. The initial training objective was to classify PD from HC, after
      which the model was fine-tuned to predict PPD from HC. During the training stage, we employed
363
      the Adam optimizer with a weight decay of 1e-5. We utilized cosine annealing warm restarts, setting
364
      the initial learning rate to 1e-3 and the minimum learning rate to 1e-5 (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017).

367   underwent a 5-fold cross validation.

413   5.3   I NTERPRETATION
414
415   In our interpretation study, we first identified 590 significant SNPs using a p-value threshold of 1e-5
416   from the GWAS results. We then applied GradCAM++ to investigate which genes our model focuses
417   on (Chattopadhay et al., 2018). Table 2 lists the top 50 genes identified by GWAS and GradCAM++,
418   sorted in descending order of importance based on their respective values. We identified genes
419   that have previously been linked to PD, according to the GeneCards database (Safran et al., 2010).
420   Notably, several genes, including DPP6, SNCA, and MAPT, were identified by both methods and
421   have been previously linked to PD in existing literature (Li et al., 2024; Konno et al., 2016; Zabetian
422   et al., 2007). Additionally, we found genes such as AKAP6, UVRAG, SAMD8, and GRID2 that
423
      were highlighted by both GradCAM++ and GWAS but have been less frequently associated with PD
      in previous studies. Among these findings, SOS1 and UVRAG emerged as the top genes identified
424
      by GradCAM++ and GWAS, respectively. UVRAG, a key regulator of autophagy and endosomal
425
      trafficking, may contribute to PD pathogenesis through impaired clearance of protein aggregates and
426   dysfunctional mitochondria, potentially exacerbating neuronal dysfunction and death (Yin et al.,
427   2011). The SOS1 gene, encoding a guanine nucleotide exchange factor for RAS proteins, may
428   contribute to PD through its involvement in EGFR-mediated neuroprotective signaling pathways
429   and potential interaction with LRRK2, a major genetic risk factor for the disease (Chardin et al.,
430   1993). The combined effects of UVRAG’s role in cellular quality control and SOS1’s influence on
431   neuroprotective signaling pathways may represent a novel axis in the complex molecular landscape
      of PD, offering potential targets for therapeutic intervention and biomarker development.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
433
434
      In this paper, we introduced GeneMamba, a model integrating genetic and accelerometer data for
435   the early prediction of PD. Our model achieved an AUPRC of 0.943 in classifying PD subjects from
436   HC, and an AUPRC of 0.859 in predicting PPD cases up to 7 years before clinical diagnosis. By
437   employing a knowledge transfer approach, we enhanced the performance of our model utilizing ac-
438   celerometer data only, which is easier to obtain through wearable devices and has greater real-world
439   applicability. Furthermore, our GWAS analysis revealed several significant genes associated with
440   PD, while GradCAM++ provided insights into the genes prioritized by our model. These results
441   were consistent with previous studies, confirming the importance of several genes already known
442   to be significant in PD. Additionally, our methods identified genes that have been less frequently
443
      associated with PD in previous research, namely UVRAG and SOS1, which emerged as top genes
      from GradCAM++ and GWAS analyses, respectively. These findings suggest potential new avenues
444
      for PD prevention, although further investigation is required to confirm their relevance to the dis-
445
      ease. Overall, GeneMamba represents a novel method for the early detection of PD, combining
446   accelerometer and genetic data to improve PD prediction and potentially uncover novel genetic fac-
447   tors associated with PD. This approach could contribute significantly to early intervention strategies
448   and personalized medicine in the field of neurodegenerative diseases.
