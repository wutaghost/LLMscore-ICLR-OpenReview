009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Random embedding assumes an important role in representation learning. Gaus-
012            sian embedding and Rademacher embedding are two widely used random em-
013            beddings. Although they usually enjoy robustness and effectiveness, their com-
014            putational complexity is high, i.e. O(nk) for embedding an n-dimensional vector
015            into k-dimensional space. The alternatives include partial subsampled randomized
016            Hadamard (P-SRHT) embedding and sparse sign embedding, which are still not
017            of linear complexity or cannot run efficiently in practical implementation. In this
018            paper, a fast and robust Rademacher-like embedding (RLE) is proposed, based
019
               on a smaller Rademacher matrix and several auxiliary random arrays. Specifi-
               cally, it embeds an n-dimensional vector into k-dimensional space in just O(n)
020                                                                 1

021
               time and space (assuming k is not larger than O(n 2 )). Our theoretic analysis re-
               veals that the proposed RLE owns most of desirable properties of the Rademacher
022
               embedding while preserving lower complexity. To validate the practical efficiency
023
               and effectiveness, the proposed RLE is applied to single-pass randomized singular
024            value decomposition (single-pass RSVD) for streaming data, and the randomized
025            Arnoldi process based on sketched ordinary least-squares. Numerical experiments
026            show that, with the proposed RLE the single-pass RSVD achieves 1.7x speed-up
027            on average while keeping same or better accuracy, and the randomized Arnodli
028            process enables a randomized GMRES algorithm running 1.3x faster on average
029            for solving Ax = b than that based on other embeddings.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Random embedding, which projects high-dimensional vectors into low-dimensional vectors while
035   still maintaining key properties, serves as a core tool for many fundamental representation learning
036   and other related fields Woodruff (2014); Martinsson & Tropp (2020). Therefore, efficient and
037   effective random embedding has attracted numerous research attention in recent years Tropp et al.
038
      (2019); Liberty (2013); Li et al. (2023). Notice that a synonym of random embedding is sketching,
      and random embedding has the same meaning as sketching in this work.
039
040   For handling high-dimensional data, random embedding is a required technique. Take singular value
041   decomposition (SVD) as an example. When the dimension of data reaches millions or even billions,
042   time cost and memory cost of classical SVD algorithm are intolerable. To tackle this problem,
043   randomized SVD (RSVD) utilizes random embedding to project the high-dimensional data into
044
      much lower-dimensional space and then performs the decomposition Martinsson & Tropp (2020);
      Tropp et al. (2019); Yu et al. (2018); Feng et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2017)Musco & Musco (2015).
045
      Another example is Arnoldi process, which can be leveraged for obtaining eigenvalues/eigenvectors
046
      or solving linear equation systems. For large-scale data, the randomized Arnoldi process based on
047   embedding is more preferable for both efficiency and robustness Balabanov & Grigori (2022).
048
049   The efficiency of random embedding plays an indispensable role. For example, random embedding
050
      in the single-pass RSVD Tropp et al. (2019) takes up majority of computing time. Besides, slow
      random embedding in the randomized Arnoldi process Balabanov & Grigori (2022) can lead to even
051
      worse performance than classical Arnoldi process.
052
053   Gaussian embedding is probably the most commonly-used embedding algorithm Gourru et al.
      (2022); He et al. (2015); Ren et al. (2016); Yüksel et al. (2021). It enjoys very good robustness,


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and can be regarded as the most natural and simplest embedding algorithm Martinsson & Tropp
055   (2020). In RSVD algorithms, Gaussian embedding is often adopted. Rademacher embedding is also
056   a commonly-used embedding algorithm Verbin & Zhang (2012); Rakhshan & Rabusseau (2022). It
057   can achieve similar embedding performance to Gaussian embedding, although its theoretic bounds
058   may be a little bit worse than Gaussian embedding Martinsson & Tropp (2020) in certain scenarios.
059   Gaussian embedding and Rademacher embedding are both dense embedding, which enjoys good
060   robustness and little risk of embedding distortion. Their main shortcoming is that the complexity of
061   projecting an n-dimensional vector into a k-dimensional vector is O(nk).
062   To make embedding more efficient, fast transform techniques and several sparse embedding algo-
063   rithms have been proposed Tropp et al. (2019); Martinsson & Tropp (2020). Partial subsampled
064   randomized Hadamard (P-SRHT) embedding adopts fast Hadamard transform to make accelera-
065   tion. For an n × k P-SRHT embedding matrix, time complexity of applying embedding one time
066   can be O(n log n) or O(n log(2k)). Sparse sign embedding is another alternative option. Notice
067   that Count Sketch is a special kind of sparse sign embedding, with the highest sparsity. For small
068   k, Count Sketch can lead to disastrous performance in practice Martinsson & Tropp (2020); Tropp
069
      et al. (2019), and so does the very sparse random projection method Li et al. (2006). An improved
      version of sparse sign embedding for practical applications was proposed in Tropp et al. (2019). For
070
      an n × k sparse sign matrix, time complexity of applying embedding one time can be O(n). How-
071
      ever, the trade-off of robustness and efficiency may restrict the application of sparse sign embedding.
072   Moreover, it relies on sparse data structure and arithmetic which may affect actual efficiency due to
073   irregular cache visiting Martinsson & Tropp (2020).
074
075
      The aim of this work is to resolve the challenge of performing robust random embedding in lower
      complexity and practical efficiency. The main contribution of this paper can be concluded as follows.
076
077   (1) A Rademacher-like random embedding (RLE) approach is proposed, which utilizes a smaller
078   Rademacher matrix and several random arrays to implicitly generate an embedding matrix and per-
079   form embedding. Particularly, it can embed an n-dimensional vector into k-dimensional space in
                                                                                                         1
080   just O(n + k 2 ) time and space complexity, which is linear complexity if k is not larger than O(n 2 ).
081   (2) Theoretical analysis of the proposed RLE approach is presented, where we prove its linear time
082   and space complexity, that the square of the 2-norm of high-dimensional vector remains unchanged
083   after embedding into low-dimensional space, and other properties as a random embedding.
084
      (3) The proposed random embedding is employed in single-pass RSVD algorithm and randomized
085
      Arnoldi process. The latter facilitates a randomized GMRES algorithm. Numerical experiments
086
      show that with the proposed approach, the single-pass RSVD is accelerated by 1.7x on average
087   while keeping same or even better accuracy, and the randomized GMRES is accelerated by 1.3x on
088   average for the problems of solving Ax = b.

100
101   Random embedding aims to project a high-dimensional vector into low-dimensional space, i.e.
102

105   At the same time, y should preserve key properties of x. It is required that the 2-norm of y satisfy
106   the following equation in mathematical expectation Martinsson & Tropp (2020):

      If Θi,j obeys Normal(0, k −1 ) distribution and each entry of Θ is independently identically dis-
109   tributed, then Θ is a Gaussian embedding matrix. It is noticeable that Gaussian embedding is re-
110   garded as the most natural embedding algorithms and have the most extensive applications Martins-
111   son & Tropp (2020). it can be proven that the rescaled Gaussian embedding satisfies (2). The time
112   and space complexity of Gaussian embedding is O(nk).
113
      If Θi,j is independently equal to √1k with probability 0.5 and is equal to − √1k otherwise, then Θ
114
      is a Rademacher embedding matrix. It can be proven that Rademacher embedding satisfies (2).
115
      Rademacher embedding can achieve similar performance as Gaussian embedding, making it favor-
116
      able in many practical applications. However, the time complexity as well as space complexity of
117   Rademacher sketching matrices is also O(nk).
118
119   A robustness criterion of embedding relies on the concepts of ϵ-subspace embedding and data obliv-
120
      ious embedding, which are explained in Appendix A.2. If the embedding matrix Θ is an (ϵ, δ, d)
      oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding, it can be applied to many basic algorithms with theoretic
121
      safety, e.g. the randomized Arnoldi process Balabanov & Grigori (2022). The Gaussian embed-
122
      ding and Rademacher embedding both satisfy this. The demand of matrix dimension for being
123   an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding reflects the effectiveness of the embedding
124   method. Theoretical results in Balabanov & Nouy (2019) shows that if Θ is a Rademacher ma-
125   trix with k ≥ 7.87ϵ−2 (6.9d + log(1/δ)) for R and k ≥ 7.87ϵ−2 (13.8d + log(1/δ)) for C, then
126   Θ ∈ Rk×n is an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding.
127
      To accelerate the process of random embedding, the sketching algorithms based on fast transform
128
      are developed. A famous example is P-SRHT embedding. If Θ denotes P-SRHT embedding       matrix,
129   Θ is the first k row of SRHT embedding matrix Θ′ , where Θ′ can be denoted with nk DHS, where
                                                                                     p
130   D is a diagonal matrix whose entrices are 1 or -1, H is a normalized Walsh-Hadamart matrix and
131   S is a randomly sampling matrix. P-SRHT embedding can be performed much faster, with time
132   complexity of O(n log n) or O(n log k). However, P-SRHT embedding is less robust than Gaussian
133   embedding or Rademacher embedding in certain scenarios.
134
135
      Sparse sign embedding  pisn even faster, with time complexity and space complexity of O(n). If Θ
      can be denoted with C       [s1 , · · · , sn ] where si are identically independently distributed random
136
      vectors with just C non-zeros, then Θ is a sparse sign embedding matrix. Cohen (2016) pointed
137   out that a sparse sign matrix is an oblivious subspace embedding with constant distortion for an
138   arbitrary d-dimensional subspace of Rn when C = O(log d) and k = O(d log d). However, in
139   many applications like RSVD, d is large so that C = O(log d) and k = O(d log d) cause a time
140   complexity higher than O(n), and ultra sparse sketching (e.g. C = 1, equivalent to Count Sketch)
141   may cause disastrous performance Tropp et al. (2019). To facilitate the practical applications of
142   sparse sign embedding matrices, Tropp et al. (2019) proposed a practically applicable sparse sign
143   embedding, which has the same form but C = min(k, 8) instead of C = O(log d). However,
144   the above setting is heuristic, and in practice induces a non-negligible time constant. Moreover,
145
      another main disadvantage of sparse sign embedding is that we must use sparse data structures and
      arithmetic to achieve its benefits Martinsson & Tropp (2020).
146
147   Therefore, whether random embedding algorithm can be even faster (has linear time and space
148   complexity with small time constant) and robust as well is still in question.

      3.1   T HE I DEA
217
218   To obtain the dense random embedding algorithm with linear complexity, it is obvious that the
219   embedding matrix can not be generated in explicit form, as the lowest time complexity for writing a
220   k × n matrix is O(nk). Thus we should generate the random embedding matrices implicitly.
221
      For Rademacher embedding, the entrices can only be √1k or − √1k , which means that many entries
222
223   in related positions have the same values. More particularly, any two rows of Θ ∈ Rk×n have n2
224
      same entries and n2 different entries in mathematical expectation.
225   Given a 2 × n Rademacher matrix, if we know which columns have same entries in the two rows
226   beforehand, then we can calculate the sketching y = Θx in just n + 4 flops instead of 2n flops
227   (supposing the factor √1k multiplied afterward). The specific way is as follows. Firstly, we calculate
228   the first partial sum of x corresponding to the Θ’s columns with same entries and then we calculate
229   the second partial sum of x on the other entries. While doing the partial sums, the signs of entries in
230   the first row of Θ are considered. After that, we can directly add the first partial sum to the two rows
231   of y, which was initialized to zero. Then, we can just add the second partial sum to the first row of
232
      y and subtract the second partial sum from the other row of y. But how to know the positions of the
      columns with same entries beforehand? The answer is, we first assign these positions randomly.
233
234   The above simple case of 2 × n matrix implies that we can utilize partial sum to fast calculate the
235   result of Rademacher embedding. Therefore, aiming at accelerating the Rademacher embedding, we
236   pursue a method which does not explicitly generate the embedding matrix, assigns the positions of
237   the matrix columns with same entries in advance, and calculates the result of embedding leveraging
238
      the partial sums of x multiplying √1k or − √1k .
239
240   3.2   T HE S ETUP P HASE AND E XECUTION P HASE OF RLE
241
242   If the Rademacher matrix is in general k × n, it is not as simple as the case of the 2 × n matrix,
243   because the positions on which two rows of the matrix have same entries depend on which two rows
244   are considered. To overcome this difficulty, we propose a new random embedding method including
245   two phases: the setup phase generates random vector, matrix and tensors, implicitly representing the
246
      embedding matrix; the execution phase calculates the result y with the embedding.
247   The mechanism of the proposed embedding can be illustrated by Fig. 1, which explains how to
248   calculate the i-th element of y in (1). The idea starts from using a smaller Rademacher matrix
249   P ∈ Rζ×n and calculating random partial sums in P x as building blocks for obtain the final result.
250   Notice that P has ζ rows, much fewer than k rows, and each row of P can be regarded as a basic
251   random sequence. Then, for each row (element) of P x, the original sum of n elements are randomly
252
      split to k ′ partial sums. We set k ′ to be a small multiple of k. These partial sums are stored in a
      temporary array sum with ζ × k ′ values. Notice the circle with blue border in Fig. 1 stands for
253
      a partial sum. This random splitting runs for ξ times, so that sum is actually a ξ × ζ × k ′ tensor.
254
      Again, ξ is also a small number. Matrix C ∈ Zξ×n is for realizing the random splitting, i.e., the j-th
255   term in the sum for calculating P x is accumulated into the C(l, j)-th column of sum(l, :, :).
256
257   While calculating the i-th element of y, we first randomly choose which ζ × k ′ partial sum array
258
      is to be used. Suppose this random choice is an index R(i). The partial sums in sum(R(i), :, :)
      are accumulated to get yi . This accumulation is performed column by column, but for each column
259
      only one element with random row index is adopted. This random row index depends on which 2-D
260
      partial sum array is chosen, i’s value and which column is for accumulation. So, it is a ξ × k × k ′
261   integer tensor, denoted by E. In Fig. 1, we assume for the specific i the 2nd ζ × k ′ partial sum array
262   is chosen. It can be see that, the formation of partial sum array and the accumulation mechanism
263   ensure that every element in x has a contribution to yi . Finally, while accumulating each partial
264   sum, an extra random sign (1 or -1) is multiplied to enforce more randomness. The random signs
                                   ′
265   are denoted by S ∈ Zξ×k×k , with same dimensions as E.
266
      The pseudo codes for the execution phase of the proposed RLE approach (i.e. computing y = Θx)

      are shown in Algorithm 3. Z denotes the set of all integers.

528
529   In this paper, a fast and robust random embedding algorithm, named Rademacher-like embedding
530
      (RLE), is proposed. The theoretic analysis of the proposed approach is given, which proves it is a
      Rademacher-like random embedding with O(n + k 2 ) computational complexity for embedding an
531
      n-dimensional vector into k-dimensional space. The numerical experiments on single-pass RSVD
532
      show that, compared with the sparse sign embedding the proposed RLE enables 1.7x average speed-
533   up with same or even better accuracy. And the experiments on randomized Arnoldi process shows
534   that the proposed RLE enables an accelerated GMRES algorithm which runs 1.3x faster on average
535   without the loss of accuracy.

671
672   A.2.1    T HEORETICAL A SPECTS OF R ANDOM E MBEDDING
673   In theory, the embedding matrix Θ is desired to have some theoretic properties. For numerical
674   stability of random embedding, the distortion after embedding is often estimated in probability.
675   With the following two definitions, embedding distortion can be depicted in theory.
676
      Definition 1. Given ϵ < 1, the embedding matrix Θ ∈ Rk×n is an ϵ-subspace embedding for
677
      subspace K ⊂ Rn , if for any vectors x, y ∈ K,
678
679                                    |⟨x, y⟩ − ⟨Θx, Θy⟩| ≤ ϵ∥x∥ · ∥y∥,                             (16)
680
681   where ⟨x, y⟩ denotes the inner product of vector x and y.
682   Definition 2. The embedding matrix Θ ∈ Rk×n is an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embed-
683   ding, if it is an ϵ-subspace embedding for any fixed d-dimensional subspace K ⊂ Rn with probability
684   at least 1 − δ.
685
686   If Θ is an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding, Θ can be applied to many basic algo-
687   rithms with theoretic safety. For example, theoretic results in Balabanov & Grigori (2022) shows
688
      that if Θ is an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding, numerical stability of randomized
      Arnoldi process can be guaranteed.
689
690   Meanwhile, to extend the embedding for the field of real numbers to the field of complex numbers,a
691   very useful proposition was proposed in the supplementary material of Balabanov & Nouy (2019).
692   Proposition 1. (Balabanov & Nouy, 2019, supplementary material) Let Θ be a real random matrix.
693   If Θ is (ϵ, δ, 2d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding for subspaces of vectors in Rn , then it is
694   (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding for subspaces of vectors in Cn .
695
696   The dimension of embedding matrices for (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2 subspace embedding is often
697   used to evaluate the effectiveness of different embedding methods. Theoretical results in Balabanov
698
      & Nouy (2019) shows that if Θ is a rescaled Rademacher matrix with k ≥ 7.87ϵ−2 (6.9d+log(1/δ))
      for R and k ≥ 7.87ϵ−2 (13.8d + log(1/δ)) for C, then Θ ∈ Rk×n is an (ϵ, δ, d) oblivious ℓ2 → ℓ2
699
      subspace embedding. Theorem 6 states that the same error bound holds for the proposed RLE
700
      approach.
