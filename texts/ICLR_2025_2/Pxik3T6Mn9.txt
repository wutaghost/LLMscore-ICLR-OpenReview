009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Given the GPS coordinates of a large collection of human agents over time, how can
012            we model their mobility behavior toward effective anomaly detection (e.g. for bad-
013            actor or malicious behavior detection) without any labeled data? Human mobility
014            and trajectory modeling have been studied extensively with varying capacity to han-
015            dle complex input, and performance-efficiency trade-offs. With the arrival of more
016
               expressive models in machine learning, we attempt to model GPS data as a sequence
               of stay-point events, each with a set of characterizing spatiotemporal features, and
017
               leverage modern sequence models such as Transformers for un/self-supervised
018
               training and inference. Notably, driven by the inherent stochasticity of certain indi-
019            viduals’ behavior, we equip our model with aleatoric/data uncertainty estimation.
020            In addition, to handle data sparsity of a large variety of behaviors, we incorporate
021            epistemic/model uncertainty into our model. Together, aleatoric and epistemic
022            uncertainty enable a robust loss and training dynamics, as well as uncertainty-
023            aware decision making in anomaly scoring. Experiments on large expert-simulated
024            datasets with tens of thousands of agents demonstrate the effectiveness of our model
025            against both forecasting and anomaly detection baselines. All code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/mobility-ad.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030   Anomaly detection of human mobility has become crucial for various applications, ranging from
031   security and surveillance to health monitoring. Accurately detecting anomalous human behavior
032   from GPS data can reveal critical insights, such as abnormal patterns that indicate security threats or
033   spread of infectious diseases Meloni et al. (2011); Barbosa et al. (2018); Stanford et al. (2024).
034
      However, the complexity and inherent uncertainty of human behavior make this task particularly
035
      challenging: (1) The first difficulty lies in capturing the complex spatiotemporal dependencies both
036   within individual activities and between multiple activities across different times and locations. The
037   former aims to capture the correlations between different spatiotemporal features (a.k.a. markers) of
038   a single activity; such as the time-of-day (e.g. 4am) being indicative of POI location (e.g. home). The
039   latter associates with the dependency patterns between different activities over time (e.g. restaurant at
040   weekday lunchtime followed by office building). (2) Furthermore, human activity data is abound with
041   uncertainty—arising from the unpredictable behavior of inherently stochastic individuals as well as
042   data sparsity—making accurate anomaly detection even more difficult.
043   Prior work on behavior modeling often focused on temporal event forecasting, either ignoring spatial
044   information Minor et al. (2015); Manzoor and Akoglu (2017) or based only on a few markers Du et al.
045   (2016); Zhou et al. (2022). With the emergence of the Transformer architecture Vaswani et al. (2023),
046   more recent work modeled complex spatiotemporal events more expressively Xue et al. (2022);
047   Corrias et al. (2023). However, to our knowledge, no existing work considered uncertainty-aware
048   anomaly detection of human mobility.
049   To address both of the above challenges, we introduce UIF ORMER for uncertainty-incorporated
050   human behavior modeling and anomaly detection, which takes advantage of a “dual” Transformer
051   architecture Truong Jr and Bepler (2023) as well as aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty into account.
052   Transformer has emerged as the de facto model for modern AI problems, due to its superior ability to
053   model long-range dependencies. Through a dual architecture equipped with both feature- and event-
      level attention, we address the first challenge in capturing the intra- and inter-event dependencies.

054
      For the second challenge, we first account for data (a.k.a. aleatoric) uncertainty, which stems from
055   the inherent stochasticity in certain human behavior. For example, it is typical of some populations
056   (e.g. retired individuals) to be less predictable relative to some others (e.g. shift workers with a strict
057   schedule). In addition to data uncertainty, we incorporate model (a.k.a. epistemic) uncertainty, which
058   typically stems from incomplete or sparse observations from a potentially complex underlying data
059   generating distribution. Explicitly modeling both aleatoric (data) and epistemic (model) uncertainty
060   benefits UIF ORMER in two ways: First, it factors into the learning objective, enabling a more robust
061   training. Second, it allows the model to reason effectively about the confidence of its predictions,
062   thus enabling more reliable inference. Specifically, while it is typical for forecasting-based detection
063
      methods to flag anomalies based on the deviation between the predicted and observed values alone,
      our uncertainty-aware UIF ORMER considers both the predicted values as well as total estimated
064
      uncertainties, thus providing a more nuanced and accurate approach to anomaly detection.
065
066   The following summarizes the main contributions of this work.
067   • Problem formulation: We cast the anomaly detection problem in human mobility onto sequence
068     prediction, where we translate the regular GPS readings (of the form ⟨x,y, t⟩) into irregular stay-
069     point events over time with spatiotemporal features. This step resembles a form of “tokenization”
070     of the raw input data to a higher-level representation for sequence modeling.
071   • Uncertainty-aware dependency modeling: We design UIF ORMER, a dual Transformer archi-
072
        tecture that is uncertainty-aware; which is equipped with (i) both feature-level and event-level
        attention—for capturing dependencies among features as well as across events, respectively; and
073
        (ii) both data and model uncertainty estimation—toward accounting for inherent stochasticity in
074
        human behavior as well as data scarcity, respectively.
075
      • Uncertainty-aware anomaly scoring: Beyond prediction, we extend our uncertainty modeling to
076
        anomaly detection, which allows the model to flag abnormal behavior with greater accuracy by
077     factoring in both prediction value and uncertainty estimates.
078   • Effectiveness: Through extensive experiments on expert-simulated benchmark datasets, we show
079     that UIF ORMER significantly outperforms both forecasting as well as anomaly detection baselines.
080     Ablation analyses demonstrate UIF ORMER’s key advantages: accurate uncertainty estimates and
081     effective uncertainty-aware anomaly scores.

135   3     P ROPOSED M ODEL
136
137   Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of UIF ORMER, which learns human mobility patterns in
138   a self-supervised manner through pre-training, with the goal of accurately reconstructing masked
139   events as well as evaluating the prediction uncertainty. In a nutshell, the feature tokenizer first projects
140   features into high-dimensional space by representing each event’s feature as an individual token (§3.1).
141
      Then, the Dual-Transformer encoder further encodes the input by both feature-level and event-level
      self-attention (§3.2). Lastly, unlike previous work, we introduce the uncertainty-aware decoder to
142
      recover all features of the masked event along with the aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty associated
143
      with each feature (§3.3). These estimates are used to compare the predicted versus observed events at
144   inference time for uncertainty-incorporated anomaly scoring (§3.4).

189   3.3     U NCERTAINTY- AWARE D ECODER
190
191   Motivation. Uncertainty modeling has shown great promise in various fields, such as computer
192   vision (Kendall and Gal, 2017; Kendall et al., 2016) and natural language processing (Xiao and Wang,
193
      2019; Gal and Ghahramani, 2016b). In particular, human mobility sequences are inherently associated
      with uncertainty. For instance, certain individuals (e.g., shift workers) may exhibit highly predictable
194
      patterns (i.e., low uncertainty), while others (e.g., retirees) can be much more unpredictable in their
195
      activities over time. This type of uncertainty, known as aleatoric uncertainty (or data uncertainty)
196   (Kendall and Gal, 2017), arises naturally from the data itself and cannot be reduced by simply adding
197   more training data. In addition to aleatoric uncertainty, our setting also involves epistemic uncertainty,
198   which refers to the uncertainty in the model itself due to limited knowledge. This type of uncertainty
199   captures the model’s confidence in its own predictions and can be reduced by acquiring more data or
200   improving the model specification or architecture. In human event sequence modeling, epistemic
201   uncertainty is crucial for identifying situations where the model lacks sufficient information to make
202   confident predictions, especially in underrepresented or complex scenarios. Furthermore, given that
203   different features can have different predictability even for the same individual/event, we design a
204
      feature-level uncertainty learning mechanism. In the following part, we elaborate on how we model
      the epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty for both numerical and categorical features.
205
206
207   3.3.1    E PISTEMIC UNCERTAINTY M ODELING
208
209
      Inspired by (Kendall, 2018), we model the epistemic uncertainty by placing a prior distribution over
      the model’s parameters, and then try to capture how much these parameters vary given observed data.
210
211   To ease the presentation, we use s to denote the input instance Ewu , and θ to denote all parameters of
212   the model M. We start by assuming θ follows a prior distribution, which reflects our initial belief
213   about the possible values of these parameters. The goal is to update this belief based on the available
214   data s by calculating the posterior distribution of θ, that is p(θ|s). This posterior captures how the
215
      parameters might vary, thereby reflecting the model’s uncertainty about its predictions. Formally,
      Bayes’ theorem (Bayes, 1763) gives us the posterior: p(θ|s) = p(s|θ)p(θ)
                                                                            p(s)  .

217   to the high dimensionality of the parameter space and the complexity of the model. As a practical
218   alternative, we apply Monte Carlo (MC) Dropout (Gal and Ghahramani, 2016a) to approximate the
219   posterior. In practice, we introduce dropout layers after the embedding layer of each feature with a
220   dropout ratio of 0.05, which randomly drops a subset of neurons during both training and inference.

285   3.4   U NCERTAINTY- INCORPORATED A NOMALY S CORING
286
      We compute event-level anomaly scores by aggregating deviations between the predicted and the
287
      observed values of all features, incorporating both epistemic and aleatoric uncertainties into the
288   computation. For each test event on a given day, we first create a sequence of events spanning
289   a 3-day window, including the previous and next day as context. We mask the event of interest
290   and use the pre-trained UIF ORMER to predict the masked features. For numerical features, we
291   calculate the deviation as: ∆f = |yf − ybf | with yf and ybf depicting the observed and predicted
292   values, respectively. For categorical features, the deviation is computed as: ∆f = 1 − pbf where pbf is
293   the predicted probability of the observed class. UIF ORMER learns the inherent patterns of human
294   behaviors using only normal data during training. Intuitively, higher deviations from these patterns
295   suggest that an event is less consistent with learned behaviors and thus, more anomalous. Then, we
296
      can compute an anomaly score (AS) for a target event e from the deviations alone as:
297                                 AS(e) = agg (pth (∆f ) | f ∈ Fn ∪ Fc ) ,                               (9)
298
      where agg(·) denotes the aggregation function (e.g., mean or max) applied over ∆f ’s across all
299
      features F. The percentile function pth(·) is used to standardize deviations across features of different
300   scales, effectively capturing the relative ranking of the deviation for each feature. This helps ensure
301   that features with smaller absolute deviations are not undervalued compared to those with larger
302   deviations. However, using deviations alone to detect anomalies is insufficient due to the diverse
303   nature of human mobility. Some agents may deviate from the general population’s behavior but are
304   not necessarily anomalous. For instance, a (stochastic) agent with a tendency to explore different
305   locations frequently (i.e., with high data uncertainty) may have higher deviations naturally, which does
306   not imply an anomaly. To account for such cases, we propose to incorporate uncertainty estimates
307   into the anomaly score. By scaling down deviations by the uncertainties, we can prevent inflating the
308
      anomaly scores of agents whose behaviors, though atypical, are still consistent with natural variability.
      As such, we compute an uncertainty-incorporated anomaly score (UI-AS) for a target event e as:

      5    R ELATED W ORK
502
503   Human Mobility Modeling. Methods in this field can be broadly categorized into traditional
504   statistical models and the more recent deep learning approaches. Traditional statistical approaches
505   typically rely on specific functional forms such as Poisson or Hawkes processes to predict event
506   arrival times (Hawkes, 1971; Daley and Vere-Jones, 2008; Ogata, 1998), which struggle to capture
507   the intricate spatiotemporal patterns present in human mobility data. Other statistical methods
508   like Markov Chains, use transition matrices to predict future locations (Chen et al., 2014; Gambs
509
      et al., 2012; Cheng et al., 2013; Monreale et al., 2009) yet they fall short in modeling long-term
      dependencies. With the rise of deep learning, models such as RNNs and LSTMs have been used to
510
      learn the complex transition patterns (Gao et al., 2017; Song et al., 2016; Du et al., 2016), leading
511
      to better performance in the next location and trajectory prediction. Most recently, there has been
512   a growing trend of using Transformer-based models for human mobility and trajectory data (Wan
513   et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2018; Xue et al., 2021; Abideen et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2020; Wang and
514   Osaragi, 2024), mainly inspired by its immense success in modeling inherently sequential data in NLP.
515   However, most Transformer-based models overlook the underlying uncertainty in the data, especially
516   as it pertains to human behavior over time. Specifically, most prior work focus on pretraining solely
517   based on the masked reconstruction loss, without uncertainty estimation of the prediction. This
518   motivates us to develop a new uncertainty-aware model to fill the gap, toward more effectively
519   capturing human behavior that is often dependent on time and context.
520   Uncertainty Estimation in Deep Neural Networks. Uncertainty estimation has been studied in
521   the field of NLP (Gal and Ghahramani, 2016b; Xiao and Wang, 2019) and CV (Kendall et al., 2016;
522   Huang et al., 2018), which shows great promise in reflecting the underlying uncertainty in outcomes
523   and thus improving task performance. One common approach is the Monte Carlo (MC) Dropout (Gal
524   and Ghahramani, 2016a), which estimates uncertainty by applying dropout during inference, thereby
525   improving model robustness. Others employ deep ensembles for model uncertainty estimation
526
      Lakshminarayanan et al. (2017). Kendall and Gal (2017) propose to learn aleatoric uncertainty
      through loss attenuation while modeling epistemic uncertainty using MC Dropout in both regression
527
      and classification tasks for CV. Mobiny et al. (2021) drop individual weights of the network during
528
      training and inference to measure uncertainty robustly. Motivated by the success of uncertainty
529   estimation in these domains, we explore the potential benefit of uncertainty estimation in modeling
530   human mobility, which forms the basis of our proposed model.

      6    C ONCLUSION
534   We introduced UIF ORMER for human mobility modeling and anomalous behavior detection.
535   UIF ORMER is equipped with both data and model uncertainty estimation to account for the stochas-
536   ticity inherent to human nature as well as the data scarcity in sufficiently observing complex human
537   behavior. While its dual-attention mechanism captures dependencies among features and events,
538   uncertainty estimation lends itself to more robust training and more nuanced anomaly scoring. Exper-
539   iments on two benchmark datasets showed that UIF ORMER outperforms existing baselines, while
      ablation analyses underscored the benefit of uncertainty-aware forecasting and anomaly detection.

      Baselines. We selected the following baseline methods for comparison: RioBus Bessa et al. (2016),
769
      Spatial-Temporal Outlier Detector (STOD) Cruz and Barbosa (2020), a self-supervised MLP (SS-
770   MLP), LSTM, and Transformer. RioBus Bessa et al. (2016) is initially applied to bus trajectories and
771   employs a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to predict the ID of the bus based on the sequence
772   of GPS coordinates. For agent-level anomaly scores, it uses the negative probability of the correct
773   prediction of the agent (bus) ID. For the stay-point (or event) level, scores are derived from the
774   anomaly score of the 20-coordinate sub-trajectory ending at the given stay point. STOD Cruz and
775   Barbosa (2020) is also originally applied to bus trajectories and is GRU-based to predict the bus
776   ID. Its anomaly score computation approaches are similar to RioBus. SS-MLP takes event features
777   including GPS coordinates and time as input. These features are first converted into embeddings,
778
      which are then concatenated to form a unified input vector. This vector is processed by an 8-layer
      MLP that outputs a probability indicating whether an event is real (1) or fake (0). During training,
779
      fake events are generated by randomly altering real events, and the model learns to classify between
780
      real and fake using binary cross-entropy loss. This approach allows the model to distinguish between
781   normal and anomalous data. Finally, the negative of the output probability is used as the anomaly
782   score, with a higher score indicating greater anomaly likelihood.
