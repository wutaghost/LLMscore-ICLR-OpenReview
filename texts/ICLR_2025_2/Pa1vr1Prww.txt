009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are a popular tool for interpreting large language
012             model activations, but their utility in addressing open questions in interpretability
013             remains unclear. In this work, we demonstrate their effectiveness by using SAEs
014             to deepen our understanding of the mechanism behind in-context learning (ICL).
015             We identify abstract SAE features that encode the model’s knowledge of which
016             task to execute and whose latent vectors causally induce the task zero-shot. This
017             aligns with prior work showing that ICL is mediated by task vectors. We further
018
                demonstrate that these task vectors are well approximated by a sparse sum of SAE
                latents, including these task-execution features. To explore the ICL mechanism,
019
                we adapt the sparse feature circuits methodology of Marks et al. (2024) to work for
020
                the much larger Gemma-1 2B model, with 30 times as many parameters, and to
021             the more complex task of ICL. Through circuit finding, we discover task-detecting
022             features with corresponding SAE latents that activate earlier in the prompt, that
023             detect when tasks have been performed. They are causally linked with task-
024             execution features through the attention layer and MLP.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Sparse autoencoders (SAEs; Ng (2011); Bricken et al. (2023); Cunningham et al. (2023)) are a
030   promising method for interpreting large language model (LLM) activations. However, the full
031   potential of SAEs in interpretability research remains to be explored, since most recent SAE research
032   either i) interprets a single SAE’s features rather than the model’s computation as a whole (Bricken
033   et al., 2023), or ii) performs high-level interventions in the model, but does not interpret the effect on
034   the downstream computation caused by the interventions Templeton et al. (2024b). In this work, we
035
      address these limitations by interpreting in-context learning (ICL), a widely studied phenomenon
      in LLMs. In summary, we show that SAEs enable a) the discovery of novel circuit components
036
      (task-detection features; Section 4.2) and b) making existing interpretations of ICL more precise, by
037
      e.g. decomposing task vectors (Todd et al., 2024; Hendel et al., 2023) into task-execution features
038   (Section 3).
039
040   In-context learning (ICL; Brown et al. (2020)) is a fundamental capability of large language models
041
      that allows them to adapt to new tasks without fine-tuning. ICL is a significantly more complex and
      important task than other behaviors commonly studied in circuit analysis (such as IOI in Wang et al.
042
      (2022) and Kissane et al. (2024), or subject-verb agreement and Bias-in-Bios in Marks et al. (2024)).
043
      Recent work by Todd et al. (2024) and Hendel et al. (2023) has introduced the concept of task vectors
044   to study ICL in a simple setting, which we follow throughout this paper.1 In short, task vectors
045   are internal representations of tasks formed by language models during the processing of few-shot
046   prompts, such as “hot → cold, big → small, fast → slow”. These vectors can be extracted and added
047   into different LLM forward passes to induce 0-shot task performance, making LLMs predict that
048   “slow” follows “fast →” without explicit context. Section 2.3 provides a full introduction.
049
      To identify task-execution features, we decomposed task vectors using SAEs. To achieve this, we
050   needed to go beyond existing methods for solving the classical dictionary problem of decomposing
051   a vector into a sparse sum of dictionary vectors (Elad, 2010). To do this, we developed a bespoke
052

054
      method for LLMs we call the TASK VECTOR CLEANING (TVC) algorithm. By running the TVC
055   algorithm, we found task-execution features: features that can partially replace task vectors taken
056   alone and have highly interpretable max-activating token patterns. We validate the causal relevance
057   of these task features through a series of steering experiments on tasks, spanning several categories
058   like translation or factual recall. The experiments demonstrate that identified task features encode
059   crucial information about task execution, are causally implicated in the model’s ICL capabilities, and
060   can play the same role as task vectors.
061   We adapted the Sparse Feature Circuits (SFC) methodology of Marks et al. (2024) to work on the
062   more complex ICL task and the larger Gemma-1 2B model (Gemma Team, 2024). This adaptation
063   allowed us to discover and analyze the subgraph of key SAE latents involved in ICL, providing a more
064   comprehensive view of the ICL circuit. Using this adaptation, we found task-detection features with
065   SFC: features that play a crucial role in identifying the specific task being performed earlier in the
066   prompt. Task-detection features are tightly connected with task-execution features through attention,
067   as part of the whole ICL circuit.
068   Our findings not only advance our understanding of ICL mechanisms but also demonstrate the
069   potential of SAEs as a powerful tool for interpretability research on larger language models. By
070   unifying the task vectors view with SAEs and uncovering two of the most important causally
071   implicated feature families behind ICL, we pave the way for future work to control and monitor ICL
072   further, to improve either the safety or capabilities of models.
073   Our main contributions are as follows:
074
075         1. We demonstrate that SAEs can be effectively used to explain mechanisms behind a complex
076
               set of ICL tasks in a Gemma-1 2B, which has 10-35x more parameters than prior models
               typically studied at this depth in comparable, circuits-style mechanistic interpretability
077
               research (Wang et al., 2022; Marks et al., 2024). We show that causal circuit finding
078
               algorithms and SFC specifically straightforwardly scale up to larger models and SAEs with
079            different architectures (Appendix B).
080         2. We identify two core bottlenecks in the ICL circuit – task-detection features and task-
081            execution features (see Appendix C, F, G) – and study their interactions (Section 3.2).
082            This provides new insights into how LLMs process and execute ICL tasks. Specifically, we
083            discover task-detection features that identify the task being performed earlier in the prompt,
084            which are then moved by attention heads to trigger task-execution features (Figure 8).
085         3. We present a novel transformer-specific sparse linear decomposition algorithm (Section 3.1)
086            that decomposes task vectors (Hendel et al., 2023) into a small set of mostly task-relevant
087
               features, enabling more precise analysis of ICL mechanisms.

      2     BACKGROUND

      2.1   S PARSE AUTOENCODERS (SAE S )
111
112   Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) are neural networks designed to learn efficient representations of data
113   by enforcing sparsity in the hidden layer activations (Elad, 2010). In the context of language model
114   interpretability, SAEs are used to decompose the high-dimensional activations of language models
115   into more interpretable features (Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). The basic idea
116   behind SAEs is to train a neural network to reconstruct its input while constraining the hidden layer
117   to have sparse activations. This process typically involves an encoder that maps the input to a sparse
118   hidden representation, a decoder that reconstructs the input from this sparse representation, and loss
119   task that balances reconstruction accuracy with sparsity 2 . The encoding step is as follows, with f
120
      denoting the pre-activation features and Wenc and benc the encoder weights and biases respectively:
121
122                                             f(x) = σ(Wenc x + benc )                                            (1)
123
124   For JumpReLU SAEs (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024b), the activation function and decoder are (with
125   H being the Heaviside step function, θ the threshold parameter and Wdec /bdec the decoder affine
126   parameters):
127
128
                                           x̂(f) = Wdec (f ⊙ H(f − θ)) + bdec                                       (2)
129
130
      In our work, we train SAEs on residual stream activations and attention outputs, and also train
131
      transcoders3 on MLP layers, all of which use the improved Gated SAE architecture (Rajamanoharan
132
      et al., 2024a).
133
134
135   2.2   S PARSE F EATURE C IRCUITS
136
      Sparse Feature Circuits (SFCs) are a methodology introduced by Marks et al. (2024) to identify
137
      and analyze causal subgraphs of sparse autoencoder features that explain specific model behaviors.
138   This approach combines the interpretability benefits of SAEs with causal analysis to uncover the
139   mechanisms underlying language model behavior. The SFC methodology involves several key steps:
140
141         1. Decomposing model activations into sparse features using SAEs
142
            2. Calculating the Indirect Effect (IE, Pearl (2001) of each feature on the target behavior
143
144         3. Identifying a set of causally relevant features based on IE thresholds
145         4. Constructing a circuit by analyzing the connections between these features
146
147   The IE of a model component is measured by intervening on that component and observing the
148   change in the model’s output. For a component a and a metric m, the IE is defined using do-calculus
149   (Pearl, 2009) as in Marks et al. (2024) as:
150
151
                                        IE(m; a) = m(x|do(a = a′ )) − m(x)                                          (3)
152   Where m(x|do(a = a′ )) represents the value of the metric when we intervene to set the value of
153   component a to a′ , and m(x) is the original value of the metric. In practice, attribution patching (Syed
154   et al., 2023) is used to approximate IE, allowing for efficient computation across many components
155   simultaneously.
156
      SFC is described in detail in (Marks et al., 2024). We describe our modifications in Appendix E.
157
158       2
            Typically, the L1 penalty on activations is used (Bricken et al., 2023) with some modifications (Rajamanoha-
159   ran et al., 2024a; Conerly et al., 2024), although there are alternatives: Rajamanoharan et al., 2024b; Farrell,
160   2024; Riggs & Brinkman, 2024.
          3
161         Transcoders are a modification of SAEs that take MLP input and convert it into MLP output instead of
      trying to reconstruct the residual stream.

      2.3        TASK V ECTORS
163
164   Continuing from the high-level description in Section 1, task vectors were independently discovered
165   by Hendel et al. (2023) and Todd et al. (2024). The key idea behind task vectors is that they capture
166   the essence of a task demonstrated in a few-shot prompt, allowing the model to apply this learned
167   task to new inputs without explicit fine-tuning. Task vectors have several important properties:
168              1. They can be extracted from the model’s hidden states given ICL prompts as inputs.
169              2. When added to the model’s activations in a zero-shot setting, they can induce task perfor-
170                 mance without explicit context.
171              3. They appear to encode abstract task information, independent of specific input-output
172                 examples.

198   3.1        D ECOMPOSING TASK V ECTORS
199
200   To gain a deeper understanding of task vectors, we attempted to decompose them using sparse
201   autoencoders (SAEs). However, several of our initial naive approaches faced significant challenges.
202   Firstly, direct SAE reconstruction, i.e. passing the task vector as input to the SAE, produced noisy
203
      results with more than 10 nonzero SAE features on average on layers of interest4 , most of which were
      irrelevant to the task. Moreover, this reconstruction noticeably reduced the vector’s performance.
204
      These issues arose partly because task vectors are out-of-distribution inputs for SAEs, as they
205
      aggregate information from different residual streams rather than representing a single one.
206
207   We then explored inference-time optimization (ITO) (Smith, 2024) as an alternative. However, this
208   method also failed to reconstruct task vectors using a low number of SAE features while maintaining
209
      high performance.

      4     A PPLYING SFC TO ICL
325
326
      After identifying task-execution features through our task vector analysis, we sought to expand our
327   understanding of the in-context learning (ICL) circuit. To this end, we apply the Sparse Feature
328   Circuits (SFC) methodology (Marks et al., 2024) to the Gemma-1 2B model. However, due to the
329   increased complexity of ICL tasks and the larger model size, the original SFC approach did not
330   work out of the box. We had to implement several key modifications to address the challenges we
331   encountered.
332
333   4.1     O UR M ODIFICATIONS
334
335   4.1.1    T OKEN P OSITION C ATEGORIZATION AND F EATURE AGGREGATION
336
      We modified the SFC approach to better handle the structured nature of ICL prompts. Instead of
337   treating each SAE feature as a separate node, we categorized token positions into the following
338   groups:
339
340           • Prompt: The initial instruction tokens (e.g., “Follow the pattern:”)
341           • Input: The last token before each arrow in an example pair
342
              • Arrow: The arrow token itself (“→”)
343
344
              • Output: The last token before each newline in an example pair
345           • Newline: The newline token
346           • Extra: Any tokens not covered by the above categories (e.g., in multi-token inputs or outputs)
347
348   Each pair of an SAE feature and a token type was assigned its own graph node. The effects of the
349   feature were aggregated across all tokens of the corresponding type. This categorization allowed us
350   to evaluate how features affect all tokens within the same category, separating features based on their
351
      role in the ICL circuit. It also enabled us to selectively disable parts of the circuit for one task while
      testing another, verifying the task specificity of the identified circuits.
352
353
      4.1.2    L OSS F UNCTION M ODIFICATION
354
355   An ICL prompt can be viewed as an (x, y) pair, where x represents the entire prompt except for
356   the last pair’s output, and y represents this output. The original SFC paper suggested using the
357   log probabilities of y conditioned on x for such datasets. However, this approach often resulted in
358   task-relevant features having high negative IEs on other example pairs in the prompt. This was likely
359   due to the circuit’s effect on those pairs being lost to either diminishing gradients in backpropagation
360   or because copying circuits were much more relevant to predicting the last pair. By considering all
361
      pairs except the first one, we amplified the effect of the task-solving circuit relative to the numerous
      cloning circuits that activate due to the repetitive nature of ICL prompts.
362
363

368   We calculated faithfulness using the following formula:

404          • Task Specificity: Ablating most tasks does not significantly impact the performance of
405            others, indicating that the discovered circuits are largely task-specific. This suggests that
406            there are no common high-IE ICL-specific nodes across tasks.
407          • Related Task Effects: Tasks are grouped into categories, and we observe that ablation of
408            related tasks has a higher effect on all tasks within the same group. This is visible as squares
409            along the diagonal, particularly noticeable in the translation group.
410          • Performance Improvement: For some tasks, we observe that faithfulness rises well above
411            1.0 after ablation of other tasks. We hypothesize that this occurs because we reduce the
412            confusion of the model by removing irrelevant execution paths.
413
414   It is worth noting that we excluded the person profession and football player position tasks from
415
      Figure 6 due to the very small difference between their fully ablated and non-ablated losses. This
      resulted in highly unstable faithfulness calculations for these tasks. We attribute this small difference
416
      partially to our modified loss function, as we found that calculating the loss only from the last pair

      results in a higher loss difference.

478   5    R ELATED WORK
479
480   Mechanistic Interpretability Olah et al. (2020) defines a framing for mechanistic interpretability
481   in terms of features and circuits. It claims that neural network latent spaces have directions in
482   them called features that correspond to meaningful variables. These features interact through model
483   components sparsely to form circuits: interpretable computation subgraphs relevant to particular
484   tasks. These circuits can be found through manual inspection in vision models (Cammarata et al.,
485   2020). In language models, they can be found through manual patching (Wang et al., 2022; Hanna
      et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023; Chan et al., 2022) or automated circuit discovery (Conmy et al.


                                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (2023); Syed et al. (2023); Bhaskar et al. (2024), though see Miller et al. (2024)). Marks et al. (2024)
487   extends this research area to use Sparse Autoencoders, as discussed below.
488
489
      In-Context Learning (ICL) ICL was first introduced in Brown et al. (2020) and refers to models
490
      learning to perform tasks from prompt information at test time. There is a large area of research
491
      studying its applications (Dong et al., 2024), high-level mechanisms (Min et al., 2022) and limitations
492   (Peng et al., 2023). Elhage et al. (2021) and Olsson et al. (2022) find induction heads partly
493   responsible for in-context learning. However, since these attention heads do more than just induction
494   (Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023), and are not sufficient for complex task-following, induction heads
495   alone cannot explain ICL. Anil et al. (2024, Appendix G) proposes a mechanistic hypothesis for
496   an aspect of simple in-context task behavior. Hendel et al. (2023) and Todd et al. (2024) find that
497   simple in-context learning tasks create strong directions in the residual stream adding which makes
498   it possible for a network to perform tasks zero-shot, but does not explain how task vectors form
499   nor what interpretable components the task vectors are composed of. A more detailed discussion
500
      can be found in Appendix H. Of particular interest is Wang et al., which investigates a simple ICL
      classification task and finds similar results with different terminology (information flow instead of
501
      circuits, ”label words” instead of task-detection features).
502
503
504   Sparse Autoencoders A major roadblock to mechanistic interpretability research is superposition
505
      (Elhage et al., 2022b), where the interpretable units of neural network do not tend to align with the
      basis directions (e.g. neurons). Sparse autoencoders (Ng, 2011; Bricken et al., 2023) are one method
506
      of addressing this roadblock, and multiple works since proposed improvements to SAE training
507
      (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024b; Bussmann et al., 2024; Braun et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Templeton
508   et al., 2024b), and we use several more in our work (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024a; Adam Jermyn,
509   2024; Conerly et al., 2024). Cunningham et al. (2023), building on Bills et al. (2023), apply Conmy
510   et al. (2023) to find circuits in small language models. Marks et al. (2024) adapt Syed et al. (2023) in
511   the SAE basis to find circuits and address a practical bias reduction problem. Kissane et al. (2024)
512   apply a slightly different automated SAE algorithm (similar to ours in that it operates on single
513   prompts) to IOI (Wang et al., 2022), using SAEs on the attention layer outputs and residual stream.
514   Dunefsky et al. (2024) introduce transcoders (which are also briefly discussed in Templeton et al.
515   (2024a) and Li et al. (2023)) to simplify analysis of circuits involving MLPs. We build on their work
516
      and train transcoders as part of our suite of Gemma-1 SAEs.
517
518
      6    C ONCLUSION
519
520
      Limitations Our work focused on the simple task vector setting to study ICL (Section 2.3), which
521
      does not capture all ways that ICL is used in practice (generally involving far more tokens and
522   open-ended tasks). We also only interpreted Gemma-1 2B. Therefore, other LLM architectures or
523   model sizes could lead to different results (though this is not likely, since task vectors exist across
524   models (Todd et al., 2024)). Finally, the complexity of the task studied meant our interpretations have
525   some approximation error: attention heads matter for the detection-execution connection, but the
526   succeeding MLP is necessary to capture the full effect (Section 4.2). This means that our explanation
527   needs to include moving parts aside from task-detection attention output features. It is possible to
528   model the effects of the MLP through transcoder features, but we leave that for future work.
529
530   Future Work Future work could extend SFC methods to work on more than a band of layers in
531   the middle of the model (Section 2.2). Since many features correspond to individual input tokens
532   and output predictions (due to the three stages of inference in LLMs; Elhage et al. (2022a); Lad
533   et al. (2024)), this will require further adaptation of the SFC methodology. Moreover, our multiple
534   contributions will hopefully spur further work that finds new tasks to interpret or explain in greater
535   depth than prior work, as discussed in our concluding paragraph below.
536   To summarize our work: we use SAEs to explain in-context learning in greater detail than any
537   prior mechanistic interpretability work. This provides strong evidence that Sparse Autoencoders are
538   valuable circuit analysis tools, and the innovations developed: TVC (Section 3.1), SFC improvements
539   (Section 2.2) and an SAE training codebase in JAX with open SAE weights (Section 7) are likely to
      help enable lots of other SAE research to tackle more ambitious tasks and larger models.

898   B    SAE T RAINING
899
900   Our Gemma 1 2B SAEs are trained with a learning rate of 1e-3 and Adam betas of 0.0 and 0.99
901   for 150M (±100) tokens of FineWeb (Penedo et al., 2024). The methodology is overall similar to
902   (Bloom, 2024). We initialize encoder weights orthogonally and set decoder weights to their transpose.
903   We initialize decoder biases to 0. We use Rajamanoharan (2024)’s ghost gradients variant (ghost
904   gradients applied to dead features only, loss multiplied by the proportion of death features) with the
905   additional modification of using softplus instead of exp for numerical stability. A feature is considered
906   dead when its density (according to a 1000-batch buffer) is below 5e-6 or when it has not fired in
907   2000 steps. We use Anthropic’s input normalization and sparsity loss for Gemma 1 2B (Conerly et al.,
908
      2024). We found it to improve Gated SAE training stability. We modified it to work with transcoders
      by keeping track of input and output norms separately and predicting normed outputs.
909
910   We convert our Gated SAEs into JumpReLU SAEs after training, implementing algorithms like TVC
911   and SFC in a unified manner for all SAEs in this format (including simple SAEs). The conversion
912   procedure involves setting thresholds to replicate the effect of the gating branch. For further details,
913   see Rajamanoharan et al. (2024b).
914   We use 4 v4 TPU chips running Jax (Bradbury et al., 2018) (Equinox (Kidger & Garcia, 2021)) to
915   train our SAEs. We found that training with Huggingface’s Flax LM implementations was very
916   slow. We reimplemented LLaMA (Dubey et al., 2024) and Gemma (Gemma Team, 2024) in Penzai
917   (Johnson, 2024) with a custom layer-scan transformation and quantized inference kernels as well
      as support for loading from GGUF compressed model files. We process an average of around 4400


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      tokens per second, which makes training SAEs and not caching LM activations the main bottleneck.
919   For this and other reasons, we don’t do SAE sparsity coefficient sweeps to increase TPU utilization.
920
921   For caching, we use a distributed ring buffer which contains separate pointers on each device to allow
922
      for processing masked data. The (in-place) buffer update is in a separate JIT context. Batches are
      sampled randomly from the buffer for each training step.
923
924   We train our SAEs in bfloat16 precision. We found that keeping weights and scales in bfloat16
925   and biases in float32 performed best in terms of the number of dead features and led to a Pareto
926   improvement over float32 SAEs.
927   For training Phi 3 (Abdin et al., 2024) SAEs, we use data generated by the model unconditionally,
928   similarly to (Xu et al., 2024)5 . The resulting dataset we train the model on contains many math
929   problems and is formatted as a natural-seeming interaction between the user and the model.
930
      Each SAE training run takes us about 3 hours. We trained 3 models (a residual SAE, an attention
931
      output SAE, and a transcoder) for each of the 18 layers of the model. This is about 1 week of v4-8
932   TPU time.

996        The task vector cleaning algorithm is a novel approach we developed to isolate task-relevant features
997        from task vectors. Figure 10 provides an overview of this algorithm.

           Our process begins with collecting residuals for task vectors using a batch of 16 and 16-shot prompts.
           We then calculate the SAE features for these task vectors. We explored two methods: (1) calculating
1000

1001

1447   F.1     N EGATIVE STEERING
1448
1449   To further explore the effects of the executor feature, we also conducted negative steering experiments.
1450   The setup involved a batch of 16 ICL prompts, each containing 32 examples for each task. We
1451   collected all features from the cleaned task vectors for every task. Similar to positive steering, we
1452   steered with features on arrow tokens, but this time multiplying the direction by -1. Prompts this time
1453   contained several arrow tokens, and we steered on all of them simultaneously.
1454   An important distinction from positive steering is that performance degradation in negative steering
1455   may occur due to two factors: (1) our causal intervention on the ICL circuit and (2) the steering scale
1456   being too high. To address this, we measured accuracy across all pairs in the batch instead of loss, as
1457   accuracy does not decrease indefinitely. We also observed that features no longer share a common

       H     ICL INTERPRETABILITY LITERATURE REVIEW
1602
1603
1604
       This section will cover work on understanding ICL not mentioned in Section 5.
1605   Raventós et al. provides evidence for two different Bayesian algorithms being learned for linear
1606   regression ICL: one for limited task distributions and one that is similar to ridge regression. It
1607   also intriguingly shows that the two solutions lie in different basins of the loss landscape, a phase
1608   transition necessary to go from one to the other. While interesting, it is not clear if the results apply
1609   to real-world tasks.
1610   The existence of discrete task detection and execution features hinges on the assumption that in-
1611   context learning works by classifying the task to perform and not by learning a task. Pan et al. aims
1612   to disentangle the two with a black-box approach that mixes up outputs to force the model to learn
1613   the task from scratch. Si et al. look at biases in task recognition in ambiguous examples through
1614   a black-box lens. We find more clear task features for some tasks than others but do not consider
1615   whether this is linked to how common a task is in pretraining data.
1616   Xie et al. proposes that in-context learning happens because language models aim to model a latent
1617   topic variable to predict text with long-range coherence. Wang et al. (2024) show following the two
1618   proposed steps rigorously improves results in real-world models. However, they do not endeavor to
1619   explain the behavior of non-finetuned models by looking at internal representations; instead, they aim
       to improve ICL performance.


                                                                                                                                                               30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       Han et al. use a weight-space method to find examples in training data that promote in-context
1621   learning using a method akin to Grosse et al. (2023), producing results similar to per-token loss
1622   analyses in Olsson et al. (2022), and, similarly to the studies mentioned above, finds that those
1623   examples involve long-range coherence. Our method is also capable of finding examples in data that
1624   are similar to ICL, and we find crisp examples for many tasks being performed Appendix I.
1625
       Bansal et al. offers a deeper look into induction heads, scaling up Olsson et al. (2022) the way we
1626
       scale up Marks et al. (2024). Crucially, it finds that MLPs in later layers cannot be removed while
1627   preserving ICL performance, indirectly corroborating our findings from Section 4.2. Chen et al. come
1628   up with a proof that states that gradient flow converges to a generalized version of the algorithm
1629   suggested by Olsson et al. (2022) when trained on n-gram Markov chain data.
1630
       Garg et al. studies the performance of toy models trained on in-context regression various function
1631
       classes. Yadlowsky et al. find that Transformers trained on regression with multiple function classes
1632
       have trouble combining solutions for learning those functions. Oswald et al. construct a set of weights
1633   for linear attention Transformers that reproduce updates from gradient descent and find evidence for
1634   the algorithm being represented on real models trained on toy tasks. Mahankali et al. proves that
1635   this algorithm is optimal for single-layer transformers on noisy linear regression data. Shen et al.
1636   questions the applicability of this model to real-world transformers. Bai et al. finds that transformers
1637   can switch between multiple different learning algorithms for ICL. Dai et al. find multiple similarities
1638   between changes made to model predictions from in-context learning and weight finetuning.
1639   While important, we do not consider this direction of interpreting transformers trained on regression
1640   for concrete function classes through primarily white-box techniques. Instead, we aim to focus on
1641   clear discrete tasks which are likely to have individual features.
1642
       The results of Wang et al. are perhaps the most similar to our findings. The study finds ”anchor tokens”
1643
       responsible for aggregating semantic information, analogous to our ”output tokens” (Section 2.3) and
1644
       task-detection features. They tackle the full circuit responsible for ICL bottom-up and intervene on
1645   models using their understanding, improving accuracy. Like this paper, they do not deeply investigate
1646   later attention and MLP layers. Our study uses SAE features to find strong linear directions on output
1647   and arrow tokens corresponding to task detection and execution respectively, offering a different
1648   perspective. Additionally, we consider over 20 diverse token-to-token tasks, as opposed to the 4 text
1649   classification datasets considered in citewangl abel2 023.
