009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Flatness of the loss surface not only correlates positively with generalization, but
012            is also related to adversarial robustness, since perturbations of inputs relate non-
013            linearly to perturbations of weights. In this paper, we empirically analyze the
014            relation between adversarial examples and relative flatness with respect to the pa-
015            rameters of one layer. We observe a peculiar property of adversarial examples in
016            the context of relative flatness: during an iterative first-order white-box attack, the
017            flatness of the loss surface measured around the adversarial example first becomes
018
               sharper until the label is flipped, but if we keep the attack running, it runs into a
               flat uncanny valley where the label remains flipped. In extensive experiments, we
019
               observe this phenomenon across various model architectures and datasets, even
020
               for adversarially trained models. Our results also extend to large language models
021            (LLMs), but due to the discrete nature of the input space and comparatively weak
022            attacks, adversarial examples rarely reach truly flat regions. Most importantly,
023            this phenomenon shows that flatness alone cannot explain adversarial robustness
024            unless we can also guarantee the behavior of the function around the examples.
025            We therefore theoretically connect relative flatness to adversarial robustness by
026            bounding the third derivative of the loss surface, underlining the need for flatness
027            in combination with a low global Lipschitz constant for a robust model.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Despite the remarkable performance of modern deep learning models, their vulnerability to ad-
033
      versarial attacks, i.e., small crafted perturbations in the input that fool a model into changing its
      prediction to an incorrect label (Szegedy et al., 2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2017a), undermine the trust
034
      in the reliability of these models. Although there has been significant progress in developing meth-
035
      ods to enhance adversarial robustness, the underlying mechanisms that dictate the susceptibility of
036   a model to such perturbations are still not fully understood. One promising approach is the study
037   of flatness of the loss surface. A lot of previous work demonstrated a positive correlation between
038   flatness of the loss surface and the generalization ability of a model (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
039   1994; Keskar et al., 2016; Jiang et al., 2019). Flat minima in the loss landscape are thought to be
040   indicative of better generalizing models, as they suggest that small changes in the parameters space
041   do not significantly affect the loss of a model. This concept has led to the hypothesis that flatness
042   is also related to adversarial robustness. While flatness measured with respect to inputs obviously
043   relates to adversarial robustness (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2019), it is not clear how flatness with
044
      respect to parameters connects to it (Wu et al., 2020; Kanai et al., 2023).
045   In this paper, we explore the relationship between flatness of the loss surface with respect to parame-
046   ters and adversarial examples. We empirically investigate this connection by analyzing the behavior
047   of the loss surface during iterative first-order white-box attacks. Our findings reveal an intriguing
048   phenomenon, of which we give an example in Fig. 1. The loss surface initially becomes sharper as
049   the attack progresses and the prediction is flipped. However, if the attack is continued, the adversar-
050
      ial example often moves into a flat region of the loss surface, which we term uncanny valley. This
      region is uncanny in the sense that the surface around adversarial examples is very flat while they
051
      are still changing the prediction of the model.
052
053   This does not only indicate that the correlation between flatness and strong adversarial examples is
      not intuitive, but also that a vicinity of such an adversarial sample will be filled with similar adver-

056   estingly, for more robust models, much stronger attacks are necessary to find these uncanny valleys.
057   For LLMs, we find that their discrete nature and the availability of only relatively weak attacks often
058   prevent adversarial examples from reaching truly flat regions.

      Adversarial Examples Szegedy et al. (2014) introduced the notion of adversarial inputs for deep
      learning models as a research subject, characterizing these as a curious property of neural networks.
094
      High-level definition of an adversarial example is a perturbation of a benign input that is hard to
095
      detect for a human but which leads to the mistake in the model prediction. By now, they have
096   become a major drawback of practical deep learning, since they not only pose a possible threat in
097   applications, but they undermine the trust in machine learning in general: How can we trust the
098   predictions of models that are so easily fooled?
099
      In the meantime, many algorithms for generating adversarial examples have been proposed, start-
100
      ing with FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015), followed by the defacto standard attacks PGD (Madry
101
      et al., 2017) and C&W (Carlini & Wagner, 2017b), and many more (Papernot et al., 2016; Ku-
102   rakin et al., 2017; Narodytska & Kasiviswanathan, 2017; Brown et al., 2018; Alaifari et al., 2018;
103   Andriushchenko et al., 2020; Croce & Hein, 2020; 2021). With the development of generative deep
104   learning, the approaches for generating adversarial samples with diffusion models (Chen et al., 2023)
105   appeared. Large language models are not an exception and susceptible not only to standard input
106   manipulation for prediction failure (Li et al., 2020; Zou et al., 2023) but also to so-called jailbreaks,
107   which are forcing the model to output text that was supposed to be excluded from the possible results
      of the inference (Wei et al., 2024).


                                                         2
108
      Defenses against Adversarial Attacks The most common way to make a model more robust to
109   adversarial examples is adversarial training, which incorporates adversarial inputs into the training
110   procedure (Szegedy et al., 2014; Shafahi et al., 2019; Kumari et al., 2019; Perolat et al., 2018;
111   Shafahi et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2018; Tramèr et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2020; Carmon et al., 2019). It is
112   usually observed that adversarial training reduces the performance of a model on clean data (Tsipras
113   et al., 2018). There also exists contradictory research stating that adversarial training approaches
114   lead to a flatter loss surface with respect to the parameters (Wu et al., 2020; Stutz et al., 2021). This,
115   in turn, is believed to lead to better generalization (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1994).
116   Rahnama et al. (2020) propose an approach where each of the subnetworks corresponding to layers
117   is robustified via insights from Lyapunov theory, connecting robustness to spectral regularization.
118   Nevertheless, later Liang & Huang (2021) show that large norms of layers do not always induce large
119   global Lipschitz constant. So regularization of norms is not an effective way to enhance robustness.
120   Moosavi-Dezfooli et al. (2019) propose to penalize the Hessian with respect to input to improve the
121   flatness in the input space and demonstrate that it improves adversarial robustness, analogous work
122   was performed by Xu et al. (2020).
123   Interestingly, Kanai et al. (2023) analyses smoothness in the input space and smoothness in the
124   parameter space and concludes that smoothness in the input space leads to a non-flat surface with
125   respect to parameters, which leads to worse performance. Flatness of the loss surface as a form of
126   smoothness in parameter space has also been linked to generalization (Liang et al., 2019; Tsuzuku
127   et al., 2020). Foret et al. (2020) proposed Sharpness Aware Minimization (SAM), that is essentially
128   similar to the work of Wu et al. (2020), but focuses on improving clean accuracy. The approach
129
      became very popular due to the ability to improve some of the state-of-the-art results in image
      classification. Dinh et al. (2017) show, however, that flatness in terms of the loss Hessian of the entire
130
      network cannot predict generalization, due to reparameterizations. Petzka et al. (2021) showed that
131
      generalization instead can be linked to a relative flatness of a single layer of a network.
132
133
      Lipschitz Continuity and Adversarial Robustness One of the drawbacks of adversarial training
134   is the absence of guarantees on the robustness of the obtained model. One of the popular ways
135   to obtain guarantees is randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019), which implicitly controls the
136   global Lipschitz constant (Salman et al., 2019). The main idea here is to produce a smoothed ver-
137   sion of a classifier by making it predict the most probable label in a normal distribution surrounding
138   an example. Directly using Lipschitz properties is not quite practical however for modern networks.
139   Global Lipschitz constants (Tsuzuku et al., 2018) are usually too vague, while local Lipschitz con-
140   stants (Hein & Andriushchenko, 2017) are impractical to compute. Liang & Huang (2021) show
141   that a large global Lipschitz does not make the local constants large, which means that even models
142
      with a large global Lipschitz constant can be adversarially robust. Small global Lipschitz constant
      on the other hand helps to control local constants, but sacrifices clean accuracy. Yang et al. (2020)
143
      showed that improving local Lipschitz constants and enforcing better generalization can serve as a
144
      method to get good generalizing and robust models , but it might not be easily achievable.

187   To relate flatness and adversarial examples, we want to measure flatness for a particular adversarial
188   example ξ ∈ X . For that, we need a sound flatness measure and an efficient way to compute it.
189
      A sound flatness measure should be correlated with a network’s generalization ability. A particular
      challenge here is that measuring flatness using the loss Hessian wrt. weights (trace or eigenvalues) is
190
      not reparameterization-invariant and thus cannot be connected to generalization (Dinh et al., 2017).
191
      By deriving their relative flatness measure directly from a decomposition of the generalization gap,
192   Petzka et al. (2021) could show that a combination of trace of the loss Hessian and norm of weights
193   for a single layer of a network is not only reparameterization-invariant, but can be theoretically
194   linked to generalization. This uses a robustness argument: Since for large weights small perturbation
195   in the representation produced by the layer in question can lead to much larger changes in the output,
196   a network needs to be in a much flatter minimum to counteract this. The “relative” part of the flatness
197   measure, i.e., the weights norm component, is a constant in our analysis since we assume a fixed
198   trained neural network. For a model that can be decomposed into a feature extractor ϕ and a predictor
199   g, i.e., f (x) = g(wϕ(x)), trained on S ⊂ X × Y, the relative flatness measure1 is defined as

233   5       E MPIRICAL E VALUATION
234
235   We perform three groups of experiments to investigate the behavior of the flatness for adversarial
236   samples. First, we demonstrate that adversarial attacks on CNNs find adversarial examples in flat
237   regions according to the relative sharpness measure. We name these flat regions uncanny valleys.
238   Second, we investigate, how adversarial training (AT) influences the phenomenon of uncanny val-
239   leys. We find that AT simply pushes the uncanny valley further away, however, it can still be easily
240
      found by stronger attacks. Last, we conduct a similar analysis on LLMs, where we find that the phe-
      nomenon is less pronounced, which is likely due to the discrete nature of the problem, making the
241
      attacks weaker and consequentially the uncanny valleys harder to find. All experiments presented
242
      below are carried out on machines equipped with an Nvidia-A100 80GB, 256 cores, and 1.9TB of
243   memory. The code is publically available 2 .
244

      6                     B OUNDING A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS VIA R ELATIVE S HARPNESS
406
      The definition of adversarial examples entails the assumption that labels are locally constant, which
407
      we made explicit in Def. 2. This assumption can lead to counter-intuitive behavior: if the true label
408   of a perturbed example ξ is yξ ̸= y and the model predicts yξ , then although correct it counts as an
409   adversarial example, while if it predicts y it does not count as an adversarial example even though
410   the model makes a mistake. For image classification, this appears intuitively reasonable since small
411   changes to images should not change the class. It follows that for such applications, the ideal model
412   is smooth, i.e., small perturbations of the input do not affect its output.
413
      Several works have shown that adversarial training increases flatness with respect to the in-
414   puts (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2019; Kanai et al., 2023), and adversarial robustness has been em-
415   pirically linked to flatness of the loss curve with respect to the parameters (Wu et al., 2020; Stutz
416   et al., 2021), but a theoretical link is non-trivial since perturbations of the input relate non-linearly
417   to perturbations of the model weights. In the following, we establish such a formal link and show
418   how it can be used to guarantee adversarial robustness. For that, we first formally define adversarial
419   robustness. A model f ∈ F is robust against adversarial examples on a set S ⊆ X × Y if no ad-
420   versarial examples exist in the vicinity of each element of S. With Bdδ (x) = {ξ ∈ X | d(x, ξ) ≤ δ}
421   denoting the δ-ball around x ∈ X with respect to the distance d from Def. 2, adversarial robustness
422
      can be defined similar to Schmidt et al. (2018) as follows.
423   Definition 3. Let S ⊆ X × Y be a dataset drawn iid. according to D and d : X × X be a distance
424   on X . A model f ∈ F is (ϵ, δ, S)-robust against adversarial examples, if for all x ∈ S it holds
425
      that there is no adversarial example with threshold ϵ in Bdδ (x). A model f is (ϵ, δ)-robust if it is
      (ϵ, δ, S)-robust for all S drawn iid from D.
426
427   Relative sharpness of a model f (x) = g(wϕ(x)) with feature extractor ϕ, classifier g, and weights
428   from features to classifier w is non-linearly linked to perturbations in the input (Petzka et al., 2021).
429   We use this property to show that relative sharpness measured for a single example can bound the
430   loss increase through perturbations. The minimum relative sharpness for all clean examples in data
431   S then provides a bound on how much any example in S can be perturbed without significantly
      increasing the loss. In other words, it provides a guarantee on the adversarial robustness of f on S.

      7   D ISCUSSION & C ONCLUSION
487
488
      Within the domain of guaranteeing adversarial robustness, a common methodology is to assume (or
489
      regularize for) a low Lipschitz constant to derive bounds. It is also known, however, that minimiz-
490   ing a global Lipschitz constant indefinitely leads to bad performance on clean samples (Yang et al.,
491   2020). The global Lipschitz constant alone is therefore not helpful for obtaining good-performing
492   and robust models. There exists, however, an intricate connection between the Lipschitz property
493   of the model and its flatness measured with respect to parameters (Kanai et al., 2023); moreover,
494   improving robustness with respect to the parameter changes also improves adversarial robustness
495   empirically (Wu et al., 2020). Using the notion of relative flatness (Petzka et al., 2021), which is
496   theoretically linked to the generalization of the deep learning models, we develop a bound that con-
497   nects Lipschitzness, flatness, and adversarial robustness. This bound means that inducing flatness
498
      with respect to parameters will improve the adversarial robustness of the model, given that the func-
      tion described by the model is smooth. Together with the results of Petzka et al. (2021) this links the
499
      adversarial robustness and generalization abilities of a model.
500
501   Measurements of relative flatness for multi-step attacks with varying parameters reveal two things.
502   First, for all architectures, we can systematically find uncanny valleys on which the trajectory in-
503   dicates a very similar geometry across the models, which might be connected to the existence of
504
      Universal Adversarial Examples (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017) and transferability of adversarial
      examples. Second, strong adversarial samples fall into flat uncanny valleys and therefore would be
505
      missed by most defenses and detection methods based on representations (Lee et al., 2018; Ma et al.,
506
      2018; Xu et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2018). This is in alignment with conclusions drawn
507   by Tramer (2022) and Carlini & Wagner (2017a): the strength of the attack can always be increased
508   in a way that the currently applied defense will fail. At the same moment, uncanny valleys give a
509   fast and easy-to-measure quantity, which could allow for the detection of adversarial attacks during
510   inference. Interestingly, and contrary to the observation of Tramer (2022), stronger attacks that fall
511   into uncanny valleys are in fact easier to detect. Simultaneously, relative flatness can also serve as a
512   tool to detect jailbreak attacks, as most of the adversarial prompts lie in sharp regions.
513
514   Limitations & Future Work We consider relative flatness wrt. the penultimate layer, because it
515   is theoretically sound and its Hessian is fast to compute , when cross-entropy loss is used . There
516   exists, however, a multitude of previous work claiming that adversarial robustness should be con-
517   sidered with respect to the properties of the individual layers; Kumari et al. (2019) propose a latent
518   adversarial training technique that improves the robustness of intermediate layers and this leads to
519
      better overall robustness; Bakiskan et al. (2022) and Walter et al. (2022) analyse which of the layers
      play a specific role for robustness. Petzka et al. (2021) show that the relative flatness measured in
520
      different layers has similar correlation with generalization. In line with that we observation, we find
521
      in preliminary experiments that relative flatness wrt. adversarial examples shows similar patterns in
522   different layers (cf. Figure 9), i.e., the uncanny valley is also present in earlier representations. The
523   layer-wise perspective on the robustness is an interesting research direction (Adilova et al., 2023)
524   that might be a continuation for this work, i.e., can we regularize for the flatness of one layer to
525   obtain better performing and more adversarially robust networks? In particular, the fact that the
526   phenomenon follows a similar pattern in shallower layers begs the questions, if the last layer can
527   serve as an avenue to influence the robustness of earlier representations, but at a much lower cost.
528   Computing the Lipschitz constant exactly is NP-hard, which limits the practical applicability of
529   our theoretical results. For 1-Lipschitz neural networks (Araujo et al., 2023) or networks trained
530   with Lipschitz regularization (Virmaux & Scaman, 2018; Gouk et al., 2021), however, the bound
531   is readily applicable. For general neural networks, exploring how a tighter approximation of local
532   smoothness via local Lipschitzness can be used to improve the practicability of our results makes
533   for excellent future work.
534   An important direction for future work is to investigate how the phenomenon of uncanny valleys
535   can be translated into an actionable detection method for image classifiers, and more importantly for
536   LLMs, as these models are already exposed to the general public and pose a larger threat. We provide
537   some preliminary results in Appendix F by simply thresholding the sharpness measure which show
538   that adversarial examples can be detected with > 90% accuracy. Currently, there is no satisfying
539   theoretical explanation for the existence of uncanny valleys. Thus, we are specifically interested in
      exploring and answering the question: What lies at the bottom of the uncanny valley?

       Definition 2 naturally expands Definition 1 by taking into account other distance metrics and formal-
1135   izing the thresholds that are not specified in the original definition. A threshold on the loss difference
1136   generalizes from classification tasks, where targeted attacks can be modeled by a specific loss func-
1137   tion. Therefore, our definition allows identifying adversarial example with respect to loss and not
1138   with respect to prediction itself, which is closer to the construction of adversarial examples with
1139   loss maximization. A distance threshold captures the requirement that adversarial perturbations are
1140   imperceptible. Using the minimum distance is reasonable from an optimization perspective, but it
1141   also highlights a flaw in the original definition: If the minimal perturbation is so large that is indeed
1142   perceptible (or, even worse, would change, e.g., the picture of a cat to that of a dog) then it would
1143
       still be considered an adversarial example under Def. 1.
1144   It seems more natural to set a fixed threshold δ on the distance that captures what it means for a
1145   perturbation to be imperceptible, as in the definition of untargeted attacks proposed by Carlini &
1146   Wagner (2017b). From all adversarial examples defined that way, one can then find the closest to
1147   the clean example. We show that Definition 2 is also a generalization of this definition by Carlini &
1148   Wagner (2017b) of untargeted attacks with general Lp -distances.

1150   f : X → Y be a classifier, x ∈ Rn , ∥ · ∥p be a p-norm, and δ > 0. Then every ξ ∈ X with
1151   ∥x − ξ∥p ≤ δ is an adversarial example for x if f (ξ) ̸= f (x).

1170   It is possible to detect adversarial examples using a simple threshold on the relative sharpness mea-
1171   sure. We did not include a practical study of this since it would go beyond the scope of this paper.
1172   Developing a sound method requires more than fine-tuning the threshold. Moreover, this requires
1173   comparing the approach to a wide range of state-of-the-art detection methods, which would be a
1174
       paper of its own. Therefore, we leave this interesting practical aspect for future work. Nonetheless,
       we provide preliminary results. We trained a decision stump on the sharpness of clean and adversar-
1175
       ial samples on CIFAR-10 for W IDE R ES N ET-28-4 using 5-fold cross-validation, which yields the
1176
       following accuracies: [0.92, 0.92, 0.93, 0.92, 0.92], i.e., adversarial examples can be detected with
1177   an average accuracy of 0.92 with little to no difference between the folds.
