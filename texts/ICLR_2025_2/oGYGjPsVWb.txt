027                                                               A BSTRACT
028
029            Most text-to-image customization techniques fine-tune models on a small set of
               personal concept images captured in minimal contexts. This often results in the
030
               model becoming overfitted to these training images and unable to generalize to
031            new contexts in future text prompts. Existing customization methods are built on
032            the success of effectively representing personal concepts as textual embeddings.
033            Thus, in this work, we resort to diversifying the context of these personal con-
034            cepts solely within the textual space by simply creating a contextually rich set of
035
               text prompts, together with a widely used self-supervised learning objective. Sur-
               prisingly, this straightforward and cost-effective method significantly improves
036            semantic alignment in the textual space, and this effect further extends to the im-
037            age space, resulting in higher prompt fidelity for generated images. Additionally,
038            our approach does not require any architectural modifications, making it highly
039            compatible with existing text-to-image customization methods. We demonstrate
040
               the broad applicability of our approach by combining it with four different base-
               line methods, achieving notable CLIP score improvements.

043   1   I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Diffusion-based generative models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;
046   Song et al., 2020b) have made significant progress in image synthesis, achieving improved diversity
      and expressiveness in generated outputs. Extending these breakthroughs, diffusion-based text-to-
047
      image models (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Balaji et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Xue
048   et al., 2024) that leverage large-scale text-image pairs (Schuhmann et al., 2021) have demonstrated
049   impressive capabilities in translating the text into visual content.
050
      More recently, leveraging the strong prior knowledge acquired by the pretrained text-to-image gen-
051   erative models, numerous approaches have been proposed to fine-tune the models for customization
052   to specific concepts (Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Voynov et al., 2023;
053   Avrahami et al., 2023). Typically, these methods use 4-5 images containing personal concepts to
      obtain token embedding aligned with the given images, which are then integrated into the novel text

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      prompts for image generation. While demonstrating its potential, existing models often suffer from
055   the concept overfitting when fine-tuned on a small set of images with limited contexts. This over-
056   fitting often causes the customized model to generate images that are highly similar to the training
057   images, and fail to faithfully follow the text prompts during inference (Figure 1).
058   Our study indicates that introducing diverse contexts during model fine-tuning can mitigate the con-
059   cept overfitting (Zeng et al., 2024). As diversifying text-image tuning pairs can be costly and often
060   impractical, in this paper, we propose to diversify the context of personal concepts solely within the
061   textual space, by first simply constructing a set of contextually diverse text prompts with concept to-
      kens, nearly at no extra cost (Figure 2, left). As the customization aligns the personal concept with a
062
      concept token, this proposed approach can be a highly cost-effective way of context diversification.
063   Then, we further adopt a self-supervised learning objective, Masked Language Modeling (MLM)
064   (Devlin et al., 2018), which drives the concept embedding to learn proper relations to its contexts
065   (Figure 2, right).
066   We later both theoretically and empirically show that adopting the MLM objective with a contextu-
067   ally diverse text prompt set during customization significantly alleviates the concept overfitting, and
068   leads to semantic enhancement in textual representation, which ultimately extends to higher prompt
069   fidelity in image generation. We conduct extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of
      our approach.

101   2   R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   Text-to-Image Generation. The introduction of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Nichol &
104
      Dhariwal, 2021; Song et al., 2020a) has paved the way for a series of text-to-image generative
      models (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Balaji et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021; 2022;
105   Ding et al., 2022) that have achieved significant success. GLIDE (Nichol et al., 2021) demonstrated
106   that using classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) can enhance both the photorealism and
107   caption alignment of generated images. DALLE-2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) further improved the pro-
      cess by leveraging CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) embeddings to derive an image prior from a text


                                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      caption, which is then decoded using diffusion models. Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022)
109   proposed a way to improve the efficiency by applying the diffusion process in the lower dimensional
110   latent space, and SDXL (Podell et al., 2023) has been proposed to make improvements over SD by
111   updating the model architecture. ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) proposed to incorporate additional
112   input conditions to improve the controllability of the T2I model using zero-convolutional layers. In
      our work, we mainly focus on baseline methods that are based on Stable Diffusion.
113
114   Personalized Text-to-Image Genearation. Textual inversion (Gal et al., 2022) pioneered a
115   method to convert personal concept images into token embeddings, enabling the use of tokens for
116
      tailored text-to-image generation. DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) extended TI by fine-tuning the
      diffusion UNet along with the prior-preservation loss to prevent forgetting of prior concepts. Since
117   the introduction of the pioneering works, a line of work has been proposed to make further improve-
118   ments. XTI (Voynov et al., 2023) proposed to invert the concept into multiple token embeddings,
119   each specialized for a different layer of the diffusion network. CustomDiffusion (Kumari et al.,
120   2023) proposed to fine-tune the cross-attention layer in diffusion UNet for efficient training. Break-
121
      A-Scene (Avrahami et al., 2023) proposed to learn multiple concepts included in the same scene by
      utilizing masked diffusion loss. There is also a growing interest in developing methods specialized
122   for facial images. (Yuan et al., 2023) constructed a set of basis tokens corresponding to celebrities
123   and optimized their weights to synthesize a given image. (Peng et al., 2024) proposed to augment
124   the concept token embedding by extracting facial features using a facial recognition model. (Shi
125   et al., 2024) have proposed a test-time finetuning-free method where a learnable image encoder is
126
      deployed to convert the input images into a textual token. (Chen et al., 2024) also proposed an instant
      method where an apprentice diffusion model learns to imitate the behaviors of multiple expert mod-
127   els specialized for each concept. Similarly, (Wei et al., 2023) proposed to use a CLIP image encoder
128   to encode personal images and then utilize global and local mapping networks to obtain enhanced
129   representations of the concept. (Chen et al., 2023) proposed a method to avoid the entanglement of
130   identity-irrelevant features by utilizing learnable masks and multi-task training objectives.

      Prompt Set Construction. To achieve customization with diverse contexts, we construct a set of

243   to minimize the effort in manually crafting a large set of prompts. For this, we query the LLM to
244   generate a list of contexts of different types, e.g., background or subjection variation. For detailed
245   descriptions of the prompt set construction process, refer to the Appendix Section A.2.
246   Pretraining. Although the CLIP text encoder of SD has the linguistic capability of comprehending
247   text prompts, it is solely trained with contrastive learning objectives (Radford et al., 2021), and does
248   not support MLM. Therefore, before proceeding with fine-tuning for customization, we first pretrain
249
      a network, namely, a contextualizer ψ to incorporate the MLM capability. We provide the pretraining

250   not involved. We only train the mask embedding and the layers of the contextualizer. The CLIP text
251   encoder and diffusion U-Net remain fixed.

303   Figure 4: Illustrative comparison between the baseline approach and ours. Left: The baseline
304   approach is prone to losing the semantics of the contexts, as the concept token embedding only
305   learns to associate the tokens within limited contexts that correspond to the same concept image. As

311   Most customization methods that fine-tune the model with limited context (Gal et al., 2022; Ruiz
312   et al., 2023; Voynov et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023) often suffer from concept overfitting (Zeng
313
      et al., 2024), resulting in generated images that primarily contain the personal concept without ad-
      hering to the prompt. We next analyze that concept overfitting leads to a high similarity between
314   the text embeddings of context tokens and the concept token, ultimately causing a loss of contextual
315   semantics.
316   Proposition 1. The model overfitting to the concept token makes the attention map mostly attend to
317   the concept token, i.e., A[i, j∗ ] ≫ A[i, j], ∀j ̸= j∗ , where j∗ is the index of the concept token. The
318   distance between the context embeddings ci and the concept embedding ci∗ is bounded,
319
                                                                       ||ci − ci∗ ||2 ≤ δV .                                                                      (8)
320
321   In contrast, the MLM regularizes the loss of contextual information and guides the learning of di-
322   verse contexts (Figure 4, right). Specifically, we focus on two types of text embeddings for the
323   concept token: (i) the embedding derived from the prompt with the concept token, referred to as
      cb , which represents the embeddings obtained through a typical customization method; and (ii) the


                                                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      embedding from the prompt without the concept token, denoted as ĉb , which are the proposed em-
325   beddings with a low MLM loss, i.e., the desired embeddings we aim to achieve.
326   Proposition 2. Optimizing the concept token p∗ with the MLM loss LMLM , the minimized dis-
327   tance between the text embedding of context token cb and ĉb is the necessary condition to minimize
328   LMLM (cb ), i.e.,
329                                  LMLM (cb ) − LMLM (ĉb ) ≤ δg ||cb − ĉb ||2 .                   (9)
330   Remark 3. According to Proposition 1, solely optimizing the concept token tends to produce text
      embeddings of context token cb that closely resemble the text embedding of concept token c∗ but
331
      deviate from desired embeddings ĉb . However, as outlined in Proposition 2, incorporating MLM
332   can significantly align cb with ĉb .
333
334   We provide the proof of Proposition 1 and 2 in Appendix A.4.
335   To empirically validate this, we provide a cosine similarity analysis using a set of 200 text prompts
336   with concept tokens and varying context tokens (Table 1). We analyze the cosine similarity, sim1 =
337   cos(ci∗ , cb ), and sim2 = cos(cb , ĉb ), respectively. The result shows that the baseline approach leads
338
      to high similarity between the concept-context tokens (sim1 ) and low similarity in the context token
      from the two prompts (sim2 ). The baseline result implies that the contextual semantics are not only
339   getting similar to the concept (high sim1 ) but also implies that the context token loses its semantics
340   as it becomes dissimilar to the semantics that are preserved in the context token (low sim2 ). In

      5.3 A BLATION S TUDY
473
474   In this section, we conduct ablation studies to provide deeper insights into our method and validate
      its effectiveness. To solely compare the effect of adjusting the textual space, we have UNet and
475
      CLIP fixed and only update the concept token embedding.
476
477   Impact of λ. We first study the impact of MLM objective weight (Table 3). We adopt TI as our base-
      line and compare the prompt and subject fidelity with the model trained with different λ. We note
478
      the general trend of improved CLIP-T score as the model is trained with increased λ. Additionally,
479   the result indicates as the λ increases, subject fidelity can be slightly decreased. We hypothesize that
480   this trade-off arises due to the model prioritizing textual context alignment over subject preservation
481   at higher λ values, as the MLM objective encourages the model to focus more on capturing the
482   semantic relationships of the contexts.
483   Impact of Contextual Semantics in Customizing Multi-concept Images. We study whether the
484   proposed method effectively guides the personal concept token to utilize the contextual semantics,
485   by applying our method to learn a single concept from images containing multiple concepts (Fig-
      ure 6). For this, we construct a set of 50 prompts that contextual semantics of the concept is highly

486
      specific to the concept (e.g., “a man petting a [v] dog” for a dog). Surprisingly, applying our ap-
487   proach leads to successful learning of the targeted concept within the multiple concepts, leading to
488   disentanglement results. This result indicates that, by training the concept embedding to predict the
489   word that best aligns with its contexts, the concept embedding is driven to be semantically aligned
490   with the context. As the overall semantics of the prompt set are highly specific to the concept (e.g.,
      a dog), the concept embedding converges toward representing that specific concept (i.e., a dog).

      contextual information, as excessively high masking value leads to contextual semantics removal.

      6     C ONCLUSION
534   In this paper, we proposed a highly cost-effective text-to-image customization method that enhances
535   the semantics of the textual representation, thereby improving the semantic quality and prompt fi-
536
      delity of the generated images. Our analysis revealed that the context overfitting problem in existing
      approaches stems from fine-tuning with limited contexts. We addressed this issue by diversifying
537   the context of the personal concept solely within the textual space. By integrating our approach
538   with different text-to-image customization methods, we observed consistent improvement in CLIP
539   scores. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through both theoretical analysis
      and extensive experimental validation.
