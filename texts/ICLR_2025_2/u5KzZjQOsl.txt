                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011                Traditional supervised learning typically assumes that all features are available
012                simultaneously during deployment. However, this assumption does not hold in
013                many real-world scenarios, such as medicine, where information is acquired se-
014                quentially based on an evolving understanding of a specific patient’s condition.
015                Active Feature Acquisition aims to address this problem by dynamically selecting
016                which feature to measure based on the current observations, independently for each
017                test instance. Current approaches either use Reinforcement Learning, which suffers
018                from training difficulties; or greedily maximize the conditional mutual information
019
                   of the label and unobserved features, which inherently makes myopic acquisitions.
                   To address these shortcomings, we introduce a novel method using information
020
                   bottleneck. Via stochastic encodings, we make acquisitions by reasoning about the
021
                   features across many possible unobserved realizations in a regularized latent space.
022                Extensive evaluation on a large range of synthetic and real datasets demonstrates
023                that our approach reliably outperforms a diverse set of baselines.

026   1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The standard supervised learning paradigm is to learn a predictive model using a training dataset
029   of features and labels, such that the model can make accurate predictions on unseen test inputs.
030   A fundamental assumption is that, at test time, all features are jointly available, however, this
031
      assumption does not always hold. Consider the example of a doctor diagnosing a patient (Kachuee
      et al., 2019b;a). Initially, there is little to no information available and, while there are many tests
032
      that could be conducted, the doctor will choose which ones to carry out based on their current
033
      understanding of the specific patient’s condition. For instance, if a patient has pain in their leg, and
034   the doctor suspects a fracture, a leg X-ray might be prioritized. Active Feature Acquisition (AFA)1 is
035   an inference time task, where the features are not assumed to be all available at once. Instead, on
036   an instance-wise basis, a model sequentially acquires features based on the observations to best aid
037   long-term prediction. A common approach is to use Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Rückstieß et al.,
038   2013; Shim et al., 2018), since this is a natural solution to a sequential decision making problem.
039   However, RL suffers from training difficulties such as sparse reward, exploration vs exploitation, and
040   the deadly-triad (Henderson et al., 2018; Erion et al., 2022; Van Hasselt et al., 2018). An alternative
041   approach is to select features that greedily maximize the conditional mutual information (CMI) (Chen
042
      et al., 2015a;b). This has a significant drawback: CMI does not capture the effects of unobserved
      features that can be acquired at a later stage due to marginalizing these out. This prevents CMI
043
      from selecting features that are independent of the label but highly informative of which feature to
044
      acquire next, resulting in myopic decision making that optimizes for immediate predictive power.
045   Additionally, we argue that CMI is not even guaranteed to be the best short-term objective to make
046   decisive predictions. Since maximizing CMI is equivalent to minimizing entropy and this can be
047   achieved by making unlikely classes even more unlikely, rather than selecting features that distinguish
048   between more probable outcomes. We explore the drawbacks of CMI in more detail in Section 4.
049
      Motivated by the shortcomings of RL and CMI maximization, we introduce a novel AFA approach
050
      using Information Bottleneck (IB) (Tishby et al., 2000). We call our approach Information Bottleneck
051   for Feature Acquisition (IBFA) and it departs from existing methods in several key ways. First, we
052   shift the acquisition problem from reasoning in a complex feature space to a latent space. This is
053

      regularized with IB such that decisions are made using label-relevant information only and not feature-
055   level noise. Second, we use stochastic encoders, allowing us to acquire features by considering
056   their effect across a diverse range of possible latent realizations. By removing marginalization over
057   unobserved features the resulting acquisitions are non-greedy by design. Third, our acquisition
058   objective places more focus on labels with higher predicted likelihood, leading to acquisitions that
059   help to disambiguate between the most likely classes. Finally, to avoid the difficulties posed by
060   RL, we do not train our model to make acquisitions directly. Instead we train with a predictive
061   loss and make acquisitions by maximizing a custom objective in a suitably regularized latent space.
062   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We re-examine the CMI objective and provide theoretical
063
      reasoning and concrete examples of its sub-optimality. (2) We introduce IBFA, our novel AFA
      approach motivated by the limitations of RL and CMI maximization. (3) We evaluate IBFA on
064
      multiple synthetic and real-world datasets, including cancer classification tasks. Comparing against
065
      various AFA baselines, we see that IBFA consistently outperforms these methods. Extensive ablations
066   further demonstrate each novel design choice is required for the best performance.

071   2.1   ACTIVE F EATURE ACQUISITION
072
      Reinforcement Learning. The most common AFA approach is to frame the problem as a Markov
073
      Decision Process and train a policy network with RL to decide which feature to acquire next (Dulac-
074
      Arnold et al., 2011; Rückstieß et al., 2013; Shim et al., 2018; Janisch et al., 2019; Mnih et al., 2014;
075   Kachuee et al., 2019a). The RL approach readily extends to a temporal setting where features and
076   labels can change over time (Kossen et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2020). Whilst a natural solution to
077   AFA, RL suffers from training difficulties, and various advances in the RL field have been applied to
078   account for this. For example, using generative models to augment datasets (Zannone et al., 2019),
079   providing mutual information as additional input to the policy (Li & Oliva, 2021), using gradient
080   information in the training process (Ghosh & Lan, 2023), and reward shaping (Peng et al., 2018).
081   Conditional Mutual Information Maximization. Conditional Mutual information tells us how
082   much we can learn about one variable by measuring a second, whilst already knowing a third. Greedy
083   CMI maximization is a common AFA approach, due to its grounding in information theory, however
084   (as we demonstrate in Section 4), it inherently makes short-term acquisitions and is prone to making
085   acquisitions that do not distinguish between likely labels. Among existing approaches, networks
086   can be trained to directly predict CMI (Gadgil et al., 2024), or policy networks can be specially
087   trained to maximize CMI without ever calculating it (Chattopadhyay et al., 2023; Covert et al.,
088   2023). Generative models are a second way to estimate CMI by taking Monte Carlo estimates over
089
      conditional distributions, (Chattopadhyay et al., 2022; Rangrej & Clark, 2021; Early et al., 2016).
      This approach suffers from associated generative modeling challenges, producing poor estimates
090
      of CMI, thus adding to the limitations. Improved performance can be achieved with advances in
091
      generative modeling (Peis et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020; Li & Oliva, 2020).
092
093   Alternative Solutions. Sensitivity-based solutions make selections based on how sensitive the
094   label is to a given feature (Kachuee et al., 2017; 2018). However, since missing values are filled
095
      with zero and measuring a feature is discontinuous, the gradient does not reliably represent the true
      sensitivity. Imitation learning has been applied (Valancius et al., 2023; He et al., 2016), however, this
096
      requires access to an oracle or to construct one. Prior to deep learning, decision trees were used, with
097
      features acquired at each branch of a tree if unobserved (Xu et al., 2012; 2013; Kusner et al., 2014;
098   Trapeznikov & Saligrama, 2013; Xu et al., 2014). This has also been generalized to ensembles (Nan
099   et al., 2015; 2016).
100
101
      2.2   I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK
102
103   Information bottleneck (IB) is a technique that aims to compress a feature vector to a new representa-
104   tion, so that as much label information is preserved while removing unnecessary feature information
105   (Tishby et al., 2000; Alemi et al., 2017). Existing applications of IB include improving adversarial
106   robustness (Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Kuang et al., 2024), integrating data from multiple
107   views (Lee & Van der Schaar, 2021; Wang et al., 2019; Federici et al., 2020), and recently imputation
      (Choi & Lee, 2023). IB has only recently been applied to standard feature selection. These methods


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      work by either scoring a feature with the optimized IB objective using only one feature (Pan et al.,
109   2023), or by using a stochastic gate to drop features before the encoder, and optimizing both the
110   encoder and gate with the IB objective (Zhang et al., 2023). We instead use IB to regularize the latent
111   space in which we will be conducting AFA, rather than scoring features to find a fixed global subset.
112   To our best knowledge, we are the first to apply IB in the context of AFA.

      4    U NDERSTANDING THE L IMITATIONS OF CMI M AXIMIZATION
143
144
      Here we more closely examine the shortcomings of greedy CMI maximization for AFA, to gain
145
      understanding into why CMI maximization can be sub-optimal and how this can be addressed. Whilst
146   grounded in theory and extensively applied, it suffers from two drawbacks previously alluded to.
147
148   First, greedy CMI maximization makes myopic acquisitions, which in some scenarios is guaranteed to
149
      be sub-optimal. We prove this with an example. Consider a feature vector with d + 1 features, the first
      d of which are binary, and the last taking an integer value from 1 to d: X ∈ {0, 1}d × [d]. The final
150
      feature acts as an indicator, informing us which of the other d features gives the label, y = xxd+1 . The
151
      optimal strategy is to first choose the indicator then its designated feature, 2 acquisitions. However,
152   to arrive at the same prediction, the expected number of acquisitions by greedily maximizing CMI
153   is 3 − d1 . We prove this in Appendix G; here, we provide theoretical insight into why CMI fails on
154   this task, motivating our solution. CMI fails because possible future observations    are not considered
                                                                                  R
155   in the present decision since they are marginalized out, p(xi , y|xO ) = p(xj , xi , y|xO )dxj . Each
156   acquisition is made like there are no subsequent acquisitions and therefore the indicator is not chosen
157   first. This is not specific to CMI, but any scoring that marginalizes out unobserved features.
158   Proposition 1. Any acquisition objective that uses the marginal p(xi , y) to score feature i will not
159   select the indicator first.
160
161   The proof is straightforward: With no other features, the indicator and label are independent, so
      the marginal is given by p(xd+1 , y) = p(xd+1 )p(y). It is therefore impossible to measure its effect


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      on the label without considering possible values of other features, regardless of how the effect is
163   measured. RL methods do not suffer from this, since during training different scenarios are seen
164   and the effects distilled into the parameters. Building on this, adjusting the CMI objective to include
165   possible values of unobserved features can solve the indicator problem under greedy maximization.
166
                                                R
      Proposition 2. Greedy maximization of I(Xi ; Y |xO , xU )p(xU |xO )dxU is an optimal strategy for
167   the indicator problem, where xU are unobserved features excluding i, U = x[d]\(O∪i) .
168
169   We prove this in Appendix G. Note we will not use this as our acquisition objective, since this is
170   intractable. The key takeaway from these two propositions is that considering possible values of other
171   unobserved features is necessary for optimality and, if the objective is chosen well, sufficient.
172   The second drawback of CMI is that, even as a short-term objective, it is not guaranteed to be the
173   best objective for identifying the most likely class. CMI maximization is equivalent to minimizing
174   entropy, and to show why this is not guaranteed to be optimal, consider two distributions over 3
175   classes and their entropies: H([0.5, 0.5, 0.0]) = 0.693 and H([0.7, 0.15, 0.15]) = 0.819. The first
176   distribution has lower entropy, but the second is more favorable for making a prediction. It is possible
177   to maximize CMI by making low probabilities lower, rather than distinguishing between possible
178   answers. We provide a detailed example in Appendix H. The insight is that reducing the entropy is
179
      not always equivalent to making a decisive prediction, therefore an effective acquisition objective
      will place more focus on the most likely labels.

185   Bottleneck for Feature Acquisition (IBFA). We provide a block diagram of our method in Figure 1,
186   showing both how the model makes predictions and calculates the acquisition objective. In short,
187

188
      architecture to construct an effective acquisition objective. Our acquisition objective is:

195

198   Training with Predictive Loss. To avoid the difficulites associated with training an acquisition
199   objective with RL, we train using a novel predictive loss (given in Section 5.3) with IB regularization
200
      (Tishby et al., 2000). Our objective is explicitly defined in equation 1.
201   Acquisition via the Latent Space. The label can be a highly non-linear function of the features,
202   and training an AFA model to make decisions directly in the feature space can be an equally complex
203   task. We sidestep this difficulty by writing the acquisition objective as an expectation in a highly
204   regularized latent space. This is why r takes z as an explicit input, and not xO , under sufficient IB
205   regularization, z contains only label relevant information and no noise associated with the features.
206   Stochastic Encodings. As demonstrated in Section 4, taking into account possible values of other
207   unobserved features is necessary for optimality. Therefore we take an expectation of r(c, z, i), over
208   the latent distribution pθ (z|xO ). This way, future possible latent realizations are taken into account in
209   the current decision. To sample the full diversity of the latent space, we take multiple samples during
210   acquisition, we empirically verify the importance of this in Section 6.
211   Weighting by Predictions. Finally, as demonstrated in Section 4, CMI maximization can be
212   achieved by focusing on reducing the likelihood of classes with already low probabilities. To
213   overcome this, r takes c as input, measuring how much observing feature i would affect the predicted
214   probability of class c. And then an expectation is taken using the current predictions pθ,ϕ (Y = c|xO )
215   so that our acquisition objective places more focus on the classes with higher predicted likelihood.
      We demonstrate the impact of this idea empirically in ablations in Appendix C.

      7     C ONCLUSION
525
      This paper considered Active Feature Acquisition, the test time task of actively choosing which
526
      features to observe to improve a prediction. We introduced a novel approach for AFA, moving away
527
      from previous solutions based on RL and CMI maximization, using IB to regularize a stochastic
528   latent embedding space of the features. Our method regularly outperformed previous methods across
529   a range of tasks, and we validated acquired features in the scientific literature.
530
531
      Limitations. Currently our method applies to classification tasks but not to regression tasks. This
      is because our method requires separation of class probabilities during acquisition and this notion
532
      is not well defined for continuous labels. We view this as an interesting avenue for future work.
533
      Our method also includes the encoding architecture that features are mapped separately to latent
534   components. This means that observed features cannot affect the latent distribution of unobserved
535   features. The trade-off is that it becomes trivial to link latent components to features, we see in the
536   experiments this choice does not prevent IBFA from outperforming the baselines. Finally, due to
537   requiring multiple latent samples, IBFA, has larger memory requirements at inference time than RL
538   baselines, depending on how many samples are used. However, CMI maximization methods with
539   generative models also require multiple samples at inference time, so this is not a new limitation for
      AFA models.

       B ROADER I MPACT
811
812
       Our paper is concerned with Active Feature Acquisition, the test time task of acquiring features to
813    iteratively improve a model’s predictions on a given data instance. Applications range from medical
814    diagnosis to polling a population. We believe on the whole these applications have a positive benefit
815    on society. Naturally, since this is a general task (any task where features are not all available
816    immediately), malicious applications do exist. For example, iteratively harvesting personal data to
817    send targeted misinformation. However, this work does not focus on those applications, and since
818    this area of machine learning research is still in its relative infancy we do not envisage this occurring
819    for the foreseeable future. An important consideration is if this work is used in a positive setting but
820    gives incorrect predictions. In the medical scenario a doctor might miss an important test to diagnose
821
       a patient, or conduct a painful/dangerous but unnecessary test. This work is not in a position to be
       deployed currently, so this is not an issue yet. However, if it were to be deployed, this problem can be
822
       mitigated by being used as a tool by domain experts to aid them in their decision making instead of
823
       replacing them.

957   Sensitivity Analysis of β. To further explore the importance of a well regularized latent space,
958   we conduct a sensitivity analysis on the hyperparameter β, keeping all other hyperparameters the
959   same. Higher β leads to the encoders removing more information about the features. We plot the
960   number of acquisitions required to select all relevant features on the synthetic datasets in Figure 10.
961   For all datasets, as expected, if β is too high, the latent space is too heavily regularized. There is not
962
      enough label information in the latent space, so decisions made there lead to sub-optimal acquisitions.
      Equally, by not regularizing the latent space enough, there is nothing explicitly enforcing the latent
963
      space to remove irrelevant information about the features, also leading to sub-optimal acquisitions.

966   using multiple acquisition samples, to sample the full latent diversity, we run a sensitivity analysis
967   on the synthetic tasks. We plot the number of acquisitions required to select all relevant features in

      Figure 11. As expected if not enough samples are used the number of acquisitions required is larger.
      We use 200 acquisition samples in our experiments which is low enough for fast acquisition, and high

1270

1271

1272

1273   independent p(y, xd+1 ) = p(y)p(xd+1 ). Substituting this into the definition of mutual information
1274   gives

1277   Now consider the mutual information for the other features. Due to the symmetry of the problem, the
1278   mutual information for one of these features is the same for all others. The mutual information can
1279   be more usefully written as
1280                                                     Z
1281                               I(Y ; Xi ) = H(Y ) − H(Y |xi )p(xi )dxi .
1282
1283   The entropy of the label is log 2 since there is equal chance of being 0 or 1. Again using the symmetry
1284   of the system, the entropy of Y if Xi = 0 is the same as if Xi = 1, so we only calculate for one
1285   case. When Xi = 0, the probability of Y = 0 is d1 × 1 + d−1             1            1
                                                                         d × 2 . Since in d cases it takes the
1286   exact value of Xi based on the value of the indicator, and in d−1    d cases Y is given by a different

1514   Here we provide details that tend to be shared across models. We explicitly state if a model does not

1516
       Input Layer. In this paper not all features are available all at once. In order to account for this we
1517
       use a binary mask to indicate whether a feature is available or not to a model. The input x and the
1518   mask m go through an input layer before the main model which accounts for missing features. For
1519   continuous features the input is given by [x ⊙ m, m], which is the element-wise product between the
1520   continuous features & their mask concatenated with the mask. Categorical features use a one-hot
1521   encoding, where we include an additional class to indicate a missing feature, i.e. if the mask value
1522   is 0 then the encoding has 1 at the first position. Continuous and categorical features are encoded
1523   separately as above and then concatenated as input to the main model. This applies to the Fixed MLP,
1524   DIME, GDFS, Opportunistic RL and VAE.
1525   Deep Networks. All deep networks follow the same structure. After any specific input layers, we
1526   use linear layers. Each hidden layer has a ReLU activation followed by Batch Normalization (Ioffe &
1527   Szegedy, 2015). All hidden layers in a given network are the same width, which is a hyperparameter
1528   that can be tuned as well as the number of hidden layers. The exception to this is the Opportunistic
1529   RL model, where we replace Batch Normalization with dropout with 0.5 probability in accordance
1530   with the method’s implementation (Kachuee et al., 2019a).
1531   Acquiring Features. To acquire features each method individually has its own way to positively
1532   score all features, where higher scores mean that feature is better to acquire. These scores are
1533   multiplied by (1 − m) so that we do not acquire features we already have. This is also multiplied by
1534   the full data mask so that we do not acquire features that are not available. This would not apply at
1535   deployment where we have the ability to measure all features if desired.

1549   GDFS. GDFS (Covert et al., 2023) has two separate networks, one for prediction, one for scoring
1550   features. Both use the same input layer previously described, both have a softmax final activation to
1551

1560   sampling feature acquisitions and training the scoring network to choose features with the best greedy
1561   prediction from the predictor network.

1587   VAE. The VAE method is a vanilla generative modeling approach to the AFA problem to analyse
1588   the viability of generative models. We use a Variational Auto-Encoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013)
1589
       to model the distribution of the features. Since our input layer allows for missing features this
       allows us to model the distribution of missing features conditioned on observed ones. We train

1591
       number of hidden layers. The encoder predicts µz and σz , where σz is diagonal and is enforced to be
1592   positive by pushing the activations through a softplus and adding 0.001 as a minimum. The decoder
1593   predicts a mean for continuous features, with σ being the standard deviation to estimate the normal
1594   log-likelihood (this is a hyperparameter). For categorical features the decoder predicts logits that go
1595   through softmax. We then train a separate predictor that uses a standard MLP. Features are scored
1596   by taking samples of the unknown features conditioned on the observed ones. These samples go
1597   through the predictor to give an estimated label distribution. The mutual information is then estimated
1598   with I(Xi ; Y |xS ) = Ep(xi |xS ) [DKL (p(Y |xi , xS )||p(Y |xS ))]. We train for 120 epochs. We prevent
1599   overfitting during training by choosing the iteration with the best validation ELBO.
1600   EDDI. EDDI (Ma et al., 2019) is an advanced generative modeling method for AFA. The encoder is
1601   a Partial VAE. For each continuous feature xi , the input to a shared encoding network is [xi , xi ei ],
1602   where ei is a learnable vector which is different for each feature. This goes through a shared network
1603   giving si for each continuous feature. For categorical features a learnable representation is created
1604   for each feature for each possible category including a missing category. So without a network we
1605   still create si for a categorical feature by learning aPmatrix and selecting the row according to the
1606
       category for each feature. We then take the sum c = i mi si so we only include the representations
       for observed features. This aggregated representation c goes through another network to give the
1607
       latent µz and σz . We enforce σz to be positive by pushing it through a softplus and adding 0.001 as
1608
       the minimum. The number of hidden encoder layers refers to both the continuous feature encoder and
1609   c-to-latent encoder, the number is divided by 2 and that many are used in each. We encode the label
1610   in the same way as a categorical feature. We do not include a separate predictor, instead we follow
1611   the original paper to make predictions: features are encoded to a latent distribution and samples are
1612   decoded to y, the absence of a dedicated predictor negatively impacts the results for EDDI. The
1613   decoder follows the same structure as for the VAE. We train for 400 epochs, to prevent overfitting
1614   we choose the iteration with the best validation ELBO. Features are scored based on a sampled KL
1615   divergence calculated in the latent space as described in the original paper, we use 50 samples.
1616   IBFA. Our method, as described in the main paper, encodes each feature separately to a normal
1617   distribution (so we have many small encoding networks, one for each continuous feature). For
1618   each continuous feature we give [mi x̃i , mi ] to that feature’s specific deep encoder, where each
1619   continuous feature also goes through a copula transform x̃i = Φ−1 (Fi (xi )) initially. The copula


                                                          30
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1620
       transform is given by Fi , the empirical CDF of the continuous feature, followed by Φ−1 , the inverse
1621   standard normal CDF. This transformation, as described in its paper (Wieczorek et al., 2018), enforces
1622   a symmetry associated with Information Bottleneck and encourages sparse, disentangled latent
1623   representations, both desired properties. The networks predict µ and σ for each feature by outputing
1624   two unbounded vectors whose size is the number of latent components per feature - a hyperparameter.
1625   Both of the unbounded vectors go through Batch Normalization which we found sped up training.
1626   The first is µ and the second goes through softplus and has 0.001 added, enforcing it to be positive
1627   giving σ.
1628   For each categorical feature we have a learnable matrix, where each row is a vector whose size is the
1629   number of latent components per feature. So for a given category (where missing is the first category)
1630   we simply select the row of the matrix. We use two of these for each feature for the µ and σ. The
1631   selected vectors are unbounded so they go through the same procedure - batch normalization applied
1632   to both, and then softplus and +0.001 to the second to get µ and σ. After concatenating, samples from
1633   the latent distribution can go through an MLP predictor network with softmax to give a predicted
1634   label distribution for that sample.
1635   During acquisition we encode the features we have to the latent distribution, and take 200 samples.
1636   Each is pushed through the predictor to give a distribution for each sample. To score the features
1637   we take the gradient of each classes’ probability with respect to every sample. This gives g =
1638   ∇z pϕ (Y = c|z). To convert this to a score we calculate the normalized length of the vector in each
1639   feature’s latent dimensions. For example, if we have two features and each is encoded to three latent
1640   components, the gradient could be
1641
                                       [1.19, −0.87, 0.81, 0.63, −0.40, 0.29].
1642
1643   We reshape to the number of features by the number of latent components per feature:
1644
1645
                                     [[1.19, −0.87, 0.81], [0.63, −0.40, 0.29]].
1646   We calculate the length of each of these giving
1647
                       p                          p
1648                  [ 1.192 + 0.872 + 0.812 , 0.632 + 0.402 + 0.292 ] = [1.68, 0.801].
1649
1650   We then normalize by dividing by the sum of these
1651
                                   [1.68, 0.801]/(1.68 + 0.801) = [0.68, 0.32].
1652
1653   And this gives us a score for each feature from this latent sample for this class r(c, z, i). We average
1654   across all samples, and sum across all classes weighted by p(Y = c|xO ). This gives a score for every
1655   feature.
1656
       To train, we subsample the feature and encode them to the latent distribution. We take 100 samples,
1657   these go through the predictor giving 100 label distributions which are averaged as the model’s
1658   full prediction. We then calculate the log-likelihood of the overall prediction. We then add the KL
1659   divergence of the latent distribution with a standard normal to enforce the information bottleneck
1660   regularization. We train for 120 epochs, using 200 latent samples for acquisition and prediction
1661   during evaluation.

1663   J.3   M ODEL RUNTIMES
1664
1665   There are two places to consider runtime: training and inference. In Table 5 we provide the scaling
1666   laws of each method with respect to number of features d.
1667
       RL, DIME and GDFS train by simulating acquisition, so each step scales linearly with the number of
1668   features. Generative models (and IBFA) are constant to train since they only train to predict well.
1669   However, during inference, RL, DIME and GDFS only require one forward pass of their policy/CMI
1670   network, whereas EDDI and VAE must individually score every feature. IBFA instead takes gradients
1671   with respect to the predicted class outputs, so the runtime is linear in the number of classes, which is
1672   typically far fewer than the number of features. The main takeaway is that IBFA scales better than
1673   half the methods at training time, better than the other half during acquisition (assuming fewer labels
       than features), and never the worst.
