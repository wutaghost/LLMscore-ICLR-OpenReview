011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for learning rep-
014            resentations from graph-structured data Kipf & Welling (2017); Veličković et al.
015
               (2018), but often rely heavily on labeled data for training. This paper introduces
               a novel hierarchical self-supervised graph contrastive learning framework that ef-
016
               fectively leverages unlabeled data to enhance node representations. Our method
017
               captures rich structural information at multiple scales by incorporating contrastive
018            objectives at the node, subgraph, and graph levels, extending previous work on
019            self-supervised learning for graphs Veličković et al. (2019); You et al. (2020). We
020            employ an adaptive graph augmentation strategy to generate meaningful views of
021            the graph while preserving essential properties. Through extensive experiments
022            on benchmark datasets, including Cora, Citeseer, PubMed Sen & Dhillon (2008),
023            and Reddit Hamilton et al. (2017), we demonstrate that our approach consistently
024            outperforms both supervised and self-supervised baseline models in node clas-
025            sification tasks. Our method shows particular strength in low-label regimes and
026
               exhibits strong generalization capabilities in both transductive and inductive set-
               tings. Ablation studies confirm the importance of each hierarchical component,
027
               while qualitative analyses illustrate the discriminative power of the learned em-
028
               beddings. This work opens new avenues for self-supervised learning on graphs
029            and has broad implications for applications where labeled data is scarce or ex-
030            pensive to obtain, such as in social networks Perozzi et al. (2014) and biological
031            networks Zitnik et al. (2017).

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graph-structured data is pervasive in numerous domains such as social networks Perozzi et al.
037   (2014), biological networks Zitnik et al. (2017), recommendation systems Ying et al. (2018), and
038
      knowledge graphs Wang et al. (2017). Understanding the complex relationships and interactions
      among entities in these domains is crucial for various tasks, including node classification, link pre-
039
      diction, and community detection.
040
041   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for learning representations from
042   graph-structured data Kipf & Welling (2017); Veličković et al. (2018); Hamilton et al. (2017). By
043   leveraging the structural information inherent in graphs, GNNs can capture both local neighborhood
044
      patterns and global structural properties. However, traditional GNNs are typically trained in a su-
      pervised manner, relying heavily on large amounts of labeled data. Obtaining labeled data in graph
045
      domains can be challenging due to the cost, time, and domain expertise required for annotation.
046
047   At the same time, vast amounts of unlabeled graph data are readily available, presenting an oppor-
048   tunity to leverage self-supervised learning methods. Self-supervised learning can exploit unlabeled
049   data by designing auxiliary tasks that provide supervisory signals. In the context of graphs, self-
050
      supervised learning enables models to learn meaningful node representations without the need for
      extensive labeled data Veličković et al. (2019); You et al. (2020).
051
052   In this paper, we propose a novel hierarchical self-supervised graph contrastive learning framework
053   that effectively leverages unlabeled data to enhance node representations. Our framework captures
      both local and global structural information by performing contrastive learning at multiple structural


                                                        1
054
      levels: node, subgraph, and graph. By generating multiple augmented views of the original graph
055   through an adaptive graph augmentation strategy, we ensure that essential structural properties are
056   preserved while providing diverse contexts for learning robust representations.
057
058
      We conduct extensive experiments on several benchmark datasets, including citation networks and
      social networks, demonstrating that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art models, espe-
059
      cially in scenarios where labeled data is scarce. Our contributions are summarized as follows. First,
060
      we introduce a hierarchical contrastive learning framework that performs self-supervised learning
061   at the node, subgraph, and graph levels, capturing rich structural information. Second, we design
062   an adaptive graph augmentation strategy that generates meaningful augmented views, balancing the
063   preservation of essential graph properties with the introduction of sufficient diversity. Third, we
064   empirically validate our method on multiple benchmark datasets, showing significant improvements
065   over baseline models in both transductive and inductive settings.

141
142   3.1   F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
143
144   Our method begins by applying various graph augmentation techniques to generate different views
145
      of the original graph G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes and E is the set of edges. These
      augmented graphs capture diverse structural variations while preserving essential properties of the
146
      original graph. A shared GNN encoder fθ is then used to learn node embeddings from each aug-
147
      mented view. We perform hierarchical contrastive learning by maximizing the agreement between
148   embeddings at the node, subgraph, and graph levels across different views.
149
150   Figure 1 illustrates the overall architecture of our framework. By integrating hierarchical contrastive
151
      objectives, our method captures rich structural information across multiple scales, leading to more
      informative and robust node representations.

316   3.15   O PTIMIZATION
317
318   We optimize the total loss Ltotal using stochastic gradient descent with the Adam optimizer Kingma
319   & Ba (2015). The temperature parameter τ and the hyperparameters α, β, and γ are tuned based on
320
      validation performance.
321   To enhance the effectiveness of contrastive learning, we employ techniques such as temperature
322   scaling and hard negative mining. Temperature scaling adjusts the concentration level of the distri-
323   bution defined by the softmax function, while hard negative mining focuses on challenging negative
      samples that are similar to the anchor.

      3.17    C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
348
349   The computational complexity of our method is primarily determined by the GNN encoder and the
350   contrastive loss calculations. Assuming L layers in the GNN and F features per node, the time
351   complexity per epoch is O(L|E|F + |V|2 F ) due to the message passing and the computation of
352   similarities between node pairs. However, in practice, we can leverage mini-batch training and
353   approximate nearest neighbor techniques to scale to large graphs.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
379
380   Data Splits For Cora, Citeseer, and PubMed, we use the splits with 20 nodes per class for training,
381   500 nodes for validation, and 1,000 nodes for testing. For Reddit, we follow the setup in Hamilton
382   et al. (2017), using 66% of the nodes for training, 10% for validation, and 24% for testing.
383
      To evaluate performance under low-label regimes, we vary the number of labeled nodes per class
384   from 1 to 10 for training, keeping the validation and test sets the same.
385
386   Evaluation Protocols We evaluate our method in both transductive and inductive settings:
387
388   Transductive Setting: The model has access to the entire graph structure during training, including
389
      unlabeled nodes.
390   Inductive Setting: The model is trained on a subgraph and tested on unseen nodes or subgraphs,
391   assessing its generalization capability.
392
393   Metrics We use accuracy as the primary evaluation metric for node classification. For multi-class
394   classification tasks, accuracy is calculated as the proportion of correctly predicted nodes over the
395   total number of nodes in the test set.
396
397   Implementation Details Our GNN encoder is a 2-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) Xu
398   et al. (2019) with hidden dimension 128. The projection head is a 2-layer MLP with hidden dimen-
399   sion 64. We set the temperature parameter τ = 0.5 and hyperparameters α = β = γ = 1 unless
400
      otherwise specified.
401   We optimize the model using Adam Kingma & Ba (2015) with a learning rate of 0.001 and weight
402   decay of 5e-4. Models are trained for 200 epochs with early stopping based on validation loss.
403   Experiments are conducted on a machine with an NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU with 32GB memory.

512

513
      information at multiple hierarchical levels and using an adaptive graph augmentation strategy, our
514   method outperforms state-of-the-art models on various benchmark datasets, particularly in low-label
515   regimes.

      Our approach demonstrates strong generalization capabilities in both transductive and inductive set-
