                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Dataset pruning, while effective for reducing training data size, often leads to
010
               models vulnerable to adversarial attacks. This paper introduces a novel approach
011            to creating adversarially robust coresets. We first theoretically analyze how exist-
012            ing pruning methods result in non-smooth loss surfaces, increasing susceptibility
013            to attacks. To address this, we propose two key innovations: 1) a Frequency-
014            Selective Excitation Network (FSE-Net) that dynamically selects important fre-
015            quency components, smoothing the loss surface while reducing storage require-
016            ments, and 2) a “Joint-entropy” score for selecting stable and informative sam-
017            ples. Our method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art pruning algorithms
018            across various adversarial attacks and pruning ratios. On CIFAR-10, our approach
019
               achieves up to 58.19% accuracy under AutoAttack with an 80% pruning ratio,
               compared to 42.98% for previous methods. Moreover, our frequency pruning
020
               technique improves robustness even on full datasets, demonstrating its potential
021
               for enhancing model security while reducing computational costs.

      further advancements in dataset pruning.

065   2     R ELATED W ORKS
066
067   2.1   DATASET P RUNING
068
      Dataset Pruning, also known as Coreset Selection, aims to shrink the dataset scale by selecting
069
      important samples according to some predefined criteria. Entropy (Coleman et al., 2019) explores
070   the uncertainty and decision boundary with the predicted outputs. GraNd/EL2N (Paul et al., 2021)
071   calculates the importance of a sample with its gradient magnitude. Forgetting (Toneva et al., 2018)
072   defines forgetting events as an accuracy drop at consecutive epochs, and hard samples with the
073   most forgetting events are important. AUM (Pleiss et al., 2020) identifies data by computing the
074   Area Under the Margin, the difference between the true label logits and the largest other logits. CCS
075   (Zheng et al., 2022) extends previous methods by pruning hard samples and using stratified sampling
076   to achieve good coverage of data distributions at a large pruning ratio. While these algorithms
077   propose various methods to enhance coreset performance, none consider the adversarial robustness
078
      of the model when trained on the coreset selected by these methods.
079
080   2.2   A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
081
      Adversarial attacks manipulate machine learning models by introducing subtle perturbations to input
082
      data, causing incorrect predictions. FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014) generates adversarial examples
083   using the gradient of a model’s loss function. PGD (Madry, 2017) extends FGSM by iteratively
084   applying small perturbations. AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020) combines multiple methods for
085   automatic evaluation without manual tuning. C&W attack (Madry, 2017) finds the smallest pertur-
086   bation causing misclassification. Despite extensive research in deep learning, no existing algorithms
087   specifically evaluate the impact of adversarial attacks on models trained with pruned datasets.

185    The motivation of our algorithm is that (Zhang & Zhu, 2019) demonstrates adversarial training can
186    improve the model’s adversarial robustness by shifting the model’s focus from texture and color
187    to shape and silhouette features. Frequency pruning removes textural details while preserving key
188    shape features, helping the model focus more on shape, as shown in Fig. 1. We proposed that
189    a carefully designed frequency pruning algorithm could potentially achieve comparable results to
190    adversarial training, offering a resource-conservation approach to improve model robustness. Our
191    approach adaptively selects important frequency components for each image, aiming to both smooth
192    the model’s local minimum geometry and maintain the model’s capacity.

272   In this section, we propose a novel coreset selection algorithm that assesses the impact of images on
273   shaping the decision surface throughout training. Drawing from reinforcement learning (Kaelbling
274   et al., 1996) and Markov decision processes (Hordijk & Kallenberg, 1979), we assign each image a
275   reward based on its actions during training and calculate the accumulated reward as its final score.
276   To track the temporal dynamics of model parameter changes, we define H(fθ (x̃)))t as the logit
277   entropy at epoch t. Our method aims to balance exploration in the early stages and exploitation in
278   the later stages of training, as suggested by (Petzka & Sminchisescu, 2021). In the early training
279   stage, we encourage the model to explore a larger parameter space to capture more features and
280   escape local minima (Soloperto et al., 2023). Later in training, we aim to reduce gradient variance,
281   signaling stable optimization toward the global minimum. To quantify this balance, we introduce a
282   local variance function V (t, w):

472       5             C ONCLUSION , L IMITATION , AND F UTURE W ORK
473
474       We introduce Adversarial Attack Robust Dataset Pruning, a method that enhances model robustness
475       against adversarial attacks on pruned datasets. Our approach improves robustness in two ways: First,
476       we use a Learnable Frequency Pruning algorithm to smooth the model’s local minimum geometry,
477       increasing robustness without additional training costs and reducing storage needs. Second, we
478       propose a “joint-entropy” data importance score for better coreset selection. Experiments show our
479       method surpasses existing pruning strategies in adversarial robustness across various datasets and
480       attacks. This work is the first to address adversarial robustness in dataset pruning.
481       We recognize several limitations and areas for future work. First, our algorithm does not consider
482       the link between image distribution and adversarial robustness, which could be explored further.
483       Improving the sampling method beyond the traditional CCS algorithm may enhance results. Ad-
484       ditionally, while we focus on adversarial robustness in dataset pruning, the research could extend
485       to areas like Dataset Distillation and Neural Network pruning to improve the model’s adversarial
          robustness.

680   Theorem 1 (Hard Samples and Local Minimum Geometry). Formally, for a model with parameters
681   θ and loss function L, we compare the local minimum geometry at the converged solution θ∗ for
682   hard samples xh and randomly sampled data points xr . For a given perturbation ε, where ∥ε∥ ≤ δ
683   for some small δ > 0, hard samples are more likely to induce less smooth geometries, which can be
684   characterized as:
685
686                                       Exh [∆Lh ] > η · Exr [∆Lr ]                                  (13)

      C.4.1     A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS M EASURE
902
903   To rigorously establish the relationship between a smooth (flat) loss landscape and higher adversar-
904   ial robustness, we begin by defining the adversarial robustness measure in terms of the ”Expected
905   Distortion Rate (EDR)” of the loss function with respect to input perturbations. By connecting
906   this metric to the gradient norm of the loss and demonstrating how smoother loss landscapes yield
907
      smaller gradient norms, we can show that a flatter landscape reduces such distortion. This ulti-
      mately supports the conclusion that smoother loss landscapes enhance adversarial robustness by
908
      limiting variations in loss under input perturbations.
909
910   To measure adversarial robustness, we define the Expected Distortion Rate (EDR). This measure
911   captures the sensitivity of the model’s loss function to adversarial perturbations in the input space.
912   The mathematical definition is:

922             L∞ norms. We specifically use the L2 norm, where ∥δx ∥2 ≤ ϵ for ϵ > 0.

928   Based on the loss landscape visualization depicted in Figure 2a, 2b and 2c, we observe that the
929   geometric characteristics of the loss landscape are predominantly determined by two fundamental
930   components. The smoothness of the loss landscape is a critical factor in determining the robustness
931
      of a model. This section explains how to characterize smoothness using first-order (gradient) and
      second-order (Hessian) properties.
932
933   The local behavior of the loss function L(θ, x, y) around a point x0 can be described as follows:
934

938
               gradient changes in different directions.
939   To achieve a smoother loss landscape, it is important to minimize both the magnitude of the gradient
940   and the spectral norm of the Hessian matrix. Prior research has proposed diverse metrics to charac-
941   terize the smoothness of loss landscapes and their correlation with model generalization, including
942   Volume ε-Flatness (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), Hessian-based measures (Dinh et al., 2017)
943   and gradient-based analysis (Zhang et al., 2023). Our work adopts a more comprehensive approach
944   by jointly analyzing both gradient and curvature characteristics across extended regions of the loss
945   surface. This broader perspective is particularly vital for understanding adversarial robustness, as
946   adversarial perturbations can push model predictions far from local minima, where the geometric
947
      properties of non-minimal regions become crucial determinants of model behavior.
948   To formally quantify smoothness, we use the concept of Lipschitz smoothness. A function f : Rn →
949   R is β-smooth if:
950                            ∥∇f (x) − ∇f (y)∥2 ≤ β∥x − y∥2 , ∀x, y ∈ Rn ,
951   where β is the Lipschitz constant of the gradient. For the loss function L(θ, x, y), this implies that
952   the spectral norm of the Hessian is bounded as:
953                                          ∥∇2x L(θ, x0 , y0 )∥2 ≤ β.
