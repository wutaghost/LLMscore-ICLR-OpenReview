009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Thanks to the principles of the scaling law, current neural networks have expe-
012            rienced remarkable performance improvements. While much of the existing re-
013            search has concentrated on upstream pretraining, the application of the scaling law
014            to downstream vision tasks remains underexplored. Understanding the scaling law
015            in downstream tasks can aid in the design of more effective models and training
016            strategies. Thus, in this work, we aim to investigate the application of the scaling
017            law to downstream vision tasks. Firstly, we explore the impact of three key factors
018            of scaling law: training data volume, model size, and input resolution. We empiri-
019            cally verify that increasing each of these factors can lead to performance enhance-
020
               ments. Secondly, to address naive training’s optimization challenges and lack
               of iterative refinement, we introduce DT-Training which leverages small teacher
021
               transfer and dual-branch alignment to further exploit model potential. Thirdly,
022
               building on DT-Training, we propose a closed-loop scaling strategy to incremen-
023            tally scale the model step-by-step. Finally, our scaled model exhibits strong ability
024            and outperforms existing counterparts across diverse test benchmarks. Extensive
025            experiments also reveal the robust transfer ability of our model. Moreover, we
026            validate the generalizability of the scaling law and our proposed DT-Training on
027            other downstream vision tasks, reinforcing the broader applicability of our ap-
028            proach. We hope that our findings can deepen the understanding of the scaling
029            law in downstream tasks and foster future developments on downstream tasks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      The scaling law has demonstrated success and effectiveness across various domains, including
035
      speech (Radford et al., 2023), language (Brown, 2020; Devlin, 2018; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Raffel
036   et al., 2020), vision (Kolesnikov et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Alabdulmohsin
037   et al., 2024), and multi-modal (Pham et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2021; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2022; Rad-
038   ford et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Cherti et al., 2023). Training large
039   models on extensive datasets over longer periods has consistently led to performance improvements
040   and enhanced transfer ability. However, most of these efforts have concentrated on upstream pre-
041   training stages. Although there have been a lot of works on scaling law training, these works mainly
042   focus on the upstream pretraining. The application of scaling law principles to downstream vision
043   tasks remains rarely explored. Understanding how scaling laws affect downstream vision tasks is
044
      crucial as it can inform the design of more effective models and training strategies.
045   In this work, we aim to explore the scaling law in downstream vision tasks. Recent researches (Ka-
046   plan et al., 2020; Brown, 2020) on scaling law in pretraining have prove that there exists a rela-
047   tionship between model performance and model parameters and size of dataset, which indicates
048   that scaling up these factors can bring consistent performance improvement. Besides, larger input
049   resolution of image can further result in enhanced accuracy (Zhai et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Al-
050
      abdulmohsin et al., 2024). Therefore, it is natural to ask whether downstream vision tasks possesses
      the same scaling signatures as the upstream tasks?
051
052   We take visual object tracking as case study to answer the above question. By systematically deflat-
053   ing model parameters, training data volume, and input image resolution, we investigate how these
      factors impact model performance in downstream vision tasks. As illustrated in Figure 1, our find-

054
      ings reveal scaling patterns similar to those observed in upstream pretraining. Increasing model
055   parameters, training data, and input resolution consistently results in stable accuracy enhancements.
056
057   Despite the improved accuracy, existing naive training methods encounter several issues based on
058
      our observation in Figure 1. Directly training a large model with extensive data may be difficult to
      optimize and challenging to fully harness its capabilities. Additionally, it is an open-loop training
059
      approach, failing to leverage knowledge gained from previous training. To address this, we intro-
060
      duce a novel training approach, DT-Training. In our DT-Training, a smaller model acts as a teacher,
061   guiding the optimization of a larger model for smoother training. Additionally, DT-Training incor-
062   porates a dual-branch alignment technique, which applies random masks to input images and aligns
063   outputs from both masked and unmasked images. This increases training difficulty, fully harnessing
064   the model’s potential. Building upon our DT-Training, we propose a closed-loop scaling up strat-
065   egy. In this process, the small model from the previous iteration serves as a teacher, transferring
066   knowledge to the larger model, which then becomes the foundation for the next iteration. This setup
067   enables continuous iterative expansion, transforming the scaling process into an evolving cycle that
068   consistently enhances performance.
069   Existing models often evaluate the performance on limited benchmarks that lack the diversity and
070   complexity required to assess robustness in real-world scenarios. Thus, we introduce GTrack Bench,
071   a comprehensive, challenging, and large-scale benchmark featuring 4,369 trajectories, approxi-
072   mately three times the size of existing benchmarks. With our DT-Training approach and closed-loop
073   scaling strategy, our scaled model shows exceptional capabilities, outperforming current counter-
074   parts on GTrack Bench. Our model achieves 64.8 mean AUC, exceeding state-of-the-art methods by
075
      at least 1.4 mean AUC. Furthermore, it exhibits strong transferability, maintaining high performance
      even after compression and proving robust to multimodal data, such as depth maps. By integrating
076
      our model into the backbones of CompressTracker (Hong et al., 2024a) and OneTracker (Hong et al.,
077
      2024b), we achieve consistent performance improvements. Additionally, we also apply our strat-
078   egy to other downstream vision tasks, such object detection, enhances the accuracy of Deformable
079   DETR (Zhu et al., 2020) by 1.5 AP, which demonstrating the generalization ability of our method.

081
      Our contribution can be summarized as following: (1) We take visual object tracking as case study to
      investigate scaling laws in downstream vision tasks, focusing on three key factors: model size, train-
082
      ing data volume, and input resolution. Although increasing these factors can enhance performance,
083
      the improvement is often constrained by optimization challenges when training larger models. (2)
084   We introduce a novel training approach DT-Training, which involves utilizing a smaller model to
085   guide the training of a larger model, and aligning outputs from clean and masked images. Our DT-
086   Training facilitates faster, smoother convergence and fully unlocks the model’s potential. (3) We
087   introduce a closed-loop scaling up strategy based on our DT-Training, transforming the scaling pro-
088   cess into continuous, iterative optimization. This step-by-step evolution enables model to improve
089   consistently across multiple iterations, fully harnessing its ability. (4) Our scaled model exhibits
090   outstanding performance across various benchmarks and demonstrates robust transfer ability. Our
091   model achieves 64.8 mean AUC on GTrack Bench, outperforming existing models by at least 1.4
092
      mean AUC. Experiments on object detection demonstrates the generalization ability of our method.

097   training data, and image resolution, using visual object tracking as a case study. Our findings in
098   the following can be applied to other tasks, such as object detection, too. We adopt OSTrack (Ye
099   et al., 2022), which features a ViT (Dosovitskiy, 2020) encoder for joint feature extraction and
100   temporal matching, and a lightweight decoder for box regression, for our experiments. This simple
101   architecture allows us to effectively assess the impact of three factors in downstream vision tasks.

122   2.2   S HORTCUTS OF NAIVE T RAINING
123
124   As shown in preceding pioneer experiments and Figure 1, we observe that while expanding certain
125
      factors like model size or training data can rapidly enhance model performance up to a specific
      threshold, beyond the certain point, further expansion results in less noticeable improvements. For
126
      example, a model using ViT-H as its backbone only achieves a 0.3% increase in mean AUC com-
127
      pared to the ViT-L model. Similarly, the performance gains from expanding training data gradually
128   slow down. We attribute these limitations to conventional training approaches. (1) Convergence
129   difficulty. Firstly, training a large model directly on extensive datasets can be challenging to opti-
130   mize due to the increased complexity and computational demands, often leading to issues like slow
131   convergence or getting stuck in local minima. (2) Underexplored Capabilities. Traditional training
132   often fails to fully exploit larger models’ capabilities. While these models can capture stronger pat-
133   terns, conventional training uses fixed training protocols and architectures may hinder their potential,
134   resulting in suboptimal performance. (3) Isolate optimization. Besides, traditional methods follow
135   a linear, open-loop process where each scaling step—whether increasing model size, data volume,
136
      or resolution—is treated in isolation. Models are trained independently, failing to utilize the insights
      and capabilities developed in previous training efforts. The absence of iterative knowledge-sharing
137
      process significantly limits the potential for more efficient optimization. This underscores the need
138
      for a new training approach to more effectively exploit model performance and a more integrated,
139   close-loop approach to fully unlock the advantages of scaling laws.

      3     C LOSE -L OOP S CALING U P S TRATEGY
143
144
      To address the aforementioned challenges, we introduce a novel training approach called DT-
      Training, and a closed-loop scaling up strategy. DT-Training integrates dual-branch alignment and
145
      small teacher transfer, to fully harness the potential of large models and improve performance. More-
146
      over, DT-Training enables our closed-loop scaling up strategy. In this process, the small model from
147   the previous iteration serves as a teacher to transfer knowledge to the larger model, which then be-
148   comes the starting point for the next iteration. This setup facilitates continuous iterative expansion,
149   transforming the scaling process into an evolving cycle that consistently enhances performance.
150
151   3.1   DT-T RAINING
152
153   While naive training can improve model performance by scaling up key factors in scaling laws, it
154   faces significant limitations. Traditional training methods struggle to optimize large models effec-
155   tively and fail to fully exploit their potential. To overcome these shortcuts, we introduce DT-Training
156
      as shown in Figure 2.

160   optimization of the larger model, facilitating smoother learning and faster convergence for the larger
161   model. Specifically, in our small teacher transfer, the original images X are simultaneously fed into

226   3.2    C LOSED - LOOP S CALING UP
227
228   To solve the isolate optimization problem, we further propose the closed-loop scaling up strategy
229   built on the DT-Training by introducing a feedback mechanism to enable continuous, iterative op-
230   timization throughout the scaling process. As shown in Figure 2, our closed-loop strategy progres-
231
      sively expands any key factor of scaling laws: model size, data size, and input resolution, which we
      explore in Section 2.
232
233   Given its iterative nature, each training phase can be viewed as a stage with different data volume γ,
234   model parameters θ, and input resolution µ. These factors scale up as the iteration process increases.
235   Based on the key idea of using a smaller teacher model to guide larger student one in DT-Training,
236   we use the trained student model fi−1 as the teacher model for stage i. The larger scale student
237
      model is denoted as fi . We use the same training objective functions within the DT-Training frame-
      work for each iteration, which includes dual-branch alignment and small teacher transfer. The goal
238
      of each iteration is to incrementally scale the student model and enhance its performance by lever-
239
      aging the knowledge embedded in the teacher model.
240
241   At the start of the ith iteration, the model from the previous iteration, fi−1 , though smaller or
242   less accurate, contains valuable knowledge that has been optimized on the tasks encountered in
243
      earlier iteration. This model serves as the teacher in the DT-Training process, facilitating faster
      convergence and smoother optimization for the current iteration. In each iteration, one or more of
244
      the three scaling factors is increased, allowing the model to progressively evolve and improve. The
245
      optimization function for the ith iteration can be formulated as:
246
247                                                    Ltotal (fi ; fi−1 |θi , γi , µi ),                                            (6)
248
      where θi , γi , and µi denote parameter amounts, data volume, and input resolution in stage i, re-
249   spectively. After the ith stage completes, the model fi becomes the new teacher for the subsequent
250   i + 1th stage, continuing the cycle of iterative scaling and improvement. In each new iteration stage,
251   we scale the model by either increasing its capacity, expanding the dataset size, or enhancing the
252   input resolution. This ensures that the student model is progressively larger and more capable while
253   leveraging the knowledge acquired in previous iterations. By iteratively expanding these key factors
254   and continuously transferring knowledge between models, our closed-loop scaling strategy guaran-
255   tees that each iteration benefits from prior learning. This approach ultimately leads to more robust
256   and efficient scaling across model size, data, and resolution, enhancing overall performance.
257   Our DT-Training enables the feasibility of a closed-loop scaling strategy, offering key advantages
258   over traditional methods. First, the iterative teacher-student relationship allows each new student
259   model to inherit the accumulated knowledge of previous iterations, leading to faster convergence and
260   better generalization. Second, while conventional training often faces diminishing returns as models
261   are scaled, our strategy transforms scaling into an iterative refinement process, ensuring consistent
262   improvement. Additionally, the closed-loop scaling strategy offers excellent scalability, making it
263
      suitable for progressively larger models and more complex datasets as the training advances.

      6.1   S CALING L AW IN U PSTREAM TASKS
507   Scaling laws in neural language processing and vision pretraining tasks have been extensively stud-
508   ied in prior works (Hestness et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2017; Brown, 2020). Studies such as (Hoffmann
509   et al., 2022; Kaplan et al., 2020; Tay et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023) explore neural scaling laws
510   in language models, demonstrating a power law relationship between model performance and the
511   scale of model size, data, and training compute. Similar power law dependencies have also been
512   observed in vision tasks (Riquelme et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2022; Dehghani et al., 2023; Kolesnikov
513   et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2024). Additionally, works like (Radford et al.,
514   2021; Pham et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2021; Alabdulmohsin et al., 2022; Radford et al., 2021; Ramesh
515
      et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Cherti et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022) leverage
      vast datasets of weakly aligned image-text pairs to strengthen the connection between vision and
516
      language tasks. While scaling laws in pretraining have been well studied, the impact of scaling laws
517
      on downstream vision tasks has been less explored. Understanding these dynamics is critical for
518   optimizing model design and performance in downstream vision scenarios.
519
520
      6.2   S CALING L AW IN D OWNSTREAM TASKS
521
522   Beyond upstream pretraining, significant attention has been directed towards scaling laws in down-
523   stream tasks. Studies like (Liu et al., 2024; Xia & Huang, 2024) investigate neural scaling laws on
524   graph-based models from both model and data perspectives. SMLPer-X (Cai et al., 2024) constructs
525   a large-scale human pose and shape estimation dataset, creating a foundational model. Other studies,
526   like (Minderer et al., 2024; Tschannen et al., 2024) focus on expanding training data size. However,
527   these works often attempt to address isolated scaling aspects without establishing a universal scaling
528
      law for downstream vision tasks. In this work, we aim to address this gap by investigating general
      scaling laws in downstream vision tasks.

532
533   In this work, we explore the scaling law in downstream vision tasks. Firstly, we examine the three
534   key factors of scaling laws: model size, data volume and input resolution, discovering similar trends
535   to pretraining tasks. To address the optimization challenges in naive training, we introduce the DT-
536   Training approach. Additionally, we propose a closed-loop scaling strategy to iteratively enhance
537
      model performance. Our model surpasses existing counterparts on the GTrack Bench. Our approach
      can also be applied to other tasks such as object detection. These results highlight the effectiveness

878   A          A PPENDIX
879
880   A.1        M ORE R ELATED WORKS
881
882   Visual Object Tracking. Visual object tracking aims to locate a target object in each frame based
883
      on its initial appearance. Traditional tracking methods (Bertinetto et al., 2016; Li et al., 2018; Zhang
      et al., 2020; Bhat et al., 2019; Danelljan et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019a; Bolme et al., 2010; Henriques
884
      et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2021a) use a two-stream pipeline to separate feature
885
      extraction from relation modeling. Recently, the one-stream pipeline have taken a dominant role (Ye
886   et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2022; 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; 2023;
887   Gao et al., 2023) combining these processes into a unified approach. These one-stream models are
888   primarily built on the vision transformer architecture, which utilizes a series of transformer encoder
889   layers. This design enables more effective relationship modeling between the template and search
890   frame, leading to impressive performance. While previous works enhance model performance by
891   increasing model parameters or input resolution, they often rely on limited training data and have
892   not systematically explored the scaling law in visual object tracking tasks.
893
894   A.2        GT RACK B ENCH
895
896
      Existing tracking models (Cui et al., 2022; 2024; Ye et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023) tend to evaluate
      performance on a limited set of benchmarks (about 3-4), as detailed in Table 9. These bench-
897
      marks offer limited trajectories and fall short of comprehensively evaluating a model’s tracking
898
      capabilities. Thus we introduce the GTrack Bench, which consists of 12 challenging benchmarks.
899   Among the 12 benchmarks, 10 are singel object tracking benchmarks, including LaSOT (Fan et al.,
900   2019), LaSOText (Fan et al., 2019), TrackingNet (Muller et al., 2018), TNL2K (Wang et al., 2021c),
901   UAV123 (Mueller et al., 2016), Avist (Noman et al., 2022), LaGOT (Mayer et al., 2024), La-
902   TOT (Zhu et al., 2023b), HOOT (Sahin & Itti, 2023), and VideoCube (Hu et al., 2022). Additionally,
903   it includes two datasets from VOS and VIS tasks, MOSE (Ding et al., 2023) and OVIS (Qi et al.,
904   2022). These datasets emphasize real and complex scenarios, offering more challenging videos. By
905   integrating these datasets, we construct a comprehensive evaluation suite with three times the num-
906   ber of trajectories (4369 in total), allowing for a more thorough assessment of model capabilities in
907
      real-world scenarios.
908
909   A.3        T RAINING DATA
910
      Currently, state-of-the-art tracking models (Cui et al., 2022; 2024; Ye et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023;
911
      Wei et al., 2023) are trained on a combination of several datasets, including TrackingNet (Muller
912   et al., 2018), LaSOT (Fan et al., 2019), GOT-10K (Huang et al., 2019), and COCO (Lin et al., 2014).
913   However, these datasets alone are insufficient for fully training highly capable tracking models.
914   We datasets from related tasks into a single object tracking format to create a large-scale train-
915   ing set. These datasets originate from tasks such as single object tracking (LaSOT (Fan et al.,
916   2019), GOT-10K (Huang et al., 2019), TrackingNet (Muller et al., 2018), COCO (Lin et al., 2014),
917   TNL2K (Wang et al., 2021c), and UAVDT (Du et al., 2018)), multi-object tracking (MOT16 (Milan
      et al., 2016), MOT17 (Dendorfer et al., 2021), MOT20 (Dendorfer, 2020), DanceTrack (Sun et al.,

918
      2022), SportsMOT (Cui et al., 2023)), video object segmentation (MOSE (Ding et al., 2023)), video
919   instance segmentation (OVIS (Qi et al., 2022)), and open-world object tracking and segmentation
920   (TAO (Achal et al., 2020) and UVO (Wang et al., 2021a)). Statistics of these datasets are displayed
921   in Table 10. By incorporating a substantial number of training trajectories, we expand our dataset to
