009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we introduce a novel perspective on Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
012            (PEFT) by viewing the weight update matrix as a k-sparse approximation in the
013            spatial domain, departing from the commonly used low-rank structure assumption.
014            We propose a compressive sensing-based approach that leverages under-complete
015            measurement matrices to analyze the approximation capabilities of the weight up-
016            date matrix. Our method ensures bounded error in the reconstruction of the weight
017            updates, as guaranteed by theoretical results in compressive sensing. However,
018
               the vectorization of the weight update matrix leads to a high-dimensional problem
               (d2 ), which can potentially result in large error bounds. To address this issue,
019
               we introduce a block-structured approximation scheme that partitions the weight
020
               update matrix into smaller blocks and applies the k-sparse approximation to each
021            block independently. We theoretically analyze the approximation error bounds of
022            our approach and demonstrate that the block-structured scheme achieves tighter
023            error bounds compared to the non-block approach. Empirically, we validate the ef-
024            fectiveness of our proposed method on various downstream NLP tasks, showcasing
025            its ability to achieve competitive performance with a reduced number of trainable
026            parameters. Our approach offers a new direction for parameter-efficient fine-tuning
027            of large language models. Notably, our experiments demonstrate competitive per-
028            formance with only 500 learnable parameters, while offering greater memory and
029
               computational efficiency than LoRA in a rank-1 setting.

      The rapid advancement of large pre-trained language models has revolutionized natural language
034   processing (NLP) tasks. Models such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), GPT (Radford et al., 2018),
035   and T5 (Raffel et al., 2020b) have attained remarkable performance in a wide range of downstream
036   tasks. However, the scaling up of large foundation models has led to soaring costs in fine-tuning and
037   parameter storage, rendering extensive adaptations impractical. For instance, regular full fine-tuning
038   of a LLaMA2-7B parameter model(Chen et al., 2023) requires more than 60GB of GPU memory,
039   which exceeds the capacity of common consumer GPUs(Pan et al., 2024). This challenge has sparked
040   the development of Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques(Houlsby et al., 2019). They
041   adapt large pre-trained language models to downstream tasks by only fine-tuning a small number of
042
      (extra) model parameters, which simultaneously diminishing the quantity of trainable parameters and
      retaining high-level performance (Ding et al., 2023).
043
044   Among PEFT techniques, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) has gained signifi-
045   cant attention due to its effectiveness and efficiency. Comparable or better downstream perfor-
046   mances has been observed on various NLP tasks, including text classification, question answer-
047   ing, and language generation (Mao et al., 2024). LoRA adapts pre-trained models by introducing
048
      low-rank update matrices to the model’s weights. Specifically, LoRA represents the weight up-
      date matrix as the product of two low-rank matrices which are learned during fine-tuning, while
049
      the pre-trained weights remain frozen. Although LoRA achieves parameter efficiency by intro-

      (Wu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024b; Bałazy et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024).

093
094   Large Language Models. Large language models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), RoBERTa
095   (Liu, 2019), GPT (Radford et al., 2018), and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a);(Touvron et al.,
096
      2023b);(inc, 2024) have achieved remarkable success in various natural language processing tasks.
      The swift expansion of the parameter scales of pre-training language models considerably improves
097
      their generalization performance and gives rise to the emergence of novel capabilities. Nevertheless,
098
      the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) on some downstream tasks remains limited due
099   to their inherent knowledge boundaries. To expand the knowledge boundaries, it is necessary to
100   fine-tune LLMs on the downstream tasks. Over the past few years, the parameter scales of pre-training
101   language models have witnessed a several-thousand-fold increase. For instance, it has gone from a
102   model with 330 million parameters like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) to one with 540 billion parameters
103   such as PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2022). This characteristic renders fine-tuning them for specific
104   tasks is extremely computationally expensive and resource-intensive.
105
106   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning. Fine-tuning large language models for downstream tasks typ-
107   ically involves updating all the model parameters using task-specific training data. Nevertheless,
      as the model size escalates, this approach becomes impractical due to the substantial computa-


                                                            2

108
      tional resources and storage overhead it demands. To reduce the computational cost, numerous
109   parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been proposed (Ding et al., 2023). These meth-
110   ods enable large language models (LLMs) to adapt to downstream tasks by fine-tuning only a small
111   number of (additional) model parameters.One notable approach is the LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
112   method (Hu et al., 2022), which introduces low-rank matrices to approximate the weight updates
113   during fine-tuning. LoRA factorizes the weight updates into low-rank matrices, significantly reducing
114   the number of trainable parameters. However, LoRA still necessitates storing the low-rank matrices,
115   which can be memory-consuming for large models. Other parameter-efficientfine-tuning methods
116   comprise adapter-based approaches (Houlsby et al., 2019),which introduce small trainable modules
117
      between the layers of the pre-trained model, and prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), which prepends
      trainable continuous prompts to the input sequences. Although these methods have demonstrated
118
      promising outcomes in reducing the number of trainable parameters, they still encounter challenges
119
      in terms of memory efficiency and the capacity to capture complex weight update patterns.
120
121   Compressive Sensing Compressive sensing has emerged as a powerful framework for efficient
122   signal acquisition and reconstruction, with applications spanning various fields including signal pro-
123   cessing, imaging, and machine learning (Candès et al., 2006; Donoho, 2006). The foundational work
124   of Candes et al. (2006) and Donoho (Donoho, 2006) established theoretical guarantees for recovering
125   sparse signals from a small number of linear measurements. These guarantees rely on properties
126   such as the Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) (Candes & Tao, 2005), which has been shown to
127   hold for various classes of measurement matrices, including random Gaussian matrices and partial
128   Fourier matrices (Rudelson & Vershynin, 2008). Simultaneously, in the domain of parameter-efficient
129
      fine-tuning, methods like LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) have garnered popularity due to their capability
      to adapt large pre-trained models with a small number of parameters. LoRA accomplishes this by
130
      learning low-rank update matrices, where the number of parameters is determined by the selected
131
      rank. Our work bridges these two areas, applying compressive sensing principles to parameter-
132   efficient fine-tuning of large language models. Unlike traditional compressive sensing approaches,
133   we concentrate on approximating weight update matrices rather than input signals. In contrast to
134   LoRA,the parameter efficiency of our method is based on the chosen number of parameters rather
135   than a rank value, thereby offering enhanced flexibility.

      In this section, we introduce the notation and formally define the problem of parameter-efficient
140   fine-tuning for large language models. We begin by discussing the general form of weight updates
141   and then present the LoRA method as a specific instantiation. Finally, we introduce the concept of
142   basis representations, which serves as a foundation for our proposed PISA method.
143
144   Notation Let W ∈ Rd×d be the weight matrix of a pre-trained language model, where d is the
145   dimension of the model’s hidden states. During fine-tuning, we aim to learn an update matrix
146   ∆W ∈ Rd×d that adapts the pre-trained weights to a specific downstream task. The updated weight
147   matrix W′ can be expressed as:
148                                          W′ = W + ∆W                                          (1)
149   The adapted weights W’ are then used in the linear layer to compute the output y:
150                                       y = W ′ x = (W + ∆W )x                                        (2)
151
152   Reparameterized Fine-tuning. The weight update matrix ∆W acts as a filter that modifies the
153   linear transformation performed by the pre-trained weights W. The filter ∆W learns to emphasize or
154   suppress certain patterns or features in the input x that are relevant to the downstream task. We can
155
      represent the filter ∆W using a basis set A and a learnable representation B:
156                                               ∆W = AB                                               (3)
157   The choice of the basis set A determines the type of patterns or structures that the filter ∆W can
158   learn to capture. In LoRA, both A and B are learned simultaneously, allowing the filter ∆W to
159   capture task-specific patterns and structures. The low-rank structure of A and B reduces the number
160   of trainable parameters. In LoRA-FA (Zhang et al., 2023a), only B is learned, while A remains fixed.
161   This approach further reduces the number of trainable parameters compared to LoRA, as the basis set
      is predetermined. The fixed basis set A captures general patterns or structures relevant to the task.

221   4.2   A DDRESSING PARAMETERS I NEFFICIENCY
222
223   To tackle the challenge of memory inefficiency in storing the large measurement matrix A, we
224   introduce two key concepts: the Kronecker product in conjunction with the Restricted Isometry
225   Property (RIP), and the use of subsampled Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) matrices.
226
227
      Kronecker Product and RIP. The Restricted Isometry Property (RIP) is a fundamental concept
      in compressive sensing that guarantees the stability of sparse signal recovery. A matrix Φ ∈ Rm×n
228
      satisfies the RIP of order k with constant δk ∈ (0, 1) if, for all k-sparse vectors x ∈ Rn :
229
230                                (1 − δk )∥x∥22 ≤ ∥Φx∥22 ≤ (1 + δk )∥x∥22                             (6)
231   To extend this property to our two-dimensional weight update matrix, we leverage the Kronecker
232   product. Let Φ1 ∈ Rm1 ×d and Φ2 ∈ Rm2 ×d be matrices satisfying the RIP. We can construct our
233   measurement matrix A as:
234                                                A = Φ1 ⊗ Φ2                                          (7)
235   where ⊗ denotes the Kronecker product. Importantly, if Φ1 and Φ2 satisfy the RIP of order k with
236   constants δ1 and δ2 respectively, then A satisfies the RIP of order k 2 with constant δ1 + δ2 + δ1 δ2
237   (Duarte & Baraniuk, 2010; 2011). This construction allows us to work with structured measurement
238
      matrices, reducing the storage requirements from O(md2 ) to O(m1 d + m2 d). However, while this
      is a significant improvement, it still requires storing parameters for the measurement matrix.
239
240
      Subsampled FFT Matrix. To further reduce memory requirements while maintaining the Kro-
241
      necker product structure, we propose using subsampled Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) matrices
242   (Xu & Xu, 2014) as our measurement matrices Φ1 and Φ2 . This approach allows us to efficiently
243   transform from the spatial domain to the spectral (k-sparse) domain. An additional crucial reason for
244   using subsampled FFT matrices is their orthogonality property. In the context of LLM fine-tuning,
245   traditional ℓ1 minimization approaches (solving A∆W = b) become computationally intractable.
246   However, with orthogonal matrices like subsampled FFT, we can directly compute δW = AH b,
247   where AH is the Hermitian transpose of A. This simple computation replaces the need for complex
248   L1 minimization, making our approach feasible and efficient for LLM fine-tuning. Let Fd ∈ Cd×d
249   denote the 1D discrete Fourier transform (DFT) matrix. We construct our measurement matrices as:
250                                       Φ1 = S1 Fd , Φ2 = S2 Fd                                       (8)
251   where S1 ∈ Rm1 ×d and S2 ∈ Rm2 ×d are random subsampling matrices. Each row of S1 and S2
252   contains a single 1 in a random position and 0s elsewhere. Our complete measurement matrix A is
253   then constructed as:
254                                   A = Φ1 ⊗ Φ2 = (S1 Fd ) ⊗ (S2 Fd )                                 (9)
255   This construction maintains the Kronecker product structure while leveraging the properties of
256   the Fourier transform. Importantly, subsampled FFT matrices have been shown to satisfy the RIP
257   with high probability (Haviv & Regev, 2017), making them suitable for our compressive sensing
258   framework. The use of subsampled FFT matrices in this Kronecker product framework offers

271
272   While our FFT-based approach significantly reduces memory requirements, we still face challenges
273   due to the high dimensionality of the weight update matrix ∆W ∈ Rd×d . High dimensionality can
274   lead to large error bounds in the compressive sensing reconstruction process. To address this, we
275   introduce two key techniques: exploiting Hermitian symmetry and implementing a block-structured
276   k-sparse approximation.
277
278   Exploiting Hermitian Symmetry. An important property of the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)
279   of real-valued signals is Hermitian symmetry. For our real-valued weight update matrix ∆W, its 2D
280   Fourier transform F(∆W) exhibits the following symmetry:
281
282                                   F(∆W)[u, v] = F(∆W)∗ [−u, −v]                                      (11)
283
284   where ∗ denotes the complex conjugate. This symmetry allows us to further reduce the number of
285
      parameters we need to learn and store. Specifically, we only need to learn and store roughly half
      of the frequency components in B while the other half can be reconstructed using the Hermitian
286
      symmetry property. This reduction in parameters not only improves memory efficiency but also
287
      potentially reduces the error in the sparse approximation by effectively doubling the sparsity level for
288   the same number of learned parameters.

511   6      C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this paper, we have introduced a novel approach to Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
514   that leverages the principles of compressive sensing. By viewing the weight update matrix as a
515   compressed representation in the measurement domain, we depart from the conventional low-rank
516
      structure assumptions prevalent in current PEFT methods. Our theoretical analysis, supported by
      empirical evidence, demonstrates that this approach can effectively adapt pre-trained models to new
517
      tasks while significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters. The key innovation of our
518
      method lies in its ability to capture complex adaptation patterns without the constraints of low-rank
519   structures. This flexibility, combined with the efficiency of working directly in the compressed
520   domain, addresses several limitations of existing techniques such as LoRA and its variants. Notably,
521   our approach maintains a constant computational overhead regardless of adaptation complexity, a
522   significant advantage over methods where computational costs increase with accumulated adaptations.
523   Our theoretical framework provides clear bounds on the approximation error, offering insights into
524   the trade-offs between parameter efficiency and adaptation quality. This rigorous foundation not only
525   enhances our understanding of PEFT but also guides practical implementations. The scalability of our
526   method becomes particularly apparent as model sizes grow, with compressive measurements scaling
527
      sub-linearly with model size, making it a sustainable approach for adapting very large models. The
      empirical validation of our method across various downstream NLP tasks underscores its practical
528
      viability. By achieving competitive performance with a reduced number of trainable parameters, we
529
      demonstrate that theoretical elegance can translate into tangible benefits in real-world scenarios.
