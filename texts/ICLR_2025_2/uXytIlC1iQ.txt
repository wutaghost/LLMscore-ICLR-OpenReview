009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) based on artificial neural networks (ANNs) have
012            demonstrated remarkable performance but face challenges in computational ef-
013            ficiency and biological interpretability. We propose BrainGPT, a novel LLM
014            architecture based on the Test-Time Training (TTT) framework and inspired by
015            spiking neural networks (SNNs) and neurobiological principles. Our approach in-
016            corporates a dual-model structure, emulating the hierarchical language processing
017            observed in the human brain, and utilizes a specialized integrate-and-fire neuron
018            model with adaptive thresholding. Through a multi-stage training strategy, includ-
019
               ing quantization-aware pre-training, ANN-to-SNN conversion, and biologically
               inspired unsupervised learning, we achieve a mathematically proven lossless con-
020
               version from ANN to SNN, preserving 100% of the original ANN model’s perfor-
021
               mance. Moreover, the biologically inspired unsupervised learning optimizes the
022            maximum time steps required to maintain 100% ANN performance. Compared to
023            the original TTT model, BrainGPT achieves a 33.4% increase in energy efficiency
024            and demonstrates a 66.7% improvement in training convergence speed. This work
025            advances the development of energy-efficient and biologically interpretable large
026            language models that match the performance of state-of-the-art ANN-based mod-
027            els while significantly improving upon the TTT framework.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) based on artificial neural networks (ANNs) have demonstrated re-
033   markable performance, as shown by OpenAI et al. (2024) and Dubey et al. (2024), but face chal-
034   lenges in computational efficiency and biological interpretability (Strubell et al., 2020; Maass, 1997;
035   Whittington & Bogacz, 2019). We propose BrainGPT, a novel LLM architecture based on the Test-
036   Time Training (TTT) framework and inspired by spiking neural networks (SNNs) and neurobiolog-
037   ical principles. Our approach incorporates a dual-model structure, emulating hierarchical language
038   processing in the human brain, and uses a specialized integrate-and-fire neuron model with adaptive
039
      thresholding. Through a multi-stage training strategy, including quantization-aware pre-training,
      ANN-to-SNN conversion, and biologically inspired unsupervised learning, we achieve a mathe-
040
      matically provable lossless conversion from ANN to SNN, preserving 100% of the original ANN
041
      model’s performance. This work advances the development of energy-efficient and biologically
042   interpretable LLMs that match state-of-the-art ANN-based models while enhancing the TTT frame-
043   work and addressing the lack of interpretability in attention mechanisms noted by Vaswani (2017)
044   and Jain & Wallace (2019).
045
      Our research addresses these issues with BrainGPT, a novel model that reduces energy consumption
046
      and achieves full biological interpretability. Inspired by biological neural networks, BrainGPT ex-
047
      tends the transformer architecture with a dual Test-Time Training (TTT) framework, overcoming the
048   O(n2 ) complexity (Vaswani, 2017). We incorporate recent neuroscientific findings (Jamali et al.,
049   2024; Khanna et al., 2024) into a dual-model structure, including spiking neural components like
050   an Excitatory-Inhibitory Integrate-and-Fire Neuron Model with adaptive thresholding and synaptic
051   plasticity (Takagi, 2000; Maass, 1997). Our training approach uses quantization-aware ANN pre-
052   training (Jacob et al., 2018), followed by a mathematically rigorous lossless conversion to SNN
053   (Esser et al., 2016), and an unsupervised learning phase inspired by Spike Timing-Dependent Plas-
      ticity (Caporale & Dan, 2008). BrainGPT achieves 33.4% energy reduction and 100% performance

060   2     R ELATED WORK AND PROBLEM ANALYSIS
061
062   2.1   RNN- BASED MODELS AND TTT
063
064   Sun et al. (2024) and Gu & Dao (2023) recently renewed interest in RNN architectures for lan-
065   guage modeling, addressing Transformer models’ energy and complexity issues. Sun et al. (2024)
066
      introduces Test-Time Training (TTT), a novel concept where a machine learning model is updated
      during inference using self-supervised learning. TTT’s key aspects include the use of expressive
067
      hidden states that adapt to new data at test time, self-supervised learning for continuous adaptation,
068
      and flexible implementation options that allow various inner-loop models and optimizers to be used.
069
070   TTT demonstrates several advantages over traditional approaches. It achieves O(n) time complexity
071   for sequences of length n, while Transformers need O(n2 ). Also, it shows consistent improvement in
072
      handling long-range dependencies up to 32k tokens. Sun et al. (2024) reports that for contexts longer
      than 8k tokens, TTT processes sequences faster than standard Transformers, and this advantage
073
      becoming increasingly significant as context length grows. Additionally, TTT outperforms in terms
074
      of perplexity with fewer FLOPs.
075
076   However, Sun et al. (2024) and Gu & Dao (2023) also notes that modern RNNs like Mamba still
077   face challenges with long sequences. While Mamba scales similarly to Transformers for shorter
078
      contexts, its performance plateaus after 16k tokens, failing to use the additional context effectively.
      In contrast, Transformers continues to improve throughout the 32k context length. This highlights
079
      the significant improvements made by TTT in addressing long-standing issues in RNN architectures,
080
      as it consistently improves handling long-range dependencies up to 32k tokens.
081
082   Despite these advancements, TTT and similar models lack full biological interpretability. Their op-
083   eration remains fundamentally different from biological neural networks, limiting insights into brain
084
      language processing mechanisms. This gap presents opportunities for further research in biologi-
      cally plausible large language models.
085
086
087   2.2   SNN AND ITS TRAINING METHODS
088
      Though efficient, TTT lacks biological interpretability. On the other hand, SNNs provide a more
089
      biologically plausible alternative, as Maass (1997) describes them as a model that more accurately
090   represents neural processing compared to traditional ANNs. SNNs offer several advantages: lower
091   energy consumption and better robustness due to inherent neuronal dynamics and event-driven spike
092   communication (Stromatias et al., 2015), compatibility with specialized neuromorphic hardware
093   (Merolla et al., 2014; Davies et al., 2018), and computational efficiency through reduced precision
094   requirements and event-driven computation (Diehl et al., 2015; Davies et al., 2018).
095
      Training SNNs for complex tasks like language processing presents unique challenges. Two main
096   approaches have emerged: direct training methods and ANN-to-SNN conversion techniques. Direct
097   training methods include Spike-Timing-Dependent Plasticity (STDP) (Bi & Poo, 1998), SpikeProp
098   (Bohte et al., 2002), and surrogate gradient methods (Neftci et al., 2019). However, these methods
099   often struggle with high computational costs, limited scalability, and reduced accuracy on complex
100   tasks.
101
      ANN-to-SNN conversion techniques, explored by Ding et al. (2021), combine ANN training with
102   SNN efficiency. Cao et al. (2015) introduced methods to replace ANN neurons with integrate-and-
103   fire or leaky integrate-and-fire models, while Rueckauer et al. (2017) developed methods to con-
104   vert continuous-valued inputs into spike trains. A notable recent advancement is the SpikeZIP-TF
105   method (You et al., 2024), which addresses the challenge of converting Transformer-based ANNs
106   to SNNs by introducing spike-equivalent operators for self-attention, softmax, and layer normaliza-
107   tion. SpikeZIP-TF has demonstrated impressive performance, achieving 83.82% Top-1 accuracy on
      ImageNet and 93.79% accuracy on SST-2, surpassing previous Transformer-based SNNs.

      Despite these advancements, challenges remain, including activation function approximation, tem-
109   poral dynamics management, increased latency, and limited SNN operations. Our research has
110   identified potential limitations in the SpikeZIP-TF approach, particularly in handling outlier data.
111   The method’s claim of lossless conversion may not hold in all scenarios, especially with input dis-
112   tributions that significantly deviate from the training data. Our ongoing work aims to address these
113   challenges by developing improved neuron models and conversion processes to enhance the robust-
114   ness and generalization capabilities of converted SNNs.

116   2.3   C HALLENGES IN BUILDING SNN- BASED LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS
117
118   Despite progress in SNN model construction, building SNN-based LLMs remains a challenge. The
119
      complexity and scale of LLMs pose difficulties that current SNN methodologies struggle to address.
120   Dubey et al. (2024) shows the extreme resource requirements of modern LLMs, making direct train-
121   ing of SNN-based LLMs infeasible. Pfeiffer & Pfeil (2018) notes the non-differentiable nature of
122   spike generation in SNNs complicates gradient-based optimization, while Neftci et al. (2019) high-
123   lights the complexity introduced by SNN’s temporal dynamics.
124   ANN-to-SNN conversion methods have shown promise, but scaling to LLMs presents challenges.
125   Rueckauer et al. (2017) notes conversion introduces approximation errors, and You et al. (2024)
126   highlights the complexity of converting LLM-specific operations. Recent approaches like SpikeZIP-
127   TF (You et al., 2024) claim to provide solutions, but our analysis reveals issues in their application
128   to LLMs. Zou et al. (2024) points out that LLMs contain outliers in activation values, rendering
129   SpikeZIP ineffective. We show these errors cause LLMs to lose language capabilities when con-
130   verted using SpikeZIP (Appendix A).
131   Our research aims to overcome these challenges by developing precise neuron models, improved
132   conversion algorithms, and techniques tailored to language processing.
133
134   2.4   B IOLOGICAL R ESEARCH F OUNDATIONS
135
136   Neurobiological research offers critical insights for biologically inspired language models. Takagi
137   (2000) described the balance of EPSPs and IPSPs in neuronal information processing, involving
138   N a+ , K + , Cl− , and Ca2+ channels, suggesting trinary neuronal responses. Jamali et al. (2024)
139
      found that 14% of prefrontal cortex neurons show selective responses to semantic domains, with
      context-dependent activity accurately encoding word meanings, indicating that biologically inspired
140
      models could benefit from selective encoding. Khanna et al. (2024) observed that 46.7% of recorded
141
      neurons in the human prefrontal cortex encoded detailed phonetic information of planned words be-
142   fore utterance, with neuronal activity exhibiting a temporal hierarchy where morphological encod-
143   ing preceded phonetic and syllabic encoding, suggesting that biologically accurate language models
144   could implement multi-stage, hierarchical processing for decoding. Neuronal plasticity, as described
145   by Debanne et al. (2019), involves changes in intrinsic electrical properties, suggesting the incorpo-
146   ration of dynamic thresholds and adaptive input-output relationships. Squire et al. (1990) described
147   key features of the hippocampus, including rapid encoding, temporary storage, associative forma-
148   tion, and context sensitivity. This suggests that biologically plausible models could benefit from
149   architectures incorporating similar mechanisms. Collectively, these findings indicate that neurobi-
150
      ologically inspired computational models could potentially achieve more accurate simulations of
      brain-like information processing and language capabilities.

210   While the dual TTT architecture provides a biologically inspired foundation for our model, it re-
211   mains fundamentally an artificial neural network (ANN). To enhance BrainGPT’s biological fidelity,
212
      we implemented structural changes to transform it into a spiking neural network (SNN) by introduc-
      ing biologically plausible neural components.
213
214   We introduce the Synapsis class to convert the TTT ANN model into an SNN model. This class
215   replaces all network structures with corresponding Synapsis instances and modifies the forward
      propagation logic to support temporal spike processing, simulating biological neural networks’ tem-

524   5     C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATIONS
525
526   This paper presents BrainGPT, a novel SNN-based language model combining TTT efficiency with
527   biological neural network interpretability. Key innovations include a brain-like hierarchical dual-
528   model structure, specialized neuron model, lossless ANN-to-SNN conversion, and STDP-based
529   unsupervised learning, significantly boosting energy efficiency and convergence. Limitations in-
530   clude restricted pre-training, where we only used two datasets for pretraining rather than a sufficient
531
      number of big ones; limited model scale, in which we only trained a 150M model due to the lack
      of hardware resources; limited evaluation, where we only tested PPL for two dataset since we’ve
532
      proved if mathematically; and simplified biological modeling. Additionally, despite the biological
533
      inspiration of our model architecture, which enhances training convergence by mimicking the hi-
534   erarchical processing of language in the nervous system, it does not account for the neural activity
535   patterns associated with mathematical logic and reasoning in the human brain. Therefore, this archi-
536   tecture may struggle to adapt to autoregressive text generation tasks in mathematical reasoning and
537   code generation domains. Future work will focus on scaling, broader evaluation, deeper optimiza-
538   tion, enhanced biological plausibility, and improved interpretability. Despite challenges, BrainGPT
539   marks a significant advance towards efficient, biologically interpretable language models, showing
      immense potential.

771

772

773   function is defined as Q(x) = s · clamp(round(x/s), α, β), where s is the quantization scale, and α
774   and β are the minimum and maximum values of the clamp range.

                 PT
783   N (x, T ) = t=1 Ot (x), and the error function as E(x, T ) = |Q(x) − N (x, T )|.

785
      A.2   M ATHEMATICAL A NALYSIS
786
787   Our analysis reveals a complex relationship between the input values, time steps, and the resulting
788   error in the SpikeZIP-TF conversion process. The quantizer function Q(x) operates on continuous
789   inputs, while the neuron output Ot (x) is discrete, taking values in {−1, 0, 1}. This fundamental
790   difference leads to potential discrepancies in the conversion process.
791
      The error function E(x, T ) exhibits several important characteristics:
792
793   1. It is bounded: 0 ≤ E(x, T ) ≤ max(|x|, s/2). 2. It has a non-linear relationship with the input
794   magnitude. 3. It decreases with increasing time steps, but not necessarily linearly.
795   These observations indicate the presence of errors in the conversion process, which can vary de-
796   pending on the input values and the number of time steps.
797

799
800   Analysis of boundary conditions reveals further insights into the behavior of the error function:
801
802

804             |x| → 127 =⇒ E(x, T ) reaches maximum value                                               (22)
805              T → 128 =⇒ E(x, T ) approaches minimum value, but not necessarily zero
806
807   A key limitation becomes apparent when we consider large input values. For x = 127 and any
808   T = 2N , 1 ≤ N ≤ 7, we find that Q(127) = 127, but N (127, T ) ≤ T < 127 (when T < 127).
809   Consequently, E(127, T ) > 0 for all T ≤ 64, indicating persistent errors for large inputs even with
      a significant number of time steps.
