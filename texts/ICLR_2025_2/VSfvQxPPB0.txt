009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have demonstrated strong
012            visual understanding and decision-making capabilities, enabling the exploration
013            of autonomously improving MLLMs in unknown environments. However, ex-
014            ternal feedback like human or environmental feedback is not always available.
015            To address this challenge, existing methods primarily focus on enhancing the
016            decision-making capabilities of MLLMs through voting and scoring mechanisms,
017            while little effort has been paid to improving the environmental comprehension of
018
               MLLMs in unknown environments. To fully unleash the self-learning potential of
               MLLMs, we propose a novel actor-critic self-learning paradigm, dubbed SELU,
019
               inspired by the actor-critic paradigm in reinforcement learning. The critic em-
020
               ploys self-asking and hindsight relabeling to extract knowledge from interaction
021            trajectories collected by the actor, thereby augmenting its environmental compre-
022            hension. Simultaneously, the actor is improved by the self-feedback provided by
023            the critic, enhancing its decision-making. We evaluate our method in the AI2-
024            THOR and VirtualHome environments, and SELU achieves critic improvements
025            of approximately 28% and 30%, and actor improvements of about 20% and 24%
026            via self-learning.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have demonstrated impressive perceptual and un-
032   derstanding capabilities across various domains, e.g., web applications (Ma et al., 2024; Tao et al.,
033
      2024; Liu et al., 2024), robotics (Xiong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b), gaming (Li et al., 2024c; Qi
      et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), and autonomous driving (Wen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Thanks
034
      to their powerful capabilities, many works, e.g., Jarvis-1 (Wang et al., 2023b), STEVE-1 (Lifshitz
035
      et al., 2023), and Cradle (Tan et al., 2024b), directly utilize the pre-trained MLLMs to complete
036   various decision-making tasks in different embodied environments.
037
038   However, the generalization ability of existing pre-trained MLLMs cannot meet the needs of all
039
      environments. For some uncommon environments, embodied MLLMs often exhibit hallucinations
      and poor visual understanding (Huang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). In more detail, they cannot
040
      distinguish left from right and fail to recognize where objects are (Tan et al., 2024b). The reason is
041
      that MLLMs have not been further grounded with the environments (Su et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024).
042   Grounding can be realized by fine-tuning on the experiences from interacting with the environments.
043   Based on the evaluation methods, experience can be categorized into three types: human feedback
044   (Dai et al., 2024; Kirk et al., 2024), environmental feedback (Tan et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024b),
045   and self-feedback (Pang et al., 2024; Madaan et al., 2023). The first two types require additional
046   efforts as illustrated in Figure 1(a). Human feedback necessitates expert annotations, which can be
047   costly and influenced by preferences (McAleese et al., 2024). Environmental feedback assumes we
048   can obtain a dynamics model of the environment and build the reward model (Sontakke et al., 2023;
049   Urcelay et al., 2024). Unfortunately, many environments including the real world do not meet such
050
      requirements, so these grounding methods are not general. Therefore, we are committed to finding
      a general way to fill in the remaining gaps.
051
052   Assuming we are facing an unknown embodied environment and cannot obtain external feedback,
053   we can only rely on the capabilities of the MLLM itself. Some work, see Figure 1(b), utilizes the
      evaluation (discriminative) ability of the pre-trained model itself (Wang et al., 2023a; Huang et al.,

      constrained by the static evaluation ability of the model to improve decision-making capability. Ide-

068   1999) in reinforcement learning, where the actor and critic can mutually enhance each other’s per-
069   formance. If so, the potential for actor enhancement will be greatly expanded. Unlike reinforcement
070   learning, which can obtain external rewards for training, we do not assume that we can get any
071   external feedback. Therefore, we aim to develop a new actor-critic self-learning paradigm for
072   embodied MLLMs in unknown environments.
073   In this paper, we introduce a novel SElf-Learning paradigm in Unknown environments, dubbed
074   SELU, as illustrated in Figure 1(c). Inspired by the actor-critic paradigm in reinforcement learning,
075   our paradigm learns to simultaneously optimize the MLLM’s ability to understand the environment
076   and to make decisions. For the actor module, we fine-tune the model based on the self-feedback
077   from the critic. As the actor gets improved, it can roll-out more successful trajectories to fine-tune
078   the critic. However, without environmental feedback, the critic may provide inaccurate feedback
079   at the beginning of the training phase, which might mislead the overall optimization. Therefore,
080   we adopt self-asking to correct self-feedback and leverage hindsight relabeling to increase sample
081
      efficiency by turning the failure trajectory into a successful one. These high-quality and diverse
      trajectories are deemed to enhance the critic’s comprehension of the environment. Ultimately, the
082
      coupling of these two components mutually promotes the improvement of each other, unleashing
083
      the full self-learning potential of MLLMs.
084
085   Our key contributions can be summarized as follows:
086
              • We propose a self-learning paradigm for embodied MLLMs, SELU, inspired by the actor-
087
                critic paradigm in reinforcement learning, which enables MLLMs to self-adapt to unknown
088
                environments.
089
              • We leverage self-asking and hindsight relabeling to facilitate the improvement of the critic,
090
                which greatly increase the sample efficiency and make the self-learning possible.
091
              • We demonstrate the effectiveness of SELU in the AI2-THOR and VirtualHome environ-
092
                ments, achieving critic improvements of approximately 28% and 30%, and actor improve-
093             ments of about 20% and 24%, respectively.

098   2.1    MLLM S WITH E XTERNAL F EEDBACK
099
      In recent years, MLLMs has achieved impressive results across various visual benchmarks (Mathew
100
      et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024), demonstrating remarkable perception and decision-
101
      making capabilities. However, these models still exhibit flaws and often generate unexpected out-
102   puts, such as perceptual hallucinations and unreasonable decisions (Yu et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
103   2024). Inspired by reinforcement learning, current approaches use external feedback to correct
104   MLLM’s erroneous outputs through a cycle of interaction, feedback, and correction (Pan et al.,
105   2024; Gero et al., 2023). Generally, there are two sources of external feedback: human preference
106   feedback and environmental feedback. Utilizing manually annotated data to align MLLMs output
107   with expert preferences (Kirk et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024), such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024),
      PRO (Song et al., 2024), etc., has proven to be effective. Environmental feedback is typically derived


                                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      from designing a reward function (Pang et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024a) or pre-training an evaluation
109   model (Schick et al., 2023), which often require substantial support from expert data. For instance,
110   McAleese et al. (2024) first trained a critic model using expert data to score program code bugs, and
111   then applied these scores to train ChatGPT with PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), enhancing its debug-
112   ging capabilities. Compared to existing studies, we focus on the self-learning potential of MLLMs,
113   as external feedback may be not professional enough in unknown environments.
114
115   2.2   S ELF -I MPROVEMENT IN LLM S
116
117   Self-improvement in Large Language Models (LLMs) has gained significant attention, as re-
118   searchers strive to develop models that can learn and adapt from their own outputs, interactions, and
119   internal feedback mechanisms, without relying on external human-labeled data (Yan et al., 2023;
120
      Haluptzok et al., 2023). Early explorations in this area are based on unsupervised learning tech-
      niques, where models learn representations from vast datasets without explicit human guidance
121
      (Winter et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2019). Expanded to LLMs, self-improvement goes further by en-
122
      abling models to critique, refine, and adapt their behavior in a more autonomous manner (Tan et al.,
123   2023; Choi et al., 2024). There are two common self-improvement methods: prompt engineering
124   and fine-tuning. The former is an efficient and intuitive approach for large-scale LLMs, as it allows
125   for the establishment of various chains of thought (CoTs) (Wei et al., 2022) to address the same
126   problem (Huang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023). For instance, Madaan et al. (2023) demonstrated
127   that GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 can enhance the rationality of responses by simultaneously inputting a
128   question and reflecting on previous answers. The latter one is more useful for small-scale LLMs,
129   as the prompt engineering is unstable. Wang et al. (2024a) developed a fine-tuning dataset by gen-
130   erating negative responses to optimize the LLMs, thereby reducing the occurrence of unreasonable
131
      answers. Based on these studies, we choose to use fine-tuning to optimize small-scale MLLMs.
      However, existing methods overlook the enhancement of MLLM’s environment understanding ca-
132
      pabilities. Therefore, we employ an actor-critic framework to facilitate comprehensive self-learning
133
      in MLLMs, optimizing both perception and decision-making abilities.

138   3.1   ACTOR -C RITIC IN R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
139
      Actor-critic (Konda & Tsitsiklis, 1999) is a widely adopted framework in reinforcement learning.
140
      The agent consists of two learning modules: an actor and a critic, which are optimized iteratively.
141
      The actor selects and executes actions based on current observations. The critic evaluates these
142   observations (and actions) by estimating their values based on reward signals received from the
143   environment, thereby guiding the actor to make improved choices in future. This framework takes
144   advantage of both policy-based learning and value-based learning and is popular nowadays like PPO
145   (Schulman et al., 2017).
146
147   3.2   ACTOR -C RITIC FOR MLLM S
148
149   With the development of MLLMs, the feedback provided to the agent is no longer constrained to
150   scalar values, like rewards; it can now include diverse modalities, such as natural language (Dong
151   et al., 2024). This enables the critic gain more specific and informative feedback on the outputs of
152   the actor. Consequently, it can provide more accurate guidance for actor improvement. A prevalent
153
      approach in this domain is incorporating human feedback into the critic and building a static evalua-
      tion module that can reflect human preference (Ouyang et al., 2022; Kirk et al., 2024). Specifically,
154
      human annotated data is used to train a critic model (McAleese et al., 2024) or a reward model
155
      (Sontakke et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b) to align the MLLM with human preferences better.
156   More rigorous approaches leverage external evaluation mechanisms, such as tool-interactive learn-
157   ing (Gou et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2021), or external knowledge sources like Wikipedia and the
158   Internet (Xu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a). However, regardless of whether preference labels or
159   external tools are used, human intervention remains inevitable. To overcome this reliance, meth-
160   ods like self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023a; Schick et al., 2023) employ a voting mechanism to
161   enable the model to evaluate its own behavior without relying on external information. However,
      self-consistency lacks a learnable critic module, thus it cannot improve its grounding knowledge

188   4     M ETHOD
189
190   The framework of our method is shown in Figure 2. It consists of two components: the actor
191   MLLM and the critic MLLM. The actor MLLM follows instructions and collects trajectories in the
192   environment. The critic MLLM evaluates the collected trajectories and acquires bootstrapped data
193   via self-asking and hindsight relabeling to optimize itself (Section 4.1). Guided by the success de-
194   tection results from the critic MLLM, the actor MLLM subsequently improves its decision-making
195   performance in the environment (Section 4.2). By combining the two processes, we can achieve
196   coupled improvements of the critic MLLM and the actor MLLM (Section 4.3).
197
198   4.1   C RITIC : S ELF -A SKING AND H INDSIGHT R ELABELING
199
200   As introduced in Section 1, enhancing the interpretation of environmental grounding information is
201
      crucial for an MLLM to improve its performance. In our framework, we achieve this objective via
      self-asking and hindsight relabeling to acquire bootstrapped data for optimizing the critic MLLM.
202
203   Specifically, given an instruction I, the actor MLLM collects a trajectory by following this instruc-
204   tion. The critic takes the last frame oT of this trajectory as input, using it as the detection frame to
205   determine whether the task depicted by I is completed,
206
                                                                           ld = Mc (I, pd , oT ),                                                                           (1)
207
208   where Mc denotes the critic MLLM, ld ∈ {“yes”, “no”} is the result of the success detection, and
209   pd is a prompt for the detection. If the detection result is ld = “yes”, we consider this trajectory
210   to be a successful sample for the given instruction and store this trajectory directly into the critic
211   fine-tuning dataset Dcritic in the format (I, pd , oT , ld ), as shown by trajectory 1 in Figure 2. This
212
      trajectory includes environmental grounding information that aligns with the knowledge contained
      in the critic MLLM.
213
214   If the detection result is ld = “no”, which means the critic MLLM views this trajectory as a failure
215   for instruction I. We first apply self-asking to examine the state of task-related objects, as the
      decision made by the critic MLLM might not be precise due to potential hallucinations. The critic

      If the critic MLLM still considers the trajectory as failure, we propose to use hindsight relabeling
      to make use of this trajectory, since it might be helpful for learning the environmental grounding of
226
      other instructions. Hindsight relabeling is a method that originated in goal-conditioned reinforce-
227
      ment learning (Andrychowicz et al., 2017). It is based on a simple principle: if a trajectory does
228   not complete the target task, it can be viewed as having accomplished other tasks or subtasks. For
229   example, as shown in trajectory 3 in Figure 2, although it does not complete the task “open cabinet”,
230   it successfully completes another task “open drawer”. Therefore, we relabel this trajectory with
231   the instruction “open drawer” to help the critic MLLM recognize the completion of the relabeled
232   instruction. We can write this process as,
233                                      I ′ = Mc (lh , aI ), lh = Mc (aI , ph , oT )                               (3)
234   where aI is the verb extracted from the instruction I by another text-processing function. ph is the
235   prompt for hindsight relabeling, and lh is the output, which is usually an object name or None. Mc
236   checks whether any objects, other than the target object, in the observation oT have completed the
237   task associated with aI . Mc generates a new instruction I ′ if lh is not None. After that, we store the
238   data (I ′ , pd , oT , “yes”) into the critic fine-tuning dataset Dcritic . Finally, if a failed trajectory proves
239   meaningless after hindsight relabeling, it is considered as not helpful for the MLLM to understand
240   the environment and discarded.
241   By applying self-asking and hindsight relabeling, we create a fine-tuning dataset Dcritic containing
242   the last frames considered as successful by the critic itself and the last frames relabeled as successful
243   after self-asking and hindsight relabeling.

245   4.2   ACTOR : C RITIC -G UIDED I MPROVEMENT
246
247   Recent work has shown that the discriminative ability of an LLM exceeds its generative ability (Pang
248
      et al., 2024). As MLLMs are typically trained the same way as LLMs, we believe that MLLMs’
      evaluation abilities would also surpass their generation abilities. For instance, we can easily prompt
249
      MLLMs to extract understanding from a given image, but it is challenging to prompt them to choose
250
      an appropriate action based on perceived task-relevant information like distance or direction. Our
251   experiment in Section 5.2 also supports this conclusion, where the critic module always performs
252   better than the actor. Therefore, we propose using the critic MLLM to guide the improvement of the
253   actor MLLM in the environment without external feedback.
254
      Specifically, the actor MLLM interacts with the environment and collects online trajectories. At
255
      each timestep t, the actor generates an action plan la,t by,
256
257
                                                    la,t = Ma (I, pa , ot ),                                    (4)
258   where Ma represents the actor MLLM, I is the task instruction, pa is the prompt for action plan and
259   ot is the current image observation. After collecting a whole trajectory, the critic MLLM determines
260
      whether this trajectory completes the instruction I, as described in Section 4.1. If the answer is
      yes, we will put the whole trajectory into the actor fine-tuning dataset Dactor with a format of
261
      {(I, pa , ot , la,t )}Tt=0 . The relabeled successful trajectories after hindsight relabeling are also added
262
      into the actor fine-tuning dataset Dactor . Since this dataset only contains task completion trajectories,
263   the actor can quickly converge towards completing tasks in the current environment by fine-tuning
264   on the dataset. Note that the actor fine-tuning dataset Dactor consists of trajectory data, while the
265   critic fine-tuning dataset Dcritic only contains the last frames of these trajectories.

280   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
281
282   Environments. In order to simulate embodied MLLM interactions in unknown environments, we
283   select AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2022) and VirtualHome (Puig et al., 2018) for our experiments.
284   Both environments offer open-ended tasks, various interactive objects, and selectable camera per-
285   spectives, facilitating data collection for the actor and critic.
286          • AI2-THOR is an interactive simulation environment designed for embodied AI research.
287            The primary tasks require agents to navigate and interact with household objects. It offers
288            highly realistic 3D environments that simulate kitchens, living rooms, and other indoor
289            settings. AI2-THOR provides locobot and robotic arms as agents and enables agents to
290            perform complex actions like picking up, opening, toggling, and so on.
291          • VirtualHome is also an embodied simulation platform designed to imitate human activities
292            and tasks in home environments. It offers a rich set of virtual household scenes where
293            agents can perform tasks, such as sitting on a bench in the kitchen or grasping a waterglass
294            in the bedroom. This environment focuses on task completion through multi-step action
295
               sequences, making it ideal for testing long-term planning.
296   Task Selection. Considering the training costs while demonstrating the feasibility of the framework,
297   we focus on three typical task categories in the AI2-THOR environment: pick up, open, and break.
298   Locobot is selected as the agent, as this work does not consider low-level control of robotic arms. To
299   ensure task diversity and feasibility, we first prompt the MLLM to explore the environment and use
300   the explore objects to initialize the instruction list which serves as the task set. We randomly sample
301   2-3 objects for each type of task. Considering the training costs, we restrict the maximum step for
302   all tasks to 10. We apply a similar approach in the VirtualHome environment, selecting ”female1”
303   as the agent and primarily testing in grab, open, and sit tasks.
304   MLLMs. To demonstrate the generalization capability of our framework, we conduct experiments
305   using two MLLMs: LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023).
306
             • LLaVA integrates a visual encoder with a language model to effectively process and re-
307            spond to multimodal inputs. It has gained prominence as one of the most popular MLLMs
308            due to its simple architecture and lower training data requirements. These features en-
309            able LLaVA to generate responses more swiftly, and suitable for inference to investigate
310            self-learning.
311          • Qwen-VL is the multimodal version of the large model series. Compared to other open-
312            source MLLMs, Qwen-VL is the first model to use a 448x448 resolution image input. Due
313            to its higher resolution, this model exhibits enhanced visual understanding capabilities.
314            We opt for Qwen-VL with the aim of better success detection, thereby facilitating more
315            efficient self-learning of MLLMs.
316
317
      Baselines. We compare SELU with five methods to investigate the feasibility of self-learning of
      MLLMs in embodied environments:
318
319          • DG refers to the results obtained through direct generation from the initial MLLM without
320            any fine-tuning.
321          • SC (Wang et al., 2023a) represents an optimization method of MLLMs through self-
322            consistency. Specifically, we employ multiple chains of thought (CoT) to prompt an MLLM
323            to answer the same question, followed by majority voting. In our experiments, we utilize
               three different CoTs to guide the MLLM, ultimately voting for the most reasonable action.

            • LMSI (Huang et al., 2023) is a self-improvement method based on SC. It generates “high-

326           enables the LLM to iteratively improve its performance based on the voting mechanism,
327           and we extend this approach to MLLMs.
328
            • Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) involves multiple rounds of self-reflection, followed by
329
              self-optimization based on the reflection results. This method focuses on prompt optimiza-

462   5.5   H YPERPARAMETER A NALYSIS
463
464   Online Dataset Size. Since the MLLMs fine-tuning process is sensitive to the dataset size, we
465   explore the amount of interaction data required to achieve effective learning for embodied tasks. We
466   conduct multiple tests on this variable based on picking up tasks in the AI2-THOR environment.
467   The results are presented in Figure 3(a).
468   The size of dataset is described by the number of trajectories for a single task. For instance, dataset-
469   10k indicates that 10k trajectories are collected for a specific task, such as picking up an apple,
470   during online interactions. We evaluate the performance through the actor in terms of task success
471   rate. We can see that the performance of dataset-1k is close to that of dataset-10k. For dataset-10k, it
472   takes about two days to collect one epoch data. To balance experimental performance with sampling
473   efficiency, we opt to sample 1k trajectories per task in our experiments.
474   Based on this conclusion, we employ a data augmentation method during the actor training process.
475   Since MLLMs tend to prioritize text over images when making decisions, they often focus exces-
476   sively on the text prompt and overlook image comprehension. To address this issue, we shuffle the
477   action lists in the prompts of training data to provide multiple prompts for the same image. This ap-
478   proach not only increases the dataset size, but also strengthens the connection between the MLLM
479   policy and the observations.
480   Learning Rate. The learning rate is a critical factor, which prevents us from using the same MLLM
481   for the actor and critic. We test different learning rates for the actor and critic separately on picking
482   up tasks in the AI2-THOR, and the results are shown in Figure 3(b). We find that, due to the varying
483   sizes of the fine-tuning datasets, using a uniform learning rate inevitably leads to overfitting or un-
484   derfitting in one of the components, thereby impacting the overall performance of SELU. Therefore,
485   we ultimately use two MLLMs to construct the SELU framework, ensuring effective self-learning.
      In our experiments, the learning rate for the critic is set to 2e-6, while that for the actor is set to 2e-5.

516   6                    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
517
518   In this paper, we introduce SELU, a method for MLLMs to achieve self-learning in unknown en-
519   vironments. SELU facilitates interaction with the environment, analyzes interaction trajectories,
520   builds an online dataset, and performs coupled optimization of the actor and critic. We employ
521
      self-asking and hindsight relabeling to enhance the critic task evaluation capabilities. Ablation
      experiments demonstrate that relabeling significantly expands the critic task judgment range. By
522
      leveraging the principle that MLLMs possess stronger perceptual abilities than decision-making
523
      abilities, we improve the performance of the actor policy. We test SELU in the AI2-THOR and
524   VirtualHome environments, achieving critic improvements of approximately 28% and 30%, and
525   policy improvements of about 20% and 24%, respectively. Additionally, to validate the applicability
526   of SELU across different MLLMs, we evaluate it on Qwen-VL, resulting in a 23% performance
527   enhancement.
528
      One limitation of SELU is the lack of a detailed evaluation of the trajectories that complete the tasks.
529
      SELU can help embodied MLLM to self-learn how to accomplish tasks in unknown environments.
530   The next urgent problem to address is how to complete tasks more efficiently and optimize actions
531   more effectively. In future work, we will explore finer-grained critic signals to perform more accu-
532   rate quality assessments of trajectories, guiding embodied MLLMs to tackle more complex tasks,
533   like long-horizon combination tasks.

853   A.2.1    E NVIRONMENT D ETAILS
854
855   Figure 4 shows our experiment environments. Both environments restrict agents to only interact with
856   visible items, limiting their operational range to guarantee behavior plans realistic. Therefore, the
857   actor MLLM makes decisions based on first-person perspective input to ensure accuracy as Figure
858   4(a) and Figure 4(c) show. Given the limitations of the first-person view, the critic MLLM uses a
859   third-person perspective to evaluate the trajectory, reducing hallucinations and obtaining accurate
860
      scene information as Figure 4(b) and Figure 4(d) show.
861   The positioning of the third-person camera is crucial, as it should accurately capture the agent’s
862   position and the objects it interacts with. Any occlusion or interference can impair the MLLM’s
863   understanding of the image, thereby affecting the results of critic success detection and hindsight
      relabeling.
